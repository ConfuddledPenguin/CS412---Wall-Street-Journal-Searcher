Taiwan's Brokerage Industry Faces A Shakeout as Stock Prices Plummet

  • Author: Cynthia Owens
  • Date: 07/12/90

A five-month plunge in Taiwan's stock prices is putting pressure on the country's securities companies, and brokers say many of the firms could be out of business by year end. The first victim was Chao Chuen Securities Co., which closed Tuesday. Brokers in Taipei say the small firm was plagued by internal problems and its closure isn't likely in itself to drag down other firms.

However, Taiwan has fewer than 200 traded stocks and, with the closing of Chao Chuen, 357 securities companies, according to authorities in Taipei. With trading volume down more than 50% from the high levels earlier this year, the market can no longer support so many firms, brokers say.

"Without a doubt, Chao Chuen is really the first of many to come, but it's not the domino that will cause the others to go," says Peter Kurz, head of the Taiwan office of Baring Brothers & Co.

Taiwan has some of the lowest trading commissions in the world and some of the highest overhead costs. As individual investors have swarmed into the market in recent years, the number of securities companies vying for customers also has soared, rising to the current 357 from only 27 in May 1988, when the government lifted a long freeze on new brokerage houses.

Customer defaults and trading losses already have taken a toll on securities companies. And now, as volume continues to shrink, some may call it quits and others may collapse.

Still, Taiwan survived a shakeout in the securities industry in the early 1970s. However difficult the coming upheaval may be, brokers believe a loss of many firms would leave the industry leaner and better able to cope with a developing market.

Taiwan's weighted stock index has dropped more than 60% since its high point in February. It fell 6.3% yesterday.

Government regulations requiring each company to have a trading floor and special equipment, and to carry out its own stock settlements, keep overhead sky-high. Those expenses may be too much for some of the smaller firms.

An additional complication is the fact that a large percentage of stock transactions was funded by gray-market margin lending, which isn't regulated by the government and may not be backed by adequate capital.

In addition, some securities firms did their own margin lending for stock transactions. To compete for customers during the run-up in prices last year, many companies offered loans and held about 30% of the shares as collateral. When the market was booming, the system worked. Now, prices for most of those shares are far below the purchase price and customers have little incentive to pay off the loan. That apparently is one of the problems that brought down Chao Chuen, brokers say.
