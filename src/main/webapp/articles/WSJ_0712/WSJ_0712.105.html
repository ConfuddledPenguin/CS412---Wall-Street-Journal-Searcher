Japanese Quietly Expand Role In Chicago's Futures Markets

  • Author: Jeffrey Taylor
  • Date: 07/12/90

CHICAGO -- Plenty of Americans know that Japanese investors have gobbled up Columbia Pictures Entertainment Inc. and New York's Rockefeller Center. But with less fanfare, the Japanese are also pouring money into trading in the booming U.S. financial futures markets. It was only three years ago that Japanese securities firms began buying seats on Chicago's Board of Trade and Mercantile Exchange, the two biggest U.S. futures markets. Since then, Japanese trading here has increased sharply.

The Japanese may already account for as much as 40% of the volume in the bustling Eurodollar pit at the Chicago Merc, where financial futures based on the fluctuation of short-term interest rates are traded. Leo Melamed, chairman of the Chicago Merc's executive committee, has predicted that by 1992 a third of the Merc's total volume will come from the Japanese.

Yet while financial markets in Japan at times are given to speculation, Japanese tastes here tend toward futures based on bonds and interest rates, contracts frequently used to hedge the risk of relatively conservative fixed-income investments like U.S. Treasury bonds.

"Certainly, Japanese investors have invested heavily in U.S. Treasury bonds and related futures, but not much in U.S. equities or the corresponding futures," said Nobuaki Ohmura, executive vice president of Daiwa Securities America Inc., a unit of one of the "Big Four" Japanese securities firms.

And as a rule, Japanese trading strategies are more methodical and patient than those employed by American traders. John Mitchell, a veteran trader in Eurodollar futures at the Merc, spent 18 months working the Merc floor for Daiwa. "A few of the Japanese traders are gunslingers, with what you'd call American aggressiveness, but the vast majority pick a trend and ride it out, for better or worse," said Mr. Mitchell, now employed by Chicago-based O'Connor & Co. "They have more money, so they have more patience. They are less likely to bail out of a position if the price drops a little."

One reason for the caution is Japanese inexperience with futures and options, so-called derivative products whose values are linked to the performance of other investments. In Japan, futures markets, while extremely active, are nascent compared with Chicago's exchanges. And until 1987, the Japanese Ministry of Finance prohibited the country's institutional investors -- banks, pension funds and insurance companies -- from trading directly in U.S. futures markets.

Another factor, analysts say, is that the big Japanese investors tend to turn their money over to managers on the basis of Japanese corporate relationships, rather than performance. That's in contrast to the U.S., where competition for returns has forced many money managers to trade futures and options in ever more sophisticated and aggressive investing strategies.

"In Japan, the fund managers are still chosen more than anything based on historical relationships," said Nicholas Ronalds, deputy managing director of the Chicago Merc's Tokyo office. "When selection is done on that basis, performance can fall pretty low as a priority for the money manager."

Many big Japanese investors have been reluctant to dabble in more speculative and abstract instruments and slow to master complicated program trades that include heavy use of stock-index futures and stock options. That's changing, albeit slowly. Some Japanese firms are now trying to learn index-arbitrage strategies, forms of program trading involving large, computer-assisted stock trades tied to offsetting stock-index futures transactions.

For instance, Nomura Securities International Inc., the U.S. subsidiary of the world's largest securities firm, recently hired two former Kidder, Peabody & Co. executives to revamp its operations here. One of the goals of Max C. Chapman Jr., the new president of Nomura's U.S. unit, and Joseph R. Schmuckler, a program-trading specialist hired as senior vice president, is to improve the capability of Nomura's sleepy U.S. division to exploit futures and options markets.

A few Japanese institutional investors such as Nippon Credit Bank also are dabbling in complicated stock-index futures trades. "We started out a few years ago in a straightforward manner, buying and selling futures, using them primarily to hedge," said Atsushi Sato, a vice president in Nippon Credit's New York branch. "Since then, we've been adding the so-called arbitrage trades."

Other big Japanese investors have sought to acquire no-wait expertise by forming alliances with experienced U.S. futures and options trading firms. This week, for example, Mitsubishi Trust & Banking Corp., Japan's largest trust bank, announced a joint venture with a major U.S. trading concern, Chicago Research & Trading Group Ltd. Chicago Research, headed by Joseph Ritchie, uses sophisticated computer trading systems to execute tens of thousands of trades a day. By forming a joint venture, Mitsubishi gains access to Mr. Ritchie's expertise, and Chicago Research benefits from Mitsubishi's rich reserves of capital.

And while the volume of business done in stock-index futures and options by Japanese investors remains relatively small, that's far less true in the pits where Treasury-bond and Eurodollar futures are traded. Treasury bond futures are commitments to buy U.S. government bonds for the prevailing price at a given date in the future. Eurodollar futures, whose name refers to dollars held by investors outside the U.S., are based on interest rates.

Japanese trading in these products, which big investors use to manage risk, is increasing quickly, according to Japanese Ministry of Finance data. Analysts say this trading reflects more speculation in the futures and a growing understanding among Japanese investors of the need to hedge to protect their large holdings of U.S. bonds and their dollar-denominated accounts.

In the Eurodollar market, for instance, trading by Japan-based financial institutions rose to a monthly average of 875,000 contracts during the first quarter of 1990. That's up 18% from a 1989 first-quarter average of 743,000 contracts a month. About 90% of those contracts were traded on the Chicago Merc.

What's more, these figures don't include trading by U.S. branches and subsidiaries of Japanese institutions, and analysts estimate that on some days the U.S. branches can do nearly as much trading as investors based in Japan.

In Treasury bond futures, Japan-based investors are trading an average of about 568,000 contracts a month, up 49% from 380,000 during the first quarter of 1989. The Chicago Board of Trade accounts for about 95% of all those contracts.
