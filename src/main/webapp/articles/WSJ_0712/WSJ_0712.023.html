International: Japan's Economy Is Seen Growing Despite Slowdown

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/12/90

TOKYO -- Japan's economy will enter into a period of gradual slowdown, but it will still expand at a respectable 4.1% pace in fiscal 1990 after inflation is taken into account, a think tank predicted. Mitsubishi Research Institute said the Japanese economy, following sustained expansion, will move on a "plateau" in the fiscal year that began in April.

Nevertheless, it said, personal consumption will go up 4.0% with the help of income growth and will underpin the economy despite a lower rate of increase in corporate capital spending and housing investment.

Japan's economy has been expanding for 43 consecutive months through June, the second longest postwar period of expansion.

In fiscal 1990, Mitsubishi said, wholesale prices will edge up 1.7% and consumer prices 2.5%, and the Bank of Japan will maintain a tight credit stance. However, the research institute said, the central bank is unlikely to raise the official discount rate during this fiscal year from its present level of 5.25%.

"As long as oil prices and other overseas conditions don't show drastic changes," it said, "inflation, a source of most serious concern in the process of economic growth in fiscal 1990 and 1991, won't worsen to the point where it could seriously impede growth."
