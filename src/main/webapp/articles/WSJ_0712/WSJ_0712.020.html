Wednesday's Markets: Stocks Rally on Oil, 2nd-Quarter Earnings

  • Author: Christopher Winans and Craig Torres
  • Date: 07/12/90

NEW YORK -- Investors pushed stock prices sharply and broadly higher, inspired by positive second-quarter earnings reports and bullish news for oil stocks. The Dow Jones Industrial Average jumped 41.83 points to 2932.67 in active trading, coming within a few points of the record set June 15. Bond prices were essentially unchanged. The dollar rose against the Japanese yen and the German mark after Tuesday's sharp declines.

Traders said investors welcomed relatively positive earnings announcements for big stocks. Favorable earnings news from Motorola, which rose 7/8 to 86 3/8, helped spark interest in other technology issues.

Intel advanced 1 3/4 to 48, for example.

Also helping to lift stock prices were strong earnings reports from such companies as Federal National Mortgage Association and Walgreen, along with positive analysts' recommendations for stocks such as Humana. Finally, late in the day, the market surge was extended by a wave of futures-related program buying-purchases of baskets of stocks with offsetting sales of futures contracts.

"People are gaining confidence that the second quarter wasn't a disaster," said money manager James Awad, president of BMI Capital. Also boosting the stock market, he said, was the successful completion Tuesday of a sale of savings-and-loan bailout bonds.

Abby Joseph Cohen, chief strategist at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Research, said investors should continue to be pleasantly surprised by earnings for the just-ended quarter, noting that the level of write-offs by corporations should be lower than in the first quarter. While she thinks that earnings disappointments probably will hurt the market later this year, that danger doesn't exist yet, she said.

But right now, Ms. Cohen said, to achieve the consensus of analysts' earnings estimates for the year on the Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index, corporate profits would have to jump roughly 40% in the third and fourth quarters. "For that to happen, we'd have to be in a robust economy, and that's not the case," she said.

Indeed, investors remain poised to punish stocks that deliver bad profit reports.

"The dark side of the whole picture is that if there is an earnings disappointment, the stocks hit air pockets and down they go," said Holly Stark, head of trading at Dalton, Greiner, Hartman, Maher & Co. Among stocks that fell on earnings news were Hilton Hotels, down 1 1/2 to 51 1/2, and Oracle Systems, which fell 2 1/2 to 19 7/8 on 9.4 million shares.

Some analysts said the stock market's broad move upward supports the notion that investors are continuing to focus attention on earnings and not on interest rates. In this regard, the gain was encouraging, analysts said, because it came in the absence of such artificial influences as end-of-the-quarter portfolio window-dressing.

A sign that institutions were more active is that trading in large blocks of stock picked up. On the New York Stock Exchange, 3,529 blocks of 10,000 shares or more traded, compared with 3,013 the day before.

The oil stocks added a boost to the market, rising on news that suggested oil prices would continue to firm with the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries showing some resolve to bolster prices. Also, there was a report that crudeoil inventories are declining more sharply than expected.

Another positive influence on stock prices was the peaceful conclusion of the economic summit in Houston, said Brian Fabbri, chief economist at Midland Montagu Economics.

"It ended with the group making the same wonderful, very anticipated points, all positive," Mr. Fabbri said, "so there was no feeling of contention."

Despite the rally, William Kufta, senior vice president at Pitcairn Financial Management, said, "We would not go fully invested at this point." He said inflation is still too high to allow the Federal Reserve to lower interest rates, which he said is needed to get stocks higher.

As a hedge against inflation, Pitcairn has 18% of its $1 billion stock portfolio dedicated to energy stocks, Mr. Kufta said.

In major market action:

Stock prices soared in active trading. Volume was 162.2 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange. Advancing issues outnumbered decliners, 971 to 506.

Bond prices were unchanged. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond stood at 8.56%.

The dollar rose.

In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6560 marks and 148.50 yen, up from 1.6425 marks and 148.30 yen Tuesday.
