Five Senators Tied to S&L Scandal Hit By More Criticism; Bush Defends His Son

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 07/12/90

WASHINGTON -- Five senators came under renewed fire for their involvement in the savings-and-loan scandal, while President Bush voiced "great confidence" in the "honor and integrity" of his son who also is under investigation for his role in a thrift failure. Archibald Cox, chairman of the self-styled citizens' lobby Common Cause and a onetime Watergate special prosecutor, argued that the senators -- Alan Cranston (D., Calif.), Dennis DeConcini (D., Ariz.), John Glenn (D., Ohio), John McCain (R., Ariz.), and Donald Riegle (D., Mich.) -- violated the Code of Ethics for Government Service in 1987. The senators accepted campaign contributions from Charles Keating, the chairman of Lincoln Savings & Loan's parent company, and then met with regulators to ask about their examination of Lincoln.

In a brief filed with the Senate Ethics Committee, Mr. Cox said the lawmakers created the "appearance" that they acted "in an official capacity in response to gifts or other benefits." Mr. Cox, who served as U.S. solicitor general in the early 1960s, also asserted the senators violated provisions of the ethics code that public officials should "never discriminate unfairly by the dispensing of special favors or privileges to anyone."

The senators have denied any wrongdoing, arguing that they were only looking into a constituent's problems, and that they didn't delay the takeover of Lincoln. Lincoln, based in Irvine, Calif., was seized by the government in April 1989. Its failure is expected to cost taxpayers $2 billion.

The ethics committee is investigating the actions of the senators. Common Cause said it filed the brief "to address arguments that Senate rules and standards do not explicitly cover the actions involved."

In a news release, Sen. McCain said Mr. Cox "is simply saying people should be judged guilty by association." The other senators couldn't be reached for comment.

Meanwhile, Mr. Bush was asked if he agreed with his son, Neil, who has suggested that savings-and-loan regulators are conducting a vendetta against him because he is the president's son. Neil Bush is being investigated by federal regulators and lawmakers because of his past relationship with the failed Silverado Banking, Savings & Loan Association in Denver. Its losses from speculative loans and insider transactions are expected to cost taxpayers as much as $1 billion.

"As president, I'm determined to stay out of this and let it {the system} work and let it work fairly," Mr. Bush said. He added: "I have confidence in the honor and integrity of my son. And if the system finds he's done something wrong, he will be the first to step up and do what's right."
