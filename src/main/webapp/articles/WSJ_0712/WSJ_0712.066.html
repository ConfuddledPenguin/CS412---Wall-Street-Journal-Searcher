International: Japan to Resume Lending Slowly To China After Lifting of Curbs

  • Author: Cynthia Owens
  • Date: 07/12/90

HONG KONG -- Following Japanese Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu's announcement that his government will resume lending to China, analysts calculate it could take several months to secure funding for individual loan proposals. Of the total package of 810 billion yen ($5.36 billion), about 120 billion yen for 17 projects is likely to be approved by next March.

Commercial lending may be equally slow to pick up. Japanese bankers remain cautious about committing new money to projects in China because of the sharp slowdown in the Chinese economy and the Beijing government's tight rein on foreign borrowing, instituted as part of an austerity program in late 1988.

Since August, larger Japanese banks have been moving back to China, finding ways to circumvent Tokyo's restrictions to participate in the few loans available to high-quality borrowers. Many of the loans have carried Chinese government guarantees, other special guarantees, or were made to the Hong Kong subsidiaries of Chinese companies. Japan accounts for about half of China's more than $40 billion of foreign debt.

As Tokyo lifts the restrictions, smaller banks may begin to join some China loans. When combined with the resumption of official aid, that lending freedom may be positive for China trade because more foreign exchange will be flowing through the country, foreign bankers in Hong Kong say.

In the past year, financing has been arranged for several top-rated Chinese borrowers. Japanese banks have participated in loans to mainland airlines, China International Trust & Investment Corp. and infrastructure-related projects. A consortium of 67 Japanese banks also activated a $2 billion standby credit approved in 1985. Other relatively big projects for big-name borrowers may be funded this year, some with Japanese participation, but owing to Beijing's austerity program, small and midsize loans will remain few in number.

"There is a common desire among Japanese banks for the normalization of economic relations with China," said an official at a large Japanese bank in Tokyo. But he stressed that banks would take a conservative stance. That view was underscored Tuesday, when a $30 million loan for China Resources (Holdings) Co. was signed: No Japanese banks participated.

Japanese banks' caution, like that of most lenders to China, stems largely from doubts about the ability of some projects to produce returns and possible defaults by loan guarantors.

Poor economic conditions have only added to banks' worries. China said in a report issued this week that the country's financial situation is grim and that the government must continue to tighten finance and credit.

For Japanese banks, caution in China is a change of pace. Until the killings in Beijing in June last year that triggered the loan moratorium, they often undercut other foreign banks on loans to China, according to bankers in Hong Kong. These bankers say that if the Japanese return to their old habits, the result would be lower interest rates on China loans.

"It's not wise for them to rush back in and flood the market with cheap money," said an economist at a U.S. bank in Hong Kong, adding that current rates "reflect the reality in China right now."

Interest rates on China loans rose about 50 basis points, to about 75 basis points above the London interbank offered rate, or Libor, soon after the June 1989 crackdown, and they remain near that level. (A basis point is one hundredth of a percentage point. Libor is the rate banks in London charge one another for U.S. dollar loans.)
