Chicago Merc Allows 2 Convicted Felons To Continue Trading for Own Accounts

  • Author: Scott McMurray
  • Date: 07/12/90

CHICAGO -- The Chicago Mercantile Exchange will allow two traders convicted on felony charges in federal court Monday to continue trading futures contracts for their own accounts at the Merc pending a hearing on exchange disciplinary charges that could result in their being expelled from the exchange. The decision reflects the widespread belief among Merc members that the two traders in Swiss franc futures, Robert D. Mosky and Danny L. Scheck, were found guilty of relatively minor trading transgressions and were unjustly singled out for prosecution by the government, the traders' attorneys said. The Merc said the two couldn't handle customer orders.

The Merc's decision is a "reflection of their sense of the nature of the charges" against the traders, said Harvey Silets, Mr. Scheck's attorney.

The two traders were found guilty in U.S. District Court here Monday on a total of eight felony charges of violating the Commodity Exchange Act. The traders -- along with fellow trader David Zatz, who was acquitted of two felony charges -- had been charged with more than 100 felony counts in the first trial to result from the government's sweeping undercover probe of futures-trading fraud at the Merc and the Chicago Board of Trade.

The eight guilty counts, seven against Mr. Mosky and one against Mr. Scheck, involved $200 in customer profits, according to the traders' lawyers.

The jury couldn't reach a verdict on more than two-thirds of the charges, including a tough racketeering conspiracy charge against Mr. Mosky. The government said its initial decision is to retry the traders on the deadlocked charges.

In filing disciplinary charges against Mr. Mosky and Mr. Scheck late yesterday, the Merc board of governors said it will "carefully consider the gravity of the offenses of which the defendants were convicted." A Merc spokesman said there is no exchange prohibition against convicted felons owning exchange memberships or trading.

Mr. Mosky, 34 years old, a broker in the Merc's Swiss franc futures trading pit, and Mr. Scheck, 28, a so-called local Swiss franc trader who didn't handle customer orders, were each charged by the Merc with one major and one minor violation of catchall exchange rules.

The major offense, for committing an act that is "substantially detrimental to the interest or welfare of the exchange," carries a maximum penalty of expulsion, a $250,000 fine, or both. The minor offense, for "conduct which has a manifest tendency to impair the dignity or the good name of the exchange," has a maximum penalty of a one-year suspension, a $25,000 fine, or both.

The board of governors' hearing on the charges isn't likely to be held for a month or two, the Merc spokesman said.

The traders could also be subject to sanctions from the Commodity Futures Trading Commission based on the guilty findings, once they have been sentenced.
