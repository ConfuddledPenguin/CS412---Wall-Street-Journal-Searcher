SEC Plans To Impose Fund Rules --- Agency to Act If Ads For Investment Trusts Of Banks Are Allowed

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 07/12/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission said it would move quickly to impose securities regulations on banks if they are allowed to advertise trust department investment funds to compete with mutual funds. The SEC said it plans to issue a mandate forcing the banks to abide by securities laws if they are cleared to advertise or market the funds, which like mutual funds, pool money into a single investment vehicle. But even if the SEC took such a step, agency officials concede that banks would be held to more lax regulatory standards than mutual funds because the SEC couldn't apply all its securities laws.

Top officials at the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, which issued the plan to allow such advertising, said a decision on the proposal is at least two months off. "No final decision has been made," said Frank Maguire, a top aide at the office.

The comptroller's plan set off a firestorm immediately after it was proposed in February. While much of the proposal contains technical changes to banking rules, one measure would allow banks for the first time to advertise the performance of their trust accounts.

That brought strong criticism from the $1 trillion mutual fund industry, which sees banks competing in its almost-exclusive domain. Yesterday, Matthew Fink, general counsel for the Investment Company Institute, said the mutual fund trade group would sue the comptroller's office if the rule is passed.

Congress is jumping into the act too. Rep. Edward Markey (D., Mass.), who heads the House telecommunications and finance subcommittee, said he plans to hold hearings on the proposal when Congress returns from its summer recess.

Banks have long boasted that their 2,900 investment trusts, which manage more than $100 billion belonging primarily to wealthy individuals, on average outperform mutual funds. They would love to compete with Fidelity Investments and other mutual fund groups for the huge amount of profitable investment business they can't now reach.

The comptroller's proposal would appear to open the door. The agency is simply trying to streamline existing regulations that allow bank trust funds to be mentioned only in general terms in advertising, said Deputy Comptroller Dean Miller. Moreover, SEC rules cover much of the same territory.

But the way the proposal was crafted, the mutual-fund industry claims, would allow banks to compare performance numbers with those of mutual funds. That would set the two head to head, the Investment Company Institute contends.

SEC rules would take some measures to prevent banks from getting too much of an advantage. For example, the regulator has made it clear that the banks immediately would be required to register with the SEC under the Investment Company Act, which covers mutual funds. "We would like to make such an interpretive release available to banks prior to the time that they may take steps that might result in violation" of securities law, SEC Chairman Richard Breeden wrote to Comptroller Robert Clarke.

But banks are exempted from some securities statutes, so the rules would be more lenient. For example, the SEC wouldn't regulate the sales practices of banks as it now monitors similar moves by mutual-fund salesmen, said an SEC spokeswoman. "We couldn't have the hands-on regulatory control," she said.

Even if the proposal is approved by Mr. Clarke, it's tough to determine how it might affect the mutual fund industry. "I don't think anybody at the SEC has been able to sit down and figure out who might win out from a competitive point of view," said Kathryn McGrath, who until recently headed the SEC's investment management division.

Still, "it may shift the competitive advantage somewhat in trying to attract pension plan money," said Ms. McGrath, who is now with the law firm Morgan Lewis & Bockius. "That could make life tougher competitively for mutual funds that go after that end of the market."

But banking industry officials argue that the whole issue is overblown. "It's been blown way out of proportion," said James McLaughlin, the American Banking Association's director of trust and securities. "It doesn't mean the comptroller would allow banks to go willy nilly to run around and sell these funds."

He said the comptroller may decide to hold back the controversial advertising measure and move the other technical provisions along.
