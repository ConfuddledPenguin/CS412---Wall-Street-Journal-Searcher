Anti-Crime Bill Wins Passage In the Senate --- Measure Includes Provisions Aimed at Invigorating Probes of Thrift Fraud

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 07/12/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate overwhelmingly approved a wide-ranging anti-crime bill that includes a partial ban on semiautomatic "assault weapons" and numerous provisions aimed at invigorating the investigation and prosecution of savings-and-loan fraud. The omnibus bill, which passed 94-6, also would expand the federal death penalty to cover 34 crimes, curb the constitutional appeals process for death-row inmates, and provide $900 million in federal assistance to local law-enforcement agencies in the coming fiscal year -- twice the amount proposed by the Bush administration.

Eager to appear tough on the widespread looting of S&Ls, senators increased penalties for financial fraud, including a new statute that would provide up to life imprisonment for high-level thrift executives who conspire with three or more other violators and derive more than $5 million from their crimes. Other provisions would provide prosecutors with broader authorization to use civil seizure and forfeiture laws in bank and S&L fraud cases.

The Senate also authorized new spending of $162.5 million for each of the next three years to hire more federal investigators and prosecutors devoted strictly to financial-institution cases. That amount would be in addition to $50 million appropriated for such cases for the current fiscal year, which ends Sept. 30. In addition, the Senate authorized funds to hire 2,500 new federal agents and 480 prosecutors to deal with drug trafficking and other crimes.

The House is considering omnibus crime legislation of its own, including various S&L enforcement provisions. But it isn't clear when the full House will vote on these measures. Equally unclear is whether President Bush would back a crime package that includes the Senate's three-year ban on nine types of assault weapons.

The Senate's rush to provide new money for thrift-fraud prosecutions by the Justice Department came as the House Appropriations Committee voted to give the Treasury Department's Secret Service expanded authority to initiate its own investigations of S&L-related crimes. The provision is attached to a fiscal 1991 bill funding the Treasury Department and is likely to touch off a turf war with the Federal Bureau of Investigation, an arm of the Justice Department that has tried to stop efforts by the Secret Service to become more involved in reducing the current backlog of thousands of cases.

Reflecting an apparent decision by the White House, the Treasury officially opposes the expanded authority. But behind the scenes, the Secret Service has promised allies in Congress that it can make as many as 100 agents available immediately, and 300 within the next fiscal year.

The Senate's election-year anti-crime package, which seemed dead only several weeks ago, jumped back to life with a high-voltage shock of S&L rhetoric. "We need to be cracking down on the orgy of fraud and lawbreaking that occurred in the savings-and-loan crisis," said Sen. Patrick Leahy (D., Vt.). The hefty batch of thrift-fraud provisions was added to the omnibus bill at the last minute, with almost no partisan bickering.

Last month, a filibuster orchestrated by the National Rifle Association had threatened to kill the entire package. But the powerful NRA repeatedly was unable to muster a majority to eliminate the prohibition on assault weapons. Meanwhile, quiet negotiations led by Sens. Joseph Biden (D., Del.) and Strom Thurmond (R., S.C.) kept the bill alive until the almost desperate desire by lawmakers to take action on the S&L issue assured its passage.

Many of the S&L-related provisions added to the Senate bill, such as the provision for life imprisonment for "S&L kingpins," appeared to be largely symbolic. Others included those that merely formalize special thrift-fraud task forces already created by the Justice Department.

The crime bill also would facilitate speedier restitution to victims of S&L fraud, allow for public disclosure of regulatory enforcement actions, and permit the government to undo fraudulent transactions made by thrift executives. Another provision would encourage individuals to report S&L fraud to authorities by providing rewards for information leading to convictions or civil recoveries. In civil cases, the amount of such rewards would be tied to the value of assets recovered and could total hundreds of thousands of dollars.

William Seidman, chairman of the Resolution Trust Corp., urged Congress to pass the crime bill, saying it would "prove to be a formidable deterrent to financial institution fraud and abuse."

In a hearing before a House Judiciary subcommittee, Mr. Seidman also defended the government's record in pursuing crooked S&L directors and officers. The agency recovered more than $100 million last year in liability claims against S&L executives, he said, and an additional $200 million in settlements and judgments in the first half of 1990.

The Senate dropped from the crime package a provision that would have allowed, under certain circumstances, the use of illegally gathered evidence in criminal trials.
