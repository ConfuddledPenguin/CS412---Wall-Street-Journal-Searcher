Overseas Investment Scams Cost $1.1 Billion in 2 Years

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/12/90

WASHINGTON -- Fraudulent international investment schemes cost Americans $1.1 billion in 1988 and 1989, the North American Securities Administrators Association said in a report to be presented to Congress today. "International scams are now the fastest growing fraud problem with which state securities agencies are dealing," said Kansas Securities Commissioner M. Douglas Mays. Fueling the growth are poor international regulatory cooperation and increased interest in overseas investing.

Three out of four schemes involve U.S.-based promoters, falsely claiming to be engaged in overseas investment programs, which include precious metals, currency speculation, bonds and foreign banking instruments, and "norisk" high-yield certificates of deposit.

In contrast, overseas-based scams usually involve high-pressure telephone sales operations, based in Costa Rica, Panama or bogus offshore banks in the South Pacific, according to the 100-page study to be presented at a subcommittee hearing on investment fraud, chaired by Rep. Douglas Barnard Jr. (D., Ga.).

International investment scams were the topic of a recent Wall Street Journal column, Your Money Matters.
