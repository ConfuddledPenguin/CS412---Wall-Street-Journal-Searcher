Marketing & Media: CBS Profit Rose By 16% to Record In Second Quarter

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 07/12/90

NEW YORK -- CBS Inc. posted a 16% increase in second-quarter net income, helped by broadcasting the National Basketball Association playoff and championship games and by a drop in programming costs from a year earlier. The profit was a second-quarter record, but CBS said the prospect for continued growth for the balance of the year was "uncertain" amid a sluggish economy and increased costs for rights to major sports events.

Profit rose to $138 million, or $5.36 a share, from $118.8 million, or $4.62 a share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 7% to $828.8 million from $771.8 million.

CBS, the No. 3-rated network in prime time for the third consecutive year, is gambling $3.6 billion over the next few years on broadcast rights to big sports events, including four-year major league baseball and football packages that will begin this year. Each four-year deal is priced at $1.06 billion.

CBS Broadcast Group, which includes the network, TV and radio stations and news and sports divisions and is CBS's only major subsidiary, posted a 31% gain in pretax operating profit to $180.9 million from $137.9 million.

Laurence A. Tisch, CBS president and chief executive officer, said in a statement that the gain was because of "improved" results at each of the three broadcast divisions, the television network, television stations and radio. The earnings at the television network, he said, were "enhanced" by an expanded number of NBA playoff and championship games and decreased entertainment programming costs related mainly to the writers' strike which pushed costs into the second quarter of 1989."

The 1989 second quarter was unusual because of the writers' strike, which resulted in a delay of the 1988-89 prime-time season. As a result, programming costs increased in that quarter because the networks broadcast more original programming than usual. Traditionally, the networks begin airing series repeats in the second quarter.

CBS's earnings "were much better than I expected," said Jessica Rief, an analyst at First Boston Corp. Nevertheless, Ms. Rief said she did not expect third and fourth quarter results would meet year-earlier levels.

Commenting on the frenzied one-week period when advertisers buy commercial time in advance of the 1990-91 prime time season, Ms. Rief said, "CBS took in $100 million less in up-front advertising this year compared to last year and, let's face it, they're getting increasing competition from Fox" Broadcasting Co. Fox, a unit of News Corp., is increasing the number of nights it programs to five from three in September.

Moreover, Ms. Rief noted, the NBA, which helped CBS in the second quarter, recently signed a deal to be broadcast on National Broadcasting Co.'s network. NBC is a unit of General Electric Co.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, CBS stock closed at $198.50 a share, up 62.5 cents.
