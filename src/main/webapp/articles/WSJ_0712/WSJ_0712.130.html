Heard on the Street: Embattled PS of New Mexico Gets Just Whiff Of an Offer, Stock Spurts Two Points to 11 3/4

  • Author: David D. Medina
  • Date: 07/12/90

A suitor's "expression of interest" in Public Service Co. of New Mexico yesterday gave Wall Street one more reason to believe that this battered stock may finally be worth investors' notice. The utility's shares surged after it disclosed that Wheeler Peak Capital Corp., a local venture-capital concern, has floated the idea of acquiring PS New Mexico for $12 a share, plus a sliver of equity in the continuing company. The stock closed at 11 3/4, up 2.

Wheeler Peak appears to be a one-man operation. That man is Albert J. Robison, a former senior vice president and chief financial officer for PS New Mexico, which like Wheeler Peak is based in Albuquerque.

The 49-year-old Mr. Robison, who retired two years ago, was traveling and couldn't be reached. He has, however, been in touch with Southwestern Public Service Co. in Amarillo and other potential investors about PS New Mexico. A Southwestern PS official late yesterday confirmed that company's talks with Wheeler Peak and its interest in certain PS New Mexico assets.

After a trading halt for the early afternoon news, the utility's stock resumed trading at 11, up one point from the previous trade, but the shares posted further gains later. Wheeler Peak's overture was seen as likely to flush out other interested buyers, and PS New Mexico is more than ready to consider offers.

The down-in-the-dumps company previously hired Morgan Stanley to help it decide by summer's end whether to sell any or all of its operations, said John Ackerman, PS New Mexico's chief executive officer. "I told my work force that my own personal direction is not to pursue a purchase," he said.

Public Service Co. of Colorado is often thought of as a natural to acquire PS New Mexico. The Colorado utility needs extra power and already is connected with PS New Mexico's system. A spokesman for PS Colorado said company policy doesn't allow comment on mergers and acquisitions.

PS New Mexico, pummeled by an ill-fated diversification move and excess capacity, has suspended common dividends, cut its staff by 20% and has sold off nonutility assets. A major customer didn't renew a $110 million-a-year contract and regulators ordered the already reeling company to reduce its rates. PS New Mexico's common stock, which peaked three years ago at 39 1/4 a share, has been languishing at about 10 recently.

But there's growing sentiment on Wall Street that PS New Mexico may be through the worst. For investors willing to hold on for a while, the utility's belt-tightening combined with moderate but steady growth in electricity demand in its service area may breathe life back into the moribund stock, some analysts say.

"All the damage has been done," says Ed Tirello of Smith Barney. He cites as a turning point the recent changes in top management at the utility. Former chairman, president and chief executive officer Jerry D. Geist, who had been intimately associated with PS New Mexico's troublesome extra capacity and diversification, resigned last month. Mr. Ackerman, former head of the utility's gas company unit, became president and chief executive.

"The prior manager had alienated the political machine in the state," says Mr. Tirello, adding: "The new management has the opportunity to mend that."

The New Mexico Public Service Commission has been wholly unsympathetic to the utility's excess power problems, refusing to let rate payers bear the cost of unneeded energy, particularly the utility's $1.2 billion investment in the Palo Verde nuclear project in Arizona.

Mr. Tirello says a turnaround hinges on the company's ability to sell its excess electricity at a profit, which he projects will happen by 1995 as the Southwest grows and power becomes tighter. Right now, the company loses 1.5 cents to three cents on every wholesale 1,000 kilowatts sold, according to analysts' estimates.

Alan Berro, utility analyst with the mutual-fund giant Fidelity Investments, is betting on a much quicker rebound, even without a buy-out. Fidelity has bought 1,350,000 shares on the theory that PS New Mexico will be out of the hole in two years. "If the stock doubles at that time, I'm a hero," says Mr. Berro. He won't say how much the stock cost but indicated it wasn't too far from $10 a share.

The two-year projection, Mr. Berro concedes, is his best-case outlook. He's willing to sit tight even if it takes longer. "I have a long-time horizon," he says.

A few analysts are ready to throw in the towel now. Barry Abramson with Prudential-Bache says PS New Mexico's woes are intractable. "Their financial progress is stuck and can't go forward until the market conditions improve," says Mr. Abramson, who recommends selling. He doesn't see a stock-price improvement until 1993.

That's too long to wait in his opinion. "I just don't see the light at the end of the tunnel," he says, "because the tunnel is such a long tunnel."
