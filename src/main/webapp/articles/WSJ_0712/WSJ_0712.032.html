Commodities: Copper Futures Rise Sharply on Metal Purchases In London, More Reports of Production Problems

  • Author: John Valentine
  • Date: 07/12/90

NEW YORK -- Copper futures prices rose sharply after large purchases of the metal in London and continued reports of production problems. The July contract, whose last trading day is July 27, rose 5.75 cents a pound to $1.2435, while the most-active September contract was up 5.25 cents to $1.22.

There was active Japanese and German buying of copper in London yesterday as well as German purchases of futures contracts that had recently been sold short, analysts and trade sources said.

German buying of futures contracts in London was associated with a rumor that a major German metals company was covering its positions in connection with a failed attempt to buy the Cananea copper mine in Mexico, analysts said.

According to the unconfirmed reports, the German company sold copper contracts to lock in prices in case of a market decline after it bought the Mexican mine. But the company's bid was rejected by Mexican officials, and so it repurchased its futures contracts, helping the copper price to rise, analysts and trade sources said.

The Mexican government took over the Compania Minera Cananea last August, declared it bankrupt and fired its workers just before they were to begin a strike. Cananea produces about 140,000 tons of refined copper annually and is Mexico's largest copper producer.

The Mexican government has been trying to sell the mine, which returned to production under government control in November. According to one analyst, the mine has encountered some production problems and is functioning at about 90% of capacity.

Late in the day, there was a report that workers at Minero Peru, which accounts for about 75% of Peru's copper production, had voted to strike Monday. Peru is the world's sixth-largest copper producer.

Analyst Richard Hirsch of Sharps Pixley Inc., New York, noted that the big Bougainville copper mine in Papua New Guinea remains out of operation. Workers for Asarco Inc., a major U.S. producer, have been on strike for almost two weeks, "and now it appears that Peru is about to be hit with a strike. There are labor problems in Zambia as well and a recent cave-in in a Chilean mine," he added.

Coupled with those production problems, he said, has been added demand. "Japan needs copper, and so does Germany, because the economies of both countries are stronger than had been expected," Mr. Hirsch said.

Another analyst observed that while American demand for copper has softened, demand world-wide has remained strong. The market is also strong technically, he said, and this has brought in computer-led funds whose buying has been a major support for futures prices.

In other markets yesterday:

GRAINS and SOYBEANS: Good weather and a forecast of more of the same forced grain futures prices lower in a continuation of last week's price slide. Though farmers were late planting corn and soybeans this year, analysts said fears that the crop would be severely affected aren't materializing. "The crop may be tight, it may be low, but it's not going to be a disaster, and that's why we have a price break," said Mickey Luth, analyst for Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. in Chicago. Prices of corn, soybean and wheat futures declined. Analysts said extremely low demand also is a factor. "The demand for wheat is abysmal," said Katharina Zimmer, analyst for Merrill Lynch in New York. "The Soviets are not expected to be in the market for another month or two, the Chinese have bought what they need, and the Egyptians have no money." A government crop report on wheat is due today, and analysts said the wheat market may be volatile in anticipation of the report.

PRECIOUS METALS: Futures prices for gold and platinum declined, while those for silver were slightly higher. August delivery gold was off $1.50 an ounce at $355.60. October platinum declined $7.60 an ounce to $481.30. September silver was up 2.2 cents an ounce to $4.8970. According to one analyst, the most significant development in gold was that the price didn't continue to drop sharply following Tuesday's declines, which were prompted by Middle Eastern selling of the metal. Gold currently is a narrowly traded market because there isn't any real incentive for traders to buy or sell aggressively. Not only is there concern about further Middle Eastern selling, but producers are poised to sell on any rally, the analyst said. As for silver, he said, the price is relatively very low, prompting little interest in selling the metal. At the same time, there's no real demand for it either, he said.

COFFEE: Futures prices fell back from early advances and ended lower. The September contract was off 0.50 cent at 88 cents a pound. Initial demand for contracts was prompted by reports that a Colombian coffee official made an unexpected visit to Brazil, which gave rise to speculation that discussions on restoring global export quotas might be renewed, said Judith Ganes, an analyst for Merrill Lynch. The quotas were suspended a year ago. Brazil recently called for a meeting of the member nations of the International Coffee Agreement, which will be held July 23, the analyst noted. "When the visit of the Colombian official was reported, traders apparently felt that there might be more substance to the speculation of reviving quotas," she said. However, Ms. Ganes noted that the new Brazilian administration has been moving more toward a free market, and it didn't seem in character that it would press for coffee export quotas. "The larger Brazilian exporters not only don't want export quotas, they'd like to see the whole International Coffee Agreement abolished," Ms. Ganes said. "Smaller exporters, however, would like to see them restored because they have been hurt by the plunge in prices that followed the suspension of quotas."
