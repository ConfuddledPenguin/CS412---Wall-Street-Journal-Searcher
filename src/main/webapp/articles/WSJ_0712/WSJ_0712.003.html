Marketing & Media: U.S. Companies, Baby Bells Plug In To Growing Cable Market in the U.K.

  • Author: Joann S. Lublin
  • Date: 07/12/90

LONDON -- Britain's infant cable-television industry is turning into nearly an all-American enterprise. With the last three franchises awarded this week, U.S. companies now own or hold majority stakes in more than 73% of the 135 U.K. cable franchises. American cable-TV operators and regional Bell telephone companies probably will spend more than $5 billion to build an industry stymied for years by skittish local investors and scarce programming. Optimists believe that the Yankee investment and marketing know-how could help raise cable-TV's penetration from today's scant 1.4% of British television households to 19%, or four million households, by 1995 -- still far below the more than 55% level seen in the U.S.

U.S. money started pouring into Britain about 18 months ago, after regulators loosened limits on foreign control of cable operations. Among the U.S. concerns committing large sums are: U S West Inc., U.S. Cable Corp., Jones Intercable Inc., Pacific Telesis Group, Nynex Corp. and Comcast Corp.

American cable-TV operators, facing an oversaturated domestic market, see Britain as a critical beachhead for future expansion Europewide.

Cash-rich Baby Bells view the U.K. as a testing ground for both Continental Europe and for expected future deregulation at home. Unlike the U.S. and much of Europe, Britain lets cable operators offer both television and phone services on a limited basis; so far, it has been unprofitable. Yet "you don't have to calculate in the telephone business to make the case that cable will be a very profitable business in the U.K." within a decade, argues Royce Caldwell, Southwestern Bell Corp.'s senior vice president for corporate development. It owns significant portions of six British cable-TV franchises.

The invasion from America carries risks, however. It's dubious how much of a foothold Britain provides for the rest of Europe. In many other European nations, cable television has spread further than in the U.K. but remains publicly owned and operated -- effectively closing lucrative markets such as West Germany. Meanwhile, U.S. legislative moves to bar foreign ownership of U.S. cable operations could have repercussions.

There's a much bigger worry for American players in the British cable-TV industry: the competitive threat from the faster installation and more aggressive promotion of "direct-to-home" satellite television. Sky Television, owned by Rupert Murdoch's News Corp., had a shaky start last year but claims nearly one million U.K. and Irish homes currently get its channels through satellite-receiving dishes.

In the U.K., most cable operators won't complete wiring their franchise areas until "a very large chunk of the potential audience already is receiving satellite television direct to home," warns Caroline Bentley, a director at GAH, London business-strategy consultants.

Ironically, it was Sky Television's arrival and availability over cable-TV wires that breathed new life into the industry. Several U.S.-backed cable operators have introduced packages that include channels from both Sky TV and its new rival British Satellite Broadcasting Ltd. -- at prices less than renting the receiving equipment from either satellite service.

This approach, along with aggressive American gimmicks such as tough sales incentives and free service for limited periods, is winning more subscribers for some cable-TV companies, such as East London Telecommunications Ltd., a joint venture between Pacific Telesis and Jones Intercable.

But despite its subscriber gains, East London Telecommunications finds door-to-door solicitation and direct-mail circulars "haven't worked as well as they do in the States," concedes Managing Director Gerald Yutkin.

Yet, Mr. Yutkin says Americans "wouldn't be putting that much time and money and effort" into the British cable-TV business unless they believed it could grow faster than the U.S. industry did. But "it's just going to take longer" here, he says. "It's tough. It's very tough."
