What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/12/90

THE SENATE VOTED to approve comprehensive anti-crime legislation. The measure, which passed 94-6, includes a partial ban on semiautomatic "assault weapons" and numerous provisions aimed at invigorating the investigation and prosecution of savings-and-loan fraud. The bill also would expand the federal death penalty to cover 34 crimes, curb the constitutional appeals process for death-row inmates, and provide $900 million in federal assistance to local law-enforcement agencies in the coming fiscal year -- twice the expenditure that has been proposed by the Bush administration.

The senators increased penalties for financial fraud, including a statute providing up to life imprisonment for highlevel thrift executives who conspire with three or more other violators and derive over $5 million from their crimes.

---

Hard-liner Ligachev failed in his bid for a top job in the Soviet Communist Party, marking a clear victory for Kremlin leader Gorbachev, who tried but was unable to keep the staunch conservative off the ballot at a party congress. Ligachev lost to a Gorbachev-backed candidate, Vladimir Ivashko, in the race for deputy leader of the party.

Soviet coal miners staged a 24-hour walkout to demand that the government resign. Hundreds of thousands of miners took part, and a strike organizer claimed that 300,000 would halt work in western Siberia alone.

---

Pro-Sandinista strikers removed barricades from several streets in Managua after the government of Nicaragua's President Chamorro indicated a willingness to negotiate an end to 10 days of often-violent walkouts. Strikers are demanding a wage boost and want the government to give up plans to relinquish state-owned firms and farms.

---

A study is expected to be released today estimating the amount of radiation emitted from a World War II weapons plant, the Hanford Nuclear Reservation in Washington state, in efforts to gauge its effect on health, researchers say. The five-year study is the first major research into the plant's release of massive quantities of radiation.

Energy Secretary Watkins said his department will take more direct control of the contaminated Hanford plant as part of a plan to clean up problems that exist at the nation's nuclear weapons facilities.

---

A subcontractor received backward blueprints for Hubble Space Telescope parts in 1981 by the telescope's prime contractor, according to documents obtained by the Associated Press. Disclosure of the faulty drawings from Perkin-Elmer, now a GM unit, indicates NASA possibly knew for many years of problems in Perkin-Elmer's work.

---

A Quebec police officer was killed in a gun battle with Mohawk Indians blocking access to a golf course expansion project in a town west of Montreal. Mohawk leaders also claimed to have taken another provincial police officer hostage. The Indians are struggling to prevent their ancestral land from being used for the town project.

---

Senate investigators accused Johns Hopkins Hospital in Baltimore of having shoddy billing practices that apparently resulted in excessive Medicare payments. The accusation came at a hearing by a Senate panel that has asserted that Medicare pays up to $1 billion a year in claims that private insurance should cover.

---

Mandela is suffering from a mild case of pneumonia, according to his doctor, and is resting in Nairobi. The 71-year-old South African black nationalist leader, who has been on a world tour to raise funds for the African National Congress, arrived in Kenya from Ethiopia, where he had attended an Organization of African Unity summit.

---

Polish farmers blocked hundreds of roads across the country in a massive protest against government economic policies. Police said tractors and other farm vehicles clogged traffic throughout Poland in the two-hour warning strike. For months, farmers have been demanding that minimum prices be reinstated for their produce.

---

A House Appropriations panel approved a $20.7 billion Treasury and Postal Service spending bill that would increase IRS funding by 11.5% in fiscal 1991 and mandate stepped-up tax enforcement against foreignowned U.S. companies. Over $6.13 billion would be provided for the IRS in the budget year starting Oct. 1.

---

The Democratic Party announced that its 1992 national convention will be held in New York City. The formal statement by party Chairman Ronald Brown had been expected and confirmed reports that New York had been chosen over New Orleans. New York officials believe the convention will pump some $100 million into the city's economy.

---

The death toll reached 20 in four days of pro-democracy protests in Kenya, but armed police reportedly quelled violence in Nairobi. The nation's fledgling opposition vowed to keep fighting for change, despite the resistance of President Moi, who has ruled out ending a one-party system until Kenya becomes "a cohesive society."
