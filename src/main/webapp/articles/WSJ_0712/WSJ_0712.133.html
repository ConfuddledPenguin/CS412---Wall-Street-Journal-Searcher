Dividend News: Hartmarx Takes $51 Million Charge And Halves Payout

  • Author: Francine Schwadel
  • Date: 07/12/90

CHICAGO -- Hartmarx Corp., hurt by a long slump in men's suit sales, halved its dividend and took a charge of $51 million, primarily for closing 65 specialty stores. The charge, which also covers Hartmarx's plan to sell its minority interest in its Mexican affiliate, brought the company's net loss for the fiscal second quarter ended May 31 to $55.1 million, or $2.80 a share. In the year-ago period, the apparel maker and retailer earned $3.1 million, or 16 cents a share.

Sales slipped slightly to $303.9 million from $304.5 million.

Hartmarx cut its quarterly dividend to 15 cents a share, payable Aug. 15 to stock of record Aug. 1. The company had paid a dividend of 30 cents a share.

Wall Street nevertheless reacted favorably. Hartmarx stock, which has lost nearly half its value in recent months, rose $1 to close at $11.375 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday. "It's something they needed to do," Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. analyst Josie Esquivel said of the restructuring.

Harvey A. Weinberg, Hartmarx's chairman and chief executive officer, said the moves were positive steps that would help position the company for a recovery in menswear sales.

Like other companies specializing in tailored clothing, Hartmarx has been hurt by sluggish consumer demand and rising wool prices that have pushed up the price of men's suits by 30% over the past two years. "It's been a pretty dismal market," Mr. Weinberg said. But, he added: "I think that's coming to an end and I'm encouraged" by spring 1991 orders received by the company's manufacturing units. He said those orders are running as much as 3% ahead of year-earlier figures, compared with fall 1990 orders.

Hartmarx operates stores under such names as Wallachs, Raleighs, Silverwoods and Baskin.

The stores slated for closing -- about 13% of Hartmarx's retail operations -- are either unprofitable or marginally profitable, Mr. Weinberg said. Hartmarx also plans to cut costs by eliminating jobs in both the field and headquarters organizations of its specialty store division. Mr. Weinberg declined to say how many employees would be affected. The company said that 15 of the 65 stores scheduled for closing are Old Mill women's sportswear stores operated by its Country Miss unit.

The 65 stores accounted for about $40 million in sales in the latest fiscal year. But Mr. Weinberg said the company hopes to transfer about 40% of the volume of shuttered stores in major markets to other Hartmarx stores.

Hartmarx said it also intends to strengthen its specialty store division by hiring two outsiders to fill senior posts in marketing and merchandising.

Hartmarx's earnings from operations in the second quarter were hurt by slack demand and markdowns, as well as deep discounting by retail competitors that have held going-out-of-business sales.

For the six months, Hartmarx said it had a net loss of $52.8 million, or $2.68 a share, compared with a gain of $12.9 million, or 66 cents a share, a year earlier. Sales were $646.1 million, compared with $631.8 million.
