Greenspan Supports Move Requiring Budget Balance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/12/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan told a House panel that America's fiscal problems have become so severe that he reluctantly supports a constitutional amendment requiring a balanced federal budget. In 1979, as a private economist, Mr. Greenspan testified against a proposal for a balanced-budget amendment. "At the time, I was skeptical it could work," he said. But now, he told the House Judiciary Committee yesterday: "The crucial issue that I think creates the desire for a balance-budget amendment is the bias that clearly exists in our system toward deficits."

The "very serious gridlock" Congress and administration officials have encountered in trying to reduce the country's massive budget deficit during the past few years, Mr. Greenspan said, "suggests there is something in our system that needs to be redressed, and I'd say regrettably by constitutional means."

Mr. Greenspan didn't endorse a specific amendment but did recommend a provision requiring more than a majority vote in Congress to pass spending bills. He emphasized that he was expressing his personal views and not those of the Fed.
