Foreign Exchange: Dollar Regains Some Ground, Aided By Profit-Taking and Pound's Sell-Off

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/12/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar regained some ground as foreign exchange traders took some profits. The greenback also was aided by a modest sell-off of the British pound and other currencies supported by high interest rates. Currency traders groped for fundamental economic reasons to buy and sell but found few. The consolidating tone of the global session was set in Asia. Dealings were thin and -- prior to a late New York surge by the dollar against the mark -- prices were bound by technical ranges.

"People are simply too tired after all the movements the past two days. They were pooped," said Paul Kazuo Okura, a senior customer dealer in New York for Bank of Tokyo. "{Tuesday} our dealers {in Tokyo} were staying up into the wee hours of the night. {Wednesday} people were going home early."

The one unknown that had overhung the early session was the contents of the final communique from the Group of Seven industrial nations. But when the G-7 leaders broke up their 2 1/2-day meeting in Houston, they issued no news for the foreign exchange market.

Ever since a French newspaper reported last week that a draft communique said the yen's "current {low} levels could have undesirable consequences for the process of global adjustment," currency-market participants had been expecting a G-7 statement supporting the yen.

The concluding comment didn't mention the yen, or any other currency. It did reiterate that inflation requires "continued vigilance."

Afterward, Horst Koehler, state secretary in the West German Finance Ministry, said: "The yen has appreciated in the right direction. We're happy about that. There's no need to comment on it."

Shortly before the close of the Tokyo Wednesday morning session, rumors that a Houston summit source said the communique would call for a stronger yen pushed the dollar to its intraday low of 147.55 yen.

The rumors "triggered the market to go really short on dollars," according to Mahiko Nagai, a trader with Manufacturers Hanover Trust in Tokyo.

Mr. Nagai and other Japanese dealers had warned that if the economic communique disappointed dollar bears, the U.S. currency could rebound to 149 yen.

No rebound occurred. The greenback weakened against the yen for most of the latter half of the North American session and ended with its most paltry gains on the day against the Japanese currency.

Mr. Okura of Bank of Tokyo explained: "Most people in the market do believe that the G-7 thinks that a stronger yen would be good for correcting the {U.S.-Japan} trade imbalance, so {the national leaders} don't have to say it."

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.6560 marks, above 1.6425 marks late Tuesday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 148.50 yen, up from 148.30 yen in New York Tuesday. Sterling was trading at $1.7990, well below $1.8230 Tuesday.

In Tokyo Thursday, the dollar was trading at 148.90 yen soon after the opening, up from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 148.10 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at 1.6580 marks, up from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 1.6418 marks.

On Wednesday, Alexis Egan, corporate foreign-exchange trader for Banque Indosuez, described the day as slow with bursts of activity. She said the global session saw many quick movements on cross rates, such as yen-mark and yen-swiss, that suggested speculative rather than corporate activity.

Ms. Egan said the dollar-yen and dollar-mark movements, on the other hand, were easy to call. For example, against the West German currency, she said, the dollar had technical resistance and support points at 1.6520 marks and 1.6380 marks, respectively.

The British pound moved consistently lower throughout the day. This wasn't the day of reckoning long predicted for sterling speculators. Instead, several large sell orders in Asia kicked off a round of across-the-board consolidation for the high-flying currency.

The pound ended global trading in New York at 2.9791 marks and 267.15 yen, well below 2.9943 marks and 270.35 yen late Tuesday.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $354.10 an ounce, down $1.40. Estimated volume was a moderate 2.7 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $353.15 an ounce.
