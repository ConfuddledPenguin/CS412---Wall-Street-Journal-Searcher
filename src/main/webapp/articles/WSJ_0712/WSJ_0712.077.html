Federal Regulators Order Banks to Take Write-Offs on Loans to Brazil, Argentina

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 07/12/90

NEW YORK -- Federal bank regulators ordered U.S. banks to write off an additional 20% of their $2.9 billion in loans to Argentina and, for the first time, 20% of their $11.1 billion to Brazil. Bankers privately confirmed that in recent days they have received letters from the Interagency Country Exposure Review Committee, consisting of the Federal Reserve Board, the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. and the Comptroller of the Currency, ordering them to take the accounting actions.

The surprise move by the committee isn't expected to affect earnings of heavily reserved banks, such as J.P. Morgan & Co. and Bankers Trust New York Corp. But at those with lower reserve levels, especially Citicorp, the action is expected to heighten pressure to increase reserves in coming months. Citicorp, for instance, will have to charge off about $650 million of Brazilian loans against its $3.3 billion loan-loss reserve. However, it won't have to charge off any more of its Argentine loans because it did so in the first quarter. Citicorp's loss reserves could be boosted through a combination of asset sales and new capital, but it said it doesn't expect that to be necessary.

For such low-reserve banks, the action comes at a particularly sensitive time. They already are being buffeted by problem real estate loans, stringent loan reviews by regulators and weak corporate borrowing demand. Furthermore, the write-down of Brazilian loans could make future debt negotiations more rancorous between the banks and Brazil, which is undergoing a wrenching economic restructuring and had hoped for some forbearance while it sought firmer economic footing. Neither Brazil nor Argentina is currently paying principal or interest on its bank debt.

James J. McDermott, research director at Keefe, Bruyette & Woods Inc., said: "Candidly, the banks didn't need this. It raises a question of whether a third round of reserves is in store for some banks. The problem becomes one of market perception about the adequacy of reserves and whether they need to be replenished."

Moreover, banks needing to bolster reserves face an uphill battle raising new debt or equity. Many banks' stocks are trading below their book values, and nearly 50 of the largest bank organizations in recent months have seen their credit ratings downgraded. Even high-quality banks are feeling the effects. Columbus, Ohio-based Banc One Corp. recently faced an unusually long and thorough 90-day review period for a new equity issue.

A Citicorp spokesman said, "While we can't comment on or confirm" actions by the regulatory committee, "we can point out that Citicorp disclosed in the first quarter that it had written down 60% of its Argentine exposure" against its loss reserve with no impact on earnings. "Any other write-downs, voluntary or mandated, would likewise be charged to the reserve without earnings impact," the spokesman added.

Spokesmen for the Fed and FDIC declined to comment on the committee's action.

Analysts in the past have criticized Citicorp for what they consider insufficient reserves, and the regulatory action is likely to intensify those concerns.

Carole Berger, an analyst for C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc., said: "For some of these banks, this isn't small potatoes. This is going to reinforce the lesser-developed-country loan issue for banks like Manufacturers Hanover Corp., Chase Manhattan Corp., Chemical Banking Corp. and Citicorp."

H. Rodgin Cohen, a partner with the law firm Sullivan & Cromwell specializing in banking law, said: "This doesn't reduce capital ratios, but it does lower the levels of reserves as a percentage of nonperforming assets. The reserves won't be as strong as they once were."

The 20% write-off of Argentine debt, the third time a reduction in valuation has been ordered, had been expected and was less alarming to analysts because banks' Argentine exposure ($2.9 billion owed the 36 largest banks) is generally far lower than the amounts owed by Brazil ($11.1 billion for those same banks.)

However, the Brazil write-off was unexpected. Under its new president, Fernando Collor de Mello, Brazil is in the midst of a major restructuring of its economy, and the new administration has indicated an interest in reaching a new bank debt agreement. Indeed, some bankers, including Citicorp Chairman John S. Reed, have indicated that they expect talks with Brazil to resume this fall or winter.

Consequently, many have assumed that the regulatory committee would give Brazil some breathing room before declaring any of the Brazilian loans impaired. However, the committee consists of bank examiners who may not be attuned to the intricacies of international diplomacy, according to regulatory sources.

In fact, some analysts expect the committee's move to make the Brazilian debt negotiations more difficult. As C.J. Lawrence's Ms. Berger said: "All this puts the banks in a terrible bargaining position."
