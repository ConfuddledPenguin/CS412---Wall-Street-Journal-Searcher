OTC Focus: Market Advances on Late Buying Surge; Oracle Falls 2 1/2 to 19 7/8 in Heavy Trading

  • Author: Craig S. Smith Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/12/90

NEW YORK -- Unlisted stock prices advanced behind a late surge of buying on the New York Stock Exchange, ignoring another earnings disappointment by Oracle Systems. Traders say buyers flooded into OTC stocks near the end of the day after watching prices climb steadily on the Big Board.

Investors have been waiting for prices to drop over concerns about weak secondquarter earnings, but when prices failed to fall, the buyers grew more brave, says Lance Zipper, manager of OTC trading at Kidder, Peabody & Co.

"Even when {the market} sells off, it doesn't do so with any gumption," Mr. Zipper says. "It just feels like a real bull market."

Losses in Oracle's stock made a dent in the OTC market's gains, however. The stock dominated trading for most of the day, dropping 2 1/2 points to 19 7/8 on volume of more than 9.3 million shares.

The company disappointed Wall Street analysts and investors for the second consecutive quarter by reporting earnings below its estimates.

Late Tuesday, the company said it earned 39 cents a share in its fourth quarter ended in May, up from 25 cents a year earlier. But many analysts had expected the company to earn between 45 cents and 50 cents a share in the quarter.

Those estimates were based on Oracle's projections, made in May, that it would earn between 90 cents and $1 a share for the latest fiscal year. Instead, Oracle earned 86 cents a share for the year.

Oracle's trouble may have put the brakes on the OTC market's rise. The Nasdaq Composite Index gained 2.93, or 0.64%, to 463.90. The New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 1.2%.

OTC advancers led decliners, 1,050 to 838. Volume grew to 159 million shares from 142.3 million shares Tuesday.

While Oracle's latest earnings disappointment left more than a few Wall Street analysts red in the face, it didn't scare investors the way past disappointments have.

In March, Oracle stunned investors by announcing that its third-quarter profit would be flat with the 18 cents a share it earned in the year-earlier third quarter. That news sent the stock plunging more than 30% in a single session on recordbreaking volume of nearly 21 million shares. The Nasdaq Composite Index dropped 2.81 points that day as the selling spread to other technology stocks.

That wasn't the case yesterday. Second on the active list was disk-drive maker Conner Peripherals, which gained 1 to 27 3/4 on volume of more than 4.5 million shares. Late yesterday, Conner priced a 6.4 million-share secondary offering at $26.75 a share. Also on the active list, Intel rose 1 3/4 to 48, and Tandon rose 11/16 to 3 13/16. Microsoft gained 1 3/4 to 75 1/2.

Analysts expressed frustration over Oracle's reluctance to be more conservative in its forecasts but were quick to point out that the problems weren't fundamental.

Chip Morris, a vice president and hightechnology analyst at T. Rowe Price Associates, said his firm was a buyer of Oracle shares yesterday.

"They've won the open systems database game" and are now the dominant player in the field, Mr. Morris said. "Oracle's going to be one of the horses you'll want to ride in the 1990s."

Oracle is one of the largest holdings in T. Rowe Price's $1 billion New Horizons Fund as well as in its Science and Technology Fund, Mr. Morris says.

But Oracle's management has yet to adapt to the slower growth of a maturing company, analysts say, and continues to plan expenses according to revenue growth targets that are too optimisitc.

"Most shareholders would be happy to see Oracle plan expenses for 35% to 40% revenue growth next year," says Mr. Morris. "Then, if they grow at 50%, they could pass along the excess to the shareholders."

Mr. Morris concedes that Oracle didn't become a $1 billion company in a few short years by being conservative. But, he says that as a public company, Oracle has an obligation to shareholders to plan more realistically.

Dean Witter Reynolds and PrudentialBache Securities yesterday lowered their estimates for the company's earnings for the year ending in May 1991.

Athletic footwear maker Nike gained 1 7/8 to 89 1/8. The stock has been climbing since it released a strong earnings report Monday.

United Banks of Colorado gained 2 1/4 to 22 1/4 after trading resumed from a halt imposed Tuesday. Norwest is in talks with the company about a possible business combination.

Oshkosh Truck jumped 2 to 11 3/4. The company said it has been selected as the producer of the U.S. Army's new generation of heavy-duty transport truck. The contract, which Oshkosh expects to sign within 30 days, is worth more than $1 billion, the company said.
