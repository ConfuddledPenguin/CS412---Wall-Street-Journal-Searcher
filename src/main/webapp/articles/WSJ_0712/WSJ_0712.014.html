Technology & Health: Johns Hopkins Hospital Accused In Medicare Probe

  • Author: Walt Bogdanich
  • Date: 07/12/90

WASHINGTON -- Senate investigators accused Johns Hopkins Hospital, one of the nation's most prominent hospitals, of having shoddy billing practices that apparently resulted in excessive Medicare payments. The accusation came at a hearing by the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations, which has asserted that Medicare improperly pays up to $1 billion a year in medical claims that should be covered by private insurance companies.

The hearing was an outgrowth of a Wall Street Journal article last year that examined Medicare's Secondary Payer program, under which private health plans -- not Medicare -- are supposed to provide primary insurance coverage for the working aged.

The subcommittee said it "uncovered failures at all levels of the health care process . . . including instances of waste, fraud and abuse." Despite more than two dozen government investigations and reports since 1983, the problems remain unsolved, witnesses said.

One reason cited for the improper payments was that some hospitals fail to fulfill their legal responsibility to collect accurate insurance information from elderly patients. "Hospitals, whether knowingly or as a result of carelessness, submit claims to Medicare using improper codes in an attempt to expedite payment," charged Sen. William Roth (R., Del.).

According to Senate investigators, Johns Hopkins submitted bills to Medicare using a billing code that stated patients were unemployed -- and thus the responsibility of Medicare -- when in fact it hadn't bothered to learn whether they were covered by other health plans. Johns Hopkins, which is in Baltimore, was one of several hospitals committee investigators looked at. The panel gave no specific figure for the amount of Medicare overpayments it believes resulted from the hospital's billing practices.

Joanne Rodgers, a spokeswoman for the hospital, denied the committee's allegations. "We are in compliance with all the Medicare regulations as we understand them," she said. "We don't believe we are guilty of anything, including shoddy billing practices."

Sen. Roth said insurance companies also contribute to the billing problem by capitalizing "on what can be charitably described as gross inefficiencies in the administration of Medicare to evade their legal obligation to pay claims."
