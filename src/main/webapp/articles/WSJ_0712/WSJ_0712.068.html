Law -- Legal Beat: Lupus Suit Opens Way to New Injury Cases

  • Author: Edward Felsenthal and Robert Daniels
  • Date: 07/12/90

A lawsuit linking an auto accident to the onset of a chronic disease seems to open yet another new frontier in personal-injury litigation. A New York appeals court has affirmed a jury's finding that a car accident caused a woman sufficient stress to trigger the symptoms of lupus, a chronic inflammatory disease that weakens connective tissues and internal organs. The woman, Corrie Collins, alleged that the accident "aggravated" her lupus condition, which had previously been "quiescent."

The court also upheld $300,000 in damages awarded by the jury for Ms. Collins' pain and suffering, although it denied her an additional $600,000 for lost wages and loss of enjoyment of life.

Two of the five judges on the state Supreme Court Appellate Division dissented from the decision, arguing that "there is no medical proof that plaintiff's accident made her alleged lupus any worse than it would have been from any other precipitating cause."

Ms. Collins was a passenger in a car driven by Matthew McGinley, a defendant in the case, who failed to stop at an intersection and collided with another vehicle. Ms. Collins, a high school student in 1981 when the crash occurred, testified that shortly afterward she developed a facial rash, a symptom of lupus. She also developed swelling of the eyes. But she wasn't diagnosed with a form of the disease, systemic lupus erythematosas, until 1986.

Thomas Leyden, a lawyer for Ms. Collins, said that although previous cases have linked traumatic experiences to the aggravation of other diseases -- such as arthritis and Alzheimer's -- he knew of no other case suggesting that stress caused a lupus flare-up. But, he said, "you should not exclude lupus because it's not printed in the book up to now."

Personal injury lawyers praised the appeals court's decision, saying that research on lupus increasingly reflects a link with stress.

Pamela Liapakis, a New York trial lawyer familiar with the disease, said that the current state of research allows doctors "to say on witness stands with a reasonable degree of certainty that a stressful event could cause a flare-up."

But Jay Tressler, president of the International Association of Defense Counsel, said the case was an example of the effectiveness of the plaintiff's bar "in trying to create expanded theories of recovery." He added, "I wish we could bring the scales of justice a little more into balance in determining what should be recoverable and what should not."

And Arthur Lambert of New York's Lambert & Weiss, a lawyer for Mr. McGinley, called the court's decision "astonishing," saying there is no way to prove that Ms. Collins had a quiescent condition of lupus at the time of the crash. "The only thing that showed after the accident was a rash, and from a rash we got a verdict of $900,000."

Both sides say they may appeal the decision, since it affirmed the jury's finding that stress caused the flare-up but reduced Ms. Collins' damage award.

UPS IS ACCUSED of bias against employees who have beards.

United Parcel Service of America Inc., the world's largest package-delivery company, prohibits all bearded employees from working with the public. But in a civil lawsuit filed in U.S. District Court in Chicago, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission said that under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Greenwich, Conn., company must exempt workers who wear beards for sincere religious reasons.

EEOC filed the complaint on behalf of Aiyub Patel, who brought the case to the agency, and others who might be affected by the UPS policy. A UPS spokesman declined to comment on the suit.

Mr. Patel, who left UPS in September 1989 after four years, was an unloader, a post requiring no public contact. When Mr. Patel, a Moslem, applied for a driver's position, "he was informed of UPS's policy, which is to not have any exceptions to their no-beard policy," said Zachary A. Tobin, trial attorney at the EEOC district office in Chicago.

Under Title VII, Mr. Tobin said, a company must reasonably accommodate such religious exceptions unless it can prove "undue hardship on the conduct" of its business.

In Islam, beards are "purely a personal thing. Nothing in the faith requires wearing the beard," said Charles L. Geddes, professor of Islamic studies at the University of Denver and director of the American Institute of Islamic Studies there.

But Mr. Tobin said that "we're not an arbiter of religious beliefs. In this case, Mr. Patel said it is part of his religious beliefs that he must wear the beard. That's what we go on."

EEOC has a similar case against UPS being litigated by its Houston district office. That suit makes the religion claim but also charges that the no-beard policy harms black males because a number of them have a skin disorder, pseudofolliculitis barbae, that makes shaving difficult.

The EEOC process requires that the agency try to settle the issue before it begins litigation. Such efforts proved fruitless, Mr. Tobin said.

ASBESTOS EMISSIONS prove costly in a New Jersey hotel-site renovation.

In Newark, N.J., U.S. Attorney Michael Chertoff said Tzavah Urban Renewal Corp., based in Brooklyn, N.Y., and four other defendants agreed to pay a total of $330,000 under a consent decree filed in federal court. The five had been among parties accused of being responsible for asbestos-emission violations in the renovation of the former Old Military Park Hotel in downtown Newark between October 1987 and last August. In the decree, the defendants made no admission of liability for violations.

The government alleged that Tzavah had ignored asbestos rules at the renovation site, where it said asbestos-insulated materials "were dry-stripped, dumped out of upper-story windows and left exposed in huge refuse piles." Airborne asbestos has been linked to diseases including cancer.

The defendants' attorney, Damon R. Sedita, was unavailable for comment, his office said. Tzavah officials couldn't be reached for comment.

Earlier in the case, a $225,000 default judgment was entered against Creer Industrial Corp., a demolition contractor based in the Bronx, and its principal, William Creer.

LAWYERS' BILLING RATES and incomes are on the rise.

Hourly billing rates for law-firm partners rose over the past year, possibly reflecting law firms' efforts to cope with escalating salaries for associates and the higher cost of doing business.

The median billing rate for partners in early 1990 was $150 per hour, up 8% from last year's rate of $139. The rate for associates jumped only 3%, from $97 to $100.

The billing-rate figures appear in a survey of law-firm economics conducted annually by Altman & Weil, a management consulting group based in Newtown Square, Pa. More than 650 law firms nationwide completed a questionnaire about their billing practices, compensation levels and staffing patterns. The survey excluded solo practitioners.

Partners' median compensation rose 5%, to $141,203 from $134,350. Associates' median salaries increased 8%, to $60,270 from $55,635.

Ward Bower of Altman & Weil said, "The rise in billing rates could be reflective of the fact that partners, who are feeling the pinch in their incomes, are looking to make that up by pushing billing rates upward."

COURT PANEL CRITICIZES ban on filming "Bonfire" scene in courtroom.

A panel of judges, lawyers, media representatives and state entertainment officials recommended that judges avoid "content review" when deciding which films may be shot in New Jersey's county courthouses.

The panel was established at the request of New Jersey Supreme Court Chief Justice Robert N. Wilentz, who last May banned the filming of a scene from "The Bonfire of the Vanities" in an Essex County courthouse in Newark. The judge complained that the scene, showing a group of black citizens rioting, "could serve to seriously undermine the confidence of black citizens in our court system."

But in an 11-page report, the 12-member panel, headed by New Jersey Superior Court Judge Ralph V. Martin, expressed concern that judges would become embroiled in non-judicial disputes. The panel said that judges can dissociate themselves from a film's content by requiring that the film's credits include disclaimers.

Judge Wilentz's decision last May sparked widespread protest from county administrators in New Jersey, particularly in Essex County, where the movie's producers had offered $250,000 for use of the courtroom.

A suit filed by Essex County pending in federal court in Newark contends that the decision amounts to illegal censorship. Judge Wilentz declined to comment while he reviews the panel's report.

ALAN I. ROTHENBERG, president of the California state bar, is leaving Manatt, Phelps, Rothenberg & Phillips, a Los Angeles firm where he has specialized in corporate litigation and sports law for the past 24 years, to join Latham & Watkins. At Latham, a Los Angeles firm with 550 lawyers nationwide, Mr. Rothenberg will be a senior litigation partner. Says Mickey Kantor, a partner at 135-lawyer Manatt Phelps, "It's a great loss to us professionally and personally, but you don't expect partners to stay with you forever."
