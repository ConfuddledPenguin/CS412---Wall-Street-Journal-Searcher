SPAIN: EUROPE'S DRUG GATEWAY

Spain tops a list of European drug seizures in 1989.

Of the 171 tons of heroin, hashish and cocaine captured in 16 European nations last year, 39% was found in Spain, according to a Spanish government report. The nation's seizures accounted for 40% of all hashish taken in Europe, 32% of all cocaine and 15% of all heroin.

The figures underscore the claims of international drug-enforcement specialists that Spain is the main gateway for cocaine entering Europe from Latin America; for heroin from the Middle East, mainly Turkey; and for hashish from North Africa, mainly Morocco.

In the nations studied, heroin seizures rose 52% to 1,569 pounds last year from 1,034 pounds in 1988, the report said.

HIGH-SPEED TRAIN FOR SOVIETS

Japan's HSST Corp. has agreed in principle to sell the Soviet Union technology for high-speed trains that float on a magnetic cushion rather than run on wheels. The trains would be used on a 20-mile line between central Moscow and Sheremetyevo International Airport, said an official of HSST. The memorandum calls for construction of the new rail system to start next year and be completed by 1996, with costs estimated at 90 billion yen ($607 million). HSST's trains can move faster than 185 miles an hour.

A TURKEY IN LONDON

"Bernadette," a musical about the French peasant girl from Lourdes who became a Roman Catholic saint, will close Saturday, less than a month after it opened to scathing reviews. The $2.25 million production, which was blessed by Pope John Paul II, was dubbed "The People's Musical" after its producer, William Fonfe, asked the general public to buy shares in the enterprise. "I am very sad," Mr. Fonfe said. "We had over 100 people on the payroll and they are going to lose their jobs. We also have 2,000 investors who are disappointed."

EAST GERMAN INVESTIGATION

East German authorities have frozen a total of 56 bank accounts that contained combined holdings of as much as 100 million marks ($61 million), a government spokesman said. The accounts belong to 26 private persons and the holders are suspected of attempted manipulation in conjunction with monetary union with West Germany, the spokesman said. There are at least 100,000 marks in each of the accounts, he said, adding that there is a suspicion that the money was obtained illegally.

MEXICAN FINANCIER RELEASED

Eduardo Legorreta Chauvet, the Mexican financier who was arrested last year on stock-fraud charges, was released on bail late Tuesday after 16 months in jail. The release followed a district judge's decision to drop charges of fraud and falsification of documents against Mr. Legorreta, formerly president of the Operadora de Bolsa investment house. The judge found Mr. Legorreta guilty of a lesser stock-law violation and sentenced him to a three-year jail term; that sentence was commuted for time already served and future community service. Bail was imposed to ensure that service.

EXPERIMENTAL JAPANESE SHIP

Japanese shipbuilders christened the world's first ship that will use superconductivity to move through water. The 185-ton Yamato No. 1 is dry-docked in the Kobe shipyard of Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Japan's largest shipbuilder and a member of a consortium developing the ship's new technology. The ship's developers said they view the vessel, which will use electromagnetic force instead of a conventional screw propeller, as a prototype for high-speed seacraft. The main purpose of their project, however, was to demonstrate that superconductivity could be used to power a ship, they said.

WATERFORD STRIKE ENDS

Workers at Ireland's Waterford Crystal glassworks ended a 14-week strike yesterday after agreeing on cost-cutting proposals with management.

The strike by 2,300 workers at Waterford's three plants began over cost cuts, including the scrapping of bonuses for 500 glass cutters. Paddy Byrne, chief executive of the crystal firm's parent company Waterford Wedgwood PLC, welcomed the agreement but said: "It must be recognized that we still have a long way to go in order to transform Waterford Crystal into a robust and profitable part of the Waterford Wedgwood group. Until this is achieved, the future of the crystal division will not be secure."

The company said unions and management agreed to refer issues still in dispute to binding arbitration.

Waterford has lost $64.2 million over the past three years. Irish politicians had feared it might move production of its crystalware to Czechoslovakia or East Germany if the strike continued.
