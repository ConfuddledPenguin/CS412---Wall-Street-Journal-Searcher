U.S. Persuades Allied-Signal to End Unfair Trade Complaint Against Japan --- By John T. Norman and Eduardo Lachica Staff Reporters of The Wall Street Journal

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/19/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. persuaded Allied-Signal Inc. to withdraw a trade complaint against Japan, at least temporarily, while the two countries seek to negotiate a solution. At issue are complaints by the U.S. company that Japan restricts imports of amorphous metals, a high-technology product used in electric-power transmission equipment. The Bush administration's decision to negotiate the issue with Tokyo, rather than launch a formal trade investigation that could lead to retaliation, signals the administration's intent to reduce the heat of the current trade dialogue with Japan. U.S. officials said the Bush administration doesn't want to give Japan a black eye so soon after that country agreed to trade concessions in so-called structural impediments talks with the U.S.

Although Carla Hills, the U.S. trade representative, said nothing about other U.S. trade complaints awaiting action, the tone of her announcement suggested that the White House is considering nonconfrontational solutions for them, as well. At the end of this month, Mrs. Hills has to determine whether to list Japan as an unfair trading partner under the "Super 301" provision of the 1988 trade act.

Mrs. Hills said that if bilateral negotiations aren't successful in resolving the amorphous metals dispute within 150 days, the U.S. will invite Allied-Signal to resubmit its petition for prompt action.

In Morris Township, N.J., Edward L. Hennessy Jr., Allied-Signal's chairman and chief executive officer, said the company will cooperate with Mrs. Hills. "The recently announced progress in the trade talks between the U.S. and Japan holds out the promise that trade issues between the two nations can be resolved in a more businesslike and less confrontational manner," he said. "We hope that Allied-Signal's case will demonstrate that this process works."

Allied-Signal filed the trade complaint March 5, alleging that Japan "has engaged in practices aimed at excluding sales of U.S. amorphous metals." The U.S. company also charged that Tokyo has blocked attempts to license technology to Japanese companies. Mrs. Hills was required under the 1988 trade act to accept or reject the complaint by today.

Mrs. Hills has used similar tactics to deal with trade complaints from other U.S. industries. For example, this past summer the Shipbuilders Council of America, an industry group, withdrew a trade complaint about government subsidies to commercial shipbuilders in South Korea, Japan, West Germany and Norway after Mrs. Hills promised that the U.S. would seek to negotiate a broad international agreement to restrict and eventually eliminate such subsidies.

The U.S. negotiations with South Korea and member countries of the Paris-based Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development on shipyard subsidies haven't been concluded, although U.S. officials say they are making progress and the talks should be completed next month.

In its petition, Allied-Signal charged that Japanese government agencies and businesses worked in concert to keep the company's amorphous metals out of the Japanese market while domestic producers tried to develop an equivalent product. Amorphous metals are used primarily to improve the efficiency of electrical transmission and have a world market estimated at about $1 billion a year.

Mrs. Hills's action drew a cautious reaction from Congress. Rep. Helen Bentley (R., Md.) said the move was a "Solomon-like decision." But Sen. John Danforth (R., Mo.) warned that the administration could be "falling into a pattern of sidestepping U.S. trade law." He stressed that the "bottom line has to be results in the form of exports for a U.S. firm that is being shut out by Japan."
