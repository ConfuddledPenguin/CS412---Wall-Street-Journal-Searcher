American's Videos Don't Mean to Be Funny

IF YOU WANT to be a video star, you don't have to record an ear-splitting rock and roll tune. All you have to do is drum up some gripes about American Airlines.

As part of a test, the airline recently set up a video booth in the baggage section of the Nashville airport to give customers a chance to tape their comments. And judging from some footage of the comments, obtained by this newspaper, people don't seem to be the least bit shy about going on camera.

"We've just had the worst flight of our life," complains one bearded traveler from Arizona, who describes how his plane made two aborted landing attempts through fog in Dallas before diverting to Austin to refuel, where he waited "hours and hours." One elderly woman fights off tears as she explains how a canceled American flight to Chattanooga is forcing her and her ailing husband to rent a car and drive three hours to get home.

Another video guest says she has ordered special meals on five flights and hasn't received one yet. Maybe she's lucky: Another woman, named Patricia, complains that a special seafood meal she ordered was so bad "even the stewardess said `You shouldn't eat that. It smells terrible.'"

The booth, designed by a company called Short Takes Inc., gives each passenger one minute. And for at least one weary soul, that wasn't enough. Holding up a $25 award certificate from American, he bitterly complains about having to go through "an act of God" to redeem it and then takes a swipe at the man in charge of American. "I don't like what Robert Crandall is doing to our hub and if he were here right now, I'd . . . ." (Oops, time's up.)

Of course, not all the comments are so mean. One American customer compliments the airline for serving bran muffins for breakfast, but can't resist adding that "I think you could lose the eggs and it wouldn't hurt our feelings bad at all." One New York women provides a glowing account of how a diligent American clerk hunted down a lost scarf. And an Atlanta man gushes that American is "one of the greatest businesses I have ever dealt with in any respect."

American insists that despite some of the negative feedback, it's pleased with the test so far. But it hasn't decided whether to open more booths, which got mixed reviews from customers. "This thing seems a little expensive to me," one patron says. "I would have been glad to do just a post card."

You Can Go Cheaply,

But Do You Want to?

IF ONLY Tallahassee, Fla., were some great mecca for business travelers.

Tallahassee turns out to be the cheapest city for business travel, according to a survey of 100 cities by Corporate Travel magazine. It costs the average business traveler just $116 a day to spend a day and night there, including overnight lodging, one-day car rental and three meals. Biloxi, Miss., at $122 and Omaha, Neb., at $124 are the next cheapest.

But of course, most business people don't have reason to go to these places. Here's the average cost for a one-day business trip in the 10 most expensive cities: New York ($312); Chicago ($256); Boston ($252); Washington ($226); Newark, N.J. ($218); San Francisco ($207); Los Angeles ($192); Dallas ($191); Baltimore ($186) and Detroit ($185).

Running Into Confusion

With Damage Waivers

FOR MOST car renters, deciding whether to take the collision-damage waiver has always been a bewildering choice. Now, it may only get more confusing.

Yesterday, Alamo Rent A Car Inc. announced it was offering three types of waivers, instead of just one, in Florida beginning this week. If it succeeds, the company plans to expand the offer to other states, and analysts say at least some competitors will have to match the policy, although they haven't so far.

The waiver is basically an agreement that a rental firm won't charge the driver for any car damage in most cases. Under Alamo's new plan, Florida drivers can buy the traditional waiver for $9 a day for all car sizes. Or, they can pay less and get less protection: For $6 a day, the waiver covers the first $3,000 in damage; for $3 a day, it covers the first $500.

For savvy renters, the new options may be very attractive. Many drivers' own insurance policies, for example, already cover car rental damage, but come with $500 deductibles. Those people, Alamo points out, can now buy the cheaper waiver to cover the deductible without paying for any unnecessary coverage.

But renters who don't have this insurance -- or who aren't clear about their coverage -- will now have only more hard choices to make. And they still may not need any waiver if they're using certain credit cards that provide free car-rental insurance.

"It was tough enough to explain their options when there was only one level of coverage," says Fred Mudgett, a car-rental industry consultant in Florida. "Now you've got three levels to explain to people."
