Credit Markets: Bond Prices Tumble on Inflation Fears, Selling By Japanese Investors; Treasurys Hit Hardest

  • Author: Tom Herman and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 04/19/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices tumbled for the second day in a row on U.S. inflation fears and more selling by Japanese investors. Treasury bond prices fell the hardest. Some actively traded bonds dropped 1 1/8 points, or $11.25 for each $1,000 face amount. Their yield jumped to 8.83%, the highest in nearly a year, and most short-term interest rates also rose.

Japanese selling is pummeling the U.S. bond market especially hard because traders are bracing for an avalanche of U.S. government borrowing soon. In the past, Japanese investors eagerly have bought large amounts of U.S. bonds. But this time around, the Japanese may not be so eager because many have been jolted by plunges in Japanese stock and bond prices.

"The atmosphere in the bond market is terrible," said Scott Pardee, co-chairman of Yamaichi International (America) Inc.

Joel D. Kazis, managing director of government bond trading at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. agreed. "Sentiment has turned overwhelmingly bearish as we head closer and closer" to the Treasury's massive quarterly financing next month, he said. There has been "sizable selling from abroad," mostly from Japan. "So the Street is wondering how it's going to take down all the new supply with the foreigners selling," Mr. Kazis said.

The U.S. Treasury probably will issue about $30 billion of new notes and bonds early in May. Details will be announced May 2. Most traders expect the department to sell $10 billion each of three-year notes, 10-year notes and 30-year bonds. All this borrowing is likely to drive up 30-year bond yields to 9% or higher, several analysts said.

But that's only part of the bond market woes. Investors still are reeling from Tuesday morning's news that U.S. consumer prices surged 0.5% last month. The increase was more than twice as large as economists had expected, bringing the first quarter's annualized inflation rate to a sizzling 8.5%.

That news quickly extinguished hopes for a credit-easing move by the Federal Reserve any time soon. Many analysts now say the Fed's next step probably will be to push interest rates higher. That speculation has intensified lately because of indications that the economy is perking up. The consumer price index report "really took the wind out of the market," Mr. Kazis said.

"Inflation concerns are starting to re-emerge, and that's a big concern," said Thomas R. Pura, a partner at Goldman, Sachs & Co. "Then you top that off with rumors of potential Japanese lack of interest" in U.S. bonds to be sold next month at the Treasury's quarterly financing, "and you get a market that trades pretty poorly."

Rising Japanese interest rates may provide another reason for Japanese investors to keep their money at home rather than invest here. "On a comparative basis, Japanese bond yields at 7.3% are more attractive to Japanese investors, now, compared to U.S. bond yields than they have been in a long, long time," Mr. Pura said.

Several leading Japanese securities dealers here say Japanese institutions are increasingly leery of U.S. bonds.

"We have seen selling by investors, including those from Japan," said Mr. Pardee of Yamaichi. "Japanese investors are lying very low. They've had big problems in their own financial markets, and they haven't shown any inclination to buy Treasurys. If anything, they've been selling Treasurys to take profits on the strong dollar."

Mr. Pardee also said "there's beginning to be a feeling among some Japanese investors that the dollar is peaking against the yen so they're taking their profits now."

Although U.S. bond yields have surged this week, neither Japanese nor U.S. investors have any incentive to buy securities ahead of the huge supply of new issues that will hit the market next month when the Treasury holds its quarterly refunding, Mr. Pardee added.

Some Fed policy makers watch bond market trends closely to gauge investor expectations of future inflation. Thus, this week's bond debacle increases the likelihood of a Fed credit-tightening move. But economic indicators still are sufficiently ambiguous that key Fed policy makers may prefer to wait for more data before acting.

All this gloom easily overshadowed other news that many traders initially thought would spark a bond rally. Early yesterday, the Commerce Department reported that the U.S. merchandise trade deficit shrank to $6.49 billion in February from $9.32 billion in January. That was the smallest gap since December 1983. It was also smaller than most economists had anticipated.

But in yesterday's bond maelstrom, the trade report hardly caused a stir. Traders also aren't reacting to recent declines in oil prices, once watched closely as an inflation barometer.

"Nobody's even talking about oil anymore," said Mr. Kazis of Smith Barney. "That shows you how bad the market's mood is."

Treasury Securities

The Treasury bond market's slump deepened yesterday.

Long-term government bonds, which dropped about one point Tuesday, fell another 1 1/8 points yesterday. The government's benchmark 30-year issue yielded 8.83%, the highest since May of last year and up from 8.72% Tuesday.

Rates rose again on short-term Treasury bills. The Treasury's latest three-month bills were quoted late in the day at a discount rate of 7.80% bid to yield 8.05%. Late Tuesday, they were quoted at a rate of 7.76% to yield 8%.

The Treasury's huge borrowing needs next month already are weighing heavily on the markets. Moreover, the Treasury said yesterday it plans to sell about $10.5 billion of new two-year notes next Wednesday. That is up from the $10 billion of two-year notes sold late last month.

The new notes will be dated April 30 and will be due April 30 of 1992. They will be sold in minimum denominations of $5,000.

Corporate Issues

Corporate bond prices slumped again.

Some long-term, investment-grade bonds lost as much as a point. Prices also fell on corporate junk bonds, or high-yield, high-risk corporate securities. Junk bonds of Quantum Chemical Corp. fell by 1 1/2 points on news of lower first-quarter earnings.

But moving in the other direction were some bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. Prices of RJR's pay-in-kind bonds, which pay interest in the form of additional bonds instead of cash, have recently surged on speculation that Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. is snapping up the bonds. A spokesman for RJR, which is controlled by Kohlberg Kravis, declined to comment.

RJR Nabisco's 13.71% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, rose a point to 83 1/2 on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely held RJR issue, the 13.71% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, rose 3/4 point to 70 3/4.

Among new investment-grade issues, Ontario Hydro, a large Canadian utility, tapped the U.S. debt market with $600 million of five-year notes. The notes were priced to yield 9.25% through underwriters led by Salomon Brothers Inc.

Ontario Hydro is the latest Canadian issuer to tap the U.S. markets to take advantage of the relatively attractive rates here. Despite the recent rise in U.S. rates, the difference between U.S. and Canadian rates remains very large. For example, Canada's 10 1/2% issue of June 1995 yields about 12.40%, while comparable U.S. Treasury notes yield about 8.81%.

Municipals

Prices of tax-exempt bonds were flat to off as much as 1/2 point yesterday.

Among active issues, New Jersey Turnpike Authority 7.20% issue due 2018 fell 1/4 point to 97 1/8 bid to yield 7.42%. Traders said the rout in the U.S. government bond market helped depress municipal bonds.

Foreign Bonds

Japanese government bond prices rose, bolstered by the yen's gain against the U.S. dollar.

Traders also cited a decline in some short-term Japanese interest rates as a factor in the bond rally. Japan's benchmark bonds due in 1999 rose to 86.09 from 85.83 Tuesday. Their yield fell to 7.345% from 7.39%. The price of the June futures contract rose 0.18 to 93.55 to yield 7.103%.

In other major markets, British government bonds slumped. Traders said inflation fears were fanned by news that power workers plan to strike unless the government meets their demands for double-digit wage increases. Britain's benchmark bonds due 2003-2007 fell 14/32 point to 96 9/32 to yield 12.28%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended 13/32 to 17/32 lower as Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. and Federal National Mortgage Association issues were supported by demand spurred by two new derivative deals.

Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae 9 1/2% securities both fell 13/32.

Two Freddie Mac real estate mortgage investment conduit securities totaling $800 million were announced yesterday.
