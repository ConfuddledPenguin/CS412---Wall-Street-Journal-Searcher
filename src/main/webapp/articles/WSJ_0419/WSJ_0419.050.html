Wednesday's Markets: Treasury-Bond Rout Shakes Stocks, Dollar

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 04/19/90

NEW YORK -- A big rout in the Treasury bond market sent stock prices tumbling and helped weaken the dollar against key currencies. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 32.89 to 2732.88, the sharpest decline in about two months.

Traders and investors said the main factor was continued Japanese selling of long-term U.S. Treasury securities. The bellwether 30-year bond dropped 1 1/8 points, or $11.25 for each $1,000 face amount, taking yields up to 8.83%, the highest level in nearly a year.

All three markets largely ignored yesterday's merchandise trade report for February, which showed a significant narrowing of the U.S. deficit. "The trade report, if anything, should have been favorable" for bond prices, said Michael Strauss, chief economist at UBS Securities Inc.

Imports of oil and consumer goods dropped, pointing to moderating inflation pressures. Non-auto capital goods exports rose to $12.9 billion, a record high, according to Louis Crandall, economist at R.H. Wrightson & Associates, Inc., an economic research firm. "All the surprises in the trade report were favorable," Mr. Crandall said.

But bonds fell sharply as talk of widespread Japanese selling spread through the market. Traders said Japan's trust banks and other financial institutions were taking profits on both bond and currency positions.

Indeed, foreign exchange traders said the dollar tumbled on steady sales from traders around the world, starting in Tokyo the night before, as the dollar traded near 160 yen.

"My guess is that Japanese investors are moving to other financial centers," said John Gaines, a foreign exchange trader at Bank of New York. "I think they are switching out of dollars and into {West German} marks."

Added James McGroarty, senior vice president at Greenwich Capital Markets: "I think Japanese investors chose this as an opportune time to take some profits."

Treasury bond prices could keep falling until yields are buoyed up to 9%, several traders predicted. Such a sharp rise in bond yields usually hurts stocks, which become a less attractive investment.

But not all investors are bearish. The improved U.S. exports confirmed what several stock strategists have been forecasting: a rebound in America's basic industries this year. "I haven't shared the notion that the economy is going to grind to a halt and that earnings are so terrible," said Joseph McAlinden, director of research at Dillon, Read & Co.

Among other cyclical stocks, which rise and fall with the pace of economic activity, some auto shares held their ground; Ford rose 7/8 to 48. But profit-taking clobbered semiconductor, technology and software stocks that have risen lately.

Michael Schonberg, chief investment strategist at Chase Investors Management Corp., said he will use declines in stock prices to start "paying more attention to cyclical stocks." Earnings of many of these companies are at the low point for the year, he believes.

The Dow industrials' drop was the sharpest since a 38.74-point slump Feb. 20. But volume was a light 147.1 million shares. Laszlo Birinyi, who heads his own stock research firm, said net dollar outflow from the stock market amounted to $163 million in block trades and $96 million in non-block trades.

"Given what the market did today, it was not an especially large amount {of net outflow}; there was very little selling pressure," Mr. Birinyi said. That meant foreign investors who were selling dollars and Treasury bonds weren't also dumping stocks.

"Stocks that had a lot of net outflows were not those that I would characterize as foreign-owned," Mr. Birinyi said.

Among typically foreign-owned issues, such as General Motors, Merck and Ford, more money flowed into these stocks through big block trades than flowed out, Mr. Birinyi said.

In major market activity:

Stocks fell. Declining issues swamped advancers on the New York Stock Exchange by 1,056 to 477.

Bonds tumbled. The benchmark 30-year Treasury issue sank 1 1/8 points, or $11.25 for each $1,000 face amount.

The dollar was off sharply. In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.6722 marks and 158.10 yen, compared with 1.6768 marks and 159.68 yen late Tuesday.
