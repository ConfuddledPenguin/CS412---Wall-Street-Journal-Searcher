Yields on Money Funds and Bank CDs Were Little Changed in the Latest Week

  • Author: Georgette Jasen and Ellen E. Schultz
  • Date: 04/19/90

Yields on both money-market mutual funds and bank certificates of deposit were little changed in the latest week. The average seven-day compound yield on taxable money funds remained at 7.99%, according to IBC/Donoghue's Money Fund Report, a Holliston, Mass.based newsletter. The average yield on a small-denomination six-month CD stayed at 7.80%, according to Banxquote Money Markets, a New York information service. Compound yields assume reinvestment of dividends.

Yields on tax-free money funds rose, however.

Assets of the 427 taxable money funds tracked by Donoghue's fell by about $1.5 billion in the week ended Tuesday, the third consecutive weekly decline. Brenda Malizia Negus, editor of the newsletter, attributed the latest drop in part to redemptions for tax purposes.

Average maturity of the funds' investments shortened by a day to 44 days, staying within the range of the past few months. Maturities are thought to indicate which way portfolio managers think interest rates are headed, with shorter maturities signaling rising rates and longer maturities indicating a decline. Average maturity has ranged from 29 to 53 days in recent years.

The average seven-day simple yield remained at 7.69%. The average 30-day simple yield stayed at 7.68% and the average 30-day compound yield at 7.98%.

The average seven-day compound yield on tax-free funds rose to 5.63% from 5.57%, Donoghue's reported. That's equal to 7.82% for an investor in the 28% tax bracket and 8.40% in the 33% bracket.

Assets of the 220 tax-free funds tracked by Donoghue's fell by $1.3 billion, to $77.5 billion. Average maturity shortened to 45 days from 46.

The only measurable change in CD yields in the week ended yesterday was in long-term "jumbo" CDs, which generally require deposits of at least $90,000. The average yield on five-year jumbos rose to 8.38% from 8.33%, according to Banxquote. That increase was led by New York's Chase Manhattan Bank, which boosted its five-year jumbo yield to 8.86% from 8.55% early last week.

That sharp rise could portend other increases, said Norberto Mehl, chairman of Banxquote. "When a major bank suddenly pushes a rate up strongly, it points to a new trend," he said.

The average yield on popular one-year small-denomination CDs, which generally require minimum deposits of $10,000 or less, slipped to 7.95% from 7.96%. Fiveyear small-denomination CD yields remained at an average 8.16%.

Portfolio Changes

Some money funds are moving to improve the credit quality of their investments, after two instances when several funds' investment advisers had to step in to avert possible shareholder losses.

T. Rowe Price Associates' $4.9 billion Prime Reserve money fund, for instance, has reduced its commercial paper holdings and increased its investments in government and agency securities. The fund also has reduced its holdings of commercial paper that doesn't carry the very highest credit ratings, said Steven Norwitz, a T. Rowe Price vice president.

Heritage Cash Trust has been upgrading its portfolio since last year, said Stephen Hill, president of Heritage Asset Management, the fund's investment adviser. Only 10% of the fund's approximately $700 million in assets is currently in paper that is below the highest grade, he said, and more than 10% is in Treasury bills.

The two funds were among 10 moneymarket mutual funds holding the commercial paper, or short-term corporate IOUs, of Mortgage & Realty Trust, an Elkins Park, Pa., real estate investment trust that filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection last week, a month after its commercial paper was downgraded by Standard & Poor's Corp.

All together, the investment advisers or parent companies bought a total of about $75 million of the paper from the funds to protect shareholders. Among others, Prime Reserve held about $42 million of the paper, Heritage Cash Trust about $12 million and Alliance Money Reserves $8.7 million. "We consider it of paramount importance to retain investor confidence," said Mr. Norwitz of T. Rowe Price. Any loss will be absorbed by the parent company.

There was similar fast action last spring, when the parents of four money funds stepped in to absorb the loss after Integrated Resources Inc. defaulted on commercial paper. Integrated Resources also subsequently filed for Chapter 11 protection.

As previously reported, the Securities and Exchange Commission is considering tighter restrictions on investments by money-market mutual funds. The SEC's division of investment management is planning to propose further limits on the amount a fund can invest in a single issuer, restrictions on its investments in less than the very highest rated commercial paper and shortening the permissible average maturity of a fund's investments.

More than half the nearly $400 billion in money fund assets currently is invested in commercial paper. That's up from 44% at the start of 1989 and 29% at the start of 1984. The SEC already limits money funds' investments to highly rated securities.
