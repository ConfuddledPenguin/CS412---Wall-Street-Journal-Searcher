Abreast of the Market: Stocks Fall as Interest Rates Surge; Retail, Technology Lead Decline

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 04/19/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market suffered its worst setback in nearly two months as a surge in interest rates sent prices tumbling. Retailing and technology issues were among the major casualties of the sell-off, which was fueled by futures-related program selling that gained momentum as the session progressed.

Blue-chip stocks, which have generally been performing better than the broad market lately, weren't able to maintain their dominance. The Dow Jones Industrial Average skidded 32.89 to 2732.88, and other market measures recorded comparable losses.

Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index fell 3.96 to 340.72, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index went down 1.98 to 186.93 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index fell 3.61 to 317.47. Decliners led by 1,056 issues on the Big Board, over 477 advancers.

Prices briefly moved higher in opening trading in response to February trade figures, which was better than expected. The data showed that the nation's trade deficit narrowed sharply, to $6.49 billion from $9.32 billion in February.

But as bond prices plunged for a second straight day amid widespread speculation that Japanese investors were heavy sellers of U.S. Treasury issues, stocks proved unable to hold those gains.

Sell programs resulting from stock-index arbitrage first surfaced in the market at late morning, traders said. The programs, involving the purchase of index-futures contracts and the sale of baskets of stocks to profit from price differences between them, struck again several times during the afternoon.

As the Dow industrial average posted its sharpest loss since Feb. 20, when the 30-stock average declined 38.74, trading activity on the Big Board swelled to its highest level since April 4. Volume totaled 147,130,000 shares on the Big Board, up from 127,990,000 Tuesday.

Even so, traders said selling pressure didn't appear to be especially heavy, as many investors remained out of the market. "They don't want to get involved because they don't know what's going to happen," said Kenneth P. Ducey, senior vice president of institutional trading at S.G. Warburg.

Prices on long-term Treasury issues fell 1 1/8 points, or $11.25 per $1,000 face amount. The benchmark 30-year issue had a yield of 8.83%, the highest since May of last year, and up from 8.72% Tuesday.

"The weakness in bonds over the last few days is taking its toll on the equity market," said Peter VandenBerg, a vice president of equity trading at Shearson Lehman Hutton.

Traders said the arbitrage-related selling in the afternoon was concentrated in futures contracts on the Major Market Index, a 20-stock average designed to mirror the Dow industrials. The index includes 17 issues that are also in the 30-stock average.

Higher rates also encouraged market participants to take profits stemming from the market's recent move toward the upper end of the trading range that has prevailed since January, traders said. The Dow industrials, at Tuesday's close, stood just 1.6% below the record high of 2810.15, set Jan. 2.

Technology stocks, which had benefited recently from the strong first-quarter results reported by International Business Machines and Motorola, were among the most prominent targets of profit-taking.

IBM dropped 1 1/4 to 109 7/8 and Motorola fell 3/4 to 67 7/8. Among other issues, Digital Equipment retreated 1 7/8 to 80, Hewlett Packard slipped 1 1/8 to 43 1/2, Compaq Computer eased 1 1/8 to 99 1/2, Cray Research skidded 1 3/8 to 45 1/2 and Texas Instruments fell 1 1/4 to 36 3/8.

However, Tandem Computers rose 1 1/8 to 24 1/8 in Big Board composite trading of two million shares as its earnings report for the fiscal second quarter, ended in March, was well-received. The company reported that net income rose more than 59% from the year-ago period, and its earnings per share of 25 cents was in line with analysts' recent forecasts.

Among retailing stocks, another strong group recently, shares of specialty retailers were hit the hardest. Tandy declined 1 3/4 to 32 1/2, Limited Inc. dropped 7/8 to 41 3/4, Gap Inc. fell 1 1/2 to 64 1/2, Circuit City Stores dropped 3/4 to 25 3/4 and Toys "R" Us slid 7/8 to 42 1/8.

Woolworth, which owns several specialty chains, dropped 7/8 to 61 3/4. As for other major retailers, Dayton Hudson fell 1 to 70 3/4, J.C. Penney fell 1 to 68 3/4, Wal- Mart Stores declined 1 1/8 to 49 3/8 and Dillard Department Stores dropped 2 3/8 to 72 7/8.

McKesson, which said it is boosting reserves for accounts receivable because of concern about the financial health of some of its retail customers, tumbled 2 1/4 to 34 3/8. The company didn't specify the amount of the increase, but said its earnings for the fiscal fourth quarter and year ended in March will be below year-earlier levels.

Merck, the best performer among the 30 stocks in the Dow industrials, gained 5/8 to 74 3/8 after reporting first-quarter earnings that exceeded the expectations of most analysts. The company's net income rose about 19% from the year-earlier level.

Philip Morris, whose net for the quarter was also better than expected, dropped 3/8 to 42 5/8 on 2.4 million shares after trading at higher levels for most of the session. The company's earnings rose 31%.

American Telephone & Telegraph dropped 1 to 41 despite reporting that first-quarter net was 11% higher than a year ago. Analysts had expected a better performance. Among other blue-chip companies issuing quarterly reports, United Technologies dipped 3/8 to 57 and Coca-Cola fell 1 to 79 1/4.

Stocks to benefit from positive earnings surprises included First Interstate Bancorp, which rose 7/8 to 36, and Phelps Dodge, which gained 1/2 to 61 7/8. Although first-quarter net at both companies fell short of year-ago levels, the declines weren't as sharp as had been expected.

Conversely, Polaroid fell 1 3/4 to 43 3/4 after posting a disappointing 23% rise in its first-quarter earnings. The company also scaled back its projected earnings growth for this year to "moderate" from "substantially higher"; it didn't provide a specific forecast.

Nervousness about the rate outlook proved favorable for precious-metals issues, generally viewed as a safe haven for investors. Homestake Mining rose 1 to 17 3/4, Placer Dome added 3/4 to 17, Battle Mountain Gold advanced 1/2 to 14 3/8 and ASA Ltd. rose 2 1/8 to 51 7/8.

Auto issues also escaped the worst of the downturn. General Motors added 1/8 to close at 46 on 1.6 million shares. Trading also was heavy in Ford Motor, which rose 7/8 to 48, and Chrysler, which slid 1/8 to 16 3/4.

Mattel rose 3/8 to 22 3/8 after Merrill Lynch raised its long-term investment rating on the stock to the firm's highest level. Merrill Lynch also reiterated its buy recommendation on Blockbuster Entertainment, which gained 1/8 to 18 3/8.

Thomson McKinnon Asset Management jumped 1 7/8 to 8. Thomson McKinnon Inc., which holds a 63% interest in the partnership, agreed in principle to sell its stake to three of the unit's managers.

CML Group fell 1 1/8 to 21 1/8. Investor Irwin Jacobs dropped plans for a proxy fight to replace its board with a slate committed to its sale. Separately, CML estimated that it would report a loss of $1.90 to $2 a share for the fiscal year ending in July.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.50 to 353.22. Volume totaled 13,370,000 shares compared with 13,510,000 Tuesday. There were 202 issues advancing and 352 declining.

American Fructose class A skidded 2 3/8 to 21 1/8. The company reported that first-quarter earnings fell about 10% from a year ago, while sales dropped 6%.
