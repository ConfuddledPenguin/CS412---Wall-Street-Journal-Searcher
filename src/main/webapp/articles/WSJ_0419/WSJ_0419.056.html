REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Gulliver for the Defense

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/19/90

Imagine that in a single year your business is the subject of hearings by 14 committees and 43 subcommittees of the U.S. Congress. Consider that about 1,500 staff members devote nearly all of their time to your issues. And imagine that they call your company 599,000 times in a year. You're beginning to understand what it's like to run the Pentagon. These and other amazing facts are contained in a study released this month by Secretary of Defense Dick Cheney. The 41-page "White Paper on the Department of Defense and the Congress" shows that it's impossible to understand gold-plated Pentagon hammers without also understanding the meddling of the U.S. Congress.

"The current congressional defense process is characterized by a multiplicity of actors, frequent decisions, lack of finality, disintegration and lack of accountability," says the White Paper, in typical Pentagonese. In other words, Dick Cheney is Gulliver in Capitol Hill's Lilliput. While influence by Congress is obviously important in a democracy, congressional control over defense has grown like Topsy in the past 20 years. It's yet more evidence that Congress has grabbed power from the executive branch since Watergate.

In 1988 alone Congress sent the Pentagon some 18,000 letters, usually in the form of all-purpose requests for documents or information. The Pentagon spent some 245,000 hours just answering this mail. Then there are congressional requests for "reports." Those increased to 861 requests in 1989 from 36 in 1970, or more than 2,000%. The average report takes about 1,000 hours to prepare and costs about $50,000. Among the urgent report topics: a new hospital at the Army medical center near Rep. Pat Schroeder's Colorado district.

Capitol Hill supervision is most inane on the budget, with the President's annual request now little more than a sideshow. Congress's budget fixes doubled in number in the 1970s and they multiplied by several hundred percent again from 1982 to 1987. Congress now alters more than 60% of the Pentagon's budget line items.

As if this weren't enough, no budget decision is ever final. The White Paper points out that a program can be adjusted on at least 14 different occasions as it makes its way through the annual legislative "process." An unneeded F-14D jet can be slipped in at any point along the way, or a B-2 bomber chopped away.

The classic examples are the four "studies" Mr. Cheney has to supply Congress before he can even think about closing a military base. Mikhail Gorbachev has an easier time closing bases. An American President's only leverage is the veto, but only if he has the nerve to veto an entire spending bill (or close down the entire government if it gets packed into one giant "continuing resolution").

The White Paper doesn't quantify the cost of all this, but it's safe to assume it's billions of dollars each year. With each dollar meaning more as defense spending is cut, we'd have thought this problem might warrant some Washington attention. Mr. Cheney's report barely rated a paragraph in most major news media. Somehow Pentagon waste, fraud and abuse is a story only when defense contractors are involved.

The complaint on the Hill just now is that Mr. Cheney hasn't proposed a new defense "strategy." But what's the point of a strategy if more than 60% of what's proposed is sliced, diced or spliced? If the Lilliputians want to strangle Gulliver, they should also get credit for the "strategy."
