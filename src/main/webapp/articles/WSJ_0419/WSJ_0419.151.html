Coca-Cola Posts 9.1% Profit Gain For 1st Quarter --- Strong International Sales Led to Earnings Growth; Revenue Increased 12.4%

  • Author: Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 04/19/90

ATLANTA -- Coca-Cola Co. said first-quarter net income climbed 9.1% to $283.5 million from $259.8 million, fueled by a strong 9% growth in international volume, as measured in gallon sales of concentrates and syrups. The soft-drink giant said gallon sales swelled 10% in Japan, one of its biggest profit centers, as a result of new packaging and additional flavors of noncarbonated products.

Per-share earnings in the latest quarter rose 15% to 83 cents from 72 cents, in part because of a 5% drop in the number of shares outstanding. Last year's quarterly results included a gain of $15 million, or three cents a share, from the sale of the company's coffee business.

First-quarter revenue rose 12.4% to $2.15 billion from $1.91 billion a year ago.

The results, which were in line with most Wall Street analysts' estimates, reflect Coke's reliance on its operations abroad as the driving force behind its profitability. International sales today account for 80% of the company's soft-drink operating profit and 65% of soft-drink revenue. Retail case sales abroad rose 10%.

"These results are a hint of things to come," said George Thompson, an analyst with Prudential-Bache. "With international sales up 10%, Coke is at the higher end of its 8% to 10% long-range target for growth. In my opinion, that can continue for some time."

For all of the Pacific Rim, gallon sales increased 6%, including an increase of more than 25% in Thailand because of added equipment and successful marketing. Coke said retail-case sales grew 13% in the Pacific.

The one dismal spot abroad was Argentina, where gallon sales plummeted 42% in the quarter as a result of the South American country's economic morass. Even so, Coke was able to post a 2% increase in gallon sales in Latin America as a whole, with a good showing in Mexico and Brazil.

The company said gallon sales in Europe soared 23% in the first quarter, largely as a result of German bottlers accumulating inventory and improved distribution in France. Analysts say the spread of democracy in Eastern Europe bodes particularly well for Coke. "With the walls coming down, Coke is in excellent position to take advantage of trade abroad," said John Maxwell, an analyst with Wheat First Securities.

By contrast, the company's domestic performance was lackluster. Retail case sales in the U.S. increased 3%, with fountain volume rising a meager 1%. Gallon sales of concentrates and syrups in the U.S. increased 2%. "Domestically, it's nothing to write home about," said Emanuel Goldman, an analyst with PaineWebber Research in San Francisco, who blamed continuing softness in fast-food restaurant operations for the disappointing fountain results. "The fountain is the drag here, and that's one-third of domestic business."

Coke's archrival, PepsiCo Inc., is expected to report that it fared better domestically in the first quarter, with a 6% growth in gallon sales, Mr. Goldman said. Among other things, the Purchase, N.Y.-based company isn't as dependent on fountain sales as Coke is and those restaurants where it is sold, particularly its own Pizza Hut and Taco Bell chains, are generally enjoying heavier customer traffic. "Certainly in the first quarter, Pepsi's numbers domestically will outshine Coke," Mr. Goldman said. "But Coke's international business is very strong, and that's where it makes its money -- overseas."

In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, the stock closed at $79.25, down $1.
