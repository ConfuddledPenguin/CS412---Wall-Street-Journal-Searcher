McKesson to Take 4th-Period Charge; Stock Falls Sharply

  • Author: Ken Wells
  • Date: 04/19/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- Debt burdened retailers are giving McKesson Corp. a big headache. Stock of the drug distribution giant plunged $2.25 to $34.375 yesterday following an announcement that it was taking a sizable charge against fiscal fourth-quarter earnings because some of its highly leveraged retail customers had fallen behind on their bills.

McKesson declined to give a dollar figure for the reserves. But the company said the charge would depress earnings for the quarter ended March 31 "significantly" below the $25.4 million, or 59 cents a share, it earned in the year-earlier period. McKesson also said full fiscal-year profit would be down "modestly" from year-earlier levels, when it posted a profit of $100.6 million, or $2.28 a share.

One analyst estimated the charge at between $10 million and $15 million and said that while McKesson had forecast problems with its accounts receivables, the size of the reserves came as something of a surprise.

"We expected McKesson to take a charge but weren't thinking of anything this large," said David Keuler of Blunt, Ellis & Loew, a Milwaukee research concern. On the "positive side, we think McKesson has pretty much taken all of the reserves it's going to need" to cover the problem bills, Mr. Keuler added.

McKesson said it decided to take the charge against earnings after a two-month credit review showed that some of its retail customers had "employed excessive leverage in recent years and are using their suppliers to supplement their bank credit lines." McKesson declined to name the retailers but analysts said they likely include a big East Coast department store chain as well as a nationwide chain of convenience stores that carry McKesson products.

"Given the narrow margins inherent in the distribution business, the risk-reward ratio for McKesson to act as an unsecured creditor in those situations is not consistent with prudent business practices," the company said.

The negative stock market reaction to McKesson wasn't helped any by a separate announcement that fiscal fourth-quarter earnings for its 83%-owned Armor All Products unit would be "disappointing." McKesson declined to elaborate but analysts said results of the car-care products subsidiary would likely be negatively affected by a recent change in accounting for marketing costs, a change in buying habits of automobile-care retailers and by a general slowdown in sales of Armor All products.

"Growth in Armor All is kind of slow," said Mr. Kueler of Blunt, Ellis. "Cars these days are being built with better finishes, which means people don't need as many products as they used to."
