Democrats on House Budget Panel Split Over Proposed Cuts in Military Spending

  • Author: Jeffrey H. Birnbaum
  • Date: 04/19/90

WASHINGTON -- Military-spending provisions of the House Budget Committee chairman's deficit-reduction blueprint ran into dissension among members of his own Democratic Party. Only a few hours after House Speaker Thomas Foley predicted that the fiscal 1991 budget resolution would pass the House, committee Democrats huddled in private to discuss vigorous demands by angry liberals that part of the military spending budget be reduced even further than Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) had recommended. Committee members gave strong indications last evening that the spending-authority portion of the military budget would be reduced.

But that change, if made, could alienate conservative members of the Democratic Party and create additional problems for the budget blueprint. The committee has scheduled a drafting session for today to discuss the document. The Republican minority on the committee is expected to oppose the Panetta plan entirely, leaving its fate fully in the hands of the Democrats. Whatever the committee decides, amendments could also be made on the House floor.

As expected, the Panetta proposal calls for a cut in military outlays of $11.5 billion and a tax increase of $13.9 billion.

The contentious element in the military-spending debate is a category separate from outlays, called spending authority. The Panetta plan would set spending authority at $286 billion; the liberals are aiming to trim that number to $283 billion, which would have the effect of reducing outlays, or actual expenditures, in future years. Actual military spending under the Panetta plan would total $295 billion in fiscal 1991, which starts Oct. 1.

The military-spending level is only one hurdle faced by Rep. Panetta. The other is that it remains unclear at best how much of the blueprint will ultimately be translated into law. More than half the $36.2 billion in the deficit reduction proposed in the document, including all of the tax increase, is contingent on negotiations with President Bush, and, so far at least, neither the president nor the Democratic leaders in Congress have shown much more than rhetorical interest in compromise.

The goal of the budget resolution is to lay out a blueprint for spending and tax legislation by other committees. It is designed to reduce the deficit in fiscal 1991 to $64 billion from the projected deficit of $100.5 billion that would occur without new legislation.

The resolution doesn't require the president's signature, but Democrats made clear it will require his acquiescence if it is to be put into effect. Speaking of talks with the president, Speaker Foley, a Washington Democrat, said, "There is a sense that we may become more engaged later on."

In the meantime, Rep. Panetta, backed by the House Democratic leadership, offered a plan yesterday that gets the bulk of its savings from a combination of militaryspending reductions that are deeper than the White House wants, and tax increases that haven't yet been negotiated with a president who is reluctant at best to accept any.

In addition, the plan claims $9.1 billion in savings from an amorphous cluster of proposals that are almost solely reliant on actions by the executive branch and that congressional Democrats had previously derided as illusory. They include, for example, a $3 billion savings from a reorganization of the Internal Revenue Service.

The Panetta plan covers fiscal year 1991, but it also purports to make recommendations for budget savings over five years. It also urges removal of the Social Security trust fund from the regular budget-deficit calculation under the Gramm-Rudman law. Legislation to authorize that change is likely to come later this year, Mr. Panetta said.

The Panetta plan is the Democrats' first detailed response to President Bush's own budget plan. Compared with the president's plan, the Panetta plan seeks almost $8 billion less in military outlays, far fewer cuts in nonmilitary spending, especially in Medicare and farm subsidies, and also recommends $6 billion in new spending on programs ranging from child care to space exploration. Rep. Panetta said it represents a transition from "a Cold War economy to a peacetime economy."

The Senate Budget Committee, its work delayed by the funeral in Hawaii of Sen. Spark Matsunaga, is planning to begin work on its budget resolution next week.
