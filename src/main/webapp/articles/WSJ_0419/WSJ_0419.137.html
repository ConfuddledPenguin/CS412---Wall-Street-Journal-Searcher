Giving a Hand: Central P&L Employees Create An Alternate Energy Source

  • Author: John D. Williams
  • Date: 04/19/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

Central Power & Light Co. to help financially strapped customers took an unintended turn when the utility's employees got involved.

CPL serves southern Texas, an area with pockets of poverty as severe as any found in the U.S. Late in 1988, as an increasing number of the utility's customers began falling behind on electricity bills, CPL came up with a deal: Let our crews help make your home energy efficient -- and bring those bills to a manageable level -- by caulking and weatherstripping away drafts.

Soon, and almost by accident, the program widened. CPL meter readers suggested that some customers' homes needed more than a window frame sealed. Other company employees took up the challenge, offering to work on weekends, without pay, to renovate homes in need.

About 200 of CPL's 2,400 employees, ranging from clerks to executives, now participate in the volunteer effort, contributing their time while their employer and local welfare agencies contribute construction materials.

Employees in the program, dubbed "Reddy Neighbor," a play on the electric-utility industry's Reddy Kilowatt logo, say the satisfaction they get from helping their neighbors is immense. Richard Earnest, CPL's southern region marketing manager during the week and construction foreman on Saturdays and Sundays, particularly recalls the work he and his fellow employees performed for 80-year-old Eusebia Quiroz, a retired field worker. Her four-room frame shanty was in such bad shape that "at times we felt we had bitten off more than we could chew," Mr. Earnest says. But after three weekends of hammering and sawing by 50 CPL volunteers working under Mr. Earnest's direction, the Quiroz home became a house, with a new roof, new sheet-rock walls, new wiring, hot water and a fresh coat of paint.

All told, Mr. Earnest and his fellow volunteers repaired 12 homes last year, donating 3,000 hours of their time.

Homeowners like the frail Miss Quiroz -- who spent her working years picking okra, melons and other fruits and vegetables -- are asked to sign waivers absolving the volunteers and CPL from legal responsibility for the work done. (Some of the volunteers are construction-trade workers for CPL, but most work in other fields.)

CPL, based in Corpus Christi and a subsidiary of Central & South West Corp. of Dallas, spent $12,000 last year on materials and is careful to say that this nominal sum comes from its "below the line" shareholder account. As a regulated business, CPL wins rates based on the costs of providing service, and not on the costs of improving customers' homes or facilities.

In return for this effort, CPL gains customer respect; it also keeps customers. Robert Carey, CPL president and chief executive officer, says that, among other things, the program "gives us a chance to help some people who can use a hand." But neither the utility nor its employees make a big deal of it. Once a year, CPL throws a barbecue for its volunteer workers and gives them a T-shirt bearing the program's "Reddy Neighbor" logo.
