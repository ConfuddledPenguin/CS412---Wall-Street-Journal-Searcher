Dun & Bradstreet Net Plunged 23% In the 1st Quarter

  • Author: Johnnie L. Roberts
  • Date: 04/19/90

NEW YORK -- Dun & Bradstreet Corp.'s first-quarter profit fell 23%, reflecting the biggest shock yet to earnings from a year-old controversy surrounding sales practices in the company's credit services operations. The information company also cited the impact of an expired contract on its Donnelley Directory operations.

Separately, directors declared a 6% increase in the quarterly dividend to 53 cents a share from 50 cents, payable June 8 to stock of record May 18.

Financial analysts said they expected the results, which the company had been clearly signaling. Yesterday, Dun & Bradstreet reiterated a forecast of 1990 per-share earnings of about $2.80, indicating an 11% decline from 1989's $3.14 a share.

In the quarter, net income totaled $110.3 million, or 60 cents a share, down from $143.3 million, or 77 cents a share, the year earlier. Revenue rose 8.5% to $1.15 billion from $1.1 billion. The company noted, however, that the gain was only 4% after adjusting for the acquisition of Management Science America, a software concern, and the divestiture of Thomas Cook Travel.

Yesterday, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, D&B closed unchanged at $47.125 a share, down 12.5 cents.

The earnings drop "was due in part to a decrease in revenue at U.S. Credit Services and substantial increase in costs" to revamp the operations, the company said. The company added that world-wide revenue for credit services declined, largely because of a "high-single-digit" drop in first-quarter revenue of U.S. credit services. A spokesman declined to elaborate.

Last year, many customers accused Dun & Bradstreet salespeople of misleading them to buy far more credit services than the company knew were needed. The company has conceded that, in some cases, customers were abused. In addition, without admitting any wrongdoing, the company reached an $18 million agreement to settle five major lawsuits that similarly alleged customers were oversold services through deceit. As part of the settlement, the company agreed to modify its business practices in several important respects. And it was those changes that accounted for substantial cost increases in the credit services business.

In the wake of the controversy, sales growth of credit services has been weak, but Dun & Bradstreet's profit had continued to increase until the first quarter.

Dun & Bradstreet revenue for its directory information services fell 21% to $90.3 million from $114.3 million the year earlier because of the absence of revenue from a Donnelley directory contract and the timing of revenue from yellow pages directories.

In other highlights, the company said, revenue grew 12% in marketing information services, 5.8% in financial information services and 30% in business services.
