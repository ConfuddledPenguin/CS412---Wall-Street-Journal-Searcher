Economy: Trade Deficit Shrank Sharply In February --- Gap, Smallest Since 1983, Is Attributed by Some To Weak U.S. Economy

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 04/19/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. merchandise trade deficit narrowed sharply to $6.49 billion in February, the smallest monthly imbalance since December 1983, the Commerce Department reported. The $2.83 billion improvement from January's deficit came from a 7.6% fall in imports, to $38.12 billion, the smallest level of purchases from abroad in a year. But exports also dropped; they slipped 1% from a record high in January, to $31.63 billion. Only aircraft exports showed marked improvement.

Many economists were surprised by the extent of the narrowing in the trade deficit. They said it stemmed from a weak economy rather than growing attractiveness of American-made products in the U.S. or abroad.

"Declining trade deficits which are led by broad-based declines in imports reflect either a weakening economy or the recapture of market share by domestic producers; in this case, I think it reflects a weakening economy," said Joseph Carson, senior economist at Chemical Bank in New York. "It doesn't reflect a good and growing economy on our part."

The drop in imports was led by a large decline in U.S. purchases of foreign oil. The country's bill for imported petroleum products declined by more than $1 billion, to $4.71 billion from $5.86 billion in January, according to the report. Analysts said the drop partly reflected falling oil prices. But the volume of oil imports also decreased -- to 243 million barrels from 291.3 million barrels in January.

In January, oil imports had surged after extremely cold weather in December boosted energy consumption, sopping up U.S. oil supplies. Nevertheless, many analysts remain concerned about the country's dependency on foreign oil.

"Oil imports went down, but they are still well above last year's average," said Gregory Fager, chief economist at First City Bancorp. of Texas, in Houston. With domestic oil production at its lowest level in years, he warned, "it will become increasingly difficult to improve our external (trade) accounts."

The U.S. has made striking headway in reducing its massive trade deficit over the past two years. Aided by a fall in the value of the dollar through much of 1987, exports have soared above their levels in the mid-1980s.

Many economists expect the improvement to continue, but they doubt gains will be very substantial in the coming months. The dollar has been rising, making American goods more expensive abroad and foreign goods cheaper here.

"Certainly, overseas markets are stronger than ours, but I think those could weaken a bit," Mr. Carson said. "We have yet to see the dollar's impact on our export performance. I don't think exports will be very strong over next quarter."

Still, Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher noted that "1990 has begun with an encouraging 11.6% increase in exports over the same two months of last year."

"It is most encouraging," he added, "to note that over half of the export increase is in advanced technology, especially aircraft."

But a number of analysts cautioned that much of the February gains in aircraft exports resulted from Boeing Co.'s shipping a backlog of orders. Boeing's production had been severely hindered by a strike last fall.

The Treasury, in a report that was ordered by Congress and released yesterday, said, "Further improvement in the U.S. current account position in 1990 is likely at best to be very modest . . . (and) the possibility of deterioration . . . cannot be excluded." The current account is the broadest measure of the U.S. trade deficit. It includes trade in goods as well as trade in services and financial flows.

The Treasury said that the U.S. isn't likely to make as much progress closing its trade gap with Japan in 1990 as it has in recent years, and warned that "a modest widening" is possible. The report added that the reunification of East Germany and West Germany, by increasing domestic demand within a united Germany, could help reduce the German trade surplus.

The U.S. trade deficit with Japan, consistently the largest the U.S. has with any country, accounts for half this country's total trade imbalance. But while the gap edged wider in February, to $3.12 billion from $2.86 billion in January, it was substantially below the monthly average for last year. The Bush administration has been employing heavy pressure in Japan to open its markets to more American products.

Autos were one of the few categories of imports that posted increase in February. Imports of industrial supplies, consumer goods, capital goods and foods all fell.

While aircraft exports doubled, exports of industrial supplies and foods dropped and exports of cars and consumer goods remained practically the same as the month before.

Based on new data, the department revised upward its estimate of January's trade gap, to $9.32 billion from the $9.25 billion it calculated last month.

All the numbers were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
