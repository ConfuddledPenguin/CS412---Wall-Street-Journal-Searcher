AMR Posts Loss Of $19.3 Million For First Quarter

  • Author: Bridget O'Brian
  • Date: 04/19/90

AMR Corp., the parent of American Airlines, reported a first-quarter net loss of $19.3 million, its first quarterly deficit in more than two years. A poor showing had been expected. AMR's vocal chairman, Robert L. Crandall, had publicly warned that the company's first-quarter results wouldn't come close to last year's $101 million profit. AMR's per-share loss was 31 cents, compared with year-earlier profit of $1.60 a share.

Nonetheless, in a statement, Mr. Crandall said he was "very disappointed" with the loss. "Our results can only be called unsatisfactory," he said.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, AMR stock closed at $64.50, down 87.5 cents.

AMR's results are frequently the bellwether for the industry, with the company's profits higher and losses narrower than those of its competitors. First-quarter results of other carriers are likely to run true to this form, analysts said.

"It's going to be a bad quarter for everybody," said Thomas Longman, an analyst with Bear, Stearns & Co. Mr. Longman said he expects losses at other carriers to surpass AMR's.

At AMR, a 9.7% increase in revenue to $2.69 billion, couldn't offset sharply higher operating costs. AMR was squeezed by higher fuel prices, soft travel demand and static fares. Plus, the first quarter is traditionally the weakest period in the airline industry because the span is sandwiched between peak holiday travel and the busy summer months.

While the company's load factor, or percentage of seats filled, held steady at 59.9%, its break-even load factor was 60.6%, compared with last year's 54.8%.

Currently, many of the factors that made for a rough first quarter seem to be receding. Fuel prices, while not down to last year's levels, are somewhat lower. Demand is up. Also, this year's Easter season, always a high-traffic period, was in the second quarter. That should help offset expected difficulties airlines may have this quarter making fare increases stick, Mr. Longman said.
