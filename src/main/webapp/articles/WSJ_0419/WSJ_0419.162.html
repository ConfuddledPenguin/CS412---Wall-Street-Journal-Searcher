Kenny Learns About Show Biz Of Another Kind

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/19/90

Kenny Rogers, a mellow sort of fellow, this week finds himself tangled in a decidedly unmellow dispute. Rolling Stone magazine has named the bearded balladeer in its "Hall of Shame" hit list of international environmental sinners. The magazine also included the Journal's editorial page on its roster -- a more predictable choice. But we were surprised to find ourselves in such affable company. Rolling Stone describes Mr. Rogers as an "ardent apologist for the Los Angeles-based Summit Cement Company, which has sought to dump toxic wastes from the U.S. mainland on Grand Bahama Island." Rolling Stone says Mr. Rogers flew the Bahamian health minister to Florida to help Summit. Wrong, says Kenny. He did fly ministers to Florida, but it was because he was thinking of buying a hotel from the Bahamian government. The singer is indeed a friend of Summit's president, Finn Moller. But the only "help" Mr. Rogers gave Mr. Moller was to aid him in investigating the environmental safety of a plant Summit thought of buying on Grand Bahama. In fact, charges Mr. Rogers, Rolling Stone only heard about the matter because his own investigation was so large.

Mr. Rogers is on a 70-city concert tour with Dolly Parton. But during a break he spoke with Amity Shlaes of the editorial page by phone from his farm in Athens, Ga. Excerpts:

What did you feel when you saw your name in Rolling Stone?

This kind of thing really takes the wind out of your sails. It makes you wonder why you do things in life.

What really hap pened with the ce ment plant in the Bahamas?

At the start I was interested in re searching whether the plant would be safe because I was thinking of investing {in the Bahamas} myself. What I found was there are already 15 cement plants of this type operating legally in the United States. They use hazardous waste not toxic waste {as Rolling Stone asserts}. Every one of those {plants} wrote me positive letters. Then I call Greenpeace in San Francisco. I say to them as a joke: "I know that you guys are the shark in this situation" -- meaning that they are the hardcore people who can answer. The guy says "off the record, I'm sure the plant is safe but I still can't approve."

What did he mean?

It appears some people would rather win than be right.

Why is it OK to build a plant that uses hazardous waste at all?

I am a strange kind of guy. In the world there are very few things that are black and white. In this case the hazardous waste, a petroleum byproduct, is used to make a product that is safe. {In general} something needs to be done with hazardous waste. Until we find an alternative source, what do you do with that waste? This seemed a good use.

Why do you think Greenpeace and Rolling Stone are picking on you?

They jumped on the bandwagon and used me because I get more press than anybody else.

What does Dolly think?

She has never mentioned it. We usually run into each other only on stage.

Are you doing anything for Earth Day?

In my business you learn early that you have to pick your causes. Mine are world hunger and the homeless. Every year I host a weekend here at my farm to raise money for the homeless.

Has environmentalism gone too far in America?

I have never been a person who admires extremists in any field.

Robert Vare, the Rolling Stone editor who handled the "Hall of Shame" section, says the magazine stands by its report. Kenny Rogers never did buy the hotel on the Bahamas. Finn Moller, the man who wanted to buy the cement plant, says environmentalist roadblocks forced him to give up the project, which would have brought 400 jobs to the underemployed islands.

As someone once sang: You've got to know when to hold 'em, know when to fold 'em.
