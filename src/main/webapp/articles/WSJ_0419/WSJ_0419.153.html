Ford May Buy Marine Midland Financing Arm

  • Author: Bradley A. Stertz and Robert Guenther
  • Date: 04/19/90

DEARBORN, Mich. -- Ford Motor Co. said it is negotiating to buy the auto finance operations of Marine Midland Banks Inc., which in the 1980s led an unsuccessful rush of commercial banks into the car-lending business long dominated by auto makers. Ford didn't disclose how much it might pay for the auto business of the Buffalo, N.Y.-based bank. But a Ford spokesman said the No. 2 auto maker hopes to complete the talks within a month and intends to keep Marine Midland's operations separate from its Ford Motor Credit Co. unit.

With a portfolio of roughly $6 billion, Marine Midland is the nation's fourth-largest auto lender behind the finance arms of Ford, General Motors Corp. and Chrysler Corp. Marine Midland is also one of the last major banks still competing against the Big Three's credit companies.

Marine Midland Automotive Financial Corp. thrived in the 1980s by signing finance agreements with car importers such as Saab-Scania AB and by lining up credit agreements with foreign car dealers who didn't have close ties with a Big Three finance subsidiary.

Just last August, Marine signed an agreement to form a jointly owned finance subsidiary with the U.S. sales arm of Jaguar PLC. The British auto maker has since been purchased by Ford.

Marine also has finance arrangements with Mazda Motor Corp., in which Ford owns a 25% stake, and Hertz Corp., which is owned by a company in which Ford has a 49% stake.

The success of Marine Midland spurred other major banks, including Security Pacific Corp. and Citicorp to try developing sophisticated auto financing operations of their own. But in recent years, the business soured on the commercial banks.

When most of the banks entered the auto finance business, car sales had been surging for several years. But by the late 1980s, the U.S. car market was beginning to slump.

In addition, the banks suffered because the Big Three finance arms became more aggressive in lining up business with import car stores owned by domestic car dealers. Cut-rate financing programs employed by the Big Three hurt the ability of commercial banks to keep pace. And many banks saw default rates soar because they screened too loosely car loan applicants.

The difficulties forced commercial banks out as serious competitors with the Big Three, although most banks still offer some consumer car loans and dealer financing.

An additional factor influenced Marine's decision to consider selling its car-loan business: the low tolerance of its parent, Hongkong & Shanghai Banking Corp., for sub-par returns. Though Marine Midland's auto finance operations have been very profitable in the past, margins have shrunk as sales of high-priced foreign cars in the U.S. fell.

For all of 1989, a large provision for loan losses pushed Marine Midland's earnings down to only $13.9 million compared to $160.5 million in 1988. In this year's first quarter, Marine earned $6 million, far below the $40.2 million of a year ago, due to restructuring charges.

Marine Midland last summer tried to move its auto loan operations into a joint venture with Aon Corp. of Chicago and Orient Finance Co. Ltd. of Japan. But that deal unraveled late last year, with Marine blaming uncertainty in the U.S. auto market.

Over the last year, Marine has sold its stakes in two banks that it intended to acquire, put a lid on loan growth and sold $160 million in students loans and its credit card processing business for merchants.

In part, Hongkong & Shanghai appears to be shepherding its resources in anticipation of some sort of closer union with Britain's Midland Bank PLC. Hongkong & Shanghai owns a minority stake in the British bank, and a standstill agreement regarding further purchases expires later this year.

A Ford spokesman said the auto maker isn't negotiating to buy Marine's $6 billion in auto receivables. Marine executives didn't return calls seeking comment.
