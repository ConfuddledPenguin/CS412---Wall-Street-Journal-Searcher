Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Haagen-Dazs Goes Whole Hog With Ads

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 04/19/90

Twenty-five years ago, Haagen-Dazs Co. opened for business and quickly created a niche in the U.S. ice cream market. Its super-premium ice cream was an especially big hit in the 1980s, when consumers turned to the pricey desserts. But as a new decade gets under way, Haagen-Dazs and its fellow players at ice cream's highest echelon are finding themselves at a difficult juncture: Consumers want to indulge -- but they don't necessarily want the fat and cholesterol that go along with super-rich ice cream.

Some companies such as Ben & Jerry's Homemade and Steve's Homemade Ice Cream are trying to address the problem by introducing new light products. But Haagen-Dazs is sticking with the status quo -- at least for the time being -- and is trying an aggressive ad campaign to recruit new takers to its rich-and-creamy menu. In the words of its new ad slogan, the Teaneck, N.J., unit of Grand Metropolitan invites consumers to "Enter the State of Haagen-Dazs."

"This is the time to make sure the consumer finds this brand inviting and accessible," says Yves E. Coleon, vice president of marketing at Haagen-Dazs. "We want to make the brand relevant to a broader group of people."

That'll be no easy task. Since 1988, the entire ice cream category, including super-premium products, has been sliding. According to the International Ice Cream Association, a trade group, consumption across the board has been falling about 4% a year for the past two years.

Haagen-Dazs concedes its rate of sales growth has slowed, but the company insists it continues to post annual increases in sales.

Haagen-Dazs's new $4 million ad campaign, created by Margeotes Fertitta & Weiss, will keep print ads in magazines from May through September. Outdoor advertising is planned in a few big markets. A radio campaign is in the works, and the company is considering TV advertising.

In addition, Haagen-Dazs is developing a number of special promotions in a bid to boost sales. The first is a Mother's Day promotion in which Haagen-Dazs will pitch ice cream as an alternative to roses as a gift for mom. Consumers can call a toll-free number and order gift certificates that the recipient can redeem for pints of ice cream. Haagen-Dazs says such promotions offer a significant opportunity to hook up with new consumers who may not have had much exposure to the brand.

The new print ads, which first appear in next week's issue of People, feature quirky portraits of various Haagen-Dazs enthusiasts eating their favorite dessert. In each ad, a model sports a wide grin. But while the subjects' grins reflect childlike excitement, the product itself is presented with a lot of respect. One ad shows a Haagen-Dazs pop resting on a velvet pillow.

Haagen-Dazs also is placing ads in tonier publications such as Vanity Fair and Esquire. And in an unusual media strategy for an ice-cream product, the company also hopes to tap the cutting-edge crowd by advertising in magazines such as Egg, Interview and Details.

Despite the high-brow imagery in some of the new ads, Haagen-Dazs's Mr. Coleon believes the campaign overall is brighter and friendlier than past campaigns, which may have reinforced an "elitist" image.

He points to recent campaigns that featured some confusing images of, among other things, a man with blue feet who was poised to jump off a diving board into a "pool" of chocolate ice cream. The copy read: "If you're afraid of emotional heights, beware of Haagen-Dazs deep chocolate."

"In the past, we may have taken ourselves a bit too seriously," he says.

CBS Touts Older Viewers

CBS Inc., whose TV network attracts older demographic groups than those traditionally desired by advertisers, yesterday released a study that showed, to no one's surprise, the advantage of marketing to older viewers.

The study, "Coming of Age: Targeting the Middle of the Market," will be used in presentations to ad agency media planners by CBS, which finished in the ratings basement for the third consecutive year in the 1989-90 prime-time season.

The key demographic for the 1990s is the 35-54 age group, according to Thomas Leahy, president of CBS Marketing. That group, says Mr. Leahy, consists of the "aging baby boomers who have the strongest economic value for advertisers."

The most sought-after demographic for advertisers is women 18 to 49 years old.

The study, conducted by CBS and Ephron, Papazian & Ephron, showed that the "middle of the market" in 1990 for most goods and services advertised on network TV is a 40-year-old consumer, not the 30-year-old from a decade ago.

John Sisk, a senior vice-president at J. Walter Thompson, said that although 35 to 49 years old is a "key age group, there are questions about how key of a demographic people over 50 years old can be." One reason networks want to attract younger viewers, said Mr. Sisk, is because that particular demographic group "tends to be open to sample new programming."

Ad Notes. . . .

COLDWELL BANKER: Coldwell Banker, Mission Viejo, Calif., awarded its $15 million account to Grey Advertising, Los Angeles. Grey will create ads for the Sears, Roebuck unit's residential real estate business. The account was previously handled by Omnicom Group's BBDO agency, Los Angeles.

GOLDEN VALLEY: Golden Valley Microwave Foods Inc., Edina, Minn., named Martin/Williams Advertising, Minneapolis, to handle its Act II breakfast foods account. Billings haven't been determined.
