AT&T Net Rose 12%, Sales 2.7% In 1st Quarter --- Long-Distance Operations, Slimmer Costs Cited; Network Failure Hurts

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 04/19/90

LOS ANGELES -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co., buoyed by continued strength in its long-distance business and slimmer costs, said net income rose 12% in the quarter on a 2.7% increase in sales. Net income for the first three months was $668 million, or 62 cents a share, from $594 million, or 55 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue rose to $8.89 billion from $8.66 billion in the 1989 quarter.

While earnings were slightly below analysts' expectations of 68 cents to 70 cents a share, AT&T posted a strong 21% increase in operating profit in the quarter, to $1.18 billion from $977 million the previous year, indicating that its struggle to control costs is paying off.

Net income was hurt by several factors including increased interest expenses, a higher tax rate and costs associated with last January's network disaster, when a software problem shut down the company's nationwide long-distance network for nine hours. In addition, AT&T's telecommunications-equipment business continues to suffer from increased price competition and a softening of orders for switching equipment from local telephone companies.

Despite these problems, AT&T Chairman Robert E. Allen said the New York-based company's results showed it was off to "a strong start toward making 1990 an even stronger year than 1989, which was our best."

Mr. Allen, who made his comments at the company's annual meeting at the convention center here, added that AT&T has set a goal for itself of increasing its earnings by at least 10% to 12% annually. "We were clearly ahead of that in the first quarter. Typically, the first quarter is a weak one for us." (Profit in the first quarter actually rose slightly more than 12%.)

However, AT&T received a boost from businesses that had not been a part of the company a year earlier, including Istel Group Ltd., a U.K. information services company acquired in the fourth quarter of 1989, and two divisions of PacifiCorp Financial Services, which were purchased by AT&T Capital Corp. during the first quarter.

AT&T's revenue from product sales, including telephone and computer equipment, increased a meager 1.2% to $2.72 billion from $2.69 billion the year before. Mr. Allen blamed the lackluster sales on the "rather weak economy" and a backlog in computer-systems sales "due to customer demand and our inability to fill that demand." To improve shipments, AT&T recently began manufacturing personal computers to augment supplies from Intel Corp.

Mr. Allen said the company is banking on new products to boost sales. These include its recently announced credit card, in which orders so far "have exceeded our wildest expectations . . . customers are calling us by the tens of thousands." In addition, he announced that AT&T will begin selling switching equipment based on an optical computer processor developed by Bell Laboratories, within five years.

Analysts reacted favorably to the company's results. "The earnings masked what was probably AT&T's best quarter since divestiture," said Jack B. Grubman, an analyst at PaineWebber Inc. Long-distance "volume is up 7% and operating margins were 13.2%, two points higher than a year ago. That's a lot of expansion for a company this size." He estimated that the network outage alone cost AT&T about three cents a share because of lost revenue and remittances to business customers covered under AT&T's 800 Assurance Plan.

Robert B. Morris III, telecommunications analyst at Goldman, Sachs & Co., said AT&T earnings were about three cents less than his estimate. And sales, general and administrative expenses were 35.2% of revenue "when I had expected 34%." But Mr. Morris said, "I wouldn't characterize this as a disappointing quarter."

At the shareholder meeting, Mr. Allen defended the company's previously reported decision to withdraw its funding of the Planned Parenthood Federation of America. AT&T announced the move on March 26 following pressure applied on the company by shareholding anti-abortion activists.

Referring to protests by Planned Parenthood, Mr. Allen said: "I'm disappointed in their actions. If someone were supporting me for 25 years, I would say `thank you.' In the last 25 years, we funded them to the tune of $600,000, and we never heard a word of thanks from them."

Mr. Allen added that AT&T will spend $2.5 million this year supporting teen-pregnancy prevention programs similar to those they had been supporting at Planned Parenthood.

Mr. Allen said Planned Parenthood has elevated the abortion issue to political advocacy and that "this caused our opponents and proponents to say that we are supporting abortion. I concluded -- I should say the {AT&T} foundation concluded -- that abortion or opposing abortion shouldn't be supported by the corporation."

A first-time shareholder proposal that AT&T stop funding organizations that advocate abortion managed to win 5.5% of the votes cast at the company's annual meeting, insuring its inclusion on the ballot for a second time at AT&T's 1991 meeting. Another resolution, calling on AT&T to drop its affirmative action program, garnered 8.8% of the vote, less than the 10% it needed to be included for the fourth time next year.

AT&T common stock fell $1 yesterday to close at $41 a share in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.
