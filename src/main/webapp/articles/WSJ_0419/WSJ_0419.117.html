What's News -- World-Wide

  • Date: 04/19/90

LITHUANIA SAID the Soviet Union severed oil supplies to the Baltic republic. Despite an offer by lawmakers in Vilnius to cease secession-related legislative action if the Kremlin opens immediate talks, a spokesman for Lithuania's legislature said Moscow cut off the supply of oil to the republic's only refinery. It wasn't known whether natural-gas deliveries also had been disrupted as part of Gorbachev's threatened economic embargo. Lithuania, which proclaimed its independence March 11, is entirely dependent on the Soviet Union for energy supplies.

Secretary of State Baker renewed a warning to Moscow, saying action against Lithuania could doom U.S. and Soviet commercial relations.

---

THE JUSTICES BACKED a judge's power to order school desegregation funds.

Defying expectations, a divided Supreme Court reaffirmed the authority of courts to correct constitutional violations, ruling that a federal judge may require a Kansas City, Mo., school district to raise taxes to pay for desegregation. The court also said a judge may block enforcement of state laws that interfere with funding the plan. At the same time, however, the justices said there must be limits on the enforcement power of federal judges.

The court, voting 6-3 in an Ohio case, ruled that a state may make it illegal to possess or view child pornography, even in the privacy of one's home.

---

Pro-Iranian kidnappers in Lebanon said they would release by tomorrow one of three American educators as a humanitarian gesture. The announcement by the group Islamic Jihad for the Liberation of Palestine, which was accompanied by a photograph of hostage Jesse Turner, said the captive would carry a message for Bush.

---

Bush closed an international conference on global warming by ignoring the differences among the delegates. Speaking at the end of the two-day parley in Washington aimed at forging a policy on the greenhouse effect, the president said the U.S. had "never considered research a substitute for action" on the issue.

---

A new cancer drug proved successful with very few side effects in treating a relatively rare form of leukemia, researchers said. The drug, 2-CdA, produced nearly complete remissions in 12 patients with hairy-cell leukemia who underwent one seven-day treatment.

---

Britain's government confirmed that steel tubes seized last week by officials in England are parts for a huge gun bound for Iraq. It was the first high-level acknowledgment of the pipes' military potential. But experts remain puzzled by the workings of such a weapon.

---

East and West Germany opened negotiations on reunification, and Bonn said talks on a monetary union could start tomorrow. West Germany's ZDF television network, meanwhile, reported that the Kremlin has told East German Prime Minister de Maiziere that it opposes quick unification.

---

Major universities are warning the Pentagon that a military policy barring homosexuals from service is generating pressure to oust ROTC from campuses. Groups from MIT, the University of Wisconsin, Northwestern and elsewhere have protested the officer-training corps policy. The Pentagon has declined to comment on the issue.

---

NASA advanced the date for the second launch attempt of the space shuttle Discovery, saying the spacecraft and the Hubble Space Telescope are ready for a Tuesday liftoff. The launch had been set for Wednesday, 15 days after the first attempt was scrubbed because of a faulty power unit.

---

A rifle-fired grenade slammed into a school bus during Christian factional fighting in Lebanon, killing at least 11 children and four other people. Police said the vehicle was hit while crossing into mainly Christian eastern Beirut as it was returning from the southern part of the capital.

---

A series of earthquakes rattled Northern California, but didn't cause major damage or injuries. The temblors, the hardest registering 5.4 on the Richter scale, shook a 100mile area from San Francisco to Salinas on the anniversary of the 1906 quake.

---

Mexico demanded that Washington explain the seizure of a Mexican doctor charged in the 1985 murder of U.S. drug agent Enrique Camarena. The physician reportedly was kidnapped by bounty hunters and brought to Los Angeles this month.

---

Nepal's prime minister-designate named eight cabinet ministers to form the Himalayan kingdom's first opposition-led government in almost 30 years. The official, dissident K.P. Bhattarai, said he would retain the defense and foreign portfolios. The list was sent to King Birendra for approval.
