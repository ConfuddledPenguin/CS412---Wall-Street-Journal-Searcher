Trade Panel Backs U.S. in Restricting Canadian Lobsters

  • Author: Rose Tamburri
  • Date: 05/29/90

OTTAWA -- A Canada-U.S. panel upheld U.S. restrictions on Canadian lobster imports, dealing a blow to Canada's depressed lobster industry. The panel, set up under the Canada-U.S. free-trade agreement, said the U.S. restrictions aren't a trade barrier because they don't apply to imports alone. Canada complained that the law violated the free-trade agreement, but the U.S. argued that the restrictions were necessary conservation measures.

The U.S. law, which took effect last December, prohibits imports of live lobsters smaller than the U.S. minimum legal size of 3 1/4 inches. The U.S. size minimum is to tighten to 3 5/16 inches by 1992. The regulations are designed to limit the catch of younger lobsters, which still are unable to reproduce.

Canada has less stringent size restrictions on its lobster catches because Canada has a larger lobster supply and because the Canadian lobsters grow in warmer waters, where they mature faster and are able to reproduce sooner. Canada also uses other conservation measures, including a shorter harvesting season.

Canadian exports of live lobster to the U.S. totaled US$100.8 million in 1989. The Canadian lobster industry estimated that the U.S. restrictions could result in a $25 million drop this year in Canadian lobster exports. The decline is expected to increase as the U.S. increases the minimum size of lobsters.

The Canadian industry already is suffering from depressed prices. Lobster prices have fallen in recent years because of a high Canadian dollar, declining demand and a 45% increase in landed catches since 1982.

"Lobster fishermen who have had to suffer through abnormally low prices are now faced with a restricted market," Russell MacLellan, a Member of Parliament from Nova Scotia, told the Canadian House of Commons. He called on the government to provide assistance to the Canadian lobster fishermen who would be hurt by the ruling.

New England lobster fishermen have complained that Canadian lobsters were flooding the U.S. market and depressing lobster prices.

In a minority finding, the panel, which includes three U.S. members and two Canadian, said the U.S. restrictions were a trade restriction as well as a conservation measure. U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills and Canadian Trade Minister John Crosbie have 90 days to consider the panel's report and decide what action to take.

The importance of the dispute was underscored last year when Prime Minister Brian Mulroney made a personal appeal to President George Bush. Mr. Mulroney called the president's decision to allow the restrictions a "deep disappointment."

The lobster ruling was the second fisheries dispute referred to a panel set up under the settlement mechanism of the free-trade agreement. The trade pact, which went into effect in January 1989, seeks to eliminate most trade barriers between the two countries. Last year, another panel ruled against Canada in a case involving West Coast salmon and herring. As a result of the ruling, Canada made 20% of its salmon and herring catch available to U.S. buyers directly without first being landed and counted in British Columbia ports.
