Summer Electric Capacity Seen Meeting Demand, but Some Brownouts Predicted

  • Author: Bill Paul
  • Date: 05/29/90

Residents of the nation's capital should hope that the only hot air they get next month is the political kind. The North American Electric Reliability Council predicted that, because of the temporary outage of a big power plant, hotter-than-normal weather in June may necessitate voltage reductions, also known as brownouts, in the Washington-Baltimore area.

While generally imperceptible, voltage reductions force machines to work harder, making them more trouble-prone. Moreover, a utility doesn't trim its output until after it has publicly appealed to its customers to voluntarily turn off air conditioners and other electrical appliances.

Otherwise, said the utility industry's Princeton, N.J.-based forecasting unit, there should generally be enough electrical capacity to meet this summer's peak demands. It added, however, that under certain circumstances -- very hot weather and unexpected plant outages -- the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic regions could have problems. It further said that Midwest utilities may be forced to trim sales to other regions if an unexpectedly high number of power plants go down.

Two private power-supply forecasters offered somewhat different assessments. In Lexington, Mass., Larry Mackovich, director of electricity services for McGraw-Hill Co.'s DRI Energy unit, said the only area where problems might occur this summer is the Mid-Atlantic region, specifically, Pennsylvania, New Jersey and Maryland.

Noting that utilities in that region are operating with only about a 13% reserve margin -- 17% to 20% is preferred -- Mr. Mackovich said there is a 50% chance of voltage reductions, and a 10% to 15% chance of rolling blackouts, during the first half of August, but only if the weather is hot and if there are unexpected plant outages.

Rolling blackouts occur when one section after another of a utility's service territory is blacked out by the utility, generally for a few hours at a time. Utilities employ the tactic only after brownouts have failed.

In New York, Marie Corio, president of Applied Economic Research Co., said the greater problem is in Virginia and North and South Carolina. Ms. Corio explained that a number of plants in that region aren't expected to be available this summer. Thus, she said, if the weather's hot, there's a 50% chance of brownouts and a 10% or so chance of rolling blackouts in Virginia and the Carolinas in July and August.

Ms. Corio agreed with Mr. Mackovich's assessment of Pennsylvania, New Jersey and Maryland. She further agreed with the Council that Midwest utilities might have to trim their "off-system" sales; Mr. Mackovich doubted that would happen. Both said they didn't know enough about the Baltimore-Washington situation to comment.
