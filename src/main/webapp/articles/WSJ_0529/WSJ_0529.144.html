Credit Markets: Bond Prices Should Turn On Job Data --- May Report Will Be A Clue to Whether Economy Is Sluggish

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 05/29/90

NEW YORK -- One clue to whether interest rates will fall further in the weeks ahead will be revealed on Friday, when the Labor Department releases the potentially market-moving employment report. In the mercurial bond market, the employment report has become a potent economic indicator with the power to instantly turn bond-market sentiment upside down.

This week, the employment report for May will be even more crucial in helping to shape investors' perceptions about the economy's strength. Analysts will be looking carefully at the report to see if the extremely weak employment numbers in March and April were aberrations, or confirmation that the economy is sluggish.

"A weak employment report is the only thing that can get the bond-market rally up and going again," said Sung Won Sohn, a senior vice president at Norwest Corp.

Immediately after the April employment report was released on May 4, bond-market sentiment suddenly shifted from bearish to bullish because the jobs data suggested that the economy was much weaker than previous economic reports had indicated.

In response to the report, prices of the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond jumped 1 1/2 points, or $15 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. Since then, yields on long-term and intermediate-term bonds have fallen one-third of a percentage point to about 8.7%, paving the way for lower mortgage and other consumer lending rates.

Another weak report for May would reinforce perceptions that business activity is winding down and that eventually, although not right away, the Federal Reserve may push interest rates lower in an effort to stimulate economic growth.

But many economists believe a moderate or strong report could create confusion and uncertainty, prompting bond yields to remain at current levels or even rise somewhat. Susan M. Hering, an economist at Salomon Brothers Inc., said strong employment growth could keep "the 30-year Treasury yield trapped in its recent trading range above 8.50%."

Mr. Sohn is more wary. He said a strong employment report -- one that shows job gains in excess of 150,000 -- would cause bond-market investors to focus on negative technical factors and lead to widespread bearish sentiment.

High on the list of negative technicals is the huge amount of new bills, notes and bonds the Treasury must sell to finance the thrift-industry bailout. Last week, Treasury officials admitted that the cost of the bailout is soaring and that the government may have to spend $300 billion over the next 10 years to clean up the industry.

The Treasury will have to borrow massive amounts of money to finance the bailout, and that could put upward pressure on interest rates.

In addition, Japanese investors, who tend to buy large amounts of U.S. Treasury notes and bonds, have sharply reduced their purchases in recent months.

"The Japanese are not all that wild about participating in the U.S. market; they have become reluctant buyers," said Mr. Sohn. He said that Japanese institutions are searching for investment opportunities in their own stock and bond markets, which were severely depressed earlier this year.

Those markets are now recovering, in part because the value of the yen is rising and long-term interest rates in Japan appear to be heading lower. Last week, for example, Japan's major long-term credit banks lowered their prime lending rate to 7.6% from 7.9%. The yield on Japan's benchmark 10-year government bond, which peaked at 7.3% in mid-March, was quoted at 6.9% on Friday.

"Clearly, things have turned around for the Japanese," said Brian Fabbri, economist at Midland Montagu Securities Inc. "This is taking some of the steam out of our market," he said. The only way to revive the U.S. bond market rally, he said, "is another piece of weak employment data."

---

Friday's Market Activity

Bond prices were unchanged to lower in quiet pre-holiday trading. Most investment banks and commercial banks closed their bond trading operations at 2 p.m. EDT on Friday and were closed yesterday in observance of Memorial Day.

On Friday, a report indicating that consumer spending advanced at a steady 0.6% clip during April pushed prices of longterm Treasury bonds lower.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell 5/8 point, a loss of $6.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The price of the benchmark bond ended at 100 25/32 to yield 8.66%, down from Thursday's price of 101 13/32 to yield 8.81%.

Short-term rates jumped. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.75%, for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.99%. That was down from a rate of 7.68%, for a bond equivalent yield of 7.92%. The rate on six-month bills fell to 7.75% to yield 8.16%, compared with 7.71% to yield 8.11% on Thursday.

In the junk-bond market, most prices ended unchanged. The major exceptions were the bonds of R.H. Macy & Co., which gyrated wildly.

The bonds, which fell a total of six points on Wednesday and Thursday, staged a partial rebound on Friday due to Macy's release of better-than-expected third-quarter financial results.

The New York-based retailer said its cash flow -- or earnings before interest, taxes and depreciation -- increased in the third quarter to $160.2 million from $154 million in the year-ago period, based on preliminary results.

The company's loss, after interest, taxes and depreciation, in the period ended April 28, totaled $63.1 million, a slight improvement over last year's $64.1 million loss. Sales in the third quarter were $1.486 billion, almost unchanged from $1.482 billion a year ago.

"These are very positive results for Macy's," said Andrew Leinoff, a high-yield analyst who covers the retail industry at McCarthy Crisanti & Maffei Inc. "What it means is that the margins are a little better than last year, and the company has recovered, as it stated, from its dismal performance at Christmas."

Macy's 14.5% subordinated debentures maturing in 2001 finished at a bid price of 60, up two points from late Thursday. Macy's 14.5% senior subordinated debentures due in 1998 also finished up two points at 69, while its zero-coupon bonds gained two points to end at 26.

Despite these gains, Macy bonds have yet to recover fully from the rout that took place earlier in the week. News that Heller Financial Corp., a major factoring agent, shifted from a "revolving credit" policy toward Macy suppliers to an "order-by-order" policy triggered the declines.

In the tax-exempt market, municipal bonds ended 1/8 to 1/4 point lower. Despite the declines, traders said the market managed to digest more than $1.6 billion in new debt that was issued last week.
