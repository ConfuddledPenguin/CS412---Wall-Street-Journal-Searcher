Telecommunications Conference Focuses On Fiber Optics and Computer Networks

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 05/29/90

STOCKHOLM, Sweden -- The telecommunications industry's leading technology symposium opened with fiber optic communications and so-called intelligent computer networks taking center stage. The triennial conference, last held in Phoenix, Ariz., in 1987, goes by the arcane name the International Switching Symposium. It is a place for the industry's most prominent scientists and developers to present their views on leading-edge technologies.

ISS in the past has offered a window on where the telecommunications industry is headed in terms of new voice and data services for business and residential customers. This year's presentations on optical switches and network management systems seemed to presage a revolution in the public phone network over the next three to 10 years.

These changes will enable companies to begin trading vast amounts of information with other firms by using fiber optics and the public network. Moreover, the presentation shows that public phone companies have begun laying the groundwork for better integrating of disparate communications technologies, which include cellular radio and private data networks.

Many of the technologies discussed were in varying stages of development at the time of the Phoenix meeting in 1987, when the thrust of the technical presentations dealt with so-called System VII common channel signaling networks that helped route voice and data calls. Others dealt with Integrated Services Digital Network (ISDN) technology for sending voice and data calls simultaneously over the same wire.

Since Phoenix, System VII networks have proliferated. In fact, one of those networks figured prominently in the software malfunction that felled American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s long-distance network last January. And ISDN systems are now being used by numerous companies, such as American Express Co., to increase the efficiency of their workers, who can now talk on the phone and share computer information on their personal computers without having to make an expensive and time-consuming second call.

Some of this year's more noteworthy presentations include those by scientists at AT&T, Japan's NEC Corp. and France's Alcatel, who are presenting papers on optical computers that switch voice and data calls using light beams from lasers rather than electronic signals. Other industry giants, such as Northern Telecom Ltd. of Canada; Ericsson of Sweden; Nippon Telegraph and Telephone Corp. of Japan, and British Telecom plan presentations on high-capacity or broad-band transmission using fiber optic networks and the methods for managing such traffic.

Systems management is becoming more critical because companies will eventually transmit their data through the public network at the rate of trillions of bits of information per second, compared with the millions and in some cases billions of bits today.

"In the future, the public networks will have to provide whatever transmission {pipeline} is needed by a customer and make it as accessible as dialing a simple voice call," said Desmond Hudson, president of Northern Telecom World Trade.

Another focus is so-called Intelligent Networks of computers that will someday enable phone companies to allow third-party suppliers, such as software companies, to send their programs to customers on a dial-up basis. Bell Communications Research Inc., the laboratory owned by the seven Bell regionals, and telecommunications authorities from West Germany, France and Italy are among the presenters in this category.

Attendance at this year's ISS underscores the globalization of the telecommunications industry, with 84 countries sending scientific representatives, compared with 48 countries three years ago.
