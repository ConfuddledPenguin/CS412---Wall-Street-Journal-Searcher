Petroleum Output Is Falling in 3 Nations Where Overproduction Led to World Glut

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 05/29/90

Petroleum output is declining in the three Persian Gulf nations whose overproduction led to the world oil glut and the recent sharp fall in prices. That is the new assessment by several leading monitors of OPEC production. They will be reporting estimates that total production in May by the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries is down as much as 500,000 barrels a day or more from the level in April.

Such a production trend, if it holds, could have a major impact on world oil markets by providing support to weak prices. It was the apparent lack of follow-up to OPEC's early May pledge to trim high output that led to the price drop of more than $2 a barrel.

The new May estimates are only preliminary and could prove to be misleading signals for oil markets. Still, they should provide OPEC with some breathing room and shrink prospects of a runaway output race.

The estimates differ some on how much OPEC is producing. But it is agreed the major reduction so far is by Saudi Arabia -- which appears to be producing slightly under its quota of 5.4 million barrels a day -- and that there are signs of modest cuts in both Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates, the chronic overproducers.

"It's not out of the question for Kuwait, which has cut back spot sales to U.S. customers significantly, to be down 200,000 barrels a day for the month to around 1.7 million," said Cristina Haus, who monitors OPEC production for Energy Security Analysis, a Washington-based company that tracks oil supplies world-wide.

As a result, she has lowered the output figure she expects to report for OPEC for May from a prior estimate of 23.5 million barrels a day. Now, she said, OPEC's May production appears to be 23 million to 23.5 million barrels a day, down from 23.7 million in April and more than 24 million in March.

Some other monitors of OPEC production see May output settling in a higher range of 23.4 million to 23.5 million barrels a day. But that still would mean a drop of 500,000 barrels a day because they have revised upward their old April estimates to some 24 million.

"If we see another decline of 500,000 barrels a day in June, that will be the one million total they were looking for," said a European-based monitor of OPEC production. Although the exporters had stated after their early May meeting that they planned to reduce OPEC output a total of 1.4 million barrels a day through July, several leading oil ministers had suggested privately that they believed oil markets would be satisfied with a drop of one million.

But even though the new production indications for May could be a firming factor for oil prices, they still don't end a major worry for many members of OPEC. Neither Kuwait nor the United Arab Emirates will have yet met in full reduction promises of 400,000 barrels a day and 200,000 barrels a day, respectively. The concern is that Saudi Arabia will retaliate by raising its output back to 5.7 million barrels a day.

"If you take the May figures at face value," said a senior OPEC official, "only the Saudis are down significantly. The issue is how long they will stay down."

Only last week, the kingdom may have signaled that its patience was wearing thin. In an official government statement, Hisham M. Nazer, the Saudi oil minister, reiterated the kingdom's commitment to the early May accord, noting that it had given its "word of honor" to market no more oil than its official quota. He called on other OPEC members to practice a similar production discipline.

Still, the authoritative Middle East Economic Survey, which has close contacts with the kingdom, reported yesterday that "the Saudis aren't now planning to take any steps to review their present {production} policy until around mid-June."
