Denison Mines Faces Selling More Assets To Meet Obligations

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/29/90

TORONTO -- Denison Mines Ltd., which is already trying to sell its oil and gas assets, said it may have to put other noncore assets on the block to meet its financial obligations. Helen Roman-Barber, chairman and chief executive officer, told the annual meeting that the company's plan to sell its overseas oil and gas assets has "proved to be a difficult" task.

Denison sold its Canadian oil and gas properties last November for 44.6 million Canadian dollars (US$37.7 million). Ms. Roman-Barber declined to place a value on the remaining assets, which are in Spain, Egypt, Italy and Greece.

"We are dealing with the prospect that the oil and gas divestiture program may not provide us with sufficient funds to meet our financial obligations," she said.

The company has about C$316 million in debt and faces payments of C$31 million this year and C$86 million in 1991. It also will need about C$175 million next March to retract its Series B preferred shares. The company has the option to pay stock for the shares, but Ms. Roman-Barber said Denison hopes to be able to pay cash.

The company decided in 1989 to concentrate on its uranium operations. But uranium prices are at all-time lows, and Denison had a first-quarter loss of C$4.8 million, or 15 Canadian cents a share.

On the Toronto Stock Exchange Friday, the company's Class B shares fell one Canadian cent at C$1.13, well off the issue's 52-week high of C$5.375.

The company continues to have interests in potash and coal mining, though it wrote off its entire interest in its only remaining coal operation in 1986.

Jake Fowler, president and chief operating officer, wouldn't say which assets might be sold. "We like the potash business very much, but our core business is uranium," he said.

Mr. Fowler also said the company won't pay dividends on its common shares until "we're generating sufficient cash." Denison doesn't have a set dividend policy. Previous payment was a stock dividend in Class B shares equal to 24 Canadian cents in Febuary 1989.
