Abreast of the Market: Big Investors Trimming Their Supplies of Cash

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 05/29/90

NEW YORK -- Institutional investors' mountain of cash is melting. Does that bode well or ill for stock prices? As the Dow Jones Industrial Average thrust upward to a series of new records earlier this month, the levels of cash available in portfolios to buy more stocks was dropping fast. By the week ended May 18, according to Indata's survey of 1,200 fund managers who together oversee about $180 billion, cash levels were the lowest since Jan. 31.

Of course, the wave of profit-taking that hit the market Friday, sinking the industrial average 34.63 points to 2820.92, probably restored some cash to institutions' coffers. But unless Friday's pattern continues, cash levels at the end of May could be the smallest this year.

A money manager who squirrels away assets in Treasury bills or other money market securities obviously thinks stock prices are headed lower. If stocks keep going up, however, that money manager's returns can lag behind the overall market, and clients may leave.

But what overall cash levels mean these days is open to debate. Money managers who have spent theirs view high cash levels elsewhere as the surest sign that stocks will climb further. They figure bearish money managers will plow uninvested funds back into stocks in a hurry as the market rises away from them.

Managers sticking with big cash positions, on the other hand, are confident that because stocks are already too expensive, the market soon will correct this situation by falling. Friday was only the beginning, they say.

Both sides agree that if stocks do tumble, there is still enough cash on the sidelines to cushion the fall. At most, stocks may fall 15% to roughly 2400 in the industrial average, they say. And the top? A few billion dollars of share purchases could easily vault the Dow industrials to 3000.

John M. McCarthy, manager of the $3.7 billion Affiliated Fund Inc. and managing partner at Lord, Abbett & Co., isn't terribly sanguine about either corporate profits or the economy. Nonetheless, he's almost fully invested, with only 3% of his fund in cash, because he thinks bearish money managers will storm into the market as the Dow and particularly other market indexes rise.

"I think there is too much cash on the sidelines," he says. "In 40 years we have never dropped the market to accommodate people with big cash positions."

Mr. McCarthy predicts that stocks will move much higher, before correcting to lower levels. Confusion plagued economic soothsayers in the first quarter, he reasons. "We had a winter freeze coupled with higher oil prices, a strike at Boeing, Hurricane Hugo and an earthquake, a big liquidation of inventories," he asserts, and "on top of all that we had summer in February." Those events clouded the economy's true colors, which are only now showing that growth is weaker than presumed. And he thinks rigorous lending practices by banks may slow economic growth still further.

Slow growth is bad for stocks, but Mr. McCarthy thinks investors will switch out of cash instruments and bonds as interest rates fall. Small investors have piled up some $11 trillion in savings, checking accounts, stocks and bonds, with a relatively small percentage of that in stocks.

There's little choice except for bonds and stocks, he argues. "Real estate, gold, limited partnerships -- none of that is going to be attractive," he says. After the thrift and junk bond disasters, Mr. McCarthy thinks individual investors will demand basic financial instruments -- high-quality bonds or stocks. And as bond yields fall, investors will plunge into stocks, Mr. McCarthy predicts.

But then will come another round of disappointing corporate earnings reports, which Mr. McCarthy sees precipitating a correction of about 10% to 15%. Such a decline probably wouldn't affect Mr. McCarthy's investment strategy, which is a bet on more capital spending and less consumption in the U.S. economy. He owns stocks such as International Business Machines (his largest position), Ingersoll-Rand, General Electric, Deere and Caterpillar.

"With all that cash on the sidelines," Mr. McCarthy says, the coming market drop "will be fairly limited."

If he is right, the correction will be curbed by that obstinate group of investors who were sweating out the rally last week. One of them is Henry Van der Eb, president of Mathers Fund. Mr. Van der Eb, who manages close to $850 million, is 90% in cash currently, one of the highest cash positions among the 689 stock funds tracked by Morningstar Inc., a mutual fund ratings service.

During the stock market's tumble in the first quarter, that position helped him stay far ahead of the market averages. But lately the industrial average is catching up to Mr. Van der Eb, who says he is still ahead of broader indexes.

"The point is that we've taken negligible risk relative to a fully invested portfolio, and we have an above-average return," he says.

Mr. Van der Eb describes himself as a "value" investor who sells stocks when they have become overpriced and won't buy any unless they're underpriced. Nowadays, when he tries to find something to buy, he can't find a stock that's undervalued in terms of potential earnings growth.

Shares of many companies are trading at twice the value of underlying assets, he says, while the historical range is about 1.75 times the value. "Even 1.75 times is a gross overvaluation," he contends; "stocks should be trading at closer to 1 to 1.25 times" the value of underlying assets.

There are some cheap stocks, he acknowledges. However, "what we find is that a number of companies qualify on some or most of the valuation models, but the earnings growth isn't there."

The 10% of his portfolio in stocks amounts to "only a handful" of issues -- "a few gold stocks, and International Technology Corp.," says Mr. Van der Eb. He bought about four million shares of the waste management company, which fits his parameters for an undervalued growth company.

Only an upturn in corporate profits would make Mr. Van der Eb cut his cash position. "We're not convinced that we are going to have a soft landing," he says, which describes a period of moderating inflation and stable economic growth. "The odds are in favor of a recession." He cites the most recent revision of first-quarter gross national product, which showed weak growth but inflation rising to a 6.7% annual rate.

Regarding the Dow industrials' recent string of records, Mr. Van der Eb says 23 of 30 companies whose stocks make up the average are buying back their own shares. "They're sucking up the marginal seller," he says. "I would make an even-odds bet that if all these companies stopped buying, the Dow would fall 10%."

Friday Market Activity

A disappointing quarterly earnings forecast from Adobe Systems sparked broad selling of technology issues, which had set the pace for the market's rise since the end of April. The Dow Jones Industrial Average sank 34.63 points to 2820.92, its steepest drop since Feb. 20.

For the week, the average gained 1.01 points, or less than 0.1%. So far this month, the 30-stock average is still up 164.16 points, or 6.2%.

Traders said light pre-holiday trading magnified the market impact of program selling linked to stock-index arbitrage, which swept over stocks twice in the last hour of trading. Sell programs involve the sale of baskets of stocks and the purchase of futures contracts.

Adobe plummeted 15 1/4 to 35 1/4 in heavy over-the-counter trading following its forecast that earnings for the fiscal quarter ending Friday would be in a range of 29 cents and 37 cents a share, well below analysts' estimates.

Motorola, which set 52-week highs Wednesday and Thursday, tumbled 3 to 80 3/4 in trading of 1.2 million shares. Among other key technology issues: International Business Machines fell 2 1/2 to 116 1/4; Compaq Computer lost 3 3/8 to 118 5/8; Hewlett Packard dropped 5/8 to 47 1/4; Cray Research fell 1 1/8 to 46 7/8, and Texas Instruments slipped 3/4 to 42 1/2.

However, Digital Equipment rose 3/8 to 92 1/2. At meetings with analysts, company officials said the board remains "comfortable" with considering an initial dividend despite lower profitability. Demand for its new VAX-9000 mainframe computers is "meeting expectations," according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Merck climbed 1 1/2 to 84 on 1.9 million shares following news that an advisory committee of the Food and Drug Administration recommended approval of its drug Losec for the treatment of acute duodenal ulcers. Ronald Nordmann, a drug analyst at PaineWebber, told the investor report the panel's decision will permit the company to go "toe to toe" with ulcer drugs produced by Glaxo Holdings and SmithKline Beecham. Glaxo fell 1 1/8 to 26 5/8 while SmithKline lost 1 1/8 to 41.

General Motors gained 3/8 to 48 3/4 on 1.2 million shares. This newspaper reported Friday in the "Heard on the Street" column that analysts are upbeat on GM as it is quietly closing plants, which should boost domestic profit. Also, President Robert C. Stempel said at the annual meeting that despite recent sluggishness in U.S. auto sales, GM hasn't changed its estimate that 14.5 million to 15 million cars and light trucks will be sold this year.

Philip Morris fell 1 5/8 to 42 1/2 on 2.7 million shares, marking the second consecutive day of heavy selling. The retreat comes in the wake of Health and Human Services Secretary Louis Sullivan's proposal for states to place new restrictions on cigarette sales.

Dividend-related trading by institutions swelled volume in Texas Utilities, the Big Board's most active issue, which rose 1/8 to 37 on 3.8 million shares.

Global Marine, which rose 1/4 to 4 5/8, was second most active at 3.6 million shares. Traders linked the activity to heavy demand among institutional investors for a secondary offering of 13.4 million shares, or a 13.3% stake, at $4.375 each, according to PIR. Another block of 16 million shares, equaling a 17% stake, changed hands among institutions in a private placement, the company said.

Dennison Manufacturing soared 8 3/8 to 27 3/8 on 1.3 million shares after signing a definitive agreement to be acquired by Avery International for stock valued at about $500 million. Avery, which will swap 1.12 of its common shares for each Dennison share, fell 2 1/4 to 26 1/4.
