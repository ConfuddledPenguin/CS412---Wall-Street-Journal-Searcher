U.S. Plans to Raise Fees For Franchisees in Parks

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/29/90

The Department of the Interior said it plans to raise the franchise fees it charges private companies to operate in certain national parks. Concessionaires operating in parks that post more than $1 million in sales would pay 22% of gross revenues to the government; the average now is 2.5%.

Monopolistic concession contracts have been criticized by those who believe the agreements are too favorable to private concerns. MCA Inc. pays 0.75% of gross revenues to provide services in Yosemite National Park. In 1989, the entertainment company had revenue from Yosemite of about $85 million, and paid the U.S. a $635,772 fee.

Interior Secretary Manuel Lujan ordered a review of concessions policy nationwide last December. Under his plan to raise fees, 5% of revenues would go to the U.S. Treasury's general fund, and 17% would be returned to the park for maintenance and upgrades.

Mr. Lujan also said he wants to reduce concessionaires' ownership interest in park facilities. For example, the Interior Department said it would cost the government as much as $300 million to buy out MCA's interest in Yosemite. Under the plan, the department could order the concessionaire to use the 17% of its fee toward reducing its ownership interest, until eventually the government would own the facilities. The agency also plans administratively to shorten concession contracts' duration and to review franchise fees more frequently.
