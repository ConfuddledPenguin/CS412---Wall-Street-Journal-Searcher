Machine Tool Orders in April Were Up 19% --- Small Manufacturers Aren't Feeling a Credit Crunch, Industry Executives Say

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 05/29/90

The nation's manufacturers are buying machinery somewhat more willingly than they did three months ago, machine tool producers say. And there's no indication so far that even small manufacturers are feeling a credit crunch, industry executives add. U.S. machine tool plants in April received $289.5 million of orders, a 19% rise from $242.6 million a year earlier, according to the NMTBA -- The Association for Manufacturing Technology , a McLean, Va., trade group. Last month's orders were up 8.9% from March, and were 49% higher than February's $194.6 million, this year's low.

Unlike March, when a big Soviet order bolstered the total, the April rise resulted from increased ordering by U.S. factories.

However, machine tool distributors, who handle foreign-made machines as well as those produced in the U.S., reported a sharp drop in orders during April. An order index published by the American Machine Tool Distributors' Association, Rockville Pike, Md., dropped 27% from the year-earlier month. The index is quite volatile, and the March report was unusually strong, which suggests that the April drop may result from an accidental bunching of orders rather than the start of a downtrend.

Machine tools are complex part-making machines ranging from computer-controlled lathes and machining centers to metal-forming presses. Orders are a good indicator of spending by durable-goods manufacturers because machine tools shape most metal parts.

"Business is very good," said Stephen M. Peterson, vice president, sales, for Giddings & Lewis Inc., Fond du Lac, Wis. "We see some real confidence out there among our customers."

Jet engine manufacturers, construction equipment concerns and pump manufacturers have been ordering actively, Mr. Peterson said. "We're getting some military business, though not as much as last year, and there's quite a bit of activity in the oil country" after a prolonged period when the oil field equipment industry was dormant.

Also, Giddings & Lewis recently sold two computer-controlled manufacturing cells in the Far East, one in Singapore and the other in Taiwan, he said. Cells consist of several machine tools served by automated materials-handling equipment and controlled by a computer that can make a variety of parts without a human operator.

"Our second quarter is better than the first," said Donald D. Bowers, president of Leblond Makino Machine Tool Co., Mason, Ohio. "Companies committed to long-term objectives" in improving productivity and product quality "are ordering equipment to carry out those programs," he added. But other companies that base their equipment buying on the immediate business outlook are holding back.

Auto companies and auto suppliers remain a weak spot, said Harold J. Winch, president of Minster Machine Co., a major producer of stamping presses in Minster, Ohio. "They're weighing a lot of decisions right now, including plastic versus metal parts, government fuel economy standards, clean air legislation and next year's auto sales outlook." Until they hash out those decisions, auto concerns are deferring some capital spending decisions.

Machine tool producers frequently sell to small machine shops, which would be among the marginal borrowers denied loans in a credit crunch. So far, at least, that doesn't appear to be happening, machine tool executives say.

"I haven't seen any evidence of a credit crunch," said Minster Machine's Mr. Winch. "A company with a decent balance sheet doesn't have any more trouble getting a loan" than it did last year.

There has been concern that banks would tighten lending standards following the high loan losses sustained by banks and thrifts, and the resulting closer scrutiny of bank loan portfolios by government regulators. There's been some tightening on real estate development lending, but manufacturing has been affected relatively little so far.

"There is no evidence of a credit crisis . . . nor is there much reason to expect one," said Gail D. Fosler, chief economist for the Conference Board, a New York business and economic research group. The slower growth in bank loans results from lower loan demand in a slow-growing economy rather than more stringent lending standards, she said.

April orders for lathes, grinders, milling machines, machining centers, boring mills and other machines that shape metal parts by cutting rose 35% to $206.9 million from $152.8 million a year earlier. However, April 1989 was the first month of an order slowdown by the auto industry, after 18 months of active buying, making for an easier comparison. Nonetheless, the latest month's orders were up 8.9% from March's $198.2 million, despite the inclusion in March of a $38 million Soviet order received by Cross & Trecker Corp., Bloomfield Hills, Mich.

Orders last month for metal-forming presses and other machines to shape metal with pressure were $82.6 million, the NMTBA said, down 8% from the year-earlier month's $89.8 million, but up 22% from $67.7 million in March.

Shipments of all types of machine tools by domestic plants totaled $258.2 million, a 3.5% rise from $249.6 million in April 1989.
