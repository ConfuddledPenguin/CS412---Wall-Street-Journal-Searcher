Tudor Investment's Jones Pleads Guilty To Filling Protected Maryland Wetlands

  • Author: Milo Geyelin
  • Date: 05/29/90

Paul Tudor Jones II, one of the country's most successful futures and options traders, pleaded guilty to a federal charge of illegally filling protected wetlands on Maryland's Eastern Shore. The guilty plea, unsealed Friday along with a seven-count indictment against a project manager hired by Mr. Jones, is the largest case ever prosecuted criminally by federal officials for illegally filling wetlands.

Mr. Jones, 35 years old, is the founder and president of Tudor Investment Corp., one of the most closely watched trading firms on Wall Street. He pleaded guilty to a one-count misdemeanor that carries a maximum jail sentence of six months and agreed to $2 million in fines and restitution. The charges stem from plans by Tudor to build a corporate retreat and wildlife habitat -- Tudor Farms -- in Dorchester County, Md.

The indictment and the guilty plea were unsealed by the U.S. attorney's office in Baltimore, which spent a year investigating the case. "The filing of today's charges reflects the seriousness with which this office and federal law enforcement in Maryland view environmental crime," said Breckenridge L. Wilcox, U.S. attorney in Maryland. "The protection of Maryland's Chesapeake Bay wetlands is of vital importance in the effort to arrest the Bay's decline . . ."

Mr. Jones, emphasizing that the plea agreement will have no impact on his company's investment operations, said that Tudor Farms had been conceived as a conservation project and that the firm retained 15 experts, including a project manager, William B. Ellen.

"We believed in error that our experts had properly addressed the complex issues related to habitat development in wetlands . . .," Mr. Jones said in a statement. "The severe drought in 1988 . . . made it difficult to recognize non-tidal wetlands. With identification so difficult, certain permits to move soil on these wetlands were not obtained."

Neither Mr. Jones nor his attorney could be reached for comment.

According to a copy of the plea agreement, Mr. Jones hired Mr. Ellen to develop a corporate retreat for duck hunting on 3,272 acres of prime shore land that Mr. Jones bought between 1987 and 1989. More than half the land was marsh, and Mr. Ellen was contracted to clear wooded areas, dig ponds, build roads and fill in tidal areas with gravel.

Federal prosecutors allege that Mr. Ellen filled in 86 acres of wetlands without a permit, despite an order to cease and desist from the Army Corps of Engineers in February 1988. Copies of the order were mailed to Mr. Ellen and Mr. Jones, according to Mr. Jones's plea agreement, yet the work continued unabated. Mr. Jones visited the site an average of twice a month, according to federal prosecutors, but it remains unclear whether he was aware of any problem over proper permitting.

According to his plea agreement, Mr. Ellen refused to stop work and the project was only halted after the Corps of Engineers contacted the independent contractors involved. Mr. Jones expressed "surprise and dismay" when told the extent of the damage in March 1989 and immediately fired Mr. Ellen.

Since then, according to the plea agreement, he has "cooperated fully" with plans to restore the land. Mr. Jones agreed to pay a $1 million fine, another $1 million in restitution and restore the damage done on his land.

Mr. Ellen, of Matthews, Va., could not be reached for comment. He was charged with six counts of knowingly violating the Clean Water Act by filling in wetlands, and one count of violating the Rivers and Harbors Act by dumping fill material in navigable water. He faces a maximum 31-year prison term and more than $1.5 million in fines.
