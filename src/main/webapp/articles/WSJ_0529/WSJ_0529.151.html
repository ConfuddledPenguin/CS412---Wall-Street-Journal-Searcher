International -- Foreign Insight: Iraqi President's Hoped-For Strong Show For U.S.-Soviet Summit Fails to Develop

  • Author: Geraldine Brooks
  • Date: 05/29/90

When Iraqi President Saddam Hussein called for an Arab summit in Baghdad, the plan was that Arab heads of state would formulate a message to George Bush and Mikhail Gorbachev when they meet this week in Washington. So far, the message seems unlikely to be the one Mr. Hussein intended. He had hoped to demonstrate that the Arab world is a united force, but yesterday's poorly attended opening session indicated lingering disunity among Arab nations. Calls in the government-controlled Iraqi press for an Arab investment embargo and use of the "oil weapon" against Western nations suggested that radical Arabs are groping for ways to impose their will.

The main item on the agenda is the mass immigration of Soviet Jews to Israel, which many Arab leaders, including such moderates as Egypt's Hosni Mubarak and Jordan's King Hussein, see as a threat to the peace process because the influx may increase Israel's appetite for land. Yet the summit opened without Hafez Assad of Syria, a crucial figure in any attempt to pressure neighboring Israel. Mr. Assad didn't attend because he and the Iraqi president have been engaged in a feud for years. Lebanon, Syria's ally, also boycotted the gathering. Algeria, Oman and Morocco sent delegations below head-of-state level, making the turnout the worst in the past four years of Arab League summits.

Last year's summit in Casablanca, called to address the Lebanon problem, failed to come up with a workable plan. A 1988 gathering in Algeria was supposed to finance the Palestinian cause, but Palestine Liberation Organization leader Yasser Arafat has complained that the funds weren't forthcoming.

The Iraqi president opened the Baghdad summit with a speech attacking Israel. He reiterated his threat of a month ago that Iraq will use "weapons of total destruction" against Israel if it attacks Iraq with similar weapons. He blamed the U.S. for arming and encouraging Israel, saying, "The Zionist entity would not be able to commit aggression and expansion territorially at the expense of the Arabs without the military might and political support of the United States."

In an interview before the summit, Nizar Hamdoon, Iraq's deputy foreign minister, said the issue of Soviet immigration is one on which the Arab position can easily be misrepresented. "We don't like to appear as if we are against freedom of movement," he said, but the rights of Soviet Jews can't be allowed to trample the rights of Palestinians. "We are for the right of people to move, but we are also for the right of Palestinians to return to their homes, whether inside Israel's pre-1967 borders or on the West Bank."

Mr. Hamdoon said the summit was motivated by fear that the Middle East could turn more violent in a short time if a combination of such factors as stagnation in the peace process, economic hardship in such states as Egypt and Jordan and the repercussions of Soviet immigration weren't addressed. "I don't want to talk about months, but there is a very small gap of time when somebody can do something. After that it will get out of control," he said.

Jordan's King Hussein, speaking at the session, asked help for his state's troubled economy, warning that "abandoning Jordan is abandoning Palestine." Previously, the king has said that since 1987 thousands of Palestinians have crossed the border separating the West Bank from his kingdom, driven out by Israeli military harassment and fear of extremist West Bank settlers. If Soviet Jews begin to settle the West Bank en masse, he says he fears such numbers could accelerate, destabilizing his already fragile kingdom in which half the population is Palestinian.
