Japanese Auto Companies Lag Behind Western Rivals in Race for Eastern Bloc

  • Author: William Echikson
  • Date: 05/29/90

BRUSSELS -- Japanese auto makers, slowed by their cautious business culture and unfamiliar with Eastern Europe, remain well behind their Western competitors in the race to enter the former East Bloc. Toyota Motor Corp. moved ahead of most other Japanese companies merely by saying that it and C. Itoh & Co. are studying the possibility of setting up an auto assembly plant in Czechoslovakia with a local car manufacturer. Toyota officials here said plans are at an early stage and no cash commitment has been made. They said the company's major effort in Europe still is a new plant in Burnaston, England, that eventually will produce 200,000 cars annually.

"We just have begun investigating the situation in Czechoslovakia," said Hidenori Tsutsui, managing director of Toyota Motor Marketing Services Europe SA. "Concrete decisions are a long way off."

Other Japanese car companies, with the notable exception of Suzuki Motor Co., take a similar cautious view. While Volkswagen AG, Opel AG, Fiat S.p.A. and other Western manufacturers have announced ambitious investment programs for East Germany and the rest of Eastern Europe, the Japanese have restricted their plans to information-gathering trips.

"This is the European's own backyard," argued Nissan Europe's president, Yoshikazu Kawana. "We don't know what the market is in Eastern Europe, what kind of political and economic system will be in force, and we don't have any type of expertise there."

In the early 1980s, well before the recent democratic revolution, several Japanese car companies investigated making Eastern Europe a low-cost production center for cracking the Western European market. But the European Community warned that Japanese cars produced in the then-Communist countries would be denied entry under anti-dumping restrictions. Toyota, Nissan Motor Co. and Honda Motor Co. responded by expanding or building factories in England.

"What we're doing in England is tremendously expensive," said Nissan's Mr. Kawana. "Along with continuing to digest our new U.S. plants, my opinion is that we aren't ready to go it alone in Eastern Europe."

The only solid foothold for a Japanese car company is Suzuki Motor Co.'s recently announced deal to build 15,000 small cars a year in Hungary starting in 1992, eventually increasing output to 100,000. Analysts pointed out that Suzuki had been negotiating for almost five years with the Hungarians and that, unlike much larger Toyota, Nissan or Honda, it has no other European production facility.

"The Suzuki deal came well before the recent warming" made the region look so interesting, explains John Lawson, an auto analyst at Nomura Research Institute in London. "Otherwise, the Japanese don't really know what to do with Eastern Europe."

A potential deal between Daihatsu Motor Co. and Poland's national auto maker, FSO, collapsed. Daihatsu had proposed that FSO assemble 500 midsized Daihatsu Charades a month, with all the parts coming from Japan. The Poles preferred a larger direct investment. Earlier, Fiat had beaten out Daihatsu for a much larger contract to build 160,000 subcompacts.

The lesson may be that the Japanese are ill-prepared to deal in Eastern Europe. Because of what he calls "language and cultural difficulties," Nissan's Mr. Kawana said his company would probably enter the region only in alliance with a European manufacturer. "It's the only way we can understand this new mosaic," he said.
