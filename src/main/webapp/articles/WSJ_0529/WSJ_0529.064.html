British Economy Is Said to Show A Mixed Picture

  • Author: Tim Carrington
  • Date: 05/29/90

LONDON -- The troubled British economy faces double-digit inflation, downward pressure on the pound and slackened capital investment, according to the latest economic bulletin by the Bank of England. But the central bank's report carried a glimmer of optimism: British companies are managing to increase exports to countries such as West Germany, where economic conditions are more robust. That finding dovetails a suprisingly sanguine report by Salomon Brothers, which predicted a boom in U.K. manufacturing exports during the 1990s.

The British inflation virus is currently the worst of any major industrial country. But the Bank of England warned that tight labor markets in the U.S. and Japan, along with skill shortages in Europe, harbinger more inflation in those regions.

German unification threatens further international inflation, according to the Bank of England, which cited higher consumer demand from East Germans, along with added spending by the West German government. In addition, the bank report argued that the West German trade surplus probably will decline as West German industry shifts its focus from export markets to shoring up the collapsing East German economy. That could lead to higher German interest rates.

Notwithstanding the worrisome inflationary trends, economic activity in continental Europe should bring a surge in exports for Britain's steel, aerospace and auto industries, according to Salomon Brothers' equity strategist Michael Howell.

Overall, Mr. Howell predicted a reversal of the economic patterns that prevailed in the 1980s, when the U.K. manufacturing sector shrunk and the service sector expanded. In the 1990s, he said, British advertisers, real estate brokers and leisure companies will suffer while manufacturers rebound.

Already, the Salomon report said, U.K. manufacturing exports are running 20% higher than in 1988. Salomon predicted 15%-20% growth in each of the next two years. Assuming such growth, Mr. Howell said it's "increasingly realistic" to consider Britain "the Hong Kong of Europe" in the new decade.

The Bank of England wasn't that optimistic, but it forecast that export growth will "remain strong" despite sluggishness elsewhere in the British economy.

Salomon predicted that British Aerospace PLC, Dowty Group PLC and BBA Group PLC should all gain from increased demand for aerospace engines and equipment. Increased demand for cellular radios and telephones should boost sales for Racal Electronics PLC, Salomon said. Meanwhile, Eurotunnel PLC, after weathering a succession of cost overruns and slowdowns, can expect profits to roll in after 1996, according to the report.
