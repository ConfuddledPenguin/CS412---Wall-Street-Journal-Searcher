Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Downdrafts in May Data Seen as Slight --- Jobs, Purchasing Reports Expected to Blow Breezy Rather Than Blustery

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 05/29/90

NEW YORK -- Fresh May data promise to bring more economic downdrafts through two favorite reports, employment data and the purchasing managers' index, both to be released Friday. But economists said they expect more breeze than bluster in the numbers. Overall civilian payrolls are expected to rebound from April's poor showing -- payrolls actually shrank after subtracting temporary census workers -- though factory jobs are likely to fall again, all contributing to an unchanged unemployment rate.

Meanwhile, the consensus view expects the purchasers' index to fall below the pivotal 50% reading attained in April, according to MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York. The week's other offerings wrap up April softness with weaker data expected from new home sales, factory orders and construction spending.

After four months of eccentric behavior in employment data, economists hope that the May numbers could return to more normal patterns; that is, after accounting for the lion's share of census workers in the one month. Even Janet Norwood, federal commissioner of labor statistics, acknowledged to Congress that unusually warm weather in January and February boosted hiring, which, in turn, made March and April look particularly weak.

Or, as Donald Fine, economist at Chase Manhattan Bank, noted, "We've had two months of ridiculously strong growth and two months of ridiculously slow growth." So, after four months of "distortions," Mr. Fine hopes that the May number, "if not yet the trend number, will be closer to a trend."

The consensus view expects overall, nonfarm payrolls -- excluding census workers -- to add about 127,000 jobs in May, according to the MMS survey. In April, nonfarm payrolls grew by 69,000 new workers. But excluding the government's hiring of 79,000 temporary workers, the April number declined. Economists estimate that census workers are expected to account for more than 100,000 jobs in May.

No matter how the May jobs numbers are sliced, they look weak to economists. Nonfarm payroll growth of 127,000 jobs, while stronger than in March and April, is much softer than during 1989, when growth averaged more than 225,000 jobs a month. What's more, factory jobs are expected to decline in May, as are positions in the services sector.

Brian Fabbri, chief economist at Midland Montagu Economics, New York, pointed out that recent flooding in the Southwest could cut employment data further. Mr. Fabbri expects nonfarm payroll growth of 130,000 jobs in May. But he said: "The flood could wipe my number right out. And it could prevail over all the numbers that come out in May."

Still, economists seem able to fit these weaker numbers into a slow-growth-but-no-recession outlook. Chase Manhattan's Mr. Fine holds that the manufacturing sector has "bottomed out." He expects real gross national product to grow 1.5% or so in the months ahead. Midland Montagu's Mr. Fabbri said he is "still in the 2% camp."

The MMS consensus expects that the National Association of Purchasing Management index slipped to 49.7% in May, after pushing above 50% in April for the first time in months. A reading below 50% indicates a generally expanding manufacturing economy, while one below 50% suggests a declining economy.

Gary Ciminero, economist at Fleet/Norstar Financial Group in Providence, R.I., said he expects the association index's decline to "mark the resumption of the contraction in manufacturing, which had been under way for 11 of the prior 12 months." Still, economists noted one month's move shouldn't carry too much weight in the index, particularly if it might be teetering on a new trend.

Often, the association index provides the first reading on the new month. But this month, the May employment data will precede the index by two hours on Friday. Most likely, the jobs data will steal thunder from the index, though economists didn't rule out the possibility of conflicting signals from the two reports.

April's leading indicators' index, to be released tomorrow, is expected to have declined 0.2%, following March's jump. Once again, building permits are expected to have dragged the index down. Other negative contributors are likely to include contracts, and orders for plants and equipment, as well as manufacturers' unfilled orders, economists said.

Last week's report of a 4.1% drop in April durable goods orders led economists to expect a falloff in overall factory orders, data for which are to be posted Thursday. April factory orders are expected to have declined about 2%, following March's steep 3.8% gain, according to MMS.

All continues to look weak on the housing front. April new home sales, the report on which is to be released tomorrow, are expected to have dropped 1%, albeit much less than March's sharp fall. The report on April construction spending, scheduled for release Friday, also is expected to show a 1% drop, according to MMS. In March, construction outlays fell 1.4%, as the spending trend retraced gains induced by warm weather earlier in the year.
