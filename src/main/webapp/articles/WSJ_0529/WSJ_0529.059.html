World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Finish the Session Firmly Higher As Yen Maintains Recent Gains Against Dollar

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/29/90

Tokyo stocks ended the afternoon firmly higher yesterday, inspired as the yen managed to maintain steady levels after its strong move against the dollar, traders said. Although a burst of morning buying had calmed to a simmer by midday, stocks on the Nikkei Index still were able to close the day notably higher.

The London market was closed yesterday for a holiday. Friday, London shares finished mostly lower, the end of a two-week trading account, in mainly sluggish trading.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues, which rallied 482.13 points Friday, was up 397.73 points yesterday to 33191.61.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the Nikkei index fell 54.30 to 33137.31.

Monday's volume on the first section was estimated at 750 million shares, down from 1.04 billion shares Friday. Advancers outnumbered decliners 541 to 430, while 163 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which swelled 24.04 points Friday, added 14.11 points, or 0.58%, at 2434.05.

But the second section index fell 8.47 points, or 0.22%, to close at 3884.38. The index slipped 0.75 point Friday, ending an 18-session winning streak. Volume was estimated at 13.9 million shares, down from 16.3 million shares Friday.

Participants returned from the weekend to find that the yen had strengthened substantially against the dollar in New York trading Friday, breaking through the 150-yen barrier.

Convinced that the Japanese currency could hold the higher levels against the dollar, many investors bought domestic demand-related stocks heavily in the morning, traders said. Construction stocks, both of large and small companies, rallied, as did shares of some environmental-energy companies.

In addition, one trader said some institutional and index-linked buying in the Tokyo morning gave the impression that the market was shooting forward with little resistance ahead.

But selling pressure broke the rally's momentum before the early session closed, as some institutional investors, who suffered heavy losses this year on special money accounts, moved to take profits on a broad range of issues.

Although volume was moderately active in the afternoon, trading consisted of a matched amount of selling against buying, allowing for only sideways movement on the indexes, observers said.

Yusuke Fujiwara, head of trading at Baring Securities Ltd., noted that volume of 750 million shares wasn't bad, particularly for a Monday, which happened to be the last day of trading on accounts for May settlement.

But further gains for the Nikkei Index will depend partly on whether trading volume picks up, Mr. Fujiwara said. And he pointed out that "it's going to be hard to buy from here," with prices having recovered substantially from the Nikkei's most recent post-crash low of 28002 on April 2.

A test for the market will be developments in the dollar-yen rate over the next few days. If the dollar doesn't recover in the short term, and slips to 145 yen, Mr. Fujiwara said, the market should advance further.

On the other hand, the yen's strength put a damper on buying high-technology electrical shares, noted Kevin Haag, a dealer in overseas operations at Nomura Securities Co.

Many investors were still disappointed with Sony's earnings forecasts of a 12% rise in pretax profit for the current year and the absence of a gratis issue for the previous fiscal year, Mr. Haag said.

Mr. Fujiwara noted, however, that high-technology stocks haven't been completely ignored during the yen's recent recovery, with interest in these high-priced issues, though somewhat more selective, still remaining at normal levels.

Traders noted special caution for investors seeking to buy at current levels. "More than resistance, the story to watch for is the selling pressure" from institutions, who needed the Nikkei Index reach around 34000 points to break even on their special money accounts, Mr. Haag said.

Although that level wasn't achieved by the March 31 end of the fiscal year, these institutions have recovered much of their losses since the Nikkei was seen bottoming out April 2, and so will likely start taking profits soon, he said.

Several pharmaceutical shares were hit by profit-taking, including Ono, down 110 yen to 5650 yen (73 cents to $37.43), Dai ichi, down 50 yen to 2470 yen, and Fuso, down 30 yen to 1530 yen.

But construction shares held their gains against such selling pressure, with Shimizu Corp. up 180 yen to 2040 yen, Ohbayashi up 50 yen to 1740 yen, and Taisei up 40 yen to 1410 yen.

Among other advancers were securities firms such as Nomura, which gained 100 yen to 2580 yen, Daiwa, up 50 yen to 1710 yen, and Yamaichi, up 60 yen to 1460 yen. In addition, some cement, real estate and a wide range of speculative issues were higher.

On Friday, London's Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index finished down 11.5 points at 2265.6, slightly below the intraday best of 2276.3 and above the low of 2258.4.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index was down 5.2 points at 1800. Volume was a scant 385.6 million shares.

Profit-talking and an obvious absence of buyers for much of the session dragged prices lower, traders said. A lack of stimulating corporate or economic news further burdened the market, causing prices to drift down, the traders added.

Equities staged a modest rally during the last hours of trading, stemming largely from fresh buying that will carry over into the new account, dealers said.

One trader with a major London brokerage firm said the last-minute influx of investors reflected a general sentiment among participants that the market has good upside potential this week.

Meanwhile, stocks closed higher yesterday in Frankfurt, Zurich, Brussels and Stockholm, lower in Milan, mixed in Paris, Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed narrowly mixed. Friday, prices closed higher in Stockholm and Milan; lower in Amsterdam, Paris and Frankfurt; and mixed in Zurich. South African gold stocks closed mixed. Brussels was closed.

In Asian and Pacific markets yesterday, prices closed higher in Sydney and Wellington; lower in Singapore, Manila and Seoul. The Hong Kong and Taipei markets were closed for holidays. Friday, stocks closed higher in Tokyo, Sydney, Hong Kong, Manila, Wellington and Seoul; and lower in Singapore and Taipei.
