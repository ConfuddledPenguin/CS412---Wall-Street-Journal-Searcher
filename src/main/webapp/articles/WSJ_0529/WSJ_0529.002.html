For Once, a War Where Nobody Is Shouting, `Yankee Go Home]'

  • Author: John R. Wilke
  • Date: 05/29/90

Rep. Bob Smith thinks one way to win votes in New Hampshire is to annex part of Maine. The New Hampshire Republican, running in a heated primary for a U.S. Senate seat, is expected to disclose soon evidence showing that the Portsmouth Naval Shipyard -- currently found in Kittery, Maine -- belongs to the Granite State. It's the latest blow in a border war simmering since 1976, when the states landed in court over where to draw the line on the broad Piscataqua River that flows between them.

The congressman can probably count on the support of most of the 4,000 shipyard workers who commute daily across the river from New Hampshire: They'd rather not pay Maine income taxes. Victor Bourre, a shipyard worker who says he uncovered the new evidence after searching through old, yellowed documents, has been staging such tea party-like protests as dumping Maine potatoes and a giant tea bag into the disputed river.

A spokeswoman for the 190-year-old shipyard -- on an island connected by a short bridge to Kittery -- says the Navy is "staying out of this one."

But New Hampshire Attorney General John Arnold says that the governor has asked him to look into the issue and that the state might support a lawsuit. "I'm sure there will be amicable discussions with Maine, which will prove fruitless, after which I expect we'll move to file before the Supreme Court."

Under law, most state border disputes go straight to the high court. The last such spat -- over lobstering rights -- was decided, in Maine's favor, when the justices agreed in 1976 that the border was drawn by a 1740 King's Court decree.

Maine officials say their neighbors are just sore because Maine bases its income-tax rate on the joint income of married New Hampshire workers, even if only one spouse works in Maine.

Maine Attorney General James Tierney says he's ready to cut a deal. "But we want {New Hampshire's} Mount Washington and the turnpike tollbooths on the table," he says. Mount Washington isn't near the border, but, he says, "We can easily see it from here."

It's a case of a congressman who's willing to go the extra mile to fight taxes, "even if it's in somebody else's state," says Joseph Brennan, a Democrat and former Maine governor. "Nice try, New Hampshire, but it won't work," he says. "Pay your taxes."
