Chrysler to Pay U.S. $2.9 Million To Settle Suit

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 05/29/90

HIGHLAND PARK, Mich. -- Chrysler Corp. agreed to pay $2.9 million to settle allegations that its Electrospace Systems Inc. unit overcharged the government in contracts before being acquired by the auto maker in 1987. Also, four top officials of Richardson, Texas-based Electrospace resigned last week. Chrysler maintains the departures were unrelated to the settlement, although a government prosecutor says the moves were related.

"There's nothing in the settlement at all that is conditional with employment changes," said John Stellman, chief financial officer at Chrysler Technologies, the subsidiary that includes Electrospace. A government prosecutor, while declining to mention names, said the government wouldn't have approved the settlement unless Chrysler agreed "to remove the officers involved in the allegations."

Marvin Collins, U.S. attorney for the northern district of Texas, added, "We typically don't allow a contractor to insulate the people involved."

The allegations involved hundreds of government contracts handled by Electrospace Systems between 1978 and 1987, said Ronald Eddins, an assistant U.S. attorney in the northern district of Texas. Many of the contracts were for electronics installed in U.S. Air Force planes, including one contract for Air Force One, the plane used to transport the president.

The government contends that Electrospace used a subcontractor to do much of the work and then billed the government as if the work had been performed by Electrospace itself. "Instead of doing the work themselves, they had (the subcontractor) using less-skilled labor at a lower cost, increasing the profit to themselves," Mr. Eddins said.

Chrysler characterized the issue as a "price dispute" and added that the civil settlement contained no admission of "improprieties" by Electrospace.

Mr. Collins, the Texas U.S. attorney, said the government had conducted a criminal investigation before agreeing to the settlement. "We didn't find a case that could be successfully prosecuted before a jury," he said.

The settlement is a "global settlement," meaning that the government won't seek criminal prosecution in the case. The $2.9 million settlement is roughly twice the amount the government contends it was overcharged from 1985 to 1987, Mr. Eddins said. The government was unable to seek restitution for contracts before 1985 because of a five-year statute of limitations.

Chrysler recently dropped plans to sell Electrospace and two other units of Chrysler Technologies because it couldn't find a buyer. "Chrysler settled to avoid the cost and disruption of a long dispute," Mr. Stellman said. "The settlement allows Electrospace management to concentrate on the main thrust of our business."

The officials leaving Electrospace included Electrospace President Robert Carrel and former Chairman James Lightner, who was still an Electrospace director at the time of his departure. Mr. Stellman said the officials left because their three-year employment contracts, signed when Chrysler bought the company, had expired.
