Avery, Dennison Plan to Merge In Stock Swap --- Deal, Valued at $456 Million, To Form a Leading Firm In Office-Products Field

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle and Joseph Pereira
  • Date: 05/29/90

Avery International Corp. and Dennison Manufacturing Co. agreed to merge in a stock swap with an indicated value of $456 million that would create one of the nation's largest office-products concerns with $2.5 billion in annual sales. The successor company, Avery Dennison Corp., would be dominated by the board of Avery International and based at its corporate offices in Pasadena, Calif. Besides marker pens, stationery and other office supplies, Avery Dennison would sell self-adhesive materials, product fasteners, and printing and labeling systems.

Under the agreement, approved by the boards of both companies last week, Dennison shareholders would receive 1.12 shares of Avery common stock for each share held of Dennison common.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Dennison shares jumped $8.375 to $27.375 on turnover of 1.7 million shares. Avery shares fell $2.25 to $26.25; 1.8 million shares changed hands. Dennison has 15.5 million shares outstanding; Avery has 44.2 million shares outstanding.

Avery and Dennison described the transaction as a "strategic merger" of companies with "complementary" products. Dennison, based in Framingham, Mass., is stronger in certain East Coast markets, while Avery is stronger in the Midwest and on the West Coast. About 35% of Avery Dennison's sales would be in overseas markets that are growing more quickly than domestic ones.

Charles D. Miller, Avery chairman and chief executive officer, would retain those titles at Avery Dennison. The merger would create a strong global competitor on the eve of the integration of the European Community in 1992, he said. Avery Dennison also would be a "more important supplier" to a growing group of office-supply superstores.

The emergence of superstores such as Staples Inc., which sells stationery goods at big discounts, has put strong downward pressure on wholesale office-supply prices. Because of this trend, there will be more consolidations like the Avery-Dennison deal, analysts said. Some of the two companies' biggest competitors are Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing Co. in St. Paul, Minn.; Esselte AB of Stockholm, Sweden, and American Brands Inc., Greenwich, Conn.

The chairmen of Dennison and Avery have known each other for 15 years. Mr. Miller said he proposed a merger with Dennison in 1987 that fell apart at the last minute over price and "organization structure." The latest talks were initiated by Dennison in March, Mr. Miller said.

Nelson S. Gifford, Dennison chairman and chief executive officer, would become vice chairman of Avery Dennison. The board would include Avery's 12 current directors and four Dennison directors. The combined companies would have 19,000 employees, of which more than one-quarter would be overseas.

Analysts generally agreed that the companies are a good fit, but some of them worried that Dennison's problems might rub off on Avery. "Conceptually {the merger} makes sense; making it work is a completely different ballgame," said David Nelson, an analyst at Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. Avery could have trouble integrating Dennison, which is restructuring, he said.

Last December, citing "a rapid slowdown" in its domestic orders, Dennison announced a plan to cut costs by $10 million annually. By the end of the first quarter, Dennison had let go 400 employees, or 4% of its work force. As conditions continued to deteriorate, the company said it would re-evaluate its assets for possible downsizing.

For the year ended Dec. 31, 1989, Dennison's profit plunged 24% to $29.1 million, or $1.82 a share, from $37.2 million, or $2.35 a share, the prior year. Its 1989 sales edged up to $770.9 million from $721.8 million the year before. Reflecting its lackluster earnings, Dennison's stock price has declined from a 52-week high of $32.875.

Avery sales rose 10% to $1.73 billion from $1.58 billion for the year ended Nov. 30, 1989. Net income increased 11% to $86.5 million, or $1.96 a share, from $77.7 million, or $1.77 a share.

If the merger is completed, Avery management said it would recommend a quarterly cash dividend of 18 cents a share. Avery currently pays a quarterly per-share dividend of 16 cents, or 64 cents a year. Dennison pays an annual dividend of $1.32 a share.

The merger is subject to shareholder and regulatory approvals, but analysts doubted that antitrust or other obstacles would emerge. Certain Avery affiliates and employee benefit plans expected to approve the merger hold about 25% of Avery shares. Dennison directors and officers and an employee stock ownership trust expected to vote for the deal own about 20% of Dennison's stock. Mr. Miller said he hoped the merger could be completed by the end of September.
