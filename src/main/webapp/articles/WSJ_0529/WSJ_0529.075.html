Labor Letter: A Special News Report on People And Their Jobs in Offices, Fields and Factories

  • Author: Selwyn Feinstein
  • Date: 05/29/90

WEAK HIRING seems likely in second half. But bright spots persist. International Business Machines Corp. plans to cut 10,000 U.S. jobs this year. Still, it looks for programmers, engineers and holders of advanced degrees. Union Pacific Corp. wants to bargain reductions in unionized rail crews. But it anticipates adding 400 to 600 truck drivers, 100 professionals for a new telemarketing center and 30 oil and gas workers. Owens-Corning Fiberglas Corp. calls its hiring plan "conservative." Yet it can't recruit enough engineers.

U.S. Homes Corp. says "all we're doing is replacing people lost to attrition." Eastern Co. expects little change. Stevens Graphics, though, wants to add 25 to 50 new workers in the second half to increase marketing efforts in Western Europe and the Eastern bloc. And Pier 1 Imports says its employment has jumped to 9,000 workers from 7,800 a year ago, and it expects to add 15 employees for each new store it opens every 4 1/2 days.

Apple Bank is "in a replacement mode" but it has trouble finding qualified workers for entry-level jobs.

LABOR SHORTAGE grows more worrisome in Japan. Can you wait five years?

Last year, for the first time in 14 years, the number of job openings exceeded the number of job seekers in Japan, and the imbalance is getting worse in 1990, says the Fuji Economic Review. But Japan's bustling export trade isn't responsible. The review lays the current worker shortage to a strong increase in domestic demand in Japan.

It may take five years, but the shortage will ease, the publication says. One reason for its optimism: "The Japanese economy has grown about 5% annually for nearly three years. This pace will not likely be maintained." And Japan, it says, could recruit more males over 55 and females between the ages of 25 and 44. Far fewer women of child-bearing age work in Japan than in the U.S., it observes.

IN MIDDLE AGE, some still fantasize about what we'll do when we grow up.

Most people between 35 and 54 continue to daydream about their careers: to anticipate a job change, compensate for something missing, or escape from day-to-day tedium. But Judith Meyerowitz, in a doctoral dissertation at Columbia's Teachers College, says something seems to happen at age 45. We shift from daydreaming about our current jobs to fantasizing about what we will do in the future.

The study -- based on interviews with 60 bankers, teachers and university personnel -- finds we look for different rewards as we grow older. In early career lives, men particularly dream of control, pay and recognition. By 45, these daydreams turn to personal expression, even aesthetics. "Women, too, look inward as they grow older," Ms. Meyerowitz says. "But as men enter late middle age they seem to fantasize more about a less complex life while women talk more about owning their own businesses as a way to assert themselves."

Most fantasies are realistic. Still, one librarian dreams of playing shortstop for the St. Louis Cardinals.

RATING THE BOSS: Only 36% of the workers surveyed by consultant Hay Group from 1987 through 1989 thought their companies were run well. But that's an improvement from the 30% who expressed such confidence in 1981-82.

STRESS RIDES one way. Three of four managers say stress at work sometimes affects their home life, finds a study by Dunhill Personnel System and Columbia Business School. But only one in three managers brings stress from home to work.

REIMBURSEMENT ACCOUNTS grow. Consultant Foster Higgins says 41% of all companies, up from 30% a year ago, let workers set aside pretax dollars to pay for health and dependent care. A further 22% are considering the benefit.

TRAINING WORKERS at colleges costs companies $2.9 billion a year, the American Society for Training and Development says. Total training expenditures come to $30 billion, of which $9 billion goes to outside contractors, including the schools.

WORKERS APLENTY are ready to mow lawns. But sprayers often need a license.

In Southern California, immigrants from Mexico and Central and South America assure an ample supply of hands for routine lawn chores. But Green Scene in Tarzana, Calif., has a hard time finding workers fluent enough in English to deal with customers. To apply pesticides and herbicides, it needs people with a high school education "who can read and understand labels."

Atlanta firms, too, see no shortage of applicants for $6-an-hour lawn-cutting jobs. But Alfred L. Simpson & Co. says even $8 to $10 an hour isn't enough incentive for some workers to handle chemicals. "A lot of people are afraid of pesticides," it says. And Landscapes Inc. worries that lawn labor is going to become a lot scarcer. "We're having to do a much smarter job of hiring" to secure people who "are prepared for their jobs and will stick with them."

Carey's Professional Turf in Des Moines says spring hires often weary of the work by summer's heat.

THE CHECKOFF: One in two senior executives never takes unsolicited calls and most see only 10% of their mail, says Blue Chip Marketing Group. . . . "Additional time spent on breaks at work apparently does raise earnings (i.e., productivity), but not to the same extent as does additional time spent working," concludes a study by the National Bureau of Economic Research.
