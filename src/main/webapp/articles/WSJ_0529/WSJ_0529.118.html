Heard on the Street: Merrill Stock May Just Be Bridge to Troubled Waters

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 05/29/90

NEW YORK -- Merrill Lynch said it bit the bullet last year when it took a $470 million pretax charge for layoffs and other retrenchment. But there might be another bullet or two on the way. Inside Merrill, executives are worried about a possible credit crunch this summer at companies whose temporary "bridge" loans Merrill has financed. There's a chance Merrill will need additional reserves of $100 million to $200 million to deal with soured bridge loans and other exposure to the struggling market for high-yield, high-risk junk bonds.

As a result, Merrill's stock, tempting though its may seem as it languishes below book value, may not be a happy hunting ground for bargain hunters just yet.

A Merrill insider says the firm is "constantly looking over {its} shoulder" at the bridge loans and "will take reserves as necessary" if a borrower stops making interest payments.

Until Merrill safely navigates out of its junk-bond dilemma and shows that its cost-cutting plan has worked, the firm's earnings potential is suspect, some analysts say.

"We don't see the commitment by management to really push this company toward a competitive {earnings} return," says James P. Hanbury of Wertheim Schroder, who maintains an unenthusiastic "hold" rating on the stock. Earnings probably won't improve without "an even more stringent cost-reduction program" and "another big write-off."

On Friday Merrill closed, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading, at 24 3/4, off 1/4. The shares traded as high as 36 3/4 early last year and 46 3/4 before the October 1987 stock-market crash.

Besides bridge loans, the major concern for investors is whether Merrill's cost-cutting has been sufficient. Merrill acknowledges that revenue in its capital markets segment -- including underwriting, trading and other non-brokerage business lines -- has been less than expected this year. Some analysts can't see any way to avoid more cost-slashing and another charge, unless the firm's investment banking business stages a miraculous recovery.

A Merrill spokesman says: "As of now, we are on target with our restructuring program, and don't envision any further charges or reserves relating to the restructuring." But he adds: "We continuously reserve against possible exposures of various kinds throughout the year." Last year's big one-time charge gave Merrill its first yearly net loss as a public company -- $213.4 million.

Merrill has a lot of exposure to markets that have been weak, including stock-and-bond underwriting, merchant banking and the junk bond market.

The company has $540 million tied up in five bridge loans, which are used to finance takeovers, and $350 million in junk-bond inventory. It now appears that Merrill is working to refinance three of the five big bridges; it wants to reduce its bridge exposure to about $200 million by the end of the third quarter, if business conditions cooperate.

Merrill may have a tougher time getting the junk bonds off its hands. Once hellbent on dethroning Drexel Burnham Lambert as junk-bond king -- it briefly considered opening a junk office near Drexel's Beverly Hills, Calif., headquarters -- Merrill has, since Drexel's demise, been adamant about reducing its financial commitment to junk. It wants to trim whatever it can from its junk inventory by the third quarter's end, and at the very least not add to it.

Although Merrill has been increasing its trading of junk bonds and its manpower devoted to related areas such as corporate restructurings, "I want to make it absolutely clear -- we're not committing more dough to this market," a Merrill insider says of the world of junk.

Merrill can't control what the junk market does. But it has a bit more control over its own expenses. For two consecutive years, Merrill has had to set aside money to pay for layoffs, retreats from excess office space and the like. If it takes another restructuring charge, it would be further evidence of the severity of Wall Street's industrywide slump. Yet some industry analysts say an additional charge is just what Merrill needs.

People inside Merrill and several securities analysts say they understand that since the fourth-quarter charge, revenue isn't coming through as anticipated. Among other things, investment banking revenue plunged 26.7% in the first quarter, when Merrill had net income of $41.3 million mainly because of a $27 million increase in money-management and custodial fees. In 1988 Merrill made a $65.5 million pretax provision for restructuring, and in 1989 it made the $470 million pretax provision ($395 million after tax).

That fourth-quarter charge covered rental losses related to excess space at its World Financial Center headquarters in lower Manhattan, as well as severance payments for layoffs this year of at least 3,000 employees.

So why isn't Merrill's empire shrinking? At rival Shearson Lehman Hutton, a cost-cutting drive has included the closing of 67 of that firm's almost 520 U.S. branches. Merrill, in contrast, has closed some branches but opened others, leaving it with the same number as last year: 460.

Perrin Long, an analyst at Lipper Analytical Services, says that "if things don't get better and better quickly," hundreds of millions of dollars in additional reserves or charges will be required "to get the company whipped into shape."

The timing of any charge is uncertain. Wertheim's Mr. Hanbury says: "My guess is there will be one, but I don't know when."

Lawrence Eckenfelder of Prudential-Bache is another brokerage analyst with a "hold" on Merrill. "I'd like to be able to get enthusiastic about it, but there's not much to get enthusiastic about," he says. "They're focused on the cost side until revenues get stronger -- but my concern is when revenues pick up that the commitment to cutting costs goes out the window, which has been the pattern in past years."

Index funds may soon double in size, the New York Stock Exchange says.

In its annual report just off the presses, the Big Board envisions tremendous growth in institutional investors' role in the markets. Currently, there is about $250 billion in index funds that buy long lists of stocks and futures in an attempt to match the performance of major indexes such as the Standard & Poor's 500. "Before the decade is half over," predicts the Big Board, "that sum is likely to reach $500 billion."

What about the little guy? The Big Board goes on to say a "top priority" of the exchange for the 1990s will be "encouraging participation by individual investors."
