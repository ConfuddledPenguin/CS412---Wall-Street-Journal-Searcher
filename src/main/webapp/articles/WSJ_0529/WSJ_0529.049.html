Campbell Soup Seeking to Sell Some Businesses

  • Author: Vindu P. Goel
  • Date: 05/29/90

Campbell Soup Co. hung a "for sale" sign on some of the company's least profitable foreign operations, conceding that the businesses can't deliver adequate returns to shareholders. In connection with the divestitures, the Camden, N.J., food company said it expects to record a one-time charge of $55 million, or 43 cents a share, against earnings for the fourth quarter ending July 29. For the year-earlier quarter, Campbell reported a net loss of $194.7 million, or $1.51 a share, including one-time restructuring charges of $261 million, or $2.02 a share.

The proposed sales of Campbell's United Kingdom frozen vegetable and seafood lines and its Italian cookie company are the latest cost-cutting maneuvers by David W. Johnson, who became president and chief executive officer in January.

Mr. Johnson's zealous campaign to improve productivity and profits is resulting in a repudiation of several initiatives by his predecessor, R. Gordon McGovern, who resigned abruptly in November 1989. The foreign businesses on the block, which were pet projects of Mr. McGovern, have annual sales of about $200 million. In his brief tenure, Mr. Johnson also has eliminated 364 staff jobs, closed several plants, offered for sale the company's Juice Bowl unit and scotched plans for a new corporate headquarters.

Industry analysts praised the latest move, which was anticipated after Mr. Johnson's tour of Campbell's European operations a few weeks ago. The Italian cookie business, D. Lazzaroni & Co., has been plagued with production and distribution problems. And investors have been calling for the disposal of the frozen-food businesses ever since Campbell acquired them in 1988 through its $201 million purchase of Freshbake Foods Group PLC.

"Lazzaroni and Freshbake have been a running sore at Campbell for a couple of years," said William Leach, an analyst at Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette. "The only surprise was that they didn't sell all of Freshbake."

Indeed, Campbell is putting only the commodity vegetable divisions of Freshbake on the block. The company maintains that the half of Freshbake operations devoted to frozen convenience foods can be made profitable by moving the low-end lines into a more premium position under the Campbell name.

In the frozen quiche category, for example, the company recently renamed its line as Campbell and has boosted its market share to about 40% in the U.K. "We are working to build that {frozen convenience foods} business because it is a strategic one for us," a spokesman said.

Given the commodity nature of the vegetable businesses, Campbell may have difficulty finding a buyer for the units. Although United Biscuits PLC recently approached Campbell about buying certain undisclosed Freshbake operations, "I don't think even it would be interested," said David Atkinson, a securities analyst with London-based County NatWest Wood MacKenzie, which occasionally represents United Biscuits.

"Freshbake has got such a poor reputation," Mr. Atkinson said. "I can't really see why anyone would like to come in."

But even a sale at a low price would improve Campbell's long-term earnings picture, said Ronald Morrow of Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. "Campbell has been underperforming for so long," he said, "that even getting it up to the industry average will look good."

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange on Friday, Campbell shares fell 25 cents to close at $53.375.
