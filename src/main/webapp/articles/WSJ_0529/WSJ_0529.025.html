Letters to the Editor -- Summer and Smoke: Assault on Cigarettes

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/29/90

Congratulations to the State of California and its campaign "to attack the image of refreshment and status" that the tobacco industry touts for smokers, especially young women ("California Ads Attack Cigarettes," April 11). Media outlets for the current California campaign include only newspapers, television, radio and billboards. But, as you state, "since 60% of tobacco users start smoking before they reach high school," why not reach these "users," specifically impressionable young women, via women's magazines? Unfortunately, this is the very place young women are most likely to be influenced by smoking ads.

The unwillingness of most women's magazines to omit cigarette ads is purely financial -- they depend on the support of the tobacco companies to stay afloat. But it is ironic that, in doing so, these magazines are killing off their own readership.

Naomi M. Stearns

Director of Social Work

Dana-Farber Cancer Institute

Boston

---

Your article reports a welcome development to the extent that many First Amendment experts have stressed that the correct response to the objectionable "commercial speech" is more speech -- not advertising bans.

However, the financing of such counteradvertising by higher taxes on cigarettes may be constitutionally questionable because it amounts to forcing companies to pay for the speech of their opponents.

Besides, such social-issue advertising suffers from a lack of clear-cut goals, apart from expressing opposition to a particular behavior. Will it reduce smoking by 10%, or 5%, or 1%? Without specific bench marks, California officials and anti-smoking groups will keep asking for more money, which may be wasted, considering all we know about similar campaigns against drugs or for belt-buckling.

J.J. Boddewyn

Professor

Baruch College (CUNY)

New York
