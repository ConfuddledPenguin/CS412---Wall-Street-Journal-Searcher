Who's News: Sun's Success Doubly Sweet for Designer

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 05/29/90

MOUNTAIN VIEW, Calif. -- It will be a long time before Sun Microsystems Inc. again ignores the advice of Andreas Bechtolsheim, its chief computer designer. Mr. Bechtolsheim, 34 years old, is a hero at Sun today because he inspired its Sparcstation family of computers, on the market for just a little more than a year and already responsible for most of the company's business. Yet when Mr. Bechtolsheim, a co-founder of Sun and the designer of its earliest computers, first proposed to build the Sparcstation in January 1987, Sun's senior executives looked askance at the project. Miffed, Mr. Bechtolsheim came within a whisker of quitting to launch his own company.

Sun is fortunate Mr. Bechtolsheim never left its fold. "Andy saved Sun from a product standpoint," said David Marquardt, a former member of Sun's board of directors.

Mr. Bechtolsheim's rebirth as a visionary at Sun after a period in eclipse illustrates how the process of bringing new computers to market is rife with conflict, pitting designers against marketers. No other senior Sun executive shared Mr. Bechtolsheim's sense of urgency that Sun must push ahead of competitiors in desktop computing.

The West German native designed his first computer in 1980 while a graduate student at Stanford University. When he couldn't sell the machine, a so-called workstation for engineers and scientists, to a large computer maker, Mr. Bechtolsheim founded Sun with software whiz Bill Joy and two entrepreneurs, Vinod Khosla and Scott McNealy. Mr. Bechtolsheim's machine was the company's first product.

Within a few years, Sun and Apollo Computer Inc. were leaders of the computer industry's fastest growing segment, and Digital Equipment Corp. and Hewlett-Packard Co., two behemoths, were hot on the upstarts' heels. By 1987, Sun had outraced Apollo and blunted the attacks of its larger competitors.

But Mr. Bechtolsheim saw a new threat to Sun's prosperity: Steven Jobs, co-founder of Apple Computer Inc. After Mr. Jobs left Apple in 1985, he formed Next Inc., vowing to build an advanced desktop computer. At the time, Sun built computers for individuals, but they were too large to rest on top of a desk. Mr. Bechtolsheim urged Sun to build a powerful desktop computer.

The clarion call was a classic move for Mr. Bechtolsheim, who relishes his role as a gadfly within Sun. He is "always in here telling me how I'm running the business wrong," says Scott McNealy, Sun's chief executive officer.

Officially Sun's vice president of technology, Mr. Bechtolsheim doesn't formally oversee any engineers, budgets or projects. "I can just tell people what I think," he says. "I don't have to worry about protecting my budget. I don't have a budget."

Mr. Bechtolsheim, who held $26 million of Sun's stock when the company first went public several years ago, sometimes personally bankrolls his unofficial work at Sun. "One way Andy gets action at Sun when the company won't back him is to pay for it himself," says James J. Ludemann, a former Sun engineer who worked on Sparcstation 1.

This was one of those times. He began work on the machine without official company support, spending $200,000 of his own money on an office and design help. "I felt if Sun wasn't going to let me do it, I was going to do it on my own," he says.

Soon afterward, Mr. Bechtolsheim distanced himself from Sun, forming a new company called UniSun to bring his computer to market. Mr. Khosla, then a member of Sun's board, agreed to be its president. Finally, he offered Sun the right to invest in the company and purchase it in the future.

Sun seriously considered both possibilities. But Mr. McNealy, fearing that Mr. Bechtolsheim's new venture might be viewed as a new competitor, agreed to build Sparcstation 1.

"Ultimately, we decided that if Sun couldn't do this internally, we weren't worth our salt," says Curt Wozniak, who was chosen to head a new division formed to house the enterprise. In return, Mr. Bechtolsheim sold his fledgling company to Sun for no gain.

But the battle over Mr. Bechtolsheim's computer wasn't over. To start with, Sun placed restrictions on which of its engineers could join Mr. Bechtolsheim; he was allowed to hire only those who said they would otherwise resign. And in the months that followed, Mr. Bechtolsheim saw many of his ideas criticized. A perfectionist -- for months he asked houseguests to sit on his living room floor because he couldn't find a couch he liked -- Mr. Bechtolsheim ferociously defended his positions.

For instance, when engineers on the project told him the package containing the guts of Sparcstation 1 needed three more millimeters in order to be manufactured, he refused to widen it. "The company's culture was to build big boxes," he says. "Mine wasn't going to be big." Finally Mr. Wozniak, the division head, overruled Mr. Bechtolsheim. The three millimeters were added.

In April 1989, Sun introduced Sparcstation 1, a blazingly fast machine, for a barebones price of $9,000. It quickly became Sun's biggest seller. Since then, two more models have been introduced, with the latest, unveiled this month, priced at below $5,000.

The Sparcstation family is now Sun's biggest seller. Sun doesn't break out sales of the machines, but Vicki Brown, an analyst at International Data Corp. said the company will sell about 87,000 Sparcstations this year, comprising nearly 75% of its total unit sales of computers. Last year, Sun sold 35,000 Sparcstations, she said.

Now Mr. Bechtolsheim is working on future Sparcstations: smaller, more powerful machines utilizing advances in semiconductors and miniaturization of other parts. He won't be specific about his work, but says: "Our future computers will be credit cards that you plug into your keyboard."
