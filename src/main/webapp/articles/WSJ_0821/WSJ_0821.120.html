Building Contracts Begin to Stabilize F.W. Dodge Data Say

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/21/90

NEW YORK -- National construction contracting activity seems to be stabilizing after a steep decline early this year. In the latest revision of its 1990 outlook, F.W. Dodge, McGraw-Hill Inc.'s forecasting unit, projected a 7% decline in construction contracting this year to $247.08 billion from 1989's record $266.97 billion.

"The worst seems to be over for the building market nationally," said George A. Christie, vice president and chief economist at Dodge. Still, Mr. Christie warned that while reduced construction because of the savings and loan crisis had already occurred, replacing S&Ls as a source of construction loans will be a "time-consuming process."

The survey said it expects non-residential building contracts to decline 10% this year, to $85.6 billion, including a 17% decline in commercial and manufacturing buildings, a 7% gain in educational building contracts, and a 1% decline in hospital and health construction. Office building contracts are expected to decline 23%.

Funding for nonbuilding construction is expected to ease 2%, to $47.7 billion, with transportation contracts flat and environmental public works down 1%. Utility construction is expected to fall 15%.

Residential construction, forecast at $113.8 billion, is expected to fall 7%, with a 3% decline in single-family houses and a 20% decline in multifamily housing.
