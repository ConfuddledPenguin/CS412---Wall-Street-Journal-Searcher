Cheney Announces U.S. Presence In Abu Dhabi

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 08/21/90

ABU DHABI, United Arab Emirates -- The U.S. has moved a contingent of Air Force transport planes to this tiny kingdom and has received pledges of military cooperation from Oman and Qatar, illustrating America's newfound acceptance in parts of the Arab world. A fleet of 16 C-130 aircraft and nearly 600 Air Force personnel secretly were moved here a week ago from Little Rock, Ark., to participate in the enormous airlift of troops and war materiel into Saudi Arabia. The arrangement was announced yesterday during a visit here by Defense Secretary Dick Cheney.

Its significance extends beyond speeding the movement of cargo. The sudden and now publicly acknowledged presence of U.S. forces in the United Arab Emirates, which had consistently rebuffed such requests over the years, demonstrates the sharp and perhaps long-lasting change in America's role in the entire region following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait.

"It is a big step for the emirate" and "a number of governments in the Gulf to invite United States forces in," Secretary Cheney told reporters after meeting at the presidential palace with Sheik Zayid, ruler of the U.A.E.

"They are deeply concerned that their very existence is threatened by Saddam Hussein," Mr. Cheney said, and are "eager to cooperate with the U.S." and other Arab governments to ensure that the Iraqi president doesn't advance any farther.

Secretary Cheney is discussing possible arms sales with several other countries in the region, and military officials said Oman already is a busy transshipment point for the U.S. military buildup in Saudi Arabia. In addition, Qatar has agreed in principle to allow U.S. planes there during a crisis.

Mr. Cheney met with the rulers of Oman and Qatar during his tour, before heading for sessions with Saudi Arabia's King Fahd and other Saudi officials.

Mr. Cheney's hectic flights crisscrossing the Middle East illustrate his increasingly important diplomatic role to isolate Iraq's Hussein, while the U.S. builds up its naval forces and ground troops in Saudi Arabia.

The United Arab Emirates, Qatar and Oman have all pledged to send troops into Saudi Arabia, and their air bases and harbors are important to keep supplies moving steadily. Use of airports in the Persian Gulf countries also would provide U.S. airmen emergency landing strips if shooting breaks out, an official traveling with Mr. Cheney said.

Mr. Cheney's comments reflected the sea change affecting the region. Amid the splendor of Abu Dhabi's seaside palace, decked with marble and an ornate rug as large as a tennis court, the secretary emphasized that "our defensive efforts of the past few weeks apply to all our friends in the Gulf." He is the highest-ranking U.S. official to visit the U.A.E. in its 18-year history.

Later, standing in the 110-degree weather, Mr. Cheney talked about the potential for longer-term military ties. Countries in the region "are now prepared to actively engage with us to enhance their security," including buying "additional equipment if that's what they need," he told reporters.

For the airmen who work in a rickety hangar and sleep packed together in makeshift air-conditioned trailers put up by the U.A.E in 48 hours, short-term concerns are paramount. "We slept in the hangar the first night, on the floor," said Col. Richard Pluchinsky, a Vietnam veteran who now serves as the unit's operations officer. "Right now we can sustain our operation for 30 days" without receiving additional spare parts or other materiel from the U.S.

Despite the immense logistical problems, the colonel said morale is pretty good. "The flyers want to fly. The maintainers want to fix," he said.

After looking over the primitive living conditions for U.S. military personnel in Abu Dhabi, Mr. Cheney said the American people must understand that "we are here because we need to be here." The Bush administration, he said, is committed "to stay as long as we're needed and as long as we're wanted. That is what I have told to troops, too."
