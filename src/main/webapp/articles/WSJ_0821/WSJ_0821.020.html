Japanese Carrier Places Big Order With Boeing

  • Author: Yumiko Ono and Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 08/21/90

Boeing Co. said All Nippon Airways Co. recently exercised five options for 747-400 jumbo jets, elevating them to firm-order status, and at the same time took out 17 new options for the wide-body aircraft. An announcement about the options placement, which eventually could be valued at as much as $3 billion to the Seattle aerospace manufacturer, is expected to be made Tuesday in Tokyo. Conversion of the five options, meanwhile, is valued at about $650 million.

Combined with orders placed during 1988 and 1989 but not yet filled, All Nippon now has booked 43 Boeing 747-400s. Of that total, Boeing said, 26 are currently counted as firm. Options are important because, while they don't add to Boeing's official backlog, they seal a carrier's delivery position.

All Nippon, which has been known until recently primarily as a domestic airline, is in need of planes to expand its international routes. Japan's second largest airline, it now connects Japan to 14 destinations in North America, Europe, Asia and Australia, and intends to start flights to Paris in October, and to New York next March. By 1995, the company aims to double the percentage of international flights from its present 18%.

All Nippon's decision follows a string of large orders for Boeing from Asia in recent months, spotlighting how air travel in the Pacific Rim continues to boom, even as signs of weakness have developed in the U.S. market. In June, the company received orders for 23 747-400s by Korean Airlines, totaling $4.8 billion. It also has a $2 billion order from China's Civil Aviation Administration and a $3.1 billion order for 747-400s from Japan Air Lines.

Japan's carriers have been increasing competition to provide international flights recently. Although Japan Air Lines was for many years the only one with the right to fly international routes, government deregulation in 1985 encouraged other airlines to provide services abroad. In addition, the soaring number of Japanese tourists recently has strengthened the demand for overseas flights.

Adding to its fleet of 747-400s, which would carry about 350 passengers in All Nippon's three-class seating arrangement, is therefore seen as a necessary move for the airline. Should All Nippon go on to purchase the planes, it will also lower the average age of its aircraft to less than seven years from the present 7.8 years. Delivery of the planes, which will be powered by General Electric Co. engines, could begin in 1995.

The planes ordered earlier will be delivered starting next week.

In addition to the 747-400s, All Nippon is also shopping around for about 300 other wide-body planes, and is looking at models made by Boeing, McDonnell Douglas Corp. and Airbus Industrie.

In the year ended March 31, All Nippon posted operating revenue of $4.3 billion, up 16.4% from the previous year. Net income rose 3% to $95 million.
