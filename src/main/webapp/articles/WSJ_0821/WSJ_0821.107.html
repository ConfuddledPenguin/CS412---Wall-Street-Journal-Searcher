Technology: Data General Is Said to Plan Further Cuts --- Layoffs Could Exceed 17%, Or 2,000, at Ailing Firm; Sale Speculation Denied

  • Author: John R. Wilke
  • Date: 08/21/90

Beset by slow sales and steep losses, Data General Corp. plans another big round of layoffs, company insiders said. The cutbacks could total at least 2,000, or 17% of Data General's work force of 11,700, and are expected to be announced in early September.

At the same time, Ronald L. Skates, president and chief executive officer, denied industry speculation that he has been seeking a buyer for the big minicomputer maker, based in Westborough, Mass. "We aren't talking with anyone about selling the company," he said in an interview.

The layoffs would be the latest in a series at the loss-plagued company, shrinking employment from a peak of 17,700 in 1985, its last profitable year. Revenue peaked at $1.36 billion in 1988.

A Data General spokesman yesterday wouldn't rule out further cutbacks, and said, "We're going to take whatever steps are necessary to become profitable," Mr. Skates added, "We are doing everything we can to downsize the company."

Data General is trying to transform itself from a traditional minicomputer manufacturer to a maker of workstations and computer network servers, built around standard Unix operating software and low-cost, standard-design microprocessors supplied by Motorola Inc.

But the transition has taken longer than Data General executives had hoped, as competition in the workstation market has become more intense and sales of the company's older minicomputer line eroded somewhat faster than expected. The new workstation line, which is aggressively priced and has received good technical reviews, is expected to bring in sales of more than $100 million this year. Still, sales from the older minicomputer line are likely to fall by more than that sum, estimated Barry F. Willman, a Sanford C. Bernstein & Co. analyst.

Analysts said Data General has a relatively strong balance sheet, with only $58 million in long-term debt and $93 million in cash. But they said that further work-force reductions are inevitable. "They've been losing money a lot faster than they've been able to cut costs," Jay Stevens, an analyst with Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., said.

In the past, Motorola, a big communications equipment maker based in Schaumburg, Ill., has been mentioned as a potential suitor for Data General, because of the strategic fit of the two companies' technology. But analysts discount this possibility, noting that Motorola recently entered the computer systems business on its own and may not want to invest in Data General's headaches. A Motorola spokesman declined to comment yesterday.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Data General shares closed near the 52-week low of $5, finishing at $5.75, up 12.5 cents, giving the company an indicated market value of $172.5 million. At the current stock price, the company's value is less than 15% of annual revenue, and well below its book value, or assets minus liabilities, of $16 a share.

For its third quarter ended June 30, Data General had a net loss of $21.4 million, or 71 cents a share, on a 1.2% decline in revenue to $302.4 million. The loss, though narrower than the year-earlier third-quarter deficit of $23.1 million, or 79 cents a share, after a $6.3 million one-time gain, was far larger than most analysts had expected. Data General also backed off an earlier forecast that it would break even or even show a profit in the fiscal fourth quarter.
