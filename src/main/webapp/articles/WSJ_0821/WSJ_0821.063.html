From the Pulpits a Mighty Roar: Firestone and Brimstone Sermons

  • Author: R. Gustav Niebuhr
  • Date: 08/21/90

Try finding a church manual that tells how to blend the squeal of racing car tires with the soprano section of a choir, or how to handle a blast of exhaust at Holy Communion. Those are real questions for 10 Denver churches that find themselves along the circuit of that city's downtown Grand Prix this Friday through Sunday. And some of the pastors aren't pleased that they have to come up with answers.

"It's not that the churches don't want to be a participant with the city" in the auto race, says the Rev. Robert West, pastor of St. Paul's Lutheran Church. "It's just that we don't want our right to worship disregarded."

Mr. West, along with the Rev. John Wilcox, pastor of nearby Central Presbyterian Church, complained to city officials months ago that street closings along the 1.9-mile racecourse would keep many parishioners home. And those who got through would hear more thunder from the streets than the pulpit. Still, the city approved the race.

As a result, Mr. Wilcox's flock plans to pull up stakes this Sunday and worship with another congregation -- one whose church is well away from the racecourse. "My philosophy of life is when you're licked, you don't waste time and stew in it," he says.

Mr. West, on the other hand, is staying put. "We may end up with just an organ recital," he concedes. The Grand Prix's organizers have agreed to provide an off-duty police officer to keep race fans from parking in the church's lot.

Race officials have been working hard to ease the concerns of the clerics (as well as those of local retailers and neighborhood groups). They are distributing more than 100,000 "traffic brochures," detailing street closings and alternate routes, and issuing parking passes for churchgoers.

That pleased the Rev. C.B. Woodrich, pastor of Holy Ghost Roman Catholic Church. Aware that he will have two weddings this weekend, race officials gave the wedding coordinators maps so that brides, grooms and guests could make it to the church on time.

Father Woodrich sees the race actually offering a way to fill pews, through a little advertising he'll do among the thousands of race fans Denver is expecting. "I'm going to make sure they know in the hotels where I am," he says. "I'm looking for good Mass attendance."
