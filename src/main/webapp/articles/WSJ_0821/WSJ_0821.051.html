CHINA'S BUDGET DEFICIT

China unveiled figures showing its budget deficit for this year is widening.

Government spending climbed 16.4% in the first seven months this year, outpacing an 11.6% rise in revenue, said the China Daily, quoting the State Statistical Bureau. The official newspaper didn't give specific budget figures for the period.

The official projection for the 1990 budget has set the deficit for this year at the equivalent of $1.8 billion, narrowed from $1.9 billion in 1989. But economists said the reduction was unlikely to be achieved. "The lower deficit called for in the budget is now looking increasingly optimistic," one foreign economist said.

The newspaper didn't say why spending was rising, but it noted that profits of state companies slid 20% in the first seven months of the year. Last year, subsidies to keep prices artificially low and to bail out inefficient state companies accounted for one-third of government spending.

EC ANTI-DUMPING ACTIONS

The European Community opened an investigation into imports of disposable Japanese lighters. The EC also said it will reconsider a decision, announced in January, to end anti-dumping duties that were imposed in 1985 on hydraulic excavators from Japan.

ISRAEL DELAYS TRANSMITTER

Israeli environmentalists won a temporary injunction barring the Voice of America from building a radio-transmitter complex in the Negev desert until environmental questions are answered. Last month, a government planning board approved the billion-dollar project aimed at broadcasting programs of the Voice of America and Radio Liberty.

VIETNAM-SOUTH KOREA TALKS

Vietnam will send a delegation to South Korea next month to discuss economic cooperation, the first official visit since before the Vietnam War, officials in Seoul said. South Korean business groups have expressed interest in mining and in building small factories in Vietnam to take advantage of cheap labor costs.

MARCOS'S SWISS ACCOUNTS

A brother-in-law of Philippine President Corazon Aquino launched a movement to pressure Swiss authorities to return millions of dollars allegedly stolen by the late President Ferdinand Marcos and deposited in Switzerland. Sen. Agapito Aquino said he formed the "Swiss Watch" organization because the government had failed to recover the money through court actions. The Aquino government has accused Mr. Marcos of stealing up to $10 billion from the national treasury during his 20-year rule.
