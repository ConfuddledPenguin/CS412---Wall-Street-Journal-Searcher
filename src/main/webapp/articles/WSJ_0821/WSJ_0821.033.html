Marketing & Media: Volvo Unveils New Luxury-Car Series To Counter Japanese Rivals in Europe

  • Author: Stephen D. Moore Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/21/90

GOTHENBURG, Sweden -- AB Volvo unveiled a new top-of-the-line car model, the first in a spate of new models planned by Swedish auto makers to fend off the imminent incursion into Europe by Japanese car companies. Volvo said its new 900 series would be followed by the launch of at least one other new model family by 1995. Swedish archrival Saab Automobile AB -- in which General Motors Corp. holds a 50% stake -- also plans to introduce three new car models by the mid-1990s. The pace is unheard of for the two Swedish companies, at which the traditional rate of model debuts has been closer to one a decade.

But officials at Volvo and Saab admit the lack of exciting new cars has intensified a recent sales slump and caused both to lose ground to more nimble rivals, such as Bayerische Motoren Werke AG and Daimler-Benz AG. The German car giants have replaced almost their entire model ranges in recent years. For Volvo and Saab, the challenge of catching up will be complicated further by the arrival in earnest of the Japanese in Europe, the officials said.

Roger Holtback, president of Volvo's car division, warned that intensifying competition from Japanese rivals promises "to make Europe the auto industry's main battleground in the 1990s. We know that other Japanese companies will join Honda Motor Co., Nissan Motor Co. and Toyota Motor Corp. in setting up manufacturing operations in Europe," he said.

Volvo's response must be more frequent model changes, despite the soaring costs of developing new models, Mr. Holtback added. He also promised that the drive for growth in Europe would steer design of new Volvo models. That strategy addresses frequent criticism in Europe of Volvos for being primarily styled for tastes in the U.S., the company's biggest single market.

The European slant is apparent in the sleek, rounded lines of Volvo's most luxurious 960 model, which breaks with the boxy profile of Volvo's previous exclusive model, the eight-year-old 760. But an even bigger surprise rests under the hood -- a powerful 3-liter, six-cylinder engine which Mr. Holtback compared favorably with high-performance engines of any other European luxury-car maker.

Industry analysts agreed that the new engine probably would be the first from Volvo muscular enough to earn serious respect from performance-minded car buyers in central Europe. But these analysts added that Volvo could have problems cultivating a more performance-oriented image among European car buffs without driving away existing customers.

Many Volvo buyers prize the company's image as an ultra-safe family car with a quirky cachet and high resale value. Reflecting that performance-safety balancing act, Volvo equipped the 960 model with an elaborate built-in child-restraint seat, which it said was a first for any car maker.

As proof of how serious Volvo is about quicker model changes, Mr. Holtback boasted that development of the new 900 series had been completed in just two years. But it cost a hefty 2 billion Swedish kronor ($350 million). The new model's engine and transmission were the first in a new drive-train program that when completed will have swallowed at least six years and an estimated 6 billion kronor, Mr. Holtback suggested.

Moreover, the advent of the 900 series leaves Volvo with five model families. According to Mr. Holtback "that's obviously a broader product range than we need for the time being. The message we're giving customers and dealers is that in the coming five years we will reduce the number of model families to three with more distinct profiles."

The 900 series will be one obvious survivor; so will the the mid-size 400 series model produced by Netherlands-based affiliate Volvo Car BV. Volvo is negotiating to acquire the 70% it doesn't already own of Volvo Car BV from the Dutch government.

The third model is almost certain to be the 600 series, a long-awaited successor to Volvo's big-selling, 25-year-old 200 series. Most analysts expect the 600 series to debut next fall. Volvo refuses to discuss possible model changes in line with company policy.

Whenever it comes, the 600 looms as a make-or-break product decision, analysts said. Buoyant sales of the new model will be necessary to meet Volvo's internal growth target -- to 500,000 cars a year by 1996 from roughly 410,000 units now.

Costs of developing the new 900 and expected 600 models have stretched Volvo, eating into the car division's operating profit in recent years amid dwindling sales in Sweden, the U.K. and the U.S. The three markets account for 70% of the company's sales.

Mr. Holtback predicted that by the mid-1990s, Volvo's alliance with France's Renault "will kick in as an insurance policy." He predicted that tighter links with Renault and expanded Volvo manufacturing operations outside Sweden would ensure a strong position in Europe. And joint development work on major components would help both concerns bear the skyrocketing costs of surviving the fierce competition expected in Europe.
