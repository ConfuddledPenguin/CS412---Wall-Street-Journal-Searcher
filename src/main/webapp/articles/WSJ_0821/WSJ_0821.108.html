First Interstate, Amid Continuing Woes, Suspends Dividend-Reinvestment Plan

  • Author: Charles McCoy
  • Date: 08/21/90

LOS ANGELES -- First Interstate Bancorp said it suspended a dividend-reinvestment plan that had been a major element of the struggling concern's efforts to rebuild its shattered capital base. The move highlights the continuing woes of the nation's eighth largest banking concern, despite recent rebounds from disastrous results in the second half of 1989 that eroded capital, hammered its stock price and made it vulnerable to a takeover bid.

Under the plan, shareholders were allowed to use dividend proceeds to buy First Interstate stock at a 2.5% discount from market values. The plan had produced $260 million of desperately needed capital since its initiation in February 1989.

First Interstate said it dropped the plan because of its flagging stock price. Although it earned $222.3 million, or $3.60 a share, for the six months ended June 30, First Interstate's stock has slid to new lows, making the issuance of discounted shares under the reinvestment plan too dilutive.

"The purpose of the plan was to allow us to replenish our capital base in a costeffective manner," said Edward M. Carson, chairman and chief executive officer. "However, at current stock price levels," it would not "serve the interests of shareholders" to boost the number of shares outstanding at cut-rate prices.

The news drove First Interstate's stock down even further. In New York Stock Exchange trading, it closed at $28.875, down 50 cents. That was believed to be a record low for the stock, which traded as high as $70 a share in the fall of 1989. Analysts say the swooning stock reflects both general weakness in bank stocks and lingering skepticism about First Interstate's prospects.

The bank declined to comment on how the suspension of the plan would affect its ongoing efforts to restore capital devoured by surprise losses totaling $341.3 million in last year's second half. A spokesman, noting that the bank has had two profitable quarters in 1990, said the bank intends to build capital through retained earnings. Meanwhile, the bank is continuing restructuring efforts aimed at trimming its cost structure, which remains one of banking's most bloated.

Still, the stock's continuing plunge raised anew questions about how long First Interstate will be able to avoid becoming a takeover target. The current stock price level is far below First Interstate's book value of $37.81 a share, and has even gone below the $33 a share at which it sold 8.6 million shares in a public offering in February. Several competitors, including Wells Fargo & Co., Security Pacific Corp. and BankAmerica Corp., are known to have studied possible bids for First Interstate. As the stock price slides, "the temptation for a bid rises," observed one investment banker.

Furthermore, Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. bought 45% of the shares sold in the February offering and owns about 10% of First Interstate. Although KKR has said it supports management's efforts to turn the company around, some other institutional holders questioned how long KKR would tolerate the low stock price. At present levels, KKR has a paper loss of about $13 million on its purchases in the February offering.

"Losses have been known to make KKR's patience wear a little thin," said one institutional holder.
