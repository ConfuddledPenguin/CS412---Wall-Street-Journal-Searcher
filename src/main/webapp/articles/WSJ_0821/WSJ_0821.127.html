OPEC Rejects Saudi, Venezuelan Bid For Meeting, Making Output Boost Likely

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 08/21/90

OPEC rejected a call by Saudi Arabia and Venezuela for an emergency meeting, leaving them free to pump more oil on their own. The two had sought a meeting of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries to endorse a plan under which they and other producers would increase output to avoid oil shortages and runaway prices during the current Middle East crisis.

The rejection of their bid was widely thought to mean that Saudi Arabia will soon produce more oil anyway. The kingdom had previously stated that it would do so if OPEC failed to act. Venezuela is expected to follow suit.

A statement issued by OPEC's Vienna headquarters yesterday said the request for a special meeting failed to get the required support of a majority of OPEC's 13 member nations. The statement added that Sadek Boussena, Algeria's oil minister and OPEC president, would "meet some OPEC oil ministers for consultations before the end of the month."

Despite the decision against an emergency session, both Saudi Arabia and Venezuela still held out hope for some sort of OPEC meeting quickly to consider their intention to replace more than half the four million barrels a day of Iraqi and Kuwaiti crudes lost to world oil markets. Also, some others in OPEC who had voted against an immediate emergency meeting appeared to be having second thoughts.

Last night, several oil ministers were trying to arrange at least such an informal meeting, possibly by Friday. Their goal is to provide some kind of OPEC "umbrella" for the increased output, mainly to avoid the appearance of a production free-for-all.

"The potential for a meeting of some sort, by at least six or seven, is still there," said Bahman Karbassioun, a Vienna-based consultant and former OPEC official. The idea would be to "use OPEC's good offices" for the production increases that are going to happen anyway "and not damage the organization," he said.

In Caracas, meanwhile, Venezuelan President Carlos Andres Perez was quoted by the government news agency as saying that several OPEC nations with the potential to increase oil production expect to meet in the next few days. And he said Venezuela will increase its production "when there is an accord within OPEC."

There wasn't any immediate official response from Saudi Arabia to the rejection of the call for an emergency meeting. But one Persian Gulf oil official close to developments suggested that OPEC's "saying `no' to a conference means saying `yes' to the idea that everybody can act on his own."

Hisham Nazer, Saudi Arabia's oil minister, had made clear over the weekend that the kingdom, which has been producing within its OPEC quota of 5.4 million barrels a day, would shortly increase output by as much as two million barrels a day with or without OPEC's approval. Among other reasons, Mr. Nazer cited the damage to the economies of developing countries from escalating oil prices. And he suggested Venezuela and the United Arab Emirates would produce an additional 500,000 barrels a day each, and urged others with lesser spare capacity to help make up the lost four million barrels a day.

Yesterday, President Bush applauded the Saudi decision. White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said, "The president notes that we are very pleased by the boost in world-wide supply being provided by Saudi Arabia, and overall we believe we have reasonably stable conditions with regard to oil production and supply."

Oil prices, which have gained as much as $8 a barrel since the Aug. 2 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, had been expected to level out this week on the basis of the Saudi public pledge to increase production. Yesterday, crudes generally were higher though the rate of climb did slow. Petroleum product prices rose sharply as market worries centered on possibly tighter fuel supplies. On the New York Mercantile Exchange, unleaded gasoline for September delivery surged 4.28 cents to settle at $1 a gallon.

Several OPEC nations remain strongly opposed to any change that would pour more oil onto world markets at this time. Iraq is among them. It vociferously objects to any OPEC meeting designed to replace its blockaded oil. And its old war foe Iran also is opposed to such a meeting.

Iran's oil revenues have increased sharply since Iraq invaded Kuwait, and Iran not only has sold most of the oil it had sitting in storage, but has received substantially higher prices for the oil. Meanwhile, it is winning new customers on long-term contracts in Japan who formerly purchased from Iraq.

There are also several members of OPEC who maintain that there isn't any need to increase production while oil inventories, including government-stored crudes in major consuming nations, remain high. Indonesia, usually a moderate on oil prices, proposes that OPEC's ministerial market-monitoring groups carefully study the supply and demand situation before considering a full meeting.

Meanwhile, a meeting of the governing board of the International Energy Agency that had been scheduled for Thursday has been postponed until Aug. 31. The delegates to the Paris-based agency are to consider the consuming nations' possible responses to the oil-supply crunch. The governing board first met earlier this month but concluded then that conditions didn't warrant the use of the emergency oil-sharing system or drawdowns of strategic oil stores.
