World Markets: After Heady Growth, West German Stock Prices Seen Flat Through 2nd Quarter, Then Rising

  • Author: A.H. Mooradian Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/23/90

FRANKFURT, West Germany -- West German stock prices' powerful rise since the East European overhaul process got into full swing last November should flatten, leading them to fluctuate around current levels through the second quarter before resuming an upward track later in the year, analysts said. Stock prices on balance may have risen too far too fast, they said. And amid uncertainty about the contemplated German monetary union and its effects on West German interest rates, inflation and the West German mark, the analysts said, operators probably will be quick to take profits on short-term jumps in stock prices.

"The {stock} market from a price-earnings standpoint is somewhat overextended, as well as from the perspective of relative yields against interest rate investments," said Walter Heinemann, head of research at Merck, Finck & Co., a Munich-based investment banking firm.

But the potential for a sustained slide in prices is just as limited as that for a major increase in the next three months, analysts said. Few investors are willing to pull out of the West German market, because the reconstruction of East Germany offers a big economic push for West Germany and promises hundreds of billions of marks in potential sales for Western companies in the next several years, they said.

"It's a question of risks, and we wouldn't want to run the risk of being out of the West German market," said Andrew Thomson, an analyst at Kleinwort Benson Securities in London.

Even the emotionally charged buying sentiment in Frankfurt in recent months, on euphoria from German reunification prospects, probably won't be able alone to sustain the recent pace of price growth that took the weighted German Stock Index, or DAX, up 10% from the beginning of the year to a historical peak of 1968.55 on March 30. The DAX rose 35% in 1989, ending the year at 1790.37. It finished Friday at 1884.33.

"Although we retain our positive stance towards the {West German stock} market in our longer-term view, we are now, however, looking for a period of cooling off, accompanied by a measure of profit-taking in selected areas," analysts at Swiss Bank Corp.'s London equities research group wrote.

Analysts forecast that the DAX will fluctuate between 1850 and 2000 in the next couple of months. "If anything goes wrong with the {German} monetary and economic union, we could very easily see the market go down 10% to 15%," Mr. Heinemann of Merck Finck said.

The construction sector appears to be one of those best positioned to capture substantial new business in Eastern Europe. Builders and engineering and materials supply companies are moving to expand productive capacity that already is being tested by West Germany's own building boom. Builders such as Hochtief AG and Philipp Holzmann AG and suppliers such as Heidelberger Zement AG and Dyckerhoff AG are likely winners from a longer-term perspective, analysts said. But they added that after rising steeply in recent months, stocks of these companies probably are in for some more profit-taking.

Analysts also are high on Siemens AG, which has strong earnings in its main businesses in electronics, computers and telecommunications. The company has been arranging joint ventures with East German monopolies and has secured major contracts to help upgrade the East German telecommunications network. Siemens, also one of the world's largest builders and outfitters of power plants, stands to benefit from anticipated East German investment in such projects.

World Market Activity

Tokyo shares finished lower Friday, after profit-taking depressed prices of steel and shipbuilding stocks, though equities were up from midday lows, leaving a large number of stocks higher.

The Nikkei index of 225 issues fell 109.97 points to 29835.44, after gains in the two previous sessions. First section volume was estimated at 700 million shares, down from 908 million shares a day earlier.

In early trading Monday in Tokyo, the Nikkei index rose 47.88 points to 29883.32.

On Friday, the Tokyo Stock Price Index, or Topix, of all first section issues, which surged 45.53 points Thursday, rose 0.67 point to 2214.16.

Friday's market performance was watched closely, as prices haven't had three consecutive daily gains since early March. Until Friday, stock prices had fallen back sharply on days following two consecutive sharp daily gains.

The decline Friday didn't cause serious damage to the market, traders said, as participants are more relaxed because of the widespread expectation the dollar won't rise far above the 160-yen level. But at the same time, Friday's results convinced many participants that the market faces strong resistance around 30000 and 31000 points on the Nikkei.

In London, share prices finished a drab session slightly firmer Friday, amid a dearth of trading influences. Shares were largely mixed during most of the session, as dealers complained of a lack of investor interest in the middle of the long three-week trading account.

The Financial Times 30-share index was up 0.4 point at 1712.6. Volume was a modest 410.7 million shares, compared with 491.9 million a day earlier. The 100-share index rose 2.4 points to 2187.1.

Market participants also suggested that investors steered clear of the market after the brokerage firm Warburg Securities warned Friday that British inflation could exceed 10% in April. The nation's March inflation rate was 8.1%.

Elsewhere on Friday, stock prices closed higher in Paris, Frankfurt, Brussels, Amsterdam and Stockholm, and lower in Milan. Prices in Zurich were mixed. In Paris, stocks climbed to a record, as institutions beefed up their positions for the start of the May trading account. Stockholm equity prices soared in slow trading, boosted by Swedish trade data that were better than expected.

Share prices closed lower in Sydney, Singapore and Manila, were mixed in Hong Kong and Seoul, and ended higher in Wellington and Taipei.
