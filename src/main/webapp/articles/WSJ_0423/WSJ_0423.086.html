Law -- Legal Beat: Judge Expands Liability of Owners And Operators of Ships in Oil Spills

  • Author: Arthur S. Hayes and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 04/23/90

A federal judge in Anchorage expanded the liability of ship owners and operators in Alaskan pipeline oil spills. The ruling is the first to interpret whether the Trans-Alaska Pipeline Act of 1973 superseded a 19th century law that limited a shipper's exposure to the cost of a vessel and its cargo. U.S. District Court Judge H. Russel Holland dismissed a claim brought by Trinidad Corp., a St. Louis shipper that hired the S.S. Glacier Bay, which struck a rock and spilled almost 150,000 gallons of oil into the Cook Inlet Bay in Alaska on July 2, 1987. Commercial fishermen and fish processors filed suit in federal court asking for more than $104.8 million from Trinidad Corp. and British Petroleum PLC, which had hired Trinidad to ship its oil.

The Trans-Alaska Pipeline Act provides that the owner or operator of a vessel involved in an oil spill must pay plaintiffs the first $14 million in damages. After that, the Trans-Alaskan Pipeline Liability Fund, which is funded by oil companies, is obligated to pay the next $86 million in damages. Both the shipper and the fund are liable for the damages.

But it was unclear whether a negligent shipper would be liable to plaintiffs for additional damages or whether the fund could recoup the $86 million from a negligent shipper.

Trinidad filed a complaint with the court arguing that the 19th century law limited its liability under a negligence claim to no more than $6.55 million, the cost of the vessel and the cargo.

In a rare partnership, the plaintiffs and the oil companies filed to have Trinidad's complaint dismissed.

"Judge Holland's order is the pivotal ruling in the Glacier Bay litigation," says plaintiffs' attorney Gerard M. Nolting of Faegre & Benson in Minneapolis. "The shipping industry will have great difficulty in the future in limiting its liability in oil spill litigation in cases arising out of Trans-Alaskan oil spills and others governed by statutes similar to the Trans-Alaskan Pipeline Act."

Trinidad declined to comment.

JUDGE RULES that actuaries' relationships with their clients aren't privileged.

The decision means that information from a partner at Kwasha Lipton, an actuarial firm in Fort Lee, N.J., can be used in a suit against West Point-Pepperell Inc., owned by investor William Farley. Fruit of the Loom Inc., also owned by Mr. Farley, is a big client of Kwasha. Kwasha had argued that use of the information posed a conflict of interest for the firm. In the case, a former official of a company acquired by Mr. Farley is suing for compensation.

In what U.S. District Judge Kenneth Conboy in Manhattan called an "extraordinary proposition," the Kwasha partner asked to withdraw an affidavit he had voluntarily submitted in the case. The actuary said he didn't know at the time that the case was against a company related to one of his firm's clients.

Kwasha claimed that actuaries have a confidential relationship with their client that must be protected just as attorney-client relationships are protected. Actuaries provide services such as calculating statistics for insurance and pension plans.

But Judge Conboy rejected the request. "It is clear that the New York courts and legislators have not thought that the benefits of protecting the confidences between an actuary and a client or an accountant and a client are great enough to create a statutory or common law privilege," the judge said in his ruling. The judge said that if New York law recognized a privilege between accountants and their clients, it would be feasible that the same protection could be extended to actuaries.

Kwasha officials declined to comment. Attorneys for West Point-Pepperell couldn't be reached for comment.

Robert Krumme, the former official who filed the suit, worked at Cluett Peabody & Co. from 1969 to 1986, when it was acquired by West Point-Pepperell.

SURVEY SAYS 1989 legal fees rose sharply in directors' and officers' liability cases.

Wyatt Co., a consulting firm, studied 1,333 corporations and found that the cost of defending D&O suits last year averaged $541,000, up 61% from $336,000 in 1988 and more than three times the 1980 figure of $162,000.

"That's the largest percentage increase we've ever shown," said Kenneth S. Wollner, a consultant in Wyatt's Chicago office.

Not surprisingly, litigation was the highest expense, with 1989 legal expenses averaging more than $818,000 per claim, compared with an average of $514,000 spent on claims that were settled. Even claims that were dropped cost an average of $203,000, the annual survey reported.

More increases are coming, the study said. It projects that defense costs for D&O claims brought in 1989 will average $1.3 million by the time they are completed. "There's a tremendous escalation in the cost of defending D&O claims," said Mr. Wollner.

He attributed the climb to increasingly complex D&O suits brought by shareholders, noting in particular a surge in the cases that arise out of corporate mergers or reorganizations. About 50% of the claims reported in the Wyatt survey involved a shareholder action. Employee suits accounted for about 20% and customer claims about 17%. Directors and officers of companies involved in mergers, acquisitions or divestitures are twice as likely to get sued as other executives, Wyatt reported.

Among the survey's other findings: recently adopted state statutes designed to narrow the potential liability of executives are having mixed results. They apparently are discouraging some types of D&O claims, particularly against private companies.

But the statutes are having little impact on suits brought by shareholders and government agencies against executives of public concerns.

MAYTAG IS SUED for deceptive trade practices involving microwave ovens.

The Alabama attorney general filed suit in federal court in Montgomery, Ala., alleging Maytag secretly reconditioned defective microwave ovens at its Magic Chef Co. division plant in Anniston, Ala., altered their serial numbers and sold them as new.

In Newton, Iowa, Janis Cooper, vice president of corporate communications for Maytag Corp., said the company is reviewing its procedures at the plant, where all Magic Chef microwaves are made. "We take a great deal of pride in our products and our name -- both Maytag and Magic Chef -- and we consider the trust of our customers very important," she said.

The suit seeks to halt the practice, which it claims has been going on for several years. It also asks for a recall of all units involved and a fine of up to $2,000 for each violation.

FIRST WOMAN DEAN of Ivy League law school to step down.

Barbara A. Black, dean of the Columbia University School of Law, will leave her post at the end of the 1990-1991 academic year after 5 1/2 years. She says her term was scheduled to end six months later but that she didn't want to bring in a new dean in the middle of an academic year. Ms. Black, 56 years old, supervised an overhaul of the school's first-year curriculum and introduced new courses in legal thought and economics and law.

She said she plans to return to several legal history books she is writing about 17th century Massachusetts colonial law and to teaching at Columbia. A successor hasn't been appointed.
