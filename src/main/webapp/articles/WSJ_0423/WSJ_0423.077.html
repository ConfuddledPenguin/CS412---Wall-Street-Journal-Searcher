CrossLand Savings Had Loss in Period Of $136.5 Million

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 04/23/90

NEW YORK -- CrossLand Savings reported a $136.5 million first-quarter net loss and a doubling of problem assets, increasing its already severe capital problems. The results reflect a $100 million provision for possible losses on problem loans. The bank said it felt the provision was appropriate "after management's regular evaluation of CrossLand's loan portfolio and after preliminary discussions with the {Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.} relating to its ongoing examination" of the bank.

To alleviate its capital shortage, which could lead to regulatory sanctions, CrossLand proposed a recapitalization plan that asks preferred shareholders for a major concession. Under terms of the plan, the bank's total $363 million of Series A and Series B preferred stock would be converted to common stock. Unlike the preferred, the common stock doesn't pay a dividend and would reduce the preferred holders' standing as creditors.

With $13.2 billion of assets, CrossLand is the largest savings bank in New York and one of the largest in the U.S. CrossLand said it hasn't yet determined the conversion ratios for each of the two series. But it said that the move is aimed at qualifying the equity that is tied up in the preferred as core capital and tangible capital, two critical measures of the health of a savings bank.

CrossLand said that the plan wouldn't help it meet, however, the FDIC's requirements for leverage capital. These require a bank to maintain tangible capital -- capital excluding good will -- of at least 4% of its assets as they are adjusted for risk.

Late last month, a plan to recapitalize CrossLand Savings collapsed when the Pritzker family of Chicago, which with a partner would have taken a controlling stake in the bank in return for a capital infusion, decided to withdraw from the transaction. The Pritzkers didn't say why they withdrew; the bank said, however, that it would propose a new plan as quickly as possible.

CrossLand's problem is that under the thrift-reform legislation Congress passed last August, its two series of cumulative preferred don't qualify as core capital. Because the preferred shares are cumulative -- if dividends are missed, they accumulate and must be paid before any dividends on common are paid -- the preferred is viewed by regulators as debt rather than equity.

CrossLand's first-period loss equaled $10.61 a share, compared with net income of $11.8 million, or 11 cents a share, in the year-earlier period. The loan-loss provision a year earlier was $7.9 million.

"During this quarter, CrossLand experienced a sharp increase in loan delinquencies and adversely classified assets," the bank said.

Delinquent loans -- those 90 or more days past due -- plus real estate on which the bank has foreclosed, climbed to $455.5 million as of March 31 from $218.4 million on March 31, 1989, and from $288 million at year's end. These loans and real estate as a percentage of total assets grew to 3.44% from 1.40% a year earlier and 2.04% at year's end.

Income from interest on loans before provision for possible loan losses fell 24% to $31.7 million from $41.8 million in 1989's fourth quarter for three reasons: CrossLand has pared interest-earning assets by $2.1 billion in the past nine months in keeping with its business plan; the bank has seen a substantial increase in nonperforming assets; and its real-estate investments have risen.

Income from real-estate investments is recorded under other operating income, which rose 49% in the first quarter to $34.1 million from $22.8 million in the year-earlier period, CrossLand reported.

The bank's interest-rate spread -- the difference between what it pays for funds and what it takes in on loans -- narrowed to 1.77% on March 31, 1990, from 2.00% on March 31, 1989, but widened from 1.67% at the end of 1989.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, CrossLand common shares fell 12.5 cents to $1.75.
