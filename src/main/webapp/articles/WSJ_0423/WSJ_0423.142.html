Economy -- Tracking the Economy: GNP Annual Growth Rate for 1st Period Seen Doubling Forecasts, to About 2.5%

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 04/23/90

NEW YORK -- Now that it's over, the first quarter looks twice as strong as most folks thought it would at the start. The consensus view estimates an annual growth rate of about 2.5% in real gross national product for the quarter, up from the 1% growth outlook that prevailed in January, according to MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc.

The government takes its first stab at estimating GNP in a report Friday. Real GNP is the inflation-adjusted value of the nation's output of goods and services. The data also are expected to show that inflation heated up in the quarter. And more inflationary timber is expected from tomorrow's report on the first-quarter employment cost index. The week's only other indicator, March durable goods orders, is still thought to be on the rebound, after January's plunge.

Economists generally have been raising their GNP forecasts, because of the unpredictable weather, imperfect statistics, inventory swings, and some plain old miscalculations.

Some, at least, are good-humored about taking the blame. "I don't usually make a habit of comparing what I say at the beginning of the quarter to what I say at the end," Robert Dederick, chief economist of Northern Trust Co. in Chicago, said. "It just depresses me." As many of his counterparts did, Mr. Dederick in January predicted 1% growth. "I'm now saying 2% to 2.5%," he said.

"Business's fixed investment is higher than we thought. So is residential construction," Mr. Dederick said, noting two areas boosted by unseasonably mild January weather. He said better-than-expected exports helped the trade deficit and added strength to the quarter.

Asked about his change-of-heart estimate, Michael Moran, economist at Daiwa Securities America Inc., said: "Oh, boy. It's much stronger than I had at the beginning of the quarter." He now expects growth of 2.3% for the quarter, up from the 1% to 1.5% he forecast at the start of the period. GNP grew at an annual rate of 1.1% in the 1989 fourth quarter.

Two improved areas that surprised Mr. Moran the most in the first quarter: the trade-sector improvement and fixed-investment spending. Besides the benefits of mild weather, he noted that overall "demand was just a bit more robust than we thought was likely."

While the first quarter may have been stronger than they thought, many economists said the growth pace won't hold.

Donald Fine, chief economist at Chase Manhattan Bank, New York, raised his first-quarter estimate to 2.5% from around 1%. But he doesn't expect the pace to continue in the months ahead. "I think capital spending will slip off," he said. "Construction will definitely fall off. And we don't know if trade will get much better." On the other side, Mr. Fine said, auto output is likely to be stronger in the second quarter than in the first. "But that's quarterly auto catch-up," he said.

Many economists, including Robert Chandross at Lloyds Bank, are sticking to their full-year forecasts but are reshuffling the quarters. "Basically, there'll be a little more in the first quarter and a little less in the second," he said. "I'm still looking for {a} 2%" annual growth rate from the 1989 fourth quarter to this year's fourth quarter.

An inflation measure of the GNP report known as the implicit price deflator is expected to have risen at a 5.5% annual rate for the quarter. Allen Sinai, Boston Co.'s chief economist, expects to see "uncomfortably strong inflation" for the first quarter, with the implicit deflator as high as 6%.

The first-quarter employment cost index is expected to add to inflationary fears, with an increase of about 5.5% to 6% on a year-to-year basis. Economists pointed out that part of the rise in employment cost results from Social Security payment increases that took effect in January.

Expectations for tomorrow's report on March durable goods orders are typically scattered. Estimates range from a decline of 1.2% to an increase of 5.1%, with the median view a rise of 2%, according to MMS. Most economists expect some kind of increase in durable goods, as orders continue to rebound from January's sharp 10.5% plunge.
