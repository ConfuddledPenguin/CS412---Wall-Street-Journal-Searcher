Abreast of the Market: Analysts See a Stock Slide, but a Muted One

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 04/23/90

NEW YORK -- As last week's 55.85-point slide in the Dow Jones Industrial Average made clear, rising interest rates and weak earnings are troubling the stock market again. Question is, will these familiar worries cause a market correction like the 267-point, 9.5% dive the industrial average took in about one month during the first quarter?

A correction, yes, but not as bad, analysts predict. The reason is that investors now are more interested in some second-tier stocks of companies whose fortunes are tied to the economy's growth. Last week, for example, PPG Industries climbed 5.8% while the market sank, partly because the company raised its dividend. With such stocks in favor, the market's correction will be muted, they say.

There's little chance of avoiding one, though, analysts believe. The yield on the benchmark 30-year U.S. Treasury bond has climbed to just under 9% from about 8% since December. Higher interest rates both threaten to slow the economy and to make bonds more attractive investments than stocks.

Meanwhile, first-quarter earnings disappointments are in full parade: Of 734 companies that have reported quarterly earnings so far, out of more than 4,000 tracked by Zacks Investment Research Inc., 52% announced disappointing results and only 39% had better-than-expected results. At that rate, aggregate earnings of the 500 companies in Standard & Poor's index will be down 9% this year, says Ben Zacks, executive vice president of the research firm.

The next question is, will a correction most hurt the blue-chip stocks that have resisted this year's market decline, or the secondary and tertiary issues that so far have borne the brunt of investor selling?

The divergence between these two groups of stocks is glaring, and many analysts think this correction will somehow bring them back into line. As of Friday, the industrial average was down 2.1% this year, while the S&P 500 was off 5.2% and the Nasdaq composite index had lost 6.4%.

Another measure of the gap between the biggest stocks and the broad market is the increase in declining New York Stock Exchange issues relative to advancing ones. Subtracting Big Board losers from winners each day and charting the cumulative difference produces an advance-decline line that has been sagging since late August -- even as the Dow industrials kept climbing, eventually hitting a record high of 2810.15 on Jan 2.

Some investing buffs say this split reminds them of the stock market of 1972-1973. That divergence was resolved in the bear market of 1974, during which the industrials dropped a colossal 48%.

But a bear market isn't necessarily in the cards for 1990, says Alfred Goldman, director of market analysis at A.G. Edwards & Sons. He declares, "The current divergence between the Dow industrial average and the broader measures is a sign that the market is operating intelligently."

Mr. Goldman elaborates: "The outlook for the market is uncertain, and therefore we should have a selective market where you pay a premium for companies that have definable earnings prospects." Neither bull nor bear, the market is in a trading range between 2500 and 2850 in the industrial average, Mr. Goldman asserts. Investors will make money by picking stocks, not by catching a ride on the market's overall direction.

The Dow shot higher in the early 1970s because investors flocked to a small cadre of growth stocks. Then, as now, the economic outlook was uncertain, and growth stocks -- shares of companies with unique products and a proven record of earnings growth -- are again a favorite investment theme.

Among the "nifty 50" stocks of the early 1970s were Eastman Kodak, International Business Machines, Procter & Gamble, Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing, Schlumberger, Polaroid and Xerox, to name a few. All but Polaroid, Schlumberger and Xerox are in the industrial average.

The attraction of these big stocks 15 years ago is the same as now, namely enough size to weather economic storms and the benefit of multinational operations. "One reason people are buying big stocks today is that multinationals are doing relatively well," says Michael Cosgrove, principal of the Dallas-based economic research firm, The Econoclast. "Growth in Europe and Japan is faster than in the U.S."

What sent the stock market into the soup in 1974 was a series of inflation shocks. Oil prices skyrocketed; labor and food costs soared; the dollar tumbled, making imports more expensive. Investors owning big stocks were partially protected, but eventually even large companies couldn't withstand the poisonous combination of slow economic growth and high inflation. By late 1974, the industrial average closed at a low of 577.60. Xerox was trading around $50, down from about $150 a year or so before; Polaroid tumbled to under $8 from $74 in the nifty-50 days.

Inflation isn't a minor threat today, Mr. Cosgrove warns. Stubborn inflation pressures are being caused by factories running full tilt and by low unemployment. And that's another argument for owning big stocks today, he thinks. "Big companies can pass along price increases; a smaller company isn't able to do that," Mr. Cosgrove says.

With many investors seeking safety and earnings predictability, big stocks may continue to outperform the broad market for months, says Gail Dudack, market analyst at S.G. Warburg Securities. She considers the divergence between the big stocks and the broad market ominous, however. Sooner or later, she says, some of the Dow stocks will hit the skids.

"This market has hurt you, but the Dow doesn't reflect that," says Ms. Dudack. Secondary issues will continue to lag behind big stocks for a while, she believes. "Secondary stocks will fall harder but bounce better" when the eventual turnaround materializes, Ms. Dudack says.

Meanwhile, the higher the climb of consumer nondurable stocks -- such as Coca Cola Co., Procter & Gamble, and Philip Morris -- in the Dow average, the harder they'll fall later, Ms. Dudack predicts. Investors will dump these stocks, which, together with financial issues that are out of favor already because of rising interest rates, constitute about 45% of the industrial average. Investors will buy cyclical stocks, she says, but they make up only 39% of the average.

Friday's Market Activity

Stock prices tumbled Friday for the third consecutive session as nervousness about the banking sector added to the market's woes.

Bank issues fell across the board in the wake of Moody's Investors Service's decision to place the debt ratings of Citicorp, the largest U.S. bank holding company, under review for a possible downgrade.

Further weakness in the bond market and worries about the inflation outlook -- stemming from last week's disappointing news of a 0.5% rise in March consumer prices -- also kept stocks under pressure.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 15.99 to 2695.95 Friday. For the week, the average of 30 blue-chip stocks fell 55.85 points, or 2%. Broader averages took a harder fall for the week: Standard & Poor's 500-stock index lost 2.7%, while the New York Stock Exchange composite index fell 2.5%. The Dow Jones Equity Market index finished the week off 3.1%.

Late buying related to the expiration of April stock-index futures and options helped stocks close well above session lows, traders said. The industrial average was off as much as 39 points during the session. Declining issues swamped advancers on the New York Stock Exchange by 1079 to 401. Big Board volume reached its highest level since March 22 as 174,260,000 shares changed hands.

Citicorp fell 1/2 to 23 3/8 on 1.6 million shares. The possible Moody's downgrading would affect $31 billion of the company's securities, increase its short-term cost of funds and dent its prestige.

Among issues of other major banks, Chase Manhattan fell 5/8 to 27 on 1.3 million shares, Manufacturers Hanover dropped 1 3/8 to 32, Chemical Banking lost 5/8 to 23 1/4 and J.P. Morgan slid 3/8 to 33 5/8. First Chicago, which reported first-quarter earnings about 45% below the previous year's quarter, slipped 1/2 to 30 3/4.

MNC Financial dropped 3/4 to 16 3/8 after reporting that its first-quarter earnings were down 89% from a year earlier. Other regional bank issues that posted sharp losses included Bank of Boston, which went down 1 to 11 3/8 on 1.1 million shares; Shawmut National, down 3/8 to 13 3/8; Wells Fargo, down 1 1/4 to 68 3/4; Sovran Financial, down 1 1/8 to 30 1/2 and Citizens & Southern, down 1/2 to 23 7/8.

Insurance stocks also were weak, including Marsh & McLennan, which dropped 1 3/8 to 70 1/8 after its first-quarter earnings failed to meet analysts' expectations. Its net income for the period was about 7% higher than a year ago.

Elsewhere in the group, Cigna lost 5/8 to 48 3/8; Aetna Life & Casualty dropped 1 1/2 to 46 7/8; Lincoln National fell 1 3/4 to 52 3/4 and General Re dropped 2 to 83 1/8. American General eased 1/2 to 37 1/4; the company noted that insurance regulators in Texas and two other states have ruled that Torchmark's solicitation of proxies in its fight for board seats violates state law.

General Motors, a standout performer amid last week's market decline, added 1/8 to 46 3/4 on 2.1 million shares. GM has boosted prices for options, which could increase the cost of some of its cars and trucks by as much as $100, despite sluggish consumer demand.

Commonwealth Edison lost 1/2 to 31 5/8 on 1.4 million shares and traded as low as 30 3/8, a 52-week low, after reporting that its earnings dropped 38% in the first quarter from a year earlier. Recent analyst projections had called for stronger earnings.

Texas Utilities gained 3/8 to 33 1/2 as 1.6 million shares changed hands. The staff of the Texas Public Utility Commission recommended an annual rise of $422 million, or 9.78%, in the company's revenue.

Caterpillar added 5/8 to 60 1/2. Although the company's first-quarter net income dropped nearly 30% from a year earlier, the results were better than most analysts had expected.
