International: Nigeria Will Cut Interest Payments; Coup Bid Fails

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 04/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The Nigerian government, which yesterday put down a military coup attempt, told its bank creditors Friday that it will unilaterally cut interest payments on $5.4 billion of foreign debt from May 1. The payments move and the military revolt aren't directly related, but growing internal unrest and the decision to slash interest expenses come after recent drops of more than 10% in international prices for oil, which accounts for about 90% of Nigeria's export revenues.

The attempted coup, which began early yesterday, was apparently put down after several hours of fighting for control of the capital, Lagos. Troops loyal to the president, Gen. Ibrahim Babangida, fought with renegade soldiers seeking to break up Nigeria's federation of states and end Moslem domination.

The coup attempt only adds uncertainty to Nigeria's troubled financial situation. While bankers now know Nigeria intends to cut interest payments, they aren't sure what rate it is prepared to pay. The Nigerian central bank, which telexed the country's intentions to banks last week, didn't specify what rate it would use to determine "the reduced rate" at which interest payments will be made.

In talks in London last month with foreign creditor banks, Nigerian officials apparently requested that interest payments be set at 3% annually for 30 years on $5.4 billion of foreign bank debt. Bankers rejected this request. Nigeria pays about 10% on its foreign bank debt.

According to news agency reports, the debt immediately affected by the move is $2.4 billion in trade credits that require monthly interest payments. Nigeria also makes quarterly payments on $3 billion of medium-term commercial bank credit, which won't be affected until early July.

Although Nigeria hasn't specifically said so, many analysts say the recent fall in oil prices is an important reason for the country's interest-payment cut. The price drop "is a very important factor if Nigeria is going to pay its overseas debts," said Avinash Persaud, monetary economist at UBS Phillips & Drew in London

Nigeria's main crude-oil export, so-called Bonny Light, is selling for about $16.15 a barrel, down almost $2 from a month ago. Its current crude production is estimated around 1.8 million barrels a day, about 200,000 barrels a day higher than the quota set by the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries.

According to the World Bank, Nigeria had $29.42 billion of foreign debt at the end of 1988. Of this, $11.4 billion is owed to private creditors, with the balance owed to governments and multilateral agencies such as the World Bank. About half of the private debt is owed to banks, with the balance consisting of suppliers' credits.

European and Mideast banks are the biggest private lenders to Nigeria, while U.S. banks account for less than one-tenth of private bank lending.
