Nantucket Industries Expects to Report Profit Rose 7% in Fiscal Fourth Quarter

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 04/23/90

NEW YORK -- Nantucket Industries Inc. indicated that it expects to report fiscal fourth-quarter net income rose 7% on 4% higher sales. George J. Gold, chairman and chief executive officer, said the manufacturer of undergarments and marketer of socks attributes the improved operating results largely to its switch of emphasis toward higher-margin apparel it markets under licensed brand names and away from the Nantucket name.

On an operating basis, the fiscal fourth period "is usually a loss quarter, and we've got black figures for this one," Mr. Gold said.

For the fourth quarter, ended Feb. 28, Mr. Gold indicated in an interview that Nantucket will report net income grew to around $109,000, or five cents a share, from $101,948, or four cents a share, in the yearearlier period. Sales reached between $7.6 million and $7.7 million from $7.4 million, he estimated.

Fiscal 1990 net fell to about $1.6 million, or 66 cents a share, from $2.1 million, or 81 cents a share, in fiscal 1989. But excluding a $1.6 million special item a year earlier -- stemming from the June 1988 sale of the hosiery division to Jockey International Inc. -- earnings for the year nearly tripled, Mr. Gold said. Sales climbed 15% to $39.7 million from $34.5 million, he estimated.

For fiscal 1991, Nantucket Industries is looking to boost sales as much as 17% and net income by an even faster pace, Mr. Gold said, adding that profit margins are expected to grow to 5% from 4.6% in the just-ended fiscal year.

Nantucket also maintains lean operations, Mr. Gold said, noting that selling, general and administrative expenses remained at around 17% of sales for the third consecutive year.

Nantucket sells underwear under a number of names, including Brut and Brittania. Recently, the company added three names to the roster: Thermos for thermal underwear, Danskin for socks and McGregor for men's and boys' undergarments.

Prior to the emphasis on licensing, "we were purely a ladies-undergarment business," Mr. Gold said. The licensing "changed the whole complexion" of Nantucket's business: "It's the same selling operations, except men's goods at a higher margin."

The big names in the undergarment industry include Fruit of the Loom and Hanes. "We let them battle it out," Mr. Gold said, adding, "The big retailers aren't too happy" with the major manufacturers because price competition keeps profit margins thin. "We don't attempt to fight on price with them. We'd be a loser before we start."

Mr. Gold said: "We're more flexible than the big guys. . . . We move faster. That's a big advantage" as styles change.

Nantucket sold the hosiery division because of differences with its management and the high capital costs involved in the business, Mr. Gold said. Daniel Hess, an executive Mr. Gold hired for the hosiery operation, instead formed Nantucket's sock-marketing lines.

The sock operation won't require any capital investment because Nantucket, like much of the industry, will buy the socks it markets from contractors, Mr. Gold says. He estimates fiscal 1991 sales from the unit at $6 million to $7 million.

A little more than a year ago, Nantucket fought off a hostile $8.50-a-share hostile takeover bid from Integrated Resources Inc. Integrated "completely undervalued" Nantucket, Mr. Gold said. "As soon as we made the deal with Jockey," Integrated "wanted to invade our cash position." Nantucket bought back Integrated's stake "in the very low $6 {a share} range," he said.

In the past three years, Nantucket's shares have been as low as $2.125 and as high as $10.25; they are now about halfway between the two figures. Mr. Gold said that with profitable growth -- he hopes to reach $100 million of sales in the next four years, excluding acquisitions -- the stock price should follow on.

At the same time, he said, Nantucket is looking for an acquisition valued at $15 million to $30 million.

Long-term debt fell to $4 million in fiscal 1990 from $5 million a year earlier, and the company's ratio of current assets to current liabilities is 5 to 1, the executive said.
