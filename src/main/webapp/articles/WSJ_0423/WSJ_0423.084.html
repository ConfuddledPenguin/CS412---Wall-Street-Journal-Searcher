Torchmark Gets Texas Warning On Proxy Fight

  • Author: Michael Allen
  • Date: 04/23/90

The Texas State Board of Insurance staff informed Torchmark Corp. that its proxy fight against American General Corp. may violate state law, but Torchmark vowed to proceed, anyway. In a letter, the deputy commissioner of the board's legal and compliance section warned Torchmark that the board will consider the Birmingham, Ala., insurer to have obtained "control" of American General if it acquires at American General's annual meeting on May 2, proxies representing 10% or more of the power to vote.

Under Texas state law, a company must obtain permission from the insurance commissioner before acquiring control of a Texas insurance company. Torchmark hasn't sought such permission.

Torchmark, which launched its proxy battle after dropping an unsolicited $50-a-share, or $6.3 billion, offer for its much-bigger rival, had argued that the vote solicitation wouldn't lead to control because it was merely attempting to place five "independent" directors on American General's 15-member board.

Noting that it has the right to appeal the decision to the Texas insurance commissioner under two sections of state law, Torchmark said it intends to pursue, if necessary, one of those alternatives "at the appropriate time."

"We believe that it is wrong for American General to spend time and money in an attempt to deny their shareholders the opportunity to vote for the independent nominees," Torchmark said. "We, however, fully intend to continue with our proxy solicitation."

The Houston insurer, meanwhile, criticized Torchmark for failing to clear its nomination with state regulators, who are charged with protecting the interests of policyholders. "We believe that the process that the state regulators are pursuing in order to determine whether to grant approval will also provide both regulators and policyholders an opportunity to understand Torchmark's intentions," American General said.

The Texas decision represents the latest round in a bruising regulatory fight being waged in almost a dozen states where American General has subsidiaries. So far, regulators in California and Virginia have declined to block the proxy solicitation, while authorities in Tennessee and New York have warned Torchmark that they consider the solicitation an attempt to obtain control of American General without obtaining required approvals.

The two insurance companies have also battled in federal court. A U.S. district judge in Houston initially blocked Torchmark from nominating its five directors, but the decision was overturned by a federal appeals court.
