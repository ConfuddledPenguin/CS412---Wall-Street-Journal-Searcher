Advertising: Seagram Taking `Light' Whiskey To All of U.S.

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 04/23/90

Hard liquor is going soft. Seagram Co. is beginning a national rollout of a "light" whiskey product that has been in test markets since last fall.

Mount Royal Light, the first effort by a major beverage company to transfer the "light" concept to hard liquor, has one-third less alcohol and one-third fewer calories than standard 80-proof whiskey. The 54-proof product, is classified as a specialty liquor by federal regulators, but Seagram has been advertising it as a whiskey product.

While three of the top-10-selling beer brands are light beers, some industry experts question whether the concept makes much sense for hard liquor. Since most liquor is served with water or mixers, consumers can easily control on their own how much alcohol they get.

"If you want less alcohol, you just add a little more mixer," says Tom Pirko, president of Bev-Mark Inc., a Los Angeles beverage consulting firm. "This is a concept that just doesn't make a lot of sense. Nobody's going to get the Nobel Prize for having concocted `light' spirits."

Mr. Pirko says also playing against Seagram is the fact that "light" claims in general have lost their punch. The American public, he says, has become skeptical as marketers slapped the label on a multitude of products ranging from potato chips to cupcakes. "Seagram is entering the 1990s playing with a 1980s marketing gimmick," he says.

Seagram, however, is so confident that the product will be a success that it is expanding distribution. It recently received approval to sell Mount Royal Light in Wisconsin, Colorado and Tennessee, and the company plans to enter other states shortly. The light whiskey has been sold in Massachusetts and upstate New York since last September. A 750-milliliter bottle of Mount Royal Light sells for about $8.

"We are obviously delighted with the response that Mount Royal Light has had," a Seagram spokesman says. "It's pretty clear to us that this is a product that has a place" in the alcohol market.

Seagram insists the product isn't simply a watered-down version of a traditional whiskey. A special production process, the company says, gives Mount Royal Light the flavor and full-bodied style of a standard whiskey.

Seagram says the product was well received in test markets by drinkers of Canadian and American whiskeys, who are principally men. While Mount Royal Light is aimed at traditional whiskey drinkers, Seagram says it also attracted more younger drinkers and women than traditional whiskey products.

But Seagram admits it has a serious marketing challenge on its hands. The spokesman says the ad campaign for the product, developed by WPP Group agency Ogilvy & Mather, goes to great lengths to underscore that the full flavor is what makes the product different. Ads tout the product as "history in the making."

"It's very difficult in the beverage alcohol business to introduce a new product, especially one that takes a bit of doing to communicate its advantages," the Seagram spokesman says.

WPP Stock Falls

WPP Group's shares tumbled sharply Friday, with its American depositary receipts falling $1.75 to $19.375 in national over-the-counter trading.

Analysts attributed the drop partly to fallout from problems at FKB Group, a similarly structured British advertising and communications company, whose shares Friday were halted from trading in London because of a credit crunch. FKB, like WPP, bought a number of companies on an earn-out basis, in which it makes payments based on earnings over a number of years. FKB apparently is facing problems meeting the payments. Julie Mackee, chief financial officer of the company's U.S. operations, said FKB is "in the process of negotiating a refinancing package with our bankers" and trading "won't resume until we're in a position to announce a detailed refinancing proposal."

WPP is far larger than FKB, and analysts said it isn't facing troubles like those at FKB. But because of well-publicized problems caused by softness in the ad business, particularly at Saatchi & Saatchi Co., "anything in that sector that implies bad news really seems to affect everyone else" disproportionately, Ms. Mackee noted. WPP executives couldn't be reached for comment.

Ad Notes. . . .

AD SPENDING: Restaurants and fast-food chains increased TV ad spending 11% in 1989 over the year before, to $1.5 billion, according to the Television Bureau of Advertising. McDonald's was the largest spender, at $412 million, up 7% from 1988; Burger King came in second, spending $161.6 million, up 8%; and Kentucky Fried Chicken, the third-largest TV advertiser, spent $115.7 million, up 17% from 1988.

WHO'S NEWS: Bill Croasdale and Jerry Bonsaing were named executive vice presidents at Backer Spielvogel Bates, a unit of Saatchi & Saatchi. . . . Randall D. Schroeder was named executive vice president and management director of media at Saatchi's Campbell-Mithun-Esty, Detroit.

KOREY KAY: American Safety Razor Co., Staunton, Va., named Korey, Kay & Partners, New York, to handle its $4 million ad account. Korey Kay will create ads for American Safety brands Personna, Flicker and LegMate.

TEAM ONE: Sapporo U.S.A., Los Angeles, awarded its $1 million draft beer account to Saatchi & Saatchi's Team One Advertising, El Segundo, Calif.
