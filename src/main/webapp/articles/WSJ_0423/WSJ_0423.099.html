Loss Disclosed By Far West Financial for '89 --- Main Thrift Unit Falls Short Of Capital Requirements And Risks Being Seized

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 04/23/90

Far West Financial Corp. joined the parade of big, once-staid California savings and loans that have run up huge losses and endangered their own survival by diversifying out of California residential real estate. Far West, a thrift holding company with assets totaling $4.35 billion, disclosed a 1989 net loss of $46.1 million, or about $5.90 a share. In 1988, Far West had a profit of $8.6 million, or $1.09 a share.

The 1989 results, including an indicated net loss of $39.5 million, or $5.05 a share, for the quarter ended Dec. 31, leave the company's main Far West Savings & Loan Association unit in violation of at least two of the government's three thrift capital requirements. Now, the company must raise at least $117 million in capital or risk being seized by federal regulators.

Other crippled California thrifts include Imperial Savings of San Diego and Mercury Savings of Huntington Beach, both of which failed recently. Valley Federal Savings of Van Nuys, Santa Barbara Savings, and Columbia Savings of Beverly Hills are currently insolvent and seeking to develop survival plans against long odds. And Southwest loans have badly hurt Great American Bank, a giant San Diego thrift, which now must raise more than $300 million of capital to stave off regulators.

Far West attributed the 1989 loss to a $105.9 million charge to write down junk-bond holdings to market prices, a requirement stemming from last year's thrift bailout law, as well as $45.1 million of reserves against problem loans. Far West has one of the largest junk-bond portfolios in the thrift industry, reflecting in part its past close ties to Michael Milken and Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. Officials of Far West, based in Los Angeles, couldn't be reached for comment.

The plummeting junk-bond market, a provision of the bailout law that requires thrifts to eventually divest their junk bonds, and the interim "mark-to-market" accounting requirement have devastated junk bond laden thrifts as a group. Seven of the top 15 thrift holders of such bonds have failed in recent months, and the biggest, Columbia Savings, although still in business, is expected to be taken over by regulators.

Far West made junk-bond bets as long as it could, according to government data collected by Miami thrift analyst Kenneth Thomas. The data show that Far West boosted its junk-bond holdings by $170 million in the 40 days preceding Congressional adoption of the junk-bond ban last August, leaving it with a total of $667 million in such bonds at Sept. 30. More recent data, according to Mr. Thomas, indicate that the company turned around and sold $274 million in the fourth quarter and held $393 million in junk at year end. Other thrifts that made similar purchases just before the ban have complained that Congress blindsided them.

As precarious as the year-end results appear, analysts said that, based on further erosion in the junk-bond market in January and February, Far West may have incurred additional large losses in the first quarter. Mr. Thomas, for one, estimated that Far West in the first quarter incurred junk-bond losses equal to another 10% of its portfolio, or roughly $39 million. That amount of loss would be enough to sink the company's key tangible capital to about half of the federal minimum requirement. The company's "core" and "risk-based" capital already violate federal standards.

In a news release, Far West indicated that a plan to solve its capital problems is being revised at the request of regulators.

Far West does have one thing going for it that many other troubled thrifts don't these days -- a deep pocket. Canada's Belzberg family is the company's controlling shareholder; William Belzberg is its chairman. The family's purchase of stock in the thrift in the 1970s was one of its first investments south of the border. But whether the family is willing to commit substantial further funds at this time isn't clear.

"It will not look good if they walk away," said Allan Bortel, a San Francisco thrift analyst. "On the other hand, it is going to be a very poor return on investment if they do heal it without government help."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Far West stock closed at $2.50 a share, down 12.5 cents.
