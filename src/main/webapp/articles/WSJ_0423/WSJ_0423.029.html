Merrill Shareholders Are a Skeptical Lot About Cost-Cutting --- Luxury of Meeting Site Makes Austerity Plan Hard Sell; California Pirates Picket

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 04/23/90

PLAINSBORO, N.J. -- In the country-club atmosphere of its sprawling conference center here, Merrill Lynch & Co. tried to convince 223 skeptical stockholders that it's serious about cutting costs. Merrill annual meetings are usually fairly routine. But the nation's biggest brokerage firm on Friday held what longtime employees called one of its most eventful meetings in years.

A swarm of Californians in pirate garb picketed the event, complaining about Merrill's treatment of residents of a mobile home park it owns in Laguna Beach. Inside, stockholders peppered Merrill management about the luxury of their surroundings and the mediocrity of Merrill's earnings. With all the questions, the firm's president and chairman decided to save time by skipping their prepared speeches.

Merrill is coming off a fourth quarter in which it took a record $470 million pretax charge to pay for a massive retrenchment plan including at least 3,000 layoffs. For 1989, the firm recorded a net loss of $213.4 million. Its stock is down 11.4% this year.

First, the serious business: Merrill officials said that because of its ongoing retrenchment, it plans an absolute reduction in fixed expenses in its 1990 budget of 3% below 1989, "and we're working to achieve an even greater reduction."

Merrill projected savings of more than $150 million in expenses in 1990, and "continuing" savings after that. That forecast was more specific than previous projections that annual savings would be more than $100 million; however, it's lower than the expense-reduction projection of nearly $200 million that Merrill had talked about shortly after it took its fourth-quarter charge.

"We believe that this represents a substantial return against the charge," said Merrill President Daniel P. Tully. Analysts had sniped about Merrill's logic in taking a $470 million charge to garner only $100 million in savings.

Still, it was a tough sell convincing shareholders that Merrill, with its free-spending reputation, will do a good job of penny-pinching.

An investor opined that the manicured Plainsboro conference center was a "nice place to play golf in." The defensive Merrill officials said the facility saves them money by allowing them to train their swarms of brokers outside New York City. William A. Schreyer, Merrill chairman, called it "one of our superior decisions as far as real-estate strategy."

The pirates, from Treasure Island Mobile Home Park, were complaining about a Merrill partnership's plan to relocate them and to develop condominiums on their island. Mr. Schreyer seemed rushed at the meeting, but said he wasn't nervous about the pirates. "I just got through `Barbarians at the Gate,' and now there are pirates at the gate," he joked. John L. Steffens, head of Merrill's consumer markets sector, said "some sort of agreement" with the mobile-home residents should be worked out soon.
