What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/23/90

MICHAEL MILKEN AGREED to plead guilty to six felony counts and to pay $600 million in fines and restitution, the largest criminal settlement ever. Friday's decision by Drexel's former junk-bond chief came just before a grand jury was to indict him on insider trading, bribery and other charges. It's unclear how much Milken will cooperate with federal investigators in implicating others. ---

Far West Financial became the latest big California thrift to run into financial problems. The S&L posted a $46.1 million loss for 1989 due to writedowns of its junk bonds. Far West now must raise at least $117 million in capital or risk a federal takeover.

---

Avon's accord to sell its 60% stake in Avon Japan to a Tokyo company for $450 million has apparently collapsed. The failed sale was viewed as a setback for Avon management, which was going to use the cash to help pay down its $695.9 million debt.

---

First Chicago's profit tumbled 45% in the first quarter, as expected, mostly due to bad real-estate loans. The bank denied it improperly told analysts about the problems before broadly disclosing the information.

---

The slumping bond market is bracing for more blows that are likely to drive down prices and boost yields further over the next few weeks. Stocks also are likely to weaken.

Fed Vice Chairman Manuel Johnson said he sees signs of a pickup in the economy, though inflation remains a threat. His comments suggest the Fed may favor higher rates.

---

Deficit-reduction talks between the White House and Congress are "highly likely" to begin within a month, Budget Director Darman said. Obstacles to an accord are likely to be defense cuts, taxes and curbing growth in entitlement programs.

---

Norton's stock rose $2 Friday, to $77.50, after the Massachusetts abrasives maker said an unidentified bidder had expressed interest in topping BTR's offer of $75 a share, or $1.64 billion. BTR is waging a proxy fight to force Norton to accept the bid.

---

Merck proposed to make its prescription drugs available at lower prices to Medicaid patients. But it isn't clear that the plan will deflect federal and state pressure to force the drug industry to bring down prices.

---

CrossLand Savings posted a $136.5 million quarterly loss and a doubling of problem assets, worsening its already severe shortage of capital.

---

GTE agreed to buy cellular phone operations in the Southeast U.S. from Providence Journal for $710 million, tightening the telephone industry's grip on cellular communications.

---

McDonnell Douglas posted a 98% drop in quarterly earnings, while operating results rose meagerly.

---

Toronto's Reichmann family proposed a reshuffling of energy assets that would create Canada's biggest domestically owned oil producer. It also would establish Canada's main oil pipeline as a separate company.

---

Nigeria told bank creditors it will unilaterally cut interest payments on $5.4 billion of foreign debt from May 1. The country, an OPEC member, has been hurt by the slide in oil prices.
