Bush Moves Toward Economic Sanctions In Bid to Cut Soviet Pressure on Lithuania

  • Author: Michel McQueen
  • Date: 04/23/90

ISLAMORADA, Fla. -- The Bush administration is moving to curtail an array of planned investment and trade assistance to the Soviet Union in the wake of Moscow's efforts to crush the Lithuanian independence drive. Although the administration hasn't specified what steps it is prepared to take, officials traveling with President Bush here over the weekend said the likely course will be to backtrack on an extensive list of trade and economic concessions urgently sought by Moscow to repair the faltering Soviet economy.

For the U.S., such minimal punitive measures could help assuage mounting pressure, especially from U.S. conservatives, for a harder line against Moscow, while preserving the momentum toward arms-control agreements important to both sides. The administration is eager to stay on track with wide-ranging arms-control talks scheduled for May, as well as to sign a conventional-forces reduction agreement later in the year.

Mr. Bush had proposed several trade measures in his meeting with Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev last December, including extensive technical assistance and an end to certain legal restrictions on U.S.-Soviet trade, in an effort to help the Soviet leader calm domestic discontent and show concrete results from his aggressive moves on arms control.

While increased trade with the Soviets would have little initial impact on the U.S. economy, officials said the measures are eagerly sought by Moscow after five years of economic restructuring has failed to improve the average Soviet's standard of living.

The administration signaled its decision Friday after days of noncommittal reaction to Moscow's campaign of intimidation against the Baltic republic, a campaign that included the cutting off of oil and gas supplies last week, the takeover of a printing plant and reports of a halt of some food supplies.

After initially saying the White House would have no comment on Lithuania, administration spokeman Marlin Fitzwater issued a statement at midday Friday saying the president is "increasingly concerned by news reports of an economic crackdown" in the republic. "Although our information is sketchy, we are disturbed that this signals another unfortunate step in the pressure being brought to bear on Lithuania by the Soviet Union."

Mr. Fitzwater said the president has stepped up consultations with the allies and expects to conclude them early this week, when he will advise Congress of his intentions. An administration spokesman said later that the administration began sending cables to the allies as early as Wednesday.

Mr. Fitzwater also said that Mr. Bush wasn't planning to coordinate a multilateral response to the crackdown, but was merely advising the allies of his intentions. And he was careful to reiterate that the U.S. wasn't planning to take any military action in the region.

Among the likely courses of action would be to withhold U.S. support for observer status for the Soviet Union at the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, the West's primary trade-regulatory body. Mr. Bush could also withhold cooperation on a broad agreement to liberalize trade, which both sides had hoped to sign by the time the two leaders meet for an arms-control summit May 30.
