Fed Aide Sees Signs of Pickup In the Economy --- Vice Chairman Says Threat Of Inflation Continues Both in U.S. and Abroad

  • Author: David Wessel and Peter Truell
  • Date: 04/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The vice chairman of the Federal Reserve Board, Manuel Johnson, said he see signs that economic activity is "beginning to pick back up." "There are not any signs -- other than this hysteria about the credit crunch -- of weakness," Mr. Johnson said in an interview. At the same time, he said, inflation remains a threat both in the U.S. and abroad. He termed "disappointing" last week's report that consumer prices shot up 0.5% in March. And he said that easier credit conditions in Western Europe outside Germany may stimulate demand for U.S. exports, possibly resulting in inflationary pressure from overseas.

He gave no clue on the crucial question of timing, noting that the warm weather earlier this year makes it difficult to get an accurate reading of the underlying pace of the economy and that the consumer price report may have been distorted by a surge in apparel prices. "This is a period to be cautious," he said.

Mr. Johnson's views are important because he is a crucial swing vote in Fed decisions at the moment. The financial market indicators that Mr. Johnson and others watch intensively don't appear to signal any urgency. Long-term interest rates in the bond market are up, but the dollar is relatively stable when viewed against currencies of all U.S. trading partners -- not just Japan -- and commodity prices are mixed.

Mr. Johnson also said he and other Fed officials don't object to international intervention in currency markets to support the yen so long as such efforts don't turn into a world-wide campaign against the dollar.

After gradually pushing down short-term interest rates for six months, the Fed paused at the end of December and has been resisting pressure from the administration and others to lower them further. Fed officials say the behavior of the economy in the past four months vindicates their stance. The economy has bounced back from its fourth-quarter languor. Inflation hasn't abated. Bond yields, seen by some as a measure of inflation expectations, have risen.

In a separate interview last week, Robert Parry, president of the San Francisco Federal Reserve Bank, said the current pace of the U.S. economy is too rapid to make much dent in the inflation rate. He put the annual growth pace at slightly above 2%, after inflation.

Although Mr. Parry expects prices to rise more slowly in the coming months than they did in the first quarter, he still predicts inflation will be higher this year than last year. "That's uncomfortable and unacceptable," he said. But Mr. Parry, who is among the Fed's staunchest foes of inflation, added, "At this point, I am not willing to say we ought to aim for a recession to produce faster adjustment" in the inflation rate.

In recent months, the Fed and the Treasury have clashed over the Treasury's eagerness to intervene in currency markets to push down the dollar. But Mr. Johnson said the Fed is prepared to support intervention to support the weak yen provided other countries use their own currencies -- not just the dollar -- to buy yen. "I've never objected to intervention to support the yen, but that has been translated into general dollar sales," he stated.

At the recent meeting of finance ministers and central bankers from the seven largest industrial countries, West Germany reportedly resisted Japanese entreaties that it spend marks to support the yen. As a result, the U.S. was unwilling to agree to stepped-up intervention.

Mr. Johnson emphasized the importance of coordinating economic policies internationally, and he expressed reservations about the current state of the coordination process. "I worry that it could flounder; I think it needs some energy," he said. The formal coordination mechanism is the Group of Seven, which includes finance ministers and central bankers from the U.S., Japan, West Germany, France, Britain, Canada and Italy.
