Marketing & Media: Sears Will End Furniture Departments In Most Stores, Open Separate Outlets

  • Author: Francine Schwadel
  • Date: 04/23/90

CHICAGO -- Sears, Roebuck & Co. plans to yank furniture out of most of its department stores and open more than 200 free-standing furniture outlets in an effort to revive its unprofitable business. "We will be pulling out of the vast majority" of Sears stores over the next several years, said Jerry A. Hand, the company's vice president of home fashions. The company will offer an expanded furniture selection in the remaining Sears stores, he added.

The nation's largest retailer is counting on the new format, which features a wider selection of mostly name-brand goods at higher prices than Sears charges for its traditional private-label lineup, to revive its stagnant, $1 billion-a-year furniture business. Sears currently commands the leading share of the fragmented U.S. furniture business -- roughly 4% of the $28 billion market. But Mr. Hand said Sears sells furniture through too many small departments to compete effectively.

By moving furniture out of its big stores, Sears also will free up space to expand such other departments as women's, men's and children's clothing, and tools and lawn equipment. Sears is attempting to broaden its appeal to shoppers and improve its financial results with more eye-catching displays and more name-brand goods that it is selling alongside its house brands.

But shipping furniture off to free-standing stores is risky because the stores can no longer count on shoppers to wander in from other Sears departments. "In a free-standing store, you have to be able to drive your own traffic with a good merchandise assortment and good promotion to get people to come to your store," said Norman McMillan, a retail consultant in Chicago. "It isn't automatic anymore."

Mr. Hand said results of a prototype Homelife store that opened in Madison, Wis., last September indicate the new free-standing format can operate profitably. "We're currently doubling our market share," he said.

The new Homelife stores range in size from 20,000 to 40,000 square feet, or as much as five times as large as the typical, cluttered Sears furniture department. That means Sears now has enough space to arrange its living room, dining room and bedroom items in attractive room settings, separated by fake walls and arches that resemble a decorator showroom. Mr. Hand hopes the displays will spur multiple purchases.

The goods themselves are more fashionable too. Much of the new merchandise is contemporary or traditional in style, rather than the "country" look that dominates Sears furniture departments today.

The new national brands -- including American Drew, Barcalounger, Broyhill, Sauder and Schnadig -- also are priced 20% to 25% higher than the private-label Sears items.

"The taste level of our customer and the non-Sears customer is higher than what we've been selling," Mr. Hand said. "The customer wants to buy better goods."
