OTC Focus: Nasdaq Launches Advertising Blitz To Boost Its Image

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 04/23/90

NEW YORK -- Nasdaq is ready for prime time. The National Association of Securities Dealers is launching an advertising blitz, seeking to give its Nasdaq automated stock-quote system the same cachet as the stock exchanges that compete with it for listings. The ads are running in national business magazines and on television in the New York area.

Without exactly saying so, the NASD also wants to establish a separate identity for itself now that the term "over-the-counter" has taken on seamy overtones in many investors' minds because of pennystock frauds.

"Nasdaq is a stock market, separate from the OTC," says Brian Holland who, as senior vice president of marketing for NASD, is coordinating the new ad campaign.

"They're really trying to make the Nasdaq stock market distinct from OTC," says Robert Flaherty, editor of the OTC Review, a magazine that covers the OTC market. Mr. Flaherty was recently turned down when he sought permission to change the name of the magazine to Nasdaq Review. "I think they're afraid the name Nasdaq will get sullied," he said, referring to the board that controls the Nasdaq market and its copyrighted name.

Companies must meet minimum networth and other requirements to be eligible for Nasdaq trading. Trading of their shares is monitored through a surveillance system designed to detect fraud. No such surveillance system is available for non-Nasdaq stocks, which have involved more than a few cases of fraud. Many non-Nasdaq stocks are known as "pink sheets" stocks because their prices are listed on pink paper by the National Quotation Bureau.

New Yorkers watching the late news tonight will be among the first to see the latest of this month's ads by the NASD, the self-styled self-regulatory body for the OTC market. In a 30-second commercial, they will see slow-motion images of frenzied, messy trading on a stock exchange as a voice asks: "Is there a rational alternative to the traditional trading floor?" Naturally, there is: "Nasdaq. The stock market for the next 100 years."

The ad budget is "several million" dollars. And the target audience? "MEPS," says Mr. Holland, referring to "managers, executives and professionals," the kind of people who watch the news, business shows and golf tournaments. For example, New York area viewers of the Masters golf tournament saw a Nasdaq ad April 8.

One goal of Nasdaq, the nation's second largest securities market, is to make itself as familiar to corporate America and the investing public as the New York Stock Exchange. Nasdaq is always striving to gain new companies and to keep its larger and higher-quality OTC companies from defecting to the New York and American exchanges.

First, however, the public has to know what Nasdaq is. The 50-year-old NASD both operates and polices the market for the 4,264 companies that meet its standards. Yet Nasdaq is far from a household word. It stands for National Association of Securities Dealers Automated Quotations system.

"It's not so much that the image is bad, it's just there's no image at all," says Ron Berger, the advertising executive hired to start a television and magazine ad campaign earlier this month by the NASD.

In surveys taken before the current ad campaign, a number of people didn't realize that Apple Computer, MCI Communications and Intel trade on Nasdaq. A major NASD goal "is to get chief executives to keep their listing on Nasdaq and gain a larger share of new listings," says Mr. Berger, a partner with Messner Vetere Berger Carey Schmetterer, a New York ad agency.

On Nasdaq, brokers compete to get the best share prices for their clients using telephones and an integrated system of computer screens -- the 19-year-old Nasdaq network -- rather than competing on an exchange floor. Over-the-counter stocks were generally traded through pink sheets until 1971, when computerized trading became available through Nasdaq. Thousands of companies are still confined to the pink sheets, where their prices and the brokerage companies that make a market in the stocks are printed each trading day but aren't available during intraday trading.

Although Nasdaq may not be the sexiest name to hit TV screens, it has caught on overseas. Next year Japan will initiate Jasdaq (Japan Securities Dealers Automated Quotations system), an automated version of that country's over-the-counter market that is modeled after the American system. And while the London Stock Exchange didn't imitate the name, in 1986 it converted to a trading system also modeled after Nasdaq.

Two of Nasdaq's three television ads focus on the systems adopted by the Japanese and British and suggest that the Nasdaq system will be the standard of the future.

But some skeptics say that if NASD wants to erase any negative connotations of the OTC market from the public's mind, it must eventually encourage every trading department, every mutual fund and every publication that now uses the term OTC to also change their names.

"OTC is in common use," says John L. Watson III, president of the Security Traders Association. "It will be a long time before the word disappears, even if you work at it."

Friday's Market Activity

The over-the-counter market sank again Friday, ending the week 2.4% below where it started.

The Nasdaq Composite Index slid 3.21 Friday, or 0.75%, to 425.79. OTC advancers trailed decliners, 764 to 1,138, on 150.3 million shares, up from 145.6 million.

Richard H. Holden, a principal of Alex. Brown, said volatility in the over-the-counter market increased in recent weeks, leaving stocks vulnerable to disappointing news. Mr. Holden added that he expects the mood to persist next week.

Granite Construction, a road-building company, got a warm reception from Wall Street. The company went public in a 2.5 million-share initial public offering priced at 17 and closed at 18. Volume was more than 2.1 million shares.

Paul Meuller added 3 to 29 after reporting first-quarter earnings, while SynOptics Communications added 2 1/2 to 39 1/8 on the strength of its earnings report.

Nesb fell 1/2 to 2 5/8. The troubled bank holding company, based in New London, Conn., reported that it had a loss of $1.80 a share after a provision for loan losses in the latest first quarter. Nesb also said it is in technical default of covenants on $50 million of long-term debt.

Stocks that fell on earnings reports were Chambers Development Class A shares, off 1 1/4 to 39, Cadence Design Systems, down 5/8 to 23 3/4, and Oregon Metallurgical, off 1 1/2 to 18.
