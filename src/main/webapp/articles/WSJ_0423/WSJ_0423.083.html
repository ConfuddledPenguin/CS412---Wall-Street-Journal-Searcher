Insurers Are Cutting Back or Halting Sales of Personal Insurance Policies

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/23/90

Stricter state regulation, heightened competition and mounting expenses have turned the once-mediocre returns from selling personal insurance, such as for autos and homes, into a flood of red ink for many insurers. Insurance companies are reassessing their strategies for this sector of the market. Some are deciding it's best to cut back, especially on auto insurance, which many insurers see as their biggest moneyloser. Other companies are deserting the personal insurance market entirely.

On Friday, Xerox Corp.'s Crum & Forster Inc. unit became the latest company to drop out. It announced that it was terminating its personal insurance business nationwide. Robert Vairo, Crum & Forster's president and chief executive officer, said the decision was based on market conditions and a belief that the business wasn't going to be profitable any time soon.

Last year, Crum & Forster paid out $1.29 in claims and expenses for every dollar of premium collected in all its personal lines. That's far worse than the industry average. Three-quarters of Crum & Forster's personal insurance business was done in such states as California, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Florida, Texas and Connecticut, where insurance regulation is in the midst of being revamped.

"The public and the regulators can't ask us to pay for insurance that has been mandated by the state and not be able to recover those costs and make a reasonable profit," Mr. Vairo said.

In recent months, other insurers have taken action.

Aetna Life & Casualty Co. plans to cut back its auto insurance business 15% to 20% in states where it's unprofitable. It will trim agents' auto insurance commissions to 10% from 15% starting in July, and it has let go some agents in California. For now, the Hartford, Conn., insurer said its cutbacks are limited to individual auto insurance.

Last month, Philadelphia-based Cigna Corp. told agents it will no longer accept new auto and personal excess liability policies after May 1 and asked agents to place renewals with other carriers.

New Hampshire Insurance Co., a unit of New York-based American International Group Inc., has accelerated its withdrawal from the personal lines market and expects to be out almost entirely by year end.

Travelers Corp., also based in Hartford, no longer sells auto insurance in California, where it had a significant share of the market, and it's selectively cutting back on its personal lines business in other states. The company is also streamlining its entire personal insurance operations, eliminating 500 to 600 jobs in over 90 field offices throughout the country.

Analysts believe more companies will take similar steps. For consumers, such moves mean they will find fewer companies willing to insure their cars or homes, especially in states where insurance reforms have been passed or where new regulation is on the ballot.

William Yankus at Conning & Co., a research firm, believes the beginning of a major structural change is taking place in the personal insurance market. First, the standard companies drop out of the market. Then, the substandard insurers expand their business.

These companies are willing to insure most motorists, regardless of their driving records, but they charge dearly for the service. Regulators could be forced to provide alternatives, such as state-run pools. Mr. Yankus believes it could be 10 to 15 years before the market corrects as tort reform and good no-fault laws come into place.

Besides what insurers consider an unfavorable regulatory environment, the very nature of the personal insurance business leaves little room for fat profit margins. Insurers are offering generally homogenous products in a highly competitive market. The direct writers, insurers such as State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co., Nationwide Mutual Insurance Co. and B.A.T Industries PLC's Farmers Group Inc. unit, which employ their own platoons of agents, have lower commission costs and thus have an edge over the agency companies, such as Aetna, Cigna and Travelers, which rely on independent agents. The agency companies have been slowly losing market share to the direct writers in the last decade.

Although Crum & Forster's action was spurred by market forces, analysts believe a recent management change also might have played a role in the company's move.

Alan Henderson of Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. believes Stuart B. Ross, the newly appointed head of Xerox's financial-services group, has been asked by top management to improve the cash flow and profitability of his unit. Dropping some unprofitable insurance lines and shrinking the entire financial-services operations are likely moves, Mr. Henderson said. But despite recent speculation, he doesn't believe Stamford, Conn.-based Xerox will sell its financial-services businesses.
