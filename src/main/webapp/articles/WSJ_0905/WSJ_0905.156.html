Tuesday's Markets: Oil Surges After U.N. Talks Falter --- Bonds Fall, Stocks End Mixed in Year's Most Sleepy Session

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/05/90

NEW YORK -- With oil prices surging and foreign stock markets slumping, U.S. bonds fell and stocks were mixed. The October futures contract for crude oil jumped $1.80 a barrel to close at $29.12. Oil prices already were rising before U.S. financial markets opened, largely due to a lack of progress in the United Nations effort to mediate a way out of the Mideast quagmire.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average finished just 0.99 point lower to close at 2613.37, though it had been down 30 points in early trading. Broader market indexes closed slightly higher: Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, for example, rose 0.53 to 323.09, and the New York Stock Exchange and Nasdaq composite indexes also eked out small gains.

But activity was light, as it was in all the financial markets. Big Board volume was even slower than Friday's, which briefly held the distinction of the year's dullest session. Yesterday's turnover of 92.9 million shares was the snooziest since the day after last Christmas.

Long-term U.S. Treasury bonds fell nearly 3/8 point, or almost $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount; earlier, they were down half a point. The dollar ended mixed, with traders finding little in the news to guide them.

John Candela, managing director of fixed-income investing at Ladenburg Thalmann, said the bond market's thin trading made it difficult to read much into the decline. Further muddying the picture is the large number of potentially market-moving events that are drawing near: Friday's release of U.S. employment data; Congress returning to deal with the ballooning budget deficit; the planned superpower summit in Finland Sunday; and uncertainties about the direction of the Mideast crisis.

The lack of resolutions to any of these issues "keeps things on edge," Mr. Candela said. "People will remain frozen until we get some direction."

Referring to the stock market, Peter Canelo, chief strategist at Bear Stearns, said, "The fact that we had this kind of light volume with many people back from vacation was probably the most significant development" in the market yesterday. "It reveals a real paralysis." And if the Mideast stalemate drags on for months and oil-price moves remain unpredictable, investors may well remain paralyzed, he said: "When investors know they don't know, investors tend not to act."

The currency markets also were quiet, with prices fluctuating narrowly. Robert White, manager of corporate foreign-exchange trading at First Interstate Bank in Los Angeles, said traders were surprised a bit by the dollar's rise early in the day despite then-slumping stock and bond prices. He speculated that investors are nervous about indications that the Soviet Union may be having a tougher time economically than had previously been thought.

"If people will riot over cigarettes, they'll surely be upset about bread shortages," he said, referring to news reports of such shortages now emerging. "That, coupled with the problems in Iraq, problems in German reunification, labor unrest and wage negotiations for immigrant workers in Switzerland -- just a lot of European stories around that aren't very encouraging -- supports a lessening of bearish sentiment about the dollar."

In major market action:

Stock prices were mixed in very slow trading. On the Big Board, decliners led advancers, 814 to 673.

Bond prices fell. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 9.01%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency rose to 1.5815 marks from 1.5755 Friday and fell to 143.50 yen from 143.79.
