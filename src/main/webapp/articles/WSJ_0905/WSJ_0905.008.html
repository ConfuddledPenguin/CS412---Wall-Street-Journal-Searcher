Desert Song: Humor on the Home Front

  • Author: Laura Gramling
  • Date: 09/05/90

When Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait, the U.S. government halted trade with Iraq and sent out troops. Ten thousand Westerners in the Middle East discovered they were hostages. Americans began bracing for a recession -- or war. And Jeff Gonzer and Mike Coleman started writing songs.

Mr. Gonzer, the host of the morning show at Boston radio station WZLX, thought he might try his hand at parody. He jotted down some lyrics. He showed them to the station's head of production, Mike Coleman, who wrote some more and set them to some familiar music. A few days later a new song -- to the tune of Paul Simon's "Me and Julio Down by the School Yard" made its debut:

Well we're on the way

American troops are coming

We're on the way

Not takin' our time so goodbye Saddam

King of Baloney, we'll clean your corona

Seein' me & Hussein down by the oilfield

Listeners in Boston have also been treated to such standards as "Please Mr. Postman" -- as the Marvelettes probably never envisioned it:

Oh yes wait a minute Mr. Hussein

Kuwaaiit isn't yours Mr. Hussein&

Messrs. Gonzer and Coleman are part of a time-honored tradition. For as long as wars have threatened nations, people have longed to, well, lighten up a little. The Gonzer-and-Coleman idea hasn't yet reached the mighty status of a "trend." But Iraq parody is definitely catching on. The folks at Dallas's Magic 102.9 FM, a soft-rock station, are treating listeners to "Hussein Is Crazy," an opus based on last year's Fine Young Cannibals hit, "She Drives Me Crazy":

Hussein is crazy, ooh, ooh

He's making news, ooh, ooh

Hussein is crazy, ooh, ooh

Worse than the 2 Live Crew, ooh, ooh

It would be nice to get Hussein

Wait 'til the time he meets his fate

Cause Noriega's getting lonely

He could use a new cellmate

To be sure, America's radio stations aren't underestimating the gravity of the situation. New York's top-40 Z100 FM has played the national anthem at 9 every morning since the invasion. "Coach" Mike Opelka, Z-100's producer, says this is the station's "salute to the men and women serving in the Middle East as well as for their families back home in the States." Even the patriotic Coach, though, is indulging in a bit of humor. His parody, "Iraqity-Raq," is based on "Yakety Yak" by The Coasters:

He's got his tanks and guns and planes

And he is totally insane

And his name is Saddam Hussein

Invading countries is his game.

Iraqity Raq, don't shoot back.

Listeners, for their part, have called the stations from all over the world -- including the Middle East -- with requests for tapes of the parodies. Americans are the kind of folk who appreciate this type of thing, says Peter Zipfel -- known to fans as "Zip" -- in Phoenix. He and Drew Lane do the morning show on Mix 101 FM there, featuring their version of "Horse With No Name" by America:

Now we're sittin' in the desert cursin' Saddam Hussein

He got too much sun, fried his brain

In the desert, you know you could go insane,

When you use mustard gas and your name is Hussein

Allah, la, la la la . . .

Songs may do fine for now, but fertile DJ-brains all over America are already expanding their war effort. WZLX, the Boston station, has already sponsored "Iraqnophobia Day," distributing gasoline to listeners for $1 a gallon. Magic 102.9 in Dallas constructed a Texas-sized greeting card. Citizens spent one enthusiastic Saturday afternoon at the local mall signing it. "We're thinking about the guys, but at the same time we're having fun," said Garry D, of the station's late-night show. As he spoke, the three-piece card, filled with signatures and messages for the troops, was already winging its way to our men and women in Saudi Arabia.
