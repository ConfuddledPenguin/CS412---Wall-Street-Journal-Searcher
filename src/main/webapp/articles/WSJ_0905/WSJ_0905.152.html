Bonn Rejects Banks' Bids for `Unity' Bonds

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit and Terence Roth
  • Date: 09/05/90

In a move that shocked international bond traders, the West German government turned down all bids from a group of banks for 2.5 billion marks ($1.58 billion) of bonds. The rejection came after the banks, burned by an underwriting of similar 8.75%, 10-year bonds on Monday, bid lower prices for the new offering. Because bond yields move in the opposite direction of prices, the banks' bids raised prospective interest rates higher than the West German Finance Ministry was willing to pay for its debt.

Traders and analysts interpreted the banks' demand for higher yields as a sign that global capital markets are becoming saturated by the large volume of West German government debt needed to finance the unification of East and West Germany.

"It also reflected a belief that inflationary pressures associated with the merger of the two countries are building and that interest rates are expected to climb further," said Nicholas P. Sargen, a director of bond-market research at Salomon Brothers Inc. in New York.

"The capital market is being burdened like never before," said Gert Schmidt, an economist for Industriekreditbank AG in Dusseldorf. "The question is, how much can it take before interest rates go higher? It looks as if higher interest rates are to be the price of unity and the price of not raising taxes."

On Monday, a consortium of West German banks and foreign financial institutions that are licensed to do banking business in West Germany purchased 2.5 billion marks of 10-year, 8.75%, so-called unity bonds at a discount of 99.20 to yield 8.88%.

This was to be the first part of a two-part offering, the second portion being yesterday's tender auction to the same banking syndicate that underwrote Monday's sale. West German bankers said yesterday's auction also was supposed to be for 2.5 billion marks.

But after purchasing the initial 2.5 billion-mark segment on Monday, the banks discovered yesterday that they couldn't sell the bonds. "This morning {Tuesday}, there was disappointment in the market, because it expected a 9% coupon. The moment the market opened today, the price fell to 98.30," an executive at a big West German bank said. "So we couldn't sell it {at a profit}. Because the {8.75%} coupon was so low, it didn't even sell at 99.20, the price at which we had underwritten it."

He added that the bank syndicate then offered to buy the second 2.5 billion-mark portion at 98.10. But "they didn't accept this offer," he said. Only the Bundesbank, the West German central bank, tendered on the government's terms, taking 500 million marks on its books.

West Germany first began using the two-step method of selling 10-year government bonds -- an underwriting syndicate, followed by an auction the next day -- last month, when it sold six billion marks of unity bonds. Traditionally, it has sold government debt through an underwriting syndicate in the same manner as U.S. corporations sell debt to investors. But traders said the new approach reflected an evolutionary process that would eventually lead to a competitive tender system similar to that used by the U.S. Treasury.

But traders criticized the two-part system as awkward. A trader at a West German bank in New York groused, "It is neither fish nor fowl."

The problem is that investors who buy the bonds on the first day can find that the securities have fallen in price at the time of the second offering on the following day. "That's what happened in this case; it was clear the issue was under water," the trader said. "If you are an investor and subscribe on day one, you are at risk, depending on the bidding for the second half of the deal. It can go for you or against you."

Still, a banker in Frankfurt saw a silver lining in Tuesday's debacle: "Maybe this was best thing. . . . What we are moving towards is a full auction, which we do not have."

Some analysts contended that yesterday's rejected bid raised the odds that the Bonn government would have to raise taxes to finance the rebuilding of East Germany, which it estimates could cost 100 billion marks ($63.37 billion) annually for the next few years.

Chancellor Helmut Kohl and Finance Minister Theo Waigel, who hope to lead the first government elected by a united Germany since the early 1930s after all-German elections scheduled for Dec. 2, have firmly ruled out boosting taxes. The Bonn government has argued that unification can be financed by existing tax levels -- enhanced by West Germany's strong growth -- and more borrowing.

Nonetheless, Chancellor Kohl's opponent in the December election, Social Democrat Oskar Lafontaine, is insisting that West Germany has no choice but to raise taxes. And some private analysts agree.

"I see tax increases after the elections," said Eckhard Cornehl, an economist with Bank Julius Baer in Frankfurt. At the very least, analysts argue that Bonn will have to forgo corporate tax cuts scheduled for next year.

Meanwhile, the bond market is feeling the brunt of such pressures. Ten-year West German government bonds closed to yield 8.97% yesterday; and at one point in intraday trading, they were yielding more than 9%. "Clearly the market has established new higher levels," said John Lipsky, an international economist at Salomon Brothers in London.
