Politics & Policy: GAO Assails RTC Over $250 Million Advances to S&Ls

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 09/05/90

WASHINGTON -- The General Accounting Office criticized the savings-and-loan bailout agency for advancing $250 million to two insolvent thrifts under government control without securing collateral, and said the total of uncollateralized borrowing by such thrifts "could be substantial." The GAO, which scrutinized only three S&Ls, said the total could be significant because the Resolution Trust Corp. had advanced $12.7 billion as of March 31 to thrifts taken over by the government. The RTC, which has taken over about 450 thrifts, was set up under last year's S&L bailout law to sell off insolvent thrifts.

The GAO, the investigative arm of Congress, said the RTC's rules require it to hold promissory notes and secure collateral for the advances but that the RTC appeared to be ignoring those requirements. The advances are loans made to the thrifts to meet their cash needs while under government control.

The GAO said the failure to follow the rules could increase the cost of the bailout if the RTC can't get its money back or if it finds itself in a battle with other creditors over collateral.

The GAO said at one of the three thrifts it visited earlier this year the RTC couldn't find 12 of the 18 promissory notes for advances made through October 1989. The GAO found $250 million in advances not backed by collateral at two of the S&Ls.

The reason for the problems, perhaps, has been the confusion that has attended some of the bailout's start-up -- and to which bailout officials have admitted. The GAO said that officials at RTC headquarters maintained that regional RTC officials were supposed to ensure that sufficient collateral backed the advances. But the regional officials told the GAO they didn't even know until recently the total amount of advances from the RTC.

The criticism came in a report about the RTC's spending. In a possibly ominous note, the GAO said recent RTC actions to accept prices lower than the appraised value for properties suggest the RTC's overall valuations of its assets, which are based on appraised value, "may be overstated." If true, that could mean the RTC has further losses that taxpayers will have to make up.

The GAO said it will criticize appraisals carried out for the government as being overly optimistic in a report soon to be issued on the strength of the government insurance fund for banks. "Because the {RTC} also uses appraisals in valuing its real estate and other assets," the GAO said, "it needs to be alert for optimistic assumptions."

The GAO's report noted that the RTC had said it was taking steps to correct the collateral problems. A spokesman for the RTC said: "Given that we are in control {of the thrifts}, it is highly unlikely that collateral would be pledged to some other obligation."
