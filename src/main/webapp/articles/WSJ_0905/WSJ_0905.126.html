Abreast of the Market: Stocks Rebound to Finish Mixed As Trading Is Extremely Light

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 09/05/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices closed mixed as some late bargain-hunting blunted the impact of an early sell-off, sparked by a surge in oil prices and further evidence of weakness in the economy. Trading remained extremely light, with volume on the New York Stock Exchange setting its second consecutive low for the year, as the passing of the Labor Day holiday failed to bring investors back from the sidelines.

"It looks like some people decided to extend their vacation another day," said Richard Meyer, head of institutional trading at Ladenburg, Thalmann & Co.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average tumbled about 30 points within minutes of the opening and showed double-digit losses through most of the session. But as a result of the market's late turnaround, the average finished with a loss of just 0.99 at 2613.37.

Broader market indexes managed to post modest gains. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index went up 0.53 to 323.09, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 0.16 to 177.13 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 0.44 to 299.25.

Just 92,940,000 shares were traded on the Big Board, down slightly from 96,480,000 shares traded Friday. Declining issues led advancers by 814 to 673.

John A. Conlon Jr., a managing director at Rothschild Inc., said the unusually low trading activity is "indicative of an inordinate amount of indecision" among traders. "It's really hard to come up with a strong case for equities at this point," he said.

One big question in investors minds is the future of corporate profits. Weak dividend payments may be one indication that profits are in a downturn. Yesterday, Standard & Poor's Corp. said that the number of companies decreasing dividends rose to 81 in the year to date through August, compared with 57 in 1989. Companies omitting dividends rose to 166 compared to 89 for the same period last year. The number of companies boosting dividends shrank to 904 for the period, compared with 1,155 in the same period a year earlier.

October crude-oil futures soared $1.80 to $29.12 a barrel as New York trading resumed following the holiday, during which oil prices rose sharply overseas. The surge helped set the tone for stocks early in the session.

A disappointing report from the nation's purchasing managers also put early pressure on the market, traders said. An index of economic activity derived from the group's monthly survey fell to 47% in August from 47.4% in July; a reading below 50% indicates that the economy's manufacturing sector is contracting.

But the market eased its fall as investors began looking forward to Friday's employment report, seen by some analysts as a possible prelude to an easing of Federal Reserve policy. The market then recovered in the final hour of trading.

Energy stocks, bolstered by the latest jump in oil prices, held steady as the overall market slumped, and closed with modest gains. USX, the parent company of Marathon Oil, climbed 3/4 to 32 7/8; Texaco went up 1/4 to 62; Mobil added 1/2 to 64 3/8; Amoco advanced 1 1/4 to 56 1/4; and Unocal rose 1/2 to 32 1/2.

Archer-Daniels-Midland, a potential beneficiary of higher oil prices, added 7/8 to 23 7/8. The Wall Street Journal said yesterday in its "Heard on the Street" column that analysts expect the ethanol industry's fortunes to improve because of the Persian Gulf crisis. The company is widely viewed as the lowest-cost producer in the ethanol industry.

Union Texas Petroleum Holdings rose 7/8 to 23 3/4 amid speculation that Chinese Petroleum, Taiwan's national oil and gas company, will make a takeover offer. An official at a U.S. unit of Chinese Petroleum confirmed the Taiwanese firm's interest in the company but wouldn't elaborate, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Allied-Signal, which holds a 40% stake in Union Texas, dipped 1/8 to 30 7/8.

Pinnacle West Capital, the Big Board's most active issue, tumbled 3 3/4 to 12 3/8 on about two million shares after PacifiCorp agreed to end its 10-month battle to acquire the company, agreeing instead to a pact allowing seasonal "power swaps" with its Arizona Public Service unit. PacifiCorp gained 1/2 to 19 7/8.

Boeing rose 3/8 at 48 5/8 after receiving $6 billion in firm orders and options from Asiana Airlines, a South Korean carrier.

McDonnell Douglas gained 1 to 43 5/8; GPA Group of Ireland, an aircraft leasing firm, placed an order valued at about $2.5 billion for its MD-11 long-range aircraft.

Advanced Micro Devices added 1/4 to 5 5/8. The company is expected this fall to unveil its version of Intel's popular 80386 microprocessor, and could have an easier time if two legal disputes are decided in its favor.

Sotheby's Holdings skidded 3/4 to 11 7/8. About 300 of its key employees became eligible for the first time to exercise options allowing them to buy up to 2.5 million shares at a huge discount and resell them at the market price.

Equimark, whose board decided to omit a quarterly dividend on its common shares, fell 5/8 to 5. The stock had jumped a point Friday amid news that the Tisch family has acquired a 6.8% stake.

Entergy gained 3/4 to 19 1/8. Its board decided to boost by $135 million the amount it can spend on stock repurchases under an ongoing buy-back program.

Raytheon, which said its Caloric unit plans to close its appliance-manufacturing operations in Topton, Pa., rose 1 1/8 to 61 3/4. The company said it expects to take a third-quarter pretax charge of $17.5 million for the shutdown.

Allergan rose 1/4 to 15 1/2 following news that it plans to repurchase up to 1.2 million of its common shares. Separately, the company said a federal court upheld its patent on Ultrazyme, a contact lens-care product that works with hydrogen peroxide.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 0.65 to 322.74. Volume totaled 8,790,000 shares.

Kinark went down 1/4 to 5. The company said its agreement to sell its Camelot Hotels for $12 million to Wilmae, a Santa Rosa, Calif.-based concern, was terminated after Wilmae ran into delays in obtaining financing.
