Economy: Manufacturing Index Fell to 47% During August --- Purchasing Managers Data's Drop Is Second in a Row; Construction Posts Gains

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 09/05/90

WASHINGTON -- The manufacturing economy deteriorated for the second consecutive month, according to the National Association of Purchasing Managers. The group's index, which charts orders, production, employment and other indicators of manufacturing health, fell to 47% in August, below July's index and its lowest since January. The association says that when the index drops below 50% it generally indicates that the manufacturing economy is contracting.

The group surveys purchasing executives in only about 300 companies around the country, but many economists and, especially the financial markets, watch their index closely because it is usually the first published report of economic activity for any month.

Analysts are particularly hungry for data on the economy's performance in August, since Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait sent oil prices soaring and cast new uncertainty on the nation's economic outlook.

Manufacturing has been anemic for months. The Federal Reserve's efforts to fight inflation by maintaining high interest rates has hurt makers of automobiles and other types of large, costly machinery and appliances that often are purchased by borrowing.

The purchasing managers' index, however, had recently indicated that manufacturing was picking up from its slump, by registering above 50% for three consecutive months in the second quarter. But in July it slipped beneath that threshold, to 47.4%.

According to the survey responses, production declined for the first time since January. New orders were down for the second straight month, and manufacturing employment declined for the 18th consecutive month -- at the fastest rate since January.

Export orders, however, continued to grow, the report said, though at the slowest pace since February.

In a separate economic report yesterday, the Commerce Department said that construction spending in July increased 0.4%, the first rise in four months. Spending on private construction grew 0.6%, but the gain was entirely in spending on business and other nonresidential construction, which jumped 2.5%. Private residential construction slipped 0.4%.

Overall construction spending amounted to a $442.20 billion annual rate in July, the department said.

The numbers in both the purchasing managers and Commerce Department reports were adjusted for normal seasonal fluctuations.
