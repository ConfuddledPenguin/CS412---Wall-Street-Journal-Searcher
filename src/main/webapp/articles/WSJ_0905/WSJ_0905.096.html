GM Unit and Pritzker-Led Group Agree To Buy Parent of Ticor Title Insurance

  • Author: Joseph B. White
  • Date: 09/05/90

Electronic Data Systems Corp., the data processing unit of General Motors Corp., and a group led by Chicago investor Jay Pritzker have agreed to buy the parent of Ticor Title Insurance Cos., a big real estate title insurance concern. Exact terms of the acquisition weren't disclosed. However, an official of Ticor's parent, closely held Westwood Equities Corp., said the purchase price is less than Ticor's annual gross revenue of roughly $500 million. EDS also didn't disclose how ownership of Ticor will be divided with Mr. Pritzker's group, New Century Enterprises Inc.

The venture gives EDS a major entrance into a potentially lucrative market. The deal "is a big opportunity for us. It fits very well with the other property businesses we're in," said Ben Sims, president of EDS's insurance business unit.

Ticor offers EDS and GM access to an enormous base of data on land transactions that could be valuable to their existing financial services marketing efforts. GM, for example, has a mortgage lending unit, while EDS is the largest supplier of data processing for the property and casualty insurance industry.

In acquiring a piece of Ticor, EDS expands its strategy of buying big customers to enter new markets.

Earlier this year, EDS jumped into the airline computer reservation business by forming a joint venture with Texas Air Corp. to take over Texas Air's System One airline reservation system. EDS was to pay $250 million for its share of the venture, and also was to receive a 10-year contract originally valued at $4 billion to handle data processing for Texas Air's Eastern and Continental airlines units. EDS still hasn't closed this deal, however, because of problems related to Eastern's bankruptcy proceedings. Company officials have said the terms of the deal could change.

In conjunction with the Ticor deal, EDS will get a data processing contract that company officials estimate will generate $200 million to $300 million in revenue over 10 years.

Ticor has been looking for a potential buyer for the past four years, said Winston V. Morrow, Westwood's chairman, president and chief executive officer. The company's core title insurance business has been unprofitable in recent years "due to a very heavy curve of losses in this industry," he said. The downturn in the real estate market has engendered a rise in claims against title insurance, Mr. Morrow said.

Ticor, based in Los Angeles, has had other problems.

In 1986, the company's mortgage insurance unit, TMIC Insurance Co., was taken over by California insurance regulators after it sustained huge losses on policies issued to a failed real-estate syndication firm, Equity Programs Investment Corp.

The TMIC Insurance mess and controversy over Ticor's handling of it "was a nightmare" that "haunted" the rest of Ticor's operations, Mr. Morrow said. Westwood, formed last year as a parent entity for Ticor's various operations, still owes $20 million of $30 million pledged to put the mortgage insurance unit back on a sound footing, Mr. Morrow said. That money will be paid as part of the acquisition, he said.

Because of the TMIC debacle and the slump in the title insurance business, Ticor couldn't afford technology needed to modernize its operations. The company's 300 company-owned offices and its more than 1,000 independent agents amass enormous amounts of information on U.S. property transactions. However, the company's data handling capability "is closer to the quill pen era" than the computer age, Mr. Morrow said.
