Letters to the Editor: Congress Shields Itself From Cobra's Bite

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/05/90

You reported that many companies are being sued for not complying with the Consolidated Omnibus Budget Resolution Act ("More Companies Bitten by `Cobra' Lawsuits," Law column, Aug. 2), but that'll never happen to Congress. Just as it has with 16 other employee-benefit laws, Congress has exempted itself from Cobra. A young man of our acquaintance was employed by a Southern congressman to supervise his three district offices. In the summer of 1989 this young man accepted a position in the private sector just after he and his wife learned that she had become pregnant. They didn't worry about health-care costs because he had excellent insurance benefits with his job and they assumed that he had the same rights as other American workers under Cobra: His present coverage could be continued for up to 18 months by his paying the premium himself.

But they were wrong] They soon found that Congress had exempted itself from Cobra. His new employer's insurance, while a good plan, would not cover the pregnancy because it was a pre-existing condition. The couple became uninsured for the pregnancy.

Recently President Bush signed the widely acclaimed Americans With Disabilities Act. Any complaints filed against private employers under that bill can go into federal court for a fair adjudication. But complaints by congressional employees will be handled by the House and Senate Ethics committees. Congress, as America's self-appointed privileged class, will be both judge and jury for charges against its members.

In July. Sen. Charles Grassley (R., Iowa) offered an amendment to the Civil Rights Act of 1990 to give congressional employees the same access to the federal court system, but the Senate tabled the amendment, 63 to 26. That vote, noted Sen. Grassley, "makes equal rights an illusion on Capitol Hill. It will ensure that Congress will never live under the same law that applies to the rest of America."

The situation recalls the story of the Kentuckian testifying before a hostile Senate committee. A senator asked: "By the way, Col. Smith, are you a real colonel who earned his rank while serving our country or are you just one of those `Kentucky colonels'?"

"You've got me," laughed the witness. "That `colonel' in front of my name is just like `the honorable' in front of yours -- it don't mean a damn thing]"

James G. Schneider

Kankakee, Ill.
