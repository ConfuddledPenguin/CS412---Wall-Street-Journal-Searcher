Private Placement Financing Plunged In the First Half

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 09/05/90

NEW YORK -- Wall Street's hopes for a quick shot in the arm from the $170 billion market for privately placed stocks and bonds are fast being dashed. U.S. private-placement financings plunged by one-third in this year's first half to $55.3 billion from $82.1 billion a year earlier -- accounting for just one-quarter of all stock and bond financings, according to IDD Information Services.

That's the first time in more than a decade that the volume of privately placed securities has dropped, IDD says; the market has grown steadily from $15.6 billion in 1980. The plunge primarily reflects the drying up of the market for high-yield, low-rated junk bond markets and a dearth of acquisition-related financing.

With new regulatory rules making it easier for securities firms to trade in the market for privately placed securities, Wall Street had been expecting that private placements would help fill the gap created by the slump in other areas of the securities business. The SEC's new rule 144a exempts U.S. and foreign institutions from registration requirements for the sale of stocks and bonds to big institutions.

But the private market, which is limited to institutions and is largely used to sell corporate debt, has been hit harder by the financing slump than the public market for stocks and bonds.

"Neither the passage of rule 144a nor the gap left in corporate financings by the virtual disappearance of the junk-bond market could boost the sagging private placement market this year," IDD said.

The recent Mideast turmoil, however, may actually boost the private market, some analysts say. That's because securities firms may increasingly tap the private market now that the public financing environment has stumbled.

"There may be more private placements if the Dow Jones Industrial average just kind of drifts away over the next eight to 12 months," said Perrin Long, senior securities analyst for Lipper Analytical Securities Corp.

Financings fell in most categories of the private market, with the biggest drops in placements of non-investment grade bonds and placements for employee stock-ownership plans; both plunged more than 70% in 1990's first half compared with the yearearlier period.

Overall, private placements made up 26% of all stock and bond financings, IDD said, down from a 38% market share in 1989.

Goldman, Sachs & Co. continues to lead the private-placement underwriting rankings, maintaining a 15% market share. But Goldman's volume fell 32% from 1989's first half, to $8.1 billion.

Underwritings by Salomon Brothers Inc., meanwhile, actually jumped by one-third during the first half, to $5.6 billion, representing a 10% market share. The increase propelled Salomon to No. 2 from the eighth position a year ago, IDD said.

Commercial banks have picked up some ground. Nine of the top 20 privateplacement underwriters in this year's first half are commercial banks, IDD said, with a combined one-third market share. Banks' particular strength: Acquisition-related placements and those for non-investment grade debt.
