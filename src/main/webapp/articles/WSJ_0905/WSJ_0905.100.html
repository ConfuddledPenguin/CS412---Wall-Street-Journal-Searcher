Autos: Japanese Car Prices Rise Sharply

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 09/05/90

DETROIT -- Two Japanese auto makers announced relatively modest price increases on several 1991 models, moves that could fuel their quest for a still-higher share of the U.S. car market. Honda Motor Co. and Nissan Motor Co. appear to be using pricing tactics sharply different from Detroit's Big Three auto makers, which are coupling steeper base-price increases with big rebates on the new models even before they hit the showrooms.

"Net of rebates, Detroit and the Japanese are raising prices by about the same amount," said auto analyst Ronald Glantz of Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. "Detroit is following the policy of the biggest rebate ever, while Japan is following the policy that rebates cheapen image."

Honda said it was raising the base price of its 1991 Accord, the top-selling car model in the U.S., by $200, or 1.6% from the 1990 model. That will put the cheapest Accord DX four-door sedan at $12,545, compared with $13,717 for the cheapest Ford Taurus, one of its major competitors.

Ford Motor Co. had raised the base price of the 1991 Taurus by $1,077, or 8.5%. But Ford yesterday announced a $750 rebate on the 1991 Taurus, thus making its effective price $12,967. The rebate leaves the least expensive Taurus priced more than $400 above the cheapest Accord. However, an automatic transmission is standard on the Taurus but a $750 option on the Accord, which comes with a five-speed manual transmission as standard.

"The Accord is already a successful car," says analyst John Casesa of Wertheim Schroder & Co., "This kind of restraint in pricing will allow it to be really successful." A Honda spokesman said the company was looking to "gain market share in 1991 as well as 1990."

Honda didn't announce prices on any of its other cars beside the Accord sedan. But that single model accounts for more than 40% of Honda's U.S. sales.

Nissan, meanwhile, said it was raising the base price of most models between 1.7% and 2.7%. However, a big exception is the Sentra, completely redone for this year. It will start at $7,999, a $600 or 8.1% increase from the current model.

Nissan's car and truck sales in the U.S. dropped 4.9% through July 31, making it the only major Japanese auto maker to lose market share this year. Overall, Japanese car makers took a record 28% of the U.S. car market in the first half of 1990.

Yesterday, Nissan released prices on all of its models except for the upscale Maxima. The base price of the 1991 Nissan Stanza will be $11,900, a 2.1% increase from 1990. The Nissan 240SX will start at $13,609, a 2.7% rise.

Nissan lowered the base price of its 300ZX sports car to $27,300 from $28,600, a decrease of 4.5%. However, the company eliminated the car's standard "T-bar" roof, which now must be purchased as an option. With the T-bar roof the car now costs $29,100, a 1.7% increase from 1990.

The Nissan Pathfinder sports utility vehicle will have a base price of $16,120, a 2.5% increase. The cheapest Pathfinder with four-wheel drive will be $17,695, a 2.3% increase.

The leading Japanese auto maker, Toyota Motor Corp., expects to release prices later this week. "All I can tell you is that our price increases will be moderate," a Toyota spokesman said.

The Japanese have gained some pricing flexibility over the past year because of the value of the yen. After several years of rising against the U.S. dollar, the yen stabilized earlier this year. The yen currently trades at 144.05 to the dollar.

Earlier, General Motors Corp. and Ford announced price increases of 4% or more on most of their models. Chrysler Corp. put a big 12% increase on its minivans, but actually cut prices on some less-popular vehicles.

As expected, Ford yesterday followed GM and Chrysler in announcing rebates on nearly all of its 1991 models. The rebates range from $1,000 on Grand Marquis and Ranger to $500 on cars such as Mustang and Escort. Buyers can take cut-rate financing instead of cash.
