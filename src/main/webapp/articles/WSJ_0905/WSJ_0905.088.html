DWG Will Add Three Independent Directors to Board

  • Author: Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 09/05/90

DWG Corp., which has been ruled by Victor Posner, the iron-fisted Miami Beach financier, agreed to yield some control of the company by adding three independent directors to its 13-member board, in a settlement with Granada Investments Inc. DWG owns such enterprises as Arby's Inc. and Royal Crown Cos. and is the crown jewel of the 72-year-old corporate raider's shrinking empire.

Granada, a New York-based investment firm, sued DWG's board in April 1989, charging, among other things, that DWG's directors shirked their fiduciary responsibility by not seriously considering a $22-a-share, or $572 million, offer that Granada had made for DWG in March 1989. The suit also attacked the high salary and other perquisites of DWG Chairman Victor Posner.

DWG, in a countersuit, claimed that Granada's bid was never a serious offer, but rather a scheme intended to manipulate the market for DWG shares and allow Granada to benefit from the increase in the stock's price when the company was in play.

The settlement -- which covers DWG, Granada, and certain third-party defendants, including Granada's chairman, New York attorney Andrew N. Heine -- is subject to approval by U.S. District Court Judge Thomas D. Lambros, who has presided over the court battle in Cleveland. DWG is incorporated in Ohio.

Leonard A. Pelullo, a Miami Beach hotelier who was a member of the Granada group but withdrew, isn't a party to the settlement.

According to Martin Rosen, an attorney for DWG, the settlement calls for the judge to pick three candidates who will be added to DWG's slate of directors. As usual, the directors will be voted on by shareholders.

The settlement also provides for the independent directors to be named to DWG's key committees, including its compensation committee, which ponders issues like Mr. Posner's controversial and hefty pay; the intercorporate-transactions committee, which controls business dealings within the Posner empire; and the audit committee. One of the independent directors will become chairman of the audit committee, Mr. Rosen said.

Of the three independent directors, two will succeed Martin Posner, who is a nephew of Mr. Posner, and Brenda Castellano. The third will fill a vacancy on the board.

Terms of the pact also call for the judge to determine how much DWG should reimburse Granada for fees and expenses associated with the litigation, up to a maximum of $5.5 million. "It's been a long, hard and bitter fight," said Mr. Rosen, who called the agreement to foot the bill for Granada's costs "a common thing."

Neither Mr. Heine nor other Granada officials could be reached for comment.
