Deep Inside This Big Old Place, There's a Mall Trying to Get Out

  • Author: Gregory L. White
  • Date: 09/05/90

MOSCOW -- Ever since Stalin ordered it built in 1939, the U.S.S.R. Exhibition of Economic Achievements has been a showcase for the supposed triumphs of socialism. But now, answering the call of capitalism, it's a sort of dotty Disneyland. Its 58 pavilions used to run officially sponsored exhibits on everything from atomic power to zoology. But government funding has been cut off, and the 11 million tourists a year no longer come just to admire the magic of Soviet animal husbandry or to peer at video recorders, designer shoes and other goods that aren't for sale in the nation's depleted stores.

"When there's nothing in the stores, it bothers people to show things at the exhibition," says Aidar Valiakhmetov. He's the exhibition's advertising director, a job once known as head of propaganda.

Previously, exhibitors weren't allowed to sell their wares. But today's visitors to the complex -- which is spread over a 500-acre park in north Moscow -- can, and do, spend much of their time standing in line for such trinkets as magnetized ear clips that are said to relieve headaches.

The consumer-goods pavilion -- fallen prey to the past, perhaps -- is closed for repairs. But at the agriculture pavilion, with its huge, stained-glass views of happy, toiling peasants, Galina Nazarova offers amateur gardeners a $4-a-bottle miracle liquid that supposedly kills all kinds of bugs and beetles without harming the environment.

Next door at the wheat pavilion, the bushels of grain and loaves of bread on display go unnoticed. In the back room, however, an exhibit of glass sculptures by a retired light-bulb maker is well attended, even though the tickets cost $1.60, six times what visitors pay to get into the complex itself.

Capitalism is also tooling along in the machinery pavilion, which now rents space to anyone who can pay. Around its dusty satellite models and spacesuits are a video salon, which shows pirate copies of foreign movies, and a "UFO shelter," which welcomes visitors who want to chat about their encounters with extraterrestrials.

The pavilion's deputy director, 36-year-old engineer Sergei Kurbatov, is a firm believer in the profit motive. He even wants to break up the pavilion into a series of minicompanies, so that unsuccessful ones will fail and not be carried along by the others. "We are convinced that a person who works hard should live much better, and a person who works less should live considerably worse."
