BCE Development Says 2 Supporters May Limit Backing

  • Author: Lynne Kilpatrick
  • Date: 09/05/90

TORONTO -- Financially troubled BCE Development Corp. said its two main financial backers may not continue to let the company use their funds to meet its debt obligations. The company said in a statement that this could lead to a restructuring of its 2.4 billion Canadian dollars ($2.07 billion) of debt.

BCE Inc., which holds 67% of BCE Development, and Carena Developments Ltd., a real-estate developer controlled by Toronto financiers Peter and Edward Bronfman, joined forces late last year to provide a C$500 million credit facility to the real-estate company.

The funding was pumped into Brookfield Development Corp., BCE Development's chief operating subsidiary, to allow work to continue on construction projects. However, some of the money has been used by BCE Development to meet debenture payments and other debt obligations. By the end of the first quarter, C$358 million of the credit had been used up.

"They {BCE and Carena} have the right to say `We don't want you using {the money} for other things like debenture payments,'" said BCE Development's president and chief executive officer, Gordon Arnell.

The company said Brookfield is its only source of funds for meeting its debt obligations. Mr. Arnell said the company's next debenture payment comes up in October and the board will meet then to decide what to do.

The company said that if Brookfield's support is withdrawn, "the company will present to debenture and other corporate debt holders a plan to restructure their obligations." Mr. Arnell said he doesn't think the company is facing bankruptcy. He said the company would hope to develop a debt restructuring plan that its creditors would agree to voluntarily.

The company also said in the statement that, given "the further deterioration in the real-estate markets since the beginning of the year, a further asset provision may be required." BCE Development took a C$550 million write-down on its real-estate assets in the fourth quarter of 1989.

"It's not to say these things are going to happen," Mr. Arnell said of the company's gloomy statement. "We want to put people on notice that these things are possible."

The company said it expects its previously reported rights offering of Brookfield shares to go ahead as planned. Under the proposal, Carena and BCE would convert up to the full C$500 million of their credit facility into equity. The two companies are also considering a proposal to convert capitalized interest that has accrued under the credit facility, the company said. This would result in about C$600 million of equity being subscribed for by Carena and BCE under the offering.

BCE Development also reported yesterday a first-quarter loss of C$38.1 million, or 26 Canadian cents a share. It said that, until it completes the rights offering, it will continue to operate at a loss.

On the Toronto Stock Exchange yesterday, BCE Development closed unchanged at 24 Canadian cents.
