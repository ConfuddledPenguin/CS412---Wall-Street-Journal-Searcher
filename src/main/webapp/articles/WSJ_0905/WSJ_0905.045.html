Dean Witter Says Connolly Resigns As Chief Strategist

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 09/05/90

NEW YORK -- Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. is losing its well-known chief investment strategist John D. Connolly, who resigned to join a big money-management firm in suburban Philadelphia. As a result of Wall Street's contraction since the 1987 stock-market crash, many stars of brokerage firms' research departments have migrated to money management, which remains lucrative.

Mr. Connolly, one of Wall Street's most bullish strategists in recent months, will become a portfolio manager with Miller, Anderson & Sherrerd, a West Conshohocken, Pa., firm that manages about $21.2 billion in assets for 115 clients, mainly pension funds and endowments.

No successor was named.

Mr. Connolly is the second high-profile market forecaster in the past year to leave Dean Witter, a unit of Sears, Roebuck & Co. Last year at this time, John Mendelson -- whose market comments once rocked Wall Street, before his persistent bearishness lost him some of his clout -- also resigned. Dean Witter executives say the two resignations aren't comparable; even while Mr. Mendelson was around, Mr. Connolly clearly had the highest profile at the firm.

Mr. Connolly, in addition to serving as chairman of Dean Witter's investment policy committee, also had the title of an assistant director of research. "We're seeking a successor now," a Dean Witter executive said. He added that Mr. Connolly will be missed: "It's our loss; it's their gain," the executive said.

Mr. Connolly, who is on vacation, prior to starting work at Miller Anderson on Sept. 17, couldn't be reached for comment.

Manny Korman, Dean Witter's head of research, said in a letter to the firm's branches that Mr. Connolly, who joined Dean Witter in 1984, wanted to become a full-time money manager and "make a change in his personal life style." He wished Mr. Connolly success.

In recent months, Mr. Connolly was the most bullish of strategists at 10 large brokerage firms whose asset-allocation advice is tracked by The Wall Street Journal. In mid-October of 1989 he began recommending that investors keep 85% of their money in stocks, a much higher allocation than most strategists. And Mr. Connolly held to that advice until the Mideast crisis recently erupted, at which time he reduced his recommended stock exposure but still kept it higher than that of strategists at other firms.

The bullish advice worked out well in the second quarter, when Dean Witter's recommended allocation led all firms. But it is certain that Dean Witter's performance will wane in the current quarter, given the stock market's large declines.
