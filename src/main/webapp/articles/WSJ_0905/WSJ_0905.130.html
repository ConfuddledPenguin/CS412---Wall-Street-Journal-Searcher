Martin Marietta Faces Intelsat Suit On Launch Failure

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 09/05/90

WASHINGTON -- The International Telecommunications Satellite Organization (Intelsat) filed a counterclaim against Martin Marietta Corp. seeking $400 million in damages for the failed launch of a $150 million communications satellite. The satellite, launched March 14 from a Martin Marietta Titan III rocket, ended up in a useless orbit after the system that separated the satellite from the rocket malfunctioned. Martin Marietta later blamed the trouble on a "wiring problem, undetected by the processes and test procedures used in pre-launch checkout."

The original claim by Martin Marietta and the counterclaim are an important test of satellite launch contracts. Martin Marietta said in its complaint that if its contract can't be enforced, the company will have to boost the price of its Titan III launches, which could make its commercial launch business uncompetitive.

Intelsat, an international consortium, argued that Martin Marietta failed to live up to its contractual obligation and that slip-ups by the company's management and employees resulted in the launch failure.

The claim, filed in federal court in Baltimore Friday, responds to a complaint filed July 6 by Martin Marietta. The giant defense contractor, anticipating a lawsuit from Intelsat, asked the court for a ruling that under the launch contract, Intelsat assumed the risk of loss of its satellite. Intelsat, it said, was offered insurance against a launch failure, but decided against buying it.
