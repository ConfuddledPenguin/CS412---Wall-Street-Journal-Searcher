World Markets: Tokyo Shares Drop 512.79 Points on Reports Crude Oil Producers Will Raise Prices to Japan

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/05/90

Tokyo stocks fell broadly on profit-taking Tuesday as news that crude oil producers would raise prices to Japanese customers soaked sentiment, traders said. London shares closed lower, but recovered from a steep drop in early afternoon trading. But the recovery, coming amid thin trading, wasn't taken as a sign of things to come.

In Tokyo, rounds of arbitrage selling, motivated by weakness on the futures indexes, helped accelerate losses, participants said.

The Nikkei Index of 225 issues fell 512.79 points to 24907.64.

In early trading in Tokyo Wednesday, the Nikkei index fell another 142.95 points to 24764.69.

Tuesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 330 million shares, down from 368.9 million shares Monday. Decliners outnumbered advancers 858 to 126, while 105 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section dropped 35.54 points, or 1.83%, to 1910.38.

The second section index fell 60.33 points, or 1.58%, to 3761.51. Volume in the section was estimated at 9.3 million shares, up from eight million shares Monday.

Sentiment was hurt by higher crude oil prices linked to expectations of increased military tension in the Persian Gulf, traders said.

Moreover, news that Qatari and Mexican crude oil producers raised prices to Japanese customers and that the Ministry of International Trade and Industry had "given in" to higher prices for oil products was discouraging to the stock market, noted Kevin Haag, a trader in overseas operations at Nomura Securities.

As the stalemate in the Gulf continues with little expectation for a negotiated settlement soon, Tokyo traders said they're locked into short-term activities.

At the same time, however, brokerage houses are under pressure from "tokkin" special money account clients to realize profits before September book closings, noted Yusuke Fujiwara, head of stock trading at Baring Securities Ltd.

Mr. Haag pointed out that the lack of institutional interest in the market leaves trading activity up to dealers, who continue to focus on speculative issues.

"It's just quick cash going into this market," one trader at a U.K. firm said. "Individuals and dealers are punching money into this thing, and then turning around to take profits."

"There's a limited amount of cash going in," he added, "so if there's a change in sentiment, everything changes. The market has been relying on these (speculative) issues. If it goes up on that, you know it's just going to come right back down again."

Tuesday, the Nikkei Index dropped after the opening on bearish sentiments, then gently bounced back on buying on speculative issues.

However, by midmorning, the impact of arbitrage selling and shifting sentiment began to surface as the Nikkei edged lower. Toward the end of the morning, support gave way to profit-taking and further selling on futures indexes, allowing for later arbitrage selling.

Throughout the afternoon, the Nikkei continued to tumble, falling easily below 25000 points and then hovering just before hitting 24800 points.

In the midafternoon, some program buying by major brokerage houses helped buoy the index above 24900 points, but wasn't enough to ignite any buying interest.

Speculative issue Honshu Paper & Pulp, whose price gains were rumored Monday to be under investigation by financial authorities for potential stock price manipulation, closed at 4310 yen, down 70 yen ($29.92, down 49 cents), on heavy volume. The price had been as high as 4600 yen, up 220 yen.

Tokyu Department Store, which Monday reported a 12% rise in unconsolidated pretax profit for the first half, rose 140 yen to 1580 yen.

Mining, metal and steel shares were lower. Tokyo Steel fell 170 yen to 3820 yen, Nippon Steel fell 14 yen to 481 yen, and NKK fell 11 yen to 454 yen. Kawasaki Steel dropped 10 yen to 467 yen, and Daido Steel retreated 30 yen to 695 yen.

Mitsubishi Metal fell 22 yen to 728 yen, while Mitsubishi Mining fell 16 yen to 601 yen. Dowa Mining dropped 32 yen to 700 yen. Nippon Light Metal, traded actively by dealers, fell 50 yen to 1240 yen.

Blue-chips fell, including TDK, down 210 yen to 5090 yen, Sony, down 80 yen to 7220 yen, Fanuc, down 220 yen to 5650 yen, and Kyocera, down 180 yen to 7470. But Pioneer rose 30 yen to 5,100 yen.

In London, poor trading volume continued but improved slightly from Monday's record low for the year. And traders reckon that both volume and sentiment will remain cool for the near future.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index dropped 18.6 points to 2148. The Financial Times 30 Share Index fell 16.5 points to 1670.9.

Volume was 286.2 million shares, up from Monday's 220.2 million shares.

Movement in Reckitt & Colman was the main feature of the market, one trader said. Adverse market comments, concerning earnings from recent acquisitions and long-term prospects, are preceding Thursday's diclosure of interim figures. The stock fell 43 pence to #11.38 (81 cents to $21.52).

Hardy Oil fell 4 pence to 208 pence following Trafalgar House's placing of its 30% stake in the company. Trafalgar House firmed a penny to 233 pence.

Other oil stocks remained firm, and one trader said they were currently sustaining the market. Ultramar gained 4 pence to 360 pence. Lasmo rose 2 pence to 502 pence.

CRH, an Irish cement and concrete concern, fell 3 pence to 203 pence following interim results showing an 18% rise in pretax profit. Analysts, however, are wary of the company's ability to sustain growth.

Sedgwick Group closed down 17 pence at 181 pence. The company's bearish comments about the outlook for the industry and its dollar-based earnings overshadowed a 2.3% increase in pretax profit.

Bowater, which reported a 19% increase in pretax profit for the half year, fell 15 pence to 488 pence. The company urged caution over the impact of economic troubles in the U.K. and U.S. as well as the Kuwait crisis on full-year earnings.

Elsewhere, shares inched higher in Oslo, but closed broadly lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Brussels, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Milan and Madrid. European stock markets were generally depressed by concerns over events in the Middle East and disheartened by losses in Tokyo and on Wall Street. South African gold stocks drifted slightly lower.

Stocks closed lower in Sydney, Hong Kong, Wellington, Manila, Seoul and Singapore and were higher in Taipei.
