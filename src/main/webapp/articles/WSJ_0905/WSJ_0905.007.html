Foreign Exchange: Dollar Drifts in Dull Session as Dealers Await the August Employment Report

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/05/90

NEW YORK -- In directionless trading, the dollar drifted below its levels in London late Monday against all major currencies except the Canadian dollar. However, compared with its late-Friday New York quotes, the greenback rose against several currencies. In the absence of fresh ideas, many dealers said they would await Friday's release of the August U.S. employment report before making any big decisions.

"The market is still trading like a summertime market," said Donald Hubbard, a Midland Bank treasury officer in New York. "It will take a few days to pick up."

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.5815 marks, down from 1.5837 marks late Monday in London, but higher than 1.5755 marks late Friday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 143.50 yen, below 143.53 yen in London late Monday and 143.79 yen late Friday in New York.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the Nikkei index plunged another 750.77 points to close the morning session at 24156.87.

Monday's Labor Day holiday in the U.S. and Canada has traditionally marked the shift to more intense market activity, but Iraq's early-August invasion of Kuwait put a twist in the normal cycle, traders said.

The eagerness for Friday's U.S. economic data is understandable. The currency market, which for a month has bought the dollar on the prospect of war and sold it on fundamentals, will get its first look at the effect higher oil prices have had on a weak U.S. economy.

John Elliott, a Bank of America vice president in Los Angeles, said the North American market had no intention of pushing the dollar significantly higher yesterday, but was poised to send it lower if U.S. stock prices matched losses of other markets around the world. However, the Dow Jones Industrial Average ended off less than a point.

Mark-yen trading continued to be a focus of dealings, but showed no more decision than dollar activity.

The mark was quoted in late New York trading at 90.737 yen, above 90.62 yen late Monday in London, but well below 91.266 yen in New York Friday.

Thomas Benfer, a senior currency marketing representative in New York for Bank of Montreal, said the mark's decline from 92.081 yen last Wednesday reflects a gradual reaction to Japan's decision Thursday to lift its discount rate to 6% from 5.25%.

The mark, like the yen, has benefited all summer from expectations that German interest rates will rise. But Monday, Karl Thomas, a Bundesbank policy maker, said the costs of German reunion won't necessarily lead to higher domestic interest rates. Three-month government paper yields more than 8% in West Germany, compared with 7% in Japan.

The Canadian dollar continued to slide from its recent 12-year peak. In late New York trading, it was quoted at 86.18 U.S. cents, dropping from 86.69 cents in London Monday and 86.42 cents in New York Friday. Only slightly more than a week ago, the Canadian dollar purchased more than 88.5 cents.

A report Friday that Canada's gross domestic product shrank at an annualized rate of 1.6% in the second quarter intensified a belief that Canada may beat the U.S. into recession. Given those poor economic prospects, Mr. Benfer said, "the feeling is that the Bank of Canada will have no recourse" but to loosen credit some more.

Sterling, after losing last week most of its 10-cent August gain, found a little support yesterday. It was quoted late in New York at $1.8790, compared with $1.8730 late Monday in London and $1.8905 Friday in New York.

The dollar ended European trading slightly higher, supported by technical considerations.

Activity in the European market was centered around the European Monetary System, where the lira and the peseta lost ground against the German currency.

Milan dealers said the Bank of Italy has recently increased liquidity in the domestic money market, pushing short-term interest rates sharply lower. Overnight lira rates dropped to less than 1% Monday night.

The dollar finished Asian trading mixed, up against the yen but lower against the pound and the mark. It was last quoted in Tokyo Tuesday at 144.05 yen, above 143.55 yen late Monday.

Traders said the market experienced a correction following the yen's strong gains over the last few trading days against European currencies.

Separately, on the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $383.70 an ounce, up $1.40. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.5 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $384.60 an ounce.
