REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): When North Korea Knocks...

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/05/90

Yesterday was one of those increasingly frequent occasions when South Koreans deserved to feel proud. North Korea's Premier Yon Hyong Muk arrived in the southern capital of Seoul to meet with South Korea's Premier Kang Young Hoon and President Roh Tae Woo. There was a brief delay to sort out a multicar fender-bender in the visitor's motorcade. But then a dialogue began aimed at reunification of the Korean peninsula, 40 years after the communist North began the Korean War with a surprise attack on Seoul. It was a war in which more than five million Americans served. For South Korea, these talks are part of the payoff for standing up to North Korea's threats and building a democratic capitalist system that has helped lead Asia's postwar boom. From the destitute rural economy of the 1950s, the South has built a world-class trading nation with a per capita income at least four times that of the misanthropic North. In 1988, Seoul successfully hosted the summer Olympics while North Korea's Great Leader Kim Il Sung sulked in isolated Pyongyang.

By now, even North Korea's staunchest comrades -- China and the Soviet Union -- have acknowledged that Kim Il Sung's Stalin-inspired utopia is going nowhere. The future lies with the South. China, despite its revived revolutionary rantings since Tiananmen, has been courting Seoul's business know-how and investment capital for several years now. In June, President Gorbachev met with Roh Tae Woo in San Francisco and announced that Moscow and Seoul would establish diplomatic ties.

There is hope in the West that these talks are part of a sweep toward peaceful reunification that might roughly follow the script of Germany this past year. The so-called demilitarized zone that cuts the two Koreas along the 38th parallel is for Asia at least as potent a symbol of the Cold War as was the Berlin Wall for Europe. But experience says that when North Korea knocks at the gates, it's still smart to beware.

The North still talks -- and lives -- in terms of communist revolution. One of the top items on Pyongyang's agenda is withdrawal from the South of American troops, currently 38,000-strong, which have helped shore up South Korea's defense since the 1953 truce. Rational calculation says that North Korea's leaders would have to be crazy to start a war now against the South. It is highly doubtful that either China or the Soviet Union would help the North, and without their resupplies Pyongyang would be doomed. Nonetheless, the cost to South Korea and its allies could be miserably high. And the North's Kim Il Sung during his 42 years of dictatorship has piled up all the credentials of megalomania. These range from his personality cult and efforts to install a communist dynasty, to such overseas projects as bombing a South Korean official delegation in Rangoon in 1983, and blowing up a South Korean commercial jet over the Andaman Sea in 1987, killing all 115 people aboard.

It is alarmingly plausible that with the communist world breaking up around him, and his own revolution marching down a dead end, Kim Il Sung might yet try a suicidal last stand. Certainly there is nothing in his history to suggest that he will graciously defer to the democrats of Seoul.

Reunification strictly on South Korea's terms would satisfy the South's 42 million people and bring vast relief to the captive 20 million of the North. But there is no reason for Seoul to rush into any compromise whatsoever with Pyongyang. Patience has paid off well for Seoul so far.

The only gifts worth offering to Pyongyang's Premier Yon this week might be a Korean translation of Adam Smith, and perhaps about 20 million copies of the June 30, 1987, speech in which Roh Tae Woo made his winning proposal for South Korea's transition to democracy.
