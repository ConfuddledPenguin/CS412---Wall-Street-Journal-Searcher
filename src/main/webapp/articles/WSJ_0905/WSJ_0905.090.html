Marketing & Media: Yetnikoff Resigns as Chief Executive Of CBS Records; No Successor Named

  • Author: Laura Landro
  • Date: 09/05/90

NEW YORK -- Walter Yetnikoff, chief executive officer of Sony Corp.'s CBS Records unit, is stepping down immediately, accelerating previous plans to leave his post after two years. Sony didn't name a successor, saying the company's three top executives under Mr. Yetnikoff will continue to operate their divisions while company directors undertake "a long-term succession review."

As previously reported, Sony and CBS Records insiders said last month that Mr. Yetnikoff, 57 years old, signed a new contract specifying that he would step down after two years, helping to groom a successor and remaining as a consultant thereafter. Mr. Yetnikoff at the time denied that he planned to step down, and Sony issued a statement supporting the flamboyant, outspoken executive.

Morever, this week's Billboard, the music industry trade publication, quoted Mr. Yetnikoff denying reports of his departure, and stating that he had a new three-year contract. Billboard said Mr. Yetnikoff would decide at its expiration whether he wanted to leave the company.

A spokesman for Mr. Yetnikoff said he was unavailable yesterday to elaborate on his statement. Although he never publicly announced any intention of leaving, he said in the statement that he has decided to accelerate his previously announced intention to step down as head of the company. "I intend to go on sabbatical now," Mr. Yetnikoff said, adding that after a few months' leave, he will work with Sony president Norio Ohga on "long-term projects affecting the future growth of Sony."

CBS Records' three top executives include Tommy Mottola, president of CBS Records' domestic division; Robert Summer, president of CBS Records International; and Neil Keating, president of Columbia Record Club. Mr. Mottola, a former rock group manager who was brought in by Mr. Yetnikoff, has been regarded by some as the most likely successor, though some industry observers note he has no experience in running a huge international record operation.

The three executives will report directly to Mr. Ohga, who is also chairman of CBS Records.

Some industry executives had speculated that the record company might be put under the control of Peter Guber, chief executive of Sony's Columbia Pictures Entertainment Inc. unit. But people familiar with Sony's plans said that scenario is unlikely. Mr. Guber has his hands full running Columbia's far-flung entertainment operations, and knows virtually nothing about the record business.

In a statement, Mr. Ohga said CBS Records "has never been in better shape, both financially and operationally . . .. This is a good time for Mr. Yetnikoff to focus on his other priorities and for us to continue to build an organization and management structure appropriate to CBS Records future growth potential."

Company insiders said Mr. Yetnikoff wasn't pushed out, but was under increasing pressure to make room for the next generation of managers to advance at the company. In his statement, Mr. Ohga praised Mr. Yetnikoff, who is credited with turning the company into the industry's leading record company. "Mr. Yetnikoff's record speaks for itself," Mr. Ohga said. "The company has grown and prospered under his leadership."
