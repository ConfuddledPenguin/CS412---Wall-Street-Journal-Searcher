Heard on the Street: Investors Tying Strategy to Result Of Super Bowl May Be Shut Out

  • Author: Stanley W. Angrist and John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 12/31/90

Many investors are forever seeking the market equivalent of the Holy Grail. They yearn to discover a pattern that would allow them to pick winning stocks ever after. They long for a technique that would require little or no thinking, yet have a high probability of success.

Three patterns that keep cropping up in Wall Street banter around this time of year are the January effect, the Super Bowl effect and the midterm-election-year effect. All three are discussed below. But first, a cautionary word about patterns in general.

Burton Malkiel, professor of economics at Princeton University, has long asserted that prices of investments tend to move in a random way -- that an investment's price today is not related to yesterday's price. Such price action is frequently described as a "random walk."

Professor Malkiel freely concedes that people can look at historical data and find lots of patterns that work on paper. But trying to profit from such information is difficult.

"Suppose you find something that appears to produce profits," Mr. Malkiel says. "If there is really something there, then investors won't wait for the starting date for the strategy." And this tendency for investors to `jump the gun' will, over time, cause the effect to disappear, says Mr. Malkiel.

Mr. Malkiel concedes that some recent research has shown persistent exceptions to the random-walk model. He argues, however, that such effects are difficult, if not impossible, to profit from, because of transaction costs. These costs -- commissions and the prices investors must pay to buy and sell, the so-called bid-ask spread -- are frequently larger than the effects themselves.

That said, let's zero in on some Wall Street buzzwords.

Midterm elections. In years past, the stock market's behavior has correlated fairly strongly with the presidential election cycle. The year after midterm congressional elections -- the third year of a president's term -- has tended to be a good year for the market.

"Since 1962, stock prices have registered a major bottom in every (midterm) election year without exception," wrote Robert J. Brinker in the Nov. 4 issue of his newsletter, Bob Brinker's Marketimer. Larry Vaden, an individual investor and longtime student of the stock market in Trenton, Texas, says he is planning to invest more aggressively because the midterm election indicator gives him confidence.

Indeed, the Dow Jones industrials have risen in 17 of the past 20 calendar years following the midterm election year. (The data run from 1911 through 1987.) The average gain for all 20 such years was 12.2%.

The industrial average was up an average 17.8% in the 17 profitable years, and was down an average 19.6% in the three losing years. A buy-once-and-hold strategy over the same 77 years would have produced an average 4.2% compounded return, disregarding dividends.

"Presidents have awesome power to sweep dirt under rugs," says Yale Hirsch, editor of the Stock Trader's Almanac. "That is why most bear markets take place in the first two years of a presidential term."

"In the first couple of years of a president's term he has to do nasty things to the economy," says Alfred Goldman, director of market analysis at A.G. Edwards & Son Inc. in St. Louis. "But in the next two years, he is going to do everything in his power to get re-elected or keep his party in power."

That, anyway, is the rationale -- but Mr. Goldman doesn't think it provides any investment guarantees. "I wish life were that easy," he says.

In fact, in the current case, the Bush administration escaped a recession early in the term, but seems to be stuck with one now. Given that the economy isn't following the traditional election-cycle pattern, there's certainly no guarantee that the stock market will.

The January effect is the pattern that receives the most attention, especially around this time of the year.

According to market lore, stocks -- especially those companies with smaller total market values -- tend to rally in January. (In recent years, the rally has often started in December.)

For the years 1980 through 1990, the industrial average rose in seven Januarys out of 11. Smaller stocks, as represented by the Nasdaq Composite Index, outperformed the Dow industrials in five of the seven "up" Januarys.

On the other hand, there were four Januarys (1981, 1982, 1984 and 1990) in which the Dow industrials declined. In each of those four cases, small stocks declined further.

Still, string the 11 Januarys together and the effect looks pretty good. The industrials rose about 27%, and the Nasdaq Composite rose about 38%.

There are a couple of possible explanations for the effect. Portfolio managers may start a new year in a mood to take some chances. As the year progresses, they may become more cautious -- either to protect their gains or to avoid looking truly foolish.

Tax factors are another often-cited explanation. People who have paper losses on stocks often sell late in the year, so they can use the losses to offset gains that would otherwise be taxable. Stocks depressed by such tax-loss selling may rebound smartly in the new year.

This year the small-stock rally was dubbed by some the "Thanksgiving effect" as it started early -- perhaps because of the "gun-jumping" described by Mr. Malkiel, perhaps because of a little-noticed aspect of the 1986 tax law that prodded mutual funds to sell many losing stocks before Oct. 31.

Money managers holding losses on small-cap stocks apparently wanted to reap those losses to offset any gains on other stocks in their portfolios. They knew that shareholders wouldn't take kindly to having to pay capital gains taxes in a year that, on the whole, was bad for stocks. But having sold small stocks early, they also replenished their holdings of such stocks early.

The Nasdaq Composite Index rose 12.9% from Oct. 31 through last Wednesday. Over the same period, the industrial average was up 8%.

For investors, the nagging question this time is: How much January effect is left to happen in January?

Super Bowl Indicator: Few market forecasting techniques appear to have less economic justification than this curio, which depends on the outcome of the Super Bowl. Yet some investors, no doubt, will be glued to their TV sets on Sunday night Jan. 27 not because they have $100 riding on their favorite team but to tell them whether they should continue to hold stocks.

Football used to have two separate leagues, the National Football League and the American Football League. The Super Bowl indicator says that if a team from the old National League wins, the market will rise.

Most old National League teams now are part of the National Football Conference. Three -- the Pittsburgh Steelers, the Cleveland Browns and the Indianapolis (formerly Baltimore) Colts -- now are American Football Conference teams. So bullish believers in the indicator usually root for the NFC over the AFC, but not always.

Until 1984, the Super Bowl predictor had an unblemished record of 17 correct market calls. But in the past seven years it has been right only four times. This year, for example, the NFC's San Francisco 49ers won, but the New York Stock Exchange Composite is down about 7%.
