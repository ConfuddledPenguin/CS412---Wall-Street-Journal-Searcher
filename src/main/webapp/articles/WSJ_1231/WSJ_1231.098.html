REPLY FROM MRS. AQUINO

Philippine President Corazon Aquino said her government would meet conditions set by a Swiss court for the return of money allegedly deposited in Switzerland by the late President Ferdinand Marcos.

In a decision made public last week, the Swiss Supreme Court ruled that the money must be returned as soon as a competent criminal court in the Philippines has issued a "legally binding" verdict in a case against Mr. Marcos and his widow, Imelda. The order set a one-year deadline for the Philippine government to complete legal proceedings.

"The decision of Switzerland's Federal Supreme Court should be hailed as a victory for the Filipino people," Mrs. Aquino said. "For our part, we are confident that we will be able to fulfill the conditions within the time frame available."

The Aquino government claims that Mr. Marcos and his associates embezzled up to $10 billion.

FRANCE RESUMES CHINA CREDIT

France agreed to restore official lines of credit for projects in China managed by French companies that were suspended after China suppressed pro-democracy demonstrations last year. In Paris, an Economics Ministry official said a protocol signed by China and France would release 1.3 billion francs ($253.6 million) of loans to help finance telecommunications and water-treatment projects.

TOKYO PHONE NUMBERS ADD DIGIT

Telephone numbers in Tokyo are adding one digit to become eight-digit numbers, beginning tomorrow at 2 a.m., Japan time. All phone numbers in Tokyo's 03 area code are adding the number "3" before the present seven-digit number. The change affects 6.14 million numbers of the total 7.18 million numbers in Tokyo. The 1.04 million numbers unaffected by the change were previously changed to eight-digit numbers, starting with the digit "5."

Nippon Telegraph & Telephone Corp. is taking the step to increase the numbers available for use.

VENEZUELA CHANGES SAVINGS TAX

Venezuela's Planning Minister Miguel Rodriguez said most savings accounts would be exempt from a new tax planned by the government in 1991. The government plans for the first time to tax interest from personal savings accounts but only when the accounts exceed the equivalent of $10,000. The original plan was to tax accounts above $5,000. The tax is expected to be about 2%.

COSTS BALLOON IN BRAZIL

Inflation in Brazil in December hit 19.39%, up from a November rate of 16.64%, according to the official index. The consumer price index, a separate, unofficial measure, showed an increase of 18.3% in December and a record 1,795% for the year. In December, price increases were led by pharmaceutical products, which rose 50%. The government ordered an investigation of what it called "perverse" increases by drug companies, and the industry last week voluntarily agreed to freeze its prices for 45 days. Also contributing to the December jump were rents, which rose 38%, and bus fares, which climbed 22%.

RECALL OF JAPANESE COSMETICS

Shiseido Co., a Japanese cosmetics maker, said its Australian and Singapore subsidiaries are recalling six skin-care products containing urocanic acid, an ultraviolet-absorbing material that an Australian researcher linked to skin cancer. A Shiseido spokesman said urocanic acid has been approved as safe by Japan, the U.S. and other nations. Shiseido doesn't plan to recall the products elsewhere. The spokesman said an Australian government advisory on Dec. 18 urged a recall; while no similar action was taken in Singapore, the local subsidiary decided on the recall after media coverage of the Australian action.

BEIJING RAISES SOME PRICES

Beginning tomorrow, residents of Beijing will have to pay double the current fare for bus and subway tickets and up to 10 times today's price to stroll in a park as the city tries to reduce subsidies.

The new rates still fail to cover the city's costs to run the public-transit and parks systems, the Beijing Evening News said. But it didn't indicate whether further increases were likely soon.

Even for Chinese, the new rates remain relatively low. A bus ride across the central city will cost three cents, for example, or less than 3% of the average worker's daily wages. Most city parks have charged less than one cent for entry and now will charge nine cents.

China has selectively increased prices over the past decade in an effort to reduce the burden of subsidies. But authorities have failed to overhaul the entire price system, as economists have urged, for fear of causing social unrest.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

The Italian Treasury is issuing a 1,000-lire bill with a picture of educator Maria Montessori, replacing the traditional portrait of explorer Marco Polo. A thousand lire are worth about 86 cents. . . . Argentine soccer star Diego Maradona was voted Italy's most hated person in a year-end poll conducted for La Repubblica newspaper. Mr. Maradona, who is captain of the Napoli team, was called "a troublemaker, a hypocrite and a sower of discord." He headed a list including Iraqi President Saddam Hussein, President Bush and rock singer Madonna.
