Technology: Data General Scuttles NTT Data Gear Pact --- Computer Maker, Focusing On Cost Cuts, New Line, Quietly Drops Accord

  • Author: John R. Wilke
  • Date: 12/31/90

Data General Corp., working to cut costs and focus its resources on a new computer line, quietly dropped a high-profile product-development agreement with Nippon Telegraph & Telephone Corp. Separately, Chief Executive Officer Ronald Skates disclosed that the company has cut its work force more deeply than had been expected, eliminating about 2,400 jobs, or 20% more than the 2,000 jobs Data General had said it would cut this year. By January, the payroll will total about 9,300, down from a peak of 17,000 in 1985, he estimated.

The agreement with NTT to build high-speed hubs for private data communications networks was unveiled with great fanfare in a Commerce Department-sponsored press conference in 1987, at a time of acute U.S.-Japan trade tension over technology issues.

The project, code-named "asparagus" because it was supposed to take two years to grow, apparently fell victim to engineering delays and five years of losses and slow sales at Data General. The move completes the Westborough, Mass., computer maker's withdrawal from the data communications equipment business after investing millions of dollars in the failed effort.

Although Data General never disclosed the value of the NTT contract, NTT executives said at the time of the announcement that the company planned to buy over a five-year period starting in 1989 some $130 million of equipment to be developed by Data General. The communications systems were to link voice, data and computers in large, private communications networks such as those used by corporations and universities. Analysts said at the time that the revenue could eventually be significant for Data General.

Barry Willman, an analyst with Sanford C. Bernstein & Co., says that killing the project now will help focus Data General on its new Aviion workstations, which he called "the company's future." He added that the NTT project had "stalled" and revenue expectations for the fruit of the venture had been "severely diminished."

Mr. Skates, the chief executive, said in an interview that the financial impact of withdrawing from the data communications equipment market "is already behind us." He said the company "still has a strong business relationship with NTT in other areas." He added that delays "on both sides" combined with a changing market to reduce the product's potential.

In a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Data General said the agreement was terminated Dec. 5 "at its request."

Mr. Willman estimates Data General will lose about 15 cents a share, or $5 million, on revenue of $298 million for the fiscal first quarter ended Dec. 30. In the year-earlier first quarter, the company had a net loss of $20.5 million, or 69 cents a share, on a 6% decline in revenue to $290.7 million.

Data General's deep cost-cutting had prompted some analysts to predict that the company could break even as early as its fiscal second quarter. But most now foresee a loss for at least two more quarters.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Data General stock closed at $4.25 a share, down 12.5 cents.
