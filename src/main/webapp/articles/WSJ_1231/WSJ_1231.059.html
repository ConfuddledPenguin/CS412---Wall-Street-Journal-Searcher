Air-to-Ground Phone Field Gains 4 Firms

  • Author: Brett Pulley
  • Date: 12/31/90

The Federal Communications Commission cleared four new companies to provide air-to-ground telephone service, setting the stage for the first-ever competition in the fledgling industry. GTE Corp.'s Airfone Inc. unit, currently the only company providing aircraft telephone service, will now face potential challenges from four companies the FCC has allowed to enter the field: In-Flight Phone Corp., Clairtel Communications Group L.P., Mobile Telecommunications Technologies Corp., and American Skycell Corp.

Airfone's immediate competition promises to come from In-Flight, which has already spent $8 million developing state-ofthe-art services that it says will include enhanced reception, fax machine capabilities and video games.

In-Flight's entrance into the airplane phone service business will pit communications entrepreneur John Goeken against his one-time employer.

Mr. Goeken founded Airfone in 1984, but two years later sold his brainchild to GTE. Complaining that the communications giant was usurping his authority, Mr. Goeken left Airfone early in 1989. Since then, he has been locked in a legal battle with GTE, in part over his attempts to set up a rival airplane phone service.

In August of this year, an Illinois court ruled that Mr. Goeken was bound by a noncompete agreement he had signed with GTE; since then, Mr. Goeken's daughter, Sandra Goeken, has officially headed In-Flight and his wife has assumed ownership of his majority of the company's stock.

Individuals close to the company said Friday that a court settlement is expected "in very short order," that will allow Mr. Goeken to resume his post as In-Flight's chairman and chief executive officer.

An Airfone spokesman said he has "no knowledge" of an agreement that would free Mr. Goeken to compete.

For the 60-year-old Mr. Goeken, who is best-known for founding MCI Communications Corp. in the 1960s, the FCC license and his reinstatement as chairman would offer him the opportunity to once again break new ground in the communications industry. Closely held In-Flight, of Oak Brook, Ill., plans to swiftly implement a digital aircraft telephone transmission system, which it contends is superior to the analog system Airfone uses.

"We took the chance that the FCC would approve this and we went ahead and spent $8 million to develop the system," Mr. Goeken said in an interview.

In-Flight said it plans to have a limited number of phones installed in aircraft owned by American Airlines, Northwest Airlines, and USAir within about four months. "We'll expand from there as the airlines want," a spokesman said.

Clairtel, formed through a limited partnership with McCaw Cellular Communications Inc. and Hughes Network Systems, will initially install systems similar to Airfone's, but also plans eventually to introduce advanced technology. The other newly licensed companies, which entered after Mr. Goeken petitioned the FCC to open the field to competition, aren't as far along.

The FCC's approval of permanent licenses for the four new companies comes on the heels of an FCC ruling this year that formally established aircraft telephones as a regular communications service, and thus opened up the frequency spectrum to competitors.

The development ended what has been a monopoly for Airfone, which continues to operate under an experimental FCC license, granted in 1984, when it began installing phones in aircraft.

An Airfone spokesman said the company expects to receive permanent licensing following further FCC analysis. "It is much more difficult to grant our license than that of a new company," the spokesman said.

Airfone currently has phones installed and operating in more than 1,400 planes -- about one-third of the U.S. domestic fleet.
