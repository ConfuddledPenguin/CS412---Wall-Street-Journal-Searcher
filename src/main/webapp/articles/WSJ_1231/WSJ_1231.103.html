U.S. Justice Department Seeks to Block Sale of Semiconductor Firm to Japanese

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 12/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration said it would try to block on antitrust grounds the acquisition of a U.S. semiconductor-equipment company by Nippon Sanso KK of Japan. The action comes even though President Bush earlier had approved the deal as posing no threat to national security.

The Justice Department said Friday that it would file a civil antitrust lawsuit seeking to block the proposed sale by Hercules Inc. of Wilmington, Del., of its SemiGas Systems unit to Tokyo-based Nippon Sanso. Critics of the controversial transaction, including many Democrats in Congress, have said it would hurt the struggling U.S. semiconductor industry.

Semi-Gas provides crucial chip-manufacturing services to Sematech, a Pentagon-supported consortium of U.S. semiconductor manufacturers and other electronic companies.

A spokesman for Hercules said that the chemical-maker would fight the Justice Department move. The spokesman, Robert Hessler, said the company's lawyers had received "only verbal notification" that the department plans to seek an injunction. "But based on that, we believe the government's claim has no merit or basis, and accordingly, Hercules is prepared to defend any claims brought by the department," he said. Hercules agreed to sell Semi-Gas for $23 million.

The Justice Department said the proposed sale would violate federal antitrust law by combining the two leading producers of gas cabinets in the world and "substantially {increasing} Semi-Gas Systems' dominant position" in the U.S. market. Gas cabinets are used to distribute specialty gases for semiconductor fabrication. The Justice Department didn't specify in which federal court it planned to file suit or when it would do so.

The proposed sale "may substantially lessen competition in the production and sale of gas cabinets in the United States," said James Rill, the Justice Department's antitrust chief.

In July, President Bush provoked criticism from congressional Democrats when he endorsed the sale of Semi-Gas contingent on certain conditions for protecting sensitive semiconductor research. Sen. Albert Gore Jr. (D., Tenn.) attacked the administration for doing a "lackadaisical" job of screening foreign takeovers of U.S. high-technology assets. The interagency panel that advises the president on such takeovers has approved almost every transaction it has reviewed since late 1988.

Mr. Rill denied that the administration had taken conflicting positions on foreign takeovers of U.S. high-technology assets. "The two actions were totally unrelated," he said. "They do not depend at all on the same statutes or on the same considerations. . . . This is a very straightforward {antitrust} case."

A Sematech spokesman praised the Justice Department for its promised legal action. "Our concern is that another American technological jewel not fall into the hands of a foreign competitor," said Mark Nelson, director of government affairs for the Austin, Texas, consortium. Sematech claims that Japanese companies currently don't have certain Semi-Gas technology.

Executives of Semi-Gas earlier had failed to raise enough money to match Nippon Sanso's offer for the Hercules subsidiary. A person familiar with the situation said Friday that in recent months "the employees' group has been trying to put their deal back together" in an effort to prevent a takeover by the Japanese company.
