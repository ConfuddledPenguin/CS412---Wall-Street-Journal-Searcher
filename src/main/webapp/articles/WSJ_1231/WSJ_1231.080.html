First Boston Giveth, But -- Before 1994 -- It Can Taketh Back --- Big Securities Firm Awards Fat Raises but Attaches Long Strings to Bonuses

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 12/31/90

NEW YORK -- CS First Boston Inc. has a New Year's gift for hundreds of senior employees: a fat salary raise. But the employees are far from thrilled. That's because late last week they also were required to sign an agreement that authorizes the big securities firm under certain circumstances to yank back as much as 40% of their 1990 cash bonuses.

The raise, which is effective in 1991, increases directors' base salaries to between $150,000 and $200,000 from the current $100,000. Managing directors will receive $200,000 to $250,000, up from the current $150,000, people familiar with the firm said.

But here's the hitch: Salary alone typically is just a tiny part of overall compensation. Even in a down year, such as 1990, a bonus may exceed 80% of total compensation. And bonuses at CS First Boston are down roughly 40% this year, according to the firm's executives.

Making matters more difficult, employees say, is the agreement they recently were asked to sign. That agreement, given to employees on Dec. 20 and required to be signed by Dec. 27, stipulated that those who quit before the end of 1992 must give 40% of their 1990 cash bonus back to CS First Boston. If they leave within three years, they also give back all or part of their 1990 bonus that they received in CS First Boston stock, which made up about half their 1990 compensation package.

"The net of the equation is that overall compensation is going to be factored down at a time when {employees are} facing a potential penalty" for leaving, says Jeffrey L. Liddle, an attorney with the New York firm Liddle, O'Connor, Finkelstein & Robinson who represents securities-firm employees.

CS First Boston officials declined to comment on the compensation changes or agreement.

The firm's salary strategy is part of a plan under which Switzerland's CS Holding agreed last month to pump $300 million in equity into its CS First Boston unit, parent of investment bank First Boston Corp.

Mr. Liddle also contends that employees didn't have enough time to consider the accord's ramifications. "They gave this thing to people to sign over the Christmas holiday," making it more difficult to get a private attorney and without much time to think about it, he says.

First Boston officials privately retort that employees have known about the proposed changes informally for several weeks.

In a separate internal memo, the firm said employees who aren't asked to sign an employment agreement will be paid at least 60% of their cash bonus in the next two weeks. "Any remaining cash payments are scheduled to be paid on or about February 15, 1991," the memo said.
