SEC Staff Says Exchanges Improved Performance Between '87, '89 Crashes

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 12/31/90

WASHINGTON -- Experience pays. That, in brief, is the conclusion the Securities and Exchange Commission staff reached after analyzing how securities exchanges coped with the stock market's mini-crash Oct. 13, 1989, when the Dow Jones Industrial Average plunged 190.58 points in the final 90 minutes of trading. The study found that the exchanges had repaired many of the weaknesses revealed by the far scarier market crash two years earlier, when the industrials dropped 508 points in one day.

Nevertheless, the study by the SEC's division of market regulation found room for improvement. Exchanges should consider several changes "to improve their ability to function as efficiently and effectively in turbulent markets as in stable markets," the study said. In particular, the report said it found some "very troubling" conditions in the markets for index options, which allow speculators and hedgers to bet on price changes in stock indexes, such as the Standard & Poor's 500.

The report recommended that the New York and American stock exchanges develop more objective standards for measuring the performance of specialists, who make markets in stocks by balancing orders to buy and sell shares. The agency repeated its concern that specialists need more capital.

Exchange officials reached after the report was issued Friday said they hadn't seen the 173-page report and couldn't comment on it.

In general, the study contains reassuring news about the steps exchanges have taken to maintain fair and orderly markets in the face of sudden surges in trading volume and sharp price fluctuations.

Market performance "has improved substantially during periods of high price volatility and trading volume as a result of the implementation of many of the recommendations" following the crash of Black Monday, Oct. 19, 1987. Acting on post-crash studies, the exchanges bolstered their operational systems and took steps to prevent highly volatile markets from spinning out of control.

Although options exchanges performed "significantly better" in 1989 than two years earlier, "concerns about the ability of the options exchanges to provide a continuous market in stock-index options during extreme market conditions, especially when the primary stock-index futures contracts have ceased trading, resurfaced during October 1989," the report said.

To provide more reliable trading conditions for index options in highly volatile markets, the SEC staff recommended that exchanges consider automatically halting index-option trading when markets in the index futures are closed temporarily by the "circuit breakers" designed to stabilize turbulent markets. In addition, the report recommended better procedures for reopening trading in index options and higher performance standards for specialists on options exchanges.

This is the second report the SEC staff has released on the 1989 mini-crash. A study released in May looked at trading patterns.
