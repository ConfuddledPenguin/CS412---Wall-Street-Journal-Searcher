Law -- Legal Beat: Rehnquist Urges Tougher Rules On Handling of Interstate Lawsuits

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel and Amy Stevens
  • Date: 12/31/90

Chief Justice William Rehnquist urged Congress to make it more difficult for individuals or businesses to sue one another in federal court over matters of state law. In his annual year-end report on the federal judiciary, the chief justice also renewed his plea to Congress to make changes in 1991 in the system of death penalty appeals. He said federal law should ensure that lawyers are available to handle the appeals of death row inmates, while "assuring to the states the necessary degree of finality."

The first proposal involves what is known as "diversity" jurisdiction, a federal law that allows lawsuits between citizens of different states to be filed in federal court, even though no issue of federal law is involved. In May 1989, Congress amended the law to require that only disputes involving at least $50,000 can be considered by federal courts. This increase -- from $10,000, the amount required for the previous 30 years -- caused the number of such cases filed in federal court to drop to 57,000 this year, compared with 67,000 in 1989, Chief Justice Rehnquist said.

The chief justice proposed that, at the very least, Congress should raise the threshold to $75,000. He didn't mention more drastic proposals to throw all such cases out of federal court and leave them to the state courts, although he has endorsed broader measures in the past.

His comments on the death penalty reflect his disappointment that Congress dropped efforts to change the system of appeals to federal court by death row inmates convicted in state courts. Many law enforcement officials blame these appeals, called habeas corpus petitions, for delaying state executions. Chief Justice Rehnquist has said the system should be streamlined by setting strict limits on the number of such appeals and the amount of time after which they can't be filed. But the proposals became mired in controversy this year and were dropped from a crime bill.

INSURERS ORDERED to pay defense costs in patent suit.

A federal judge in Los Angeles ordered three insurance companies to pay $500,000 in attorneys' fees to a company that is defending itself against a patent infringement suit.

The ruling appears to follow a trend of holding carriers liable for policyholders' legal expenses in other kinds of cases.

Lawyers for the company, Intex Plastics Sales Co., Long Beach, Calif., said the decision will be a boon to companies seeking relief from costly intellectual property litigation. "Now people who are sued for patent infringement can remain in the marketplace because they will have the backing of a large commercial insurer," said one of the lawyers, Daniel J. Callahan of Irvine, Calif.

New York patent lawyer Steven D. Glazer said the court's ruling is likely to encourage similar suits in other states. "If the trend continues, this could result in insurance carriers rethinking potential losses and may affect the reserves they'll have to maintain to cover those losses, which can only exacerbate problems at insurance companies already in financial trouble," he said.

The issue arose when Intex, which makes waterbeds, sued the providers of its commercial general liability policy, claiming that a clause covering "advertising injury" applied to legal fees in patent suits. Federal District Judge Consuelo B. Marshall agreed.

"If I were an insurer with a policy out there like the one litigated in this case, I'd be having a heart attack right now," said David J. Lee, a New York patent lawyer who wasn't involved.

But Mr. Lee noted that federal courts in other states, including New York, haven't been willing to interpret similar clauses as broadly, resulting in "an area of major dispute" likely to be resolved only at the appellate court level.

Lawyers for the insurance companies, United National Insurance Co., First State Insurance Co., and New England Reinsurance Co., couldn't be reached for comment.

WIDOW CAN BE DENIED federal aid for lack of a marriage license, court rules.

A federal appeals court ruled that the Social Security Administration properly canceled federal widow benefits that were being paid to a 74-year-old New York woman who had lived with her husband for 47 years.

The woman, Gertrude Thomas, began receiving the benefits when her husband died in 1985. But a year later the Social Security Department concluded that her marriage had been invalid because evidence of a marriage ceremony couldn't be found and because Joseph Thomas apparently had never divorced his first wife.

The U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals ruled that federal law does allow benefits to be paid to a widow whose marriage turns out to have been invalid -- but only if a marriage ceremony was held. Living as common-law husband and wife for 47 years -- and having 10 children, as in Ms. Thomas's case -- doesn't qualify the woman for benefits, the court in Manhattan said.

Ms. Thomas said the marriage was performed by a minister in Georgia in 1938 when she was 21 and Mr. Thomas was 45, but she couldn't prove it. She told officials that she never verified whether a marriage license was issued "because I was quite young at the time and I just took what {Mr. Thomas} said to be true."

The appeals-court panel said the 1960 law covering the benefits fails to mention widows of common-law marriages.

Some states grant extensive rights to common-law spouses. But the court said Congress may have "believed that persons who have doubts about whether a prior marriage has been validly ended will be more reluctant to go through a marriage ceremony than they will to enter into an informal cohabitational relationship."

Ms. Thomas's attorneys couldn't be reached for comment.

SEXUAL HARASSMENT suit filed by EEOC is settled by law firm.

The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission sued Paul, Hastings, Janofsky & Walker in October for alleged sexual discrimination in the Los Angeles firm's New York office. The civil suit, filed in federal court in Manhattan, alleged that women employees were subjected to sexual harassment and threats, and then punished for complaining to partners at the firm.

Under the settlement, the firm didn't admit any wrongdoing, but agreed to fully abide by federal sexual-discrimination laws. The law firm also agreed to provide training about the problem of sexual harassment to all its attorneys. Other details about the settlement weren't disclosed.

"The matter has been satisfactorily resolved," said Paul Hastings partner Dennis H. Vaughn.

COLUMBIA LAW faculty selects new dean.

The faculty of Columbia University School of Law chose Harvard Law School Prof. Lance M. Liebman, 49 years old, to succeed Dean Barbara A. Black, who announced in April that she would step down at the end of the academic year to return to teaching and research at Columbia.

A Columbia spokesman confirmed that the dean search committee had relayed a faculty recommendation to the university president but declined to comment further. The spokesman said the president and the board of trustees must still approve the selection.

Columbia has traditionally chosen its dean from within the school's ranks. The two other finalists were Columbia professors Harvey J. Goldschmid and Peter L. Strauss, according to people familiar with the process.

Vincent A. Blasi, a professor and chairman of the dean search committee, said he couldn't comment while the process is under way.

Mr. Liebman has been a professor at Harvard since 1970. He began teaching there three years after graduating from the law school. He teaches employment and property law and was associate dean of the law school from 1981 to 1984. Mr. Liebman declined to comment.
