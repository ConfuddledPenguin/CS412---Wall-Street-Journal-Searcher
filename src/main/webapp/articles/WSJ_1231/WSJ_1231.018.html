Letters to the Editor: Look Deeper: Fear Of Drugs Is Still High

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/31/90

Your Nov. 19 page-one article about drugs and public opinion revealed the persistence of the drug crisis but understated Americans' resolve to confront the problem. You described a "falloff in public concern about drugs" and cited evidence from a Wall Street Journal/NBC poll. But you should be careful in references to opinion polls. When someone is surveyed, they are usually asked two kinds of questions. One is open-ended, asking the respondent to list what he or she thinks are the most critical issues facing the nation. The other kind of question is more structured, asking respondents to rate a list of issues on a scale, for example, from "unimportant" to "crucial." Answers to the open-ended questions can change week to week, depending on current events. Answers to the rating-type questions provide a more accurate picture of people's long-term concerns.

According to Gordon Black, the pollster your reporter interviewed, people's responses to the structured questions about drugs "haven't changed at all." Americans consistently rate drugs as a crucial issue that will require long-term attention by government. However, if someone were asked today to state the most important issue facing the nation, he most likely would say the Persian Gulf crisis or the impending recession. In other words, the drug crisis is no less important to Americans that it was a year ago. There simply are a greater number of critical issues today.

I agree that as crises recede from the middle class, they often become "ghettoized," as you call it, and fall from public view. As acceptance and casual use of drugs decrease, we run the risk of complacency with the current state of anti-drug efforts. But the drop-off in drug acceptance has not just occurred among the white middle class. Mr. Black maintains that African-Americans and Hispanics have changed their attitudes as much, if not more, than white Americans. The drug crisis hinges on the ever-increasing number of hard-core addicts of all classes and races.

Drugs directly affect a number of issues, such as crime, AIDS and health care, that Americans rank high in importance. References to opinion polls often do not show the interrelatedness of these topics and therefore fail to reveal the extent of people's concern.

Rep. Charles B. Rangel (D., N.Y.)

Washington, D.C.
