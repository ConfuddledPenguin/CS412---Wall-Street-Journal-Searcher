Marketing & Media: Offer for MCA Is Completed By Matsushita

  • Author: Richard Turner
  • Date: 12/31/90

Matsushita Electric Industrial Co. completed its $6.59 billion tender offer for MCA Inc., accomplishing the year's largest acquisition and the biggest takeover yet of an American company by a Japanese one. By receiving about 97% of MCA's outstanding shares, the consumer electronics giant becomes the majority shareholder. A technical merger is expected to be achieved within two weeks, after various legal filings.

Since a buy-out agreement was announced Nov. 26, attorneys for the two companies were able to plow smoothly through various government approvals and legal challenges. Completing the tender offer by the end of the year allows the merger to be treated as a 1990 taxable event. MCA holders who tendered their shares by last Friday, Dec. 28, will be able to declare their capital gains for this year even though their payment checks will be dated Jan. 3.

Merger talks began late last summer, and endured only two rocky moments. One was when the news broke in late September, which dismayed the Japanese and "threw the process into chaos for two weeks," recalls a principal. Later, during a furious Thanksgiving week of negotiations, talks nearly broke down over the price. Matsushita was persuaded to raise its bid to $66 a share plus equity in a spunoff TV station, WWOR of Secaucus, N.J., thought to be worth about $5 per MCA share. This final price was still less than MCA, based in Universal City, Calif., is said to have expected two months before.

Five shareholder suits were filed in state court in Delaware and Los Angeles, charging that MCA management accepted an unsatisfactory price in order to entrench management. These lawsuits were tentatively settled, the plaintiffs receiving attorneys' fees and a loosening of anti-takeover provisions regarding the TV station, which is expected to be sold. Shares of the station, called Pinelands Inc., begin trading today on the New York Stock Exchange.

Another suit in federal court in Los Angeles charges that MCA Chairman Lew Wasserman's payout from the acquisition violates federal securities laws mandating equal treatment for all shareholders. Mr. Wasserman is to receive preferred stock rather than cash from Matsushita because many of his nearly five million shares were acquired at a low-cost basis and would have been subject to severe capital gains taxes. U.S. District Court Judge Manuel Real denied a motion for summary judgment Friday, refusing to grant a preliminary injunction to block the merger. The suit, now seeking damages, is still pending.

Also on Friday, U.S. Supreme Court Justice Antonin Scalia refused to block the merger as requested by an antitrust suit filed in federal court in Phoenix by Go-Video Inc., of Scottsdale, Ariz. A shareholder suit filed in federal court in New York was dismissed: That suit charged that the buy-out violated the constitutional rights of American citizens by allowing foreign ownership of a U.S. media company.

All in all, no serious obstacles arose to consummating the transaction. By its own admission, Matsushita was nervous about a political uproar over a Japanese takeover of a famous American movie studio. But reaction was muted. Politicians in Washington seemed distracted by events in the Persian Gulf, and few were willing to challenge MCA Chairman Lew Wasserman's wishes. MCA was in many ways Mr. Wasserman's own company -- he had built it for 54 years -- and he has powerful political connections. MCA and Matsushita assembled a host of Washington insiders to lobby for the transaction.

Similarly, because of the high price and again perhaps because of a reluctance to take on Mr. Wasserman, no other companies joined in bidding for MCA or criticized the transaction too vocally.
