The Outlook: Dollar Depreciation Has Its Limits

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 12/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The greenback has fallen dramatically in the past year to its lowest level in years. Yet the U.S. still is running a persistent trade deficit of about $100 billion annually. In fact, the latest trade statistics, for October, showed a worrying 25% deterioration in the trade account, to a monthly deficit of $11.6 billion from $9.3 billion in September. This defies conventional economic wisdom. A plunging dollar should at least begin to correct the trade imbalance. What's wrong?

Oil and autos. Period.

Excepting those, the trade imbalance has disappeared, thanks in part to the depreciating dollar.

But there are limits to depreciation. It can do nothing to counter the economic effects of Saddam Hussein's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait, which pushed up oil prices sharply to more than $30 a barrel during the fall. Even now, oil is hovering around $26 a barrel, up from $18 a barrel early this year. Because of higher oil prices, the nation's oil deficit rose to $6.4 billion in October from $5.5 billion in September.

A resolution of the Gulf conflict should lead to a large drop in oil prices, probably to as low as $20 a barrel, says James Fralick, an economist at Morgan Stanley & Co. in New York. That, in turn, would knock as much as $85 billion off the trade deficit, he estimates. "The run up in oil prices has camouflaged most of the improvement that has gone on in the trade account," he says.

The trade role of oil imports is massive. They represented 53% of the overall deficit of $85.9 billion in the first 10 months of 1990.

Massive as well is the trade role of automobiles, which are also the largest consumers of oil. In the same 10 months, the deficit in cars and automotive products -- the other major component of the trade deficit -- was about $42.2 billion.

Looking ahead, if deficits in oil and cars plus auto parts are taken out, the trade account is projected to reach a surplus of about $20 billion in 1991. That's a $60 billion improvement from a deficit of about $40 billion for the non-oil, non-auto trade account in 1987.

There are further benefits still to filter through from past depreciation of the dollar.

"Over the coming months, there should be some more improvement in the trade account," says John Williamson of the Institute of International Economics, a think tank here. He and his colleagues have stopped advocating dollar depreciation. In his organization, at least, "I don't think there's anybody now who thinks it should go down further," he says.

In fact, there may be risks to promoting further depreciation. "The potential costs of further depreciation -- such as inflation, a loss of confidence in U.S. financial assets and other distortions -- far outweigh any theoretical benefits," says Lawrence Kudlow, an economist with Bear, Stearns & Co.

Depreciation also carries political costs. Some in Europe, especially in France, see it as predatory, a sign that the U.S. is subsidizing its exports and resorting to a beggar-thy-neighbor exchange-rate policy. This view has made it harder for the U.S. to get its trading partners to drop their own protectionist habits.

Further depreciation also won't solve the nation's basic economic problems. The U.S. still hasn't achieved the productivity gains, the cutbacks in consumption and the increases in savings that it needs for a competitive economy over the long haul.

"The U.S. has to correct its overconsumption, underinvestment, undersavings mentality," says Rimmer de Vries, a managing director of Morgan Guaranty Trust Co. "U.S. policy makers," he adds, "could do more to encourage private savings and stimulate equity and plant-and-equipment investment, rather than real estate investment."

Further depreciation also is no substitute for an energy policy that emphasizes less importing of oil, for even if oil prices should fall the U.S. would still face a significant deficit in oil trade. The U.S. could look to its supplies of natural gas, "an abundant and environmentally clean natural resource that is highly underutilized," says Mr. de Vries. "A breakthrough in this area would strengthen greatly the U.S. trade balance, the dollar, real incomes and net savings."

Finally, further depreciation wouldn't address the necessity of getting a new, strong accord under the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade. Only such an agreement will give the U.S. the kind of access it needs to international markets to allow its competitive companies to export freely.

A lawless trading system wouldn't be a pretty sight. In recent weeks, for example, Japanese securities firms reportedly have been doing their best to block some firms there from using the services of Salomon Brothers Inc. for securities transactions. And South Korea has recently shown an increasing willingness to pursue protectionism against U.S. and other foreign companies.

All the dollar depreciation in the world wouldn't begin to help stop such activities.
