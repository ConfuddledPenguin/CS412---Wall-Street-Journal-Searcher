OTC Focus: NASD Set To Propose Uptick Rule --- Association Asserts Companies Want It Citing Fear of Shorts

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 12/31/90

NEW YORK -- The self-regulatory body for the over-the-counter stock market is considering imposing a new rule designed to make it tougher for bearish investors to spark a big sell-off in an OTC stock. The National Association of Securities Dealers today is mailing out requests for public comment on whether to impose the new curb, known as an "uptick rule."

Already in place on the competing New York and American stock exchanges, uptick rules are designed to curb abuses of short-selling -- the practice of betting that a stock's price will fall rather than rise. Short-sellers borrow stock and immediately sell it, hoping to profit later when they purchase cheaper shares to return to the lender.

Critics of short-selling have long charged that the bad-news bears can trigger a price avalanche by selling heavily just as prices are falling. Under an uptick rule, short-selling is forbidden except at moments when the last previous trade in a stock was higher -- that is, made on an uptick.

"We are getting a growing number of requests from companies and investors" for an uptick rule or similar test, says NASD President Joseph R. Hardiman. "They have the perception -- and I suggest it is just that -- that it's easier to sell short a Nasdaq-listed security than a comparable exchange-listed security." Nasdaq is the computerized market that handles OTC trades.

"We have lost some companies to the exchanges this year because of the absence of a comparable uptick test," Mr. Hardiman adds, declining to name those companies. Nearly 75 companies defected to the exchanges from Nasdaq this year, less than 2% of all Nasdaq companies and about the same rate as last year.

The Securities and Exchange Commission first imposed an uptick rule on the major exchanges in 1938 following public outcry over stock-price swoons of the Depression era. This year, regulators have faced renewed pressure to monitor short-selling, as bank stocks among others have become popular targets. The total amount of stock sold short on the two major exchanges and Nasdaq hit record levels this fall, reflecting widespread gloom about the economy and the Mideast crisis.

The NASD's move comes as both the SEC and lawmakers are dusting off proposals to control short-selling abuses. Next month, the SEC staff will present the full commission with a year-old suggestion that short-sellers be required to publicly disclose short positions of 5% or more of a company's common shares outstanding, said Richard Ketchum, director of the SEC's division of market regulation.

A draft bill embracing a similar provision is expected to be introduced in the next session of Congress by Rep. Doug Barnard (D., Ga.), and prompted the SEC review, Mr. Ketchum said. The Association of Publicly Traded Companies, a Washington trade group of smaller companies, has actively lobbied for the disclosure requirement. Mr. Ketchum, however, noted that he has previously questioned whether such a restriction would be effective enough to justify the expense of implementing it.

Investors, company officials, regulators and traders are divided on whether further requlation of short-sellers would discourage some professionals from ganging up on OTC stocks.

An uptick rule "would prevent people from unmercifully banging a stock down, which creates its own version of panic among regular holders," said John LaPorte, manager of T. Rowe Price Associates' New Horizons Fund of Baltimore. "I'm happy to hear it's going to be considered."

Others, however, question how effective the limit has been on exchange-listed shares. "The general consensus indicates that the uptick rule really doesn't prevent (abusive) shorting," said John Guion, president of the Association of Publicly Traded Companies. A 1976 SEC staff study of the so-called "tick test" concluded that it was costly and ineffective.

"Its most important benefit would be to keep the exchanges from beating Nasdaq over the head with the fact that they have an uptick rule and Nasdaq doesn't," Mr. Guion added.

Two companies that now trade on the New York Stock Exchange say they haven't seen any noticeable change in the heavy short-selling of their stocks since they left Nasdaq.

"It didn't seem to make much of a difference for us," says a spokeswoman for L.A. Gear, which reported a short position of more than 6.2 million shares in mid-December -- more than one-third of the shares of the athletic-footwear maker. The short position in L.A. Gear dropped more than 22% within six months after the company's 1989 listing on the New York exchange, but resumed climbing this year as the company's 1990 earnings fell below the previous year's. At Friday's close of 13 5/8, down 3/4, L.A. Gear is trading at less than a third of the 50 3/8 peak reached in June.

Golden Valley Microwave, which made headlines starting in 1988 as one of the most popular short-selling targets in the OTC market, still attracts bears. "If you look at the activity and the short position, it's actually greater now than it was a couple of years ago," says a spokesman for the Minneapolis maker of microwave-oven foods. Since it peaked at 25 7/8 in June, Golden Valley's stock has toppled to Friday's close of 10 1/4, down 3/8, as the company warned of flat earnings.

Traders, who have no love for increased regulation and who earn commissions from the shorts' trades, aren't lobbying for any uptick rule for the OTC market. "It wouldn't make a difference," says E.E. "Buzz" Geduld, president of Herzog, Heine & Geduld in New York. "People still short stocks on the New York exchange" even with its uptick rule, he says.

The nation's largest short-selling pool is increasingly active in exchange-listed stocks, says Joseph Feshbach, who oversees the nearly $1 billion pool of short positions for Stockbridge Partners of Palo Alto, Calif. "We're short a lot of exchange-listed stocks now and it hasn't made one bit of difference," he said.

Mr. Feshbach conceded that an uptick rule would limit the number of transactions his firm could execute on the OTC market. But he questioned whether the rule could be easily implemented. Unlike the auction-type market or the exchanges, where orders are funneled to one specialist and result in single "last" trades, the OTC market consists of multiple negotiations among dealers.

Friday's Market Activity

Over-the-counter stocks edged higher in moderately active year-end trading.

The Nasdaq Composite Index inched up 0.15 to 371.20, a 0.04% gain, but ended the week 0.6% lower. Trading accelerated Friday to 132.3 million shares from 102.7 million Thursday. Declining issues outpaced advancers, 994 to 874.

The OTC's most active issues were mixed. MCI Communications, the day's most active stock, eased 1/4 to 18 3/4, Sun Microsystems fell 1/4 to 21 7/8, United Healthcare slipped 1/8 to 23 and Apple Computer declined 1/2 to 43.

CoreStates Financial, the day's second most active stock, closed unchanged at 31 5/8; Oracle Systems rose 3/8 to 8 and Intel added 7/8 to 37 7/8.

VeloBind skidded 2 1/8 to 7. The U.S. Department of Justice confirmed that it will ask a federal court to block General Binding's proposed $9.77-a-share acquisition of VeloBind, on antitrust grounds. VeloBind is a Fremont, Calif., maker of book and document binding equipment and General Binding, which fell 1 to 15, is a Northbrook, Ill., maker of business machines and supplies.

Great Lakes Bancorp slipped 1 1/2 to 8. The Ann Arbor, Mich., savings bank said that it plans to add between $15 million and $18 million to its reserves and to separately write off a portion of its good will. As a result, the thrift said that it expects to report a fourth-quarter loss and a loss for the year.

Applied Biosystems jumped 2 to 11 1/4. Merrill Lynch biotechnology analyst Stuart Weisbrod upgraded his intermediate-term investment opinion on the Foster City, Calif., biotechnology company to "above average" from "neutral," citing the increased demand he expects for the company's instruments.
