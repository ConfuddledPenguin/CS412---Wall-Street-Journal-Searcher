Foreign Exchange: Sustained Dollar Recovery Is Unlikely Until Second Half of '91, Economists Say

  • Author: Nicholas Hastings Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 12/31/90

LONDON -- The dollar isn't likely to begin a sustained recovery until the second half of 1991, when the U.S. economy has started to climb out of its slump and U.S. authorities have room to boost interest rates again, international economists forecast. They predict that while the Persian Gulf crisis will continue to provide limited support for the dollar in the early months of 1991, there is still a high risk that the U.S. currency will fall back to its 1990 lows before the middle of the year.

"In the first quarter, the dollar will remain vulnerable to {further} U.S. economic decline and further Fed easing," predicted David Morrison, chief international economist at Goldman Sachs International in London.

Indeed, on Friday the dollar dropped in very light trading in reaction to further indications the U.S. economy is weakening rapidly. In late New York trading, the greenback was quoted at 1.4936 marks and 134.85 yen, down from 1.5255 marks and 136.40 yen late Thursday in New York.

In early Monday trading in Tokyo, the dollar was at 134.32 yen, down from Friday's Tokyo close of 136.05 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at 1.4925 marks, down from 1.5210 marks Friday.

Michel Girardin, head of foreign exchange research at Union Bank of Switzerland in Zurich, warned that a conflict in the Middle East will trigger a short-term dollar rally. But he said this "support will soon disappear as the market starts to concentrate again on U.S. economic fundamentals."

Economists also see little chance that the Group of Seven leading industrialized countries will run to the dollar's aid, because member nations seem to be at odds on the issue.

How long and how strongly the Gulf crisis will work in the dollar's favor remains to be seen. Most economists think uncertainty about the chances for an armed conflict is the main factor preventing the U.S. currency from falling at the moment. They suggest that this could make the currency vulnerable to a downturn if a war starts and sentiment swiftly refocuses on the U.S. economic slowdown and the possibility of further reductions in U.S. interest rates.

Nigel Rendell, international economist at James Capel & Co., says he expects the U.S. federal funds rate to decline one-half of a percentage point to about 6 1/2% in the first quarter of 1991. About the same time, Germany will probably raise interest rates another notch and further undermine support for the dollar, he predicted. The federal funds rate is the interest rate banks charge each other for overnight loans.

Mr. Rendell predicted the dollar could drop to 1.45 marks in the second quarter. UBS's Mr. Girardin thinks it could even fall below 1.40 marks. The dollar's all-time low was 1.4625 marks, set Dec. 10.

"I would be surprised to see any rebound in the dollar until there are signs of the {U.S.} recession passing," Mr. Girardin said. Most economists don't expect any such signs of recovery to appear until midyear. By then, they also expect signs of a slowdown in Japan and Germany.

How far the dollar recovers will depend largely on how quickly U.S. authorities increase interest rates. Some economists suggest that the need to attract funds for the Treasury's massive refunding programs will create pressure to nudge rates higher as soon as possible.

But Robert Thomas, senior international economist at Midland Montagu & Co. in London, said officials will have to show caution because the economic recovery is likely to be gradual and the fragile state of the U.S. financial system will argue against any abrupt credit-tightening.

By contrast, German rates are widely expected to rise another half to full percentage point in the first half of 1991 as the Bundesbank attempts to curb inflationary expectations kindled by last October's reunification of East and West Germany.

German inflation is likely to have peaked by the middle of 1991, though, and with the mark's strength depressing export volumes and economic growth starting to slow, authorities should be more receptive to lower interest rates and a weaker mark, economists say.

Japanese interest rates are also expected to rise early in 1991 because the Bank of Japan remains concerned about high wage demands and persistently high money-supply growth.

However, economists doubt that Japan will tighten policy significantly, given signs that growth could slow sharply next year. At the same time, Mr. Girardin of UBS added, the yen isn't likely to benefit much from higher rates, which would merely increase fears of a possible collapse in land prices that would prompt another shakeout in Japan's stock market.

Such concerns, says Mr. Thomas of Midland, could force a decline in Japanese rates well before German rates head down in 1991. And, after falling as low as 120 yen in the first half of 1991, the dollar could recover to its current level of around 135 yen by the end of 1991, he predicted.

This year, the dollar fell to a low of about 123.50 yen in mid-October before rebounding as high as 137.05 yen near the end of the year.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery settled at $394 an ounce, up $8.60. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.5 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Monday, gold was quoted at $391 an ounce.
