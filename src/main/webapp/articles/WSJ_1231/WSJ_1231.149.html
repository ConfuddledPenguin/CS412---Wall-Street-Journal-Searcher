International: Two Foes of Mobutu Demand Inquiry Into de Groote's Ties to Zaire Regime

  • Author: Edward T. Pound and Martin du Bois
  • Date: 12/31/90

Two leading political opponents of Zairian dictator Mobutu Sese Seko called for an investigation into whether a top official of the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank acted improperly in his dealings with Mr. Mobutu's regime. They urged that the IMF and the World Bank conduct an inquiry into the ties between the Mobutu regime and Jacques de Groote, a Belgian native and one of the most senior executive directors of both institutions. "I think it's in the interest of everybody's credibility -- the IMF, the World Bank and Zaire -- to launch an investigation," said Nguza Karl-i-Bond, a former Zairian prime minister, foreign minister and ambassador to Washington.

Tshisekedi wa Mulumba, a prominent opposition figure and, like Mr. Nguza, a candidate for the presidency of Zaire, went even further. He urged that Belgian finance authorities, to whom Mr. de Groote reports, conduct a separate inquiry into conflict-of-interest questions raised by this newspaper in a front-page profile last Friday of Mr. de Groote.

An IMF spokesman said yesterday that "at this point, we are not in a position to comment." A World Bank spokesman also declined to comment. In an interview last October, however, Barber Conable, the World Bank's chairman and president, said: "If we were aware of an impropriety, we would contact" Belgian authorities "and urge them to look into it."

Mr. de Groote, who doesn't represent Zaire at the IMF and the World Bank, has acted as an adviser to the Mobutu regime on finance policies for many years. He acknowledged in interviews that even at the two institutions some believe he was acting as a paid Mobutu agent. He strongly denied receiving any money from Mr. Mobutu or his associates.

The story also focused on Mr. de Groote's money problems and his efforts to press the African nation of Rwanda into devaluing its currency in the early 1980s. The devaluation was being promoted by a Brussels businessman who was assisting Mr. de Groote with his finances. Again, Mr. de Groote denied he acted improperly.

Belgian government officials haven't commented on the story. But Arthur Gilson, a former Belgian defense minister, said in an interview that the Belgian "prime minister and the minister of finance should immediately and seriously start looking into" the allegations.

The IMF and the World Bank each have 22 directors. Mr. de Groote and the others direct the affairs of the multibillion-dollar institutions; their duties include approving large loans to poorer countries and helping nations adopt sound monetary policies. Mr. de Groote has been elected to represent Belgium, Austria, Luxembourg, Turkey, Hungary and Czechoslovakia -- but not Zaire.

President Mobutu's opponents, who hope to unseat him in an election in 1991, have campaigned against the corruption that has pervaded his rule since he took power in the African country in 1965. Mr. Mobutu himself is reputed to be a billionaire.

Mr. Nguza, once an insider, found it extraordinary that Mr. de Groote had characterized Mr. Mobutu, a strong U.S. ally, as "corrupt" in an interview with this newspaper. "That's a serious accusation directed at a head of state by a senior official of a highly respected institution." Mr. Nguza said. President Mobutu didn't respond to written questions submitted earlier this year.

Mr. Tshisekedi, a prominent opposition leader who often has been arrested by Mr. Mobutu's security forces, said in Brussels that he feared any investigation by the World Bank, the IMF and Belgian authorities wouldn't be thorough because it might embarrass them. He also said that Mr. Mobutu wouldn't likely cooperate with any inquiry.
