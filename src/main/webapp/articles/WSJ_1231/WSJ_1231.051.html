Paper Makers Allege Dumping By Europeans

  • Author: Robert J. Brennan
  • Date: 12/31/90

NEW YORK -- The U.S. paper industry charged that European producers of magazine-grade paper are dumping their products at below-market prices and pushing it out of that segment of the business. The American Paper Institute said its Committee to Safeguard the U.S. Coated Groundwood Paper Industry filed an anti-dumping petition with the Department of Commerce and the International Trade Commission. It said the price undercutting by European paper companies range from 28% to 88% of fair market value, currently about $800 a ton. Coated groundwood paper is used primarily for magazines, catalogs and commercial printing.

A. Paul Victor, an attorney at the New York firm Weil, Gotshal & Manges who represents the committee, said he believes it is the first such trade action by the U.S. paper industry in more than a decade. He said the petition "contains no allegation of government subsidies" in Europe to paper makers. "It's all a matter of companies making private pricing decisions" to garner market share in the U.S., he said.

Based on the paper institute's recent 1990 capacity survey for U.S. paper makers, coated groundwood production for the year is expected to be about 4.4 million tons, indicating a market of about $3.5 billion. Mr. Victor estimated imports are about $300 million. But according to various industry statistics, imports in this category have been growing, more than four-fold in the last decade.

The institute represents substantially all U.S. producers of paper and pulp, and said the committee's member companies account for about 80% of shipments of the product. Nine countries were named -- Austria, Belgium, Britain, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands and Sweden -- as bases for various under-pricing producers. A copy of the petition wasn't immediately available to identify specific European companies.

"The European companies have dramatically increased their capacity for making this product and are using the U.S. as the prime market in which to sell their excess capacity," said Virgil K. Horton Jr., the institute's vice president for paper products.

As a consequence, Mr. Horton said, U.S. paper makers are "suffering from decreases in productivity, efficiency and profitability, resulting in a drop in investment levels, reduced employment opportunities, and an inability to justify the dedication of capital to coated groundwood paper operations."
