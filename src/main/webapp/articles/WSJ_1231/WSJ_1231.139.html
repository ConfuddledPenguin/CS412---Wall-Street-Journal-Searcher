Credit Markets: Year-End Rush Puts Short Rates Through Roof

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Stuart Drown
  • Date: 12/31/90

NEW YORK -- The annual year-end jump in short-term interest rates is unusually intense this year as banks, particularly Japanese banks, scramble to get their hands on a diminished pile of available money. Short-term rates usually soar around year-end as banks race to borrow short-term funds to put on their balance sheets. This maneuver is done for accounting purposes and is commonly known as "window dressing."

While it's not unusual for year-end rates to rise into double digits, eyebrows were raised in midweek when the closely watched federal funds rate charge on overnight loans between banks briefly reached 100% -- far above its target of 7% -- and some Eurodollar deposit rates hit 50%.

"There has been frantic bidding for year-end funds" for more than a month, said Louis Crandall, economist at W.R. Wrightson & Associates. He said the jump in rates reflects unsually strong demand for funds at a time when many lenders in the market -- which consists mostly of other banks -- are limiting supply.

Although most economists expect the pressures to be relieved later this week or next week, they say that rising short-term bank borrowing rates is a major reason commercial banks haven't yet lowered their prime lending rates, a common benchmark for consumer loans.

Late in the week, brokers who trade in the federal funds market were quoting rates for U.S. banks of between 10% and 12%. Yet some Japanese banks -- a group that has been severely squeezed in the short-term funds market -- were paying between 20% and 50%.

Essentially, these borrowers were locking in commitments to take delivery of funds at year-end that they plan to repay in early January, a phenomenon known to the market as turn-of-the-year financing.

It might seem surprising that the Japanese banks have been hit much harder than their U.S. counterparts, considering that many of the largest Japanese banks are thought to be healthier than the big U.S. banks. But there are widespread concerns that some Japanese banks will stumble amid further erosion of Japan's stock and real estate markets. This fear is causing U.S. investors to discriminate against all Japanese banks. The Japanese stock market declined nearly 40% in 1990.

Rates have been moving higher and higher as Japanese banks apparently worry that overnight funding supplies will be exhausted before their needs are met.

"If somebody is afraid that his credit line might be cut off, he is willing to borrow now to make sure he has the money he needs at year-end," said Samuel D. Kahan, economist at Fuji Securities Inc.

Moreover, some lenders have reduced their exposure to Japanese banks in the interbank funding market and some money market funds have cut back their purchases of deposits of Japanese banks.

Reflecting the year-end funding squeeze, one-week Eurodollar deposits were quoted late last week at 11% bid, 15% asked; one-month Eurodollar deposits were quoted at 10% bid, 10 1/2% asked.

Friday's Market Activity

Prices of long-term bonds deteriorated as renewed concerns about the Middle East prompted investors to sell bonds ahead of the weekend and New Year's holiday. But some short-term notes posted modest gains.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell 5/8 point, a loss of about $6.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, rose to 8.30% from 8.24%. The benchmark bond was quoted late Friday at a price of 104 28/32 compared with 105 16/32 on Thursday.

Prices of two-year notes rose slightly to 100 1/32 from par on Thursday. The yields on the issue fell to 7.20% from 7.23%.

Charges from the Bush Administration that Iraq is blocking progress in setting up meetings to discuss a resolution of the Middle East crisis, and ill-founded rumors that shots had been fired in the Persian Gulf, spurred some selling.

Trading activity was light, partly because of the holiday season and an East Coast snowstorm that kept many players away. Most bond dealers closed their trading desks before 3 p.m.

In the corporate bond market, prices were little changed. For all of 1990, corporate debt sales fell to a five-year low, according to Securities Data Co. Only $105.6 billion in corporate debt was sold this year, down 20% from the $134.1 billion in corporate financing last year.

Expectations of lower interest rates kept many corporate borrowers on the sidelines during the first half of the year, and a spike in interest rates after Iraq's invasion of Kuwait stifled issuance in the third quarter.

Only $19.8 billion of corporate debt was sold in the third quarter, compared with $27.8 billion in the comparable period last year. But offerings rebounded in the fourth quarter when falling interest rates sparked a flood of financings.

According to Securities Data, $31.2 billion of corporate debt was offered in the fourth quarter, a slight dip from the $33.3 billion sold in the year-earlier period.
