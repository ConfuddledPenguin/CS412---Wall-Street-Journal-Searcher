Letters to the Editor: Readin' and Writin' and No TV Ads

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/31/90

Contrary to Chris Whittle's assertions in your Nov. 26 article "Whittle Asserts School-TV Project Draws Subscribers," California continues to ban the use of advertising programs such as "Channel One" in our public-school classrooms. State law has never given schools the ability to barter access to their students for television or any other kind of equipment. In addition, the law is quite clear that the state will not pay schools for the time "Channel One" viewers spend watching ads for M&Ms.

Whittle Communications' lobbyists tried, and failed, to get a special-interest bill through the legislature. The law remains as before. The State Board of Education, after intense lobbying by Whittle's representative, did pass a resolution stating that local school boards' decisions regarding "commercial products and services" must be "consistent with state law." Whittle Communications may characterize that as a flip-flop. It is not.

This state's PTA and the associations of school board members and administrators support the continued ban of broadcast advertising in the classroom. The state will soon cut aid to the one public school that has used "Channel One" since the beginning of the year.

Beyond the local legal issues, the "Channel One" controversy has raised serious educational and ethical concerns, including the piecemeal selling of the school day and the improper use of the compulsory-attendance law. It has also focused attention on sales techniques that, while quite legitimate in the free market, become exploitative in the protected, monopoly environment in which Whittle Communications seeks to operate.

Bill Honig

California Superintendent of Public Instruction

Sacramento, Calif.
