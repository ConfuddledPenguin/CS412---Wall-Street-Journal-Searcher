Economy: U.S. Economy Will Keep Growing in '91 But Slowly, Commerce Department Says

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 12/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. should experience "continuing but slow growth" in most sectors, the Commerce Department said in its annual industrial outlook. "Overall, we see continued growth in 1991 for the majority of industries, both manufacturing and services," said J. Michael Farren, undersecretary for international trade. Exports, which represented more than 70% of sluggish U.S. growth in the first three quarters of 1990, will partly counteract weakness in other aspects of the U.S. economy, he added.

The growth rate for all industries covered by the survey, however, is expected to slow slightly to 1.13% from 1.38%, according to Jonathan C. Menes, director of the department's office of finance, industry and trade information. But since the report was prepared, many private economists, who say the country is in a recession, have become increasingly pessimistic about the country's economic prospects.

The study, a series of forecasts for specific industries prepared by more than 100 department analysts, makes certain assumptions about the future of the U.S. economy. In particular, it assumes that real growth in 1991 will be between 1% and 3%, with the "most likely case being growth at the lower end of that range." In the first three quarters of 1990, economic growth averaged about 1.3%.

Among manufacturing businesses, the report said most of the high-technology and health-related industries will continue to outperform the rest of the economy. It forecasts 8.9% growth in 1991 in the semiconductor and related industries and expects computer industry shipments to increase by 6% during the year. Medical instruments, supplies and appliance businesses also are projected to grow at rates of between 7% and 9% in 1991. Leather tanning and finishing, with a projected 9.4% growth rate in 1991, should outperform most manufacturing businesses.

But there are laggards as well. Motor vehicles and construction "will experience negative growth" in 1991, Mr. Farren said. Manufacturers of butter, mobile homes and frozen baked goods -- other than bread -- will be hit particularly hard, the report said.

The aircraft industry, including engines and parts -- which was the boom manufacturing business during the past year -- will experience a 4.6% growth rate in 1991, down sharply from 17.9% in 1990. But the aerospace industry is expected to have a record 1991 trade surplus of $31 billion, up from more than $28 billion in 1990. Machine tool production also is expected to continue to be strong, with a predicted 5% growth rate in 1991, after a 5.3% rise in 1990.

In service industries, the strongest sectors will be electronic information, computer software and data processing, with revenues growing as much as between 16% and 20%, according to the report.

Despite the report's predictions for continued sluggish growth, many private economists paint an even gloomier picture. They say the economy is in a recession, with no guarantee that it will perk up before late 1991. Recession is commonly defined by economists as at least two consecutive quarters of negative growth.

Generally, the Commerce Department's industrial outlook is based partly on midsummer economic projections released by the Office of Management and Budget. This year, however, the Aug. 2 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, with the subsequent turmoil in oil and other markets, forced the department to use a revised set of economic assumptions prepared in August. In general, the department's analysts "assumed that the economy would avoid a recession, though a soft economic environment would persist into the first half of 1991, followed by a gradual pickup," according to J. Antonio Villamil, the department's chief economist.

The report's assumptions project an inflation rate of between 4% and 4.5% for 1991, down slightly from 4.5% to 5% in 1990. They also assume a slight reduction in interest rates, with the Treasury bill rate falling to between 6.8% to 7.2% in 1991, from between 7.2% to 7.5% in 1990, while the 10-year Treasury note rate would fall to a 1991 range of 8.0% to 8.5% from between 8.5% to 9.0% in 1990. According to the report, the unemployment rate may pick up slightly to between 5.5% to 6.5% in 1991 from between 5.5% and 6% in 1990. The report also assumed a 1991 oil price of $25 a barrel.

These projections, which were prepared in recent months, are already somewhat dated. The administration will soon release its economic report of the president, which will include more current economic forecasts for 1991.
