Economy: Leading Index Of Economy Declines 1.2% --- November Drop Was Fifth In a Row in New Signal Of Recessionary Trend

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 12/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The government's main economic forecasting gauge declined for the fifth consecutive month in November, in yet another sign that the economy is headed into a recession. "The economy has been flattened by everyone's reaction to world events," said Charles Renfro, chief economist with Alphametrics, an economic forecasting concern in Bala Cynwyd, Pa. Iraq's invasion of Kuwait appears to have sparked a sudden downward spiral in consumer confidence, which is rippling through the economy, although some analysts believe the economy was weakening before the August invasion.

The index of leading indicators -- a collection of statistics intended to predict economic activity in the months ahead -- dropped 1.2% in November after a revised slide of 1.3% in October and a 0.8% fall in September, the Commerce Department said. The October decline was reported last month as 1.2%.

Since World War II, the index has turned down, on average, nine months before the beginning of a recession. But it sometimes also has predicted recessions that didn't ensue. In fact, the November decline marked the greatest number of consecutive decreases since the index fell for six consecutive months in mid-1984. No recession followed those earlier signals.

The index has been declining sporadically since the middle of last year, with a brief show of strength early last summer. Most economists now believe the economy is in a recession -- generally defined as two consecutive quarters of decline in output of goods and services -- but there is wide disagreement about when it started and how deep and lengthy it will be.

Eight of the 11 components of the leading indicators declined in November. The biggest negative factors were falling manufacturers' orders for consumer goods, a decrease in materials prices that respond quickly to supply and demand (this component doesn't include oil prices), and an increase in manufacturers' unfilled orders.

The three gauges that indicated a positive trend were rising stock prices, an increase in delays of deliveries of supplies (which indicates increased demand), and a slight rebound in the index of consumer expectations. The consumer index, based on surveys, fell steeply from 79.3 in May to 50.9 in October. It rose last month to 52.8.

The department's index of coincident indicators, which is supposed to track the current pace of economic activity, dropped 1.1% in November after an identical tumble in October.
