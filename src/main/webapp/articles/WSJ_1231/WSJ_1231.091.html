Economy: Tool Makers Say Caution Rules Project Orders

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 12/31/90

Manufacturers are cautious about launching major factory investment projects these days, but most intend to carry out previously planned modernization programs, machinery producers say. U.S. machine tool plants received $203.4 million of orders in November, 10% more than during the weak month of November 1989, but 24% less than October's $268.1 million. Furthermore, last month's bookings were down 18% from the average for the first 10 months of 1990, according to the NMTBA, the Association for Manufacturing Technology, a trade group based in McLean, Va.

Machine tool distributors, who handle imported machines as well as those made in the U.S., saw November orders drop 32% from a year earlier and 8.1% from October, said the American Machine Tool Distributors' Association, Rockville, Md.

Because lathes, machining centers, metal-forming presses and other machine tools are used to shape most metal parts -- and to make the molds for many plastic products -- machine tool orders are a good barometer of investment in factory machinery. Also, machine tool purchases usually can be postponed without creating an immediate crisis, which makes them a sensitive indicator.

November frequently is a weak order month because Thanksgiving eliminates two business days. However, machine tool executives say last month's scanty orders also reflect reluctance to make major commitments while the economy is weakening rapidly.

Thus far, there's no sign of widespread cancellations of planned factory improvements such as those that occurred early in the 1973-74 and 1981-82 recessions. Only $7.6 million of previously placed machine tool orders were canceled in November, NMTBA said, a figure below the monthly average for the last two years. By contrast, monthly cancellations averaged $53.3 million during 1981, when the last major recession was brewing.

"Orders from aerospace companies, both domestic and overseas, continue strong, but general industry orders have been very slow," says Christopher C. Cole, group vice president for Cincinnati Milacron Inc., the nation's largest machine tool maker. "We didn't see the year-end rush to use up capital spending budgets that we've had in previous years."

Spending for individual "stand-alone" machine tools has weakened recently, says Brian D. McLaughlin, president and chief executive officer of Hurco Cos., Indianapolis, a major producer of computerized controls for such machines. Many stand-alone machines are bought by smaller factories.

Larger factories often buy groups of machines that form a parts-making system. Orders for these manufacturing cells haven't dropped off as sharply. "We hear a lot of talk about recession, but we don't see a precipitous pullback by our customers," says John F. Townsend, vice president of Giddings & Lewis Inc., Fond du Lac, Wis., a leading producer of manufacturing cells. Orders for the company's automated assembly systems also have held up well.

Forecasting into 1991 is unusually difficult because no one can forecast the severity and length of the economic downturn. Manufacturers continue to discuss new factory equipment programs, but they don't sign order forms, says Milacron's Mr. Cole. "You can be discouraged that orders haven't been signed, or optimistic because plans aren't being canceled," he says.

A Commerce Department survey made in October and November of capital spending plans didn't signal a sharp drop. Major machine tool buyers -- manufacturers of automobiles, airplanes, appliances, machinery and other durable goods -- said they would reduce capital spending less than 1% next year.

And Eli S. Lustgarten, machinery analyst with PaineWebber Inc., thinks manufacturers will cut purchases of data processing and communications equipment rather than factory machinery. He's looking for a "single digit rise" in machine tool orders next year because U.S. auto makers must spend more on production systems to make new engines and transmissions, or they will fall dangerously behind Japanese competitors.

Based on the recent trend, 1990 orders for U.S.-made machine tools are likely to be slightly higher than the $2.81 billion for all of 1989. Shipments in 1990 also will be up a few percentage points from last year's $3.20 billion.

Shipments have exceeded new orders during much of the last two years as machine tool builders filled backlogged orders. At the end of November, the backlog of unfilled orders totaled $1.52 billion, NMTBA said, down from $1.80 billion at the start of 1990 and from a recent high of $2.32 billion in February 1989.

November orders for lathes, machining centers, boring mills, milling machines, grinders and other machines to cut metal totaled $137.5 million, up 6.6%, from $128.9 million a year earlier, NMTBA said. Last month's orders for metal-forming presses and other machines to shape metal with pressure were $66 million, up 19%, from $55.5 million in November 1989.
