Oklahoma, Where the Wavy Hair Gets to Be an Unusually Big Affair

  • Author: Todd Mason
  • Date: 12/31/90

OKLAHOMA CITY -- Television newsman Jack Bowen claimed his ex-employer had pulled the rug out from over him. When KWTV Channel 9 took Mr. Bowen off the air in June after a stalemate on his contract, the deposed anchorman went shopping for a new job. He also went shopping for new clothes and a new toupee. The CBS affiliate refused to reimburse its former anchor for his $994.67 tab, so he sued in small-claims court.

Not one to split hairs, county judge Charles Humble noted that Mr. Bowen's contract hadn't yet expired, so the station must honor its contractual allowances for his hair and clothes, even though he wore them to cross the street to rival KOCO-TV, the ABC affiliate.

So it goes in the nation's 40th largest television market, where the industry is often as entertaining as its broadcast fare. Puffed up by the 1970s booms in oil and gas and broadcast television, Oklahoma City now has six commercial stations. But with the economy in the ditch since 1982, competition has been fierce over on-air personalities, slogans and even charity drives.

Like most of these clashes, the Bowen affair produced considerable smoke and dust but little progress. Mr. Bowen was back where he started, at the station that employed him for 13 years before KWTV wooed him away in 1987. KWTV rehired the anchor Mr. Bowen replaced then.

Both sides managed to stay cool at first. Mr. Bowen shelved his plans to sue KWTV for bad-faith bargaining. For its part, the station held its fire after KOCO aired advertisements urging viewers to "Get Back with Jack," starring the back of a Bowen look-alike. Only KWTV could promote Mr. Bowen's name and image until his contract expired in August, says KWTV Vice President Jerry G. Dalrymple, "but we didn't see any point in making a stink over it." That is, until Mr. Bowen submitted his final expense account.

Mr. Bowen's new employer warned him that his lawsuit would peel away not only his cranial cover-up but also his privacy, disclosing his last salary at KWTV of $147,300, plus $3,000 for clothes and $1,500 for hair. Mr. Bowen says he wasn't daunted. "I said, `Ooh, are they going to tell the public I wear makeup too?'" he recalls.

The court ordered KWTV to reimburse Mr. Bowen and dismissed the station's counterclaim for breach of contract. "After years of reporting on other people," he says, "it felt so good to see the system would work for me."
