Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Debate Grows About Whether Economy Will Recover From Recession Rapidly

  • Author: Kathryn Graven
  • Date: 12/31/90

NEW YORK -- As the New Year opens, the gap is widening between economists who say the worst of the recession is over and those who say more bad news is yet to come. In recent weeks, economists generally have agreed that the significant drop in fourth-quarter economic activity pushed the nation into a recession. Now, however, the debate is growing over when and how fast a recovery will start.

Debbie Johnson, an economist at Prudential-Bache Securities here, believes the economy could start to pick up early next year. While sticking to her forecast of a fourth-quarter decline in gross national product of 2% to 2.5%, she says the nation's output of goods and services could rise 2% in 1991's first quarter. "We've probably seen the worst of it," she says.

That doesn't wash with Sung Won Sohn, chief economist at Norwest Corp. in Minneapolis. "It doesn't make any sense to me that the economy is going to recover quickly," he says. Mr. Sohn argues that those in the optimistic camp incorrectly trace most of today's economic woes to the Middle East crisis and higher oil prices. He maintains that the problems stem from the nation's heavy debt burden. Furthermore, citing an increasing rate of credit-card delinquencies for even smaller, less-expensive items, Mr. Sohn worries that consumers, too, have taken on more debt than they can handle.

In the shorter term, economists find more room for agreement. Many predict that December's economic data, while still showing important declines, will not slump by the same magnitude as November's. Further evidence of moderation may arrive in this week's economic indicators.

On Wednesday, the purchasing managers' index for December is due. Predictions for the index, which is compiled by the National Association of Purchasing Management, are for a drop of 0.5 to 1.3 percentage points from November's level of 41.3%. While still reflecting weak economic activity, that range would be less than the 2.1% decline reported in November. Generally, a reading above 50% indicates an expanding economy, while a reading below 50% suggests contraction.

Norwest's Mr. Sohn doesn't expect this month's purchasing managers' report to be as bad as last month's, but that doesn't necessarily mean the index has bottomed. He sees the manufacturing sector of the economy continuing to deteriorate, depressed further by poor Christmas sales. He notes that the weakness in durable goods now is spreading from big-ticket items such as refrigerators and laundry appliances to smaller items such as toasters and cassette tape players.

Similarly, economists don't expect to see the same sharp plunge in employment during December that they witnessed in November. On Friday, the government releases December's unemployment rate and the tally on non-farm payrolls.

Kenneth Mayland, chief economist at Society Corp. in Cleveland, sees the unemployment rate rising to 6.1% from November's 5.9% and expects another cut in payroll jobs of 150,000. In November, a striking number of non-farm payroll jobs -- 267,000 -- were eliminated.

While not willing to go so far as to say the worst of the recession is over, Mr. Mayland does argue that an analysis of past recessions shows that bigger payroll declines occur in the second half of a recession than in the first. "Sharper rates of decline could be consistent with an imminent rebound of the economy," he says.

Other economic indicators due to be released this week include new-home sales and construction spending for November, and auto sales for mid-December. With consumer confidence still discouragingly low and banks more carefully scrutinizing loan applications, economists don't see much room for improvement in home or auto sales for awhile. The construction industry remains in a profound slump, though a bulge in public spending lifted the October construction figures.
