Your Money Matters: Latest Look in Mutual Funds: Cushions

  • Author: Ellen E. Schultz
  • Date: 07/23/90

With stock prices in record-high territory, mutual-fund marketers are trying to lure investors who are afraid of heights. They're pitching funds -- including equity-income, balanced and convertible-securities funds -- that are supposed to cushion the fall if the long market rally should finally run out of steam. That's because funds in these groups contain bonds, high-dividend stocks or other yield-producing securities intended to provide protection in down markets.

But although some funds have done a lot better than others in times when the market has headed south, investors should be careful about buying a fund simply because of its category. In the past, some equity-income, balanced and convertible funds got clobbered in down markets, and their returns lagged when the market was up.

Instead of relying on labels, investors should "look for funds that do reasonably well on the downside -- regardless of the fund's category," says A. Michael Lipper, president of Lipper Analytical Services Inc.

Investors who take this advice will discover that some of the funds that have performed best in down markets turn up in categories they might have passed over as too risky.

For example, according to an analysis by Lipper, one of the best down-market performers of the past 10 years was Lindner Fund. It did better than the average equity-income, balanced and convertible-securities fund in each of the six periods during the past decade when the market as a whole fell at least 10% from its previous peak.

But risk-averse investors may never have given Lindner a look. Why? Because Lipper and some other firms that track mutual funds classify it as a growth fund. And growth funds typically invest in stocks that often get slammed when the market falls.

Lindner, however, uses a classic "value" investing strategy -- buying companies' stocks at a discount to what Lindner thinks the companies are worth now, says Kenneth Gregory of Litman/Gregory & Co., a San Francisco investment adviser. In contrast, growth-stock investors generally give a lot of weight to future growth and earnings.

Lindner is a no-load fund, which means it has no sales charge. Other value-oriented funds that are often lumped into the growth category include Neuberger & Berman Partners (a no-load fund) and Quest for Value (which has a 4.5% load). Both outperformed most equity-income, balanced and convertible funds in down markets over the past decade -- and delivered top returns in up markets as well.

"We try to estimate the downside risk in every stock we buy, and outperform the market," says Eileen Rominger, Quest for Value's portfolio manager. "The best way to make money over time is not to lose money," she says.

Another fund that turns up among good performers in down markets and up markets is Phoenix Growth (which has a 6.9% load). Though invested primarily in high-growth stocks, its managers concentrate on traditionally "defensive" stocks in pharmaceuticals, health-care and consumer staples. Earnings in such companies aren't cyclical, which means they tend to hold up well in recessions. In addition, the fund currently holds 17% cash and 13.5% in Treasury securities, which would soften the blow if the stock market fell.

Phoenix Balanced and Phoenix Convertible Securities funds (with 6.9% loads) also turned up as top performers in their categories during down markets, according to Lipper. Cathy Dudley, co-manager of Phoenix Growth, says the Phoenix group uses a similar conservative, top-down approach for all three funds. "We start by looking at the economy, then the most favorable sectors. We then buy the most growth at the least cost, looking for predictable earnings and international exposure."

Investors interested in unearthing other good down-market performers won't find it easy. Since mutual funds generally quote performance figures on an annual basis, investors will have to ask the fund representative what happened during specific down cycles.

"By looking only at total returns on a year-by-year basis, you're not going to see how well the fund performed in down markets," says Mr. Lipper. He recommends looking at funds that have been around at least 10 years. If the fund is only five years old, it will have weathered only one downturn.

Why look at down-market performance at all, rather than total returns? Because even though two funds can have the same results over 10 years, they may have given investors two completely different rides, depending on how well they did in down markets.

For example, Lindner Dividend and Oppenheimer Equity Income, both equity-income funds, had annualized average total returns of 18.8% and 17.45%, respectively, over the past 10 years. However, during that period, Lindner Dividend performed within the top 20% of equity-income funds during all six down cycles. The Oppenheimer fund was in the bottom 40% in the two earliest cycles, the bottom 20% in the next three cycles and the top 40% in the latest cycle, according to Lipper.

While volatility may not matter to some investors, it's no plus to those who prefer steady-as-you-go returns. "If someone were going to pick a fund and close his eyes and not look at it for 10 years, he'd probably want a pure growth fund," says Mr. Gregory, who is also editor of L/G NoLoad Fund Analyst newsletter. "But most people don't invest that way. Their eyes are open all the time, and they get real nervous."

For those people, funds that do well in down markets make the most sense, he says. "They don't drop as much, so they're less likely to panic investors," he says, and derail them from their buy-and-hold strategies.

Finally, investors should plan to keep their money in place even if the market tanks. Those who fled the market following the 1987 crash missed a lucrative rebound: Since that time, the average equity fund has gone up 61.24%. "Being on the sidelines was an expensive seat," says Mr. Lipper.
