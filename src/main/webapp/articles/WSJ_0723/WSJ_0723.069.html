Estimates of Duties On Some Sweaters Are Raised by U.S.

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 07/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The Commerce Department sharply raised its estimates of anti-dumping duties that could be imposed on imports of synthetic-fiber sweaters from Hong Kong. In a final ruling, the department recommended that Hong Kong suppliers be subject to remedial duties of 5.86%, compared with a 2.25% rate recommended in the department's preliminary finding. The potential assessment on Comitex Knitters Ltd., one of the largest sweater makers in Hong Kong, was raised to 5.86% from 1.89%.

The department also recommended that duties for two related suppliers, Prosperity Clothing Co. and Estero Enterprises Ltd., be raised to 115.15% from 12.04%. But the department exempted Crystal Knitters Ltd. and Laws Fashion Knitters Ltd. from the prospective duties.

The assessments, which stem from a trade complaint filed by the National Knitwear and Sportswear Association, will go into effect if the U.S. International Trade Commission affirms that Hong Kong imports are hurting U.S. producers. The ITC ruling is due in 45 days.

The Commerce Department said the U.S. imported $208.8 million of such sweaters from Hong Kong last year. The prospective duties are intended to offset unfair pricing in which Hong Kong producers allegedly sell at less than their production costs or home-market prices.

Hong Kong trade officials called the ruling a "disappointment," insisting all along that manufacturers in the free-trading entrepot don't need to engage in unfair export pricing. However, lawyers for the Hong Kong manufacturers and U.S. importers are relieved that the average potential duties didn't go any higher.

Bruce Mitchell, counsel for the Hong Kong Woolen and Synthetic Knitting Manufacturers Association, said the trade body succeeded in persuading the Commerce Department not to let the unusually high estimates for Prosperity and Estero send the rates for all other Hong Kong suppliers soaring. "Otherwise, we could be facing duties of 61% for all the others," he said.

Hong Kong officials confirmed that Prosperity and Estero were penalized for refusing to cooperate with U.S. trade investigators. The U.S. petitioners have charged that the two companies obtain some of their sweaters from other countries in violation of U.S. textile-quota rules. However, P.C. Leung, Hong Kong's Washington-based economic counselor, insists that "there could be many other reasons why a company decides not to cooperate."

Lawyers for the Hong Kong side estimated that these two companies account for no more than 2% of synthetic-fiber sweater exports to the U.S., but American industry officials said that percentage could be higher.
