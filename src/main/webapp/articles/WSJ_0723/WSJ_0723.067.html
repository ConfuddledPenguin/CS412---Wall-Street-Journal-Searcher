REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): An Era Closes

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/23/90

Supreme Court Justice William Brennan was appointed by President Eisenhower in 1956 only soon to become the intellectual godfather of the judicial activists. What was called the Warren Court could as well be called the Brennan Court. On a personal level, we join the chorus saluting Justice Brennan. He did have the decency of candor in his opinions. His resignation at age 84 is similarly honest and clear-headed. Justice Brennan was the only justice left who took the time to read all the appeals to the Supreme Court instead of leaving the task to young clerks. And unlike other liberal judges, he was not afraid to admit there was no clear constitutional justification for much of what he did.

His ideology was that courts can decide all issues, whether or not they are legal issues. The Constitution envisions capital punishment in several clauses, but this didn't stop Justice Brennan from invoking his own view of "human dignity" to declare all death penalties unconstitutional. Justice Brennan's expansion of the exclusionary rule helps explain why we so rarely convict criminals. His creation of broad due-process rights for government benefits locked in the welfare state.

It is fitting that Justice Brennan's enormous influence extends to defining the debate about his successor. With all the issues that our court system must confront, somehow the biggest impact of his departure is supposed to be over abortion. Yet this is not mentioned in the Constitution, and even an outright reversal of Roe v. Wade would have only a minimal effect on the actual availability of abortion. This is a Supreme Court issue only because Justice Brennan and his allies made it one.

For many years the activist view prevailed over the idea that judges should strictly construe both the Constitution and their own roles, but the pendulum is swinging back. President Reagan's brilliant nominations to the lower federal courts created a long list of qualified potential nominees whose views begin with the certainty that Brennan-style judicial activism is illegitimate. Their intellectual leader is Robert Bork, defeated for the court by a smear campaign by those now demanding that President Bush look for "balance" in choosing a new nominee.

This time, in short, the fight has started even before the nominee has been named. But the Bork experience should be a lesson to the left, for they ended up with Anthony Kennedy, a more consistently conservative vote. We don't know whom President Bush will pick for the seat, but if his first nominee is knocked out for political reasons we'd say a fine second choice would be Senator Orrin Hatch, confirmable because of Senatorial courtesy and more conservative than any other likely nominee.

More broadly, President Bush won a mandate by running on the clear platform that he would pick an original-intent justice, that it's time to close the activist era personified by Justice Brennan. The electorate's view must count for something; the selection of a nominee is the only time the voters get their say on the Supreme Court.
