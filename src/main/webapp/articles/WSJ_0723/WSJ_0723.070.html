McKinzie Gets 20-Year Term In Thrift Case

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 07/23/90

A federal judge in Santa Ana, Calif., sentenced Janet Faye McKinzie to 20 years in prison for stealing millions of dollars from North America Savings & Loan Association, a now-insolvent Orange County thrift. Ms. McKinzie, a 41-year-old former business manager and executive consultant for the thrift's late president, also was ordered to pay $13.4 million in restitution to the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.

Ms. McKinzie was ordered Friday by Judge Alicemarie H. Stotler to begin serving her prison term immediately. Ms. McKinzie's attorney, Richard "Racehorse" Haynes of Texas, filed a notice of appeal at the sentencing hearing, said David Schindler, one of the two assistant U.S. attorneys who prosecuted the case. Neither Ms. McKinzie nor her attorney could be reached.

After an eight-week trial, a jury found Ms. McKinzie guilty in March of stealing $15.5 million from North America Savings. She was convicted on 22 criminal counts, including racketeering, conspiracy, wire and bank fraud and interstate transportation of stolen property.

Federal banking and thrift regulators say the sentence was among the toughest ever levied for fraud. Last month, Woody Lemons was sentenced to 30 years in prison for his role in defrauding a Texas thrift. That sentence also is being appealed.

"The government considers the sentence to be appropriate in light of the egregious misconduct of McKinzie," the two prosecutors said in a statement. The demise of North America Savings is expected to eventually cost taxpayers $118 million.

Ms. McKinzie had pleaded innocent because of insanity. Her attorneys had argued that her judgment was impaired by prescription drugs she was taking.

Duayne D. Christensen, a Westminster, Calif., dentist, invested $6.4 million to open the thrift in 1983. Ms. McKinzie was a consultant to Dr. Christensen in operating the thrift. In 1986, the concern lost $8.9 million in the first 11 months. Government prosecutors charged that the two lived a lavish life style, using thrift funds to buy Rolls-Royce autos, a $125,000 gold eagle statue and an $18,500 gold letter-opener.

In 1987, a week before federal regulators declared the thrift insolvent, Dr. Christensen, 57 years old, died when his Jaguar crashed into a freeway overpass. The Orange county coroner classified the cause of his death as undetermined, failing to rule whether it was an accident or suicide.

Federal regulators have also filed a civil suit seeking $40 million for negligence and breach of fiduciary duty in the case, an FDIC attorney said. "This sentencing was a very gratifying victory, but it could also speed things up in our civil case," he said.
