Commodities: Production, Refining and Shipping Problems In Central Africa Are Pushing Up Price of Cobalt

  • Author: Neil Behrmann Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/23/90

LONDON -- Resistance against one-party rule in central Africa is pushing up the price of cobalt, an important strategic metal. In only the past three weeks, the price surged to $10.50 a pound from $7.80 because of acute production, refining and shipping problems in Zaire and Zambia, says Oscar Prager, managing director of Ayrton & Partners Ltd., London metal dealers.

Cobalt is an integral part of "super alloys" used in military and civil jet engines and in machine tools. It is strong, corrosion-resistant and able to withstand very high temperatures, analysts say.

It is "strategic" because such things as jet engines can't be built without it. About two-thirds of normal annual world cobalt supplies of around 20,000 metric tons is mined as a byproduct of copper in politically unstable Zambia and Zaire, Mr. Prager says. He estimates that supplies from these two landlocked countries could fall as much as 4,000 tons short of normal.

Production difficulties are the result of deteriorating conditions in the economies of the two nations, says Gavin Sanderson, chief executive officer of China Industrial Resources, a London metals company, adding that Chinese and Eastern European industries were buyers of scarce cobalt.

Moreover, skilled expatriate technical and administrative managers left Zaire and Zambia in recent months, Mr. Sanderson says. An attempted coup in Zambia and fighting in the mineral-rich Shaba province of Zaire are discouraging foreign personnel from working there, he says.

"The shortage could be critical," Mr. Sanderson says. Central African output has fallen in recent years because of depressed prices, he says.

But mines and refineries won't be in a position to raise production markedly, Mr. Sanderson says. There hasn't been sufficient maintenance to raise capacity, and they won't attract replacements of foreign qualified staff. They fear tribal strife if President Mobutu Sese Seko of Zaire and Zambian president Kenneth Kaunda are deposed, Mr. Sanderson says.

In 1978, an invasion of Zaire's Shaba province precipitated a ballooning of cobalt's price to $50 a pound from $7, dealers recall. The price then slumped to around $4 as mines raised output rapidly and speculators dumped stocks.

Prices could rise sharply again, unless metal is released from consumer inventories or the U.S. National Defense Stockpile, Mr. Prager of Ayrton & Partners says. The U.S. has built up a cobalt stockpile of around 24,000 tons, equivalent to about 14 months of world consumption, as an insurance against political or other disruptions, officials say.

It is included with 91 other commodities in a $10 billion stockpile. But stocks of cobalt will only be released for defense purposes and won't be sold if there is a shortage in the markets, the officials say. Meanwhile, the U.S. is upgrading the quality of cobalt in its stockpile, and its long-term goal is to raise inventories to 38,000 metric tons.

But Phillip Crowson, chief economist of RTZ Corp., a major international mining company, contends metals merchants are being alarmists about cobalt. Fears of short supplies of strategic metals arise periodically, he says, but there are large stocks of metal world-wide.

Moreover, the defense industry, a major user of cobalt, is likely to cut back purchases of cobalt because of detente between the North Atlantic Treaty Organization and the Warsaw Pact, analysts say.

Cobalt is traded in a narrow, illiquid market, dealers say, which means speculators can drive prices up or down rapidly.

Rhodium, another strategic metal, which is used in automobiles to reduce pollution, is a prime example, Mr. Crowson says. Prices rose to a peak of $7,350 an ounce early July from $1,250 a year earlier because of supply disruptions at a refinery of South African producer Rustenburg Platinum Mines Ltd. The price then fell to $4,300 an ounce early this week and bounced back to $5,000 on Friday on extremely low volumes, dealers say.

Rhodium supplies are likely to rise in coming months, say Barry Davison, managing director of Rustenburg Platinum Mines, who insists that the mine's refinery has overcome its problems.

Although rhodium has risen swiftly because of a temporary shortage, the price could easily tumble when more supplies hit an illiquid market, dealers say. The same applies to cobalt.

Looking ahead to commodity markets this week:

Livestock and Meats

After the close of trading Friday, the Agriculture Department reported that the number of cattle on feedlots in 13 major ranch states on July 1 rose 1% from a year earlier.

The report was in line with the expectations of analysts.

The department reported that the number of young cattle placed on feedlots for fattening in the second quarter dropped 2% while sales by feedlots to slaughterhouses were flat.

According to analysts, heavy rains this spring improved the condition of pastures, which in turn reduced pressure on ranchers to sell their cattle to feedlots.

On Friday, the Agriculture Department is slated to release a survey of the nation's cattle herd, which began to slowly rebound last year after slipping to its smallest number since 1961.

Industry analysts expect the government to report that the number of cattle is increasing slightly.

In the hog market, traders will watch whether pork belly prices recover from last week's plunge. The price decline was fanned by speculation that high retail prices are damping consumer demand. Pork bellies are used to make bacon.

Energy

Crude oil prices soared Friday on the belief that members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries would toe the line in the face of Saddam Hussein's strong-man tactics. For the first time in almost two months crude for August delivery broke through a resistance level and finished trading at more than $19 a barrel.

With oil ministers preparing to sit down and talk in Geneva on Wednesday, traders say they don't want to be caught short in the market. Iraq's threat to discipline cartel members who are pumping beyond their quota may cause OPEC producers to rein in output to no more than 22.5 million barrels a day. That level is still above the overall OPEC quota, but far below real OPEC production in recent months.

Demand for the cartel's crude is expected to increase during the third and fourth quarter on a seasonal basis, another factor that will bring some relief to a market glutted with crude supplies, experts say.

Copper

Futures prices Friday rose almost two cents a pound; the September contract closed at $1.1980. Traders will be watching to see if the gains are sustained.

Friday's advance was a rebound from five consecutive days of declines, which saw the September price drop 6.15 cents. The rally occurred despite a 15,000-ton increase in London Metal Exchange copper warehouse stocks and a tentative settlement of the Asarco strike that began July 1.

However, the stock increase had been predicted, and a labor settlement had been expected after Asarco and the United Steelworkers union sat down Friday for the first time since July 3 to resume contract negotiations. Basically, analysts said, the selling pressure just seemed to have run its course.

The copper industry is behaving normally this summer, in contrast to the past two years when demand for copper was strong and prices were high, according to Fred D'Agostino, president of Metal Resources Inc., of Riverside, Conn., a scrap and refined copper trading firm.

"Brass, tube and wire mills this summer are closing down for their regular overhauls, which is leading to a lack of demand for copper," he said. "Japan, on the other hand, for several months has been a major buyer of U.S. copper and this has contributed an underlying support to the market."

There have been spurts of buying, such as early in the month when the July contract rose 20 cents on no discernible reason, Mr. D'Agostino said. "If that demand were to come in August and September for fourth quarter consumption, I could understand it. Maybe speculators see something the rest of us don't," he said.
