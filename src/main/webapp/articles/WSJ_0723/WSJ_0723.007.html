Disney, EPA Settle Charges Of Dumping --- Entertainment Company `Accepts Responsibility,' Must Pay $550,000 Fine

  • Author: Richard Turner
  • Date: 07/23/90

BURBANK, Calif. -- Walt Disney Co. and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency reached a settlement over allegations that hazardous paint thinner and other cleaning solvents used at Disneyland for two years were dumped illegally at sites in Utah and Wyoming. Under the settlement, Disney "accepts responsibility" for the violations, but the EPA agrees these violations were unintentional. Disney will pay a $550,000 fine and develop both an environmental audit for Disneyland and a training program which will include techniques for supervising third-party contractors.

"It's by leaps and bounds the largest penalty ever for this region," said EPA enforcement attorney David Janik.

Disney last week filed a lawsuit in state court in Orange County, Calif., against Ken's Oil Co. of Garden Grove, Calif., charging breach of contract and fraud and asking for unspecified damages. It was Ken's that between May 1987 and August 1989 hauled about 14,000 gallons of waste from Disneyland to sites in Utah and Wyoming which, according to the EPA, often were mislabeled and dumped at facilities unauthorized to dispose of this kind of waste.

Ken's co-owner, Juanita Schow, said she didn't know about the lawsuit. "We're just the haulers," she said. "The responsibility is theirs."

Although the EPA has been investigating the alleged violations for about one year, the affair was kept quiet and the charges were filed at the same time as the settlement.

"No company wants to splash this kind of thing in the papers," said Kim Murphy, the former marine biologist and Imagineering executive whom Disney appointed vice president of environmental policy earlier this year in the wake of other environmental controversies.

"We're a very visible target," added a Disney spokesman. "There's an old saying, `It's the nail whose head sticks up that gets hit.' "

In January, Walt Disney World in Florida was fined $10,000 after pleading guilty to a charge of violating the federal Migratory Bird Treaty Act after 15 black vultures that had been confined in a small shed on Discovery Island died.

In April, Mr. Murphy said, the EPA fined Disney more than $300,000 over the discharge of secondarily treated sewage into wetlands. Mr. Murphy said the program was an experiment to provide nutrients to the area and that permits were obtained, but that the EPA closed the program down because Disney was exceeding federal nutrient-level guidelines.

In 1988, Disney settled a similar hazardous waste case with the state of Florida over storage and labeling of materials at Walt Disney World. Disney paid a $150,000 fine.

In 1989, Disney paid a $2,500 fine to the EPA in Boston for violations of the Toxic Substances Control Act in a case involving the shipping of monorail cars with coolants containing toxic polychlorinated biphenyls.

In the case of the Disneyland waste material, "we relied on {Ken's Oil} to give us instructions on how to do this kind of thing," said Mr. Murphy. "They're the experts. The thing we're guilty of, and it's not a good excuse, is naivete."

Mr. Murphy said he has assembled a task force of Disney managers to focus on environmental issues. He also said that a training program has been in effect for a year and that a $100,000 environmental audit of Walt Disney World has been completed. He is interviewing consultants for a similar audit of company facilities in California.

Disneyland generates a lot of such hazardous waste. "We do a lot of painting and cleaning," the spokesman said. "We're well-known for it."

The EPA said Disney had cooperated fully with the agency. Mr. Janik said it still was assessing the extent of the environmental damage caused by the dumping.
