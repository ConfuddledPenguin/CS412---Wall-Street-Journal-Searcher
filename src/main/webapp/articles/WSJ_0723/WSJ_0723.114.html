Credit Markets: Anxieties Over Rates Surge Anew --- Worry Over Inflation Spreads, But Some Remain Optimistic

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 07/23/90

NEW YORK -- Bond investors, always a mercurial and hypersensitive lot, are up in arms about inflation again. But a few Wall Street economists think such fears are overblown. There is nothing bond investors dread more than inflation. So, it came as no surprise when bond prices fell sharply last week after the Department of Labor reported that consumer prices rose an unexpectedly strong 0.5% in June.

What did come as a surprise was the lack of hard-nosed, inflation-fighting rhetoric in Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan's testimony before Congress last week. That, along with the consumer price index report, led to accusations that the Fed is abandoning its battle against inflation and giving in to White House calls for lower rates. The Bush administration says lower rates are needed to avert a recession.

But several economists, including Lawrence Kudlow at Bear, Stearns & Co., Robert Barbera at Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. and Edward Yardeni at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc., believe that investors are getting all worked up for nothing. Despite the consumer-price report, these economists believe that inflation is moderating and that the Fed is on the right track. And they all were perplexed at the market's harsh reaction to Mr. Greenspan and the consumer-price report.

Bond investors' rising anxiety about inflation "is a tempest in the tiniest teapot," said Mr. Barbera. Bear Stearns' Mr. Kudlow went so far as to call the latest price report "a scam" and said it should come with a warning label that says "use of this release for investment decisions could pose grave risks to your financial health."

Mr. Yardeni blamed the market's negative reaction to the consumer-price report and Mr. Greenspan on the uncomfortable heat and humidity that hugged New York and other Northeast cities last week. "The bond market is a collection of humans who are prone to emotional mood swings," he said. "Right now, it's hot and everybody's a little cranky."

But these optimistic economists are bucking the consensus view on Wall Street. Most analysts, economists and professional investors are very worried about inflation and suspicious of the Fed's motives for cutting short-term interest rates July 13.

When Mr. Greenspan failed to talk tough about inflation last week while raising the Fed's inflation forecast for 1990, bond investors fretted that the Fed has shifted emphasis away from inflation and now plans to save the economy from recession. (Mr. Greenspan said the economy isn't deteriorating and the credit easing was to avert a credit crunch, but many bond investors didn't buy his explanation.) Many bond investors would rather see the economy crash land, than have the value of their portfolios erode due to rising inflation.

Economist Richard B. Berner, of Salomon Brothers Inc., holds the consensus view. In Salomon's weekly "Comments on Credit," Mr. Berner warned investors that the Fed's priorities have indeed shifted and warned that a less-hawkish monetary-policy stance by the Fed, high overseas interest rates and a weaker U.S. dollar will "reinforce the upward drift in bond yields." He forecasts that U.S. long-term bond yields will remain at current levels of 8.5%.

By contrast, Messrs. Kudlow and Yardeni see inflation moderating -- and not rising as the consumer-price report would indicate.

For one thing, they think that falling prices of gold, aluminum, silver, oil and other commodities are signaling lower inflation ahead.

Mr. Kudlow believes the consumer-price report overstated housing and rental-cost inflation, which comprises about 40% of the inflation index and was partly responsible for the large monthly rise in the June price index. The government reported that housing costs rose about 0.6% in June and by 4.4% over the past 12 months.

Mr. Kudlow says the housing component is flawed. He notes that home price data collected from trade associations reveal that prices are rising at a very modest rate of about 2%, and prices for apartments are declining by about 1%. "In other words, housing and real estate are presently experiencing their worst slump since 1982," Mr. Kudlow said. That makes the housing price increases reported in the June data "very hard to swallow," he said.

Among other problems, Mr. Kudlow claims that some prices used by the Bureau of Labor Statistics to calculate housing costs represent homeowners' asking prices, and not actual transaction prices, which are nearly always far lower than asking prices. "Perhaps {the consumer price index} gave us an inflation reading for the third ring of Saturn, but not for the planet Earth," Mr. Kudlow scoffed. The economist believes that yields on the Treasury's 30-year bond could fall as much as one percentage point this summer as evidence of lower inflation becomes more visible.

Mr. Yardeni forecasts an 8% long-bond yield by the end of the summer. He's expecting the next employment report, due out August 3, to be weak enough to trigger a bond market rally.

Friday's Market Activity

Treasury bonds ended modestly higher as fears of inflation eased at least for a day and investors looked at other economic news.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose less than 1/4 point, a gain of about $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield eased slightly to 8.53%. That was after Treasurys fell a total of 1 1/8 points Wednesday and Thursday in reaction to the consumer-price report and Mr. Greenspan's comments.

Traders attributed the firmer tone largely to the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia's report that manufacturing activity in that region is slowing. In its monthly report, the Philadelphia Fed said that 29% of the firms surveyed said business activity slowed down last month, while 16% reported an upturn.

That report "provided a positive impetus for the bond market," said Robert Chandross, a vice president and economist at Lloyds Bank PLC. Market participants focused on the regional Fed report because there were few other developments.

Meanwhile, dealers said investors continue to favor short-term securities over longer-term securities, as evidenced by the continued steepening of the yield curve. The yield curve measures the spread, or gap, between short-term rates and long-term rates.

The Treasury's bellwether 30-year bond yields 8.53%, which is 0.44 percentage point more than the 8.09% yield on the outstanding two-year notes. But just a month ago, 30-year bonds yielded only 0.10 percentage point more than two-year notes.

Junk bonds, which had a sharp run up for much of the week, slipped between 1/8 and 1/4 point Friday. Junk bonds are speculative-grade issue that carry low ratings or no ratings at all. Traders said that investors used the publication of the Resolution Trust Corp.'s $3.7 billion junk-bond portfolio as an excuse to take profits after the nearly weeklong rally. The RTC is the federal agency overseeing the thrift-industry rescue.

Traders, however, said the publication of the RTC's junk-bond holdings should not have any further negative impact on the market and may actually help by erasing some of the uncertainty concerning which issues the RTC might sell.

Earlier this year, some analysts had warned that the RTC's sale of its huge junk-bond holdings would flood the market for high-yield junk bonds and put further pressure on already weakened prices.

Among major market movers:

R.H. Macy & Co.'s 14.5% junk bonds gained about three points Friday to trade at 65 1/2 on news that the company reached an agreement with its bankers that allows some added financial leg room during the crucial Christmas shopping season.

Aztar Mortgage Funding Corp.'s junk bonds fell after issuer Aztar Corp. reported a second-quarter loss from continuing operations. The casino builder's 13.5% firstmortgage bonds fell 1 1/2 points to trade at 95.

Quantum Chemical Corp. bonds weakened on speculation that it will report weak second-quarter earnings. Quantum's 12.5% senior subordinated notes fell one point to 87 3/8.
