Technology: United Telecom's 2nd Period Dive Spurs Six Lawsuits

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 07/23/90

NEW YORK -- It wasn't a very good week at United Telecommunications Inc. The Westwood, Kan.-based firm and its chairman William T. Esrey were hit with six class-action lawsuits resulting from its poor second-quarter showing. And US Sprint Communications Co., United Telecom's long-distance telephone subsidiary and the source of many of its problems, said that three top executives have left the company.

The lawsuits, filed separately last week in the U.S. District Courts in Kansas City, Kan., and Kansas City, Mo., allege that Mr. Esrey and United Telecom misled investors about the company's earnings prospects.

A $42 million operating loss at US Sprint caused United Telecom's net income to fall 55% in the quarter to $40.6 million, or 18 cents a share, on a 12% rise in revenue to $2.07 billion. Included were non-recurring charges totaling $72 million, to cover a 5% reduction in US Sprint's work force and other expenses.

The results stunned Wall Street analysts -- who had already cut their second-quarter estimates for United Telecom to around 50 cents -- and shareholders. In his complaint, Raymond Campbell, a private investor, who owns 100 shares of the company's stock, accused Mr. Esrey of making optimistic statements about United Telecom's future profitability while at the same time planning to take charges against the company's second-quarter earnings because of problems at US Sprint.

Mr. Campbell's complaint noted that when United Telecom posted a 42% rise in its first-quarter net income in April, Mr. Esrey at that time said in a press release that "We expect . . . revenue growth and cost control to generate further improvements in operating margin." When the second-quarter results were posted, the complaint said Mr. Esrey told Dow Jones News Service that the charges had been planned for some time. The other five lawsuits made similar claims, according to a United Telecom spokesman.

Mr. Esrey was unavailable to comment on the suits. The spokesman said the company is "not in the habit of commenting on matters that are in litigation."

Meanwhile, David Gergacz, who led US Sprint's successful effort to win 40% of the multibillion dollar FTS-2000 federal telephone project, has left the company and been replaced by Gary Forsee, who used to head American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s 60% share of the project. Also gone are Clifford Hall, who had been hired to fix US Sprint's problem-plagued billing system, and Jerry W. Cosley, vice president of communications for United Telecom and US Sprint. None of the three executives was available for comment. United Telecom's spokesman said their exits were unrelated to the second-quarter problems and that they left to "pursue other opportunities."

Last week, Mr. Esrey told this newspaper that he regarded United Telecom's second-quarter showing, which came after several profitable quarters at US Sprint, as "a serious setback." He pegged the decline to severe competition in the consumer and small-business sector of the long-distance market. He also said that more than 1,000 workers would be cut from US Sprint's 20,000 member work force to deflate ballooning costs.
