Sigma Designs Aims to Be First to Cut ELF Emissions of Computer Monitors

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 07/23/90

A major supplier of computer monitors plans next year to begin selling models that give off sharply reduced amounts of extremely low frequency, or ELF, electromagnetic emissions. These emissions, which are produced by standard computer monitors and many other electrical devices, are considered hazardous by some medical researchers. Sigma Designs Inc., which makes large-screen monitors generally bought as an upgrade to standard computer equipment, is the first supplier to pledge to build low-ELF products. Other companies, notably International Business Machines Corp. and Digital Equipment Corp., announced plans last year to reduce the amount of very low frequency, or VLF, emissions, but none has disclosed plans to reduce ELF emissions.

The Fremont, Calif., company said it is responding to pressure from some consumers and health researchers, who contend that extended exposure to ELF emissions can cause cancer and other illnesses. Sigma is scheduled to formally announce its plans today.

"This is a sign that the computer industry is finally taking this {ELF} risk very seriously," said Louis Slesin, editor of VDT News, a bimonthly publication that reports on health and occupational concerns involving computer monitors.

While Sigma hopes to capitalize on growing demand for monitors that emit lower ELF levels, it doesn't plan to describe the new monitors as safer in its promotional and advertising materials.

"We think the data is absolutely indeterminate whether there is a safety issue or not," said Charlie Waters, Sigma's vice president of marketing, "so we don't want to add to the alarm about emissions."

Nevertheless, Mr. Waters said Sigma is convinced that a market exists in the U.S. for low-ELF monitors, and he expects other companies to enter it. "The attention being given this issue is creating concern," he said. "We believe that's going to generate demand."

Already, some makers of personal computers, which ship most of the monitors sold in the U.S., are coming under fire for failing to reduce electromagnetic emissions from their products. In June, for instance, MacWorld, a monthly magazine devoted to the Macintosh computer brand, began a campaign to pressure the manufacturer, Apple Computer Inc., into taking action. So far, Apple has agreed to fund health research on the question, but it insists that the monitors it ships with its computers are safe.

Sigma's new monitors, which will work with the most popular brands of personal computers, will give off only about one-tenth of the ELF emissions produced by the company's current monitors. The new monitors, which will come in monochrome but not in color, are outfitted with special circuitry that reduces both ELF and VLF emissions. They will be priced at $200 more than Sigma's standard monitors, which range in price from $1,299 to $3,495. The company will begin selling the low-ELF models in the first quarter of next year.
