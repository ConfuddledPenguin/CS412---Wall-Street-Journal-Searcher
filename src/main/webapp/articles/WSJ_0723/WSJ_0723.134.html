Several Defendants In GTE-Related Case Identified by SEC

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission identified several of the defendants in its insider-trading case in connection with GTE Corp.'s agreement to merge with Contel Corp. The agency said it identified four West German residents, all of whom traded through accounts at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. in New York from accounts maintained at Simonbank AG, Dusseldorf. All four traded call options within two days of the announcement of the deal, the SEC said.

A call is an option to buy a security at a specific price, usually above the current price and usually within a limited period.

The agency identified the four as Ortwin Heider of Hofheim, Hans-Jurgen Klisch of Meerbusch, Hannelore Mirbach-Krahn of Meerbusch and Bodo Pawlik of Ratingen.

The SEC also charged a German mutual fund, Union Investment, and two Swiss companies, Nalasag Corp. of Geneva and Sigesse SA of Lausanne, with trading Contel stock and options ahead of the announcement. The two companies bought the securities through Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. and Union Investment bought its shares through Dean Witter.

Officials of Nalasag and of Sigesse couldn't be reached for comment.

Mr. Heider, one of the four West German defendants, said: "I'm not an insider -- I heard a rumor, and the investigation will clear that." He added that he knows one of the other West German defendants, but wouldn't say whether it was one of the other three named by the SEC. "Dealing on rumor is not insider trading -- at least I hope not," he said.

Mr. Klisch, another of the individual defendants, is a Duesseldorf-based broker for Dean Witter. He says he is cooperating with the SEC, but insists that he cannot speak regarding the case in general. For himself, however, he says: "To my knowledge, this was rumor and a recommendation by Shearson Lehman -- that's why I bought." He said Shearson gave a buy recommendation; in addition, there were very good fundamental reasons to buy. Mr. Klisch says Union Investment, is a Frankfurt-based group and one of his customers.

The other two West German defendants couldn't be reached immediately for comment.

The SEC charges were brought last week, soon after Contel agreed to merge with GTE for $6.2 billion. At the time, the agency didn't identify any of the defendants but was granted a temporary restraining order by a federal court barring the defendants from selling their stock or options.

On Friday, the SEC also issued a laundry list of trades of both stock and options that it says were insider trades on material non-public information. It also dropped charges against buyers of some call options through Advest Inc., Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. and Smith Barney.

None of the banks or brokerage firms have been charged with any wrongdoing in the case.
