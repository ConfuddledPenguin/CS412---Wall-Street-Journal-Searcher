Politics & Policy: Many States Will Be Watching Massachusetts To See Whether New 5% Service Tax Pays Off

  • Author: Joseph Pereira
  • Date: 07/23/90

Whether or not to tax services is the question more states will be asking now that Massachusetts has slapped a 5% tax on many services. The service tax will add $223 million a year to the $2 billion raised from the existing 5% sales tax, which now accounts for 22% of the state's total tax revenue.

While not the first of its kind in the nation, Massachusetts's newly adopted service tax is the broadest of any major industrial state. And it is the boldest step toward taxing services since Florida repealed its tax three years ago, after vociferous protests and threats by some businesses to pull out of the state.

It is inevitable, tax experts say, that many states will consider following Massachusetts' lead. "There seemed to be a lull after Florida, and now it appears that states are taking another look at broadening the sales-tax base," says Corina Eckl, a tax specialist at the National Conference of State Legislatures, a research group.

Last month, New Jersey decided to raise additional revenue by taxing, among other things, a number of services including telecommunications and janitorial services. And last year, Connecticut extended its sales-tax base to 21 services, from public relations to landscaping and window cleaning.

"I'm receiving more and more calls from legislators and legislative staff assistants with inquiries about taxing services," says Ronald Schreiner, secretary of the Department of Revenue for South Dakota, which long has taxed services in the state.

Fiscal deterioration in many states -- as in Massachusetts -- and the fact that the nation's economy is becoming more and more service-oriented are catalysts spurring legislatures to move toward taxing services, experts say. "The question is bound to pop up in legislatures every time somebody needs money," says Hal Hovey, editor of State Budget and Tax News in Alexandria, Va. "As states look for new tax bases, services consumed by individuals will be up for inspection before the more sensitive issue of corporate taxes."

From a fairness standpoint, taxing services makes sense, many contend. "There's no reason why buying a color TV should be taxed and having that color TV repaired should not be subject to a sales tax," argues John Mikesell, professor of public and environmental affairs at Indiana University and co-author of a book titled "Sales Taxation."

But there are drawbacks. For starters, almost any new tax is politically unpopular, a fact lost on few legislators. Moreover, until service taxes become common in states, those that don't have them will have an economic advantage over those that do.

"You can tax your services, but you make them less competitive," says James W. Wetzler, commissioner of taxation and finance for New York state. "States like Massachusetts and Connecticut are going to have to fall behind the field in competitive ability." Stiff opposition to a broad service tax prompted New York state to back down and instead levy a watered-down tax on only cleaning and security services.

Not all services in states with service taxes risk losing customers to out-of-state competitors, of course. Customers aren't likely to cross state lines for an out-of-state television repairman or studio photographer, for example, just to avoid the tax.

And there are ways to try to limit the effect on other service providers. Massachusetts, for instance, will impose a "use fee" equal to the service tax on in-state businesses that employ an out-of-state accounting firm.

No doubt, a tax on services could create a paper quagmire, especially with scores of exemptions tacked onto bills. In Massachusetts, for instance, separate electric meters may have to be used to track the energy used by businesses specifically for manufacturing (which won't be taxed) as opposed to electricity used for lighting and air conditioning (which will be). If collection difficulties permit many to avoid paying the tax, it could be perceived as inequitable and become even more controversial.

The state's Department of Revenue concedes that tax enforcement won't be without snags. "Certainly there're going to be glitches," a department spokeswoman says. "Things, at first, aren't going to be crystal clear; there's some educating to be done to clear up confusion."

The department is underestimating tax revenue from services during the first year, partly in anticipation of collection difficulties.

As a result, some states are expected to proceed cautiously. This year, Kentucky toyed with the idea of a service tax but backed off, afraid of possibly blunting state business. California, still locked in a struggle to balance its fiscal 1990 budget, isn't considering broadening a sales tax.

The Massachusetts Legislature modified its tax bill with a pastiche of exemptions to overcome some of the objections and win political support. For example, it exempted services provided by Massachusetts firms to out-of-state clients from having to pay the tax, so that Massachusetts companies wouldn't be at a competitive disadvantage.

To appease small businesses and individual taxpayers, the Legislature decided to waive the tax on professional bills of less than $20,000. It also exempted advertising costs and medical services from the tax. And Boston-based Fidelity Investments, the nation's largest mutual-fund company, used its lobbying clout to get mutual funds exempted from the tax.

But the law will tax 594 types of services, including fees charged by lawyers, accountants, architects and engineers, as well as parachute training for pleasure, yoga instruction, fortune-telling and piped-in music.

Opponents still haven't given up their campaign against the new tax. While the state's Supreme Judicial Court gave an advisory opinion that the tax doesn't violate the state's constitution, several businesses and professional groups say issues addressed in the ruling were narrow. They plan further legal challenges to the tax, raising U.S. constititutional issues.

"For instance, the opinion doesn't treat the Sixth Amendment issue of an individual's constitutional right to (legal) counsel," says Charles Swartwood, president of the Massachusetts Bar Association, a reference to the effect the service tax would have on legal fees. In bypassing that issue in the advisory opinion, "the Supreme (Judicial) Court in essence has invited us to come back to court," he says.

In addition, a Massachusetts citizens group is seeking to dismantle the law this November by bringing before voters an initiative that would in effect rescind the tax package.
