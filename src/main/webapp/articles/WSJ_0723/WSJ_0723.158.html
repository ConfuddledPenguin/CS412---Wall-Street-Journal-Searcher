Politics & Policy: House Panel May Cut Funds For Weapons

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 07/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Armed Services Committee appears ready to cancel or sharply slash funding for the Air Force's B-2 Stealth bomber, the Army's new LH helicopter and a host of other multibillion dollar proposed weapons. Cuts in procurement will account for nearly half of the overall $24 billion the committee is expected to carve out of the Bush administration's nearly $307 billion defense budget request.

The first clear indication of that trend is likely to come today, when committee chairman Les Aspin (D., Wis.) is expected to urge the House to eliminate procurement money for additional B-2 bombers built by Northrop Corp. The move could save at least $35 billion by the late 1990s.

Meanwhile, the House panel is ready to eliminate some 125,000 active-duty troops in 1991, but lawmakers and committee aides said savings from those cuts are likely to total well under $1 billion for the fiscal year starting Oct. 1. That's primarily because the committee will provide expensive health-care benefits, temporary housing, relocation assistance, severance payments and other programs intended to help cushion the blow for both officers and enlisted personnel removed from the Pentagon's payroll.

Rep. Aspin told reporters last Friday that the committee, which is slated to approve a defense authorization bill at the end of the month, will rely more heavily on savings from procurement cutbacks than the Senate's version of the measure. "We're sympathetic to the problem of" soldiers being forced out of the military, he said.

The Senate Armed Services panel envisioned saving nearly twice as much in military personnel accounts next year, although it called for fewer troop cuts. The Senate committee also approved the bulk of the Pentagon's request for the radar-eluding B-2 and the LH helicopter.

In the case of the LH, some senior House Republicans are leading the battle to remove it from the budget entirely. Moreover, Defense Secretary Dick Cheney in the next few weeks is expected to propose deep cuts of his own to reduce the program's $42 billion price tag, according to Pentagon officials.

For the radar-eluding B-2, each of which carries an estimated price tag of $800 million or more, Rep. Aspin's anticipated attack could be the most serious blow yet. Last year, the Wisconsin lawmaker played a major role in keeping the embattled bomber alive. But in the past few months, cost estimates have skyrocketed and the Air Force has come under increased criticism on Capitol Hill for failing to effectively monitor contractor performance or justify the need for a full fleet of B-2s.

Rep. John Kasich (R., Ohio), a leader in the drive to kill the B-2, declined to talk about Rep. Aspin's anticipated change of heart. But in an interview, he said that at least 207 House members have indicated they support an amendment to complete flight tests but cut off production after 16 planes. "The program is literally falling apart," Rep. Kasich asserted. A Northrop spokesman declined to comment.

In the face of escalating congressional opposition, the Air Force is defending the B-2 by emphasizing what it claims would be a nearly $4 billion penalty for canceling proposed production plans. In a letter earlier this month, Air Force Secretary Donald Rice warned members of the House Appropriations Committee that the government would have to bear the high cost of closing facilities, compensating contractors and disposing of tools, parts and other materials. Temporarily shutting down production for just one year, Mr. Rice maintained, would increase those costs to $4.6 billion.

Members of the House committee, however, have challenged the termination cost estimates released by the Air Force.
