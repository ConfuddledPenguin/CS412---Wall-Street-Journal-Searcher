Marketing & Media -- Advertising: More Sponsors Put Their Money On This Fall's Kiddie TV Shows

  • Author: Joanne Lipman
  • Date: 07/23/90

"The Cosby Show" used to be mass advertisers' ideal way to reach consumers. This coming season, though, the venerable Cosby is getting some stiff competition from Peter Pan, Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles and the New Kids on the Block. After years of talking about the spending power of kids, advertisers are finally betting real money to try to prove it. Advertisers that used to ignore the kids' market are waking up and discovering after-school and Saturday morning TV. "Up-front" sales of network ad time for kids' programming ballooned for the coming season, fueled by new advertisers ranging from microwave dinners to fastfood chains. The big jump came even as prime-time ad sales fell short of expectations. Kiddie cable and syndicated kid shows had blockbuster ad sales too.

"The influence on spending by kids has become apparent to everybody, and that's the reason there's more money for everyone," says Paul Rittenberg, vice president of daytime and kids' sales for Capital Cities/ABC's network. Despite mounds of research and years of lecturing by ad industry gurus on kids' buying power, he says, "it's taken a while to sink into the ad agency planning process."

Anyone up early enough to catch the Saturday morning cartoons on TV this fall will find a new group of advertisers joining the usual candy, cereal and toy hawkers. Ad executives say microwave foods, such as Tyson Foods' new Looney Tune meals for kids, are becoming major spenders. So are sneaker companies that apparently have decided they had better hook the kids before they grow into brand-obsessed teens. And Burger King is making a major new kids' push, in an aggressive bid to invade McDonald's warm-and-fuzzy Ronald McDonald turf, ad executives say.

Adding to the spending spree, traditional toy companies are beefing up their ad budgets this year, people in the industry say.

The kids' market has a "relatively small number of advertisers," says Jerry Dominus, senior vice president of entertainment marketing and sales for CBS. "When you have key players stepping up spending, and add in a few totally new contenders, it really ratchets up the market."

Loretta Volpe, senior vice president and director of media and marketing services for ad agency Griffin Bacal, whose clients include Hasbro, Playskool and Milton Bradley, adds: "This was one of the craziest kid marketplaces of any time. It just exploded."

All told, the kid-show boom adds up to tens of millions of new kiddie-TV dollars. Industry executives estimate that the three major networks brought in a total of $155 million to $165 million for this fall's kids' TV ad time, up sharply from about $135 million a year earlier. On top of that, News Corp.'s Fox network is offering its first kids' lineup, including a daily animated Peter Pan cartoon and several Saturday morning shows, that ad executives estimate took in an additional $30 million. And this fall, ABC will roll out a new animated New Kids on the Block show.

Meanwhile, syndicated kiddie shows, aired on independent stations and network affiliates, saw an equally powerful ad surge. That market was propelled largely on the strength of "Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles" and Buena Vista Television's new two-hour kiddie schedule called "Disney Afternoon."

The Disney shows, aired Monday through Friday and including the popular "Chip 'n' Dale's Rescue Rangers" and "Duck Tales," proved to be some of the strongest contenders in the kiddie market. Mark Zoradi, vice president and general manager of Buena Vista Television, says the four shows in the two-hour block pulled in $71 million in upfront ad sales.

That would make Buena Vista a bigger player than any of the three major networks. Industry executives estimate that ABC was the biggest network player, with about $65 million in upfront sales, followed by CBS with $50 million to $55 million, and then General Electric Co.'s NBC with $40 million to $45 million.

Certainly, advertisers had reasons for moving money into the kids' market other than just deciding the ad industry's research departments were right about kids after all. For one, prime time has gotten stratospherically expensive: "The networks keep seeing an erosion in ratings, but the prices don't go down. The kids' market is so much less expensive," Griffin Bacal's Ms. Volpe says. She also credits the catfight shaping up among the networks, Disney's powerhouse programs and upstart contender Fox.

At its heart, though, the boom in the kiddie market suggests that advertisers are finally realizing just how tight a hold on the family pursestrings kids really have. Not only do kids personally spend about $4 billion in allowance money each year, estimates Children's Market Research Group, a New York-based research firm, but they influence families' decisions on another $60 billion worth of purchases -- everything from vacations to computers.

"I'm beginning to get calls from advertisers who are recognizing it's both the parents and the children who make decisions," says Selina Guber, Children's Market Research president. And while she has been preaching about kids' spending power for years, she adds, the advertisers' interest "is definitely a recent trend."

FCB Wins Coors Job

Coors Brewing Co. pulled its estimated $20 million to $25 million Coors Extra Gold account from GSD&M and handed it to Foote, Cone & Belding, Chicago.

Golden, Colo.-based Coors wouldn't say what prompted the switch of the big beer account. A spokeswoman would only say that the move resulted after a "periodic agency review process," adding, "We're fortunate to have agencies that are very talented, and we need to draw resources from them as needed."

The win is a big addition to the Coors business at Foote Cone, the shop that already handles ads for the company's Coors Light, Keystone and Killian's Red brands. New ads for Coors Extra Gold are expected to begin airing in December.

GSD&M, in Austin, Texas, continues to handle the ad account for the regular Coors brand and Hispanic ad responsibilities for Coors and Coors Light brands.

JHIRMACK SWITCH: Playtex Family Products Corp., less than a year after awarding its Jhirmack hair-care account to Backer Spielvogel Bates, switched the business to Rosenfeld, Sirowitz, Humphrey & Strauss. According to Leading National Advertisers, Playtex spent nearly $13 million advertising the brand last year. New York-based Rosenfeld Sirowitz handled the account from 1978 until last September. Backer Spielvogel is a unit of Saatchi & Saatchi. Stamford, Conn.-based Playtex couldn't be reached for comment.
