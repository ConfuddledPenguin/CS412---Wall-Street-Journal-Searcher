Citicorp Trims Management Structure At Developed-Country Banking Unit

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 07/23/90

NEW YORK -- Citicorp said it eliminated two layers of management in its commercial and investment-banking operation for North America, Europe and Japan, in the start of an effort to cut the unit's operating expenses. The flatter management structure isn't expected to trigger any massive dismissals of employees, but it is expected to result in piecemeal layoffs over the next couple of years.

Rumors have been rife among Citicorp employees that about 1,000 jobs will be cut from the 15,000 in the unit.

Michael A. Callen, Citicorp's head of global finance for developed countries, said in an interview: "There is no number {of jobs to be eliminated} that has been settled on. I know that revenues and costs have gotten out of whack. We have to sit down with the 50 activity-center managers and look at revenue and costs and say: `You tell me what the correct number is.' There won't be any slashing and burning like we've seen elsewhere."

Eight group and division heads will lose their administrative roles and become, in the words of Mr. Callen, "player-coaches." They are Rodney F. Ballek, Peter Burnim, Michael T. Welch, Philip B. Lassiter, Michael Kelland, Ernst Brutsche and Jeffrey Grandy.

The elimination of the eight group and division executive titles is the latest step in Chairman John S. Reed's reshaping of Citicorp's commercial and investment-banking business. For much of his five years as chairman, Mr. Reed has been preoccupied with the developing-country debt problem. Only in the last 18 months has he immersed himself in the commercial and investment bank.

Like many of its competitors, Citicorp's commercial and investment banking for developed countries has suffered from rising costs of problem loans and sluggish corporate demand for loans and services.

In the first six months of this year, Citicorp's commercial and investment-banking business for developed countries earned $85 million, down 72% from a year earlier. That's largely because loss provisions and other related costs for problem commercial real estate and highly leveraged transaction loans soared to $501 million from $149 million.

In recent weeks, several large banking companies, including Chase Manhattan Corp., Manufacturers Hanover Corp. and Continental Bank Corp., have announced sweeping staff cutbacks. Some analysts have said that Citicorp, too, was overdue for cutbacks.

Mr. Callen said Citiorp has no further plans to scale back any businesses, as it recently did in its municipal securities and London securities units. Citicorp, however, is expected to concentrate its resources on what Mr. Callen has called its "seven towers of excellence" -- foreign exchange, risk-management products, real estate finance, transaction processing, highly leveraged transactions, securitization of assets and loan syndications.
