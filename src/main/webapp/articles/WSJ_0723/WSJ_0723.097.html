Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Second-Quarter GNP Data Due Friday As Consensus Puts Growth Rate at 1.5%

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 07/23/90

NEW YORK -- The government unwraps its initial tally of second-quarter gross national product this week as economists grapple with revisions, concerns about the rest of the year, and data flaws. The consensus view expects that GNP, or the total value of the nation's output of goods and services, grew at an annual rate of 1.5% in the second period, after adjusting for inflation, a bit slower than the first quarter's 1.9% pace. The GNP report, due out Friday, also includes benchmark revisions stretching back three years. The week's only other major offering is June durable goods orders, due out Wednesday. Orders are expected to show an increase of 1.5% on the heels of May's 4% jump as capital-goods orders continue to rebound after several months of decline.

Although this week's calendar is thin, there's plenty to puzzle over. In a quite informal survey, several economists were quizzed on their estimate for the quarter, on the most significant positive and negative forces in sight for the balance of the year, and on their pet peeves about data and interpretation of the data.

"The weakest aspects of second-quarter numbers are likely to be in both residential and business fixed-investment" plus weak consumption, said Stephen S. Roach, senior economist at Morgan Stanley & Co., New York. As one positive, he cited auto production, which he credits for about two-thirds of his 1.8% GNP growth estimate for the second quarter. Mr. Roach said the swing in autos is largely driven by inventory-building in advance of a possible strike, and he expects an "equally strong or stronger boost to the third quarter."

Mr. Roach's pet peeve? "A lot of people are presuming the consumer will stay weak. I think the consumer will snap out of lethargy in the second half -- not for a big surge, but for something closer to trend," he said.

Brian J. Fabbri, chief economist at Midland Montagu Economics, New York, cited inventory rebuilding in the second quarter as "a big variable" that could swing the GNP growth rate as much as one percentage point. His GNP estimate for the period is from 1.5% to 1.75%, which takes part of the variable into account.

Looking ahead, Mr. Fabbri said "the principal negative right now is consumer psychology." He cited "recession in the home-building industry" as a major factor affecting prices of new and existing homes and "depressing" homeowners. "If {homeowner} confidence is down, it could also imply that spending could be a trickle for the remainder of the year," he said.

As for positives, Mr. Fabbri noted "we could fashion something out of a deficit-reduction package. It would be the first in years, and that's positive." His peeve concerns the premier indicator these days: employment data. "It's a number we're discovering we don't forecast well on a month-to-month basis" as well as one that's subject to huge revisions, Mr. Fabbri said.

Neal Soss, a managing director of First Boston Corp., New York, expects "essentially all" second-quarter GNP growth (his estimate is 2%) to be attributable to inventory accumulation. Among the negatives ahead, he cited: domestic demand that continues to be stressed by weak employment, tighter credit and weaker profits; fiscal drag stemming from federal, state and local belt-tightening; and softer exports.

On the plus side, Mr. Soss noted that the Federal Reserve Bank "almost guaranteed" not to use monetary policy to force a recession. Until recently, his pet peeve has been that the slowdown in money-supply patterns means something significant -- such as a slowing of banks' willingness to lend money. "It was a pet peeve," said Mr. Soss. "But it's hard for me to claim it now that {Fed} Chairman {Alan} Greenspan has himself claimed it."

Francis H. Schott, chief economist at Equitable Financial Cos., New York, said second-quarter GNP "might be a positive surprise, closer to a 2% than a 1.5%" growth rate. He expects a recovery in the industrial sector to ensure more growth down the road. "Export orders will start to show through in the second quarter, and even more in the third and fourth quarter. Exports are my big positive," he added.

Mr. Schott stopped short of naming a negative ahead, but cited "contingencies" that include weakness in residential and commercial construction that "could drag the economy down." But he countered that declining mortgage rates "might alleviate that."

As for pet peeves, Mr. Schott cited the "continuing misconception" of economists who view monthly job growth of 100,000-plus positions "as weakness." Last year, growth averaged well over 200,000 jobs a month. Mr. Schott said the slowing this year simply corresponds with the pace of the economy and full employment. He added: "All the people on the `200-pitch' may have it wrong."
