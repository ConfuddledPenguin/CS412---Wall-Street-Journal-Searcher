`Dear Buyer: Sorry Your Widget Broke, but It's a Nice Day and...'

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 07/23/90

It ain't necessarily so that the customer's always right. The old adage has been given new meaning by a contest to find the best written response to a consumer complaint. In bestowing its "Silver Pen" award, the Major Appliance Consumer Action Panel, a nonprofit mediation board sponsored by the Association of Home Appliance Manufacturers, considers such qualities as sensitivity and responsiveness to a consumer's situation and complaint. Clear and concise language and proper writing style also are de rigueur.

What the letter doesn't have to do is give the consumer what he or she wants.

Indeed, say experts in the field of consumer relations, it's not what you tell consumers that counts, but how you tell it.

"You tell people to go to hell in such a way that they look forward to the trip," John Goodman, president of Technical Assistance Research Programs, quotes one of his trainers as saying.

Complaining consumers who fail to receive a satisfactory response from a company that participates voluntarily in the program may write to the panel and ask it to review the complaint. The panel also forwards letters it gets directly to a company and gives it time to respond. The panel's recommendations aren't binding.

Marian Stamos, who runs the well-regarded program, says the reason the award doesn't require that consumers get what they seek is simple: "Consumers may not be right in what they want."

The panel's 1989 Silver Pen award went to Julie Cicero Bezotte, customer-service manager at Emerson Electric Co.'s InSink-Erator division, a garbage-disposal company and dishwasher marketer.

Ms. Bezotte's letter responded to one by an engineer who complained about his dishwasher. "He was listening to it," Ms. Bezotte says, and he thought the water in the unit might not be clean.

The letter, which is exceedingly cordial, won't get any prizes for liveliness. But then, nobody would expect a detailed description of a dishwasher's timer sequence to set the world on fire. Ms. Bezotte cautioned the consumer not to "confuse the `wash' cycle and the `purge' cycle." And, she wrote, "as long as all plumbing and drain connections are correct, waste water cannot back up into the dishwasher."

The world will have to take it on faith that the letter satisfied his complaint. Both the company and the panel prefer to keep the identity of consumers confidential.
