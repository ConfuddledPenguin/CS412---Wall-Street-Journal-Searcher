Moen Purchase By American Brands Agreed --- At $975 Million, Sale Price Represents Big Profit For Forstmann Investors

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 07/23/90

American Brands Inc. agreed to pay $975 million to acquire Moen Group Inc., a faucet maker, from Forstmann Little & Co., a leveraged buy-out firm. For American Brands, the acquisition sharpens the focus of its hardware and home-improvement business. That business is one of four areas where the Old Greenwich, Conn., company is trying to diversify to reduce its dependence on tobacco, which still generates 59% of its sales and 67% of its operating profit.

For Forstmann Little, the sale price represents a fat sixfold profit for its equity investors since the New York concern paid $840 million to acquire Stanadyne Inc., Moen's predecessor, in March 1988.

American Brands stock price rose $1 a share to $70.50 in composite trading Friday on the New York Stock Exchange despite a broad market decline, mainly because of higher-than-expected second-quarter earnings.

For the quarter, American Brands said profit rose 32% to $158.4 million, or $1.51 a share fully diluted, from $120.2 million, or $1.18 a share; revenue rose 8.4% to $2.95 billion from $2.72 billion. The results were helped by strong sales of cigarettes, including Benson & Hedges, in Britain.

Allan Kaplan, a beverage and tobacco analyst at Merrill Lynch & Co., said American Brands paid "a rich price" of 10 times operating cash flow for Moen that he estimated will dilute earnings 20 cents to 30 cents a share in 1991. American Brands also sells life insurance, liquor and office products.

Emmanuel Goldman, beverage and tobacco analyst at PaineWebber Inc., said American Brands is targeting the market for kitchen and bathroom repairs because homeowners believe that in a flat real estate market, such repairs can boost their resale price. Even so, American Brands said it plans to sell its Waterloo tool-box business.

Brian Little, a founding partner of Forstmann Little, said the sale is "a classic example of how a deal can go right, even as the critics look back on leveraged buy-outs of the 1980s that reached a point of excess."

Forstmann financed the Stanadyne purchase with $75 million in equity, $530 million in bank debt and $235 million in subordinated debt from Forstmann's own investors. The firm then sold five businesses in metals and auto parts for $240 million.

At the same time, it renamed the company Moen and boosted its sales to $380 million from $300 million, Mr. Little said, "by pushing distribution toward the replacement market" via such sales outlets as Channel home centers and away from the weaker new-housing market. Moen, which pioneered single-handle kitchen and bathroom faucets, has about 25% of the U.S. faucet market.

After debt repayment, the sale will yield Forstmann about $500 million, giving its equity investors a gross annual profit, before fees, of 112% and the firm's debt investors a 29% annual return. Morgan Stanley & Co. advised Forstmann, Little & Co. on the transaction and Goldman Sachs & Co. advised American Brands.

The sale is Forstmann's third big deal this year. The buy-out firm acquired Gulfstream Aerospace from Chrysler Corp. for $825 million and has agreed to acquire General Instrument Corp. for $1.47 billion, or $44.50 a share.

General Instrument shares have been trading above the Forstmann bid because two other concerns, Kelso & Co., a leveraged buy-out company, and Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Inc., have asked for information about General Instrument, saying they might want to make a bid before a mid-August deadline.
