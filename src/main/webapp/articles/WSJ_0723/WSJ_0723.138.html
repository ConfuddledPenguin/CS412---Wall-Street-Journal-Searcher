World Markets: Japan's Big Securities Firms Are Easing Back Into Underwriting Equities, but Curbs Remain

  • Author: Clay Chandler Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/23/90

TOKYO -- Big securities companies are tiptoeing back into the underwriting business, 14 weeks after they suspended all new equity issues to protect the then-faltering Tokyo stock market. But the brokerage houses, still chastened by the market's precipitous decline in the first four months of the fiscal year, have imposed strict limits on the volume of new issues the firms will back. The cautious pace will cost Japan's securities houses tens of millions of dollars in lost revenue and delay capital expansion plans of many of the nation's manufacturers.

"Business is not going to be easy for us this year, and probably the next," concedes Yasuo Kanzaki, executive vice president of Nikko Securities Co.

For the stock market, though, the go-slow approach is crucial. The market's recovery from the crash has been wobbly. On Friday, unnerved by Bank of Japan Gov. Yasushi Mieno's continuing hints of tighter credit, the Nikkei Index of 225 issues plunged 634.10 points, or 2%, to 32421.52. Volume was thin, at about 500 million shares, down from 620.9 million Thursday.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the Nikkei retreated another 102.54 points to 32318.98.

The securities companies' reluctance to lift restrictions on equity-linked financing reflects deeply rooted uncertainty about the Tokyo market's prospects.

The combination of higher interest rates and investors' new wariness has impaled Japan's brokerage firms on the horns of a nasty dilemma: The firms desperately need the fat commissions they typically earn by underwriting new issues, but fear that churning out too much new stock too quickly might send the Tokyo market into a second tailspin.

"The market's going to have to be nurtured very carefully," says J. Brian Waterhouse, a financial analyst at James Capel & Co., a British brokerage house. "If you stuff it too full of paper, investors will back away."

It would be hard to put more paper into the market than the firms put through last year: Japanese securities firms raised $173 billion in equity-linked issues in the year ended March 31.

Those big numbers meant big profits to the Japanese brokerage houses. The four largest securities companies' revenues from underwriting topped 400 billion yen, or nearly $3 billion, and accounted for about 7% of their total income.

Executives at the Big Four securities companies -- Nomura Securities Co., Daiwa Securities Co., Yamaichi Securities Co., and Nikko -- voluntarily suspended equity-linked financing on the Tokyo market on March 20 in an effort to pull the Tokyo exchange out of a nosedive. Before the plunge was over, the market had lost more than 27% of its value on the last day of trading in 1989.

Executives of the top trading houses initially agreed to refrain from underwriting new issues for a month, but later extended the ban amid uncertainty about the market's recovery prospects. Underwriting resumed July 6 when Nomura issued warrants for a $300 million offering by Daikyo Real Estate. That deal was quickly followed by about a dozen others.

Trying to keep the pace of new issues under control, top executives in the Big Four brokerages' underwriting divisions are meeting weekly to coordinate underwriting operations, according to market analysts. Ministry of Finance officials deny direct participation -- "All I know is what I read in the newspapers," insists an official in the ministry's capital market's division -- but it is widely believed that the ministry is monitoring the discussions closely.

So far, the brokers have moved cautiously. They have agreed to steer clear of fixed-rate equity deals for now, instead confining underwriting to convertible bonds and warrants, which allow investors to buy stocks without the obligation to commit cash to the market immediately. Those companies planning to issue new stock before the crash are being given precedence by the securities houses; other companies are being told to cool their heels.

At the moment, analysts believe Japan's stock market can handle a slow, steady diet of new issues. The market is buttressed by a solid array of fundamentals. Japan's economic growth charged ahead at a 10.4% rate in the year's first quarter, and corporate and consumer spending remains vigorous.

Phillip Dodd, chief economist at S.G. Warburg Securities, a British brokerage firm, thinks the Tokyo exchange will "struggle to see good news over the next several months," but argues that Japanese stocks remain a sound investment over the long haul.

Akira Suzuki, a senior Morgan Stanley analyst who was one of the first analysts to turn bearish on Tokyo market in the weeks before it crashed, takes a different view. He predicts that the Nikkei will break the pre-crash high of 38915.87, reached at the end of last year, before the end of August. But he worries that the market may slip below 30000 before year end, as the effects of two quarters of high interest rates filter through Japan's economy, pushing up prices and dragging down exports.

In either case, the Big Four aren't anxious to force recovery before the economy can support the market. For an extra measure of safety, they've set the upper limit for new issues in convertible bonds at 100 billion yen and for issues in warrants at 50 billion yen. That's a far cry from the pace of dealing in the heady days before the market's crash, when firms would routinely announce several issues of more than $1 billion in a single day.

The securities companies self-restraint will cost them. The moratorium on new issues wiped out this year's first-quarter underwriting income, and many analysts estimate that new issues will be off by as much as half for the rest of the year.

Not surprisingly, investor interest in the stock of the securities companies themselves has also plummeted. Financial analysts at many of the major foreign brokerage firms in Tokyo confess they've stopped monitoring the Big Four altogether.

Unlike Wall Street's brokerage houses, though, Japan's Big Four aren't contemplating layoffs. Mr. Kanzaki says Nikko plans to make up for its losses by shifting resources to foreign securities and new products like mortgage-backed and asset-backed securities. And in the long run, he says, "We think there's still money available here" in the Tokyo market.

Friday's Market Activity

London shares ended relatively robust trading just off intraday highs, helped by institutional buying on the last day of the current two-week trading account.

A bomb explosion at the exchange didn't affect share trading but prevented options trading. One dealer said this led to some liquidity problems but otherwise there was considerable buy interest, albeit on a selective basis.

Overseas earners recovered from midweek lows, despite a continued strong pound. This was mostly ascribed to heavy U.S. buying, particularly of Glaxo and Reuters.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index gained 12.8 points to 2400.1. The Financial Times 30 Share Index rose 9.4 points to 1898.1.

Dealers noted the considerable buy interest in British Gas, following on activity generated by the issue of over 50 million shares earlier in the week. British Gas rose 5 pence to 228 pence (9 cents to $4.14).

The oil sector generally gained, as the oil price climbs on the border dispute between Kuwait and Iraq. Shell rose 10 pence to 484 pence, while British Petroleum gained 7 1/2 pence to 334 1/2 pence. BP also rose on news that the government is retiring its "golden share" in Britoil, BP's subsidiary. Dealers said the move gave BP more control of Britoil.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Paris, Madrid, Amsterdam, Brussels and Stockholm. Shares closed lower in Frankfurt and mixed in Zurich and Milan. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Share prices closed higher in Hong Kong and Taipei, mixed in Manila and Seoul, and lower in Sydney, Singapore and Wellington.
