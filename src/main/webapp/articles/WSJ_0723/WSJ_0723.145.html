Abreast of the Market: Stock Market Loses Vital Corporate Crutch --- Dip in Buy-Backs, Takeovers Cited

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 07/23/90

NEW YORK -- One of the strongest muscles in the stock market's long bull run is withering with the decline in takeovers and share buy-back programs. Corporate purchases gave stock prices a major push in the 1980s, but this mode of propulsion lost steam quickly this year as prices pushed to record highs.

So far, this lost supply of buying power has been more than replaced by other players, among which are mutual funds that are growing bigger as individual investors pour cash into them. But fewer corporate buy-backs means less underlying support when these other investors bolt for the exits, which happened Friday when more earnings disappointments helped sink the Dow Jones Industrial Average 32.67 points to 2961.14.

Corporate stock buy-backs and takeover mania, which rapidly shrank the supply of stock available to investors, reached a frenzied pace in the 1980s. From 1984 through 1989, the combination of takeovers and buy-backs reduced the market value of all New York Stock Exchange-listed issues by an average 6.5% a year, Goldman Sachs estimates. A smaller supply of stock during a period of high investor demand helped buoy prices. Goldman figures overall corporate purchases accounted for more than a fifth of the 114.8% rise in the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index in the decade's second half.

But this year, corporate demand for stocks through takeovers and buy-backs is waning. After adjusting for a quickened flow of new stock issues, the annualized rate at which shares are being taken out of investors' hands has fallen to the lowest level since 1984. Goldman Sachs estimates that the rate of shrinkage in Big Board market value will slow to 3% or less this year.

Robert Farrell, chief market strategist for Merrill Lynch, suspects corporations are on the way to becoming net sellers of stock after seven years of being net buyers. Companies will use proceeds from selling new shares to retire debt, he predicts.

Throughout the 1980s, companies gobbled up their own shares or took over other concerns because both tactics were cheaper than the cost of replacing corporate assets or starting a business from scratch. But in the process, many companies took on a lot of debt.

"Now corporations will start using any opportunity they get to sell equity in order to pay down debt and reduce interest costs," says Mr. Farrell.

At the start of the bull market in 1982, says Steven Einhorn, portfolio strategist at Goldman Sachs, stocks generally were selling at 50% of the per-share cost of replicating companies' assets. Now many stocks are trading at a premium to replacement value. That means, he says, buying its own stock isn't any longer a smart way for a company to invest its cash.

The buy-back fad is fading before many large companies have completed purchases of all the shares they announced months ago that they might buy, notes Laszlo Birinyi of Birinyi Associates, a research firm. As of the end of 1989, the 30 companies whose stocks make up the Dow Jones industrials had purchased only a fourth of the 685.4 million shares they had authorized themselves to buy.

Theoretically, these companies could come back into the market when prices head lower, but because of a sluggish economy and high interest payments on debt, companies have less cash these days to buy shares.

Reduced corporate stock purchases isn't necessarily all negative for the market, though, analysts say. For example, the much-slowed pace of takeovers means pension-fund capital is being diverted from once-hot buy-out pools. "Part of that money is coming in the front door {of the stock market, as direct investment} instead of the back door," Mr. Birinyi suggests.

Mr. Einhorn of Goldman Sachs says the decline of takeover speculation should free up more capital for investing in a broader array of stocks. The latest rally has been concentrated in growth stocks, which many analysts complain makes for a cockeyed advance that can't be sustained. Theoretically, says Mr. Einhorn, as the focus on big stocks declines, smaller stocks have a chance to outperform the larger ones. In practice, it hasn't happened yet.

But James D. Awad, president of BMI Capital, a money manager, says that while 1980s-style takeover speculation has ebbed, a new wave of takeover activity in smaller companies will help spur investor enthusiasm.

"Investment bankers still have to make a living," he says. "The theme is to move away from highly leveraged financial takeovers toward all-cash strategic acquisitions of small to medium-sized growing companies, especially niche companies." This is the undervalued part of the market, he says.

As for whether individual investors can take up the stock-buying slack left by corporations, the jury is still out. Some analysts think individuals are ready to become more active in the stock market. While the stock portion of individually held assets has shrunk to a historically low level, the recent heavy flow of cash into mutual funds suggests that trend is reversing.

Carmine Grigoli, vice president, equity research, at First Boston, notes that the average individual's net worth currently is just 14% in stocks, down from 26% in 1965. During the same period, the real estate portion, including the individual's home, has risen to 29% from 21%. Realigning those relationships presumably would help offset a slowdown in corporate buying.

Individuals whose net worth now is heavily in real estate will increasingly move cash into stocks as they seek investments that will outperform the rate of inflation, Mr. Grigoli says. A real estate recession, which has beset many regions of the nation, makes people feel less wealthy and inclined to save more and spend less; much of that cash is likely to go into mutual funds, with stocks promising a higher rate of return than real estate, he says.

"More people are going to be renting and saving rather than buying real estate," says Mr. Grigoli. He predicts that the strong flow of money into mutual funds will continue for three to five years, when a recovered real estate market, among other things, may compete more effectively for investors' funds.

Not all analysts agree with this scenario. Among them is Abby Joseph Cohen, market strategist at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Research, who has been steadfastly bearish throughout the market's latest rally.

Ms. Cohen agrees that corporate buying has been "enormously important" in lifting the stock market to its current levels. But she says no matter how unattractive other investments such as real estate may look by comparison, institutional and individual investors eventually will realize, as corporations apparently have, that many stocks are overpriced. Besides, she says, there are investment alternatives, such as U.S. Treasury bonds and bills.

Meanwhile, Ms. Cohen is bothered that much of the buying in the recent rally has been centered on the big-name companies with strong, dependable earnings growth. "You end up with a tiering of the market," she says, as a small, elite group of stocks zoom way out front and the rest plod along in the dust.

"How long can that last?" she asks. Not very, she says. And when the next big market break comes, "These {elite} stocks will be the hardest hit. You can only sell what you own, and investors like to sell what they can at a profit."

Friday's Market Activity

Stock prices dropped sharply as the latest shock waves on corporate earnings reverberated through the market, especially in the technology sector.

Although the market held firm through most of the session amid disappointing earnings developments at Storage Technology and Advanced Micro Devices, prices tumbled near the close after Texas Instruments' second-quarter results and outlook provided more bad news.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which rallied past 3000 at midday, fell more than 34 points in the final hour of trading.

The expiration of July stock-index futures and options contributed to the market's slide, traders said. A number of expiration-related buy orders that were placed in advance of the earnings report from Texas Instruments were quickly canceled when the news came out, said Thomas Gallagher, managing director in charge of capital commitment at Oppenheimer & Co.

Then sell orders in the Chicago stock-index futures markets triggered stock-index arbitrage sell programs. The tactic involved buying futures contracts and selling stocks to lock in price differences between the two.

The late wave of selling pushed New York Stock Exchange volume to 177,810,000 shares, compared with 162 million Thursday. On the Big Board, declining stocks beat advancers by 912 to 585.

Among broader market measures, the S&P 500 fell 3.71 to 361.61; the New York Stock Exchange composite index lost 1.73 to 197.65; and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index tumbled 3.18 to 335.87.

The Friday retreat left the Dow industrials with a loss for the week of 19.06 points, or 0.6%. Other indices posted sharper declines: the S&P 500 fell 1.6% for the week; the Big Board's composite index lost 1.3%; and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index retreated 1.7%.

With the market "breaking one stock after another" in response to disappointing earnings, Oppenheimer's Thomas Gallagher said institutions are likely to become more hesitant in committing more cash to stocks now.

"People are getting annihilated, losing tons of money here," Mr. Gallagher said. "There is no chance to get out of a {stock} position when things go bad."

Texas Instruments dropped 5 3/4 to 33 1/8 following its report that second-quarter net income tumbled 89.6% from a year earlier. The company also said it may report a loss for the third quarter, noting that it expects the semiconductor market to be "sluggish" for the next few months.

Advanced Micro Devices, another chip maker, plunged 1 3/4 to 7 1/4 on 1.4 million shares in the wake of its disappointing second-quarter results. The company reported a loss of 10 cents per share. Analysts had expected the company to break even or report a modest profit.

Storage Technology, which had been one of the best-performing stocks of the year, plunged 8 3/4 to 25 3/4 on 4.8 million shares. Although the company reported that its second-quarter earnings surged about 250% from a year earlier, it warned analysts that earnings for the full year "may end up being at the low end instead of the high end" of Wall Street's current forecasts. Demand in New England and United Kingdom markets has been weaker than expected, the company noted.
