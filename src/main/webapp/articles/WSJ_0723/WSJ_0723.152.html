JAPANESE INVESTMENT IN U.S.

Japanese investment in the U.S. is helping revitalize U.S. manufacturing, the Japan Development Bank said.

Investments made since the early 1980s should be appreciated, rather than resented, the bank said in a report on the role of Japanese manufacturers in the U.S. Ultimately, Japanese investment will help stop the "hollowing out" of the U.S. manufacturing sector, the bank's study asserted.

The Development Bank undertook the report in response to growing condemnation in the U.S. of Japanese investment. Of Japanese corporations' overall investments, 80% are made in developed nations and two-thirds of that in the U.S.

By investing in local capacity, Japanese manufacturers are adding to the U.S.'s capital stock and making a long-term contribution to the nation, the Development Bank said.

COMPENSATION FROM HUNGARY

Hungary said it would compensate people for loss of personal liberty under communism. A government spokesman said a compensation bureau would be set up in September to start handling complaints. "We are talking about compensating those who lost their personal liberty for political reasons and not about compensation for lost or nationalized belongings or real estate," the spokesman said. "I mean those who were interned, deported or were taken for {hard labor} to the Soviet Union."

NEW ITALIAN TAXES

The Italian government is increasing the tax on gasoline by 60 lire (five U.S. cents) a liter, raising the price per liter of super gasoline to 1,485 lire ($1.23). There are 3.8 liters in a gallon. The government is also raising the price of automotive diesel oil by 55 lire a liter. The excise tax on mineral water and beer was raised to 19% from 9%, while the manufacturing tax for liquor was increased 40%, the government announced. The revenue from new taxes is expected to be around 1.925 trillion lire in 1990.

A WARNING FROM MR. DELORS

The creation of new currencies by independence-minded Soviet republics could be "devastating" for the Kremlin's attempt to integrate its sickly economy into the global economy, European Community Chairman Jacques Delors said. Four of the 15 Soviet republics have said they want to replace the ruble with their own currencies. Mr. Delors, wrapping up the first official visit by an EC chairman to the Soviet Union, said the republics' actions would run counter to the EC's moves toward a single currency.

CHINESE INFLATION

Inflation in China dropped to its lowest level in five years in May, when the retail price index climbed only 2.6%, the State Statistical Bureau said. The average retail-price increase for the first six months was 3.2%, down from the 25.5% increase for the same period last year, the bureau said. In the fall of 1988, with inflation running at more than 30%, China instituted an austerity program to cool an overheated economy and ease inflation.

BMW'S EAST GERMAN PLANS

West Germany's Bayerische Motoren Werke AG said it would invest an initial 100 million marks (about $61 million) to build a factory for large machine tools in Eisenach, East Germany. BMW, which started operations in Eisenach before World War II, said it bought the rights to use a piece of property there and would buy this land for the factory as soon as the two Germanys have cleared the way for such cross-border property-ownership arrangements. BMW, which earlier said it had no intention of making cars in Eisenach again, said it was putting the tools project there "because of the availability of qualified labor."

PANAMA'S AILING HEALTH SYSTEM

Panama's state-run health system is near collapse, government officials and doctors say.

Three years of political crisis leading up to last December's downfall of Gen. Manuel Noriega have left hospitals and health centers throughout the country in serious disrepair, and a scarcity of medicial supplies has forced the closing of some operating rooms. "To repair all hospitals and health centers we would need about $10 million," said Minister of Health Jose Trinidad Castillero.

A lack of medical supplies, including penicillin and painkillers, has sometimes forced the closing of operating rooms. "Each day, more people are going to die who don't have to . . . because there aren't supplies," said Ruben Echavarria, the president of an association of Panamanian doctors.

The government, deeply in debt and facing a host of other problems, increased the health budget by 26% over last year to $101.5 million; but doctors say more money is needed. Panama has received some foreign donations to help resolve the crisis, including $1 million promised from Spain.
