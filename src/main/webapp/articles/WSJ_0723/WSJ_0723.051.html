Chrysler Offers Union Workers a Share In Ownership, Bonuses for Productivity

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 07/23/90

HIGHLAND PARK, Mich. -- Chrysler Corp. offered its union employees part ownership of the company and incentive payments in return for helping the auto maker slash costs. Chrysler officials didn't provide details such as how much stock the company would offer workers, and the sketchy proposal seemed to raise more questions than it answered. Union officials immediately said they didn't take the offer seriously. But Chrysler says it needs radical measures to close the cost gap with Japanese competitors.

"What we desperately need at Chrysler is cost parity," said Anthony P. St. John, Chrysler vice president for employee relations. "The long-term result of not being competitive . . . is failure." In addition to offering the UAW a piece of the company, Mr. St. John said Chrysler would be willing to give UAW workers bonuses for meeting specific productivity goals.

Chrysler's sales and earnings are mired in a steep slump, and crucial new products are still two years away. The company wants to be the UAW's target for negotiating a pattern contract for the Big Three auto makers, because Chrysler believes it can't afford to be as generous as General Motors Corp. or Ford Motor Co. If it isn't the target, Chrysler would like a different contract -- although breaking a pattern is near-anathema to the UAW.

In return for stock and bonus payments, the auto maker wants greater flexibility to operate its plants. It also wants the union's support for a plan to build a health care network with other employers that would help cut Chrysler's steep medical care bills, which are about $700 for each vehicle sold.

Chrysler is asking similar moves from its white-collar employees. They have been invited to bet a portion of their salaries on the performance of the company -- specifically, on whether Chrysler can meet ambitious cost-cutting goals.

This isn't the first time Chrysler has offered stock in return for the union's assistance. A decade ago, when Chrysler teetered near bankruptcy, it got wage concessions from the union. In return, Chrysler paid stock into a fund to be distributed to workers. In 1985 the company and union agreed to terminate the fund, and Chrysler distributed $550 million to 87,500 hourly workers in the U.S. and Canada. The workers had a choice of getting stock or cash, and most took cash.

Also in return for concessions, the company named Douglas Fraser, then the UAW president, to its board. Owen Bieber, the current UAW president, still sits on Chrysler's board.

Mr. Bieber has called on the Big Three auto makers to give union workers more say in management decisions, and to view workers as equal partners. But several union officials said privately that Chrysler's proposal was wildly unrealistic. Publicly, however, a UAW spokesman said, "It's far too early in the process to begin responding in the press to specific proposals."

Analysts responded favorably to Chrysler's employee ownership and incentive initiatives. "We've seen in company after company that if you own part of the company, you work smarter," said Ronald Glantz, an analyst with Dean Witter in San Francisco.

On Friday, Chrysler stock closed at $16, down 50 cents in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

Officials of GM and Ford, which also opened negotiations with the UAW last week, said they would wait for Chrysler to be more specific before commenting on its move.

Chrysler has the highest labor costs of the Big Three -- $33 an hour, compared with $31 an hour for Ford, and $30 an hour for GM. And the Japanese factories in the U.S. that have eroded Chrysler's share of the car market in recent months enjoy labor costs about $10 an hour below Chrysler's.

While these Japanese-owned plants simply operate more efficiently, some of the cost gap is beyond Chrysler's control. Its employees are older, and the ratio of retired employees to active workers is higher than GM's or Ford's. The Japanese plants are new, and have virtually no retirees.

These problems are compounded by the 13% drop in Chrysler's vehicle sales this year. The auto maker has said it expects a second straight year of negative cash flow in 1990. The company is expected to post a sharply lower second-quarter profit and said last week it expects a "substantial loss" in the third quarter.

In outlining Chrysler's initiative, Mr. St. John said the gain-sharing concept would reward workers at Chrysler plants for meeting specific productivity targets, such as lower absenteeism rates. The concept is similar to plans in many factories around the nation.

Chrysler said it also needs the flexibility to institute three shift operations at certain plants. Last fall, the company and union leaders agreed to such a plan for Chrysler's minivan plant in St. Louis. But workers at the plant voted down the plan. Three-shift production would allow the company to boost output and profit from a plant, without substantial spending for additional facilities.

In a related cost-cutting initiative, Chrysler said it wants to join with another large employer to build a multi-employer health care network. That would enable the auto maker to cut medical costs through efficiency of scale, Chrysler said.

Unless it creates a multi-employer health care trust fund, Chrysler's health care costs will increase by about $7 an hour, per employee, Mr. St. John said. That's because a new federal rule will force employers to allow health care retirement benefits to accrue while employees are still on the job, he said.
