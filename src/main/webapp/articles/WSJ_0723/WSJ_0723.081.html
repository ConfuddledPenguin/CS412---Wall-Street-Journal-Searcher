Economy: S&L Withdrawals Outpaced Deposits In Early Part of '90

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 07/23/90

WASHINGTON -- Withdrawals at savings and loans continued to outpace deposits in January and February, and produced a $13.3 billion net outflow in January, the Office of Thrift Supervision said. For the two months, the net outflows totaled $19.9 billion, compared with $20 billion in the same period last year. This past January's outflow grew from the $10.7 billion in net withdrawals a year earlier.

The outflows result from efforts by savings and loans to meet regulations under the S&L cleanup law, which requires thrifts to hold a greater amount of capital as a percentage of assets.

S&Ls with lower deposits don't have to hold as much capital, and many thrifts have lowered the rates they pay depositors in order to allow controlled outflows of deposits. Capital is the equity that the thrifts hold, and acts as a cushion to taxpayer-backed deposit insurance.

"It just tells us that, clearly, so many institutions still don't meet the new capital requirements," said James Barth, an Auburn University economist.

Mr. Barth said the deposit withdrawals may also indicate that some depositors are concerned that with so many federal S&L takeovers, their funds could be tied up. The deposits are all guaranteed for as much as $100,000.

"Either way, it still suggests that we have massive consolidation and shrinkage, and that's been the story for a year now," Mr. Barth said.

Total deposits at all 2,527 solvent and insolvent S&Ls -- 354 fewer institutions than a year ago -- fell to $796.3 billion in February 1990 from $927 billion a year earlier.

Mr. Barth said it's possible that a recent spate of media coverage about S&L woes could fuel an acceleration in withdrawals, but later data haven't been released yet. "Unfortunately, we are getting numbers for January and February, and it's July," he said.

The thrift office, which was overhauled a year ago under the S&L bailout law, was recently reorganized and has frequently delayed the publication of its data.
