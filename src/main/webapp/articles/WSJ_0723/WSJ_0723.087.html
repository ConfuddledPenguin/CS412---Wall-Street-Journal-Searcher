Texas Instruments Net Fell Sharply In Second Quarter

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 07/23/90

DALLAS -- Texas Instruments Inc. contributed to the sell-off of high-technology stocks Friday by reporting sharply lower second-quarter earnings, largely because of lower prices for semiconductor memory chips. The company also said that it might report a loss for the third quarter, citing depressed memory-chip prices, earthquake damage to its Philippines plant and research and development and start-up costs for its new products, including its four-megabit memory chip.

The company reported second-quarter net income of $11 million, or two cents a share, compared with $106 million, or $1.14 a share, a year earlier. Revenue fell to $1.59 billion from $1.63 billion. For the six months, net was $24 million, or seven cents a share, compared with $171 million, or $2.04 a share. Revenue in the six-month period declined to $3.13 billion from $3.23 billion.

Although analysts called the earnings disappointing, they said that a drop in second-quarter earnings had been expected. Estimates had ranged from five cents to 20 cents a share. Richard Whittington, analyst for Kidder, Peabody & Co., said he thinks the company's downturn is temporary, although he now expects it to show a third-quarter loss. "If they're saying {a loss is} possible, it'll probably happen," he said.

The company blamed its lower earnings on weak prices in the commodity end of the memory-chip segment of its semiconductor business. That caused a loss at the company's components division, which also includes electrical controls, custom semiconductors and integrated circuits, Texas Instruments said. Memory-chip orders increased slightly in the second quarter, though, and the losses in the division were smaller than in the first quarter, the company said.

Texas Instruments also said that startup operations and plant construction depressed earnings. The company is building a $250 million memory-chip plant in Avezzano, Italy, which may be operating by early 1991, in time for what the company expects to be a turnaround in the semiconductor market. In addition, the company is developing a four-megabit memory chip that should be marketed late next year and a 16-megabit chip that may be available late in 1992 or in 1993, a spokesman said.

Other semiconductor manufacturers that are exposed to the commodity end of the chip market are also suffering.

National Semiconductor Corp. about a month ago reported lower net for its fourth quarter, and Advanced Micro Devices Inc. contributed to Friday's high-tech stock sell-off with a report of its own second-quarter loss. But other semiconductor makers not so heavily dependent on the lower-priced chips, including Motorola Inc. and Intel Corp., have reported higher quarterly earnings recently.
