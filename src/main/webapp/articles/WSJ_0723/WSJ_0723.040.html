Calfed Posts Deficit Totaling $53 Million For Second Quarter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/23/90

LOS ANGELES -- CalFed Inc., stung by California's weakening real estate market, posted a second-quarter net loss of $53 million, or $2.10 a share. Citing "declining values" in real estate, CalFed, parent of California Federal Bank, a savings and loan with about $25 billion in assets, said it took its first quarterly loss in years after setting aside $91.7 million to provide for loan losses and losses on real estate investments. A year ago, CalFed earned $19.1 million, or 76 cents a share.

CalFed's poor results didn't spook investors, who traded the stock up by 37.5 cents to $14.875 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading, slightly above its yearly low.

But the news confirmed that California's once go-go real estate market is now sputtering, a trend that could spell trouble for many banks and thrifts in the state. And it was a reminder that with real estate prices slumping in much of the country, even relatively healthy thrifts will find it difficult to produce earnings as they scramble to revise the value of their real estate loans and holdings.

"With changing market conditions, it is prudent to take a more conservative view of potential future losses inherent in our real estate loan portfolio," Jerry St. Dennis, CalFed's chief executive officer, acknowledged in a statement.

The increase in loss provisions wiped out benefits gained from several positive steps taken by CalFed. Expense cuts in its banking operations are likely to save $35 million this year, and general and administrative expenses fell by 9.3% compared with a year earlier. And in the second quarter, new loan originations grew by 20% to $1.2 billion, and the banking segment's interest rate spread improved to 2.3% from 1.8% a year earlier.

For the six-month period, CalFed posted a loss of $34.1 million, or $1.35 a share, against net income of $36.6 million, or $1.46 a share, for the 1989 first half.
