Who's News: Former Grumman Chairman Linked To U.S. Inquiry

  • Author: Charles W. Stevens and Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 07/23/90

The name of John O'Brien, who resigned suddenly last week as chairman of Grumman Corp., has arisen in a federal inquiry into Pentagon contracting fraud, although he isn't believed to be a target of the probe. Before his abrupt resignation, Mr. O'Brien became embroiled in the federal criminal investigation of Pentagon procurement fraud and corruption involving Long Island-based companies, according to defense and law-enforcement officials. The grand jury inquiry began in Uniondale, N.Y., more than a year ago, according to these officials, but in the past few months investigators stepped up their interest in Mr. O'Brien and his activities.

Prosecutors have questioned witnesses and collected documents related to his involvement with the late James Kane, a Long Island businessman who helped run a now-disgraced political lobbying group representing area defense firms.

Other individuals associated with Mr. Kane funneled thousands of dollars of illegal campaign contributions to various lawmakers during the 1980s, according to investigators and court documents filed by the government. Messrs. O'Brien and Kane were friends, according to law-enforcement officials, and prosecutors are trying to determine whether Mr. O'Brien had a role in the operation of the lobbying group, called Long Island Aerospace Political Action Committee.

Mr. Kane's name originally surfaced two years ago as part of a broader Alexandria, Va., grand jury investigation of Pentagon bribery and influence-peddling. A search warrant issued at the time for one of Mr. Kane's companies asked for material "pertaining to schemes" to "extort campaign contributions" from certain defense contractors. Federal agents also sought documents "relating to the ownership of stock certificates" or other "things of value" that may have been given to public officials by one of Mr. Kane's companies or associates.

Federal investigators, among other things, are now trying to determine whether Mr. O'Brien, members of his family or other current or former Grumman officials received loans or other benefits from Mr. Kane or his firms, according to law enforcement officials. A member of the law firm representing Grumman declined to comment.

In May, a former shipping manager at Grumman's aerospace unit pleaded guilty to charges of conspiring to defraud the government by submitting to the Pentagon $300,000 of bogus bills submitted by a subcontractor controlled by Mr. Kane. The former Grumman manager is cooperating with prosecutors.

Previously, Grumman has maintained that it isn't a target of either grand jury investigation. Company spokesmen on Friday declined to comment on the matter.

Meanwhile, Grumman's new chairman said the Bethpage, N.Y., maker of jet fighters won't change course. Renso L. Caporali said his first priority is "to try to convince everyone that the change at Grumman is not so radical." Mr. Caporali, who also succeeds Mr. O'Brien as chief executive officer and president, said he plans "a continuation of the agenda that my predecessor was pushing so hard" at Grumman.

Topping that agenda is an effort to reduce Grumman's size in line with decreasing defense spending. "The downward pressure (in defense spending) is going to be on everyone (in the industry) and the only way to deal with it is to get yourself into fighting trim," Mr. Caporali said. Last month, Grumman announced it would offer early-retirement incentives to 6,000 employees, or 21% of its work force, to cut costs.

Grumman's announcement of the management change said that Mr. Caporali will serve in his new duties while the board considers other candidates for the positions, both from inside and outside the company. However, the new chairman said "I hope to stay in the job permanently." Mr. Caporali, the former vice chairman, technology, said the likely inside candidates would be Grumman's other vice chairman, Robert G. Freeze, and the heads of the major divisions.

Mr. Caporali said he first learned he would be assuming the company's leadership a week before the announcement.
