Politics & Policy: House Approves $170.7 Billion Domestic Bill

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 07/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The House approved a $170.7 billion measure funding the Departments of Labor, Education and Health and Human Services for fiscal 1991, after boosting anti-drug spending by $300 million to quiet "drug czar" William Bennett. The spending bill, approved 359-58, also includes large increases for medical research and aid to public schools.

The Appropriations Committee leadership amended the bill to add $321.4 million for drug treatment and the drug-free schools program, raising such spending to the levels requested by the Bush administration. Mr. Bennett got his money even though several members are still seething that, according to Rep. Silvio Conte (R., Mass.), he called them "a bunch of clowns" earlier in the week as he pressed for the money.

"If our distinguished drug czar is going to end up hyperventilating every time he doesn't get what he wants, then I suggest he go back to smoking cigarettes to calm down," said Rep. David Obey (D., Wis.).

The House rejected several attempts to trim the bill, which is about $17 billion, or 10%, above the 1990 level and about $3.9 billion over President Bush's request. The total is almost certain to be pared by the budget summit between the White House and Congress -- if those talks produce an agreement. "This is very likely the high water mark," Rep. Conte said.

The labor and education measure is the eighth and largest of the annual domestic spending bills to clear the House. Attention now turns toward cutting defense spending to offset proposed increases in domestic spending.

To that end, the House Appropriations Committee approved an $8.3 billion military-construction bill, which is 10% below President Bush's request and significantly pares the administration's plans for spending overseas. While the administration sought to spend $153.7 million in Germany, for instance, the panel approved only $33.2 million. The committee also amended the bill to bar the Air Force from moving its Ballistic Missile Organization away from Norton Air Force Base, Calif.

In a separate vote, the committee approved a $3.9 billion District of Columbia spending measure.

The rush of legislation from the House has increased pressure on the Senate to act. The Senate Appropriations Committee yesterday approved the first of its fiscal 1991 measures, a $20.8 billion bill funding energy and water programs. That is $580 million more than the administration requested and $2.3 billion above the current year's level, largely because of added spending to clean up nuclear waste at defense plants.

To accommodate individual senators on the committee, the panel endorsed 11 projects that weren't mentioned in the subcommittee version of the bill -- including projects in the home states of Sens. James Sasser (D., Tenn.) and Pete Domenici (R., N.M.), who are key negotiators at the deficit-reduction talks.

The bill directs the Army Corps of Engineers to spend $550,000 to protect the banks of a park on Lake Barkley in Clarksville, Tenn. The accompanying report earmarks $400,000 for a study of pollution problems in Nashville if local opposition prevents the corps from spending that sum on another Tennessee project. It also earmarks $500,000 for the Velarde ditch project in New Mexico.
