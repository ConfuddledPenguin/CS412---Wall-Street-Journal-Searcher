THE EC AND PERSONAL DATA

The European Community Commission is seeking to protect personal data against abuse.

Five EC members -- Italy, Belgium, Spain, Portugal and Greece -- lack legislation protecting an individual's right to control personal information in private or public data banks. Worried that varying levels of protection throughout the EC may obstruct the flow of information, the commission has proposed a directive based on a Council of Europe convention already ratified by seven EC states.

The directive would give individuals the right of access to information about themselves and the right to correct erroneous data. Data relating to race, health or political opinions would receive extra protection against unauthorized use.

Filippo Pandolfi, commissioner of science and research, says he fears that modern telecommunications technology means there is a greater risk that sensitive information will be misused or stolen.

KAIFU ADMINISTRATION IS POPULAR

Public support for the administration of Japanese Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu registered 60%, according to a survey taken last weekend by Japan's Yomiuri Shimbun newspaper. The support rate for Mr. Kaifu was the second highest in postwar Japan, ranking after the 60.5% rate of former Prime Minister Kakuei Tanaka's cabinet in a 1972 survey. The newspaper said the support rate for Mr. Kaifu, which was up 3.6 percentage points from a similar survey in June, was the highest since he took office in August 1989.

SAVING THE WORLD'S CORAL

The Swiss-based World Conservation Union will launch a program to conserve coral at its November meeting in Australia. The organization said scuba divers and indiscriminate developers are destroying the world's coral reefs. It said hundreds of thousands of divers breaking off small samples and locals harvesting coral to sell are chipping the reefs away. The organization aims to increase public awareness of the endangered reefs and the importance of not buying coral necklaces and shells. It will also try to encourage countries with reefs to allow diving only in certain areas or at certain times.

FRENCH CAR-IMPORT LIMIT

French Premier Michel Rocard, on a visit to Tokyo, defended his country's limits on Japanese automobile imports by saying France's auto makers were too important to sacrifice, the Japanese Foreign Ministry reported. Mr. Rocard told Japanese Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu that both countries advocate free trade but still hold to some protectionist policies, ministry officials said. France has advocated a 10-year transitional period to protect national car industries before the integrated European market is fully open to car imports. France limits Japanese car imports to 3% of its total market.

SOUTH KOREAN-SOVIET VENTURE

South Korea and the Soviet Union have agreed to open the first regular shipping service between the two countries. The line will run between the South Korean port of Pusan and Vostochny in the Soviet Far East, said officials of the South Korea Maritime and Port Administration. They said the regular container service is expected to start in October. They said the accord calls for splitting profits from the operation equally between the two sides. Only one container ship is expected to be used initially.

SWISS CIGARETTE RULING

A chain smoker may be addicted, but cigarettes are not drugs, according to the Swiss Supreme Court. The court's ruling upheld a lower court that had ordered a health-insurance company to pay sick benefits to a heavy smoker who had fallen ill. The insurers had refused payment, arguing that their contract didn't provide for sick-benefit money for illnesses caused by abusive use of drugs or medicines. The high court said while smoking may lead to addiction, cigarettes cannot be legally categorized as drugs.

WOMEN IN ITALY'S MILITARY?

Women could volunteer for service in Italy's military, one of the last all-male armed forces in Europe, under legislation approved by a parliamentary committee.

Increasing numbers of Italian women have been joining police forces in recent years, but the armed forces have remained closed to them.

The bill would open up the Italian services to women as volunteers and offer careers to them as officers and non-commissioned officers. However, women would still be excluded from combat roles.

The bill, passed by the Senate Defense Committee, must go before the full Senate and then to the Chamber of Deputies. It also would reduce the draft for men from 12 months to 10 months and offer alternative service in such fields as civil defense and environmental protection.
