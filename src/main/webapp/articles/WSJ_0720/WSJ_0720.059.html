Law: Panel Considers a Uniform Trial System For Libel, Stressing Truth Over Damages

  • Author: Amy Dockser Marcus
  • Date: 07/20/90

A proposal to establish a national libel law will be considered today by a group appointed by the nation's governors to draft model legislation that can be adopted in any state. The so-called Uniform Defamation Act would establish a nationwide system for resolving libel disputes by placing the emphasis on determining the truth or falsity of a statement rather than on awarding damages.

Over the past five years, lawyers have made several proposals to change libel law. But this is the first major effort to draft and implement uniform state libel legislation.

Media defense lawyers say that defending a libel suit is extremely expensive. And plaintiffs' lawyers complain that the current system doesn't adequately protect citizens from harm to their reputations.

Under the proposed act, libel trials would be divided into two stages. The first would be limited solely to the question of the truth or falsity of the disputed statements. Only in the second stage would the question of damage awards be addressed and the media allowed to raise constitutional defenses.

News organizations could limit their liability by offering to publish a retraction and pay plaintiffs' legal fees at the end of the first stage of the trial.

Supporters of the draft say libel trials tend to place less emphasis on truth than on issues such as whether the plaintiff is a public figure or the defendant acted with malice.

"The law is designed to provide someone a means of receiving vindication for reputational harm. It turns the law on its head to in effect eliminate the question of falsity," says Randall P. Bezanson, dean of Washington and Lee University School of Law and a draftsman of the bill.

But media defense lawyers have greeted the proposal with alarm. In a report prepared by members of the Libel Defense Resource Center, defense lawyers said the proposed legislation would allow plaintiffs to circumvent constitutional privileges the media has under the First Amendment and a line of U.S. Supreme Court cases.

The bill rests on the assumption that "what most libel plaintiffs want is a quick setting-of-the-record straight and not damages. That has not been my experience," says Lee Levine of the Washington firm of Ross, Dixon & Masback, who worked on the report.

While no one is certain that plaintiffs would settle for a retraction and legal fees, "the bill would give them the option to stop" the litigation, says John J. Walsh of the New York law firm of Cadwalader Wickersham & Taft, who has represented plaintiffs in libel suits.

The 350-member National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws will discuss the bill and consider changes. A vote is not expected before next year.

Harvey S. Perlman, dean of the University of Nebraska College of Law and chairman of the drafting committee, says he expects to hear "whether we're marching along the right path or if it's so offensive we should change directions or give up."
