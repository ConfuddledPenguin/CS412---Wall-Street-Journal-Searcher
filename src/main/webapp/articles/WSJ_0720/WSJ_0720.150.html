World Markets: Tokyo's Nikkei Index Declines, Then Rebounds To Close Above 33000 on U.S. Program Buying

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/20/90

Tokyo's Nikkei Index spurted higher in the last three minutes of trading, as program buying on the index by U.S. brokerage houses helped maintain a close above 33000 points. Meanwhile, London shares trailed Wall Street and closed at their intraday lows.

The Nikkei Index of 225 issues gained 7.51 points to 33055.62, after dipping as low as 32847 points, or down 201 points.

"Someone obviously wanted to see the index hold that 33000 level," said one trader at a U.K. firm, noting that futures weren't at a high enough premium for arbitrage.

In early trading in Tokyo Friday, the Nikkei fell 361.16 points to 32694.46.

Thursday's volume on the first section was estimated at 600 million shares, off from 621.5 million shares Wednesday. Decliners outnumbered advancers 560-383, while 176 issues were unchanged.

Traders said the market was broadly lower, as reflected by the average-based index, as selling pressure in the afternoon reversed morning gains by "inflation hedge" stocks.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues in the first section slipped 4.70 points, or 0.19%, to 2393.08.

The second section index dropped 17.19 points, or 0.39%, to 4384.89. Volume was estimated at 20 million shares, off from 23 million shares.

Some investment trust funds were canceling on index funds yesterday, traders noted. The investment trusts regularly adjust the imbalance between cancellations and buying, with yesterday's adjustment showing more cancellations, the traders said.

One trader said that related selling was "rather minor," but another noted that whenever the funds come with the intent to sell, the psychological impact shouldn't be ignored.

Profit-taking in the afternoon also contributed to selling pressure, as traders took their short-term capital gains after three days of advances by speculation-driven stocks.

In the morning, investors had been readjusting positions and purchasing some shares that would safeguard their positions in case of visible inflationary movement, traders said.

But as the profit-taking set in, other investors moved to the sidelines, holding back on buying and joining the generally bearish financial market sentiment, traders said.

One trader said that with currently high interest rates, institutional investors aren't willing to lower cash positions and add to their positions.

"If anything, they'll buy things that aren't already in their portfolios, but their average costs will go up if they just increase their {current} positions," he said.

Interest in light metal and mining shares, viewed as inflation hedges, lasted only through the morning, after which profit-taking clipped gains.

Mitsubishi Metal rose 9 yen to 909 yen (6 cents to $6.16), Nippon Light Metal gained 10 yen to 1200 yen, and Dowa Mining rose 21 yen to 970 yen.

Trade centered on Ajinomoto, which according to reports developed a drug that may prevent the spread of cancer to different organs in patients who already have the disease. The drug was allegedly found to be effective on laboratory rats. The stock soared 400 yen to 2420 yen in heavy volume.

Oil and refinery stocks including Arabian Oil, Nippon Oil and Teikoku Oil were higher, with oil prices managing to maintain higher levels.

Speculative buying on Jujo Paper continued; the stock rose 30 yen to 1000 yen.

London dealers said market sentiment there was still relatively buoyant, and prices merely drifted lower after several consecutive days' rally.

One trader said the fall gave market makers an opportunity to pick up stock as prices fell.

The market gained support from the disclosure of U.K. money supply data for June and earnings and employment figures for May. The data suggested that government efforts to slow the economy were finally working.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index ended down 14.7 points at 2387.3, the intraday low. The Financial Times 30 Share Index dropped 11.9 points to 1888.7.

Volume was 412 million shares, down from 595.2 million Wednesday.

In company news, Great Universal Stores said pretax profit in the year ended March 31 grew 4.3% to #417.3 million ($757.6 million). Although the result was above market forecasts of #400 million to #415 million, the market perceived the results as "dull," according to one dealer. GUS fell 10 pence to #10.68.

Unconfirmed rumors that Hong Kong Telecom, which is a unit of Cable & Wireless, would issue a trading statement, caused nervousness about C&W shares. The stock dropped 15 pence to 535 pence.

STC confirmed it is holding talks with Japan's Fujitsu, which could lead to the latter's taking a majority stake in STC's computer subsidiary, ICL. STC gained 24 pence to 281 pence.

An unconfirmed report that Redland would be pulling out of the plasterboard market, following charges of price-fixing by competitor BPB and a referral to the Monopolies and Mergers Commission, helped boost Redland a penny to 625 pence, after standing as high as 630 pence.

Reckitt & Colman sold its fine art and graphics division to AB Wilh. Becker of Sweden for #60.7 million. Reckitt & Colman eased 16 pence to #13.09.

Elsewhere, shares closed higher in Brussels but lower in Frankfurt, Paris, Zurich, Milan, Stockholm and Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed mixed.

Also, stocks ended higher in Taipei, mixed in Hong Kong, Wellington and Singapore, and lower in Seoul, Sydney and Manila.
