SEC Starts Review Of Law Proceedings To Speed Up Cases

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission is starting a review of its administrative law proceedings, with an eye toward speeding up cases. The administrative proceedings task force, headed by Commissioner Mary Schapiro, will take up the agency's inhouse process of trials against investment advisers, brokerages, exchanges and others that typically are handled by an administrative law judge.

The review comes at a time when the SEC is poised to get more power to impose administrative actions. The Senate this week approved a bill giving the agency more leeway to impose fines on wrongdoers in the securities industry and to block firms from defrauding investors through cease-and-desist authority. The House is expected to vote on the measure Monday. "If we're lucky enough to get the remedies bill, we are likely to see more administrative proceedings," according to Ms. Schapiro.

The panel also is the SEC's response to complaints from the securities bar that the administrative process is too slow and occasionally misses the mark badly.

An American Bar Association task force is drafting proposals that may recommend that the SEC's system of administrative law judges doesn't work and should be abolished, according to a lawyer who has seen a draft. It isn't clear whether the final ABA proposal will include the recommendation, he said.

One Washington lawyer, Arthur Mathews at Wilmer, Cutler & Pickering, said, "I don't think the commission has paid enough attention to the role of administrative law judges." For example, he said he is currently trying a case of pennystock broker Stuart James Co. before a law judge that is expected to take a year and a half.

Ms. Schapiro has spoken in the past about the slow pace at the SEC. While she said she doesn't have particular rule proposals in mind, she added that "I want to be sure the delay -- if it is undue delay -- is not the failing of our rules."

She said the study will look at the discovery process, in which defendants and witnesses offer evidence. It also will examine whether the SEC is delaying the process of handling appeals from the law judges.
