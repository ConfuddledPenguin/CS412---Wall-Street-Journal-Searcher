Quote: `I Hate Quotations. Tell Me What You Know' -- Emerson

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 07/20/90

WASHINGTON -- Treasures sometimes turn up in the most unexpected places. In U.S. Government Bookstores, for example, nestled on the shelves among "Mineral Facts and Problems" and "A Basic Guide to Exporting," is a 520-page tome entitled "Respectfully Quoted: A Dictionary of Quotations Requested from the Congressional Research Service."

The book contains 2,100 quotes on topics ranging from "Abolition of slavery" to "Youth" that the Library of Congress has researched over the years. The research service says it gets more than 1,500 congressional inquiries of all types daily, including requests to verify statements for use in campaign speeches and debates in Congress.

Some quotes offer insight into an issue that is on everybody's lips today: taxation. "A government which robs Peter to pay Paul can always depend on the support of Paul," quipped George Bernard Shaw. And there's notable commentary on the U.S. legislative process. "Congress is so strange. A man gets up to speak and says nothing. Nobody listens -- and then everybody disagrees," observed Boris Marshalov, a Russian visitor.

There are classics, such as President Truman's account of Joseph Stalin's famous question to Winston Churchill: "How many divisions did you say the Pope had?" And there are boners, such as the remark Alan Greenspan made in 1974, more than a decade before he became chairman of the Federal Reserve Board: "Everybody is hurt by inflation. If you really wanted to examine who percentage-wise is hurt the most in their incomes, it is the Wall Street brokers."

Trivia buffs will be able to annoy their friends with some of the book's findings. For instance: Pro football coach Vince Lombardi said, "Winning isn't everything; it's the only thing." Right?

Wrong. Red Sanders, a Vanderbilt University coach, said it and didn't get the credit. But as President Kennedy, not President Carter, said, "Life is unfair." (Mr. Carter's observation was that there are "many things in life that are not fair.")

The quotes indicate that there's not much said that's really new. The library's researchers traced the modern commandment "Do your own thing" to the 14th-century writer Geoffrey Chaucer, who put it this way: "Dooth with youre owene thyng."
