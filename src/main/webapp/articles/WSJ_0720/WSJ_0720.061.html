AT&T Earnings Show a 6% Drop For 2nd Quarter --- Revenue Declined by 2.5%; Net Was Hurt by Slide In Long-Distance Sales

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 07/20/90

NEW YORK -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co., hurt by a slide in long-distance revenue and sluggish equipment sales, said net income fell 6% in the second quarter on a 2.5% drop in sales. AT&T said it also paid heavily trying to keep up with demand for its new Universal credit card, which has garnered at least two million cardholders since its unveiling in March.

Net fell to $657 million, or 60 cents a share, from $699 million, or 65 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue fell to $9.03 billion from $9.26 billion.

In the first half, net rose 2.4% to $1.33 billion, or $1.22 a share, from $1.29 billion, or $1.20 a share, the year before. Sales of $17.92 billion were unchanged.

AT&T's results were expected after it announced last month that earnings would decline. Since that announcement, the company's stock, which had been hovering in the low 40s, has fallen to the mid-30s. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, AT&T rose 50 cents to $37.625 on volume of 1.9 million shares.

Despite the bland profit performance, AT&T Chairman Robert E. Allen said, "We still expect 1990 to be better than 1989, a record earnings year. These results don't alter our confidence in our direction or our future prospects."

Included in the results were charges of about $100 million to cover consolidations, layoffs and other costs in AT&T's services businesses, including long-distance and maintenance and installation.

AT&T also took a charge of about $290 million to cover consolidations and personnel reallocations in microelectronic parts and systems and for reclaiming scrap metal.

All this was offset by a credit of $400 million, which came from a net reduction in the reserves set up in December 1988 associated with modernization of AT&T's network. AT&T attributed the reduction to lower removal and disposal costs of obsolete equipment. The company said the reduction would have been even greater if not for increased costs from force reductions and facilities closings.

Sales of equipment continued to be hurt by a slowdown in the business market. While sales of switching and transmission equipment to phone companies inched ahead 1.8% to $1.8 billion, other equipment sales suffered. Business, data and consumer equipment revenue fell 11% to $1.7 billion. AT&T doesn't break out its profit for its equipment businesses, but analysts estimate the company is losing more than $300 million a year on office phone systems and computer equipment.

On the services side, long-distance revenue fell 3.9% to $4.8 billion from $5.02 billion. AT&T attributed the decline to $100 million in additional payments to the local phone companies for completing long-distance calls over their local networks, and a shift by an undisclosed number of business customers to newer, lower-priced communications services.

Still, Morris Tanenbaum, AT&T vice chairman and chief financial officer, was optimistic about long-distance operations. He noted that AT&T's traffic volume grew 7%, an indication that it has begun to stanch a loss of business to its primary competitors, MCI Communications Corp. and US Sprint Communications Co., a unit of United Telecommunications Inc.

"We don't see any decrease in overall strength of the market, said Mr. Tanenbaum. "And our profitability remains strong."

Jack Grubman, analyst at PaineWebber Inc., said the quarter "was probably better than expected," given that product sales decreased a meager 2% in a very tough pricing environment and "their margins of 43% are the highest they've been in three years." Moreover, he said AT&T's long-distance volume grew 15% to 20% in the small-business arena, where MCI and Sprint have experienced sluggishness.
