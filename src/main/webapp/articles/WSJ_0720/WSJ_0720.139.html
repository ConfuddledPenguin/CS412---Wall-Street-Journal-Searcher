Troubled Stotler Says It Accepts Offer From Group

  • Author: Scott McMurray
  • Date: 07/20/90

CHICAGO -- Stotler Group Inc., scrambling to shore up its finances, said it accepted a conditional offer from an investor group to buy a "substantial equity interest" in the troubled futures brokerage holding company. Stotler, whose chairman, Karsten "Cash" Mahlmann, also is chairman of the Chicago Board of Trade, didn't identify the group or the size of the proposed investment. Board of Trade members said there were widespread rumors that the group originated in Taiwan and that the conditional offer was for at least $10 million.

Stotler said the offer required the approval of the group's board and that it will expire if that approval isn't given by 10 a.m. CDT Monday. Stotler Group said this week that without a capital infusion, it might be unable to make certain debt payments.

Stotler Group officials, who announced the offer in a brief news release, didn't return calls seeking additional comment.

The company's stock rebounded on news of the proposed investment, spurting $1 to close at $6.625 a share on the American Stock Exchange.

Late last week, the Board of Trade gave Stotler & Co., the company's futures brokerage unit, until the close of business Wednesday to boost its capital cushion supporting customer accounts or risk being forced out of the customer business.

Stotler met the deadline for capital compliance -- raising its capital to 4% of segregated customer funds -- mainly by transferring tens of millions of dollars of customer accounts to other futures brokers to lower its capital needs.

Large blocks of accounts were transferred to Rosenthal Collins Group, LIT America Inc. and Refco Group Ltd., among other futures brokerage firms.

The mass transfers, which included many of what were considered Stotler's best accounts, left many futures industry officials wondering whether the remaining accounts will be sufficient to make Stotler an attractive investment. "When you lose your quality accounts, you're not in better shape, you're in worse shape," said a futures trader who clears, or processes, his futures trades through Stotler.

If Stotler hadn't met the Board of Trade deadline, it could have been forced to allow customers to trade only to liquidate holdings and to transfer all its accounts to rival brokerage firms.

Neither the firm nor the exchange has said what caused the old-line grain-trading firm to slip below minimum capital requirements. Industry officials familiar with the situation said the problem arose when regulators determined that certain assets and liabilities on the holding company's balance sheet be reflected on the balance sheet of the Stotler & Co. futures brokerage subsidiary. That action, in turn, led the Board of Trade to find that the company's remaining capital was shy of the futures industry's minimum levels, exchange members said.

The Securities and Exchange Commission regulates the Stotler Group holding company, which is registered as a securities dealer, and is believed to have triggered the reclassification of the assets and liabilities. The futures brokerage unit is regulated by the exchange and the Commodity Futures Trading Commission.
