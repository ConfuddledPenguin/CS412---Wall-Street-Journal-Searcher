Short Interest Declines 2.8% On Big Board --- But Figure Climbed 4.2% On Amex to New High In Month Ended July 13

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 07/20/90

NEW YORK -- Short interest for the month ended July 13 fell 2.8% on the New York Stock Exchange but advanced 4.2% to a record on the American Stock Exchange. Eli Lilly & Co., the Indianapolis pharmaceutical manufacturer, took over the top Big Board short-interest spot with 13,737,401 shares, unseating Varity Corp. Short sellers appear to have targeted Lilly for the past few months as media reports raised questions about the company's prominent anti-depressant Prozac.

New York exchange short interest eased to 642,585,339 shares at the July 13 settlement date from 661,212,877 shares at mid-June. On the Amex, short interest climbed to 77,026,110 shares from a revised 73,950,683. The month included the 19 trading days through July 6.

At mid-July 1989, short volume was 484,818,847 shares on the Big Board, indicating a 33% advance, and 48,922,594 shares on the American exchange, indicating a 57% rise.

Traders who sell short borrow stock and sell it, betting that the stock's price will decline and that they can buy the shares back later at a lower price for return to the lender.

Short interest is the number of shares that haven't yet been purchased for return to lenders. Although a substantial short position reflects heavy speculation that a stock's price will decline, some investors consider an increase in short interest bullish because the borrowed shares eventually must be bought back.

Fluctuation in short interest of certain stocks also may be caused partly by arbitraging. The figures occasionally include incomplete transactions in restricted stock.

The level of negative sentiment measured by the Big Board short interest ratio leaped back up to 4.53 from mid-June's 3.86; the figure stood at 4.61 in mid-May. The ratio is the number of trading days at the exchange's average daily trading volume that would be required to convert the total short interest position. Average daily New York exchange volume for the 19 trading days was 141,937,207 shares. Some analysts suggest, however, that the ratio has weakened in value as an indicator because options and other products can be used to hedge short positions.

A spokesman for Eli Lilly said the company's policy is not to comment on matters involving its stock.

For the 16th consecutive month, Continental Airlines Holdings Inc., formerly Texas Air Corp., led the Amex short-volume list, this month with 7,285,188 shares, down 3.3%.

Two closely watched issues on the Big Board dropped: International Business Machines Corp. volume fell 14% to 4,198,541 shares, while Exxon Corp. slipped 15% to 4,662,310 shares.
