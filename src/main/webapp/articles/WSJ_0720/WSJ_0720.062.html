Measure Aimed at Shifting Regulation Of Index Futures to SEC Is in Trouble

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 07/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The administration's bill to shift power to the Securities and Exchange Commission from commodities regulators is in severe trouble, and key backers in the Senate are scrambling to find a compromise. When the bill comes before the Senate, probably later this year, it may not include a provision to shift oversight of stock-index futures to the SEC from the Commodity Futures Trading Commission. That's partly because heavy lobbying by the futures industry and what critics consider a weak push by the administration has failed to convince most senators of the provision's usefulness. Moreover, some senators believe the current regulatory structure is better for the futures markets.

Administration officials, led by Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady and SEC Chairman Richard Breeden, contend that the split regulatory clout of the SEC and CFTC encourages stock-market volatility and hurts enforcement efforts. The SEC oversees stocks and options, and the CFTC regulates stock-index futures.

Those futures, which are primarily used by large institutional investors for hedging purposes, allow investors to bet on the movement of a basket of stocks without owning the shares. They are frequently used in computerized program trading strategies.

Sen. John Heinz (R., Pa.), a staunch supporter of the administration's bill, said he is seeking a "compromise that will solve the major issues."

Another backer, Sen. Timothy Wirth (D., Colo.), said he was "disappointed" by the administration's efforts on behalf of the bill. "I don't see any great momentum here," he said. "The case hasn't been very well made by the administration."

Some Senate aides fear that negotiations over a compromise are hurting the bill's chances because they indicate a lack of support for the administration's proposal. Several aides even noted that draft proposals without an index-futures shift are in the works.

A Treasury official denied that the administration's efforts have been weak and said Mr. Brady strongly supports all parts of the bill.

"Some people have been offering compromises from the beginning," the official said. "We think the administration's proposal is what needs to be done." He added: "There's obviously very strong opposition on the other side. It's a jurisdiction issue: Those are the toughest issues on the Hill."

Vote counts show that the bill would have a tough time passing if the Senate voted today. But a huge number of senators haven't made a decision about the administration's proposal. A compromise clearly would give them two benefits. First, it would relieve the senators of having to learn the nuances of complex markets, and second, it would allow them to tell constituents that they voted against volatility.

The draft bills specifically focus on one of the administration bill's key proposalsgranting oversight of futures margins to a federal agency. Currently, margins -- the good-faith money needed to trade futures -- are set by the exchanges and can't be changed by the CFTC or any regulator except in emergencies. Messrs. Breeden and Brady contend that low margins in the futures markets helped exacerbate the market drops of October 1987 and October 1989. They contend that unless federal oversight of margins is ordered by Congress, the futures exchanges will once again bring them down to what Mr. Breeden calls "dangerously low levels."

Mr. Breeden appears to be focusing almost exclusively on the margin question. In testimony yesterday before the Senate Banking Committee, Mr. Breeden repeatedly decried "excess leverage" in the futures markets that he said threatened the stock investments of 50 million Americans.

Senate staffers said the compromise measure also will focus on a clause in the Commodity Exchange Act that requires any new product with elements of futures contracts to be traded on a futures exchange and regulated by the CFTC.

Sen. Wirth also criticized the White House's renomination of Wendy Gramm as CFTC chairman. "The single most important statement they are making is reappointing the person" who most staunchly opposes a shift of jurisdiction, he said.

In his testimony yesterday, Mr. Breeden expressed caution about the proposed stock transfer excise tax, which would put a 0.15% surcharge on the sale of investments. Although it hasn't been brought up at the summit table, the proposal is one of several being discussed by the administration and Capitol Hill leaders currently negotiating budget accords.

Mr. Breeden refused to criticize the excise tax explicitly, but he told senators, "We have to be extraordinarily careful in creating taxes on capital formation." Saying that U.S. securities taxes are higher than those in many other countries, Mr. Breeden said he is concerned about taking "steps to drive trading offshore."
