Tax Deductions May Face Curbs In Budget Talks --- Negotiators Weigh Limits On Mortgage Interest, Charity and Other Items

  • Author: Jeffrey H. Birnbaum and David Wessel
  • Date: 07/20/90

WASHINGTON -- Budget negotiators of both parties are considering proposals that would curb itemized tax deductions, including those taken for mortgage-interest expenses, charitable giving and state and local tax payments. No decisions have been reached. But broad and narrow limitations on deductions taken by individuals have been discussed in private meetings. Documents that include such limitations have been drafted by congressional Democrats and Bush administration aides.

The idea of restricting itemized deductions has emerged because of the Democrats' demand that any tax package hit the wealthy harder than others. The search for ways to meet this objective has intensified as it begins to appear more likely that the tax plan will be anchored by a big new broad-based tax on energy, which would hit low-income individuals harder than those who are better off.

Curbing itemized deductions is an effective way to raise the tax burden on upperincome Americans. At last count in 1986, 43% of all itemized deductions were taken by the 9% of taxpayers who had adjusted gross income of more than $50,000 a year, according to the Internal Revenue Service. At the same time, however, some of the deductions that might be trimmed are among the most politically popular in the tax code and have withstood assaults in years past.

Proposals discussed by the budget negotiators range from reducing all deductions by, for example, 10% to placing ceilings on such deductions for the highest-income individuals. The Bush administration has floated the idea of limiting the amount of deductions taken for state income tax payments to $15,000 a year.

Other options that have been discussed include restricting allowable deductions to a percentage of adjusted gross income, reducing the value of the deductions by applying them against only the lower, 15% tax bracket and disallowing deductions of more than a given dollar figure, say $10,000, for any individual. Another option is to allow deductions only if they exceed a certain percentage of income.

"Both sides are looking at (limiting) itemized deductions," a Democratic staffer said. "There are scores of ways to do it."

Budget summit negotiators met for about two hours yesterday, their first formal meeting in a week, for what House Budget Committee Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) diplomatically described as "a frank discussion . . . about the difficulty in trying to arrive at an agreement while political weapons are being used day to day."

The summit tentatively agreed to reconvene early next week. President Bush has invited top congressional leaders to meet with him on the budget talks Monday.

The Democrats are determined to structure a progressive tax package that hits higher-income taxpayers at least as hard as lower-income people. The Bush administration is trying to find ways to accomplish this without increasing the top individual tax rate to 33% from the current 28% as the Democrats want.

For their part, Democrats don't think raising the top rate is enough, particularly if the tax plan includes both excise taxes and a cut in capital gains taxes, which is sought by President Bush and almost exclusively benefits the very richest Americans. The excise tax increases most discussed are a broad-based energy tax, tobacco taxes and taxes on alcoholic beverages.

Tax writers note that there is plenty of precedent for placing limits on deductions. Tax law already allows the deduction for medical expenses only if they exceed 7.5% of an individual's adjusted gross income. Only 80% of dining and entertainment costs are deductible as a business expense. And a variety of miscellaneous deductions can be taken only if they exceed 2% of adjusted gross income a year.

Congressional aides note that a wholesale scaling back of deductions mightn't be needed to achieve a balance in the tax burden between rich and poor. Specific deductions could be targeted for cutback, as the Bush administration has privately suggested. Or, they said, poor people could be aided by increases in the refundable earned-income tax credit and an increase in the standard deduction taken by taxpayers who don't itemize. Increases in those tax benefits are being considered on their own merits.

Considerable revenue can be raised by curtailing deductions. By way of illustration, the respected Congressional Joint Committee on Taxation has estimated that limiting the deduction for mortgage-interest payments to $12,000 a year for an individual and $20,000 a year for a joint filer would raise $600 million in the first year. Restricting the deduction of state and local taxes to 9% of adjusted gross income would raise $700 million in the first year.

Talk about any such restrictions has compelled lobbyists for a number of interests to step up their efforts on Capitol Hill, particularly those who represent state and local governments. Real estate interests also are considering how to defend their tax breaks.

Judging from the past, their chances of success could be high. Speaking of previous battles over deductions, Rep. Bill Frenzel (R., Minn.) said, "If they didn't go through then, they may have some difficulty going through now."
