Top Two Waste-Handling Companies Report Higher June-Quarter Earnings

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 07/20/90

The nation's two largest waste-handling concerns reported higher earnings for the June quarter, with results reflecting not only the industry's rapid growth, but the problems that can accompany such expansion. Waste Management Inc.'s solid impetus continued in its second quarter, with earnings and revenue jumping 26%.

Browning-Ferris Industries Inc.'s profit from continuing operations in the fiscal third quarter rose 8.8% on a 19% revenue increase. Net income rose 16% from the year-earlier period, which included a loss from discontinued operations.

Browning-Ferris' results came in slightly below what some analysts had projected, and the company's shares tumbled $1.875 yesterday in composite New York Stock Exchange trading, closing at $43.625 on volume of more than 1.1 million shares.

Waste Management's earnings were largely in line with investor expectations; its shares slipped 12.5 cents in Big Board trading, to close at $42.625.

Both companies have seen the price of their shares climb more than 35% from the lows of early 1990, and their fully priced position makes them vulnerable to any investor skittishness. They have become something of a sacred cow among institutional investors, particularly the many new environmental-sector funds.

Waste Management's net was $178.9 million, or 38 cents a share, up from the year-earlier $141.9 million, or 31 cents a share. Per-share figures reflect an increase in average shares outstanding to 470.7 million from last year's 458 million.

The Oak Brook, Ill., company's revenue climbed to $1.41 billion from $1.19 billion.

Waste Management said its domestic and international units reported improved results. In addition, the company's 77%-owned hazardous-waste and remediation subsidiary, Chemical Waste Management Inc., which on Wednesday reported record second-quarter results, made a "strong contribution."

A spokesman cited several trends behind the company's improved earnings, including an ongoing corporate cost-control effort, "expansion of (the company's) solid-waste services into new markets," and swelling revenue from international operations, which more than doubled from a year earlier. At year-end 1989, international operations represented about 8.5% of Waste Management's revenue.

At Browning-Ferris, net rose to $82.4 million, or 54 cents a share, from the year-earlier $71.2 million, or 47 cents a share -- which included a $4.5 million loss from discontinued operations. Revenue rose to $767.6 million from $647.4 million.

The latest earnings were reduced by interest expenses of $18.4 million, nearly double the year-earlier outlay of $10 million. A spokesman cited the company's 1989 borrowing for acquisitions under a growth program for the higher interest payments.

Those earnings were "a little bit of a disappointment," said Richard M. Hanley, an analyst with the firm of McDonald & Co. in Cleveland.

Mr. Hanley said the "biggest issue" in the earnings coming in below expectations was that volumes in the company's high-margin disposal business -- operating landfills and charging clients for the right to dump waste -- was down from a year earlier.

The downturn was most acute in the company's big Ohio operation, where they accept loads from Eastern states: Browning-Ferris "needed to develop a better climate with regulators and maintain good relations," so they cut back on the amount of out-of-state waste they accepted at the Ohio sites, said Mr. Hanley.

Browning-Ferris conceded that the reduced volume in Ohio played a role, as did a downturn in the industrial waste collection and disposal in the economically sluggish Northeast. But Mr. Hanley said the higher debt load was a more significant factor, and added that because the company is currently adding debt much more slowly, future quarters will see "more of the top-line growth go to the bottom line."

Also contributing to the Houston company's margin erosion is its continued expansion in international markets, Mr. Hanley said. Growth potential is high overseas for such big players as Browning-Ferris and Waste Management, but profits are historically thinner and expected to remain so.
