American Continental Reorganization Plan Is Disclosed by U.S.

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/20/90

PHOENIX -- The federal government and a committee representing unsecured creditors of American Continental Corp. provided details of a proposed reorganization plan under which American Continental would be liquidated, Charles A. Keating Jr. and other executives would be ousted and Lincoln Savings & Loan Association would be dropped from creditors' claims. The settlement agreement provides about $21 million for creditors and in return insulates the government from certain claims by these creditors. "This provides the {ACC} bondholders with sure recovery of some assets, when they faced dismal prospects, and it relieves the government of exposure to litigation," said Michael Tucci, senior counsel for the Resolution Trust Corp. "This will eliminate a lot of attorneys' fees."

The agreement, if approved, would also have the effect of preventing Mr. Keating from retaking control of Lincoln, the thrift seized by federal regulators last year a day after American Continental filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection. ACC has unsecured debt of about $350 million; about two-thirds of that comes in the form of junk bonds, many sold through Lincoln branches.

In an interview, Mr. Keating said he'd "personally argue" against the plan and also formally introduce a proposal in federal bankruptcy court to buy back $500 million of Lincoln's assets for no down payment, then manage it himself so the U.S. doesn't lose money and creditors get another $50 million.

The plan, which a U.S. District Court judge in Arizona would hear arguments on in September, also provides that Lincoln and its subsidiaries, which filed for bankruptcy protection along with ACC, would be dismissed from bankruptcy protection.

Furthermore, the RTC, which oversees the U.S.'s thrift bailout, would remain in control of Lincoln Savings even if U.S. District Court Judge Stanley Sporkin, in Washington, D.C., gives Mr. Keating approval to take Lincoln back.
