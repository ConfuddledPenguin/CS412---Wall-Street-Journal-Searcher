Foreign Exchange: Dollar Loses Ground Against the Mark But Climbs Against Japanese Currency

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/20/90

NEW YORK -- A show of strength by the mark against the yen and the pound led the dollar lower against the West German currency. By the end of New York trading yesterday, the U.S. currency was slightly higher against the yen and slightly lower against sterling.

A feature of the trading day was a retreat by the Australian dollar, a move that traders said the Federal Reserve Bank of New York sought to exaggerate by selling the Australian currency.

Foreign-exchange traders tried to make what profits they could from yesterday's small and often random price movements. "This is a troublesome and difficult market to trade in," said Robert White, manager of corporate foreign-exchange trading for First Interstate Bank of California in Los Angeles.

"The market just doesn't have the power to break out of its ranges, and for the next couple of days, with no U.S. indicators due out, I think we're just going to continue this messy trading," said Dan Brooks, chief New York dealer for Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.6410 marks, down from 1.6480 marks late Wednesday. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 148.10 yen, up from 147.75 yen a day earlier.

In Tokyo Friday, the dollar was trading at 148.15 yen soon after the opening; it closed at 147.60 yen in Tokyo Thursday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6395 marks, down from Tokyo's Thursday close of 1.6445 marks.

Sterling climbed to $1.8157 in late New York trading from $1.8155 late Wednesday but slid to 2.9796 marks from 2.9919 marks.

The mark closed at 90.25 yen, up from 89.67 yen. Dealers attributed the mark's rise to profit-taking on long, or overbought, yen and sterling positions.

The Australian dollar was last quoted in New York at $0.7820, down from $0.7905 Wednesday.

After a low-volume session of Australian dollar trading in Sydney and Melbourne Thursday, European and North American traders opened their offices aware that the market was long on the Australian currency, said Rick Porter, New York manager of corporate foreign-exchange for Westpac Banking.

After an early rise, the currency hit resistance at about $0.7880 to $0.7900, Mr. Porter said. Then, "it turned upon itself and fell a half cent in 15 to 20 minutes."

Many market investors and dealers took advantage of the listless session to ponder the previous day's Senate testimony by Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan.

Before pushing the dollar out of its current range of about 147.50 yen to 148.10 yen, the market is "going to wait to digest what Mr. Greenspan said," commented Mr. Brooks of Dai-Ichi Kangyo.

While it seems, Mr. White said, that the Fed chairman "is very stubborn about lowering rates," market confidence remains low. Mr. Brooks said, "People still think U.S. rates will go down and that the dollar will go down with them."

Sterling got a slight boost, Mr. White said, when British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher told the House of Commons she would prefer a strong pound whether it is outside or inside the price bands of the European Monetary System.

The prime minister's cheerleading "obviously didn't hurt" the pound, said Donald Hubbard, a New York treasury officer for Midland Bank. But, he added, "she's always wanted a strong pound."

Britain's high interest rates, along with speculation that it may soon be a full EMS participant, will discourage much sterling-selling. "I don't think anyone can go short sterling and make money," said Mahiko Nagai, an interbank trader at Manufacturers Hanover Trust in Tokyo.

The dollar ended European dealings narrowly mixed, although its modest advance against some currencies was largely the result of cross-rate action surrounding the mark.

The dollar ended sterling-centered Japanese dealings higher. The U.S. unit was last quoted Thursday in Tokyo at 147.60 yen, above 147.25 yen Wednesday.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $361.40 an ounce, down 20 cents. Estimated volume was a light 2.4 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $361.42 an ounce.
