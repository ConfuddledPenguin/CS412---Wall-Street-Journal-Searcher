Technology: Lotus Effort to Please All With 1-2-3 Confuses Many

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 07/20/90

CAMBRIDGE, Mass. -- A year ago, Lotus Development Corp. finally delivered a long-delayed improved version of its best-selling 1-2-3 software, appearing to end the industry's longest-running soap opera. Troubles over? Not exactly. The old version, 1-2-3 Release 2.01, was used by an estimated six million customers when Lotus introduced the new, improved Release 3.0 version last June. But older, less powerful PCs couldn't use Release 3.0, so Lotus came out with another updated version, Release 2.2, a month later.

Then two months ago, it said it will come out with yet another new version, Release 3.1, which will offer fancier printing capabilities that customers wanted but which Lotus didn't include in its Release 3.0 product.

Confused? So are a lot of Lotus's customers. Lotus's effort to provide a different product for everyone, while perhaps sounding good in theory, has prompted many to just sit tight with their old program. Lotus's strategy and its results with customers point up the marketing problems that dominant companies have in defending their market positions.

Until this week, everything looked fine, with Lotus showing spectacular earnings gains for the past four quarters. But late Wednesday, it disclosed that earnings may decline in the current quarter, and that it isn't on the strong growth path most analysts had forecast. Executives conceded for the first time that competitors' spreadsheets are cutting into its dominant market share.

That announcement caused Lotus's stock to take a terrible beating yesterday. It plummeted $6.125 to close at $25.125 in national over-the-counter trading. More than 10 million shares changed hands as technology investors bailed out of Lotus and moved on to faster-growing PC software stocks. To many, Lotus now looks like a mature company nursing a cash cow without the prospects for spectacular growth that other personal-computer software firms offer. Despite repeated efforts to move beyond spreadsheets, Lotus still lacks any other fast-growing programs with significant sales.

Lotus became the paradigm of an explosive growth company after it introduced 1-2-3 in 1983 and racked up $63 million in sales in its first year. Until 1988, it remained the biggest company in the PC software industry, with 1-2-3 accounting for two-thirds of its sales. Although far surpassed in size and growth rate by Microsoft Inc. in recent years, it remains No. 2 in the industry. And 1-2-3, by most estimates, still captures 60% to 70% of all spreadsheet revenue.

However, at a time when the PC business is booming and many other software companies are soaring, Lotus is now floundering. Even beyond the issue of quarterly earnings, "there are many questions about Lotus," says David Readerman, an analyst with Shearson Lehman Hutton.

For one thing, its core strategy of introducing a plethora of versions of 1-2-3 turns off some users. Ronan McGrath, vice president for accounting at Canadian National Railways in Montreal, who supervises some 2,000 PCs running 1-2-3 spreadsheets, says that only 40% of his users have upgraded. Overall, analysts say that upgrades have fallen far below expectations.

Moreover, Lotus still lacks a 1-2-3 version for Apple Computer Inc.'s Macintosh, and it doesn't have a version specifically designed to work with Microsoft's Windows, a new operating system to which many users are switching. Lotus promises such a version in the future. Frank King, senior vice president of the software business group, says that for now it will advertise Release 3.1 as a Windows product, because it can work with Windows.

Meanwhile, the competition is getting tougher. Robert Schechter, chief financial officer, says Lotus is holding on to its traditional customers, but that many customers in new markets, such as small business and home businesses, are buying competitors' products.

Borland International Ltd., for example, has sold many copies of its Quattro Pro spreadsheet in a promotion under which 1-2-3 users can send in the cover page of their Lotus manual and get Quattro Pro for $99, less than they would pay to upgrade to a new Lotus product. Quattro Pro also has the advantage of running on older PCs while providing the good-looking printouts that users want.

"There's definitely an effect from competition," says Richard Sherlund, an analyst with Goldman, Sachs & Co. He estimates that recently Lotus has been selling an average of 120,000 copies of 1-2-3 a month and 25,000 upgrades, with Borland selling 45,000 copies of Quattro Pro and Microsoft selling 35,000 copies of Excel.

Mr. King of Lotus says that in the past, Borland and Microsoft attempted unsuccessfully to dislodge Lotus on its main turf, the large corporation. Now, he says, Borland in particular is trying to establish brand new markets. "Last year, they came straight up the beach at us. Now they're trying a flanking move," he says. "It's war. No doubt about it."

To counter, Mr. King says Lotus has established new territorial sales representatives to market 1-2-3 to smaller companies and software stores. It also has devised a way to identify where individual software products end up so that territorial representatives can be given commissions.

Lotus also maintains that Borland is cheating. It has sued Borland, charging that Quattro infringes its copyright on 1-2-3. "They've used our intellectual property," Mr. King says.

Lotus has managed to keep the loyalty of the corporate data-processing managers who buy 1-2-3 in bulk by introducing 1-2-3 on mainframes and minicomputers. It also outlines longer-term plans to let users get information from corporate databases. But the repeated slip-ups with bringing out a core version of 1-2-3 are embarrassing. "They're like the man who has his eye on the stars and slips in a mud puddle," says a corporate customer who remains loyal.

But some erstwhile fans say Lotus has made a mistake by concentrating too much on the big corporations. Michael Kolowich, a former Lotus executive who is now publisher of PC Computing, a magazine for users, says: "In concentrating on the very large corporate sites with an approach that is reminiscent of IBM's account control strategy, Lotus has probably lost some of its magic with the end users." Mr. Kolowich says that individual users remain the driving force in buying new software programs. "Lotus has lost a lot of support at the grass roots," he adds.
