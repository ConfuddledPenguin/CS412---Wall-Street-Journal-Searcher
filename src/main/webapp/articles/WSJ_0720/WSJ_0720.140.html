PPG to Pay New Jersey $82.5 Million For Chromium-Waste Cleanups, Fine

  • Author: Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 07/20/90

PITTSBURGH -- PPG Industries Inc., threatened with multimillion-dollar lawsuits over toxic chromium wastes, agreed to pay New Jersey's Department of Environmental Protection $80 million for cleanups, as well as a $2.5 million fine. The glass, chemical and coating company, which has been pursued by state regulators for eight years, also agreed to pay $900,000 to cover administrative expenses and the cleanup of a Jersey City school, among other things. In addition, PPG dropped its lawsuits against New Jersey.

Under the agreement, PPG will clean up chromium waste at 53 sites in Hudson County, N.J., where until 1964 the company processed chromium ore to make paint pigments. The resulting slag-iron laced with chromium and other chemicals -- was used as landfill in the area. Chromium is a hazardous substance that can cause serious ailments, including cancer.

The sites include 32 residential properties, where PPG has agreed to excavate contaminated soil "to a very stringent standard," said Michael Schuit, a state regulatory lawyer involved in the settlement. At the remaining industrial and commercial sites, PPG will propose and the state will approve various cleanup remedies.

To cover some of the cleanup costs, PPG is taking a $25 million pretax charge for the second quarter. It reported record net income of $141 million, or $1.31 a share, on sales of $1.57 billion. In the year-earlier quarter, net was $127.3 million, or $1.13 a share, on sales of $1.49 billion. The company said the charge, along with earlier reserves and "other measures," would cover its cleanup bill. PPG wouldn't elaborate further.

However, Mr. Schuit noted that if the cleanup bill for the 53 sites exceeds $80 million, "PPG will have to pay. There's no spending limit." The company will also be responsible for any additional sites that the state discovers and links to PPG.

Analysts were optimistic that future earnings won't be hurt. The charge "is the only impact we should see on earnings," said Charles LoCastro, a securities analyst at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette. "This essentially puts the matter behind them."

The settlement came after state regulators ordered PPG to pay $31.5 million in cleanup costs and threatened to seek treble that amount in court if the company resisted. It's the second chromium-waste settlement secured by New Jersey. In April, Maxus Energy Corp., a Dallas petroleum producer formerly called Diamond Shamrock, agreed to provide as much as $51.5 million for cleanups and to pay a $2.5 million fine.

The two settlements comprise the "largest privately funded cleanup -- and will go a long way toward the renaissance of Jersey City," said Thomas McKee, a state enforcement agent. The cleanup cost in New York's celebrated Love Canal case, which was publicly funded, totaled about $120 million.

State regulators added that they plan action against a third company, Allied-Signal Inc., which acquired a closed chromium-processing plant in Jersey City as part of its 1954 purchase of Mutual Chemical Co. of America. Allied sold the plant site just a few months later, but the state still holds it responsible for the plant's slag. A spokesman for the Morristown, N.J., automotive and aerospace company said, "We've been talking for some time" with New Jersey regulators.
