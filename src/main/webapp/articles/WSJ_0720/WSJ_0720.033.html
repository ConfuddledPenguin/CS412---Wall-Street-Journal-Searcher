SEC Inquiry Looks at Issues Of Accounting --- Focus Is on How to Reduce Expense and Complexity Of Rules for U.S. Firms

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 07/20/90

NEW YORK -- Securities and Exchange Commissioner Philip R. Lochner Jr. has launched an inquiry into ways to reduce the complexity and cost of accounting rules for U.S. companies. While, at this point, the SEC hasn't begun an official proceeding into accounting rules, Mr. Lochner said his exploration has the backing of SEC Chairman Richard Breeden.

"We've got a set of accounting rules that are so complicated and so costly . . . there may be a significant detriment to U.S. business," Mr. Lochner said in an interview. "You end up with an accounting statement which ordinary investors don't use." He said he is concerned that companies are churning out more and more information that isn't particularly understandable.

Commissioner Lochner already has raised the issues with members of the Financial Accounting Standards Board, the rule-making body for the accounting profession. Mr. Lochner emphasized that his purpose in launching the inquiry is to "urge the FASB to focus on cost-benefit analysis" in creating new rules and amending others.

Of particular interest to the commissioner are issues relating to accounting for income tax, post-retirement employment benefits, pensions and leases. The FASB is currently considering rule changes on several of these issues.

Accounting rules, which often address very complex issues, are of great concern to companies, because in some cases their adoption can result in huge costs. For example, a pending rule on accounting for retirement benefits other than pensions will require companies for the first time to reflect on their books the present and future costs of benefits, such as medical and insurance coverage, that employees will receive after they retire. The total cost has been estimated at $400 billion.

Although the FASB "by and large has done a good job," Mr. Lochner said he undertook his effort to make the SEC's feelings known. He emphasized that he would accomplish this through continued discussions with the FASB, particularly on costbenefit analysis and coordination of U.S. and foreign accounting rules. Mr. Lochner is scheduled to attend a meeting of the Financial Accounting Standards Advisory Council next Tuesday to discuss the impact of accounting standards on U.S. competitiveness abroad.

Last month, Mr. Lochner wrote to members of the accounting-principles task force of the Business Roundtable, a group of chief executives, to solicit suggestions on how current U.S. accounting rules relate to "the competitiveness of U.S. businesses in the international marketplace."

Mr. Lochner also has spoken privately to chief financial officers and at least one accounting firm.

He said that he hopes an outgrowth of the discussion would result in a greater attempt to coordinate U.S. and foreign accounting principles, which are generally perceived as less costly to business.

The accounting inquiry by the commissioner is part of a broader focus by SEC Chairman Breeden that is concerned with the competitiveness of U.S. business and the U.S. capital markets.

Mr. Lochner emphasized that he doesn't want to see the SEC taking over the function of drafting accounting standards, although he said, "theoretically, the SEC could get into the business of setting standards itself rather than endorsing the FASB. I don't think that would be a healthy development." The FASB is a privately financed organization that is recognized by the SEC as being the official standard setter for accounting. On rare occasion, the SEC has declined to endorse an FASB recommendation.

The FASB in recent months has come under increasing pressure from business groups. In May, the FASB's oversight body made it more difficult for the FASB to issue accounting-rule pronouncements by boosting as of Jan. 1, 1991, the voting approval requirement for the seven-member FASB to 5-to-2. Currently, only a simple majority is required.

Dennis R. Beresford, chairman of the FASB, said he has been discussing accounting issues with Mr. Lochner. Although Mr. Beresford said the FASB wants to support the competitiveness of U.S. business, "we don't believe financial reporting should favor or disfavor any particular business."

Mr. Beresford, who opposed the tighter voting approval requirements for FASB, said the board was concerned that "taken to some extreme the argument might be made that we have to loosen accounting standards in the U.S. to make companies more competitive." But he added that accounting standards "to be most useful must be cost effective," and said the board would be open to considering whether the cost of rules exceeds the benefit.
