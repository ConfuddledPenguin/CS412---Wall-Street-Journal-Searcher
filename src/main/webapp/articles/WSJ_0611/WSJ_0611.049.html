Who's News: Daiwa Unit Names Merrill's Bradley As Co-Head of Capital Markets Business

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 06/11/90

NEW YORK -- The U.S. subsidiary of Daiwa Securities Co. said it named Stephen W. Bradley, a former managing director at Merrill Lynch & Co.'s capital markets group, as an executive vice president and co-head of its new capital markets operations. The Japanese securities firm also said that it planned to add 30 to 50 people over the next year to increase its U.S. presence in mergers and acquisitions, capital markets, real estate, foreign exchange, and mortgage-backed securities.

As is the custom at many Japanese financial institutions, Mr. Bradley will share the top title of his unit with a Japanese executive. But Daiwa officials said that, in practice, the 45-year-old American, a specialist in taxable debt instruments, effectively would run the firm's capital market operations. His appointment takes effect tomorrow.

In eight years at Merrill Lynch, Mr. Bradley was mostly involved in evaluating and structuring debt transactions for big multinational corporations and foreign governments. Before joining Merrill Lynch in 1982, he worked for 14 years at PaineWebber Inc.

Two-and-a-half years ago, Mr. Bradley established a unit at Merrill that specializes in putting together debt offerings for foreign companies and governments. But when that unit was moved to London at the beginning of this year, he chose to remain in New York. He said that while he was offered other positions at Merrill in New York, he decided to look elsewhere.

"While leaving the capital-markets opportunities at Merrill Lynch was difficult, I find the opportunities at Daiwa challenging and unlimited," said Mr. Bradley.

In the past, Daiwa and other big Japanese firms have had difficulty cracking the U.S. corporate finance market, particularly because of stiff competition from American firms determined to protect their own turf. In addition, the Japanese didn't have strong ties to U.S. companies, couldn't match the ability of U.S. firms to develop sophisticated products and had trouble retaining talented American professionals.

Some investment bankers also questioned the Japanese firms' commitment to the U.S. market. For instance, Daiwa in November 1988 laid off about 10% of its professional staff in the wake of the 1987 stock market crash. Some of those cuts were in fields the firm now plans to beef up, particularly corporate bonds and mortgage-linked securities.

But Daiwa now sees the large cutbacks taking place on Wall Street as an opportunity to pick up American talent that otherwise hadn't been available. A Daiwa spokesman said that most of the 30-to-50 new hires would be professionals. The U.S. unit currently employs about 370 people.

Unlike some other big Japanese brokers -- such as Nomura Securities Co. and Nikko Securities Co. -- Daiwa has refrained from expanding in the U.S. by purchasing stakes in American firms that specialize in areas such as mergers, real estate, leasing and money management.

With an infusion of $100 million two weeks ago from its Tokyo parent, Daiwa's U.S. subsidiary now has slightly more than $300 million in capital. The parent company has the equivalent of $6.2 billion in shareholder equity, making it one of the world's most heavily capitalized securities firms.
