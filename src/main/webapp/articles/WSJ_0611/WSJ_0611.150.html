He Isn't Keats but He Sure Beats The Usual D.C. Song and Dance

  • Author: Greg Hitt Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/11/90

WASHINGTON -- Move over M.C. Hammer and you other rappers. Here comes Rep. Major R. Owens, and he's Baaad. Rep. Owens, a liberal Democrat from New York and a former Brooklyn librarian, has been moved to use "the language and the attitude of a street constituent" to put the government's fiscal vice to verse in rap poems.

In a poem entitled "The Budget Summit," he likens the talks between the White House and congressional leaders to "a meeting of the mob," where aid for the poor and children is likely to get whacked in order to cut the deficit.

At the big white D.C. mansion

There's a meeting of the mob

And the question on the table

Is which beggars will they rob.

There's a meeting of the mob

Now we'll never get a job,

All the gents will make a deal

And the poor have no appeal.

The thrift bailout is a recurring theme for rapmaster Owens. One ditty called "The S and L Riot" paints a biting vision of thrift executives invited by former President Reagan to loot the Treasury:

When our mint is picked clean

Hear these haughty hogs all scream

That there's no free lunch]

Only this plump bunch

Deserves a national free lunch.

While the rhymes may be stilted, and the rhythm a bit unsteady, the message is always clear. "I feel the frustration after seven years in a Congress where no funds can be found for job creation or education to benefit my constituents," Rep. Owens says. "I sit there unable to do anything about it, and one way to relieve my anxiety is to start writing."

Rep. Owens isn't the only federal official to dabble in creative writing about fiscal matters. White House Budget Director Richard Darman has written floridly of Cookie Monsters and hidden Pacmen lurking within the budget. But while Mr. Darman is at the center of the budget summit with House and Senate leaders, Rep. Owens can only wish that there were a place for him and his would-be street poetry at the negotiating table.

The lawmaker says he'd have a lot to say. If they got around to cutting social benefits, he might start with:

This rape is obscene

These gents are real mean

Keep your children inside

Teach them true national pride.

There's a riot at the mint]
