Perkin-Elmer Corp. Should Go on Block, Ex-Chairman Says --- Nimitz Takes Issues With Recent Share Buy-Back, Firm's Stock Performance

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 06/11/90

NEW YORK -- Perkin-Elmer Corp.'s onetime chairman and chief executive, Chester W. Nimitz Jr., said he proposed that shareholders ask management to put the company up for sale. Mr. Nimitz submitted the proposal for consideration at the company's annual meeting in November. "The essence of the proposal is that they put the company up for sale, and sell it in a manner most advantageous to shareholders," Mr. Nimitz said.

Based on Friday's closing price of $24.50 a share on the New York Stock Exchange, the company would have an indicated value of more than $800 million. Perkin-Elmer, Norwalk, Conn., is an analytical instruments and material-sciences concern.

The 75-year-old Mr. Nimitz, a retired rear admiral, was a Perkin-Elmer executive for about 15 years; he left the chief executive post in 1977 and the chairmanship in 1980. He is the son of Adm. Chester W. Nimitz, the commander of the U.S. Pacific fleet during World War II.

In a telephone interview, Mr. Nimitz, who owns 1,000 shares of Perkin-Elmer's 33 million shares outstanding, said he took issue with the company's recent buy-back of 10.1 million common shares at $24.50 each. The company used part of the proceeds from the sale of several of its businesses to buy back the stock. Mr. Nimitz said "the poor suffering long-term shareholder should have gotten the $240 million," or at least had a say in how the money was spent. The company has never announced how much it received from sales of the businesses.

A Perkin-Elmer spokesman confirmed that the company received a letter from Mr. Nimitz, and said "his views will be carefully considered by our board."

Perkin-Elmer announced a restructuring program in April 1989 to sell three businesses: semiconductor equipment, German avionics and government systems. At the time the company said it would consider using proceeds of the sales to pay dividends to shareholders, but so far hasn't done that. For the year ended July 31, 1989, Perkin-Elmer had a net loss of $24.2 million on revenue of $784.3 million.

In May, Perkin-Elmer sold its semiconductor-equipment business to a group of computer companies, but retained a stake of less than 20%. The sale had been the focus of some concern because a Japanese company was reported to have expressed interest in part of the business. Mr. Nimitz said, however, that he understood Perkin-Elmer had been in licensing talks with the Japanese concern. Concerns had been raised by U.S. semiconductor companies fearing inroads in to the business by the Japanese.

Mr. Nimitz said that in comparison with the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index from 1984 to 1989, Perkin-Elmer's stock "has gone nowhere." In the statement supporting his proposal, Mr. Nimitz said the dividends "don't even approach the inflation rate." Perkin-Elmer's quarterly dividend is 17 cents a share.

The statement says that although the downsized company may grow modestly, "going forward offers no suitable return" to the long-term holder who is "already so far behind on his or her investment."

Mr. Nimitz, who also sent a letter to Perkin-Elmer chairman Horace G. McDonell, said he doesn't want to start a proxy fight, and that he would withdraw the proposal if the company puts itself up for sale. He said he would begin contacting major institutional holders soon to seek support.
