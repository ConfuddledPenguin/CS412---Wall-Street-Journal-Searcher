Colorado Utility Plans First Operation To Turn Landfill Gas Into Diesel Fuel

  • Author: Bill Paul
  • Date: 06/11/90

NEW YORK -- A Colorado utility plans the world's first plant to convert landfill gas into diesel transportation fuel. The $14 million plant, to be built in Pueblo, Colo., will be owned by Fuel Resources Development Co., a unit of Public Service Co. of Colorado. The plant is being designed and built by Ultrasystems Engineers and Constructors Inc., a unit of Hadson Corp., Oklahoma City, an energy, defense and construction firm.

The plant, which will produce 100 barrels a day of diesel, plus 50 barrels of naptha and 80 barrels of high-grade wax, should be completed around year end, Ultrasystems' president, John P. Coyle, said. The site is estimated to have 20 years' worth of recoverable gas.

While there are other plants that recover landfill gas and turn it into compressed natural gas and other products, officials of the American Gas Association, a Washington-based industry group, agreed that Ultrasystems' plant appears to be the first for converting landfill gas into diesel motor fuel.

Ultrasystems Vice President Jackson Yu explained that the plant will take the landfill gas -- which is roughly 50%-plus methane and 40%-plus carbon dioxide -- and first chemically break it down into carbon monoxide and hydrogen, or syngas, using basic petrochemical-plant technology.

The syngas then will be run over an iron catalyst developed by Rentech Inc., Denver, and be reformed into a number of hydrocarbon compounds and water. The hydrocarbon compounds then essentially will be refined into diesel, naptha and wax. The refining process is similar to the technology that's long been used by South Africa's state oil company to make a wide range of petroleum products from coal, AGA officials said.

A spokesman for Public Service of Colorado said the utility believes there is a big market for its version of diesel fuel, which it says will be cleaner burning, sulphur free, and nearly odorless compared to ordinary diesel fuel. Both Colorado and Southern California have major air-pollution problems and strict pollution-control regulations. The spokesman said tests have shown that the fuel can be used without a major retrofit of vehicles.

The spokesman declined to say how much the new diesel might cost, although he said that it will be more expensive than regular diesel. He added, however, that the price will be competitive with regular diesel.

In an interview, Richard Braun, executive vice president of Fuel Resources, said the company has so far identified more than 100 landfills in the U.S. and Canada that would be economically viable candidates for similar plants. He added that there are probably many more that haven't yet been located.

A spokesman for the American Trucking Association said he hadn't heard of the new fuel. He added that the association is currently testing several alternative fuels that promise to be cleaner-burning than regular diesel, and that the industry is looking for one that is as close as possible in characteristics to today's diesel fuel.
