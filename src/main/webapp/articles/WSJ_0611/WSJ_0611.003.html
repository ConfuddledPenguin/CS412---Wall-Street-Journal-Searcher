Technology: Group Wins Funds To Study Network Of Fast Computers

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 06/11/90

WASHINGTON -- The National Science Foundation and a Pentagon research agency awarded a three-year, $15.8 million contract to the Corporation for National Research Initiatives to conduct research on a possible, ultra high-speed computer network. Charles Brownstein, the NSF's acting assistant director for computers, said that partners from private industry, including the regional Bell telephone companies and International Business Machines Corp., would chip in "many times" the amount provided by the federal government. The NSF is sharing the cost of the contract with the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency.

Computer researchers hope the small research contract will be a precursor to a federal effort to build a $390 million national computer network that would operate at speeds 1,000 times faster than currently possible. In September, D. Allan Bromley, President Bush's science adviser, urged building such a network as part of a proposed $1.92 billion initiative to advance U.S. supercomputing.

But Mr. Bromley couldn't win any federal funds for his plan. Administration and congressional sources say he is pushing hard to get the administration to provide money for the initiative for the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1, 1991. His plan envisions first-year spending of $151 million for all supercomputing, including $30 million for the network.

Unlike many proposals for government financing of commercial research -- for instance, high-definition television -- the supercomputing initiative hasn't yet run into opposition from White House economic officials who oppose government direction of industry. The computer network is considered the high-technology equivalent of a public highway and thus hasn't set off ideological alarms.

The Corporation for National Research Initiatives, a nonprofit research organization in Reston, Va., plans to use the contract money to explore alternative designs for the proposed national network and ways the network could be used.

One of the possibilities is putting CAT-scan images on computers so that surgeons could view the images like a movie-rotating the images as they like, and visually stripping away layers of tissue.

The corporation is run by Robert Kahn, a former Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency manager who helped to develop the U.S.'s first computer network.
