U.S. and Japanese Officials Approach Trade Negotiations With Apprehension

  • Author: John T. Norman
  • Date: 06/11/90

WASHINGTON -- In what could be the final round of bilateral negotiations between the U.S. and Japan on trade-related "structural" economic issues in Tokyo late this month, U.S. officials are hoping that Japanese policy makers won't openly advocate a new doctrine that Japan's huge current-account and trade surpluses are desirable in maintaining world economic stability. For their part, Japanese officials are apprehensive over the amount of pressure U.S. officials might exert in the June 25-26 negotiations for more specific commitments on future actions to open up Japanese markets for more imports and private foreign investments.

In advance of these talks, Japanese officials are indicating that they aren't planning to make any new concessions to the U.S., such as making advance commitments on the amount of planned public works spending in Japan as a percentage of the country's gross national product.

Japanese officials also are less than enthusiastic about another U.S. proposal that negotiations on structural trade issues be "institutionalized" in the form of continuing consultations several times a year, to rehash old issues and discuss new ones.

Meanwhile, the new economic "theology" about the merits of continued Japanese international trade surpluses, put forward by some Japanese "think-tank" experts, is viewed as a potentially troublesome issue, particularly if the Japanese government were to openly endorse such a strategy.

Thus far, U.S. reaction has been low-keyed, but several top U.S. economic policy makers have said this idea lacks merit.

As things stand, the U.S. and Japan are slated to produce in mid-July the final versions of their reports on policy actions, both in Tokyo and Washington, to achieve a better balance in trade on a longer-term basis.

In interim reports issued in April, both governments managed to ease some of the tensions in their bilateral trade relations. Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu and President Bush, who are expected to get together early next month in Houston, ahead of the economic summit, probably will be looking for ways to keep things on a smooth track.

Delays in Congress: General Motors Corp. and other companies are signing some long-term agreements to supply equipment and technology to the Soviet Union.

But they may have to wait until September or even longer to know whether Congress will approve a bilateral trade agreement to reduce U.S. tariffs on Soviet goods and, perhaps more important, clear the way for the Export-Import Bank to help finance exports of goods and services the Russians want to buy.

Separately, it also may take several months for U.S. companies doing business with China to know, for sure, that the U.S. will continue its "most-favored-nation" treatment for imports of Chinese-made goods.

The House Ways and Means international trade subcommittee, headed by Rep. Sam Gibbons (D., Fla.) will begin hearings June 19 on various bills designed to overturn President Bush's decision to extend for one-year most-favored-nation treatment for Chinese products, which means lower U.S. import duties.

Mr. Gibbons is promising other members of Congress, irked over the White House decision on China, a comprehensive set of hearings that will explore, among other things, Chinese government policies and the impact of trade and investment ties on the U.S., China and Hong Kong.

Technically, Congress must decide no later than Sept. 3 whether to uphold (through inaction) or disapprove (through legislative action) the White House China trade decision of May 24.

But some House members are proposing entirely new legislation that would change the ground rules and set new conditions for any future extensions of the U.S. tariff concessions on imports of apparel, footwear, toys, machinery or other products from China.

On the Soviet Union, U.S. trade-policy officials were saying recently they couldn't predict how soon President Bush would formally transmit the bilateral trade agreement with that country to Congress, coupling this with approval of "waivers" under a 1974 Trade act to lower U.S. import duties on Soviet exports.

They suggested it won't happen soon because Moscow may take several months to enact a law allowing unrestricted emigration of Soviet residents to other countries.

How Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev resolves Moscow's problems with the breakaway Baltic states also will be an important factor in this situation, according to congressional leaders who discussed this issue with President Bush at the White House recently.

Credits To Finance Trade: Although the Soviet Union now is appealing openly for credits from Western nations, including the U.S., Moscow hasn't gotten around yet to applying for membership in the International Monetary Fund or the World Bank, both of which are pumping out funds to other Eastern and Central European countries to support economic reforms.

Last week, for example, the World Bank approved a $250 million loan to Poland to help expand natural-gas production in that country. The bank also has assigned experts to help Poland work out contractual arrangements to attract foreign participation in the country's oil industry.

While some Russian officials apparently are claiming that they have sought informal, unofficial advice from the IMF about currency-related issues, IMF officials say that hasn't happened.

In the bilateral trade agreement negotiated between the U.S. and the Soviet Union, it was agreed that commercial transactions between these two countries would have to be settled in freely convertible currencies.

In a separate "side-letter" agreement that could create new drains on Moscow's limited holdings of dollars, the Russians promised to begin discussions with the U.S. on repaying about $714 million in old U.S. government loans, after the main U.S.-Soviet trade agreement takes effect.

U.S. officials said these old debts include $674 million in World War II lend-lease accounts, plus $44 million the Soviet Union still owes to the Export-Import Bank on loans approved many years ago.
