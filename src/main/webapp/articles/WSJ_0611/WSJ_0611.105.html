SEC Urges Firms To Broaden Venues For Arbitration

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 06/11/90

NEW YORK -- Small investors could soon have more elbow room in bringing arbitration cases against their brokers. The Securities and Exchange Commission is quietly prodding Wall Street to give investors the option of bringing disputes with brokers to independent arbitration forums -- rather than just to arbitration panels sponsored by the exchanges themselves.

In a letter to top Wall Street executives, the SEC is urging the securities industry to adopt a rule giving investors the choice of resolving their arbitration disputes at "a reputable forum that is unrelated to the industry."

The issue goes to the heart of a hot topic in securities arbitration. The SEC says that giving customers such flexibility would allay concerns that arbitration stacks the deck against small investors. Securities executives have long denied that the industry's arbitration panels are unfair.

"Customers won't feel they're being tied to an industry-sponsored forum," says Richard Ryder, editor of the Securities Arbitration Commentator, a Maplewood, N.J., newsletter.

Investors typically sign a standard agreement when they open a brokerage account that binds them to accept arbitration in case of a dispute, rather than filing a lawsuit.

And many customers are required by the agreements to take their cases to industry-subsidized forums. Most go to arbitration panels sponsored by the National Association of Securities Dealers or the New York Stock Exchange.

The major independent forum, the American Arbitration Association, is privately funded and widely perceived to be the most sympathetic to investors. (Investors win about 60% of the time at the AAA, compared with about 50% at exchange-sponsored forums, arbitration specialists say.)

The trouble is, the AAA is more expensive. And that, coupled with brokers' perception that they don't fare as well at the AAA, is why most securities firms oppose giving investors the freedom to take disputes there. Moreover, AAA arbitrators often have less securities experience than those at exchange-sponsored forums, Wall Streeters argue, thus lengthening cases. AAA arbitrators, not surprisingly, dismiss those concerns.

The controversy has sparked litigation. Last month, the influential U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals in New York effectively closed the so-called Amex Window, which affected investors with brokerage agreements involving the American Stock Exchange. This "window" allowed investors to go to the American Arbitration Association if their brokerage contract didn't bind them specifically to the Amex's own arbitration panel.

To keep the window shut, the Amex has asked for SEC approval to amend its constitution by allowing investors to go to the AAA only if their contract doesn't specify any exchange-sponsored forum.

The recent SEC letter comes as securities firms study a broader plan to establish a single arbitration forum to resolve customer complaints. But the commission meantime wants the industry to address the "significant imbalance" in the arbitration caseload, according to the letter, which was signed by the SEC's director of market regulation, Richard G. Ketchum, and dated May 10. Steering cases to independent arbitration forums would help the caseload imbalance.

NASD and Big Board panels together handle 92% of all arbitration cases, and often hear disputes involving trades that occurred on other exchanges. For example, 30% of a recent list of arbitration cases published by the Securities Arbitration Commentator involved options -- even though these products aren't widely traded on either the Big Board or over-the-counter market, which is regulated by the NASD.

The SEC wants securities executives to respond to the letter by July 1; even if the industry were to give investors the flexibility to go to the AAA, any new rule would be at least many months away.

This isn't the first time the SEC has tried to push Wall Street to be more flexible. In 1987, the commission recommended that brokerage firms include the AAA as an available arbitration forum in their brokerage agreements. But the SEC never before had asked for a uniform rule.

But most firms still balk. One exception is A.G. Edwards & Sons Inc., which gives investors the choice of using the AAA because it says it finds no substantial difference among arbitration panels.

"We're not afraid of arbitration," says David Mesker, a senior vice president at A.G. Edwards. "The only thing we want is someone on the panel who is familiar with the business."
