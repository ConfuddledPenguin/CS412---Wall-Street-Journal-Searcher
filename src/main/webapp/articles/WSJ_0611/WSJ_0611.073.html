REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): `Sinful and Tyrannical'

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/11/90

When we think of coercion, we usually think of government as the culprit. Sometimes the government can be the solution, as when the Supreme Court justices last week repeated their admonition that groups with mandatory membership may not force members to fund political activities they oppose. This time the offenders were, ironically, lawyers. The defendant was the state bar of California, which all lawyers in the state must join and fund through dues. Dissident lawyers brought Keller v. State Bar of California because they objected to politicking by the state bar funded with their compulsory dues. The California bar has actively supported gun control, nuclear-freeze ballot initiatives and an unlimited right to sue anyone who causes air pollution. This kind of ideological lobbying has nothing to do with the purpose of the bar, the "regulation of the profession and improvement to the administration of justice."

A unanimous Supreme Court cited Thomas Jefferson. "To compel a man to furnish contributions of money for the propagation of opinions which he disbelieves," barrister Jefferson wrote, "is sinful and tyrannical." It also violates the First Amendment free-speech rights of dissenting members. The court did uphold the practice in some 30 states of requiring fee-paying bar membership by all lawyers. This looks like a closed-shop union, but the justices couldn't quite bring themselves to view fellow lawyers as mere guild members.

So lawyers can continue to be required to belong to state bars and pay dues, but at least none of their dues can go for pushing ideological goals they oppose. This could also quash proposals in several states to have mandatory "pro bono" (free service) requirements, which could be met by financial contributions to special-interest and typically left-wing litigation groups.

While this is good news for lawyers, other workers keep getting misinformation from the National Labor Relations Board. We wrote last year that the NLRB was not informing workers of their rights under the 1988 Supreme Court decision Beck v. Communications Workers of America. This case said that workers don't have to become full financial members of a union, and workers are obliged to pay union dues covering only collective bargaining. Workers do not have to pay for a union's political activities or any other non-bargaining activities.

The National Right to Work Committee last year discovered that NLRB literature made no mention of the workers' new rights granted by Beck, and that NLRB officials tended to describe incorrectly what union activities workers were compelled to pay for and which they were not.

The NLRB hasn't cleaned up its act. Steve Kerekes of the NRWC cites NLRB literature that states the so-called financial core members who "object" to the "use of their payments for non-representational purposes" might be compelled to pay for the "union's costs of representational activities (such as collective bargaining, contract administration, and grievance adjustment)." Although the NLRB defends this language, it's at the least misleading.

Beck doesn't even allow the collection of dues for non-bargaining purposes. Further, the "such as" before the list of union activities workers must fund seems to leave the door open for union activities, such as politics, that are prohibited.

Whether or not NLRB bureaucrats agree with Beck, it's the law. Even lawyers sometimes have to sue to ensure their rights against their bar groups. It's time the NLRB at least made sure that workers know what their rights are.
