Letters to the Editor: Wellesley and Barbara Bush Redux

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/11/90

In response to Enola G. Aird's May 8 editorial-page article "Feminism's Skewed Definition at Wellesley": The debate at Wellesley centered around the appropriateness of Barbara Bush as a graduation speaker for a group of women who have completed four years of a unique education. This education is based on the philosophy that women historically have made valuable contributions in all areas of society including business, politics, law, medicine and family. Their contributions often have been unnoticed or unappreciated, but attest endlessly to their abilities. Wellesley women are encouraged to use their talents and skills in striving to accomplish whatever their goals and aspirations may be, regardless of the barriers with which they are faced. They have become successful writers, actresses, lawyers, bankers, politicians and educators. In addition, many have become important community volunteers. Many of these women are also mothers and homemakers.

With so many accomplished women from whom to select, why then did the students choose Barbara Bush? Mrs. Bush was born into a wealthy family and dropped out of college to marry into another fortunate position. Perhaps she is a good mother. No one doubts her dedication to public service. However, her ability to give is not based on her own, earned accomplishments but on those of her husband and her family. Mrs. Bush's achievements are derived from a class status that allows her the time, money and luxury to be a full-time mother and volunteer. I do not mean to belittle her contributions, for they are noble and valuable. I believe, however, that her activities did not qualify her to speak to the graduating class of 1990.

Rebecca A. Hatch

Wellesley Class of 1987

New York

---

It's about time we moms at home stood up for ourselves and Ms. Aird did that beautifully.

I am sick of living in a community and a country where snotty feminists are allowed to dominate public opinion with their narrow outlook. I am a college graduate who gave up pursuing a second degree to stay home and care for my children, and few of my "enlightened" professors or fellow students could understand why I would be content being a full-time mom. Yet I can't think of a better use for my learning than to benefit my family. After all, anybody can be a doctor, lawyer, secretary, etc. these days, but nobody else can be Mom to my children.

It is interesting to note that the decline of marriage and family has paralleled the decline in the number of women at home. Despite an increased number of college graduates (a "smarter" population, you might say), families seem to be suffering more. I await Ms. Aird's "Militant Mothering," but with such blind and deaf opponents the battle should be easy to win.

Barbara L. Wiedenbeck

Waunakee, Wis.

---

Ms. Aird misinterprets the thrust of the protest as primarily anti-motherhood, misrepresents all Wellesley students as full of "disdain for women who choose to care for their children full-time," and ends her convoluted argument by accusing the protesters of somehow devaluing children. Her reaction to what she perceives as the protesters' lack of respect for motherhood is written in an I'm-not-gonna-take-it-anymore rhetoric usually reserved for flag-burning, drug-dealing terrorists. But she neglects to address the point most central to the protest: Although Barbara Bush is now an advocate of education in her campaigns for literacy, she dropped out of college to marry her husband. Because she did not ever complete her college degree, she is an inappropriate role model for a graduation ceremony of a women's college. She lacks the one value obviously shared by the Wellesley Class of '90: a commitment to women's higher education.

Celeste A. Cleary

Brooklyn, N.Y.

---

Ms. Aird has indeed been fortunate in receiving a fine education, practicing her profession, and being able to be a full-time mother and serve her community. However, most young women today do not have those choices.

My own experience has run the gamut: full-time mother and community volunteer, three children, back to graduate school, divorce, and working outside my profession to support my family. Frankly, although Barbara Bush is an admirable person, she does not represent a role model to me, my daughter or her contemporaries. Accomplishments like hers are celebrated -- when community activists are recognized and on Mother's Day -- but not at college commencements.

Let's face it -- women today still have a difficult time being recognized as equals in so many fields. New graduates need to hear from women who have faced those obstacles and triumphed in spite of them. They need that inspiration.

Kathy Kaufman

Livermore, Calif.

---

Mrs. Bush's role as the mother at home is fast becoming obsolete. For me, the alternative of not working has never been an option, nor has it been, nor will it be, for a growing portion of American women. Both Mrs. Bush and Ms. Aird have that option. That is wonderful. But the simple facts now are that living costs have gone so high that most families cannot exist on one income. A few months ago, I listened with amazement when Mrs. Bush told an interviewer on public radio that women with young children should not be working for that extra television set. My salary -- I shouted to the radio -- pays the mortgage] And, I know I am not the exception.

So, the real question facing most of the new mothers of this generation which includes these Wellesley graduates -- is not whether full-time motherhood is a waste of talent. That is not the issue here. Many of these graduates, like me, are going to have to continue to work when their children are born and when they are young. There will be no choice. The problems that cry out for more attention -- to name a few -- include support from business for the working mother or father, more child-care options and more job flexibility.

Eileen E. Spinner

Mother and Wife and Senior Economist

Nakagama & Wallace Inc.

New York
