Tracking the Economy: Producer, Consumer Price Reports Pack A Double Punch on Inflation This Week

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 06/11/90

NEW YORK -- For the first time in memory, producer and consumer price reports hit back-to-back in the same week. Not for the first time, both inflation gauges are expected to rise in the same month. Normally, wholesale prices precede the important CPI by about a week, giving economists time to adjust their main inflation forecasts. "They're going to get no chance at all to revise" this time, said Patrick Jackman, chief of the CPI branch of the Bureau of Labor Statistics. The May CPI, due Friday, comes right on the heels of Thursday's PPI report.

The week's other indicators take the latest pulse of an economy that has recently been slowing. They include: May retail sales, industrial production and capacity utilization, as well as April's trade deficit and business inventories.

But the inflation data are likely to steal the statistical show because already-disclosed reports have set the tone for most of the other numbers. Economists expect an uptick in producer prices after two months of declines. The consensus view calls for a 0.2% increase, according to MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc. Consumer prices are thought to have again increased, with the consensus also expecting a 0.3% rise. In April, the CPI rose 0.2% and in March it jumped 0.5%.

The CPI trackers have been working for some time to shave days off the release date of the index. Traditionally, the CPI takes longer to gather since its market basket is compiled throughout the month. The PPI reflects one sample day. Economists noted that eclipsing the time between reports (bureau rules prohibit sameday disclosure) could heighten analysts' anxiety on months the gauges move in different directions. Such discrepancies aren't that uncommon, largely because the CPI is heavily weighted toward services, while the PPI doesn't measure services at all.

Richard Trainer, senior economist at Bank of Tokyo in New York, is in the camp that expects both inflation gauges to rise a bit in May. But he's expecting only a 0.1% increase in May producer prices, largely because of the past behavior of prices for basics like feeds, foodstuffs and natural gas. "I think we'll see some increase in some food and energy prices at the consumer level, too," Mr. Trainer said. He expects about a 0.2% rise in the CPI.

He and some others expect cooler inflation over time. "We're coming off some very, very high inflation numbers that were driven by special factors late last year," he said. Among the quirks, he cited unusually cold weather and a sharp runup in apparel costs. Mr. Trainer noted that the recent economic sluggishness indicates "we're going to see some relief in demand pressure on prices. Then we'll actually see some dust settling down." But he added: "It takes a while, perhaps {until} late summer or early fall. But then we're back into the winter season."

Mickey D. Levy, chief economist at First Fidelity Bancorp. in Philadelphia, believes that a flattening trend in money supply portends cooler inflation down the road. A decelerating money supply would be "very significant" to produce the slow-growth scenario necessary to sustain his lower-inflation forecast.

Mr. Levy has one of the most optimistic inflation outlooks for the rest of 1990 and for 1991. "By the end of 1991, we'll be in the 3.25% range, maybe a little lower," he said.

The consensus view on retail sales, due Wednesday, expects a 0.1% rise, following April's 0.6% drop. Sluggish auto sales are expected to again act as a drag on the overall number. Economists are particularly interested in any gains in apparel sales to determine if discounting drove up volume or whether prices held.

Industrial output and capacity utilization, due Friday, are expected to rebound slightly. The consensus view calls for output to rise 0.4%, which would put the operating rate around 83.2%. In April, industrial production slipped 0.4% and capacity utilization was 83%.

Economists continue to expect slow improvement in the trade deficit. April's deficit report, due Friday, is expected to narrow to $7.5 billion, following March's widening to $8.4 billion.
