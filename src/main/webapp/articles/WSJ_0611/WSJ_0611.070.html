Law -- Legal Beat: Commerce Department Draws Fire Over Correcting Minority Count

  • Author: Wade Lambert and Christi Harlan
  • Date: 06/11/90

A federal judge criticized the Commerce Department for taking a "bare minimum" approach to complying with an agreement that was meant to correct the Census Bureau's undercounting of minorities. Judge Joseph McLaughlin also declared that it would be constitutional for a statistical adjustment to be made in the actual census count, if that would make the total more accurate. Lawyers for New York, Los Angeles and other major cities say that such an adjustment is necessary to correct the undercounting of minorities. The Commerce Department, which oversees the Census Bureau, has questioned whether the adjustment would be constitutional.

"The concept of statistical adjustment is wholly valid, and may very well be long overdue," said Judge McLaughlin, of Brooklyn. Judge McLaughlin also raised questions about the guidelines the Commerce Department has developed for evaluating whether a statistical adjustment will be made. But he stopped short of ordering the department to scrap the guidelines, as the cities and other critics of the census had requested.

The attorney handling the case for the Commerce Department couldn't be reached for comment.

Peter Zimroth, a partner in the New York office of Arnold & Porter, who represents New York City, said that under Judge McLaughlin's decision, "if the Commerce Department chooses not to adjust {the count}, it will have to prove that the unadjusted numbers are more accurate."

The judge's decision stems from a suit filed by New York and other cities in 1988 over the undercounting. In what was considered a victory for the cities, the Commerce Department last summer agreed to develop guidelines for adjusting the census to make up for the large number of people who aren't counted. Census figures are used to determine congressional representation and some federal funding.

Noting that some of the guidelines "lend themselves easily to abuse," Judge McLaughlin said the department is "on notice, if it is not already clear, that backdoor attempts to evade their commitment will not be countenanced."

The judge also accused the Commerce Department of improperly depleting an account that was meant to fund an independent advisory panel overseeing the issue. The judge said he will order the Commerce Department to come up with more funds if necessary.

BANKRUPTCY JUDGE APPOINTS special adviser to review funding of Manville trust.

U.S. Bankruptcy Court Judge Burton Lifland, who presided over Manville Corp.'s Chapter 11 reorganization, named Leon Silverman to look into allegations that the fund is mismanaged and inadequately funded.

It was Judge Lifland's first public response to decisions issued by two judges raising questions about the trust. Federal Judge Jack B. Weinstein and New York state Justice Helen Freedman had told the trust and Manville to return to bankruptcy court to address the problems, or face possible harsh measures.

Judge Lifland said the judges' orders "raise important issues about the trust and the consummated plan of reorganization." The trust, which is independent of the company, was formed as part of the reorganization plan to pay asbestos personal-injury claims.

The trust and the company praised the appointment of Mr. Silverman.

In turning to Mr. Silverman, a partner at Fried, Frank, Harris, Shriver & Jacobson, Judge Lifland is bringing in one of the chief architects of the reorganization plan that allowed Manville to emerge from bankruptcy proceedings in 1986.

Mr. Silverman, who represented future personal-injury claimants, was closely involved in deciding how the trust would be funded.

FDIC WILL APPEAL reversal of $6.5 million verdict in bankers' bonds case.

The case involved a dispute over a bond covering C.H. Butcher Jr., former chairman of United Southern Bank of Nashville, Tenn. The ruling has broad implications for claims against officers and directors of failed banks. Bankers' bonds are a form of insurance against fraud or other misdeeds by officers and directors.

"I think it {the ruling} has the potential to be read beyond the facts of this case," said Mark I. Rosen, deputy general counsel for litigation at the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.

In this instance, Mr. Butcher and other bank employees were covered under a bankers' blanket bond issued by Aetna Casualty & Surety Co., a unit of Aetna Life & Casualty Co. Under the terms of the bond's provisions, coverage would be canceled "immediately upon the taking over of the insured {bank} by a receiver or other liquidator or by state or federal officials." Coverage could be extended under some circumstances, but the bond denied state and federal agencies the right to extend the coverage. The provisions are common to most bankers' bonds.

Aetna cited the provisions when it rebuffed the FDIC's request to purchase bond coverage after the bank failed. When Aetna rejected the FDIC's claims for losses on five fraudulent loans arranged by Mr. Butcher, the FDIC sued to collect under the bond.

During the trial last year, a judge ruled that Aetna couldn't use as evidence the provisions of its bond involving renewal and cancellation by a federal agency. The judge said the provisions were void because they were "contrary to public policy."

The jury awarded the FDIC $3 million under the bonds and $3.5 million in punitive damages. It ruled that Aetna had acted in bad faith in failing to extend the bond coverage.

Aetna, represented by the Washington, D.C., office of Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher, appealed, and late last month, a panel of the Sixth U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals reversed the verdict and ordered the suit against Aetna dismissed. The appellate panel took exception to the trial judge's exclusion of the bond provisions. "The dominant public policy exposed by this review is that the parties' freedom of contract must not be disturbed," the panel wrote.

Last week the FDIC filed notice that it will seek a rehearing by the panel, saying that "this reversal presents several issues of exceptional importance, including an adverse impact on the stability of the national banking system and the FDIC's insurance fund."

PACIFIC NORTHWEST MERGER forms region's second-largest law firm.

Lane Powell Moss & Miller of Seattle, which has 150 lawyers, has merged with 88-lawyer Spears, Lubersky, Bledsoe, Anderson, Young & Hilliard of Portland, Ore. The new law firm, called Lane Powell Spears Lubersky, also has offices in Alaska, London, Tokyo, Olympia, Wash. and Vancouver, British Columbia.

A spokesman for the new law firm said it is now the second-largest in the Northwest. Seattle-based Perkins Coie, with 330 lawyers, is the Northwest's largest law firm.

Lane Powell specialized in insurance and commercial litigation. Spears Lubersky concentrated in commercial litigation, employment and labor law.

Clients of the merged law firm include SPTC Holding Inc.'s Southern Pacific Transportation Co. unit, San Francisco; Nordstrom Inc., Seattle; Union Pacific Corp.'s Union Pacific Railroad unit, Bethlehem, Pa.; Underwriters of Lloyds, London; Exxon Corp., New York; and Chevron USA, San Francisco.

PUBLISHING MILLIONAIRE is sentenced for failing to maintain tax records.

Peter M. Brant, 43 years old, of Greenwich, Conn., was sentenced to three months in federal prison, five years of probation and ordered to serve 500 hours of community service and pay a $200,000 fine for two tax violation counts. Mr. Brant owns a newsprint company, Bato Co., its subsidiary, Riviere du Loup Newsprint Ltd., and Brant Art Publications.

Mr. Brant, along with his cousin and business partner, Joseph Allen of New York, pleaded guilty last April to a scheme to defraud the federal government of $1.5 million by charging substantial personal expenses to their companies, then reporting the expenses as business deductions and failing to maintain records from which their taxable income and tax could be determined.

Mr. Brant and his attorney were not available for comment.
