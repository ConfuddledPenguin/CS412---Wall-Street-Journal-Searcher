LEISURE & ARTS -- Business Bookshelf: Don't Be Alarmed by FDI

  • Author: Susan W. Liebeler
  • Date: 06/11/90

The recent acquisitions of Rockefeller Center and Columbia Pictures have caused some worry that foreign investors are buying the soul of America. While such transactions are highly visible, the actual causes and effects of foreign investment are much harder to discern. This task is made more difficult still by the surfeit of books and articles about foreign investment in the U.S., most of which rely heavily on anecdotal evidence. In my view, much of this literature has sacrificed credibility in favor of alarmist tones and superficial analysis. The notable exception is "Foreign Direct Investment in the United States" (147 pages, $11.95) by Edward M. Graham of Duke's Fuqua School of Business and Paul R. Krugman of MIT's Department of Economics. Published by the Institute for International Economics in Washington, D.C., it offers an excellent analytical treatment of the many issues raised by foreign direct investment (FDI). It also is accessible to laymen, a rare occurrence in the literature of economics, and it should lay to rest much of the irresponsible rhetoric about FDI heard inside the Capitol Beltway.

The focus of the debate on foreign investment in the U.S. has been on FDI, which, unlike portfolio investment, gives foreign investors the ability to control specific assets. The Commerce Department, which collects data on foreign investment, classifies an interest as FDI when the investor holds at least a 10% equity interest in U.S. business or real estate.

Messrs. Graham and Krugman dispel the most popular explanation for the rise in FDI -- that it is a result of the growth of the trade deficit -- by demonstrating that the growth in FDI began back in the early 1970s, when the U.S. frequently enjoyed trade surpluses and FDI surged between 1977 and 1981, tailing off before the rise of the huge deficits. Nor is the claim that the growth of FDI is spurred by lower foreign interest rates convincing. Even assuming that foreign interest rates were lower, this would not explain the heavy reliance on local financing. Messrs. Graham and Krugman also reject the old saw that foreigners are buying America on the cheap. Although the falling U.S. dollar makes U.S. companies less expensive in foreign currencies, it also makes their profit stream less valuable.

In the authors' view, the best explanation for the growth of FDI is the standard economist's explanation, that it allows foreign multinationals to produce goods locally when they have an advantage in technology or management skills over their domestic competitors.

This explanation accords with the change in the world economic system after World War II. Instead of being the pre-eminent economic power, the U.S. now is the leading economic power among such peers as West Germany, Japan, the United Kingdom and France. U.S. multinationals no longer have a monopoly on the best technology and management. As a consequence of the maturation of the economies of other nations, FDI in the U.S. is approaching the levels existing in other industrial economies, although inward direct investment still has a long way to go to reach the level of FDI common in other industrialized countries.

Many of the critics' arguments about the dangers of FDI fare no better than their explanations for its growth. Claims that FDI costs America jobs, transfers good jobs overseas, reduces local research and development and worsens the trade deficit all are systematically refuted by the evidence. On other arguments, that FDI increases imports or that foreign firms have bought high-tech U.S. firms and transferred their technology abroad leaving hollowed-out shell corporations, the authors point to a dearth of hard evidence either way. In sum, no case has been made that FDI presents a danger to the U.S. economy.

The authors successfully debunk the myths circulated by the critics of foreign investment. Their research is careful, their writing clear, and their analysis incisive. What their book lacks, however, is a slick cover, a catchy title, and self-serving quotations from politicians and representatives of special interests. Without sensationalist innuendoes about takeovers and a doomsday message, this serious and important study is likely to be swamped by its more flashily marketed competition.

Curiously, this seems to be true even in Japan, where Japan-bashing books enjoy surprising popularity. The Japanese have a great thirst for information about the U.S., their largest and most important trading partner. Japan-bashing books are readily available in a number of Tokyo bookstores, and their authors lecture in Japan for hefty fees. But on a recent trip to Tokyo I was unable to find a single copy of the Graham/Krugman book. It is ironic that the Japanese are doing so much to encourage their critics, while neglecting the more balanced work of serious scholars.

---

Ms. Liebeler, the former chairman of the U.S. International Trade Commission, is a partner in the law firm of Irell & Manella in Washington, D.C.
