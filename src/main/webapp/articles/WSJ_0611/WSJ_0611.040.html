Technology: Japan Could Surpass the U.S. in Output Of Electronics Soon, New Report Says

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 06/11/90

WASHINGTON -- In its bleakest report yet on the competitiveness of the U.S. electronics industry, the Commerce Department said Japan could surpass the U.S. in production of electronic goods by the early 1990s. "Except for software and medical equipment, the U.S. has been losing market share in all products since 1984," said John McPhee, director of the department's office of computers and business equipment and a co-author of the report. Although the U.S. still leads the world with more than $180 billion in annual output of electronic goods, Japan could spurt ahead by 1994 if the current trend continues, the report said.

The report urges various measures to improve the U.S. industry's health, including adoption of a permanent research-and-development tax credit, changes in antitrust policies, and government actions against so-called unfairly traded imports and foreign patent violations. But the report places most of the burden for responding to the Japanese challenge on the private sector.

The report shows declines in the U.S. share of world markets even in such fields as personal computers, supercomputers and microprocessors, which the U.S. has long dominated. The study also notes that practically all capacity for fabricating silicon wafers, the basic material for manufacturing semiconductors, is now in Japanese and European hands.

Moreover, Japan outspends the U.S. in X-ray lithography, the most promising method for printing the multibillion-transistor memories of the future, the report says.

In 1987, the latest year for which reliable global-trade figures are available, Japan already led the world in electronic exports with shipments valued at $38 billion. The U.S. trailed with $35 billion in exports, followed by the European Community with shipments of $26 billion.

A comparison of corporate growth rates between 1983 and 1987 shows Japanese electronic producers to be outpacing their U.S. rivals; the Japanese companies' sales grew 27.9%, compared with 10.8% for the U.S. companies.

In another indicator of how industrial strength is shifting, three Japanese companies won more U.S. electronic patents in 1987 than any other companies -- Canon Inc., Hitachi Ltd. and Toshiba Corp. General Electric Co. was next and N.V. Philips of the Netherlands was No. 5.

In the past few years, the Japanese have invested heavily in U.S. electronic capacity, partly to avoid additional import duties imposed on their personal computers, semiconductors and electric typewriters. Although these Japanese-owned operations create U.S. employment, they also contribute to the U.S. trade deficit because the value of their imports of components exceeds that of their exports of final products, the report said.
