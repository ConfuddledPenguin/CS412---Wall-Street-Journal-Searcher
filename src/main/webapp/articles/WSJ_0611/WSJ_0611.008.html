Boeing Expects Korean Airlines Order For 23 Jets Valued at Nearly $4 Billion

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 06/11/90

Korean Airlines is expected to sign a contract today for 23 747-400 Boeing jumbo jets in a deal valued at almost $4 billion. The transaction would mark the third giant order for Boeing Co. from Asia in the past few weeks, underscoring the rapid growth in that market.

Seattle-based Boeing, the world's leading commercial airplane maker, has scheduled a news conference for 11 a.m. in Washington, D.C., to mark the signing.

In addition to the attendance of Boeing Chairman Frank Shrontz and C.H. Cho, a senior executive vice president of Korean Airlines, lawmakers also have been invited, a signal that the South Koreans hope will show that they're continuing to try and reduce the U.S.'s merchandise trade deficit with their country. The deficit, according to Commerce Department figures, stood at $6.3 billion at the end of 1989.

Boeing declined to disclose what kinds of planes Korean Airlines will purchase, but an official at the carrier said it will make 12 firm orders for 747-400s, as well as 11 "re-confirmations" for the four-engine aircraft. A re-confirmation gives Korean Airlines the right to cancel at a future date, but it is considered more of a commitment than taking out options. Deliveries are planned to begin in 1991.

Sources close to the transaction cautioned that the numbers could slightly change before the contract signing.

The airline executive said Korean Airlines will pay about $170 million each for the 747s, adjusted for inflation.

The official said that Korean Airlines also considered Airbus Industrie's A-330 planes, but he said the Boeing aircraft "are particularly suited" for trans-Pacific and European routes. Airbus's A-330s also won't be ready until several years after the Boeing planes are available.

It wasn't immediately clear which of the world's jet-engine manufacturers -- General Electric Co., Rolls-Royce PLC or United Technologies Corp.'s Pratt & Whitney unit -- will power the planes. An engine order often lags an aircraft order. Pratt & Whitney, though, has been named the supplier for nine other 747-400s bought in the past by Korean Airlines.

The Korean Airlines order -- which follows a $2 billion deal with China's Civil Aviation Administration and a $3.1 billion booking for 747-400s for Japan Airlines -- could boost Boeing's stock price.

The stock hit a 52-week high last Monday at $87. It slumped later in the week, closing Friday in New York Stock Exchange composite trading at $82.50, off $1.375.

Judith Comeau, aerospace analyst for Goldman, Sachs & Co., attributed the fall to profit-taking, noting that a number of other blue-chips have suffered a similar fate over the past few days. But she believes there is plenty of upside potential left in the stock. "It is much too early to sell Boeing," she said.

Ms. Comeau pointed out that the 747-400 is a particularly hot commodity right now. She said she recently visited AMR Corp.'s American Airlines unit, for instance, where officials are struggling to find a leasing company or another source that can provide six 747s. "They can't get them anywhere," she said, explaining that they'll "probably have to settle for" smaller McDonnell Douglas MD-11 trijets.

The demand for the 747 is particularly acute in the Pacific Rim. Boeing's own forecast is that air traffic to Asia from North America and Europe will soar 9% annually through the next decade. Within Asia, air traffic is expected to surge over the same period almost 8%, and within the U.S. only 5.2%.
