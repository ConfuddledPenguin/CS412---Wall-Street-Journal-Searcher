Letters to the Editor: The Students Weren't Out of Line

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/11/90

I object to your May 9 editorial "The Ivory Censor" describing Wellesley College as part of the national trend toward censorship on college campuses. Unlike Northern Colorado University, which rescinded its invitation to Linda Chavez to be its commencement speaker, Wellesley College did not revoke its invitation to Barbara Bush. A significant minority of the college's graduating class does not think that Mrs. Bush should have been chosen by its classmates because her distinction is based on the success of her husband, not on professional achievement. Nor does it think that one of the nation's premier colleges for women should honor someone because of her success as a wife and mother. The majority clearly did not agree, because it picked her. The action of the protesters is not an example of censorship because Mrs. Bush has not been and will not be censored.

The students were not out of bounds in expressing their views. Had the college invited Mamie Eisenhower to address the class of 1960 when I was graduating from Wellesley, I would have led the protest then. The tiny and diminishing number of institutions that still maintains standards of excellence and achievement should honor those values at their commencement ceremonies.

I admire the response of Wellesley College's administration to the affair: The college protected the right of the minority to express its dissent, as well as the right of the majority to prevail. Above all, it insisted upon an atmosphere of civility and reasoned discourse. That's the American way.

Diane Ravitch

Adjunct Professor of History and Education

Teachers College

Columbia University

Wellesley, Class of 1960

New York

---

There is a phenomenon that seems to be pervasive in academic and corporate America. While professing a quest for "cultural diversity," institutions have sought refuge in positioning themselves as the guardians of quality and excellence.

Your editorial assumes Prof. Derrick Bell's insistence that Harvard Law School appoint a black female to its faculty diminishes his commitment to excellence. You decry that "merit apparently must take a back seat to race and gender." We all would join you -- if it were true.

To frame the argument in those terms takes an arrogant presumption that there are no black females who can meet and enhance the highest standards of excellence -- and, by their mere presence, further diversify the culture of the institution. Both goals, excellence and cultural diversity, are ideal, yet achievable.

The notion that they are mutually exclusive, thus undesirable, was recently advanced in Allan Bloom's "The Closing of the American Mind." By documenting and, through association with his considerable academic credentials, codifying this halfbaked notion, Prof. Bloom gave hordes of conservatives air cover to denounce the goal of cultural diversity -- and to continue their embedded doctrine of cultural exclusion.

Preston G. Foster

JFK School of Government, '91

Atlanta
