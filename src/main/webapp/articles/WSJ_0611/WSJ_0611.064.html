Credit Markets: Junk-Bond Indexes Tell a Confusing Story

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 06/11/90

NEW YORK -- Junk-bond indexes are proliferating on Wall Street these days. Securities firms, vying for the junk-bond mantle formerly worn by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., are eager to see their names on a widely quoted junk bond index. And so three different junk bond indexes are being promoted by Merrill Lynch & Co., First Boston Corp. and Salomon Brothers Inc.

In recent months the broad junk-bond indexes have shown signs of strength for the first time in more than a year. But before taking the leap into junk bonds, take note: Even the analysts who compile the indexes warn that the broad averages can be highly misleading.

To start with, the three indexes don't even agree on how well the junk market is doing. Though all three show junk bonds posted solid gains in May, the returns they show for the first five months of the year vary.

While Salomon's index of 472 junk bonds posted a negative total return of 1.04% for the five months, First Boston's index of 349 junk bonds showed a negative 0.13%, and Merrill Lynch's measure of 735 junk bonds reported a total return of 0.20%. Total return measures price changes plus interest income.

The disparities -- which reflect the indexes' differences in composition and sample size -- highlight the difficulties that investors face when trying to determine how the $200 billion junk bond market is faring. It's an issue that is getting more attention as investors try to figure out whether the time is right to venture into that battered market.

"The average return is a less meaningful number in the high-yield market" than in other sectors of the bond market, said Thomas Doyle, an analyst at Salomon Brothers.

Like many other analysts, he says investors should look at one small group of junk bonds at a time, considering such factors as the credit rating, industry group, size of issue, type of payment (cash, deferred payment or payment in kind-interest in the form of additional securities) and even whether the junk bonds were issued as part of a leveraged buy-out.

Looking at them in this way brings out some extraordinary differences. First Boston said its index of information technology companies' junk bonds showed a total return of 9.5% for January through May, while junk bonds issued by health care companies posted average returns of a negative 13.1%.

Sam DeRosa-Farag, an analyst at First Boston, said that firm can "slice the market into 200 different subsets."

The broader junk indexes differ largely because each one is constructed differently. The Merrill Lynch and Salomon Brothers indexes remove bonds that have defaulted on interest payments. As a result, these indexes receive a lot of criticism from money managers, who can't easily purge bad bonds from their own portfolios. "The indexes that eliminate busted deals tend to overstate performance," said Thomas Madden, high-yield portfolio manager at Federated Investors Corp.

Merrill and Salomon also exclude noncash-paying bonds, which are highly volatile. This omission understates overall market performance when prices are rising and distorts losses when the market is falling. For example, Salomon's zero-coupon component, which is not part of the overall index, rose 5.35% in May while the broad market index rose 2.63%. But in the past 12 months, when the market was mostly falling, the zero-coupon component fell 15.72% while the broad market index fell 2.84%. Zero-coupon bonds, instead of paying interest, are sold at a deep discount, with the investor collecting the full face amount when the bond matures.

Of the three indexes produced by the brokerage firms, many money managers say they like First Boston's the best, because it includes some of everything.

Dan Baldwin, a high-yield investment manager at Chancellor Capital, said he pays a little more attention to statistics provided by Lipper Analytical Services Inc. that measure the portfolio performance of other money managers than he does to the brokerage firms' indexes.

This way, Mr. Baldwin said, he's competing against other "people who are forced to make decisions," rather than "a theoretical portfolio." The Lipper numbers, he said, represent "the real world."

Another measure often used to appraise the relative value of junk bonds, the yield spread, also falls short when used as a barometer of the market's health. This compares yields on a given security with those on a U.S. Treasury security that has the same or a similar maturity. Because U.S. government notes and bonds are considered the safest securities of all, other fixed-income securities are priced at yields that are higher than Treasurys to reflect their higher risk.

When yields on corporate or mortgage-backed bonds, or taxable equivalent yields on tax-exempt municipal bonds, move closer to yields on Treasury bonds, they are considered expensive relative to Treasurys. When yields on these other bonds are farther from yields on Treasurys, the other bonds are said to be cheap. Yields on bonds move in the opposite direction from their price.

By this measure, the junk bond market is holding a fire sale. According to Merrill Lynch, the spread between junk bonds and Treasurys widened from 4.6 percentage points in March 1989 to 10.2 percentage points in March 1990.

In a recent Merrill Lynch report, high-yield research director Martin Fridson and analyst Scott L. Thomas compiled a list of 118 of the largest non-defaulted junk bond issues and found that on March 30, 1990, the average yield was 18.83%, or 10.19 percentage points higher than the yield on the 10-year Treasury note. But within the group, they found, only eight of the 118 issues actually had yields within one percentage point of the average in either direction. They concluded that the concept of an average yield was "highly misleading," as 93% of the issues were "nowhere close" to this average.

There also are numerous technical problems related to pricing. Unlike the stock market, where there are central processing systems that verify prices, the bond market is a market in which each brokerage firm sets its own prices.

Moreover, the market is so fragmented and illiquid that most dealers don't even trade 200 different bonds in a day, out of the 3,000 or so junk bonds that are out there. That means the folks who produce the indexes often must rely on price data gathered from so-called street brokers, which raises questions about the reliability of the quotes.

And because junk bonds don't always move in sync with each other, there is no such thing as an average bond that can be used as a benchmark. Instead, "there are a couple of hundred in the high-yield market" that could be used as benchmarks for segments of the market, said Joseph Bencivenga, managing director of high-yield research at Salomon Brothers who once held a similar post at Drexel Burnham Lambert.

Despite these problems, investors and money managers insist on measuring performance whether or not perfect indexes exist. That has led a number of investment firms to scramble to beef up their research abilities to try to attract new customers.

Mr. Bencivenga said Salomon is revising its high-yield index and is considering adding a lot more bonds including zero-coupon issues, defaulted bonds and securities that have resettable interest rates.

This year, Salomon purchased a computer junk bond database and software system from Drexel, which put the system up for sale after it filed for bankruptcy-court protection. The database has names, contacts and trading histories of about 3,000 issues. Salomon also is working on a new "flash" junk bond index to measure the market's daily performance and plans to soon begin compiling a daily list of the market's best and worst performers.

Friday's Market Activity

Treasury bond prices ended unchanged to 1/8 point lower, or down $1.25 or less for each $1,000 face amount, after drifting in quiet trading.

The benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late in the day at a price of 103 9/32 to yield 8.44%, compared with 103 14/32 to yield 8.42% Thursday. Short-term rates were little changed.

Bond dealers said investors continued to mark time ahead of this week's deluge of economic statistics, starting with Wednesday's release of May retail sales data. Also in prospect are Thursday's report on last month's producer prices and Friday's report on the May consumer price index.

In the corporate market, junk bond prices followed the stock market lower. Most junk bonds, which are speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, ended about 1/4 point lower.

Traders said the nearly 35-point drop in the Dow Jones Industrial Average prompted some high-yield investors to sell junk bonds short, in effect making a bet that their prices would decline.

Among the issues that finished lower were RJR Nabisco Inc. payment-in-kind securities, which pay interest in the form of additional securities. RJR's 14.07% convertible PIK debentures due in 2009 finished one point lower at 83 on the New York Stock Exchange.

The other widely held RJR issue, the 14.07% exchangeable PIK debentures maturing in 2007, ended one point lower at 69. RJR's payment-in-kind bonds are widely held by stock investors and tend to decline or rebound in step with major movements on the stock market.

Junk bonds of Beatrice Co. jumped on news that ConAgra Inc. agreed to buy Beatrice for $1.34 billion in cash and stock. The company's 13.71% senior subordinated reset notes maturing in 1997 rose 2 1/2 points to 104 1/2. Dirk van Doren, an analyst at McCarthy Crisanti & Maffei Inc., said the bonds rose on the perception that ConAgra is financially stronger than Beatrice. In addition, he said, the bonds can be called at 105 on Nov. 1, and he said ConAgra is likely to call them because it has a significantly lower cost of funds than Beatrice does.
