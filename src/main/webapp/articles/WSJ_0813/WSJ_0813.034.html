Deloitte & Touche Declines to Continue As Auditor of Troubled First Executive

  • Author: Frederick Rose
  • Date: 08/13/90

LOS ANGELES -- First Executive Corp., the wounded insurance holding company, said its longtime auditor, Deloitte & Touche, has declined to continue in that capacity. In a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, the company said it hasn't had any disagreements with Deloitte & Touche in the past 2 1/2 years, and the reason for the firm's decision to remove itself remains unclear.

First Executive, the parent of Executive Life Insurance Co., a California concern, and Executive Life Insurance Co. of New York, has been the subject of widespread news coverage since earlier this year, when it reported an $859 million write-down of its junk-bond portfolio. The unexpected write-down, which applied as of Dec. 31, 1989, brought the company's net loss for last year to $775.6 million, or $9.67 a share.

First Executive noted in its filing that, in June, it hired William L. Sanders as chief financial officer, treasurer and a corporate vice president. Mr. Sanders had been the Deloitte & Touche partner in charge of First Executive's audit since 1986.

Subsequent to hiring Mr. Sanders, First Executive said, it recruited a number of other Deloitte & Touche employees who also had worked on its audit. First Executive said it employed the former members of the group that audited it "in order to expand its accounting staff."

The company said that, because of the resulting changes at Deloitte & Touche, it had begun considering a possible change of accountants but hadn't reached any decision when Deloitte & Touche apparently beat it to the punch and resigned. However, the filing doesn't explicitly connect Deloitte & Touche's decision to resign with First Executive's unusual decision to employ what appears to have been a substantial portion of its outside auditor's team.

First Executive's disclosure was made in an 8-K extraordinary events filing with the SEC. The filing, dated July 30, was signed by a corporate officer Aug. 2 and received by the commission the next day.

For its part, Deloitte & Touche, in an attached one-sentence letter, said it concurred with First Executive's "comments" in the 8-K filing but didn't provide further disclosure. Robert Garland, managing partner of Deloitte & Touche's Los Angeles office, declined to elaborate.

Fred Carr, chairman and chief executive officer of First Executive, said the company hasn't selected a successor to Deloitte & Touche. Before a merger that created Deloitte & Touche, a predecessor firm, Touche Ross & Co., had audited First Executive. "They are an excellent firm, and we have enjoyed an excellent relationship with them," Mr. Carr said, adding that he had discussed Deloitte & Touche's decision to withdraw as auditor at First Executive's July 30 annual meeting.

Mr. Carr said he wasn't sure how many members of Deloitte & Touche's former audit team had been recruited by First Executive. "I'm guessing that it's seven or eight," he said.

Deloitte & Touche is a defendant with First Executive in shareholder suits focusing, among other things, on alleged inadequate disclosure of the company's troubled financial condition.

Over the years, the accounting firm has issued a series of unqualified audit opinions on First Executive. However, in its March 29, 1990 report on the 1989 financial statements, Deloitte & Touche included extensive cautionary explanations. Among other things, it said that the estimated market value of First Executive's $8 billion-plus junk-bond holdings was based on the company's and certain brokers' subjective determinations because the market for some of the securities was either too volatile or too illiquid to get a clear reading.

However, the auditor's letter didn't flag or explain the $859 million write-down or note that, even after the write-down, the company's market value of the bond portfolio remained about $1 billion below the $13.26 billion value at which it is recorded on First Executive's books. Deloitte & Touche did note, however, that if troubled market conditions continue, the company could suffer greater portfolio losses than then estimated.

First Executive is the subject of a number of regulatory inquiries, among them a formal SEC investigation begun in March that is looking into the company's financial statements, including the bond-portfolio write-down.

As its financial woes deepened this year, including more than $1.33 billion paid in the first quarter to holders cashing in policies, First Executive began pointing to studies by consulting actuaries Milliman & Robertson, which it said proved that its California-based Executive Life Insurance unit was financially sound and able to withstand a variety of hypothetical adversities.

But, while reporting the good news, First Executive thus far has refused to make the reports themselves public.

Friday, New York's state appeals court delayed ruling on an appeal by First Executive to forestall disclosure of the reports to Joseph Belth, publisher of "Insurance Forum," and a frequent critic of First Executive. A lower court had ordered the New York State Department of Insurance, which has copies of the reports, to make them public under state freedom of information statutes.
