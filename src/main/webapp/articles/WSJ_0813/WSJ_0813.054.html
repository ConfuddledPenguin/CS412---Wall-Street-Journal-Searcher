Shawmut Enters Accord Sought By Regulators --- U.S. Demands Tightening Of the Lending Practices Of New England Bank

  • Author: Ron Suskind
  • Date: 08/13/90

BOSTON -- Shawmut National Corp. said that it entered into agreements with federal bank regulators to strengthen certain lending practices, credit review, liquidity planning and management information systems. The agreements follow a regulatory review of the bank concluded during the second quarter, which ended June 30.

Of several agreements with regulators, the most significant is a "formal agreement" with the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency. That document, which the bank will sign in a few days, is a regulatory measure exceeded by a formal enforcement action -- like that brought against Bank of Boston Corp. last October -- and, after that, by a cease and desist order. Of the large regional banks in New England, only the Bank of New England Corp. has received a cease and desist order -- a regulatory action just a step below an actual bank seizure.

Joel B. Alvord, Shawmut's chairman and chief executive officer, said "We believe that the provisions of these agreements will serve to assure our long-term strength."

Along with other New England banks, Shawmut, with $26.6 billion in assets, is wrestling with a large portfolio of real estate loans in a softening regional real estate market. The bank's nonperforming loans and foreclosed property rose to $1.31 billion at the end of the second quarter, up from $1.16 billion in the first quarter. Total loan loss reserves stood at $748.3 million, or 4.48% of total loans outstanding, compared with $287 million in reserves, 1.53% of total loans, on June 30, 1989.

Carrying $1.31 billion in nonperforming assets -- money lent out that is bringing no return -- causes a heavy drain on Shawut's capital because the bank must continue to pay interest on that money to depositors. The losses tend to be about 9% of the total nonperforming portfolio, or, in Shawmut's case, approximately $120 million annually.

In reviewing the bank's nonperforming assets, Thomas F. Theurkauf, the bank's senior vice president, noted Shawmut took one step forward in clearing away nonperformers in the second quarter, but was pushed two steps back -- with $237.5 million in fresh, non-accruing loans appearing on the bank's books.

"The content of the {OCC} agreement we are not giving out," said a bank spokesman. "All I'll say is that the economic scenario this is all being driven by is we have a regional economy that is at least six to nine months from stabilizing. All the banks up here are fighting the same situation we are."
