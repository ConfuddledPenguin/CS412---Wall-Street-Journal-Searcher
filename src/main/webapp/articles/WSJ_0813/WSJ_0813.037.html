The Mideast Conflict: Iraq Can Make Case For Claim to Kuwait, But It's a Shaky One --- Two Nations Considered One Under Ottoman Empire, But British Split Them

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 08/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Iraq's claim to Kuwait, though summarily rejected by the international community, has some shaky basis in history. Iraq had asserted its right to control Kuwait even before the Kuwaiti state won its independence from Britain in 1961.

Above all, that claim is based on the fact that, before the British dominated the region, the Ottoman Empire had declared that Kuwait was part of a province controlled from the Iraqi city of Basra. Iraq argues that the lines of control established in that Ottoman era should still apply, not borders later worked out by the British.

Iraq's claim is similar to those made over the years by other Middle East states dissatisfied with international borders largely drawn up by colonial powers decades ago. But it isn't very solid by current international standards. The al-Sabah family ousted by Iraq's invasion has ruled the area around Kuwait City for 234 years. Even before aligning themselves with the British, the family had refused to acknowledge that it was subject to Ottoman control.

Kuwait's leaders in 1899 signed an agreement giving the British responsibility for their defense and foreign affairs, and in 1913 the British concluded an agreement recognizing the autonomy of the leader of Kuwait. Kuwait's borders were established at a conference held by the British in 1923, and the country was granted its independence from Britain in 1961.

Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein "has a very uphill battle" to make the case, says Christine Helms, a Middle East scholar who has written a history of Iraq.

Still, in Iraqi eyes its claim is buttressed by longtime cultural and economic links between Kuwait and Iraq. Kuwaiti sheiks, for instance, once owned large date plantations in Iraq. And Iraq once provided nearly all of Kuwait's fresh water, until the Kuwaitis built big plants to desalinate Persian Gulf water, Ms. Helms says.

And there's a more current dispute that underlies the Iraqi move. An oil field known as the Rumaila field straddles the Iraq-Kuwait border, and Iraq claims that Kuwait had been drilling diagonally from its side of the border to tap reserves from the field lying under Iraqi territory. U.S. officials say there is reason to think the Iraqi claim may be true.
