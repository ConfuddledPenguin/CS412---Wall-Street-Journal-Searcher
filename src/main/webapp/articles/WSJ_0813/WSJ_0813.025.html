Economy: Producer Prices Declined 0.1% For Last Month --- Latest Report Suggests Fed Now Will Find It Easier To Drive Down Rates

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 08/13/90

WASHINGTON -- July's 0.1% drop in producer prices suggests that inflationary pressures were easing before the recent tumult in the Middle East pushed up oil prices. The Labor Department report will make it easier for the Federal Reserve to push down interest rates to help spur the slowing U.S. economy. Turbulence in the bond markets and jitters about inflation may have delayed a Fed move that now is widely anticipated in financial markets and among Bush administration officials.

"Absent the still undecided energy situation, producer inflation is not threatening the economy," said Ron Schreibman, vice president of the National Association of Wholesalers-Distributors. "Thus, the economic evidence favoring lower interest rates is still compelling."

Higher oil prices, of course, will push up producer prices in coming months, and some economists predict this will stop the Fed from lowering rates enough to avoid a recession.

"Perhaps the supreme irony in these figures is that without the Middle East crisis, the Federal Reserve's gradualist disinflationary strategy would have been successful," commented Gordon Richards of the National Association of Manufacturers.

Excluding food and energy, producer prices also declined 0.1% in July, the government statistics show. Some economists argue that food and energy costs are so volatile they should be ignored in trying to gauge the underlying inflation rate.

The drop in producer prices in July, the first decline since April, follows a 0.2% increase in June. It was led by a 0.5% drop in the cost of energy, the sixth consecutive monthly decline. The price of energy that producers pay had fallen steadily since January, when it soared 13.7%.

The index for consumer food prices showed no change in July, after dropping 0.4% in June. Like energy, food prices for producers have shown an overall decline since their January increase of 2.3%. The January increase in food and energy costs has been blamed on bitterly cold weather that increased energy usage and destroyed many winter crops.

The producer price index measures prices received by domestic producers. It doesn't reflect import prices or the price of services, which has been climbing much faster than prices of goods in recent months.

In the past year, producer prices have risen 3.4%.

Prices of intermediate goods, which have had some processing, were unchanged in July. Prices of crude goods were up 0.6%, but are still below year-earlier levels.
