The Mideast Conflict: Massachusetts Acts to Stop Gouging by Oil Companies

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/13/90

BOSTON -- Massachusetts, responding to rising fuel prices in the wake of Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, adopted emergency regulations to protect consumers from price-gouging in sales of gasoline and other petroleum products. The regulations, which will be in effect for the next 90 days, make it "an unfair act or practice for a petroleum related business to charge an unconscionably high price for petroleum products during any market emergency." They define gouging as a "gross disparity" in the price of petroleum products immediately before and after a market emergency that isn't "substantially attributable to additional costs imposed by suppliers." The regulations carry a civil penalty of $5,000 for each violation.

The regulations were put in place by Massachusetts Attorney General James M. Shannon under the state's Consumer Protection Act. Mr. Shannon called the rules "essential safeguards in dealing with this crisis. I am in no way prejudging the situation, but I want to put these oil companies on notice that in Massachusetts we're watching, and that we will take whatever action we can to stop price-gouging."

He added that officials from the U.S. Justice Department and the Federal Trade Commission have agreed to meet with him and other attorneys general on Aug. 20 in Washington to discuss the issue.
