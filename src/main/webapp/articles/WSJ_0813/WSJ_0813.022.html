Securities Trading Arm of Fuji Bank Bucks Industry Trend, Hires More Staff

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 08/13/90

NEW YORK -- Running counter to the industry trend, Fuji Securities Inc., the U.S. government securities trading arm of Japan's Fuji Bank Ltd., is adding staff. The firm said it is hiring 33 people from the defunct Westpac Pollack Government Securities Inc., the primary dealer subsidiary of Australia's Westpac Banking Corp. Westpac closed the unit in June, citing lack of profitability.

Fuji's move is a surprising one in a market devastated by losses and littered with dropouts. Primary dealers are an elite group of 41 firms that deal directly with the Federal Reserve Bank of New York when it buys and sells U.S. government securities as part of its activities to influence U.S. monetary policy. Once, a dealership was one of the most coveted franchises in international finance.

But lately many firms have cut staff and, so far this year, five financial institutions have given up or lost their primary-dealer status. The latest are Bank of New York and Midland Bank PLC, both of which abandoned the business within the past month. Dealers' earnings are being squeezed by low trading volume and -- except for the last few days following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait -- reduced volatility, which traders need to enhance profits.

But mostly, too many firms chasing too few customers has reduced profit margins and left many firms with bloated overhead costs.

Nonetheless, Fuji is gambling that with the 33 new hires "we should be able to get a large part of {Westpac Pollack's} business with 20% of their people and 10% of their cost," said Mitchell Shivers, president and chief executive officer of Fuji Securities, which is based in Chicago. "We didn't have a strategy to rapidly increase our New York operation, but we didn't want to turn down an opportunity to hire very good talent." Westpac Pollack had about 160 employees.

The former Westpac people will remain in New York and be merged with Fuji's small office here. Fuji executives said there were no plans to cut any of the firm's current staff of 107.

Last December, Fuji acquired 100% of the primary dealership that had previously been controlled by Britain's Kleinwort Benson Group PLC.

People familiar with the government securities market said that Fuji currently has a little over a 1.5% share of the primary dealer market and that before closing, Westpac Pollack had roughly 2% or a bit more. They also said while Fuji currently was breaking even, the added Westpac staff could increase both Fuji's profits and market share. They said that Westpac Pollack was regarded as a successful trading operation, but that its revenue was chewed up by very high overhead costs.

Mr. Shivers said that the former Westpac people give Fuji a new or strengthened ability to trade Treasury bills, U.S. federal agency securities and odd-lot orders. The latter involve amounts less than the $1 million and above round-lot sizes normally traded.

Moreover, Mr. Shivers said: "We consider ourselves to be a low-cost producer," something a growing number of executives consider a key to surviving in the U.S. Treasury market.
