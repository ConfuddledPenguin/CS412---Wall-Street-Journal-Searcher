Chrysler Faces $7.6 Million Fine For Mail Fraud

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 08/13/90

DETROIT -- Chrysler Corp. was sentenced to pay $7.6 million in criminal fines for mail fraud in connection with the sale of approximately 30 previously wrecked vehicles as new automobiles. The $7.6 million in criminal fines is another embarrassing reminder of the 1987 scandal in which the auto maker acknowledged that it sold as new cars that factory officials had driven, with the odometers disconnected.

The latest judgment comes less than two months after the company was hit with a $1 million penalty in a Missouri court for selling a Missouri couple a "new" Chrysler New Yorker that had been wrecked during a factory official's test drive.

Chrysler said it is considering appeals of both penalties. The auto maker already has sent $500 apiece to 27,500 customers as part of the settlement of a class-action lawsuit stemming from the same issue, said Charles Newman, a Chrysler lawyer.

Chrysler pleaded no contest in December 1987 to charges of selling the wrecked vehicles, as part of a larger case brought by federal authorities in St. Louis. Federal officials charged that Chrysler employees disconnected the odometers on approximately 60,000 new cars that were then driven by Chrysler executives. The cars were later sold as new, including some that had been damaged and repaired, federal officials said.

The mail fraud charges stemmed from the fact that titles which described the vehicles as new were sent to consumers through the mail, the Postal Service said in a statement.

Shortly after the charges came to light in 1987, Chrysler Chairman Lee A. Iacocca said disconnecting odomoters to conduct test drives "went beyond dumb and reached all the way to stupid." Mr. Iacocca's candor, and his offers to extend warranties on affected vehicles, largely defused the issue at the time. Still, the court actions this year are an unwelcome echo of the episode at a time when Chrysler is trying to break out of a sales and earnings slump.

"Certainly the sooner we can get this behind us, the happier we can be," a Chrysler spokesman said Friday.
