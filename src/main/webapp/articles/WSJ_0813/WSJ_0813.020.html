UPS, Teamsters To Continue Talks Toward Accord

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 08/13/90

WASHINGTON -- United Parcel Service of America Inc. and the Teamsters union have agreed to keep negotiating on a new labor contract even if members reject the company's latest offer, the package-delivery service said. Results of the vote by the 140,000 members who work for UPS are expected today.

A UPS spokesman said both sides acceded to a request by the Federal Mediation and Conciliation Service to continue bargaining over a new three-year contract.

No union spokesman who would discuss the issue could be reached. Teamsters for a Democratic Union, a dissident group based in Detroit, said one local had received two messages from the union's headquarters, the first denying any agreement to keep negotiating, but the second acknowledging that the union would continue the talks.

A spokesman for the mediation service said the agency does intend to ask the union and the UPS to keep bargaining, and he said the agency is "optimistic" that they will agree to do so.

The current contract expired Aug. 1. The Teamsters agreed to keep working and to extend the deadline for counting the ballots until last Friday. Any majority vote against ratifying the offer would empower the Teamsters leaders to call a strike. Both the union's leadership and the dissident group have recommended that the rank-and-file reject the UPS offer.

TDU officials say this isn't a good time a strike. The impact on UPS business would be diminished, because August is a slow month for small-parcel deliveries. The union would do better to wait until the busy October-December delivery season approaches, they say.

But union leaders and members may also be concerned that UPS, the nation's largest small-package delivery firm, might lose market share from a strike. The Postal Service is gearing up to absorb some of the UPS business if there's a strike, and some other firms have developed new distribution schemes to make up for loss of UPS service.

The TDU, in fact, has urged negotiators to return to the bargaining table. It suggested that members aren't "eager to strike" and said that "UPS Teamsters are proud of the top-notch service UPS provides."

The UPS has called its offer "final," the same term it used for an earlier offer that union negotiators spurned. The latest offer includes a wage increase of 50 cents an hour, or about 3%; a cost-of-living raise capped at 20 cents an hour, in the second and third years of the contract; signing bonuses; a lower-wage tier for new employees, and an increase of 35 cents an hour in the employer contribution to the health and welfare fund and the pension fund.
