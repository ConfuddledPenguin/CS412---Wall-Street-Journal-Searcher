The Mideast Conflict: Iraq, Kuwait Boosted Crude Sales to U.S. Before Restrictions

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/13/90

NEW YORK -- Thirty supertankers, each carrying one million to 2.5 million barrels of Iraqi and Kuwaiti crude oil, will arrive in the U.S. over the next week, suggesting that Iraq and Kuwait had sold substantially more crude than previously estimated to the U.S. before an embargo was imposed Aug. 2. The oil was loaded and shipped before the embargo, and therefore can't be turned away from their destinations at ports in the U.S. Gulf of Mexico. In addition, U.S. Customs officials in the region said Friday that many more ships are expected to follow close behind throughout the month.

The number of ships suggests that Iraq and Kuwait had sold substantially more crude oil for delivery to the U.S. in August and September than the approximately 800,000 barrels a day of imports that the U.S. Energy Department estimates the two Persian Gulf producers averaged from January to May 1989.

Assuming the ships are of a median size of 1.75 million barrels and averaging the volumes over 30 days, imports of Iraqi and Kuwaiti crude would total together about 1.75 million barrels a day over that period.

It isn't clear, however, whether buyers were expecting such elevated volumes to continue or whether those countries simply made substantial sales in advance of the last Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries meeting, when the group agreed to cut its output to a new ceiling of 22.5 million barrels a day.
