  • Date: 08/13/90

RISING OIL PRICES will cut the nation's already sluggish economic growth by half but won't cause a recession, Treasury Secretary Brady said. He again called on the Fed to ease credit, and said those in Congress who view the current problems as reasons to put off cutting the budget deficit are "dead wrong."

The likelihood of a rebound in corporate profits in the second half all but evaporated with Iraq's invasion of Kuwait. The oil crisis makes it more likely that profits will continue to decline until sometime in 1991.

---

Producer prices fell 0.1% in July, suggesting that inflationary pressures were easing before the recent jump in oil prices. The report will make it easier for the Fed to push down interest rates, although higher oil prices might prevent it from lowering rates enough to avoid a recession.

---

Thousands of asbestos cases could be dealt with under an unusual plan approved by federal judges across the U.S. The move is intended to pressure the defendants to either agree to a consolidated nationwide settlement or go to trial in a class-action suit.

---

Robert Campeau was removed as chief executive of Campeau Corp. and replaced by representatives of the troubled firm's major creditors.

---

Tokyo stock prices sank Monday morning on heavy selling by arbitragers. At midday, the Nikkei index was down 763.12 points, or 2.8%.

---

Chase Manhattan plans to offer a severance program to several thousand U.S. employees as part of its effort to trim costs by $300 million.

---

Pic 'N' Save deflected a proxy fight by agreeing to name three people to its board, widen a buy-back and drop anti-takeover measures.

---

Donald Trump intends to hire First Boston Corp. to organize a restructuring of $1.3 billion in casino bonds, according to several individuals.

---

GM signaled that it plans broad price increases on most of its 1991 cars and trucks, but will continue to offer discounts if buyers balk.

---

First Executive Corp. said Deloitte & Touche has decided not to continue as its auditor. The insurance firm earlier this year reported an $859 million write-down of its junk-bond portfolio.

---

Chrysler was ordered to pay $7.6 million for mail fraud in connection with the sale of about 30 previously wrecked cars as new vehicles.

---

UPS agreed with the Teamsters union to keep negotiating on a new labor pact even if employees reject the delivery-service firm's latest offer.

---

Fuji Securities is adding personnel to its U.S. government securities trading operation, a surprise in an industry that has been devastated by losses.

---

Homestead Financial posted a surprising $30.2 million second-quarter loss, reflecting loss reserves tied to the California real estate market.

---

Southmark emerged from Chapter 11 reorganization and began distributions to creditors and shareholders.
