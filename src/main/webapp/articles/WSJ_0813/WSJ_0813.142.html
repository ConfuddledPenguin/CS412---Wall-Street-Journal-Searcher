International: Japan's Trade Surplus Falls 10.3%, But Is Expected to Widen Again

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 08/13/90

TOKYO -- Despite a 10.3% decrease in Japan's trade surplus during July, economists continued to say that the imbalance is likely to widen later this year. The Finance Ministry said Friday that the surplus fell to $5.38 billion in July from $6 billion a year earlier, as declines in the surpluses with the U.S. and the European Community offset a 43% rise in the trade imbalance with other Asian nations.

Even though the overall surplus narrowed, exports -- measured on a customs-cleared basis -- rose for the second consecutive month following eight consecutive monthly declines. They gained 1.8% from a year earlier, to $23.89 billion. And although imports rose 6%, to $18.51 billion, economists said the growth in imports appears to be tailing off.

"Exports are going up and import growth is weakening," said Yoichi Tazawa, an economist at Nomura Research Institute.

Officials at the Finance Ministry, however, indicated they didn't share that view. While one-time factors contributed to a jump in the June surplus, the July figures suggest continued improvement in the balance, they said.

The officials also noted that manufactured goods accounted for 54.3% of total imports in July -- the highest level since officials began following that measure several years ago. Imports of manufactured goods are considered significant because critics of Japanese trade practices assert that the country tends to discourage imports with a high value-added content.

The July figures, of course, didn't reflect the surge in oil prices that followed Iraq's invasion of Kuwait. Should oil prices remain high, Japan's import bill would rise. Nevertheless, at their current levels, it is unlikely that higher oil prices will prevent Japan's overall surplus from rising in coming months, economists said.

The yen's weakening during the past year has increased the competitiveness of Japanese exports by making them less expensive in terms of other currencies. At the same time, it has made imports more costly. And massive investment by Japanese corporations in plant and equipment has further strengthened their competitive position.

S.G. Warburg Securities (Japan) Inc. expects the surplus to grow in the second half. But chief economist Jesper Koll said the firm has trimmed its forecast for the overall 1990 surplus to $68 billion from $70 billion and has lowered its 1991 projection to $70.3 billion from $80 billion -- assuming that oil prices remain between $22 and $25 a barrel. (Oil for September delivery was quoted at $26.23 a barrel in New York on Friday.) Japan's trade surplus was $64.32 billion in 1989 and $77.56 billion in 1988.

In July, the politically sensitive surplus with the U.S. declined 20.5% from a year earlier, to $3.07 billion from $3.86 billion. Imports from the U.S. rose 5%, to $4.45 billion. Exports to the U.S. fell 7.2%, to $7.51 billion.

The surplus with the European Community fell 29.9%, to $1.15 billion from $1.64 billion. A nearly five-fold increase in the value of imported paintings helped account for a 36.4% rise in imports from the EC, to $3.19 billion. Exports to the EC rose 9.1%, to $4.34 billion.

Japan's surplus with the rest of Asia, excluding China, surged for the second consecutive month, jumping 43%, to $2.49 billion from $1.74 billion. Exports to the rest of Asia rose 7.3%, to $7.53 billion. Imports from the rest of Asia declined 4.5%, to $5.04 billion.

After seasonal adjustment, Japan's overall trade surplus was $4.76 billion in July, down 30.9% from June.
