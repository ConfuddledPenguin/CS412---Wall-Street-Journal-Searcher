World Markets: Tokyo Shares Slip to 1990 Low on Mideast Fears; London Prices Also Decline in Cautious Trading

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 08/13/90

Tokyo stocks retreated to a new 1990 low, with waves of arbitrage selling accelerating the slide Friday afternoon as investors held off buying ahead of a possible weekend worsening of the Persian Gulf crisis. London shares finished lower in subdued dealings as events in the Middle East overshadowed any genuine interest in the market.

Tokyo's Nikkei index of 225 issues lost 286.18 to finish at 27329.55. The index plunged 893.41 points Thursday. Volume on the first section was estimated at 320 million shares, down from 409.4 million Thursday. Losers outnumbered gainers 616-303, with 169 unchanged.

At the end of the morning session in Tokyo Monday, the Nikkei index had tumbled 763.12 points, or 2.8%, to 26566.43 on selling by arbitragers.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which lost 31.61 Thursday, was down 11.53, or 0.56%, at 2056.88 Friday. The Second Section Index, which gained 22.25 Thursday, was down 0.83, or 0.02%, to close at 4112.11. Volume in the second section was estimated at 11 million shares, down from 14.8 million.

"There was no bad news for the market, with both currency and bond markets stable and no significant developments in the Middle East," said a trader at James Capel Pacific.

"Investors who are nervous about the Iraqi issue have already sold off their holdings," the trader said. "But investors can't buy, due to fears that something may happen over the weekend."

Petroleum shares ended mixed, with Teikoku Oil down 110 yen at 1,610 yen ($10.75) a share, but Mitsubishi Oil edged up 10 to 1,280.

High-priced technology shares ended generally lower. Sony lost 120 to 7,810, while TDK fell 160 to 6,450.

Isetan advanced 140 to 4,420, prompting speculation that the buying may be linked to a merger and acquisition attempt, traders said.

Paper and pulp makers were sold, with Jujo Paper down 21 to 730 and Oji Paper falling 20 to 960.

Shionogi lost 90 to 1,210 on rumors about side effects from the company's blood pressure-reducing drugs, traders said.

Plant engineering companies fell sharply, as their activities in Iraq and Kuwait have been frozen by Japan's economic sanctions against Iraq. Chiyoda lost 150 to 1,660.

In London, investors were said to be flattening out positions ahead of the weekend in case the Middle East became more volatile before today. Most participants fled to the sidelines and were unwilling to open fresh positions, commentators said.

In addition, a murky start on Wall Street and a drop during the London afternoon caused players to take a more cautious footing, dealers said. At the close of the London session, the Dow Jones Industrial Average was off 27.23 points at 2731.68.

There was also a dearth of major corporate or economic news, giving players little incentive to adjust current short-term positions, traders said. And some strong falls in international stocks, which were nervous in the wake of the Middle East turmoil, also sent the market lower late in the session.

At the closing bell, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index was down 11.1 at 2233.8, the day's low and well below its opening peak of 2249.2.

The FT 30-share industrial index was down 9.8 at 1743.0. Volume was a meager 312.4 million shares, compared with 429.2 million issues a day earlier.

Among the few corporate features, health and household products group Glaxo Holdings fell 43 pence to 761 pence ($16.08) a share. Forecasts for the company's 1990 pretax profit were downgraded to #1.05 billion by Schroder Securities Ltd. from a previous estimate of #1.15 billion.

The downgrading came on worries that the strength of the pound against the dollar could affect the company's earnings. In addition, Glaxo's American Depositary Receipts were sold heavily on Wall Street overnight, dealers said.

Carlton Communication had another steep fall, shedding 41 to 319. Dealers said the stock dropped as one large sell order had difficulty being placed.

ICI, Britain's largest chemical concern, shed 25 to 941 as market-makers tried to get rid of stock picked up since the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait Aug. 2, dealers said. They said investors are shying away from international stocks because of uncertainty over world economies in the wake of higher crude-oil prices.

Anglo-Dutch health and household products group Unilever fell 9 to 661. The company said second-quarter pretax profit rose 4.8% to #477 million, although analysts suggested that the 1989 purchases of Faberge/Elizabeth Arden and Calvin Klein had hindered results because of large interest payments.

Among other oil stocks, British Petroleum rose 2 to 360, and Ultramar retreated 5 to 345.

Ultramar said it contracted to buy three one million-barrel capacity oil tankers for $212 million from Samsung Shipbuilding & Heavy Industries Co. of South Korea.

Ultramar said the ships will secure Ultramar's anticipated long-term crude oil transportation requirements to its Quebec refinery on the St. Lawrence River in eastern Canada "at an advantageous cost."

Brewers Whitbread and Allied-Lyons both dropped sharply, with Whitbread falling 11 to 411 and Allied-Lyons losing 17 to 477. Whitbread said it agreed to sell its 50% stake in a joint venture to partner Allied-Lyons. The price wasn't disclosed.

Under the agreement, Whitbread will sell its half of European Cellars (Holdings) Ltd., a British shipper and wholesaler of wines, to the wines and spirits division of Allied-Lyons.

Elsewhere, share prices closed lower in Frankfurt, Amsterdam and Madrid, were unchanged in Paris and Zurich and ended mixed in Brussels, Milan and Stockholm. South African gold stocks were higher.

In Asian and Pacific markets, shares closed lower in Tokyo, Singapore, Hong Kong, Wellington, Taipei and Manila and higher in Seoul and Sydney.
