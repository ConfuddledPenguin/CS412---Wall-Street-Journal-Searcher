UNMILITARY JAPAN

Few young men want to join the Japanese military, and those who are in it complain of low pay, bad working conditions and little public recognition.

Those serving in what are officially called "the Self-Defense Forces" labor under a handicap unique among the world's major countries. The constitution, adopted a year after Japan's defeat in World War II, renounces war.

Thus, Japanese soldiers haven't been involved in combat since 1945. Overseas military involvement is banned, the military adopts a very low profile in public life and the public remains acutely sensitive to any hint of militarism.

"We are facing difficulties in recruiting young people," said Rear Adm. Yasuaki Imaizumi at the Etajima First Service Naval School in Kure on Japan's Inland Sea. A senior naval officer said more than half of his 50 classmates in aviation school now work for commercial airlines. "I can't blame them. As far as pay is concerned, there is no way I can compete with them," he said.

LOANS TO VENEZUELA

Japanese banks won't make new loans to Venezuela because they are dissatisfied with the progress of its economic restructuring, international banking sources told a Japanese news agency. The decision is based on a perception that the Venezuelan economy has shown little improvement despite higher oil prices, the sources said. Venezuela's medium-term to long-term foreign debts total about $20 billion, of which 20% is owed to 30 Japanese banks.

NISSAN'S CFC PHASE-OUT

Nissan Motor Co. said it will completely phase out chlorofluorocarbon gas from air conditioners on its new cars by the end of 1995. In addition, the company will install CFC recycling devices at its service centers and used-car dealerships world-wide by March 1992. Two other major auto makers, Toyota Motor Corp. and Honda Motor Co., have announced similar plans to eliminate the gas. CFC gas is suspected of damaging the atmosphere's ozone layer, which acts as a shield against the sun's harmful rays.

LUFTHANSA'S BERLIN PLANS

Lufthansa said it applied to serve West Berlin and East Berlin as of the start of its winter flight plan on Oct. 28. The carrier, which is 51%-owned by the West German government, hasn't been allowed to make scheduled flights to Berlin since the end of World War II. Only airlines of the four victorious World War II powers have been allowed to fly to Berlin.

CHINA-HONG KONG RAILROAD

China plans to construct a 1,465-mile railroad line between Beijing and Hong Kong in preparation for the scheduled return of the British crown colony to China in 1997, the China Daily reported. The Ministry of Railways estimated the total construction cost at the equivalent of $3.3 billion, of which 20% to 25% will be borne by the provinces and municipalities along the projected route. The railroad will be built between the present Beijing-Shanghai and Beijing-Guangzhou lines, linking 10 provinces and municipalities.

ARGENTINE INFLATION

Argentine President Carlos Menem threatened to open the door to imported goods to cut the nation's inflation rate. His comments were in response to reports that prices have risen by 10% so far this month. The rise in the nation's consumer prices had slowed steadily in the past few months, to a monthly rate of 10.8% in July from 95.5% in March, but now shows signs of accelerating.

MEXICAN DRUG ABUSE

Although narcotics abuse hasn't reached epidemic proportions in Mexico, such cheap inhalants as glue, paint thinner and other industrial solvents are becoming popular among the nation's poor, and fears are growing that Mexico is fertile ground for drug addiction.

One reason is the availability of a wide variety of illegal drugs. Marijuana and opium poppies are grown in Mexico, and the country is a transit point for cocaine and heroin bound for the U.S. from South America.

The latest National Survey of Addictions, conducted in 1988 and released this year, found that 4.3% of Mexicans, more than half of them under age 34, had experimented with drugs at least once. And the federal attorney general's office said that of an estimated 500,000-plus drug addicts in Mexico, 300,000 use inhalants, 200,000 use marijuana, 30,000 use cocaine and the rest use heroin or opium.

A half pint of paint thinner costs the equivalent of seven cents, and a tube of glue $1.07. Street prices for marijuana and cocaine, respectively, are about $1.80 a cigarette and $35 a gram.
