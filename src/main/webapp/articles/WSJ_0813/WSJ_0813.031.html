Letters to the Editor: Who's That Stranger in the Nursery?

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/13/90

Regarding your July 6 page-one article "Parental Concern: As Nanny Agencies Proliferate, So Do Gripes About Service": First, personally check all references. Second, personally interview the candidates. Third, give some time to developing the working relationship.

I bet the parents do at least as much when hiring someone for the steno pool at the office. What are they thinking of, to depend so utterly on the word of any agency as to the person who will be in their home caring for their precious children?

Barbara Goering

Chicago

---

It is reprehensible that some agencies do not screen as thoroughly as they claim. Agencies must follow through or be prepared to refund fees. But parents must assume some responsibility as well. I am distressed when a client to whom I have referred a nanny declines the opportunity to double-check my reference work on the applicant. What might she learn about that candidate's specific suitability to her own situation that I, not being part of her family, could not have known to ask?

The question that haunts this industry is, how well can any screening process predict the likelihood of a particular nanny's success on the job? Written references double-checked by telephone, driver's license and criminal checks, a personal letter from the applicant, medical history, numerous lengthy interviews and reports, situational questions relating directly to the candidate's working style -- a comprehensive application is at least 10 to 15 pages long. Some agencies do psychological tests, too, but their success rates are no higher than those of careful agencies that do not test.

Nannying is hard work. Like any other responsible job it deserves medical insurance, paid holidays and vacations, as well as overtime pay for extra hours. It needs thoughtful and frequent, open communications with parents, better pay and, yes, training. Raising someone else's kids these days is no piece of cake. Nannying, if it is to be improved, needs recognition as a challenging, rewarding, long-term career option for qualified, loving, intelligent women of various ages and for an occasional young man as well.

But please let's stop looking for Mary Poppins. She was a creation of the Victorian imagination, which liked to pretend that it did not need to know what went on in the nursery. Today's involved, conscientious parent needs today's nanny, a real person.

Mary Clurman

Apple Pie

(Nanny Placement Agency)

Montclair, N.J.

---

As owner of one of the oldest agencies in the country, I am all too familiar with the problems that arise when trying to match families with in-home child-care providers. There will always be unsuccessful placements due to homesickness, personality clashes, poor communication and differing expectations, but it doesn't mitigate the need for the best screening and recruiting services possible for the client.

In 1985, I and a handful of other nannies, placement-agency owners and educators founded the International Nanny Association to educate the public about hiring in-home child care. Since then, we have established standards for the industry and codes of conduct for nannies and agencies. We have also printed definitions of "au pair," mother's helpers, nannies and governesses.

My advice to the client: Take part in the screening process. My agency always recommends to the client that he or she follow up our screening process with calls to the nanny's references, and most families don't do it.

Marla Sanders

Capable Care Agency

Washington
