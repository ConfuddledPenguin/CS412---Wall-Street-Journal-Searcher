Brady Sees Rise In Oil Prices Cutting Growth --- He Says Rate of Expansion Will Decline to 0.75%, But Rules Out Recession

  • Author: Alan Murray and David Wessel
  • Date: 08/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady said rising oil prices resulting from the Middle East crisis will cut already sluggish economic growth in the U.S. by half but won't cause a recession. Mr. Brady again called on the Federal Reserve to ease credit and "tip the scales toward growth." But he said those in Congress who view the Iraqi invasion, higher oil prices and a weak economy as reasons to put off cutting the budget deficit are "dead wrong."

The Treasury secretary, in an interview Friday, said that if oil prices stay near their current levels, that will reduce the nation's growth to an annual rate of 0.75% -- down from the 1.5% pace in the first half of the year.

But Mr. Brady continued to argue that a recession isn't in the works. "Even if growth dips under 1%," he said, "we'll still be growing."

He blamed the slower growth on the budget deficit, which he said has "held interest rates at a high level," and on the savings and loan fiasco, which he said fueled overbuilding in the 1980s.

Mr. Brady also said a credit crunch "has got something to do with it." But he called it a "chicken and egg" problem. "Part of the credit crunch is banks are being more careful, especially on real estate loans, and part is demand for loans is down," he said.

Repeating his call for the Federal Reserve to lower interest rates, Mr. Brady said the country should "avoid an over-single-minded emphasis on inflation." He noted the Fed "is an independent institution," but said its independence "shouldn't prevent us from speaking out."

Mr. Brady also said the Middle East events and the slowing economy make it "all the more important to move apace" on budget cuts. He said he anticipates the budget negotiations, which reconvene after Labor Day, will produce an agreement before the November elections.

Because of the weakness in the economy and the likely difficulty in agreeing on cuts in defense spending, some congressional budget negotiators are reconsidering their goal of shrinking the deficit by $50 billion in the fiscal year that begins Oct. 1 and by $500 billion over the next five years.

Although Mr. Brady reiterated his support for those objectives, he said, "It is not so critically important to have exactly $50 billion in the first year as long as we have $500 {billion} over five years." He emphasized that the administration will insist on a growth-oriented package -- a reference to the administration's proposed cut in capital-gains taxes -- and on alterations to the budget process to assure that the deficit reductions projected for future years are realized.

Mr. Brady acknowledged that an energy tax, an idea that had gained support from negotiators from both parties, "doesn't make a whole lot of sense" if the recent rise in oil prices persists. If oil prices come down, however, such a tax might be attractive, he said.

Mr. Brady tried to put the onus on the Democrats for the slow pace of the budget talks, which began in mid-May and have accomplished little. He said the president took a "large political risk" in convening the talks and that the Republicans were prepared to put forward a package of spending cuts and tax increases last month. Democrats weren't, however. "You can't negotiate with yourself," he said.

On other topics, Mr. Brady said:

-- Recent events in the turbulent stock market demonstrate the success of mechanisms installed to prevent a market crash -- mechanisms such as the so-called circuit breakers that stop trading in stock and futures markets when prices fall by a set amount. "The circuit breakers . . . worked and they worked well," he said. Mr. Brady, who chaired a presidential commission that recommended such changes after the 1987 stock-market crash, added that some who opposed the changes have written him recently to admit they were wrong.

-- A securities-transfer tax, one of many options examined as a way to reduce the deficit, has good points and bad, and Mr. Brady declined to take a position on such a tax. "There's a lot of economic theory on both sides of that," he said.

-- President Bush will move to fill a vacancy on the Fed's seven-member board of governors "very quickly" after Congress returns in September. Manuel Johnson, the Fed's vice chairman, announced his resignation two months ago.
