Trump to Hire First Boston to Organize $1.3 Billion Casino Bond Restructuring

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 08/13/90

This will be a busy week for Donald J. Trump, who it now appears will spend the rest of 1990 negotiating with bankers, contractors and bondholders to keep his shaky empire afloat. Today, he plans to hire First Boston Corp. to organize a restructuring of $1.3 billion in casino bonds, the developer has told several individuals.

Tomorrow, he is scheduled to sign the first closing documents on a $65 million loan that gives his bankers second mortgages on his most valuable properties, and gives him three to five years to pay interest on nearly half of his $2 billion in bank debt.

He is also soon expected to name a chief financial officer to oversee his operations. That job has already been offered to Stephen F. Bollenbach, Promus Cos. chief financial officer.

"But I will still be the top guy, don't forget that," Mr. Trump said in a brief telephone interview.

Meanwhile, Mr. Trump still hasn't come to an agreement with contractors that helped build the Taj Mahal and say that they're owed about $80 million. The contractors have threatened to force Mr. Trump into bankruptcy proceedings if an agreement isn't reached shortly.

But Mr. Trump's biggest challenge is expected to be persuading his bondholders to forgo interest payments in exchange for equity in his casinos. Unlike the banks, the bondholders' loans are fully secured by first mortgages on the properties, which means if Mr. Trump fails to make interest payments, they could end up owning the casinos.

First Boston's task will be to prepare an acceptable proposal to bondholders, many of whom have already informally organized committees to challenge Mr. Trump. A conference call for bondholders organized by First Executive Life, an insurance company that owns Trump casino bonds, is scheduled for today, says a participant.

First Boston apparently wasn't Mr. Trump's first choice. This past week, Merrill Lynch & Co. abruptly stopped working on the restructuring, which put pressure on Mr. Trump to come up with a new investment adviser quickly.

Mr. Trump must have a restructuring plan approved by Nov. 15, which is when he is scheduled to make a $47 million interest payment to holders of junk bonds secured by the Taj Mahal casino. The property isn't believed to be making enough money to meet its debt service and had a $14.7 million pretax operating loss in the second quarter.

Mr. Trump already has a comfortable working relationship with several First Boston officials. Casino analyst Daniel Lee, a former Drexel Burnham Lambert analyst who has been a vocal booster of Mr. Trump's Taj Mahal casino, has in the past discussed working for Mr. Trump, according to several individuals familiar with the discussions. William Jacobs, a First Boston bankruptcy specialist, got to know Mr. Trump when Mr. Jacobs represented holders of Resorts International bonds in negotiations with Mr. Trump last year. Mr. Jacobs was working for Prudential-Bache Securities.

Both Mr. Lee and Mr. Jacobs met with Trump officials about a bond restructuring last week, people familiar with the situation said. Mr. Lee did not return phone calls. Mr. Jacobs declined to comment.

People familiar with Mr. Trump's bank deal say it makes allowances for his Atlantic City, N.J., casinos' poor showing. One person who has read the thousands of pages of documents cementing the bank arrangement said that a default on the bank loans wouldn't necessarily be triggered if Mr. Trump defaults on payments due his bondholders.

However, this person added, should Mr. Trump be forced to give up any of his casinos it would still imperil the $65 million bank deal because that arrangement calls for banks to hold a second mortgage on the Trump Plaza casino.
