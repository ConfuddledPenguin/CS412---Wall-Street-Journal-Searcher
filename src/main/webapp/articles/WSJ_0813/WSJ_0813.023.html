Chase to Offer Severance to Employees, Take $30 Million 3rd-Quarter Charge

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 08/13/90

NEW YORK -- Chase Manhattan Corp. plans to offer severance to "a few thousand" U.S. employees as part of its plan to cut $300 million of costs. The banking company said employees with at least 10 years' time would be eligible, and would start with two weeks' pay for each year of service. Chase wouldn't specify the number of employees eligible, but expects to report about a $30 million third-quarter charge for related costs of the voluntary program. At the same time, a spokesman said, Chase estimates it will save $10 million in the fourth quarter and "commensurate" amounts later.

The charge and savings depend on the number of workers who choose the severance, the spokesman said, adding that Chase reserves the right to accept or reject severance applicants.

Analysts had expected the bank to take less than $100 million of charges for the cost-cutting effort. The spokesman, calling the severance plan "a small part" of the overall cost cuts, confirmed that the bank plans further steps "that will result in additional charges but additional savings" as well.

Chase Manhattan's chairman-elect, Thomas G. Labrecque, and president-elect, Arthur F. Ryan, are determined to make the major cost cuts and boost profitability as Chase, like other major banks, has been hammered by bad commercial real estate loans.

At June 30, past-due real estate loans plus foreclosed properties rose 52% to $1.75 billion, from $1.15 billion at March 31. In the first half, Chase's net income sank 64% from a year earlier to $96 million, or 44 cents a share, as its provision for possible losses on bad loans grew 50% to $450 million.

For 1989's third quarter, Chase reported a net loss of $1.11 billion, or $12.45 a share, after $1.26 billion of special provisions, including a special addition to the bank's reserve for possible losses on loans to countries that were refinancing their debts.

Under the severance plan, those eligible employees with 10 to 19 years' experience at Chase would get two weeks' pay for each year worked. Those with 20 or more years would get three weeks' pay per year, to a maximum of 104 weeks.

Chase, which employs 40,000 world-wide, isn't offering the severance plan to, among others, non-U.S. workers, part-time employees or certain senior officers. But for the eligible employees "who've been here longer term," the plan "increases their benefits and flexibility in making career decisions," the spokesman said.

The eligible employees aren't concentrated in any particular departments or divisions, Chase's spokesman said.

Part of Chase's plan to boost profitability is possible sales of assets. "I have nothing to report on that," the spokesman said, though analysts expect the bank to sell certain buildings overseas.

Separately, Fitch Investors Service Inc. pared its ratings on $8 billion of Chase Manhattan's securities, including a cut in the senior debt rating to triple-B-plus from single-A-plus. Standard & Poor's Corp. and Moody's Investors Service Inc. made similar reductions in June.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Chase shares fell 50 cents to $18.625.
