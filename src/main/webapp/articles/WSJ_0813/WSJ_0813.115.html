California Agency Passes Air-Pollution Standards

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/13/90

California's air pollution control agency said it passed additional emission-control standards that will cut by 80% emissions of smog-forming hydrocarbons generated by evaporating gasoline in new cars and trucks. "Evaporating gasoline can account for as much as one third of the pollution generated by a new car," said board chairwoman Jananne Sharpless.

The California Air Resources Board said that the about 1.4 million new cars and trucks made in the state each year will have to be fitted with larger charcoal-filled canisters that absorb evaporation from fuel lines and gas tanks and route them to the engine, where they are burned. The filters and other changes required, which the state said were opposed by various auto makers, will add $18 to the cost of each vehicle. The requirement will be phased in between 1995 and 1998.

In 1989, California, which has the nation's strictest tailpipe standards, required equipment that would reduce by 41% the emission of hydrocarbons for cars made in 1993 and after.
