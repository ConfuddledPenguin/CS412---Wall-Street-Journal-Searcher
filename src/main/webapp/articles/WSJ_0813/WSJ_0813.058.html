REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Radio for A New Europe

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/13/90

For decades Albania was viewed as a hermit country, its people completely cut off from news of the West and content with Communist poverty. The recent exodus of 5,000 refugees to the West has shattered that myth. It turns out Albanians are well informed about freedom, in large part from listening to U.S.-sponsored broadcasts such as the Voice of America. Amazingly, many of the refugees speak English, which they had painstakingly taught themselves by listening to the VOA. The knowledge that the Iron Curtain had fallen gave them the courage to try fleeing from the last Stalinist regime in Europe.

For 40 years, the VOA along with Radio Free Europe and Radio Liberty (which broadcasts to the Soviet Union) have beamed objective news into nations lacking a free press. They played a significant role in the collapse of communism in Eastern Europe. Lech Walesa has said that without Radio Free Europe's Polish broadcasts, Solidarity would have ceased to exist.

Now that communism is becoming a spent historical force, many in Congress are calling for a swift end to U.S. funding of Radio Free Europe and a curtailment of VOA's budget. They fail to recognize that having helped win the Cold War, U.S. radio stations are greatly needed to secure the peace in Eastern Europe. In addition, people in oppressed regions of Asia and Africa still desperately need the news and culture they provide.

Radio Free Europe can still perform a vital role in the nations of Eastern Europe, which may have freedom of speech but still lack broadbased broadcasting outlets independent of the state. RFE's programming, for instance, now includes tutorials on building democratic institutions, the role of a free press and creating free markets.

The new democratic leaders of Eastern Europe welcome RFE's continued competition. In May, Czechoslovakia's President Vaclav Havel said: "I believe the freedom today enjoyed by our mass media does not at all mean that RFE has lost its meaning." He added, "There are numberless spheres where it can continue to serve our nations well at this time -- better than our own mass media."

It is likely that in five or 10 years Radio Free Europe will have outlived its usefulness in countries such as Czechoslovakia. At that point, Steve Forbes, head of the Board for International Broadcasting, suggests that certain RFE services be privatized and sold to local interests. Until then, continued funding of Radio Free Europe's mission is one of the best ways the U.S. can help ensure a prosperous and stable Europe.

This fall a bipartisan commission will evaluate the work of both Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty and the VOA, and make recommendations to Congress on their future. Despite budget pressures, the $360 million annual investment in U.S.-funded radio networks should be maintained. The dividends from their work will continue to pour in for years.
