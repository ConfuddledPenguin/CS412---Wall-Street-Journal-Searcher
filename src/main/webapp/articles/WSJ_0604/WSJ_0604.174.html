Synchrotron Facility Construction Begins By Energy Agency

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/04/90

ARGONNE, Ill. -- The Department of Energy's Argonne National Laboratory is scheduled to break ground today for the $456 million Advanced Photon Source, a device that will generate X-ray beams 10,000 times brighter than those from any existing source. The new-generation synchrotron, an aluminum circle two-thirds of a mile in length, will succeed DOE's Brookhaven National Laboratory on Long Island as the world's most powerful X-ray source.

Synchrotrons, originally developed for particle research, produce powerful X-rays as a by-product of accelerating electrons through magnetic fields. Scientists can use such rays to determine the precise positions of atoms in chemical reactions.

The Argonne device will produce X-rays with higher energies: There are "more photons, or light particles, in the beam so you get data faster," said a lab spokesman. Scientists will be able to "do experiments at APS in a few hours that would require months at Brookhaven," he added.

Experiments at the Argonne lab aren't scheduled to get under way until 1996, however, by which time a slightly less powerful machine now under construction in Europe will be operational, and another device in Japan will also be starting up.

Argonne is operated by the University of Chicago for the DOE.

The lab says the APS will be "fundamentally for basic research." The spokesman, however, stressed that it is "research that's expected to produce lots of practical results," in fields such as computer science, ceramics, chemistry and physics.

Argonne said scientists from companies including Allied-Signal Inc., Exxon Corp., International Business Machines Corp., and American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s Bell Laboratories have helped plan and design the facility, and have indicated interest in using the facility when it's completed.
