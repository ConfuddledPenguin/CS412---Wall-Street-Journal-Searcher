Workplace (A Special Report): Where We'll Be --- Gearing Up: Here's a Primer on What You'll Need And What It Will Cost to Set Up Your Own Workplace

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 06/04/90

Take a loathing for commuting and office life, combine it with the electronics revolution, and what do you have? The home-office boom. These days, an individual with a few thousand dollars' worth of equipment can imitate a fully staffed law office, accounting firm or engineering company. And the future promises even lower prices -- and more sophisticated machines.

Equipping a basic home office costs under $4,000, and even a lavish setup is under $10,000. Here's a list of some of the items that most home businesses are likely to buy today, with prices culled from recent advertisements.

Basic Needs

IBM-compatible personal computer -- $1,000

Prices have dropped sharply during most of the past six months, and the most basic machines can be had for under $500. But much of the best software today requires a hard disk instead of cheaper floppy disks. The minimum computer today should include an Intel 80286 processor, a 20-megabyte hard disk and a monochrome monitor. Prices will keep dropping 30% a year.

Letter-quality printer -- $270

The 24-pin models print characters almost as well as electric typewriters do.

Answering machine -- $70

After a telephone, the most important single item for any home business.

Facsimile machine -- $480

The most basic models don't cut the paper or dial numbers automatically, but they get the job done.

Two-line telephone -- $120

A must, unless you want to risk annoying clients with busy signals or with constant requests to hold on while you answer your call-waiting line.

Computer modem -- $100

The 2400-baud versions save lots of money when sending electronic mail.

High-Powered Office

More powerful personal computers -- $3,099

PCs with 100-megabyte hard disks will run faster and are more likely to accommodate software packages that come out in the future. Home workers say that the bigger the hard disk, the better off you are.

Hard-disk prices have been plunging lately. Color monitors don't make much difference in the business, but they make the kids' educational software look great -- and that's one of the benefits of having a home office.

Tape drive -- $380

When all your business information is on your computer, you need to back it up at least weekly. Keep the tape in the car or elsewhere out of the house.

Laser printers -- $995

They let users print out headlines, different fonts, and graphs and charts that look professionally produced. They are almost silent and print at four to six pages a minute. Once limited to print shops, they fell below $1,000 last fall.

Personal copiers -- $530

They're much less common in home offices than in corporate offices. In emergencies, a fax machine can be used to make a copy. And big jobs still require a trip to the print shop.

Facsimile machine -- $800

You might want one that includes an answering machine that switches automatically to voice or fax.

Software

Buy a simple integrated package with word processing, filing and spreadsheet built in; then add specialized software as you find a need for it, advises Russ Walter, author of "The Secret Guide to Computers." Unless you're building a relationship with a computer retailer, buy software by mail order. Join a user group for access to many useful free programs. Some of the most popular software includes:

Integrated programs -- $100-$225

Microsoft Works or Software Publishing's PFS First Choice. Symantec's Q&A is more expensive, but it combines easy but sophisticated filing with adequate word processing.

Word Processing -- $200-$300

WordPerfect is the best seller. Microsoft Word, Samna's Ami and Xyquest's Xywrite all have their fans.

Spreadsheets -- $250-$330

Lotus 1-2-3 version 2.2 is the best seller; many recent reviewers prefer Borland's Quattro Pro and Microsoft's Excel.

Accounting -- $35

Intuit's Quicken, the best-selling check-writing program, is easier than double-entry bookkeeping.

Telecommunications

Keeping a residential phone rate typically saves $5 a month or so, but it keeps you out of the Yellow Pages. Some telephone companies have special marketing plans for home-based businesses. Experts recommend at least two lines, one of which is hooked to the fax machine. Following are monthly rates:

Two lines, residential rates -- $20-$30

Call waiting -- $5

Voice mail boxes -- $6

Just becoming available. The phone company will store messages even if your line is busy, reducing the need for another line and eliminating the need for call waiting.

800 Number -- $20

Lets distant customers call you at your expense. Be careful about giving out the number because you get billed for the incoming calls.

MCI mail box -- $25 annual

Lets people send and receive computerized messages.

On the Road

Without secretaries or assistants sitting in the office, the home-based business person is even more dependent on electronic lifelines while traveling.

Cellular car phone -- $500

Pocket cellular phone -- $1,200

Portable computer -- $600-$4,000

You pay for portability and screen quality.

Portable facsimile machine -- $1,700

Remember, it uses thermal paper, which will turn black while sitting in a hot car.

Portable printer -- $360
