International -- Washington Insight: White House Is Under Pressure to Allow More Soviet Jews to Immigrate to U.S.

  • Author: Robert S. Greenberger
  • Date: 06/04/90

WASHINGTON -- Pressure is building on the Bush administration to permit more Soviet Jews to immigrate to the U.S. Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev, who said at yesterday's post-summit press conference he was being "bombarded" with criticism from Arab governments about the flow of Soviet Jews to Israel, has been urging the U.S. to accept more Russian-Jewish refugees. Arab governments are asking Washington to take more refugees so that fewer of them settle in Israel; they say that would reduce tension that has been building in the Middle East. Some U.S. Jewish and evangelical-Christian groups warn that the U.S. must be prepared to absorb more Soviet Jews if there is an outburst of violence against minorities in the Soviet Union, where ethnic and religious tensions are also on the rise.

U.S. relations with Israel, already frayed, would worsen if Washington succumbs to such pressures. Jerusalem is encouraging Jewish migration because it fulfills Israel's historic role as the Jewish homeland and because a swelling Jewish population would counter the country's more rapidly increasing Arab population. Jerusalem would fight any Washington effort to divert refugees to the U.S.

The U.S. quota on Soviet refugees is 50,000 this year; the great majority of them will be Jewish. The Bush administration has been saying it doesn't plan to increase the number. But following Saturday's Camp David session between Messrs. Bush and Gorbachev, White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said, "We continually want to take more and are trying to work on those kinds of arrangements."

Most U.S. Jews support the immigration of Soviet Jews to Israel, but wouldn't object if Washington accepts more Soviet refugees out of concern for their safety. "I don't see a groundswell to change things right now," says Rep. Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.). "But if things deteriorate in the Soviet Union, the whole situation would have to be re-examined."

Some U.S. groups worry about anti-Semitism in the Soviet Union. "Our perception is the situation in the Soviet Union is so unstable, the probability of economic collapse and social turmoil so high, we think it's in our interest to remove as many Jews as fast as possible," says Sergey Broude, a member of the executive board of the Union of Councils for Soviet Jews. Serge Duss of World Relief, an arm of the National Association of Evangelicals that represents 50,000 U.S. churches, says: "Based on Soviet history, persecution against Jews and Christians comes in peaks and valleys. The last five years under Gorbachev has been a valley."

With more than 10,000 Soviet Jews entering Israel each month, the mass migraton issue has become the major potential flashpoint in the Middle East, eclipsing even the Palestinian uprising in the West Bank and Gaza. Only a small percentage of these refugees have settled in these territories. But the anti-Arab rhetoric of right-wing Israelis and Prime Minister Yitzhak Shamir's remark earlier this year that it will take a "big" Israel to absorb so many Jews have led Arabs to believe a mass immigration would mean they would be displaced.

Last week's Arab summit in Baghdad denounced the immigration and blamed the U.S. for encouraging it. Recently, Jordan's King Hussein wrote President Bush a letter saying the U.S. had an obligation to take in more Soviet Jews. Egyptian President Hosni Mubarak also raised the issue in a telephone call with Mr. Bush.

The U.S. took in 3,694 Soviet refugees in 1987, 20,421 in 1988 and 39,553 last year. The list of those wanting to leave the U.S.S.R. far exceeds this year's U.S. quota of 50,000; a State Department official says the U.S. has given out 750,000 applications so far this year in Moscow.

After decades of urging Moscow to let Soviet Jews go, Washington says it can't afford to take more. Each refugee costs the U.S. about $7,000 in benefits and expenses. Rep. Stephen Solarz (D., N.Y.) has introduced legislation to give certain able-bodied refugees loans rather than payments. Such a plan could allow 20,000 to 40,000 more refugees to enter the U.S., a Solarz aide estimates.

The exodus of Soviet citizens is growing. Moscow is permitting emigres to leave the Soviet Union by train for East Europe, and Poland announced last week it will begin charter flights to transport 500 Soviet Jews each week to Israel.

"Will the U.S. be able to respond if the situation collapses and even more people need to get out of the country?" asks Mr. Duss of World Relief. "That's the big question."
