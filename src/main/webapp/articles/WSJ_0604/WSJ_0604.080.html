More Limits Planned By EPA on Gasoline Evaporation Rate

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/04/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency, in an effort to curb smog, plans to require further reductions in the evaporation rate of gasoline sold during the summer. The agency estimates that the changes, which won't take effect until 1992, will cost the nation's oil refiners about $464 million a year and increase retail gasoline prices about a penny a gallon. Partly offsetting this cost, the agency figures, will be about $234 million a year in benefits to consumers through increased fuel economy and reduced loss of gasoline through evaporation.

The delay is designed to give the industry time to convert refineries to produce adequate supplies of the new fuel.

The regulations set limits that will require oil refiners to reduce the volatility, or tendency to evaporate, of fuel to be sold from May 1 to Sept. 15. Gasoline vapors form smog when they react with other chemicals in sunlight.

Cars contain canisters to catch fumes, but these devices have become inadequate as gasoline volatility has increased in recent years. The EPA addressed the problem last year with its first set of warm-weather standards, which the agency says has reduced smog-forming emissions as much as 6% nationwide.

The latest rules are expected to cut smog-forming emissions an additional 7%. They will require gasoline volatility ranging from 7.8 to 9 pounds of pressure per square inch of evaporating gasoline, with the lower limit required in warmer states. States can request higher or lower standards, within a range of seven to nine pounds, if local economic or environmental conditions warrant it. Currently, summer volatility is required to range from nine to 10.5 pounds a square inch.

EPA administrator William Reilly said, "Gasoline volatility control will achieve the largest emissions benefit of any remaining control strategy EPA has identified to reduce emissions of the volatile organic compounds that produce smog."

Pending clean-air legislation also would require auto makers to enlarge or change the canisters to catch vapors that escape during refueling. The legislation would require a Transportation Department study of safety issues before these changes could be required, however.
