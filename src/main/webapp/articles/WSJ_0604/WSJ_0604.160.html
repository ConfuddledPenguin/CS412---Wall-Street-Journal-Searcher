Letters to the Editor: Pleading to a Crime That Doesn't Exist

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/04/90

Your May 4 editorial defending citizens who trade shares on inside information, "Justice Reaches Into Allenwood," is exactly right, but does not go far enough. Any citizen who owns an asset, and who has no fiduciary responsibility over its disposition, should be permitted to call anyone in the world and ask for information on its prospects for appreciation or depreciation -- and buy or sell on any information gleaned. If I own shares or am thinking of buying shares in General Motors, I am protected by the First Amendment to the Constitution if I call the chairman of GM, the directors or their investment bankers and ask them any question I can imagine. And I can buy and sell on the information they provide. Any laws that make it a crime for me to do so are patently unconstitutional. The government can make it a crime for me to own a particular asset, to sell an asset at a price the government deems unfair. But the Constitution does not permit the government to criminalize my communications in inquiring as to its prospects.

The frenzied outrage over the fact that a lot of people have made money buying and selling assets on Wall Street in recent years should not obscure the fact that there are fundamental rights we possess and should defend. Federal prosecutors, judges and juries who have been swept up in this frenzy over tales of untrammeled greed on Wall Street should bear this in mind. In the Waldbaum case, you correctly point out that Robert Chestman was convicted and jailed for a "crime that he did not commit -- and that the judges ruled does not exist."

Another egregious example in this vein was the conviction of Robert Freeman of Goldman Sachs for trading on "inside information." Mr. Freeman was actually indicted on a variety of charges that were dropped when he agreed to plead guilty to trading on the news that his "Bunny has a good nose." Because Mr. Freeman had no fiduciary responsibilities violated in the subsequent transactions, he thus pleaded guilty to a crime that does not exist in order to give the feds their pound of flesh. I suggest a new law, perhaps even a capital crime, that prohibits U.S. citizens from pleading guilty to crimes that do not exist.

Jude Wanniski

President

Polyconomics Inc.

Morristown, N.J.
