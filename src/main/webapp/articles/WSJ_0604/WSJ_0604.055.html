International: Soviets Deposit Gold in Western Banks As Collateral for Easing Cash Squeeze

  • Author: Neil Behrmann Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/04/90

LONDON -- The Soviet Union, caught in a cash squeeze and hounded by trade creditors around the world who are owed hundreds of millions of dollars, is depositing large amounts of gold with Western banks as collateral for loans, London and Swiss bullion dealers say. Delays in payments to West German, Japanese, Scandinavian, Australian, New Zealand and other suppliers have discouraged foreign bankers from lending to the Soviet Union, bankers say. So Moscow is raising dollars through a series of gold swaps and commodity sales to repay its creditors.

State organizations will sell "goods out of our stocks" to raise 1.5 billion rubles ($2.5 billion), said Viktor Gerashchenko, chairman of Gosbank, the Soviet state bank, according to a Reuters report from Moscow. He added that the U.S.S.R. would continue to offer gold swaps to Western banks.

"The Russians already have around 200 to 300 metric tons of gold tied up in swap deals," says Edwin Arnold, metals analyst at Merrill Lynch & Co. in London.

The gold, which is from Moscow's monetary reserves, bullion dealers say, is deposited in the lender's vaults in return for a foreign-exchange credit. The transaction is normally pegged at 25% below the market value of gold for periods of six to 12 months. Meanwhile, the Soviet Union is actively selling its current gold production, estimated at 250 to 300 tons a year, dealers say.

There will be more swaps in coming months, bullion analysts say, and the gold is likely to be supplied from the Soviet Union's gold reserves, estimated at 2,000 metric tons.

"Huge quantities of Soviet gold were shipped to Zurich and London during the past few weeks," says Philip Klapwijk, an analyst at Gold Fields Mineral Services Ltd., a London consultant that specializes in gold. Included in these shipments are deliveries of 100 to 150 metric tons of "`substandard gold" to a British precious metals refinery, Mr. Arnold says. This gold, which is being purified to acceptable international market standards, was obviously produced years ago, he says.

Moscow's inability to pay in a timely fashion for such key imports as steel pipe from Japanese foundries, wool from Australia and New Zealand, and more than $1 billion of grain from the U.S. and other nations, has focused bankers' attention on the U.S.S.R.'s large net international debt of $36.5 billion, analysts say. So it has little alternative but to use its gold reserves to bolster its tarnished credit rating, they say.

Moscow's unpaid trade bills began piling up after Soviet authorities allowed individual companies and other state entities to import directly from abroad without having to go through the official importing organizations. Many of the enterprises apparently ordered more than they could pay for in hard cash, and Moscow has refused to guarantee payment as it did in the past. Soviet officials said last month that they were setting up an internal collection agency and had begun drawing on Moscow's hard currency reserves to help cover overdue bills abroad, as the slowdown in payments was affecting the country's credit rating.

Most of the Soviet gold swaps already in place were negotiated in the first quarter, bullion dealers say, partly because of a slump in the nation's oil production and exports, a major source of Moscow's hard-currency earnings. Problems at a major Siberian oil field and shortages of equipment and pipelines contributed to a decline in Soviet oil output to about 11.8 million barrels a day in the past few months from about 12.4 million barrels in the first quarter of last year, estimates Mari Buglass, an oil analyst at Shearson Lehman Hutton Ltd. in London.

Gold swaps normally don't have any impact on the price of gold, dealers say. Yet some of the Soviet swaps were negotiated at prices of more than $400 an ounce, according to Mr. Arnold, the Merrill Lynch metals analyst. If the Soviets can't raise funds to repay the loan, the gold collateral might have to be sold on the open market, he says.
