Campeau, Bank Are Mum on Status Of Personal Loans

  • Author: Robert Melnbardis
  • Date: 06/04/90

TORONTO -- Robert Campeau and National Bank of Canada were silent last week about whether Mr. Campeau had regained control of Campeau Corp. by settling 153 million Canadian dollars (US$130 million) of personal loans he owes the bank. Mr. Campeau, chairman and chief executive officer of the real estate and retailing company, had until last Thursday to come up with the first C$40 million payment on the loans, after which National Bank would return Campeau shares it seized from him in January. But the deadline passed without comment from National Bank or Mr. Campeau.

National Bank said last Thursday that it would comment on the agreement the next day, but on Friday it said an announcement wouldn't be forthcoming. Neither Mr. Campeau nor his lawyer could be reached to comment. Campeau officials said they didn't have any information on the status of the agreement between Mr. Campeau and National Bank. The bank was expected to announce that it had extended the deadline under the agreement.

In January, the bank seized Campeau securities from Mr. Campeau, claiming that he had defaulted on about C$153 million of personal loans, against which the securities were pledged. The securities, which included 13 million Campeau common shares, C$60.4 million of convertible debentures and four million convertible preference shares, amounted to a 38% stake in the company on a fully diluted basis. After the seizure, Mr. Campeau's fully diluted stake in Campeau dwindled to about 21% from 43%.

Under Mr. Campeau's agreement with National Bank, he was to pay a further C$40 million by Jan. 31, 1992, or, failing that, pledge the C$60.4 million of debentures back to the bank. Mr. Campeau hasn't indicated whether he has financing for the first C$40 million transaction.

Mr. Campeau's agreement with National Bank was crucial to a vote last Friday by holders of Campeau's 7 1/2% debentures, approving deferral of interest payments on the debentures until Dec. 31, 1991. National Bank allowed him to vote the C$60.4 million of debentures it holds, which, together with his own stake in the issue, entitled him to vote a total of 42% of the debentures. The Reichmann family's Olympia & York Developments Ltd., Toronto, which holds 25.3% of the debentures, was viewed as supportive of the interest-deferral resolution. With Olympia & York's support, Mr. Campeau apparently had the assurance that holders of 67.3% of the debentures agreed to the interest deferral.

Campeau officials said they were seeking agreements from other creditors and debenture holders on deferrals of interest payments. In March, Campeau stopped paying interest on the 7 1/2% debentures and US$260 million of convertible debentures held by Olympia & York. It also stopped paying interest on US$225 million owed to Olympia & York and US$480 million owed to a Youngstown, Ohio-based developer, Edward J. DeBartolo Corp.

Campeau plans to sell a substantial part of its Canadian real estate assets to restructure the finances of its Canadian operations, which it said have a "significant negative cash flow." The Canadian parent company couldn't meet interest payments on its debt after its U.S. retailing units, Federated Department Stores Inc. and Allied Stores Corp., sought protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code in January.
