International: Japan's Current-Account Surplus Slims, Adding Fuel to Debate on Trade Policy

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 06/04/90

TOKYO -- Japan said its current international payments account surplus narrowed sharply in April, an announcement that is likely to heat up a simmering policy debate over the country's need for such surpluses. The Finance Ministry said the country's unadjusted current-account surplus, which measures trade in goods and services, was only $2.0 billion in April, off a sharp 70% from $6.58 billion a year earlier and an even steeper 76% from $8.3 billion in March. The country's overall trade surplus shrank 50% to $4.02 billion in April from $8.10 billion in April 1989.

The rapidly diminishing surpluses will add impetus to an ongoing government review of its policy of reducing such surpluses, which in the past have generated huge amounts of capital for Japan Inc. On Friday, Finance Minister Ryutaro Hashimoto said he welcomed a report from a government advisory panel that said Japan must determine an "appropriate" level for its surpluses -- suggesting that official acceptance of such surpluses might be near.

Until recently, severe trade frictions, chiefly with the U.S. and Europe, have sustained the policy. But a looming international capital shortage, caused by the huge cost of reunifying Germany, rebuilding Eastern Europe and covering chronic U.S. budget deficits, has prompted the government here to consider altering its policies to preserve the surpluses.

Such a change in policy could inflame tensions with trade groups and some politicians in the U.S. After battling Japan for years to trim its persistent surpluses, Americans may resent a switch to what effectively would be an endorsement of perennial imbalances.

In Paris this past Thursday, the first sign of resistance came from Treasury Secretary Nicholas F. Brady, who warned against confusing the need for capital in Eastern Europe with the need to reduce external surpluses.

On Friday, the American Electronics Association sent a note to U.S. journalists in Tokyo noting that the Japanese study panel's conclusion that a current-account surplus was necessary was "likely to cause comment" in the U.S.

The report, issued Thursday by a special advisory council to Japan's Finance Ministry, said that Japan may have to assume a role as a global creditor nation. That would entail keeping its surplus, which the panel said was likely anyway in light of Japan's industrial prowess. Japan's current-account surplus already has shrunk to about 2% of total economic output, the panel noted, from 4.3%, and no longer can "be considered a cause of world economic disorder."

The report echoed the conclusion of an International Monetary Fund economic survey that Japan's current-account surplus and high domestic savings rate might be desirable to help cover capital shortages elsewhere in the world.

The sharp decline in the current account during April isn't likely to last. The Finance Ministry ascribed the decline to a plunge in automobile exports, together with a sharp rise in aircraft imports.

The decline in automobile exports was due in part to aggressive localization of production by Japanese manufacturers. Auto exports were down 19% in April from the year earlier, the first time in 20 years that levels have dropped so drastically.

The yen's weakness also was a factor. During April, the dollar traded at an average of 157.20 yen, compared with 132.12 yen in April 1989, a 16% depreciation. Although the yen also fell in January and February 1990, a ministry official said April's 16% devaluation was particularly "unusual." In the long run, a weaker yen tends to make Japan's products cheaper to buyers overseas and could help exports, but in the short run it reduces the dollar value of Japan's export figures.
