Commodities: Stotler Unit Seeks Seasonal Trade Bargains With Profit Potential; 12 Recommendations Made

  • Author: Stanley W. Angrist
  • Date: 06/04/90

Some commodity traders stake real money on the idea that price patterns for many commodities repeat themselves about the same time each year. Such seasonal price action once was detectable only with painstaking and haphazard study. Today, computers sniff out such patterns easily.

Pacific Research Center in Eugene, Ore., a wholly owned operation of Stotler & Co., a Chicago-based futures broker, looks for seasonal trades with a high probability of being profitable. Recently, the firm issued its recommendations for 12 seasonal trades that begin in June and have been profitable at least 80% of the time over the past 15 years. (Seasonal reliability for Treasury notes and Eurodollars could only be checked for eight years because their contracts have only been trading that long.)

In many cases the seasonal price pattern can be attributed to the seasonality that affects all agricultural commodities. For example, prices for some agricultural commodities drop just after their harvest begins. However, traders who use seasonal price patterns don't look for explanations but reliable repetition. Surprisingly, even when a price pattern becomes well-known among traders it doesn't necessarily disappear -- contrary to what market theorists assert.

John Crane, research director at Pacific Research Center, urges traders not to buy into every highly repetitive seasonal trade without considering other factors. "We suggest to clients that they try to combine our seasonal work with other technical studies before taking a trade," he says. If, for example, short-term price moves in the market being studied are going in the same direction as the seasonal pattern, he says traders can be more confident that a trade will work.

"Sometimes we will pass up a seasonal trade completely if the price patterns of the commodity seem to be out of line," adds Mr. Crane.

Just because a seasonal price move has demonstrated high reliability in the past is no guarantee that it will do so again. For that reason Pacific Research Center has used its computer to calculate an optimum amount of money that should be risked on each seasonal trade. If a seasonal trade goes the wrong way and produces a bigger loss than the amount shown in the table accompanying this story, Mr. Crane suggests abandoning the trade. Pacific Research uses closing prices only to determine profits and losses.

Mr. Crane also recommends that when a seasonal trade has a paper profit at least equal to the average amount shown in the table, traders should protect that profit with an order that would close out the position at a price that would leave at least half the profit intact.

"Many times I have seen seasonals quickly move to a profit and then give up most, or all, of that profit in subsequent days," he says.

The report prepared by Pacific Research Center gives not only the data shown in the nearby table but also shows for each year studied the maximum profit and the biggest loss produced during the trade. In addition it gives the dates these profits and losses were made. Mr. Crane says readers who wish to receive a copy of the June report may call 800-365-8000, or write to Pacific Research Center, 85180 Loraine Highway, Eugene, OR 97405.

Looking ahead to commodity markets this week:

Precious Metals

Gold futures fell to new life-of-contract lows Friday, further darkening the metal's already-gloomy situation. Traders this week will be watching to see if and where underlying support develops to brake the decline.

The latest pressure on gold came from reports that the Soviet Union has run out of hard currency and has asked for credit extensions to pay for a number of commodities it has bought, analysts said.

The belief among traders is that the Soviet Union has been swapping gold with foreign banks to obtain hard currency, said William O'Neill, vice president of research for Elders Futures, New York. Foreign banks, in turn, are hedging the swapped gold to protect themselves against a loss in value while they hold it, further depressing prices, he said.

"We'll likely see more of this in coming months, and this doesn't take into consideration South Africa, which has foreign exchange problems of its own," he said.

Mr. O'Neill also observed that traders have been negatively conditioned by the two recent big Saudi Arabian sales of gold that caused prices to plunge, and they're braced for another round. "This is keeping a damper on investment enthusiasm," he said.

However, Jeffrey Nichols, president of American Precious Metals Advisors, of Toronto, notes that the National Commercial Bank of Jeddah, which was credited with making the big gold sales, mightn't do it again. "The Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority and the Saudi royal family, {which hold} significant gold reserves and investments, could not have been pleased to see the metal's price fall so sharply," he says.

The National Commercial Bank has important ties to the central bank and the royal family, "so much so that a word from the authorities could guarantee there are no further disruptive dealings," Mr. Nichols says.

Energy

This is the week for OPEC numbers -- estimates of May production and perhaps even preliminary indications for June. The Paris-based International Energy Agency will release its estimates of May crude oil volumes for the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries. Some other OPEC output monitors may be out with early-June guesses.

These reports are important because they could signal the direction of petroleum prices over the summer. Most analysts agree the major factor now in determining the price outlook is how much OPEC trims its output to shrink the world oil glut. And most forecasters are bearish after the roller-coaster oil market that sizzled briefly last week before its gasoline-fed rally collapsed.

But some, such as Fereidun Fesharaki of the Resource Systems Institute at Honolulu's East-West Center, see oil prices recovering. "Although the reduction in oil production has been slow in the OPEC countries," Mr. Fesharaki said, "the reduction is nevertheless real and {the} impact of such cuts will soon be observed." In the U.S. cash market, he expects West Texas Intermediate crude to rise $1 a barrel in June to $19 and by another $1.75 in July before falling back some to $20 in August.

Grains & Soybeans

Today analysts will try to determine whether last week's dry weather allowed Midwest corn farmers to complete their rain-delayed planting.

Analysts expect the weekly progress reports issued by the Midwest states today to show that corn farmers have nearly completed planting their fields. The general rule of thumb is that most Midwest fields must be planted by June 1 for the corn crop to obtain maximum yield. After that date, many farmers will shift their fields to other crops, such as soybeans.

If the weekly reports show that a significant amount of corn fields haven't been planted as planned, analysts will trim their estimates of the corn harvest.

Grain traders will also watch interest rates this week. A drop would imply that a deflationary mood is building in the financial markets -- which might signal that commodity prices would come under downward pressure.
