Illinois Power May Have to Suspend Dividends on Preferred, Official Says

  • Author: Jeffrey Taylor
  • Date: 06/04/90

DECATUR, Ill. -- Illinois Power Co. may have to suspend payment of dividends to holders of its preferred stock. "I cannot say with certainty whether we will able to pay the preferred dividend," Larry Haab, president and chief financial officer of the electricity and natural gas concern, conceded in an interview. "There is the potential for a legal interpretation that when retained earnings are negative, we cannot pay a dividend."

Illinois Power, which already has eliminated common stock dividends, is awaiting the outcome of a rate case pending before the Illinois Commerce Commission. The regulator's ruling, viewed as critical to the utility's short-term financial health, is expected Wednesday. The company had about $474 million face value of preferred stock outstanding, as of May 23, according to Moody's Investors Service Inc.

On May 23, Moody's changed the direction of its review of its rating on Illinois Power's preferred stock to "down" from "uncertain." The rating concern cited an unfavorable preliminary rate-order proposal from a hearing examiner in the state commerce agency's case, a delay in a pending court case on a previous agency order, and lower-than-anticipated corporate earnings.

The pivotal issue in the current commerce commission case is whether the regulators will declare Illinois Power's Clinton nuclear power plant 100% "used and useful," Mr. Haab said. State regulations restrict utilities to billing customers for only as much of a plant's cost as is necessary to meet the overall demand for power.

The seven-member commission ruled in March 1989 that only 27% of the plant was needed. Four members of the commission have been replaced since then; how they intend to vote on the Clinton issue isn't clear.

"It is vital to us to get it (the Clinton plant) declared `used and useful,' even if it's not incorporated into rates in one shot," Mr. Haab said. "There is a possibility of having to write off that portion of Clinton which is not declared `used and useful.'"

As of March 31, Illinois Power's retained earnings were listed at $120.7 million. If retained earnings become negative through a write-off, both the Federal Power Act and the Illinois Public Utility Act stipulate interruption of preferred stock dividends.

In November, the state commerce commission's staff recommended that the Clinton plant be declared 100% "used and useful," and that Illinois Power be granted $147 million of its $215 million rate-increase request. The agency's hearing examiner, who acts as a judge, later recommended that Clinton be declared 42% "used and useful," and proposed a far smaller rate increase of $27 million.

"My prediction is that what they'll get will be somewhere between the staff's recommendation and the hearing examiner's," Barry Abramson, an analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc., said.

Illinois Power's problems trace back to the commerce commission's March 1989 refusal to allow the utility to use 100% of the Clinton plant. In the order, the commission also said Illinois Power imprudently spent $666 million too much on the plant. Following the 1989 order, the company took a $346 million write-off.

At the time, analysts said, the commission's membership was strongly consumer-oriented. With four new members on the commission this year, Mr. Haab hopes for a ruling more favorable for the utility.

Meanwhile, Illinois Power is awaiting the result of the lawsuit it filed, challenging the 1989 order. The case is in Illinois's Third District Appellate Court, and a ruling is expected this fall.

"The critical thing is that the appellate ruling be in our favor," Mr. Haab said. "If we get a favorable decision from the appellate court, there will be no write-off this year."

Such an outcome is unlikely, Mr. Abramson, the Prudential-Bache analyst, said.

The court is expected to rule both on whether the utility can use all of the Clinton plant and on whether it spent too much money to build it.

"I don't see any scenario under which they're not going to have to write off $248 million, after tax, for imprudent spending," Mr. Abramson said. "Based on that assumption, they'd have a negative retained income, which would preclude them from paying preferred dividends for a time."
