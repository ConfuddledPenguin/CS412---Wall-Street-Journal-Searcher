Manager's Journal: How the Stealth Fighter Got Off the Ground

  • Author: Ben R. Rich
  • Date: 06/04/90

Lockheed's Advanced Development Projects (commonly known as the "Skunk Works") has produced a series of top-secret, highly advanced aircraft for the U.S. armed forces and intelligence agencies since World War II. The P-80, U-2, F-104, and SR-71 have been widely recognized as among the most significant achievements of the aerospace industry. Those aircraft were produced in an atmosphere of creativity by engineers under the leadership of Clarence L. "Kelly" Johnson. Mr. Johnson devised 14 basic operating rules for handling small, sensitive projects. Many are applicable to other business situations, both inside and outside the aerospace industry. Mr. Johnson didn't base his rules on any formal statistical data or on scientific observations, yet they are more than just gut feelings. They worked for Mr. Johnson for over 30 years, and they continue to work today.

A few of Mr. Johnson's rules have been modified to accommodate new technologies, concepts of operation and the effects of government regulations. Yet these rules and the management philosophy they fostered continue to guide today's operations at the Skunk Works.

But a Skunk Works is not a panacea. It does not usually work on large programs. A true Skunk Works encourages mavericks -- men and women who go off on tangents and devise highly creative solutions to seemingly insurmountable problems. But in large organizations, mavericks are surrounded by regulations and stifled by layers of management. Try as hard as they can, mavericks cannot bushwack the bureaucracy that is endemic in any large business or governmental entity.

A Skunk Works performs best with small programs, such as building prototypes, or for short production runs. In small programs people get to know one another. They are not so hung up with titles. They speak comfortably with one another. They trade information freely.

The success of the F-117 "Stealth Fighter" program is the most recent example of how a Skunk Works operation can perform. Lockheed and U.S. Air Force team members followed Mr. Johnson's basic principles on this program. A small military project office and small Lockheed project office diligently managed all elements of the program. There was proper accountability as well as mutual trust every step of the way. The cost and schedule performance on the program was outstanding. Compared with similar non-Skunk Works operations, the F-117 program achieved significantly lower overhead costs and better overall communications.

The secret of the Skunk Works' success is that its management style is no secret, but a unique culture within the larger Lockheed Corp. that has been nurtured and encouraged since its inception. The basic operating rules are in black and white for anyone to read and to apply.

Is the Skunk Works a national asset? Perhaps. But the true national asset are those leaders who are willing to embrace Kelly Johnson's principles of management, efficiency, accountability, integrity . . . and make them work. Such leaders will help America realize the dreams made possible by the end of the Cold War and the decline of communism. That would provide a real peace dividend.

---

Mr. Rich is executive vice president and general manager of Lockheed's Advanced Development Projects.

Basic Operating Rules Of Lockheed's `Skunk Works'

1. The Skunk Works manager must be delegated practically complete control of his program in all aspects. He should report to a division president or higher.

2. Strong but small project offices must be provided both by the military and industry.

3. The number of people having any connection with the project must be restricted in an almost vicious manner. Use a small number of good people.

4. A very simple drawing and drawing release system with great flexibility for making changes must be provided.

5. There must be a minimum number of reports required, but important work must be recorded thoroughly.

6. There must be a monthly cost review covering not only what has been spent and committed but also projected costs to the conclusion of the program. Don't surprise the customer with sudden overruns.

7. The contractor must be delegated and must assume more than normal responsibility to get good vendor bids for subcontract work on the project.

8. The inspection system as currently used by Advanced Development Projects (ADP), which has been approved by both the Air Force and Navy, meets the intent of existing military requirements and should be used on new projects. Push more basic inspection.

9. The contractor must be delegated the authority to test his final product in flight. He can and must test it in the initial stages. If he doesn't, he rapidly loses his competency to design other vehicles.

10. The specifications applying to the hardware must be agreed to in advance of contracting.

11. Funding a program must be timely so that the contractor doesn't have to keep running to the bank to support government projects.

12. There must be mutual trust between the military project organization and the contractor, with very close cooperation and liaison on a day-to-day basis. This cuts down misunderstanding and correspondence to an absolute minimum.

13. Access by outsiders to the project and its personnel must be strictly controlled by appropriate security measures.

14. Because only a few people will be used in engineering and most other areas, ways must be provided to reward good performance by pay not based on the number of personnel supervised.
