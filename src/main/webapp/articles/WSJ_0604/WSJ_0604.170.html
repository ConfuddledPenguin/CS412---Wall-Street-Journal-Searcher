NCNB Acquires Most Remnants Of Texas Firm

  • Author: Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 06/04/90

CHARLOTTE, N.C. -- NCNB Corp. acquired most of the remnants of the failed National Bancshares Corp. of Texas, based in San Antonio, increasing its stake in the state by purchasing $1 billion in assets and assuming $1.5 billion in liabilities. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. approved NCNB's proposal to assume the deposits and secured liabilities of nine of the 12 bank subsidiaries of National Bancshares. The banks are expected to reopen today as units of NCNB Texas National Banking Corp.

Since its entry into Texas in 1988, the ever-aggressive NCNB has grown to be the largest bank in the state by picking up sundry pieces from the Texas banking disaster. With this acquisition, NCNB Texas will have $31.4 billion in assets and 250 branches in 77 communities.

In a statement, Kenneth D. Lewis, president of NCNB Texas, called the transaction "a major step in NCNB's expansion strategy and an excellent addition to our trust business." He said it doubles the number of locations in San Antonio, which NCNB considers an attractive market, and gives NCNB a presence in seven new communities. The FDIC said the nine bank units are based in Austin, Boerne, Corpus Christi, Houston, Kerrville, Mission, San Antonio, Sequin and Uvalde.

Under terms of the transaction, the FDIC will infuse $581.2 million into the assuming bank and will retain assets of the failed banks with a book value of $613.7 million, the agency said. NCNB agreed to pay a premium of $59.5 million for the $1.5 billion in deposits it is assuming.

Until the end of April, it appeared that National Bancshares would be acquired by Equimark Corp., the Pittsburgh bank holding company. Under a recapitalization plan drafted in mid-1989, Equimark agreed to invest or raise about $116 million of new capital for National Bancshares. At the same time, the FDIC was expected to invest about $250 million to eliminate the negative net worth -- assets minus liabilities -- of subsidiary banks and cover losses from problem assets.

In late April, however, the FDIC unexpectedly scuttled the plan rather than bow to bondholders' demands that it sweeten its offer. Some banking industry observers also speculated that the recapitalization failure owed as much to Equimark's problems at home as it did to recalcitrant bondholders. Buffeted by the weak commercial real-estate market, Equimark had a $15.4 million loss in the first quarter.
