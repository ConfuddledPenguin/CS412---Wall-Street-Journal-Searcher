Wal-Mart's Walton Predicts Company Could Quintuple Sales by the Year 2000

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 06/04/90

FAYETTEVILLE, Ark. -- Saying he views his multibillion-dollar retail empire as "just a small regional company," Wal-Mart Stores Inc. Chairman Sam M. Walton said he thought the company could quintuple sales to about $125 billion by the year 2000. Last year, Wal-Mart's earnings increased 29% to $1 billion on sales of $25.8 billion. If achieved, Mr. Walton's goals for the end of the decade would boost the discount retailer into the same league with corporate giants like General Motors Corp., whose 1989 revenue was $126.9 billion.

"I really believe it's possible," Wal-Mart's 72-year-old founder said at the annual meeting as he stood at the end of a lighted walkway that extended into the middle of a University of Arkansas sports arena. Though treated for a form of cancer of the blood and bones earlier in the year, the Arkansas billionaire had the fire and vigor of a revival preacher as he led more than 8,000 shareholders and employees in a series of call-and-response cheers.

"Can we do it?" he shouted.

"Yes we can," they roared back.

Mr. Walton predicted the company would double the number of Wal-Mart and Sam's Wholesale Club stores, increase both their average size and annual sales and continue to expand outside the company's traditional base in the South and Midwest.

By the end of the decade, he said he expects the number of Wal-Mart stores to climb to about 3,000 from about 1,400 now and their average size increase to about 80,000 square feet from about 66,400 square feet. He predicted that Wal-Mart store total sales would climb to about $96 billion annually from about $21 billion last year and that per-square foot sales would jump to $400 annually from about $250 last year.

While talking little about the disappointing results at the corporation's four huge Hypermart U.S.A. outlets, Mr. Walton said he hoped that Sam's Wholesale Club outlets would increase in number to about 300 from 127 and that Sam's annual revenue would increase to about $30 billion from about $4.8 billion last year.

"It all rides on our people," said Mr. Walton, who added that although Wal-Mart is "getting to be a large company, we have to act small and move quickly."

William W. Whyte, an analyst with Stephens Inc. in Little Rock, Ark., said WalMart, which concentrates its operations in small towns, could probably double its number of outlets just by moving into new markets in the 28 states where it already has operations.

Noting innovations such as the company's recent efforts to add discount grocery operations to some of its Wal-Mart outlets, Mr. Whyte added that he would not be surprised if Wal-Mart achieved the lofty goals laid out by its chairman and founder. "They never cease to amaze me," he said. "With them, we just take it one quarter at a time."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Wal-Mart jumped $2 to $58.375.
