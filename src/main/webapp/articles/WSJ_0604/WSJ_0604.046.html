Foreign Exchange: Signs That U.S. Economy Is Losing Kick Aren't Likely to Undercut Dollar for Now

  • Author: Candace Cumberbatch Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/04/90

NEW YORK -- Signs that the U.S. economy is losing its kick won't undercut the resilient dollar any time soon. So say a number of currency analysts who point to the dollar-boosting potential of economic and political uncertainty in West Germany and elsewhere.

"I really wouldn't be looking to throw in the towel on the dollar," says Victor Polce, head of corporate dealings at Algemene Bank Nederland N.V. in New York.

Few traders gave up on the dollar Friday despite an ominous sign in the government's employment report. The data show that although the May unemployment rate fell to 5.3% from 5.4% in April, non-farm payrolls increased by only 164,000 jobs, compared with the 253,000 economists expected. Economic weakness can lead to lower interest rates, reducing the dollar's appeal compared with other currencies.

Friday's data sparked only a short-lived setback for the dollar, however.

Shortly after the report was announced, the dollar sank to intraday lows of 1.6815 marks and 150.80 yen. But then the currency strengthened.

In late dealings here, the dollar changed hands at 1.6980 marks and 151.85 yen, compared with 1.6965 marks and 152.10 yen at Thursday's close.

In Tokyo Monday, the dollar was trading soon after the opening at 152.50 yen, up a bit from Friday's Tokyo close of 152.17 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.7010 marks.

Although economists warn against reading too much into any single report, the financial markets often move sharply on the jobs figures. That's because they give hints about the health of a number of sectors and sometimes encourage adjustments in the Federal Reserve's monetary policy.

And even though the economy is still giving warning signals of weak growth and there is persistent speculation that Fed easing may be down the road, many traders believe there will be continued demand for the dollar in the near term.

"They're still looking to buy. People are skeptically playing long dollar positions," says David Lapin, national foreign exchange sales manager at Bank of America, Los Angeles.

The dollar stumbled against a number of foreign currencies recently, but the declines didn't last.

In dollar-yen dealings, buyers have been backing the dollar even though sales by Japanese institutional investors pounded it recently.

And in dollar-Swiss franc trading, the dollar has weathered the selling that occurred because of the market's enthusiasm about Switzerland's higher interest rates. High rates generally boost a currency because they draw buyers to investments denominated in the unit.

Despite the softness in the U.S. economy, investors still have confidence in the dollar at a time when uncertainty surrounds international politics and economics, currency specialists say.

"What other currency are investors going to buy?" asks Carl Weinberg, chief economist at High Frequency Economics Ltd., an international consulting company in New York.

Mr. Weinberg rattled off a number of reasons dollar buying is likely to continue: The mark is being held back by concerns about German monetary union. The yen isn't seen as a safe bet because the condition of Japan's economy and financial markets is unclear. Canadian dollars are being shunned because of Canada's constitutional impasse. Pound buying is on hold because of uncertainties about the course of British interest rates.

The dollar is "the first choice," he says, adding that it is "pretty comfortable" in long-range trading bands of 1.65 to 1.75 marks and 150 to 160 yen.

Mr. Polce of Bank Algemene Nederland says the dollar has the potential to hit 1.72 marks and 153.50 yen pretty soon.

Traders say the dollar probably won't have much trouble building momentum against the mark because of the rocky road to the monetary linkup between East Germany and West Germany, which is scheduled to go into effect July 1.

Still unknown is how West Germany's powerful economy will fare after its mark becomes the official currency of East Germany. Furthermore, the military allegiances involved in German reunification have yet to be sorted out.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery dropped $5.10 to $359.20 an ounce. The close was the lowest since Sept. 9. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.8 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Monday, gold was quoted at $359.35 an ounce.
