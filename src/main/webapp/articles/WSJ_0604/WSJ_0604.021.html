Normandy Securities, Two Principals Are Fined Total of $141,000 by NASD

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 06/04/90

NEW YORK -- Normandy Securities Inc. and two principals consented to fines totaling nearly $141,000 to settle charges by the National Association of Securities Dealers that they violated certain rules. The Normandy settlement, in which the firm and principals didn't admit or deny any wrongdoing, was among a number of disciplinary actions announced by the self-regulatory agency for the over-the-counter market.

The NASD alleged that Normandy Securities, through principal Norman Gottlieb of East Chester, N.Y., bought for its account shares of a new issue that began trading in the secondary market at a premium to the offer price. The agency also charged that Normandy financial and operations principal Paul Lenok, New Rochelle, N.Y., bought units in the new issue through an account of a corporation that he and his wife owned.

An NASD official explained that a securities firm that handles such a so-called hot new issue must make a "bona fide public distribution" of the shares and can't buy them for its own account or accounts of certain related parties until after public distribution.

Mr. Gottlieb said that "I was advised incorrectly by in-house counsel, whom I've since terminated. . . . The NASD realized that it was in fact an error and not deliberate."

Prescott, Ball & Turben of Cleveland and three employees also settled NASD charges without admitting or denying wrongdoing.

The agency charged that principal Andrew Geller, Bayside, N.Y., and two representatives, Theodore Geller, Bellmore, N.Y., and Leonard Greenberg, New York, caused the firm to execute transactions in a security at or near the market's close for the purpose of affecting the last-sale price at the close. The transactions were executed at prices higher than the previously reported trades, the NASD said.

Prescott was fined $15,000 and ordered to "comply with certain undertakings." Each of the three individuals was fined $15,000 and suspended from association with any NASD member for 10 business days.

Gerald Baker, senior vice president and compliance director for Prescott, said, "The charges involved technical and interpretive violations and were settled to put the matter behind and to avoid the time and costs associated with defending them. The transactions involved were legitimate trades and were fully paid for; there were no losses to customers as a result of the trades."

Charles A. Roth, a Denver representative, was fined $510,038, suspended for six months, and ordered to requalify by exam as a representative.

The NASD charged that Mr. Roth conducted business as a broker-dealer without being properly registered and effected private securities transactions without properly notifying his member firm. The agency also found that he received the above amount in fees and expenses from seven insurance companies for his participation in securities transactions involving these companies.

Mr. Roth has appealed the decision to the Securities and Exchange Commission.
