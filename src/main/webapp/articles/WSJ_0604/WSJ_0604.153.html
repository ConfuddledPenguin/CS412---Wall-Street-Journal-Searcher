Technology: IBM Agrees to Sell Computers to Soviets For Use in Schools

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/04/90

ARMONK, N.Y. -- International Business Machines Corp. said it agreed to sell more than 13,000 personal computers to the Soviet Union for use in high schools and third-level education. IBM didn't disclose the value of the contract, but it is likely one of the biggest sales ever of electronic equipment to the Soviets.

An IBM spokesman also said the company is talking to Soviet officials about establishing a personal-computer assembly plant within the Soviet Union, but he declined to give details. Soviet officials couldn't be reached for comment.

IBM said the education contract, which was signed last month and unveiled to coincide with the U.S.-Soviet summit, is the first fruit of a ripening relationship between the computer giant and the superpower.

IBM established an office in Moscow in the mid-1970s but, until recently, trade restrictions have limited its role to representation and goodwill. Last year, the company donated about 40 personal computers to the Soviets for various purposes, including a pilot program in several schools; education of blind and hearing-impaired students; and familiarization with business applications.

IBM said the education contract will be paid in dollars. The personal computers will be a mix of models from its Personal System/2 line, and will be sold at an undisclosed discount. The spokesman said the discount is greater than the generally accepted discount for educators, which is 40%.

Computer makers are barred from selling to the Soviets machines based on microprocessors more powerful than Intel Corp.'s 80286 chip, the basis of IBM's AT-class computers. Given that fact, the contract, which also includes some printers, is likely to be valued between $10 million and $20 million.

The IBM spokesman said "precious few" educational programs have been written for the Soviet market, and that even fewer will run on the new computers. But he noted that IBM has agreed to adapt about 150 of its educational software packages for Soviet schools, a move he said should encourage further computerization.

IBM said it will start shipping the computers from its Scottish manufacturing plant in September.
