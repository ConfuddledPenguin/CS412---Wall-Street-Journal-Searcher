Sid and Lee Bass, Airlie Group Sue Kidder Peabody

  • Author: Frederick Rose
  • Date: 06/04/90

Interests linked to Texas investors Sid and Lee Bass have sued Kidder, Peabody & Co., alleging that the securities firm defrauded them of more than $52 million in an October 1989 rights offering by First Executive Corp. The litigation, filed early last month in Fort Worth federal court, surfaced publicly only recently. It seeks unspecified actual and punitive damages as well as recision of the purchase of First Executive securities. At March 31, Airlie Group held an 8.9% stake in First Executive, which isn't named as a defendant in the suit.

A Kidder Peabody spokesman disputed the suit's allegations and said "we don't think the complaint is well grounded and expect that our view of the matter will prevail."

The plaintiffs include two personal holding companies bearing the brothers' names -- Sid R. Bass Inc., Lee M. Bass Inc.; Airlie Group L.P., a limited partnership based in Greenwich, Conn., whose general partners are investor Dort A. Cameron III and a holding company operated by Fort Worth investor Thomas Taylor; and Mr. Cameron individually.

The action by the Bass brothers' interests and Airlie is the latest of a number of suits relating to the rights offering of First Executive, a Los Angeles-based insurance holding company. Much of the earlier litigation has been consolidated as a purported class action in federal court in Los Angeles.

The Bass brothers and Airlie allege that Kidder, a General Electric Corp. unit described in the suit as in financial straits last year and badly in need of the estimated $5 million in fees that would result, underwrote a $284 million "exotic securities package" for First Executive. The package consisted of rights units which entitled holders to buy a package of convertible preferred stock and warrants to buy common stock.

The suit alleges that the prospectus related to the issue omitted key information about First Executive. Not long after the successful securities sale, First Executive disclosed that its junk bond holdings were overvalued by hundreds of millions of dollars. Insurance regulators rejected a complicated securities transaction intended to boost legally required reserves. And an anticipated $460 million sale of First Executive's New York unit fell through.

First Executive's stock price plummeted from about $12.25 a share when the rights offering was completed to as low as $2.25 a share earlier this year.

First Executive stock closed Friday at $2.50 a share in over-the-counter trading. The company, noting that it isn't named as a defendant in the latest lawsuit, declined comment.
