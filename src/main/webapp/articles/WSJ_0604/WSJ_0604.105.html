EAST GERMAN NUCLEAR SHUTDOWN

East Germany said it shut down another reactor at a Soviet-designed nuclear power plant because of safety deficiencies.

Environmental Minister Karl-Hermann Steinberg criticized management of the Greifswald plant, near the Baltic Sea, for permitting operation of a reactor even though a leak had been discovered in its "cleaning system." The reactor was "shut down because of grave safety deficiencies," he said.

Two of the plant's four reactors had been shut down early this year for safety reasons. The remaining reactor, which has been upgraded, will be closed down in December, the minister said.

Mr. Steinberg didn't provide details, but news reports have said the plant was sloppily managed and poorly constructed.

WORLD'S AIDS CASES RISE

World-wide cases of AIDS rose by nearly 9,000 in May, mostly in the U.S. and Africa, reaching a global total of 263,051 cases in 156 countries, the World Health Organization said. The U.S. recorded 6,309 new cases, raising its overall tally to 132,436. There were 903 new cases in Africa, 690 of them in Congo, bringing the continent's total to 64,475. European cases increased by 1,125 to 35,021. France alone accounted for 835 more cases, lifting its total to 9,718, highest in the region.

VENEZUELAN SUBSIDIES

Venezuela will more than double transport subsidies to some three million workers to soften the effects of proposed, and widely opposed, gasoline-price increase. Workers earning less than $195 per month -- about half the labor force -- will see their $4.25 monthly transport subsidy raised to $10.63 by June 30, Planning Minister Miguel Rodriguez said. The domestic price of gas is expected to increase by 36%.

JAPAN-SIBERIA PIPELINE

The Soviet Union asked Japan to cooperate in a plan to build a 3,100-mile gas pipeline, partly undersea, between Siberia and Japan. Soviet officials told Shintaro Abe, former secretary-general of the ruling Liberal Democratic Party, that the pipeline would connect the gas field in western Siberia to Japan via North and South Korea, said a spokesman for Mr. Abe's office. The Soviet side said it had already received a positive reaction from both Koreas and that the U.S. had also shown interest in participating in construction of the project, the spokesman said.

SOUTH AFRICAN SECURITIES BILL

South Africa's parliament is considering a bill aimed at tightening legislation on insider trading in the nation's securities markets. The bill is designed to strengthen the powers of the Johannesburg Stock Exchange and the police to prosecute offenders. The new proposals clarify the circumstances under which possession or use of unpublished price-sensitive information is considered illegal. Under existing legislation, no prosecutions have ever been brought against insider trading.

GERMANY BANS BRITISH BEEF

West Germany and Luxembourg joined France in banning British beef imports because of the "mad cow" virus that has killed thousands of British cattle, while the European Community offered to buy 20,600 tons of meat to support Britain's troubled cattle industry. French officials had said some of the British meat might be tainted by the disease. West Germany -- which already had barred imports of British beef still on the bone -- emulated France by extending the ban to canned beef as well. London accused both France and Germany of seeking to protect their beef industries and of exaggerating their concern for public health.

CHINESE PROTEST

China's central bank has reportedly ordered its public companies to suspend doing business with Bank of Tokyo Ltd. in an apparent protest against the Japanese bank's plan to open a representative office in Taiwan.

A Japanese news report, quoting international banking sources, said the People's Bank of China also called for the suspension of staff exchanges between the Bank of Tokyo and Chinese banks and other financial institutions.

The Bank of Tokyo and other Japanese banks have been trying recently to expand into Taiwan by taking advantage of that country's plans to give foreign banks greater access to the long-protected banking market. Currently, only Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank Ltd., among Japanese banks, has a branch in Taiwan, as the number of foreign banks there has been limited to one per country since 1959.

In another Taiwan-related matter, China strongly criticized the West African state of Guinea Bissau for recognizing the Taiwan government. Beijing also accused Taiwan of launching a "dollar offensive" to secure diplomatic relations with small, destitute countries.
