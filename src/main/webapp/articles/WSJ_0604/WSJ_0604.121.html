Optimism on Earnings, Rates Boosts Markets --- Credit Markets: Rates Expected To Ease Further

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 06/04/90

NEW YORK -- Economists' charts and graphs are flashing a green light for the bond market, bolstering the case for lower interest rates this summer. Economists now believe that yields on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond could fall to between 8% and 8.25% -- about where they stood in January. They think this will happen even if the Federal Reserve doesn't lower interest rates -- although some are beginning to whisper that the Fed might do so.

"The rally has just begun," said Philip Braverman, a longtime bond market bull who is senior vice president at DKB Securities Corp., a subsidiary of Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank Ltd. He said recent economic data confirm that the economy is weak and inflation is losing momentum, a situation that often leads to lower interest rates.

Mr. Braverman isn't the only raging bull in the bond market. Last week many economists trimmed their forecasts for second-quarter economic growth, automatically heightening the recent bullishness in a market that thrives on bad economic news.

The speed at which bond prices rise will depend upon the Federal Reserve. Economists are sharply divided over how the Fed will react to the weak economic reports. A growing number believe the Fed will push short-term rates lower in an effort to stave off a recession. But a smaller group believes the Fed will keep credit policy on hold, using the current slow-growth environment to fight stubbornly high inflation.

Even without the Fed's help, economists say, bond yields should continue to fall as the market retraces the sharp spikes of January through April, when investors sold bonds on signs that the economy and inflation were picking up. Now, the economists are starting to view the four-month period as an aberration in a longer-term decline.

Bond prices don't yet reflect prospects for Fed easing. Samuel D. Kahan, chief financial economist, Fuji Securities Inc., notes that yields on the Treasury's two-year notes ended Friday at about 8.3% -- still above the 8.25% federal-funds rate, which banks charge each other on overnight loans. The Fed often uses the rate to signal changes in credit policy. "If the yield on the two-year notes dips below or matches the federal-funds rate, that would signify that the financial markets have begun to incorporate future declines in the federal-funds rate into the bond market," he said.

Speculation that the Fed may choose to push rates lower began on Friday, after the Department of Labor released the May employment report. The government reported that nonfarm employment rose by 164,000 in May, but most of that growth reflected the hiring of temporary census workers. Moreover, the labor statistics revealed that there has been virtually no private-sector job growth in the past three months. The unemployment rate declined slightly to 5.3% in May, but only because 145,000 census workers were hired.

After the data were released, institutional investors plunged into the bond market. The buying spree pushed prices of the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond 1 1/2 points higher, or $15 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The yield on the bond issue eased to 8.44%, the lowest rates on a benchmark 30-year bond since February.

Some economists were clearly alarmed by the weak employment report. They fear that a sluggish economy could prompt a wave of corporate bankruptcies (especially among highly leveraged companies), exacerbate the credit crunch that appears to be brewing and thwart the government's savings-and-loan bailout efforts. They say the Fed's only recourse is to lower interest rates.

"Only two months ago, some of the Fed Governors dissented from a vote of unchanged policy for tighter policy; this new set of data may convince the Hawks to at least swing back to middle ground while some of the other members who would have welcomed additional easing may now be wringing their hands with worry," said economist Maria Fiorini Ramirez.

Joseph Liro, an economist at S.G. Warburg Securities Inc., said it's likely that the Fed will vote to ease credit conditions in July at the next Federal Open Market Committee meeting. Mr. Liro said the Fed will reduce rates because the next batch of economic data-consumer prices, commodity prices, retail sales and industrial products -- will reveal weakness and confirm the findings in the employment report.

"If the Fed gets confirmation, that will set the stage for a 1/4 percentage point decline in the federal-funds rate," to about 8%, said Mr. Liro.

But economists at Salomon Brothers Inc., James Fralick at Morgan Stanley & Co. and several others aren't ready to hit the recession panic button just yet -- and they don't think the Fed will either. These economists say that Fed officials will use the slow economy to fight inflation.

"As long as they can continue to keep (real gross national product) growing between 1.5% and 2%, they can continue to damp inflationary pressures," said Mr. Fralick. "That is their stated objective and that is their goal."

Donald Fine, chief market analyst at Chase Manhattan Bank, said that if the Fed eases now, "they run the risk of having the economy pick up more than they want." Mr. Fine said too many people are overlooking signs that the industrial sector is picking up strength.

One such indicator is an index compiled by the National Association of Purchasing Managers. The index, which measures overall health in the manufacturing industry, rose to 50.7% during the month, the highest reading since April 1989. The association says a reading above 50% signals the manufacturing economy is generally growing. Last month the index increased to 50.2%, the first time it had crossed the 50% threshold since May 1989.

Mr. Fralick notes that even the employment report contained a few signs of economic strength. Although employment in the key services and manufacturing sectors declined, the number of hours worked in the manufacturing industry rose. That suggests that manufacturers could be gearing up again, although with fewer employees.

Friday's Market Activity

Prices of Treasury bonds surged Friday on speculation that interest rates are heading lower.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 103 8/32 to yield 8.44%, compared with Thursday's price of 101 24/ 32 to yield 8.57%.

Short-term rates were also lower. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.70% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.94%, down from 7.76% to yield 8.00% on Thursday. Rates on six month bills ended at 7.62% to yield 8.01% , down from 7.74% to yield 8.15% on Thursday.

In the corporate market, falling rates prompted a handful of corporations to issue new bonds.

More than $800 million of new debt was sold to the public, including a total of $425 million of notes issued by Dayton Hudson Corp. and Bellsouth Savings & Employee Stock Ownership Trust.

In trading, most active investment-grade corporate bonds surged as much as 1 1/4 points in tandem with gains in the Treasury market.

Meanwhile, rising stock prices helped boost junk bonds, which are speculative-grade corporate securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all.

Most actively traded cash-paying junk bonds climbed 1/4 point.

The pay-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc., which pay interest in the form of additional securities instead of cash, also advanced strongly. RJR Nabisco's 14.07% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, finished 5/8 point higher to close at 84 7/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely held RJR issue, the 14.07% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, ended 1 1/4 points higher to close at 71.

Despite the overall strength in the market, junk bonds of Southland Corp. fell. Southland's 16 3/4% subordinated debentures, maturing in 2002, shed 1/2 point to close at 4. After the market closed Friday, Southland said it won't make the June 15 pay-in-kind stock dividend on its 15% cumulative exchangeable preferred stock.
