Economy: Jobless Rate Fell To 5.3% in May Due to Census --- But Private Sector Hiring Is Stagnant, Data Show, As Economy Stays Weak

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 06/04/90

WASHINGTON -- Growth in private employment has nearly come to a halt in the past three months, suggesting widespread stagnation in the U.S. economy. The Labor Department's latest job statistics paint virtually the weakest private employment picture since the economy began expanding in December 1982.

Payrolls in private industry barely grew in May, after shrinking by 125,000 workers in April and staying essentially flat in March, the department said. But because the government hired 145,000 temporary census workers in May, the nation's civilian unemployment rate declined a notch, to 5.3% of the work force from 5.4% in April. Including the military, the unemployment rate held steady at 5.3% last month.

"I believe that labor market developments over recent months are now fairly clear," the commissioner of labor statistics, Janet Norwood, told Congress after the report's release. "Employment growth has been very slow, and what growth there has been was concentrated in a very few industries. The job market has been bolstered only by census hiring."

The job figures, which provided the government's first reading of the country's economic strength in May, signaled continued frailty in the U.S. economy, economists said. But whether the stagnation in private employment will persist remains in question, and few economists yet believe the country is on the verge of a recession.

"The economy, though not in danger of a recession, is very flat," said Lynn Reaser, senior economist at First Interstate Bancorp in Los Angeles.

Most economists view employment as a lagging indicator of economic strength because many companies are loath to trim their work forces until they are certain that conditions require it.

An index compiled by a private group, the National Association of Purchasing Managers, suggested that manufacturing, the weakest spot in the U.S. economy for some time, could be headed up.

While the purchasing managers, like the government, reported that manufacturing employment declined in May, the group's index of overall manufacturing health rose to 50.7% during the month, the highest reading since April 1989.

The association says a reading above 50% signals the manufacturing economy is generally growing. In April the index increased to 50.2%, the first time it had crossed the 50% threshold since May 1989.

Despite the further improvement last month, Robert J. Bretz of Pitney Bowes Inc., the association's chairman, said the reading "does not necessarily say we're going to race off into another overheated economy." He concluded, "It's still very modest growth."

Though the purchasing managers' index is merely based on survey responses from purchasing executives in about 300 companies, the financial markets watch it closely because it is one of the first measures of economic activity for any month.

The new employment report also contained a few hints of vim in manufacturing. The average factory employee worked 0.4 hour a week longer than in the month before. "It's surprising to see that big of an increase," said Alan Levenson, director of financial services at the WEFA Group, an economic forecasting firm in Bala Cynwyd, Pa. "To me it's a signal that we've still got some reasonable order backlogs, so we're still going to get some decent output growth in current quarter."

On the other hand the employment statistics indicated that softness is seeping into many areas of the economy, after being concentrated in heavy manufacturing for much of last year.

In manufacturing, the textile, apparel and food-processing industries all saw job losses in May, Mrs. Norwood told Congress. The auto industry, which has been one of the country's weakest in the past year, saw payrolls dwindle further, though the loss was slimmer than the previous month.

Construction employment failed to meet seasonal expectations. In the service sector, job levels were virtually flat in transportation, finance and insurance. Retail trade employment shrank.

"In the service-producing sector, only health services has had steady and strong growth so far this year," Mrs. Norwood said. "In contrast, retail trade has been experiencing by far its most sluggish employment period of this long economic expansion, with no net job gains since January."

After slowing sharply last year, the economy appeared to pick up a bit at the start of this year, aided partly by unseasonably warm weather. But the government reported declines in industrial production, factory orders, and housing construction and sales in April. With the May employment report, Stephen Morrell, chief economist at Southeast Bank in Miami, said, "It's clear now that some of the increases we had {at the beginning of the year} were just temporary, due to seasonal factors and warm weather, but not sustainable."

As further evidence of economic softness, the Commerce Department also reported that spending on construction projects declined 0.7% in April, to $432.4 billion, after falling 0.7% in March as well. Spending on residential building dipped 0.3%, while spending on office, industrial and other types of non-residential construction grew 0.6%.

The numbers in all the reports were adjusted for normal seasonal fluctuations.
