What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/04/90

DONALD TRUMP is in intense talks with bank creditors that could force him to give up big chunks of his empire. The bankers, concerned about the developer's critical cash shortage, are likely to seek asset sales, new management and a much more conservative operating style. ---

The Dow Jones industrials closed above 2900 for the first time Friday as stocks and bonds surged in anticipation of falling interest rates. The stock average rose 24.31 to 2900.97. Last week's record-setting equity prices also reflect rising expectations for 1990 and 1991 corporate earnings.

---

Private-sector payrolls barely grew in May after shrinking in April, the government said. But temporary census hiring lowered the civilian unemployment rate to 5.3% from 5.4%. Meanwhile, the purchasing managers' index of manufacturing health rose to its highest level since April 1989.

The soft jobs picture is leading economists to prune forecasts of second-quarter gross national product.

---

The Soviet Union is depositing large amounts of gold with Western banks as collateral for loans. Trade creditors owed hundreds of millions of dollars have been hounding the cashpoor Soviets for overdue payments.

---

Chevron and the Soviets agreed to study the feasibility of jointly developing the huge Tengiz oil field. It could be the biggest U.S.-Soviet venture yet.

---

Steel prices are rising, leading some firms to restart blast furnaces and to recall workers laid off last fall. The rebound reflects brisk demand -- especially auto-related -- higher raw-material costs, and falling imports.

---

Hitachi will unveil a mainframe computer this week far more powerful than those on the market, boosting the Japanese company's bid to become a major player in the U.S.

---

Manville Corp. was ordered back to bankruptcy court by two judges who urged changes in the funding and operation of a trust that the company formed to pay asbestos claims.

---

L.A. Gear disclosed it will post its first quarterly drop in profit in three years. The news sent the sneaker maker's stock plunging 22% to $30.75.

---

Japan's current-account and overall trade surpluses narrowed sharply in April. Tokyo is considering altering its policy of reducing the surpluses.

---

The failure of Western S&L will add $1.7 billion to the thrift-bailout cost, more than any other single rescue. BankAmerica bought the Phoenix thrift's deposits, branches and certain other assets for $81 million.

---

Esprit's married founders settled their feud as Doug Tompkins agreed to quit as image director and sell his 50% stake in the apparel firm's U.S. operations to Susie Tompkins.
