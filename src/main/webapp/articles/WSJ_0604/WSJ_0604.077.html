Comfed Bancorp Discloses Big Loss; Chairman to Quit

  • Author: John R. Wilke and Diane Tracy
  • Date: 06/04/90

Comfed Bancorp Inc. of Lowell, Mass., disclosed a huge loss, announced that its chairman would resign and said its auditors are expected to question its ability to continue as a going concern. Separately, Home National Bank, the main unit of Milford, Mass.-based Home National Corp., became the third Massachusetts financial institution to be taken over by regulators in the past five weeks.

Comfed, with assets of $1.4 billion, reported a net loss of $38.2 million, or $4.57 a share, for the fourth quarter ended March 31, compared with a loss of $3.5 million, or 42 cents a share, a year ago.

In a statement, Comfed said that "as is customary with banks with negative tangible capital," it expects to receive a report from its auditors expressing doubt about its ability to continue as a going concern. The company said it is subject to a range of potential regulatory actions that may be imposed by the Office of Thrift Supervision, including conservatorship or receivership, which "would likely cause a total loss of stockholders' investment."

Comfed attributed the losses to "the continued deterioration of residential construction, income property and mortgage loan portfolios," and a substantial provision for estimated losses on loans. Because the declining real-estate market has further eroded the bank's ability to recover its assets, provisions for estimated losses of $42.6 million were added during the most recent quarter.

The company also announced that Jefferson F. Allen, chairman, director and chief executive officer of the company and its subsidiary, Comfed Savings Bank, would resign, effective June 30. The company named Jack Zoeller, president of Comfed Savings and Comfed Mortgage Co., to succeed Mr. Allen as president and chief executive of Comfed.

For the full year, Comfed's net loss was $47.9 million, or $5.73 a share, compared with a year-earlier loss of $3.5 million, or 42 cents a share.

The Home National Bank was declared insolvent Friday by the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, with the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. as receiver. The failed bank's 10 branch offices will reopen today as branches of BayBanks Inc.'s BayBank Middlesex subsidiary, which will assume Home National's $440 million in deposits.

The BayBanks unit has agreed to pay the FDIC a purchase premium of $2 million. The unit will also purchase Home National's cash and securities and will have options to buy other assets later.

To facilitate the transaction, the FDIC said it will advance the BayBanks unit $350 million and the FDIC will retain Home National assets with a book value of about $380 million.

Two weeks ago, Merchants Capital Corp.'s main unit, MerchantsBank of Boston, was declared insolvent and its $340 million in deposits assumed by Bank of Boston Corp. And in late April, federal thrift regulators took control of Home Owners Savings Bank in Burlington, Mass.

Scores of New England financial institutions have been wounded by the region's slumping real-estate market, and more failures are expected.

In a statement regarding Home National, the comptroller's office said it noted a "substantial deterioration" in the bank's loan portfolio in 1987, "due to liberal lending practices and weak supervision by both the board of directors and management."

The statement said, "The OCC obtained a formal commitment of the board to address the bank's problems. The board subsequently replaced management, but a downturn in the local economy exacerbated the problems in the bank's loan portfolio."

The agency said the bank's board attempted to raise capital as losses mounted, but was unsuccessful. "Losses finally exhausted the bank's capital, resulting in insolvency," the comptroller's office said.
