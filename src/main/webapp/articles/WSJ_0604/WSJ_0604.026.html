International: Alan Bond Loses Control of TV, Radio Empire --- Kerry Packer Will Regain Australia Media Concern 3 Years After Selling It

  • Author: S. Karene Witcher and Tony Patrick
  • Date: 06/04/90

SYDNEY, Australia -- Alan Bond's ambitions as a media mogul were dashed during the weekend when the Australian entrepreneur lost control of his nationwide television and radio empire. Early Saturday, directors of Bond Media Ltd. agreed to turn over control of the company to Kerry Packer, a publisher and investor, who is the richest person in Australia.

Mr. Packer sold the top-rated Channel Nine television network to Mr. Bond in 1987 for about A$1 billion (US$767.7 million). If Saturday's complex transaction is approved by regulators, bank lenders and Bond Media shareholders, Mr. Packer will regain control of the network at a fraction of the price he sold it for.

The agreement came two days after two units of General Electric Co., of Fairfield, Conn., offered to inject funds into the debt-burdened company in return for a stake of about 15%.

The GE units -- National Broadcasting Co. and GE Capital Corp. -- proposed to join Hellman & Friedman, a funds manager, in investing A$200 million. Bond Media directors Friday rebuffed the offer, saying it was "highly conditional." But they said they would consider any revised version.

Warren Jones, chairman of Bond Media, said Sunday on Channel Nine that he will continue talks with the U.S. group, although the chances "are slim" that a U.S. plan will supersede Mr. Packer's.

Mr. Packer's agreement with Bond Media came about because his closely held Consolidated Press Holdings Ltd. is owed A$200 million from when Mr. Bond bought Channel Nine. Mr. Packer demanded repayment of the debt and threatened court action to liquidate Bond Media.

Under Saturday's accord, Mr. Packer will convert his debt -- held in the form of preference shares and overdue interest -- into ordinary shares of Bond Media. That effectively gives him nearly 60% of the company, Mr. Jones said.

Bond Corp. Holdings, Mr. Bond's floundering flagship company, will see its interest in the media concern diluted to about 9% from 52%. A Bond family company will hold 3%, while Bell Resources Ltd. -- run by Mr. Bond until he lost control of its board in December -- will own as much as 20%. Minority shareholders will hold the rest.

The proposal involves a reconstruction of Bond Media's shares and an issue of rights and options. Mr. Packer receives control of the board and will become chairman of the company in August. The operation will chop A$300 million off Bond Media's debt, which early this year was stated at A$699 million. A sale of radio stations will further lower the figure.

Bond Media stock was sold to investors in 1987 at A$1.55 a share. It closed Friday at 14 Australian cents.

Mr. Jones said directors didn't have much room to maneuver. That became especially true when the Australian government last month limited total foreign ownership in television stations to 20% and the stake of any one foreign investor to 15%.

To ward off Mr. Packer, Bond Media discussed possible capital infusions with NBC, CBS Inc., Paramount Communications Inc., and Television New Zealand Ltd. Mr. Jones said Canberra's decision torpedoed those talks and dropped Bond Media into Mr. Packer's lap.

But he conceded that Bond Media's link to Bond Corp. complicated its efforts to attract funds. If Bond Media had "a stronger parent company," he said, Mr. Packer likely wouldn't have gained control.
