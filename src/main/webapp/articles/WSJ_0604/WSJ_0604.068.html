Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Forecasts for the Second-Quarter GNP Trimmed, Given Soft Data on May Jobs

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 06/04/90

NEW YORK -- Economists are pruning second-quarter gross national product forecasts, in light of flat May payrolls. Data jockeys are particularly concerned about slowing in the service sector, which so far has managed to buoy the economy, while manufacturing, construction and business spending flagged. The outlook for GNP, or the value of the total output of the nation's goods and services, wasn't very hearty before the jobs report appeared: The consensus called for annual growth of about 2% in the current quarter. MMS International Inc., a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., said it lowered its own GNP estimate to 1.8% from 2.3%, in the wake of last Friday's weaker-than-expected employment numbers.

"The economy looks increasingly unbalanced," Donald Straszheim, chief economist at Merrill Lynch & Co., said. "Trade's the only thing holding it up." Mr. Straszheim had been predicting an annual growth rate of 2.7% for second-quarter GNP. "That looks too high {now}. We'll have to mark it down," he said, declining to pinpoint the markdown yet. "It's going to be more than a haircut, but {GNP} will still be a positive number." He is sticking to his weak-but-no-recession outlook, but noted that some other economists may cross over into the negative-GNP range this quarter, on the basis of recent soft data. Economists in the positive camp said that, in addition to decent export levels, consumer spending appears to be besting its first-quarter average, at least so far this period.

But David Resler, chief economist at Nomura Securities Ltd., New York, questioned whether softness in May service jobs presages softness in consumer spending. "Consumers may be cutting back on discretionary services," he said. "If they stop spending, the economy is in deep trouble."

Analysts get little help in foretelling spending patterns from this week's thin calendar: May auto sales and April consumer credit. Car sales are believed to have been weak again, and consumer credit is expected to have increased by about $2.3 billion, following March's $2.6 billion gain.

Through April at least, consumers were besting the average they spent in the first quarter, Nomura's Mr. Resler said. But he added, "Consumer spending and trade hold the key for keeping the economy out of recession." Mr. Resler, who had been predicting a 2% growth rate for GNP in the current quarter, said he "will notch it down, maybe a half a percentage point or so."

Morgan Stanley & Co. economist Stephen S. Roach also lowered his GNP outlook, in the wake of May's employment data. "Real GNP growth in the current quarter now seems likely to be closer to the 1.5% to 2% range, rather than the 3% figure we had been carrying," he said in his weekly newsletter.

"Trends in the service sector are particularly disturbing," Mr. Roach said. He noted that that during the first five months of this year, job growth in private, service-producing industries has averaged only 118,000 -- about 25% below the average pace for the second half of last year. Economists noted that while the jobs report was soft, they expect slightly higher May industrial production, largely owing to overtime worked in the auto industry.

The weaker employment data last Friday all but swamped any positive reaction to a stronger report from the nation's purchasers. In fact, Mr. Straszheim of Merrill Lynch found something most discouraging in the purchasing managers' report: lower inventories in May. The decline may indicate that manufacturers are operating with tighter and tighter inventory these days, through better control methods. If so, the building-for-inventory boost that many economists expect to help second-quarter GNP isn't realistic.

The National Association of Purchasing Management said the May inventories index fell "significantly" to its lowest level since January 1983.
