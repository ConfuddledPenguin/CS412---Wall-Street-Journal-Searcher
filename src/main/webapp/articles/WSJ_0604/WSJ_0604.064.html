Movies: Hollywood Turns to Russia, With Love

  • Author: Kathleen A. Hughes
  • Date: 06/04/90

Hollywood producers are turning Red Square into their latest back lot, and they have the blessing of the Soviet Union's top mogul. In fact, when Mikhail Gorbachev chatted with entertainment magnate Ted Turner, actress Jane Fonda and physician Robert Gale during a lunch at the Soviet Embassy in Washington last week, they talked a bit about movies made in the U.S.S.R.

It was a timely subject. Last Wednesday in Moscow, Carolco Pictures Inc. and Turner Network Television began filming "The Final Warning," a television movie about Dr. Gale (played by John Voight), who treated radiation victims after the nuclear accident at Chernobyl.

"I told Gorbachev the film was going very well," Dr. Gale says. "Jane told him what it was like to make the first co-production with the Soviet Union in 1976 {`The Blue Bird'}. It's light-years of difference now. We're proceeding without interference."

The Soviet movie industry is changing rapidly in the wake of glasnost and perestroika. In a bid for hard currency, Soviet film companies have been aggressively trying to lure more big-spending Hollywood film crews inside Soviet borders for co-productions and other types of service agreements. And more producers are starting to take the bait.

Soyuzkinoservice, a Soviet film finance, production and distribution company also known as SKS, even opened a new office in Hollywood last year, the USSR Film Service Corp., as a joint venture. To make its presence known, it held gala parties in October and February. More than 800 movie types, including Mr. Voight and producer Paul Maslansky, went to the latest party and were courted with vodka, caviar and blinis.

Progress has been somewhat slow. After a year, the USSR Film Service, headed by Soviet-born Yuri Spilny, has negotiated or is in the process of negotiating 10 co-ventures or service agreements for movie projects. There are two projects on Chernobyl in the works, a TV miniseries about Leon Trotsky, a contemporary version of "Crime and Punishment," a movie about the Nazi siege of Leningrad and a science-fiction project, among others.

What are the Soviets offering? Locations such as Red Square and the Winter Palace, a lot of extras at very low prices, military equipment, period costumes, translators, hard-to-get hotel reservations, Soviet production teams.

In exchange, they typically request hard currency and distribution rights in the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe. In some cases, a cut of the profit. They are also upgrading their rental equipment, and they want to train their own film makers in special effects.

For "The Final Warning," SKS is providing the use of a nuclear power plant 500 miles east of Moscow, Soviet army troops, helicopters, equipment and crew for around $1 million. The Soviet Union will also get the distribution rights in the U.S.S.R. and Eastern Europe.

Co-productions between the Soviets and U.S. film makers aren't entirely new. The Soviets set up SKS 20 years ago, and several U.S. movies, such as "Blue Bird" and "Red Heat," have been filmed in Russia. But before glasnost, the Soviet bureaucracy made it difficult to get movies made. There are legendary stories in Hollywood about films brought to a screeching halt by KGB agents and customs officials.

In the past two years, the largest of the Soviet Union's 39 movie studios have been decentralized and are now allowed to negotiate deals on their own to earn some profits. So a few are now competing with SKS to provide film services.

"The Soviet Union is starting to look a lot like Hollywood," says Ilmar Taska, director of Soviet projects for Fries Entertainment. "New companies are growing like mushrooms in the rain."

The mostly independent producers who are starting negotiations are wary of the uncharted territory. "It's a whole new world with the Soviets. The rules are changing literally as we speak," says Bob O'Connor, who is talking to USSR Film Service about making a contemporary version of "Crime and Punishment" in Leningrad. "You're a babe in the woods."

Some recent veterans say negotiating a contract in the Soviet Union can be slow going. The Soviets also lack much of the equipment that Hollywood producers take for granted, such as a large number of portable toilets, dressing rooms and big hotel suites. Just making a phone call from the Soviet Union to the U.S. or Europe often requires a reservation and may take hours.

Dr. Gale says that in working on "Final Warning," he suggested actors use an ophthalmoscope, the flashlight device doctors use to examine eyes. "In Los Angeles we would have had one in two seconds, but there it became a major undertaking," he says. A number of doctors contacted didn't have one, and when the crew found a doctor who did, he wasn't eager to part with it for a day.

One recently completed film, "The Russia House," a Soviet love/spy story starring Sean Connery and Michelle Pfeiffer, is scheduled for release by Pathe Communications Co. later this year. Mr. Maslansky, producer of the $20 million movie, says he negotiated the film services he needed through Corona Film, a private company that worked with Mosfilm Studios, one of the main Soviet studios. Mr. Maslansky says it took about 4 1/2 weeks to iron out the details of what the Soviets would provide, and the process was "arduous and rather complex."

He finally agreed to pay about 2.8 million rubles (in dollars at a rate he won't disclose) in exchange for Soviet production services, including hotel reservations, transportation, technical personnel and some equipment. But most of the movie equipment he needed just wasn't available in the Soviet Union. So Mr. Maslansky brought in about 40 vehicles, including portable dressing rooms and toilets, along with trucks for electrical equipment.

The producer also flew in a caterer from England to survey food supplies. The caterer concluded that "we better bring everything in," the producer says. So Mr. Maslansky brought in four catering trucks with enough beef, frozen vegetables and fruit to feed 120 crew members breakfast and lunch for about two weeks.

Despite the physical obstacles, Mr. Maslansky describes the Russian production team as "first rate." And he says the filming "rates as one of the best film experiences I have had." He also notes: "You go to the Soviet Union for the locations. You don't go to save money. . . . It's terribly difficult to get a movie done, but everything is possible."

For others, negotiations within the Soviet studio system can become confusing. Fries Entertainment agreed to make a movie called "In Siberian Captivity" with a Soviet director; Fries later found that the director had moved the project from one studio to another, requiring renegotiation of services. In addition, the director kept asking for "more and more money." At one point, "It wasn't clear who had the rights to the project," says Fries's Mr. Taska. Fries finally dropped the project at the end of last year. "It wasn't a workable situation," says Mr. Taska. Fries still plans to produce two other movies in the Soviet Union, and Mr. Taska says: "Generally those talks have been satisfactory. It's not always such a problem."

The Soviets have a more active role in actual co-productions, such as "900 Days: The Siege of Leningrad," a movie about the Nazi effort to take the city. Alberto Grimaldi, a producer who negotiated through USSR Film Service and SKS, says the Soviets are going to provide "everything necessary to film the movie," including tanks, armies and crowds. In exchange, the Soviets will get 40% of the net income, plus distribution rights in Eastern Europe, India and the U.S.S.R.

Producers have been pleasantly surprised by the Soviet willingness to participate in projects that might seem controversial. Producer Josef Shaftel says he met with USSR Film Service about making a four-hour miniseries on Trotsky. "They flipped out over the idea," he says. Mr. Shaftel, who made an earlier movie on Trotsky, says the Soviets agreed to provide a Russian writer who is translating previously undisclosed material from historical archives on Stalin, Lenin and Trotsky.

The Soviets are also paying for living expenses in the Soviet Union for more than a week for a research trip for Mr. Shaftel, his wife and a writer. "With the opening of the archives, they are being very honest and open. It's extraordinary," says Mr. Shaftel. "Now I have access to material I didn't have 20 years ago."

As Hollywood interest grows, some film executives expect that the Soviets may become less generous in their negotiations. "The dollar has a lot of value right now, and the terms are affordable and interesting for Americans," says Mr. Taska. "But as the Soviets enter the world market, their prices may catch up. They know how much films cost in Hollywood and France."
