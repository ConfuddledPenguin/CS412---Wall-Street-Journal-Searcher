Letters to the Editor: None Dare Call It Art

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/31/90

Irving Kristol is correct ("It's Obscene but Is It Art?" editorial page, Aug. 7) that the debate about "censorship" has been used as a smoke screen to avoid discussion of what art should receive the national endorsement that National Endowment for the Arts funding provides. The NEA and most private entities funding art are dominated by modernist art "professionals." This view of art is founded on the notion that there is no reality exterior to the self; whatever is called art by an artist is art. In its denial that judgment is even possible, this view fosters precisely the iconoclasm we are seeing. In arts-establishment parlance, "only the initiated can understand" -- the initiated being those who deny that art has intentional meaning and can be assessed independently of the artist. This is today's "arts community."

One practical consequence of the embrace of this set of delusions is that the NEA routinely denies applications from such entities as the New York Academy of Art, the nation's only graduate school dedicated to the teaching of the classical figurative disciplines and techniques that informed fine-arts tradition during the 25 centuries prior to our own. According to the NEA summary of its panelists' comments on the school's 1989 application to its grant program, the "Panel disagreed with basic philosophical impetus of the application for being too rigidly modeled on European academicism with no acknowledgment of 20th-century concepts of Modernism. It was feared that this revisionist approach would stifle creativity in young artists."

Unless conservatives and liberals alike start addressing the validity of the modernist/deconstructionist assumptions, we shall continue the current progression toward complete obliteration of expression grounded in the Western Judeo-Christian tradition.

Elizabeth B. Lurie

President

W.H. Brady Foundation

Maggie Valley, N.C.

---

The controversies on tax-supported pornography at the NEA have obscured a much more alarming development. Projected budgets for fiscal 1992 indicate that, for the first time, a larger proportion of the agency's budget will support the infrastructure of the arts than the artists themselves. Somewhat under 50% -- approximately 47% in one projection -- of the agency's budget will go to the artists, more than half of the budget -- about 53% in the same projection -- will pay for support activities. The NEA is spending more on ancillary activities, e.g., organizations that service the arts, paying for mayors to meet and talk about the arts, and less on the artists themselves. The main issue is just how much direct and immediate good the NEA does for the working artists, obscene or otherwise.

Jacob Neusner

Member, National Council on the Arts, 1984-90

(Advisory Council of the National Endowment for the Arts)

St. Petersburg, Fla.
