Cathay Pacific Profit Rose 4.4% in 1st Half

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/31/90

HONG KONG -- Cathay Pacific Airways said first-half profit rose 4.4%, but the airline warned that the Persian Gulf crisis will likely hurt results for the rest of the year. The Hong Kong-based airline said profit rose to 1.42 billion Hong Kong dollars (US$182.7 million), or 49.5 Hong Kong cents a share, from HK$1.36 billion, or 47.6 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue climbed 13% to HK$9.36 billion from HK$8.26 billion.

Cathay's board proposed keeping its interim dividend at 10.5 cents.

D.A. Gledhill, the group's chairman, said results were hurt by the colony' slowdown in tourism and labor shortages. He said second-half results are expected to be hurt by higher energy costs and a likely fall in demand for passenger and cargo services. That could lead to the full-year profit dropping below last year's HK$3.32 billion, he said.

Virtually the entire rise in first-half earnings was accounted for by a lower tax bill compared with the same period a year earlier, as operating costs were inflated by higher expenses for personnel and fuel. On a pretax basis, profit was virtually unchanged at HK$1.6 billion from HK$1.59 billion.

The group was particularly hard-hit by a sixfold rise in net finance charges to HK$154 million from HK$25 million, as weak bond markets resulted in a lower return on its invested funds.

Cathay said it suffered marginally lower load factors for both passengers and cargo, although its flight capacity rose briskly because of four aircraft acquired in the first six months, which brought its total fleet to 40 aircraft.

While the airline carried 14% more passengers and 6% more cargo than a year ago, these gains were offset by higher operating costs, the company said.
