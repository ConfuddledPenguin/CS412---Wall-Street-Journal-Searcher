World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Are Sharply Higher on Positive View Of Discount Rate Boost; London Shares Also Rise

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 08/31/90

Tokyo stock prices finished sharply higher Thursday, reflecting a perception by participants that the discount rate boost in the morning was a positive factor for the market as it eliminated long-existing concerns about the timing of the action. Shares were up at London and in most other markets.

As buying centering on speculative shares gradually spread to blue-chip issues, Tokyo stock prices moved up strongly late in the afternoon session, helped by dealer and corporate buying, traders said.

The Nikkei index of 225 selected issues was up 775.17 points at 25669.96. The index lost 815.97 points Wednesday.

In Tokyo Friday, the Nikkei index surged 244.49 points to close the morning session at 25914.45.

Thursday's volume on the first section was estimated at 530 million shares, up from 430 million Wednesday.

Advancing issues outnumbered declining issues, 791 to 211, while 99 remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index (Topix) of all issues listed in the first section, which lost 36.09 points Wednesday, was up 44.60 points, or 2.33%, at 1956.02.

The second section index, which fell 4.96 points Wednesday, was up 83.46 points, or 2.26%, to close at 3770.84. Volume in the second section was 11 million shares, up from 9.8 million Wednesday.

The Bank of Japan raised its official discount rate -- the amount the central bank charges on loans to financial institutions -- by 0.75 percentage point, to 6%, 20 minutes before the market opened.

Market participants said the 0.75 percentage point increase had already been discounted at current stock prices.

The Nikkei index gained 380 points within 20 minutes of the opening. Stock prices dropped later in the morning session, however, because of speculation that another increase could follow if Middle East unrest and further oil price rises cause inflation to accelerate.

After finishing the morning session little changed from Wednesday's close, stock prices gradually climbed in the afternoon session, encouraged by the firmness of the bond market, traders said.

At first the pace of gains was set by speculative shares. But as the target of buying activity widened to blue-chip shares, the Nikkei index was pushed up strongly during the last hour of trading.

Many participants expressed uncertainty about whether domestic interest rates will peak out soon.

But growing expectations that the Mideast crisis might be solved without an outbreak of hostilities, together with a decline in oil prices this week, calmed investors' concerns that the Persian Gulf situation will prompt another interest rate increase, observers said.

Takatoshi Okuyama, general manager of the investment advisory department at Daiwa Securities, said market participants, who responded only to bad news from the Middle East over the past few weeks, have gradually been turning their eyes to positive factors.

Mr. Okuyama said the decision by a majority of Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries members to sanction greater oil production has convinced many investors that stocks have been oversold.

Investors were also expecting fruitful negotiations at the scheduled meeting between United Nations chief De Cuellar and the Iraqi foreign minister later Thursday.

Shin Tokoi, general manager of stock trading at County NatWest Securities, said some of his clients with high cash positions are ready to buy on Friday if any positive news came out of the meeting.

Traders said, however, that institutions are for the most part major potential sellers when stocks rebound further.

They are unlikely to buy stocks actively ahead of the November release of corporate earnings reports, said Masami Okuma, a senior trader at UBS Phillips & Drew International.

Concern about the impact of higher interest rates and higher oil prices on corporate earnings is discouraging institutions from buying stocks now, he said.

Stocks that appeared to be targets of speculative buying collected buy orders from dealers and corporate investors who tried to take wide gains in a short term.

These shares were attractive, as they could move up regardless of changes in the market environment, traders said.

Among them was Kurabo, which surged 260 yen ($1.81) to 1,820 yen. Ube Industries was up 100 to 731 and Honshu Paper rose 260 to 4,990.

High-priced shares gained widely, with Sony advancing 290 to 7,550 and Kyocera up 260 to 7,790.

Hitachi finished at 1,390, up 40, after a tug of war between brokerage house buying and profit-taking by institutions.

Toyota Motor added 50 to 2,050, while Shimizu Corp. rose 80 to 1,680.

London share prices closed higher Thursday, but below the day's best levels, as uncertainty over the Middle East crisis eased its grip on the London market.

Wall Street's steady decline from its opening shook some of the strength from the London market, which reached its peak near midday.

But despite the late-afternoon softening, one trader said he expects the London market to find its resistance level soon, following the marked decline in London stocks over the past three weeks.

At the close, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index was up 27.9 points at 2153.6. The day's high was 2164.1, and its low 2139.6.

The narrower FT 30-share index closed up 29 points at 1687.9.

Volume was 382.7 million shares, well above Wednesday's 297.3 million.

In the futures markets, the September 100 index contract was trading at 2179, up from Wednesday's close of 2142.

Thursday's stronger performance by the dollar and the expectation of an imminent easing in U.K. interest rates helped international stocks. The pound fell to $1.9225 Thursday from $1.9475.

Among overseas earners, Glaxo rose 14 pence (27 cents) to close at 736 pence, and SmithKline Beecham jumped 18 to 523.

Brewing stocks, which have recently been under pressure, had a strong day. Bass shares rose 4 pence to #10.53 ($20.24); Allied Lyons leapt 18 pence to 490 pence; shares in Guinness climbed 28 pence to close at 703.

Following an 11% profit-forecast downgrading by County Natwest, B.A.T Industries fell 5 pence to 529. The broker said B.A.T is likely to have pretax profit of #1.58 billion in the latest year, down from the #1.76 billion previously forecast.

Elsewhere, stock prices closed higher in Paris, Zurich, Brussels, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Madrid and Milan and mixed in Frankfurt. South African gold stocks closed higher.

Stocks closed higher in Sydney, Hong Kong, Singapore and Wellington, and lower in Seoul and Manila. Taipei was closed.
