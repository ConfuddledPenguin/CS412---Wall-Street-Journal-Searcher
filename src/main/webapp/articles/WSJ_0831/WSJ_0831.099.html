Technology & Health: Repetitive Motion Injuries Are Target Of OSHA Program

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 08/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The Labor Department said it began a major program to reduce injuries to the arm and wrist and other disorders common to repeated-motion jobs. As a first step, the department's Occupational Safety and Health Administration issued guidelines for preventing such ailments in the meatpacking industry. OSHA will seek agreements with 16 large packers with more than 1,000 workers each to carry out those guidelines. The agency will conduct special inspections of large plants if the companies refuse to sign such pacts. Inspections would begin in January.

Afterward, similar agreements may be sought with 20 packing companies that have 500 to 1,000 workers each, with inspections planned for non-signers. The inspections could lead to OSHA citations for violating health standards.

In addition, the agency said it will soon begin rule-making proceedings aimed at upgrading rules on repetitive-motion injuries in a wide range of industries. It would take several years to develop such standards, Labor Secretary Elizabeth Dole said.

She said the meat industry, which employs 140,000 workers, will get first attention because its rates of "ergonomic illnesses" are at least 10 times those of the general population. As new meatpacking technology puts more emphasis on repeated-motion tasks under stressful conditions, "the problem has been getting worse," Mrs. Dole said.

Ergonomics involves adapting the workplace environment to the needs of workers, rather than trying to make workers adjust to the workplace. Occupational health experts have been finding increased ergonomic hazards in recent years in such industries as meatpacking, auto production and communications. They can cause disorders such as carpal tunnel syndrome, a crippling condition that often afflicts the hands, wrists or arms of meatpacking or other workers. OSHA said that 48% of workplace health disorders involve repetitive motion.

The guideline techniques were developed recently by ergonomists. They include detailed suggestions for analyzing repeated-motion hazards; taking engineering or other steps to reduce or eliminate them; monitoring the health of workers, removing them from the hazardous work and providing timely treatment; and training managers and workers to avoid the ergonomic problems.

The United Food and Commercial Workers union, whose members work on meatpacking lines, praised the guidelines. But Allen Zack, a union spokesman, faulted the plan for continuing to rely on company records to measure how well a plant is eliminating repeated-motion hazards.

"We should have learned our lesson by now," Mr. Zack said, noting criticism of meatpackers by some members of Congress as well as OSHA enforcement actions, both dealing largely with inadequate recordkeeping.

Mr. Zack also complained that packers would be able to avoid inspections by signing agreements with OSHA that won't be subject to close monitoring by the union. By contrast, he cited a 1988 ergonomics agreement that involved the food workers union as well as OSHA and IBP Inc., a big meatpacker in Nebraska. OSHA has signed some other agreements with meatpacking and auto companies since then.

The guidelines were praised by J. Patrick Boyle, president of the American Meat Institute, as using many recommendations made by the trade group's safety committee. "Flexibility is the key to these guidelines. Because they are not rules or standards, plant management can pick and choose as they tailor ergonomic programs to their individual needs," while also "exploring new ways to protect their workers," he said.
