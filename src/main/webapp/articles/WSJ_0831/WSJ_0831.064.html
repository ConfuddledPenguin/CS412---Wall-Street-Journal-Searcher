Marketing & Media: Gannett Co. Names Watson to Head Newspaper Group

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 08/31/90

Gannett Co. named Gary L. Watson president of its newspaper group, the country's largest chain of newspapers, which like most of the newspaper industry, has been battered by widespread declines in advertising. Mr. Watson, 45 years old, will oversee the newly consolidated newspaper group at the Arlington, Va.-based media company. Effective Oct. 1, Gannett will consolidate its Community Newspaper division and its Metro Newspaper division into one newspaper group, bringing together Gannett's 83 small, medium, and large circulation newspapers. However, Mr. Watson won't oversee Gannett's national daily newspaper, USA Today.

A former editor and publisher at several Gannett newspapers, Mr. Watson had been president of the Community Newspaper division. As he has in the past, Mr. Watson will report directly to John J. Curley, Gannett's chairman, president, and chief executive. Brian J. Donnelly, 56, current president of Metro Newspapers, will become executive vice president of the newly consolidated newspaper group, reporting to Mr. Watson.

Daily newspapers are the core of the company and they have suffered in 1990. Gannett remains the largest U.S. newspaper publisher, with total daily circulation of more than six million. But in the second quarter, Gannett reported its first quarterly earnings drop in 22 years. The loss was attributable largely to a drop in retail advertising in virtually all of its local newspaper markets. Through the first half of this year, newspaper advertising revenue declined 2.9%, with falloffs in national, retail, and classified advertising.

Mr. Watson said in an interview he would concentrate on improving news coverage at Gannett papers by increasing newsroom budgets. "We've increased budgets over the years and will continue to," said Mr. Watson. "In the end, that will help us build circulation penetration."

He also said Gannett will increase its number of zoned editions for specific geographical areas, and will expand its experimental publishing of yellow pages directories.

Gannett executives denied published reports that Mr. Watson's appointment was an indication he was being groomed to succeed Mr. Curley. Mimi Feller, Gannett's vice president for public affairs, said Mr. Watson will not be Mr. Curley's "second-in-command," and that no changes will be made among the top corporate officers.

The new newspaper group is a dismantling of changes made under former chairman Allen H. Neuharth. In 1985, as Gannett was acquiring series of large-circulation metropolitan newspapers, Mr. Neuharth created two newspaper divisions. "At the time, new acquisitions {in Des Moines, Iowa and Louisville, Ky.} required special attention and two divisions made sense," Mr. Watson said. "Now its a natural evolution to go back to one division."
