Law -- Legal Beat: Wayne Newton Libel Award Overturned

  • Author: Amy Dockser Marcus and James P. Miller
  • Date: 08/31/90

A federal appeals court overturned a $5.2 million damages award to entertainer Wayne Newton in his long-running libel suit against General Electric Co.'s National Broadcasting Co. unit. The suit is one of a growing number of cases in which public figures sue on grounds that an article or broadcast created a false impression, even if each statement taken separately was true. In a ruling yesterday, the U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals in San Francisco found that most of the factual statements in the broadcast were undisputed and that any false impressions had not been shown to be deliberate or reckless.

The U.S. Supreme Court has ruled that public figures can't win a libel case unless they prove that a statement was false, defamatory and published with knowledge of its falsity or with reckless disregard of whether it was true or false. But Mr. Newton had alleged that he had been defamed by implication and that the reporters must take responsibility for the impressions they intentionally conveyed.

The suit arose out of an Oct. 6, 1980, segment on NBC's evening news titled "Wayne Newton and the Law." Mr. Newton argued that the NBC report created the impression that he had received financial assistance from organized-crime figures to finance the purchase of a hotel. Among other things, he cited the reporters' failure to include in the broadcast the fact that he received a bank loan to finance the purchase.

In December 1986, a jury in a Las Vegas, Nev., federal district court found that the broadcast had created a false impression and awarded Mr. Newton $19.2 million in damages. Judge Myron Crocker, who heard the case, reduced the award to $5.2 million, setting aside the jury's award for loss of income and reducing an award for injury to reputation to $50,000 from $5 million.

"Wayne Newton's case poses the danger that First Amendment values will be subverted by a local jury biased in favor of a prominent local public figure against an alien speaker who criticizes that local hero," the appeals court wrote.

The appeals panel said that NBC could not be held liable even if the broadcast created a false impression as Mr. Newton alleged. The panel said the lower court improperly considered its own view of the supposed false impression instead of the intentions of the journalists who prepared the broadcast. Such an approach, the panel wrote, "would permit liability to be imposed not only for what was not said but also for what was not intended to be said."

The panel did not rule on whether the broadcast as a whole was false. Instead, it based the ruling on the fact that Mr. Newton had failed to show sufficient evidence that the reporters deliberately lied or had been reckless with the truth.

Morton Galane, the Reno, Nev., lawyer who represented Mr. Newton, said he could not comment because he was unable to reach his client.

"This is a case that never should have been brought or gone to a jury in the first place. But the decision reaffirms the willingness of the courts to afford protection in the libel area when the jury system goes haywire," said Floyd Abrams of the New York firm Cahill, Gordon & Reindel, a lawyer for NBC.

RACKETEERING CHARGES FILED against Asian fraternal organization.

A federal grand jury in Chicago indicted the On Leong Chinese Merchants Association, and chapters of the nationwide organization in three U.S. cities, on racketeering, gambling and tax-fraud charges.

The 34-count indictment -- which followed a 30-month investigation -- alleges the operation of what prosecutors call a "nationwide illegal gambling business." On Leong chapters in Chicago, Houston, and New York were also accused in the indictment of operating "casino-style" centers featuring games of chance such as pai gow and fan-tan. Also named as defendants were 29 officials and members of the so-called tong, or association.

Under racketeering law, prosecutors are seeking forfeiture of certain On Leong properties, including the national headquarters in New York, as well as about $11.5 million in proceeds from the alleged gambling operation.

On Leong officials couldn't immediately be reached for comment.

The indictment leans heavily on telephone conversations at the Chicago chapter, which federal agents raided and closed down in 1988.

The indictment also cites conversations between the defendants and Simon Au Yeung, an informant hired by the Federal Bureau of Investigation to penetrate illegal Asian organizations in the U.S. Mr. Au Yeung played a role in the FBI's 1988 investigation.

More recently he worked undercover to assist federal and New York authorities in seizing 850 pounds of Southeast Asian heroin allegedly imported by a different Chinese group.

DECISION BARRING REPORTERS from taking notes during trial is appealed.

Attorneys for the Philadelphia Daily News are appealing a judge's order prohibiting a reporter from taking notes during a first-degree murder trial.

The order, handed down by Philadelphia Common Pleas Judge Edward J. Maier, is thought to be unprecedented. Judge Maier ruled at a hearing in his chambers that he had a "gut feeling" against allowing reporters to take notes, according to a court transcript.

The judge said the notes could be used to challenge the official court record and that the taking of notes could distract jurors.

"It might cause the jury to give greater attention to some testimony rather than other testimony," Judge Maier ruled.

The ruling came after Daily News reporter Joseph P. Blake was told by a court bailiff to stop taking notes on Aug. 20. The judge noted that few reporters ever cover trials in his court but that he has nonetheless forbidden note-taking for 10 years. Allowing reporters to take notes, he said, could lead to allowing tape recorders and cameras.

Courts have generally been protective of the right of spectators to take notes and sketch pictures during trials, particularly news gatherers, said Katherine Hatton, of the Philadelphia law firm Kohn, Savett, Klein & Graf, which represents the Daily News, published by Philadelphia Newspapers Inc., a unit of Knight-Ridder Inc. of Miami.

In 1980 the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that reporters at trials function as "surrogates for the public," and therefore can be entitled under the First Amendment to special seating and priority entry, Ms. Hatton wrote in her appeal to the Pennsylvania Superior Court.

The issue of whether reporters can be barred from taking notes, however, has never come up in a court case, Ms. Hatton said.

SUPERFUND ENFORCEMENT CHIEF is to join law firm.

Lloyd Guerci will leave the Environmental Protection Agency next week to become a partner in the Washington office of the Chicago law firm Mayer, Brown & Platt. Mr. Guerci, who as a career civil servant has extensive experience in toxic and hazardous waste regulation, said he chose Mayer Brown because of its environmental practice, which he hopes to expand.

Mr. Guerci said that his work at the 45-lawyer Washington office will include representing businesses before the EPA on pollution-enforcement matters. Currently, federal law permits an official to lobby his former agency immediately after his departure provided the lobbying does not involve matters in which he was personally and substantially involved, and provided the official's staff was not involved in the matter during his last year on the job.

Tougher restrictions go into effect Jan. 1.
