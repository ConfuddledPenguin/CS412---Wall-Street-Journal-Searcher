Politics & Policy: Texans Use Their Influence With Bush To Open U.S.-Mexico Free-Trade Talks

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 08/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The Texans in the Bush administration have prevailed in a behind-the-scenes struggle to give Mexico a prominent place on the nation's agenda. President Bush's announcement this month that he would soon notify Congress of plans to negotiate a U.S.-Mexico trade pact came as a surprise to many in Washington. They thought the Mexican free-trade negotiations wouldn't begin until late next year.

It also marked a defeat for White House Trade Representative Carla Hills, who will have to negotiate the trade pact. Mrs. Hills, along with Agriculture Secretary Clayton Yeutter, had argued that early negotiations with Mexico could interfere with efforts next year to win congressional support for a new global trade agreement under the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade.

But in making his decision, Mr. Bush, who counts Texas as his adopted home, was influenced by two other highly placed Texans in the administration: Secretary of State James Baker and Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher. Both Mr. Baker and Mr. Mosbacher argued vigorously that a free-trade pact with Mexico should be a top priority for the administration.

The Texans' success in persuading the president suggests that Mexico may get better treatment in the Bush administration than it has in the past.

"It's very obvious in Texas, and I hope that it will become as obvious to the rest of the country, that every time you start a new business in Mexico it creates new jobs in the U.S.," said Mr. Mosbacher. "There's a natural supplier situation that you don't get if you go overseas. The machinery and plant are likely to come from the U.S."

Mr. Baker and Mr. Mosbacher also got an assist from another Texan, Democratic Sen. Lloyd Bentsen, who helped persuade other key members of Congress to support the talks. In order to proceed with the negotiations, the administration wants Congress to authorize a "fast-track" procedure for approving the Mexican agreement.

Ambassador Hills and Secretary Yeutter have argued that the administration should concentrate on the current round of GATT trade talks, leaving the negotiating of a U.S.-Mexico pact to well into next year, after the GATT talks are all squared away.

The so-called Uruguay round of GATT talks, set to end this December, is the most ambitious round of trade talks to date. Begun in the Uruguayan seaside resort of Punta del Este four years ago, the talks aim to extend the world's trade treaty to agriculture, textiles, intellectual property, services and investment. Even after the completion of the GATT talks in December, it will be several more months before the necessary enabling legislation is passed by Congress.

Also opposed to an early start for the Mexican talks was Labor Secretary Elizabeth Dole. Secretary Dole expressed the reservations of organized labor to a U.S.-Mexico pact. The AFL-CIO and other labor groups fear that a trade pact will harm the interests and wage rates of their members. In its latest policy statement on the proposed pact, the AFL-CIO's executive council said "tens of thousands of Americans have lost their jobs as U.S. companies move to take advantage of the poverty of Mexican workers in the absence of any effective regulations on company operations." It maintains that at least 400,000 Mexicans now work for wages of less than $1 an hour.

Earlier this year, House Ways and Means Chairman Dan Rostenkowski also argued against launching U.S.-Mexican negotiations aimed at a trade pact. But a Congressional aide says Mr. Rostenkowski "isn't prepared to fall on his sword over the issue" of the U.S.-Mexico trade pact, although he adds that the Illinois Democrat still is committed to the Uruguay round as the top trade priority.

The Texans make no apologies for their championing of a U.S.-Mexico free-trade pact. "There's no country in my view that is more important" to the U.S. than Mexico, says Mr. Mosbacher. "It's already our third biggest trading partner, and is our biggest contiguous neighbor." The rate of growth of population points to 100 million Mexicans by the end of the century, a huge potential market for U.S. companies, the commerce secretary says.

Mr. Mosbacher says the administration will shortly launch a campaign to show the advantages of a U.S.-Mexico trade pact. If U.S. companies are "going to move plants to somewhere where there's cheaper labor, it's a hell of a lot better if it moves next door where we have the supply advantages," Mr. Mosbacher argues.

In the coming weeks, Mr. Mosbacher and Mexican Commerce Secretary Jaime Serra will make a tour of Los Angeles, Chicago, Houston and New York to drum up U.S. investment and business interest in Mexico.
