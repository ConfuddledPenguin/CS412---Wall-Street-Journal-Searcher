Study Rejects Bush Claim On Capital Gains Tax Cut

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The Congressional Budget Office released a study concluding that cuts in the capital-gains tax "could not be counted on to significantly boost output and increase economic growth." The study runs directly counter to claims by the Bush administration that its proposal to cut the tax on capital gains would give a big boost to the economy and would pay for itself.

The new study was made public by House Ways and Means Committee Chairman Dan Rostenkowski (D., Ill.). In it, the congressional agency reviews eight different quantitative analyses, including two by the CBO itself, of how a capital-gains tax cut would affect savings, investment and growth. Of the eight studies, five -- including both CBO studies -- found that cutting taxes on capital gains is "not likely to increase saving, investment and GNP much if at all."

Mr. Rostenkowski said the study suggests the best way to boost economic growth isn't to cut the capital gains tax, but rather "through successful conclusion of the budget summit which would lower the budget deficit significantly over a five-year period."
