Economy: Factory Orders Showed Increase In July of 1.6%

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 08/31/90

WASHINGTON -- Factory orders rebounded in July, nearly making up for June's decline, the Commerce Department reported. Orders for manufactured goods ranging from chemicals to airplanes grew 1.6% during the month, after falling 1.9% the previous month, the department said. Aircraft orders were particularly strong. But orders fell for motor vehicles, another important U.S. manufacturing industry.

Since the beginning of the year, factory orders frequently have risen one month only to decline the next. Economists view the choppy pattern as an indication of softness and uncertainty in the manufacturing sector.

U.S. manufacturing has been hurt by high interest rates, the result of Federal Reserve efforts to battle inflation.

Most of the increase in factory orders in July came in durable goods industries -- that is, businesses that produce big-ticket items expected to last at least three years, such as household appliances and industrial equipment. Orders for durable goods rose 2.8% in July following a 3.1% drop in June, while orders for nondurable goods, including food, textiles and paper products, edged up only 0.2% after slipping 0.4% in June.

Economists generally dismissed the latest factory orders numbers, as they have most reports on the country's economic activities in July. They note that rising oil prices brought on by Iraq's invasion of Kuwait Aug. 2 have cast new uncertainty on the economic outlook.

Orders totaled $240.35 billion in July. Factories' shipments of goods slipped 0.8% during the month, after inching up 0.2% in June. Order backlogs grew 0.6%, offsetting a 0.6% decline in June. Inventories increased 0.7% following a 0.5% drop.
