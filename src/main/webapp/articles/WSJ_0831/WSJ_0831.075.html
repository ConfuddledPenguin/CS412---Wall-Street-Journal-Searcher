WARNING FOR EUROPE'S AIRLINES

Europe's airports and airspace cannot meet the needs of the future unless airlines and national governments work together to improve the industry's creaking infrastructure, warned Karl-Heinz Neumeister, secretary-general of the Association of European Airlines.

"I don't understand how policy-makers can seek a barrier-free market on the ground for 1992 and not allow the same for the air," the official told an aerospace and transport conference in London.

At present, European air-traffic control is a patchwork of 42 centers and 22 systems with different standards and communications procedures. Mr. Neumeister said inefficiency is responsible for 8% of the price of an airline ticket.

Mr. Neumeister said that although the airlines had the money to finance the creation of a single European traffic-control system, they lacked the power to implement change without political consent from governments.

NO SRI LANKAN TEA FOR IRAQ

Sri Lanka will stop selling tea to Iraq in compliance with sanctions imposed by the United Nations as punishment for Iraq's seizure of Kuwait, a cabinet minister said. "We have got clarification from the U.N. that tea cannot be an exception to the sanctions of food items to Iraq," said Plantation Minister Ranjan Wijeratne. Sri Lanka had hoped that it would be permitted to continue selling tea to Iraq, which last year bought 30,000 tons of Sri Lankan tea -- nearly 24% of the Indian Ocean island's export crop. Bangladesh, another of Iraq's chief tea sources, barred Iraqi buyers from its tea auctions earlier in the week.

CIGARETTE-SHORTAGE CASUALTY

Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev dismissed one of his top ministers yesterday, blaming him for the acute shortage of cigarettes that has caused demonstrations in Moscow and other cities. Vladilen Nikitin, a first deputy prime minister and head of the State Procurement Commission, was relieved of his duties one day after Mr. Gorbachev summoned him and other officials to demand an explanation for the shortages.

SINGAPORE'S FOREIGN WORKERS

Singapore, suffering from a severe labor shortage, said it was relaxing restrictions on hiring foreign workers. The government now allows foreign maids and overseas workers in the manufacturing, shipyard, hotel and construction sectors. A Labor Ministry statement said the government had decided to "progressively allow other sectors of the economy also to recruit foreign workers" with immediate effect. It didn't specify which sectors.

KOREAN AGREEMENT

North and South Korea agreed on details of talks between their prime ministers scheduled for next week, a South Korean government spokesman said. "The two sides concluded all procedural matters for the high-level talks to be held in Seoul from Sept. 4," the spokesman said after liaison officials from the two governments met in the border village of Panmunjom. The two countries agreed to hold two rounds of prime ministers' talks in the South Korean capital and confirmed the itinerary for the North Korean delegates' four-day stay in Seoul.

PHILIPPINE PROTECTIONISM

Philippine President Corazon Aquino bowed to protectionist demands and suspended her order for sweeping tariff cuts, an action likely to undermine attempts to make the ailing Philippine economy more competitive internationally. In an unexpected reversal, Mrs. Aquino ordered that the lower tariffs, which were to have taken effect tomorrow, be held "in abeyance" while the two houses of Congress try to draw up a resolution calling for a six-month delay in implementation.

AIDS TESTING IN ZIMBABWE

Life-insurance companies in Zimbabwe said AIDS-related claims in that nation have tripled in the past year and announced they would require medical tests from policy applicants.

To sidestep local laws prohibiting selective AIDS testing, the companies increased the minimum life-policy payout to the equivalent of $40,000, the amount that allows insurance organizations to legally screen clients for AIDS. "We had to raise the sum because so many policyholders are dying of AIDS," industry spokesman Nalin Kapadia said.

At least 9,000 people have AIDS in Zimbabwe, and about 466,000 are estimated to be carriers of the virus that causes the disease.

The insurers' announcement was rare in Africa, where few governments have admitted the full extent of the AIDS epidemic in their countries.
