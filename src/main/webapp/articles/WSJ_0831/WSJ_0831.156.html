Credit Ratings: Charter Medical's Junk-Bond Ratings Lowered by Moody's

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/31/90

NEW YORK -- Moody's Investors Service Inc. again lowered ratings on about $1 billion of Charter Medical Corp.'s high-yield, high-risk junk bonds. The ratings firm, which had downgraded Charter's debt in early July, said the action reflects concerns about the company's ability to service its debt because of deteriorating earnings and cash flow.

Charter had a loss of $40.6 million on revenue of $315.2 million in its third quarter ended June 30. That compared with a loss of $5.1 million on revenue of $308 million in the year-earlier period.

Charter, based in Macon, Ga., is one of the largest psychiatric hospital operators in the U.S. and also operates 13 general hospitals. In the face of a tightening market for psychiatric care, it has been struggling this year under $1.6 billion in debt from the 1988 management-led buy-out of the company.

Moody's reduced Charter's senior debt ratings to Caa from single-B-2. It also downgraded the company's senior subordinated debt to Ca from single-B-3 and its other subordinated debt to Ca from Caa. The rating of Charter's preferred stock remains unchanged at Ca, Moody's said.

Moody's said the wider third-quarter loss stems from declining occupancy rates in Charter's hospitals, a drop-off that exceeds the industry average and that "may well extend into the coming year."

J. Rodney Laughlin, executive vice president of human resources and development at Charter, acknowledged that the company has been faring worse than other psychiatric chains, citing widespread publicity about the company's financial problems as the source of the trouble. But he added that cost-cutting measures, as well as a plan to sell and lease back 12 hospitals, should enable Charter to "bounce back quickly."

"We are current with all of our debt obligations," Mr. Laughlin said, "and we expect to remain so."
