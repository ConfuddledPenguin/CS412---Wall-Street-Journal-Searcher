Bush Calls for Global Effort to Assist Economies Hurt by Persian Gulf Crisis

  • Author: David Shribman and Robert S. Greenberger
  • Date: 08/31/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush, asserting that the shape of the post-Cold War world is at stake in the Persian Gulf, called for a multinational effort to underwrite the blockade of Iraq and to provide "substantial economic assistance" to countries being harmed by the disruptions in the region. Meanwhile, Saddam Hussein put new bureaucratic obstacles in the path of the Western women and children he had promised could leave Iraq and Kuwait earlier this week. State Department spokeswoman Margaret Tutwiler called the new restrictions "inhumane and disgraceful."

As the standoff between the U.S. and Iraq continued, President Bush said at a White House press conference, "It is important that the considerable burden of the effort be shared by those being defended and those who benefit from the free flow of oil."

He said that the administration is calling on Japan, South Korea, West Germany, Saudi Arabia and the oil-rich Gulf states to provide financial and energy resources to help meet the cost of deploying troops and to assist nations such as Turkey, Egypt and Jordan, which are suffering economically as a result of the sanctions. Mr. Bush plans to dispatch Secretary of State James Baker to Europe and the Gulf and Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady to Asia to spearhead the "consulting and coordinating effort."

President Bush added, "There's no total price tag I have in mind" for the effort.

But the president also said, "U.S. forces should never appear to be mercenary forces, and that will not be the outcome of this, I can guarantee you."

Japan announced a wide-ranging assistance package earlier this week that it said had a value of $1 billion, but Mr. Bush indicated at his news conference that he would be pushing Tokyo for more.

Responding to the president's call, Sulaiman Mutawa, the exiled Kuwaiti minister of planning, said his country has "made a straightforward commitment" to pick up part of the cost of the military efforts under way in the Middle East. "The precise numbers haven't been thrashed out," he said. "But our government stands ready to provide economic support."

Ms. Tutwiler said Iraq now is demanding that foreign women and children write letters stating that they want to depart and that the letters must be translated into Arabic. In addition, she said, the Iraqis are demanding that the letters bear a stamp from unspecified "appropriate fiscal authorities" showing that all Iraqi taxes have been paid.

Iraqi officials earlier had asked for a list of those who wish to depart. Such a list hasn't yet been provided by U.S. embassy personnel in Baghdad.

Privately, a State Department official said that some Americans being held hostage by Iraq may be reluctant to comply with the procedures. The official said that some of the women hostages are worried that providing the information could inadvertently reveal the whereabouts of their husbands, who haven't been told they can leave.

At yesterday's news conference, Mr. Bush said, "I feel very concerned about Americans that are held against their will." But he added: "We cannot permit hostage-taking to shape the foreign policy of this country, and I won't permit it to do that."

The president also said he doesn't anticipate "major imbalances in the oil market," adding that the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve was "available if it is truly needed." He vowed that U.S. efforts would "go on as long as it takes" to force Iraq to withdraw from Kuwait and release its foreign hostages. He dismissed suggestions that U.S. costs be underwritten by a war tax.

Mr. Bush praised the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries for being willing to increase output to offset the loss of oil supplies created by the isolation of Iraq.

During his press conference, Mr. Bush stated several times that "we have no argument with the people of Iraq." Although Mr. Bush doesn't list toppling Saddam Hussein as one his goals, U.S. officials are hoping that the United Nations-backed economic sanctions and military show of force will create sufficient domestic pressures to lead to the Iraqi dictator's downfall. Mr. Bush said "it wouldn't disappoint me" if the Iraqi people conclude that Saddam Hussein was the cause of their problems. The president's remarks came as U.N. Secretary-General Javier Perez de Cuellar arrived in Jordan for talks today with Iraq's foreign minister, Tariq Aziz.

President Bush also expressed support for the Kuwaiti resistance and "anybody that can have a hand in restoring legitimacy there to Kuwait."

He acknowledged that economic measures against Iraq may be hurting civilians in that country more than the government, but he said: "There's nothing that's painless." He dismissed Saddam Hussein's suggestions that he, Mr. Bush and British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher have a debate. "You can put an empty chair there {for me}," the president said.
