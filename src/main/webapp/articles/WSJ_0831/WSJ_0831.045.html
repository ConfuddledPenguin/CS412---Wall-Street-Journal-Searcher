OTC Focus: Unlisted Shares Fall With Big Board; Sierra Tucson Dives on Earnings News

  • Author: Craig S. Smith Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/31/90

Yet another new member of the Nasdaq club got hammered as investors shun OTC stocks that deliver earnings disappointments. Sierra Tucson, an operator of treatment centers for drug abusers and people with mental disorders, lost 4 3/4 to 7 1/2 after lowering its sights on this year's earnings. The company went public less than a year ago for $12 a share.

While Sierra Tucson was the worst hit OTC stock yesterday, it joins a growing list of stocks with short track records that have been dealt devasting blows by nervous investors in recent weeks.

Digital Sound, Pharmacy Management Services, and In-Store Advertising are among those stocks that are trading well below their initial public offering prices set earlier this year.

Most of those companies have not lived up to the expectations set during their offerings, and have paid dearly as a result. Traders say there is little tolerance for uncertainty in a market where even the best and the brightest have lost their shine.

The Nasdaq Composite Index fell 3.10 points, or 0.81%, to 378.68. The New York Stock Exchange Composite Index took a bigger hit, dropping 1.4%.

Tony Cecin, director of equity trading at Piper Jaffray & Hopwood, says Nasdaq dealer stock inventories are the lowest that he has seen them in 10 years. That means OTC market makers aren't willing to hold stock for long, and any shares for sale will drive the stock price lower until a buyer is found.

OTC advancers trailed decliners, 796 to 1,015, while volume fell to 106.3 million shares from 118.1 million shares Wednesday.

Piper Jaffray's Mr. Cecin says he won't feel comfortable buying stocks until the market is much lower.

He says that even if the Middle East crisis were settled today and the Dow industrials rallied 150 points, there would still be plenty of investors ready to sell their shares -- leading to another slide.

"I would feel more comfortable about stepping in at 2,200 {on the Dow Jones Industrial Average} than at 2,450," he says. The DJIA closed at 2,593.32, down 39.11 points yesterday.

Sierra Tucson's stock has lost more than half of its value since Tuesday. Wednesday afternoon, Oppenheimer & Co. analyst Carl Sherman told his sales force and trading desk that Sierra Tucson wouldn't meet his 1990 earnings projections, traders of the stock say.

Mr. Sherman lowered his 1990 projection to 73 cents a share from an earlier range of 90 cents to 95 cents a share.

Word of Mr. Sherman's views spread quickly, sparking a sell-off that sent Sierra Tucson's stock plunging 4 points to 12 1/4 before the trading was halted late Wednesday.

Later that day, Sierra Tucson announced that its new German operations aren't going to break out of the red as early as the company had hoped. Sierra opened a treatment center in Germany late in 1989, but has yet to turn a profit there.

As a result, the company said profit for the third quarter will fall below the 20 cents a share earned in the period last year.

"It's a timing issue," said Sierra Tucson's Chief Financial Officer John Schmitz. "We made a mistake in terms of projecting when we will break even" in Germany.

Mr. Schmitz says the company now expects the German operations to pass the break-even point early next year -- by February at the latest. But Mr. Schmitz is dismayed by the violent market reaction to the company's news.

"The reaction is a little more negative than it needs to be," he says of the decline in the company's stock. Even at 73 cents a share, Sierra Tucson's 1990 profit will have grown 15% year-over-year.

Intel led the most-active list, falling 1 7/8 to 33 1/8 on volume of more than two million shares. At least part of that move was because of jitters over the impact of a patent granted to a California-based inventor that could cover many of the chips made by the semiconductor industry. This newspaper reported that if the patent survives challenges, chip-makers like Intel might have to fork over millions in royalties.

MCI Communications rose 7/8 to 32 5/8. In a meeting Wednesday, the company assured Wall Street analysts that their 1991 earnings estimates of $3.20 to $3.45 a share were reasonable. That would translate into revenue growth of as much as 20% for 1991, an MCI spokesman told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Sunrise Medical added 1/2 to 14 1/4 after reporting that it earned 40 cents a share on a fully diluted basis in its fourth quarter ended in June, up from 32 cents a share in the quarter last year.

Envirosafe Services tumbled 4 7/8, or about 32%, to 10 3/8. The Ohio Hazardous Waste Facility Board denied an application by one of the company's principal units to construct an additional waste-disposal site in Ohio.
