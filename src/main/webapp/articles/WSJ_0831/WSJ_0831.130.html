Abreast of the Market: Industrials Retreat 39.11 as Hopes For a Mideast Settlement Fade

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 08/31/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market resumed its retreat as nervousness about the crisis in the Middle East, along with higher crude-oil prices, took its toll. "A lot of the optimism we had that this" crisis would soon be over "now seems to be fading," said Philip J. Eagan, manager of equity trading at Sanford C. Bernstein & Co. "It seems to be drawing out now."

Stocks dropped sharply and showed weakness across the board, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average snapping a four-session string of gains by falling 39.11 to 2593.32. Decliners topped advancers by 998 to 482 on the New York Stock Exchange.

The market's fall came amid the lightest activity on the Big Board since July 9. Volume totaled 120,890,000 shares, down from 134,240,000 shares Wednesday, and trading slowed to a crawl during the afternoon as many players wound down their dealings in advance of the Labor Day weekend. "It looks like we're just wasting away until the holiday is over," Mr. Eagan said.

Among broader market indexes, Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index dropped 5.48 to 318.71, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index dropped 2.55 to 175.23 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index lost 4.55 to 295.58.

Several rounds of sell programs linked to stock-index arbitrage swept through the market during the session, traders said, and fueled a late sell-off that led the Big Board to put its "uptick" requirement for arbitrage-related stock sales into effect.

The "uptick" rule was implemented at 3:50 p.m. EDT after the Dow Jones industrials showed a loss of 50 points. For the rest of the session, traders had to wait until stocks had risen in price to carry out arbitrage selling.

Although stocks bounced off their lows after the rule went into effect, September S&P 500 futures contracts closed -- as they did at the close of Wednesday's session -- at a level that could spark sell programs at the opening of today's session.

The futures dropped 5.30 to 318.55 -- a discount of 0.16 point to the actual S&P 500 index. Arbitrage selling took effect once the S&P contracts were about even with the index.

Meanwhile, U.N. Secretary General Javier Perez de Cuellar arrived in the Middle East to begin discussions with Iraqi Foreign Minister Tariq Aziz, but there was little else in Middle East news to reassure the market that progress toward a peaceful settlement of the conflict could be reached.

Amid the resulting jitters, crude-oil prices retraced part of Wednesday's sharp decline. October crude-oil futures rose 85 cents a barrel, to $26.77, in New York trading after tumbling nearly $2 a barrel in the previous session.

As a result of the stock market's latest slide, the industrial average is showing a double-digit percentage decline for August. The average has dropped 311.88 points, or 10.7%, from its July 31 close of 2905.20.

"August was a tough month. There was a lot of damage done to consumer psychology, to investor psychology," said Charles I. Clough Jr., chief investment strategist at Merrill Lynch & Co.

Looking ahead, Mr. Clough said the Bank of Japan's decision to raise its discount rate by 0.75 point, to 6%, points toward yet another potential barrier to higher stock prices: the relatively tight monetary policies of central banks, including the Federal Reserve.

Stocks are unlikely to rebound from their sell-off anytime soon, Mr. Clough said, "unless we see a time when central banks can feel free to put liquidity into the {banking} system," or ease credit.

Energy stocks, which held firm Wednesday despite the steep drop in oil prices, headed lower in the face of the oil market's rebound. Chevron slipped 1 1/2 to 75 1/4, Exxon fell 1 to 48 7/8, Texaco dropped 1 1/8 to 62 1/8, Mobil lost 7/8 to 63 and Amoco dropped 1 3/8 to 54; among oil-services issues, Schlumberger eased 3/8 to 64 and Halliburton fell 3/4 to 52 1/2.

Unisys dropped 1/2 to 8 1/8 as 1.3 million shares changed hands. Prudential-Bache Securities downgraded the stock to "hold" from "buy," although analyst Rick J. Martin told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report he still expects a major turnaround at the company in the fourth quarter. Analysts at Cowen & Co. and Goldman Sachs cut their estimates of the company's 1990 earnings, and Cowen lowered its 1991 forecast as well.

Selling spilled into other technology issues. International Business Machines fell 2 1/8 to 101 3/4, Compaq Computer dropped 2 1/4 to 48 1/4, Hewlett-Packard slid 1 1/4 to 35 1/4 and Tandem Computers lost 3/8 to 16.

Rumor-driven selling hurt Bank of New England, which skidded 5/8 to 1. Ted Dimauro, a Massachusetts congressional candidate, issued a press release saying -- erroneously -- that the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. was about to seize the financially troubled bank holding company.

"This is ridiculous," says Lee Cross, spokesperson for the comptroller of the currency's office. "First off, the FDIC doesn't even recommend the closing of a bank. The office of the comptroller does. And we haven't. This is a categorical denial. We have not recommended the bank be closed."

Melville tumbled 3 to 42. Smith Barney, Harris Upham, which had maintained a buy recommendation on the retailing stock since November 1988, downgraded it to hold and reduced 1990 and 1991 earnings estimates.

Analyst William N. Smith told the Dow Jones investor report that Melville's August performance has been "very disappointing" and its sales appear soft.

Other retailing issues also ran into selling. Wal-Mart Stores dropped 1 1/4 to 27 3/4 on 1.6 million shares, May Department Stores dropped 2 to 44 1/2, Circuit City Stores lost 1 1/4 to 17 7/8, Home Depot slid 1 3/8 to 33 7/8 and TJX dipped 3/4 to 13 1/4.

Newmont Mining, which filed to offer 12 million common shares and warrants to purchase an additional six million shares, fell 3 3/8 to 44 1/2. Through the offering, Consolidated Gold Fields will cut back its 49% stake in Newmont.

Other precious-metals stocks finished unchanged to higher as the price of gold held steady. Homestake Mining gained 1/4 to 20 1/8, Hecla Mining went up 1/8 to 11 1/8, Placer Dome closed unchanged at 17 5/8, Battle Mountain Gold closed unchanged at 10 3/4 and ASA went up 1 to 49 5/8.

Student Loan Marketing Association gained 5/8 to 42 1/4. A number of analysts who follow Sallie Mae are trying to lure portfolio managers back into the stock, based on the belief that it won't suffer any losses as a result of financial difficulties facing Higher Education Assistance Foundation, a major student-loan guarantee agency, The Wall Street Journal reported yesterday in its "Heard on the Street" column.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.32 to 322.91. Volume totaled 10,750,000 shares.
