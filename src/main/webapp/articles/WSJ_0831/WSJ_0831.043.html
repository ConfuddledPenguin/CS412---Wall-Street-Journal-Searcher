Letters to the Editor: A Weighty Distinction

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/31/90

Your July 12 page-one article concerning the world standard of weight, "Le Gran K," failed to distinguish between mass and weight. It was not clear whether Le Gran K had lost 75-billionths of a kilogram of mass or 75-billionths of a kilogram of weight in the past 100 years. The mass of an object is a measure of the amount of matter in that object. The weight is the force due to the gravitational attraction between the object and the Earth. If Le Gran K is losing mass, then it is losing matter. In that case, the cause could be the infrequent polishings, which actually remove matter. On the other hand, if Le Gran K is losing weight, it means that the force due to gravity is lessening. This would make for a very grave situation, indeed, for it would indicate either that the universal constant of gravitation is not, in fact, a constant or that the mass of the Earth is slowly decreasing.

Neither of these alternatives is palatable, for they both violate fundamental concepts of physics. The second alternative, that the mass of the Earth is decreasing, could possibly be attributed to the detonation of nuclear bombs, each of which destroys a small amount of matter.

In view of the gravity of this matter I believe we must mass our forces to lessen the burden of the weight of the problem.

Irving R. Tannenbaum

Professor of Chemistry

West Los Angeles College

Culver City, Calif.
