Four California Utilities Will Profit From Energy Savings Under New Plan

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 08/31/90

California's four largest publicly owned energy utility companies will be allowed to profit directly from some of the energy savings generated from their conservation efforts under a new program approved Wednesday by the California Public Utilities Commission. The companies also have agreed to boost their collective spending for consumer rebates, education and other conservation-related programs by roughly 50% to about $500 million over the next 28 months. The programs could produce $1 billion in energy savings, the commission estimates.

"Conservation has been low on the totem pole" of priorities for most utilities because "we're asking them not to sell their product," said Patricia M. Eckert, one of California's five utility commissioners. "This (program) gets their attention by giving them an incentive and reward."

An unusual coalition of energy companies, consumer groups, industry associations and regulators hammered out the plan over the past year in response to a commission request for ideas about how California could push conservation and energy-efficiency more aggressively.

In recent months, some other states also have given financial incentives to utility companies to support conservation programs. Besides California, "Massachusetts, Rhode Island and New York have gone the furthest, though it's hard to make direct comparisons," said Don Schultz, a California commission planner who has been closely involved in the state's program.

To pay for the California utilities' conservation programs, small rate increases -- less than 1% in most cases -- have been approved. "It will cost money in the short run, but in the long run the savings could be four or five to one if new power plants won't have to be built," Ms. Eckert said.

Different mechanisms for measuring conservation and related financial benefits will be used by the participating utilities San Diego Gas & Electric Co., Southern California Edison Co., a unit of SCEcorp., Southern California Gas Co., a unit of Pacific Enterprises, and Pacific Gas & Electric Co. Each company helped to devise its own performance formula.

Southern California Edison, for example, will add the costs of its conservation programs to its rate base just as it would the costs of a new power plant, Mr. Schultz said. Pacific Gas & Electric is expected to split an estimated $200 million in benefits this year on an 85%-15% basis, with customers getting 85% and the company and its shareholders 15%.

Mr. Schultz said that the companies could incur penalties if they fall short of minimum goals, but "it's more likely they will be rewarded than penalized."

Southern California Edison said it will nearly double its conservation spending to about $63 million over the next two years. Pacific G&E said its conservation program costs this year will jump about 40% to $138 million. Southern California Edison said it will spend about $39 million this year, and San Diego Gas & Electric said it will spend $25 million over the next 16 months on new energy-saving programs.

Most of the programs pay cash rebates to residential and commercial builders to encourage use of energy-efficient designs, materials and fixtures. Cash rebates also will be made to consumers and retailers who buy and sell energy-saving appliances and lighting systems.

California regulators said they view the new financial incentives for utilities as "experimental." The commission will reassess the program in 1992, Mr. Schultz said.
