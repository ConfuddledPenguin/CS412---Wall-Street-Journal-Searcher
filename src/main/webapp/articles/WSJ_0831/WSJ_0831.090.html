Technology & Health: Safety Group Cites Fatalities Linked to Work

  • Author: Ron Winslow
  • Date: 08/31/90

Occupational diseases kill more than 70,000 U.S. workers yearly, says a workplace safety group that calls the issue the nation's "most neglected public health problem." The National Safe Workplace Institute, an advocacy group with headquarters in Chicago, based its estimate on an analysis of 1987 data. The institute's report is among the few efforts made to determine the prevalence of workplace-related illnesses nationwide.

"The report claims that occupational disease is a substantial problem that isn't sufficiently recognized," says Howard Frumkin, director of environmental and occupational health at Emory University, Atlanta, and an adviser to the institute.

The report also says the nation's regulatory apparatus for occupational health is in disarray. Standards for exposure to many dangerous materials are much more lax for the workplace than for the community at large, the report notes. It also says funding for the U.S. Occupational Safety and Health Administration and the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health, as a percentage of the federal budget, fell sharply through the 1980s.

Diane Porter, assistant director of NIOSH, said the study "covers an important area that we need to look at." She also noted that while the National Centers for Disease Control tracks such infectious diseases as AIDS and measles on a weekly basis, no national reporting system tracks occupational disease. A spokesman for OSHA said agency officials hadn't yet evaluated the study.

The institute said it based its fatality report on data from the National Center for Health Statistics and on established methods to estimate the proportion of deaths from major diseases that could be attributed to workplace exposures. For instance, it estimated that 5% to 10% of the nation's 483,000 cancer deaths in 1987 were related to previous occupational exposures to dangerous substances, while 1% to 3% of 964,000 cardiovascular disease deaths and 2% to 4% of 164,000 pulmonary disease fatalities were caused by such exposures. The estimate of total occupational disease deaths ranged from 47,377 to 95,479.

Such numbers are inherently imprecise, the institute acknowledged, because the onset of many occupational diseases occurs many years after the initial exposure and doctors often can't distinguish between illnesses that begin in the workplace and those that develop from other causes.

The institute said a broad effort by industry, medicine, labor and government is necessary to develop more information about the problem as well as effective programs to address it.
