IRS Retains Tax Restrictions on Bonds That Could Be Freely Converted Abroad

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 05/07/90

WASHINGTON -- The Internal Revenue Service retained a tax restriction that some investors believe hinders recent steps to open the global capital markets. The IRS said Friday that "because of general tax compliance concerns," it won't allow a special type of bond that could be freely converted between the different types of debt sold in the U.S. and overseas.

U.S. tax law requires that bonds issued to U.S. investors be registered in the name of the owner. That way, the tax authorities can tell who is receiving the interest income. But foreign debt markets deal primarily in paper known as bearer bonds, which don't carry the name of the owner.

In light of recent securities-law changes intended to facilitate the free international trade of securities, the tax agency had been considering allowing bonds that could be converted from one form to another, so U.S. investors could resell their debt to a wider secondary market.

James Duncan, a partner at the law firm Cleary, Gottlieb, Steen & Hamilton in New York, called the IRS's decision not to permit the new bond "a lost opportunity."

"The continuing inability to offer securities within the United States that are genuinely fungible with the same securities offered outside the United States limits the opportunities available to issuers," he said. "It restricts the ability to make a genuinely global offering of debt securities."

Esta Stecher, a tax attorney at the law firm Sullivan & Cromwell in New York, said, "It really would be consistent with the SEC's policy in globalizing the capital markets to allow registered-form securities to be resold outside the U.S. in bearer form."

The IRS position on the convertible bonds was contained in the preamble to a set of regulations covering the issuing of bearer debt to investors outside the U.S.

Attorneys said the regulations sever a longstanding link between securities law and tax law covering international capital market offerings. Generally in the past, if a deal was found to satisfy the requirements of securities law, it was acceptable to the tax authorities, too. These regulations remove that so-called safe harbor.

The securities and tax regulations, for example, now differ in their definition of "U.S. person." Therefore, the securities and tax laws have different rules for who can buy bearer bonds.

In the preamble, the IRS wrote, "These final regulations are separate and independent from the rules and interpretations that the SEC chooses to adopt in its administration of the securities laws."

While promising to consider the SEC's interpretations "where appropriate," the IRS said it "must ultimately base" its own interpretations on "the tax policies" underlying the law.
