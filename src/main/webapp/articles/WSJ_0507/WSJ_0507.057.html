American Express To Accept Deposits From Cardholders

  • Author: Georgette Jasen
  • Date: 05/07/90

NEW YORK -- American Express Co. wants more of your money. The travel and financial-services giant has a new "Membership Savings" account, which will pay interest on deposits made with a cardholder's regular monthly bill. The interest rate is guaranteed to be higher than the average on bank money market accounts, and deposits up to $100,000 will be insured by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.

Market research showed that cardholders "really want to begin saving more," said Sarah M. Nolan, president of the investment and insurance services group of American Express Travel Related Services. "They want to have savings made as easy and habit-forming as spending."

Under the program, which American Express plans to introduce in eight states and the District of Columbia this month and nationwide by the end of the year, cardmembers who sign up will be billed a specified amount to go into the savings account each month. To withdraw funds, they can call a toll-free telephone number to receive a check, or, for a $10 fee, the money can be wired into a cardholder's bank account.

American Express will "make prudent investments" with the funds deposited, Ms. Nolan said, "nothing different than what any financial institution would do." She declined to be more specific.

Separately, American Express is offering certain cardholders a line of credit at an interest rate of nine percentage points over the prime rate. With the prime currently at 10%, the rate on the line of credit would be 19%.

"We consider ourselves a broad-based financial institution," said Ms. Nolan, adding that the two new products are "attempts to satisfy our cardmembers."

The line of credit is being offered through American Express Centurion Bank, based in Wilmington, Del. The savings account is offerd by American Express Resource Co., an industrial loan corporation chartered in Utah, which Ms. Nolan said "meets the regulations and requirements" for FDIC insurance.

The interest rate on the savings account is pegged at half a percentage point more than the average of the 100 largest banks and thrifts in the nation, as published weekly by Bank Rate Monitor, a North Palm Beach, Fla.-based newsletter. The average is now 6.06%, which would mean a 6.56% rate on the American Express account.

A number of banks and thrifts, however, are offering more than 7% on money market accounts, and the average yield on money market mutual funds is currently just below 8%. Nevertheless, Ms. Nolan said, American Express expects "an enthusiastic reception" for the new savings account because of its convenience and the small minimum deposit of $50.

Participants in the program can deposit more or less than the specified amount, or miss a deposit. The account is being introduced in Massachusetts, New Hampshire, Maryland, Virginia, Georgia, Wisconsin, Ohio, Oregon and in Washington, D.C.
