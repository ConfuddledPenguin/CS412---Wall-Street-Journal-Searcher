Campeau Units Post a Deficit Of $2.43 Billion --- Federated and Allied Stores Attribute Much of Loss To Non-Cash Charges

  • Author: Robert Melnbardis
  • Date: 05/07/90

Federated Department Stores Inc. and Allied Stores Corp. had after-tax losses totaling $2.43 billion in fiscal 1990 after big non-cash charges that include a $1.15 billion write-down of good will in Federated in the fourth quarter. In their Form 10-K filings with the Securities and Exchange Commission, the Campeau Corp. units attributed the bulk of their losses to non-cash accounting treatments of their Chapter 11 bankrupcty status.

Federated's $1.5 billion net loss for the 53 weeks ended Feb. 3 includes a $1.15 billion write-down of good will, reflecting the company's "deteriorating financial situation" during the year. It said further adjustments may be made to good will during its efforts to restructure and emerge from Chapter 11 proceedings.

Allied's share of Federated's write-down and equity in losses at Campeau's Ralphs Grocery Co. chain totaled $660.3 million, bringing its net loss to $925.2 million for the year. Allied owns 50% of Federated and has direct and indirect equity stakes in Ralphs, a Southern California supermarket chain.

In a summary of their 10-K filings, the department store operators said that despite "extreme pressures" on their operations in the third and fourth quarters, comparable-division sales were up by 7.2% to $4.87 billion at Federated and increased by 5.2% to $2.71 billion at Allied. They said combined net interest expenses of $806.7 million took one of the "biggest bites" out of their annual earnings. The retailers said the write-down of good will and interest charges had "little or no bearing" on their operations since they sought Chapter 11 bankrupcty protection on Jan. 15.

Under Chapter 11, a company continues to operate under court protection from its creditors while it tries to work out a plan to pay its bills.

In other financial information provided to the U.S. Bankruptcy Court in Cincinnati, Federated and Allied said operations continue to improve since January, when many vendors resumed shipping merchandise to their department stores. Their net losses for the five weeks ended April 7 totaled $18 million, compared with $48 million for the four weeks ended March 3. Sales at Federated and Allied in the latest five weeks were $405.3 million and $234.4 million, respectively.

In a statement, Allen Questrom, chairman and chief executive of the retailers, said they have "turned the corner on stabilizing the business."

"The results do very little other than confirm something which we already know, namely that Federated and Allied are sound and reasonably good retailing operations," said Kurt Barnard, publisher of Retail Marketing Report, a New York-based industry newsletter.

But the figures also show, he said, that despite their improving retail health since January, Federated and Allied can't escape from the "intolerable ocean of debt" Campeau plunged them into. "That's why we all shuddered when we saw what Campeau was doing to those stores," Mr. Barnard said.

In its 10-K filing, Federated blamed its financial woes on the massive debt it was burdened with by Campeau's $6.6 billion highly leveraged buy-out of Federated in May 1988. "Ultimately, this drain on revenues from the company's {Federated's} retail operation contributed to cash shortfalls which precipitated the company's Chapter 11 filing," it said.

Federated said most of its cash interest outlays are frozen by its Chapter 11 status and it is no longer accruing annual interest charges of about $200 million on its unsecured debt. Its interest expense is expected to drop by between $375 million and $400 million, it said. In fiscal 1990 Federated had interest expense of $516.1 million, it said. Allied is no longer accruing annual interest of about $90 million on its unsecured debt. It had net interest expense of $290.6 million in fiscal 1990.

Federated said it also wrote off $120.5 million of unamortized deferred financing costs and reorganization expenses incurred after its acquisition by Campeau. Allied had $21.6 million in reorganization costs and $138.8 million in income tax expense.

The units said they had combined cash on hand of $552 million on April 25, and hadn't drawn on a total of $700 million in debtor-in-possession financing available for working capital needs.

Separately, in an information circular for a special meeting of holders of its 7 1/2% convertible debentures, Campeau said it plans to sell a "substantial portion" of its real estate assets to restructure its own ailing finances. The circular said Campeau has a "significant negative cash flow" as a result of its debt-service obligations, and the assets sales would leave it with a positive cash flow.

The sales are expected to generate enough cash to repay secured debt on the properties, it said. Secured loans on other properties would be serviced using cash flow from the properties, and senior unsecured creditors would begin receiving payments after "substantial completion" of the planned asset sales "only to the extent that cash is available following certain deductions." Campeau also proposes the accrual of dividends on preferred shares, with no retractions or redemptions.

Campeau said its investment in and advances to its U.S. units totaled $1.27 billion as of Jan. 31, and it plans to participate in their restructuring.

Campeau has 184 million Canadian dollars (US$157.7 million) of 7 1/2% debentures outstanding. At the June 1 meeting, debenture holders will be asked to approve a resolution to waive existing and future defaults and to defer interest payable on the debentures until Dec. 31, 1991. Support for the resolution by holders of two-thirds of the debentures is almost assured because Campeau Chairman Robert Campeau and his wife are able to vote 42% of the debentures, and the Reichmann family's Olympia & York Developments Ltd. holds another 25.3%.

Campeau defaulted on the debentures in March when it missed interest payments on that series and $260 million of convertible debentures held by Olympia & York. Sources close to the Reichmanns have said previously that Olympia & York would support the interest deferrals.
