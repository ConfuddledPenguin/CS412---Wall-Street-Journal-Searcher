OTC Focus: Pink Sheets Stocks Will Seek Respect With June 1 Start-Up of Electronic System

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 05/07/90

NEW YORK -- It remains to be seen whether Pink Sheets stocks, the Rodney Dangerfields of the securities industry, will get some respect with the June 1 launch of the OTC Bulletin Board. "You can't make a silk purse out of a sow's ear," says Lawrence J. Goldstein, general partner of Santa Monica Partners in New York. As an active investor in the fitfully traded Pink Sheets stocks, he wonders how soon the new electronic system can meet its two-fold purpose: to provide greater ease of trading and closer surveillance for these stocks.

But if the computerized market catches on, Mr. Goldstein and others say, it is likely to make the shares of thousands of obscure companies more visible and less susceptible to fraud. It also will boost the exposure of several hundred foreign companies such as Japan's Nintendo Co. and West Germany's Deutsche Bank AG, which trade over the counter because they don't meet larger markets' tougher disclosure rules.

The OTC Bulletin Board, approved last week by the Securities and Exchange Commission, was developed by the National Association of Securities Dealers, the self-regulatory body for the over-the-counter market. NASD President Joseph Hardiman says the system is designed to better regulate some of the thousands of OTC stocks that aren't traded on NASD's electronic trading system, and to give these stocks greater visibility among investors.

NASD estimates that 47,000 issues are traded over the counter in the U.S. Of these, 4,300 meet its minimum-net-worth and other standards for trading electronically on the National Association of Securities Dealers Automated Quotations system, or Nasdaq.

About 10,000 to 12,000 of the more active issues are available to dealers once a day in the Pink Sheets, published since 1913 by New Jersey-based National Quotation Bureau Inc., a subsidiary of Commerce Clearing House Inc., Riverwoods, Ill. First printed on colored paper in the 1920s to reduce glare from incandescent lights, the Pink Sheets now consist of a 330-page volume or electronic list delivered daily to 2,000 customers, says David Burnett, president of the National Quotation Bureau.

"The Pink Sheets are a primitive communications device," concedes Mr. Burnett, whose company has a one-year contract to provide NASD with data for the bulletin board. He adds that there are some crucial differences between the OTC Bulletin Board and the Nasdaq system launched in 1971.

"It's a Nasdaq with no Ten Commandments," he contends.

The OTC Bulletin Board is designed to provide dealers electronically with Pink Sheet stock quotes throughout the day, instead of once daily. Using their current Nasdaq terminals, NASD member firms' market makers will be able, but not obliged, to enter or update price quotations on Pink Sheets stocks.

Dealers in Nasdaq stocks now are required to provide firm bid and asked, or selling, prices at the risk of facing fines or suspension if they don't honor their quotes. But OTC Bulletin Board dealers will be able simply to list their names and phone numbers, with no bid or asked prices. They can list "unfirm" prices or firm quotes.

The system will sort the listings, with firm quotes at the top and the least-firm interest at the bottom. A dealer failing to honor a firm quote may face enforcement action. But there's nothing to make a dealer offer a firm quote, notes Mr. Burnett. He predicts that as Nasdaq slowly tightened its requirements as it evolved, so will the bulletin board become stricter.

Mr. Goldstein says several dealers told him that they will be reluctant to tip their hands on the bulletin board. Some dealers may be surprised to find there simply aren't any sellers for some Pink Sheets stocks, he adds. In Mr. Goldstein's view, the market won't become truly competitive until stock quotes and other information are available to investors through their brokers rather than one step removed at the dealer level. On the other hand, intraday information "may shed some light where there's been none before."

Since last week's approval, 13 NASD members have signed contracts to use the bulletin board, according to S. William Broka, NASD vice president of business development. Troster Singer Corp., one of the nation's largest traders of Pink Sheet stocks, is ready to go with the new trading system, says Ralph Valentino, president of the Jersey City, N.J., brokerage firm. He explains that now his brokers make at least three telephone calls to get competing quotes on Pink Sheets stocks before executing an order. With quotes available on a computer screen, they will be able to execute orders faster. Data on foreign stocks will be updated only twice a day while that for U.S. stocks will be updated continuously.

While the Pink Sheets stocks may become easier to trade, NASD's Mr. Hardiman cautions, they're not for everyone. "A lot, if not all of them in this market, are speculative stocks," he warns. He excludes from this group some of the larger foreign issues whose American depositary receipts trade in the Pink Sheets.

A more upbeat view comes from Mr. Goldstein, the Pink Sheets investor. "It's just like Nasdaq," he says. "There's junk and blue chips and everything in between."

Friday's Market Activity

Last week's rally in technology stocks helped the over-the-counter market recover to its highest level in three weeks.

Friday, the Nasdaq Composite Index gained 3.17 points, or 0.75%, to 428.61. OTC advancers led declining shares, 1,091 to 776. Volume slipped to 136.8 million shares from 160.9 million shares. The Nasdaq Composite Index added 2.5% for the week, lagging behind the 4.4% gain for the Nasdaq 100 index, which includes some of the largest technology stocks.

AST Research gained 2 1/4 to 23 1/4 after the Irvine, Calif., maker of personal computer systems said it signed a patent cross-licensing agreement with International Business Machines.

Drexler Technology tumbled 2 to 4 1/2. Late Thursday, the Mountain View, Calif., maker of optical data products reported a loss from continuing operations of 28 cents a share for the fiscal fourth quarter ended March 31, compared with profit from continuing operations of 85 cents a share a year earlier.
