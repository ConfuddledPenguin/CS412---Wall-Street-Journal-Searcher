Marketing & Media: Coors to Tout Home Of Colorado as It Looks Elsewhere

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 05/07/90

Adolph Coors Co. plans to test a new corporate ad campaign that brags about the company's Colorado home and the mountain spring water it brews into beer. Why then is the company shopping outside the state for new breweries?

The new television advertisements, which will start this summer with four small markets, pose the question: "Where was your beer born?" The 30- and 60-second segments cut back and forth between quiet scenes of Colorado mountains and streams and scenes of smokestacks and crowded highways of big cities.

"Clean, fresh, pure Rocky Mountain spring water," the voice-over says. "Next time you reach for a beer, remember." Words on the screen boast: "Coors. Great Beers Start Here." The spots were created by Foote, Cone & Belding Communications Inc., Chicago. Coors declined to say how much it plans to spend on the campaign.

The commercials, said Gary Naifeh, Coors vice president, brand management, "show the contrast of Colorado to other parts of the country." But, he maintained, the ads aren't meant to "take a shot at our competition." Coors's biggest competitors are Anheuser-Busch Cos. of St. Louis and Miller Brewing Co. of Milwaukee, a unit of Philip Morris Cos. "We think most people recognize that most places in the country aren't as nifty as Colorado," he said.

But the timing of the campaign is curious. The ads, which stress that Coors beers are made in Colorado, come at the same time the brewer is searching for new brewing capacity that will likely be outside of its home state. Coors's recent successful introduction of Keystone, a popular-priced beer, and double-digit annual growth of Coors Light have pushed the brewer's production right up against its Golden, Colo., brewery capacity of 20 million barrels a year. A deal that would have given Coors some Stroh Brewery Co. breweries (none in Colorado) fell apart late last year, but the company is continuing to look at both Stroh and G. Heileman Brewing Co. breweries as possible new brewing locations.

The campaign "is self-defeating in the long run because it's impossible to operate a national brewing company from a single location," said Robert S. Weinberg, a consultant and former Anheuser-Busch marketing executive, who also eschews the emphasis on the spring water. "The water necessary to brew good beer is available in many, many other places," he said.

Mr. Naifeh defended the campaign, saying it communicates "the roots of Coors's heritage, the people, the culture. It's much deeper than the water." He added that the company "might be" brewing beer out of the state soon, but "we might be just packaging beer somewhere else."

Also puzzling is the advent of the campaign at a time Coors has had phenomenal growth. Its sales grew 6.5% in 1989, the largest increase among domestic brewers, in a relatively flat market. "Their key brands are doing well," said Emanuel Goldman, analyst for PaineWebber Inc., San Francisco. "It's not clear to me that Coors needs a corporate campaign."

But by starting out in only four markets -- Fresno and Sacramento, Calif., Erie, Penn., and Rochester, N.Y. -- Coors isn't gambling a lot of money on the venture, Mr. Goldman pointed out. "They're going to have an opportunity to find out it's not going to work before spending a lot of dollars," he said. "I think they're going to find out it's not worth the money."
