Credit Markets: Mood Shift Boosts Odds For Auction --- Jobs Report Cheers Traders on the Eve Of U.S. Bond Sale

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 05/07/90

NEW YORK -- The mood in the bond market has suddenly shifted from sour to sweet, increasing the odds that investors will develop a hearty appetite for the $30.5 billion of Treasury securities to be sold this week. The change in sentiment, which was evident after the Department of Labor released the April employment report Friday, also means that some interest rates may not exceed current levels.

"It's kind of crazy to think that one report can create wild swings" in investor sentiment and interest-rate expectations, said William Brachfeld, manager of government trading at Daiwa Securities Inc. But that's exactly what happened.

And crazy or not, the mood swing is likely to help Uncle Sam easily auction $10.5 billion of three-year notes tomorrow. The two other auctions, $10 billion of 10-year notes Wednesday and $10 billion of 30-year bonds Thursday, should also run smoothly.

Just a few days ago, sentiment in the bond market was so ugly that traders were bracing for big trouble at the auction.

They worried that the Federal Reserve would soon jack up interest rates in an effort to squash inflation. They warned that yields on the new Treasury securities would have to exceed 9% to lure buyers to the record bond sale. They said the government's bond auction could be a fiasco because institutional investors, who have seen the value of their fixed-income portfolios plummet this year, didn't want any more bonds. Sure, small investors were expected to buy bonds at the auction because of attractive yields, but their purchases wouldn't be large enough to ensure the auction's success, the traders said.

While traders' nightmares could still come true, many economists believe such concerns are overblown.

The reason: Friday's employment report, which suggested that the economy is much weaker than previous economic reports may have indicated. The Labor Department reported that nonfarm jobs grew by just 64,000 in April. Excluding the 78,000 increase in employment related to temporary census workers, the April total would actually have declined by 14,000 last month.

The Labor Department also reported that average hourly earnings rose three cents to $9.95 in April on an adjusted basis, a sign that labor-cost inflation is well under control.

The jobs report was widely viewed as a sign that the spike up in interest rates may be ending. Moreover, economists now believe that the Federal Reserve will vote to keep interest rates unchanged at its May 15 meeting of the Federal Open Market Committee. Prior to the employment report, there was concern that the Fed would raise rates because other recent economic reports have suggested that the economy was picking up and inflation accelerating.

The employment report "reduced the market's fear that the Fed has to tighten credit policy," said Brian Fabbri, an economist at Midland Montagu Securities.

Immediately after the data were released, Treasury bond prices soared and yields eased to the lowest levels in weeks. The benchmark 30-year bond jumped more than 1 1/2 points, or $15 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount, while the yield fell to 8.8%. Two days earlier, the yield had been more than 9%.

Although yields could rise slightly ahead of the auction, "my guess is that it will be difficult to push the bond back over 9%, since sentiment has been bolted," said Mr. Fabbri. He expects yields on new Treasury securities to average 8 7/8%, which he says is attractive enough to lure investors.

Elliott Platt, chief economist at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp., is urging clients to buy bonds. "We believe that inflation in the U.S. will be contained at 4.5%-5%. In this context, 30-year Treasury obligations at roughly 8.8% are offering at least a 3.8% real yield. We regard that as an extraordinarily high yield for long-term Treasury obligations."

Dealers are more uncertain about the level of Japanese participation at the auction. Tokyo markets were closed most of last week for holidays, so there won't be a good indication of Japanese demand until today.

Philip Braverman, senior vice president and chief economist at DKB Securities Corp., a subsidiary of Japan's Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank, said that Japanese investors should participate sizably.

"Japanese insurance companies have reportedly decided to hold the percent of their dollar assets to total assets stable this fiscal year, implying investment of their premium income in U.S. Treasurys in the same proportion to their current dollar asset holdings. This suggests a takedown of some 30% or so of the Treasury refunding," said Mr. Braverman. Moreover, Japan's Ministry of Finance and the Bank of Japan have encouraged Japanese institutions to put in a "good showing" at the auction, according to Mr. Braverman.

Thomas Sowanick, senior fixed-income strategist at Merrill Lynch & Co., believes that Japanese participation could be "a pleasant surprise." He notes that yields on 10-year Treasury notes are 1.65 percentage points higher than yields on 10-year Japanese government securities. That is up from March, when the yield-spread was less than 1.20 percentage points. He says that Japanese investors are flush with cash, largely from recent bond redemptions. "The combination of available cash, wider spreads and a stable dollar suggests that the foreign-sector bid {at the auction} could in fact be larger than the market is expecting," Mr. Sowanick said.

Mr. Brachfeld also expects Japanese investors to show up in large numbers at the auction, but he isn't sure whether that will have a lasting positive impact on the bond market after the auction. The question, he said, is whether the bulk of Japanese buyers will be speculators or long-term investors. While buying by long-term investors could help the market, speculators may simply buy bonds at the auction and turn around and dump the securities in the days and weeks following the auction.

---

Friday's Market Activity

The employment report triggered an explosive rally in the bond market.

The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond rose more than 1 1/2 points, or $15 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The price was quoted late Friday at 96 19/32 to yield 8.81%, up from 95 1/32 to yield 8.97% on Thursday.

The yield on the benchmark bond, which recently rose above 9% for the first time in nearly a year, edged down to its lowest level in more than two weeks.

Yields on shorter-term securities fell even faster. Two-year Treasury notes, which were quoted late in the day at a price of 100 9/32, compared with 99 27/32 on Thursday, were yielding 8.698% on Friday, compared with 8.926% on Thursday.

In the corporate bond market, investment-grade bonds followed the Treasury market. Most issues ended about 1/2 point higher.

Junk bonds however, were little changed. Junk bonds are high-yield, speculative-graded corporate bonds that have low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all.

Among Friday's major market movers were the junk bonds of Greyhound Lines Inc., which sank on news that the strikebound carrier won't make a semiannual interest payment on its publicly traded 13% senior notes due May 15. In addition, the bus company said it had a loss of $55.7 million during the first quarter, sharply wider than a $14.4 million loss during the same period a year ago.

Prices of Greyhound's 13% senior notes due in 1995 slid six points to a bid price of 42. However, dealers said that very few bonds actually traded hands.

Traders said that activity in the junk bond market was extremely quiet on Friday, partly because many market participants were attending Morgan Stanley & Co.'s high-yield conference in Phoenix, Ariz.

Meanwhile, First Boston Corp. said its junk bond index produced a positive 0.42% return during the month of April. First Boston also reported that buy-backs of junk bond debt hit record levels in the first quarter. The firm said that about $1.5 billion of junk bonds was bought back by issuers during the first quarter, and another $1 billion in high-yield debt was retired.

Ever since the junk bond market went into a tailspin last September, companies such as Mark IV Industries Inc., Burlington Holdings Inc. and Colt Industries Inc. have been in the market buying back their debt at discounts.

"The market prices that we have now are such that the best investment companies can make is to buy back their debt at these levels," said Kingman Penniman, senior vice president at McCarthy Crisanti & Maffei Inc. "I think as we start looking at the numbers, and we start seeing the 10-Qs coming in, we are going to see continued repurchases of debt." A 10-Q is a filing a company makes with the Securities and Exchange Commission that gives details of earnings and related financial information.
