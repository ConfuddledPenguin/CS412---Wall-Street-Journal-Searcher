Risks Are Seen If Milken Testifies Before Congress

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 05/07/90

WASHINGTON -- Securities and Exchange Commission Chairman Richard Breeden said that any congressional testimony by Michael Milken before the former financier's legal settlement becomes final could introduce "complications into the case." Last week, a House subcommittee voted to offer immunity to the former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. head of junk-bond operations in return for his testimony. A spokesman for Mr. Milken has said the financier would testify before Congress if he is granted immunity. The vote now goes to the full House Energy and Commerce Committee.

But at a news briefing, Mr. Breeden said, "If you had immunized testimony before . . . the plea agreement becomes irrevocable on the day of the settlement, it does create the potential for introducing complications into the case."

He also said that the agency would "work with Congress to make sure they understand the status of the case." He didn't elaborate.

Mr. Milken, whose settlement is scheduled to become final on Oct. 1, pleaded guilty to six violations of federal securities laws and agreed to pay penalties and restitution of $600 million.

Separately, SEC enforcement chief William McLucas said he doesn't expect completed settlements to be reopened in the wake of a U.S. appeals court ruling that overturned the insider-trading conviction of former Gruntal & Co. broker Robert Chestman. That decision last Wednesday attacked the underpinnings of many SEC insider-trading cases.

"You make a settlement and you live with it," Mr. McLucas said, adding that the agency is "not happy" with the decision. Chairman Breeden declined to say whether the agency would appeal.

Mr. Breeden also said he will tell the Senate Agriculture Committee tomorrow that he supports a three-pronged strategy for changing the "very real problems" of split regulation in the stock and futures markets. The SEC regulates stocks and options and the Commodity Futures Trading Commission oversees stock-index futures, a hedging product that allows investors to bet on the movement of a basket of stocks without buying the actual shares.

Mr. Breeden said he will support switching stock-index futures to the SEC's purview. In addition, he proposed putting a single government agency in charge of both stock and futures margins, or the good faith money needed to make a trade; currently, the Federal Reserve sets margin requirements for stock, and commodity exchanges set them for futures.

The SEC chairman also said he favors ending a provision in the Commodity Exchange Act that allows products with futures elements to be traded only on futures exchanges.
