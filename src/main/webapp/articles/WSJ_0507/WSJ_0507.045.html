Railroad Emergency Panel Is Named to Avert Walkout

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/07/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush formed an emergency board to try to resolve bargaining issues between railroads and rail unions, heading off the threat of a nationwide strike. "The creation of this board . . . is necessary to forestall the possibility of a crippling nationwide rail strike," White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said. A rail strike, besides idling railroad workers and stranding riders, could cause more than 500,000 layoffs within two weeks in industries served by railroads, the White House said.

The railroads and unions are especially at odds over health-care costs, the stickiest issue in collective bargaining in the past two years. The roads want to shift more of the cost burden to employees, which the unions oppose.

Under the Rail Labor Act, an emergency board usually has 30 days to make contract recommendations; afterward, the railroads may put their own contract terms into effect and the unions are free to strike. But in this case both sides have agreed to give the board until Sept. 15. They also say they won't act until 30 days after the board reports or if Congress isn't in session. Typically, when the parties have rejected an emergency board's recommendations, Congress has averted a long rail strike by putting the recommendations into effect by statute.
