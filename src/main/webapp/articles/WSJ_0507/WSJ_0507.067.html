Uncertainty Over Intentions of Soviets On Arms, Germany Clouds Summit Hopes

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 05/07/90

WARSAW -- With the Bush-Gorbachev summit only three weeks away, U.S. officials are concerned that the Soviet leadership has yet to develop a clear position on the major issues of arms control and German unity. After two days of talks in West Germany with Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze, including the opening of the six-power negotiations on German unity, Secretary of State James Baker and his advisers were left with the impression that Moscow is still wrestling with the issues.

In the meetings, Mr. Shevardnadze continued to press the tough positions that first emerged a month ago at a meeting in Washington. But U.S. officials said there were hints from the Soviet delegation that these stands aren't necessarily the final word from Moscow. Mr. Shevardnadze bolstered this view by going to great lengths in his public appearances to play down the Soviet opposition to Western proposals.

"I don't think we should dramatize," he said at a news conference. "After all, this is quite a normal phenomenon around a bargaining table."

The uncertainty about Soviet intentions is frustrating to Mr. Baker and his aides, who flew here yesterday to assure Poland's leaders that the German unity talks will respond to their worries about guaranteeing the German-Polish border.

Mr. Baker and his aides believe that, until a few weeks ago, they were closing in on a series of historic accords that would seal the Soviet retreat from Eastern Europe, remove major Soviet advantages in nuclear weapons and reunify Germany on Western terms.

Now, the secretary of state can't be certain whether he will find any flexibility in Moscow next week when he flies there for crucial pre-summit bargaining. "We won't know where they really stand on things until we get to Moscow," said a senior State Department official after the Baker-Shevardnadze meeting. "It may not emerge until Gorbachev sits down at the summit."

Mr. Shevardnadze laid out an uncompromising position at the first meeting of the "two-plus-four" talks in Bonn with Mr. Baker and the foreign ministers of the two German states, Britain and France. He told them that the Soviets couldn't accept a unified Germany as a member of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization with unlimited armed forces. And he rejected a Western push to bless German unity even before such issues are resolved.

Meeting privately with Mr. Baker, the Soviet official also clung to Moscow's hard line on such arms-control issues as cruise missiles.

American officials concede they aren't sure why Moscow has dug in its heels. They see a possibility that the change is a late-inning bargaining maneuver or a reflection of the paralysis in the Soviet leadership caused by the distractions of the economic and ethnic crises in the U.S.S.R.

A more pessimistic view that is gaining ground in the administration is that Moscow may resist further compromises until the end of an historic Communist Party congress set to start July 2. The theory is that Mr. Gorbachev may be unwilling to anger hard-line opponents before the congress convenes. That would mean that agreements on arms, trade and other matters, which were expected to be reached at the summit, could be delayed.

But the American officials still express confidence that the Soviets will come around. On Germany, they believe, Moscow will have to drop objections to unity because the two Germanys are rapidly merging and the Soviets wouldn't want to risk losing German economic ties by playing the spoiler.

On arms control, the administration is pinning its hopes on unspecified "new ideas" that it is offering the Soviets to resolve the sticking points. And officials hope that President Bush's recent decision to abandon new nuclear weapons in Germany will give Mr. Gorbachev more maneuvering room with his internal critics.
