What's News -- Business and Finance

BUDGET TALKS MAY BEGIN soon between the White House and Congress. Four top congressional leaders told Bush yesterday they are ready to begin negotiations on a package of big tax increases and spending cuts aimed at sharply reducing the federal deficit. Bush also signaled his willingness to consider raising taxes, House GOP leader Michel said. "Everything is on the table," Rep. Michel quoted the president as saying. ---

The Group of Seven backed a 50% capital increase for the IMF and appeared to agree on revised rankings for the IMF's top contributors. The group also said the yen's continued weakness "may have undesirable consequences" but noted currency markets have stabilized recently.

---

Corporate profits fell 18% in the first quarter, hurt by increased labor costs, higher interest payments and unusually big write-offs, a Journal survey shows. Analysts expect profits to remain soft in the current quarter but to pick up in the second half.

---

The jobless rate rose to 5.4% in April from 5.2% the previous month, indicating continued weakness in the economy. The increase surprised many analysts because of recent signs the economy was picking up.

Stock and bond prices rallied Friday as the jobless report eased speculation that the Fed may tighten credit soon. The dollar finished mixed.

---

Drexel paid creditors over $600 million in the three months before it filed for Chapter 11 protection, which means the payments may be recoverable under federal bankruptcy law, according to bankruptcy lawyers.

---

First Executive has hired First Boston to explore a restructuring of the struggling Los Angles-based insurer that could include the sale of one or more operating units, sources said.

---

Two Campeau units, Allied Stores and Federated, posted fiscal 1990 losses totaling $2.43 billion.

---

Insurers won another legal battle against California's Proposition 103. A Los Angeles judge invalidated a key aspect of the 1988 auto-insurance law.

---

Columbia S&L posted a $293.3 million loss for the first quarter, reflecting further decay in its junk-bond holdings. But officials claimed progress in an effort to sell the bonds.

Far West Financial reported a $26.2 million quarterly loss, leaving the Belzberg-controlled California thrift firm short of a capital requirement.

---

Fox Broadcasting won FCC permission to offer added programming without having to adhere to network TV rules affecting rerun sales.

NBC News plans to launch a 24hour news service similar to CNN's Headline News, sources said.

---

Brazil began opening its market to more imports, including telephone equipment, as promised. But high import duties were imposed on most items to protect domestic firms.

---

Saatchi & Saatchi fired two top executives in a power struggle that was won by the ad giant's new chief executive, Robert Louis-Dreyfus.

---

Wheeling-Pittsburgh is likely to tap D. Leonard Wise, a steel industry veteran, as chief executive after the company emerges from Chapter 11.

---

Several small Western firms are being severely hurt by continued payment delays by Soviet concerns. Some of the Western businesses have filed claims with export insurers.
