Abreast of the Market: One Longtime Bull Believes a Rally Is About to Burst From the Gloom...

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 05/07/90

The stock market sure can be discouraging these days. Inflation is at worrisome levels. The bond market has been slumping all this year, with interest rates high and maybe headed higher. Corporate profits are lousy. And stock prices remain stubbornly stuck in a trading range.

But is it possible that lurking somewhere in the gloom are the makings of a big rally not too far in the future? Some of the market's biggest bulls are contending these aren't really big problems at all. And once that becomes evident to investors, these optimistic analysts see nothing ahead but blue sky for stock prices.

"From sometime in the second quarter to sometime in the second half, we should have a strong move up," says Peter Canelo, the chief market strategist for Bear, Stearns and a longtime bull. "We'll break out of this trading range we've been in and be making new highs by midyear, possibly pushing the Dow to 3,000 sometime in the second half."

The logic behind Mr. Canelo's ebullience is straightforward: None of the problems that are bothering investors are as bad as they seem, and most of them will soon get a lot better. Corporate profits, for instance, "are not quite as bad as most people thought they would be and certainly not as bad as the fourth-quarter profits," he says.

Shrugging off Friday's weaker-than-expected employment report as just another statistical fluctuation, Mr. Canelo says recent reports are signaling stepped-up economic growth, which will turn out to be stronger than most people thought. That momentum will enhance corporate profits for the second quarter and beyond.

Key to Mr. Canelo's argument is that he doesn't think the bond market -- the principal determinant of interest rates -- is nearly as sick as it appears. In part, that's because inflation isn't as bad as it seems. While prices clearly rose at an unusually rapid pace in the first quarter, he points out that oil prices, one of the big factors in that rise, are falling now along with gold, usually a reliable barometer of inflation.

And while many worry-warts focus on the consumer price index, which rose 0.5% in March, Mr. Canelo argues that a better guide to future inflation rates is the producer price index, a measure of wholesale inflation that actually fell 0.2% in March.

Some bond bears also complain that the economy is reaccelerating at a pace that will drive up credit demands, putting more upward pressure on interest rates, at a time when the savings-and-loan industry bailout is already forcing rates up.

Not so, says Mr. Canelo. For one thing, he argues that the S&L crisis won't result so much in creating new debt as in replacing individual S&Ls' short-term funding with long-term debt managed by the government.

And is 3% growth really excessive? "People are arguing that the economy may be growing at a 2% to 3% annual rate," he says. "But in a historical context, that's not even hitting the average rate of growth we've seen in the past."

All these budding silver linings may become evident in the next week or so, says Mr. Canelo. Friday's employment statistics support his argument that the economy is growing at only a moderate pace. And the Treasury's big $30.5 billion quarterly refunding auction later this week should demonstrate that interest rates needn't rise higher to keep investors interested in the bond market. Once that issue is settled, stocks will become considerably more attractive, he says.

"The place to be will be in technology, capital goods and some of the consumer and basic industry cyclicals," says Mr. Canelo. Companies in those areas will benefit from continued U.S. growth, and they stand to be the real winners over the next several years as European economies begin expanding.

"I don't believe we're going to be supplying Europe with lots of McDonald's, Coca-Cola and Levis," he says. "What we're going to be able to provide are Apple computers, Lotus 1-2-3 software, Caterpillar tractors, John Deere agricultural machinery and Briggs & Stratton motors."

But Mr. Canelo's arguments don't sway nonbelievers. "Bad news isn't necessarily followed by good news," says Abby Cohen, market strategist at Barclays de Zoete Wedd in New York. Indeed, she thinks the stock market has to go down, not up, to reflect the true investment environment. "It's remarkable to me that the stock market has been able to stay in a state of suspended animation as long as it has."

The problems that Mr. Canelo dismisses are precisely the ones that have convinced Ms. Cohen the market is at unsustainably high levels. She thinks profits are likely to remain disappointing for several quarters. Inflation is and will continue to be a problem, she argues.

"A lot of people presumed that when the economy got sluggish, they would see inflation come down," she says, "but that isn't necessarily the case. It takes a pretty good wallop to the side of the head for an extended period of time to really reduce inflationary pressures, and we haven't had that kind of downturn." And that's why the other big problem, high interest rates, isn't likely to be solved anytime soon, either.

Ms. Cohen suspects U.S. stock prices have held up, not because the U.S. outlook is so bright but because it isn't nearly as bad as the outlook for other markets around the world. The result is an influx of foreign money into U.S. financial markets, encouraged by the dollar's relative strength against other currencies. But can it continue? "Who knows what happens to the dollar," asks Ms. Cohen. But unless some unforeseen event comes along, she figures the stock market is 15% to 20% too high.

Suresh Bhirud, the chief strategist at Oppenheimer, concurs. A big earnings rebound could make the stock-market picture a lot brighter, he says, "but it's hard to make a case that earnings would rise that spectacularly, because profit margins are under enormous pressure." He says companies are incurring steadily higher costs, especially labor costs, that they're unable to pass along in sharply higher price increases. For example, he notes, Caterpillar's first-quarter revenue was up 8%, but profit fell nearly 30%.

He also expects interest rates to keep rising as a consequence of that inflation. His forecast is for the yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond to hit 10% later this year, compared with the current worrisomely high 9%.

Like Ms. Cohen, he suspects stock prices are 15% to 20% too high. They'll come down, he says, probably in a prolonged decline. Unless an investor is prepared to weather that kind of market, cash is king, he says. "In this kind of environment, how can we recommend stocks?" he asks.

Friday's Market Activity

Stock prices extended their week-long rally after a surprisingly weak report on April employment took pressure off interest rates.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 14.19 to 2,710.36. The close above 2,700 was the first since April 19. Broader measures fared well, too, and advancing issues on the New York Stock Exchange outnumbered decliners, 1,020 to 465. For the week the Dow Jones industrials rose 65.31 points, or 2.5%.

Pfizer fell 7/8 to 57 on 2.3 million shares. The California Supreme Court cleared the way for a trial in a lawsuit filed by a recipient of a heart valve manufactured by Pfizer's Shiley unit. The suit seeks damages for emotional trauma suffered from having the valve, which was taken off the market after a number of patients died following ruptures in the devices. Responding to the ruling, Pfizer noted that 19 similar cases in other jurisdictions have been dismissed and another 19 have been withdrawn voluntarily.

Actively traded technology stocks posted solid gains. International Business Machines rose 2 1/2 to 110 1/2 on 1.5 million shares, Compaq Computer advanced 2 1/4 to 103 3/4, Digital Equipment climbed 1 3/4 to 88 and Hewlett-Packard rose 3/4 to 44 3/4.

Banking and financial-services stocks also paced the market's rally, reflecting the sharp decline in rates. Among bank issues, Citicorp rose 1/2 to 22 3/4 on 1.8 million shares; J.P. Morgan went up 1 1/8 to 34 3/4; Bank of New York rose 1 1/8 to 32; and Security Pacific rose 7/8 to 37 3/8. In financial services, Federal National Mortgage Association advanced 1 1/8 to 34 7/8, and American Express added 3/8 to 26 7/8.

But Kemper dropped 5/8 to 34 1/2 after a former public-finance executive with its Prescott, Ball & Turben unit won a $38.2 million arbitration award in an employee contract dispute. Kemper said it would take a $19.5 million charge in the second quarter for the award, although Prescott will contest the decision.

Several casino issues posted especially sharp gains; Caesars World fared best by gaining 1 3/8 to 18 7/8, a 7.9% advance. Golden Nugget gained 1 5/8 to 34 1/8, and Circus Circus Enterprises added 2 to 54 1/2.

Hibernia went up 1/4 to 16 1/4 on 1.7 million shares, with one pair of large block trades designed to capture its next quarterly dividend payment accounting for 1.6 million of the total. The stock carries a 6.2% dividend yield and went ex-dividend Thursday.

A strong performance on the London stock market spilled over into U.S. trading in the shares of a number of British-based companies. British Petroleum rose 1 7/8 to 63 7/8; British Telecommunications rose 1 1/2 to 43 1/2; British Gas gained 1 3/8 to 33 5/8; Glaxo Holdings added 3/4 to 26 1/8; SmithKline Beecham firmed 5/8 to 36 1/8; and Shell Transport & Trading rose 1 1/4 to 45.
