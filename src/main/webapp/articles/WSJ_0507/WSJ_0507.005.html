International: ANC Says `White Minority Rights' Issue May Be a Hurdle in Talks With Pretoria

  • Author: Roger Thurow
  • Date: 05/07/90

SOWETO, South Africa -- After three days of historic talks with the white government last week, the African National Congress is pressing Pretoria to drop its insistence on protecting "white minority rights" in a new constitution. At a report-back rally attended by 30,000 blacks in Soweto yesterday, ANC leader Nelson Mandela said the meetings went a long way toward breaking down suspicion between the participants, who have been bitter enemies for the past three decades. The talks were designed to clear away obstacles to eventual negotiations over ending the racial-segregation system known as apartheid and shaping a new constitution where the white minority will share power with the black majority.

The government said it will review its existing security legislation, including possibly lifting the four-year-old state of emergency, while the ANC said it will look "hard and earnestly" at suspending its armed struggle. The two sides formed a joint working group to hammer out agreement on the release of political prisoners and immunity for political offenses.

But Mr. Mandela said the government's opposition to a one-man, one-vote majority rule system and its insistence that the rights of minorities, such as the whites, be constitutionally protected, "means that white South Africa doesn't yet trust us." South African President F.W. de Klerk, who earlier this year legalized the ANC after a 30-year ban and released Mr. Mandela from prison, says simple majority rule will lead to black domination and wants to make "group protection" a subject for future negotiations.

"At a time when we want to move away from all kinds of racism we don't want discussions, which have started so well, to be bedeviled by any solutions based on race," Mr. Mandela told the crowd. "I'm going to urge Mr. de Klerk that he mustn't bring this {group concept} to any conference between the ANC and the government."

Rather, Mr. Mandela said, "Our duty, both as black and white leaders, is now to go out into the country and talk to our people not in terms of blacks and whites but in terms of South Africans. We have to convince them that any form of racism is a formula for disaster."

The talks fulfilled the ANC's dream of sitting down with the government as equal partners, something it has pushed for since its inception in 1912.

While the talks were hailed across South Africa, there were dissenting voices. Former President P.W. Botha said he wouldn't renew his membership in the ruling National Party because it earlier lifted the ban on the South African Communist Party and allowed the communist leader, Joe Slovo, to be part of the ANC's negotiation team.
