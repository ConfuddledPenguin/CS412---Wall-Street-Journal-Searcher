First Boston Hired to Advise First Executive

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 05/07/90

First Executive Corp., the struggling Los Angeles-based insurer, hired investment banker First Boston Corp. to explore a restructuring that could include the sale of one or more of First Executive's operating units, people familiar with the company said. First Executive last year tried to sell its second-largest insurance subsidiary, but the proposed buyer, a private investor group, was unable to complete the purchase of the New York-based unit. First Boston, which is owned by CS First Boston Inc., will examine alternatives for First Executive's New York subsidiary and other units, people familiar with the company said.

Meanwhile, a top official of Kidder, Peabody & Co., one of First Executive's traditional investment bankers, said Kidder is representing another company "that is interested in a transaction with First Executive."

Scott Newquist, an executive managing director at Kidder in charge of investment banking, declined to identify Kidder's client or specify the nature of its proposed transaction.

However, speculation within the insurance industry has centered on Kidder's parent, General Electric Co., which may be interested in purchasing part of First Executive's junk-bond portfolio or one of its insurance units. In particular, GE could be interested in First Executive's New York-based unit.

Fred Carr, chairman of First Executive, confirmed in an interview yesterday that First Executive had hired First Boston. He declined to specify First Boston's assignment. First Boston officials declined to comment.

One person familiar with First Executive said First Boston's assignment is "to help the board and the management understand what options the company might have in addition to what it's doing now."

First Executive's move comes as the company, which has assets of more than $19 billion, is under pressure on several fronts. For the fourth quarter of last year, First Executive reported a stunning loss of $835.7 million, or $9.98 a share, after writing down the value of its $8 billion junk bond portfolio by $859 million. First Executive, which was a major customer of now-failed Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., is believed to hold the nation's largest portfolio of high-yield, high-risk junk bonds and has been hurt by the recent swoon in the market for such bonds.

News of the write-down caused a surge in redemptions by holders of policies issued by First Executive's two main units, Executive Life Insurance Co. of California and Executive Life Insurance Co. of New York. Policy surrenders and redemptions were $559 million in the first two months of this year, more than double the year-earlier rate. First Executive has told securities analysts that the pace of withdrawals slowed in March and April, but hasn't provided specific figures.

First Executive also could face problems from proposed new insurance regulations that would force insurers to put up more capital as a reserve against possible losses on the some of the lowest-rated junk bonds they hold. Executive Life is also being closely examined by California insurance regulators and First Executive's securities offering last year is being investigated by the Securities and Exchange Commission.

As a result of these problems, First Executive's common stock has tumbled from a price of about $17 a share within the last 12 months to close at $2.75 a share on Friday in the national over-the-counter market.

One disgruntled major shareholder, Rosewood Financial Corp., Dallas, which owns a 9.9% stake in First Executive, offered earlier this year to buy the company for cash and stock that it valued at $6 a share. First Executive rejected the offer as inadequate. Rosewood, an arm of the Caroline Rose Hunt trust, hasn't renewed its offer.

Herbert E. Goodfriend, an insurance analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities, New York, views the hiring of First Boston as a sign that First Executive will become more aggressive in defending itself. "We surmise that this new step by the company will include possible recapitalization and/or new money/new blood," Mr. Goodfriend wrote in a recent report. Mr. Goodfriend declined to elaborate, citing a Prudential-Bache policy that bars him from discussing First Executive with the press beyond his written reports. A Prudential-Bache spokeswoman declined to explain the firm's reason for barring Mr. Goodfriend from speaking about First Executive.

Mr. Newquist of Kidder Peabody said his firm had discussed with First Executive the possibility of evaluating First Executive's portfolio. But, Mr. Newquist said, "we feel we're conflicted from working for First Executive because of another assignment we have." He said the assignment is from "another company that is interested in a transaction with First Executive." He declined to identify the company or the nature of the transaction.

For First Boston, the hiring by First Executive represents the fourth major insurance or junk-bond assignment in recent months. First Boston is trying to sell the insurance units of AmBase Corp., New York; American General Corp., a Houston insurance and finance company; and the $3 billion junk bond portfolio of insolvent Columbia Savings & Loan Association, Beverly Hills, Calif.
