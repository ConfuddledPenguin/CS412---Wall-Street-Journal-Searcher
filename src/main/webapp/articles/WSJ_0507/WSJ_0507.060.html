Technology: Computer `Hacker' Robert Morris Gets 3 Years Probation

  • Author: David Stipp
  • Date: 05/07/90

Robert T. Morris Jr., the computer "hacker" who unleashed a virus that disrupted a nationwide electronic mail network in 1988, was sentenced by a federal judge in Syracuse, N.Y., to three years probation and a $10,000 fine. Judge Howard Munson also ordered Mr. Morris, 25 years old, to perform 400 hours of community service.

Computer experts said the sentence was too light to serve as much of a deterrent to similar crimes by hackers.

In January, Mr. Morris was convicted of violating the federal Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, under which he could have been imprisoned for as long as five years and fined as much as $250,000. The case has been closely watched in the computer industry because of rising concern about breaches of computer security.

"I don't think people in this country are quite ready to send someone to the slammer" for launching a computer virus, said Belden Menkus, a computer-security consultant in Hillsboro, Tenn. "If there had been a theft of funds, a jail term would have been more likely." Added Clifford Stoll, a Harvard University astronomer known as an authority on computer breakins: "I'm not surprised {by the sentence} because of the nature of what {Mr. Morris} did and the lack of malice involved."

During Mr. Morris's trial, his attorney argued that he was guilty of a mistake rather than a crime. Mr. Morris's virus, or "worm" program, was designed to spread quietly through Internet, an electronic mail system used by academic, corporate and government researchers. But because of a programming error, Mr. Morris said in his testimony, it careened out of control. The program sped through thousands of computers nationwide and disrupted operations at many computer centers linked by the network.

Federal prosecutors argued that Mr. Morris was guilty of more than an innocent prank, noting that he stole computer passwords and designed his program to be difficult to eradicate from computers.

Among computer professionals, "the consensus was that {Mr. Morris} should have gotten at least a modest amount of jail time as a deterrent," said Jack Bologna, editor of Computers and Security magazine in Plymouth, Mich. "Even 90 days would have been better than nothing at all."

Mr. Morris plans to appeal his conviction in an attempt to erase the felony from his record, said Thomas Guidoboni, his attorney, at a news conference. He added that Mr. Morris already has had offers from computer-service providers willing to put him to work to discharge his community-service requirement. One offer came from a state agency in West Virginia that wants Mr. Morris to work on computer programs that help in collecting child-support payments, Mr. Guidoboni added.
