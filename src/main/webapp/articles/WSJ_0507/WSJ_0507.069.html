FarWest Financial Posts 1st-Period Loss Of $26.2 Million Due to Junk Portfolio

  • Author: G. Christian Hill
  • Date: 05/07/90

The Belzberg family's FarWest Financial Corp.'s $26.2 million first-quarter loss leaves the big California thrift-holding company roughly $140 million short of a federal capital requirement it must satisfy to survive. FarWest's loss, disclosed late Friday, is equal to $3.36 a share. It resulted from a $36 million reduction in the carrying value of the company's junk-bond portfolio, once one of the largest among thrifts. The company now has a tangible capital ratio of slightly more than 1% of assets, nearly $20 million short of the required 1.5%. But more importantly, it appears to be about $140 million short of meeting a stricter so-called risk-based capital requirement. In the year-earlier period, FarWest earned $5.6 million.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, FarWest shares closed at $2.125, unchanged.

Canada's Belzberg family is FarWest's controlling shareholder; William Belzberg is chairman. Unless the family provides a capital infusion, FarWest will have difficulty complying with the capital requirements. The company said it continues to negotiate a compliance plan with regulators. Failure to meet capital standards can result in federal sanctions, including seizure of the institution.

FarWest is one of about a half-dozen big California thrifts who have faltered or failed this year because of diversification either outside California or outside traditional home mortgage lending. Junk-bond losses are likely to cause the failure of another big Los Angeles thrift, Columbia Savings & Loan Association.

FarWest accumulated a junk-bond portfolio of $667 million, much of it purchased in the 40 days prior to the August 1989 passage of the thrift-rescue legislation. That law required thrifts to get out of the junk-bond business and, in effect, carry those bonds at the lower of cost or market value. The junk-bond market has plummeted since last fall as some highly leveraged companies failed or sought refinancing. Junk-bond sales by thrifts have worsened the decline in values.

FarWest reported a 1989 net loss of $46.1 million, or $5.90 a share, mostly because of junk-bond write-downs taken in the fourth quarter. According to data compiled by Miami thrift analyst Kenneth Thomas, the company sold $274 million of high-yield, high-risk bonds in the fourth quarter, leaving it with $393 million of junk bonds. FarWest shrank again in the first period, to $4 billion in assets from $4.35 billion, but it didn't say whether the decline resulted from more junk-bond sales.
