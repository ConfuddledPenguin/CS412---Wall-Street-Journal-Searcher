Quarterly Review of Corporate Earnings: First Quarter, 1990 --- Companies' Net Income Fell 18%; Despite Some Pickup in Economy

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/07/90

The U.S. economy is displaying fresh signs of life, but corporate earnings still are sagging. Many securities analysts look for another soft profits showing in the current quarter but expect a pickup in the second half. In the 1990 first quarter, net income of 654 major corporations fell 18% from a year earlier, a Wall Street Journal survey finds. This compared with a 14% drop in last year's fourth quarter. After-tax earnings on continuing operations fell 15%.

The inflation-adjusted gross national product rose at an annual rate of 2.1% in the first quarter -- hardly spectacular but a substantial improvement over the 1.1% gain in the fourth quarter. But profits were depressed by increases in labor costs and interest payments. In addition, earnings were held down by unusually large writeoffs. In part, banks were writing off bad loans, but Susan Lakatos, a Kidder, Peabody & Co. economist, says that, in addition, "corporate America is still cleaning up its balance sheet."

Melissa R. Brown, a Prudential-Bache Securities analyst, has been pessimistic about earnings for some time, but she says first-quarter profits "came in worse than expected. Although earnings for the quarter are not wildly disappointing, there's not much to cheer about." Even sectors that did a little better than expected, she says, did less well than a year ago.

Richard D. Rippe of Dean Witter Reynolds, however, believes that continuing moderate increases in growth will raise business volume. By later this year he expects rising capacity utilization to widen profit margins. He expects pretax reported profits for the full year to decline somewhat less than they did in 1989 and predicts a sharp gain in earnings in 1991.

The 51 economists interviewed monthly by Blue Chip Economic Indicators in general share Mr. Rippe's view. Last month on the average they forecast a 2.4% drop in pretax profits this year, down from the 8.3% drop in 1989. But they predicted that earnings would rise by 7.4% in 1991.

Among major groups, profits were down sharply in autos, oil and steel. Most aerospace concerns posted gains but some had major downturns. Computer company results were mixed.

The Big Three auto makers took strong medicine to cut bulging North American vehicle inventories and suffered the side effects of sharply lower profits. General Motors, Ford and Chrysler shut nearly all of their U.S. factories for one to two weeks during January, and slowed output during the rest of the January-to-March period. At the same time, the Big Three continued to offer heavy discounts in an effort to stimulate a sluggish market and hold ground against Japanese competitors.

Detroit's first-quarter retrenchment rippled back to auto parts makers, who saw sales volumes and profits squeezed hard during the period. And it didn't help relations with the United Auto Workers union, which will negotiate new contracts with the Big Three this fall. On the positive side, overall Big Three inventories now are at relatively lean levels.

One-time gains last year at Boeing, McDonnell Douglas and GenCorp create the deceptive appearance of a sharp drop in first-quarter aerospace and defense industry profitability. But quarterly earnings for most companies actually rose this year, and Boeing's operating profit -- after negating a $298 million credit in 1989 from an accounting change -- surged 87% on the strength of its booming airliner deliveries. Northrop's rise in net income included $67 million in the latest quarter from the sale of its headquarters building. Lockheed got better results from its technological services and aeronautical systems segments -- and found itself free of write-downs for a change. Wolfgang Demisch of UBS Securities noted that despite having operating earnings that generally have been "really quite upbeat," defense companies, with few exceptions, have had their stocks hammered because of the turmoil in the Pentagon budget. Wall Street's reaction, he says, reflects that even if short-term earnings are strong, "it's hard to make out a scenario where the aerospace results will accelerate from here on out."

Computer makers showed wide variations in profits. But industry watchers were generally heartened by better-than-expected news from the two biggest players, International Business Machines and Digital Equipment, and they talked of a possible revival in domestic demand, which has been sluggish for years.

IBM's strong showing, which included the company's best U.S. results in recent memory, surprised Wall Street and sent the stock surging. But IBM has had isolated good quarters before, and analysts cautioned that the company soon may face competitive pressures in its backbone business of mainframe computers.

Even though Digital's profit fell 90% on a $150 million charge, analysts were relieved that the company didn't post a loss, as had been feared. In addition, Digital posted its first rise in domestic sales in four quarters. Because NCR and Tandem Computers also had reported modest gains in U.S. business, analysts smelled a trend and said reports of the home market's death might have been exaggerated.

Crude oil prices that were $3 to $4 a barrel higher boosted exploration and production profits of the major oil companies. Higher natural gas prices early in the quarter also contributed. But petrochemical profits declined, largely because crude is the main feedstock for chemicals. Gasoline refining and marketing profits were generally weaker for the same reason. Texaco a year earlier took a $1.19 billion gain from the sale of Texaco Canada Inc., throwing off results for the company and the group. Phillips had gains totaling $238 million in the latest quarter and a $90 million charge. Exxon had a $535 million gain a year earlier.

Steel industry profits were reduced by sharply lower volumes and weaker prices because of the general slump in manufacturing. Auto plant shutdowns and efforts by other major steel users to cut inventories whacked steel operations to less than 80% of capacity during January, and the mills didn't get up to 85% of capacity until the last half of March. By contrast, the industry operated at nearly 90% of capacity during the 1989 first quarter. Bethlehem Steel had a $55 million restructuring charge in the 1989 quarter.

While the outlook is brighter for the second quarter, profits, nonetheless, will fall well below the year-ago period, when most manufacturing was booming. "It remains to be seen whether the second quarter will be just the seasonally strongest quarter in a weak year, or something better than that," says Jeffrey F. Miller, metals analyst with Duff & Phelps. Shipments of many types of steel are relatively good, reflecting a general improvement in U.S. manufacturing and an end to six months or more of inventory reduction.

Aluminum companies felt the manufacturing slowdown in the quarter. Profits dropped sharply, mainly because of lower prices. Aluminum ingot prices dropped 40% from a year earlier; prices for sheet and most other products also declined, but by a lesser amount. In addition, Aluminum Co. of America lost can sheet sales because it refused to match an industry price cut last fall. Aluminum's outlook is brightening as the manufacturing economy picks up. Shipments are increasing, and the price of ingot has started to rise. Nonetheless, second-quarter profits are expected to fall well below the year-ago period, the peak earnings quarter in an extremely strong year.

Some gold companies boosted output significantly and sold their gold for higher prices, mitigating the impact of higher cash mining costs. Homestake, for example, increased gold production by about 33% and sold its gold for about $14 an ounce more than the year before, posting a nearly twofold increase in earnings.

Copper companies' earnings declined on a 20% to 30% drop in realized copper prices.

Forest products concerns saw profits drop, citing softening pulp and paper markets. Georgia-Pacific was also hurt by higher interest expenses. Besides a decline in price and demand for pulp, Louisiana-Pacific blamed unusually wet weather that slowed building activity and precluded some logging, and uncertainty over interest rates. Willamette was helped by increased operating efficiencies and stronger-than-expected building materials markets. At Weyerhaeuser, lower prices and margins in most pulp and paper markets were only partially offset by improved results in forest products.

Paper companies saw profits fall, citing price discounting. Bowater's profit drop reflected increased discounting in the newsprint, coated-paper and pulp markets. Union Camp cited lower prices for linerboard and uncoated business paper. Boise Cascade was hurt by lower prices and sluggish demand for most of its paper grades.

Chemical industry earnings were down because of weak demand, price declines and cold weather-related increases in raw materials costs. Many companies, including Dow, Du Pont and Monsanto, were affected by slowdowns in the U.S. auto and construction industries. Analysts say most companies will continue with flat to negative earnings comparisons this quarter.

A pickup in oil field activity in the U.S. and continued exploration activity world-wide contributed to strong gains for the equipment companies and drillers. While the increase in activity was not huge, companies have trimmed so much from their cost structures that any increase in business flows quickly to the bottom line. Even some long-suffering contract drilling companies, which narrowed their losses, believe an end to their red ink is in sight.

Natural gas pipeline companies, despite one of the warmest first quarters on record, managed generally higher net in come, what with special items, non-pipeline operations and Panhandle's acquisition of Texas Eastern. Panhandle's results for the rest of 1990 will be hurt by high interest expense, possible cash refunds to customers and ambitious capital spending.

Coal industry results were mixed, with good weather, productivity gains and mining expansions helping some producers boost profits. Ashland Coal revenue grew 18%, largely from the recent acquisition of new mines.

The rail group's earnings decline reflected a drop in carloadings of motor vehicles, metal products and other higher-value freight. But Burlington Northern and Norfolk Southern benefited from strong coal shipments. First-quarter losses at Santa Fe resulted from a litigation settlement. Some analysts predict modest rail-traffic gains in the second quarter.

Trucking company profits were hurt by higher fuel and labor costs as continued rate discounting held down revenue. Consolidated Freightways' first-quarter results reflected losses at its Emery Worldwide air-freight unit.

The two largest air-freight carriers, Federal Express and United Parcel Service, are still racking up large operating losses on their efforts to expand overseas, offset somewhat by recent price stabilization in the U.S. Federal Express is also still struggling to meld its Tiger purchase, particularly the seniority lists of its pilots. Paul Schlesinger, an analyst with Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette, expects revenue for the industry to continue growing in the 10%-to-15%-a-year range, with profit growth remaining spotty.

Among the marine transport concerns, American President's loss stemmed from competitive pressures in its Asia transportation business and thinner profit margins at its freight-brokerage operation.

The sharp slump in the market for big trucks, diesel engines and other parts continued to depress profits. Cummins, which supplies about half the diesel engines for heavy-duty trucks, said first-quarter production in North America for those trucks fell 23% from a year ago. Although they expect no immediate rebound, some companies, including Cummins and Eaton, predict improved truck demand late in 1990.

Several heavy-machinery makers continued to benefit from increased demand for their products, though some reported slowdowns in certain areas. Clark Equipment, for example, said its U.S. forklift and off-highway components businesses are in a "slight downturn." Although business for equipment used in road repairs and mining remains strong, Caterpillar continues to be hurt by soft demand for machines used in private construction projects, particularly in the U.S. It's not optimistic about the rest of the year. But Deere is likely to continue its strong gains in agricultural machinery as farmers continue their mini-spending spree. Larry D. Hollis, an analyst at Robert W. Baird & Co. in Milwaukee, said improved industrial production later in the year should help machinery makers.

CONSUMER, CYCLICAL

1ST QTR. 1990 1ST QTR. 1989 % CHG.

4,962,068 8,109,396 - 39

Many builders reported higher earnings in the quarter, but the profits mask underlying trouble. Relatively stable mortgage interest rates during the winter months helped buoy sales; among national concerns, diversification into healthy markets such as California and the Pacific Northwest also helped. But recent rate increases on mortgages and a liquidity crisis for builders induced by tighter thrift lending laws have created worries for the second-quarter outlook.

Retailers are expected to report mixed results for the April quarter. Discounting wasn't as prevalent as in the important fourth quarter, and some apparel stores fared well. But demand for goods sold by general merchants was soft, hurting profits for some. Sears Roebuck, which is on a calendar year, reported a big drop in profit because of what it called an "unsatisfactory" performance by its giant U.S. retail operation; the unit's loss widened to $134.2 million from $42.7 million a year earlier, when results were helped by publicity surrounding the company's launch of its "everyday low pricing" strategy. K mart is expected to report earnings that, at best, will be slightly higher than a year ago.

Lodging companies reflected the usual seasonal fluctuations, while casino companies reported mixed results largely because of the vagaries of luck at the gambling tables. Marriott's results didn't include airline catering operations that were shed last year. Hilton Hotels bene fited from sharp improvement in its casino segment. Golden Nugget soared because of business at its new Mirage resort, which is expected to give Las Vegas a boost during the spring and summer seasons.

Restaurant companies are still battling fiercely for market share, particularly in the high-profile, fast-food segment. That the latest quarter is also the industry's slowest of the season was evidenced by the failure of both McDonald's and Wendy's International to meet sales expectations. But smaller, niche companies seem to remain in control of their own destinies. For example, the benefits of franchising growth, aggressive advertising and tighter quality controls are starting to kick in at Shoney's, a big regional player in the family-restaurant market.

In recreational products, the first quarter is a seasonally low one for movie companies, particularly those that don't enjoy a big "spillover" from their Christmas films. This hurt Paramount, which was also affected by the seasonality of its publishing business. Disney managed another record-breaking quarter in spite of a disappointing slate of films (redeemed by the March release of "Pretty Woman") and the lack of the Easter holiday falling in the quarter, which affected the theme parks. Summer box office projections so far look as strong as last year's record level.

In the media sector, heavy demand for commercial time on the television networks led to a strong quarter for some concerns. CBS was boosted by its Super Bowl telecast. Capital Cities/ABC climbed with help from strong gains in broadcast revenue, which includes the network and eight TV stations. ABC's network and stations saw strong advertising demand and healthy ratings, except for the daytime schedule.

The nation's economic sluggishness dragged down earnings for most of those media companies with major daily newspapers and magazines. They were hit on two levels: falling spending by national advertisers and a continued slump in local retail ads. National and retail ad spending levels aren't expected to improve through 1990.

Newspaper publishers' earnings were helped by lower newsprint costs and industrywide cost cutting.

Fuel costs that soared by as much as 27% pummeled airline earnings. In addition, soft demand for travel early in the quarter made it hard for airlines to get fare increases to stick. Those factors caused many airlines to post losses, with the rest reporting meager profits. Analysts expect a better second quarter, although the improvement will be small. AMR, parent of American Airlines, ended a string of eight consecutive quarters of profit with its first loss since 1987. United's parent, UAL, was hurt by a one-time, $77.5 million payment to Texas Air's Continental to settle charges that United competed unfairly in the computer-reservation business.

For some apparel and textile makers, the seasonally sluggish first quarter saw lackluster retail sales and depressed earnings, though others posted healthy gains. At Hartmarx, menswear sales at retail and wholesale continue to lag.
