New Hampshire Savings Bank Posts Loss for First Quarter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/07/90

CONCORD, N.H. -- New Hampshire Savings Bank Corp. reported a first-quarter net loss of $6.7 million, or $1.09 a share, and warned that its turnaround strategy has "experienced setbacks in the face of a weakening economy." In the year-ago quarter, the thrift reported a net loss of $6 million, or 97 cents a share. Like other New England financial institutions, New Hampshire Savings's performance has been battered by a sluggish economy and floundering real estate market.

In the latest quarter, New Hampshire Savings took a provision for credit losses of $6.4 million, compared to a $9.4 million provision in the year-earlier quarter. It currently has a $58.1 million allowance for credit losses, nearly four times the yearago level.

The bank said that its nonperforming assets now amount to 9.6% of its $1.38 billion in assets. That's three times the yearago level of nonperformers and slightly wider than the level three months ago. Its allowance for credit losses is 43.5% of its actual nonperforming assets.

Joseph J. Butare Jr., chairman, said that "our ability to implement our (turnaround) plan successfully depends on a variety of factors, many of them beyond our control."

He said that the bank's primary capital ratio declined slightly to 5.74% from the year-end level, and remained below the 6% regulatory minimum capital requirement.

He said the bank had asked the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation to extend a temporary waiver allowing it to continue to accept "brokered deposits on which its liquidity in large part depends."

The bank also said that it has received from the FDIC a draft consent cease and desist order covering almost all aspects of its operations. Such orders have been signed by several troubled area banks.

Mr. Butare said that he is working to reduce the size of the bank and its costs. He said total assets have been reduced 10% in the past year; the bank has cut its work force 10% as well.

He added, "We don't expect the economy to rebound in the near term, and (we) continue to strengthen measures to safeguard asset quality against what promise to remain difficult and challenging conditions."
