WEST GERMAN UNEMPLOYMENT

West Germany's unemployment rate fell to its lowest level in almost eight years, dropping to 7.3% in April from 7.7% in March and 7.9% in April 1989, the Federal Labor Office said. The unadjusted number of unemployed fell to 1,914,585 from 2,013,057 in March.

The April unemployment rate was the lowest since 7.2% in July 1982. In September and October, the unemployment rate had also slipped to as low as 7.3%, but it began rising sharply after that as a steady stream of resettlers from East Germany turned into a flood.

"This illustrates that the people coming from the East are complementing the work force here," said Norbert Walter, chief economist at Deutsche Bank AG in Frankfurt. "They're not taking jobs away from Germans but are instead taking jobs that Germans aren't mobile enough for or aren't vocationally qualified for."

WORLD'S AIDS CASES GOING UP

The number of AIDS cases world-wide increased by nearly 17,000 in April over the previous month, the World Health Organization said. WHO put the cumulative total of officially reported cases at the end of April at 254,078, compared with 237,110 a month earlier. But the health agency believes the true global number of AIDS cases to be 600,000, with three to five million thought to be infected with the AIDS virus.

NOVEL SOVIET TV FARE

The Soviet Union will televise nationwide a Bible cartoon series aimed mainly at children, a senior official of the Finnish company supplying the film said. Hannu Haukka of the religious-broadcasting company Hyva Sanoma said the series based on the Old and New Testaments would be screened in all 15 Soviet republics for up to 200 million viewers by agreement with Soviet television authorities. "It will be the first time children in the Soviet Union have ever experienced such an exposure to religion," he said.

JORDANIAN EDICT

The Jordanian government announced that men will no longer be allowed to purchase beauty parlors or hire male beauticians to work in them. The Interior Ministry order, which took effect yesterday, doesn't apply to men who already own such shops or work in them, a ministry official said. The decision came in response to demands by Islamic fundamentalists in Jordan's 80-member parliament. The Moslem Brotherhood, the main Islamic faction, and Islamic independents hold about three dozen seats.

RELIEVING SPAIN'S CONGESTION

The Spanish government unveiled a mammoth public-works and transport program intended to alleviate the congestion that is strangling Spain's major cities and turning highways into permanent traffic jams. The project involves building alternate routes around Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, Seville and Malaga by the end of 1992. Prime Minister Felipe Gonzalez said the program's goal is "to bolster the hope that big cities can be made more livable in a few short years."

CHINA'S FOREIGN DEBT

China's foreign debt stood at $41.30 billion at the end of last year. The State Administration of Exchange Control said the debt included $37.03 billion in medium- and long-term loans and $4.27 billion in short-term loans. Chinese officials said the debt-service ratio, the principal and interest to be repaid in a year as compared with total foreign-exchange earnings, will remain between 15% and 20%. The internationally recognized danger line is 25%.

MRS. THATCHER BREATHES EASIER

British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher, in deep trouble over the economy and a new local tax, won some breathing space with her Conservatives' better-than-expected results in local elections last week.

Although the Tories suffered major losses in local-council seats, the outcome fell short of the rout forecast by pre-election opinion polls. And the Conservatives' victories in some key London boroughs allowed Mrs. Thatcher to argue that the controversial new local-government tax at last is beginning to work.

The opposition Labor Party fought the election as a referendum on the flat-rate community charge, or poll tax. The results make it easier for Mrs. Thatcher to resist calls for sweeping changes in the new law -- and calls for her own departure as party leader.

The outcome gave a boost Friday to government securities, the pound sterling and the London stock market. Nevertheless, any respite for the prime minister is likely to be brief as political attention swings back to the economy. Figures due to be released this Friday are expected to show that the annual rate of British inflation rose sharply last month from its 8.1% level in March.
