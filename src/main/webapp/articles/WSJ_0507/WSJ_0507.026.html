Machinists Union, McDonnell Douglas Reach Agreement

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 05/07/90

Machinists members at McDonnell Douglas Corp.'s St. Louis operations approved a three-year contract yesterday after the company and the union reached agreement during a marathon, 11th-hour bargaining session. As of last Thursday, the Machinists' negotiating committee had rejected what was described as the aerospace concern's final offer, and the rank and file seemed to be clamoring for a strike. But, at the urging of McDonnell Douglas, the two sides returned to the bargaining table at 10 o'clock Friday morning and stayed there until 3 a.m. Saturday, when a new accord was reached.

"We've been through a tough round of negotiations, but it all paid off," said Vince DeBlaze, the company's top official for the labor talks.

The membership ratified the contract, which features improvements in both wages and benefits over the earlier proposal, by 69%. It covers about 11,000 workers at McDonnell Douglas's jet-fighter plant, and another 1,000 employees at the company's missile factory.

The biggest concession that the union wrested during the weekend was the ability to fold wage gains and cost-of-living adjustments into the base salary of those workers who are at the bottom of the scale. That means everyone signed to the contract -- no matter what their experience level -- will enjoy the full value of the 5.5%, 3% and 3% wage increases the pact provides. Lump-sum bonuses of 4% will also be given annually.

"Bringing up the minimum wage rates significantly improves the situation for future hires," said Lee Pearson, a representative of the Machinists' international office. He added, for example, that if the company hadn't succumbed on the wage and COLA issue, a newly recruited hydraulic press and hammer operator could have made $13.36 an hour. Now, that person will be entitled to at least $15.75 an hour.

The union also captured better health benefits for its retirees, including prescription-drug cards.

"We drained about as much financial blood as we could," said Cassell Williams, president of Machinists District Lodge 837. He added that he thought McDonnell Douglas couldn't afford a walkout given the company's "weak financial position" and the uncertain outlook for defense contractors.

At the fighter plant, McDonnell Douglas assembles F-15 Eagles for the Air Force, F/A-18 Hornets for the Navy and AV8-B Harriers for the Marine Corps. The White House and members of Congress are eyeing all of these projects as possible targets for termination, given the improvement in East-West relations.
