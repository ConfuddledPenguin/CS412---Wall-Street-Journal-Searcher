Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Weak Employment Data Temper Fears On Inflation, Prompt Revised Outlooks

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 05/07/90

NEW YORK -- April's weak jobs report has taken the wind out of many economists' sails as well as some of the worry out of this week's inflation report. The April producer price index, due out Friday, is expected to rise again after two calm months. But concerns about inflation are being tempered somewhat by last Friday's employment data, which showed a drop in overall employment and another significant slump in factory jobs. The figures immediately prompted economists to begin cooling their projections for next week's April industrial production and personal income as the consensus shifted to a lower economic gear. This week's indicators include March consumer credit and business inventories plus April retail sales data.

"The {jobs} data clearly had a significant impact," said Charles Lieberman, managing director of financial-markets research at Manufacturers Hanover Securities Corp., New York. Before the report, Mr. Lieberman thought that the economy was improving at a rate that would stoke inflation. "Now, growth may be moderate enough that I see no upward pressure on inflation," he said. However, conditions "aren't sufficiently moderate" to prompt the Federal Reserve Board to stimulate the economy through lower interest rates.

Richard S. Peterson, chief economist at Continental Bank in Chicago, reluctantly lowered his outlook on April industrial production to a decline of about 0.2%. He previously expected a gain of that amount. "And it looks like personal income won't be so strong either," he added.

Nonetheless, Mr. Peterson believes that the economy may yet rebound. For one thing, he thinks employment data is still being skewed by unseasonal weather. And he believes some of the lost jobs represent an attempt by manufacturers to trim labor costs by controlling work-force size through attrition and other means. "I'm going to look for a turnaround," he said.

In the meantime, the consensus calls for at best a flat reading on April industrial production, according to a survey taken after the jobs report by MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York.

The consensus view on April producer prices calls for an increase of 0.2%, following March's 0.2% drop and February's flat reading. Several economists noted that much of the increase is due to a return of higher gasoline prices following what's turning out to be a surprisingly brief respite. Next week's data on the consumer price index is expected to show an increase of 0.3%, according to the MMS survey.

A Federal Reserve Board spokeswoman said the consumer credit data would be released Thursday, rather than today. The Fed, unlike the Commerce and Labor departments, doesn't always follow expected release dates. March consumer credit is once again likely to be dominated by slower auto sales. The consensus calls for an increase of $3 billion, following growth of $3.4 billion in February.

April retail sales, due out Friday, are expected to rebound following March's 0.6% decline. Easter sales helped the general retailers in the month, although not as much as they had hoped. The consensus view calls for a gain of 0.3%.

The March business inventories report, due out today, is expected to fall a bit. Stephen S. Roach, economist at Morgan Stanley & Co., noted that the continued liquidation of factory stocks should be only partly offset by a resumption of modest stockpiling at the retail level. Mr. Roach, who expects a 0.2% drop in inventories, added in his weekly newsletter that "business remains intent on tight inventory control."

POSITIVE SWING: For the first time since January of 1989, Sindlinger Co.'s monthly tally of positive household liquidity found that the number of states in its "recession" column decreased. The Wallingford, Pa., pollster interviews households around the country about their personal finances and job prospects. States with fewer than 50% of residents giving "positive" answers fall into the recession category. In April, the recession states fell to 35 from 41 in March. Improvement centered on the Midwest and Northwest.
