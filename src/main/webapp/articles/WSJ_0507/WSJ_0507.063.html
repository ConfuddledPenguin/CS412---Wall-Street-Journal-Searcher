Letters to the Editor: Auto Makers' Decision Is Tragically Wrong

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/07/90

As your April 5 article "Ford Settles Big Lap-Belt Injury Suit" correctly noted, the Institute for Injury Reduction is predicting that rear lap-belt deaths and injuries could reach 1,200 a year at current rates. In fact, they may have done so already. The important issue is that these injuries could be largely headed off if auto makers would voluntarily undertake vigorous programs to retrofit rear-seat lap-and-shoulder belts into tens of millions of existing cars. That means offering the belts at cost (no more than $30-to-$40 a car, installed), advertising them aggressively, and keeping them in stock at dealers.

In both cases described in your article, Miller v. Ford and McKenna v. GM, the families of the injured people offered to reduce substantially the amounts of the settlements if the manufacturers would agree to make an effort voluntarily to having similar models retrofitted with rear lap-shoulder belts. Their goal was to save others from these devastating, largely preventable injuries.

And in both cases, tragically, the auto companies declined. They appear to prefer paying money after the injurious damage has been done to paying the same money in advance, to prevent the injuries in the first place.

That is bad public relations for the companies and bad policy for national health. We can't afford the medical costs, lost time, pain and grief that lap-belt injuries are producing -- and in greater volumes as responsible parents buckle up their youngsters with lap-only belts in the back seats.

Benjamin Kelley

President

Institute for Injury Reduction

Dunkirk, Md.
