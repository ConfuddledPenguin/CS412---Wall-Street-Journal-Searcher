Settlement Plan Is Set for Parent Of Lincoln S&L

  • Author: Pauline Yoshihashi
  • Date: 05/07/90

PHOENIX, Calif. -- The government and the unsecured creditors committee of American Continental Corp. unveiled a settlement agreement that includes partial payment to American Continental bondholders and the ouster of Charles A. Keating Jr. and other senior officials. The settlement proposal, presented to Federal District Judge Richard M. Bilby, requires the approval of the court and creditors. The plan seeks to liquidate American Continental, which is operating under Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection from creditors. The company's Lincoln Savings & Loan subsidiary of Irvine, Calif., was taken over by the government shortly after the bankruptcy filing in 1989.

The plan would set aside $21 million to be paid to American Continental's unsecured creditors, most of whom invested $200 million in American Continental subordinated debentures. Those investors, who would be among the last to be paid in a reorganization, could recoup more if American Continental is liquidated.

Mr. Keating, who has sued the government to regain control of Lincoln, wasn't consulted on the agreement. James J. Feder, American Continental's lead bankruptcy laywer, said that the proposal "settles nothing," adding that Mr. Keating may step down as American Continental's chairman next week to head up a shareholders' committee to negotiate a reorganization plan.

Details of the plan haven't been determined, but may be presented to the court within a week. They include issues such as which of American Continental's assets would be assigned to the Resolution Trust Corp., the government agency overseeing the U.S.'s thrift bailout.

Despite the uncertainties, the proposal was seen as a workable solution by some bondholder representatives. "We believe the money itself is as well as the bondholders are going to do, although we don't necessarily agree with all the terms of the settlement," said Joseph Cotchett, a leading bondholder attorney. The plan also is a step forward for the RTC and the unsecured creditors committee, which had been at odds on many issues.

American Continental, the government and the bondholders are still mired in a tangle of litigation not affected by the settlement plan. The agreement doesn't affect the government's $1.1 billion racketeering suit against Mr. Keating and others, in which the U.S. charges that they engaged in phony tax and loan transactions to divert money from Lincoln into American Continental. Bondholder suits against Mr. Keating and others also are pending.
