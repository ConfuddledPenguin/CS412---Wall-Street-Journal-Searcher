Campbell Soup To Break Even In Its Fiscal Year

  • Author: Vindu P. Goel
  • Date: 07/17/90

Campbell Soup Co. said it expects to break even in the year ending July 29, reflecting continued troubles overseas and recent weakness in several product categories. The projected results include a fourth-quarter charge of $302 million, or $2.33 a share, for plant closures and businesses put up for sale.

In fiscal 1989, the Camden, N.J., food company posted net income of $13.1 million, or 10 cents a share, including a charge of $201 million, or $2.02 a share, for a restructuring begun in August.

Speaking to securities analysts in New York, Campbell Chief Executive David W. Johnson said per-share operating earnings for the year would be about $2.33 to $2.35, significantly below analysts' estimates of $2.50 to $2.60.

In an apparent attempt to quell takeover rumors, Mr. Johnson also said he believes members of Campbell's controlling Dorrance family, who own 41% of the stock, are opposed to a takeover.

Responding to a question, Mr. Johnson said two previously undecided family members, Charlotte Weber and Tristram Colket Jr., now favor independence, joining four cousins who have disclosed their opposition to a sale.

"Charlotte and Tris have, as far as I'm concerned, shown a move to the other line," Mr. Johnson said.

Mr. Johnson cited continuing problems at Campbell's Italian cookie business and some British frozen-food operations as major reasons for Campbell's disappointing financial performance. Campbell put both units up for sale earlier this year.

In the current quarter, Campbell has also lost market share in three U.S. food categories: dry soup, red-and-white condensed soup and Le Menu frozen entrees.

But Mr. Johnson predicted that Campbell's earnings would soon resurge. Over the next few years, he said, the company will seek to achieve 20% annual growth in earnings, 20% return on equity and 20% cash return on assets. Mr. Johnson vowed to "make Campbell a premier performer in the peer group of food companies."

In composite trading Monday on the New York Stock Exchange, Campbell shares fell 12.5 cents to $55.875.

Separately, Standard & Poor's Corp. lowered its rating on Campbell's senior debt to double-A from triple-A and affirmed the company's A-1-plus commercial paper rating, affecting about $500 million of debt.

In reducing the rating, S&P cited Campbell's recent operating results, "balance sheet deterioration" and possible changes in family ownership of the company. In addition, "uncertainties exist about when and whether operations will again reach levels consistent with the highest rating," S&P said.

Moody's Investors Service Inc. also lowered its rating on Campbell's long-term debt to double-A-2 from triple-A.
