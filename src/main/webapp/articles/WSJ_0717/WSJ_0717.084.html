Abreast of the Market: Industrials Climb 19.55 to 2999.75 On Strength of Earnings Reports

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 07/17/90

NEW YORK -- The Dow Jones Industrial Average climbed to a new record yesterday, closing just a fraction under 3000. The industrials closed 19.55 higher at 2999.75, the third consecutive high.

In early trading it seemed as if the industrial average would easily close above the 3000 mark. Many key earnings reports turned out better than expected. International Business Machines hit the high end of analysts' expectations, announcing earnings of $2.45 a share for the second quarter. Investors pushed the stock up 1 3/8 to 122 3/8 in turnover of 2.2 million shares.

United Technologies, another stock in the industrial average, gained 1 1/2 to 60 3/8 after an upbeat earnings announcement late Friday.

NCR Corp., a computer maker, rocketed 3 7/8 to 68 after reporting strong second-quarter earnings, and other technology issues followed the stock higher. Digital Equipment, for example, climbed 7/8 to 85 1/8.

Yet for all the celebration, caution prevailed. As the industrial average seesawed around the 3000 level yesterday, the pace of trading began to slow in the afternoon -- a clear sign that money managers aren't enthusiastically chasing stocks to new highs. New York Stock Exchange volume was a moderate 149,430,000 shares. The rally was fairly narrow: 868 issues advanced on the Big Board while 678 fell.

"It is probably the most unexciting record high I've seen," said Robert Devine, head of equity trading at UBS Securities. "There is no euphoria. A lot of money managers have been in the wrong stocks, such as cyclicals," or shares whose fortunes swing with the economy. "It's been a wacky trip up; portfolio managers are not happy."

There are dozens of buy orders below current prices, says Thomas Gallagher, head of equity trading at Oppenheimer & Co. But many investors think the market is too expensive right now. "Nobody is committing," said Mr. Gallagher.

Foreign investors, who can really push the stock market when they jump in, were also buying lightly yesterday. "I am surprised that the Japanese investors have not been in here in a bigger way," said Thomas Callahan, senior vice president at Yamaichi International (America) Inc. "The market's on the move and that's the kind they like, but the way the currency has been acting is a definite negative for them." Lately, the dollar has been falling against the yen, which would diminish returns on U.S. stocks for a Japanese investor.

Joseph DeMarco, head of equity trading at Marinvest, added: "I don't know of any other industry where they are setting new highs and people are so miserable." Mr. DeMarco said he's cautious because of the lagging Dow utility index, which fell 0.31 to 205.86 yesterday. The weak utility average is a sign that interest rates aren't falling much further, he said. "The stock market wants to see further easing" of interest rates before it moves higher, he added.

Yesterday, investors sought out shares of banks with sound earnings, while dumping those of several other financial concerns.

J.P. Morgan jumped 2 1/8 to 37 7/8; NCNB rose 1 3/4 to 36 1/2. Second-quarter net at Morgan rose slightly from a year ago, while NCNB reported a 50% earnings increase.

Chase Manhattan lost 1/2 to 20 3/4 on 2.4 million shares after reporting that its second-quarter net fell 62%. First Chicago declined 1 to 27 5/8; its net for the quarter fell 35%.

Manufacturers Hanover tumbled 7/8 to 30 7/8. Prudential-Bache Securities issued a sell recommendation on the stock and said the company's dividend may be vulnerable. Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan said the Fed is developing additional steps to tighten banks' capital requirements.

Among broader averages, Standard & Poor's 500-stock index set an all-time high, climbing 1.64 to 368.95; the previous mark was 367.40, set June 4. New records were also established in the Dow Jones Equity Market Index, which gained 1.45 to 342.67; and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index, which rose 0.80 to 201.13.

McDonnell Douglas dropped 2 1/2 to 42 3/8 after outlining a cost-cutting program, calling for a reduction of about 11% in its work force and other moves, aimed at saving more than $700 million annually.

Warrants of RJR Nabisco Holdings slipped 3/8 to 7 as the company launched a $6.9 billion recapitalization program, which will reduce its debt load. Among other things, the plan calls for RJR to sell $1.7 billion of newly issued common stock to Kohlberg Kravis Roberts for $6.25 a share.

Cummins Engine advanced 3 1/8 to 54 3/8 after agreeing to sell a 27% aggregate stake to Ford Motor, Tenneco and Kubota of Japan for $250 million.

Mattel, which skidded 2 1/8 Friday, rebounded 1 7/8 to 22 3/4 after Kidder Peabody analyst Gary Jacobson made positive comments on the stock. Mr. Jacobson said the earlier sell-off was "unwarranted" and presented a buying opportunity.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index gained 1.59 to 365.58, but remains well below its all-time high of 397.03, established last October. Volume totaled 13,690,000 shares.

Wells-Gardner Electronics climbed 7/8 to 3 7/8 after estimating that its second-quarter earnings at between 19 cents and 23 cents a share, compared with less than one cent a share a year earlier.

New Line Cinema gained 7/8 to 16 1/4.

Live Entertainment announced an Oct. 4 release date for its home-video version of the company's "Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles" movie.

Mitchell Energy & Development rose 3/4 to 18 1/8. Earnings for the fiscal year ending next January could rise to around 75 cents a share from 65 cents a year ago, Barron's magazine reported.
