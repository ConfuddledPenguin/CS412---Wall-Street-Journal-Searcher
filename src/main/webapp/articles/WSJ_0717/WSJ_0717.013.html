If Two of Your Friends Write In, This Lovely Knife Set Is Yours]

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 07/17/90

Sometimes it takes a little fertilizer to make the grass roots grow. Fighting a bill by Sen. John Danforth to break up cable-TV monopolies, American Cablevision Co. wanted the senator and other lawmakers to know that it isn't just the cable industry that is opposed to the proposed regulation. And the Kansas City company figured it didn't have to look far to find private citizens who would want to send that message to Washington. Grassroots support could be cultivated right there in the office.

In a memo circulated to its employees, the company urged them to write letters of protest to Sen. Danforth and other lawmakers -- as private citizens. "Use plain stationery and your home address," the memo said. "We don't want this to look like an organized effort." Letter writers were told they could identify themselves as employees of the company, "but Congress has already heard all our good arguments from the industry's point of view."

The memo offered a little incentive for people to raise a ruckus. Their reward, as promised in the memo from the public relations department: "an attractive gray Comedy Channel T-shirt (Offer limited to the first 100 employees who write)."

For those who could persuade others to write, there was more: "Copies of a letter from your aunt in Marshall or your neighbor or anyone else are worth a mug."

Why a T-shirt prize? "We had T-shirts coming out our ears," says Carol Rothwell, the company's public relations director. "We are always giving away T-shirts -- that's part of our internal culture."

In case correspondents couldn't think of anything to say, the memo offered suggestions. Write "that you don't see any need for government intervention in a private entertainment business." Or: "I wonder why Congress is spending so much time on cable when I have more serious concerns such as -------." The company even provided stamps.

Ms. Rothwell defends the campaign as a time-honored American tradition. "Every industry does grass-roots politics," she says. "It's been around as long as we've had a Congress."

American Cablevision declines to estimate the number of letters generated by the campaign, but Steve Hilton, a press spokesman for Sen. Danforth, says they haven't exactly been pouring in. He puts the total at about 20. "I don't think the postal service has been overly taxed," says Mr. Hilton.
