CBOT Orders Unit Of Stotler Group To Boost Capital

  • Author: Scott Mc Murray
  • Date: 07/17/90

CHICAGO -- The Chicago Board of Trade directed Stotler & Co., the futures brokerage unit of Stotler Group Inc., to boost its capital by the close of business tomorrow or begin liquidating customer accounts. Stotler, whose chairman, Karsten "Cash" Mahlmann, is also the chairman of the exchange, confirmed industry assertions that it wasn't in compliance with Board of Trade capital requirements.

Stotler's capital problems are an acute embarrassment to Mr. Mahlmann, whose administrative talents were touted in his campaign for the exchange's top job, even though he hasn't been involved in the company's day-to-day management for the past few years.

Low capital levels also may prompt a cash flow crisis for Stotler & Co., an old-line grain trading firm that has been eclipsed in recent years by larger financial futures trading rivals. It said it was transferring some customer accounts to other brokers and was looking for additional capital to remedy its problems. If a capital infusion isn't found, "Stotler Group may be unable to meet its debt obligations unless those obligations are refinanced or restructured," Stotler Group said.

Stotler said that transferring customer accounts, and thereby forgoing trading commissions generated by those accounts, and other planned changes would reduce revenue and net income for Stotler Group. For the first quarter, Stotler Group reported net income of $370,000, or 17 cents a share, and commission revenue of $28.4 million.

Neither Stotler Group nor the exchange disclosed the amount of capital Stotler has to support customer accounts.

Stotler's capital problems may be tied to a dispute with regulators over counting certain assets, specifically commercial paper, as regulatory net capital, exchange members said. Commodity regulations require that futures brokerage firms maintain liquid assets in a separate account equal to a certain percentage of funds in customer accounts.

Restrictions are placed on a firm's activities when its capital as defined by regulators falls to 7% of customer funds. The firm can be immediately forced out of the customer business if the capital level hits 4% of customer funds.

Stotler Group had shareholders' equity of $8.5 million as of March 31.

Stotler Group's stock, which is listed on the American Stock Exchange, didn't open for trading Thursday or Friday. The trading halt prompted disclosures from exchange members that the company hadn't maintained an adequate capital cushion to support its customer business.

The company's stock opened for trading late yesterday morning on the Amex and closed at 5, down 2 7/8.

Stotler Group said that one of its five subsidiaries, R.G. Dickinson & Co., a Des Moines, Iowa, securities broker, isn't affected by the Board of Trade capital directive.
