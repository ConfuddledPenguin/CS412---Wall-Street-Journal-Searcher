Credit Markets: RJR Nabisco's Junk Bonds Rise Sharply in Price As Investors Respond to Refinancing Program

  • Author: Tom Herman and Robert Sherman
  • Date: 07/17/90

NEW YORK -- Investors rushed to buy RJR Nabisco Inc.'s newly glamorous junk bonds, propelling prices sharply higher, in reaction to the company's huge refinancing program. The tobacco and food company touched off the buying spree by announcing details of a $6.9 billion refinancing designed to strengthen its balance sheet and ward off a potential financial crisis. The plan includes retiring many of the company's most troublesome junk bonds by buying them back in the open market and by an exchange offer.

RJR Nabisco's announcement helped lift prices of other high-yield, high-risk junk bonds yesterday. Elsewhere in the U.S. bond markets, though, most interest rates were little changed, taking a breather after having fallen late last week as the Federal Reserve eased credit slightly.

Long-term Treasury bond yields hovered in a narrow band all day yesterday, finishing the session almost exactly where they began. For example, 30-year Treasury bonds now yield 8.45%, the same as late Friday. Treasury bill rates also were flat, and municipal bond prices ended little changed.

Federal Reserve officials probably won't cut interest rates any further until a clearer picture of the economy emerges in August, investment managers and economists say. Several money managers fear that the Fed acted too soon, that inflation will remain stubbornly high, and thus that short-term bonds represent a much better buy right now than long-term bonds.

Moreover, traders are bracing for a huge wave of U.S. government borrowing next month that some analysts predict will nudge interest rates back up over the next few weeks. The Treasury's quarterly financing, which typically consists of three-year notes, 10-year notes and 30-year bonds, is expected in early August. "There's also a great deal of concern about the budget deficit and concern that politics is ruling the day" once again, said William Brachfeld, executive vice president at Daiwa Securities America Inc.

While U.S. bonds stalled yesterday, British government bond prices surged in brisk trading. Traders cited the British pound's recent strength in the foreign-exchange markets as well as unexpectedly weak U.K. economic reports that could indicate reduced inflationary pressures. West German government bond prices also climbed.

To keep the U.S. bond market rally going, "new evidence of a slower economy probably will have to emerge," said John Lonski, senior economist at Moody's Investors Service Inc. "However, we do not foresee a deep enough decline in economic activity that would open the way for a significant bond rally."

Thomas G. Moles, managing director in charge of fixed income investments at J&W Seligman Inc., agrees that the Fed probably won't cut rates again in the near future. "What the Fed's going to do is sit back and see what the market reaction is" to last week's credit-easing move before taking further action, he said.

Still, Mr. Moles recommends buying longer-term bonds, especially top-rated issues. "We're increasingly leaning toward higher-quality issues," he said. "Reaching for yield" by buying lower-rated issues wouldn't be smart, he said, in view of the economy's listlessness.

Junk, Corporate Bonds

Investors reacted enthusiastically to RJR Nabisco Inc.'s refinancing plan.

RJR's 14.07% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, which were reset to yield 17.375% under the new plan, soared 8 1/4 points to 114 3/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. Pay-in-kind debentures pay interest in the form of addition securities.

The other widely held RJR issue, the 14.07% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, which have been reset to yield 17%, jumped 4 5/8 points to 91 3/4.

Investors who bought RJR Nabisco's bonds early this year and held them have made a fortune. Prices of some issues have nearly doubled over the last six months amid speculation that the company's controlling owner, Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., would take steps to lighten RJR's enormous debt burden. KKR took the company private in a $25 billion leveraged takeover, the largest in history.

Prices of other corporate junk bonds rose about one point, traders said. Also, while volume in RJR Pay-in-kind bonds was triple the recent daily average, activity in other bonds was about double.

Among high-grade corporate bonds, prices were little changed. Wal-Mart Stores Inc. and J.C. Penney Co. tapped the markets with new debt issues.

Treasury Securities

Treasury bond yields hardly budged all day. Trading activity was sluggish.

Among actively traded issues, the government's 30-year bonds were quoted late yesterday at a price of about 103 1/4, to yield 8.45%, the same as late Friday.

"The bond market had its big excitement on Thursday and Friday" of last week, said Joseph Liro, a vice president at S.G. Warburg & Co. Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan disclosed Thursday that the Fed was poised to ease credit. Then on Friday, the central bank acted, trimming rates slightly. The Fed's move resulted in a drop in the interest rate on federal funds, or funds that banks lend each overnight, by 0.25 percentage point to 8%.

But banks haven't yet responded by lowering their prime lending rate, which remains stuck at 10%. The prime rate is closely watched because banks use it as a peg to set rates on a large number of loans to individuals as well as small and medium-sized companies.

Foreign Bonds

British government bond prices surged again yesterday.

U.K. government bonds are attracting more investors because of growing signs that Britain's weak economy will help depress inflation, pull down interest rates and drive up bond prices.

Investors also are attracted by Britain's high interest rates and its "inverted yield curve," which means short-term rates there are higher than long-term bond yields.

Britain's 11.75% bonds due 2003/2007, which traders watch as an indicator of long-term bonds in general, climbed yesterday by 22/32 of a point yesterday to 102 31/32, to yield 11.30%.

Among other active issues, the 12% bonds due 1995 rose 14/32 of a point to yield 12.01%. Traders cited retail sales and industrial production figures as evidence of U.K. economy weakness.

In other major markets:

-- West German government bond prices also rose. Bonn's 8.75% benchmark bonds, due 2000, rose 0.45 point to 101.75 to yield 8.47%.

-- Japanese government bond prices inched higher. Trading was relatively light.

Mortgage-backed Securities

The mortgage securities market ended unchanged to marginally higher after a lifeless session in both the primary and secondary markets.

No major new offerings were priced yesterday.

Although price movement has been negligible, the secondary market continues to benefit from the dearth of new issuance, which has been combined with solid demand from dealers and retail accounts in recent days.

Among actively traded secondary issues, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were up 2/32 at 99 23/ 32, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities were 2/32 higher at 99 2/32 and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were up 2/32 at 99 18/ 32.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds ended narrowly mixed, with a large number of issues unchanged to as much as 1/4 point higher.

Municipal players are already turning their attention to the week's new-issue calendar, where more than $1.5 billion is expected to be priced.

In two of the week's largest issues New York will sell $225 million various purpose general obligation bonds today.

Also Tuesday, a Merrill Lynch Capital Markets group is expected to price $325 million Connecticut general obligation bonds.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bonds of 2018 were unchanged at 99 1/2 to yield 7.30%.
