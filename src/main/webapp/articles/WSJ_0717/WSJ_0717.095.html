World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Advance as Trading Focuses On Issues That Have Bright Earnings Outlooks

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/17/90

Tokyo stock prices moved up strongly in moderate trading that was focused on stocks with bright earnings outlooks, traders said. Buying was prompted by widespread expectation that domestic interest rates probably won't rise further, the traders said. In London, shares gained in a moderately active session, supported by futures-led buying, a strong showing on Wall Street, and better-than-expected U.K. retail sales figures.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues gained 377.36 points to 33021.73, ending above 33000 for the first time since June 6.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the Nikkei index advanced 145.41 points to 33167.14.

Monday's volume on the first section was estimated at 580 million shares, up from 512.6 million shares Friday. Advancers outnumbered decliners 691-277, while 163 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section was up 30.32 points, or 1.28%, at 2400.16.

The second section index advanced 9.91 points, or 0.22%, to 4477.16. Volume was estimated at 33 million shares, down from 41.3 million shares Friday.

The market opened amid improved momentum on the eased credit stance by the U.S. Federal Reserve Board, which led to strong advances in New York stocks Friday.

The Fed's move helped to reduce expectations for a further rise of Japanese interest rates, which have been holding investors off from active buying, traders said.

They added that surprisingly little selling helped push up stock prices widely. Advances for the futures indexes, which ended the day at or just below intraday highs, made investors hold on to their stocks without taking profits at yesterday's level, traders said.

While higher-rates possibilities have faded, there's also little expectation among participants that the Fed's move will help to push down Japanese interest rates, as Japan's money supply has remained at high levels in recent months and the country's economy continues to perform well.

With domestic short-term interest rates remaining at attractively high levels, institutional investors are unlikely to become active buyers, said Shigeru Akiba, head of equity at UBS Phillips & Drew International.

If June money supply data to be disclosed Friday show high-paced growth, investors may again start talking about the possibility for interest rates to rise, the Phillips & Drew official said.

Institutions are also waiting to see results of a series of U.S. economic indicators due later this week, including consumer prices, as well as the testimony of U.S. Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan tomorrow, to judge the direction for U.S. interest rates, traders said.

With both exchange rates and bond prices remaining relatively stable yesterday, stock investors mostly focused on corporate earnings outlooks as incentives for buying, traders said.

Strong advances for Sony and Kyocera early in the morning session prompted gains for other companies whose earnings are expected to grow strongly in coming years, traders said. A wide range of electronics shares and public spending-related shares set the pace of advances during the afternoon session.

Gains for some construction and housing companies prompted heavy selling in midafternoon on profit-taking, said Yoshihiro Ito, general manager of stock trading at Okasan Securities.

"But they managed to finish higher, absorbing the selling," Mr. Ito said. "That's a good sign that market turnover is improving," he said.

Sony finished up 80 yen to 9080 yen (54 cents to $61.31). Kyocera ended at 9220 yen, up 60 yen.

Shipbuilding companies closed higher, with Ishikawajima-Harima gaining 29 yen to 1020 yen, and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries rising 20 yen to 1000 yen.

Blue-chip issues advanced briskly. Toyota Motor gained 70 yen to 2340 yen, while Fuji Photo Film advanced 200 yen to 4920 yen.

In London, shares were in demand from the outset, dealers said, as this past week's rise in New York equities filtered into optimism for the U.K. market, on the belief that the broad-based index would be able to breach the 2400-point barrier.

By late morning, news of a larger-than-expected drop in June's retail sales helped lift the index across the 2400-point line and beyond. Provisional June retail sales showed a 2.8% decline from May, and a 0.9% drop from a year ago.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index gained 24.3 points to 2406.5. The index reached as high as 2410 points.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index advanced 20.8 points to 1900.9. Volume amounted to 346.9 million shares, compared with 399.2 million shares Friday.

Although some profit-taking set in during the late afternoon to topple the index from midafternoon intraday peaks, good demand for stock-index futures contracts and a buoyant early effort on Wall Street left the market with a firm undertone, dealers said. A shortage of stock in some of the blue-chip leaders will likely lead to another rise today, analysts predicted.

Glaxo gained 15 pence to 870 pence (27 cents to $15.70) and Reuters rose 25 pence to #13.14; both issues were cited by dealers as stocks specifically helped by a shortage of their shares.

Wellcome fell 10 pence to 585 pence after a downgrading by Hoare Govett Ltd., following downgradings a week ago by Barclays de Zoete Wedd Ltd. and James Capel & Co.

Carlton Communications continued to suffer from a downgrading last week, finishing down 25 pence at 435 pence.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Paris, Amsterdam, Brussels, Madrid and Milan, mixed in Frankfurt and Zurich, and lower in Stockholm. South African gold stocks closed higher.

Also, stocks closed higher in Sydney, Wellington, Manila and Singapore, and lower in Hong Kong, Seoul and Taipei.
