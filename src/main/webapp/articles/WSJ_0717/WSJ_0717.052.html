Southland Agrees With Japanese Suitor, Bondholder Panel on Restructuring Plan

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 07/17/90

DALLAS -- Southland Corp. said it agreed in principle on a fourth restructuring proposal with its bondholders' steering committee and a Japanese suitor, averting a possible bankruptcy-law filing and avoiding a showdown over late interest payments. The agreement changes significantly the details of a debt-exchange offer from the company this month, increasing the face value of the publicly held bonds offered in exchange for current bonds while reducing the interest rates. The accord also increases the cash infusion by Ito-Yokado Co. to $430 million from $400 million and reduces the equity retained by the founding Thompson family.

This year, Ito-Yokado, operator of 7-Eleven stores in Japan, offered to buy 75% of the company, contingent on a restructuring of Southland's debt and preferred. Yesterday's agreement reduces that stake to 70%.

Southland, the troubled parent of the 7-Eleven retail convenience stores, had said that it didn't intend to make a June 15 interest payment on its senior subordinated debt. That payment officially went into default Sunday. Without an agreement with senior debtholders, the company could have been forced into bankruptcy proceedings yesterday.

This plan is the fourth restructuring proposal written by the company this year, but it is the first accepted by the bondholders' committee, which represents about 70% of the public debtholders. The latest proposal reduces the equity of existing shareholders including the Thompson family to about 5% from 6.5% in the third proposal and 15% in the original plan. And while reducing Ito-Yokado's equity to 70%, the proposal gives bondholders about 25% of the company's common, up from 18.5% in the third proposal.

Acknowledging that the bondholders took "meaningful haircuts" in this restructuring proposal, bondholder committee chairman John Gordon of Deltec Securities Corp. said the proposal gives them "as much as they could reasonably expect to get" out of the restructuring. "All of us are very enthusiastic about what the new owners will be able to do with this business," said Mr. Gordon. He said the proposal increased the value of the exchange offer to bondholders by about 15% and was unanimously approved by the committee.

All bondholders weren't pleased, though. "I'm not happy," said George Nicholas, a Dallas holder of some of the company's 15 3/4% and 16 3/4% bonds who filed suit against Southland in a Dallas County court yesterday, seeking payment of the interest due June 15. The new agreement doesn't change his sentiments, he said. "It's supposedly a great deal for us, but it's just smoke and mirrors to me," he said. Mr. Nicholas said the deal cuts interest payments on his bonds to about one-third of the original levels, and although it increases the face value of the notes, the new proposal raises the value of the deal by "not much more than a couple of cents."

The exchange offer still requires the approval of numerous creditors, preferred stockholders and banks. The company said it will file its prospectus for the debt exchange with the Securities and Exchange Commission as soon as possible and hopes to complete the debt restructuring by mid-August.

Southland, which owns about 6,700 7-Eleven and other convenience stores in the U.S. and Canada, went private in a $4.9 billion leveraged buy-out in late 1987 to avoid a possible hostile bid by the Belzberg family of Canada. Increased competition eroded sales and profit margins and made it difficult for the company to service the huge debt from the buy-out.
