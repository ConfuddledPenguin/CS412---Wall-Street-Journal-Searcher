OTC Focus: Market Ends Mixed in Light Trading; International Holding Capital Falls 8

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 07/17/90

Over-the-counter stocks posted their fourth straight gain as the Dow Jones Industrial Average flirted with the 3000 mark. The Nasdaq Composite Index rose 1.16 to 469.60, a 0.5% advance, but still remains below a high of 485.73 set last Oct. 9. Volume dropped to 138.1 million shares from 160.6 million Friday as advancing shares trailed decliners, 946 to 969.

Mirroring this year's trends in the OTC market, technology stocks gained but financial issues sagged, felled partly by a steep plunge in International Holding Capital. Shares of the Honolulu thrift fell 8 to 17 after it announced that Sumix Corp. failed to gain timely approval from the Office of Thrift Supervision for a merger of the two thrifts. Three of Nasdaq's four financial indexes fell slightly.

"It's a very splintered market," said Richard Bruno, head of OTC trading at PaineWebber. Mr. Bruno said he continued to see strong interest in technology and biotechnology shares, while investors largely shunned financial stocks. "I wouldn't jump in with both feet and buy everything that's for sale," he said, adding that investors continue to be selective.

At A.G. Edwards & Sons in St. Louis, small investors are returning to OTC stocks, according to William Sulya, director of OTC trading. In the last few trading sessions, Mr. Sulya said prospects of lower interest rates has attracted individual and institutional investors to stocks among all industry groups. He added, however, that small investors seem to be attracted only to "quality names" with strong operations.

Technology shares were mixed. Intel added 1 1/4 to 51 1/4; Microsoft advanced 7/8 to 79 1/4, and Lotus climbed 1 to 33 3/4. However, Oracle Systems, continuing to lose ground after last week's earnings report, eased 3/8 to 18 3/8 and was the day's most-active issue. Apple Computer dropped 1 1/8 to 45 5/8.

The Nasdaq 100 Index of larger stocks, including many technology issues, rose 2.88 to 492.36.

Micropolis jumped 1 1/16 to 9, a new 52-week high, on more than three times average volume. The Chatsworth, Calif., maker of high-capacity disk drives reported net income for the second quarter ended June 29 of 20 cents a share, compared with a year-earlier loss of 92 cents a share.

American Depositary Receipts of Telefonos de Mexico, Mexico's government-subsidized telephone company, were among the day's most-active issues, with more than 4 million ADRs changing hands at 1 23/32, unchanged from the previous close. Among other communications issues, MCI Communications gained 1/2 to 40, leading a 0.6% gain in the Nasdaq Utility Index.

Other active stocks included Class A shares of Tele-Communications, which rose 5/8 to 15 1/8. A week ago, the Denver cable television company offered three million Class A shares at $14.25 apiece.

Nike raced to yet another new 52-week high, as shares of the athletic footwear maker surged 2 1/4 to 94.

Shares of Goulds Pumps continued to climb on takeover speculation, although a company official flatly denied rumors that Britain's BTR PLC made an offer for the Seneca Falls, N.Y., maker of centrifugal liquid holding pumps. Investor relations liaison James A. Nelson told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report the company hasn't had any acquisition offers and "has no interest whatsoever" in being acquired. Gould rose 1 to 26 on nearly 1.1 million shares, more than six times average volume. The stock was active last week on similar rumors.

Mr. Nelson said that second-quarter earnings are due to be reported July 23 and added that he's comfortable with consensus estimates the company will earn 40 cents a share, up from 39 cents a year earlier, and $1.49 for the full year, up from $1.41 in 1989.

Industrial Funding Class A shares plunged 3 5/8 to 5 7/8 after the provider of capital equipment leases reported a loss for the second quarter ended May 31 of three cents a share, compared with net income of 18 cents a year earlier.

C-Cor Electronics lost 1 to 9 1/4 after the State College, Pa., maker of cable-television equipment said it plans to lay off about 90 workers at two Pennsylvania plants. C-Cor cited weak orders due to uncertainty about regulation of the cable television industry.

Westmark International tumbled 2 7/8 to 38 3/4. Anne Marie Bugge, vice president of investor relations for the Seattle maker of medical equipment, told DJPIR the fall was an "overreaction" to the introduction of a new ultrasound system by a competitor, Acuson Corp. of Mountain View, Calif.

Multimedia fell 7/8 to 69 5/8. A 400,000-share block representing about 3.5% of the Greenville, S.C., media company traded early in the day.
