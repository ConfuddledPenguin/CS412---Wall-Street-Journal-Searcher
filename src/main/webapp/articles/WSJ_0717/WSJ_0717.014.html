AT&T Increases Rates for Services Used by Businesses

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 07/17/90

NEW YORK -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co. plans to increase by the end of the month its charges for several long-distance communications services to businesses. AT&T's price boost, when it becomes effective July 27, will amount to a 2% overall increase in rates for two outbound-calling services sold to businesses and a 0.5% boost in the monthly bill for business customers who use multiple AT&T services, the company said.

That boost, while small, would be one of the most significant since the Federal Communications Commission last year changed its method of regulating the company's profit from long-distance service.

The FCC used to make AT&T set its rates according to its costs of delivering service. Now the commission sets a "price cap" on what AT&T can charge. The idea is to motivate AT&T to cut its costs of delivering service, thereby allowing customers to benefit from lower pricing while AT&T gets to keep more of what it makes on each call.

Since late 1989, the company has increased certain flat fees, such as private-line hookups and consumer calls to AT&T operators. The latest increase would apply to so-called usage charges covering the time of day a call is made and the distance it travels.

AT&T, which is expected to announce lower second-quarter earnings later this week, derives more than 50% of its revenue from long-distance service and, with a more than 65% share, dominates the U.S. market.

The company said the price increases will affect about 8% of its business customers, and are being made to cover increased costs of installing new digital equipment and backup systems in its network, inflation and other unspecified costs.

"Over the past five years we've lowered {overall long-distance} prices by 45%. Now we're talking about a 2% bump," said a company spokeswoman. "It's fine-tuning to maintain our service {profit} margins."

AT&T estimated that it has reduced outbound-calling prices for businesses by 32% since 1984. It said the "price-cap" formula by which it sets its pricing would be adjusted to show an additional $46 million in annual revenue this year compared with 1989, because of the latest pricing change.

Specifically, pricing increases will apply to a customer's network usage, including time-of-day charges and the distance that a call travels between two points, said AT&T. The affected services are AT&T Software Defined Network Service, or SDN, and several plans within the AT&T PRO WATS long-distance service.

For example, a six-minute call between San Francisco and Washington using PRO WATS service will increase to $1.52 from $1.49 today. A five-minute daytime call between Chicago and Denver over AT&T's SDN network will climb to 97 cents from the current 95 cents.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, AT&T closed at $37.125, unchanged.
