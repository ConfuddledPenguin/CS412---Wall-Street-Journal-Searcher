Pennzoil to Boost Stake in Chevron To Almost 10%

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 07/17/90

HOUSTON -- Pennzoil Co. said it intends to raise its stake in Chevron Corp. to just under 10% by year end from its current 8.8% stake. In a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Pennzoil said it would stop purchases slightly below the 10% threshold to avoid triggering Chevron's poison pill plan. Pennzoil said that, based on Chevron's recent stock price, that would limit purchases to about another $291 million.

In San Francisco, a Chevron spokeswoman said, "We can't comment until we have a chance to evaluate" Pennzoil's filing.

Pennzoil, which first amassed Chevron shares late last year as a "long-term investment," praised Chevron's recent operating performance but was critical of what it views as steps taken to "dilute" earnings and shareholder value.

Pennzoil's plans to raise the stake aren't surprising. Pennzoil has indicated that it would invest in Chevron all the $2.6 billion of proceeds it received from Texaco Inc. to settle their mammoth legal dispute. So far, Pennzoil has paid roughly $2.1 billion for about 31.5 million Chevron shares. Chevron has 355 million common shares outstanding.

Analysts were puzzled at why Pennzoil would make its filing now. "The timing is a little odd," said Frederick Leuffer, an analyst with C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc. in New York. "Chevron's stock is up."

Chevron shares closed yesterday at $75.125, up $1.125, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. Pennzoil bought its other Chevron shares at an average price of $67 each. Pennzoil finished yesterday at $79.625, up 50 cents, also on the Big Board.

When Pennzoil disclosed its stake in December, Chevron reacted swiftly by reducing the trigger point for its poison pill to 10% from 20%. The company also filed suit against Pennzoil, accusing it of misrepresenting its intentions in its SEC filings. So far, Pennzoil has won most of the court battles and a hearing is set for Sept. 14 to rule on Pennzoil's request to dismiss the rest of the claims.

In a letter to shareholders, Pennzoil said that "Chevron appears to be doing well in its efforts to improve its operating performance," citing a number of asset sales and plans for oil exploration in the Soviet Union. It was the potential for improvement that enticed Pennzoil as a holder, the company said.

But Pennzoil then appeared to raise the rhetoric against Chevron management compared with previous disclosures in which Pennzoil Chairman J. Hugh Liedtke went out of his way to praise Chevron executives. In the shareholder letter, he wrote, "Chevron's hostility is extremely disappointing to us."

He cited concern about Chevron's Employee Stock Ownership Plan, for which the company recently borrowed $1 billion. Pennzoil also criticized a new management incentive plan in which Chevron can award as much as 1% of its shares outstanding each year to about 300 key employees. Pennzoil said that with Chevron's stock price above $70, the value of the plan this year could come to about $250 million.
