Lead, Zinc Prices Fall as Bargainers Near Cominco Pact

  • Author: Gary Lamphier
  • Date: 07/17/90

VANCOUVER, British Columbia -- Prices for lead and zinc dropped sharply as bargainers for Cominco Ltd. and the United Steelworkers of America appeared close to a settlement that could end a strike that began July 9. "We're almost in a position to recommend to the membership" a new two-year contract, said Ron Schmidt, the union's chief negotiator. He said the union has already won acceptable increases to wages and pensions, following a 21-hour weekend bargaining session.

"There are just a couple of things to resolve," he said, including a plan to reopen the currently dormant Sullivan lead-zinc mine, at Kimberley, British Columbia. The proposed two-year pact, which the union's bargaining committee hopes to present to striking workers today, would replace one that expired June 30, a week before the walkout began.

The accord would cover about 700 currently inactive workers at Sullivan, and an additional 2,900 striking workers at Cominco's big lead-zinc smelter complex, at Trail, British Columbia. The shutdown at Trail, which at full capacity produces about 5.8% of the Western world's refined zinc and about 2.6% of its refined lead, has helped drive up lead and zinc prices in recent weeks.

Reports of an impending settlement sent lead and zinc prices plummeting yesterday on the London Metal Exchange. Spot prices for lead and zinc closed at about 38 cents (U.S.) and 70 cents (U.S.) a pound, respectively, down from 41 cents and 75 cents at Friday's close.

Mr. Schmidt wouldn't elaborate on the terms of the wage and pension proposals from Cominco. But a union official said it calls for a wage increase of about 18% over the two-year life of the accord. The average hourly wage at Trail under the previous pact was 16.82 Canadian dollars (US$14.54).
