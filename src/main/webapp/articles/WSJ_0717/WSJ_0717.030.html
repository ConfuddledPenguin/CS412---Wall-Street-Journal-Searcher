Letters to the Editor -- Dear Philip Morris: Do You Really Mean It?

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/17/90

John R. Nelson, Philip Morris's vice president for corporate affairs, presented the cigarette industry's standard reply to assertions by critics that the industry targets children for tobacco-product sales (Letters, June 12). It is heartening to see that Mr. Nelson agrees with public-health authorities on several substantial matters. We believe, however, that Philip Morris should back up its policy with action. The areas of agreement are these: Children should not smoke, and it is a good thing tobacco-product sales to people below age 18 are generally against the law.

Mr. Nelson goes even further when he says, "We at Philip Morris do not want minors to smoke."

Since Philip Morris does not want minors to smoke, its sales to minors are unwanted, and its profits on sales to minors are also unwanted. At a minimum, then, Philip Morris's resolve to prevent children from using its tobacco products should be measured by how it uses its unwanted profits from sales to kids.

How much is this? Mr. Nelson indicates that "A small number of {the company's} cigarettes are unfortunately obtained by minors."

Given the enormous size of his company's cigarette sales, Mr. Nelson can be forgiven the characterization of its sales to underage people as "small." These only amounted to 1.5% of cigarettes sold in the U.S. last year. Still, that's a substantial number of smokes.

We estimate that last year, 750 packs of Marlboros were consumed by kids younger than 18 every minute, and Philip Morris made a profit of $130 million for the year from these largely illicit sales to the underage. While these numbers are small compared with overall corporate performance, they are substantially larger than the company's efforts to prevent smoking by children.

We invite Philip Morris to back up its policy: As a minimum, the company should give its profits on mostly illegal sales to the underage to state health departments across the country to support programs for the prevention of nicotine addiction among the young. Anything less has to be regarded as window dressing.

John Slade, M.D.

St. Peter's Medical Center

University of Medicine and Dentistry of New Jersey

New Brunswick, N.J.

Ed Crane

Evangelical Lutheran Church in America
