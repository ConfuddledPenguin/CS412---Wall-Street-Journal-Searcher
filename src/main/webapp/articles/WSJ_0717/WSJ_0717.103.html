SEC Is Assailed Over Supervising Financial Planners

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 07/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The investigative arm of Congress delivered a scathing report on the financial-planning industry and contended that the Securities and Exchange Commission's oversight "may be doing more harm than good." The General Accounting Office study said that investors may be misled by the SEC's implied "seal of approval" in licensing financial planners. The process must be improved, the GAO said, "to provide people who might use an adviser a minimum level of assurance" that the adviser operates within the law.

The GAO report focuses on an industry that has more than tripled over the past decade to about 14,000 registered advisers who now manage roughly $4.6 trillion in assets. As its basis, the GAO used figures from other studies showing that advisers defrauded investors of $90 million to $200 million a year. But the agency added that it thought the figures were low, and some consumer groups put the annual figure much higher.

In a response by Kathryn McGrath, former head of the investment-management division, the SEC conceded that its oversight is below par but quickly added that doing more would require much more money. The report offers "laudable goals that the staff would support in a cost-free environment," Ms. McGrath said.

Last spring, Rep. Rick Boucher (D., Va.) unveiled a bill that would force all financial planners to register with the SEC, provide full written disclosure to clients of compensation and allow customers to sue for damages. As important, the legislation would give the SEC broader ability to attack abuse by financial planners, including fining power.

Rep. Boucher's bill isn't considered likely to go far this year, and the first hearing on the matter will be held tomorrow by the House subcommittee on telecommunications and finance.

The Investment Advisers Act of 1940 requires financial planners to register with the SEC. But the GAO reported that a financial planners group, the International Association of Financial Planning, estimated that there are 100,000 to 300,000 financial planners in the U.S.

Registration doesn't necessarily improve an investor's chances of getting a competent adviser, the GAO said. Planners don't need any training, said the GAO, which quoted one industry official as saying, "It is easier to become a registered investment adviser than to become a licensed driver."

In one example, the GAO described a woman who lost her life savings of $60,000 despite being "impressed" that the adviser was SEC-registered.

Moreover, the report criticized the agency's inspection of licensed advisers. The SEC usually inspects advisers for the first time an average of three years after they are registered, but many have never been inspected, the report said.

The SEC has proposed setting up a self-regulatory organization for financial planners, but that plan hasn't gone far.

The GAO recommended that the SEC inspect all newly registered advisers within a year and periodically afterward. The SEC called the proposal "a good idea." Advisers shown to be riskier should have a shorter leash, with inspections every six months, the GAO said. In addition, the report proposed that the SEC verify education and other experience before granting registration.
