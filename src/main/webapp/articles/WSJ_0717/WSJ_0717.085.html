International: U.S. Will Pursue Ties to Opposition In the Soviet Union

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 07/17/90

PARIS -- The U.S. will try to build bridges to the non-Communist political opposition in the Soviet Union, just as Washington has reached out to other non-Communist political groups in Eastern Europe, Secretary of State James Baker said. "I don't think that's inappropriate just because it's the Soviet Union," he said. He added that he agreed to some extent with those who say the U.S. should step up contacts with such Soviet opposition figures as Russian Republic President Boris Yeltsin and the mayors of Moscow and Leningrad, all of whom quit the Communist Party last week.

But the secretary of state said only that the U.S. should "touch base" with the Soviet opposition "to make sure that we understand where the opposition is coming from, that they understand where we are coming from."

That stops well short of the outright political support the U.S. granted to non-Communist groups in Eastern Europe. Mr. Baker gave no hint that the administration is planning to withdraw its strong backing for President Mikhail Gorbachev, who still heads the Soviet Communist Party.

Nevertheless, the new policy represents a change. When Mr. Yeltsin visited Washington last year, he was given a curt, low-key reception, and administration officials later expressed a dim view of him.

Separately, the U.S. has proposed that the four victorious World War II powers retain their rights to approve Germany's borders, even after that country is unified, until the Germans sign a treaty guaranteeing their border with Poland.

Mr. Baker, who was flying here for the "two plus four" talks on German unification, told reporters that the U.S. will introduce this idea at the negotiations starting today. The talks are being held by the foreign ministers of the two Germanys and the four World War II victors -- the U.S., the Soviet Union, Britain and France.

For this week's session of the talks, the third in the series, the Polish foreign minister also will be present. Last week, Poland demanded that the allies retain their rights in Germany to help force adoption of a border treaty, and Mr. Baker's comments appeared designed to reassure the Poles.

The U.S. idea on the Polish border represents a rare move by the Bush administration to pressure West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl, who in recent months has been the Western leader closest to the U.S. The aim is to reassure Poland that a powerful united Germany won't violate Poland's border as Nazi Germany did in 1939.

Mr. Kohl has insisted that for legal reasons such a treaty can't be written until Germany is unified. But the Polish government fears the Western powers will have no leverage if they give up their World War II legal rights at the moment of unification.

A senior administration official traveling with Mr. Baker hinted that the U.S. might back off its idea if West Germany and Poland can agree at today's meeting on some guarantee that a simple, narrowly written border treaty will be ratified quickly after the first all-German elections. The elections are set for Dec. 2. He noted that Poland's government has told the other powers it isn't aiming at disrupting Germany's unification.

Even as a negotiating tactic, however, the U.S. proposal represents a significant policy change. The U.S. has insisted previously that the World War II victors relinquish all rights when unification occurs.
