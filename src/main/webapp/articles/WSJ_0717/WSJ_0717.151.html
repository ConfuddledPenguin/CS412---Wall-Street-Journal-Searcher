Letters to the Editor: First Courageous Foe Of Cigarette Smoking

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/17/90

I enjoyed Michael Gartner's letter to Dr. Louis Sullivan (Viewpoint, June 7). However, a small correction is in order. Mr. Gartner states, ". . . the constant campaign against smoking waged first by Surgeon General C. Everett Koop. . . ." Of course, the "constant campaign against smoking" was waged first by my father, Dr. Luther L. Terry. His courageous, ground-breaking report in 1964 when he was surgeon general represented the first salvo against the largest preventable cause of death and disability in our nation. Dr. Koop himself has on numerous occasions cited Dad's work as providing the scientific and moral foundation for the 26 years of efforts that have followed.

To quote from Joseph A. Califano's eulogy at my father's funeral:

"Luther Terry saved as many lives as any Salk or Pasteur or Madame Curie. He is as much a medical giant. But he is more. No powerful economic forces rose up to defend polio, or smallpox or unsafe milk. When Luther Terry told us of the dangers of smoking, political and financial powers of magnum force rose to attack him. His discovery was one they had tried to hide, one they didn't want to talk about. But Luther spoke out. Softly, but firmly he stood his ground. He persevered, and in the end he revolutionized our thinking about the causes of modern-day diseases. Many of us have walked and will walk in his footsteps, but none of us can fill his shoes. . . ."

The Terry family is gratified with the work of those who followed Dad, particularly Dr. Koop, whose dedication and unquestioned integrity in the latest assault on smoking are familiar echoes. But I ask you to remember the man who started it all, a brave, self-effacing hero, Luther Terry.

Michael D. Terry

Moscow, Tenn.
