IBM's Profit Advanced 5.2% In 2nd Period --- Solid Result Boosts Stocks; Problems May Be Ahead As Mainframe Sales Fell

  • Author: Paul B. Carroll
  • Date: 07/17/90

International Business Machines Corp. reported a solid 5.2% increase in second-quarter earnings, the first time since the mid-1980s that the company has put together respectable quarters back to back. "While first-half results are encouraging, much remains to be done this year," IBM Chairman John Akers said in a statement that was slightly more measured than the one that accompanied the first-quarter earnings report.

IBM did exceed securities analysts' expectations for the second consecutive quarter, reporting net income of $1.41 billion, or $2.45 a share, while analysts had generally estimated $2.25 to $2.35 a share. A year earlier, IBM earned $1.34 billion, or $2.31 a share. (Per-share earnings grew faster than the total figure because IBM has been buying back shares, cutting the average number outstanding to 573.3 million from 581.4 million a year earlier.) Revenue climbed a surprisingly strong 8.4%, to $16.5 billion from $15.21 billion.

The biggest surprise in the quarter was the strength of software revenue, which grew 26% to $2.35 billion. Although software revenue had increased a heady 30% to 35% for years, growth had recently slowed into the single digits as IBM had begun providing hefty discounts on software as a way of increasing hardware sales. IBM indicated that the strong showing was an aberration but suggested that percentage increases in the mid-teens were possible in the second half.

IBM said results continued to be good in personal computers, where IBM has been gaining market share for the past year and a half. Demand also remained strong for a line of high-end disk drives that IBM began shipping late last year. IBM also singled out for praise the AS/400, a line of minicomputers that had been going gangbusters since its introduction two years ago but that had finally seemed to be losing momentum in the first quarter.

Revenue rose again in the U.S., even though the whole computer industry has been having trouble here for years -- although the increase was apparently in the low single digits, as opposed to the double-digit growth of the first quarter and the 1989 second quarter.

IBM also said it is seeing some early benefits from a job-reduction and plant-consolidation program that occasioned a $2.4 billion write-off in the 1989 fourth quarter. Benefits from the program are expected to really start to show up in the second half.

At the same time, IBM acknowledged that 20 cents a share of the latest quarter's earnings came from the drop in the dollar -- because IBM does nearly 60% of its business overseas, and a drop increases the value of that business when it is translated into dollars.

In addition, operating profit margins were disappointing. Profit before taxes, interest expense and other income was just 15.7% of revenue, while many securities analysts had been predicting the margin would be at least half a percentage point higher. The difference apparently came because a heavier-than-expected percentage of revenue came from products such as personal computers, where profit margins are lower than on the big systems.

The decline in mainframe revenue wasn't a big surprise, given that a manufacturing problem in the 1989 first quarter packed far more deliveries into last year's second quarter than are normal. Still, IBM indicated that second-quarter mainframe revenue was flat compared with this year's first quarter -- even though the second quarter typically accounts for 25% of mainframe revenue and the first 15%.

In the first half, IBM earned $2.45 billion, or $4.26 a share, up 6.8% from $2.29 billion, or $3.92 a share. Revenue climbed 9.8% to $30.68 billion from $27.94 billion.
