House Clears Tougher Bill For Scrutiny of Old Planes

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The House voted for legislation that would require more vigorous scrutiny of older jetliners to ensure they are safe to fly. The bill, approved by voice vote, would require the Federal Aviation Administration to inspect aging planes to determine whether they are in compliance with maintenance procedures mandated in recent years to guard against cracking, metal fatigue and other age-related problems.

The special FAA inspections would occur every four or five years for planes 15 years or older. Proponents said the stepped-up reviews are needed to ensure the safety of older jetliners; 28% of the nation's jetliners are over 20 years old, and that percentage will rise as planes are kept in service longer than was originally planned.

"Under the legislation, an inspection will be required in which a straightforward question will be asked: `Is this old airplane safe?'" said Rep. James Oberstar, (D., Minn.), chairman of the House Aviation Subcommittee. "Aircraft will not be permitted to return to service until the answer is `yes.'"

The potential problems involving aging airliners was illustrated in April 1988, when an Aloha Airlines plane lost the top of its fuselage, killing a flight attendant. The incident prompted the FAA and the airline industry to develop more stringent policies for older aircraft.
