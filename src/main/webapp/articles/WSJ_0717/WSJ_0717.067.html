Monday's Markets: Dow Sprints to High, Just Shy of 3000 Close

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 07/17/90

NEW YORK -- The Dow Jones Industrial Average sprinted to its third consecutive record, but fell short of closing at 3000 by just a quarter of a point. Much of the market's stride came from reports of strong second-quarter earnings at some companies. International Business Machines jumped 1 3/8 to 122 3/8 after announcing a quarterly profit that was at the high end of analysts' estimates.

Late profit-taking kept the industrial average below the 3000 benchmark, finishing at 2999.75, up 19.55. Trading activity was a moderate 149.4 million shares in New York Stock Exchange trading -- considerably slower than the 200 million-plus share turnovers on Thursday and Friday last week.

Stocks dominated the action in financial markets. Bond prices closed essentially unchanged. The dollar ended mixed.

Stock prices rose in early trading partly on IBM's earnings news and similar good tidings from NCR. "Stocks have some forward momentum, with the worst earnings news behind us and earnings generally not declining as much as expected," said Sung Won Sohn, chief economist at Minneapolis-based Norwest Corp. He also thinks investors are expecting further declines in interest rates, which should help boost corporate profits.

Other analysts are harder put to explain why the industrial average has leaped 108.91 points over the past four sessions.

Steven Einhorn, portfolio strategist at Goldman Sachs, said investors are so busy buying stocks they are overlooking fundamental concerns that normally would slow the market's gains.

"We are in an overvalued market that looks like it wants to get more overvalued, independent of bonds, independent of earnings, independent of the dollar," he said. "Investors appear to want to own only one asset. That's the default reason for it going up, but that isn't the kind of market that will play to the strengths of a fundamentalist. The market has been irrepressible."

Stock investors have had all the right reasons one could want to sell, Mr. Einhorn said: a Fed easing on interest rates that was slow in coming and modest, a weaker-than-expected dollar, some earnings disappointments and a yearlong decline in the margin of rising stocks over falling stocks.

But a rising market makes people who have a lot of cash nervous, Mr. Einhorn said. "They don't want to miss something that could be powerful and extended, whether they're fundamentally bullish or not." While the 3000 mark itself has no magical significance, "it can add impetus psychologically as we get close to it" for those portfolio managers feeling pressure to jump on the bandwagon, he said.

Frank A. Cappiello Jr., president of San Francisco-based McCullough, Andrews & Cappiello, a money manager, agreed that stocks are the place to be for investors. "The only game in town now in total return is stocks," he said. "Gold is bad news. Real estate is more big chasms and bottomless pits to come. Bonds have been more volatile than stocks over the past two years, and I think people have been intuitively aware of it."

Mr. Cappiello said there is ample reason to believe the market has room to climb further. He cited continued positive earnings news and the belief that interest rates are going to come down further before they go up.

Bond traders didn't act like they saw lower rates coming. Bond prices closed unchanged, with investors having digested Friday's drop in interest rates and awaiting pivotal economic news later this week.

The dollar was stronger than expected but ended mixed; traders are awaiting Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan's semiannual economic and monetary review tomorrow for signs that fighting inflation remains the Fed's top priority.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose. Advancing issues outnumbered decliners, 868 to 678.

Bond prices were flat. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond stood to 8.45%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6547 marks, compared with 1.6510 marks Friday, and unchanged at 148.15 yen.
