Law -- Legal Beat: One Lawyer's Deeds Tax State Legal Fund

  • Author: Ann Hagedorn and Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 07/17/90

The fraudulent dealings of one New York attorney are costing members of the state bar millions of dollars in restitution funds for dozens of bilked clients. Last week the Lawyers' Fund for Client Protection, in Albany, awarded $2.3 million to 61 former clients of Jack B. Solerwitz, a former Long Island attorney now in prison for grand larceny. There are 50 more claims pending, with losses of more than $2 million.

"If not the largest case of client theft, it is certainly close to the largest in the state's history," says Martin Minkowitz, a partner at Stroock & Stroock & Lavan and chairman of the state bar's Committee on Professional Discipline.

Since March 1988, 149 former clients across the country have claimed huge losses resulting from their dealings with the 33-year old Mr. Solerwitz, who was disbarred in February and who listed $4 million in debts on his personal-bankruptcy filing in Nassau County (N.Y.) Court.

The Lawyers' Fund is an agency created by the state finance law in 1981 to pay full or partial restitution to victims of dishonest lawyers. There is a $100,000 limit for each individual claim. The source of the agency's funds is the membership of the New York bar -- about 90,000 practicing lawyers.

Until this year, New York lawyers were required to pay a $100 registration fee to the state every two years. Of that, $50 went to the Lawyers' Fund. As of June 1990, the biannual fee requirement has tripled. Of the $300 payment, $60 will go to the client-security fund. And for at least the next year most of the $60 will go to the victims of Mr. Solerwitz.

"There are those who now call the registration fee the `Solerwitz Memorial Surcharge,'" says Grace Moran, deputy counsel for the state's grievance committee for Nassau and Suffolk counties on Long Island, who handled the part of the case against Mr. Solerwitz involving his right to practice law in the state.

In April, Mr. Solerwitz, who was once renowned for representing 800 disenfranchised air traffic controllers in the aftermath of the 1981 strike, pleaded guilty to grand larceny in Nassau County Court. He admitted taking $1.8 million from escrow and other accounts of many clients. He was recently sentenced to a prison term of 5 to 15 years.

Mr. Solerwitz, who practiced in Mineola, Long Island, handled employment discrimination cases, divorces, and real estate transactions. According to information in his victims' claims, he bamboozled his clients in three ways, says Ms. Moran, the lawyer at the grievance committee.

He would take fees without doing the requisite work, she says. He would take down payments for houses, divorce settlements and personal injury proceeds, place them in escrow and use the money for his own purposes, she says. And he would persuade people to lend him large sums of money. "You would be amazed at how many people remortgaged their homes to lend him money," says Ms. Moran.

"To this day, we are still getting complaints," she says. "This morning, one called claiming $9,000 in losses. It was a $9,000 personal injury settlement in which he had written a bad check. I don't know when it will end."

JUSTICE DEPARTMENT admits it defamed former official.

In an unusual admission of error, the Justice Department conceded that it had mistakenly accused Roger Pilon, a former department official, of disclosing classified information to a foreign government and then resigning from his post in the face of removal proceedings.

The department made the admission, together with a formal apology and a settlement of $25,000, in documents signed on Thursday. But department officials didn't announce the action publicly. Mr. Pilon, now an officer of the Cato Institute, a Washington research group, disclosed the documents yesterday.

In a letter to Mr. Pilon's lawyer, Assistant Attorney General Stuart Gerson acknowledged that a report widely distributed last November by the department's Office of Professional Responsibility "incorrectly stated" that a counter-intelligence investigation into allegations that Mr. Pilon had provided the South African government with classified documents "discovered that sufficient cause existed to terminate (Mr. Pilon's) political appointment." The report also mistakenly stated that Mr. Pilon resigned from his job as director of the department's office of asylum policy "prior to the initiation of removal proceedings."

In fact, Mr. Gerson continued, the 1988 investigation exonerated Mr. Pilon. After having been put on administrative leave of absence, Mr. Pilon resumed his post and his "top secret" security clearance was returned. Moreover, his subsequent resignation "was not made in the face of removal proceedings," Mr. Gerson wrote.

A department spokesman declined to answer questions as to whether anyone in the Office of Professional Responsibility would be disciplined for issuing the incorrect report.

Mr. Pilon called the incident a "Kafkaesque nightmare . . . because we never knew where the accusations came from." His wife, Juliana, also was investigated for allegedly disclosing classified information, but she was never charged.

Rep. Don Edwards (D., Calif.), one of several lawmakers who had pressured Attorney General Dick Thornburgh to publicly exonerate Mr. Pilon, praised the admission, but he added, "This apology is long overdue."
