Aircraft Retrofitter Gets Jury Award Of $70.8 Million

  • Author: David J. Jefferson
  • Date: 07/17/90

VAN NUYS, Calif. -- A state court jury here ordered General Electric Capital Corp. to pay $70.8 million in damages to a small aircraft retrofitter that claims it was nearly put out of business when the General Electric Co. unit pulled the plug on financing for a joint venture between the companies. The jury awarded the Van Nuys-based retrofitter, American Aviation Industries Inc., $65 million in compensatory damages and $5.8 million in punitive damages against GE Capital, plus ownership of engine housings from the venture valued at about $1.5 million. On counterclaims, the jury awarded GE Capital and its parent $9.8 million for loans owed it by American Aviation plus engines and an aircraft valued at an additional $6 million.

"GE Capital regards the awards to AAI of compensatory and punitive damages as unwarranted and is confident that they will be set aside in further proceedings," GE Capital said.

American Aviation was set up in late 1983 by Aviation Consultants Inc., an aircraft leasing concern, with the intention of retrofitting aging aircraft with new, longer-range engines. In 1984, American Aviation entered into a joint venture agreement with GE to retrofit Lockheed Corp. JetStar corporate aircraft with GE turbofan engines that would allow the aircraft to make transoceanic flights.

The updated aircraft, called FanStars, would have cost one-third to one-half as much as comparable planes, American Aviation said, and the company estimated that the project could bring in profit of as much as $105 million. All that was needed was an investment of about $23 million to get the aircraft refitted and certified by the Federal Aviation Administration. GE Capital provided financing.

But in early 1987, GE Capital began to tighten the purse strings, claiming that its studies showed that only 20 of the retrofitted aircraft could be sold instead of the 75 or so originally projected. GE Capital required American Aviation's owners to put up 100% of their stock as collateral for a $2.5 million loan, then refused to fund the loan without concessions from management, American Aviation said.

The small company's efforts to arrange alternative financing through Advest Corp. and a separate investor group fell through when GE Capital argued against it with potential investors, American Aviation has claimed. In the end, the FanStar was never certified, and no retrofitted planes were sold.

The jury in the case found against GE Capital for breach of contract and "intentional interference with prospective economic advantage."
