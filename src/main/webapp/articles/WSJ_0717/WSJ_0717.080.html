Fed Plans Relaxed Limits on Securities, Business Activities of U.S. Banks Abroad

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 07/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Reserve Board issued proposals to significantly relax limits on securities activities by U.S. banks abroad and broaden the businesses the overseas units can enter without specific approval from the central bank. Under the package of proposals, overseas securities units of U.S. banks would be allowed to underwrite as much as $60 million or 25% of the parent's so-called Tier 1 capital. Currently, the limit is a flat $15 million. In the U.S, banks are allowed to underwrite a limited amount of debt, and although the Fed has indicated it will approve equities underwriting, it hasn't yet approved an application by any U.S. bank to do so.

Tier 1 capital, considered the most important form of capital, consists of common stock and other "tangible" investments that provide a cushion for losses.

The proposal would also raise the limit on the amount of securities from a single issuer that an overseas bank unit could hold in its trading account to $30 million or 10% of Tier 1 capital, whichever is lower. The current limit is $15 million.

The Fed also proposed that underwriting life insurance, operating as a futures commission merchant and acting as a principal or agent in interest-rate and foreign-currency swap transactions be added to the list of approved activities for the overseas units. The Fed has allowed banks' foreign subsidiaries to engage in some of those activities on a case-by-case basis, but the proposal would allow the units to engage in the activities simply by notifying the Fed.

The proposal, which is expected to be published in the Federal Register soon and will be open for comment for 60 days, also would allow the foreign securities units to underwrite 100% of an issuer's securities. Currently, the bank units are allowed to underwrite only 20% of an issue. The Fed said it was seeking comment on allowing banks to exceed the underwriting limits if the parent company would remain well-capitalized even after the excess underwriting amount was deducted from its capital.

Within 30 days of the close of an underwriting period, the bank subsidiary would have to divest enough of the issue it has underwritten to meet other limits, such as the restriction on subsidiaries' investment holdings. That limit is currently $15 million. The proposal would increase the limit to $25 million. The increase is warranted, the Fed said, because of the growth in capital at U.S. banks operating overseas.
