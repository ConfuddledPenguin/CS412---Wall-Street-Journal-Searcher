`Tire King' Wins: Merchant Defeats Vietnam Dogma

  • Author: Barry Wain
  • Date: 07/17/90

HANOI -- In his own quiet way, Nguyen Van Chan revels in the fact that he has beaten the system. The system in question is that practiced by the Vietnamese Communist Party in this nation of 66 million. Like all totalitarian systems, it is at times tyrannical, abusive and arbitrary. Despite extensive political, social and economic reforms it remains a system of men and caprice rather than one of institutions and principle.

Mr. Chan, 65, is one of its better-known victims. The majority of the tormented have remained anonymous, suffering out of sight. But for various reasons, though mostly luck, Mr. Chan became a cause celebre at home and abroad, and that accounts in large measure for his success against the system.

Largely uneducated, Mr. Chan has an entrepreneurial flair, but he had the misfortune to try to put his natural business instincts to work in Hanoi at a time when socialist principles required everyone to live equally, usually in poverty.

First, he invented a fountain pen that he and his family produced from plastic waste. Later, he marketed glue that he developed to enable him to make rubber sandals. Both his pens and his glue were immensely popular, for the simple reason that they worked better than what came off the government production line.

For his troubles, Mr. Chan was jailed three times between 1960 and 1974. His operation was properly registered and used only scrap, yet he was accused of conducting illegal business and possessing illegal materials. On one occasion he was held for three months without being charged.

Undeterred, he graduated to heavy-duty bicycle tires, finally producing one that, once again, easily outlasted its state-produced rival. By 1982, he was selling thousands of tires. Newspapers published articles about him, and he was widely hailed as "the tire king." His efforts earned a gold medal at a national exhibition -- and attracted renewed envy and attention from local authorities.

In familiar fashion, district police in 1983 descended on Mr. Chan's suburban home, which he and his large family used as both residence and factory, and demanded proof that the house and equipment were his. "When I proved that everything was legal, that was the first time that they lost and I won," he says.

Invited to the district police station, he was required to explain the production process step by step, supposedly to show whether he was a real working man or an exploiter. "When I showed it was from my own brain, they lost for the second time," he says.

Nevertheless, a few days later the police accused him of stealing electricity, acquiring rubber without permission and buying stolen socialist property. Warned that he was to be jailed again, he fled to the countryside while firing off protests to higher authorities. His family was given only three hours to leave before the house and contents were confiscated.

After six months on the run, Mr. Chan learned that the order for his arrest had been canceled. He rejoined his family in January 1984 and began a long struggle for justice. Mr. Chan achieved a breakthrough in 1987 when an investigative reporter, capitalizing on reforms then being introduced, got Mr. Chan's story published in a weekly newspaper. In 1988, 18 months after the Communist Party's sixth congress endorsed family-run private enterprise, district authorities allowed him to resume work, but they released only one of his machines, and they didn't return his rubber stocks or his house.

Although he was invited back to display his products at a national exhibition in late 1988, and many of the country's leaders stopped by his stand to commend his achievements as an example of private initiative, he wasn't satisfied. He wanted his property -- and his "dignity" -- back.

When I met Mr. Chan at his son's home in Hanoi in April 1989, he was about to mount his bicycle to deliver yet another petition to district officials. His wife was in tears, and fearful of the consequences. But a determined Mr. Chan assured me, "I will win. They will lose a third time."

Finally, after seven long years, he did triumph. Early this year, newly installed district officials handed back his house keys, apologized for the misdeeds of their predecessors and promised to pay him 200 taels of gold valued at $90,000 for irrecoverable stock and personal possessions.

Now his business is flourishing, with branches in Ho Chi Minh City. His company is turning out motor bikes as well as bicycle tires, and starting to generate big profits with heat-resistant rubber belts for use in electricity generators. Other new products on the way include tires for small cars.

For the benefit of those who weren't around to share his moments of victory, Mr. Chan has made a video that shows his family's return to the house, which the authorities had used as a health clinic. Neighbors greet them with exploding firecrackers, and help them clean the yard and scrub interior walls and furniture.

One of the first things Mr. Chan did when he settled in was to throw a party for all his friends who had stood by him in his darkest hours. In the video, he introduces them to the camera: This one hid him from the police; that one allowed her place to be used as a mail drop so that he could keep contact with his family while on the run.

If Mr. Chan's experience has led him to question the system that he has battled for half a lifetime, he stays silent about it. He is philosophical. "I am fortunate," he says. "The wheel of history keeps turning."
