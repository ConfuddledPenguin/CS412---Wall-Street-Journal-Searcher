Marketing & Media: Newsprint Workers Vote to Continue Canadian Strike

  • Author: G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 09/18/90

MONTREAL -- Workers at eight newsprint mills in Eastern Canada voted to remain on strike, prompting fears that the shutdown of about 13% of North American newsprint capacity will drag on for several weeks or more and send publishers scrambling for supplies. "We'll have a minicrisis on our hands" if the mills are still out at the end of next week, said James Fitzgerald, senior vice president at Newsday, the Long Island, N.Y., and New York City daily. Mr. Fitzgerald buys newsprint for all Eastern newspapers of Los Angeles-based Times Mirror Co.; they include Newsday, the Hartford Courant and Baltimore Sun. He said that while most publishers have at least 30 days worth of newsprint on hand, "it's amazing how quickly supplies dwindle."

Workers walked out at seven Abitibi-Price Inc. mills a week ago. Six of those mills produce newsprint. Workers went on strike at two Canadian Pacific Forest Products Ltd. newsprint mills last Thursday.

Together, the closed Abitibi and CP Forest mills have annual capacity of 2.1 million metric tons, or 13% of North American newsprint capacity. Also, a strike at a Tribune Co. unit's mill in Ontario that accounts for just under 2% of North American capacity is now in its sixth week.

Publishers in the Northeast U.S. and Eastern Canada would be hardest hit by an extended strike. Most of the newsprint usually produced by the closed mills is shipped to customers in those regions.

"We have an adequate supply {on hand}, and at this point it doesn't look too bleak," said Robert Shadrick, director of operations at Providence Journal Co., a newspaper publisher based in Providence, R.I. "But you're always concerned when 30% of the newsprint supply {to the region} is taken out of the market," he added.

Indirectly the strike may have already cost publishers tens of millions of dollars. Publishers and many analysts believe that the threat of a strike at Canadian mills helped newsprint producers push through a price increase in June. After discounts, the benchmark Eastern price of newsprint rose about $30 a metric ton to about $555 a metric ton. Publishers of U.S. dailies consume over 700,000 metric tons of newsprint a month.

It wasn't clear late yesterday if or when talks between the Canadian Paperworkers Union and the companies would resume. An Abitibi-Price official said there is "nothing scheduled." A CP Forest spokesman declined to comment.

A union official said workers at the striking mills strongly rejected the last offers from each company. He said the exact voting results weren't available and declined further comment. Workers at certain Abitibi and CP Forest mills aren't on strike and are understood to have accepted the offers.

The separate talks between the union and each company broke off last week, so that the union could hold a vote. The Abitibi and CP Forest offers are modeled on a contract signed by workers at Stone Container Corp. mills in Quebec in July. That contract was supposed to set the pattern for the remainder of the Eastern Canadian industry. But workers at some other mills are balking at certain concessions to which the Stone workers agreed in their contract.
