Technology & Medicine: Charter Medical Set To Cut Advertising For Psychiatric Care --- Campaigns Were Criticized As Scare Tactics Aimed At Attracting Patients

  • Author: Ron Winslow
  • Date: 09/18/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- Charter Medical Corp. said it plans sharp cutbacks in advertising, including campaigns that critics have said use scare tactics to lure patients to psychiatric hospitals. The decision reflects the financially troubled hospital company's effort to stem expenses. But it also comes as purchasers of psychiatric care bristle at soaring costs of inpatient psychiatric care, particularly for adolescents.

Several private psychiatric hospitals and hospital companies, including Charter, have used emotional commercials portraying parents distraught over troubled teenagers to market their facilities. Some research suggests that more than half of teen-age admissions to psychiatric hospitals are unnecessary.

"There are some forms of advertising that do bring people into hospitals who don't need to be there," said Stephen L. White, Charter's vice president, customer service. "It's something we want to get away from."

Robert Friedel, the Macon, Ga., company's vice president for psychiatric medicine and research, disclosed the advertising plans in a presentation at a conference here on mental health benefits. The weekend conference was sponsored by the Institute for Behavioral Healthcare.

The company's 90 hospitals spent about $40 million on advertising last year. Dr. Friedel and Mr. White said that could be reduced as much as 80% this year and next. Advertising will be consolidated at the company's headquarters and developed by one agency -- not yet selected -- instead of the more than 35 agencies that handled individual accounts at Charter's hospitals around the country. Mr. White said in an interview at the conference that the company's own evaluation indicated television advertising was only minimally effective in attracting patients. "It's ironic because advertising is the thing that angers and annoys most of the group purchasers," he said.

New advertising will take on an "educational theme," Dr. Friedel said, aimed at informing people of broad offerings of mental health care. He said studies suggest that just 20% of people suffering from some mental illness are actually getting treatment, while 80% of people with physical ailments are getting care.
