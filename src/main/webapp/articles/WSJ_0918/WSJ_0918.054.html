Letters to the Editor: Teacher's Losing Battle With `the Blob'

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/18/90

Reading Barbara D. Phillips's perceptive description of the public-education bureaucracy ("Off to the Education Front," editorial page, Aug. 17) made me want to write from the front about my most recent encounter with "the Blob." Perhaps my experience may serve as a cautionary tale for members of Teach for America. I have taught English in New York City and Omaha public schools for the past 13 years. Recently I won a National Endowment for the Humanities Teacher-Scholar Award. The Teacher-Scholar Program is governed by the conviction that students benefit most when teachers have a mastery of the subjects they teach and are, like other professionals, actively engaged in their fields. One teacher from each of the 50 states is selected on the basis of exceptional teaching and scholarship. A stipend of $27,500 accompanies the award to replace the teacher's salary or supplement sabbatical pay and allow him to devote a year to full-time research in his field.

At the end of the year the teacher returns to the classroom, intellectually revitalized and eager to pass along what he has learned to both students and colleagues. The example of his scholarship is a model to his students; his own fascination with what he teaches is contagious. The program is thus one of the few now offering hope of real educational reform from within the system itself. Enter "the Blob."

When I told the administrators of my school district that I won the award, they said that in order to accept it I would have to take a year's leave of absence without pay. This meant facing a significant cut in salary and paying my own medical-insurance premiums -- something that has now put our family of five on or just above the national poverty level. The local teachers' union echoed the district's position: There is nothing that can be done; the contract has no provision for teachers winning national teaching awards. Are they afraid that too many other teachers will win and bankrupt the system?

I suppose someone outside public education or a young teacher just starting out might think that an educator nationally recognized for excellence in teaching and scholarship would, perhaps now more than ever, be a welcome asset to the public schools. "The Blob" obviously thinks otherwise. Let the members of Teach for America be forewarned.

Edward A. Rauchut

Omaha, Neb.
