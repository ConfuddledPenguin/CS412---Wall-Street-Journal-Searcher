OTC Focus: Market Eases Slightly on Lower Volume; Barden Jumps 30 1/2 on Buy-Out News

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 09/18/90

NEW YORK -- Over-the-counter stocks eased slightly as news of buy-outs and corporate earnings enlivened an otherwise dull day. The Nasdaq Composite Index eased 0.31 to 374.11, 0.1% loss, as volume fell to 94 million shares from 103.5 million Friday. Declining shares kept their lead over advancing stocks, 1,022 to 776, while 2,453 shares remained unchanged.

Buy-out news ignited Barden, which almost doubled in value after FAG Bearings Corp. of Stamford, Conn., said it will pay $66.25 a share for the Danbury, Conn., bearing maker. Barden shares soared 30 1/2 to 63 on 18,700 shares, more than 11 times average volume of only 1,600 shares.

High-flying Nike stumbled 3 to 72. The Beaverton, Ore., athletic footwear maker reported earnings for the first quarter ended Aug. 31 rose to $2.62 a share from $2.01 a year earlier, at the high end of Wall Street's expectations. Analysts suggested investors who bought stock last week in anticipation of a strong report took profits yesterday.

However, slow growth continued to haunt other companies as Oracle Systems, for instance, traded heavily amid concern about its future.

Generally, investors remained wary of OTC stocks as they waited for economic news and developments in the Persian Gulf. "Overall, it's just wait-and-see," said Hedi Reynolds, managing director of OTC trading at Morgan Keegan & Co. in Memphis, Tenn. Institutions continued to buy stocks selectively, while small investors remained inactive, she said. "Retail {business} is very, very slow, which is what we're hearing from everybody," she said.

Technology shares closed mixed. Microsoft rose 1 to 60 3/4 after International Business Machines Co. extended three licensing pacts with the Redmond, Wash., software maker. However, Oracle, the day's most active stock, slid 5/8 to 6 7/8. The company has begun a round of layoffs to cope with slower growth.

Microsoft was buoyed by the news that IBM extended its licensing pacts for the company's DOS, Windows and OS/2 software. It was the first time that IBM sealed a broad-scale licensing arrangement for Microsoft's highly successful Windows 3.0 software, Goldman Sachs analyst Rick Sherlund said. But he said IBM hasn't been forthcoming about details of the Windows arrangement, making it too soon to gauge the financial impact.

After the two companies' long-running arguments over the marketing of OS/2, the extension of that licensing agreement is positive, Mr. Sherlund added. "It's an acknowledgement these guys have to live together," he said.

Oracle skidded near its 52-week low as analysts spoke of continuing troubles at the Redwood City, Calif., software maker. Dean Witter Reynolds analyst Timothy R. McCollum said he thinks three high-level executives already have left the company, in addition to a number of lower-level employees, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Oracle's stock has plummeted from 15 just a month ago and is trading at one-fourth its 52-week high of 28 3/8.

Other technology shares were mixed.

Intel rose 1/4 to 32 7/8; Conner Peripherals added 1/8 to 20 3/4; and Sun Microsystems gained 1/8 to 28 1/2. However, Apple Computer fell 1/4 to 33 3/4 and 3Com fell 1 to 6 7/8.

Barden's planned acquisition prompted investors to buy other machinery stocks:

Applied Materials rose 3/4 to 25; and Kaydon gained 1 3/8 to 35.

Kaydon also got a boost from Shearson Lehman Brothers analyst Susan Hirsch, who repeated her "buy" opinion on the Muskegon, Mich., maker of bearings and other machinery components.

Justin Industries climbed 3/4 to 14 1/4 after a dissident shareholder group said it offered to pay $18 a share for all outstanding shares of the Fort Worth, Texas, company. The group has an 11.4% stake in the company.

Speculation of an acquisition fueled a 1 1/8-point jump in VeloBind to 8 5/8. The Fremont, Calif., maker of book and document binding equipment confirmed it's in buyout discussions with an undisclosed company.

American Greetings gained 1/8 to 28 1/2 after the Cleveland greeting card company reported that net income for the second quarter ended Aug. 31 rose to 20 cents a share from 16 cents a year earlier.

Clean Harbors slid 1 3/8 to 5 1/8 after Massachussetts health officials opposed construction of a hazardous-waste incinerator planned by the Quincy, Mass., company. Among other environmental stocks, Western Waste Industries eased 1/2 to 18 3/4 and Mid-American Waste, which doesn't handle hazardous wastes, fell 1/4 to 25 3/4.

Mail Boxes Etc. tumbled 1 1/4 to 21 1/4 after the Pennsylvania attorney general sued the San Diego franchiser of mail services for allegedly misleading the public by marketing mailboxes that customers are allowed to identify as "suites" or "apartments."

Geriatric & Medical Centers lost 1 to 1 1/4. Late Friday, the Philadelphia concern said that Pennsylvania is seeking Medicaid audit adjustments and business-use and occupancy-tax assessments that could result in a total $12.1 million charge.
