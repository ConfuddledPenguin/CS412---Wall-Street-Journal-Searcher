U.S. Plan to Modernize Beef Inspection Needs Revamping, Science Panel Asserts

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 09/18/90

WASHINGTON -- A National Academy of Sciences panel criticized the government's plan for modernizing beef cattle inspection as being "probably no better," and in some instances worse, than current procedures at U.S. slaughterhouses. The committee said the Agriculture Department should proceed with its so-called streamlined inspection system, or SIS, but only after revamping it. At the same time, the committee emphasized that much more fundamental changes in meat inspection are needed to protect consumers from such health hazards as salmonella, campylobacter and other bacteria as well as chemical residues.

In a bluntly worded critique, issued yesterday, the panel said that the SIS plan isn't "designed to detect and eliminate microbial or chemical hazards." The department's Food Safety and Inspection Service was urged, among other things, to develop speedy methods for testing beef before it is shipped and to establish mandatory finished-product standards.

Streamlined inspection, proposed for the beef industry's 80 largest packing plants, would be the first major change in bovine meat inspection since the government began inspecting cattle in 1906. Under the plan, plant quality-control employees would take greater responsibility for visible contamination, such as hair and mud, freeing federal inspectors to focus more on food safety, rather than quality.

The plan would allow meatpackers to operate production lines faster, boosting productivity as much as 40%. But consumer advocates denounce streamlined inspection as "deregulation in disguise" and a threat to public health, while union officials see it as a move to drastically cut the force of inspectors.

The committee, headed by Robert Kahrs, dean of the University of Missouri's College of Veterinary Medicine, also expressed misgivings about the SIS plan, which is being tested at several pilot plants.

"From a food safety viewpoint," the panel said, "SIS-Cattle alone is probably no better and, in some situations, can be less effective than traditional inspection because the reduced oversight by government inspectors isn't compensated by a total commitment to product quality on the part of the industry."

The panel cautioned that streamlined inspection should be inaugurated only at plants where managers and employees have demonstrated a commitment to effective quality control. In recent months, several federal inspectors at SIS test plants have reported serious lapses in food safety.

"The academy's report is a gratifying vindication of what 50 whistle-blowers stated in sworn affidavits: In its present form, SIS weakens the USDA seal of approval," said Thomas Devine, attorney for the Government Accountability Project, a Washington-based defense group for meat inspectors and other whistle-blowers.

The committee also faulted the Agriculture Department for failing to heed key recommendations from two other academy panels, which studied meat inspection and other food safety issues in 1985 and 1987. For one thing, the department hasn't yet developed a method for tracing contamination in beef products back to specific feedlots or farms shipping tainted livestock, the panel said. For another, the department hasn't yet done any risk assessment.

"There is no evidence that SIS-Cattle confers greater benefit to the public health than traditional inspection," the panel asserted.
