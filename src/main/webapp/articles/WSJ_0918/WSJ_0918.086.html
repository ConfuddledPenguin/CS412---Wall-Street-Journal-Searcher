Westinghouse Affiliate Charges Award Of Japan Airport Job Was `Preposterous'

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 09/18/90

TOKYO -- The president of AEG Westinghouse Transportation Systems Inc. said he will urge the U.S. Congress to retaliate for Kansai International Airport Co.'s "preposterous decision" to award a ground-transportation contract to a Japanese company. AEG Westinghouse, based in Pittsburgh, is a joint venture of Westinghouse Electric Corp., which has a 20% stake, and AEG AG of West Germany, which has 80%.

Following a meeting at Kansai International headquarters in Osaka, AEG Westinghouse President John R. Tucker said the airport's effort to explain why it awarded the contract on July 10 to the Japanese company only confirmed U.S. suspicions of "irregularities" in the procurement process.

Mr. Tucker said presentations at the meeting indicated that important elements of the so-called people-mover system proposed by Niigata Engineering Co. differ from the systems Niigata has previously built. That, along with the fact that Niigata has never installed an airport system, flies in the face of previous assurances by Kansai International's president that the airport would place a high priority on proven equipment and proven reliability, Mr. Tucker charged.

A spokesman for the airport and a Niigata executive dismissed Mr. Tucker's allegations. They insisted that Niigata won because its bid was competitive -- though not the lowest of the 13 submitted -- and its proposed system well suited to the airport's needs.

People movers, also known as automated guideway transit systems, are electric-powered, trainlike systems used to shuttle passengers among airport terminals, between mass-transit systems, and the like. Westinghouse has installed 14 of the 18 airport systems in the world, beginning with the Tampa, Fla., airport in 1970, according to Mr. Tucker.

Kansai International spokesman Nobuyuki Higuchi said Niigata's bid was "in the same price range" as those of seven other bidders, while AEG Westinghouse's was among the highest. AEG Westinghouse and its three Japanese partners bid just under eight billion yen (about $58 million), while Niigata and its partner, Osaka-based Sumitomo Corp., bid about five billion yen. A United Technologies Corp. subsidiary that has won a people-mover contract for the next phase of Tokyo's Narita International airport also was among the unsuccessful bidders.

Shinichi Ohno, head of Niigata's transportation-systems business division, acknowledged that the number of car doors and certain other features of the Kansai International system differ from those Niigata has built to shuttle rail commuters in Osaka and Yokohama. But he said the basic elements of the Kansai International system closely resemble those in the fully automated system the company has had in Osaka for 10 years.
