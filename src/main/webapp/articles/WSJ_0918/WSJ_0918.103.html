Politics & Policy: Souter, Concluding Senate Testimony, Fights Off Attempts to Pin Him Down

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 09/18/90

WASHINGTON -- Supreme Court nominee David Souter, concluding his testimony before the Senate Judiciary Committee, parried continued efforts by senators to pin down his views on the right to abortion and other issues. After 18 hours of testimony, it appeared that Judge Souter is practically certain to be confirmed. The hearings are scheduled to continue today, with witnesses testifying for and against Judge Souter, who celebrated his 51st birthday yesterday. But even as Senate aides were distributing lists of scheduled witnesses yesterday afternoon, some liberal groups, such as People for the American Way, were deciding they wouldn't oppose the nomination.

Some of the toughest questioning yesterday came from conservative Republicans who seemed concerned that some of Judge Souter's statements last week invited too broad a role for the Supreme Court.

Sen. Charles Grassley (R., Iowa) said he was "a little troubled" by Judge Souter's suggestion that the courts must sometimes fill the "vacuum" created by the failure of Congress or the president to take action.

But Judge Souter said he was referring to courts exercising their existing authority, not expanding their jurisdiction to fill vacuums. He said that in 1954, the Supreme Court ordered school desegregation after Congress and the states declined to do it. But he said the court acted under its authority to implement the 14th Amendment guarantee of "equal protection of the laws," not under some usurped power.

He repeatedly declined senators' invitations to criticize a Supreme Court ruling last spring upholding the authority of federal judges to order local officials to raise taxes to fund school desegregation plans. He suggested that perhaps the Supreme Court shouldn't have heard the case, which involved school desegregation in Kansas City, Mo., but he wouldn't find fault with the 5-4 ruling.

Judge Souter said he doesn't agree with the view of retired Justice William Brennan, whom he was nominated to succeed, that the death penalty in all circumstances is unconstitutional. He said the authors of the Bill of Rights envisioned the existence of capital punishment.

Reiterating his pledge to approach the right to abortion with an open mind, he generally refused to discuss the 1973 Roe vs. Wade decision that recognized the right to abortion, as well as varied questions about how to define legally when human life begins. In the only specific response he gave on the abortion issue, he said that if the 1973 decision were overruled by the Supreme Court, state legislatures would pass a variety of different abortion laws.

In other aspects of his testimony, the former New Hampshire Supreme Court justice, who is currently a federal appeals court judge, said that based on his five years as a state trial judge, he has "enormous respect" for the jury system in criminal cases and civil damages cases.

Although he didn't elaborate, his answer might mean that he is skeptical of arguments that juries are approving excessively large punitive-damage awards. The constitutionality of punitive-damage awards is an issue to be decided by the Supreme Court in its term that begins Oct. 1. It is unclear, however, whether Judge Souter will be confirmed by the Senate in time to vote in the case, which will be argued Oct. 3.

Judge Souter said he doesn't "set myself up as an expert in antitrust matters." But he said he doesn't believe antitrust enforcement should be eliminated for vertical price-fixing cases in which manufacturers dictate retail prices.
