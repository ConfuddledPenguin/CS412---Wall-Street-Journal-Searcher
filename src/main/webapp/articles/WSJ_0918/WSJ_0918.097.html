Bankers Are Worried by the Prospect Of More Defaults on Credit-Card Debt

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 09/18/90

Problem loans come in all sizes for America's bankers. Their latest worry is that hard economic times will lead to increased defaults on credit-card debt. Bankers attending the American Bankers Association's Bank Card Conference this week in New York said that higher default rates already are showing up in New England. And even though the card business is enormously profitable, they are bracing for increasing defaults to slice into those margins in coming months.

Figures released yesterday by the American Bankers Association show that bad consumer credit-card debt already is on the rise. U.S. banks last year charged off $400 million more of credit-card debt than in 1988, the ABA said. That brought the total write-off to $3.7 billion, or 3% of the total $121.6 billion outstanding credit card debt.

In 1988, a year of more vigorous economic growth, credit card charge-offs totaled $3.3 billion, or 2.8% of the total.

Bankers are expecting it to get worse. "In a deteriorating economy we can expect to see a 10% to 15% increase in charge-offs," says James Bailey, group executive of U.S. credit-card operations at Citicorp, the nation's largest issuer of credit cards.

At the same time, though, the bankers are in a fighting mood because of American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s push into their business.

"Banks are caught in a squeeze play," says Spencer Nilson, editor of a credit-card newsletter, "They're trapped between deteriorating economic conditions and horrible competitive pressures from AT&T."

The program for the three-day conference highlights the conflict. Following one of the opening sessions yesterday, entitled "Our Changing Competition," were others such as "Bankruptcy Risk Control" and "Cardholder Fraud Risk Control."

To minimize the increased risks, bankers say they are turning down more applicants for new credit cards, granting smaller credit lines to those they accept, and keeping a closer watch on consumer spending habits.

"We're looking at our credit policy more carefully in New England, no question," says George Fesus, executive vice president of State Street Bank & Trust Co. in Boston.

Yet at the same time competitive pressures are leading many other banks to charge ahead with aggressive efforts to get their plastic into more wallets. Signet Bank is just one credit-card issuer that is offering college students with no credit history a charge card with a $2,000 credit limit. MNC Financial Corp. tells analysts it plans to double to $13 billion the outstanding consumer charges on MNC-issued credit cards in the next three years.

Bankers are competing fiercely because of AT&T's new, no fee credit cards, an estimated five million of which are expected to be in consumers' hands by year-end. AT&T began its promotions in the spring.

Other analysts, however, contend that while charge-offs might rise in coming months, credit card portfolios will remain among the most profitable products bankers can offer.

"I think banks are intellectually ready to see charge-offs go up," says James McCormick, president of First Manhattan Consulting Group. "But the business is so massively profitable that they can afford to sit back and make 35% returns on equity for a few years instead of 40%." Most banks are happy to earn a 15% return on equity in their overall business.
