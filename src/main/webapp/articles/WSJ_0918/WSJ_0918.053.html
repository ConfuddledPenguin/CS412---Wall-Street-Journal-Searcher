International -- Foreign Insight: U.S. Muzzles Talk of Long Gulf Presence That Stirred Fears Among Arab Allies

  • Author: Barbara Rosewicz
  • Date: 09/18/90

CAIRO, Egypt -- Recent U.S. talk of stationing troops permanently in the Persian Gulf is backfiring in the Arab world. Even in a country as supportive of the U.S. role as Egypt, the suggestion of long-term foreign military involvement in the Gulf is viewed as ominous and plays into the hands of political forces already suspicious that the U.S. is only after oil or control of the strategic region.

"It wasn't a bright idea on the part of the Americans to raise it at this time," says Akmed Kamal Abul-Magd, an Egyptian lawyer and academic. "It's very alarming. It's most destructive and most divisive."

Secretary of State James Baker broached the concept of a long-term U.S. presence as an element of a regional security structure, partly to pacify critics at home who were pressing officials to come up with long-range plans after the hasty buildup of a U.S.-led Western and Arab military force in Saudi Arabia. But instead of reassuring Arabs, it sounded alarm bells in certain quarters in countries near the conflict. Now, to recover from its tactical blunder, U.S. officials are promising to remove the troops as soon as they aren't needed any longer.

But the mixed signals have stirred sentiments in Arab society that Egyptian President Hosni Mubarak is trying to subdue. To forge an Arab military coalition to confront Iraq after its Aug. 2 invasion and occupation of Kuwait, Mr. Mubarak had to maneuver around a split among Arab nations over the use of foreign troops to defend Saudi Arabia. Inside Egypt, while there is near-unanimous condemnation of the invasion of Kuwait, the public is divided over the deployment of foreign troops on Saudi soil. Even the Islamic movement is split over the presence of Western "infidels" in the land of Moslem's holiest shrines.

The balance of support still tips strongly in favor of Mr. Mubarak's pivotal decision to deploy Egyptian soldiers as part of an Arab force to defend Saudi Arabia against possible Iraqi attack. But Mideast experts say a permanent security force raises more troubling issues.

"Arabs wouldn't welcome a long-term presence," said an Egyptian official privy to foreign-policy decision-making, "because the Arab mind turns in a conspiratorial way." Unlikely as it may seem to Americans, many Arabs would tend to think the U.S. somehow contrived to create the Kuwaiti controversy to win a toehold in the oil-rich region, he said.

Any security arrangement, the official said, "has to emanate from the important powers in the region," namely Egypt and Saudi Arabia, even if it ultimately relies on international military help. The sentiment suggests that U.S. officials will have to play down talk of security arrangements for the Gulf at least until the immediate crisis -- the occupation of Kuwait -- is over.

Iran's Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, publicly blasting Mr. Baker's plan, last week called for a "holy war" against the U.S. presence in the region. Mr. Mubarak hasn't made an issue of long-term security arrangements, but his government is subtly trying to calm resentment stirred by the presence of foreign forces.

For example, government-controlled Egyptian television reran a show in which a popular TV-personality cleric, Sheik Mohammed Metwalli Sharawi, talked about how even the prophet Mohammed, father of the Moslem faith, relied on non-believers for arms and for guidance through the desert. And when Western news reports recently seemed fixated on the U.S.-Soviet summit in Helsinki, Finland, Cairo radio led its newscasts with a statement by President Bush calling the deployment of U.S. troops in the Gulf "temporary."

President Bush himself appeared to go out of his way yesterday to defuse Arab concern about the U.S.'s long-term intentions. In speaking to reporters about U.S. forces in Saudi Arabia, Mr. Bush said: "I want those soldiers out as soon as possible. I want them all out -- all out, period." But the president then added: "And yet, I can't say when that will be."

In a further sign of its struggle to present a united front, the Egyptian government last week barred several journalists and opposition party members who object to the presence of foreign troops in Saudi Arabia from traveling to Jordan for a conference with Arabs sympathetic to Iraqi President Saddam Hussein.

Arabs' deep-seated disdain for foreign military involvement in the region is rooted in sour memories of foreign domination by the Turks, French and British and fears that foreign occupiers will pursue their own special interests, such as ensuring the flow of Middle Eastern oil to the outside world.

"It also touches on something very deep in the heart of Moslems," says Ibrahim Shoukry, an opposition leader whose party is allied with the religious Moslem Brotherhood movement and who was one of those barred from going to Jordan. "Jews {in the U.S. armed forces} will be defending the Islamic holy land."

At least among moderate Arab intellectuals, discussions are beginning not just on the need for regional security arrangements but also on new long-term economic, political and social arrangements. "If the Arabs don't come up with some solutions, others will come up with something for them," warns a leading political analyst, Ali Dessouki of Cairo University. "Arabs have to get their acts together."
