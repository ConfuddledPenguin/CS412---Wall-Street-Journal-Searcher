World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Fall 2.14% as Buying Interest Cools; London Shares Erase Early Losses and End Mixed

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/18/90

Tokyo stock prices retreated broadly in thin dealings Monday with many investors on the sidelines because of a fall in bond prices and continuing uncertainty about the Mideast situation, traders said. In London, stocks closed mixed after erasing early losses on the back of a good start on Wall Street and a late buying spurt in London.

Tokyo's Nikkei index of 225 issues was down 531.86 points, or 2.14%, at 24365.60.

At the end of the morning session Tuesday in Tokyo, the Nikkei index fell 932.53 to 23433.07.

First-section volume Monday was estimated at 300 million shares, down from 546 million shares on Friday. Decliners outnumbered gainers 822 to 144, and 125 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section fell 35.49 points, or 1.87%, to 1860.19.

The second section index was down 42.67 points, or 1.19%, to 3544.15. Volume in the section was estimated at five million shares, down from 6.6 million shares Friday.

"Investors' buying interest was completely cooled off," said Yoshihiro Ito, general manager of stock trading at Okasan Securities.

As bond prices fell through the morning session and failed to recover during the rest of the day, buying interest in the stock market dried up, Mr. Ito said. He said investors fear stock prices may drop below the year's previous closing low of 23737.63 posted Aug. 23.

"There is no perception among participants that {domestic} interest rates have peaked out. That means there is no perception that stock prices have bottomed out," Mr. Ito said.

Such fears were reflected by the sharp falls of stock index futures Monday as institutions sold futures for short hedging, traders said.

The index of the December futures contracts "remained below the cash index. That shows there are strong expectations that stock prices will decline," said Shigeru Akiba, head of equity at UBS Phillips & Drew International.

Hiroyuki Murai, general manager of stock trading at Nikko Securities, said stock prices were pressured down mainly because of a small amount of selling by special money accounts that will close their books Thursday for midyear accounting. Because it takes four trading days to process transactions, Monday was the last trading day for these accounts, the official said, predicting less volatile trading for the rest of the week.

Mr. Akiba also sees the stock market in a lull this week, with the Nikkei index hovering from 24000 to 25000 points.

Mr. Akiba said what he fears most is dumping by individual investors or a heavy sell-off by investment trust funds as the result of orders by investors to close their trust fund accounts. But judging from Monday's trading, "neither may happen after all," he said.

Strong advances for stocks aren't expected, however.

Mr. Ito of Okasan Securities noted that the majority of institutional investors probably will take every opportunity to sell whenever stock prices rise to profitable levels. Expectations of such strong selling pressure discourages individual investors from buying, he said.

Many sectors were sold Monday, including large-capital shares such as those of steel makers, shipbuilding companies and electric-power companies, as well as such small-capital shares as high-priced, high-technology concerns.

Higher interest rates prompted further selling of interest rate sensitive large-capital shares. NKK was down 20 yen to 455 yen (15 cents to $3.32), while Tokyo Electric Power fell 120 yen to 3180 yen.

Among high-tech shares, Kyocera fell 150 yen to 6850 yen, Sony declined 140 yen to 6860 yen, and TDK was down 200 yen at 5400 yen.

Construction companies and car makers also ended lower, with Kajima dropping 20 yen to 1360 yen and Toyota Motor down 10 yen to 1900 yen.

In London, traders said that U.K. company interim results and economic figures all due this week are contributing factors to a nervous market in which volume levels remain at record lows.

"Money does want to come into the market but investors are just bidding at lower levels," one trader said. At the current low prices, traders spotted only selective buying across the board, although it wasn't sufficient to raise volumes to even moderate levels.

A good start on Wall Street late in London trading session generated buying activity there. "With such a nervous market anything can move it," one trader said.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-Share Index edged up 0.5 point to 2094.3. The Financial Times 30-Share Index closed down 3.5 points at 1606.1. Volume was 286.6 million shares, well below Friday's 418 million shares.

Among several companies reporting interim results, auction house Christie's International posted a 17% rise in pretax profit, but warned that the prospects for the second half were unlikely to be as favorable. The shares ended a penny lower at 257 pence ($4.87).

Hawker Siddeley, which recorded a 44% drop in pretax profit on the back of a poor long-term outlook, had a 44 pence slide to 429 pence.

Industrial services and marketing group Inchcape lost 8 pence to 221 pence after posting a rise in pretax profit of 5.2% for the period.

Dalgety, a food and agribusiness concern, was unchanged at 356 pence after reporting a 7% rise in pretax profit.

Elsewhere, international employment services concern Manpower, which reported only a marginal improvement in pretax profit for the half year, closed down 1/2 penny at 58 pence.

Retailing concern Storehouse, which sold two major units, ended 2 pence higher at 130 pence.

Shares closed lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Brussels, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Milan and Madrid. South African gold stocks closed marginally higher.

Stocks closed lower in Sydney, Hong Kong, Singapore, Seoul and Manila, and higher in Taipei and Wellington.
