Chemical Closes Swiss Unit, Related Tokyo Subsidiary

  • Author: Glenn Whitney Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/18/90

ZURICH -- Chemical Banking Corp. is closing its Geneva unit due to insufficient profitability. Chemical was one of the most active market-makers in Swiss franc bonds but high local interest rates and the collapse of the Tokyo stock market have made the bank's activities in the Swiss market harder to justify. With Chemical's departure, the so-called "grey market" loses a key player.

The decision to close down the operation was part of the U.S. banking group's strategy to "focus only on activities where maximum profitability can be achieved," says Francois Larsen, director of the Geneva unit, called Chemical N.Y. Capital Market Corp.

At the same time, Chemical announced the closure of Chemical Securities Co. in Tokyo, which concentrated on the marketing and trading of Japanese equities and equity-linked bonds and was under the direction of the Geneva office. The Tokyo unit has a Japanese securities license.

Mr. Larsen says the Geneva operations had been "sometimes profitable, sometimes not" in the past year as increased volatility of financial markets, especially in Tokyo, made business more difficult.

He also says the capital market's group found it increasingly difficult to market its services after selling its Geneva private banking subsidiary in November 1988 to the French financial firm Dumenil-Leble S.A., Mr. Larsen said.

"Institutional and private banking is the essence of business in Switzerland," Mr. Larsen says. "It's very difficult to deal in securities without the backing of captive customers."

Chemical is also closing its foreign exchange and money market operations in Geneva, leaving a seven-person cash management branch office of the New York-based bank in Zurich as its sole Swiss presence.

The 55 Geneva-based employees, including 15 traders and salesmen, will be dismissed after clearing operations are complete, probably in the next three months. Trading formally ended yesterday. The Tokyo unit's approximately 30 employees will be relocated elsewhere in Chemical's Japan operations, Mr. Larsen says.

Patrick Voegeli, head trader at the bank, says two factors hurt profitability most in the past year: high short-term Swiss interest rates, which made it very expensive to finance bond positions, and sharp drops on the Tokyo stock exchange, which made Swiss investors reluctant to trade Japanese equity-linked instruments.

New issuance of Japanese equity-linked paper is down by almost 50% in the first eight months of the year compared with the same period in 1989.
