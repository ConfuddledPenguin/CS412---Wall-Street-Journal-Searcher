Letters to the Editor: Crude Reason for Hike In Airlines' Fares

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/18/90

Your Aug. 16 "Tracking Travel" article stated, "Airlines have been doing a lot of howling recently about rising fuel costs. But a look at those costs suggests they may not be quite so bad." Rising jet-fuel costs are very bad for the U.S. airline industry and I believe a few facts should demonstrate that point clearly. Airline jet-fuel costs are essentially a fixed percentage of per-barrel crude-oil prices. That percentage for the past several years has held at 3.5%. Thus, before the recent Middle Eastern crisis, crude oil at $18 a barrel brought about 63 cents a gallon for jet fuel. If the current fuel crisis places crude-oil prices at, say, $25 a barrel, U.S. airlines will pay about 88 cents a gallon for fuel, a rise of 25 cents a gallon. These increases occur immediately; there are no long-term price contracts anymore, not even 30-day contracts. Every one-cent rise in jet fuel increases airline expenses by $160 million annually. A rise of 25 cents raises the jet-fuel costs $4 billion annually.

I consider that quite bad, so bad that even as the airlines raise their fares about 5.3%, which is not enough, to try and recover these increased fuel costs, their profitability outlook diminishes. It moves from an earlier $500 million operating profit for 1990 to a $1 billion operating loss.

George W. James

Chairman

Airline Economics Inc.

Washington

You state that travel-agent commissions account for 13% of every travel dollar, basing this on a Runzheimer International analysis of a New York-Los Angeles sample trip.

The 13% is highly overstated. On domestic tickets, airlines pay 9.26% commissions based on consumer cost (they pay 10% of the base fare, which is less than consumer cost because of an 8% tax). Car-rental companies pay only 5% on most rentals, and even less on corporate rates. The standard commission paid by U.S. hotels is 10%. In addition, it is frequently difficult if not impossible to collect commissions from hotel and car-rental companies.

I know of no way to average 9.26, 5 and 10 and get 13. Did the Runzheimer analysts learn the old "new math"?

Hedy Knoth

Travel Consultant

Destinations Inc.

Wilmington, Del.
