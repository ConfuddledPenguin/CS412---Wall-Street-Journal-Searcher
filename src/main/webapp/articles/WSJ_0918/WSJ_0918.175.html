Abreast of the Market: Stocks End Mixed in Slow Trading; Oil-Price Rise Boosts Energy Issues

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 09/18/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices closed mixed in sluggish trading, but ended the session on an upswing as another surge in the price of oil rekindled buying interest in the energy sector. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 3.22 to 2567.33, but gains in Exxon, Chevron and Texaco more than accounted for the advance; the performance of the three oil stocks lifted the average by nearly six points.

Broader market indexes also posted modest gains. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index went up 0.94 to 317.77, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index rose 0.47 to 174.62 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 0.79 to 294.40.

But declining issues led advancers on the Big Board by 768 to 681, and traders said the market remained plagued by uncertainty regarding the Persian Gulf situation and the deficit-reduction talks.

"Nobody wants to make any commitments for fear that they're going to be wrong tomorrow," said James C. Andrews, first vice president in charge of institutional trading at Janney Montgomery Scott in Philadelphia.

Only 110,600,000 shares were traded, down from 133,390,000 shares Friday. Concerns about today's reports on the August consumer price index and the July trade deficit also kept investors out of the market, traders said.

Both reports are expected to reflect the impact of higher oil prices, even though the July trade data was collected before Iraq's invasion of Kuwait.

The CPI is expected to show an increase of 0.8%, with prices excluding food and energy up 0.4%, according to a consensus of economists polled by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report. The July trade deficit is expected to widen to $7.3 billion from $5.1 billion in June, with a rise in oil imports accounting for the move.

Although analysts have cited oil prices as a key influence on the market recently, stocks declined only briefly in the latest session as crude-oil futures surged to the highest levels in New York trading since being introduced in 1983. October futures ended the session at $33.63 a barrel, up $1.87.

Jon Groveman, president of Ladenburg, Thalmann & Co., said the previous surges in oil prices blunted the impact of the latest advance on the market. "A lot of people who wanted to react to that {rally} already have done so," Mr. Groveman says.

Scattered bargain-hunting by institutional investors helped the market recover its initial losses, traders said. Stocks then proceeded to spend most of the session drifting within tight trading ranges; buying in the final hour pushed prices to session highs.

Chevron advanced 1 3/4 to 77 5/8, while Texaco rose 7/8 to 65 3/8 and Exxon gained 1/4 to 51 5/8. Among other oil stocks, Mobil added 1 to 66 1/2, Amoco added 1 1/8 to 58 3/8, Unocal rose 1 to 33 1/4 and Atlantic Richfield climbed 1 3/4 to 141 5/8. Within the oilfield-services sector, Halliburton rallied 1 1/4 to 55 7/8, Schlumberger went up 1/4 to 66 1/2 and Parker Drilling jumped 3/4 to 8 3/4.

Similarly, precious-metals stocks took their direction from gold prices; October gold futures rose $4.20 an ounce, to $390.60 in New York trading. Homestake Mining went up 5/8 to 20 1/2, Newmont Mining rose 7/8 to 43 7/8 and Placer Dome added 1/4 to 17 3/8.

Digital Equipment, which fell 9 7/8 last week as the company cautioned against expecting any quick turnaround in its fortunes, rebounded 1/4 to 56 5/8 in composite trading of 1.6 million shares. But Digital traded as low as 54 during the session, and The Wall Street Journal reported in yesterday's "Heard on the Street" column that few analysts are willing to bet it has hit bottom.

International Business Machines gained 3/4 to 105 1/8. IBM and Microsoft are expected to realign some of their responsibilities for the development of the OS/2 operating system for personal computers, which has flopped so far, the Journal reported.

Unisys went down 1/4 to 7 7/8 in active trading, and other key technology stocks also headed lower. Hewlett-Packard dropped 3/8 to 32 3/4, Compaq Computer dipped 1/2 to 43 5/8, Cray Research slipped 3/4 to 34 5/8 and Control Data lost 7/8 to 11 5/8.

UAL slipped 1 5/8 to 97 3/8. A group of unions at its United Airlines unit is likely to abandon its most recent buy-out offer -- valued at $201 a share, or $4.38 billion -- and may submit another, lower bid, the Journal reported.

McDonnell Douglas fell 1 7/8 to 50. Smith Barney, Harris Upham downgraded its investment rating on the stock to "avoid" from "hold."

American Home Products gained 3/4 to 48. As expected, the company reached an agreement to help MicroGeneSys in developing VaxSyn HIV-1, an experimental AIDS vaccine. Of three vaccines now under intense study, VaxSyn has undergone the most advanced human tests.

U.S. Surgical jumped 2 5/8 to 56 1/2. Salomon Brothers upgraded its rating on the stock, which the firm expects to outperform the market, and increased its earnings estimates for 1990, 1991 and 1992 by 10 cents, 20 cents and 70 cents a share -- to $1.65, $2.10 and $3, respectively.

Capital Holding rose 1/4 to 35. The company said it plans to repurchase up to two million common shares, including about 550,000 shares to be bought through its employee pension fund.

Apple Bancorp, which dropped its opposition to a $38-a-share takeover bid from New York real estate investor Stanley Stahl, tacked on 3/8 to 33 1/8. The bank holding company said it is now taking a neutral position on the offer.

Paired certificates of Hotel Investors Trust and Hotel Investors Corp. gained 5/8 to 3 1/2. First West Capital is offering $5 each for the securities in a takeover offer.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.26 to 321.71. Volume totaled 9,810,000 shares.
