Monday's Markets: Oil Price Jump Again Hurts Dollar, Bonds

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/18/90

NEW YORK -- Soaring oil prices prolonged the dollar's slide and depressed bonds again, but blue-chip stocks managed to wiggle a little higher. The October crude-oil futures contract jumped $1.87 to $33.63 a barrel, the highest close since these futures started trading in 1983. The dollar plunged in late New York trading to 1.5480 marks from 1.5710, and eased to 136.55 yen from 136.95 Friday. Traders said yen were being sold to buy marks.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average edged up 3.22 points in quiet trading to 2567.33. However, declining issues outnumbered advancers, 768 to 681, on the New York Stock Exchange. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices eased, also in very light activity, in response to oil's advance.

Traders bought currencies of countries that export oil and have high interest rates. "The investment currencies are back in vogue," said Robert White, manager of corporate foreign-exchange trading at First Interstate Bank in Los Angeles, referring to the British pound and the Canadian and Australian dollars. Interest rates are higher in all three countries, which also are oil exporters.

Mr. White said he was surprised that the dollar drew no strength from news-service headlines early in the day that the U.S. State Department viewed Iraq's conduct in Kuwait as ominous. "The currency market is determined to take the (U.S.) dollar down," he said.

Sal Gilbertie, oil futures trader at Merrill Lynch, said oil rose because the likelihood of Mideast war seemed to be reinforced by the Iraqi embassy violations and subsequent saber rattling. In the oil futures market, "traders are chanting, `$39 (a barrel) before $29,'" he said.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose. Big Board volume was 110.6 million shares.

Bond prices fell 3/8 of a point, or $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 9.06%.
