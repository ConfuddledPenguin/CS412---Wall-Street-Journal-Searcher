Cordless Phones Migrate From Pools to Offices

WIRELESS telephone and data systems are about to invade the office.

Several North American and European communications makers are readying systems for the corporate market that will eliminate costly wiring and allow workers to keep in touch when they are wandering around an office building or a factory floor.

The Federal Communications Commission has asked manufacturers to submit ideas for office wireless systems, including compatibility standards. The agency also has approved many applications from start-up companies for radio-based voice and data-transmission systems. Most of these units will encode each signal to prevent overlapping voices and use "spread spectrum" equipment to scatter the signals over numerous frequencies, increasing system capacity.

Sweden's LM Ericsson has begun selling a phone system in Europe that uses radio-frequency transmission to link tiny phones that can fit in shirt pockets. Engineers at Northern Telecom of Canada are working on several radio-based systems that can be attached to conventional phone systems to add wireless service.

American Telephone & Telegraph, New York, created a stir recently when it introduced a version of its Merlin office-phone system with cordless handsets. Still, only a handful of phones can work off a single AT&T system, and the base of each phone must be wired to the main switch. AT&T says its engineers are developing "hybrid" systems that will appear in a couple of years that combine wired and radio technology.

CEOs Can Give Snoops

An Earful of Static

MOTOROLA is hoping to cash in on businesses' vulnerability to high-tech crime with its secure telephone technology.

The Schaumburg, Ill., company, which already sells phones to the government that encrypt voice signals, has developed a new unit to market the devices to businesses whose top executives want to protect secret recipes or competitive battle plans.

Motorola's phone, called SECTEL, uses a microprocessor to digitalize voice signals and turn them into a special code that can only be decoded by a similar phone on the other end. The chip also encodes data-communications signals when a personal computer is plugged into the back of the phone. A user pushes a special button on the phone that changes it from a normal phone to a "secure" one. After that, an eavesdropper would hear only static.

Motorola has sold more than 85,000 secure phones to the Pentagon and other government agencies, but the corporate market for its $3,000-plus phone could prove tougher. Robert Wade, product marketing manager for Motorola's Secure Telecommunications Business unit, said test-marketing in San Francisco and Dallas drew fewer than 30 inquiries from potential customers last month.

Freeway Dialers May Get

A Lift From Chips Soon

CELLULAR phone systems around the country are poised for a much-needed capacity boost from digital technology in the next couple of years. But will all the phones, old and new, be able to talk to each other over the upgraded airwaves?

AT&T and San Diego-based Qualcomm are designing a set of electronic chips that can be installed in cellular phone systems to allow future digital phones, including pocket-sized models, to communicate over networks that employ two different kinds of digital-transmission technology.

Dale Stone, department head of cellular system engineering at AT&T Bell Laboratories, says the chip set should allow phones to switch automatically between "time-division multiple access" networks and higher-capacity "code-division multiple access" networks. Many industry experts believe that CDMA equipment will be the basis for future systems that accommodate tens of millions of pocket phones used by pedestrians, while TDMA will be used to handle vehicle-based cellular phones.

The new chip set will get a big tryout in late 1991 when AT&T and Qualcomm install a network for pocket-phone calling in Manhattan for Nynex's mobile communications unit.

One Bin for Voice Mail,

Electronic Mail and Faxes

NOW that "Fax it to me" is firmly fixed in the corporate lexicon, facsimile developers are seeking new uses for the technology.

A potentially huge market will be hybrid products that bundle document-transmission technology with electronic-mail and voice-mail systems, according to a report from Probe Research Inc., a Cedar Knolls, N.J., firm that follows the communications and computer markets.

Probe expects 1991 to be the year when voice/fax products begin to take off. By 1995, Probe predicts that 14% of all voice-mail and voice-response systems, a $2 billion-plus industry, will contain integrated fax equipment for storing and retrieving "fax mail."

One company, Brooktrout Technology, Wellesley, Mass., recently landed a patent for its method of blending the technologies into a single system. It makes a voice/fax system that the Weather Fax, Maynard, Mass., uses to turn National Weather Service information into charts, which are then faxed to a caller who punches in the command on a pushbutton phone.

SpectraFAX, Naples, Fla., makes similar voice/fax systems.
