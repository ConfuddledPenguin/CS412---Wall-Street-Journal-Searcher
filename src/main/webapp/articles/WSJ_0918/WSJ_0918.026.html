Letters to the Editor: Accounting Change Drove Pension Cost

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/18/90

Your Aug. 14 Heard on the Street, "Pension Expense May Drag Down '90s Corporate Profits," missed the biggest single reason for fluctuation in pension expense from 1982 to 1989. A change in accounting method propelled companies toward low pension expense with an inevitable uptrend of pension expense in years following the adoption of the new method. The Financial Accounting Standards Board Statement No. 87 required adoption of this new policy in fiscal 1987, though many companies adopted in 1986 and a few in 1985. It is easy to see from the chart in the article the enormous drop in 1986 expense for companies that found it advantageous to adopt FAS No. 87 early. Virtually every client of ours that forecast pension expense at the time saw an immediate substantial decrease in pension expense followed by a gradually increasing expense in future years.

The statement required acceleration of gains in the market value of pension assets that had accumulated from the late 1970s and the 1980s. For most companies the rules also required a less conservative (lower) measurement of liability accompanied by less conservative actuarial assumptions. The analyst you quoted commented that pension costs cannot go to zero. In fact, many public companies have experienced negative pension expense -- positive pension income -- in recent years, mostly due to this accounting change.

The rules also allowed accumulated benefits of the change in policy to be recognized in one year if liabilities were settled with a third party such as an insurance company. This approach was used widely by banks to offset losses due to write-down of nonperforming loans, as well as by other companies that were capturing surplus pension assets through reversions.

Your conclusion appears correct. Pension expense will increase in the future and may drag down 1990's corporate profits. Given the FASB requirements, this should not be a surprise to analysts.

Roger P. Ingenthron

Principal

William M. Mercer Inc.

St. Louis
