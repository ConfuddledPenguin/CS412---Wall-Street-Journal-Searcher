Pentagon Seeks Authority to Disburse Foreign Contributions to Mideast Effort

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 09/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The Pentagon is seeking sweeping authority to accept and disburse, without future congressional approval, billions of dollars in aid from foreign governments to help pay for the cost of U.S. troops in the Mideast. New legislation backed by the Defense Department is proposed as part of a nearly $1.89 billion supplemental appropriations bill sent to Congress by the Bush administration. The measure, which must be rushed through before the end of the current fiscal year which ends Sept. 30, would help cover initial costs of the operation and carries a second provision sought by the State Department to forgive Egypt's $6.7 billion military debt.

Both issues face challenges in Congress, and new estimates within the Congressional Budget Office indicate the cost of canceling Egypt's debt will be more than $2.4 billion over five years, or more than twice the $1 billion figure given by the administration.

Though it has received little attention so far, the prospect of foreign governments financing a major portion of a U.S. military operation poses unprecedented problems for Congress, which relies on its control of the purse as a check on the executive branch and its war-making authority.

Both sides in the debate hope to avoid an outright confrontation but, absent approval of the proposed legislation, the Defense Department has signaled it may invoke a little-known statute empowering the Treasury to accept gifts for defense purposes. Adopted in 1954, the law was designed to foster patriotism and to facilitate gifts from American citizens, but no specific limitation exists to bar its application to the current situation.

"I would like updated legislation but the exisiting legislation works," said Terrence O'Donnell, the Pentagon's general counsel.

Preliminary estimates by the Pentagon indicate foreign donations could cover as much as $7 billion of the $15 billion in costs over the next year for the "Desert Shield" operation.

The new legislation, unlike the 1954 law, removes the Treasury and General Services Administration from the process and gives the Pentagon its own gift account through which money could be moved without specific appropriation or authorization by Congress.

Mr. O'Donnell said expenditures would be reported to lawmakers and subject to existing legislative limitations, but Congress would clearly give up leverage it now enjoys over spending.

By a simple majority vote in both houses today, members can withhold money as they did in the Vietnam War or the more recent Nicaragua conflict. But in order to put any curb on the use of foreign support for the present operation, opposition lawmakers would have to be prepared to command two-thirds majorities in both chambers to override a veto.

Mr. O'Donnell said the monies, once allocated by the Pentagon, would merge with existing appropriations accounts for operations or personnel, for example, but would be exempt from any across-the-board Gramm-Rudman cuts. The specific use of the funds would be guided by the stated intentions of the donor, he said. But lawmakers are concerned that this gives broad discretion to the executive branch, which has the most direct relationship and leverage over the foreign governments and leaders involved.

"Neither branch of government, the legislative or executive, should be able to combine the power of the sword and the power of the purse," said Senate Armed Services Committee Chairman Sam Nunn (D., Ga.) in a warning last week. "The founding fathers did not want that to happen because that was the way kings operated."

Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell (D., Maine) said, "Serious concern exists about accountability of third-party contributions to the government. In the case of contributions in support of Desert Shield, the Congress intends to insure full accountability and oversight over the expenditure on the funds."

The separate dispute over Egypt's military debt centers less on the $6.7 billion in capital than on interest costs. The new CBO estimates are significant as a measure of the long-term impact on deficit projections, but the more immediate impact may be to encourage other nations to seek relief as well.

The State Department has sought to blunt this pressure by arguing that Egypt is a special case since the U.S. has no chance of recovering the debt. As the cost estimates are revised upwards, however, the result is that other countries such as Turkey may argue for comparable consideration on their military financing.
