Nintendo Tries to Zap Game Rentals by Video Stores

  • Author: Michael W. Miller
  • Date: 09/18/90

Nintendo Co. is trying to stomp a new enemy that's sprouting up faster than the dread Micro-Goombas in Super Mario Brothers 3: video stores that rent Nintendo games. With game prices in the range of $40 to $50, rental has become an increasingly popular way for Nintendo addicts to get a quick fix. Game rentals make up an estimated 5% to 15% of the revenue of the nation's roughly 25,000 video stores, up from virtually nothing three years ago.

Nintendo, based in Kyoto, Japan, and the companies that make games for the Nintendo system are lobbying Congress for a law to ban game rental, which the House Judiciary Committee is scheduled to vote on today. They argue that the rental boom is cutting into Nintendo game sales, which they project will total 70 million units this year.

Both sides can claim legal precedent thanks to the patchwork of federal regulations covering rental of published works. For instance, videos can be rented whereas records cannot. Congress seems likely to pass a bill that would ban the rental of personal-computer software. Game makers point out that some titles are available both for PCs and Nintendo machines, and say it makes no sense to ban rental of one format and not the other.

The video-store industry argues that movie rentals haven't damaged the movie business, despite Hollywood's anguished predictions that they would. Even though video stores typically don't pay royalties on rentals, the movie industry makes huge revenue from sales to the stores.

In fact, some video-store owners say they ultimately help Nintendo and the game-makers, who also don't earn royalties from rentals. "I sell games mainly through rentals -- people come in here, try a game, like it, and buy it," says Franklin Sellars, owner of Videotape Home Systems Inc., a Pasadena, Texas, store.

Nintendo and its allies deride this argument. They say kids are renting games over and over again, not just once to try them out. "Consumers can obtain complete use and enjoyment of many video games through rental without purchasing them, creating lost sales to the copyright owner," said Bruce Davis, chairman of gamemaker Mediagenic, in testimony to Congress this summer.

The video-store lobby argues that the rationale for anti-rental laws should be to prevent copying. It is easy for consumers to copy records and PC software, but there is no simple way to duplicate a Nintendo game (although Nintendo has been circulating advertisements for a game-copying device marketed overseas by a Taiwanese concern, Baelih Co.).

The fight over game rentals has emerged as the most contentious part of the broader bill to ban PC software rental. That's still a tiny business, but the software industry wants to be sure it doesn't explode the way video rental has.

In the early stages of the bill, the video-store industry swiftly pushed through a clause exempting video games from the ban. Nintendo fought back, but the exemption survived in the version that passed the full Senate in May and a House subcommittee last week.

This summer, Rep. Joe Barton (R., Texas) proposed a compromise bill, backed by Nintendo, that would permit rental one year after a video game's release. The Nintendo camp compared this arrangement to a movie hitting the video stores after it has had a chance to play in theaters.

Video-store owners were able to shoot down that version of the bill. The first year of release "is precisely when a video game is most popular," testified Thomas Carton, general counsel of the giant Blockbuster Entertainment Corp. video-store chain. "A one-year ban on rentals is not really a `compromise'; it would gut the video game rental industry."

The tradition of American law favors the video-store owners in this fight. In general, the "first sale doctrine" holds that the buyer of a piece of property can dispose of it however he likes, through sale, rental or almost any other means.

The record industry, however, successfully persuaded Congress six years ago to overrule this doctrine and ban record rentals.

The PC software industry appears to have a consensus in Congress that it deserves the same special treatment -- with or without a clause banning video-game rentals. Nevertheless, even apart from the Nintendo debate, the industry has run into tremendous problems defining what it means by "software."

The bill that passed the Senate and originally went before the House made an exception for video games, but proposed to ban rental of any other computer program, including any tape, disk, or other medium embodying such a program.

This summer, some legislators and executives grew alarmed that the bill's language appeared to cover the millions of microchips in consumer and industrial products that have programs embedded in them. For instance, Hertz Corp., the car-rental giant, protested that the bill might cover the microchips in automobiles.

The measure was rewritten last week to exclude software "embodied in a machine or product and which cannot be copied during the ordinary operation or use of that machine or product."
