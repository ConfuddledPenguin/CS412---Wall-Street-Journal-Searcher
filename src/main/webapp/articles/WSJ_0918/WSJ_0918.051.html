REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): A Splendid Little War

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/18/90

"As we move from the Cold War to the trade war, there is. . . ," -- Senator Ernest Hollings, (D., S.C.) Sept. 9, 1990

This space has often warned about trade war, but now we learn that Senator Hollings has already declared one. He's even planned his own protectionist Sarajevo, the "Textile, Apparel and Footwar Trade Act of 1990." If war results, at least we'll know where to find the assassins.

They'll sit in the U.S. Congress. The Senate has already passed (68-32) the textile bill, and the House will take its turn today. Members no doubt figure it's a cheap vote that won't matter because President Bush is certain to veto. But coming when it does, amid the Iraq crisis and at endgame in the Uruguay Round of global-trade talks, the textile bill is a shot heard 'round the world. The last trade war, which the U.S. did much to start with the Smoot-Hawley Tariff, was called the Great Depression.

Yet today's protectionists are going all-out to override a veto. The Cotton Lobby, for one, is gripped with war fever. "Second phase of telephone blitz campaign is aimed at generating calls to 80 congressional districts considered crucial to House passage of bill and possible veto override," reports the lobby's newsletter. In addition, Reps. Marilyn Lloyd of Tennessee and Newt Gingrich of Georgia have generously "appeared for satellite media tour to generate additional support throughout country." And "ads began running in USA Today and other newspapers."

The industry can afford all this because it's already the most coddled in the U.S. Cotton growers benefit from a quota that limits raw-cotton imports to one day's consumption each year. This virtual ban means U.S. companies pay more for cotton than the rest of the world. It's also the reason many big clothing makers oppose this bill; they can't get the cotton they need at competitive prices.

Worse, textile and apparel makers are already protected by a cartel known as the Multifiber Arrangement. Under the MFA, the U.S. has negotiated more than 1,000 quotas with 38 countries. About 75% of all textile and apparel imports are already covered by quotas, and the average tariff is nearly 20% (compared with 4% on all manufactured goods). A nice monopoly, if you can get it.

But the industry wants more. The bill would force the U.S. to tear up those 38 treaties and impose a global quota of 1% growth a year. This will save a few textile jobs for a while, but at a cost of some $70,000 a job a year. The payers will be consumers who cough up more for shirts, towels, blue jeans, socks. According to economist William Cline, the total cost would run to more than $500 billion through the year 2000.

All of this for an industry that isn't even suffering. Textile employment actually rose from 1985 to 1989, while fiber consumption by the industry jumped to an all-time record last year. Exports rose by 27% and capacity use was close to 90%. Unemployment in the states that would benefit the most from the bill -- the Carolinas, other parts of the South -- is actually lower than the national average. Liberals could be denouncing this transfer of wealth from poor to the rich, but their loyalty to unions counts for more than equity.

The timing of this bill also suggests malice toward the global economic system; a trade war would make everyone poorer, as it did in the 1930s. Also, the textile bill is in particular a stick in the eye of many countries President Bush has adeptly persuaded to join an alliance against Iraq. Turks and Egyptians can fight next to Americans in the Mideast, but they can't sell their shoes in Chicago.

In a recent letter to House Speaker Foley, Secretary of State Baker wrote, "This legislation would compound the problems being faced by a number of countries which have stood with us in the Gulf crisis despite the economic losses they are incurring." The fact that his trade war might help us lose a real war doesn't seem to bother Senator Hollings.

The world is more economically entwined than ever, but Congress and the textile lobby seem to think this is the mercantilist 17th century. The textile bill is a warning shot to the Bush White House not to return from the Uruguay Round of trade talks with more liberal rules for textiles. Laying aside the effects on the world economy or the Middle Eastern hostilities, a collapse at Uruguay will harm efficient U.S. exporters -- farmers, high-tech companies, Boeing workers. The Honorable Members don't seem to care; every war has its casualties.
