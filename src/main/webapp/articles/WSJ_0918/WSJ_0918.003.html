Thrift Regulators Dealt a Setback In Oregon Case

  • Author: Charles McCoy
  • Date: 09/18/90

A federal judge in Portland, Ore., dealt regulators another setback in their increasingly troubled effort to enforce thrift reforms mandated by last year's savings and loan bailout bill. As expected, Judge Owen Panner issued a permanent injunction that bars regulators from nullifying capital forebearances and other breaks regulators granted to Portland-based Far West Federal Bank in 1988. The breaks exempted the thrift from normal accounting and capital standards and have enabled it to stay in business; without those exemptions, Far West would have negative tangible capital of $270 million, making it one of the most deeply insolvent thrifts in the country.

The bailout bill invalidated such forebearances and accounting gimmicks and called for stricter accounting and capital standards, in hopes of avoiding the kind of financial legerdemain that helped swell the cost of the U.S. thrift debacle to its present estimate of $500 billion. Regulators informed Far West early this year that they would wipe out the thrift's special treatment, instantly rendering it insolvent. Far West sued in January to block that move.

Judge Panner's latest ruling was his court's final say in that lawsuit. It wasn't a surprise because it echoed his previous rulings, in granting Far West temporary and permanent restraining orders last spring, that federal regulators could not renege on their previous promises of forebearance.

Nonetheless, the ruling underscores the intensifying legal muddle surrounding the bailout. Earlier this month, a federal judge in Kansas ruled that regulators did not have the right to seize Franklin Savings Association of Ottawa, Kansas, an unorthodox thrift that did little lending and focused instead on complex investments in mortgage-backed securities and hedging instruments. That judge ruled that regulators were incompetent to understand Franklin's high-finance strategies and ordered the thrift returned to the control of its controversial chairman, Ernest Fleischer. However, the government won a stay of that order while its appeal is pending.

In other cases, courts have held that regulators can renege on capital forebearances similar to those granted Far West. The legal twists and turns have stalled regulators' efforts to overhaul the industry and eliminate practices that in the past have led to huge losses.

A spokesman for the Office of Thrift Supervision said it would appeal Judge Panner's decision in the Far West case. Ultimately, lawyers believe the Supreme Court will be asked to determine what new powers regulators have under the thrift bailout bill.
