Commodore Savings Former Executives Convicted of Fraud

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/18/90

DALLAS -- A U.S. District Court jury convicted two former executives of defunct Commodore Savings Association of bank fraud charges in connection with a 1984 plan to divert $1 million of Commodore funds to an affiliate through a sham real estate transaction. Former Commodore President Woodrow O. Brownlee and John W. Harrell, former vice chairman and chief executive officer, were each convicted of one count of misappropriation of funds and one count of making false statements. Each count carries a maximum sentence of five years in prison. Sentencing is scheduled for Oct. 30.

A 1989 trial on the same charges had ended in a mistrial.

The executives were accused of arranging the illegal transfer of $1 million of Commodore funds to National Mortgage Corp. of America, a financially troubled company owned by Commodore Chairman Robert H. Hopkins Jr. They also were accused of causing Commodore to spend an additional $1 million to channel the funds to National Mortgage and of lying to federal regulators about the transaction.

Mr. Harrell's attorney, William T. Hill, Jr., said his client hadn't decided whether to appeal. Mr. Brownlee's attorney couldn't be reached for comment.

In May 1989, Robert Hopkins, his brother, E. Morten Hopkins and Mr. Harrell were convicted on federal charges in connection with a scheme to hide Commodore's illegal donations to federal political campaigns. Robert Hopkins was sentenced to 15 years in prison in that case and the other two men received six-month prison sentences.
