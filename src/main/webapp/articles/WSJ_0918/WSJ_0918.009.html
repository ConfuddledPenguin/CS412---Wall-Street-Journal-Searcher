Economy: Inventories Rose 0.7% During July; Sales Fell 0.5%

  • Author: Helaine Olen
  • Date: 09/18/90

WASHINGTON -- Business inventories rose 0.7% in July while sales fell 0.5%, the Commerce Department reported, another indication that the economy is vulnerable to recession. Economists have been watching inventory levels closely, fearing that if stockpiles of unsold goods grow too high, businesses will cut back on orders and production. With the economy barely growing, the chain reaction could lead to a recession.

July's rise in inventories followed a 0.5% decline in June.

The figures "are not worrisome in themselves, but taken with other data seen of late they suggest we are dealing with an economy that is fragile," said Robert Dederick, chief economist at Northern Trust Co., Chicago, pointing to recent declines in retail sales and housing starts and an increase in the unemployment rate.

Aubrey Zaffuto, vice president and economist at IBJ Schroder Bank & Trust Co., New York, predicted there will be a cut in production to offset the growing inventory figures. "People are intolerant of small increases in inventories," she said.

Inventories equaled 1.49 months' sales in July, meaning it would take companies just under a month and a half to sell off current stocks. This is up slightly from June's figure of 1.47 months but the same figure as in May and is still considered manageable by most analysts.

Joan Schneider, senior domestic economist at Continental Bank National Trust & Co., Chicago, said she saw "nothing truly alarming" in the July figures. "The inventory-to-sales ratio is up a little from June but it's not out of line from the past year," she said.

Inventories of retailers, wholesalers and manufacturers totaled $801.94 billion in July, up from $796.47 billion in June.

But total sales fell to $539.52 billion from $542.44 billion, the 0.5% decline just about wiping out June's gain of 0.6%.

All the numbers were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
