House Passes Bill Allowing Regulators To Raise Deposit Insurance Premiums

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 09/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The House approved a bill that would allow federal regulators to increase deposit insurance premiums paid by banks and savings and loans to whatever level the regulators saw fit. The House moved with unusual speed to pass the bill, which also would give the regulators freedom to change the premiums whenever they believed it necessary, in the wake of dire reports last week that the bank deposit insurance fund could require taxpayer assistance if a recession struck.

The bill is nearly assured of becoming law because it has the backing of the Bush administration and strong support in the Senate, where it is likely to come up shortly. In the face of that consensus, the banking industry isn't lobbying heavily against it.

Separately, the federal banking agencies settled a year-long dispute over the minimum level of capital -- the cushion against losses that is drained before deposit insurance funds are called in -- banks must hold. The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency acceded to a Federal Reserve Board proposal to set the minimum at $3 for every $100 of assets for banks that receive the highest ratings from their regulators -- about one bank in five. Other banks would be required to hold somewhat more capital.

The deposit-insurance measure passed by voice vote under an expedited procedure. It was sponsored by Banking Committee Chairman Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas) and the committee's ranking Republican, Rep. Chalmers Wylie of Ohio, and had broad bipartisan support.

In a clear sign of Congress's election-season sensitivity that it not be blamed for dawdling if a crisis like the savings and loan mess strikes the banking industry, the bill moved directly to the House floor without any hearings or Banking Committee action. Under the rules that expedited the bill, a two-thirds vote was required for passage, but there appeared to be no opposition and there were only a few congressmen on the House floor when the measure was cleared.

The legislation would allow the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. to set the annual premiums for banks and thrifts as high as it wishes, removing the cap of 32.5 cents per $100 of deposits. Currently the FDIC can raise premiums a maximum of 7.5 cents in any one year. Just a few weeks ago, after expressing fears that continued bank failures could cause a $2 billion loss to the bank insurance fund this year, the agency increased the premiums to 19.5 cents per $100 for 1991 from 12.5 cents in 1990.

If the bill becomes law, the FDIC isn't expected to move to raise the premiums quickly, but the FDIC's chairman, William Seidman, wants the added flexibility.

Edward Yingling, a top official of the American Bankers Association, said the trade group wouldn't fight the legislation but added that "the premiums could quickly reach the point where additional premiums are counterproductive" by draining funds from banks. Another trade group, the National Council of Savings Institutions, noted that "institutions are already experiencing very severe operating budget problems."

On capital requirements, the Comptroller pushed for a 3% minimum capital ratio -- $3 for every $100 of assets -- while the FDIC wanted a 4.5% level that would accept some less tangible forms of capital in the extra 1.5%. Under the Fed rule accepted by the comptroller, banks rated the strongest by regulators would have a 3% floor, while others would be required to hold as much as 5%, determined on an individual basis. The FDIC is expected to pass the Fed's capital rules this afternoon.

Because savings and loan capital requirements by law must be as stringent as those for banks, the Fed's position will affect some thrifts. A spokesman for the Office of Thrift Supervision said it expected that 730 of the nation's 2,450 thrifts won't meet the capital requirements when they go into effect at the end of the year.
