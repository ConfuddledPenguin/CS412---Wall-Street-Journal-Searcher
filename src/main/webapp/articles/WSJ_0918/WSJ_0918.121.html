Exchanges Rush Out New Vehicles For Betting on Tokyo Stock Trends

  • Author: Craig Torres and Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 09/18/90

NEW YORK -- Three major exchanges are rushing out new vehicles that allow investors to bet on the direction of the Japanese stock market. Next week, the Chicago Mercantile Exchange, the Chicago Board of Trade and the American Stock Exchange will offer two new futures contracts and three new options contracts based on Japanese stock indexes. The Amex and the CBOT expect, but are still waiting for, U.S. regulatory approval.

All three exchanges are trying to capitalize on the successful launch earlier this year of warrants on the Nikkei Index of 225 leading stocks on the Tokyo Stock Exchange. Active trading in the warrants, which are traded on the Amex, indicated to exchanges that large and small investors were interested in speculative bets on the world's second-biggest stock market, after the U.S. market.

Most of the warrants were bets on a fall in the Tokyo market, and the first of them came out just before Japanese stocks plunged. From its record high of 38915.87 at the end of last year, the Nikkei index has dropped 37%. Yesterday, the index closed at 24365.60, a mere 2.6% above its low for the year of 23737.63 on Aug. 23.

The warrants irked Japanese officials, who eventually asked the Amex to hold off on listing more warrants. That's one reason why the Amex is moving on to new ways to bet on Tokyo stocks, though there still is resistance from Tokyo exchange officials.

"In Tokyo, firms have been blaming index arbitrage for the volatility in the stock markets," said one Japanese executive familiar with the Tokyo exchange's thinking. The new Amex options, he added, "would just add to that volatility."

Still, Amex Chairman James R. Jones said: "We commented {to Tokyo exchange officials} that if the four products go forward in Chicago, we were going to go forward. They are sensitive and would like none of them to go forward."

Unable to get a license to the Nikkei Index, the Amex developed its own "Japan Index" of 210 Japanese stocks. The Amex boasts that it correlates better than 99% with the Nikkei index.

The Amex, pending approval from the Securities and Exchange Commission, wants to start trading options on the Japan Index next Tuesday. Each morning, the Amex will make public the closing price of its index, based on the performance of the Tokyo market earlier that day. The Amex product is a dollar-denominated index that will trade in values of about 1/100 of the Nikkei; with the Nikkei index at 25,000, the Amex index value would be $250. Options trading on the index are so-called European style, meaning they can be cashed in only on the expiration date.

The Chicago Merc is offering futures contracts on the Nikkei index, and options on the futures. The Merc is also converting its index to a dollar denomination from yen, in which the Nikkei index is normally calculated. The Merc has secured approval from the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, and plans to launch its products next Tuesday.

The CBOT is offering futures on the Tokyo exchange's own Topix index, which is based on the 1,229 companies listed on the first section of the exchange, and options on Topix futures. The CBOT plans to start trading the new products next Thursday.

Only the CBOT products will trade briefly with the Tokyo market in a two-hour-and-fifteen-minute night session. Also, the CBOT's product is denominated in yen. Currently, the CBOT has a night session in U.S. Treasury bonds.

An option generally gives its holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell securities, currencies or in this case the value of an index at a specific price on or before a certain date. Some options, such as the new Amex issue, can be exercised only on their expiration date. Warrants are similar, but are often less flexible than options.

Initially, some money managers and brokers are skeptical about the Amex's Japan Index options. "I don't think European investors, which are some of the most substantial holders of Japanese stocks among non-Japanese, will be willing to trade this new product," said an executive at Japan's Nomura Securities Co. He said the Amex index hasn't any recognition among investors, as do the Nikkei and Topix indexes.

"People will be reluctant to invest," added the Nomura executive. "There's got to be some sort of a super selling point to be successful; I don't think the Japan Index has that."

The Amex declined to comment, but privately exchange officials said the Japanese don't appreciate having their indexes imitated.

Barry M. Gillman, managing director at PCM International, an international money management unit of Prudential Insurance Co. of America in Newark, N.J., also said his firm probably wouldn't be interested in buying the Amex options.

PCM already uses over-the-counter options on the Nikkei average that are sold by big U.S. investment banks to hedge its Japanese stock holdings. Like the warrants, they are American-style options, which allow the holder to exercise the options prior to their expiration.

"We like the flexibility to be able to exercise it at any time," said Mr. Gillman. "And in terms of hedging against market risk, this {the Amex options} doesn't seem to offer any tremendous advantage."

Many Chicago traders say the key to whether the Board of Trade and Merc stock-index futures fly is the response of Japanese traders.

"I think they might be slow to enter when they're already using their own local markets," says Joseph Cole, senior vice president and research director for Indosuez Carr Futures Inc. in Chicago. "You have to ask what the incentive is. I don't necessarily see it." Futures and options on the Topix and Nikkei indexes trade actively in Japan.

At least one small investor was intrigued by the Amex product. Jeffrey Grossman, a real estate consultant in Framingham, Mass., says he made money in Nikkei warrants and would consider playing the Japanese stock market through options. "I would be very interested" in options on a Japanese stock-market benchmark, he said.

The outlook for the Tokyo stock market is anything but clear. While some money managers believe -- even hope -- that it is near bottoming out, others aren't sure.

Mr. Gillman said, "We'd rather see the direction turn upward before buying. If it went through 20000 on freefall, there's no particular level at which we'd say this is a bargain," he said.
