First Boston Names Gantsoudes to Post As Welling Resigns

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 09/18/90

NEW YORK -- In another high-level management change at First Boston Corp., Curtis R. Welling was forced out as head of the big securities firm's 450-person equity department. Mr. Welling, a 41-year-old managing director, was succeeded by James G. Gantsoudes, 48, who had headed a smaller equity group at Travelers Corp.'s Dillon, Read & Co. securities unit for the past 5 1/2 years. Mr. Gantsoudes is a former associate of Archibald Cox Jr., First Boston's newly installed president and chief executive officer.

As head of equity operations, Mr. Welling was in charge of First Boston's sales, trading and research of North American stocks. He got his job only 15 months ago, after his predecessor, Luiz Mendez, who helped build First Boston's equity department into a powerhouse in the 1980s, was forced out in a power struggle.

First Boston, a unit of CS First Boston Inc., has continued to undergo wrenching management changes amid a severe downturn in its profits. In January, CS First Boston Chief Executive John Hennessy demoted William Mayer and assumed his duties as head of the First Boston unit. This month, Mr. Cox took over those duties from Mr. Hennessy, who remains head of the CS First Boston parent.

The Gantsoudes appointment is the first major personnel move made by Mr. Cox in his new job.

First Boston said that Mr. Welling resigned "to pursue other interests." In an interview, Mr. Cox called Mr. Gantsoudes "one of the best people you could get." He noted Mr. Gantsoudes's experience at Dillon Read and before that as head of stocks sales and trading at American Express Co.'s Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc. and at Mr. Cox's former firm, Morgan Stanley Group Inc. "He's done everything he's been put in charge of very well," Mr. Cox said.

Reached at his office late yesterday, Mr. Welling said he resigned "on my own," even though he had no plans for a new job. He declined to comment further.

People close to First Boston said Mr. Welling was forced out after it was determined he wasn't First Boston's choice to lead the equity department in coming years. CS First Boston officials privately have groused that the firm, despite its strong presence in New York, London and Tokyo, has done a poor job in coordinating its international stock business. They say the firm hasn't made full use of its farflung global operations. Mr. Cox declined to comment on the issue.

CS First Boston is 44.5%-owned by the parent company of Swiss bank Credit Suisse.

John Birkelund, chairman of Dillon Read, said Mr. Gantsoudes left his firm on good terms. "He's a nice man and we're sorry to lose him," Mr. Birkelund said. "He's going over to First Boston to head a much larger equity unit. He's worked with Cox before." He said Dillon Read will attempt "an internal solution" to replacing Mr. Gantsoudes before seeking outside candidates.

Mr. Gantsoudes is a graduate of West Point and Harvard Business School.
