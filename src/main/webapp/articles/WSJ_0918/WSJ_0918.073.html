Keating Slated To Face Counts Of Fraud Today --- American Continental Corp. Ex-Chief Is Said Indicted By a Jury in California

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and Roy J. Harris Jr.
  • Date: 09/18/90

Charles H. Keating Jr., the man the federal government has said is responsible for the biggest thrift collapse ever, is scheduled to be arraigned today on criminal fraud charges in state court in Los Angeles. A California state grand jury last week voted to indict Mr. Keating, according to individuals who are familiar with the government's investigation of Mr. Keating. The indictment, which was sealed, marks the first time that criminal charges have been filed against Mr. Keating, the former chairman of American Continental Corp.

Mr. Keating resigned from his post at American Continental late last month, after a federal judge dismissed Mr. Keating's challenge to the government takeover of Lincoln Savings & Loan Association, American Continental's main operating unit. The government seized Lincoln in April 1989, shortly after American Continental filed for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code.

The charges filed against Mr. Keating are said to include allegations that he defrauded 23,000 investors through the sale of $200 million in unsecured debentures. The debentures were mostly sold through Lincoln branch offices in Los Angeles and Orange counties. Many purchasers of the bonds were retired investors, some of whom claim that Lincoln branch officers falsely told them the debentures were federally guaranteed and were as safe as insured certificates of deposit.

The indictment also is understood to include charges that Mr. Keating violated California state banking laws. American Continental is based in Phoenix, Ariz. Lincoln is based in the Orange County, Calif., city of Irvine.

Los Angeles County District Attorney Ira Reiner was named last December to head a special statewide "prosecutors' task force" looking into the collapse of Lincoln Savings. In February, Mr. Reiner asked for a special grand jury to be empanelled in Los Angeles County to assist in investigating the Lincoln case.

Sandi Gibbons, a spokeswoman for Mr. Reiner, yesterday declined to say whether Mr. Keating had been indicted or was about to be arraigned. She did read a statement, however, saying that "the district attorney's office has concluded its presentation of evidence to the grand jury." She added that Mr. Reiner wouldn't "comment until such time a court lifts the seal of confidentiality."

Stephen C. Neal, a Chicago attorney who is representing Mr. Keating, declined to comment on the indictment.

Mr. Keating, who claims he is broke, already faces a multitude of civil suits. The federal Resolution Trust Corp., responsible for the thrift bailout, has filed a $1.1 billion civil racketeering suit against Mr. Keating. And the federal Office of Thrift Supervision has filed administrative charges against Mr. Keating and others, seeking $40.9 million.

The state of California also filed civil charges of investment fraud against Mr. Keating earlier this year seeking restitution for bondholders. The state has come in for criticism, too, however. Bondholders at one point filed a claim saying that the state Department of Corporations "knew or should have known. . . . that the subordinated debentures constituted an extreme risk to the investing public."

Mr. Keating's position has been that the bondholders wouldn't have lost anything if the government hadn't seized Lincoln and forced American Continental into bankruptcy court. The outspoken Mr. Keating also has claimed that the government has conducted what amounts to a vendetta against him because of his criticism of the thrift regulatory system after American Continental bought Lincoln in 1984.

Meanwhile, a federal grand jury in Los Angeles is continuing its investigation of Mr. Keating and others, according to individuals with knowledge of that investigation. These individuals say that federal prosecutors may soon bring additional charges against Mr. Keating that are similar to those being brought by the state of California. Defense lawyers say that it is likely that Mr. Keating will be tried first on federal charges.

Federal prosecutors may be waiting for the sentencing of Michael R. Milken, the former junk bond chief of Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., scheduled for next week. Mr. Keating was once a top Drexel client and a close associate of Mr. Milken's. Mr. Milken has agreed to cooperate with the government after he is sentenced.
