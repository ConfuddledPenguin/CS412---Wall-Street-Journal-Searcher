Politics & Policy: Bush Still Seeks Capital-Gains Tax Cut As Negotiators Haggle Over Trade-Off

  • Author: David Wessel and Jackie Calmes
  • Date: 09/18/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush renewed his call for lower capital-gains tax rates as budget negotiators continued to haggle over the price Democrats will demand for agreeing to such a tax cut. Last night, people close to the negotiations said that the Democrats and Republicans had reached an impasse over the capital-gains questions -- a development, they said, that threatened to make further deliberations fruitless. The best chance for a quick compromise, the sources indicated, was the kind of change in bargaining position that only President Bush and the Democratic leaders of the House and Senate could authorize.

Indicating that he isn't letting the divisive subject die as negotiations drag on, Mr. Bush yesterday called for a deficit-reduction package "that is oriented toward growth . . . one which contains incentives like the capital-gains tax cut, which I am absolutely convinced will create jobs."

Meanwhile, as the closed-door budget talks at Andrews Air Force Base continued, Democrats modified their plan to pair the cut in capital-gains tax rates with an increase in taxes on the upper-income Americans who would most benefit from such a tax cut.

The Democrats dropped a proposal to raise the marginal income-tax rate for the richest Americans to 35% from the current 28%, and substituted a broader version of an income-tax surcharge they proposed earlier, people close to the talks said.

Although details of the new proposal weren't immediately available, individuals familiar with the negotiations indicated it would impose a 21% surcharge that would kick in somewhere below $250,000. That would be the equivalent of a 33.8% tax rate. Democrats previously proposed a 20% surcharge on incomes above $500,000.

Republicans were no more receptive to the surcharge idea than they were to a simple increase in the top tax rate. "Generally speaking, a surtax is an increase in rates," said House Republican Leader Robert Michel of Illinois. "That's not one of our favorite things to do."

On Sunday, Republicans responded to the Democrats' first formal offer to accept lower capital-gains tax rates with a plan to limit all tax deductions for taxpayers making more than $200,000 a year. Democrats haven't agreed to that approach.

The proposal would shave a couple's total deductions by 1% for every $2,000 in additional income above $200,000 up to $250,000, people familiar with the proposal say. That is, a couple with $224,000 in income could take only 88% of their total deductions. Deductions would be further limited for couples with incomes above $475,000.

At last night's meeting, Democrats were prepared to propose a new approach under which they would drop their demands for a variety of new spending initiatives if Republicans would abandon their pursuit of a capital-gains tax cut. A person close to the talks said Democrats expected Republicans to be no more receptive to that proposal than they have been to the others.

Democrats' opening offer Sept. 7 to cut $500 billion over five years represented net savings. They proposed $622.5 billion in total deficit reduction, but included $106.4 billion for new spending proposals covering education, science, transportation, environmental cleanup and other social programs important to Democrats.

House Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.) yesterday once again raised the possibility that despite Mr. Bush's statements a deficit-reduction compromise might exclude both the capital-gains tax cut and any offsetting tax increases aimed at upper-income taxpayers.

Republicans have proposed setting the maximum capital-gains tax rate at 15%; Democrats have countered with a 23.1% rate, aides say. Currently, capital gains are taxed at a maximum rate of 33%.

As talks dragged through an 11th day, lawmakers began discussing the need for stopgap legislation to keep government programs funded after the Oct. 1 start of fiscal 1991. Congress has been proceeding with regular appropriations bills, but final action awaits the budget summit's outcome. Even if agreement is reached soon, it couldn't be put into effect in time to avoid at least a short-term emergency spending bill, a Democratic leadership aide said.

Rep. Michel and Rep. Silvio Conte of Massachusetts, the top Republican on the House Appropriations Committee, also suggested that Democratic leaders would move to delay the imposition of across-the-board budget cuts, which by law would take effect after Oct. 1 if the projected 1991 deficit hadn't been reduced before then. But the Democratic aide said any such proposal would be hard to pass, since lawmakers would fear such action would relieve the pressure on the administration and congressional leaders to reach a budget accord.

President Bush, speaking at the White House to out-of-town reporters, also said that Congress must "enact real spending cuts, not these smoke-and-mirror cuts that simply don't cut spending as advertised."

The president asserted that there isn't a national recession under way, and added: "But I do think that a budget agreement is the best antidote to further economic slowdown, because I think it'll result in lower interest rates and a renewed sense of confidence in investment in America, both of which are necessary to guarantee a more robust growth."
