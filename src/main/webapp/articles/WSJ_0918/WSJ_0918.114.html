Credit Markets: Long-Term Bond Prices Decline on Oil's Rise, Short-Term Securities Steady on Fed Rate Hopes

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 09/18/90

NEW YORK -- The bond market limped along in another torpid trading day, with prices of long-term bonds falling in response to higher oil prices, and short-term securities holding steady on hopes the Federal Reserve will eventually ease interest rates. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended more than 3/8 point lower yesterday, a decline of $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, rose to 9.06%.

James Capra, a senior vice president at Lehman Government Securities Inc., said trading activity has come to a virtual standstill, because investors are "too afraid to do anything." He said the fear stems from uncertainties about how economic and political issues will be resolved.

"I think the market is torn between the concern about a Middle East war, a weaker dollar, and oil prices that keep trading higher every day," vs. the potentially positive aspects of a budget-reduction compromise and the possibility that the Federal Reserve might reduce short-term interest rates.

Meanwhile, World Savings & Loan Association, a subsidiary of Golden West Financial Corp., sold $100 million of seven-year subordinated notes yesterday at a price to yield 10.25%.

The World Savings note sale was considered important, because it showed that investors aren't avoiding the securities of all financial institutions, but instead are dividing the industry into two groups, composed of those institutions perceived to be in good shape and those considered to be headed for trouble.

Last week, some market participants were surprised when Chase Manhattan Corp. was forced to offer yields in excess of 13% to reset the rate on $200 million of two-year auction-rate subordinated notes. The yield was 4.92 percentage points above the yield on two-year Treasury notes, which was considered a high spread. The spread on the World Savings notes was 1.5 percentage points above the yield on the Treasury's seven-year notes.

James Quigley, debt syndicate director at Merrill Lynch Capital Markets, said the narrower yield spread for World Savings is an indication that investors perceive the Oakland, Calif.-based savings and loan to be a relatively good credit risk.

"It's false to say that all financial institution spreads have widened out in the past few months," Mr. Quigley said. "Investors do differentiate between the money-center banks and credits like World S&L," which has assets consisting primarily of single-family home mortgages and government securities. In contrast, the big New York money-center banks and many large regional banks have been plagued by troubled commercial real estate loans and loans to lesser-developed countries.

Ann Robinson, a banking analyst at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp., said debt offerings made on the bank or thrift level, like the World S&L issue, are more appealing than those made by holding companies such as Chase Manhattan.

Investors fear that if a bank or thrift becomes insolvent and must be taken over by the government, the regulators will "seize banks and save the bank but not the banking holding company," Ms. Robinson said.

Treasury Securities

Treasury prices ended mixed yesterday. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond was quoted at a price of 96 23/32 to yield 9.06%, compared with 97 4/32 to yield 9.02% on Friday.

Prices of two-year notes were unchanged. The two-year notes were quoted at a price of 100 5/32 to yield 8.02%.

Meanwhile, rates on three-month and six-month Treasury bills were lower yesterday at the Treasury's weekly bill auction, compared with last week's auction.

The average discount rate set on new three-month bills at yesterday's auction was 7.39%, and the average discount rate on new six-month bills was 7.3%. The rates were down from last week's auction, when they were 7.41% and 7.34%, respectively.

Corporate and Junk Bonds

Junk bonds ended weaker again, the result of a lackluster stock market and declines in the government bond market.

Many junk bonds declined about 1/2 point, underperforming investment-grade corporate bonds, which fell between 1/4 and 3/8 point.

Among high-yield bonds that bucked the market trend and advanced were pay-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. RJR's 17.375% convertible pay-in-kind debentures rose 7/8 point to finish at 112 on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely held RJR issue, the 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures maturing in 2007, ended 1/2 point higher at 87 3/4.

Some market participants said investors bid up the bonds on the expectation that the company's exchange offer for the convertible debentures will be declared effective by the Securities and Exchange Commission soon.

Other dealers said the bonds were buoyed by Friday's news that RJR, a food and tobacco giant, will export cigarettes to the U.S.S.R. to help alleviate that country's cigarette shortage.

In addition to the World S&L issue, Champion International Corp. offered $150 million of five-year notes Monday that were priced to yield 9.65%.

Municipal Bonds

Feeling the effects of rising oil prices, municipal bonds were 1/4 to 3/8 lower, with some bonds posting declines of as much as 1/2 point.

"There's not really enough trading to get a feel for it," a municipal trader said of the market. Participants anticipate that secondary activity will be slow as investors focus on new deals this week.

In limited new-issue dealings, a Goldman, Sachs & Co. group preliminarily priced an issue of $228 million of New York State Urban Development Corp. correctional capital facilities revenue bonds to yield from 6.50% in 1992 to 8% in 2016. Ratings of single-A/triple-B-plus are expected.

A Goldman Sachs group also repriced $126 million of Rhode Island Housing and Mortgage Finance Corp. home ownership opportunity bonds to lower yields by five to 10 basis points. A basis point is equal to 1/100th of one percentage point. The bonds are priced to yield from 6.4% in 1992 to 8.05% in 2022. Interest on part of the issue is subject to the federal alternative minimum tax. Ratings of single-A-1/double-A-plus are expected.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.2% issue, due 2018, was 3/8 point lower, at 97 1/4 to 97 3/4, to yield 7.39.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities prices, taking their cue from the U.S. Treasury market, eroded.

Helping weigh on the mortgage securities market was selling of securities outstanding by the Resolution Trust Corp. But losses were capped by the continued collateral collection by underwriters putting real estate mortgage investment conduit, or remic, deals together. A remic is a type of security authorized by the 1986 tax law. Underwritten by Wall Street firms, remics provide a method of dividing up the income flows from many mortgages into new securities with various interest rates and maturities to attract banks, insurers and other big investors to the home-mortgage market.

The RTC unloaded "$180 million Ginnie {Mae} 8 1/2s and $100 million {Freddie Mac} PC 9s," a trader said, characterizing the volume of sales as "a small amount" of the Government National Mortgage Association and Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. issues. PCs are participation certificates. He said the securities "traded right on the market and were well bid for. They're deal-related securities."

The RTC selling is expected to continue in the next session, with roughly $100 million more of seasoned Ginnie Maes and as much as $200 million of various Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac coupons, according to another participant. Fannie Mae is the Federal National Mortgage Association.

Most market players consider the supply manageable.

Aside from the RTC activity, secondary dealings were subdued.

Ginnie Mae 9 1/2% issues for October delivery were down 1/4 at 99 10/32, Freddie Mac 9 1/2% Gold PCs were off 5/32 at 98 27/ 32, and Fannie Mae 9 1/2% issues were off 5/32 at 98 21/32.

Conventional mortgage securities have been outperforming Ginnie Maes, because underwriters have been seeking the Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae securities for remics.

Pricings of two new remic offerings, one from Freddie Mac and one from Fannie Mae, totaling $600 million were announced yesterday. A $300 million Freddie Mac remic is being offered in 10 classes by Merrill Lynch Capital Markets in Freddie Mac Gold 1007. The securities are collateralized with Freddie Mac 10 1/2% Gold PCs. A $300.2 million issue of Fannie Mae remics is being offered in 11 classes by Bear, Stearns & Co. The issue, Series 1990-127, is backed by Fannie Mae 10% securities.

Foreign Bonds

Speculation that financial firms may be forced to mark more securities to market value caused Japanese government bonds to end weaker.

The yield on the benchmark No. 119 10-year government bond rose as high as 8.58%, the highest yield for a benchmark in about a decade. When the market closed, the yield was quoted at 8.57%. The price of the benchmark bond fell 0.63 point, to 81.17. (When calculated using the semiannual compound method, the yield on the No. 119 ended at 7.75%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)

The mark-to-market talk began late Friday on reports that the Finance Ministry was considering making accounting rules stricter. The move might force firms to realize losses on bonds and make holding such securities less attractive, a dealer said.
