Marketing & Media: New York Post Union Votes to Accept Cuts

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/18/90

NEW YORK -- The Newspaper Guild voted overwhelmingly to accept New York Post owner Peter S. Kalikow's proposal of wage-and-benefit cuts to keep the 189-year-old tabloid in business. After a loud and emotional meeting that lasted for two hours, Guild members, made up largely of editors, reporters and advertising staff, voted 242-45 to approve concessions, which call for a four-day workweek, a 20% cut in salary and the elimination of dozens of jobs. Ten other newspaper unions are expected to approve similar cuts.

If the proposal had been rejected, Mr. Kalikow vowed to shut down the paper with a final edition for today. Folding the paper would have put 960 people out of work. Mr. Kalikow has sought $27 million in cost cuts so the paper can break even in 1990.

Later this week, Mr. Kalikow and the unions are expected to sign a new three-year contract. "Three years, for the Post that's a long time," said Paul Tharp, a Post reporter after emerging from the union hall in Manhattan.

Though many union members said they were against cutting their own pay and workweek, they were swayed by a last-minute sweetener by Mr. Kalikow to boost severance pay for those who agree to resign. At the Guild meeting, several members spoke out saying they would approve the contract but they would take the buyout. Mr. Kalikow has also promised the unions 20% of the company's stock and a representative on the board.

The settlement at the Post is expected to put pressure on Tribune Co.'s Daily News, which is in the midst of stalled and bitter negotiations with its unions. The Daily News, however, is asking its unions for extensive changes in hiring and operations, whereas Mr. Kalikow was looking only to cut costs.
