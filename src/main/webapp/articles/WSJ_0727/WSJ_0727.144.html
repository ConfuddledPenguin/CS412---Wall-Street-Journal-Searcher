Credit Markets: Bonds Barely Move in Dull Session as Investors Wait for Cues in Today's 2nd-Period GNP Report

  • Author: Susan Scherreik and Kevin L. Dunn Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/27/90

NEW YORK -- The bond market barely budged during a listless trading session yesterday as most prices ended unchanged or slightly lower. "The bond market is moribund," said Christopher Rupkey, a financial economist at Mitsubishi Bank.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended unchanged while prices of short-term and intermediate-term securities ended just 1/16 point lower, a loss of less than 63 cents for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The yield on the benchmark 30-year bond stood firm at 8.54%.

Robert T. McGee, a senior vice president at Tokai Bank Ltd., attributed the sparse activity largely to investor caution ahead of this morning's release of second-quarter gross national product.

"People don't want to stick their necks out," ahead of potentially market-moving economic data, he said.

Investors and bond traders will be scrutinizing the GNP data for further signs that the economy is weakening following the unexpected 3.2% decline in June durable goods reported on Wednesday.

Economists polled by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report expect, on average, that inflation-adjusted GNP advanced at a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 1.8% in the latest quarter, down marginally from 1.9% in the first quarter.

Among other things, the GNP data are expected to reflect sluggish sales. If that proves true, bond market players will try to determine whether the slow sales are a correction to the robust sales in the first quarter, or a sign of a weakening economy.

Dealers said bond-market players focused on the GNP report because there was little else to discuss.

The only other point of interest, they said, were reports suggesting that there won't be quick progress on the federal budget-reduction talks. The reports caused a minor flurry of selling in the cash market after bond futures had finished at their session peaks.

The budget-summit negotiators had hoped to reach an agreement before the Congressional summer recess, which begins Aug. 3. But congressional and administration officials now say a compromise may not be reached until Oct. 1, the beginning of fiscal 1991.

Treasury Securities

The price of the benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at 102 5/32 to yield 8.54%, unchanged from Wednesday. New two-year notes ended slightly lower at 99 26/32 to yield 8.09% compared with 99 28/ 32 to yield 8.07% on Wednesday.

Treasury bills were slightly higher. The discount rate on six-month bills rose to 7.58% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.81% while the rate on six-month bills inched up to 7.47% to yield 7.85%.

The Treasury sold a record-size $10.5 billion of 52-week bills yesterday at an average rate of 7.34%. The average rate was down from an average rate of 7.52% at the previous 52-week bill auction held June 28. Yesterday's rate was the lowest since 7.21% at the Jan. 11 auction.

Foreign Bonds

Japanese government bonds ended weaker in heavy selling as comments by Bank of Japan officials reinforced perceptions that interest rates will be moving higher.

The selling reversed the market's advance of the previous day and lifted the yield on Japan's benchmark 10-year bond to a new six-year high of 7.505%. Meanwhile, officials at Japan's long-term credit banks, which reset their prime lending rate at the end of each month, said it is highly likely that they will set the rate at 7.9% next week, up from its current level of 7.6%.

Bond prices started to fall after Bank of Japan Governor Yasushi Mieno said that price stability is the central bank's highest concern. Speaking at a private forum in Tokyo Thursday afternoon, Mr. Mieno noted that Japan is facing an acute labor shortage and that money supply growth is very high, factors that could cause prices to rise quickly, he warned.

His remarks were taken as an indication that the central bank is disposed to guide interest rates higher to reduce inflationary pressures.

On top of that, another Bank of Japan official suggested that the central bank may be forced to slow the economy in an effort to slow money supply growth. That was interpreted as a indication that the Bank of Japan is willing to guide interest rates higher even at the risk of reducing business activity.

At the 3 p.m. JST close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, Japan's benchmark No. 119 10-year bond was quoted at a price of 85.79, down 0.21 point, to yield 7.46%. In interdealer trading after the exchange's close the yield rose to 7.505%. (When calculated using the semiannual compound method, the yield was 6.97%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)

West German, British and other major foreign bond markets were somewhat quiet yesterday.

Junk Bonds

The Junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. gave back early gains to close sharply lower on news of an adverse court ruling against the company and the tobacco industry.

The New Jersey state Supreme Court ruled yesterday that warnings on cigarette packs don't protect tobacco companies from liability suits. That was a major setback for RJR and two other cigarette makers named in a 1982 lawsuit.

The news not only sent RJR bonds lower but dragged down the broader junk bond market, which finished unchanged to modestly lower. Junk bonds are speculative-grade issues with low ratings or no ratings at all. In early trading, most junk bonds had posted gains.

RJR's 17 3/8% convertible payment-in-kind debentures finished down 2 1/4 points to close at 115 3/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. The company's 17% exchangeable payment-in-kind debentures ended 1 3/4 points lower to close at 92 3/4. Payment-in-kind bonds pay interest in the form of additional securities instead of cash.

RJR's cash-paying 13.5% subordinated debentures fell 1 1/4 points to 100.

The junk bonds of casinos operated by real estate developer Donald Trump were also battered. The bonds slid on a report, which appeared in this newspaper on Thursday, that Mr. Trump may ask bondholders to accept equity in his casinos in exchange for letting him cancel some interest payments.

Trump Taj Mahal Funding Inc.'s 14% first mortgage bonds fell 1 1/2 points to 46 1/2.

Trump Castle Funding Corp.'s 13 3/4% first mortgage bonds fell three points to 55 while Trump Plaza's 12 7/8% first mortgage bonds were down one point to 83.

Mortgage-backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended a lackluster session with slight price gains.

In the primary market, pricing details of just one sizable offering were unveiled -- a $700 million First Chicago Master Trust II 1990-B issue backed by credit-card receivables issued and serviced by FCC National Bank, a unit of First Chicago Corp.

The new securities have a coupon rate of 8 7/8% and a yield of 8.907%. The amount of the offering was an increase from the expected $500 million.

Lead underwriter Salomon Brothers Inc. had no comment on investor response to the deal, but market players said the increase in size and post-pricing indications reflect strong investor demand.

In the secondary market, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were up 2/32 at 99 23/32, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% issues were up 2/32 at 99 2/32 and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were up 2/32 at 99 18/32.

Municipal Securities

Municipal bonds ended unchanged to 1/4 point higher in light trading.

Among actively traded secondary issues, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 were 1/4 point higher at 99 5/8 to yield 7.22%.

A lack of supply is keeping municipal prices firm, traders said.

In new issue dealings, a Lazard Freres & Co. Inc. group repriced $61 million of New York State Dormitory special act school districts revenue bonds to lower some yields by two to five basis points.

The issue is priced to yield from 6.10% in 1993 to 7.17% in 2013.

No large competitive issues were priced yesterday.
