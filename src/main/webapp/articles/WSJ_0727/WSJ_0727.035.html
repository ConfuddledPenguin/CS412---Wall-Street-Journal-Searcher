Talk of Tokyo: Favors for Clients Of Brokerage Firms

  • Author: Masayoshi Kanabayashi and Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 07/27/90

TOKYO -- Was Japan's stock market rigged? For years, conspiracy theorists have hinted that the mammoth securities houses that dominate trading on the Tokyo Stock Exchange routinely act in collusion with their major clients. Until now, there had been little official support for such allegations.

Yesterday, though, Japanese newspapers reported that Japan's National Tax Agency allegedly discovered that about 20 securities firms -- including two of the biggest, Yamaichi Securities Co. and Daiwa Securities Co. -- had paid more than 16 billion yen ($106.7 million) in compensation to cover losses logged by big customers in the 18 months through March 31, 1989.

The Tokyo Tax Administration Bureau refused to comment on the allegations, which were carried in articles in all the major evening newspapers here. According to the newspapers, the authorities are seeking from the securities firms seven to eight billion yen in back taxes on the compensation. The authorities also are said to be continuing their investigations at other houses, including the giants Nikko Securities Co. and Nomura Securities Co.

Because the compensation that is paid should be reported as profit by the securities firms -- such compensation isn't permitted under Japan's Securities and Exchange Law -- firms that made these payments without paying taxes on them first could be found to have violated the country's tax code and still to owe back taxes on the money.

Both Daiwa and Yamaichi issued statements, denying the allegations. A spokesman for Daiwa said he couldn't understand why the matter is surfacing now, as the company already has been audited for the tax year involved.

Still, this isn't the first time Daiwa has been charged in this kind of case. Last year, the firm paid about 6.2 billion yen in back taxes on 10 billion yen in compensation it paid to some big institutional investors for losses they incurred during the late 1970s.

In that instance, as well as in the latest alleged tax violations, Japan's tax authorities have uncovered what fund managers have said in the past is a common practice in Japan: securities firms' guaranteeing returns for big investors.

Nihon Keizai Shimbun, Japan's leading economic newspaper, said the securities firms compensated major clients for losses by buying back from them, at a profit for the clients, bonds and other securities that the securities firms initially sold to those same clients at a discount. The newspaper said many major clients demanded such compensation after the Black Monday stock market crash in October 1987.

This means the securities firms would have been taking losses on such transactions to bolster their big clients. Small stockholders aren't given the opportunity to recoup their losses in a similar way, and, analysts said, effectively lose on the deals because of the higher fees they must eventually pay to cover the securities firms' costs.

"They're discriminating against the individual," Stuart Matthews, a financial analyst for Barclays de Zoete Wedd Securities Ltd., a British firm with offices in Tokyo, said. "They're saving the big guys from harm."

The implications of the latest allegations against the securities firms are significant, Mr. Matthews said. "It raises the question of what the Japanese firms did after the crash" of share prices earlier this year in Tokyo, he said. "Clearly, they allow their profits to leak away" by paying them out to clients.

Partly as a result of the newspaper reports, the share prices of the Big Four Japanese securities houses fell yesterday. Daiwa shares fell 40 yen to 1,330 yen, Yamaichi was off 60 at 1,120, Nikko was down 50 at 1,120, and Nomura lost 50 to 2,050.
