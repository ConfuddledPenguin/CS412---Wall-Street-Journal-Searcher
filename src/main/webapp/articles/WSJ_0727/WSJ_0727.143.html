CBOE Market Maker Given $35,000 Fine, 6-Month Suspension

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/27/90

CHICAGO -- The Chicago Board Options Exchange said it fined Carolyn J. Olshansky, a market maker at the exchange, $35,000 and suspended her for six months from associating with any exchange member. Ms. Olshansky agreed to the sanction without admitting or denying that she violated CBOE rules. She allegedly allowed a floor broker to submit for clearance numerous option transactions for her account, which she allowed to clear into her account. She also allegedly knew or should have known that the floor broker inappropriately favored her account by making trades with her without using the exchange's open outcry system.

CBOE members said the floor broker, who wasn't named, was Robert M. Donnelly. He was disciplined by the exchange late last year, fined $70,000 and suspended from membership or association with the exchange for a year.

Neither Ms. Olshansky nor Mr. Donnelly could be reached for comment.

In a separate case, Peter H. Wright, a former registered representative in the Palo Alto, Calif., office of Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., was censured and suspended for six months from association with any exchange member. Mr. Wright allegedly traded options for two customer accounts without the prior written approval of the customers and Dean Witter, made unauthorized stock sales in a customer account and exceeded an agreed-upon loss limitation in a customer account.

Mr. Wright agreed to the action without admitting or denying he violated CBOE rules. Dean Witter wasn't disciplined.

In a third case, Darryl N. Myers, a former registered representative in the Memphis, Tenn., office of Thomson McKinnon Securities Inc., was censured, fined $20,000 and permanently barred from association with any exchange member.

Mr. Myers, who failed to answer or deny the charges, allegedly assured several customers that option trades for their accounts would earn a specified minimum return, misled customers about the performance of trades in their accounts and used his own discretion in allocating profitable and unprofitable orders among customers.

Neither Mr. Wright nor Mr. Myers could be reached for comment.
