Boeing's Net Nearly Doubles To $387 Million

  • Author: David J. Jefferson
  • Date: 07/27/90

Boeing Co.'s second-quarter net income nearly doubled to $387 million, or $1.12 a share, on the continued strength of the Seattle-based company's commercial aircraft business. A year earlier, the aerospace concern earned $195 million, or 57 cents a share, after a 3-for-2 stock split paid last month.

Sales rose 37% to $6.97 billion from $5.07 billion.

Boeing said commercial aircraft sales volume and operating margins for the latest quarter were partly offset by a higher effective federal tax rate.

Despite the overall increases in results, Boeing's chairman and chief executive officer, Frank Shrontz, painted a gloomy picture for the company's defense business. He warned, as he has previously, that "technical, cost and schedule problems" on certain defense contracts will result in "a significant 1990 operating loss" for the company's defense and space business. Last year, Boeing's military transport subsidiary had a $559 million loss, while the missiles and space segment earned $85 million.

"The market environment for Department of Defense business continues to be influenced by world events and strong pressures to reduce federal expenditures," Boeing said. "A number of programs involving the company are under DOD, NASA {National Aeronautics and Space Administration} and congressional funding reviews."

Still, robust commercial business made Boeing the clear winner among U.S. aerospace companies for the second quarter. Boeing received orders for 128 jetliners and five commuter planes valued at a total of $11.5 billion in the latest period. A year earlier, the company had a record 502 jetliner and eight commuter aircraft orders valued at $25 billion.

The company reported its earnings after the close of the market yesterday. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, Boeing shares finished at $59.125, up 87.5 cents.

Boeing said the "key challenges" for its commercial aircraft business during the second half will be maintaining schedule and cost controls at the current high level of production, and obtaining firm orders from top airline customers for the proposed 777 wide-body, twin-engine jet.

Boeing's total backlog as of June 30 was $84.3 billion, of which $78.1 billion was for commercial customers. At the end of 1989, its backlog was $80.6 billion, with $74 billion from commercial business.

In the first half, net rose 5.4% to $689 million, or $1.99 a share, from $654 million, or $1.89 a share, adjusted for the split, a year earlier, when net income included $298 million, or 86 cents a share, related to an accounting change for taxes. Sales rose 48% to $13.4 billion from $9.07 billion.
