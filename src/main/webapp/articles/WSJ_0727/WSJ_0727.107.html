Politics & Policy: Conferees Agree on Oil-Spill Measure; Bill Would End 16-Year Impasse on Issue

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 07/27/90

WASHINGTON -- House and Senate conferees reached an agreement on comprehensive oil-spill legislation that would end a 16-year congressional stalemate on the issue. Approval of the measure came after the lawmakers agreed to make vessel owners solely liable for damages resulting from tanker spills, rather than making cargo owners responsible as well. The conferees also settled on liability limits that are significantly higher than those under current law.

Previously, the conferees agreed to require that all new tankers be equipped with double hulls and rejected provisions that would have provided for U.S. participation in an international treaty limiting shippers' liability in spills. That action means states are free to set unlimited liability for shippers or other parties responsible for spills.

The congressional effort to pass oil-spill legislation gained momentum after last year's grounding of the Exxon Valdez in Alaska and a rash of spills in U.S. waters in the 16 months since. Congressional aides said lawmakers hope to bring the measure to the House and Senate floors for final approval as early as next week.

Conservation groups were cheered by approval of the measure. Clifton Curtis, an oceans specialist for Friends of the Earth, called the measure an "environmentally strong bill." The Bush administration and the tanker industry, however, strongly opposed the rejection of the treaty, contending that companies will curtail shipments to U.S. ports rather than face the potential for unlimited liability under state laws.

Galen Reser, assistant transportation secretary for governmental affairs, said "we may live to regret" the failure to accept the international accord. At the same time, he said the administration "is pleased that this has come to a close" and said the transportation secretary probably won't recommend a presidential veto of the legislation.

Under the federal liability limits set yesterday, tanker owners would generally be liable up to $1,200 a gross ton. Limits under current law are as low as $150 a gross ton, congressional aides said. Limits also would be established for accidents involving offshore facilities. Federal cleanup and damage assessment costs, plus compensation for damages, beyond the liability limits would be paid from a new $1 billion fund financed by a five-cents-a-barrel tax on domestic and imported oil. That fee has been collected since Jan. 1.

The fund could pay out the full $1 billion for a single incident, with as much as $500 million going to repair natural resource damage.

The fund could be tapped only if the accident doesn't involve gross or willful negligence. The bill also improves contingency planning for spills and enhances the Coast Guard's ability to respond to accidents.

Before approving the measure yesterday, the lawmakers added an amendment that would place a moratorium on oil leasing in the Outer Continental Shelf off North Carolina until additional environmental studies can be done. The proposal was offered by Rep. Walter Jones (D., N.C.).
