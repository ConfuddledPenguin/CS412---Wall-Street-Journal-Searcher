What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/27/90

THE HOUSE VOTED to reprimand Rep. Frank, rejecting a censure penalty. Frank, a liberal Democrat from Massachusetts and an acknowledged homosexual, was punished for using his official influence on behalf of a male prostitute. The 408-18 vote, which came after the House rejected a GOP-backed motion calling for the harsher censure penalty, upheld the recommendations of the Ethics Committee. The issue split the chamber emotionally and reflected more partisanship than in any ethics case in the past decade. The censure motion, which failed 287-141, was opposed by all but 12 Democrats, while Republicans were almost 3-1 in favor of it.

In a statement before the vote, Frank explained that he took some of the actions in part to "conceal" his homosexuality, which he later disclosed.

---

Bush signed the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, which bans discrimination against the nation's 43 million disabled people in employment, public accommodations, transportation and telecommunications. The legislation includes a variety of sanctions for violators and provides new protection for AIDS-infected workers.

---

Souter brushed aside questions about a letter he wrote nearly a decade ago opposing legislation that would have forced judges to make decisions on abortions for teenagers. The Supreme Court nominee said he would disclose what is "constitutionally appropriate" at his confirmation hearings, expected to begin in September.

---

The EPA said last month's easing of federal controls on low-level atomic wastes raises public health concerns. Meanwhile, members of a House subcommittee said at a hearing that the new policy could turn some states into dumping grounds for radioactive waste in landfills.

---

Waldheim met in Salzburg, Austria, with the presidents of Czechoslovakia and West Germany in a break with the international diplomatic isolation imposed on the Austrian leader because of his wartime past. Waldheim has acknowledged he concealed his World War II service as an officer in a German army unit linked to atrocities.

---

Iraq prepared for direct talks with Kuwait on ending a dispute over land rights and oil production. Egyptian President Mubarak sent an adviser to Baghdad and Kuwait to help organize the talks, expected to start over the weekend in Saudi Arabia.

---

Armenia's Parliament set up a panel to respond to Soviet leader Gorbachev's threat to use force against illegal groups unless they surrender their arms. One Parliament member said "we can't be left defenseless," adding that Armenians had lost faith in Soviet security forces on the Azerbaijan border and had to rely on armed nationalists.

---

A federal jury in Los Angeles found a Honduran, Juan Ramone Matta Ballesteros, guilty of racketeering, kidnapping and conspiracy in the 1985 murders of U.S. drug agent Enrique Camarena and his pilot. But the jury acquitted Matta on a single charge of murdering Camarena. Three other defendants are awaiting verdicts in the case.

---

Researchers said a woman apparently got AIDS from her dentist during a tooth-pulling procedure, though he wore gloves and a mask. The case has prompted the government to review its recommendations for AIDS prevention. American Dental Association officials said the Centers for Disease Control may have jumped to conclusions.

---

North and South Korea reached an accord to have their prime ministers meet in a bid to end hostilities, but a new dispute developed soon after the pact was signed in Panmunjom. The controversy erupted after a plan by five North Koreans to cross into South Korea was scrapped because of a feud involving their accommodations.

---

A juror was dismissed from deliberations in a money-laundering trial in federal court in Tampa, Fla., after reports that a member of the jury apparently telephoned people linked to a Colombian drug ring. The judge decided not to declare a mistrial and ordered the 11 remaining jurors to continue deliberations.

---

Secretary of State Baker rejected criticism from Asian allies over how to deal with Vietnamese boat people seeking refuge in neighboring countries. Baker said the U.S. remained opposed to forcibly repatriating the bulk of the refugees to Vietnam. He arrived in Indonesia for a conference of foreign ministers scheduled to start today.

---

East Germany's coalition appeared likely to survive after politicians from both German states narrowed their differences on how an all-German Parliament will be elected in December. The two governments agreed they should conclude a treaty by the end of August outlining voting procedures.

---

The U.S. death rate from asthma rose over 30% in seven years, and nearly 10 million Americans are affected by the disease, the Centers for Disease Control reported. Officials said a number of factors may be contributing to the increase, including air quality and exposure to infections and other "triggers" for bronchial constriction.

---

Salvadoran officials and rebels agreed to have a U.N. commission monitor human rights in El Salvador, where tens of thousands of civilians have died in a decade-old civil war. The commission wouldn't begin work until a cease-fire agreement is reached between the government and the guerrillas, who are holding talks in Costa Rica.
