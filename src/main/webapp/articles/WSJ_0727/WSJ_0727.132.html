Technology: Microsoft Starts Effort to Catch Novell In Market for PC Networking Software

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 07/27/90

Microsoft Corp., turning up the heat on competitors, is planning a costly campaign to win a bigger piece of the market for software that links personal computers. The campaign will cost an estimated $40 million in the year that began July 1, but much of this cost will come out of the company's plan to increase its work force in the fiscal year to 8,000 from about 5,600. Analysts also expect added revenue to cover part of the networking costs, so most didn't revise earning estimates. Still, Microsoft's stock fell $2.75 a share in over-the-counter trading yesterday to $68.75.

Microsoft is best known for its DOS operating software, which controls the basic functions of personal computers and compatibles made by International Business Machines Corp. and others. But the Redmond, Wash., company covets the market for networking software, which it considers a "very logical" extension of its core businesses, said Steve Ballmer, Microsoft's senior vice president for system software.

So far, however, Microsoft has offered weak products and an ineffective and confusing sales effort, leaving Novell Inc. to garner most of the software dollars spent on networking PCs. Microsoft's most recent network offering, Lan Manager, is widely considered to be underpowered. Sales also were sluggish because Microsoft relied mainly on PC manufacturers to sell the product.

When it first began chasing the network business several years ago, "Microsoft's view was that Lan Manager would take over the world, that it would sell itself," said Eric A. Benhamou, chief executive officer at 3Com Corp., which is marketing Microsoft's networking products, in a recent interview. "They thought it would be sold in a shrink-wrapped box. You can't sell (networking) that way."

Mr. Ballmer, conceding that past efforts have been disappointing, vowed the company will win a bigger share of the PC networking business.

Microsoft's new version of Lan Manager, to be unveiled Aug. 14 and shipped to customers later next month, is a decided improvement over the earlier product and "it will put performance issues to rest," Mr. Ballmer said. The company also plans to hire about 300 networking sales and supporting staff and sign up 600 resellers in the U.S. and 400 abroad to peddle the product. It also plans to spend about $15 million advertising Lan Manager over the next year.

"You really get the sense they are serious about going after Novell," said Rick Sherlund, an analyst at Goldman, Sachs & Co.

Though endowed with great marketing clout because of the sweep of its products, Microsoft faces stiff competition in the area of network software. Provo, Utah-based Novell dominates the market with its Netware family of software and in recent months has been expanding its traditional base of resellers to include computer manufacturers, who agree to bundle Novell's software with their hardware.

"Novell is entrenched," said Richard Edwards, an analyst at Robertson Stephens & Co. Novell, which had revenue of $421.8 million in its year ended Oct. 28, is generally considered to hold about 70% of the market for PC networking software.

Because it makes only networking software, Novell believes it holds a technical edge on Microsoft. "Netware is two years ahead of Lan Manager," said Darrel Miller, an executive vice president at Novell.

Mr. Ballmer disagrees but conceded that with the new Lan Manager, "I can't say we blow them away."

Still, on the heels of the Microsoft announcement, Novell shares fell to $47.875, down $1.375, in over-the-counter trading.
