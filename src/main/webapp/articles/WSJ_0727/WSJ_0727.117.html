Law -- Legal Beat: GE Is Fined $10 Million in Criminal Case

  • Author: Milo Geyelin and Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 07/27/90

General Electric Co. was ordered to pay a $10 million criminal fine and agreed in a settlement to pay an additional $20 million in civil penalties in connection with fraudulent defense contract billings to the U.S. Army. The criminal fine is the second-largest ever against a defense contractor and the largest against a company accused of fraudulent pricing. Earlier this year, Los Angeles-based Northrop Corp. was fined $17 million after pleading guilty to 34 counts of making false statements to the government involving the company's testing of weapons parts.

The penalty against GE was announced yesterday in a federal district court in Philadelphia, where the company and two suspended employees were convicted of fraud and related charges last February following a 14-week trial. The two suspended employees were fined and both received prison sentences yesterday.

The trial concerned a 1983 GE contract with the U.S. Army and GE's aerospace unit, Management & Technical Services Co., or MATSCO, to produce a mobile battlefield computer system.

Prosecutors alleged that the Fairfield, Conn., concern, the nation's third-largest defense contractor, misrepresented subcontracting costs and concealed millions of dollars in cost reductions from government auditors between 1983 and 1985.

GE was convicted of four counts of mail fraud and 282 counts of filing false claims. Gerald A. Leo, a former purchasing manager, was convicted of four counts of mail fraud and one count of submitting false documents to a government auditor. James Badolato, a former subcontracts manager, was convicted of one count of obstruction of justice for trying to cover up the fraud.

Mr. Leo was sentenced yesterday to 10 months in prison and was fined $15,000. Mr. Badolato received five months in prison and was fined $10,000. Both remain free pending their appeals.

In addition to paying the $10 million criminal fine, GE agreed to settle several pending federal civil suits related to the case for $8.3 million and to pay an additional $11.7 million in connection with several unrelated defense contracting matters pending in eastern Pennsylvania. Prosecutors said the latter also involved improper billings, some of which GE voluntarily disclosed.

Prosecutors and the company said in a joint sentencing recommendation to federal Judge Lowell A. Reed Jr., who presided over the trial, that GE "has taken substantial measures" since 1985 to ensure ethical conduct in all defense contracting.

Nevertheless, prosecutors said that the trial of Messrs. Leo and Badolato produced evidence of complicity by others at the company, though not enough to bring additional criminal charges. "Individuals above Leo and Badolato turned a blind eye and adopted a look-the-other-way mentality," said Assistant U.S. Attorney Nicholas C. Harbist, who helped prosecute the case.

In handing down his sentence against GE, Judge Reed called the company's behavior "reprehensible" and said the fine was a sad commentary on GE's "corporate character." GE's MATSCO unit, in King of Prussia, Pa., was suspended from defense contracting for two months in 1989.

As part of GE's settlement of the cases, the company agreed to forgo all appeals.

REINSURERS win important victory in federal appeals court.

In a typical reinsurance arrangement, insurers give up a portion of the premiums they collect on a policy to a reinsurance company, which promises to take a portion of any losses.

The issue in this case, in the Seventh U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals in Chicago, involved what actions the reinsurer can take if an insurance company with which it is working becomes insolvent.

State statutes governing the liquidation of insolvent insurers generally allow the reinsurer to withhold its loss payments to the insurance company to offset debts the insolvent company owes it. This leaves the insolvent insurer with less money for its liquidation.

But insurance company liquidators have long argued that money owed to an insolvent insurer by reinsurance firms can't be offset by obligations or debts the insolvent firm might have with its reinsurers.

In the decision earlier this week, the federal appeals court sided with the reinsurer and allowed the offset.

A small amount of money is involved in the Illinois case, which stemmed from the liquidation of Reserve Insurance Co., declared insolvent in 1979. But there are millions of dollars at stake in court battles over the same issue involving some of the largest insolvencies in insurance industry history, most notably the liquidation of Mission Insurance Co. in California. Regulators are trying to recover $300 million from Mission's reinsurers.

If courts generally side with the reinsurers as did the Seventh Circuit, the amount of funds available to liquidators to settle the claims of insolvent insurers could be reduced.

"Anything that allows more offsets will reduce the amount of money" eventually going to policyholders, said Deborah Anderson, general counsel for Illinois department that handles the liquidation of insolvent insurers.

The issue of reinsurance offsets with insolvent companies is a hotly contested one in the insurance industry these days. The National Association of Insurance Commissioners at its annual meeting last month adopted a model law that would severely restrict offsets. It's now up to the individual state legislatures to decide if they want to make the association's guidelines part of their state statutes.

The Seventh Circuit is the highest court so far to wrestle with the thorny issue. The California Supreme Court has agreed to hear the case stemming from the Mission insolvency that involves this same offset issue. Reinsurance executives say the California statutes regarding reinsurance offsets are similar to the Illinois regulations.

Ronald A. Jacks, a Chicago attorney who represented the reinsurers in the Illinois case, believes California's highest court "will have to take into account the decision of a highly respected court of appeals."

KENYAN UNREST has forced a change of venue for September's International Bar Association meeting. Instead of meeting in Nairobi, the 12,000-member London-based organization will assemble in New York City Sept. 19-23. William Reece Smith Jr. of Tampa, Fla., president of the IBA, cited safety concerns and the association's disapproval of the recent imprisonment of several Kenyan lawyers as reasons for the switch. Three of those lawyers were released yesterday.
