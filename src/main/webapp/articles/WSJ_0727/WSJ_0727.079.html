Technology: Compaq Computer Profit Rose 24% In Second Quarter

  • Author: Todd Mason
  • Date: 07/27/90

Compaq Computer Corp., riding a crest of European sales, posted a 24% gain in profit for its second quarter. That isn't the kind of torrid growth Compaq investors are accustomed to, but analysts drew comfort from the Houston company's 42% gross profit margin in the period. "The margins were impressive," said James Poyner, an analyst with William K. Woodruff & Co., a Dallas-based institutional brokerage firm. "Revenues were not."

Compaq said it earned $104 million, or $1.18 a share, compared with $84 million, or 98 cents a share, a year earlier. Pershare figures are fully diluted. A one-time investment gain added five cents a share to the 1990 quarter's figure.

Sales rose 19% to $862 million from $722 million.

Compaq also scored practically all of its sales gains in Europe, where it ranks second in computer sales to International Business Machines Corp. in computer sales. While international sales rose 42%, Compaq managed only a 2% improvement at home. For the second consecutive quarter, Compaq sold more personal computers abroad than at home.

The lackluster North American sales raised anew the question of whether Compaq can hold on to its 29% share of domestic retail sales of personal computers. Mr. Poyner noted that both the market and smaller competitors such as Dell Compute Corp. enjoyed faster sales gains. "I'm sure they gave up market share," said Mr. Poyner.

Rod Canion, Compaq's chairman, disputed that assessment. Compaq's unit sales grew faster than revenue because the manufacturer is cutting prices to hold share, he said. Mr. Canion pronounced the surgery a success. "Our market strength is at a high point, and we've introduced seven new products in the last three months," he said.

Compaq's strength in Britain, West Germany and France strained the company's production capacities. In turn, the resulting efficiency gains and slimmer inventories improved Compaq margins. Mr. Canion noted that Compaq established a subsidiary in Finland and appointed dealers in East Germany and Yugoslavia during the quarter.

Compaq also suffered parts shortages that created backlogs for three of its Deskpro 386 models. Those back orders, and the recent revival of U.S. sales, also heartened analysts. "I'm looking for quite strong earnings growth in the second half," said Eugene G. Glazer, an analyst with Dean Witter Reynolds.

Compaq earned $196 million, or $2.23 a share, in the first half, up from $167 million, or $1.94 a share, a year earlier. Sales were $1.7 billion against $1.4 billion.

Compaq shares closed yesterday at $57.875, off $1.50, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.
