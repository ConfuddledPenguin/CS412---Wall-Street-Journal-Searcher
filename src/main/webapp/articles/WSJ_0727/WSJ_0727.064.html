Goodyear Reports $9.4 Million Loss For 2nd Quarter

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 07/27/90

AKRON, Ohio -- Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co. reported a second quarter net loss of $9.4 million, or 16 cents a share, mainly as a result of a North American automobile slowdown, a price war in Europe and economic problems of Brazil. Those problems will continue in the second half, Goodyear said. Sales rose to $2.87 billion from $2.81 billion.

The loss also reflected charges of $21.4 million, or 37 cents a share, partly offset by a tax-loss carry-forward of $600,000, or two cents a share. The charges include costs of restructuring parts of Goodyear's North American tire sales and distribution operations and closing the New Bedford, Mass., roofing systems plant.

In the 1989 second quarter, net income was $27.1 million, or 47 cents a share, after charges of $95 million, or $1.65 a share, partly offset by a tax-loss carry-forward of $4.9 million, or eight cents a share. The charges included losses on the sale of Goodyear's South African subsidiary and of unused pipe owned by the All American Pipeline subsidiary.

Goodyear had expected a loss of up to $10 million for the 1990 second quarter.

Prices for replacement tires in North America were depressed in the latest quarter because the slowdown in automobile production created a tire surplus, according to industry officials. Producers dumped into the replacement market some tires they had produced for Detroit, resulting in widespread discounting. While major producers have announced price increases of 2% to 4% on replacement tires effective next month, it remains to be seen how much of the increase they will collect.

In Europe, Japan's Bridgestone Tire Corp. and Michelin & Co. of France are fighting aggressively for market share, slashing prices in the process. Goodyear, which has a major market position in Europe, is trying to at least hold its own.

In addition, Goodyear has continuing strikes at its Turkish tire plants and is feeling the impact of the economic change by the Brazilian government.

The 1990 quarter also reflects depreciation of the All American Pipeline System and its operating and interest expenses, which were capitalized until the 1989 fourth quarter. Had those items been recognized in the 1989 second period, it would have reduced profit $26.8 million, or 47 cents a share, the company said.

In the first half, profit before extraordinary gains fell to $6.5 million, or 11 cents a share, from $112.3 million, or $1.95 a share, a year earlier. After tax-loss carry-forwards, net was $11.5 million, or 20 cents a share, down from $121.6 million, or $2.11 a share, a year earlier. Sales rose to $5.56 billion from $5.45 billion.
