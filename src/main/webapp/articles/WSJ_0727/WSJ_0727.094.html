Union Asks Ruling Making Greyhound Reinstate Strikers

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 07/27/90

The Amalgamated Transit Union has asked the general counsel of the National Labor Relations Board to seek a court injunction forcing Greyhound Lines Inc. to end the union and the troubled bus company. The ATU already has an unfair labor practices case pending against the company which, if successful, could force Greyhound to reinstate 6,300 striking ATU members and give them back pay totaling an estimated $3 million a month or more. But in a letter this week to NLRB General Counsel Jerry Hunter, union attorney Martin J. Burns said a resolution of the case could take years, and by that time Greyhound's deteriorated financial situation could make a decision in the union's favor worthless. Meanwhile, "striking employees' resources are now depleted and their health care and homes are at risk," Mr. Burns wrote.

An NLRB spokesman said Mr. Hunter was reviewing the union's request for immediate reinstatement but wouldn't comment further.

Mr. Hunter has already obtained an injunction barring the union from the kind of violence that marked the early months of the strike.

In May, he also authorized an NLRB regional office in Milwaukee to issue the unfair labor practices complaint against the company. That complaint, stemming from Greyhound's decision to unilaterally assert its last contract offer, is scheduled to be argued before a federal administrative law judge in September. "We're ready for trial," said Greyhound spokesman George Gravley. "We expect to win."

The unfair labor practices suit and the related request for a court injunction have given the union some leverage in its efforts to wrest control of Greyhound away from

ATU members walked off the job at Greyhound on March 2. Crippled by the subsequent violence, last month Greyhound joined five other units of its parent, Dallas-based GLI Holding Co., in filing for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code. (Under Chapter 11, a company continues to operate under court protection while it devises a plan to repay creditors.)

The ATU has used the prospect of its huge back pay claim to gain a seat on the unsecured creditors committee in Greyhound's Chapter 11 case, where it has been seeking support for its call to immediately reinstate union drivers. In recent weeks, union leaders and advisors are said to have held several meetings with other creditors in an effort to garner backing for a possible union takeover bid. ATU General Counsel Earle Putnam said the union hasn't made such a plan final or obtained the necessary financing. "We're working to see if there is somebody willing to put their money where their mouth is," he said.
