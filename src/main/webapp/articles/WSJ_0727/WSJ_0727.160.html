Mary Kay Seeks To Refinance Part of Debt

  • Author: Hal Lancaster
  • Date: 07/27/90

DALLAS -- Mary Kay Corp. is seeking to refinance about $172 million of the debt it took on to go private in a leveraged buy-out five years ago. The company said it is "actively exploring" the possibility of acquiring a "substantial amount" of the publicly traded, 15% deep-discount debentures it has outstanding and will seek amended terms on the remainder. The refinancing is intended to lower the cosmetics company's debt costs and ease some operating restrictions that came along with the borrowing.

This fall, the company must start calling some of those bonds at a cost of nearly $25 million a year, said a person familiar with the company's refinancing plans. Because of that, he said, company officials considered it an appropriate time to refinance at a lower rate.

First Boston Corp. has been retained by Mary Kay as an adviser on the refinancing.

A spokesman for the company said the refinancing isn't related to the option Mary Kay holds to acquire 1.2 million shares of Avon Products Inc. from financier Irwin Jacobs and Amway Corp. The company has a 45-day option, which began July 5, the spokesman said, but can be extended.

Mary Kay owns a 25% stake in Chartwell Associates Limited Partnership, which in turn is Avon's largest holder. If Mary Kay, or another Chartwell partner buys the 1.2 million-share block, the group would then control 19.8% of Avon's voting shares.

Although the company could exercise the option without the refinancing, said the person familiar with Mary Kay's plans, transactions of that nature would be easier, both because of the anticipated easing of some operating restrictions and because of the improved balance sheet. For the time being, he said, any takeover ambitions on Chartwell's part have been stymied by Avon's anti-takeover defenses, which are triggered when a potential suitor acquires more than 20% of the company's stock.

Mary Kay has been portrayed as an LBO that worked. The company, with its huge sales force, has doubled its operating cash flow since the leveraged buy-out and has nearly doubled its sales. In 1989, the company spokesman said, sales were more than $450 million, up from $255 million in 1986; operating cash flow has risen in that time to $72 million from $35 million. The spokesman said that Mary Kay's sales force also grew in that time, to nearly 200,000 from 140,000.

In a leveraged buy-out, a small group of investors acquires a company in a transaction financed largely by borrowing. Ultimately, the debt is paid with funds generated by the acquired company's operations or sale of its assets.
