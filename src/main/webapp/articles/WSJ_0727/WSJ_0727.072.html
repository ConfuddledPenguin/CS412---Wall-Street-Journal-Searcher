OTC Focus: Market Is Mixed as Technology Shares Slide on Microsoft Profit Announcement

  • Author: Anne Newman and Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 07/27/90

Early gains in over-the-counter stocks evaporated after computer software leader Microsoft cautioned analysts that increased spending next year will crimp its profit margins. Microsoft skidded 2 3/4 to 68 3/4, after first rising to a high of 74. Other technology shares followed suit, pressuring the broader OTC market.

The Nasdaq Composite Index eased 0.02 to 445.45, a 0.1% decline, after gaining more than 1.5 points before the Microsoft outlook was reported. Volume continued to slip as 136.1 million shares changed hands, down from 141.6 million Wednesday. Advancing shares again outpaced decliners, 1,054 to 923; the gap between stocks trading to new lows and new highs narrowed, 94 to 58.

"Anytime you have a company the size of Microsoft getting knocked like that, it affects the general tone of the market," said Nelson Gold, senior vice president of OTC trading at Interstate-Johnson Lane Corp., Atlanta. Kidder Peabody senior OTC trader Peter Coolidge agreed, adding that the reaction to the Microsoft news underscores the nervousness of the market.

Some investors shifted from technology shares into biotechnology stocks, noted James Tarantino, head of OTC trading at Hambrecht & Quist, San Francisco. Among the day's strong biotech issues, Amgen rose 1 to 91 1/2, Centocor jumped 2 1/4 to 43 and Xoma added 3/4 to 25 3/4. Mr. Tarantino said that institutions have been this week's busiest investors, adding that "futures programs seem to have driven retail {investors} out of the market."

Regional stocks have been in favor among institutions trading through Morgan Keegan & Co. in Memphis, according to Hedi Reynolds, managing director of OTC trading there. She said that in the last two days she's seen more orders to buy stocks than sell among larger clients.

Surprising strength in insurance stocks helped shore up the OTC market. The Nasdaq Insurance Index rose nearly 0.8%, or 3.93 to 498.21

There seemed to be no apparent fundamental reason for the advance of the insurance index, according to Mr. Coolidge and Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette insurance analyst David Seifer. Mr. Seifer noted that second quarter insurance underwriting losses seem to have increased while the industry's investment income growth slowed.

Traders said that insurance stocks seemed to gain strength after Ohio Casualty reported net income for the second quarter of $1.14 a share, down from $1.68 a year earlier. However, analysts said that figure wasn't surprising and they were mystified by the advance. Ohio Casualty rose 1 3/4 to 45; St. Paul Cos. added 3/4 to 60; Safeco rose 3/4 to 35 3/8, and 20th Century Industries gained 1 1/8 to 25.

Among the day's most-active stocks, United Banks of Colorado rose 1 3/8 to 21 1/2 after the Denver commercial bank signed a definitive merger agreement with Norwest. Under the agreement, United Banks stockholders will receive 1.22 Norwest shares for each of their United Banks shares.

Other active stocks didn't fare so well.

Wholesale Club plunged 5 5/8 to 18 1/2 on more than 1.1 million shares after the Indianapolis operator of membership merchandise warehouses said it expects to report net income for the second quarter ending in August will range between seven and nine cents a share, down from 15 cents a year earlier.

Intellicall gained 1 to 13 5/8 after the Carrollton, Texas, maker and marketer of pay telephones reported second quarter net income rose to 27 cents a share from 17 cents a year earlier.

Michael Foods climbed 1 1/4 to 19 7/8 after the Minneapolis food processor reported second quarter net income rose to 43 cents a share from 20 cents a year earlier.

Vikonics rebounded 3/4 to 5 3/8. In the first three days of the week, shares of the Secaucus, N.J., security systems concern lost one-third of their market value. Dow Jones Professional Investor Report said Vikonics lost a bid on Monday to provide a security system for the U.S. Treasury Department. John J. Strong, Vikonics vice president of finance and administration, and President John L. Kaufman told DJPIR that contrary to market rumors, the loss of the contract doesn't jeopardize the company's chance of winning other awards.
