Foreign Exchange: Canadian Currency Nears 10-Year High Against Dollar, Backed by High Rates

  • Author: Charles Tenny Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/27/90

NEW YORK -- The Canadian dollar, backed by high interest rates, climbed closer to a 10-year high against the U.S. currency. The U.S. unit was mixed against other major currencies in quiet trading ahead of the scheduled release of U.S. gross national product figures today.

Late in the New York trading day, the Canadian dollar stood at 86.75 U.S. cents, up from 86.69 cents in New York late Wednesday. The Canadian dollar hasn't closed above 87 cents since July 10, 1980, when it finished at 87.14 cents. Yesterday's rise prompted a modest intervention by the Bank of Canada early in the North American trading day.

The U.S. currency slipped to 1.6245 marks in late New York trading from 1.6250 marks late Wednesday. Against the Japanese currency, the dollar was changing hands at 150.37 yen, up from 149.89 yen. Sterling was trading at $1.8140, up from $1.8117.

In Tokyo Friday, the dollar was trading at 150.45 yen soon after the opening; it closed at 150.11 yen in Tokyo Thursday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6253 marks, up from Tokyo's Thursday close of 1.6248 marks.

Commenting on the Canadian currency's rise, David Glowacki, a foreign-exchange trader at National Bank of Detroit, said the market is bound to try to push the currency higher. However, "the fact that we're so close to the {10-year} high is also scaring some people off," he said.

Canada has joined Australia and Britain as countries whose currencies have strengthened largely because of high domestic interest rates.

For weeks, investors unsure of U.S. interest rates and German economic stability have favored short-term investments denominated in Canadian dollars.

In fact, some traders attribute the Canadian dollar's strength mostly to short-term worries about other currencies. These jitters stem from mixed signals on U.S. rates by Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan, some evidence that German monetary union may be less than smooth, the potential effect on Europe of Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev's domestic troubles and uncertainty about the timing of Britain's full entry into the European Monetary System.

Margaret Stur, vice president for foreign exchange at Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce in New York, said she expects the Canadian dollar to stay within a narrow range against the U.S. unit for the near future. She added, "We have no corporate interest, long-term, in selling {U.S.} dollars for Canadian."

The Bank of Canada intervened to damp its currency's rise early in the North American session, traders said, by selling Canadian dollars for U.S. dollars. The traders described the intervention as "modest."

In Europe, the dollar was modestly higher, helped largely by the weakness of the yen against other major currencies.

Traders in London, as in North America, said they were hesistant ahead of the release of preliminary U.S. second-quarter gross national product figures today.

The dollar finished higher in Asia Thursday, ending in Tokyo at 150.11 yen, compared with 149.13 yen Wednesday.

Traders in Tokyo said the dollar will test the 151 yen level over the next few days, with the next major target at 151.50 yen. The Japanese stock market's continued weakness contributed to the yen's decline against most currencies. The yen has also weakened on signs that the Bank of Japan won't raise interest rates in the short term.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $369.30 an ounce, down $2.70. Estimated volume was a moderate four million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $369.05 an ounce.
