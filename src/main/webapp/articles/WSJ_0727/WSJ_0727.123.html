Law: Tobacco warnings Don't Pre-Empt Claims, Court Rules

  • Author: Kathleen Deveny and Alix M. Freedman
  • Date: 07/27/90

In the first sign of hope for tobacco liability plaintiffs, the New Jersey Supreme Court ruled yesterday that warnings on cigarette packages don't protect tobacco makers from product liability lawsuits. The decision, which was hailed by smoking foes and plaintiffs' attorneys, opens the door for a review of the issue by the U.S. Supreme Court. It also marks a sharp break from previous circuit court rulings. In recent years, five federal courts and the Minnesota Supreme Court have ruled that the 1966 federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act -- which requires warning labels on cigarette packs and in cigarette advertisements -- protects the tobacco companies from liability.

"This is precedent setting," says Marc Edell, the plaintiff's attorney, who also represented Antonio Cipollone in another landmark tobacco case last year. "This is the first time a state supreme court has held that cigarette warnings don't preclude plaintiffs from suing manufacturers."

Yesterday's decision stems from a lawsuit filed by Claire Dewey against Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., American Brands Inc. and RJ Reynolds Tobacco Co. in 1982. Her husband, Wilfred Dewey, died of lung cancer at age 49, after smoking Camels, Tareytons and Viceroys for 40 years. The New Jersey Supreme Court ruling allows Mrs. Dewey to proceed with the product liability case.

In the 59-page opinion, a majority of the judges found that Congress didn't intend to "immunize" cigarette manufacturers against suits alleging that they had failed to warn consumers of the risks of smoking. If that had been the intent, Congress "knew how to do so with unmistakable specificity," the judges said.

Tobacco analysts played down the significance of the case, noting that the decision has no immediate financial implications because Mrs. Dewey has yet to win her case. Nonetheless, tobacco stocks were buffeted yesterday. In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, American Brands closed at $66.25 a share, down $3.625, and Philip Morris Cos. finished at $47.25 a share, down $1.75. American depositary receipts of B.A.T Industries, the parent of Brown & Williamson, fell 12.5 cents, to $10.875 a share, in American Stock Exchange composite trading. RJ Reynolds is privately held.

"This is a psychological negative," said Diana Temple, an analyst at Salomon Bros. "But it's only one negative decision in an overwhelmingly positive flow of liability decisions." Fifty-three cases are pending against the industry, down from a peak of 157 in 1986.

Longer term, however, the implications could prove significant. Two of the three tobacco companies named in the Dewey suit -- Brown & Williamson and RJ Reynolds -- indicated they will consider appealing the decision to the U.S. Supreme Court. Some legal experts said the high court would be likely to accept the case.

Even if the tobacco companies accept the Dewey decision, there are six cases pending in New Jersey or federal court that would potentially allow plaintiffs to bring the matter before the Supreme Court.

A high court ruling against the tobacco industry would certainly trigger a flood of new tobacco liability suits, according to some experts. "If the conflict is resolved against the tobacco industry, this will be very serious," predicted Calvert Crary, managing director of Labe Simpson & Co. in New York. "The plaintiffs will win these kinds of cases all day long."

For their part, attorneys who represent the tobacco industry conceded that the justices in the Dewey case had departed radically from previous rulings. But Martin London, an attorney for Brown & Williamson, summed up the prevailing sentiment, describing the case as "aberrant." He said the case isn't precedent setting because federal courts have so consistently found that the federal warning law protects tobacco companies.

"This isn't a chink in the industry's armour because of its relatively limited applicability," added David Kentoff, an attorney at the Washington, D.C.-based firm of Arnold & Porter, which represents Philip Morris. "The decision only applies to cases brought in state courts in New Jersey."

In another positive development for tobacco plaintiffs, the New Jersey justices also held that cases filed before 1987 aren't affected by a state law that makes it more difficult to file cigarette liability suits. The law shields tobacco manufacturers from claims that cigarettes are "defective" products because of their health hazards.

For smoking critics, the Dewey case is especially welcome because it comes at a time when tobacco plaintiffs have suffered a series of crushing defeats. Earlier this year, a Minnesota court dismissed a similar case against RJ Reynolds, ruling that the 1966 labeling act preempted claims that cigarette makers had failed to adequately warn smokers of health hazards.

In 1988, a New Jersey jury awarded $400,000 to Mr. Cipollone, whose wife had died of lung cancer, in the first defeat for the tobacco industry, but the verdict was later thrown out by a federal appeals court in Philadelphia.

"We've been watching one court after another come out with the wrong decision," said Richard Daynard, chairman of the tobacco products liability project at Northeastern University, an anti-smoking activist group. "This is an 11th hour reprieve for tobacco liability plaintiffs."
