REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Congress's Wheel of Fortune

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/27/90

With all the talk of "needed revenue," we should watch whether the Washington budget summiteers propose ending the cellular-phone lottery scam. In the past six years, Congress has forced the Federal Communications Commission to give away an estimated $30 billion to $40 billion worth of licenses to operate cellular-phone systems. The winners are a few favored corporations and speculators; the losers are the taxpayers. The cellular-phone industry is booming. The number of subscribers could grow from 3.5 million now to 20 million by decade's end, once car makers decide to install cellular phones as regular features. You'd think Congress would have charged for these license gold mines by holding an auction. After all, it already charges for mineral, grazing and timber rights. Auctions for offshore oil and gas leases alone have brought in $55 billion over the past 30 years.

Instead, the cellular lottery scam came about like this. In 1984 Congress abandoned the awarding of cellular rights through arbitrary "public interest" hearings. But its 60-year-old theory on the electromagnetic band remains: It's public property and can't be sold. Rep. John Dingell, chairman of the House Commerce Committee, pushes through frequent amendments barring any use of auctions. So instead, the FCC gives away spectrum space by lottery.

The cellular lotteries led to perverse windfalls for a few, lucky individuals. Rural licenses are now worth upward of $100 a person. Not surprisingly, some 280,000 applications flooded in just for the 428 rural markets. But "applications engineering" firms charged only $5,000 for someone to enter every lottery in partnership with others, and those applicants stood a 50-50 chance of winning part of a license. Some congressional offices kindly helped favored constituents through the maze of paper work.

Of course, most winners have no interest or expertise in operating a cellular system. They sell their winning "tickets" to large companies building nationwide cellular networks. Many lottery winners realized instant windfalls. One couple in Ovid, Michigan, sold a license to McCaw Communications for $4 million, and then won another worth $10 million.

Both the Reagan and Bush administrations have pleaded with Congress to end such giveaways to a well-heeled, lucky few. Last year, the Bush FCC proposed using auctions to assign six currently unassigned megahertz of the spectrum to cellular users. The sales would bring in at least $3.3 billion over two years. Congress refused to act, so the FCC recently had to assign four of the unused megahertz for air-to-ground telephone users. It will collect zero revenue from them.

Congress may be about to repeat its folly. In June, a House committee approved a bill by Rep. Dingell to make available for cellular use 200 megahertz now reserved for the government. The potential revenue from an auction system is enormous, but Rep. Dingell still doesn't think it's worth giving up political control of the spectrum.

Other countries aren't so foolish. In New Zealand, a left-wing Labor government recently privatized the spectrum by leasing it to firms on a long-term basis. This gives lessees a virtual property right; they can, for example, use their part of the spectrum for whatever use they think best.

It's now clear that the technical justifications for government ownership of the electromagnetic spectrum are specious and outdated. Congress's cellular-phone giveaway makes it imperative to begin questioning government control of the airwaves on basic grounds of fiscal common sense.
