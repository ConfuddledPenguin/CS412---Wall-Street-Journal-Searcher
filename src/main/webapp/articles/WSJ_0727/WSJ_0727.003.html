Letters to the Editor: Procter & Gamble's President Responds

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/27/90

I must comment on your July 10 article concerning CEO succession, which importantly involved Procter & Gamble and me. This article represented a sad day for journalism in general, and for The Wall Street Journal as far as I am concerned. I say that from a professional standpoint, not just because of my personal involvement. We knew this story was being written. We had been told by your reporter that she had heard from several sources that I had interviewed for and had been among the final contenders for the Campbell Soup CEO job. When I learned about this I was on a trip in the Far East. Gerry Gendell, head of our public-affairs division, called the reporter personally to relay our conversation on this subject. We told her the reports she had received were "flat wrong." I didn't have one conversation, or one contact about the Campbell position with anyone. Moreover, we confirmed this in writing.

Your staff knows our company. We do not lie. At a minimum I would have expected, with this flat denial, a professional job would have been done of finding out what the truth was. I feel sure that a contact with the appropriate senior people at Campbell could have revealed that it was not true.

Beyond this, there are statements in the article about my personal feelings that are absolutely wrong. Of course, there is no way for a reporter who doesn't know me or my associates closely to be aware of this. But what valid purpose does it serve to speculate on this?

We try to work with the Journal in an open way. We gave you the right input on this story. It is depressing to me that despite that input, unsubstantiated rumors were allowed to control the day -- and that the appropriate research was not done to come to a proper determination of the facts.

John E. Pepper

President

Procter & Gamble Co.

Cincinnati
