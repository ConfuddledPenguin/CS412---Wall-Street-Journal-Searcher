Dividend News: PepsiCo Inc. Splits Shares 3-for-1 After Stock Run-Up

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 07/27/90

PepsiCo Inc. declared a 3-for-1 stock split. The move followed a doubling of its stock price in the past 19 months. The stock price for the snack food, soft drink and restaurant company, which has been one of Wall Street's darlings, had increased about 25% this year alone.

After a brief halt in trading to announce the split, the news yesterday pushed Pepsi up another $1.375, to close at $79.75, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

The run-up in Pepsi's stock largely reflects the company's strong and reliable earnings growth. For nearly four years, the Purchase, N.Y., concern has reported lockstep quarterly gains of at least 20% in per-share operating earnings. Investors have also been attracted by the recession-resistant nature of Pepsi's businesses and the company's prospects for expansion in fledgling markets overseas.

What's more, Pepsi's stock had gained a bit of momentum earlier this year on speculation that food and tobacco concern Philip Morris Cos. might want to acquire Pepsi. In March, a business magazine tossed Pepsi into a smorgasbord of companies considered to be appetizing acquisition candidates for cash-rich Philip Morris. Pepsi and Philip Morris won't talk about the speculation, which analysts and investors have since dismissed as unfounded.

Pepsi, which hasn't split its stock since May 1986, said the latest split "signals our interest in individual shareholders -- the men and women who buy our products." Likewise, when Coca-Cola Co.'s stock got a little pricey earlier this year, the Atlanta-based soft-drink company itself declared a 2-for-1 stock split.

The Pepsi split, which is payable Sept. 4 to stock of record Aug. 10, will triple the number of authorized shares to 1.8 billion. As of June, Pepsi had about 266.8 million common shares outstanding. On a post-split basis, Pepsi declared a quarterly dividend of 10 cents a share, payable Sept. 30 to stock of record Sept. 14. The company has been paying 30 cents a share quarterly, indicating no change in the payout rate.

The split won't require approval from shareholders under the laws of North Carolina, the state where the company is incorporated and where a turn-of-the-century pharmacist whipped up the first Pepsi-Cola soft drink.
