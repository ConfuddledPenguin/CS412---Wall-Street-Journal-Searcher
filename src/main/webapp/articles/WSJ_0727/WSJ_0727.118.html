LBO Funds Hang Out the For Sale Sign --- Strong Stock Prices Provide Incentive

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 07/27/90

NEW YORK -- The 1980s leveraged buy-out boom is a memory, and now Wall Street's big LBO funds are rushing to cash out with a burst of big-ticket sales of companies in their portfolios. In the past two months alone, LBO powerhouse Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. agreed to sell its Beatrice Co. to ConAgra Inc. for $1.34 billion and Motel 6 L.P. to France's Accor S.A. for $1.3 billion.

In April, KKR sold 10% of Safeway Stores Inc. to the public in a $112.5 million stock offering.

And last week, Forstmann Little & Co. agreed to sell Moen Inc. to American Brands Inc. for $975 million.

That could be only the tip of the iceberg. From 1981 to 1989, LBO funds and their pension-fund investors took hundreds of companies private in a buy-out binge totaling more than $250 billion.

One reason the companies are so ripe for sale, either to the public or corporate acquirers, is that LBO funds are anxious to sell to generate returns for their investors. That's unlike publicly traded corporations, where managements often resist takeover bids to avoid losing their jobs.

Some buy-out funds executives make no bones about their keen interest in selling some of their holdings in coming months.

Brian Little, a founding partner of Forstmann Little, says buy-out funds typically "start thinking about taking a profit" within a few years, once they have made any planned changes in a company and paid down buy-out debt sufficiently to generate a healthy return.

Now that Forstmann has agreed to sell Moen, he said: "We've got two or three other companies which we think over the next 12 months could also be sold. Our business is acquiring assets, helping make them worth more, and selling them."

Wall Street's investment bankers are licking their chops over more deals in the offing. "Every investment bank has a list," says Steven Rattner, general partner at Lazard Freres & Co.

One reason for buy-out firms to strike now is the still-buoyant stock market, which despite this week's weakness remains within 80 points of the 3000 mark on the Dow Jones Industrial Average. That should enable buy-out fund operators to seek substantial prices for their treasure trove of companies, whether in a public offering or from a corporate buyer.

However, what still isn't clear is whether there are sufficient buyers to go around. Stock-market investors have been penalizing companies that still carry a heavy debt load, as they did with KKR's offering of Safeway shares. KKR had to slash the offering price 22% from the midrange of its original estimated sale price.

But whether or not the buyers are ready, there is a bulge in the number of private companies ready for sale. Scott Newquist, head of investment banking at Kidder, Peabody & Co., says buy-outs typically generate the highest annual rates of return for their investors over a three-to-five-year period. The flurry of sales is occurring now, he says, because "we're at a time three to five years from when a lot of buy-outs were done."

James Burke, president of Merrill Lynch Capital Partners, a leveraged buy-out fund, says: "If you look at the burst of LBOs that were done in 1984-1987, there are a lot of deals that should be ripe for the plucking."

However, other fund operators argue -- perhaps partly as a negotiating posture -- that they aren't in a hurry to sell. "You may see some more {sales by buy-out funds} because the stock market is relatively strong," says KKR partner Henry Kravis. "But if we don't get the right price, we're not under the gun to do anything."

One LBO manager maintains: "If you talk to our partners and you said to them, would you rather get a quick 60% to 70% annual return in one or two years, or would you rather have long-term annual returns of 30%, my guess is they'd pick the 30%."

Joseph L. Rice, president of LBO firm Clayton & Dubilier, attributes the recent burst of sales to "just chance." He doesn't think there is any particular holding period that will maximize investor returns. "If you have a business that is performing well, sooner or later something good is going to happen to you," he says.

Nevertheless, Lazard's Mr. Rattner says buy-out funds have good reason to seize an opportunity to cash out. "For people who want to raise new funds in this suspicious environment in which we live, it's important to show investors profits. And one thing you certainly learn watching the events of the last year or two: things that at one instant look like spectacular deals can quickly look not so spectacular."

One buy-out fund executive, who asked not to be identified, notes that Forstmann and KKR have spent more than half of their current buy-out funds and may want to post some hefty return rates on some buy-outs as they prepare to seek new investment dollars.

KKR declined to comment. Forstmann's Mr. Little said that any potential plans to raise more money "are not a factor" in the timing of company sales.

In seeking buyers, fund operators' first choice is generally U.S. or foreign corporations with deep pockets. Leon Kalvaria, a managing director at First Boston Corp., says that corporate acquirers will generally pay a higher price for any given company than a public stock offering would attract. The reason, he says, is "a strategic buyer is willing to pay a premium price" to gain control of an entire company.

But another category of buyer -- other LBO funds -- has faded, due to weakness in the market of high-risk, high-yield junk bonds whose sale typically used to finance about 30% of a buy-out. After KKR bought Beatrice in 1986, several of the food company's larger pieces were sold to other LBO funds, notes Lazard's Mr. Rattner.
