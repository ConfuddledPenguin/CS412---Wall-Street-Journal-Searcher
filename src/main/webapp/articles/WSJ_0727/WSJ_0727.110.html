FDIC Moves To Sell Stake In Chicago Bank

  • Author: Jeff Bailey
  • Date: 07/27/90

CHICAGO -- Continental Bank Corp. moved another step toward full private ownership as the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. approved a shelf registration for the sale of its 26% stake in Continental. The move follows an apparently unsuccessful effort by the FDIC's investment bankers at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. to sell the stake to an institutional investor or another banking concern -- including First Chicago Corp., Continental's rival here.

A banking official said that Smith Barney recently approached First Chicago's chairman and chief executive officer, Barry F. Sullivan, and tried to interest him in the stock, but that he declined. Mr. Sullivan, who over the years has expressed a keen interest in combining the city's two biggest banks, wouldn't comment, nor would Smith Barney.

Continental announced its intention to make the shelf registration on the sixth anniversary of its $4.5 billion federal bailout. A spokeswoman said the actual filing will be made by the middle of next week, covering all of the FDIC's common and preferred, which together equal about 14 million of Continental's 53.7 million common shares outstanding.

The sale of the stock, however, could well have to wait months or longer because bank stocks have been depressed. Continental stock closed yesterday at $13.50 a share, about $1.50 lower than an offer the FDIC refused early last month.

The offer was made by Continental, some of its top officers and some outside investors.

Last fall, the FDIC refused to let Continental sell the shares in a public offering when the stock was trading at around $20.

Since those two events, Continental's chairman and chief executive, Thomas C. Theobald, has been critical of the FDIC. After his offer was turned down last month, he said he was "dumbfounded" and called the FDIC's continued ownership of Continental shares "a wholly unwarranted role for a regulator."

Yesterday, in a statement, he was more conciliatory, however, calling the approval of the filing "a milestone with both financial and psychological significance for us."

Some customers still limit their dealings with Continental because of the FDIC's ownership, and the Federal Reserve Board has indicated it won't look kindly on acquisitions by Continental as long as it is a partial ward of the government.

The FDIC wouldn't say at what price it will sell, and any public offering of the shares or sale to an investor group is still up to the agency.

At yesterday's price, the 26% stake had an indicated value of about $185 million. The agency previously sold to the public, to a Continental employee stock ownership plan and to Continental itself common and preferred stock for a combined $1.06 billion.
