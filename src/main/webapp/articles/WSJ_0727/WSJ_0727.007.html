Letters to the Editor: Hussein Takes Some Royal Liberties

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/27/90

The June 25 article "King Hussein Fears Mideast Strife Could Flare Into War, Chides U.S" by Karen Elliott House and Geraldine Brooks is a further example of royal disinformation and majestic misconceptions. If we assume that the king has not forgotten his ill-fated military alliance in 1967 with Egypt and Syria (despite clear messages from Israel that if he stayed out of the hostilities not an inch of the territories he then controlled would be touched) and the demagogy of the Arab leadership that succeeded in convincing itself of Israel's imminent demise, surely this "longtime U.S. ally and Arab moderate" should not resort to such provocative history-twisting remarks. It should be noted that, as in 1967, there is no warmongering on the Israeli side. We are indeed involved with the great event of Jewish immigration, mainly from the Soviet Union, and the immigrants' successful absorption into our free society. Peace is, therefore, our basic and absolute need and desire. We have never taken a belligerent attitude toward those who have accepted our presence. If "the region is approaching the same state of hair-trigger tension," such tension comes from the king's best friend and ally -- the Iraqi president, Saddam Hussein.

Combine this with the recent announcement of the setting up of a joint Iraqi-Jordanian air squadron and other joint military maneuvers between these two countries and one can perhaps begin to understand which path the king has started to tread.

"With a photo of the Iraqi president just behind his {King Hussein's} shoulder," the U.S. and the entire world should recall that old adage: Tell me who your friends are and I'll tell you who you are. Your majesty, take down that picture of Saddam Hussein and replace it with that of Anwar Sadat. This would symbolize a much more fruitful expression of your concern for the future welfare of your people and the entire Middle East.

J. Eshel

J.J. Boock

Tel Aviv

---

Since its establishment in 1948, Israel has been trying to reach a comprehensive peace agreement with its Arab neighbors. In contrast, 20 Arab countries have refused to negotiate with or recognize Israel.

The 1967 war into which Israel was pulled saw Israelis at a moment of fear, despair and extreme anxiety for their fate. Arab countries, on the other side, coordinated their aggression toward Israel by closing vital water channels, mobilizing their armies and threatening Israel for months before the war. Israel has never talked of starting a war. As to the Arabs, historical documents and speeches attest to belligerent statements made by Gamal Abdel Nasser (then president of Egypt) and King Hussein of Jordan right before the 1967 war. King Hussein must be a hypocrite to say that the Arabs "fell into a trap." He should read his own 1967 speeches, to remember how he predicted the Israelis would be pushed into the sea.

King Hussein's preoccupations are not the poor Jewish immigrants who are only trying to escape anti-Semitic beatings and who plan to live in peace. Since practically none want to settle in the territories, Soviet immigrants pose no danger to Arabs who search for peace. They pose an obstacle only to those who dream of destroying the Jewish state.

King Hussein is acting out of self-fear. The land where he "reigns" is about 70% Palestinian (not 50% as your reporters write), and he realizes that the Palestinian people are growing unhappy with his authoritative regime.

What is the true formula for disaster? King Hussein's instability, and his desperate alliance with Saddam Hussein, the butcher of Iraq.

Daniel Lubetzky

Tokyo

---

King Hussein of Jordan is at it again. As he has done in the past, he rewrites history, blames Israel and the U.S. for Middle East problems, conjures up irrational fears that Israel will expel the Palestinians from the territories, and refuses to accept his own responsibility for continuing conflict in the region.

He has the gall to say that Israel lured the Arabs into the 1967 war. Arab aggression against Israel was a constant since the Jewish state's creation in 1948 and reached a high point in 1967, including Egypt's closure of the Straits of Tiran, Syria's massing of troops and Jordan's attack on Israel despite Israeli pleas to King Hussein to stay out of the war. King Hussein knows his decision to attack was a historic blunder; rather than face up to that fact, he continues to blame the party he attacked, which had the nerve to defend itself and win the war.

As usual, the king criticizes U.S. support for Israel and suggests that such support could lead the region to war. He omits to say that the U.S. continues to be a staunch ally of the king and that if there is a threat of war in the region it comes from the bellicosity of Iraq's leader Saddam Hussein and the massing of ever-more-sophisticated arms -- missiles, chemical and biological weapons -- by the Arab world.

King Hussein conveniently ignores the fact that, after all these years, he still has not recognized Israel, established relations or moved toward peace. His words indicate that he isn't quite the moderate that he is widely perceived to be.

Abraham H. Foxman

National Director

Anti-Defamation League of B'nai B'rith

New York
