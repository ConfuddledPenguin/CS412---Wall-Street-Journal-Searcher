Coast Savings Posts 2nd-Quarter Loss Amid Fears About California Real Estate

  • Author: Charles McCoy
  • Date: 07/27/90

The national real estate virus appears to be finally taking hold in California, and that is giving bankers and thrift executives all over the country the chills. California's gravity-defying economy in recent years has made its real estate market the most robust in the country. Lately, with New England, the Southwest and several other regional markets in the tank, it has been about the only robust one left. Big California banks and thrifts, as well as many lenders across the nation who have heavy exposure to California real estate, have been dreading the prospect of a crack in California, which would leave them with practically no good markets anywhere in the country.

Fissures have now become evident. Yesterday Coast Savings Financial Inc. reported a net loss of $28.7 million, or $1.89 a share, for the second quarter. The loss reflected increased loan-loss reserves, primarily for bad assets outside California but including some California problem assets too. And Great American Bank FSB, as expected, reported a second-quarter loss of $107.9 million, or $4.49 a share. Its troubled assets ballooned to $1.8 billion from $929.2 million as of March 31, in part because of a "slowdown" in California real estate, Great American said.

That comes on the heels of several recent disclosures by financial institutions of mounting California woes. CalFed Inc. cited deterioration in its California portfolio as the main cause of its $53 million, or $2.10 a share, second-quarter loss.

HomeFed Inc., which reported a second-quarter loss of $108.2 million, or $5.04 a share, said 37% of its problem assets now are in California, a much higher percentage than in years past.

Banks have been feeling it too. First Interstate Bancorp, trying to fight back from a string of surprise losses and blunders that have badly weakened it over the past three years, told analysts last week that its nonperforming real estate loans would probably increase in the second half of the year because of growing trouble in California real estate.

Such news has pounded financial institution stocks in recent days. First Interstate, for example, skidded $2.25 a share last Friday to a New York Stock Exchange close of $34.75. It lost 13% of its market value in the two days after the analysts' meeting. Yesterday, it closed at $34.75, down 25 cents.

Also yesterday, Moody's Investor Service Inc. issued a special report predicting softening in major sectors of the California market over the next two years. It said significant declines in asset value could appear by late this year. Moody's recently lowered its debt ratings on San Francisco-based Wells Fargo & Co. and Los Angeles-based Security Pacific Corp., citing potential for such declines.

"You can no longer be sanguine about the California real estate situation," says Donald Crowley, an analyst with Keefe Bruyette & Woods. "It is not good news for lenders."

California isn't on the verge of a Texas-style collapse; its economy remains the most buoyant in the nation. But the state has been suffering from a gradually worsening commercial real estate glut for about two years that now is beginning to produce some notable vacancy rates.

In Los Angeles, for example, Mr. Crowley says, the office vacancy rate is about 15%, with enough office space scheduled to hit the market in the next few years to produce vacancy rates of 20% to 25%. Orange County already has a vacancy rate of 22%, up two to three percentage points in the past two years. By comparison, Phoenix, at the epicenter of Arizona's real estate tremors, has a vacancy rate of roughly 29%.

Reflecting concern about the overbuilt commercial market, Security Pacific's Jack Hancock, executive vice president of the bank's real estate industries group, says Security Pacific anticipates no significant growth in its $7 billion commercial real estate portfolio.

More worrisome, the residential market, heretofore seemingly on a one-way ticket skyward, has crested. Home prices, which in some areas of the state have doubled or tripled in the 1980s, dropped statewide for the third month in a row after five years of almost uninterrupted increases. House resales in June fell to an annualized rate of 443,451, their lowest level since 1986.

Mr. Hancock says he believes this is "an aberration" and that the housing market is still strong. Nonetheless, he says, "Clearly, we have slowed down in California."

Economic factors may further damp the market in coming years, especially if defense spending continues to wind down. About 8% of California's jobs are in the defense industry. Recently, McDonnell Douglas Corp. announced its intention to eliminate 14,000 to 17,000 jobs, mainly in Southern California, because of reduced defense spending.
