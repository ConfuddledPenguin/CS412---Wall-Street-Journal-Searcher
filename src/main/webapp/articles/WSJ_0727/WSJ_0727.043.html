Suffering From a Lack of Water, They Start Plumbing the Depths

  • Author: David J. Jefferson
  • Date: 07/27/90

Fish don't normally float in toilet bowls until they're off to meet their Maker. But commodes soon may make cozy homes for marine life, thanks to some ecologically minded officials in Southern California who want to dump old water-guzzling toilets into the Pacific Ocean.

Worried about the state's continuing drought, water districts have been urging residents to install ultralow-flush toilets to save water; some districts are even offering rebates to residents who switch. But that has led to another environmental dilemma as the older toilets are dumped into already backed-up landfills.

So the bureaucrats came up with the idea for toilet-bowl reefs.

"Porcelain fixtures are just as good as rock as far as the fish are concerned. They're funny-shaped devices with lots of places for them to hide," says John Grant, a marine biologist with the U.S. Department of Fish and Game, which is considering proposals from the city of Santa Monica and the Helix Water District near San Diego to dump toilets into Santa Monica Bay and off Point Loma, San Diego. He says a pile of 300 commodes dumped illegally into Santa Monica Bay a few years ago has become "a great place to dive for lobsters."

Harold Ball, president of the Helix Water District, says toilets would make ideal reefs for the very reason they're terrible in landfills: "A toilet literally lasts forever. . . . It's identical material to a dinner plate. When they went down to the Titanic, they found the dinner plates were in perfect condition."

Flush with suggestions for getting rid of old toilets, Helix Water District is also testing a plan to crush commodes and use them to fill potholes. Goleta Water District, near Santa Barbara, which abandoned the idea of a toilet-bowl reef because of cost and other issues, is using ground-up toilets as roadbed.

With artificial reefs already made of sunken ships, old cars and rubber tires, "I can't see why using those toilets wouldn't be very acceptable," says James Stewart, a diving safety officer with Scripps Institution of Oceanography in La Jolla, Calif.

Even so, officials are bracing for a swirl of controversy over their toilet-bowl reefs. "If you look at it aesthetically, there are some troubling aspects," concedes Fish and Game's Mr. Grant. Though giant kelp would camouflage the commodes in a matter of years, "There's still a stigma attached to a toilet bowl."
