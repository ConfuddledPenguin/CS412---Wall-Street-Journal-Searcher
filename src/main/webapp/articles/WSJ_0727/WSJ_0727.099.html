Politics & Policy: House Approves Reprimand Motion For Rep. Frank

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 07/27/90

WASHINGTON -- The House voted to reprimand Rep. Barney Frank after rejecting a Republican-backed motion calling for a stiffer censure penalty for the Massachusetts liberal on charges related to his past relationship with a male prostitute. The final 408-18 roll call upheld the bipartisan recommendations last week of the House Ethics Committee, but the issue first split the chamber emotionally and reflected more partisanship than in any ethics case in the past decade.

The censure motion, which failed 287-141, was opposed by all but 12 Democrats, while Republicans lined up almost 3 to 1 in favor of the tougher penalty. "It was the homosexual issue," said Rep. Charles Wilson, a conservative Texas Democrat, "and in my opinion a Republican effort to say Democrats are more supportive of alternative lifestyles."

As the fall elections approach, the debate underscored the politics of ethics issues this year, and both parties are competing for the high ground in meeting promises of campaign-finance revision. After weeks on the defensive, House Democrats are increasingly confident of offering a plan to cut contributions by political action committees and wealthy individuals, and provide public matching funds of as much as $100,000 for candidates who accept spending limits.

Negotiations will continue today, but major elements include a provision that PACs can account for no more than 40% of a candidate's contributions, compared with a 50% cap previously discussed. The maximum individual donation, now $1,000, would be cut in half. To encourage small contributors, the measure would include a tax credit for the first $50 donation by an individual. And though critics say PACs will soon adjust to the new rules, one option calls for a $55,000 limit on how much an individual lawmaker can take from those groups, such as business and pro-Israel PACs, that now draw a greater proportion of their funds from large individual donors.

The long investigation of Mr. Frank, touched off by press reports last year, focused on his relationship with Steven Gobie from 1985 to 1987, when the congressman paid for sex with Mr. Gobie and subsequently hired him as a household employee and driver. Mr. Frank, an acknowledged homosexual, had used his position to fix parking tickets for the prostitute and prepared a 1986 memo supportive of reduced probation for Mr. Gobie, a convicted felon.

The committee concluded that Mr. Frank "knew or should have known" that the memo might be communicated to law enforcement officials, and the panel argued that the misleading statements in the document "could be perceived as an attempt to use political influence to affect the outcome of Mr. Gobie's probation."

"That the memorandum apparently had no such effect does not detract from the inappropriateness of communicating known misleading statements," the panel said.

Within the GOP leadership, the drive to impose censure reflected the pressure of Minority Whip Newt Gingrich, who mocked the notion of the lesser penalty allows Mr. Frank to keep his subcommittee chairmanship on the House Judiciary Committee. "People are looking for a guidepost," the Georgia conservative said. But Democrats argued that the GOP sought only a social issue for itself at a time when the party could be forced to vote for taxes before the November campaign.

More broadly, the Ethics Committee had an institutional stake in seeing its recommendations upheld. And after handling politically sensitive investigations of former Speaker James Wright as well as Mr. Gingrich himself, the committee leadership saw yesterday's vote as a test of its own strength.

"The case boils down to who do you trust," committee Chairman Julian Dixon (D., Calif.) said bluntly. And perhaps the strongest defense came from a third-term Republican from Iowa. "We don't enshrine traditional family values by damning individuals whose lifestyles are different than our own," Rep. Fred Grandy said. "We are here to prosecute, not to persecute."
