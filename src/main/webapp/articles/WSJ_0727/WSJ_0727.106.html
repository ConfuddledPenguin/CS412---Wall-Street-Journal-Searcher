Health: IBM Moves To Cap Mental Health Costs

  • Author: Ron Winslow
  • Date: 07/27/90

International Business Machines Corp. named a small health care company to handle its mental health program, reflecting corporate America's search for ways to contain spiraling psychiatric costs. The computer giant picked American PsychManagement to set up a network of 10,000 psychiatrists, psychologists and social workers, as well as hospitals and other treatment centers, to serve IBM's 700,000 U.S. employees, retirees and dependents.

The move to separate mental health and substance abuse benefits from other health benefits reflects an emerging trend among companies that have found psychiatric costs particularly troublesome in the battle to contain overall health costs.

IBM is the latest company to arrange for a network of preferred health care providers and encourage employees through financial incentives to use them. The networks are intended to enable companies to manage better their employees' use of health services and to obtain discounts from doctors and hospitals in return for a designated flow of patients. Last week, American Telephone & Telegraph Co. announced the establishment of a system of networks covering all health care for its U.S. work force.

More and more companies say that "the only way we are really going to be able to offer health benefits is if we manage them well," said Veronica Vaccaro, manager of mental health promotion at the Washington Business Group on Health.

Although terms of IBM's move into managed mental health care weren't disclosed, Ms. Vaccaro described it as "the biggest deal dollar-wise" yet for a managed care vendor. "We expect this will {continue to} happen as companies struggle with ways to provide benefits and also manage their costs."

While overall health costs at IBM have increased at a 10% to 11% annual rate over the past few years, according to W.E. Burdick, senior vice president for personnel, mental health and substance abuse costs have soared at a 20% rate. "It's the single highest cost escalation area of all of our {health benefit} offerings," he said.

Among companies with 5,000 or more employees, mental health and substance abuse costs soared 47% last year over 1988, according to a survey by A. Foster Higgins & Co., a New York benefits consultant.

That's one reason some companies have decided to break mental health and substance abuse out of health benefit programs. Those problems "are dramatically different from other acute care medical issues," said Daniel C. Smith, director of employee assistance and human risk management resources at McDonnell Douglas Corp., which has developed innovative mental health treatment programs. "The treatment of these conditions individually is far less predictable than it is for acute health problems and there is so much individual variance in response to treatment. It doesn't make sense for us to try to make a square peg fit in a round hole."

Still, says Foster Higgins, just 5% of all companies, and 10% of companies with more than 10,000 employees, have set up separate preferred provider networks to manage mental health costs.

IBM, based in Armonk, N.Y., coupled the change in its mental health plan with other benefits revisions, including new programs for long-term care insurance and assistance with catastrophic health problems such as spinal injuries, organ transplants, and premature births. Mr. Burdick said the overall intent is to meet new benefits needs of employees in these areas while cutting annual growth in costs to single digits. IBM spends about $3,400 a year per employee on health care, or more than $750 million.

American PsychManagement, a unit of closely held Value Health Inc., Avon, Conn., was selected from more than a dozen vendors who bid on the contract, which Value Health said was "in the tens of millions of dollars."

Moving to managed care is a "big departure from IBM's culture," said Bryan Lane, a principal with Towers Perrin, a benefit consultant that helped the company design the plan. Although participation in the network is voluntary, the company is recommending providers for its employees, breaking from past practice of completely free choice. But Mr. Burdick said many employees are eager for recommendations, especially for mental health problems. He said response to the program, announced this week, is already positive.

The mental health plan, as well as other benefits changes, were designed after the company made numerous surveys of its work force. That groundwork, as well as financial incentives, should help persuade employees to use it, the company says.

For instance, for those in the network, the company will pay 100% of inpatient hospitalization for an unlimited period after an annual deductible. For those seeking care outside the network, coverage drops to 80% of hospitalization for up to 90 days a year. For outpatient care, including visits with psychiatrists, the plan pays 80% of expenses up to $15,000 and then 50% up to $25,000; non-network reimbursement is 50% of expenses up to $25,000.

In addition, employees will be permitted to nominate their own providers for the network, who would then have to fulfill criteria specified by American PsychManagement. And officials believe the sheer number of participants will make it easy for employees to use the network.

As one of the nation's largest employers, IBM considers itself in the vanguard of employee benefits and says it has pioneered programs in such areas as prevention, child care networks and geriatric services. By its sheer size, IBM's work force reflects a variety of health and social trends -- ranging from substance abuse to care for elderly dependents -- that affect companies and their employees around the country. How it addresses such issues is of broad interest among benefits officials and health policy makers, especially as companies seek successful cost-containment strategies.

For Value Health's unit, the IBM contract represents enormous growth. American PsychManagement has set up a unit devoted exclusively to IBM that will have 100 employees, said Thomas J. Neville, the unit's chief operating officer. The unit's current work force is 130.
