OPEC Is Close To Reaching Pact on Output --- Most Ministers Favor Level Of 22.5 Million Barrels A Day to Push Up Price

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 07/27/90

GENEVA -- OPEC ministers neared agreement on a new output ceiling intended to reduce the current world oil glut and push up crude oil prices. The ministers of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries remained at odds, however, over just how far those prices should rise. Most seek an eventual boost of about $2 a barrel but others are aiming for much more. Late last night, they held informal talks aimed at resolving those differences. They'll resume their formal conference today.

Before the meeting started this week, Iraq had moved troops onto Kuwait's border and angrily alleged it was being damaged by low oil prices brought on by excessive Kuwaiti ouput. Yesterday, however, Baghdad scaled back its weeklong intimidation campaign, and leaders of the two nations are scheduled to meet this weekend in Saudi Arabia.

A majority of the OPEC ministers are supporting a new total production ceiling of 22.5 million barrels a day for the remainder of the year. While 400,000 barrels a day above OPEC's current ceiling, that ceiling would be well below the organization's actual output most of this year because the old ceiling was routinely exceeded.

Among the attractions of the new ceiling proposal are a pledge by Kuwait to adhere to its official quota from now on, and what amounts to a return to the OPEC fold by the United Arab Emirates, which would get a new quota. Previously, the UAE all but ignored its quota.

If all members honor a 22.5-million-barrel ceiling, the supply of OPEC oil on world markets would shrink and prices would rise as current excess inventories are worked off. Continued output at that new ceiling also should strengthen prices more later in the year because oil demand increases in the fall and winter.

The problem for ministers has become the setting of a new "reference", or target, price for OPEC oil. For the past four years, OPEC has had an official reference price of $18 a barrel, averaged over its various grades of crude. But that has been rarely sustained because chronic quota "cheating" by some members has created excess supply. Even after a recent quick run-up from the year's low of $14, OPEC oil prices still are shy of $18 a barrel.

Sadek Boussena, Algeria's oil minister and president of OPEC, justifying the call for a higher figure, estimated the old reference price would be $22.70 a barrel today if adjusted into current dollars. A majority of the 13 OPEC nations consider an increase of $2 a barrel realistic, but an increase of $7 a barrel has been demanded by President Saddam Hussein of Iraq.

"We are arguing very hard" on the pricing issue, said Gholamreza Aghazadeh, Iran's oil minister who has been leading the push for an increase of $2 a barrel in the $18 reference price.

OPEC's reference price is largely symbolic; a goal it hopes to achieve in the open market through manipulation of its production, rather than through a set selling price. Nonetheless, the reference price has emerged as the centerpiece of the campaign started in February by the Iraqi president against Kuwait and the UAE for what he claimed was their "economic warfare" against Iraq.

That confrontation grew rapidly, with Kuwait becoming the chief target. It climaxed with last week's troop and tank movements and this week's demand here by Iraqi oil minister Issam Abdul Raheem Al-Chalabi for an increase in OPEC's reference price to $25 a barrel.

So far, that figure has been supported only by Libya. Although it is understood Mr. Al-Chalabi would need government permission to change his stance, some ministers attempted to negotiate a figure of $21 or $22. But they ran into an unexpected obstacle. "There is a stalemate," said one. "One group {believed to be led by Venezuela} won't go above $20."

While Iraq isn't likely to prevail in its bold bid to push the OPEC reference price all the way to $25, several oil ministers conceded the attempt has at least revived waning discipline within the organization.

"It's a whole new ballgame," Ginandjar Kartasasmita, Indonesia's oil minister, said in an interview. "There are two countries that can take the credit." He named Saudi Arabia "for its courage in cutting production last May to set the pace" and Iraq "for its determination to restore quota discipline."

Separately, as expected, Ramzi Salman, Iraq's oil-marketing director, was named to fill the vacant post of deputy secretary-general of OPEC.
