Merrill Quits Work on Restructuring Of Bond Debt Tied to Trump's Casinos

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 08/10/90

Merrill Lynch & Co., which has been working on a restructuring of $1.3 billion of Donald J. Trump's casino bond debt, is no longer working for the developer, according to several people close to Mr. Trump. Mr. Trump is interviewing other investment houses to work on the deal, these people said. They said that among the firms being considered are Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette, Smith Barney & Co. and First Boston Corp.

Meanwhile, people close to the situation said Mr. Trump has offered the critical position of chief financial officer of Trump Organization to Stephen F. Bollenbach, who currently holds that position for Promus Cos. As part of a restructuring agreement with his banks, Mr. Trump had agreed to appoint a "senior executive officer" no later than Sept. 30.

Mr. Bollenbach, a highly regarded executive who helped fend off Mr. Trump's takeover overtures to his company in 1986, declined to comment. Mr. Trump didn't return phone calls.

It couldn't be learned why Merrill is no longer working on the bond restructuring. One possible reason suggested by people close to Mr. Trump was that Merrill was in an awkward position because it still holds a substantial portion of Mr. Trump's Taj Mahal casino bonds, and was the underwriter for that issue in 1988. The Taj Mahal is one of Mr. Trump's casinos in Atlantic City, N.J.

Mr. Trump is believed to be preparing a restructuring plan calling for holders of at least some of his bonds to convert their debt into equity in Mr. Trump's three Atlantic City casinos. Trump officials believe the casinos aren't making nearly enough to meet about $200 million in interest and principal payments due in the next 12 months.

Although Merrill hadn't formally signed a commitment letter with Mr. Trump, people close to the deal said, the firm was working under a tentative agreement for about the past month. About three weeks ago, the Taj bonds were put on Merrill's "restricted" list of securities that the firm couldn't trade. That signaled to investors that the firm was working on a restructuring.

A Merrill spokesman declined to comment.

With Merrill out of the picture, Mr. Trump will have to act fast. On Nov. 15, he must make a $47 million interest payment on $675 million in junk bonds issued to buy the Taj Mahal casino. Informal groups representing holders of Taj Mahal and Trump Castle bonds have already formed, and have indicated that they intend to extract heavy concessions from the developer.

Meanwhile, Mr. Trump is still working on closing the bank agreement that would include a $65 million loan and a suspension of interest payments on more than $800 million in debt.

Lawyers for the banks and Trump officials continue to work around the clock, trying to complete the accord. By now, people close to the agreement said, legal fees have reached close to $10 million. Mr. Trump will be responsible for paying those fees, once the bank deal closes.
