Credit Markets: Treasury's 30-Year Auction Finds Good Response Due to Lure of Highest Yields Since May 1989

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Robert Sherman
  • Date: 08/10/90

NEW YORK -- After shunning the first two segments of the Treasury's $32.25 billion quarterly securities auction, investors were lured to the final segment of the auction by the highest yields in more than a year. The Treasury auctioned $10.26 billion of 30-year bonds yesterday as it completed its three-part refunding of the federal debt. The 30-year bonds had an average yield of 8.87%, the highest yield for a benchmark bond since May 1989 when the average yield was 9.11%.

In when-issued trading, the yield slipped to 8.75%, a sign that the 30-year bonds were selling briskly and at hefty premiums in the secondary market.

Cheered by the successful completion of the quarterly refunding, the bond market rallied. The old benchmark 30-year bond ended about 3/4 point higher, a gain of about $7.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

William Brachfeld, manager of trading in the New York office of Japan's Daiwa Securities America Inc., said that investors pushed aside worries about Mideast turmoil and rising oil prices and purchased bonds in an apparent bet that interest rates will soon head lower.

The auction went smoothly despite scant interest on the part of Japanese investors, who have purchased anywhere from 25% to 50% of the 30-year bonds sold at previous auctions. Mr. Brachfeld estimates that long-term Japanese investment accounts may have purchased between 5% and 10% of the 30-year bond at yesterday's auction.

"The demand from Japan continues to be quite small," said Mr. Brachfeld. "This was not a Japanese auction, this was a domestic auction." Japanese investors have been reducing their purchases of U.S. securities for most of the year, primarily because they view securities in their own market as equally or more attractive than U.S. securities.

The auction results were a surprise to some market participants, many of whom had predicted that the 30-year bond auction would be by far the weakest of the Treasury's three auctions. As it turns out, the 30-year was the strongest.

The quarterly auction kicked off Tuesday with the sale of $11.5 billion of three-year notes and continued on Wednesday with the sale of $10.5 billion of ten-year notes. The first sale was considered lackluster and the second sale was viewed as a disaster, although demand for the notes picked up in the secondary market late Wednesday as prices fell and yields rose to levels considered attractive.

Stephen G. Thieke, manager of the government trading desk at J.P. Morgan Securities Inc., said that political news developments and rumors of developments disrupted the two early auctions and made it difficult for dealers to assess investor interest. (Any institution or individual can bid on bonds at a Treasury auction, although most of the bonds are acquired by Wall Street brokerage firms and large commercial banks. Individuals usually buy securities through so-called noncompetitive tenders.)

But after the market fell sharply after Wednesday's auction, investors suddenly materialized. At that point, "you saw a greater sense of confidence on the part of the dealer community about where yields had to be to attract investors in this climate," said Mr. Thieke.

In addition to the price gain of the old benchmark 30-year bond, other aspects of the sale also confirmed that the auction went smoothly. The bid-to-cover ratio -- a measure of the amount of bids received compared with the amount of securities sold -- was 2.53, reflecting a healthy interest in the new bonds.

The Treasury received bids totaling $25.96 billion, including $505 million of noncompetitive tenders at the average yield, up from $463 million of noncompetitive bids accepted at the previous 30-year bond auction on May 10.

Now that the record $32.25 billion quarterly refunding obstacle has been hurdled, the focus of the Treasury market, likely will return to economic fundamentals, analysts say.

The crisis in the Mideast and its effects on world-wide oil prices and inflation will remain at center stage, but slumping real estate and stock prices, rising unemployment and tightening credit conditions also will be a focus of concern, analysts say.

The outlook for Friday is cautious. Analysts say that while the optimism that swept through the market late Thursday may carry over through the night, the market will remain captive to any new events surrounding the conflict with Iraq.

The producer price index for July is scheduled for release this morning at 8:30 a.m. EDT. The report is expected to show a 0.1% rise in producer prices, up 0.2% if the volatile food and energy components are excluded, according to a Dow Jones Capital Markets Report survey of economists.

Treasury Securities

Treasurys rallied after the government completed its $32.25 billion quarterly refunding of the federal debt.

The old benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 99 26/32, up from 99 3/32 on Wednesday. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, eased to 8.75% from 8.82% on Wednesday.

The new ten-year notes ended at a price of 100 26/32 to yield 8.64%, down from Wednesday's average auction yield of 8.77%. New three-year notes were quoted at a price of 99 22/32 to yield 8.10%, the same as the average yield set at Tuesday's auction.

In addition to 30-year bonds, the Treasury sold $10 billion of 36-day cash management bills yesterday.

Corporate, Junk Bonds

A strong performance by the stock market helped boost confidence in the junk-bond market, driving prices of better-quality speculative-grade issues higher.

Overall, prices of blue chip high-yield names ended the session about 1/4 point higher. Investment-grade corporate bonds also advanced with long-term corporate bonds flagging gains of about 5/8 point.

The junk bonds of Texas Air Corp., now Continental Airlines Holdings Inc., skyrocketed on news that Frank Lorenzo, chairman and chief executive, will step down and will sell most of his direct and indirect investment in Continental to Scandinavian Airlines System.

Texas Air's 15.75% senior notes, maturing in 1992, shot up about eight points in over-the-counter trading to end at a bid price of 89. Eastern Airlines, a unit of the airline company, saw the fortunes of its preferred stock rise.

Eastern Airlines' Series D preferred stock rose 3 1/8 points, or 31.6%, to close at 13 in over-the-counter composite volume of 193,500 shares. Average daily volume in the issue is about 17,900 shares. The issue was as high as 14 1/2 earlier in the day.

Meanwhile, some recently-depressed junk-bond issues like R.H. Macy & Co. and Trump Plaza Funding Corp. continued to show signs of strain, and lost between 1/2 and 1 point.

In the new-issue market for investment-grade corporate debt, about $450 million in new securities were priced Thursday, including a $250 million floating-rate note offering by the Student Loan Marketing Association.

Municipal Bonds

Reacting to the stronger-than-expected demand seen at the Treasury's 30-year bond auction, municipal issues ended steady to 1/2 point higher, with some issues posting gains of 3/4 point.

Among actively traded secondary issues, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% of 2018 were up 1/2 point at 98 1/2 to yield 7.32%.

Despite the market's gains, participants remain cautious due to the volatile situation in the Middle East.

No large new municipal issues are expected to be priced today.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended as much as 3/4 point higher in a rally that followed the Treasury's 30-bond auction.

In the primary market, a $300 million Federal National Mortgage Association real estate mortgage investment conduit issue backed by 10% Fannie Mae 30-year securities was offered by Lehman Brothers.

Also, there was a $300 million issue of Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. Remics backed by Freddie Mac 9.5% 30-year securities offered through Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp.

There was also a $500 million Freddie Mac Remic backed by Freddie Mac 9% 30-year securities offered through Morgan Stanley & Co.

In the secondary market, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were up 5/8 point at 99 1/32, Freddie Mac 9.5% issues were 15/32 higher at 98 21/32 and Fannie Mae 9.5% issues were 15/32 stronger at 99 6/32.

In the asset-backed sector, there was a two-part Chrysler Financial Corp. offering totaling around $215 million.

One of those was a $139 million Grantor Trust issue backed by marine receivables.

The other was a $75 million Grantor Trust issue backed by recreational-vehicles receivables.

Both issues are offered through Chemical Securities Inc., a unit of Chemical Banking Corp. Both issues had a coupon rate of 8.90% and were priced at 99 41/64 to yield 9.072%.

The originator and servicer of the underlying loans in both trusts is Chrysler First Inc. Both Chrysler First Inc. and Chrysler Financial Corp. are units of Chrysler Corp.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese government-bond market ended softer yesterday in its first subdued trading session in a week.

Participants in the financial markets say they have grown more accustomed to the tension in the Middle East, so that dealings were less vulnerable to rumors about the stand-off between Iraq and now the U.S.

At the 3 p.m. JST close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the price of Japan's benchmark No. 119 10-year bond was 83.44, down 0.47 point. The yield was 7.99%. (When calculated using the semi-annual compound method, the closing yield on the No. 119 bond was 7.37%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)

Elsewhere:

-- West German bonds ended 0.35 point firmer in light trading. The bulk of the day's gains came on overnight short-covering, dealers said.

-- British government bonds, called gilts, ending firmer as the high yields on the securities have begun to attract investors. Britain's benchmark 11.75% bond due 2003/2007 was quoted late at 99 17/32, up 19/32 from late Wednesday to yield 11.80%.
