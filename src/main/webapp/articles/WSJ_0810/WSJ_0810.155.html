U.S. Sees Strength In the Harvests Of Corn, Wheat

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 08/10/90

WASHINGTON -- The government forecast robust corn and wheat production but lagging soybean output, according to the Agriculture Department's first full-scale crop estimates for the year. The department forecast a 1990 corn harvest of 7.85 billion bushels, up 4% from last year's output and unchanged from its July estimate. The department said that "excellent growing conditions" last month offset late Corn Belt plantings and dry weather in the Southwest.

Soybean production for this year was estimated at 1.84 billion bushels, down 5% from 1989 and 1% below the July estimate, with soybean plantings off to a late start and plant development continuing "behind normal," the department said.

These were the first field-survey forecasts for 1990 corn and soybean production; previous estimates were based on incomplete information, according to the department.

The crop estimates, released after the close of futures trading yesterday, were below those projected by many futures analysts in recent weeks. With tighter-than-expected supplies in the offing, soybean futures prices are expected to rise to 10 cents a bushel from five cents in early trading today on the Chicago Board of Trade, analysts said.

Corn futures prices may jump two to four cents a bushel today in reaction to the government crop estimates, analysts said.

The figures released for the country's wheat crop aren't expected to have as much of a market impact, analysts said.

The corn estimate reflects good growing conditions, which overcame wet weather earlier this year. Corn yields are expected to average 117.7 bushels per acre, up 1.5 bushels from last year but still below the record high of 119.4 bushels an acre in 1987. The department estimated that 1990 production of corn and other feed grains would be the highest since 1986.

It also forecast a sharp rise in wheat output to 2.71 billion bushels, up 33% from the drought-affected 1989 wheat crop and slightly above the July estimate. Average yield was estimated at 38.7 bushels an acre, 5.9 bushels more than in 1989, with output of wheat and other food grains expected to be the largest since 1982. Winter wheat, the bulk of the wheat crop, is nearly all harvested.

With wheat stockpiles climbing, the department last week said it would order a 10% to 20% cutback in 1991 wheat acreage under the government-subsidized program. Wheat surpluses fell sharply in recent years because the government subsidized exports; the stockpiles this year are projected to rise to 846 million bushels by June 1, 1991, from 535 million bushels on June 1, 1989.

The National Association of Wheat Growers blamed the rising stockpiles on a recent decline in exports and the department's cutback in spending on export subsidies programs.

Grain growers are expected to get less help from exports than previously thought, according to a separate Agriculture Department report released yesterday. The department's forecast of Soviet Union grain imports for the marketing year that began July 1 was reduced by six million tons to 30 million tons, the lowest level in four years. The latest estimate would be down from 38 million tons in the previous year.

The department's forecast of 1990 Soviet grain production was boosted by 15 million tons to 230 million tons, with "bumper yields" cited. Both total Soviet grain production and the wheat harvest were estimated at the highest levels since 1978, and the coarse-grain harvest was forecast at only slightly below the 1987 level and two million tons under the record high 1976 total.

"World and U.S. wheat trade prospects have weakened, mainly because of improved crops and reduced imports likely for the Soviet Union and China," the department said.
