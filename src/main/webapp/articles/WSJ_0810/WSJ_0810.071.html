Law -- Legal Beat: U.S. Challenges Georgia Election System

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett and Amy Dockser Marcus
  • Date: 08/10/90

The Justice Department, in an action civil-rights advocates hailed as a landmark lawsuit, asked a federal court to end Georgia's runoff election system, which requires a candidate to receive over 50% of the vote to be elected. The majority-vote requirement, which is similar to that of eight other southern states, was adopted in 1964 and maintained as a means of preventing blacks from being elected, the Justice Department charged in a suit filed in Atlanta.

The suit is the department's first across-the-board challenge to a state's elections for federal, state and county positions. The Georgia system, the federal suit charged, violates the Voting Rights Act of 1965 and the 14th and 15th Amendments to the Constitution. In a separate pending legal proceeding, the department claims that Georgia discriminates on racial grounds in the way it elects many of its judges.

Perry Michael, Georgia's executive assistant attorney general, declined to comment on the suit. But he said state officials were "very surprised" by the action, because a group of Georgia citizens already had challenged the majority requirement in a similar suit filed in May.

The federal suit "is very significant because it shows that the Justice Department will be much more active than it had been (under former President Reagan) in trying to stop discriminatory voting practices," said Robert McDuff, an attorney with the Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law, a Washington advocacy group.

The majority-vote requirement has its most obvious impact in primaries in which black candidates win the most votes but don't achieve an outright majority. In subsequent runoff elections, whites in Georgia typically have voted along racial lines to defeat black candidates, the Justice Department charged.

The Georgia system, said the department's civil-rights chief, John Dunne, "has denied the state's black citizens an equal opportunity to participate in the political process." He said that 26.5% of Georgia's voters are black, according to 1980 census figures, but only 10% of the state's elected officials are black.

Mr. Dunne said news reports at the time the Georgia system was created had described state legislators' desire to prevent "Negro bloc voting." That purpose provides "the smoking gun" in demonstrating the illegal discriminatory intent behind the majority-vote requirement, Mr. Dunne said.

The other states with runoff systems similar to Georgia's are: Alabama, Arkansas, Florida, Louisiana, Oklahoma, Mississippi, South Carolina and Texas. Mr. Dunne said that the Justice Department isn't aware of discriminatory results related to the majority-vote requirements in any of these other states and that it isn't investigating them.

He added that the department's suit wouldn't affect this year's gubernatorial election in Georgia. In a Democratic primary runoff this week, Lt. Gov. Zell Miller beat former Atlanta mayor and congressman Andrew Young, who had sought to be the state's first black governor.

APPEALS COURT RULES Alabama hazardous-waste curbs unconstitutional.

The 11th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals in Atlanta ruled that an Alabama law prohibiting disposal of out-of-state hazardous waste in Alabama -- unless the state where the waste was generated has similar facilities -- violates the commerce clause of the U.S. Constitution.

Under the law, Alabama issued a "blacklist" barring the acceptance of hazardous waste from 22 states and the District of Columbia. The law, passed last fall, forced many companies to ship their hazardous waste to other disposal facilities, adding significantly to the companies' costs.

The appeals panel's decision had been closely watched by the hazardous-waste industry, as well as legislators in other states considering adopting similar laws. The panel overturned a ruling by a lower court in January upholding the constitutionality of the law.

The case arose from a lawsuit filed by Chemical Waste Management Inc., which runs the nation's largest and Alabama's only commercial hazardous-waste management facility, located in Emelle. The National Solid Wastes Management Association, a trade association for the waste-management industry, is also a plaintiff in the case.

The appeals panel acknowledged that many states are concerned about minimizing the dangers to public health and to the environment created by hazardous wastes. But the panel found that Alabama's law barred the wastes only of certain states, placing an unfair burden on interstate commerce and violating the Constitution. In addition, the panel ruled that Alabama's regulation of hazardous-waste land disposal was pre-empted by Congress's intention that those decisions be regulated by the Environmental Protection Agency on a nationwide basis, in order to insure that certain states do not become dumping grounds.

In a prepared statement, Alabama Gov. Guy Hunt said the state was still looking at the decision but intended to seek a rehearing or review by the U.S. Supreme Court. He said that the Alabama law had received "broad support in this country among the states" and that he felt each state should create their own hazardous-waste management program.

But Allen Moore, president of the National Solid Wastes Management Association, said in a statement that the decision means that states cannot act on their own to create such programs.

"This is not to say that only a few states should satisfy the nation's hazardous-waste disposal needs. There is a role for every state, and the federal government must assure that all states do their part," Mr. Moore said.

SEC ACCUSES BROKERS and company of teaming up to manipulate IPO.

The Securities and Exchange Commission accused Beres Industries Inc., two of its officers, the president of a now-defunct brokerage firm and four other individuals of fraud and stock manipulation.

In the civil suit, filed in federal court in Newark, N.J., the SEC said the scheme began with the over-the-counter company's initial public offering in June 1986 and ended in March 1989 when the brokerage firm, Monmouth Investments Inc., went out of business. Beres' stock, which had traded between $1 and $4 during the alleged scheme, immediately collapsed with the closing of Monmouth, the SEC said, and recently traded as low as six cents a share.

The individual defendants include Charles Beres Sr., the founder and chairman of Beres; Charles Beres Jr., chief executive officer; and Arden Brown, formerly president of Monmouth, which was based in Englishtown, N.J., before it closed. Monmouth wasn't named in the lawsuit. Beres, which makes plastic molds, is based in Lakewood, N.J.

The SEC charged that Mr. Brown told Beres that Monmouth would underwrite the initial public offering only if Mr. Brown was given 10% of the company's stock in addition to its usual underwriting fees. The SEC charged that this arrangement was never disclosed to investors.

Before the initial public offering, there were nine million restricted Beres shares, mainly held by Beres insiders, the SEC said. About a third of those shares were eventually sold to Monmouth for far less than the prevailing market price, the SEC alleged. Monmouth turned around and sold the stock at higher prices through misleading boiler-room techniques, the lawsuit said.

The SEC said that during the initial stock offering, Monmouth solicited customers and arranged for later purchases at preset, arbitrary prices. These arranged sales made it appear that the stock was in demand after the initial public offering, the SEC said. The defendants' lawyers couldn't be reached for comment.

The SEC is seeking a court order banning the defendants from securities law violations. It also asked the court to order them to turn over their allegedly illegal profits, estimated to total $4.5 million.

STATE JUDGES begin effort to resolve asbestos litigation.

Although recent actions by federal judges have put asbestos personal-injury litigation in the news, the bulk of the cases are before state courts. Working in conjunction with some of the federal judges, three state judges from New York, Baltimore and Philadelphia have begun planning a conference for this fall at which about a dozen judges from across the country will meet to discuss the issue.

Several federal judges are meeting today in Washington to discuss a possible asbestos personal-injury class action and other nationwide approaches to resolving the cases. New York state Justice Helen Freedman of Manhattan said the state judges plan to focus on alternative ways to encourage the parties to settle the suits and avoid going to trial.

Many judges have become frustrated with the growing backlog of asbestos cases, and some fear that the defendants may run out of funds before all the claims are paid. Asbestos, once widely used in shipbuilding and construction, causes cancer and respiratory ailments.

Justice Freedman, with 2,000 asbestos cases pending, said a claims-resolution system needs to be set up to ensure that the more seriously ill claimants are paid first and to speed up the compensation process.

A FEMALE MINISTER charges that she was sexually harassed by a male pastor.

In a lawsuit believed to be the first of its kind, the Rev. Susan E. Black alleges that the Rev. William R. Snyder, pastor of St. John's Lutheran Church in Minneapolis, made unwelcome sexual advances toward her during the 15 months she served as the church's associate minister. She claims Mr. Snyder described the two of them as being "in love."

In her suit, filed in Hennepin County District Court, Ms. Black is also charging Mr. Snyder, the church and its denomination, the Evangelical Lutheran Church in America, with violating Minnesota's "whistleblower statute," which bars organizations from firing employees engaged in a legal action against those organizations.

St. John's congregation dismissed Ms. Black in June, two months after she filed a complaint against Mr. Snyder with the Minnesota Department of Human Rights.

In a statement, Bishop David W. Olsen of the denomination's Minnesota Area Synod denied that church officials had retaliated against her for filing the complaint. He said a church committee, which investigated Ms. Black's charges earlier this year, concluded that Mr. Snyder's behavior was "extremely inappropriate for professional relationships," but found no grounds for taking disciplinary measures against the pastor.

Karl Cambronne, a lawyer for Mr. Snyder and St. John's, said he will file a motion to dismiss the suit on First Amendment grounds. He added, "Factually, (Ms. Black's) claim is lacking."
