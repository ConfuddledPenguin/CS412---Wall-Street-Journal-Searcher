Technology & Medicine: Scientists Find First Clues on How Gene Could Cause Nerve Tumors

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 08/10/90

Less than a month after reporting discovery of the gene for neurofibromatosis, often but mistakenly called the Elephant Man's disease, scientists uncovered their first clues on how the gene may cause the mysterious malady. The neurofibromatosis gene is closely related to genes involved in cancer, reported scientists with the Howard Hughes Medical Institute at the University of Utah in Salt Lake City.

The new finding bolsters hopes for a treatment for the disease as well as for new insight into the genetic bases of cancer, researchers said.

The sudden and rapid advances in understanding neurofibromatosis, a genetic disease that has baffled medical scientists for a century, illustrates the power of biologists' new ability to find or "map" genes. The discovery of the NF-1 gene, as it's now being called, was announced July 13, the climax of a search that had begun less than five years earlier. Now, only four weeks later, the scientists report they've found the first indications of what the mysterious gene does.

"We didn't expect this {discovery} for at least five years," said Allan Rubenstein, a neurologist at Mount Sinai School of Medicine in New York and medical director of the National Neurofibromatosis Foundation which, along with the Hughes institute, funded the research.

The rapid progress against neurofibromatosis is likely to be cited by backers of the new federally funded Human Genome Project as evidence of the payoffs that can come from locating or "mapping" human genes. The project, predicted to cost $200 million a year, is aimed at mapping all 50,000 to 100,000 genes, collectively called the human genome, that are the biological blueprints for human life.

Neurofibromatosis, which afflicts about 100,000 Americans, is so named because its most notable features are fibrous lumps that grow from the nerves in the skin. In rare cases, nodules proliferate in the skin and over the bones to produce grotesque features. John Merrick, the "Elephant Man" who became a celebrity in England in the 1890s and was the subject of a 1980 movie, originally was thought to have had neurofibromatosis but is now believed to have had a different and far rarer disorder known as Proteus syndrome.

Most people with neurofibromatosis are of normal or near-normal appearance but many suffer non-malignant brain and nerve tumors and/or neurological problems that can lead to learning disabilities, blindness or deafness. The disorder also is known as von Recklinghausen's disease, after the German physician who first described it in 1882.

The Utah scientists, headed by molecular geneticist Ray White, reported in the biology journal, Cell, that they had taken the next step following discovery of the NF-1 gene. The gene, like all genes, is the molecular blueprint for a protein. With the gene in hand the scientists said they've now been able to "read" part of this molecular blueprint. It produces a protein that is remarkably similar to the GAP protein, which is known to be involved in the cancer process, they reported.

The discovery led the scientists to speculate that the NF-1 protein, like the GAP protein, normally may serve as a brake on cell proliferation, preventing a cell from multiplying into a tumor. If the NF-1 gene is damaged, as it appears to be in neurofibromatosis, it either cannot make the protein or produces a non-functioning form of it and the cell is no longer held in check. Instead it proliferates uncontrolled and grows into a tumor. Whether the tumor is malignant may depend on other genes in the chain of genetic events that lead to cancer.

Mr. White cautioned that speculation that the NF-1 gene is a "tumor suppressor" gene is based solely on its molecular similarities with the GAP protein and its gene and with two cancer-related genes found in yeasts. But he and other scientists said that if the speculation is proved correct, it raises hopes that a treatment for neurofibromatosis can be found. If true, "we would know from which direction a therapy might come," said Peter Bellermann, president of the neurofibromatosis foundation. If the disorder is the result of the lack of the NF-1 protein, then the disease might be treated by supplying the protein as a medication, for example.

The Utah scientists' chief competitors, a scientific team at the University of Michigan, agreed. "This is a very nice, happy outcome" resulting from the discovery of the NF-1 gene, said Michigan molecular geneticist Francis Collins. "It allows us to leap-frog over two to three years of research; it puts us in a position of having a path to follow" to gain a treatment for the disease.

Mr. Collins, whose team tied the Utah group last month in the race to discover the NF-1 gene, said that his Michigan team has made the same discovery as Mr. White's group and will publish a paper soon confirming the Utah findings.
