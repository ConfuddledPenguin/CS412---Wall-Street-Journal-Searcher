Law: Plaintiffs Strike a Blow to Shift Blame For Accidents Caused by Their Own Acts

  • Author: Amy Dockser Marcus
  • Date: 08/10/90

Shortly before Christmas 1985, Raymond Belknap shot himself in the head with a 12-gauge shotgun after drinking beer and listening to heavy metal music. Now, a Nevada court is trying to determine just who is to blame for Mr. Belknap's death. Judges and juries have become increasingly willing to allow plaintiffs to spread -- and in some cases shift entirely -- the responsibility for tragic events caused by their own behavior.

Some legal specialists say the courts are sending a message to consumers that their own role in accidents is no longer important. And they say the Belknap case is one of several pending around the country that may change the legal meaning of personal responsibility.

Defense lawyers see that as an ominous prospect. They say consumers are forced to pay for the misbehavior of others in higher prices and the unavailability of new products. The American Ladder Institute, a trade group, says 15% to 25% of a ladder's price goes to legal and liability costs. Most suits, the group says, are brought by plaintiffs harmed in accidents while under the influence of alcohol or drugs.

Twenty-five years ago, most juries couldn't have returned a verdict in favor of a plaintiff if he was in any way responsible for his injuries. Now most states let juries and courts weigh the fault of each party. In some states, a company found to be as little as 1% responsible for an accident may be held liable for some of the damages.

The new rules developed partly out of a belief by many judges that corporations are better equipped than individuals both to prevent accidents and to absorb their cost. "People do stupid things because they don't expect the consequences to occur," says John F. Banzhaf III, a product liability specialist with the George Washington University National Law Center. "Corporate behavior is readily affected by changes in the legal rules. Individual behavior is not."

Worries over these liability issues have attracted the attention of Congress. Robert W. Kasten Jr. (R., Wis.) and 30 other senators have sponsored a bill that would bar plaintiffs who were under the influence of alcohol or drugs from collecting damages if their condition was more than 50% responsible for their injuries. The bill's sponsors have reasoned that society shouldn't bear the costs of such accidents and that the measure "will encourage persons to take responsibility for their own safety and the safety of others."

The Belknap case, in state court in Reno, is being closely watched because the makers of albums -- unlike makers of power lawn mowers or swimming pools -- have generally been shielded from such suits by the First Amendment. Two similar cases, involving the rock singer Ozzy Osbourne, are pending in Georgia.

Mr. Belknap's parents say subliminal messages in the music of heavy metal band Judas Priest induced their son to kill himself. And they say the band, CBS Records, which distributed the album, and CBS Inc. bear responsibility. (As part of its purchase of CBS Records in 1988, Sony Corp. agreed that the record unit would pay any judgment against CBS Inc.) The judge in the case ruled that subliminal messages aren't protected by the First Amendment.

Kenneth McKenna, a lawyer for the Belknaps, says CBS is liable because the company should have foreseen that someone such as Mr. Belknap might respond violently to the music. "Drinking beer, smoking pot, being a dropout from school, and having depression is normal for a Judas Priest purchaser," says Mr. McKenna, a Reno lawyer. "CBS and Judas Priest failed to protect for that lowest-common-denominator consumer." The parents of James Vance, who shot himself in the same incident but survived, are also plaintiffs in the suit.

CBS argues that Mr. Belknap and Mr. Vance were troubled youths who abused drugs and alcohol and whose behavior wasn't connected to the music.

Plaintiffs' lawyers contend that people shouldn't bear sole blame for behavior encouraged at least partly by others. In 1985, Eric Abreu, then seven years old, fell off a 15-foot-high sculpture in a Manhattan playground accident that left him a quadriplegic. Lawyers for the city argued that, while tragic, the accident could have been avoided because Eric's mother had warned him not to climb the sculpture.

A jury verdict in the city's favor was thrown out by the judge, and a state appeals court ordered a new trial. Harvey Weitz, who represented Eric at the second trial, says the city knew children were climbing to the top of the sculpture and should have had safeguards. In the new trial, a jury found Eric bore a small part of the blame but awarded him $33.3 million in damages. The award, later cut by the judge to $11.5 million, is on appeal.

D. Daniel Engstrand Jr., a partner in the New York law firm Doniger, Garland & Engstrand, who represented the city at the second trial, says he personally feels Eric is entitled to some compensation, but calls the enormous verdict "a miscarriage since Eric's {behavior} played a great deal in the injury he sustained, and he knew he should not climb up there."

In some recent cases, juries and judges have been receptive to plaintiffs' claims that they can't be blamed for their behavior if the harmful consequences of their actions aren't commonly known. The rulings have opened up new liability for alcohol distillers, who had been protected from product-liability suits on the grounds that the risks of drinking are well-known.

In November 1983, college freshman Marie Brinkmeyer died after an evening of drinking shots of tequila with some friends in Kingsville, Texas. Her mother sued Brown-Forman Inc., the distiller that made the tequila, alleging the company should have warned consumers about possible dangers.

In 1988, a state appeals court in Corpus Christi, Texas, reversed a lower court's dismissal of the suit. The appeals panel wasn't convinced that the public understood that consuming excessive quantities of alcohol can result in death and that a trial should be held on the issue of whether Brown-Forman had a duty to warn consumers of that hazard. A trial is set for next year in Corpus Christi.

James B. Ragan, a lawyer for the plaintiff, says it isn't fair to blame Ms. Brinkmeyer for her death. Advertising portrays drinking as glamorous and plays down possible dangers, he says. "She's a victim of the misperception of alcohol as something worthwhile and enjoyable," says Mr. Ragan, a Corpus Christi lawyer. Lawyers for Brown-Forman say they can't comment because the case is pending.

A federal appeals court in Philadelphia in 1988 reversed a lower court's dismissal and ordered a trial in the case of William Hon, who died of pancreatitis at the age of 26. The suit, brought by his wife, alleged that Mr. Hon consumed two to three cans of Stroh Brewery Co.'s beer four nights a week for six years. The court wrote that the public was unaware of the risk of pancreatitis from moderate, though long-term, consumption of beer, and that Stroh could be held liable for failing to warn of the risk. A trial is scheduled for October in federal district court in Scranton, Pa. A lawyer for Stroh says he can't comment because the case is pending.

Plaintiffs' lawyers say they are unsure what effect a new federal law requiring alcoholic-beverage containers to carry health warnings will have on such suits. But these lawyers say a recent decision by the New Jersey Supreme Court in a suit involving cigarette smoking could be harmful to the alcoholic beverage industry.

In 1982, Claire Dewey filed suit in Hackensack, N.J., against Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., American Brands Inc. and R.J. Reynolds Tobacco Co., alleging that they are liable for the death of her husband, Wilfred Dewey, from lung cancer at age 49. Mr. Dewey smoked Camels, Tareytons and Viceroys for 40 years.

In the decision, reached in July, the New Jersey high court ruled that Mrs. Dewey can proceed with her lawsuit because Congress didn't intend to immunize cigarette makers against such suits when they ordered warnings on cigarette packages in 1966. Courts previously had found that the federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act protected tobacco companies from liability.

The notion that a consumer can sue a tobacco company after years of smoking and despite warning labels is "a sign our legal system has gone awry," says Martin London, a partner in the New York firm Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison, a lawyer for Brown & Williamson. "It is a kind of thinking that is totally at odds with the history and spirit of American jurisprudence and social mores." Brown & Williamson, a unit of B.A.T Industries PLC, and R.J. Reynolds, a unit of RJR Nabisco Inc., haven't yet decided whether to appeal; American Brands didn't participate in the appeal before the state high court.

Marc Z. Edell, a partner in the Short Hills, N.J., law firm of Budd, Larner, Gross, Rosenbaum, Greenberg & Sade, a lawyer for Mrs. Dewey, says companies bear responsibility because they deliberately attempt to undermine warning labels through ads and other means.
