Politics & Policy: U.S. Acts to Learn if Keating Has Millions For Restitution Months After S&L Failed

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 08/10/90

WASHINGTON -- A full 18 months after the costliest thrift failure ever, federal savings-and-loan officials are trying to learn whether the thrift's top executive, Charles Keating Jr., has $40.9 million he can return to the failed S&L. Under administrative proceedings begun yesterday, thrift regulators are seeking an accounting of Mr. Keating's finances as well as $40.9 million in restitution -- a fraction of the $2.5 billion in losses stemming from the failure of Lincoln Savings & Loan Association.

The regulators also are seeking to remove Mr. Keating as chairman of American Continental Corp., Lincoln's Phoenix, Ariz., parent, along with five other officers. In addition, the regulators also would require Mr. Keating to give them two days' notice before transferring assets in excess of $5,000.

"We do not believe his statements to the press or to the public that he is broke," said Timothy Ryan, the director of the Office of Thrift Supervision. "There was too much money that was available to him, and too short a time for it all to disappear."

The proceedings began on the first anniversary of the savings-and-loan cleanup law and federal officials have been putting the best face possible on the deteriorating mess in a flurry of recent speeches and news briefings.

"This action is literally bolting the barn door after the horse left," says Robert Litan of the Brookings Institution, a Washington think tank. "The money is already long gone. This will join the many other cases in which they {the regulators} may get successful judgments, but not any money."

Lincoln, of Irvine, Calif., was seized by the government in April 1989, one day after parent American Continental filed for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code.

The amount of restitution is the largest ever sought by the thrift office, Mr. Ryan said. He added that he didn't believe that 18 months was a long spell before getting a grasp of Mr. Keating's finances. "I think it's prompt action," Mr. Ryan said, but he added: "I've only been here four months." Previously, Mr. Ryan was an attorney at the Labor Department.

An American Continental spokesman, Brad Boland, who is Mr. Keating's son-in-law, said that federal regulators are explicitly familiar with Mr. Keating's finances through a separate federal suit filed by the Resolution Trust Corp.

"As usual, when they can't get the results they want in a court of competent jurisdiction, they resort to administrative fiat," he said, adding: "They know every nickel he has." Mr. Boland said that Mr. Keating's extensive legal bills are being paid through insurance companies.

The administrative case is being pursued alongside a tangle of several other suits between the government and Mr. Keating, who has become a symbol of the high-flying thrift executive investing in wild land schemes.

American Continental is in the midst of bankruptcy court proceedings in Phoenix. The company has sued the thrift office in an effort to overturn its seizure of Lincoln. American Continental bondholders have sued the company alleging fraud. The Resolution Trust Corp. is in the midst of a $1.1 billion civil racketeering lawsuit against Lincoln. And a grand jury in Los Angeles also is investigating Lincoln for possible criminal violations. Throughout all of these proceedings Mr. Keating has consistently denied any wrongdoing.

The administrative case focuses on three transactions involving Mr. Keating and several associates of American Continental. Regulators contend that these transactions piled up the $40.9 million of losses.
