N.V. Philips Net Declined 84% In 2nd Quarter --- Dutch Electronics Firm Cites Component Sales, Tight Economy in Brazil

  • Author: Bob Hagerty
  • Date: 08/10/90

Despite better-than-expected sales of television sets, N.V. Philips posted a sharp drop in second-quarter earnings, and the Dutch electronics company reaffirmed that it expects a huge loss for the full year. Net profit plunged 84% to 37 million guilders ($21 million), or 13 Dutch cents a share, from 235 million guilders ($131 million), or 91 Dutch cents a share, a year earlier. The drop reflects red ink from the computer and semiconductor operations.

Analysts, however, had forecast a small loss for the quarter.

Sales in the latest quarter edged down to 13.54 billion guilders from 13.55 billion guilders. Net in the year-earlier quarter included an extraordinary gain of 38 million guilders.

In early July, Philips's new chairman, Jan Timmer, announced plans to cut 10,000 jobs, or about 3.5% of the company's world-wide total, and to make provisions for 2.7 billion guilders of restructuring costs, to be recorded in the second half. Those provisions will leave the company with a loss of about two billion guilders for all of 1990, Philips forecast.

Some analysts have predicted that Philips won't pay a dividend this year, but a spokesman said no decision has been made on that question. He said it would depend on progress of the company's cost-cutting moves and the general course of business.

Eleanor Buss, an analyst at Robert Fleming Securities, predicted yesterday that Philips would pay a dividend, particularly because many of its shareholders are retired people who rely on the income. Dorian Foyil of UBS Phillips & Drew Securities said he expects Philips to reduce its dividend by about 50% from last year's two guilders per share and to pay it in the form of shares rather than cash.

For the first half, net profit slipped 19% to 373 million guilders, or 1.34 guilders a share, from 458 million guilders, or 1.77 guilders a share. The latest period's net included 330 million guilders of extraordinary gains, mostly from the sale of defense units, compared with 38 million guilders of gains a year before. Before extraordinary items, profit plunged 90% to 43 million guilders from 420 million guilders, partly because of higher financing costs.

Sales in the half rose 1% to 26.36 billion guilders from 26.2 billion guilders. After stripping out the effects of currency changes and asset sales, sales were up 8%, the company said.

Operating profit at the consumer products division in the first half surged 73% to 825 million guilders, and sales rose 11% to 11.48 billion guilders. The company said June's World Cup soccer championship spurred sales of television sets and video recorders.

In the lighting division, long regarded as Philips's strong point, operating profit dropped 19% to 327 million guilders on a 6% decline in sales to 3.54 billion guilders. The company said prices were pushed down by "fierce competition" in Europe and the U.S. but "remained at an acceptable level." A spokesman said the division also was hurt by a slowdown in the construction industry.

The professional products and systems division showed an operating loss of 126 million, compared with profit of 85 million guilders a year earlier. Sales declined 13% to 6.13 billion guilders.

The division includes telecommunications, medical-electronics and computer equipment and other items. Philips said all lines in the division showed improvement, except for computers, which produced a major loss. Analysts estimated the loss at more than 250 million guilders. Philips has announced plans to scale back computer operations, focusing on assembling systems from standardized products and on serving certain segments of the market, such as financial companies.

Philips's other major problem area, components, showed an operating loss of 59 million guilders, compared with a profit of 4 million guilders. Sales were flat at 4.2 billion guilders. The company said its loss on integrated circuits was slightly smaller than a year earlier but that results from color-television picture tubes and other electronic components deteriorated, mainly because of adverse exchange-rate movements.

The company said its results also were hurt by Brazil's economic austerity program. Latin America accounted for 18% of Philips's operating profit last year, and Brazil is by far the company's biggest market in the region.
