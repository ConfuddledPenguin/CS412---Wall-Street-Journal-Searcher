Politics & Policy: RTC Discloses Insolvent S&Ls' Losses in Period

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 08/10/90

WASHINGTON -- The 247 insolvent savings and loan associations in line to be sold or liquidated by the government posted losses totaling $2.3 billion for the second quarter. The financial results of the insolvent S&Ls are the first to be disclosed by the Resolution Trust Corp., the savings and loan bailout agency created a year ago. Previously the results of the entire thrift industry, with no separate earnings figures for the insolvent ones, were announced by the Office of Thrift Supervision.

The second-quarter results compare with first-quarter losses of $3.1 billion for 350 sick S&Ls under government control, the RTC said yesterday. In the last quarter of 1989, roughly the same number of institutions reported losses of $6.4 billion.

Federal regulators now have divided the financial reporting process between the RTC and the Office of Thrift Supervision, contending that the financial results of sick thrifts drag down the figures for the rest of the industry. The 2,500 S&Ls not under government control posted first-quarter losses of $271 million. They have yet to report second-quarter results.

The bifurcation is another sign that the thrift cleanup is a bigger undertaking than Congress or the administration conceived of a year ago. The RTC was originally envisioned as a brief stop for thrifts before they were quickly sold. But some thrifts have been held and managed by the agency for the entire year.

A substantial portion of the losses, regulators said, consisted of recognizing prior losses not previously reflected on the S&Ls' books. Second-quarter losses included $1.3 billion in asset write-downs, compared with $1.8 billion in the first quarter.

"To a large degree, these charges result from the RTC's ongoing efforts to reflect more accurately asset values of conservatorship institutions," said William Seidman, chairman of the RTC.

But it is misleading to discuss improvements in the results of seized thrifts, industry analysts say, as institutions file in and out of the agency. "These are just part of the cost of warehousing these institutions until they can finally be sold off," says Bert Ely, an S&L consultant in Alexandria, Va. "We won't know the final losses until they are sold."
