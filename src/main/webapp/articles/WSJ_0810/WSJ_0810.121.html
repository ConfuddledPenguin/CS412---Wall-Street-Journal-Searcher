Foreign Exchange: Dollar Rises Against Most Currencies; Yen Is Firm Despite Tokyo Stocks' Fall

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/10/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar rose marginally against most currencies as world financial markets remained fixated on the Persian Gulf. Five previous sessions of tough trading had clearly taken a toll on currency dealers. Between bursts of activity, trading practically dried up. Those who stayed involved kept their heads down.

Between its high and low for the day, the dollar strayed less than a pfennig and little more than half a yen.

In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.5965 marks, up from 1.5925 marks late Wednesday in New York. The U.S. currency was changing hands at 1.3433 Swiss francs, above 1.3385 francs late Wednesday.

Against the yen, the dollar was unchanged at 149.80 yen.

In Tokyo Friday, the dollar was trading soon after the opening at 149.55 yen, down from Thursday's Tokyo close of 149.68 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5930 marks; in Tokyo Thursday, it closed at 1.5913 marks.

Thursday provided some unsettling Mideast developments. Iraq closed its border in both directions to foreigners, trapping many inside. U.S. forces in Saudi Arabia swelled as the Defense Department reported threatening Iraqi troop movements in Kuwait.

None of this swayed the dollar much. "So many one-liners are coming across our screens that the market is a bit desensitized to news," said Thomas Benfer, a senior currency marketing representative in New York with Bank of Montreal. He was refering to the newswire headlines that traders receive electronically.

Also keeping rates stable yesterday was the absence of speculators, many of whom were counting their losses from previous sessions rather than actively trading, Mr. Benfer said.

But unlike previous days, when the dollar sank every time the Mideast situation seemed to calm down, the U.S. currency remained well bid up throughout New York trading. A successful auction of 30-year bonds in the third leg of the U.S. Treasury's quarterly refunding helped to buoy the dollar.

Meanwhile, the yen reclaimed ground against European currencies. The mark fell to 93.83 yen in late New York trading yesterday from 94.066 yen late Wednesday. The British pound eased to 279.83 yen from 280.07 yen.

Pent-up demand for the yen was released by declining crude-oil prices. The September futures contract on the New York Mercantile Exchange fell 29 cents a barrel to $25.67 a barrel.

Interest in the Japanese currency was somewhat restrained, however, by another reversal on the Tokyo Stock Exchange. After having recovered Wednesday, the Nikkei stock index fell 893.41 points to 27615.73, its lowest level in nearly two years.

Among other currencies, investors sought out those of nations with high domestic interest rates. The Canadian, Australian and New Zealand dollars and the British pound all saw buying interest.

"People are waiting for all this to blow over. A lot of people are going for the high yields, because they don't want to take a risk on the dollar," said Robin Cheung, a New York vice president for Unibank. "They know these currencies won't be hurt too much by the situation in the Middle East."

However, the pound was trading slightly lower against the dollar late in the day, declining to $1.8680 from $1.8696 late Wednesday.

The dollar ended European dealings mostly weaker, although it cut little fresh ground and moved within relatively narrow ranges throughout the day.

In Tokyo, the dollar finished lower in sluggish dealings Thursday, with the market's ranks thinned by vacationers. Those who remained were apparently exhausted by a week of tension in the Middle East.

The dollar finished in Tokyo at 149.68 yen, below Wednesday's close of 150 yen.

Tokyo foreign-exchange traders said position adjustment before the peak of the Japanese vacation season next week could carry the yen higher against European currencies in coming days.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $385.80, up $2.60. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.5 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $387.05 an ounce.
