Western Union's 2nd-Quarter Loss Hit $27.6 Million

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 08/10/90

NEW YORK -- Western Union Corp. reported a widened second-quarter loss amid mounting debt problems and contract negotiations with its biggest union. The company, which has been struggling to recapitalize its huge debt load and avoid bankruptcy proceedings, posted a loss of $27.6 million, or 44 cents a share, compared with a loss of $4.9 million, or 40 cents a share, a year earlier. Furthermore, the company missed a $11.6 million preferred dividend payment. But a proposal to reclassify its preferred shares into common would eliminate its unpaid preferred dividends, currently totaling $69.8 million.

Second-quarter revenue eased to $146 million from $159 million.

For the six months, Western Union had a loss of $45.7 million, or 81 cents a share, compared with $5.1 million, or 72 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue slipped to $291 million from $317 million.

The 1989 revenue figures included $4 million for the second quarter and $8 million for the six months from services which are no longer provided by Western Union.

The second-quarter loss was based on a weighted average of 89.1 million shares outstanding versus 44.4 million last year. The six-month loss was based on a weighted average of 84.9 million shares outstanding versus 43.5 million. The increase came mainly from conversions of Western Union Class B convertible preferred shares into common shares during the past 12 months.

The company attributed its losses to some $29 million in quarterly interest payments on $640 million in debt. The second-quarter loss also included $5.4 million of costs associated with Western Union's proposed recapitalization plan. These included legal and printing fees and payments to its investment advisers, Merrill Lynch & Co. and Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette.

Moreover, the results included $5.6 million from settlement of claims relating to the purchase in 1987 of ITT World Communications Inc., $5 million from termination of a pension plan, and $6 million of restructuring charges.

A spokesman said that if the costs and restructuring charges were eliminated, Western Union would have shown a $22.2 million profit in the 1990 second quarter compared with a $9.5 million profit in the like 1989 period.

"Both of our businesses (business services and financial services) are operating profitably," the spokesman said. "But the corporate interest expense is so high that it's offsetting those profits."

Meanwhile, Western Union continues to negotiate a new three-year contract with the Communications Workers of America, covering about 2,500 workers. The previous contract expired last Monday.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, Western Union closed at 42 cents a share, up three cents.
