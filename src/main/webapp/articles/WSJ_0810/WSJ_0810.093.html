Appeals Court Stays Order To Redistrict Los Angeles

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/10/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- The U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals here granted a one-week stay of a federal judge's ruling that would create a Hispanic majority supervisorial district in Los Angeles, potentially changing the balance of power in the nation's most populous county. Judge David V. Kenyon ruled in June that the county discriminated against Hispanics when redrawing district boundaries after the 1980 Census. He ordered the governing board of supervisors to submit a new plan that would help a Hispanic win a seat.

Last week, the judge rejected the board's plan and ordered it to accept a new supervisorial district map devised by civil-rights groups.

The board, which filed the appeal, applauded the stay. "It is the first step towards restoring common sense" to the reapportionment process, said Michael Antonovich, one of three conservative supervisors on the five-member board who opposed Judge Kenyon's plan.

The case was originally brought by the Mexican American Legal Defense and Educational Fund and the U.S. Justice Department. Although Hispanics represent an estimated 35% of the county's 8.7 million people, no Hispanic has won a seat on the board since 1875.

The appeals court agreed to hear oral arguments in the case on Aug. 16 in San Francisco to decide whether to extend the stay, which expires the next day. A full hearing was set for Oct. 1 in Pasadena, Calif.
