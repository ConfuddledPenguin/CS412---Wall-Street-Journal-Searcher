It's a scenario that sends shivers down the backs of many homeowners; depressed housing prices for many, many years. But for some analysts, it's the only possible scenario. And for a simple reason: Prices are beynd the reach of too many potential home buyers.

"Housing prices have gone up a lot faster than people's income," says Michael Aronstein, a principal at Comstock Partners Inc. "It's going to take a long time to get them back in line."

According to figures compiled by Comstock, the hours of factory work required to meet monthly mortgage payments have been, for the past decade, at historical highs. The 1980s, Mr. Aronstein says, were a transition period -- the real-estate market was lukewarm, as it moved from hot to cold. The '90s, he says, will be cold, with price increases trailing the inflation rate through the decade.

"Factory workers are highly paid in relation to other nonprofessional workers," Mr. Aronstein says. "If they can't buy the average-priced house, who can?"

David Seiders, chief economist of the National Association of Home Builders, agrees that in some areas of the country -- particularly the Northeast and West Coast -- affordability is a problem. But he expects that beginning next year, price increases, adjusted for house-quality changes, will at least match the inflation rate. His reasoning is also simple: "The price of housing is linked closely to the cost ofproducing new housing," he says. "And with the limited supply of land and land-use controls spreading, prices may rise slightly above the inflation rate."

(Source: Comstock Partners, based on statistics published by National Association of Realtors and Department of Labor)
