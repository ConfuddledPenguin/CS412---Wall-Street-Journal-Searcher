Shearson Merger Into American Express Clears

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 08/10/90

NEW YORK -- Shearson Lehman Brothers Holdings Inc. ended its three-year run as a public company by proceeding with a merger into American Express Co., but not before shareholders got in their last licks at a final annual meeting. At the meeting, held in the American Express Tower here, about 92% of shares voting approved the previously announced merger into American Express, which had sold a minority stake in Shearson to the public in 1987. The outcome wasn't in question; American Express already owned about 60% of Shearson's common shares outstanding.

Though it had long wanted to reduce its Shearson holding rather than expand it, American Express is absorbing Shearson as part of a major effort -- including a cash infusion of more than $1 billion -- to revive the big securities firm's sagging fortunes. Shearson holders will get 0.48 of an American Express share for each Shearson share.

Shearson's new chairman and chief executive officer, Howard L. Clark Jr., said he is "optimistic" about his firm's prospects now that it is a wholly owned subsidiary of American Express. Despite enormous challenges, "the longtime Shearson spirit is very much alive," Mr. Clark said.

Some individual investors didn't let Mr. Clark get off that easy, however.

One investor groused about the plunge of Shearson's stock price from its initial offering at $34 a share three years ago to $12.75 currently. "Something certainly is wrong," either with the initial-offering price or the present one, the investor complained.

Mr. Clark responded: "There certainly is something wrong with the current situation. We lost $915 million in the first quarter of this year." But he said Shearson is working to correct its profit problems.

In response to a question, Mr. Clark also said the firm's legal costs are too high, and said he wanted to cut down the number of lawsuits and arbitration cases the firm faces.

There were also gripes about inaccurate account statements (a problem Mr. Clark says is being worked on), and about untimely responses to investors' complaints (also being worked on, said Mr. Clark). One investor said it would be a good idea if Shearson directors, which include former President Gerald Ford and actress Dina Merrill, traveled more to branch offices to get to know the company. "Now that Shearson will be 100%-owned by a travel company, I think we'll get around," Mr. Clark said, drawing guffaws.

The chairman said Shearson has attained a 12.5% share of the individual-investor market this year, up 1%.

As part of Shearson's huge retrenchment program, he said, the firm is still in the process of selling off the properties and paying off the loans of its Balcor Co. real-estate unit based in Skokie, Ill., a wind-down that Mr. Clark said should take "three to four" years.
