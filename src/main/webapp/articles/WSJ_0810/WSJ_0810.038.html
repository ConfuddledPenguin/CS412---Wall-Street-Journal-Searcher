Operating Profit At Coors Units Rose in Quarter

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 08/10/90

GOLDEN, Colo. -- Adolph Coors Co.'s non-beer businesses reported operating profit of about $10 million in the second quarter, compared with about $2 million in the year earlier period, marking the first time the non-beer operations contributed significantly to the company's bottom line, Coors chief financial officer, Harold Smethills, said. "These businesses are coming into their own," Mr. Smethills said. Coors began its diversification in 1910, when it bought a small ceramics company here. The brewer started to develop additional businesses during the past decade, including the manufacturing of ceramic parts for electronics, aluminum processing, and packaging and biotechnology businesses. The company began reporting its non-beer operating profit by segments in the first quarter of 1990.

For the second quarter, the company reported operating profit of about $4.7 million for its ceramics business compared with about $2 million in the year-ago second quarter. The second quarter profit more than made up for a first-quarter loss in that business, which was due to a $7 million write-down for work force adjustments, resulting in $200,000 in operating profit for the first half of the year. Aluminum manufacturing, which includes the production of aluminum sheets for can lids and tabs for both Coors and other can users, reported operating profit for the second quarter of about $5 million compared with less than $1 million a year ago.

The company's other businesses, which include Graphics Packaging Corp., a biotechnology company, an energy company and a small business incubator, reported operating profit of about $200,000 for the quarter, compared with a loss of about $900,000 in the year-ago quarter. The segment's performance was led by the packaging business, which had "much stronger sales, lower costs and more efficient operations" compared with the year-ago second quarter, Mr. Smethills said.

The company's biotechnology division, which is just starting to market some commercial nutritional ingredients, reported a loss, but the losses were substantially smaller than before, Mr. Smethills said.

Meanwhile, the company's flagship beer business reported increased beer shipments of 14% and an increase in operating profit of about 25%. The improvements were due to a comparison with a poor second quarter in 1989 and due to increased shipments of Coors Light. In addition, the company introduced Keystone, its popular-priced beer, in the third quarter of 1989, significantly boosting beer sales.

"Our diversification strategy that we've been talking about for about 10 years is starting to provide a return to our shareholders," said Mr. Smethills. Currently about 38% of Coors' $1.6 billion in assets is held by its non-beer businesses. "The overall return on equity isn't where we want it yet," he said, "but we're making progress." Coors has stated that it wants to make a 10% return in each of its businesses.

Earlier, Coors reported second-quarter earnings of $25.1 million on sales of $475.3 million, compared with year-earlier profit of $16.8 million on sales of $425.5 million.
