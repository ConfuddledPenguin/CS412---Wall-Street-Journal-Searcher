  • Author: Author Unknown

Nearly 400 commercial real-estate professionals were asked to forecast annual real-estate appreciation through 1994. No sector was predicted to even keep pace with inflation. The main culprits: overbuilding, identified by nearly 90% as one of the factors having the greatest impact on investment performance, and economic recession, cited by 75%. Survey respondents also named Houston as the single local market with the most investment potential. The most overvalued market: New York City.

Boomers and Bust

Demographics have clearly been on housing's side. No longer. During the '70s and '80s, house-hungry baby boomers pushed consruction -- and prices -- to astronomical levels. The boomers are still hungry, and they're eager to shed their old homes and buy new, bigger ones. There's only one problem: The baby bust is coming up behind them, and there aren't enough busters to buy the boomers' homes. That's going to put a crimp on new-home construction. But the constructoin industry isn't despairing entirely. If homeowners can't sell and trade up, they do have an alternative: remodeling. The National Association of Home Builders foreasts that by the end of the decade, additions and alterations to existing homes will rise to about one-third ofconstruction activity from about one-fourth currently. "The trade-up is going to happen," says David Seiders, chief economist of the home builders' group. "It's just going to take a different form."

(Source: Joint Center for Housing Studies, Harvard University)
