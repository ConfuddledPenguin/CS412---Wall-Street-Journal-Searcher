Marketing & Media: Mideast Tensions Spur Ratings Boost Of TV Newscasts --- CBS Executive Says News Viewership Could Soon Exceed Entertainment's

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 08/10/90

NEW YORK -- The Iraqi invasion of Kuwait and countermoves by U.S. forces have sparked sharply higher ratings for the three major networks' nightly newscasts -- so much so that the news ratings may surpass the networks' prime-time entertainment fare. Since the conflict in the Middle East intensified earlier this week with the deployment of U.S. troops, the combined ratings -- provided by A.C. Nielsen Co., a unit of Dun & Bradstreet Corp. -- for the evening newscasts on ABC, CBS and NBC are up 15% from the previous two weeks, according to David F. Poltrack, a CBS Inc. senior vice president.

If the trend -- and the conflict -- continues, next week's combined ratings for the three newscasts may exceed the ratings of prime-time shows. "If it happens, it would be the first time," Mr. Poltrack said.

Individually, the "CBS Evening News with Dan Rather" was up 22% in ratings, "ABC World News Tonight with Peter Jennings" increased its audience by 16% in ratings, and ratings for "NBC Nightly News with Tom Brokaw" rose by 8% from two weeks ago, Mr. Poltrack says.

The Iraq story also has boosted the ratings of the 24-hour news channel Cable News Network, owned by Turner Broadcasting System Inc. CNN nearly doubled its ratings, attracting 1.5% of all cable homes on Wednesday, the cable channel's highest 24-hour average this year. CNN typically gets a rating of 0.6% of cable homes.

For the broadcast networks, ratings for the nightly news aired at 6:30 p.m. or 7 p.m. EDT usually dip in the summer as viewers go on vacation or stay out later to enjoy the extra hours of daylight. The three broadcast-network newscasts had a combined rating of 30.8% of all television homes for the 1988-89 season. But that number dropped 20% in July to a combined 24.7 rating. A ratings point represents 1% of all television households or 921,000 homes.

While the Iraq invasion has been good news for network news, it could deflate the networks' aggressive programming strategy used in prime time this summer. Instead of airing the typical parade of series reruns because of low summer viewing, the networks have been running more original episodes in a bid for higher ratings -- yet now their nightly newscasts could draw even bigger audiences.

To date, the networks are stymied in efforts to cover the Middle East story with correspondents and camera crews. CBS News anchor Dan Rather continues to report from Jordan, cutting short a vacation in the south of France last week with "CBS Evening News" executive producer Tom Bettag. Both men flew to London when Iraq first invaded Kuwait.

ABC's "Nightline" anchor Ted Koppel flew to Cairo, as did NBC's "Meet the Press" anchor Garrick Utley. Both networks decided against sending their lead anchors to the Middle East because officials believed the newsmen were better equipped to monitor events from New York.
