Letters to the Editor: A Positive Paradox Of `Bad' S&L Loans

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/10/90

In differing with a previous article by Martin Feldstein and my article of June 19 arguing that the S&L bailout should not be viewed as adding to the current deficit, Herbert Stein ("A Little S&L Budget Honesty, Please," editorial page, June 25) uncharacteristically adds to the widespread confusion. Mr. Stein argues that money that the S&Ls "stole" or "invested in projects that were no good . . . crowded out . . . productive investment." He suggests that an increase in taxes or a reduction in government expenditures would bring about now an increase in compensating investment.

But an increase in taxes or a cut in transfer payments like Social Security, forcing households to spend less, may well discourage business investment as the need for capacity to meet consumer demand falters. And cuts in government expenditures for investment -- in roads, bridges, airports, research, education and health -- would only compound the problem for the economy and for business investment as well.

Mr. Stein is on most questionable ground, though, in concluding that the S&L losses resulted in less "productive" investment. Paradoxically, most of the S&L loans resulted in more, not less, investment. Many homes were financed by interest rates that were, at least in hindsight, "too low." But those homes are generally now being lived in. Many office buildings and shopping centers were built that did not turn out to be the best investments, but investments they were. The economy will undoubtedly get considerable use from them over the years ahead.

In fact, the S&L fiasco turned out to be akin to a large investment credit, no worse I might argue than the direct-investment tax credits that too many advocates of a free market have been happy to endorse in the past. It may have drained some resources for more productive investment from elsewhere, although this should be shown, not asserted. But it almost certainly brought on more total investment than we would have had if more prudent policies had held down S&L deposits and loans.

In any event though, the bailout now has nothing to do with current government expenditures. It is and hence should be viewed as a set of financial transactions to prevent a significant portion of the money supply -- S&L deposits -- from being wiped out. It is no excuse for raising taxes or, for that matter, reducing worthwhile government expenditures.

Robert Eisner

Economics Professor

Northwestern University

Evanston, Ill.
