P&G Reports Net Rose 66% In 4th Period --- Inproved Results Were Due To Strength in Diaper, Detergent Core Lines

  • Author: Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 08/10/90

Procter & Gamble Co. reported fiscal fourth-quarter net income soared 66%, reflecting strength in the company's core detergent and diaper products. In the quarter ended June 30, the Cincinnati-based consumer-products giant earned $284 million, or 78 cents a share, compared with $171 million, or 50 cents a share, a year earlier. Sales in the quarter climbed 14% to $6.22 billion from $5.43 billion.

Much of P&G's earnings growth stems from U.S. and international sales of traditional products, such as detergents, diapers and shampoos. "The company has invested a lot in new product development for those products in recent years and it's paying off handsomely," said Deepak Raj, an analyst with Merrill Lynch Capital Markets.

For instance, Pert Plus, a combination shampoo and conditioner, was introduced in 22 countries during fiscal 1990, building on its base as the leading U.S. shampoo brand.

The company's latest results also reflect continued improvements in operating profit margins from the company's continuing program to trim excess manufacturing and distribution costs. Chairman and Chief Executive Edwin L. Artzt has pledged to cut $1 billion in costs during the next two years. "It's really starting to kick in," said Bonita Austin, an analyst with Wertheim Schroder & Co.

One area where costs are rising is international marketing as the company expands product lines abroad. P&G introduced super-concentrated detergents -- preferred by many consumers because smaller amounts are needed per washload and packaging is more compact -- in Europe, Japan and parts of Latin America. In Japan, those products account for about 80% of the detergent market. In the U.S., the detergents are sold as the "Ultra" versions of the Cheer, Tide, Gain and Oxydol brand names.

For the 1990 fiscal year, P&G reported net income of $1.60 billion, or $4.49 a share, up 32% from $1.21 billion, or $3.56 a share in 1989. All year-ago per-share figures have been adjusted to reflect the 2-for-1 stock split paid in November 1989. Year-earlier results also included a $78 million charge related to closing some plants and consolidating certain operations.

Analysts said they expect strong results to continue into 1991, regardless of any weakening in the economy, because consumers continue to buy the company's products in recessions. Moreover, P&G is introducing more of its U.S. product lines around the world. For the year, P&G posted international earnings of $467 million, up 12% from $417 million last year.

Ms. Austin of Wertheim expects fiscal 1991 earnings of $5.20 a share, while Mr. Raj of Merrill Lynch estimates $5.25 a share.
