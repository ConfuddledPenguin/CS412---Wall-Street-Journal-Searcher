NARCOTICS IN JAPAN

Japan's affluence has made it a target for international narcotics cartels, and it must act fast to escape the drug-inflicted maladies of other wealthy societies, a United Nations official said.

"Japan needs immediate action, or its economy could be easily destroyed by drug-trafficking," Giuseppe di Gennaro, executive director of the U.N. Fund for Drug Abuse Control, said in Tokyo. The amount of intercepted cocaine smuggled into Japan in the first six months of 1990 was 137 pounds, up from only three pounds in the same period in 1989, Japanese police said.

"The fact that such a large quantity of drugs was seized in this country is evidence that there are already well-constructed distribution networks here," Mr. di Gennaro said.

Illegal drug use is still rare in Japan, and penalties are severe. Sentences of life imprisonment are possible for cocaine and heroin trafficking.

BRAZIL'S ANTI-SMOKING CAMPAIGN

Brazil's Health Ministry said smoking would be prohibited on domestic airline flights of two hours or less. The new rule will take effect after a public-education campaign by the airlines and the Department of Civilian Aviation, the ministry said. It said it also was considering creating special shuttle flights for smokers. Three months ago, the government approved a package of anti-smoking measures including a ban on cigarette sales to minors and prohibition of cigarette ads on television between 6 a.m. and 9 p.m.

NO INTER-KOREAN TRAVEL

North Korea effectively rejected a South Korean proposal to open the Korean border to unrestricted travel for five days next week. By repeating its longstanding demand for the repeal of Seoul's anti-communist national security laws, the North appeared to have squelched any last-minute chance to set in motion the procedures to allow inter-Korean exchanges. The North also imposed two other conditions unlikely to be met by South Korea: that the South allow a North Korean "consolation" team to visit jailed dissidents in the South and that it permit South Korean dissidents to attend a unification rally at the border on Aug. 15.

CORRUPTION IN CHINA

China disciplined 17,000 government officials in the first half of this year for corruption and other illegal activities, an official report said. One-third of the 23,645 cases of official wrongdoing filed from January through June involved bribery and embezzlement. Other cases involved "violation of economic policies," profiteering, abuse of power for personal gain and dereliction of duty, the Xinhua news agency reported. Most of those punished were local officials, the report said.

MERCEDES-BENZES FOR TOKYO

Nissho Iwai Corp., a Japanese trading house, will set up a wholly owned subsidiary within a month to sell Mercedes-Benz automobiles in the Tokyo area, a company spokesman said. The announcement reflected a move by Mercedes-Benz Japan to expand the network of dealerships selling its models imported from the West German parent company, Mercedes-Benz AG. The new subsidiary will have a showroom and a service shop in central Tokyo.

VIETNAMESE BAN

Vietnam is banning cigarette imports because they drain foreign exchange and hurt the local industry, the official Vietnam News Agency reported. The order bans the import, circulation and consumption of foreign-made cigarettes starting Oct. 1, the agency said. The report said diplomats, foreign businessmen and officials of international organizations were exempt from the ban but must obtain permits from the Ministry of Trade. If they sell the foreign-made cigarettes, that would be considered smuggling, the report said.

NEW ZEALAND CHAMPAGNE RULING

A New Zealand High Court judge has ruled that only wines produced in the Champagne region of France can be labeled as champagne for local sale.

The judgment was the result of a lawsuit brought by the Comite Interprofessionel du Vin de Champagne, a French champagne producers' agency, against Wineworths Group Ltd., a New Zealand liquor concern that distributes Australian wine labeled as champagne. The producers' agency was joined in the lawsuit by French companies Veuve Clicquot Ponsardin S.A., Champagne Lanson Pere et Fils and Champagne Bollinger S.A.

Wineworths's defense of the lawsuit was funded by Penfolds Ltd., an Australian wine producer that markets Seaview brand wine under the champagne label.

The High Court ruled that "champagne" isn't a generic word and that to sell wine produced other than in the Champagne region of France under that name contravened New Zealand's Fair Trading Act.
