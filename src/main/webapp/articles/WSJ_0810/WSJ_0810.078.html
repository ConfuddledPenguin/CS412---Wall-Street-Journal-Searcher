Thursday's Markets: Bond Prices, Stocks Rally As Oil Falls --- Treasury Sale Helps Dow Close Up 24.01; Dollar Edges Higher

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 08/10/90

NEW YORK -- Bargain hunters bought stocks and bonds, sparking a rally in both markets. The dollar was little changed against key currencies as traders awaited developments in the Middle East. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 24.01 to 2758.91, the first back-to-back gains in two weeks. Bond prices rose about 3/4 point, or $7.50 for each $1,000 face amount.

Stock investors continued to favor companies that might be less affected by a recession. Food stocks, a classic recession-resistant sector, advanced sharply. CPC International gained 2 3/8 to 78 5/8; General Mills rose 2 3/8 to 88.

But many big investors suspect that stocks' recovery won't last. "The rally is not sustainable," said Joseph DeMarco, managing director in charge of equity trading at Marinvest. He thinks the weak U.S. economy will re-emerge as the central focus once the Mideast crisis is resolved.

But continuing evidence of weak growth prompted some investors to step up and buy long-term Treasury bonds. Roberta Olsen, bond portfolio manager at Banc One Asset Management, bought in yesterday's sale of $10.26 billion in 30-year bonds.

"I definitely did buy, I am happy to say," said Ms. Olsen. At about 1:30 p.m. EDT, talk of a strong auction circulated through the markets and bonds and stocks rallied. Yields on the new bond dropped from the average auction level of 8.87% to 8.75% by the market's close.

Crude oil prices weakened yesterday -- another support for both stocks and bonds. Oil contracts for September delivery fell 29 cents a barrel to $25.67.

Stock trading volume was light, however, and the currency markets were quiet, too. Many investors continue to stand aside, waiting for some clear resolution to tensions in the Middle East.

One of the biggest players in the Treasury market, the World Bank, sat out yesterday's auction because of the uncertainty. Said Althea Duersten, senior manager of the World Bank's $9 billion bond portfolio: "The markets are better at quantifying economic risk than political risk. We're not making a major stand when political affairs could blow up when the market's closed."

In major market activity:

Stocks finished higher. New York Stock Exchange volume totaled 155.8 million shares, the least activity since July 30.

Bond prices rallied. The new 10-year note, auctioned Wednesday, gained 1/2 point, or $5 for each $1,000 face amount. Yields dropped to 8.64% from the average auction level of 8.77%.

The dollar finished little changed against most currencies. In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.5965 marks, up from 1.5925 marks late Wednesday, and unchanged at 149.80 yen.
