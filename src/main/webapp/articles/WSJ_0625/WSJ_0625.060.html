REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): The SEC's Blind Alley

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/25/90

Birds gotta fly and regulators gotta regulate, but these primal instincts do not forgive the Securities and Exchange Commission its latest overstepping. The unanimous federal appeals court that recently invalidated the SEC's one-share, one-vote requirement may have done the agency a favor. Perhaps now its bureaucrats can remember that their job is to give shareholders more say, not less. The SEC has indeed opposed management-entrenching anti-takeover devices, but this was no reason to invoke its authority with dual classes of stock. Unlike standard poison pills, every state requires that adopting more than one voting class of stock must be preceded by the vote of a majority or supermajority of shareholders. Several companies in recent years approved a higher class of stock, notably when the possible alternative was losing the benefits of a controlling family interest. Dow Jones, which publishes this newspaper, adopted dual classes of stock after non-family and non-management shareholders voted 2 to 1 to approve the change.

Judge Stephen Williams of Washington, D.C., was not amused by the agency's argument that it could substitute its rule for state corporate law on the ground that it was only regulating how stock exchanges list firms. "To argue that Congress's `equal regulation' mandate supports SEC control over corporate governance through national listing standards," Judge Williams wrote, "is to gamble that the court will accept a Commission spin on a statutory fragment without even a glance at its context. Wrong court, bad gamble."

That said, a word of caution to the winner of this case. The Business Roundtable brought the lawsuit because many chief executives oppose restrictions on what they can do to foil unwanted takeovers. This opinion is not a clear victory for that view. The court emphasized that shareholders get to vote on dual classes of stock, in effect agreeing to rewrite the ownership contract. This case again raises the question of why managers should be allowed to take actions that risk harming share price without first getting shareholder approval.

One understandable reason the SEC hoped for a different ruling was to add to its power to stop state legislatures from interfering in the free national market for corporate control. Massachusetts and Pennsylvania are the latest states to throw a protective cloak around firms they incorporate. The market eventually could take care of the problem. The law passed to protect Armstrong World Industries has cost shareholders in Pennsylvania firms some $1 billion in share-price losses, according to Institutional Shareholder Services.

There's probably not too much the SEC can do about poison pills unless it wants to lobby Congress for a federal rule that all firms must get shareholder permission before adopting defensive tactics.

SEC Chairman Richard Breeden has had some wise things to say about reducing the cost of capital in the U.S. compared with Europe and Japan. The SEC recently has deregulated the private-placement market and made it easier for foreign firms to register securities in the U.S. Still, Mr. Breeden refuses to define insider trading, despite courts reversing convictions, or pull back on the long, expensive list of SEC disclosure requirements for most transactions. Mr. Breeden might consider that the instinct to find things to regulate in the U.S. by his agency remains the single greatest difference among the world's securities markets.
