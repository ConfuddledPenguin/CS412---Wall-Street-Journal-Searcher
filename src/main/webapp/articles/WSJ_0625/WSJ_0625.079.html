Foreign Exchange: Mark May See Further Gains In Unification

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 06/25/90

Though the West German mark has been stalled for the past five months, some analysts still think German unification will lift the currency to new highs and into a more dominant role in world markets. But this journey will be no high-speed, trouble-free dash on the autobahn, they warn. "There is no precedent for this; it's never been done before," says Kurt Morgen, a Frankfurt-based member of the managing board of Dresdner Bank AG.

The engine driving the mark's upward mobility, predict the bulls, will be a combination of a booming German economy, higher interest rates, enlarged domestic financial markets and wider use of the currency in trade and investment, particularly in Eastern Europe.

"The D-mark is going to be the basis for German political and economic power in Central and Eastern Europe, and therefore throughout Europe," predicts Peter Rona, president and chief executive officer of IBJ Schroder Bank & Trust Co. in New York. Among the implications of this are stronger German stock and bond markets.

But these forecasts come with timetables that are at least six months to a year away.

Meanwhile, monetary and economic union between East and West Germany is scheduled to take place Sunday. Apprehension about its effects has had currency markets on edge since early February. That's when the mark's 20% rise against the dollar and yen -- a climb that began last September and accelerated after the fall of the Berlin Wall in November -- stopped dead in its tracks.

In late New York trading Friday, the dollar stood at 1.6745 marks and 154.85 yen, compared with 1.6778 marks and 154.80 yen late Thursday. Meanwhile, the British pound rose to $1.7330 from $1.7200.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the dollar was quoted at 155.10 yen; it closed Friday in Tokyo at 154.75 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6743 marks, down from Friday's Tokyo close of 1.6796 marks.

Since early February, the mark has mostly traded in a narrow band of 1.65-1.71 to the dollar. Slowing its advance were worries over the inflationary impact of the planned currency union and the huge outlays needed for overhauling the East German economy.

The mark also has been weakened by concern about growing turmoil in the Soviet Union, and it is especially vulnerable to political instability in Eastern Europe because of Germany's geographic proximity and trade links to the region.

Few traders or economists expect these negative factors to disappear anytime soon. The West German budget deficit will be about 50 billion marks ($29.8 million) this year and rise to 100 billion marks in 1991, predicts David D. Hale, chief economist at Kemper Financial Services Inc. in Chicago.

"The Bundesbank will have to increase its money supply to make credit available for growth in East Germany and make it possible for corporations to raise funds they want to invest in Eastern Europe," says Thomas Barman, head of treasury operations for Credit Suisse in New York.

At the same time, "it's very clear there will be Pan-German elections in December or January. Meanwhile, unemployment in East Germany will rise toward two million, which will lead to more pressure for government spending, which raises inflation," warns Mr. Hale. What's more, he adds: "There's the unknown cost of Germany having to support the Soviet Union." Last week, Bonn said it will guarantee a five billion mark bank loan to Moscow.

This spending spree puts the Bundesbank in a bind. If it tightens monetary policy too quickly, "it could strangle growth," warns Eric Taze-Bernard, senior economist at Banque Indosuez in Paris. Yet if its policies are too lax, Germany risks accelerating inflation and a weakening mark.

"The big short-term, six-month question is the independence of the Bundesbank," says Mr. Hale. "Can {Bundesbank President Karl Otto} Poehl be allowed to do what {Federal Reserve Board Chairman Paul} Volcker did in 1981-1982?" -- temper faster growth and inflation with a tighter monetary policy.

In the long run, Mr. Hale and others are betting he will. And as a consequence, many of the short-term mark bears remain long-term bulls.

Economists at Merrill Lynch & Co. see the combination of a relaxed fiscal and tight monetary policies propelling the mark to new highs, just as a similar U.S. policy mix sent the dollar soaring in the early 1980s. Michael Andrews, a Merrill economist, predicts the mark will eventually rise toward 1.50 marks to the dollar, surpassing its record of 1.56.

"The {mark} will strengthen, because it will attract capital," says Mr. Barman of Credit Suisse. "The German stock market is going to look very, very attractive in years to come."

Mr. Rona of IBJ-Schroder envisions the evolution of a huge trading bloc in Eastern Europe that uses the mark as its payments currency. Neither the Soviet Union nor any of its six former East bloc satellites -- Poland, East Germany, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, Romania or Bulgaria -- have convertible currencies. Up to now, trade within the group -- the Council for Mutual Economic Assistance, or Comecon -- has been paid for in so-called "convertible rubles," a hybrid that is essentially a system of credits and debits.

But as of Jan. 1, 1991, trade between the seven will be conducted in hard currencies. Besides East Germany, Mr. Rona believes other East bloc nations will adopt the mark for trade transactions. "Poland, Romania, Hungary and Czechoslovakia will be filing up to join," he argues. "Imports, exports, investment decisions and returns will all be calculated in D-marks." Even domestic prices and wages will be figured in marks, he predicts.

Germany "can create a Deutsche mark zone throughout Europe," asserts Mr. Rona. Already, the Dutch, Swiss and Austrian currencies are closely tied to the mark, and the currency has long been the linchpin of the European Monetary System's exchange rate mechanism, a joint float of nine European currencies.

"Given the economic links in the region, the mark could emerge as a more important global currency," says Kemper's Mr. Hale.

The mark's status could be further cemented by the pivotal role German companies and banks are expected to play in Eastern Europe's development. Already, "Germany is the biggest supplier of credit to Eastern Europe and its biggest western trading partner," adds Michael Rosenberg, a vice president at Merrill Lynch.

George Noble, a portfolio manager at Fidelity Management and Research Co. in Boston, says that one top Italian executive called Eastern Europe "a private German hunting preserve."

Analysts also predict that the large costs of funding German reunification will lead to a rapid growth in the German bond, stock, short-term money and futures markets. "That," says Mr. Hale, "will encourage increased use of the mark."

Not everyone, however, buys the supermark argument. David M. Mars, European economist for S.G. Warburg Securities Ltd. in London, predicts the Bundesbank won't be able to check the inflationary pressures that unification unleashes. He argues that despite the central bank's solid reputation as an inflation fighter, it has oversold its ability to control the German money supply and remain independent of politics.

Mr. Mars says that at some point, five new members of the central bank's governing council will come from East Germany and "they will constantly fight for easier credit policies." He adds: "Poehl and the rest of the council aren't going to raise interest rates to fight inflation, and the mark will weaken."

Mr. Mars also questions East Germans' work ethic after more than four decades of life under communist rule. Within six months, he says, "people will start to look for an alternative to the mark." He predicts it will be the French franc. Backed by a big economy and a government committed to low inflation, "France will look like a zone of stability," he says.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery settled at $350.40 an ounce, down 30 cents. Estimated volume was a moderate 2.5 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Monday, gold was quoted at $350.35 an ounce.
