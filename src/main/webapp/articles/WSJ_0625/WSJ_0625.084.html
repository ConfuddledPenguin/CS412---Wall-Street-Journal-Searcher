Two Banks Seem To Hold Fate Of Trump Loan

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 06/25/90

Donald Trump's pending $65 million bank loan appears to be in the hands of two foreign banks who, as of yesterday, still hadn't agreed to the proposal. Negotiations with the banks, West German-based Dresdner Bank and French-based Societe General, are expected to resume today, as Mr. Trump's creditors race to complete the proposal by tomorrow night's deadline. If Mr. Trump doesn't receive the $65 million by then, he will likely default on bond payments on Trump's Castle, which could trigger other defaults on his $2 billion in bank debt.

All told, 70 lending institutions must agree to the plan that would allow Mr. Trump to suspend interest payments on some $800 million of the bank debt. While a vast majority of the banks have agreed to the proposal, a single dissent would wreck the deal, bankers say.

The $65 million loan would be supplied by Mr. Trump's major creditors: units of Bankers Trust Corp., Citicorp, Chase Manhattan Corp., First Fidelity Bancorp, Manufacturers Hanover Corp., Midlantic and the U.S. unit of National Westminster Bank PLC.

On Friday, three Japanese banks that, like Dresdner, hold first mortgages on the Trump Tower in Manhattan, lifted their opposition to the bailout plan. Those banks have purchased pieces of an original $75 million loan issued to Mr. Trump by Chase Manhattan.

The overwhelming majority of the 54 participants in more than $1 billion in loans issued by Citicorp have also agreed to the proposal, though a few holdouts remain, according to people close to the negotiations.

After a long silence, Mr. Trump issued a statement Friday that expressed optimism that the deal would get done. "An overwhelming number of the participating lenders, representing both national and international financial institutions, have now joined the major banks in supporting our plan," the statement said.

On Saturday, attorneys representing Chase Manhattan met with representatives from Dresdner to get the German bank to accept a second mortgage on the property, to no avail. Today, senior executives from Chase are expected to call on Dresdner officers to lobby for the proposal. Societe General owns less than $15 million of a $200 million loan Chase issued to Mr. Trump for his purchase of a 72-acre vacant site on Manhattan's Upper West Side, according to an individual close to the talks.

The plan's purpose, say bankers, is to help Mr. Trump avoid bankruptcy court. Instead, the banks want to have an orderly sale of assets, and to give Mr. Trump time to improve operations in his airline, casino and hotels empire.

Lawyers for the banks worked through the weekend, while many of the bankers took yesterday off. If the deal is agreed to by tomorrow, the seven banks have arranged for a bridge loan to be extended in case the paperwork for the $65 million loan can't be completed in time, said a person close to the talks.
