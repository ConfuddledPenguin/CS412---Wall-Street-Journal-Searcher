Technology: Canadian Court Says Apple's Silicon Chips Can be Copyrighted

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/25/90

OTTAWA -- The Canadian Supreme Court ruled that Apple Computer Inc.'s silicon chips are software eligible for copyright protection. The court dismissed an appeal by a group of companies that manufacture Apple II clones, computers that run the same programs as Apple II. Mackintosh Computers Ltd., Repco Electronics Co., House of Semi-Conductors Ltd. and Microcom had copied Apple's Autostart ROM and Applesoft programs from Apple's silicon chips. Apple holds a copyright for these programs, which enable its computers to carry out such tasks as word processing.

The companies said they hadn't infringed Apple's copyright because silicon chips aren't software eligible for copyright protection. They argued that only the written version of the programs is protected by copyright, not the chips on which the programs are etched.

The Supreme Court, upholding a lower court ruling, said the programs embedded in the chips are a reproduction of the programs in computer language and are protected by copyright.

The court also upheld a lower court order directing the companies to turn over any chips containing the copied programs and an order for an accounting of profits made from the sale of illegal chips.
