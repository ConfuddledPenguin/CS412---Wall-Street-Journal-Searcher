Machine Tool Orders Dropped in May From April but Topped Year-Ago Pace

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 06/25/90

Machine tool orders placed with U.S. producers dropped abruptly in May from the relatively high April rate, but remained slightly above a year earlier. However, machine tool distributors had a strong sales month. Industry executives say the disparity illustrates the danger of placing too much importance on any single month's figures. Machine tool producers say underlying demand is relatively good, except from automotive and defense-related customers.

U.S. machine tool plants received $206.2 million of orders last month, down 29% from April's $289.3 million, according to NMTBA -- The Association for Manufacturing Technology, a McLean, Va., trade group. However, orders remained slightly above the $205.3 million of May 1989, and were about in line with the monthly average for the final quarter of last year and the first quarter of 1990.

Machine tool distributors, who sell imported as well as domestic machines, had an 8.8% order increase in May from a year earlier, and a striking 38% jump from April, according to the American Machine Tool Distributors' Association, Rockville, Md. In April, however, distributor sales were unusually weak, while U.S. machine tool factories had a good month.

Machine tools are sophisticated parts-making machines, including lathes, machining centers and metal-forming presses, and are bought mainly by factories that produce durable goods. While plants that make machinery, household appliances and other metal products are operating at respectable rates right now, few are being pressed to add capacity.

That gives them time to shop for the most suitable equipment to raise productivity, and there's a lot of shopping going on, machine tool producers say. "Our activity level is incredible," says Michael O. Harrington, vice president, sales, for S.E. Huffman Corp., Clover, S.C., producer of specialized grinders, laser systems and water-jet cutting equipment. But some customers will defer signing an order until after the international machine tool show in Chicago in September, he says.

Buyers want to see the latest machines on display at the show. Also, some will find it easier to justify the cost of a trip to Chicago if they haven't already ordered the machines they need.

Some large machine tool users have more basic reasons to hold off buying. Producers of cars, trucks and automotive parts, for instance, continue to order cautiously, partly because their own sales have been sluggish. Also, some haven't yet decided what vehicles to produce in 1993 and beyond in light of tightening governmental regulations on fuel economy and exhaust emissions.

Listening to the debate in Congress, it is becoming increasingly clear that defense spending is going to be slashed, machine tool executives add. Defense-oriented aerospace concerns will soon have a lot of surplus capacity, discouraging them from buying new machinery.

However, other manufacturers are ordering machine tools to increase plant productivity. "We're seeing order activity from a good cross section of industry and from all across the country," says Stephen M. Peterson, vice president, sales, for Giddings & Lewis Inc., Fond du Lac, Wis. "The oil patch is coming back," and the company is getting orders from jet engine manufacturers in the Northeast, machinery producers in the Midwest and aerospace concerns on the West Coast. May orders were higher than April, he says, and the proposal rate is 20% higher than a year ago.

Automotive companies are buying equipment to modernize their tool and die shops, says Richard R. Burkhart, chairman and chief executive officer of Pratt & Whitney, West Hartford, Conn. And some are placing orders for machines to make a new generation of engines for 1993-model cars, he says. But like most other producers, he says current orders are lower than inquiry rates would indicate they should be.

May orders for lathes, grinders, milling machines, machining centers, boring mills and other machines to shape metal by cutting were $137.6 million, the NMTBA said, down 7% from $147.9 million a year earlier and off about one-third from April's $206.7 million.

Domestic producers of metal-forming presses and other machines to shape metal with pressure received $68.7 million of orders in May, up 20% from $57.4 million a year earlier but 17% below April's $82.6 million.

Machine tool shipments by U.S. plants totaled $272.6 million last month, up 13% from $240.8 million a year earlier, the NMTBA said. The industry's backlog of unfilled orders declined in May, as it has in four of the first five months of 1990. At the end of the month, the backlog totaled $1.65 billion, the NMTBA said, down 8.7% from the end of last year and off 29% from the high for the current economic expansion, which came in February 1989.
