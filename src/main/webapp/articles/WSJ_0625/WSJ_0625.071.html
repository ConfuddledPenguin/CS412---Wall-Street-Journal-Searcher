If You Answer to Another Name, You Might End Up Spending Less

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 06/25/90

The Murphy Card. Don't leave home without it. Too bad] Somebody else already has that slogan. But no matter. In a new foray into the realm of prestige charge cards, Maryland National Bank is offering the Murphys of this world and the Bowes, Careys, Finnegans, Gallaghers, Hubers, Kellys, Kennedys, McCarthys, O'Briens, Stewarts and Williamses -- the chance to have the ultimate in personalized plastic.

Maryland National, the nation's sixth-largest issuer of credit cards, is soliciting Americans with those 12 ancestral British Isles names to order "family cards," with their own names boldly emblazoned across the top. Applicants can choose a handsome black card or a more-upscale gold card. Both sport the family's crest -- well, something like it, anyway.

"The design of the crests is a somewhat educated guess," concedes Terrance R. Flynn, senior vice president of MBNA America, the bank's credit-card subsidiary. "There are some where there's basically just a description and not much artwork. In that case we do an artistic rendering of the verbal description. I won't guarantee it's 100% accurate."

In reality, the family cards are little more than the standard pauper's Visa cards dressed up in princely garb. They're the latest offshoot of the affinity-card phenomenon in which banks cook up cards designed to appeal to various special-interest groups. Of the 211 million Visa and MasterCard charge cards in consumers' hands, an estimated 31 million are affinity cards.

As such, the family cards join such earlier prestige plastic as Leader Federal Bank's "Elvis card" and a proposed card for the Young Astronauts of Utah that, not surprisingly, never got off the ground.

Credit-card critics don't take well to such seeming frivolity. "There are only a couple of dozen really valid affinity groups," says Spencer Nilson, publisher of a credit-card newsletter. "All these others are mutants, like those Ninja Turtles."

But at Leader Federal in Memphis, Tenn., Executive Vice President Brad Champlin isn't about to concede that the Elvis card is anything less than a national treasure. He warns that the current card, featuring a guitar-pounding Elvis in front of a Wurlitzer jukebox, is "a limited first edition." When 25,000 have been issued (10,000 are outstanding right now), "we'll retire the current design and register it in the national archives or somewhere."
