First Brands Holders Seek Buyer for Firm --- Stock Soars on Disclosure; Partial Sale Is Possible, Public Offering Mulled

  • Author: Leon E. Wynter
  • Date: 06/25/90

The controlling shareholders of First Brands Corp. said they were seeking buyers for all or part of the company, setting off a 20% runup in the share price. In a filing last week with the Securities and Exchange Commission, the holders, which include CS First Boston Inc. and Metropolitan Life Insurance Co., said they would actively solicit bids for all or a portion of the consumer products company's shares or assets. The holders also indicated they might consider selling their stake in another public offering.

In composite New York Stock Exchange trading Friday, First Brands shares closed at $28.375, up $4.75. About 1.8 million shares traded Friday, making First Brands the fifth most active Big Board stock. At the closing price, First Brands has a total indicated value of $613 million for the more than 21.6 million shares outstanding.

First Brands, based in Danbury, Conn., manufactures and markets such products as Prestone anti-freeze, Glad plastic bags and STP automobile additives. It was created in a leveraged buy-out of these businesses from Union Carbide Corp. in 1986. In December the buy-out group, which includes company management and other individuals, sold 30% of the company in a public offering.

At the time of the buy-out, the investors' equity was about $80 million, according to Donald DeSantis, vice president and chief financial officer at First Brands. A 30% stake in the equity was then about $24 million. The public offering of the 30% stake, at $19 a share, netted the investors $115 million -- almost a fivefold return.

The timing of Friday's announcement by CS First Boston and Metropolitan Life, which together hold about 48% of the shares, surprised some analysts, who wondered why such a move wasn't made when the 30% stake was offered in December.

The stock's runup also was unexpected. Pat O'Brien, who follows Union Carbide for Anantha Raman & Co., was surprised at the share price jump because "the company has been on the block from day one."

Spokesmen for CS First Boston couldn't be reached for comment Friday. Metropolitan Life officials declined to comment. In the SEC filing, the investor group said that while it still thinks the shares are "an attractive investment," it has "received expressions of interest" from third parties.

First Brands reported an 83% decline in profit for its fiscal third quarter ended March 31, to $2.7 million, or 12 cents a share, but the company said the results were distorted by gains from the sale of certain businesses in the year-earlier period. The company started up heavily in debt, and posted a loss of $7 million on sales of $837 million in 1987. Cost cutting and quality improvements led to a $61 million profit in 1989 on sales of $1.2 billion.

Mr. DeSantis said that he wasn't surprised at the move by the major investors. "But the timing is a bit sooner than we thought," he said.

"Typically, investment bankers are not long-term investors," Mr. DeSantis said. "One possibility, which would be good for the business, would be to find a permanent home, rather than be controlled by financial investors."

According to the fiscal third-quarter statement, First Brands' long-term debt was $370.7 million, down from $434.4 million at June 30, 1989.

Ms. O'Brien noted that CS First Boston led the buy-out creating First Brands under pressure to assist Union Carbide's defense against a hostile takeover bid from GAF Corp. "When the investment banks started taking ownership, it was one of the signs that the price was too high. These people would rather be back to their own business rather than run a polyethylene bag business," she said.

The most likely buyer would be one with a strategic interest, "a chemical company seeking vertical integration, or an offshore company that wants our company's distribution strength," Mr. DeSantis said.
