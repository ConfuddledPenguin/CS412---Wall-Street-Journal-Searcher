World Markets: Hong Kong Stock Exchange Sets Drive To Draw Local Investors Back to Trading

  • Author: Jesse Wong
  • Date: 06/25/90

HONG KONG -- Conventional wisdom has it that Hong Kong's stock market has such a wide local following that everyone -- from the rich to the proverbial housekeepers and cabdrivers -- stands ready to invest in just about anything that moves. So it has come as a surprise for the Hong Kong Stock Exchange to learn from a survey that the colony's stock-owning public numbers only about 363,000 people, or 9% of the adult population. "The figure is disappointingly low. It's a shock," said Francis Yuen, the exchange's chief executive officer.

Commissioned by the exchange and conducted late last year, the survey points to a big gap in efforts to repair the local stock market's dented image. Such efforts have been under way since the 1987 Black Monday crash exposed serious management flaws in Hong Kong's market. They took on added urgency after last year's brutal suppression on June 4 of pro-democracy protests in Beijing. In addition to exacerbating public concern over this British colony's 1997 return to Chinese rule, that event helped underscore local stocks' vulnerability to political turmoil in China.

The consequent market reforms and promotion campaigns undertaken by the government and the exchange have been aimed mainly at allaying concerns of foreign investors. Little, though, has been done to woo the local public. One result, as the survey indicates, is dwindling participation by Hong Kong investors.

Professing alarm at the phenomenon, the stock exchange is moving to win back its home market. Its campaign, announced Thursday in conjunction with release of the survey, consists of a five-week promotional exhibition. Moving from site to site in mostly middle-income neighborhoods, the exchange's display is designed to remind the public that Hong Kong has a stock market and that people should consider putting money in it.

"Too much has been done since 1987 to promote the stock market to foreign investors," Mr. Yuen said. "It's the individuals we need to do more for."

By his estimate, trading activity used to be split relatively evenly among three camps -- rich local individuals, small local investors and institutions, most of them based overseas. With data only on those local individuals, rich and otherwise, who owned stock as of late 1989, the survey doesn't show how the overall trading pattern has changed over the years. Mr. Yuen argued nevertheless that the role of small-time local investors has diminished noticeably since the 1987 crash.

Investment interest generally has picked up over the past couple of months as the market slowly regains ground. As a result, the value of shares traded each session on many recent days has exceeded HK$2 billion (US$256.8 million), double the daily average during the first quarter of 1990. Much of the increase is attributed to Taiwan investors switching out of their ailing market. But Mr. Yuen said Hong Kong can't count on such a boon to last and must try harder to rebuild investor confidence at home.

He cited comparable surveys that put stock-ownership levels in the U.S. and Canada at around 16% of the adult population, saying Hong Kong should do at least as well. But judging by the Hong Kong Stock Exchange's survey, much more than the five-week promotion may be needed to address what may be a lack of confidence by the colony's potential investors.

"Some of it may be just the result of misconceptions about the market," Mr. Yuen said. "But it will take a long time to correct."

Hong Kong's survey, conducted from September to December, bases its conclusions on 2,000 randomly selected adults. For the survey's purposes, the term stock owners refers only to those who invest directly in stocks and not those who do so only indirectly, such as through unit trusts. Among those 2,000, 307 owners and 301 non-owners were picked for in-depth interviews.

Among the respondents who previously owned stock but no longer did so at the time of the survey, the most oft-cited reason for not investing is that they have had an "unpleasant experience" in the market. Though not specified in the survey, such experiences, according to Mr. Yuen, stem mainly from losses suffered in the 1987 crash or during last year's June 4 political crisis.

As for stock owners, the survey undermines to some extent the often-cited stereotype of them as greedy, ill-informed speculators willing to take illogical risks.

White-collar workers, at 29.2%, form a core of the stock-owning population. Professionals and businessmen follow with 28.1%. Next come blue-collar workers at 21.7%, housewives at 12.5%, the retired at 7.3% and, finally, students and the unemployed at 1.2%.

Friday's Market Activity

Tokyo stocks ended lower in thin trading Friday as participants took conservative positions ahead of the weekend, while the yen's softness discouraged buying, traders said.

London shares were moderately higher.

The Nikkei index of 225 selected issues was down 392.70 points at 31694.57. The index slipped 0.49 point Thursday.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the Nikkei index gave up another 339.09 points to 31355.48.

Friday's volume on the First Section was estimated at 350 million shares, down from 409 million Thursday.

Losing issues outnumbered winning issues, 703 to 227, while 189 were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index (Topix) of all issues listed in the first section, which fell 5.24 points Thursday, sank 20.50 points, or 0.87%, to 2341.06.

The second section index, which rose 4.15 points Thursday, fell 16.31 points, or 0.39%, to close at 4135.42. Volume in the second section was estimated at 20 million shares, down from 24.5 million Thursday.

Growing concern that Japan may not be able to avoid inflation in the coming months is suppressing buying activity, although volume indicates that the bearishness hasn't gotten out of hand, observers said.

Issues hit by moderate profit-taking and general bearishness Friday included Canon Sales, down 140 yen (91 cents) to 4,880 yen, Chubu Electric, down 100 to 3,300, Fujisawa Pharmaceutical, down 30 to 2,020, and Dainippon Pharmaceutical, off 30 to 3,160.

Nippon Telegraph & Telephone fell 20,000 yen to 1,090,000 yen, KDD dropped 200 to 18,500 and Toho Co. fell 400 to 37,200. NTV fell 200 to 34,700.

But Nintendo rose 200 to 26,100, while JAL was up 100 to 18,100.

Hirose Electric added 140 to 6,990 and Intec rose 100 to 4,870.

Sony ended 40 higher at 8,570 while TDK rose 30 to 6,780.

London share prices finished moderately higher as investors chose to sit out the session before the opening of this week's new trading account and expiration of June stock-index futures and options contracts.

Dealers said there were too many corporate and economic uncertainties lurking in the background for institutional investors to get more deeply involved in the market. Moreover, ongoing major U.K. sporting events, combined with an unimpressive start on Wall Street, set the market adrift for most of the session.

The broad-based Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index closed up 8.2 points at 2378.5, down from the intraday peak of 2381.1 just after the release of British trade figures. The day's low of 2368.2, reached after one hour of official trading, was down 2.1 on the day.

At the final tally, the narrower FT 30-share index was up 5.2 points at 1913.5. Volume registered a moderate 497.8 million shares compared with the 415.4 million traded Thursday.

In the futures market, the June FT-SE 100 index contract closed at 2390.0, up from its close of 2389.0 Thursday.

Midmorning trade figures, which were slightly better than market expectations, had only a temporary impact on an otherwise sluggish market. There was little incentive to shift existing positions before the forthcoming close of the second quarter, market professionals added.

May's current account deficit stood at #1.32 billion ($2.27 billion), down from #1.60 billion in April and compared with estimates of #1.35 billion.

Amid a session of sparse corporate developments, shares in pharmaceutical company Wellcome were the biggest gainers on the day. Dealers said the move came in the wake of a hefty sell-off earlier in the week on bearish forecasts for the company's Retrovir drug used to combat the effects of the HIV virus. Dealers contended the drop was overdone and Wellcome gained 25 pence (43 cents) to 625 pence on volume of 1.6 million shares.

Stocks closed higher in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Amsterdam and Madrid and lower in Milan. Stockholm was closed for a holiday. South African gold stocks closed mixed.

Share prices closed lower in Taipei and Wellington, were mixed in Hong Kong and Singapore and higher in Sydney, Seoul and Manila.
