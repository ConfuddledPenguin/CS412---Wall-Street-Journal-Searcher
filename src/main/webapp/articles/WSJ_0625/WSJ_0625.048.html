JAPAN: WORLD'S NO. 1 DONOR

Japan for the first time replaced the U.S. as the world's largest donor of official foreign aid in 1989, the Finance Ministry said. "It's an epoch-making development," said a ministry official.

Japan's official development-assistance payments totaled $8.95 billion in 1989. The U.S. was next at $7.66 billion and France third with $7.46 billion, the ministry said.

Japan's 1989 total, at 1.235 trillion yen, was the country's largest ever in yen terms, up 5.6% from a year earlier, the official said. But it dropped 1.9% in dollar terms, ministry figures show, because of yen depreciation against the dollar.

Growth in Japanese foreign-aid spending was less significant in Japan becoming the world's No. 1 donor than a slowdown in U.S. aid spending, the ministry official said. He said U.S. development-aid spending declined to $7.66 billion in 1989 from $10.14 billion in 1988.

HONG KONG PRISON CHANGE

Hong Kong is to end the use of corporal punishment in its prisons, the government said. It said a Correctional Services Department study had shown there were better ways to rehabilitate prisoners than beating them with a cane. Canings, mainly used against young offenders, have become less frequent in the British colony in recent years.

MORE VIETNAMESE EXPORTS

Vietnam substantially increased exports in the first half of this year but failed to increase the growth rate in agriculture and industry, said the chairman of the State Planning Commission, Pham Van Khai, according to the Vietnam News Agency. Mr. Khai said first-half exports -- including crude oil, rice, pork, rubber, coffee and tea -- were up 40% from the same period last year. He said imports for the period were up 6.8%. Inflation averaged 2.7% a month in the the first five months of 1990, Mr. Khai said.

ROMANIAN PETROCHEMICALS

Romania hopes to upgrade its petrochemicals industry through joint ventures with Western companies, the state news agency Rompress said. Rompress said the Ministry of Chemistry has contacted more than 20 foreign companies, including Dow Chemical Co. of the U.S., Imperial Chemicals Industries PLC of the United Kingdom, Rhone-Poulenc S.A. of France and Mannesmann AG of West Germany to discuss possible cooperation in chemical products. Romania now exports various petrochemical products including polyvinyl chloride and solvents.

NEW HONDA PROGRAM

Japan's Honda Motor Co. announced that it has begun a world-wide program to equip its dealers with chlorofluorocarbon-gas retrieval devices designed to prevent the release of CFCs into the atmosphere during servicing of vehicle air-conditioner systems. In Japan, 2,000 of Honda's 2,600-outlet network will be equipped by autumn with the gear, a spokesman said. The devices cost 260,000 yen ($1,680) each, with dealers contributing some of the expense but with no cost to the customer, he said. Toyota Motor Corp. recently said its overseas service units are installing similar gear, while about 30% of its Japanese units have already been equipped.

WAR CRIMINALS IN BRITAIN

The British government has decided to defy the unelected House of Lords and reintroduce a bill to allow the prosecution of elderly suspected Nazi war criminals living in Britain. Sir Geoffrey Howe, Conservative leader of the House of Commons, said the government intends during the next session of Parliament to resubmit the War Crimes Bill defeated by the Lords June 5. The Lords' powers are limited to delaying legislation for one year. The elected House of Commons had passed the bill in December 270-60. Peers who opposed the bill said fair trials were impossible and dragging aging emigres before courts would only stir old hatreds.

BRITISH SHIPYARD BAN

The European Community Commission confirmed it will continue to block the reopening of shipyards in northeast England even though world-wide demand for new ships is likely to double by the end of the decade.

The commission said that when North East Shipbuilders Ltd. closed last year, with the loss of 4,000 jobs in the Sunderland area, it allowed the British government to give state aid to the area on the condition that the shipyards remain shut.

The decision has angered politicians and union leaders lobbying for the yards to be reopened. They claim that the Sunderland yards are among the most modern in Europe and that several foreign companies have approached the government offering to take them over.

A commission spokesman said the commission wanted to eliminate overcapacity in the European shipbuilding industry and that marginal yards like Sunderland's would have to remain shut. He concluded: "There's no way 'round that decision."
