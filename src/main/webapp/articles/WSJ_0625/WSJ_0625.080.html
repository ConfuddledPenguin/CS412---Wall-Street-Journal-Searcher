Western States Backed by Court In Gasoline Case

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/25/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- A federal appeals court ruled that four Western states have a right to take major oil companies to court over accusations that the companies conspired to fix wholesale gasoline prices in the 1970s and to create a gasoline shortage in 1973. The unanimous opinion by three judges of the Ninth U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals overturned a 1986 court decision and is the latest step in a case that has been in and out of the courts for 15 years. The lower court decision had dismissed the suit on grounds it didn't have sufficient chance of success at trial.

The appellate court held Friday that the states presented sufficient evidence of a conspiracy to send the case to a jury trial in Los Angeles.

The litigation, brought by California, Oregon, Washington and Arizona, seeks damages that could total billions of dollars, according to Michael Spiegel, a lawyer acting for California.

The states are seeking the return of money they contend they were overcharged for bulk purchases of gasoline between 1971 and 1975, when the suit was filed. There is a separate action in state court that seeks to recover losses by consumers from alleged overcharges for gasoline.

The defendants in the case are Atlantic Richfield Co; Exxon Corp.; Mobil Corp.; Shell Oil Co., a unit of Royal Dutch/Shell Group; Standard Oil Co., a unit of British Petroleum Co.; Texaco Inc.; and Unocal Corp. A number of the companies said Friday they were studying the opinion and would decide later whether to appeal.

The appellate court said the states' evidence of a potential conspiracy to affect prices included the use of news releases announcing wholesale price increases, advance posting of price changes at company offices and direct contact with competitors. In the court's opinion, Judge Dorothy Nelson wrote that "a jury could conclude that the oil companies agreed, either implicitly or explicitly, to create market conditions that would facilitate tacit or express price coordination."

The defendants argued that the evidence was "too anecdotal and fragmentary to support a broad inference of conspiracy of the sort alleged."

Florida, Kansas and Connecticut originally were plaintiffs in the suit but dropped out of it several years ago.

The litigation isn't directly related to an antitrust lawsuit filed by California and by the city of Long Beach, Calif. That action alleges that major oil firms conspired to depress crude oil prices to reduce the taxes and royalties they paid on crude oil from state-owned tidelands.
