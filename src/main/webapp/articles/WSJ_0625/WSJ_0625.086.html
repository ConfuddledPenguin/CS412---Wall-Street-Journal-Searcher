Marketing & Media: NBC Is Willing To Discuss Offer From Cable System

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 06/25/90

NEW YORK -- National Broadcasting Co. said it is willing to discuss Tele-Communications Inc.'s proposal to use its cable systems as NBC affiliates in rural areas not served by broadcast affiliates of NBC. But the No. 1 rated network chided the nation's largest cable company for using the request to gain political leverage. And privately, NBC executives said there was virtually no chance of Tele-Communications' proposal moving forward.

Tele-Communications President John Malone made the request last week in a letter to Robert Wright, chief executive of NBC, a unit of General Electric Co. The request was widely viewed in the media industry as a ploy by Tele-Communications to pressure NBC, which is lobbying Congress to ban exclusive arrangements between cable operators and programmers.

In its letter, NBC emphasized that any discussions would include its 200-plus affiliates, which firmly oppose any network-affiliate partnership with Tele-Communications.

Tele-Communications said its proposal was aimed at providing NBC programming combined with local newscasts in markets where the company has cable systems that currently have to import NBC affiliate signals from distant cities. For example, Tele-Communications proposed to launch an NBC affiliate on its cable system in Olympia, Wash., which doesn't have a local station, instead relying on Seattle.

"We're delighted at the prospect of negotiating with NBC," Robert Thomson, a Tele-Communications vice president, said after reviewing NBC's letter, sent Friday. "We thought we'd get a hell, no."

While the response wasn't nearly so negative, NBC's Mr. Wright scolded Mr. Malone in Friday's two-page letter by writing: "But let's be honest, John. Your representatives have admitted that you are raising the issue now in an attempt to draw some . . . parallel between the availability of NBC programming to non-broadcast distributors, and the issue being debated in Congress about access to cable program services by new technologies such as {direct-to-home satellite TV}. . . . Whatever parallel you are trying to make doesn't bear scrutiny."

NBC is interested in dropping exclusivity rights because it is partners in a direct-to-home satellite TV service, called Sky Cable, that is scheduled to premiere in late 1993 with 108 channels. Sky Cable needs programming now available exclusively on cable to ensure survival.

Mr. Wright wrote that NBC was willing to talk with Tele-Communications if the cable giant is "really interested in localism." NBC Cable is partners with Cablevision Systems Corp. in a 24-hour regional news channel titled News 12 Long Island.

In an interview, Thomas Rogers, president of NBC Cable, said: "NBC has done a lot in regional news. If John Malone is serious about that, then we could talk. But we've been trying to get into discussions with TCI for years, and they've never been supportive of it."

Mr. Thomson of Tele-Communications said NBC always wanted to maintain editorial control in local news operations.

Mr. Rogers dismissed Mr. Thomson's allegations and said, "If this debate {on program exclusivity} hadn't been going on, Tele-Communications' letter would never have been sent."
