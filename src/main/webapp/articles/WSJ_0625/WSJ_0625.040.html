Grumman Sets Retirement Plan For 21% of Force --- Offer of Incentives Is Aimed At 6,000; Half Expected To Accept the Proposal

  • Author: Charles W. Stevens
  • Date: 06/25/90

Grumman Corp. is offering early retirement incentives to 6,000 employees, or 21% of its work force, in an effort to bring its employment level in line with dwindling government contracts. If half the eligible employees accept the offer, as expected, the defense contractor could save $100 million a year in expenses, say securities analysts who follow the company. But that wouldn't lead to any lifting of earnings estimates for Grumman because "they almost have to {cut employment} to stay whole," said Michael S. Rosen, an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., New York.

Grumman, based in Bethpage, N.Y., said that the retirement offer is the result of a companywide cost-cutting study and that acceptance "is completely voluntary." A spokeswoman said the company is "hoping" the response will obviate future layoffs. Dismissals may still be necessary, however, because Grumman's "employment target is still moving" as the cost-reduction study continues, the spokeswoman said.

The company, which primarily makes Navy warplanes and other aerospace systems, said the retirement inducements are being offered to "pension eligible" employees. The plan provides for a supplement to accrued pension benefits, the Grumman spokeswoman said.

Grumman's need to cut employment follows decisions in recent Defense Department budgets that slashed spending and set expiration dates for the company's biggest production programs. These include the carrier-based A-6E bomber and the EA-6B radar-jamming aircraft, which are scheduled to go out of production next year. Production funds for Grumman's F14D Navy fighter will run out in 1993.

Through layoffs and attrition, Grumman pared its employment about 10% last year to its current level of about 28,000 employees.

Mr. Rosen, the Wall Street analyst, said Grumman officials told him that they expect about half the 6,000 eligible employees to accept the early retirement offer, an estimate on which Grumman declined to comment. Nevertheless, Mr. Rosen said he is sticking to his estimate for Grumman to earn about $60 million, or $1.75 a share, this year, on $3.6 billion in revenue. In 1989 Grumman earned $67.3 million, or $1.91 a share, on revenue of $3.6 billion.

"When you do the math on these things, it never seems to get to the bottom line," Mr. Rosen said of the expected savings. "These are necessary savings that Grumman and other defense companies have to do to keep their heads above water," he said.
