Tax Revolt Forces In California Ready Fresh Initiatives --- Two Groups Claim to Have Enough Signatures to Put New Plan Before Voters

  • Author: Ken Wells
  • Date: 06/25/90

Reports of the death of the California tax revolt movement may have been premature. Two tax revolt groups in California say they've gathered enough signatures to put before voters in November a measure that would require electoral approval of general local tax increases, while boosting the voter majority needed to raise taxes under the state's ballot initiative process.

Under the initiative, dubbed by its sponsors the "Taxpayers Right to Vote Act," an increase in general local taxes, currently the prerogative of elected officials, would require a majority vote of local citizens. The measure would also raise to two-thirds the percentage of voters required to pass a statewide tax increase by ballot referendum. Such increases now require only ratification by a majority of voters.

Sponsors, including the Howard Jarvis Taxpayers Association and the Paul Gann's Citizens Committee, say their measure is partly spurred by recent adoption by voters of two statewide tax measures. One doubled the state's gas tax to pay for freeway construction while another authorized $1 billion in bond funding to upgrade the state's mass transit system.

Passage of the measures, which required only simple majorities, has been widely hailed as the symbolic end to a voter antipathy over taxes that began in 1978 by the landmark passage of Proposition 13 sponsored by the late Mr. Jarvis. But a spokesman for the Paul Gann's Citizens Committee said the "Taxpayers Right to Vote Act" would allow voters to determine for themselves whether the tax revolt is dead. Mr. Gann, who helped Mr. Jarvis write Proposition 13 and helped push through other limits on state spending, died last year.

The act has already garnered the opposition of California Attorney General John Van de Kamp, Lt. Governor Leo McCarthy and a number of state legislators. Opponents say such a law could paralyze state and local governments ability to raise revenues while interfering with the constitutional authority of elected officials to decide certain tax matters.
