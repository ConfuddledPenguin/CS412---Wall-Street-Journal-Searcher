Canada Brokers Share the Woes Of Wall Street

  • Author: Suzanne McGee
  • Date: 06/25/90

TORONTO -- Like their counterparts on Wall Street, investment dealers on Canada's Bay Street are in the throes of a major shakeout. Prudential-Bache Securities Inc.'s Canadian unit, citing continued losses, bowed out of Canada last week. Prudential-Bache sold its retail operations to Toronto-based Burns Fry Ltd., where about 400 Prudential-Bache employees will find jobs. But at least another 225 workers will find themselves scrambling to find new jobs. Merrill Lynch Canada Inc. sold its retail operations in January.

New York-based Prudential-Bache is a unit of Prudential Insurance Co. of America. Merrill Lynch Canada is a unit of New York-based Merrill Lynch & Co.

Only 28 of the Toronto Stock Exchange's 75 member firms managed to make a profit in the first quarter of 1990. April and May were even more difficult, many firms report.

"We're seeing a shrinking revenue base in a more competitive market that also suffers from overcapacity," said Lyle Stein, vice-president and portfolio strategist at Toronto-based investment dealer Nesbitt Thomson Deacon Inc.

While many of these problems are familiar enough on Wall Street, Canadian securities firms must grapple with additional dilemmas, ranging from the country's sky-high interest rates to a great degree of corporate concentration.

"In a rising interest rate environment, it's very compelling for most investors to be in the money market and fixed income market," said Robert Schultz, president and chief executive of Midland Walwyn Capital Inc., one of Canada's largest investment dealers. Investors buy these vehicles through brokerage firms, too, he acknowledges, "but these types of transactions are much less lucrative for us" than equity investments.

Short-term interest rates are around 13.6% in Canada, more than five percentage points above similar U.S. rates. Investment industry executives say that encourages investors to get out of stocks and into Treasury bills or similar instruments.

"The retail investor, our bread and butter, is alleged to be coming back to life in the U.S.," said Nesbitt Thomson's Mr. Stein. "But unfortunately he hasn't arrived up here yet."

Institutional clients are still in the game, but they're playing hardball with the brokers.

"In the last 12 months, there's been a contraction of what institutions are willing to pay as commissions," said John Ing, president and chief executive of Maisons Placements Canada Inc., a Montreal-based securities dealer. "It's no longer a question of negotiating the commission, it's a question of the institution deciding what commission they intend to pay."

Mr. Ing said those commissions have dropped from about eight cents a share three years ago to about three cents this year, figures confirmed by other major investment dealers.

But it's not only interest rates that keep Canadians away from stock markets, some investment dealers argue.

Mr. Stein said that Canada's economy continues to underperform those of other Western industrialized nations, including West Germany and the U.S. The investment environment, already seriously affected by rising interest rates and economic uncertainty, has been further clouded by the failure of Canada's Meech Lake constitutional accord, aimed at bringing Quebec back into Canadian Confederation. While debate over the accord and Quebec's future in Canada hasn't directly affected the stock market, it has made the outlook for foreign investment in Canada less certain.

Mr. Stein also cited the specific characteristics of the Canadian equity market as a reason for the lack of investor interest. "The equity market here has also gotten thinner," he added, "with many large companies having been taken over and taken out of the market in the last few years."

In 1989 alone, Mr. Stein said, major Canadian firms such as Connaught BioSciences Inc., Falconbridge Ltd. and Consolidated-Bathurst Inc. were taken over. The high rate of corporate concentration in Canada means many of the companies listed on the stock exchanges have only relatively few shares available for public trading.

A high proportion of Canada's major publicly traded firms are in natural resources, such as Abitibi-Price Inc., Inco Ltd. and Noranda Inc. These companies, hit by the high Canadian dollar and a downturn in the cyclical markets for their products, are reporting significantly lower levels of profitability that makes their stocks less attractive.

There are also too many brokers chasing too little business, further eroding margins, investment dealers say.

"Between 1982 and 1987, we had a very buoyant market, and everybody had the view that this was going to go on," said Midland Walwyn's Mr. Schultz. "But that is capacity that can't be supported now, and it has impacted profitability."

Added Reay Mackay, chief operating officer of Toronto-based RBC Dominion Securities Inc. in Toronto: "Obviously, the industry has an overhead problem as much as it does a revenue problem." RBC laid off 125 of its 200 London-based employees last month, and said in December it planned to cut its Canadian staff by 175 people this year.

"If markets stay like this, there will be a further shakeout" in the form of both mergers of firms and takeovers of some by others, Mr. Mackay said.

But though bulls are an endangered species on Bay Street, they aren't extinct.

Charles Winograd, president and chief executive of Richardson Greenshields of Canada Ltd., said that although the retail business in Canada is weaker than in the U.S., Canadian firms haven't had to deal with the problems caused by the frenzied merger and acquisition activity of the past few years, or with junk bonds.

"In 1988 and 1989, U.S. firms were cushioned by that business," Mr. Winograd said. "Our toughest times came immediately after the 1987 crash, and nothing's really changed: This is a cyclical industry."

John MacNaughton, president of Burns Fry, said his company plans to generate interest among its extremely cautious clients by seeking out "creative" new products.

"We just completed the first placement by a British firm of preferred shares in Canada by overcoming tax, accounting and regulatory hurdles creatively," Mr. MacNaughton said, referring to a 135 million Canadian dollar (US$114.8 million) preferred share issue by Cadbury Schweppes PLC. He said the company's retail brokers can overcome investor pessimism by being more aware of the wide variety of products and issues available.

"Ultimately, we'll see a resurgence in investor confidence," he said.
