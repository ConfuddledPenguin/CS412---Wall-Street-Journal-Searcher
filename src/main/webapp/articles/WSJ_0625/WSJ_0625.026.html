Nelson Mandela, quoted in Friday's early edition of the New York Times, on the Puerto Rican nationalists, seated in the VIP row of a Mandela rally in Harlem, who spent 25 years in prison for shooting five congressmen from the House gallery in 1954:

We support the cause of anyone who is fighting for self-determination, and our attitude is the same, no matter who it is. I would be honored to sit on the platform with the four comrades whom you refer to.

---

James E. McClure and T. Norman Van Cott, of the Ball State University economics department, in the summer issue of the Indiana Policy Review:

Advocates of educational choice in primary-secondary schooling . . . direct attention to fragmentary evidence offered by primary-secondary school districts that have implemented choice -- East Harlem in New York City being cited most frequently. While the latter evidence is important, it surely strikes many Americans as case-specific, anecdotal, and insufficient to warrant a general restructuring of primary-secondary education.

Bringing the evidence from U.S. higher education to bear on the educational choice issue overcomes these concerns. Indeed, educational choice is revealed to be alive and well in all 50 states. It is not the "pig in the poke" many suggest. . . .

With higher education, students satisfying admissions requirements can attend any publicly supported school in their state. Colleges and universities, therefore, must compete for student enrollment by offering attractive educational packages.

Each year, parents of high school seniors become aware of this competition. Deprived of captive students, colleges and universities employ a variety of marketing techniques to entice students to their campus. . . .

No doubt many professors, considering themselves above the marketplace, label these competitive activities "raw commercialism." Nevertheless, the competition keeps these "professionals" attuned to their customers. From society's point of view, of course, the salutary consequences of colleges' self-interested pursuit of students is a system of higher education without equal.
