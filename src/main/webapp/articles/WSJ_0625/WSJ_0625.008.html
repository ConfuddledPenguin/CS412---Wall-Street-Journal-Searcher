Who's News: Manager Revamp Continues at Bank Of New England

  • Author: Gary Putka
  • Date: 06/25/90

BOSTON -- Bank of New England Corp. moved Leo R. Breitman to a new vice chairman's post and hired D. Bruce Wheeler to succeed him as president of its main bank unit. The changes were the latest in a nearly continuous management reshuffling this year, since the bank began dealing with losses and a mountain of bad loans in the slumping Northeast economy. Mr. Wheeler's hiring as president of Bank of New England N.A. comes only five months after Mr. Breitman was moved into the post and bears the imprint of Lawrence K. Fish, the parent company's chairman and chief executive officer since March.

Mr. Wheeler, currently executive vice president of First Nationwide Bank, of California, said he plans to pursue opportunities in mortgage lending and in retail banking in an effort to "get on the offense a little bit" in solving the bank's problems. Mr. Fish reversed a plan to sell home-equity loans after taking the reins and also said he wants to exploit the bank's extensive retail operations in his recovery plan.

In his new post, Mr. Breitman will also become senior credit policy officer. Mr. Wheeler, who managed Massachusetts banking operations for Bank of Boston from 1982 until 1989, will be responsible for corporate and retail banking, commercial real estate and trust and private banking.

Mr. Wheeler said in an interview that recent thrift failures and cutbacks in mortgage lending in New England presented an opportunity for new lending to the bank. He said a large portion of any new loans generated would be sold into the secondary market so they wouldn't put pressure on regulatory capital ratios.

Bank of New England reported a $1.11 billion loss in last year's fourth quarter, and a $46 million loss in its first quarter. It lost about $7 billion in deposits during a run that followed disclosure of its problems, and is cutting about a third of its staff, while total assets are down to $25 billion from a peak of about $30 billion. Further asset sales are planned.

Mr. Wheeler said only 2% of the bank's customers have been lost, suggesting a possibility of rebuilding its commercial loan portfolio. "There's still a lot of creditworthy customers in New England," he said.
