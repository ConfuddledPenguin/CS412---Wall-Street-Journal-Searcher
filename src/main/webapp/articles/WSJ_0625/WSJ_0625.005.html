Law -- Legal Perspective: Justice Byron White's Enigmatic Record Puzzles Both Liberals and Conservatives

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/25/90

WASHINGTON -- For most of his 28 years on the Supreme Court, Justice Byron White has been an enigmatic figure, and the court's current term, expected to end this week, is no exception to that rule. He has long been considered part of the court's conservative majority, a characterization that remained apt this term. Liberal pundits have mused for decades that his appointment in 1962 was one of President Kennedy's biggest blunders.

Yet, he provided the court's liberal wing -- Justices William Brennan, Thurgood Marshall, Harry Blackmun and John Stevens -- with their fifth vote in each of the handful of 5-4 decisions they have won thus far this term, except for the ruling striking down the federal flag-burning law.

At first glance, some of his votes this term appear surprising. He wrote the 5-4 decision in April that upheld federal judges' broad authority to correct illegal school segregation; he ruled that a judge may order the Kansas City School District to raise taxes to pay for desegregation.

Although he is generally loath to expand constitutional protections for accused criminals, he joined Justice Brennan's 5-4 opinion in January barring the use of illegally obtained evidence to shake the credibility of a defendant's witnesses. And last month, he joined another Brennan 5-4 opinion barring the manslaughter prosecution of an individual who has already pleaded guilty to a misdemeanor of drunken driving for an accident that caused a death.

Last Thursday, he joined still another 54 Brennan ruling. This one said that the First Amendment prohibits the hiring, promotion and transfer of state and local government workers based on their political party affiliations.

Justice White's vote in that case came despite a dissenting admonition by Justice Antonin Scalia that most federal judges and Supreme Court justices owe their appointments to the patronage system. Justice Scalia might well have been addressing the 73-year-old Justice White, who was a friend and campaign supporter of President Kennedy and was serving as an aide to Attorney General Robert Kennedy when they picked him for the court.

These decisions by Justice White seem out of place in a voting record that is largely conservative; he has been a leading critic of the constitutional right to abortion, affirmative action that discriminates against whites, the court's effort to erect a wall between church and state, and the court's rulings affording the news media protection against libel lawsuits.

But court experts who have followed his tenure closely don't find his half-dozen votes with the liberals this term surprising. "A review of his opinions reveals a significant consistency in perspective, method and conviction," says Kate Stith, a Yale Law School professor and former White law clerk.

His consistency becomes apparent when one considers the court's gradual swing to the right, according to Ms. Stith. In an article she recently completed, she says Justice White was in the majority in fewer than half of the 5-4 decisions in the 1960s, a period in which the court vastly expanded constitutional rights. In the 1970s, as the expansion of rights slowed in some areas and halted in others, he was in the majority in about two-thirds of the 5-4 cases.

In the 1980s, as the court took an ever narrower view of its role and of the Constitution, Justice White was on the winning side of three-fourths of 5-4 rulings. And his percentage is still increasing.

But his occasional liberal votes also are consistent, she says, with principles and precedents of the court that he has accepted. She says the Kansas City schools case demonstrates how Justice White has long taken a broad view of the authority of federal courts to eliminate past discrimination in the public schools.

That consistency may be put to one of its toughest tests in the court's next term, when the justices grapple with the issue of when a city may be released from a court-ordered obligation to desegregate its schools.
