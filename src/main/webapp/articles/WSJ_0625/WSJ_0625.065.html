Economy: Goods Orders Posted Surge Of 3.9% for May --- Data Said to Show Stability In Manufacturing Sector; Personal Income Gained

  • Author: Tuesday Georges
  • Date: 06/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The jump in orders for big-ticket goods last month suggests to some analysts that the manufacturing sector may be beginning to stabilize. Orders received by makers of durable goods -- factory equipment, planes, cars, appliances and other items intended to last at least three years -- leaped 3.9% in May, to $128.19 billion, after dropping 4.2% in April and soaring 6.7% in March, the Commerce Department said.

But a separate report suggested that other parts of the economy may be softening. That data showed personal income edged up only 0.3% in May, while personal spending was flat.

"The durable goods report reinforces the suspicion that the manufacturing sector is no longer declining, but we are by no means seeing an upturn," said Laurence H. Meyer, senior economist for Laurence H. Meyer & Associates in St. Louis. "I wouldn't anticipate a rebound."

Mr. Meyer said he was disappointed by the weak consumer income figure. "Usually the consumer sector is a stabilizing sector" he said. "This sector usually tends to grow even as the economy weakens, but now that it is slowing down so much . . . we are losing that protection that we normally have. That makes the economy more vulnerable to a recession."

Many economists say they are skeptical about the upbeat durable goods figure and suggested that the report could be misleading.

"It was a stronger number than we were looking for . . . and on the surface the report looks positive, but when you look under the surface it is not as positive as it seems," said Daniel Friel, an economist for NCNB Corp. in Charlotte, N.C.

According to some analysts, the durable goods industry is still demonstrating softness. Last month's climb in orders was led by transportation equipment, which tends to fluctuate widely from month to month. These bookings rose a strong 9.9% in May, but didn't make up for the 13.5% decline in April. The April decline followed a 24.1% increase in March. The bulk of last month's increase came in bookings for airplanes, although orders for automobiles increased strongly. Overall, however, the automobile industry remains weak, said economists.

Excluding transportation, manufacturers' orders increased only 1.8% in May and dropped 0.5% in April. Orders for primary metals rose 2.2%, while orders for nonelectrical machinery, which includes computers, surged 3.2%. Orders in the electrical-machinery category fell 0.2%.

Orders for nondefense capital goods -- which include the increase in aircraft orders -- declined 1.4% in May, after a 10.5% plunge in the previous month. Overall, capital-goods orders edged up 2.4%, following an 8.2% drop in April.

Unfilled orders for durable goods rose 0.4%, to $500.31 billion, compared with a 0.3% increase in April. Manufacturers' shipments in May climbed 3.5%, to $126.12 billion, after a fall of 2.6% a month earlier.

Last month's rise in personal income brought it to a $4.701 trillion annual rate. Personal income increased 0.3% in April, compared with a 0.8% rise in March.

Americans saved 6.2% of their after-tax income last month, compared with 6.0% in April and 6.3% in March.

After adjusting for inflation, personal spending fell 0.3%, following a 0.4% rise in April and a 0.3% drop in March.

All the figures are adjusted for seasonal changes.
