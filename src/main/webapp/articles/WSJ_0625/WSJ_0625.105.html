International -- Washington Insight: Yeltsin, Boosted by Political Success In Russia, Gains Respect in Washington

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 06/25/90

WASHINGTON -- In the wake of his remarkable political comeback at home, maverick Soviet politician Boris Yeltsin is making another sort of comeback as well -- in the inner councils of the Bush administration. After consistently dismissing Mr. Yeltsin publicly and privately as an eccentric or even dangerous demagogue, administration officials now say they are gaining a new respect for the beefy radical. They cite his political skill in winning election as president of the Russian republic when mounting political woes are afflicting the man who is still President Bush's favorite Soviet politician, Mikhail Gorbachev.

For fear of offending the Soviet president, top officials still won't publicly say a kind word about Mr. Yeltsin, who has been Mr. Gorbachev's chief left-wing critic. But in private they're much more positive about him now.

"There's been a certain evolution in people's views here lately," says one important U.S. foreign policy official. "He has shown a political adeptness that has made people here sit up and think." A second senior official says: "He has to be taken seriously. He represents real democratic forces in the Soviet Union."

That isn't to say the U.S. is rooting for Mr. Yeltsin or shying away from Mr. Gorbachev. All recent evidence points to a continuing close tie between President Bush and the current Soviet leader. The Bush administration doesn't even have a post-Gorbachev plan. And the newest generation of Soviet reformers -- such as Moscow Mayor Gavril Popov and Leningrad leader Anatoly Sobchak -- rarely makes it onto the administration's foreign policy radar screen, even though many Soviet experts in the West view them as more genuinely inclined toward democracy than either Mr. Gorbachev or Mr. Yeltsin.

The new regard for Mr. Yeltsin is largely a result of respect for the raw political skill and appeal he has demonstrated in climbing from political oblivion to head Russia, which occupies two-thirds of Soviet territory.

There is still grave doubt about his dedication to Western-style government and economics and considerable nervousness about his tendency to pop off in public.

U.S. officials weren't thrilled recently when Mr. Yeltsin said he wanted Russia to have a foreign policy "independent" of the Soviet Union's. And they are deeply skeptical when he promises an unspecified market reform plan that he pledges won't burden average citizens. But officials here are now unwilling to write him off on the basis of some of these off-the-cuff remarks. "We don't know what he'll produce," says a high official. "But we're taking a wait-and-see attitude."

It was Mr. Yeltsin's blustery rhetoric that turned the administration against him in the first place. Last September, while visiting the White House and State Department, he came across as disorganized and impractical in talks with Mr. Bush, Secretary of State James Baker and National Security Adviser Brent Scowcroft. Among other things, he touted wild schemes -- proposing, for instance, that U.S. companies build a million apartments in the U.S.S.R.

The view of Mr. Yeltsin as simply an erratic populist was reinforced by tales of heavy drinking during his U.S. tour and by private criticism from Mr. Gorbachev right up through the recent Washington summit, where the Soviet leader told some U.S. officials Mr. Yeltsin was only a "destroyer" and had no constructive reform proposals.

Even some leading Soviet liberals reinforced that view. "I cannot imagine how Yeltsin could go to Washington and have negotiations with Bush and Baker," says Andrei Kortunov, a reformist Moscow foreign policy expert whose views are followed closely in Washington. "It would be ridiculous, because he doesn't know anything about foreign policy."

But one senior administration official now concedes that the initial negative U.S. assessment was an overreaction and that "Yeltsin isn't some drunken buffoon."

One reason for the cautious turnaround may be pressure from U.S. conservatives, who have pressed the White House to hedge its bets on Mr. Gorbachev. They argue that his political survival isn't as crucial as it once seemed because he might be succeeded by an even more liberal figure, notably Mr. Yeltsin or Mr. Popov or Mr. Sobchak.

"We should not be comfortable about him {Mr. Yeltsin}, but we should give him the benefit of the doubt," says Leon Aron, a Soviet expert at the conservative Heritage Foundation who is writing a book about Mr. Yeltsin. "For years, we've been pining for a democratically elected leader there, and in Yeltsin we've come closer than ever to having that kind of a Russian politician. So we should have a cautious attitude, but we can't dismiss him."
