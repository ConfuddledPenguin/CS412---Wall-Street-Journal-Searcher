Abreast of the Market: Is It Time to Lie Low on High-Growth Stocks?

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 06/25/90

NEW YORK -- Is growth overvalued? Growth stocks have been the darlings of 1990. They're at the head of the market's climb to records this year, and some of the growth stocks have soared two or three times as much as broad market indexes.

But second-quarter earnings reports loom on the horizon, and many investors wonder if the markups on growth have pushed prices too high. Stocks in the good-earnings-news club weren't spared from Friday's 44.55-point, or 1.55%, knockdown in the Dow Jones industrials. Wal-Mart Stores tumbled 1 to 61 1/4, a 1.6% decline.

The ratio of stock prices to per-share earnings for many of these issues is more than double the 16.4 multiple on the 500 stocks in the Standard & Poor's index.

Home Depot, a high-growth company, is trading at a P-E of 38 times earnings, or 2.3 times the multiple of the S&P 500. Wal-Mart Stores is trading at 30 times earnings, or 1.8 times the S&P multiple. Bargain hunters are shocked by growth-stock investors' ambitious earnings expectations.

"If we were coming out of a recession, it would be a time to buy long-term growth prospects," says William Leugers, president of Gradison Growth Trust. But, as he points out, the economy looks closer to the end of an economic cycle than the beginning. "We don't want to pay for growth," he says, and an average P-E of 9 on Gradison's Established Growth Fund shows he means it.

Gradison is making a bet on some cyclical stocks, such as International Paper and Federal Paper Board, that its stockpickers think can survive an economic downturn. Both of those stocks trade at less than half the market P-E, yet both companies have seen earnings rise more than 20% annually over the past five years.

Growth-stock investors, who couldn't be happier this year, won't argue that growth is commanding a high price. But, "Are we overvaluing growth?" asks Eldon Mayer, chairman of Lynch & Mayer, which manages $2 billion. "My answer is a clear and resounding, `No.' "

Indeed, he argues that growth stocks are near record cheap valuations. If that's true, the stock market could rise further still with growth issues leading the way. Mr. Mayer figures about 35% of the S&P 500 consists of growth stocks. "If 35% of the market is going to be working its way higher, it rules out a serious bear market," he says.

Mr. Mayer's idea of growth stocks is shares of companies with average annual earnings growth in excess of 15% over the past five years, and no down years in that period; and expected earnings growth for the next five years must exceed 15% annually. Recent returns on equity must be at least 15% a year; and, finally, debt must be less than 50% of total capitalization.

Yes, the earnings outlook for many companies is dim, Mr. Mayer agrees. But something happened in the 1980s that will allow companies with strong earnings ability to survive and flourish in an economic slowdown.

"You've had an absolute mania in mergers and acquisitions culminating in transactions that haven't even stood the test of the short-term, never mind the long-term," says Mr. Mayer. Conversely, many growth issues have clean balance sheets and are grabbing market share from their highly indebted competitors.

It was merger mania that caused many investors to dump growth issues so they could bet on potential takeover targets, Mr. Mayer says. "Every year," in the mid-1980s, "they sold more growth stocks and bought more junk," he says. That's one reason why the multiple of the top 50 growth issues was only 1.3 times the S&P index in November 1988, a historic low.

Mr. Mayer says growth stocks are worth their high multiples. "If the rest of the world grows earnings at 5%, and you can assemble a group of companies growing at 20%, it's not outlandish that you should pay three or four times the market multiple," he says.

That's not to say he isn't worried. Growth stocks have caught on with large investors now. As big banks and insurance companies jump on board the bandwagon, growth-stock prices could balloon to multiples more than three times greater than the market; the most recent time that happened was in the "Nifty 50" rally of the early 1970s. But here's the danger: If growth issues fall short of current expectations, big investors might start dumping shares as fast as they're buying them today.

Growth stocks are typically the most sensitive to earnings surprises -- either positive or negative -- and to estimate revisions, says Benjamin Zacks, executive vice president of Zacks Investment Research.

"Even the slightest estimate reductions in Home Depot would have a very significant price effect; and if the company should report a negative quarterly earnings surprise, investors would probably take the stock down 20%," says Mr. Zacks. Home Depot is expected to earn $1.89 a share in fiscal 1991, up 33% from $1.42 a share in fiscal 1990.

Warren Shaw, managing director at Chancellor Capital Management, owns many growth stocks, including Home Depot. He thinks the stock may be getting a bit rich. But he is happier betting that growth stocks will fulfill earnings expectations than he would be putting money into stocks of companies tied to economic growth; besides obviously being vulnerable to an economic slowdown, he thinks many of these concerns have too much debt.

"The issue you have to raise is, what's the risk of being in growth stocks or not being in them?" says George Boyd, director of quantitative research at Kidder Peabody. "Is not the {greater} risk in the low-growth stocks, that value investors have been buying for a long time?"

Interest rate changes and tax cuts can affect earnings valuations dramatically, as they did in the early 1980s. Taking into account current rates, and past valuations, growth stocks "are fairly valued -- not rich or cheap," says Mr. Boyd.

But Graham Tanaka, president of Tanaka Capital Management, says many growth-stock bargains can still be found.

"We're beginning to see a two-tiered growth-stock market," he says. One tier contains the high P-E stocks that big institutional investors have discovered. And the other contains cheaper, sometimes smaller stocks that have been overlooked by the crowd.

"You can buy the same kind of growth in Novellus {Systems}" -- an over-the-counter semiconductor stock selling at 1.16 times the market P-E -- as in some of the high-profile stocks, he says. "The real opportunity is to find them before brokerage houses and institutions put them on their recommended list," he says. "And there are a ton of them {still} out there. We feel like kids in a candy store."

Friday's Market Activity

The stock market ended the week the way it began -- with a steep sell-off. The Dow Jones Industrial Average lost a net 78.71 points this past week, down from the record of 2935.89 on June 15 and the biggest weekly decline since January.

Friday, the average traded in a narrow range most of the day, mainly higher, then plunged more than 40 points in the last half-hour as futures-related program selling kicked in on heavy volume.

The Dow Jones industrials fell 44.55 to 2857.18, the lowest level since the Memorial Day weekend. The late selling surge was attributed to index arbitrage -- offsetting trades in the stock and index-futures markets to profit from differences in price between the two.

Traders said little stood in the way of the program selling, because a number of institutions had already closed the books on the second quarter and other activity was light. Friday was the final day for many portfolio managers to make trades that can be included in second-quarter statements.

New York Stock Exchange volume was 172.6 million shares, the busiest day of the week. Declining issues outnumbered advancers, 893 to 607.

Trading was especially heavy in American Telephone & Telegraph, American Express and Philip Morris, accounting for 9% of total Big Board volume.

Philip Morris was one of only three stocks among the 30 Dow Jones industrials to close higher, up 3/8 to 44 3/4. The company confirmed it agreed to an acquisition that fits with a plan to expand international food business.

Dividend-related trades swelled volume in AT&T, the Big Board's most active issue, which dropped 1 1/8 to 42 1/2. American Express, second most active, rose 1/4 to 30 1/2 following an offering of 17.5 million shares.

International Business Machines fell 2 3/4 to 116 1/4; Boeing slipped 1 5/8 to 57 3/8; General Motors tumbled 1 1/2 to 48; Merck slid 1 7/8 to 81 3/4; and United Technologies dropped 2 1/8 to 58.

First Brands surged 4 3/4 to 28 3/8 after the company disclosed that shareholders with about 70% of the common shares plan to solicit takeover bids.

Beverly Enterprises advanced 5/8 to 5 7/8 after County NatWest USA reiterated a buy recommendation on the stock. Harley Davidson dropped 3/4 to 25 7/8, with 6.9% of the company's common stock outstanding sold in a single block.

Eli Lilly rose 1/2 to a 52-week high of 79 1/2, on a positive analyst report. Compaq Computer fell 3 3/4 to 120 1/4 on a report that sales of one of its products have been slower than expected. Digital Equipment lost 2 1/2 to 83; Hewlett-Packard dropped 7/8 to 46 7/8, and Motorola fell 2 1/8 to 86.

Four defense contractors moved lower on news that the companies face possible debt-ratings downgrades by Moody's Investors Service. Raytheon dropped 2 to 62 1/2; Litton Industries plunged 3 5/8 to 71 1/2; McDonnell Douglas fell 1 1/4 to 34 1/2; and Lockheed fell 3/8 to 34 7/8.

MGM/UA Communications rose 5/8 to 17 1/8. Pathe Communications sweetened its bid for the company.

Rayonier Timberlands fell 1 to 20 and IP Timberlands went down 5/8 to 21 3/8 on news of a federal endangered-species decision that is expected to curtail logging in the Pacific Northwest.

Quaker Oats eased 1/2 to 48 1/4 on the company's announcement that earnings for the fiscal year would be flat to slightly lower than a year ago. Curtice-Burns Foods slipped 5/8 to 17 3/4, also on news of expected lower earnings.
