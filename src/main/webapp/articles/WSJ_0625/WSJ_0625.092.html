Law -- Legal Beat: Lisa Jones, Convicted Ex-Assistant At Drexel, Gets 10 Months in Jail

  • Author: Ann Hagedorn and Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 06/25/90

Lisa Jones, a former trader's assistant at Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. who was convicted of perjury last year, was resentenced to 10 months in prison. On Friday, U.S. District Judge Leonard B. Sand, prompted by a recent decision from the Second U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals, reduced Ms. Jones's prison term from 18 to 10 months. The judge also cut Ms. Jones's financial penalty from $50,000 to about $40,000.

The 27-year old Ms. Jones, a former runaway who made $100,000 a year at the now collapsed Drexel, sobbed as Judge Sand announced his decision. Her attorney, Daniel Bookin, had urged the judge to reduce her punishment to probation. He expressed his concern that her fragile mental condition would be further damaged by a stay in prison.

Mr. Bookin read from a letter his client had sent Judge Sand in which she described "never-ending sleepness nights and days filled with anxiety." He also stressed that Ms. Jones's sentence was unduly harsh in comparison with those of more powerful players convicted during the government's crackdown on insider trading on Wall Street.

Ms. Jones was convicted of five counts of perjury and two counts of obstruction of justice for lying to a grand jury looking into allegedly illegal trading involving Drexel. Her trial drew considerable attention because she had worked for former Drexel trader Bruce Lee Newberg, who was later convicted along with five partners of now-defunct Princeton/Newport L.P. for engaging in a scheme with Drexel aimed at generating bogus tax losses. Her attorney depicted her as a hapless pawn in the government's sweeping investigation.

Mr. Bookin, of the San Francisco law firm Farella Braun & Martel, appealed the conviction and the original 18-month sentence on the basis of alleged conflicts of interest by her trial lawyer who also represented other Drexel employees. The court upheld the conviction. But it ruled that Judge Sand had improperly increased Ms. Jones's sentence beyond the level deemed appropriate under the federal sentencing guidelines.

The government sought a steep penalty based on the argument that Ms. Jones's perjury had cost the government time and money in its effort to prove its case against Princeton/Newport. The appeals panel, seeing no evidence of such cost, asked the judge to resentence Ms. Jones.

At Friday's hearing, prosecutor Neil Cartusciello stressed that Ms. Jones "could have added very relevant evidence" to the Princeton/Newport investigation. After her conviction, according to individuals familiar with the case, the government urged Ms. Jones to cooperate in their probes, but she declined. Mr. Cartusciello told the judge that Ms. Jones "still has information concealed."

FORMER HOLLY FARMS CEO sues Wachtell Lipton.

The former president and chief executive officer of Holly Farms Corp. sued Wachtell, Lipton, Rosen & Katz, alleging that an error by the New York law firm cost him and other employees of the poultry and feed-products concern millions of dollars.

In a suit filed earlier this month in Memphis, Tenn., Chancery Court, R. Lee Taylor II alleged that Wachtell failed to "exercise due skill, care and diligence in connection with rendering advice" to Holly and its former board. Mr. Taylor has charged that Wachtell made an error in an amendment it drafted for Holly's stock bonus plan in January 1989.

The former Holly executive is suing Wachtell on behalf of himself and some 600 participants in Holly's stock bonus plan. Mr. Taylor had previously sued Holly in connection with the alleged error. The litigation is pending.

Mr. Taylor is seeking a jury trial and unspecified damages. While the suit doesn't state how much money the drafting error cost employees, attorneys familiar with the matter had previously estimated the cost at more than $35 million.

In recent years, state courts have increasingly ruled that if lawyers act negligently, they may be liable to third parties.

Wachtell declined to comment on the suit. But in a court filing opposing Mr. Taylor's action against Wachtell, lawyers representing the New York firm say that Mr. Taylor, in his capacity as a director and chief executive of Holly, "had direct responsibility to supervise and control the review and approval of the stock bonus plan." They say that Wachtell could also sue Mr. Taylor for contributory negligence in connection with the plan.

Mr. Taylor was fired from his job last August, less than two months after Tyson Foods Inc., another poultry producer, acquired Holly for about $1.29 billion.

Tyson's purchase ended eight months of bidding and litigation with Holly.

INS LIST bars 340,000 aliens from entering U.S. for political reasons.

According to the Lawyers Committee for Human Rights, which obtained an edited version of the "Lookout" list from the Immigration and Naturalization Service under a court order, the number of people listed is far greater than many people have estimated. The list dates back to 1904, but two-thirds of the entries were made since 1980.

"This is the first time we've gotten a sense of the magnitude," said Arthur Helton an attorney with the human-rights group. The 370-page document lists the dates that individuals were added to the list and is broken down by country. A federal judge previously ruled that for privacy reasons, the individuals' names couldn't be disclosed. The committee plans to ask U.S. Judge Leonard Sand in federal court in Manhattan to order the INS to divulge further details, such as the individuals' professions.

The list is mainly used to identify non-Americans who fall under three sweeping political categories under the 1952 McCarran-Walter Act. Under that law, people can be prohibited from entering the U.S. if they are considered a threat to the "public interest," a threat to "national security," or affiliated with any Communist or totalitarian party. Nearly all the individuals listed were listed under the third category.

In the first three months of this year, 4,399 people were added to the list, including 401 from China and 3,486 from Eastern Europe.

The list includes people from 146 different countries and other jurisdictions, such as Palestine and Hong Kong. China accounts for the largest number of entries on the list, 106,777; second is the Soviet Union, 33,064; third is Yugoslavia, 31,734; fourth is Hungary, 28,450; and fifth is Poland, 24,608. Mexico ranked 10th, with 5,402, and Japan ranked 11th with 4,393.

It is unclear whether some people were listed more than once over the years, so the exact number of individuals can't be determined. Often individuals don't learn that they are on the list until they apply for a visa or until they arrive in the U.S. A spokesman for the INS couldn't be reached for comment.

Mr. Helton said he thinks the committee's findings will spur legislators to change the law. "I think they will be stunned by the magnitude of the lookout list and its recent escalation," he said.

AMERICAN EXPRESS TESTS prepaid legal services for cardholders.

The prepaid legal services of Signature Group of Schaumburg, Ill., are being offered to about one percent of American Express Co.'s 25.6 million U.S. cardholders.

The solicitation is the financial-services company's first test of whether cardholders are interested in paying monthly fees to be eligible for fixed-price legal services. Signature Group is a unit of Montgomery Ward & Co., which is a subsidiary of Mobil Corp.

For $6.75 a month, cardholders will be able to retain an attorney to handle an uncontested divorce for $210, a will and a minor's trust for $170 or a noncommercial real estate closing for $185, as well as other services.

Maureen Bailey, a spokeswoman for American Express, says that if a second solicitation proves successful, the company may expand the service to all cardholders by the end of the year.

KAYE SCHOLER WILL MERGE with a bankruptcy law firm.

Kaye, Scholer, Fierman, Hays & Handler, a 355-lawyer New York law firm, approved a proposal to merge with the 14-lawyer Levin & Weintraub, Crames & Edelman, also in New York. All five Levin & Weintraub partners will become partners at Kaye Scholer. The merger will become effective July 1.

Levin & Weintraub, one of the nation's best-known bankruptcy law firms, primarily represents debtors, including Revco D.S. Inc. and Manville Corp.

Kaye Scholer's bankruptcy lawyers represent mostly creditors, including Equitable Life Assurance Society of the U.S.

HBO IS ACCUSED of violating song copyrights.

In a trial that began this week in Manhattan, Broadcast Music Inc., a not-for-profit group that represents thousands of composers, claims that Home Box Office is transmitting movies and programs that use BMI-licensed songs without permission.

BMI has asked U.S. District Judge John Sprizzo to issue an injunction barring HBO, a Time Warner Inc. subsidiary, from broadcasting the programming unless it pays licensing fees for the songs. BMI also asked the judge to rule that it can require separate licensing agreements from national cable programmers, such as HBO, and from the programmers' local cable-system customers.

HBO maintains that it has an oral agreement with BMI allowing it to transmit movies using the songs. The company has also charged that BMI is a monopoly and that the group has taken steps to make it more difficult for the cable industry to license its music.
