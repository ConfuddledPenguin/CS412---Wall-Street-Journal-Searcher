Who's News: Circle K Names Brown, a Lawyer, To Chairman's Post

  • Author: Frederick Rose
  • Date: 06/25/90

Circle K Corp., which is operating under Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection from creditors, said Bart A. Brown Jr. was elected chairman. The post had been vacant since the May 7 resignation of Karl Eller, who, as chairman and chief executive officer, led the Phoenix, Ariz.-based convenience-store chain through a period of frenzied and ultimately unsustainable growth.

The 58-year-old Mr. Brown, a tax and business law specialist, will take a leave of absence from the Cincinnati law firm Keating, Muething & Klekamp, where he is a senior partner, a Circle K spokesman said.

Keating Muething -- its past partners include Charles Keating, the prominent onetime thrift executive -- is one of the principal law firms for the Cincinnati financier Carl Lindner's American Financial Corp. Mr. Lindner, through American Financial and other interests, is Circle K's largest shareholder, with a 38% stake, and a substantial creditor.

William A. Franke, a Circle K director, said Mr. Brown was chosen to be chairman because, "It was felt that he had the necessary skills to manage a relatively large and complicated rehabilitation."

Circle K operates the nation's second-largest chain of convenience stores. It filed last month for protection from creditors' lawsuits under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code, listing assets of $1.94 billion and liabilities of $1.59 billion as of Jan. 31. Under Chapter 11, a company continues to operate, while trying to work out arrangements with creditors to pay its debts.

Mr. Franke, a Phoenix investor, is the chairman of a newly created, three-member "committee of independent directors" of Circle K who aren't directly tied to Mr. Lindner. Circle K's four other directors include Mr. Lindner and his son, Keith E. Lindner. Mr. Franke said Mr. Brown has performed only "a very limited amount of work for the Lindner interests" as a partner at Keating Muething. Mr. Brown couldn't be reached to comment.

Mr. Franke said Mr. Brown will work on negotiations with creditors, while Robert A. Dearth, who was named president last January, will look after company operations. Mr. Franke said the chief executive officer's post has been left vacant since Mr. Eller's departure, because both Mr. Dearth and Mr. Brown are new to the company.
