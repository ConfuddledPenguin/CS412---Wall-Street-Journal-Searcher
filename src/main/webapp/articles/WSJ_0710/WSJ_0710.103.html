Law -- Legal Beat: More Americans Are Called for Jury Duty

  • Author: Stephen J. Adler and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 07/10/90

Jury duty is becoming a fact of life for a rapidly growing percentage of Americans, according to a new national survey. Forty-five percent of adults in the U.S. have been called at least once for jury duty, up from 35% in 1984, according to the study by the consulting firm of Research & Forecasts, Inc.

Jury scholars link the increase to innovations in court administration, including better methods of identifying and finding potential jurors and greater efforts to limit the length of most jurors' service.

The survey's sponsor, a group of corporate defense lawyers called the Defense Research Institute, also suggests that the expanded need for jurors may be a byproduct of increased litigiousness in the society. Archie Robinson, president of DRI, says more cases are being filed and prepared for trial than in the past. In addition, he says, a higher percentage are high-stakes cases that often take a long time to try. In such cases, he says, it's customary to call a larger pool of potential jurors, since many will be unable to serve in a long case and will have to be disqualified.

But jury researchers dispute Mr. Robinson's conclusion. Marc Galanter, a litigation expert at the University of Wisconsin law school, says available data in several large states indicates that the number of civil jury trials did not increase between 1984 and 1988. In federal court, there were 5,555 civil jury trials in 1984 and 5,676 in 1989, he said.

Any increased need for jurors, Mr. Galanter says, is probably the result of "a lot more of an intent to use jurors efficiently." He adds, "In a lot of places they instituted a one trial, one day system, so they call more people and don't keep them sitting as long."

Under such a system, a person is called for a single day, and if he is not assigned to a trial that day he is not required to return. If the person is selected for a longer trial, he must serve for the duration of that trial. The one day, one trial system is now used in jurisdictions that embrace about 25% of the U.S. population, according to an estimate by Thomas Munsterman, director of the Center for Jury Studies of the National Center for State Courts.

Mr. Munsterman says that another reason more people are being called for jury duty is that many courts are expanding their methods of finding potential jurors. While most jurisdictions used to rely primarily on voter-registration lists, in recent years many have added back-up lists, including driving records. Drivers' lists generally include 90%-95% of adults, while only about 70% of adults are registered to vote in most areas, Mr. Munsterman says.

In other findings, the Defense Research Institute survey determined that people with incomes over $40,000 a year are much more likely to be called for jury duty than those with incomes income of less than $40,000. In addition, men are slightly more likely to be called than women.

The survey also found that most people who are called on to serve only sit and wait; with most potential jurors disqualified for one reason or another before trial, only 17% of adult Americans get to serve on a jury in an actual trial.

WOMEN HARD HATS win an unusual settlement with construction company.

Tishman Construction Corp. was sued for alleged sex discrimination in 1988 by several female construction workers and by a group that trains women for blue-collar jobs. To settle the suit, filed in federal court in Manhattan, Tishman recently agreed to begin hiring women for one out of every three available laborer jobs.

Tishman, a construction-site manager, said that under the settlement its goal will be for women to make up 10% of its laborer work force. Currently, about 5% of Tishman's 197 laborers are women. According to Nontraditional Employment for Women, or NEW, the group that helped bring the lawsuit, women hold only 3% of all laborer jobs on construction site in New York City.

NEW said it has a waiting list of 80 women looking for construction jobs. A spokeswoman said the group hopes the 10% goal will be reached within six months, although the settlement doesn't provide a deadline for reaching that level. The settlement is in effect for three years.

Henry Estrada, a Tishman vice president, said in a statement: "While Tishman doesn't believe there has been discrimination on its part in hiring women for laborer jobs, we are pleased to join with NEW in additional efforts that will result in more women entering the construction industry labor force."

NEW said it isn't seeking special treatment for women but wants to ensure that female job applicants are evaluated fairly.

FORMER WEST VIRGINIA governor fails in motion to withdraw guilty plea.

U.S. District Judge Walter E. Hoffman denied former Gov. Arch A. Moore Jr.'s request to withdraw his guilty plea on five felonies related to a scheme to extort money and to buy votes. Sentencing is set for today.

In April, a Charleston grand jury accused Mr. Moore, a three-time Republican governor of the predominantly Democratic state of illegal activities while campaigning and serving as governor.

Attorneys for the former governor maintained that his guilty plea was "the result of a hasty decision forced by a deadline imposed by the government."

McMARTIN child-abuse case goes to jury in retrial.

After hearing two months of testimony, jurors in Los Angeles Superior Court yesterday began their task of deciding whether Raymond Buckey is guilty of eight counts of child molestation. The charges involve three girls who were once pupils at the now-defunct Virginia McMartin Preschool in Manhattan Beach, Calif., where Mr. Buckey was a teacher.

In January, after almost three months of deliberations and 28 months of testimony, another jury acquitted Mr. Buckey and his mother Peggy McMartin Buckey on 52 of 65 counts of child molestation. The jury was deadlocked on the remaining 13 counts. The prosecution later decided to retry Mr. Buckey on eight of the unresolved counts.

Throughout both trials, Mr. Buckey, who was indicted in March 1984 and has already served over four years in prison, has proclaimed his innocence.

MULHEREN DELIBERATIONS CONTINUE: A federal court jury began its second week of deliberations in the trial of John A. Mulheren Jr. The jury is considering whether Mr. Mulheren, the former chief of Jamie Securities Co., is guilty of conspiracy, securities and mail fraud and keeping false records. The government has alleged that Mr. Mulheren conducted numerous illegal securities trades with Ivan F. Boesky, the convicted former stock speculator. Leonard DeStefano, a former assistant to Mr. Mulheren, was also charged in the 30-count indictment. Jurors have asked numerous times over the last week to have testimony from the seven-week trial read to them and for definitions of various trading terms. Deliberations are set to resume today.

PIPER & MARBURY, in Baltimore, doubled the size of its business litigation and creditors rights department with the addition of partners Richard M. Kremen and David S. Musgrave and three associates from Semmes, Bowen & Semmes, also based in Baltimore. The move brings to five the number of partners at Piper & Marbury who specialize in creditors rights, one the fastest growing specialties in the mid-Atlantic region, said Piper chairman Decatur H. Miller.
