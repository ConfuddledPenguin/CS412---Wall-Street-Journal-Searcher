Abreast of the Market: Industrial Stocks Rise 9.16 Points On Late Rally; Trading Is Quiet

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 07/10/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices moved higher as a late burst of futures-related buying set the course for an otherwise directionless market. Trading activity, which dropped to the lowest levels of the year last week amid the observance of Independence Day, remained light.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average, boosted by strong performances from General Motors and International Business Machines, gained 9.16 points to 2914.11 -- its seventh advance in the past eight sessions.

Broader market measures also moved higher. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index rose 1.10 to 359.52 and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index added 0.45 to 196.23.

Advancing issues outnumbered decliners by 764 to 673 on the New York Stock Exchange, where 119,390,000 shares were traded. By comparison, the Big Board's average daily volume last week was 125.8 million shares, including just 111.7 million on Friday; both totals were 1990 lows.

Second-quarter earnings reports will begin flooding the market late this week, and traders said nervousness about the possibility that the data may produce some unpleasant surprises kept investors on the sidelines.

"Everyone's in a holding pattern here; it's pretty self-evident," said Christopher Pedersen, head of trading at Twenty-First Securities. "A lot of people are leery about those numbers coming out."

Last month, analysts' consensus earnings projections for 1990 were revised downward on 44.9% of the more than 3,000 companies that are followed by Nelson's Earnings Outlook, a Port Chester, N.Y.based service.

Earnings forecasts for this year were revised upward on only 27.6% of the companies, Nelson's reported, while the other 27.5% were left unchanged.

Amid the earnings jitters, program buying linked to stock-index arbitrage-buying baskets of stocks and selling index-futures contracts to profit from price differences between the two -- surfaced in early trading to help the market rally to session highs. The industrial average peaked with a gain of more than 15 points.

But the rally faded once the buy programs subsided, and the market stood little changed from midday until two more rounds of arbitrage buying lifted share prices in the last hour of trading.

The programs hit the market amid the release of results from the Treasury's weekly auction of three-month and six-month bills, at which a record $18.04 billion of securities were sold. Treasury notes and bonds bounced off their lows following release of the auction results.

GM, whose Hughes Aircraft and Delco Electronics units unveiled a new electronics joint venture, rallied 1 1/8 to 49. IBM rose 1 to 119, reflecting strength among major technology issues; Compaq Computer rose 3 1/4 to 64 3/4, Digital Equipment advanced 1 1/2 to 84 7/8 and Cray Research gained 1 to 46.

Philip Morris dropped 3/8 to 48 7/8 in Big Board composite trading of 2.7 million shares after a story in Barron's magazine highlighted the problems facing the tobacco industry, including anti-smoking sentiment world-wide and cigarette-tax increases. Loews, the owner of Lorillard, eased 1 3/4 to 111 1/8 and Liggett Group fell 1/4 to 12 1/8, but American Brands added 5/8 to 67 1/4 after trading as low as 65 7/8.

UAL climbed 4 3/8 to 157 1/8 after the company's unions disclosed that Citicorp, Chase Manhattan, Bankers Trust New York, Manufacturers Hanover and Chemical Banking have agreed to lead the labor group's effort to obtain financing for their proposed $4.38 billion buy-out of United Airlines, UAL's major unit.

Wal-Mart Stores gained 1 1/2 to 32 3/4 on 2.7 million shares on anticipation of a strong June sales report, expected Thursday. Analysts estimate that last month's sales at stores open at least a year were 10% to 14% higher than a year ago, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Separately, Wal-Mart agreed to acquire the book and record distribution and warehousing units of Western Merchandisers for stock valued at $28 million.

Wal-Mart Stores and Compaq Computer both advanced yesterday after 2-for-1 stock splits. The post-split gains may be a sign of avid buying by small investors, said Bernard Schaeffer, executive director of the Investment Research Institute in Cincinnati. "Stock splits are starting to attract a wider spectrum of investor interest," he said. Splits in Philip Morris, Federal National Mortgage Association and Du Pont in the past year didn't result in a surge of new buying interest, he added.

TCBY Enterprises dropped 1 5/8 to 15 1/2 on 1.9 million shares after Shearson Lehman Hutton reduced its investment rating on the stock for the second time since the July Fourth holiday. In addition, analyst Caroline Levy lowered her projections of the company's earnings for 1990 and 1991 to last year's level of $1.10 a share.

She told the investor report her revised forecasts reflect expectations that because of growing competition in the frozen-yogurt market, the company will cut the price of yogurt to its franchisees in the third quarter and development of new stores will slow next year. A company spokeswoman told the investor report that TCBY "strongly disagrees" with Shearson's revised estimates and expects to meet its target of about 400 new stores next year.

General Instrument went up 5/8 to 46 1/4. The company, which has accepted a buyout offer of $44.50 a share from Forstmann Little, disclosed that another party requested certain financial information from it last week.

Quantum Chemical, which fell 1 1/4 Friday, dropped another 1 to 14 3/8 as Salomon Brothers and Mabon Nugent lowered their estimates of the company's 1990 and 1991 earnings, based on lower polyethelene prices. Goldman Sachs had reduced its estimates Friday.

Crown Cork & Seal gained 2 1/4 to 63 1/8 following news that Chairman John F. Connelly, 85 years old, died Sunday night. Mr. Connelly had controlled a stake of about 17.6% in the company, which hasn't paid a common-stock dividend since 1956, the year before he became chief executive.

Maytag gained 1/2 to 17 3/8. USA Today reported, without elaborating, that Hanson PLC of Britain is rumored to be eyeing the company as a possible takeover target.

Long Island Lighting went up 1/4 to 20 5/8. Standard & Poor's raised its ratings on some of the company's debt issues to investment grade, based on expectations that the company will be able to end its involvement with the Shoreham nuclear plant and on other factors.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 0.22 to 358.65. Volume totaled 11,520,000 shares.

B&H Ocean Carriers rose 7/8 to 12 7/8. An investor group including the company's chairman, Arvid Bervall, disclosed that it owns 8.28% of B&H's common shares.

Environmental stocks traded on the Amex also moved higher, including Sanifill, which advanced 1 3/4 to 24 3/4. Alex. Brown & Sons raised its rating on the stock to "aggressive buy" from "buy" and boosted its 1990 and 1991 earnings forecasts. Additionally, Air & Water Technologies climbed 2 to 30 and Chambers Development Class A gained 1 to 24 1/4.
