Technology: IBM May License DRAM Technology To Motorola Inc.

  • Author: Laurence Hooper
  • Date: 07/10/90

International Business Machines Corp. is considering licensing some cutting-edge chip technology to Motorola Inc., industry executives said. The move would help Motorola hedge a huge bet it has made in the memory-chip area. It also would give IBM another domestic supplier of memory chips, and it would help strengthen the beleaguered U.S. semiconductor industry.

The pairing would be a natural one, because IBM and Motorola already have a research agreement covering some advanced semiconductor technology. Any agreement also would continue a trend in which IBM has shared its semiconductor technology with U.S. and European companies to strengthen them against Japanese competitors that IBM fears may become too powerful.

For instance, the technology that would be licensed to Motorola -- for four-megabit dynamic random access memory chips, or DRAMS, the densest now in commercial use -- is already licensed to Micron Technology Inc. IBM also had planned to license the technology to U.S. Memories, the ill-fated consortium of U.S. chip makers and consumers.

Both Motorola and IBM declined to confirm or otherwise comment on their licensing negotiations, which were first reported yesterday in the New York Times.

Some consultants and securities analysts speculated that Motorola is attempting to reduce its dependence on Toshiba Corp., with which it already has an agreement covering chip technology. They said that Motorola may be trying to protect the huge investments it has made in DRAM factories in the event that there are problems with implementing Toshiba's design. By licensing the IBM design, Motorola would be simply lining up a backup technology to ensure that those factories get up and running, they said.

"Motorola is making way too big a bet to depend on a single technology, especially one from a partner that's 6,000 miles away," said Drew Peck of Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. "This way, either Toshiba's or IBM's technology will come through, and they'll be okay."

Analysts also speculated that the Motorola move may put a damper on the recent rush by U.S. companies to find Japanese partners. Five major U.S.-Japanese joint ventures in semiconductors have been announced since January.

"This certainly is going to cause some people to think twice about the commitments they're making," said Will Strauss of Forward Concepts Co., a Tempe, Ariz., market-research firm.

Like some other analysts, Mr. Strauss pointed to a growing perception that Japanese companies may be getting the better deal in such technology-sharing partnerships. "If it turns out that Motorola isn't happy with a relationship that's a few years old, people will be more cautious about getting into these deals," he said.

Officials from both Motorola and Toshiba denied any discord between their companies. "Toshiba has been a very honorable, very equitable partner," a Motorola spokesman said.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Motorola shares closed at $85.50, up 37.5 cents.
