World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Gain on Index-Linked Trading; London Shares Ease on Limited Buying Interest

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/10/90

Tokyo stocks rose on index-linked buying, but volume remained slack as investor interest continued to flow into the smaller markets, traders said. In London, stocks ended cautious, quiet trading modestly softer, as limited buying interest is proving highly selective.

Although profit-taking checked gains in Tokyo throughout the day, traders noted that the inability by arbitragers to support strong index-linked buying was more the reason that the Nikkei Index closed off its intraday high of 32608 points. The index of 225 issues gained 93.16 points to 32538.28.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the Nikkei index fell 75.67 points to 32462.61.

Monday's volume on the first section was estimated at 400 million shares, down from 462.1 million shares Friday. Decliners narrowly outnumbered winning issues 476 to 469, while 178 issues were unchanged.

Program buying was tied mostly to the Nikkei Index, while the Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section reflected the more general market softness. The Topix dropped 1.79 points, or 0.08%, to 2362.41.

The second section index surged 74.69 points, or 1.73%, to 4397.72. The section's volume was estimated at 45.8 million shares, down from 48.6 million shares Friday.

Traders said that the market hasn't yet been aroused by the economic summit of the Group of Seven industrialized nations in Houston, not anticipating a change in the global interest rate situation will result.

"I don't expect anything revolutionary" out of the summit to affect foreign-exchange rates, one trader at a top domestic securities firm said. He added that the current macroeconomic scenario provides "no real reason to buy the yen right now."

Until news lures aggressive buying on the yen, pulling the dollar below 147 yen, institutional investors are seen unlikely to return to the broad market, noted the chief trader at a U.K. securities firm in Tokyo.

As the first section is being "artificially" manipulated by program trading, he said, light selling should continue while institutional buying interest continues to enter the smaller markets, particularly the Tokyo Stock Exchange second section, over-the-counter and Osaka trading.

"Unless it's small, regional, cheap or heavily foreign-oriented," the U.K. firm's trader said, "institutional investors are unlikely to buy" any of the first section stocks for some time.

Of interest yesterday was Mitsubishi Oil, which with Mitsubishi Petroleum Development will purchase Merlin Petroleum from Bond Corp. Holdings for $75 million, Bond Corp. said.

The news sparked initial buying in Mitsubishi Oil, but support later faltered for the entire oil sector. The stock closed flat at 1260 yen ($8.31).

Soft-drink can maker Toyo Seikan surged 200 yen to 4550 yen, as some speculators reasoned the hot, muggy summer weather would induce the seasonal rise in beverage sales, analysts said.

Retail shares were also higher, helped by news this past week that retail prices continue to rise in Japan. Ito-Yokado rose 30 yen to 4060 yen, Isetan gained 50 yen to 4500 yen, Marui advanced 60 yen to 3330 yen, and Daiei gained 70 yen to 2070 yen.

Blue-chip shares Sony and TDK continued to attract value buying, although both shares are expected to see a correction soon, analysts said. Also helped by index-linked buying, Sony was up 10 yen to 8810 yen, while TDK added 30 yen to 7360 yen.

Kyocera was hit by profit-taking after a long run of advances, closing at 8900 yen, down 70 yen.

Also lower were steel, construction, mining and banking shares.

In London, meanwhile, there were again several company profit downgradings by equity analysts, adding to the growing wariness among investors, who may be reluctant to trade ahead of the U.K. June inflation report Friday.

A firm pound is encouraging for some segments of the market, particularly those stocks with predominantly U.K.-based earnings, but these same equities are being hit by current high interest rates, dealers noted.

The resulting mixed picture has sidelined some investors, although one dealer noted that there is still a surprising amount of liquidity in the market, albeit in a very selective fashion.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index ended 2.5 points lower, at 2337.5. The Financial Times 30 Share Index fell 5.7 points to 1859.3, while the September FT-SE 100 Index was quoted at 2403, off from Friday's close of 2412.

Volume was light, with 369.8 million shares changing hands. Monday was the first day of a new two-week trading account.

Highlights included Cadbury Schweppes's losing 6 pence to end at 361 pence (11 cents to $6.44), on a weekend newspaper report that the concern would bid for the confectionery business of Switzerland's Suchard, following its recent takeover by Philip Morris.

Although Cadbury denied the report, the market takes the view it is still interested in mounting a bid, one dealer said, adding, "This can't be good for its share price."

Glaxo gained 17 pence to 830 pence on strong buying by one U.S. investor. However, the company was downgraded by a handful of analysts, reflecting its exposure to dollar earnings.

Thorn EMI shed 34 pence to end at 735 pence. Dealers said 21.5 pence of the fall was because of the share going ex-dividend while the balance was ascribed to heavy selling of the stock.

Construction concerns were also downgraded on the view that they had had a good run in the previous quarter and were ripe for profit-taking. Redland fell 4 pence to 604 pence.

Meanwhile, stocks closed higher in Zurich, Stockholm and Madrid, lower in Paris and Frankfurt, mixed in Brussels and Amsterdam, and unchanged in Milan. South African gold stocks fell slightly.

Also, stocks ended higher in Sydney, Wellington, Taipei, Manila, Hong Kong and Seoul and lower in Singapore.
