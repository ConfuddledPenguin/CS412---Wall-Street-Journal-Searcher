House Panel Seeks Perjury Inquiries Of HUD's Ex-Chief

  • Author: Joe Davidson
  • Date: 07/10/90

WASHINGTON -- The House panel investigating federal housing corruption will ask a special prosecutor to investigate possible acts of perjury by former housing Secretary Samuel Pierce. The House employment and housing subcommittee will send a letter of "at least 20 pages" to Arlin Adams, the special prosecutor who was appointed this year to look into irregularities in the Department of Housing and Urban Development's moderate-rehabilitation rent-subsidy program.

The subcommittee will ask Mr. Adams, a retired federal judge, to seek permission from the three-judge appellate court panel that appointed him to expand his authority. The subcommittee wants Mr. Adams to examine three additional areas, the most serious of which is perjury, according to Stuart Weisberg, the subcommittee's chief counsel.

The letter to Mr. Adams, which Mr. Weisberg expects to be delivered next week, will "cite the specific quotes, the specific statements" in testimony of Mr. Pierce that the House panel believes may be false.

Mr. Pierce's lawyer, Paul Perito, said it is "inconceivable" that Mr. Adams would find that the former secretary lied. Mr. Perito added that the subcommittee's effort is a "bold and unwarranted attempt to put the secretary in unfavorable light with the independent counsel."

Mr. Weisberg wouldn't give examples of possible Pierce inconsistencies. But members of the House panel previously have pointed to testimony from former HUD officials, who contradicted the former secretary by indicating that Mr. Pierce had direct involvement in awarding contracts through political favoritism.

In May, Attorney General Dick Thornburgh asked the special prosecutor to expand his inquiry to include allegations of favoritism in grants from the secretary's discretionary fund and from an urban development program. Mr. Adams office wouldn't say what decision, if any, has been made on the Thornburgh request.

Mr. Weisberg said the subcommittee won't accuse Mr. Pierce of committing perjury, but will suggest there is reasonable cause to believe that further investigation is warranted. "We believe that standard has been more than met in these instances," he said.

He wouldn't disclose the other two areas the panel wants the special prosecutor to examine, but Mr. Weisberg did add: "Perjury is the one Pierce will have the most trouble with."
