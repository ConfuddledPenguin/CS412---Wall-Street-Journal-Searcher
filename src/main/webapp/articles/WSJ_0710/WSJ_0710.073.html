Heard on the Street: Quality of Du Pont's Revisionist Management Draws Raves From Wide Range of Analysts

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 07/10/90

NEW YORK -- How's this for a rare bird: A U.S. industrial giant's stock is being recommended because of the quality of its management, of all things. Wall Street chemical analysts are stretching their metaphors to the limit to praise the new leadership at Du Pont, where Chairman Edgar S. Woolard Jr. took control little more than a year ago.

"You're seeing the same kind of change in the management at Du Pont that you've seen in the Kremlin," Paul Leming, an analyst at Morgan Stanley, says. "You've literally seen a generational shift from an old guard to the new guard." He also notes that Du Pont's board has been slimmed to 23 members from more than 30, sharply reducing directors' average age in the process.

For the first time, Mr. Leming says, Du Pont has a management system where "in underperforming businesses, they're taking managers out and shooting them. You don't have lifetime job security anymore."

As an example, Mr. Leming points to Du Pont's decision, announced last January, to cut 900 jobs from its polymer products department, as part of an effort to trim costs in that unit by $100 million a year. The cutbacks followed a management shake-up in the polymer unit late last year.

Even analysts who have a neutral stance on Du Pont's shares are impressed by the new approach at Du Pont headquarters in Wilmington, Del. "The management is working hard to change the culture and the cost structure," Leslie Ravitz, a chemical analyst at Salomon Brothers, says. He notes that Du Pont has closed an operation that made acrylic fiber, which the company well nigh invented.

In addition, the analysts note that Du Pont's management, while continuing a huge capital spending program, has been more aggressive in raising the company's dividend and buying back stock. Du Pont raised its dividend 10.5% in January 1989 and an additional 14% in October 1989.

To some analysts, Du Pont seems undervalued, compared with other big industrial companies. George Krug of Oppenheimer, who put the stock on his firm's recommended list in May, says Du Pont shares are selling at about 10 times his estimate of the company's 1990 earnings, a multiple that is about 40% less than the earnings multiple for stocks in the Standard & Poor's 500 index.

Mr. Krug also says Du Pont should benefit from an expected drop in commodity chemical prices over the next few years. Though Du Pont produces some commodity chemicals, Mr. Krug argues that the company is, on balance, a buyer of commodity chemicals, which it uses to produce other chemicals with fatter profit margins.

Despite the raves for Du Pont management, Mr. Ravitz of Salomon and Paul Christopherson, chemical analyst at Bear Stearns, point to a series of uncertainties that could keep Du Pont's earnings from growing rapidly in the next year or so.

With more than $35 billion in annual sales, Du Pont is a huge company exposed to vagaries in many sectors of the economy. As a big supplier of basic chemicals for paints and coatings, and a big producer of carpet fibers, Du Pont has been hurt by slowdowns in auto and home sales. As the owner of Conoco, a major oil producer and refiner, Du Pont is affected by oil price swings. And though the company's medical products unit is expected to revive from a $25 million loss last year (on revenue of about $1.5 billion), it remains a weak spot.

At yesterday's closing price of 37 1/8, Mr. Christopherson says, Du Pont is "probably at the low end of the range" of fair valuation, "but it's not enough to make it a buy with us."

Mr. Ravitz, who is estimating that Du Pont will earn $3.50 a share this year and $3.75 a share next year, believes its earnings won't really take off until 1992 or 1993, because the company now is in the midst of a major capital spending program. Du Pont, which had capital outlays of $5.09 billion last year, is projecting $5.2 billion of spending this year.

Among the spending programs are two that will expand Du Pont's production of two mainstay fibers: nylon and Lycra. Over the next decade, Du Pont will spend $1 billion to expand nylon production in Asia alone.

But the real snap for Du Pont earnings could come from Lycra, a fiber that got its start in women's underwear, moved into swimsuits, and now is widely used in pantyhose and exercise clothes. Du Pont is betting between $400 million and $600 million that more designers will weave Lycra into dresses, suits and skirts, even after the spandex minidress fades from the scene.
