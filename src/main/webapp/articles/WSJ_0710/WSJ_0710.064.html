Mutual Funds: Junk Beats Other Domestic Bond Funds

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 07/10/90

NEW YORK -- The brave souls who tiptoed back into junk bond mutual funds in the spring are already starting to get rewarded. According to New York's Lipper Analytical Services, junk funds produced a total return, or share price change plus dividends, of 4.1% over the past three months, better than any other category of domestic bond fund. Investing in longer-term bonds "was right for this quarter, and the lower quality funds also worked," said A. Michael Lipper, president of Lipper Analytical. "We're back to normality."

For the mutual fund companies, which have been bleeding junk fund assets for much of the past year, the junk market turnaround is welcome news. According to the Investment Company Institute, total junk fund assets dwindled from $35.2 billion in June 1989 to $24.5 billion in April 1990, as junk bond prices fell and investors yanked $6.3 billion more out of junk bond funds than they invested. But junk fund investors turned mildly bullish in May, with junk funds enjoying net inflows of $57.2 million. Fund groups say junk funds are continuing to pull in cash.

But, warned Ronald Lout, a bond fund manager at Denver's Financial Programs, "The {junk bond} rally is pretty narrow." He noted that most of the quarter's gain came from the interest paid out by the high-yield bonds and that to date there's been little in the way of appreciation in junk bond prices.

But that was also true for other fund categories. Ian MacKinnon, head of Vanguard Group's fixed-income department, dubs the past six months the "era of the coupon," with investors earning the yield on their bonds but not enjoying or suffering much in the way of capital gains or losses.

In the past six months, the yield on the benchmark 30-year Treasury bond has moved in a relatively narrow band. The yield at the end of June was 8.4%, down from 8.63% at the end of the first quarter but up from 7.97% at the end of 1989.

Mr. MacKinnon said he anticipated the becalmed bond market back in June 1989, when the yield on long Treasurys was about 8.5%. "We thought rates wouldn't go much one way or the other," he said. "At the time, we figured inflation was around 4% or 4.5%, and that a real rate of interest of 4% or 4.5% was a reasonable rate of return. Our outlook hasn't really changed. I would say that market timing will not prove a lucrative endeavor over the next 12 months, just as it hasn't been over the past six months."

Recently, the dull but safe money market funds lagged those that held longer maturity bonds or riskier credits. But for the past six months and the past year, money market funds rank as the top-performing category among fixed-income funds.

So far this year, money market funds have also outstripped most stock funds. Money markets returned 3.84% through the first six months of 1990, vs. 3.46% for the diversified domestic equity funds.

Although junk fund investors were paid to take risks in the second quarter, that pattern didn't always hold true when it came to investment grade corporate credits. Government bond funds tended to outperform corporate bond funds holding securities of comparable maturity.

"I think there's a concern about credit," said Mr. Lipper. He noted that investors also weren't paid to take additional risk in the money market area. The second-quarter return for money market funds that buy only short-term U.S. government paper was just 0.03 percentage point less than the return for those money market funds that dabble in the corporate IOUs known as commercial paper.

GNMA funds, which buy mortgage-backed bonds that are guaranteed by the Government National Mortgage Association, rank just behind money market funds in terms of one-year performance. These funds continued to do well in the second quarter, as the gap between the yields on Ginnie Maes and Treasurys narrowed to 1.2 percentage points. The spread had jumped to 1.6 percentage points in early 1989 because of fears that the mortgage bond market would be swamped when troubled savings and loans liquidated their portfolios. Jack Lemein, manager of the $11 billion Franklin U.S. Government Securities Fund, thinks the spread isn't likely to narrow much further. "From here on out, you're starting to bet against historical numbers," he said.

The second quarter was kindest to the small band of international bond funds, which rose 4.76% in dollar terms. Paul Suckow, director of fixed income at Oppenda and the United Kingdom, short-term interest rates are higher than the yields on longer bonds. "You can go overseas and get a handsome yield without taking a lot of interest rate risk," said Mr. Suckow.

Dale Rabiner, Bartlett & Co.'s director of fixed income, is also intrigued by the foreign bond markets. He is already dabbling in Canadian government bonds and eyeing West German government bonds. "From a global perspective, our real rates in the U.S. are the lowest of all industrialized countries," noted Mr. Rabiner.
