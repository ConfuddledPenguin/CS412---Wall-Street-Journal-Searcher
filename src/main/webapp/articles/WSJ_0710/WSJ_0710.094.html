Timber Industry Opposes Proposal for Spotted Owl

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/10/90

WASHINGTON (AP) -- The timber industry urged the government yesterday to reject a spotted-owl protection strategy proposed by government scientists, saying it would cost 102,000 jobs and turn some towns into rural ghettos. Researchers commissioned by the American Forest Resource Alliance said logging communities in Washington, Oregon and Northern California accept the need to reduce timber harvests to protect the environment. But they said the government should consider human as well as environmental needs in developing a plan for the endangered owl.

"Yeah, we're going to lose jobs," said John Beuter, a forest economics consultant from Portland, Ore. He said the question is, "How much of this is really necessary and how will it be implemented over time?"

The Forest Service has estimated that 8,000 timber jobs would be lost by the end of the decade under owl-protection policies recommended in an April report by government scientists.

Mr. Beuter said his research shows the figure would be twice that, plus thousands more jobs lost outside the timber industry. The direct and indirect job loss would come to 102,000 by the end of the decade if the report's strategy is applied to public and private lands, he said.

A federal timber task force is scheduled to come up with an extensive owl-protection plan by Sept. 1.

Estimates of the remaining spotted owl population range from 3,000 to 5,000 pairs. Each nesting pair needs a large tract of undisturbed old-growth forest, biologists say.
