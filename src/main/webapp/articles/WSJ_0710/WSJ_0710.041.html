MORE FRENCH GIVE UP WINE

Most French people say they never drink wine, according to a study by a French wine-industry organization.

The nationwide survey of 4,000 people was conducted in March and April by the National Interprofessional Office of Wine. Participants were asked about the drinking habits of all adults in their household, and the responses reflected the practices of 12,400 people, the organization said. In a 1980 survey conducted by the group, 38.7% of the respondents said they never drink wine. That figure climbed to 45.1% in 1985 and to 50.7% this year.

Wine consumption varies sharply by sex, according to the survey. It said 10.9% of the women and 28.1% of the men called themselves daily wine drinkers.

Asked why they didn't drink wine, 73.4% of the respondents said they didn't like it and 25.3% cited health concerns. The beverage most often chosen to replace wine with meals was water.

ROBBERY IN BRAZIL

A 15-man team of bank robbers may become the latest contributor to Brazilian inflation. The robbers stole cruzeiros valued at more than $13 million Sunday morning from a federal Central Bank branch in Salvador, Bahia. Most of the stolen cash reserves hadn't yet become part of the money supply. The Central Bank said it would publish a listing of all the serial numbers today, but this may not prevent the banknotes -- believed to be in small denominations -- from circulating. Central Bank officials told the Jornal do Brasil newspaper that the heist was "as if the thieves were issuing currency."

JAPAN'S COMMUNISTS

Japan's Communist Party opened a congress yesterday with an attack on China and the Soviet Union. The party, which has 500,000 members, has long tried to distance itself from the two giants of world communism by advocating multiparty democracy. However, its support has fallen sharply in two national elections over the past year in part because of public revulsion at China's repression of democracy protests and the anti-communist tide of change in Europe.

TOWARD PEACE IN AFRICA

The six countries of the Horn of Africa agreed to help each other find peace. The leaders of Ethiopia, Uganda, Kenya, Sudan, Somalia and Djibouti made their pledge just before the annual summit of the Organization of African Unity opened yesterday in Addis Ababa. Each country promised to "refrain from any action against the national unity and territorial integrity of other member states." Of the six nations, only Kenya and Djibouti are free from civil war.

PLANS FOR EAST GERMAN AIRLINE

West Germany plans to reduce state ownership of the East German airline Interflug to 51% from 100% following the full unification of the two Germanys, Lufthansa said. That would leave Lufthansa, West Germany's 51% state-owned flag carrier, with its previously agreed upon 26% stake in Interflug, with the other 23% being sold to third parties, Lufthansa Chairman Heinz Ruhnau said. He didn't identify the third parties.

CHINA'S FALTERING ECONOMY

China released figures for 1990's first six months that showed poor performance by state-owned industry. Finance Minister Wang Bingqian characterized the nation's financial situation as "still fairly grim," the official Xinhua News Agency reported. Xinhua quoted Mr. Wang as blaming China's economic woes on low industrial production, a sluggish market and insufficient control over expenditures.

GLOOMY OUTLOOK FOR EAST EUROPE

The Soviet Union and its East European neighbors are plunging deeper into recession, United Nations economists said.

Some radical austerity policies, particularly in Poland, have led to worse recession than expected, the U.N. Economic Commission for Europe said. And attempts by the Soviet Union and some other countries to stabilize activity within the old communist-style economic system have generally failed.

The commission said East European governments had encountered considerable difficulties in obtaining a consensus for new solutions. "The policy landscape at midyear is thus dominated by uncertainty. The only certain element appears to be that the recession in Eastern Europe and the Soviet Union is far from having run its course," it said in a midyear review of the economies of its 34 member states.

"The problems facing the reforming economies are immense," the commission said, "and their solution will take time and require considerable assistance from the West."

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

Japan's Yasuda Trust & Banking Co. has replaced disposable wooden chop sticks with reusable plastic ones in the company cafeteria. In so doing, "we are helping to preserve forests and reduce urban waste," a spokesman said. . . . Britain will hold an inquiry into the conduct of a steamship captain who was censured for not helping the Titanic when it sank 78 years ago. The investigation will examine testimony at the original inquiry as to the role of Stanley Lord, captain of the Californian. The new inquiry comes in response to a campaign by relatives to clear the name of Mr. Lord, who maintained until his death in 1962 that he was too far from the Titanic to be of help.
