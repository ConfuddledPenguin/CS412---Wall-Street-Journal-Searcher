Credit Markets: Bonds Fall on Continued Reaction to Jobs Data, Worry Over Today's Thrift `Bailout' Issue Sale

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 07/10/90

NEW YORK -- Continued fallout from Friday's employment report and caution ahead of today's $5 billion sale of "bailout bonds" bruised bond prices. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell nearly 1/2 point yesterday, or $5 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, rose to 8.54%.

The loss followed a sharp 1 1/8-point decline on Friday, which was triggered by the Department of Labor's employment report. Although jobs growth was sluggish in June, investors were frustrated because the report overall wasn't weak enough to prompt the Federal Reserve to ease monetary policy anytime soon. Many investors bet heavily that the report would show substantial weakness in the economy.

"The employment report was a big disappointment," said Lacy Hunt, economist at Carroll McEntee & McGinley Inc. He said that investors sold bonds actively during the morning session, but that the market remained extremely quiet for the remainder of the day with very little trading taking place. There was very little reaction later in the day to the government's report of stronger-than-expected expansion of consumer credit.

In addition to economic concerns, analysts said investors were a bit jittery ahead of today's planned $5 billion sale of 30-year bonds by the Resolution Funding Corp. Refcorp is the federal agency responsible for raising money to finance the nation's thrift industry bailout.

Today's auction will be Refcorp's fourth and will mark the agency's return to issuing 30-year bonds after selling 40-year bonds in January and April. The previous two 40-year Refcorp auctions were considered disasters on the day of the sales and sparked sharp sell-offs in the broader bond market. However, demand for the securities picked up later when the bonds were "stripped." Strips are created when dealers separate the interest-paying "coupon" portion of the a bond from its principal or "corpus" portion and sell the two parts separately. Strips also are divided into different maturities.

"Refcorp is a very difficult issue," said Mr. Hunt. "It's a different type of animal and there is some concern," about how it will fare.

In when-issued trading activity, yields on the Refcorp bailout bonds were quoted late yesterday at 8.93%. On July 3, the day that Refcorp when-issued trading began, the 30-year bonds were quoted at yields of 8.81%, 0.40 percentage point higher than yields on the 30-year Treasury bonds.

Treasury Securities

The government sold a record $18 billion of Treasury bills yesterday at rates that were much higher than at the previous auction a week earlier.

The average discount rate on threemonth bills was 7.81%, up from 7.73% at last week's auction. The rate on six-month bills was 7.75%, up from 7.60% last week.

Traders said the average rate for three-month bills at yesterday's auction was the highest since the average of 7.91% at the auction on April 30, while the average rate on six month bills was the highest since 7.79% at the May 21 auction.

In trading yesterday, the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 102 4/32 to yield 8.54% compared with 102 18/32 to yield 8.50% on Friday. The latest 10-year notes ended at 102 to yield 8.55% compared with 102 10/32 to yield 8.50%.

Foreign Bonds

In a report on the global bond markets titled "Sterling Crowned Queen of the Quarter," Salomon Brothers Inc. said the British bond market outperformed other foreign markets during the second quarter.

The report said that British bonds, which are called gilts, reversed exceptionally weak performance in the first quarter to post a total return of 7.14%, when measured in sterling. When measured in U.S. dollars, the gilts market rose 13.78%, Salomon said.

Yields on 10-year gilts fell one full percentage point during the second quarter, due largely to a 6.20% gain in the value of sterling.

In U.S. dollar terms, the other top-performing markets were Switzerland, 9.77%; Australia, 8.89%; Japan, 6.75% and Denmark 6.18%. The worst performers were West Germany, 3.41%; Netherlands, 3.53%; France, 4.47% and Canada 5.04%.

But gilts weren't winners yesterday. Traders said the market ended weaker despite another surge in sterling and a smaller-than-expected rise in wholesale prices.

Traders attributed the drop in gilts mostly to technical factors, including the September long-term gilt futures contract's move below a key support level.

Among benchmark gilts, the 11.75% bond due 2003/2007 fell nearly 1/2 point to 100 15/32 to yield 11.68% while 12% notes due 1995 fell 3/8 to 98 26/32 to yield 12.35%. (These yields are quoted in local terms and therefore differ somewhat from yields calculated for these markets by U.S. investment firms, which tend to use the semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets.)

Junk, Corporate Bonds

Bucking the trend in the broader bond market, junk bonds ended slightly firmer yesterday. Most junk bonds, high-yield speculative grade securities that have low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, ended between 1/8 and 1/4 point higher.

The junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. led the market. The pay-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco posted gains on reports that the company was deluged with $7 billion in loan commitments from banks for its debt refinancing plan. Pay-in-kind bonds pay interest in the form of additional securities.

The food and tobacco concern plans to use $2.25 billion in bank loans to help buy back some of its most troublesome high-yield issues, the pay-in-kind bonds that must be reset to trade at par by next April.

The company's 14.07% convertible pay-in-kind debentures due in 2009 finished 7/8 point higher to close at 104 1/2 on the New York Stock Exchange. The 14.07% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures maturing in 2007 ended 5/8 point lower to close at 85 5/8.

Prices of investment grade bonds fell between 1/8 and 3/8 point.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities prices continued their recent slide on the heels of the Treasury market, although losses in mortgage securities were less pronounced.

Among actively traded secondary issues, Government National Mortgage Association 10% issues were down 6/32 at 101 11/32, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities were down 5/32 at 98 11/32 and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were off 5/32 at 98 27/ 32.

In the primary market for mortgage-backed securities, pricing details were announced for three real estate mortgage investment conduits now being traded,for a total of $1.3 billion.

A $500 million issue of Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. Remics is being offered by Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. The offering is backed by Freddie Mac 9.5% securities.

A $400 million issue of Federal National Mortgage Association Remics is being offered by Morgan Stanley & Co., backed by Fannie Mae 9.5% securities.

And a $400 million Remic issue from Denver-based Fund America Investors Corp. is being backed by Ginnie Mae 10% securities and offered by Kidder, Peabody & Co.

Municipal Bonds

Tax-exempt issues ended 1/8 to 1/4 point lower.

In new issue activity, a First Southwest Co. group set the preliminary pricing for an issue of $50 million Texas zero-coupon college student loan bonds.

The bonds were priced to yield to maturity from 6.20% in 1992 to 7.25% in 2006 to 2009.

This week more than $2 billion in new issues are scheduled to be priced.

In secondary action, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 were off 1/8 point at 98 5/8 to yield 7.30%.
