OTC Focus: Market Is Mixed as Nike, K-Swiss Jump; Carrington Laboratories Falls 2 1/2 to 17

  • Author: Craig S. Smith Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/10/90

The stocks of athletic-footwear makers Nike and K-Swiss jumped, while the rest of the OTC market stumbled into a mixed close. Nike leaped 7 1/4 to 87 1/4 after turning in a shining profit report for its fourth quarter ended May 31. The company earned $1.77 a share, up from 97 cents a year ago. The earnings were better than analysts had expected and were over the top of Nike's own projected profit range, which it had boosted higher in June.

The OTC market's new athletic footwear stock, K-Swiss, bounded along with Nike, adding 1 1/4 to 27 1/2. K-Swiss, which specializes in tennis shoes, has gained an incredible 57% since it went public for $17.50 a share in early June.

But the Nasdaq Composite Index failed to keep up with the rubber-soled stocks, adding only 1.15, or 0.25%, to 461.68. Much of that rise came late in the day.

The New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained only 0.23%.

More stocks fell in price on the OTC market than gained, however. OTC advancers trailed decliners 925 to 1,030.

Volume rose to a weak 118.5 million shares from Friday's anemic 101.4 million shares.

William R. Rothe, managing director of OTC trading at Alex. Brown & Sons, says that despite the recent light trading volume institutions are continuing to buy stocks.

"People are still putting cash to work because they don't have any viable alternatives," he says.

Nike drew the loudest cheers in the OTC market. The company said its world-wide orders for athletic footwear and apparel scheduled for delivery between June and November 1990 are 42% higher in dollars compared to the same period last year. Foreign orders are up 70%, Nike said.

Nike's international business could match its domestic sales within the next three to five years, says Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst Josie Esquivel. International sales comprise only a little more than 20% of the company's business currently, she says.

"Basketball is the fastest growing sport in Europe," Ms. Esquivel says, giving Nike an opportunity to exercise one of its strongest suits there. Meanwhile, Nike is pursuing other European sports markets such as soccer, she says.

"All that put together will continue to give Nike a tremendous edge overseas," says Ms. Esquivel. She expects the company's earnings to grow 20% annually for the next three years. Yesterday, Ms. Esquivel raised her estimate for Nike's fiscal 1991 profit to $7.80 a share from her earlier estimate of $7.50 a share.

Eager investors may get a buying opportunity soon, she says. Ms. Esquivel believes the market overreacted to yesterday's earnings news and says the stock will fall back a bit in the next few days.

Apple Computer led the most-active list, gaining 1 7/8 to 46 5/8. Soundview Financial Group upgraded its long-term investment rating on the stock to a "buy" from a "hold" because of expectation that new product introductions will help boost Apple's share of the desktop computer market over the next year, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Elsewhere on the technology front, 3Com rose 7/8 to 17 5/8. International Business Machines announced a joint development effort with 3Com to write specifications that they say will result in customers being able to more effectively manage different kinds of networks.

MCI Communications eased 3/4 to 40 7/8. PaineWebber's telecommunications analyst Jack Grubman says the stock is reflecting underlying nervousness about second-quarter earnings in the long-distance telephone industry. He says, however, that MCI has told analysts that it will meet expectations.

MCI's drop and the weakness in other telecommunications stocks translated into a 1.97-point loss by the Nasdaq Utility Index to 643.82.

Laidlaw added 3/4 to 24 1/8. The company's founder, Michael Degroote, has named Donald Jackson as the new head of the $2 billion transportation and hazardous-waste managment company.

Carrington Laboratories fell 2 1/2 to 17. Yesterday's "Heard on the Street" column in this newspaper raised questions about the prospects for use of the company's Carrisyn drug in treating AIDS. Carrington said its stock has been the target of a "bear raid" by so-called short sellers, who sell borrowed stock in hopes of profiting from the decline in the stock's price.

DEKALB Genetics lost 1 to 42 1/2. The company said it expects to report that an unexpectedly large Argentine currency loss will cut its third quarter profit to less than half of the 85 cents a share earned in the quarter last year.

Microlog lost 1 1/4 to 3 1/2. The company said Hambrecht & Quist cut its earnings estimate for the company's fiscal year ending in October to 40 cents a share from a previous estimate of 60 cents. In fiscal 1989, the Germantown, Md.-based manufacturer of voice-processing systems earned 46 cents a share.
