Perelman Plans To Sell Stock Of Revlon Unit --- Sale of 13 Million Shares In National Health Labs Would Help Repay Debt

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 07/10/90

NEW YORK -- Revlon Inc. owner Ronald Perelman plans to sell stock valued at up to $182.1 million in National Health Laboratories Inc. in a move to repay bank debt and highlight the value of Revlon's assets. The proposed public sale of 13 million shares of National Health Laboratories, a Revlon subsidiary, follows the restructuring in May of a $1.8 billion bank loan to Revlon. Revisions in the loan agreement boosted the amount Revlon must repay by 1992 to $650 million from $150 million.

Although some bankers said the bank-loan change was designed to force Revlon to sell assets to make the repayment, aides to Mr. Perelman said he was free to repay the debt any way he could, including through asset sales or refinancing.

Mr. Perelman, who has operated Revlon with a heavy debt load since acquiring the cosmetics and health care company in a hostile takeover in late 1985, wants to reduce Revlon's debt leverage in coming months, his aides say.

Some highly leveraged companies have faced financial pressure in the past year following the collapse of the market for high-yield, high-risk junk bonds and a reluctance by banks to make loans for highly leveraged transactions. The new bank loan for Revlon, which also has $800 million in junk bonds outstanding, was classified a highly leveraged transaction.

In the stock offering announced yesterday, Revlon plans to reduce its stake in National Health, a medical testing concern based in La Jolla, Calif., from 95% to 80%. Revlon founded the company in 1968 in an effort to diversify its core beauty business.

Revlon proposed a maximum offering price of $14.50 a share, the price at which National Health's stock closed on Friday.

The stock to be offered consists of 12.6 million shares owned by Revlon and 441,358 shares owned by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., the securities firm which financed the Revlon takeover but whose parent earlier this year sought bankruptcy-law protection from creditors.

Some bankers applauded Mr. Perelman's move. "A transaction like this demonstrates the liquidity of Perelman-controlled assets," said Michael Zarrilli, a managing director of Manufacturers Hanover Corp., one of the four lead banks on the new Revlon loan.

The stock of National Health Laboratories declined $1.25 a share to $13.25 a share in national over-the-counter trading yesterday. With 99 million shares outstanding, that implied a market value of $1.31 billion for the entire company.

Some analysts believe that boosting the percentage of stock traded publicly from 5% to 20% could increase the accuracy of market value indicated by the stock price. Revlon sold 5% of the company in 1988.

Joel M. Ray, a health care industry analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., said the stock offering should be positive for the price because the increased number of shares will make it more accessible to institutional investors.

Since 1985, National Health's revenue has grown 24% annually and its earnings have grown 31% annually. In 1989, its earnings were $64.7 million, or 65 cents a share, on revenues of $402.4 million. Mr. Ray estimates the company will earn 80 cents a share this year on revenue of $500 million.
