Bank Earnings Fell 14% in 1st Quarter; Real-Estate Problems Are Worsening

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 06/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal regulators said first-quarter earnings at the nation's banks dropped 14% to $6.23 billion from $7.25 billion a year earlier, and warned that problems with real-estate loans are increasing in the Midwest and Northwest, adding to the well-documented woes of the Northeast. The earnings decline was mostly attributable to large provisions that banks set aside for possible losses on real-estate loans, said Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. Chairman William Seidman.

Despite the real-estate situation, the first quarter's earnings were a rebound from meager net income of $2.1 billion in the fourth quarter of 1989 and a loss of $603 million in the third. "It's not a bad result, given all the real-estate problems," Mr. Seidman said.

Results for the first quarter included $5.76 billion set aside for possible loan losses, an increase of more than $2 billion from the year-earlier quarter. Federally chartered banks in the Northeast set aside $1.2 billion, more than triple the provision in the first quarter of 1989. Regulators have been sweeping through New England banks, pushing them to take write-downs and provisions for losses.

The FDIC's report showed that woes from real-estate loans are becoming a bigger problem for banks throughout the country. Overall, the percentage of noncurrent real-estate loans -- those that are 90 days overdue or on which banks aren't earning interest -- rose in all but 11 states. In particular, banks in the Midwest and Northwest are joining those in New England in showing troublesome increases in noncurrent real-estate loans.

In Ohio, such loans rose to 2.17% of all real-estate loans at the end of the first quarter, from 1.89% at the end of 1989. In North Dakota, the figure jumped to 2.38% from 1.78%. Washington, Montana and Colorado also registered increases, as well as Utah.

Meanwhile, noncurrent real-estate loans dropped significantly in Texas, to 6.76% at the end of the first quarter from 10.34%. They also fell a bit in Arizona, to 11.18% from 11.8%, though that is still the highest in the country.

Regulatory scrutiny of the real-estate situation is sure to increase, because real-estate loans are currently the biggest single category of bank lending and continue to show the fastest growth. Banks ended the first quarter with $770.7 billion in real-estate loans outstanding, up 11% from last year's first quarter. That increase accounted for about 94% of total loan growth during that period.

The FDIC also revised its report of fourth-quarter bank earnings to reflect restatements made by Northeast banks because of problems from real-estate loans. The revised net income of $2.1 billion originally was reported as $2.68 billion. Third-quarter results, originally reported as a $744 million loss, were restated because of other adjustments to show a $603 million loss.
