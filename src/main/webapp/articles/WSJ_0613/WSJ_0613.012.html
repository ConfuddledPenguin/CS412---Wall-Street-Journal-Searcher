Appeals Panel Negates SEC's Rule on Voting --- Agency Exceeded Its Powers By Having Firms Adopt `One Share, One Vote'

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 06/13/90

WASHINGTON -- A federal appeals court panel struck down the Securities and Exchange Commission's "one share, one vote" rule, a move that could reshape the way some companies protect themselves from takeovers. The panel, in a 3-0 decision, told the SEC that it had overstepped its authority under federal securities law and had entered corporate governance, an area traditionally determined by state law. The court told the SEC that if the rule were validated, "the commission would be able to establish a federal corporate law by using access to the national capital markets as its enforcement mechanism."

The ruling could encourage more companies to try to create multiple classes of stock in efforts to help protect incumbent management. Several lawyers and academics suggested that the ruling is likely to help boost the role of "white knights," who are often brought in by management to help fend off hostile bidders. Those investors could get shares with enhanced voting rights as part of their stake in a company.

The SEC, which said it was "disappointed," hasn't decided whether to appeal the ruling by a panel of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia in favor of the Business Roundtable, a group of 200 major corporations. The agency could appeal either to the full appeals court or to the Supreme Court. But the agency is likely to ask the exchanges to adopt uniform voting procedures on their own, amending the rules that way.

Even if the appeals court ruling stands, it's unclear how many companies would be able to get shareholder approval for multiple classes of stock with unequal voting rights. Institutional investors have emerged as a substantially more active group in recent years and aren't likely to placidly watch corporate managements take steps that could entrench themselves.

Dual voting is "clearly the most potent and ultimate protective device because it does a lot more than prevent hostile takeovers," said John Pound, finance professor at Harvard University's Kennedy School of Government. "Management is effectively insulated from unwanted changes."

He said institutional investors, particularly public employee funds that have been the most active in pressing shareholder-rights issues, were likely to take a strong stand against the introduction of new dual stock classes. "I think this would generate real outrage among those institutions concerned with good governance issues," he said. "That might be a real restraint at major companies" tempted to introduce new stock classes, he added.

At the time the rule, called 19c4, was enacted by a 4-1 commission vote, 60 New York Stock Exchange companies had disparate voting rights or planned to introduce them. In addition, 182 companies on the National Association of Securities Dealers automated quotation system and 117 American Stock Exchange concerns had such structures, according to SEC figures.

While those companies with two classes of stock already in place were "grandfathered" and allowed to retain those classes, the SEC rule said that companies couldn't in the future abolish or reduce shareholder voting rights and remain on an exchange or Nasdaq.

By setting up the rule that way, the SEC tried to skirt state corporate-governance problems because the agency regulates the exchanges. But the appeals court rejected the SEC argument, citing a 1977 Supreme Court ruling that said, "we are reluctant to federalize the substantial portion of the law of corporations that deals with transactions in securities."

The exchanges, led by the Big Board, will be watching the SEC carefully. The New York exchange pressed the SEC for rule-making help in 1988 when it was forced to reconsider an exchange rule that prohibited multiple classes of stock. That was during the takeover binge of the 1980s when a host of companies were eager to protect themselves from corporate raids.

At the time, the Amex had a more permissive rule allowing companies to list several classes of shares, and the Nasdaq didn't have any rule governing the voting structure.

After the SEC passed its rule, the exchanges adopted similar rules. If the appeals court decision stands, the exchanges will be forced to decide how to handle their own rules.

The Business Roundtable applauded the ruling. "American shareholders won an important victory today," said Bruce Atwater, chairman of the group's corporate governance task force and chief executive officer of General Mills Inc. "The court has resoundingly affirmed their right to determine for themselves what kinds of equities best serve their interests."

But some observers said the ruling could hurt shareholders. "It's hard to justify having national policy on issues like this made by a handful of individual states at a time when the capital markets are becoming more international," said Ronald Gilson, an expert on corporate governance at Stanford Law School. "The effect of it is to leave decisions about share voting largely to individual state law."

Washington lawyer Richard Phillips said he expected several companies to seek multiple classes of voting stock "as the hostile takeover comes back into vogue." But he added that the SEC certainly would appeal the decision. "No way they'll leave this one alone," he said.

SEC market regulation director Richard Ketchum called on the exchanges to adopt uniform 19c4-type rules. "There's always been a wide degree of consensus {among the exchanges and the NASD} that there should be voting rights rules," he said. "I hope that staring into the vacuum, they will respond."

One potential benefactor of the ruling may be "white knights," investors who are brought in by companies to protect against hostile bids. In the past few years, those investors weren't able to demand better voting terms before making their investments. That could change, said Meredith Brown, the head of mergers and acquisitions at Debevoise & Plimpton. "This might open up the possibility of voting arrangements" favorable to white knights, he said.

Arthur Fleischer, a takeover lawyer at Fried Frank Harris Shriver & Jacobson, agreed. "Assuming you can justify it from a corporate point of view, you are no longer restricted by 19c4 to have a proportional voting test," he said.

But he added that he thinks the court ruling is "more symbolic than practical." Except in certain specific cases, he said, he doubts shareholders would approve multiple classes.
