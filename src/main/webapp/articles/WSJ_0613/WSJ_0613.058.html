U.K. Firm Puts Oppenheimer Fund Unit on the Block

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 06/13/90

NEW YORK -- Financial distress at British & Commonwealth Holdings PLC is forcing the sale of its U.S. subsidiary, Oppenheimer Management Corp., which manages $14 billion in mutual funds. Oppenheimer, which is one of the country's 20 largest mutual fund companies, manages more than 40 mutual funds sold largely through financial planners. (The firm isn't related to Oppenheimer & Co., the U.S. securities firm.)

A management group, led by Oppenheimer President Jon S. Fossel, is putting together a bid for the company. But British & Commonwealth's investment banker, S.G. Warburg & Co., said at least 10 companies in Europe and the U.S. have expressed an interest in acquiring Oppenheimer.

"Management is one of the potential buyers," said Alistair Defriez, a corporate finance director with S.G. Warburg in London. "But it's by no means clear that the management proposal is the one that will succeed."

The planned sale, which was first reported in Fund Action, a mutual fund industry newsletter, is part of a larger effort by British & Commonwealth to raise $2 billion to pay off debt. British & Commonwealth, a United Kingdom financial services group, has been looking to sell assets since April, when it was forced to write off more than $900 million in losses at another subsidiary, Atlantic Computers PLC. Earlier this month, the firm entered "administration," the U.K. equivalent of Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law proceedings.

Shortly before the 1987 stock market crash, British & Commonwealth acquired Oppenheimer Management from another British firm, Mercantile Holdings. "I think, given how well the company has done, it is likely to be sold at a substantial gain," said Michael Goldstein, an analyst with Sanford C. Bernstein & Co.

"I heard {a price for the planned sale of} $150 million, but that seems like a very low figure to me," said Stanley Egener, president of New York's Neuberger & Berman Management Inc. Mr. Egener noted that a purchase price of $150 million is barely more than 1% of Oppenheimer's assets under management. "The last few sales I've seen have been closer to 2% than 1%. But who knows what the Brits are doing?" Other observers put the likely purchase price at closer to $200 million.

Mr. Egener speculates that if the fund group isn't bought in a management-led leveraged buy-out, then it will probably be acquired by a company not currently involved in the fund industry.

In terms of mutual fund performance, "they have consistently scored as being very, very mediocre," said Tyler Jenks, chief investment analyst with Kanon Bloch Carre & Co., a mutual fund research firm in Boston. "But they are getting better," he added. Kanon Bloch gives high ratings to Oppenheimer Gold & Special Minerals and Oppenheimer Global funds.

The fund business saw a flurry of acquisitions last year. In 1989, Criterion Group Inc. was bought by Transamerica Corp., and Keystone Management and Federated Investors were purchased in management-led leveraged buy-outs. Since the stock market crash of 1987, many other mutual fund company acquisitions have been rumored, but few have gone through.
