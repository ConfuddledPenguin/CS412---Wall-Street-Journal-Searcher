Corn Estimates Expected to Lift Futures Prices

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll and Scott Kilman
  • Date: 06/13/90

Corn-futures prices are expected to rise today on the strength of a government estimate of greater corn-export demand and projections of dwindling corn stockpiles. The Agriculture Department boosted its estimate of corn exports for the marketing year ending Aug. 31 by 50 million bushels and lowered, in turn, its estimate of corn stockpiles next September to 1.28 billion bushels.

Stronger export demand and tighter supplies also are expected to raise cash-corn prices by five cents into the range of $2.35 to $2.40 per bushel during the rest of the marketing year.

At the same time, the department gave wheat farmers a double dose of reassuring news, sticking with its May forecast for the largest winter-wheat crop since 1981 and boosting its forecast of Soviet wheat buying by one million tons.

Government analysts projected a winter-wheat harvest of 2.09 billion bushels, up 44% from last year's drought-damaged crop. The forecast was almost unchanged from the previous forecast -- down fewer than five million bushels from last month.

Private commodity analysts said they don't expect wheat prices to change significantly when trading opens today.

Winter-wheat yields are expected to average 41.2 bushels per acre. Winter wheat, which is planted in the fall and harvested in June and July, accounts for 75% of U.S. wheat production; spring wheat makes up the rest.

The harvest is just beginning in Kansas, the nation's No. 1 wheat state. The Kansas crop is expected to total 460.2 million bushels, more than double last year's crop.

The Agriculture Department didn't cut its one-month-old forecast for an 8.1 billion-bushel corn harvest. However, private analysts speculate that unusually cold and wet spring weather may have forced farmers to plant roughly two million acres fewer than government analysts expected.

The department would probably reduce its corn-production estimate if the condition of the emerging crop doesn't improve markedly. About half of the crop is in fair to poor condition. The early season situation is the worst in six years, according to Daniel W. Basse, director of market research at AgResource Co., Chicago.

All these factors are expected to keep pressure on corn prices for the foreseeable future. The department's projection of a 1.28 billion-bushel stockpile by Sept. 1 would be the smallest since the 1983 drought helped deplete the nation's stockpile.

If the 1990 harvest proves mediocre, some analysts speculate that the stockpile would fall below the one billion-bushel level by the time farmers begin harvesting the 1991 crop.

In a separate report on the Soviet Union's grain outlook, the department stuck with its May prediction that Soviet grain production will total 215 million tons in 1990. Also unchanged from a month ago is the department's projection that the Soviets will import 36 million tons of grain during the 1990-91 marketing year beginning July 1.

But department analysts raised their estimate of Soviet wheat imports by one million tons to 16 million tons, which would be the largest since 1987-88. At the same time, they lowered their estimate of Soviet coarse-grain imports by one million tons to 19 million tons. Rice and miscellaneous grains will account for the rest of the import total.
