Freddie Mac Revamps Mortgage Securities Program

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale and Andrew Bary
  • Date: 06/13/90

The Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp., known as Freddie Mac, revamped its mortgage securities program to meet increasing competition from its chief rival, the Federal National Mortgage Association, called Fannie Mae. Freddie Mac has been losing market share, because many investors and lenders viewed its securities as inferior to Fannie Mae's. Both Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae are government-sponsored, publicly traded companies that dominate the secondary mortgage market. They buy loans from lenders and package them into securities for sale to investors.

Freddie Mac said that beginning with mortgage securities issued in October, the gap between the time homeowners make monthly mortgage payments and when investors receive their payments from Freddie Mac will be shortened to 45 days from 75 days. Fannie Mae's mortgage securities have a 55-day payment delay.

The new securities, called Gold PCs, for participation certificates, will pay investors actual mortgage prepayments, not estimated prepayments. And Freddie Mac will guarantee investors the timely payment of principal and interest, even if mortgage servicers fail to make the payments.

Barney Buscemi, vice president, structured finance and investor marketing, said the company had been studying how it could meet investor needs better, and came up with the Gold PC program. The company's objective, he said, is to raise the security price, pleasing both investors and seller-servicers who are motivated by price.

Meanwhile, the part of Freddie Mac's earnings derived from the short-term float will be reduced, while income from guarantee fees will rise. Guarantee fees, Mr. Buscemi said, not only are a more stable source of income, but also are easier for analysts and others to predict.

"We didn't simply want to mimic Fannie Mae," Mr. Buscemi said. "We designed a superior product."

Nevertheless, Peter Treadway, an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., said Freddie Mac had been slipping in product development. "What you're seeing is that the company is coming back to life," he said.

At the same time, Freddie Mac announced that Citicorp Mortgage Inc. agreed to swap $5 billion of single-family mortgages in exchange for Gold PCs over the next year -- a record transaction for the secondary market.

Mr. Buscemi said that Freddie Mac doesn't know how many holders of its securities will convert to the Gold PCs, but that liquidity of the old securities won't be hampered. The company, which has nearly $290 billion of mortgage securities outstanding, will give investors the option of converting to Gold PCs for a fee. About one-third can't be converted, because they are held for collateral for derivative securities such as Remics, or real estate mortgage investment conduits. A Remic is a type of security authorized by the 1986 tax law; underwritten by Wall Street firms, Remics provide a method of dividing up the income flows from many mortgages into new securities with various interest rates and maturities to attract banks, insurers and other big investors to the home-mortgage market.
