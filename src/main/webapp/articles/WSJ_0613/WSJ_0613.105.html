International: U.S. Won't Seek Trade Retaliation Against India

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 06/13/90

WASHINGTON -- U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills will reaffirm later this week that India's policies governing insurance and investment are unreasonable and hurt U.S. commercial interests, but she won't order trade retaliation, administration officials said. Instead, Mrs. Hills will say that the U.S. will seek to address its concerns about the Indian trade barriers in the so-called Uruguay Round of current multilateral trade liberalization talks under the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, the same officials said.

The U.S. will reserve the right to take unilateral action against India's trade barriers if they aren't addressed in the GATT talks, which are expected to conclude in December. India is an important participant in the talks because it has substantial influence over other developing countries.

Mrs. Hills is required to reach a determination about India's insurance and investment practices under the so-called Super 301 clause of the 1988 Trade Act. That law was intended to help the U.S. open foreign markets through negotiation backed by the threat of trade retaliation. India was cited in April 1989, along with Japan and Brazil. But while Japan and Brazil settled their differences with the U.S., India has refused to negotiate.

Several U.S. insurance companies are eager to invest more in India, particularly to have access to that country's growing middle-class market. But they say India's prohibitive investment regulations prevent that. The U.S. had an $851 million merchandise trade deficit with India in 1989.
