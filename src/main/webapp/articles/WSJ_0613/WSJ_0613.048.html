Law: Mobil's Hefty Ads Knowingly Misled Public, Suits Claim

  • Author: Randolph B. Smith
  • Date: 06/13/90

Lawsuits filed by seven states contend Mobil Chemical Co. exploited popular misconceptions by making environmental claims for Hefty trash bags that it knew were false. The actions allege the unit of New York-based Mobil Corp. claimed Hefty bags are "degradable" to obtain a marketing advantage, even though documents show company officials knew the bags wouldn't break down in landfills or help reduce solid waste.

"It's a myth that degradable products quickly disappear or provide any benefits to the environment in a landfill -- and Mobil knew it," says New York Attorney General Robert Abrams.

Mobil Chemical spokesman Allen Gray says the company denies the allegations and will fight the lawsuits. "We have scientific evidence fully substantiating our degradability claims," Mr. Gray says. The company acknowledges plastic Hefty bags won't break down fast enough in landfills to make room for more garbage, but denies that its claims would mislead consumers.

As previously reported, a task force of attorneys general in seven states filed lawsuits to stop Mobil Chemical from selling or distributing trash bags making degradability claims. The states are also requesting fines totaling several million dollars. No hearings have been scheduled, although two states, New York and Wisconsin, have requested immediate injunctions. In New York, Mr. Abrams is requesting warning labels on existing inventory stating that Hefty "provides no environmental benefit when disposed of in a landfill or incinerated." State officials say a two-month to three-month supply of Hefty bags with offending labels exist in several states, including California. Hefty has about 25% of the plastic trash bag market.

The New York lawsuit says Mobil Chemical's marketing surveys found consumers would respond positively to "degradable" bags because they believed they would "simply disappear." Hefty bags contain an additive that makes plastic break down faster after exposure to sunlight.

According to statements by Mobil officials included as exhibits in the lawsuits, however, the company didn't believe this attribute would help reduce solid waste. "The only time photodegradability comes into play is when materials are on the surface of the earth and can be reached by sunlight. If these materials are buried in a sanitary landfill, then photodegradability is not a useful additive," a Mobil official said at a company-sponsored symposium on degradable plastic and the environment, according to the suit.

In addition, the suit says degradation can harm the environment by producing polluting byproducts.

In response to widespread criticism, Mobil agreed in March to remove all environmental claims from Hefty packaging. But the lawsuits complain that existing inventory is still being sold and that Mobil has taken no steps to warn consumers. Mr. Gray says new packaging, which makes no environmental claims, is starting to reach stores and "soon" 25% of the Hefty line will have it.

The lawsuits were filed in New York, California, Texas, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Massachusetts and Washington. They represent the first actions in a continuing investigation of products claiming to be degradable, recyclable or otherwise environmentally friendly.
