Technology: For High Tech, Diamonds May Be a Lab's Best Friend

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Diamonds may become one of the world's most glittering technologies. A report by the National Research Council said that new ways of making thin coatings of synthetic diamond and other superhard materials may revolutionize electronics, optics and machinery. "The ultimate economic impact of this technology may well outstrip that of high-temperature superconductors," said the report, referring to the discovery of a new class of materials that conduct electricity without resistance.

The report by the council, an arm of the National Academy of Sciences, projected a host of uses for diamond technology. Computer chips made from superhard materials should be able to function at higher power, speed and temperature than conventional ones. That could improve the performance of machinery ranging from spacecraft to supercomputers, the report predicted.

The report did not predict when these potential uses would come into being. "Despite this promise, there are relatively few applications currently being realized," it cautioned. That's partly because chemists don't yet understand well enough exactly how the synthetic diamonds form and grow, and how to speed up the process.

John Venables, a former Martin Marietta Corp. scientist who led the council's study, said he expected it will take about five years to develop advanced optical coatings and 10 years to develop diamond computer chips.
