International: Japan's Surplus In Trade Shrank Slightly in May --- But Some Economists Say Measure Might Widen Again in a Few Months

  • Author: Yumiko Ono
  • Date: 06/13/90

TOKYO -- Japan's May trade surplus narrowed slightly from the year before, but economists said the rate of decline is slowing and the surplus may start to expand again in a few months. The Finance Ministry said that the May surplus, measured when goods pass through customs, dropped 16.6% to $3.13 billion from $3.76 billion a year earlier. In April, by contrast, the surplus fell a sharp 51.4% from the year before. Exports in May declined 0.9%, to $21.52 billion from $21.7 billion. Imports grew at a slower pace than recently posted, rising 2.4% to $18.38 billion from $17.95 billion.

The trade surplus with the U.S. also narrowed, falling 11.4% to $2.44 billion from $2.76 billion in the previous year. Exports to the U.S. dropped 4.5% to $6.82 billion, but imports also fell 0.2% to $4.38 billion.

Ministry officials said that although Japan's global surplus continued to shrink, "the amount of decline seems to have narrowed." One reason for the slowdown, the official explained, was falling oil prices. Higher crude-oil prices last year made an important contribution to import growth. In May, however, crude-oil prices dropped from the year before to $16.79 per barrel from $18.02 a year earlier. So although the volume of crude oil imports rose 3.1% to 16,200 kiloliters, imports in value terms dropped 4% to $1.71 billion.

Economists said the lower value of the yen against the dollar was also putting a brake on the pace of decline in the surplus. The ministry calculated that the dollar's average exchange rate in May was 156.98 yen compared with 133.88 yen a year ago. A weaker yen makes imports more expensive. At the same time, the exchange rate makes the price of exports more competitive in the world market.

Although the dollar-denominated value of exports dropped, the volume of exports actually grew 8.4%, said Kazuko Mizuno, an economist at Baring Securities (Japan) Ltd. Export growth was especially high for motorcycles, rising 32.2% to $150.67 million, and ships, increasing 30.9% to $319.1 million. Exports of office equipment, another area in which Japanese companies are very competitive, also rose 8.9% to $1.54 billion.

Import volume, on the other hand, grew at a slower pace of 6.5%, compared with the average pace of 7.8% in 1989. Although economists expect the surplus to continue narrowing for a few more months, "this can't go on forever," said Takayuki Tanaka, an economist for Japan's Long Term Credit Bank. "The drop in the growth of imports may lead to an expansion in the surplus," added Ms. Mizuno of Baring Securities, possibly starting in August or September.

That would bring additional pressure to bear on the Japanese government from the U.S. Recently, Japanese officials have sparked a debate by asserting that although Japan is committed to reducing its trade surplus further, retaining an overall external surplus that allows Japan to remain a capital exporter is essential to a healthy world economy.

On a seasonally adjusted basis, the overall trade surplus grew 6.9% in May from April to $3.41 billion from $3.19 billion. Adjusted exports grew 2.2%, to $21.48 billion from $21.03 billion, while adjusted imports also grew 1.3%, to $18.07 billion from $17.84 billion. Adjusted figures on a yen basis showed a wider increase, with the surplus in May rising 37.2% from April to 656 billion yen from 479 billion yen.

In trade with the U.S., Japan's exports dropped in a number of areas, in part because Japanese companies' American factories supplied more and more U.S. demand, said Mr. Tanaka of Long Term Credit Bank. In particular, exports of engines fell 32.2% to $193.87 million from the year before, while exports of semiconductors dropped 19.7% to $299.95 million.

The trade surplus with the European Community narrowed 26.4% to $1.2 billion. Exports increased 7.1% to $4.07 billion, while motorcycle exports rose 52.4% to $89.9 million. Meanwhile, imports from the EC rose 32.3% to $2.87 billion. The growth in imports was seen in cars, mainly from West Germany, which doubled to $535 million. Imports of alcoholic beverages rose 34.5% to $114.7 million, and Japan's purchases of art from the EC countries doubled, reaching $157.1 million in May.
