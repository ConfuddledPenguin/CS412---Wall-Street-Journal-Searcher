Foreign Exchange: Dollar Ends Mixed as Sterling Rises On Report of U.K. Timetable for EMS

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/13/90

NEW YORK -- Sterling rose following a report that the U.K. Treasury favors making Britain a full member of the European Monetary System as early as September or October. The dollar ended trading mixed. The report in the London Financial Times, which quoted senior figures involved in government discussions on the topic, also said the British government would strive to negotiate a relatively high EMS entry level for the pound. The Financial Times didn't specify a level.

A wide EMS trading range that would allow fluctuations of as much as 6% rather than the standard 2.25% is an additional goal of U.K. authorities, according to the report. Market predictions for the pound's entry level have ranged between 2.60 marks and 2.90 marks. The pound, which currently fluctuates independently of the EMS exchange rate structure, is now trading near the top of that range.

The dollar, meanwhile, was moderately higher against the mark and a bit lower against the yen in activity muted ahead of the release beginning today of a series of U.S. economic indicators.

The market seized on the EMS news as a good reason to buy the British currency, which already was favored for its comparatively high interest rate returns, market participants said. Traders have long maintained that the U.K. currency's resiliency in the face of poor economic fundamentals has stemmed from the attractiveness of relatively high British interest rates. Base U.K. interest rates are currently at 15%.

"The fact that someone is naming months" gives the market the impression that there is a planning process under way, whereas market enthusiasm for the pound's full entry into the EMS previously was restrained by confusion about timing, said Susan Babcock, vice president of corporate foreign exchange at Hongkong & Shanghai Banking Corp. in New York.

Entry is seen as positive for the pound based on the reasoning that a preset trading band for sterling would enforce discipline on British monetary authorities and obligate other EMS central banks to support the currency.

In the aftermath of the Financial Times report, U.K. Chancellor John Major reiterated that inflation remains an obstacle to full British EMS membership, but the market didn't pay much attention. Inflation currently is expected to reach an annual rate of 10% by fall.

Late in New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.6965 marks, up from 1.6930 marks late Monday. The U.S. currency also was quoted at at 154.55 yen, down from 154.77 yen. Sterling was trading at $1.7035, up from $1.6860 late Monday.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the dollar was trading at 154.80 yen soon after opening at 155.08 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6970 marks, up from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 1.6900 marks.

U.K. Chancellor Major said that reduction of inflation must precede full EMS membership for the pound, the same position he has taken in the past. Market participants said the comment, though discounted, did create hesitation to push the British currency over long-term resistance at $1.7075, a level it didn't approach in world trading yesterday.

In addition to rising against the dollar, the pound recorded strong gains against the mark and yen.

The dollar strengthened modestly against its West German counterpart on continued nervousness about the domestic German cost of unification. The potential impact of Soviet opposition to a united Germany's membership in the North Atlantic Treaty Organization also weighed on the mark, traders said.

Conversely, the yen stopped the dollar's recent advance. The Japanese currency was propped up by market sentiment that there is some room for the Bank of Japan to increase interest rates, said Charles Spence, a vice president of foreign exchange at First Interstate Bank of California in Los Angeles.

The Japanese currency is receiving additional support from a market consensus that the Japanese economy is performing well despite concern about rising inflation and the stability of financial markets there, Mr. Spence said.

"As long as the promise of higher rates is there, it keeps people from bashing a currency," he said.

Mr. Spence and Ms. Babcock of Hongkong & Shanghai noted that trading of the dollar was slowed by anticipation of a slew of U.S. economic indicators scheduled for release beginning today.

The report today on U.S. retail sales for May isn't likely to have a big impact on trading unless it's much different from expectations, market participants said. Retail sales for last month are estimated to be unchanged from April.

The market is much more interested in the release tomorrow of the U.S. producer price index for May and Friday's announcement of May's consumer price index. Those reports are being watched for their potential impact on U.S. interest rates following last month's weak jobs growth, traders said.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $355.70 an ounce, down 70 cents. Estimated volume was a light 2.5 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $354.50 an ounce.
