IBERIA-AMERICAN DISPUTE

Iberia, Spain's national airline, said it is withdrawing today from Sabre, the computerized reservation system of AMR Corp.'s American Airlines unit, despite possible sanctions from the U.S. Department of Transportation.

The move may result in a sharp drop in Iberia's U.S. sales if other U.S. companies ban Iberia from their computerized reservation systems, Iberia officials said. By pulling out of Sabre in Spain, Iberia automatically is barred from using the system in the U.S., forcing the company to seek alternative sales outlets there, the officials said.

The dispute broke out late last year after Iberia announced its decision to withdraw from Sabre to protest what it called the system's unfair trade practices in Spain. An Iberia official said Sabre "dumped" its services in Spain by offering its computers free of charge to all travel agencies meeting a minimum sales quota on its reservation system. Iberia's booking company, Savia, charges clients a yearly fee to rent a computer terminal.

Iberia officials said the carrier will take its case to the U.S. courts if it is excluded from other reservation systems.

ARRESTS IN CAMBODIA

The human-rights group Asia Watch expressed concern over the arrests in late May of at least six senior government officials and army officers in Phnom Penh, Cambodia. All are suspected of trying to form a new political party, Asia Watch said. "These men were apparently trying to take advantage of a changed international climate and move Cambodia toward a multiparty system," said an Asia Watch official. "It looks like they may have jumped the gun."

There were 3,000 registered nurses in Trinidad and Tobago in 1987; since then, some 1,200 have quit. Registered Nurses Association spokeswoman Yvonne Pilgrim said the flight of nurses, attributed to early retirement or better-paying jobs in North America, has caused a dramatic shortage of nurses in area hospitals. She made her remarks to explain a one-week protest by Trinidad nurses last week over working conditions and salaries.

FRANCE'S FAST TRAINS

France plans to spend 190 billion French francs ($33.3 billion) to expand its high-speed-train network 2,200 miles. Transportation Minister Michel Delebarre said the expansion would require construction of 400 new engines. The project requires approval by regional officials, Mr. Delebarre said. Winegrowers already have protested plans to lay tracks for the high-speed trains through prized vineyards in southern France.

FIGHTING PHILIPPINE POVERTY

Philippine President Corazon Aquino launched a movement to combat poverty and denied it was intended as a political force to fight for her re-election in 1992. In an Independence Day speech yesterday, Mrs. Aquino said the Kabisig ("Arm in Arm") movement was designed to speed up grass-roots projects aimed at improving living standards. She said the movement would be spearheaded by cabinet ministers, provincial governors and mayors and community organizations.

VENICE BOWS OUT

Italy gave in to a world-wide protest and withdrew Venice as a candidate to be the site of the Expo 2000 world fair. The decision was announced to parliament by Prime Minister Giulio Andreotti yesterday. Tomorrow, a Paris-based organization meets to select the host city for the world fair; the other two candidates are Hanover, West Germany, and Toronto. Venice lovers around the world had argued the city would collapse under the strain of accommodating hordes of extra tourists if the fair were staged there.

JAPAN'S SAGGING BIRTHRATE

Quoting unnamed cabinet sources, a Japanese news agency reported that Finance Minister Ryutaro Hashimoto suggested the trend for Japanese women to obtain higher education is responsible for the nation's sagging birthrate.

The agency said the suggestion was part of a cabinet discussion yesterday about ways to encourage women to bear more children. Government officials are reportedly alarmed at statistics showing that Japan's birthrate hit an all-time low of 1.57 children per woman in 1989, down from 1.66 babies per woman in 1988.

Mr. Hashimoto was quoted as suggesting that the government alter its policy of enabling all students who so desire to pursue higher education so that Japan won't suffer shortages in the labor force.

At a news conference, Chief Cabinet Secretary Misoji Sakamoto said the government is hoping to reverse the sharp birthrate decline because otherwise the welfare burden for Japan's aging society will be difficult to manage.
