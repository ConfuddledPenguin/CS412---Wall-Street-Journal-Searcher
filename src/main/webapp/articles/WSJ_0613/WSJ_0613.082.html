U.S.-Japan -- Tension Between Partners --- Strained Alliance: U.S., Japan Struggle To Redefine Relations As Resentment Grows --- Americans Fear Dominance Is Slipping, While Japan Fights to Avoid Isolation --- Six Views of Emerging Gulf

  • Author: Alan Murray and Urban C. Lehner
  • Date: 06/13/90

{First of a Series}

The U.S. 7th Fleet is chasing a renegade Japanese nuclear submarine through the Straits of Moluccas in the Western Pacific when suddenly the high-tech sub decides to show the Americans that bigger is not always better. In maneuvers of unsurpassed skill, it singlehandedly cripples six U.S. submarines. Captain Umieda, its commander, declares as his goal "a war for independence" from U.S. domination.

"Japan hasn't changed a bit since Pearl Harbor," snarls the commander of the defeated American fleet.

Sound far-fetched? It's the plot of a comic book called "Silent Fleet," read today by a quarter-million young Japanese. The story is pure fantasy, but it is rooted in sentiments spreading rapidly through the popular culture of both countries.

The underlying message: Something is seriously askew in U.S.-Japan relations.

The two nations, of course, remain fast allies, and with each passing year their interests become more closely aligned and their economies more thoroughly intertwined. But with each year mutual resentment grows as well.

Trade disputes are the most visible sign of the problem, though those disputes are mere surface ripples. Strong undercurrents of fear and distrust are flowing through the national psyches. In the U.S., people worry that Japan's rising economic power is threatening America's future prosperity and its place in the world. Across the ocean, the Japanese are torn between pride in their new affluence and fear that foreign resentment could leave them isolated in an unfriendly world.

"There seems to be a race," says James C. Abegglen, a longtime business consultant in Japan, "between economic interdependence and political and social antagonism."

In the past year, that race has become even more intense. Trade talks between the two nations have evolved into an extraordinary series of negotiations in which each side is criticizing the other's internal economic affairs. At the same time, the political glue that has bound the two allies -- fear of the Soviet Union -- has been weakened by communism's new travails. In both countries, some people are now questioning the very basis of the alliance.

Is Japan really a threat to the United States? Does its rapid rise in economic power mean America is on the decline? And do the Japanese have reason to worry that a resentful U.S. will turn against them?

These are questions The Wall Street Journal has been exploring since the first of this year, in interviews with hundreds of people ranging from top business and government leaders on both sides of the Pacific to ranchers in Nebraska and Judo students in Kyoto. The result is a series of articles, beginning today.

The anxiety in America is strikingly demonstrated in a new Wall Street Journal/NBC poll, which shows that while 63% of American voters say it is "very important" for the U.S. to be the leading economic power in the world, 69% believe Japan has already passed the U.S. Fully 63% say the Japanese have leaped ahead in science and technology, and 69% feel Japan invests "too much" in the United States.

Meanwhile, in a separate poll conducted for the Journal in Japan, 43% of Japanese say it's time for their government to develop a foreign policy more independent of the U.S. Only 17% oppose such a move.

In the years since World War II, Americans have grown accustomed to the idea of being at the top, and they don't want to give it up. Virtually all Americans "feel they have to be the leader of the world," contends Mitsuko Shimomura, a Japanese journalist who recently did an extensive tour of the U.S. "They are like missionaries. They really believe there is nothing better than America in this world."

The Bush administration's budget director, Richard Darman, agrees. "If Americans aren't No. 1," he says, "forget it."

In the end, relations between great nations come down to relations between human beings. For an understanding of where U.S.-Japan relations have come from and where they may be headed, here are six conversations with people at the forefront of dealings between the two countries.

Mitsuya Goto: The Vanquished

It was May of 1945, and young Mitsuya Goto sat wide-eyed on the roof of his ancestral home in Nagoya as more than 500 B-29 bombers roared overhead. Most of the city's inhabitants had scrambled into air-raid shelters, but the young Goto sat entranced by the awesome sight.

"I wasn't afraid," he recalls. "I could see the faces of the pilots, and I was very impressed. I felt the strength and the power of the U.S., and the industrial might of a country that could deploy so many B-29 bombers. I wasn't mad at the U.S. I was mad at the Japanese government. I said aloud: `My God, what have we done?'"

In the years that followed, Mr. Goto came to embody the postwar attitudes of many Japanese: He was awed by the superior power of the U.S. and, despite the war's destruction, embraced his nation's former enemy.

The young Goto worked on his family's farm in Nagoya, but he rushed home each evening to listen to Armed Forces Radio, eagerly mimicking the words of the GI announcers. When his English was good enough, he applied for a job as a translator with Gen. Douglas MacArthur's occupation forces. And when the mother of an American friend offered him a chance to study in the U.S. he jumped at it, earning a degree from Wabash College in Crawfordsville, Ind. His commencement speech focused on the image of a frog in a well. "The frog in the well," he explains, "doesn't know the existence of great oceans."

The quiet, slender man crossed the great oceans many times over the next forty years, first as a delegate to the International Labor Organization and then as a special assistant to the chairman of Nissan Motors. He became one of Japan's rare breed of "internationalists," working to solidify friendships with his country's conquerors. His generation, he explains, "is still fascinated by Americans. We remember how generous Americans have been."

But in recent years, as managing director of the Japan Center for International Exchange, Mr. Goto has watched attitudes between the two countries take a disturbing turn. Especially worrisome, he says, is the younger generation of Japanese, which didn't see the sky-darkening hordes of bombers overhead. "Some Japanese have become a bit arrogant," he says. "I don't like it. The Japanese need to understand the kind of problems that America has that Japan doesn't."

There is little alternative, he believes. "We have so much at stake. We have to remain partners."

If Mitsuya Goto is the face Japan showed to the U.S. after World War II, then Edwin Reischauer is the face the U.S. showed to Japan.

Born and reared in Japan and married to a Japanese woman, Mr. Reischauer has lived a life between two cultures. After starting a Japanese-studies program at Harvard University, he was named ambassador to Japan by President Kennedy and served five years in the post. Today, sitting in his retirement home in San Diego next to a picture of Mount Fuji, he can recite chapter and verse the major U.S.-Japan scuffles of the postwar years.

"The first" he recalls, "was the one surrounding the security treaty of 1960." A residue of harsh feelings from the war led many Japanese to oppose their country's decision to form a close military alliance with the U.S. In 1964, one dissident even attacked Ambassador Reischauer, driving a knife into his leg. But the security treaty, which was designed to counter the spread of communism, became a firm bond that held the two countries together for three decades.

Today, Mr. Reischauer argues, the decline of the Soviet empire is one reason U.S.-Japan relations have become strained: The common threat is gone. "You unfortunately find some people have to have an enemy," he says. "We lost the Soviet Union, so people got excited about the economic competition" with Japan. For the first time, he says, relations between the two countries are "getting close to what you might call a crisis."

Mr. Reischauer's bicultural background gives him an unusual understanding of the problems Japanese have in dealing with the West. There is, for example, Japan's attention to hierarchy, which leads Japanese to think of themselves as either subservient or superior to others. "The Japanese jump immediately from Pax Americana to Pax Nipponica," he says.

But he puts most of the blame on the U.S. "We've allowed our industrial skills not to improve as much as we should," he says. "We are in danger of becoming a second, a third world country.

"Cleaning up our own problems," he adds, "would be the quickest way to foster good relations with Japan."

Wataru Hiraizumi: Newly Confident

"When you look back at what the 20th century was all about," says Wataru Hiraizumi, slowly sucking on a piece of Swiss candy, "the big problem was not the cold war, but the question of Germany, Japan and the U.S." And the biggest problem of the three, he makes clear, is the U.S. "People don't realize how dangerous the U.S. can be."

Born in 1929, Mr. Hiraizumi is the son of Kiyoshi Hiraizumi, a professor of Japanese history who, before the war, was a passionate nationalist and a believer in the superiority of Japanese culture. The younger man shared his father's intellectual pursuits. Now a member of the National Diet and the ruling Liberal Democratic Party, he sits in the midst of a huge library with titles in Japanese, French, English and German.

As he speaks, Mr. Hiraizumi pulls an occasional book off the shelf, such as a 1937 work called "Forty Years of American-Japanese Relations," which documents "the decline of American influence and prestige in the Far East before the relentless growth of Japanese political and economic power."

Most political leaders in Japan try to avoid offending foreign visitors, but Mr. Hiraizumi enjoys being provocative. "The U.S. is very difficult to deal with," he explains. "It is an artificial creation. It was created by people who left their country to create the best nation in the world. You consider yourselves superior."

After a pause, he grins and adds: "You have to consider yourself the best. Otherwise, why don't you go back to Italy?

"The original sin of your culture is your cult of individuality. It means you cherish `A' people. But the important thing is how to organize and discipline and educate `B,' `C,' `D,' `E' and `F' people. You value A people; we value B, C, D, E and F people. Who wins?" he asks, and with a smile suggests the answer.

Nevertheless, Mr. Hiraizumi believes Japan should be careful about flexing its muscle and assuming the mantle of world leadership. "Americans can become very wicked. You should always try not to anger them," he says.

Robert Mosbacher: Trade Warrior

A needlepoint pillow that used to sit on the sofa in Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher's office read: "Happiness is Being an American." Lately, he has replaced it with one slightly less offensive to his foreign guests: "Free Trade Avenue is a Two-Way Street."

Mr. Mosbacher is an American -- no apologies. He is also the Bush administration's leading trade hawk. It is Mr. Mosbacher who argued in April, unsuccessfully, that the U.S. should once again cite Japan for unfair actions under the Trade Reform Act. And it is Mr. Mosbacher who has tried, with only limited success, to get Washington to provide more aid to industries fighting Japanese competition.

"I think the American people are just about filled to the top with this feeling they've been had," he says.

When he talks about relations with Japan, the commerce secretary uses the term "economic security" as if the nation were under siege. He happily admits to his desire to see America remain No. 1. "You could be No. 2 or 3 and lose control over your economic and possibly military security," he says. A picture on one wall shows him standing between two Sumo wrestlers. Nearby hangs a plaque with a quote it attributes to Vince Lombardi, the legendary football coach: "Winning is not a sometime thing, it's an all-time thing."

On at least one occasion the White House has taken him to task for his hardline views. In response he has learned to toe the adminstration's line, and even express support for Secretary of State James Baker's proposal for a "global partnership" with Japan. But he repeatedly returns to the idea that Japan is unfair. "You can't be allies," he says, "with somebody you think is getting to you."

Shunji Taoka: Taking Offense

Mr. Taoka, gaunt-faced writer and editor for the popular Japanese magazine Aera, sits in a coffee shop in Tokyo and spins out the following scenario:

"It is the year 2000. Total American overseas debt is more than $2 trillion. Annual interest payments are $160 billion, far exceeding today's trade deficit. The U.S. faces two solutions. It can default on its debt. Or it can print more dollars and create inflation.

"America decides to default. In response, Japan and other countries refuse to lend any more to the U.S. So, in the American tradition, your nation loads up its guns, jumps on its horse, and heads for the bank." Translation: The U.S. invades Japan.

Mr. Taoka is quick to say he doesn't believe the likelihood of such an invasion is high. But he clearly places it in the realm of possibility.

"This year is the 30th year of the U.S.-Japan security treaty," he explains. "There have been three radical developments in this 30th year." The first is the change in U.S.-Soviet relations. The second is America's soaring federal debt and the resulting pressure for defense cuts. And the third is "rising anti-Japan feeling as a result of Japanese economic power."

Together, he argues, these three changes have cut the foundation out from under the U.S.-Japan security alliance.

On the coffee shop table, Mr. Taoka lays a copy of the latest issue of his magazine. The cover story quotes Major General Henry C. Stackpole, a Marine divisional commander in Okinawa, as saying that if the U.S. cuts back its forces in the Pacific, Japan will beef up "what is already a very, very potent military." The U.S. forces, he said, "are a cap on the bottle."

Mr. Taoka finds the "cap on the bottle" image -- and other recent suggestions that Japanese power needs to be "contained" -- insulting. "It's like giving a room of your house to a friend who asks for food, and then money, and then finally goes to your neighbors and says, `My landlord is a strange person. I'd better stick around and keep an eye on him.'"

Yet for all his resentment of American attitudes, Mr. Taoka says he, too, is not yet ready for Japan to chart a course that is independent of the U.S. "It's not necessary for Japan to become a superpower," he says. "What is the benefit? Somebody who is satisfied as head of the Chamber of Commerce doesn't need to become mayor."

Harry Lonsdale: No Colonization

"My hot button is trees," says Harry Lonsdale, eating salmon in a 30th-floor restaurant overlooking the city of Portland. "I think we have to preserve our forests." His proposal for doing that: Stop exporting logs to Japan.

Flying over Oregon, it's easy to understand why Mr. Lonsdale feels so strongly. The mountainous state looks like a patchwork quilt from the air, scarred by rectangular and trapezoidal bare spots -- the work of loggers. Roughly a quarter of this timber is exported, mostly to Japan.

Mr. Lonsdale is a candidate for the U.S. Senate, challenging incumbent Mark Hatfield. As an environmentalist, he has opposed cutting trees. But as a politician, he has had to consider as well the interests of the state's lumber industry.

At a time when the U.S. trade deficit with Japan is still running at $50 billion a year, stopping any U.S. export to Japan may seem foolhardy. But Mr. Lonsdale has caught on to a fundamental truth: It's not just whether the U.S. exports that matters, but what it exports. "Exporting raw materials," he says, "is a third-world task." Or worse, the task of a colony.

If he could force the Japanese to buy finished wood and lumber products rather than logs, Mr. Lonsdale figures he could create more jobs and destroy fewer trees. "In 1980, Indonesia was shipping raw logs to Japan, and in addition providing only 1% of Japan's plywood," he notes. "Now they prohibit raw log exports, and they're shipping 18% of Japan's plywood."

Mr. Lonsdale's fears about losing pre-eminence to Japan recall a prediction made by the late Shigeru Yoshida, who was Japan's prime minister from 1946 to 1954 and who signed the security treaty giving the U.S. rights to bases in Japan. At the time, critics said Mr. Yoshida was going to make Japan a colony of the U.S. But he took a longer-term view.

"Just as the United States was once a colony of Great Britain but is now the stronger of the two," he said, "if Japan becomes a colony of the United States, it will also eventually become the stronger."
