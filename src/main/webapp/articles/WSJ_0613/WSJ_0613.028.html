Economy: U.S. Trade Gap Eased in Quarter To $22.9 Billion --- Smallest Deficit in 6 Years Is Due to Strong Exports Of Goods and Services

  • Author: Alan Murray
  • Date: 06/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Strong exports of goods and services brought the U.S. trade deficit in the first quarter to its lowest level in six years, according to new Commerce Department figures. The current-account trade deficit, also called the balance of payments, was $22.9 billion in the first quarter, down from a revised $26.7 billion in the fourth quarter of last year. Analysts say strong growth overseas is boosting exports, while slow growth in the U.S. is damping imports.

"It's a great improvement, much more than we expected," said Lawrence Krohn, a senior economist at Shearson Lehman. "The trade picture this year is going to be a bright one."

The current account is the broadest measure of U.S. trade, including goods, services and earnings on investments. In the first quarter, the U.S. had a deficit in merchandise trade of $26.4 billion. But that was partly offset by a surplus in services of $6.3 billion, which was boosted by travel in the U.S. by Japanese and other foreigners.

The balance on investment income in the first quarter showed a slim surplus of $600 million. That means U.S. companies are still earning slightly more from their investments overseas than foreign companies are earning in the U.S. But the rush of foreign investment into the U.S. in recent years leads many economists to believe that number will turn negative in the future.

The U.S. also transferred a net $3.5 billion overseas in grants and other unilateral payments.

The relatively good news in the first quarter suggests the current-account deficit for the year will be under $100 billion for the first time since the early 1980s. But Nigel Gault, senior economist for Data Resources Inc., Lexington, Mass., says he doesn't expect much further improvement in the near future. "I would expect us to average just under $100 billion for the next three years," he said.

Merchandise exports increased to $96 billion in the first quarter, a big rise from $91.7 billion in the fourth. Imports also increased, but by a much smaller ammount, to $122.4 billion from $120.5 billion.

Receipts from the sale of services to foreigners were $31.6 billion in the first quarter, up from $30.2 billion in the fourth. Payments to foreigners for services also rose, to $25.3 billion from $24.1 billion.

The income earned by U.S. investments abroad was $31.1 billion in the first quarter, down from $32.6 billion in the fourth. But U.S. payments to foreign investors also fell, to $30.4 billion from $32.1 billion in the previous quarter.

The current-account deficit for all of 1989 was $110.03 billion.
