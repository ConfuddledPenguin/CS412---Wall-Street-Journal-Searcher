Heard on the Street: Analysts Lower Masco 1990 Profit Estimates, Management Backs Off Forecast of New High

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 06/13/90

NEW YORK -- The noise around Masco Corp. these days isn't just the grinding, hammering and sawing that goes with the making of faucets, furniture and cabinets. It is also the unwelcome thud of analysts dropping their earnings estimates.

Citing weakness in the housing industry, at least four major Wall Street securities firms have lowered their 1990 earnings estimates or investment opinions on Masco in recent days. The estimates currently range from $1.83 a share to $2 a share.

Both ends of that range are below the target of $2.04 a share that Masco's management set in a meeting with analysts Feb. 28. Masco makes building and home-improvement products, home furnishings and other specialty consumer products.

The outlook is such that Masco's management now is backing away from its prediction that 1990 per-share earnings would set a new record, exceeding the $2.03 the company posted for 1988. In 1989, earnings fell for the first time in 32 years to $1.42 a share.

John C. Nicholls Jr., Masco treasurer, said in an interview yesterday: "Unless our markets reflect a significant improvement, I would agree that we won't make the record." He added that second-quarter earnings will decline from the year earlier's 50 cents a share.

"April was indeed a very poor month," said Mr. Nicholls, who said he plans to review May's results on Friday.

Mr. Nicholls also said the projection of record earnings, made by Masco Chairman Richard A. Manoogian, came with a caveat that the record could be achieved if "macroeconomics did not worsen." Adds Mr. Nicholls: "Things have materially changed since February."

Housing analysts would agree. Barbara Allen, a Kidder Peabody analyst who yesterday lowered her 1990 earnings estimate for Masco to $1.90 a share from $2.15, said the action resulted from her lower estimate for national housing starts. Ms. Allen is now projecting 1,225,000 housing starts this year, down from her earlier projection of 1,375,000, which would have been a slight increase from last year's level of 1,370,000.

Ms. Allen expects a continuing slowdown in Masco's sales to builders, which she says represent about 35% of the company's revenue. "I think in the second half of the year there will be very limited amounts of new construction starting," she says, adding: "You can't build houses without construction funds, and construction funds are very difficult to come by now."

Even after reducing her estimate, however, Ms. Allen remains positive on Masco's long-term outlook. And analysts who follow Masco for other Wall Street firms -- including Goldman Sachs, Merrill Lynch, Shearson Lehman Brothers, and Smith Barney -- believe that Masco will outperform other stocks over the long term, as the company digests its recent furniture acquisitions and as housing recovers.

But Masco also is beginning to attract the attention of aggressive short sellers, who are betting that Masco made an expensive mistake when it spent about $1.7 billion to buy four furniture companies from 1986 through 1989. The short sellers are selling borrowed shares in the hopes of replacing them with shares bought later at a lower price. They doubt that Masco will be able to improve the efficiency of furniture-making the way it cranked up faucet manufacturing and marketing.

Masco, which invented the single-handle Delta brand faucet, has spent 30 years transforming the lowly faucet into a high-profit item. In 1984, it spun off 51% of its original auto and industrial parts-making business as Masco Industries, to focus on consumer goods. It moved quickly into kitchen and bathroom cabinets, then tackled furniture, initially by acquiring Henredon.

"The assimilation process has not gone ahead as quickly as they {Masco} had hoped," says Gregory Nejmeh of Shearson Lehman. "As to whether or not the strategy itself is faulty, I don't think we're going to know for another five years." Stephen Dobi of Smith Barney is more optimistic. He expects 1991 profit margins to expand as Masco restructures internally. "There's real value in this stock," he says. "The risk is that the stock is dead money for six months or so."

Masco also has a $250 million investment in a leveraged buy-out, mostly in a temporary "bridge" loan that turned long-term when it couldn't be repaid by selling junk bonds. The LBO is Payless Cashways, a chain of home-improvement material stores.

Masco Capital, of which Masco effectively owns 75%, bought $41 million of junior convertible preferred stock and $168 million of zero-coupon junk bonds from Payless, which, in turn, agreed to buy more of Masco's products. Though Payless seems to be doing well, another issue of its publicly traded junk bonds is trading at about 75 cents on the dollar.

Masco hasn't written down the Payless investment and doesn't plan to. Shearson's Mr. Nejmeh says a write-off, if needed, would cost Masco $121.9 million after taxes, or about 78 cents a share.
