International: Dutch Anti-Takeover Ploy Based on Stock Is Upheld

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/13/90

ZAANDAM, The Netherlands -- In a case that was viewed as a challenge to the defensive tactics of Dutch companies, the District Court in Haarlem has upheld Dutch food retailer Koninklijke Ahold N.V. in its battle against being taken over by West German retailer Asko Deutsche Kaufhaus AG. At issue was whether Ahold had the right to issue preferred shares to a friendly foundation with voting rights equal to those carried by Ahold's common shares. Ahold issued the preferred shares in August 1989, after it learned that Asko had surreptitiously accumulated a 13.5% stake in Ahold without informing the Dutch company.

Under Dutch law, such an issue of preferred shares is allowed and is routinely implemented as an anti-takeover defense. In Ahold's situation, the foundation, Stichting Ahold Continuiteit, was set up in 1973 as a contingency in case the company was threatened.

In a broader perspective, however, the case was viewed as a challenge to the formidable Dutch takeover defenses that corporations routinely implement.

An Ahold spokesman said Asko could appeal the decision to the Court of Appeals in Amsterdam but added that Ahold had no idea whether they would or not.

Officials at Asko were unavailable for comment.
