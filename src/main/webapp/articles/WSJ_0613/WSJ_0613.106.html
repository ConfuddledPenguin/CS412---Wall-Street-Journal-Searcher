GPU Excludes Two Provisions Of Takeover Law

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 06/13/90

PARSIPPANY, N.J. -- General Public Utilities Corp. amended its bylaws to exclude two provisions of Pennsylvania's controversial new anti-takeover statute. Widely regarded as the U.S.'s toughest anti-takeover statute, the law was enacted in April and gave corporations that are incorporated in Pennsylvania 90 days from the time of its enactment to opt out of certain provisions. GPU is incorporated in Pennsylvania, with headquarters here.

GPU's board chose to exclude the so-called disgorgement provision that would force short-term holders who sell their shares within 18 months after a failed attempt to "control" a company to turn over the profits on the stock sales to the target company.

GPU also deleted the "control-shares provision" that withholds the voting rights of holders who control 20% or more of a company's stock until "disinterested" holders agree to restore their votes.

The company took no action on the statute's provision regarding the fiduciary duty of directors. The law allows directors to base decisions not only on shareholders' interests, but also on the interests of employees, customers, suppliers and other "stakeholders." The provision also insulates directors from most lawsuits by aggrieved stakeholders and guarantees labor contracts and severance pay in the event of a takeover.

Standley H. Hoch, GPU chairman, said that "it was inappropriate to deprive shareholders of their voting rights and profits under the circumstances provided in the Pennsylvania statute." As for the decision to keep the fiduciary provision in the statute, Mr. Hoch said that "a public utility's statutory obligation to provide adequate and safe electric service to its customers has long been held to be consistent with the long-term interests of its shareholders."

Pennsylvania enacted the law at the behest of Armstrong World Industries Inc. The Lancaster, Pa., company had been the target of a possible bid by Canada's Belzberg family, which also initiated a proxy fight against the company. At Armstrong's annual meeting, only a few days after the law was passed, the Belzbergs managed to win only one seat of the four up for election on the 13-member board and were later forced to sell the family's 11.7% Armstrong stake at a loss.

At least two lawsuits challenging the law's constitutionality have been filed since Gov. Robert P. Casey signed the bill.
