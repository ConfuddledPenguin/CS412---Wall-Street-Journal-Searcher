Senate Panel Aide Advises Rebuke Of Durenberger

  • Author: Jill Abramson
  • Date: 06/13/90

WASHINGTON -- A special counsel to the Senate ethics committee recommended that Sen. David Durenberger (R., Minn.) be denounced by the Senate for "knowingly engaging in unethical conduct which has brought dishonor and disrepute to this institution." The recommendation, which came in the opening day of trial-like ethics committee proceedings, means that Mr. Durenberger, 55 years old, almost certainly has escaped the stiffest sanction the panel could recommend -- expulsion from the Senate. Denunciation is the equivalent of a censure, the next most serious punishment senators can level on a colleague.

"It's a major rebuke and carries the same weight as a censure," explained Donald Ritchie, the Senate's associate historian. "In a collegial body, it's a severe blow."

In a news release, Mr. Durenberger said, "I feel the sanction of denouncement, as special counsel recommends, is harsher than warranted." He added that while he has admitted some mistakes, "all of my actions were taken in good faith in an attempt to comply with all laws and rules." Mr. Durenberger also expressed disappointment that there wasn't time for him to give his opening statement on the first day of his Senate hearing.

After hearing all the evidence in the case, the bipartisan, six-member ethics committee must decide whether to approve the recommendation of the special counsel, Robert Bennett. It then would take a majority of the Senate to approve a formal denunciation of Mr. Durenberger. The ethics committee also can recommend to the GOP Senate conference that Mr. Durenberger be stripped of his seniority and committee assignments.

In 1979, Sen. Herman Talmadge (D., Ga.) was denounced by the Senate for submitting false expense vouchers and failing to report campaign contributions. Mr. Talmadge lost his re-election bid in 1980. Mr. Durenberger, who has served in the Senate for 12 years, doesn't face re-election until 1994.

At yesterday's hearing, Mr. Bennett gave a scalding summary of the evidence against Mr. Durenberger. The central charges involve a book promotion deal for which Sen. Durenberger received $100,000 in an alleged scheme to circumvent Senate limits on honoraria, and a condominium partnership through which Mr. Durenberger may have improperly received Senate reimbursement for lodging expenses. In both matters, Mr. Bennett said the senator engaged in a "pattern of unethical conduct."
