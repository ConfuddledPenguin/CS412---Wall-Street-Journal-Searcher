Economy: Bankruptcy-Law Filings by Firms Spurt --- Soft Economy and Heavy Debt Burdens Are Cited

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter and Thomas F. O'Boyle
  • Date: 04/06/90

The soft economy and heavy debt burdens are triggering a spurt in bankruptcy-law filings by U.S. businesses. Many of the companies now seeking court protection from creditors aren't household names. Rather, they are typically small-to-medium-sized concerns, mainly in the real estate, apparel, retailing and construction industries. And though the filings haven't grabbed headlines like the celebrated busts earlier this year of Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. and Campeau Corp.'s U.S. retailing units, they are proving a boon to bankruptcy attorneys and a bane to creditors.

Business filings nationwide jumped 34% from the third to the fourth quarter last year, the latest period available, according to the Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts in Washington.

And in just the past week, at least four publicly held companies have sought bankruptcy-court protection after failing in last-ditch efforts to obtain new financing. They range from Amdura Corp., a Denver construction equipment concern, to Casual Male Corp., a Shrewsbury, Mass., men's clothing retailer.

"Bankruptcy filings are up significantly," says Harvey R. Miller, co-managing partner of Weil, Gotshal & Manges, a New York law firm with a large bankruptcy practice. In his area, real estate, retailing and manufacturing concerns are having trouble, as are highly leveraged companies in general. "It's a result of the generally weak state of the economy," he says, combined with a drying up of some sources of financing.

Moreover, the situation appears likely to worsen before it improves. More companies are past due in bill payments, which is a good barometer of financial health. The next step for these slow-paying companies may be bankruptcy court.

Nationwide, companies in March paid their bills an average nine days past due, up from seven days in December, according to data compiled by TRW Inc.'s business credit division. "That's a significant change," says Robert Wheeler, marketing vice president for the division. "It's a strong signal that companies are in a cash bind."

In certain sectors, the cash bind has become acute. For instance, the past-due indicator jumped in the same period to 17 days from nine for apparel stores and to 14 from eight for general retailers, Mr. Wheeler says. In building and construction, it increased to 14 days from 12.

Most bankruptcy specialists link the increase in filings to greater uneasiness among bankers. Though the soft economy clearly has weakened cash flow for many businesses, the credit environment has also become more cautious. Savings and loan associations, many of them in trouble, aren't able to come to the rescue of debt-burdened real estate concerns. And the junk-bond market, where a lot of less-credit-worthy companies previously found financing, has all but disappeared.

As a result, banks have become the lenders of last resort -- and increasingly they are skittish. "We ran out of cash," says Melvin Jaffee, president and chief executive officer of National Lumber & Supply Inc. The Fountain Valley, Calif., operator of building-supply stores filed under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code after the company's lenders refused to provide it with more cash. Under Chapter 11, National Lumber continues to operate while seeking an arrangement with creditors.

Even though outstanding loans were secured with liens against inventories and other assets, "our lender wouldn't give us any more," Mr. Jaffee says. "I think they were scared by what they saw happening to other retailers."

For their part, commercial loan officers say they are more cautious partly because bank examiners are scrutinizing their loans very closely. Adding to bank uneasiness is a new Federal Reserve guideline, adopted Feb. 6, that sets a uniform definition for highly leveraged transactions and requires banks to report them.

"The world has changed so dramatically in just a few months time. There's been an incredible tightening up of bank credit," says Richard M. Cieri, a partner at Jones, Day, Reavis & Pogue in Cleveland, the nation's second-largest law firm. Mr. Cieri, who co-manages the firm's bankruptcy practice, cites tight lending as the major reason for a significant increase in Jones Day's bankruptcy caseload in recent months.

The tight credit situation "becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy," adds A. Gary Shilling, a New York economic consultant. "As lenders get more worried, they stop lending to the people who most need to borrow, forcing what they most fear, which is bankruptcy."

Mr. Shilling expects the problem to get worse before it gets better. "The economy is pretty much dead in the water," he says, making it unlikely that troubled companies will be relieved by an improvement in earnings. "And once lenders get scared, they tend to stay scared for awhile. We're advising clients to be very, very cautious, because we think the economy is very close to a recession, which will be global in scope." Such a scenario, he adds, would drive a large number of highly leveraged businesses, from New York to Tokyo, into a financial crisis.

To be sure, bankruptcies in 1989 and so far this year don't appear to be as high as they were in the mid-1980s, when the rust, farm and energy belts suffered a near depression. Nevertheless, after falling back, filings are rising again -- and higher debt levels are a big contributor.

The soft economy has eaten into income. As a result, interest expense is rising far faster than the cash flow from which it must be paid, says Mr. Shilling. In the 1989 fourth quarter, he notes, net interest cost equaled 27.5% of corporate cash flow plus interest costs, up from 18.8% in the first quarter of 1986, when the buy-out trend was just getting under way.

Many of the companies bought during that period were financed with high-risk, high-yield securities, commonly known as junk bonds. And that heavy burden of debt has set the stage for some big filings in recent months. In fact, Beard Group Inc., a Washington, D.C., research firm that keeps tabs on bankruptcies, says 10 of the 27 largest U.S. bankruptcies in history occurred in the past 15 months.

The debt bomb permeates the economy. "I can explain {the increase in bankruptcies} with three letters: LBO," or leveraged buy-out, says Herman Glatt, a partner with the Los Angeles law firm of Stutman, Treister & Glatt. "A lot of companies moved too heavily into the debt market, and they can't refinance the debt."

Nor is that debt burden likely to lessen anytime soon. A record $15 billion of high-interest junk bonds are likely to go into default this year, says Richard Lehmann, editor of Defaulted Bond Newsletter, a Miami Lakes, Fla. trade publication. That would be a 28% increase from $11.7 billion last year and more than three times the $4.97 billion of 1988, he says. Bloated by the $3.5 billion default last January by the Federated and Allied department-store units of Campeau, first-quarter defaults totaled $5.7 billion.
