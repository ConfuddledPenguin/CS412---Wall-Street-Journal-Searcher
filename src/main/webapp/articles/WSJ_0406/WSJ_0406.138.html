Beyond Half-Measures

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/06/90

Excerpts from the Blue Ribbon Commission's report on Hungary's transformation to a free market:

Since 1968's New Economic Mechanism, which decentralized (some) decision-making, Hungary has seen repeated half-measures to reform the exhausted command economy. Politics did not permit full transformation. But an economy only partially free, like a people only partially free in their political life, ultimately is demoralized when false hopes are dashed by experience.

Any large program will take years to put into place even at maximum speed. The keys are a preannounced path and a firm timetable. . . . Certain benefits should be apparent within months -- for example, improved quality and availability of consumer goods and services, cuts in waste and inefficiency, and better employment opportunities for skilled, diligent employees.

Practically all retail trade, service establishments, workshops and similar sized firms should be privatized within one year. . . . Parent companies should be forced to sell their outlets and shops to private buyers who will obtain real assets in exchange for cash and real payment obligations.

A new Act on Agricultural Land (should) guarantee the right of private ownership and the right to dispose of land. . . . Land transferred earlier to the cooperatives, or similar land, should be offered for repurchase to the original owners.

Convert present import restrictions into temporary tariffs and concurrently announce a firm timetable for the reduction of such tariffs.

The current proportion of foreign ownership in many small West European countries is about one-third of the country's industry. At the beginning of 1990 foreigners own only about 2% of Hungary's industry, so there is ample room for expansion, by any reasonable standard.

Hungary's (past loans) were not used for sound investments that could generate foreign exchange earnings to service the debt. Hungary (now) has to obtain additional capital imports of about $1.5 billion a year for each of the next three years in order to turn a potentially large net outflow into a net inflow. . . . In large part, this current-account gap can be financed though an aggressive policy of foreign investment.

The central government should retain broad planning responsibility for the railroad system and for the national networks; e.g., main roads, oil and gas pipelines, electricity grids, and the telephone system. But it should look for innovative ways of cooperating with the private sector to finance, build, and operate (them).

The entire sector of state-owned apartments should become globally self-financing in three years. Rent should be adjusted to reflect real conditions, based on location, size, and quality. (Any) subsidies should be given (upfront) to people because they are poor and not because they occupy particular dwellings. . . . Free-market rents should be allowed to emerge for state-owned apartments of the best quality. . . . The current process of selling off state-owned flats should continue, though prices should be set closer to real market values. . . . The government should not enter into any more mortgage contracts which involve interest subsidies.

Initiate steps to seek associate membership in the European Community.
