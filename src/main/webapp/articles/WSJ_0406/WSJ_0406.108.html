Big Beverage Firms Are Expected to Post Mixed Earnings for the First Quarter

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 04/06/90

The big beverage companies will report mixed first-quarter earnings, as the nation's thirst for low-calorie drinks offsets some worries over the impact of price fluctuations. Coca-Cola Co. and PepsiCo Inc. are expected to report earnings gains of at least 13%, analysts estimate. Anheuser-Busch Cos. is seen posting an increase of at least 3.5%. Adolph Coors Co. may report break-even results or post a profit, compared with a year-earlier loss. Meanwhile, bottler Coca-Cola Enterprises Inc., keeping its dismal earnings record intact, will probably post another quarterly decline.

Analysts are looking for Coca-Cola, which derives the bulk of its earnings from overseas, to show a 6% rise in concentrate shipments abroad, while retail sales climb a strong 10%. In the key Western European market, Coke is benefiting from its increased involvement in bottling operations. Volumes will probably be strong in England, for example, where Coke has a joint-venture bottling business, and in France, where Coke last year acquired virtually all the distribution business, says George Thompson, an analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. Japan, one of Coke's biggest markets, should show a solid volume gain at the retail level.

Closer to home, Coke's domestic volume is expected to increase a slim 3% or so, reflecting continued soft sales among fast-food restaurants, one of the biggest clients for fountain soft drinks. Amid a lack of any really jazzy consumer promotions anywhere in the business, Pepsi's volume should be up only about 3% as well.

Both companies snapped a several-year discounting spree late last year and have taken some lumps in recent quarters as consumers felt some sticker shock. In the first quarter, though, "moderate price increases didn't cause any unusual resistance," said Martin Romm, a First Boston Corp. analyst. The increasing popularity of diet soft drinks, which are priced the same as regular ones, is tempering consumer resistance to higher prices, he said. "Diet soft drinks are a value-added product that provide the consumer a reason to buy the product," he said.

In the first quarter, Atlanta-based Coke is expected to earn between 81 cents and 84 cents a share, according to analysts' estimates, compared with 72 cents a share a year earlier.

Purchase, N.Y.-based Pepsi, aided by stronger fast-food and snack operations, will earn between 70 cents and 72 cents a share, up from the year-earlier 62 cents a share. The results would have been higher except for the earnings dilution resulting from Pepsi's multibillion-dollar acquisition binge last year. PaineWebber Inc. estimates that the dilution sliced about 11 cents a share off Pepsi's earnings in the latest quarter.

Earnings at Coca-Cola Enterprises are expected to fall to between two cents and five cents a share from the year-ago seven cents. One optimistic analyst sees flat earnings. Higher marketing costs again appear to be the main culprit, analysts said. The giant Coca-Cola bottler, whose recent profit performance has disappointed investors, has been increasing marketing and promotional spending to lessen consumer resistance to higher soft-drink prices.

But low pricing, which has been squeezing soft-drink bottlers' margins until recently, has been creeping up. That trend has led some analysts to predict higher earnings for Coca-Cola Enterprises in 1990, although the comparative 1989 earnings were poor.

Amid a moderate 2.5% to 3% increase in beer shipments in the first quarter, Anheuser-Busch, St. Louis, is expected to earn between 60 and 64 cents a share, compared with 58 cents a year earlier. Although the major brewers raised prices 3% early this year, some uncertainty remains about the effect on profits of sporadic and regional price discounting in the beer business. But, said Mr. Romm of First Boston, "the beer industry seems to be very healthy in the first quarter. All points of reference suggest that the consumer is drinking more light and premium light beers."

Coors, of Golden, Colo., is expected to have at least a break-even quarter, compared with a loss of 13 cents a share. Analysts credit the company's Keystone beer brand, introduced last September. The so-called popular-price beer, Keystone has been selling well above expectations because of a strong marketing campaign and the flagging fortunes of some smaller competitors.
