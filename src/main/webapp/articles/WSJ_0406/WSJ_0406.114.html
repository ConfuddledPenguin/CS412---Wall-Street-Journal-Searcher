Browning-Ferris Plans to Leave Business Of Hazardous Waste and Take Charge

  • Author: Robert Johnson
  • Date: 04/06/90

Browning-Ferris Industries Inc., stumbling over environmental regulations, plans to sell its hazardous waste-disposal business and to take a charge of $295 million for the fiscal second quarter ended March 31. For the huge waste hauler, No. 2 in its industry, the abrupt exit from hazardous waste signals a defeat for its strategy begun in 1988 to boost its environmental image. That year, the company, based in Houston, named former Environmental Protection Agency head William D. Ruckelshaus chairman and chief executive officer.

Despite the presence of Mr. Ruckelshaus, previously praised by both environmentalists and industry competitors, Browning-Ferris has been plagued with compliance problems in New York, Louisiana and Ohio.

In an interview, Mr. Ruckelshaus insisted the environmental problems weren't decisive in the company's departure from hazardous waste, but he conceded the problems have caused "tension" within the company. He said Browning-Ferris will focus on solid waste, recycling and medical waste as its growth areas.

Mr. Ruckelshaus blamed the move away from hazardous waste on low profit margins, compared with its other businesses. "They {margins} just aren't attractive enough, even assuming we can overcome the regulatory problems, which I think we could."

Last month, a Dallas regional office of the EPA said two of the company's units in Louisiana agreed to pay about $1.5 million to settle allegations that they violated hazardous waste-disposal rules.

Moreover, the New York State Department of Environmental Conservation last month denied a Browning-Ferris subsidiary the state permits necessary to construct a new hazardous waste cell at a landfill in Niagara Falls, N.Y.

In its statement yesterday, Browning-Ferris said a unit has agreed with the Ohio attorney general's office to pay a $25,000 fine and plead guilty to violating an environmental law at its Williamsburg, Ohio, facility. The company also will pay a civil penalty of $1.5 million, establish an environmental trust fund of $1 million for the state and pay an additional $1 million to Clermont County. The cost of the Ohio penalties are included in the one-time $295-million charge.

Hazardous waste produces just 2% of Browning-Ferris's revenue, and the segment has had operating losses for the past year and a half, the company said.

In the year-ago quarter, the company had net income of $58.8 million, or 39 cents a share, on revenue of $594.9 million.

In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, the company's shares fell 25 cents to $35.375.

Analysts said Browning-Ferris's decision to quit hazardous waste comes at a time of increasing litigation in the industry, concerns about liability for the health of residents near landfills and rapidly rising costs for technology that satisfies stricter environmental regulations. "Managing a hazardous waste-disposal site in 1990 is much more complicated and costly than it was 10 years ago," said Kan Hahn, an analyst at Chicago Corp. She said Browning-Ferris entered the hazardous waste field in 1982.

"Today, your disposal site has to have synthetic double-liners, costly air emissions systems and much more detailed record-keeping," said Ms. Hahn. "{Mr.} Ruckelshaus tried to make major improvements in Browning-Ferris's operation but the company was already too far behind when he arrived, without committing a lot more capital."

The company's newest hazardous waste-disposal site is scheduled to open soon in Last Chance, Colo. A spokesman said the new state-of-the art facility is part of the assets being put up for sale.

It is uncertain where the company will find a buyer for hazardous waste-disposal sites. One possibility is Chemical Waste Management Inc., based in Oak Brook, Ill., the nation's largest hazardous waste concern. But a Chemical Waste spokesman, reached late yesterday, said it was too soon to comment on the likelihood of such an acquisition, and other company officials couldn't be reached.

Mr. Ruckelshaus said he plans to continue running Browning-Ferris. Yesterday's announcement "has no effect on me," he said, adding, "It just didn't make sense to stay in that business."
