UAL Begins Merger Talks On Union Offer

  • Author: Randall Smith and Judith Valente
  • Date: 04/06/90

UAL Corp. directors launched an effort to nail down a purchase agreement with United Airlines' three unions following a sweetened bid from the group valued at more than $4.36 billion, or $200 a share, people familiar with the situation said. However, people following the talks noted that major differences, including the price, remained between the board and unions. "As a rational matter, they're so close they could reach agreement," one official said. "But it's very emotional, very tense on both sides. It could just as easily break down."

The board's decision to begin acquisition talks came yesterday evening following a day-long meeting in Chicago and signaled the directors may be ready to accept the union offer rather than fight a proxy battle with UAL's largest shareholder, Coniston Partners. The New York investment firm is pressing for an employee buyout.

Coniston, which owns 11.8% of UAL, was threatening to begin soliciting shareholder votes to oust the board if the directors didn't accept the offer at yesterday's meeting.

Although the offer was said to fall short of the board's asking price of $220 a share, the directors were faced with an unappetizing alternative of a proxy slugfest with its major employee groups that could have further damaged the nation's second largest airline.

The unusual union coalition of pilots, mechanics and flight attendants coalesced earlier this year at the prodding of both the board and Coniston following the collapse of a $6.79 billion, $300-a-share, labor-management bid in October.

This time, the union decided to proceed without the cooperation of UAL Chairman Stephen Wolf, who stands to be ousted from his job if the labor group can pull off the difficult task of obtaining financing.

If the board accepts the union offer, the question will become whether or not banks will be willing to lend more than $3 billion in cash for a buy-out led by a labor group that so far lacks a major equity investor.

News of the board meeting to consider the union offer lifted UAL stock as much as $3 a share in early trading yesterday. But as the day wore on without any news of an agreement, the shares weakened, closing down $1 a share to $165.50 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

The mechanics of the talks at yesterday's meeting were complicated by the opposition of Mr. Wolf to an offer that would cost him his job if the unions later obtain financing. He is likely to remain in office as a lame duck until then if a merger agreement is reached.

The board meeting began at 10:30 a.m. Central Daylight Time, and the merger talks were still under way more than 10 hours later.

The unions sweetened the offer this week from their old bid of roughly $4 billion, or about $185 a share, which they made March 19. The offer, negotiated with Coniston, included $150 a share in cash, $25 in debt, and an equity interest totaling 25% in the airline.

This week, the unions increased the debt portion, eliminated the 25% equity interest, and instead threw in proceeds, expected to be about $500 million, of a prospective sale of UAL's 50% interest in its Covia computer reservation system.
