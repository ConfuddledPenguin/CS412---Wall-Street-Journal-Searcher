Technology: Hitachi Seeking Settlement In Dispute With Motorola

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/06/90

TOKYO -- Hitachi Ltd. is apparently declaring victory but also scrambling to retreat in its semiconductor-patent dispute with Motorola Inc. By all accounts, Motorola was the big loser in a U.S. federal court ruling handed down last week. But Hitachi is now the one seeking a settlement. And Motorola is so far holding out and apparently considering an appeal.

A Texas judge last week declared that both companies violated patents owned by each other, and ordered them to stop U.S. sales of the chips in question. The ruling affected Motorola's flagship microprocessor, the 68030.

Hitachi, meanwhile, was ordered to stop selling its H-8 microprocessor, which accounts for only about 1% of its semiconductor revenue.

U.S. District Judge Lucius Bunton, at Motorola's request, later stayed the order, allowing both companies to sell their chips pending an appeal.

Industry officials say Hitachi may have gotten more than it bargained for from the court fight. A protracted legal battle could hurt Hitachi politically: The 68030 is crucial for many companies, and a snag in its supply could stir up a backlash against Hitachi.

But Hajime Yasuda, deputy general manager of Hitachi's semiconductor design and development center, maintains that Hitachi is eager to settle because it is concerned about possible damage to its reputation as a reliable supplier.
