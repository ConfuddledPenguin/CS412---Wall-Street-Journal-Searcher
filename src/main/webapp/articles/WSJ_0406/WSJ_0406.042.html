House Committee Approves Its Own Clean-Air Measure

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 04/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Energy and Commerce Committee approved its version of clean-air legislation after adopting an acid-rain package that would help Midwestern utilities facing steep cleanup costs. Approval of the acid-rain amendment, which came on a 39-4 vote, cleared the last major obstacle before passage of the overall bill. The bill would impose about $20 billion in costs annually on industry to clean up acid rain, smog and toxic emissions from chemical plants and other facilities.

Committee Chairman John Dingell (D., Mich.) called the bill "necessary" and said it would yield environmental benefits, but would also "have a significantly adverse impact on jobs and the economy." He predicted that the bill, approved 42-1 by the committee, would reach the House floor around the middle of May. William Dannemeyer (R., Calif.) cast the only negative vote.

Significantly, the acid-rain amendment doesn't include an explicit subsidy for Midwest utilities, something that the advocates of such cost-sharing have fought for over the past year.

Providing financial aid for the Midwest was "never accepted at any point, at any time, anywhere," said Rep. Phil Sharp (D., Ind.), who led the cost-sharing drive.

Instead, the measure would aid utilities in the Midwest as well as utilities in Western states and other regions that have been seeking assurances they can expand. It would do this by tinkering with the pollution-trading system to be created by the legislation.

Under the system, utilities that make especially deep cuts in sulfur-dioxide emissions would get pollution credits that they could sell or swap with other utilities, which could use them to expand.

The acid-rain provision would impose costs of $4 billion annually on industry to clean up acid rain, which harms lakes and trees in New England and Canada. Under the bill, 111 dirty coal-burning power plants in 21 states would have to make deep cuts in sulfur-dioxide emissions by 1996. More than 200 additional power plants nationwide would have to make cuts by 2001. A total 10 million tons a year in emissions would be cut.

The bill would include a nationwide cap on total power plant emissions, designed to preserve the pollution gains well into the next century.

The agreement on acid rain was reached after several long nights of intense bargaining between lawmakers from the Midwest and those from the so-called clean states.

The amendment would generate additional pollution credits for utilities in the Midwest and clean states by requiring deeper emissions cuts from all utilities affected by the first phase. In addition, it would require cuts from several power plants owned by the Energy Department and certain small utilities that weren't previously covered.

Midwest plants would also benefit from certain provisions designed to encourage utilities to install emission-scrubbing devices rather than switching to cleaner, low-sulfur coal. These changes are designed in part to preserve mining jobs. Bill Samuel, a lobbyist for the United Mine Workers, said he believed the House provisions would result in fewer job losses among miners than the bill passed this week by the Senate.

The Senate bill would generate additional allowances to help the same groups of utilities, but does so by moving up the two deadlines for cutting emissions.

The committee's overall bill is stronger than the Senate's legislation in some respects, and weaker in others. On smog, the House bill calls for a stronger federal role in forcing cleanups by states and cities. It would also tailor requirements to the severity of an area's smog problem.

Environmentalists also prefer the committee's provision on cancer-causing industrial emissions.

The Senate, however, calls for a significantly stronger program requiring the use of reformulated gasoline or other cleaner alternatives to conventional fuel in the smoggiest cities. The Senate's provisions on auto-tailpipe exhaust are also more stringent.
