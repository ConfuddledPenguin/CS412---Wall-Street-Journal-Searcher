Letters to the Editor: HMOS: You Left Out the Good News

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/06/90

Concerning your Feb. 27 article "As HMO Premiums Soar . . .": Yes, the number of HMOs is less than a year or so ago -- but HMO membership continues to increase. The smaller number of HMOs is mostly the result of consolidation and mergers. HMOs continue to attract more and more new members.

Yes, HMO premiums have increased -- but "soar" is an overstatement, especially when you compare HMO increases of 16.5% for 1989 with indemnity increases of more than 20%.

Yes, HMOs have to find new ways to control costs and still provide a quality product and provide better data to employers. But great progress has been made and will continue to be and, contrary to your pessimistic view, the HMO, or "Managed Care Industry," is proving to be more and more popular as a way for employers and employees to meet health-care needs.

Frank R. Mascia

President and CEO

Physicians Health Plan

Greensboro, N.C.

---

You focused on a finding by A. Foster Higgins & Co. that not many employers perceive they are saving money with HMOs. Were you unaware the survey included a reality? The reality is, employers pay roughly $300 less per employee for HMO coverage than for indemnity insurance, a figure that translates into about $4 billion in savings a year.

More encouraging news in the Foster Higgins survey went unreported: Employers who get the data they seek are more likely to believe HMOs save them money; the greater the number of employees enrolled, the more likely the employer is to be satisfied. Furthermore, where employers have the greatest experience with HMOs -- the Far West -- they are the most satisfied.

HMOs and employers are experimenting with "open-ended" products so that employees will give HMOs a try. Once they do, employees are likely to experience the levels of satisfaction, often as high as 90%, reported by current members, and to select their providers from within the HMO network.

James F. Doherty

President

Group Health Association of America

Washington

---

It has been our experience that HMOs rely completely on arbitrary methods for controlling costs. Typically, an HMO negotiates with physicians and, in our own case, neurosurgical specialists, to reduce our fees. At first look this might seem to be a prudent, cost-effective way to do business. However, the real cost-savings in health care will not be in the reduction of surgical fees, but rather by the overall reduction of costs that are built into the health-care system. Fee negotiation proves to be counterproductive; the real savings in health care is by tighter controls of unnecessary costs.

The goal of health-maintenance organizations to control costs is certainly admirable. Our concern is the approach that is often taken. For example, recently, one of our patients was having a cervical-disk operation that requires an intraoperative cervical X-ray. Because of the inexperience of an X-ray technician, at least 25 additional minutes were required in the operating time (which essentially doubled the surgical time). This added between $500 to $750 onto the patient's bill for the unnecessary delay in time.

We believe it is more prudent and cost-effective to reward health-care providers that identify these "wastes" and that work to eliminate such inefficiencies, rather than penalizing them. We should all contribute to and expect more effective and efficient patient care through education and collaboration among physicians, nurses, insurance carriers, institutions and health agencies, such as HMOs.

Oliver D. Grin Jr., M.D

Chief, Division of Neurosurgery

Blodgett Memorial Medical Center

Dorothy W. Bouwman, R.N.

Neurosurgical Clinical Specialist

Neurologic Orthopaedic Institute

Grand Rapids, Mich.

---

You say HMOs are "under attack" by consumers long "dissatisfied" with HMOs, while employers are suddenly dissatisfied. In fact, quite the opposite is true: Every consumer-satisfaction survey available shows that consumers prefer HMOs to traditional insurance. By contrast, employers have been less satisfied for years because the savings from HMOs is focused on lower employee cost-sharing.

The value of the benefits provided by HMOs is greater than traditional insurance, even at a lower price, but the employee receives most of the advantages. Employers simply want to share in the savings, which is much different from wanting lower prices or being dissatisfied with HMO premium increases.

It is true that employers recently have shown tremendous concern about the proliferation of HMOs with poor track records, but this is not caused by higher prices. The real problem for employers is that they cannot get enough employees to go into HMOs. If they could get rid of traditional insurance, their premiums would really be low because there would not be any wasteful insurance products. This is what most major benefits advisers are telling Fortune 500 firms every day.

William R. Boyles

Publisher

Health Market Survey

Washington

The same applies when you indicated that only one-third believe HMOs are effective in controlling health-care costs: Does that mean 67% disagreed with your statement and were satisfied that HMOs controlled costs?

Diana Tchida

Executive Director
