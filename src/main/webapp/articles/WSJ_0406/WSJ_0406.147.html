First Executive Unit Is Part of Suit Citing Resorts Debt Offering

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/06/90

LOS ANGELES -- The principal subsidiary of First Executive Corp. is a plaintiff in a lawsuit filed here yesterday alleging that Merv Griffin, Donald Trump, Resorts International Inc. and Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. misrepresented Resort's prospective financial results in the sale of its debt in 1988. The suit, filed in federal court, cites registration statements related to the sale of $325 million in notes. It alleges that financial information provided in the statements failed to reveal that the resulting company would lack sufficient cash to cover its debt.

Drexel, now in bankruptcy proceedings, was named in the suit as underwriter of the securities. Mr. Trump allegedly acquiesced in the misstatements in order to spur the sale of his interest in Resorts. Also named as a defendant was the investment banking firm of Houlihan, Lokey, Howard & Zukin.

First Executive's holdings of Resort debt had a book value of $148.7 million at Dec. 31. Executive Life Insurance Co. unit, the plaintiff, is the principal holder of the notes, which were issued in connection with Mr. Griffin's 1988 purchase of Resorts after a takeover battle with Mr. Trump.

The suit alleges the defendants fraudulently misrepresented the condition of Resorts properties, implying that they were in good condition and had excellent cash flow. It also alleges securities fraud and seeks unspecified compensatory damages.

Mr. Trump and his spokesmen, Mr. Griffin and his lawyer were all attending the grand opening of Mr. Trump's Taj Mahal hotel-casino in Atlantic City, N.J., and couldn't be reached for comment. A Drexel spokesman in New York also was unreachable late last night.

Mr. Griffin issued the $325 million in senior junk bonds on top of $600 million in existing Resorts notes. The entertainer and businessman soon ran into serious cash-flow problems when it became clear that assets tagged for sale to pay off debt couldn't be sold as originally planned, and that the resort required more capital improvements than previously thought.

In August 1989, Resorts suspended payments on the $925 million in notes. In late December, Resorts simultaneously filed a petition for protection under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code and a reorganization plan.
