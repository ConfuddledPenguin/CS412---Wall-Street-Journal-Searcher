REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Mitterrand's East Bank

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/06/90

The politics of Eastern Europe have held the world's attention. But if these brave people are to succeed, the world had better keep paying attention when the talk turns to the harder subject of money. This weekend at the G-7 meeting in Paris, finance ministers will hear that a $12 billion development bank will do wonders for Eastern Europe. In fact, history suggests that it will prolong the shift from socialism to capitalism. The Eastern European Development Bank (EEDB), with 27 Western and seven East European member nations, was largely the brainchild of French President Francois Mitterrand. He insisted that the European Community provide most of the capital and make most of the decisions. The U.S., Japan and the Soviet Union each are invited to chip in 8.5% to 10% of the capital.

The U.S. was leery of French insistence that 40% of the lending in Eastern Europe go to governments rather than private firms, but it went along. It is drawing a line, however, on loans to the Soviet Union. Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady told Congress that the U.S. won't join if the Soviets are allowed to borrow more than their share of the capital. He noted that the Soviet Union is clearly not yet an emerging democracy. Since it still spends 15% to 20% of its GNP on defense it has ample resources to finance perestroika.

But U.S. concerns go deeper. If the record of the World Bank and its offshoots is any guide, an EEDB would help preserve statism. The French insist that the EEDB will be different, with 60% of its loans going to the private sector. But this is a distinction without a difference.

Such bodies fund private-sector borrowers by giving the money to state-run development banks whose bureaucrats then "on-loan" to private borrowers of their choosing. Last year a World Bank internal report noted that in a sample of 18 of these "directed credit" programs, an average of nearly 50% of loans were in arrears. Yet, the first World Bank loan to post-communist Poland this February was $265 million to the National Bank for "directed credit" to export industries. Is it likely that the EEDB's private-sector lending will be more rational?

Such infusions often act as a narcotic, delaying needed economic reforms and thus making matters worse. Politicians can't help but look at aid as a way to delay privatizations and painful price reforms. The Czechs seem to agree. President Vaclav Havel told Margaret Thatcher during his British visit that "we value your advice and moral support far more than your money."

U.S. policymakers are going along with the EEDB partly out of concern that the U.S. will be ridiculed if it doesn't. But rather than fret about its image, the U.S. should stand up for the incentive-based policies that the rest of the world is now emulating.

Any money the U.S. does send to Eastern Europe would be better spent in the forms recommended by the commission on Hungary, whose conclusions are summarized in an article nearby (See: "A Recipe for a Free-Market Hungary" -- WSJ April 6, 1990). Additionally, Eastern Europeans could be brought to the U.S. to work as trainees and interns, much the way American corporations routinely bring foreign nationals to the U.S. for intensive training.

The West should be helping Eastern Europe. But the proposed EEDB looks no better than the other development banks. If they are so good, how come so many of their Third World clients are still doing so badly?
