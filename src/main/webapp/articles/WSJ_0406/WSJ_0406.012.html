Growth in Consumer Debt Slowed Again in February

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/06/90

WASHINGTON -- Growth in consumer debt continued to slow in February, the Federal Reserve Board reported. Consumer installment credit expanded $3.42 billion during the month, a 5.7% annual rate, according to the Fed. That was the smallest rise since last October and followed rates of 6.4% and 7.3% in January and December, respectively. Originally, the January rate was estimated at 5.9%.

A drop-off in growth of automobile loans held back the expansion of consumer credit, a statistic that includes all consumer borrowing except mortgages. Car loans expanded $889 million, a 3.7% annual rate, after spurting $2.02 billion in January, an 8.4% annual rate. Nevertheless, February's growth in car financing is an improvement from late last year when debt from auto loans was declining.

Revolving credit, largely bank card and retail borrowing, showed weak expansion for the second consecutive month. Borrowing in this category grew $485 million, for a 2.9% annual rate of increase, after inching forward at a 1.8% annual rate in January.

At the end of February, consumer installment debt totaled $718.67 billion, up 5.3% from a year earlier.
