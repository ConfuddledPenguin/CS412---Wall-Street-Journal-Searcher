Heard on the Street: TCBY, Frozen Yogurt Concern, May Benefit From New Competition, Some Analysts Say

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 04/06/90

NEW YORK -- To hear Wall Street tell it, investors in TCBY Enterprises should positively be doing cartwheels over the impending entry of two giant fast-food companies into the frozen yogurt business. TCBY, a Little Rock, Ark., chain that now dominates the frozen yogurt market, already is in a war with smaller yogurt-chains and with Baskin-Robbins. In May, International Dairy Queen will roll out a frozen yogurt product in 2,200 stores. And that same month, McDonald's plans to go nationwide with a similar product in 8,000 outlets.

Wall Street, however, isn't fazed. "The fact that McDonald's is replacing ice cream with frozen yogurt sends a clear message that frozen yogurt is bullish," says Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst Caroline Levy. Yogurt, she says, has captured only 14% of the frozen dessert market. With McDonald's pushing it, the market is certain to expand. "If someone tries a frozen yogurt in McDonalds," she adds, "eventually they will become a TCBY customer."

With its shares trading at 18 times trailing per-share earnings and at five times book value, TCBY will need new customers. In fact, the stock is trading at nearly four times sales. Those are growth-stock multiples. The question is whether the business is maturing. In its past fiscal year, ended in November, sales in TCBY stores open at least 12 months rose by an ample, but hardly explosive, 3.6%. Sales in stores open at least 24 months rose only 2%. And in the first quarter of this year, same-store sales fell 8%. The company blames bad weather in December (January and February were mild) and a tough comparison with an unusually good quarter last year.

Analysts are believers. The Street is estimating that TCBY's earnings growth will resume, and that net for the year will outstrip last year's net by 30%.

The bullish case is that TCBY has established a distinct product and a dominant market share (roughly 34%). Its customers can choose from among 50 flavors -- from white chocolate mousse to pumpkin -- of which half a dozen are available on any day, and a variety of toppings. McDonald's restaurants will serve only one or two flavors.

According to market research, one third of all Americans have yet to savor their first frozen yogurt. And according to bulls, there are only two types of Americans: those who eat frozen yogurt now, and those who will. "We know there is competition," Ms. Levy says. "We are simply betting that the market is growing."

The party line at TCBY is similarly jubilant. The chain has close to 1,700 stores, mostly franchised, and plans to go to 2,000 by November. "I don't think it will adversely impact us," a spokeswoman says of the mushrooming competition. "If anything, it broadens the market. Our plans are to continue growing."

It is possible, of course, that TCBY is planning a party to celebrate its rush of good news. But don't bet on it.

TCBY has recently had to deal with unrest among franchisees, some of whom complained that TCBY was charging them too high a price for its yogurt. Prices at McDonald's are expected to be lower, analysts say, putting more pressure on TCBY's price. At a suburban Atlanta TCBY, for instance, a small yogurt is $1.09 and a medium-sized portion is $1.51, plus 30 cents for a topping. McDonald's prices are expected to be in the vicinity of 70 cents.

Frozen yogurt executives say TCBY and other frozen yogurt chains have more to fear from restaurants that specialize in ice cream, such as Dairy Queen and Baskin-Robbins, than from McDonald's, because ice cream is a natural competitor to frozen yogurt.

"If a Dairy Queen opens across the street from a TCBY, they could take half its business," says Jeffrey Frankel, president of Dallas-based I Love Yogurt. And as more Dairy Queens promote yogurt there will be less room for new TCBY franchises.

But, Mr. Frankel says, "people aren't going to go to McDonald's for yogurt." Analysts, in general, say the Golden Arches are a turn-off to health-conscious yogurt lovers.

They are, perhaps, writing off the nation's No. 1 fast-food chain too quickly. McDonald's is unmatched as a marketer, and if it chooses to redefine itself in the direction of yogurt eaters, people will take notice. And by all appearances, some such redefinition is in place.

McDonald's also plans to introduce lower-calorie shakes, fruit sorbets and apple bran muffins. A spokesman says "our mission is to respond to customer tastes in an evolutionary way, not in a revolutionary way. People are eating a little lighter and keeping an eye on their health." It is hardly news that people are eating lighter, but when McDonald's notices, that is news indeed. Regardless of what they say in public, it's a good bet that competitors -- in yogurt and other products -- agree.
