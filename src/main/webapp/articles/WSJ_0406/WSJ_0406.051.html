Cineplex Says Profit Is Unlikely Until '91 or '92

  • Author: Suzanne McGee
  • Date: 04/06/90

TORONTO -- Allen Karp, president and chief operating officer of Cineplex Odeon Corp., said the company expects to end the year with positive cash flow, "significantly" lower debt levels and higher revenue. But he said shareholders "ought not to look for" profits until 1991 or 1992, after the company's restructuring is completed.

Mr. Karp succeeded Cineplex's founder and former chairman, Garth Drabinsky, in December after Mr. Drabinsky quit the company after his failure to launch a takeover bid for the Toronto-based theater-distribution chain.

Mr. Karp's current task is to reduce, through asset sales and other measures, the debt built up by Mr. Drabinsky during an aggressive expansion in the late 1980s, and return the company to profitability.

He said that in the first quarter the company saw a nearly 20% increase in revenue from box office admissions and concessions from a year earlier.

"Our {operating} revenues are outstripping both 1989 figures and our budgets," Mr. Karp said. "This will trickle down to the bottom line."

Improved relations with major film studios also means Cineplex won't be shut out of distributing high-grossing films this year, as happened in 1988 and 1989, he added.

Although Mr. Karp said he expects to see positive cash flow by the end of the year, he declined to quantify it, saying it would be affected by what assets are sold, and when the sales are concluded.

Cineplex already has "very attractive offers" for some of its theater chains, Mr. Karp said, and is negotiating the possible sale of, among others, theaters in Virgina, Oregon and Washington state.

He said he expects Cineplex to receive around seven or eight times cash flow for the properties. Most analysts say eight times cash flow is the maximum any buyer is willing to pay in the current buyer's market.

Cineplex may report losses on the sale of some of the chains, but Mr. Karp said he expects "some very large gains on other sales" to result in a net benefit to the company.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Cineplex closed at $5.50, up 50 cents.

Asset sales will help to cut Cineplex's debt, which reached a high of $620 million in the first quarter, to between $400 and $500 million by year end, he said.

Mr. Karp said the company is eyeing a number of ways to refinance its debt load, including the possible sale and leaseback of some of its theaters. Other options under study include a joint venture partnership with another company, or stand-alone financing arrangements for some of the individual theater chains. He didn't identify any potential partners.

The key to returning the company to profitability, Mr. Karp said, is keeping a tight rein on capital spending and other expenses. Cineplex's capital spending budget will be limited to $12 million in 1990, he said, although under agreements with its bankers the company is able to spend up to $17.5 million refurbishing and building new theaters this year.

He said general and administrative expenses are expected to drop from $35 million in 1989 to less than $22 million this year.

Mr. Karp said that in 1991 "we will be reaping the benefits" of the restructuring efforts and begin showing profits again.
