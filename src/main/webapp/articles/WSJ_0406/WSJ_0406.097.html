Thursday's Markets: Wary Mood Dampens U.S. Trading --- Fears About Tokyo, U.S. Economy Keep Stocks, Bonds Flat

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 04/06/90

Wariness about the Tokyo stock market, the U.S. economy and the fate of the dollar kept a tight lid on financial markets. The Dow Jones Industrial Average eked out a gain of 1.80 points to 2721.17 in light trading. But the advance was concentrated in blue-chip issues. Broader market averages were off slightly. Long-term bond prices eased, and the dollar was mixed.

Analysts said worries about Japanese stocks restrained U.S. stock prices. The Dow industrials lost more than 17 points Wednesday after rumors spread that a big player in the Japanese stock market was in financial trouble. The Nikkei index of 225 stocks lost about 1,200 points in early trading Thursday, but recouped most of that by the end of trading.

Treasury bonds, which benefited Wednesday from a flight to safety on the unconfirmed Tokyo rumors, were nearly moribund yesterday as investors awaited this morning's March employment report. Economists expect the report to show the economy generated about 178,000 non-farm jobs last month, substantially below the growth rate in January and February.

Currency traders, meantime, awaited the results this weekend of the Group of Seven major industrialized nations meeting in Paris, where officials will discuss steps to rein in the dollar's surge against the Japanese yen. Although traders don't expect any such efforts to be successful in the long run, they nevertheless bid the dollar lower against the yen yesterday.

Charles Clough, chief portfolio strategist at Merrill Lynch, said investors are correct to be concerned about the Tokyo stock market. If nothing else, the disruptions there could undermine investor confidence in the U.S. market. But he added that even if Tokyo's troubles spread to the U.S., any turmoil is likely to be short-lived. He said U.S. interest rates are among the lowest in the world, the dollar is strong and Americans are saving an ever-growing portion of their incomes -- all signs of fundamental economic strength.

"Right now, Japan is a loose cannon," he said. "But I think that if we did see the U.S. markets destabilized, it wouldn't last long."

In major market action:

Stock prices crept higher in light trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 144.2 million shares. But declining issues on the Big Board were ahead of gainers 744 to 703.

Bond prices eased. The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond lost an eighth of a point, or $1.25 for each $1,000 face amount. The yield on the issue rose to 8.51%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6995 marks, unchanged from Wednesday, and at 157.4 yen, down from 158.95.
