Who's News: Wharton School Picks Thomas Gerrity Of Computer Sciences Corp. to Be Dean

  • Author: Vindu P. Goel
  • Date: 04/06/90

PHILADELPHIA -- Thomas P. Gerrity, president of Computer Sciences Corp.'s rapidly growing commercial consulting division, was named dean of the University of Pennsylvania's Wharton School. Mr. Gerrity, 48 years old, is expected to guide the redesigning of the business school's curriculum, while continuing the international emphasis on research and teaching that began under his predecessor, Russell E. Palmer.

Mr. Palmer, 55, who has been the dean for seven years, said this past summer that he plans to step down June 30. He will pursue some entrepreneurial ventures.

Mr. Gerrity brings extensive academic and corporate experience to the assignment. As head of CSC Consulting Corp., he has been leading the computer-services firm's efforts to expand into commercial computer-systems integration in the U.S. and Europe, decreasing the firm's dependence on federal government contracts.

For four years, Mr. Gerrity taught at Massachusetts Institute of Technology's Sloan School of Management, where he founded Index Group, a consulting firm that specializes in counseling executives on strategic management of changing information technology. In the early 1970s, he left Sloan to become a full-time consultant. Index Group is now part of CSC Consulting.

At Wharton, Mr. Gerrity is expected to infuse both the curriculum and the research programs with more emphasis on practical business techniques and problems. Although the school is already considered to be among the best in the U.S., faculty members say students still need better preparation for confronting international markets and competitors.

Wharton "needs to work continually to see that globalization is reflected throughout the curriculum," Mr. Gerrity said in an interview. "There are no more purely domestic arenas."

Another challenge facing the new dean is the reorganization of Wharton's core courses, said Anita Summers, a professor of public policy and a member of the search committee that selected Mr. Gerrity. She said the school needs to evaluate whether it is "slicing the subjects correctly."

In addition to creating more cooperation among Wharton's segmented departments, Wharton's current dean, Mr. Palmer, is credited with almost quadrupling Wharton's endowment, to more than $100 million at current market prices from $27 million in 1983. "He was able to convince a lot of people that this was a good place to invest their money -- in human capital," Ms. Summers said.

Mr. Gerrity is expected to continue the fund raising, said Edward Bowman, the deputy dean who led the search. "But he brings more than that," Mr. Bowman said.

Computer Sciences, El Segundo, Calif., said Chairman William R. Hoover will assume Mr. Gerrity's responsibilities until a successor is named. "We regret losing an executive of Tom's caliber," Mr. Hoover said. "At the same time, we are proud that Tom has been chosen to lead one of the world's truly outstanding business schools."
