Law: Vernon Savings' W.F. Lemons Gets 30 Years in Prison

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 04/06/90

DALLAS -- The former chairman and chief executive officer of the defunct Vernon Savings & Loan Association was sentenced to 30 years in federal prison, one of the stiffest sentences to date in thrift-related fraud prosecutions. Woody F. Lemons, the seventh officer of Vernon to be convicted, was found guilty in December of 13 charges brought as a result of a continuing investigation by the U.S. Justice Department's Dallas Bank Fraud Task Force.

The 30-year sentence, which stunned defense lawyers and elated government attorneys, came less than a week before the House Banking Committee is scheduled to hold hearings in Dallas on the effectiveness of prosecution in fraud-related thrift failures, such as Vernon's.

The Justice Department has been investigating Vernon's lending practices since 1986 but has yet to bring criminal charges against the thrift's former owner, Don R. Dixon. The banking committee has invited Mr. Dixon to testify next week but his attorney, William M. Ravkind of Dallas, said his client won't appear. Mr. Ravkind called Mr. Lemons's 30-year sentence "absurd."

In view of the scheduled hearings, Mr. Lemons's defense lawyer, Michael P. Gibson of Dallas, had urged U.S. District Judge Robert B. Maloney to be mindful of the prosecutors' "trophy mentality."

"Mr. Lemons is the trophy for the federal agencies because they need to maintain their position that they're out there doing good for the community," Mr. Gibson said.

But Judge Maloney, who has presided in at least two other criminal cases related to Vernon Savings, referred to Mr. Lemons as "a thief in every sense of the word" and said he wanted to use the sentence "to assure the public" that thrift fraud won't be treated lightly.

Mr. Gibson called the 30-year sentence "way disproportionate to the crime." Mr. Lemons was convicted of bank fraud and 12 other charges, including a charge that he accepted a $212,000 kickback from the proceeds of a $46 million real estate development loan.

Judge Maloney didn't impose a fine but will rule later this month on how much restitution Mr. Lemons will be ordered to make. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. is seeking $9 million to $10 million, according to Howard Feinstein, chief of the conflicts and restitution unit of the FDIC's legal staff.

The FDIC is taking a stronger role in criminal cases, as evidenced by a letter from FDIC Chairman William Seidman to Judge Maloney.

"Woody Lemons abused his position of trust and defrauded the institution, causing damages of over $18 million," Mr. Seidman wrote earlier this week. "While I have considered the fact that Mr. Lemons has a family, I have also taken into consideration . . . that virtually all the costs of Mr. Lemons's criminal acts will be borne by the {thrift} resolution fund and, ultimately, the U.S. taxpayers."

Vernon was closed in November 1987 by the Federal Savings and Loan Insurance Corp., which found a stunning 96% of the thrift's loans in default. The FSLIC committed $1.3 billion to fold the thrift's assets into Montfort Savings Association, which became part of First Gibraltar Bank in late December 1988.
