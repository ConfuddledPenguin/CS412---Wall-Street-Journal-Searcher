Abreast of the Market: Stock Prices End Mixed as Worry About Earnings Overhangs Market

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 04/06/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks finished mixed as worries about first-quarter corporate-earnings reports offset relief that the Tokyo stock market hadn't plunged overnight. Consumer stocks that were considered largely insulated from earnings disappointments attracted buyers yesterday, just as they did earlier this week. Bank issues met with across-the-board selling in response to indications that federal regulatory reviews may put continued pressure on their results.

In addition, shares of Tandem Computers and several other companies whose estimates for the just-completed quarter failed to satisfy market expectations were hit with heavy selling and fell sharply.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 1.80 to 2721.17 with the help of gains among blue-chip consumer issues such as Philip Morris, which rose 3/4 to 41 1/4, and Merck, which advanced 7/8 to 72 1/8.

Broader market measures ended the session with slight losses. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index dropped 0.36 to 340.73 and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index slid 0.16 to 187.15.

Losers led gainers by about 744 to 703 on the Big Board, where trading activity continued at the moderate pace set earlier this week. Big Board volume totaled 144,170,000 shares.

Prices moved higher at the opening in response to the Tokyo market's performance, which went against speculation Wednesday among U.S. market participants that Japanese share prices would fall sharply. The Nikkei index of 225 Japanese stocks finished with a loss of just 193.88, or 0.7%, at 28249.06.

But the gains proved short-lived, and the market drifted through the rest of the session amid periodic rounds of futures-related buying and selling as the focus shifted to earnings.

"The market is more vulnerable to earnings disappointments today than at any time in recent history," because price-earnings ratios for stocks have been on the rise, said Carmine Grigoli, chief equity portfolio strategist at First Boston.

Mr. Grigoli said he expects first-quarter earnings for the companies included in the S&P 500 to be down 12% to 15% overall from the year-ago period -- a drop for which he says the market isn't prepared. In recent weeks, those issues have traded at an average of about 15 times their earnings over the past 12 months.

With prices near the upper boundary of their trading range during the past several months, "We're due for some sort of setback, and I think earnings are going to be the catalyst," he said.

The sell-off in bank stocks came after analysts met Wednesday with representatives of the Comptroller of the Currency, who indicated that banks are facing a tough, sweeping review of their real-estate lending practices and that the same standards will be used in reviewing their portfolios of consumer loans and loans to highly-levleraged comanies.

Two major commercial banks issues were among the most active stocks on the Big Board: Citicorp, which dipped 1/4 to 22 1/2 on composite volume of 2.1 million shares, and BankAmerica, which dropped 1 1/4 to 26 3/8 on 1.2 million shares.

Also, Chase Manhattan fell 5/8 to 27 7/8, Bankers Trust New York declined 1 3/4 to 36 5/8, First Chicago declined 1 to 29 and J.P. Morgan eased 1/2 to 35.

Prudential-Bache lowered its investment ratings to "sell" from "hold" on Chase and two regional bank stocks: PNC Financial, which skidded 2 1/4 to 37 7/8, and Bank of New York, which retreated 2 1/2 to 31 1/4. Among other regionals, Security Pacific dropped 1 1/4 to 35 5/8, Wells Fargo lost 1 5/8 to 68 3/4, NCNB fell 1 5/8 to 36 1/8, Citizens & Southern fell 1 1/4 to 25 1/4, and Bank of Boston declined 1 1/8 to 12 3/4.

Bank of New England fell 3/8 to 3 7/8 on 1.2 million shares. Shares of the troubled regional bank holding company had declined 3/8 Wednesday following its acknowledgment that it faces further increases in nonperforming assets and loan-loss provisions.

Tandem, the Big Board's most active issue, plunged 4 5/8 to 22 5/8 on 4.8 million shares. The company said it expects to report that net income for the second quarter, ended March 31, was lower than expectations.

Other stocks hit with earnings-related selling included:

-- Ogden, which plunged 3 1/8 to 24 3/8. The company told analysts its first-quarter earnings, excluding a gain resulting from a sale of stock by its Ogden Projects unit, would be lower than the year-ago level. In response, Kidder Peabody and Dillon Read both lowered their 1990 earnings estimates.

-- Businessland, which slid 1 1/2 to 8 7/8 after lowering its earnings estimate for the March quarter. The company said it now expects to report break-even operating results for the period on revenue of about $320 million.

-- WorldCorp, which fell 7/8 to 8 3/4 on news of a fourth-quarter loss, compared with a profit a year ago. The company said it also expects to report a first-quarter loss as well and doesn't anticipate meeting its target of $21 million in net income for the full year.

Baxter International, which announced a planned restructuring Wednesday, dropped 1 1/2 to 22 7/8 on 4.5 million shares after a meeting with analysts proved disappointing. Both Kidder Peabody and Prudential-Bache lowered investment ratings on the stock following the meeting.

Amax advanced 5/8 to 28 on two million shares amid continuing speculation the company could become a takeover target.

Peter Kiewit Sons', a private firm, has been identified as a possible bidder.

McDermott International climbed 2 to 28 1/8 after receiving a $250 million contract for 15 offshore sulfur mining platforms. Also, traders said there was speculation that Schlumberger may be interested in acquiring the company; however, McDermott said it hadn't been approached by any suitors. Schlumberger declined comment.

Rollins Environmental Services gained 5/8 to 13 3/8 on 1.3 million shares. Smith Barney, Harris Upham put the company on its research list with a "buy" recommendation, citing promising growth in the market for incinerators.

L.E. Myers Group, which rose 5/8 Wednesday, gained another 1 1/4 to 14 1/2. Analysts attributed the stock's rally to the Senate's passage of a clean-air bill earlier this week; the company provides construction services for electric utilities, among the targets of the measure.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.11 to 361.23. Volume on the exchange totaled 15,830,000 shares. There were 262 issues advancing and 278 declining.

Salomon's newly issued call warrants on the Nikkei index topped the exchange's list of most active issues. The warrants, which become more valuable as the index moves higher, rose 5/8 to 3 3/4 in response to the Tokyo market's showing overnight; more than 3.3 million changed hands in composite trading.

International Telecharge, whose board decided against pursuing a takeover offer from a suitor, who wasn't identified, rose 1/8 to 3 1/8. The company also signed a service agreement with MCI Communications and named Allan L. Rayfield, a former GTE executive, as chairman and chief executive officer.
