Another Economic Development Bank Is Forming in Europe, but Is It Needed?

  • Author: Tim Carrington
  • Date: 04/06/90

LONDON -- Europe's Berd is going to fly. The so-called Banque Europeene pour la Reconstruction et Developpement, or Berd, is back on the launch pad, planners say -- although this comes amid nagging questions over the need for yet another international financial institution. According to a negotiator for the European Community, there's a solid consensus on 54 of 58 articles of confederation. The remaining differences, he said, can be settled or at least smoothed over at a Paris meeting Sunday and Monday.

As the West's principal vehicle for transfusing the ravaged East bloc economies, the new bank will begin with about $12 billion to lend to companies and selected public works. Planners stress that the Berd's loans will come wrapped in lots of advice about how to set up a private-enterprise economy and help it flourish.

So far, though, the East Europeans mainly have been given a lesson in how Western aid gets filtered through a mesh of egos and jostling political interests.

The French uncorked the idea of a new reconstruction bank in November at a summit in Strasbourg. Britain was initially cool to it, but came around in time to churn out a glossy, trilingual brochure explaining that "from opera to athletics," London is the logical home for the bank. Meanwhile, other would-be hosts were elbowing in: Serious contenders include Amsterdam, Berlin, Copenhagen, and Vienna, as well as Prague and Budapest from the East bloc.

Within this competitive caldron, compromises bubble up almost daily. One talked-of bargain has London hosting the bank, but accepting as its President Jacques Attali, aide to French president Francois Mitterrand. Another scenario floats the Berd back to the continent, but places Onno Ruding, the former Dutch finance minister and a strong British ally, in the top job. (This scenario collapses if Algemene Bank Nederland NV and Amsterdam-Rotterdam Bank NV merge and ask Mr. Ruding to run the combined bank.)

Even the working labels seem to flow from Pan-European compromises: the acronym, Berd, comes from the bank's French name, while the official title is the Anglicized European Bank for Reconstruction and Development.

Lost in the debates over name and place is the fundamental question of why the Berd should exist. "It's another of Mitterrand's monuments," says one London economist who insists on staying anonymous. "It's the multiplication-of-entities principle."

The World Bank and its affiliated International Finance Corp. are already doing the sort of financing jobs that the Berd would take on. Then there's the European Investment Bank and the International Monetary Fund. Not to be outdone, the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development has set up a new office to advise East bloc economic reformers.

Sir William Ryrie, head of International Finance, said in a recent interview that he worries about his organization and the Berd both pursuing the same projects. If the Berd's terms are more generous, he warns, International Finance could see clients drift away.

Even the Berd's most vigorous supporters concede that it will take more time and money to set up a new institution than to expand those already up and running. "You could probably do more lending faster by using the existing institutions," says one European central banker. However, critics risk looking like spoilers who won't support the East bloc's courageous march to freedom and economic health.

Discussing the Berd at a congressional hearing in Washington, U.S. Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady said, "Let's conjure up the headlines if we didn't join this." The U.S., after an internal fracas over its level of participation, has assured its allies that it will weigh in with a contribution of about 10%.

Berd's planners also have agreed that the new bank will devote at least 60% of its resources to private-sector enterprises. The remainder would go to government "infrastructure" projects that would help private companies function: more working telephones and rail cars, fewer poisonous dumps. There are continuing worries that even with the emphaisis on private-sector projects, the new bank will add to the debt burdens of nations already heavily indebted.

The biggest worries involve the Soviet Union. "The size of the country is such that they could bankrupt the bank," says the EC negotiator. He adds that in the coming Paris meeting, "we will come up with a way to let them in -- but fence them."

The U.S. Treasury's negotiator, David Mulford, has suggested limiting Soviet borrowings for five years to amounts equal to the capital it paid in. Moreover, Berd loans to the Soviet Union would be restricted to private companies and special technical assistance under the Mulford proposal.
