USX Chairman Says Icahn Plan To Cost Firm More

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 04/06/90

PITTSBURGH -- USX Corp. Chairman Charles A. Corry said investor Carl Icahn's proposal to split the company's steel and energy operations would cost the company an additional $100 million to $120 million a year. Moreover, Mr. Corry asserted that Mr. Icahn's $48-a-share value on a divided USX is the result of "a lightweight analytical job." He noted, for example, that the more cyclical steel segment was given the same high asset and market ratio as the more attractive oil operations. Mr. Icahn's analysis was also based on asset values, rather than earnings and cash flow, he said.

If accepted, Mr. Icahn's proposal would result in steeper interest expenses incurred by a separate steel company borrowing at about an 11 1/2% rate, compared with the company's average 8 1/2% rate. That alone would cost $45 million more annually to cover the steel company's $1.5 billion in debt, Mr. Corry said.

In addition, separating the steel business from the parent would add $20 million to $30 million a year to the combined entities' insurance expenses. Two separate companies would also result in higher state taxes and new administrative and management costs. "The companies aren't going to be as profitable as they are in a common shell," because they'd each have costs currently spread between them, Mr. Corry argued.

The executive said one issue -- the $900 million in tax-loss carry-forwards -- would resolve itself by year end because if the businesses are kept together USX would earn enough to use up the steel-related tax losses that have sheltered energy-related earnings.

But he questioned whether the proposed spinoff would be tax-free, as Mr. Icahn indicated. That condition would have to be determined by the Internal Revenue Service, which would require a number of complex conditions, Mr. Corry said.

Mr. Icahn defended his analysis by saying it did take cash flow into account, and did assign different valuation multiples to the steel and energy units. He also said his lawyers advised him the split-up could be executed on a tax-free basis.

He said any extra interest cost for steel could be offset by lower interest costs for energy operations, but added that he didn't agree there would be extra steel interest costs. As for extra state taxes and administrative costs, he said, "If you take that to its final conclusion, then everything should be conglomeratized, and we saw the devastating results of conglomerates in the 1970s."

In proxy materials mailed this week to USX shareholders, Mr. Icahn advocated the split-up of the Marathon Oil unit and U.S. Steel, arguing that the separate businesses would be valued at about 30% above the current market price, or $48 a share. USX closed yesterday at $36.375, down 12.5 cents, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. Mr. Icahn is USX's largest investor with a 13.3% stake. Shareholders will vote on his proposal at a May 7 annual meeting.
