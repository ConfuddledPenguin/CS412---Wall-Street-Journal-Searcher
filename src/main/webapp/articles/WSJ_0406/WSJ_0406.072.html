Kerry Packer, an international investor and media magnate, remains Australia's richest person, with assets valued at $1.8 billion Australian dollars (US$1.38 billion), according to an annual list published by Australia's Business Review Weekly. Robert Holmes a Court placed second, with A$800 million.

The magazine's ranking of the country's 200 richest persons dropped 27 from last year's list, following a spate of corporate disasters over the past year. Among those omitted this year were Alan Bond of Bond Corp. Holdings Ltd. and Christopher Skase of Qintex Australia Ltd.

For the first time in the eight-year history of the ranking, the threshold for entry was lowered, reflecting the declining fortunes of Australia's rich. Individuals and families needed an estimated minimum net worth of A$30 million to reach the list, down from A$35 million in 1989.

The Smorgons, with assets valued at A$1 billion, replaced the family of Rupert Murdoch, with A$870 million, as Australia's richest family.

REVIEWING THE BHOPAL SETTLEMENT

India's Supreme Court began a review of last year's $470 million settlement between the government and Union Carbide Corp. for the 1984 Bhopal gas disaster. A five-judge panel will hear arguments until at least April 10, after which it will decide whether to overturn the deal or accept it. Yesterday, a lawyer for the Bhopal Women Gas Victims Organization argued that counsel for the government of previous Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi "had no authority to enter into a settlement." Union Carbide says the settlement is fair and binding.

PAPUA NEW GUINEA'S OFFER

Papua New Guinea has offered to place a two-year moratorium on new logging to save its rain forests in return for millions of dollars in aid projects. Minister of Forests Karl Stack said: "We're challenging the international community to stop pontificating. We've told them, `You do the right thing and we'll do the right thing.'" A spokesman for Australia's Foreign Affairs Minister Gareth Evans said Australia "stands ready to support the moratorium. It's just a question of how much." The moratorium, which will start in July, would mean a loss equivalent to about $73 million in potential earnings, Mr. Stack said.

A VISIT TO NAMIBIA

The United Nations Council for Namibia, set up in 1967 to administer the territory but never allowed to set foot there, will finally visit Namibia, three weeks after it attained independence. The 31-member council will convene in Windhoek, the capital, from April 9 to 11. South Africa, which ruled Namibia for 75 years, refused to cooperate with the council and prevented it from establishing its authority in the territory. The council's visit will end with a declaration recommending that the council be disbanded.

CHINA'S SLUMPING AUTO INDUSTRY

The Chinese government pledged to bail out its auto industry, now struggling with plunging sales and rising stockpiles, the official China Daily said. The paper said auto makers produced 54,500 vehicles in the first two months of the year but sold only 44,390, down 35% from the same period in 1989. The daily said the government has allotted $80.9 million in credit to help stimulate the auto market. The market has collapsed under a 20-month-long austerity drive that has limited purchases by government institutions, the industry's main customers.

`YEAR OF THE CATASTROPHES'

Zurich's Swiss Reinsurance Co., calling 1989 the "year of the catastrophes," estimates in a new study that various disasters around the world caused insured damage of at least $13.3 billion last year.

Saying this amount was "unprecedented," the study said that 292 disasters last year killed 15,000 people. The 1989 insured-damage total was more than double the amount recorded for 1988.

Four of the costliest calamities occurred in the U.S. Hurricane Hugo led the U.S. disaster list, causing $4.31 billion in insured damage, followed by an explosion at a petrochemical plant in Pasadena, Texas, costing $1.3 billion. The San Francisco earthquake cost at least $1 billion in insured damage, and the Exxon Valdez oil tanker spill at least $425 million in insured losses, the study said.

This year, nature has struck hard in Europe. Several storms that occurred in the first quarter caused "immense property damage, provisionally estimated to be at least $8.80 billion," the study said.
