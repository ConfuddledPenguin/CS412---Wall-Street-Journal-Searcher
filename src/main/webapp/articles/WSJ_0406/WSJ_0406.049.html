National Bancshares Says Debt-Swap Plan Faces Possible Snag

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/06/90

SAN ANTONIO, Texas -- National Bancshares Corp. of Texas said it has come under pressure from certain creditors to sweeten the terms of its debt-swap offer, but added that federal regulators said they would block any changes in the proposal. National Bancshares said the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. threatened to use its newly endowed powers to close the troubled bank holding company's solvent member banks -- scuttling a pending open-bank recapitalization plan -- if at least 85% of the company's $25 million in debentures aren't tendered by the April 26 deadline.

Terms call for holders to receive $1,000 face amount of increasing-rate senior subordinated debentures, due 2006, and 10 common stock warrants, for each $1,000 face amount of its 10% convertible subordinated debentures, due 2006. Creditors estimate that the proposed swap would give them about 30 cents on the dollar of original debentures.

Regulators say the swap is crucial to the recapitalization plan, under which Equimark Corp. of Pittsburgh agreed to invest or raise about $116 million of new capital for National Bancshares. The FDIC is expected to contribute about $255 million to the recapitalization. If the debt swap isn't completed, the FDIC has said it would attempt to use the so-called "cross-guarantee" provisions of the 1989 thrift bailout bill to seize assets belonging to solvent National Bancshare banks to help defray the costs of rescuing insolvent subsidiaries. Such a move would likely render the healthy banks insolvent, too, National Bancshares said.

Some creditors decried the FDIC's tactics, predicting that the move would backfire. "They're doing this as a Gestapo-like threat to bondholders," said Jay Lustig, an analyst at Drake Capital Securities Inc., Santa Monica, Calif. He predicted that few bondholders would agree to the swap unless they receive a better offer.

But an FDIC official said the agency won't back down. "It's a free world," he said. "If {creditors} would rather have bankruptcy, they have that option."
