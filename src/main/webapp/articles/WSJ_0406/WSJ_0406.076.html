Crude-Oil Prices Register Sharp Drop On Worries of Possible Glut in Supply

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 04/06/90

Petroleum prices fell sharply on worries of a possible supply glut. Prices of European and Middle East crude oils dropped as much as 25 cents a barrel -- some falling for the second straight day. In the U.S., futures prices of crude oil plunged as much as 45 cents a barrel while refiners rushed to reduce field postings by 50 cents.

"The problem is a buildup in excess supply," said Philip Verleger, a visiting fellow at the Institute for International Economics and an authority on oil-price behavior. "OPEC is not cutting production enough," he added.

Oil prices had been expected to soften some in the second quarter, which is traditionally a sluggish demand period. But this week's declines are earlier -- and far larger -- than had been expected.

The declines began Wednesday in the wake of a Wall Street Journal article on a rise in production by the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries and after industry reports that crude oil inventories in the U.S. are expanding faster than anticipated. OPEC production now is at the highest level in eight years, and U.S. crude oil inventories are the largest in at least two years.

Most oil prices now are below those of a year ago when they were rising because of fallout from the Alaskan oil spill and from North Sea production mishaps. Prices of a few crudes still are near $20 a barrel, but most are below that psychologically important level.

In European spot markets yesterday, North Sea Brent for May delivery was down 20 cents a barrel to $18.10, according to the Dow Jones International Petroleum Report. In U.S. futures trading, the May contract for West Texas Intermediate, or WTI, fell 35 cents to settle at $19.43 a barrel. And new field postings for this U.S. benchmark crude generally are $18.75 a barrel.

"As it cracks $20, WTI is the last indicator {among the world's benchmark crudes} to reflect global fundamentals," said William H. Brown III, international oil analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co.

Until now, Mr. Brown and other analysts added, WTI has continued to hold relatively firm -- even as prices of other crudes eroded -- because it is a light "sweet" crude that is low in sulfur content. These crudes are best for yielding preferred fuels such as gasoline.

Most of the pricing pressure has been on the "sour" crudes -- those that are high in sulfur and often are very dense such as the heavy crudes of the Middle East. With Kuwait and some other Middle East members of OPEC producing beyond their quotas, the heavy and high-sulfur crudes have been pouring into the market. Some countries such as Iraq, Iran and Saudi Arabia are understood to be offering steep discounts to some customers to maintain their market shares.

"The big overhang {in inventories} is in the sour crudes," said Marshall Thomas, editor in chief of the Oil Daily.

Despite the surplus of sour crudes, West Texas Intermediate and other light crudes still would have held firm in price if the gasoline market had remained as strong as expected, analysts said. But there suddenly are signs of new weakness in the gasoline market.

As a result, prices of gasoline are coming down, at least at the wholesale level. The futures price of unleaded gasoline for May delivery fell 1.57 cents a gallon on the New York Mercantile Exchange yesterday, settling at 63.29 cents. The June contract fell 1.71 cents to 62.16 cents.

"We are looking for a decline in gasoline demand this year," said Mr. Brown of Kidder Peabody.
