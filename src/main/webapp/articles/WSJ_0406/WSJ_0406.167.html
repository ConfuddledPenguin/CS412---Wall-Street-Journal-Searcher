Foreign Exchange: Dollar and Mark Weaken Against Yen; Trading Range Is Extremely Narrow

  • Author: Randolph Walerius Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/06/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar and mark slid against the yen on further squaring of long dollar positions against the Japanese unit ahead of the Group of Seven industrial nations meeting tomorrow in Paris. The dollar finished generally softer against other major currencies in trading that grew so narrow in the U.S. that one dealer was even reluctant to describe it as moving within ranges.

New York traders had walked into a trap Wednesday, buying dollars on rumors of financial difficulties at Japanese securities houses and expectations of another plunge in the Tokyo stock market, said Dan Brooks, chief dealer at Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank Ltd. in New York. When the Japanese didn't follow through by pushing the dollar up early yesterday, the New York dealers had to dump their dollars, he said.

Tokyo traders gave the yen a strong boost even during their morning activity, despite a decline of 1,088.94 points in the Nikkei stock index during the morning. The index finished the day down 193.88 points, at 28,249.06.

Traders in Tokyo, where most of the U.S. unit's slide occurred yesterday, also attributed the yen's advance to growing sentiment that a correction is under way after the dollar's recent surge to 160 yen.

The market is watching closely for signs that the trend has turned, said Christa Bleyleben, vice president at the Bank of Boston in Boston. Dealers aren't sure what might emerge from the U.S.-Japanese trade talks in Washington this week and from the G-7 meeting, she said. "If in doubt, be careful."

And after the U.S. unit's surge from the mid-140-yen level in February to 160 yen this week, she said participants are taking profits.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6995 marks, unchanged from late Wednesday, and at 157.40 yen, down from 158.95 yen late Wednesday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6422, up from $1.6401 Wednesday.

The dollar opened in Tokyo Friday at 156.90 yen, down from the close in Tokyo Thursday of 157.90 yen.

Many dealers said it will be difficult for the G-7 to agree on a means to prop up the yen, particularly because of doubts about the risks in the two most likely tools to do so. Recent intervention hasn't succeeded and a Japanese interest-rate increase could jolt Tokyo stock markets further.

Jack Griffin, assistant vice president and corporate trader at Banque Indosuez in New York, said the currency market is especially jittery ahead of the G-7 meeting because of recent events. He cited Germany's unification and nervousness in Japanese markets.

Dai-Ichi's Mr. Brooks said he expects the G-7 to agree on intervention to show unity. He added that if nothing else changes, the market will view central bank dollar sales as an opportunity to buy dollars at a lower price.

And Mr. Griffin said that there is some speculation in the market about central bank intervention that would involve mark sales against yen rather than just the more common dollar sales.

The mark slid to 92.66 yen in late New York trading from 93.49 yen late Wednesday.

The dollar finished mixed in Tokyo. The U.S. unit ended at 157.90 yen, down from 158.90 yen late Wednesday in Tokyo, and it ended at 1.7008 marks yesterday in Tokyo, up from 1.6971 marks the day before.

The dollar ended European dealing generally steady against the major currencies, although the U.S. unit skidded moderately lower against the yen.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery dropped $2.30 to $374.80 an ounce in moderate trading. Estimated volume was three million ounces.

In Hong Kong, gold opened Friday at $378.25 an ounce.
