Big Board Specialists' Return on Capital Plunged to Record Low of 5.9% Last Year

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 04/06/90

NEW YORK -- The Wall Street recession has found its way inside the New York Stock Exchange, where last year's return on market makers' capital plunged to 5.9%, the lowest level on record. The specialists, the 46 firms that make markets in individual stocks on the Big Board floor, found their after-tax annualized return on capital fell to about half the 1988 level of 11.7%.

The latest return is "the lowest since the exchange began reporting these figures in 1981," said a spokeswoman for the exchange.

The small return underscores the price wars sweeping the Wall Street stock-trading business and the pressures faced by many brokerage firms, who pay the specialists commissions. It also reflects the growing competition faced by the Big Board from regional and off-exchange trading.

"It is indicative of the same squeezes in the rest of the industry," said Robert B. Fagenson, president of the specialist firm Fagenson & Co. "Volume is down and trading opportunities are down. Our revenue is someone else's expense, and when our customers, the brokerage firms, are under tremendous strains, we are one of the sitting ducks."

In the past, Big Board specialists have had years of stupendous returns. In 1987, they finished with a 15.5% return on capital despite the October crash. The best year on record is 1983, when they earned 17.4%.

But recently, specialists and Wall Street brokers have been feuding over trading costs. The big brokers argue that their clients incessantly demand lower commissions. In turn, the brokers want the trading-floor firms to cut fees. Goldman, Sachs & Co. in January even started shipping orders of 5,000 shares or fewer to the Midwest Stock Exchange to pressure the floor firms to cut rates, though specialists say that after about a month Goldman sent the orders back to the Big Board.

"The {specialists'} portion of the commission pie is in most cases higher than it should be," says Dudley Eppel, head of equity trading at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette. "Rates on the floor have not come down the way they have upstairs."

The pressure is still on, though negotiations are quieter these days. Trading volume has stunted commissions so far in 1990 for both the "upstairs" traders and the floor firms. Average daily New York Stock Exchange volume fell 3.7% in the first quarter to 159.2 million shares from 165.3 million in the year-earlier period. Also, some brokerage firms are parting with their traditional alliance to the Big Board and sending orders to regional exchanges and off-exchange market makers where trades are cheaper.

In another sign that times are tough on the Big Board floor, New York Stock Exchange seat prices are falling sharply this year. In February, a seat was sold for $351,000, the lowest price since January 1985.

Despite the gloom, a few brokerage and specialist executives think the bottom of the Wall Street recession may be in sight.

"Wall Street is going through a cycle that we've been through many times before," says Donald Stone, New York Stock Exchange vice chairman and senior partner at the specialist firm Lasker, Stone & Stern. "In the 1990s, there will be the realization that equities are the place to be and we will go into a very big stock market."

Charles B. Milligan, co-director of PaineWebber Group Inc.'s equity department, which recently bought a Big Board specialist firm, adds: "The New York Stock Exchange has had a lot more difficult times than it's weathering now. If a specialist firm is a franchise, and if you staff it correctly, you should be able to make a great deal of money."
