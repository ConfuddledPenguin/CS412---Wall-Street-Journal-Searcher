Big Retailers' March Sales Were Weak --- Sluggish Demand Is Cited In Durable Goods, Along With Late 1990 Easter

  • Author: Francine Schwadel
  • Date: 04/06/90

Several of the nation's big retailers reported weak sales for March, reflecting this year's later Easter holiday season and continued sluggish demand for some durable goods. Analysts estimate sales at stores open at least a year rose an average of less than 3% from a year earlier, or less than the inflation rate. The figures are sketchier than usual because a number of large chains with different fiscal calendars won't report March sales until next week. They include K mart Corp., Dayton Hudson Corp., May Department Stores Inc. and Limited Inc.

"The weak hard-goods sales reflect a somewhat flat consumer economy across the country," maintained Thomas Tashjian of Seidler Amdec Securities Inc. in Los Angeles.

He predicted a stronger showing next week when department stores and specialty stores that rely more heavily on clothing sales report their results. "The early warm weather really helped," he said. "Business for apparel retailers is coming in better than management planned."

With Easter's shift into April this year from late March, some analysts favor combining the two months for comparison purposes. "When all is said and done, you're going to see a same-store increase averaging 6%," predicted Robert Buchanan of Alex. Brown & Sons Inc. in Baltimore.

After adjusting for inflation, which he believes is increasing at a rate of around 4% or 5%, Mr. Buchanan predicted little growth in sales. "Consumer spending is still slow," he said. "Disposable income growth has slowed. People seem determined to save at least part of their income."

Sears, Roebuck & Co., the nation's largest retailer, posted a 1.6% gain in U.S. same-store sales, after excluding discontinued items from its year-earlier results. A Sears spokesman, however, said executives were "very pleased" with the showing because last year's figures were inflated by an advertising blitz that accompanied the launch of Sears's everyday low-price policy.

Woolworth Corp. said its U.S. same-store sales dropped 3.7%. Same-store sales at Ames Department Stores Inc. fell 13.5%. Montgomery Ward & Co. said same-store sales rose 1% and Melville Corp. said its sales rose 2.8%.

Wal-Mart Stores Inc. and J.C. Penney Co. reported the strongest showings. Same-store sales at Wal-Mart, the fast-growing discounter that has been gaining market share, rose 12%. Penney rang up an 8.7% increase in same-store sales, partly because it compensated for the later Easter by moving up its 25% discount promotion for credit card customers to March from April last year.
