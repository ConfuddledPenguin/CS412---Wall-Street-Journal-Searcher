World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Manage to Avoid Another Plunge; London Shares Advance in a Lackluster Session

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 04/06/90

Tokyo stocks managed to avoid another plunge, helped by a strong rebound in the last hour. The Nikkei index of 225 issues fell 193.88 points to finish at 28249.06. The index lost 316.78 points Wednesday. In trading Friday, the index rebounded to rise 899.11 points, or 3.2%, in the morning session to 29148.17.

In London, meanwhile, stocks Thursday finished a lackluster session firmer and at intraday peaks, with little motivation to pilot the market.

The Nikkei index plunged 1200 points midday because of a fast-paced sell-off spawned by growing concern about reported financial troubles of speculative groups.

Individual investors dumped their holdings beginning in the morning, regardless of whether or not the shares were related to speculative groups, traders said.

Lack of confidence in the market and fear that further price drops would increase the burden of additional deposit for margin buying hastened selling by individuals, analysts said.

Fully stifled buying activity in the morning left a number of shares unpriced.

However, after rounds of sell-offs were complete, stock prices gradually turned upward, helped by a return of buying activity led by the Big Four Japanese brokerage houses, participants said.

Some stocks, which are believed to be held by speculator groups and which have been falling sharply in the past week, were pushed up by such dealer buying, which appeared to be an effort to support the market, said a trader at Jardine Fleming (Securities).

The Nikkei increased its upward pace as institutional investors and investment trust funds started to hunt for bargains among high-tech and pharmaceutical shares, as well as among some large-capital shares, traders said.

Also, index-linked buying helped push up the Nikkei later in the afternoon session, traders said.

They said that since the Nikkei fell sharply in the morning due to a lack of buy orders, a small amount of buying was able to push up the index.

Volume on the first section was estimated at 580 million shares, down slightly from 596 million shares Wednesday. Losers outnumbered gainers 648 to 185, while 44 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which lost 35.15 points Wednesday, was down 17.14 points, or 0.83%, to finish at 2058.82.

The impact of the sell-off by individual investors hit the second section severely, reflecting that the stocks in the section are largely held by individual investors. The second section index, which fell 95.45 points Wednesday, plunged 176.70 points, or 5.06%, to 3313.92. Volume was estimated at eight million shares, down from 11 million shares.

Rumors that speculative groups are having financial difficulties because of the continuous decline of stock prices this year have been whispered now and then among participants in the past month, traders said.

Wednesday, rumors that the groups' financial conditions might be worsening to levels that would force them to drastically reduce their holdings spawned a sell-off of speculative shares.

And then yesterday, investor uncertainty was fueled by a local newspaper's report that about 30 small-sized and midsized securities companies suffered defaults on payments for stock purchases by one of the speculative groups, totaling 30 billion yen ($188.8 million).

All sorts of rumors swept through the market in the morning, including that these securities firms are being rocked and that a branch manager of a securities company committed suicide.

During the lunch break, the Tokyo Stock Exchange denied such rumors, calling them "totally unfounded" and urged investors not to be carried away by them.

In trading yesterday, Honshu Paper ended at a bid of 1420 yen, up 160 yen. Nippon Beet Sugar gained 100 yen to 790 yen.

Sankyo closed at 2430 yen, up 160 yen.

Ono Pharmaceutical was up 210 yen to 4850 yen.

Large-capital shares fared well. Nippon Steel was up 30 yen to 540 yen. Sumitomo Metal was up 22 yen at 520 yen. Toshiba gained 60 yen to 1090 yen, while Hitachi finished at 1650 yen, up 70 yen.

In London, dealers said today marks the end of a difficult two-week trading account. Investors were content to close out positions and wait until the next account opens, the dealers said.

Some traders said investors were still digesting the ramifications of Tokyo's gyrations, with whispers that should some large Japanese investors find themselves short of cash, they might have to liquidate British equity positions to meet margin calls.

Investors "haven't got a clear picture of what's happening in Tokyo so they're saying they'd rather not risk the money," said one chief dealer. "Also, we seem to be in quite a tight range where we're running into buyers at 2222 {on the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 share index} but above that, there's nothing there."

At the close, the FT-SE 100 share index was up 7.9 points at 2239.5, its intraday best level and well above its midmorning minimum of 2222. Some market watchers contended that the index's early 10-point drop and subsequent eight-point gain underscore the market's dearth of direction.

The Financial Times 30 share index gained 6.6 points at 1756.3. Volume was a moderate 499.5 million shares, compared with 505.4 million shares Wednesday.

There was little corporate news to enliven the market. In fact, the next major company results aren't expected until well into next week. Of these, food retailer Tesco added 3.5 pence to 198 pence (6 cents to $3.25) ahead of Wednesday's full-year earnings results. British Aerospace surged 18 pence to 528 pence on news that its Rover Group unit saw its sales rise 33% to 31,963 from 23,989 in February and 7.2% from 29,809 a year earlier. Rover's market share edged to 15% from 14% in February and 14% a year ago.

Sun Alliance, a British composite insurer, firmed 8.5 pence to 312 pence following Wednesday's earnings announcement. The shares are still being supported by the 22% increase in its full-year dividend, giving investors a good value-for-money argument.

Elsewhere in Europe, prices closed higher in Paris, Zurich, Madrid and Milan, lower in Frankfurt, Oslo and Helsinki, mixed in Stockholm and Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Meanwhile, prices closed lower in Sydney, Wellington, Manila and Singapore. Hong Kong, Taipei and Seoul were closed.
