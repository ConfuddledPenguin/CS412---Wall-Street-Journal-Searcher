Politics & Policy: Cheney Urges Cut In Congress Control On Defense Budget

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 04/06/90

WASHINGTON -- In the face of spreading opposition to his budget, Defense Secretary Dick Cheney counterattacked by calling for sharply reduced congressional control over the details of Pentagon spending. The sweeping proposals by Mr. Cheney take aim at some of the most cherished prerogatives of lawmakers, including annual authorization of big-ticket weapons purchases and line-item review of the entire Pentagon budget. The package also contains specific legislative proposals to make it easier for the Pentagon to encourage or force thousands of officers out of active service as military spending shrinks in the next few years.

Many of the radical ideas, especially at a time of escalating bipartisan criticism of Mr. Cheney, are almost certain to languish in Congress.

But by focusing attention on lawmakers' own shortcomings and parochial concerns in shaping the defense budget, Mr. Cheney apparently hopes to build public support for the Bush administration's beleaguered spending blueprint.

Urging Congress to concentrate on "broad missions rather than on individual items of hardware," Mr. Cheney told reporters that the current system often leads to continual battles over which congressional districts benefit from weapons contracts. "If next year's budget debate is only a few days away from the end of this year's debate," he said, lawmakers have "no reason to accept a negative decision as final."

Barely two hours earlier, however, two of Mr. Cheney's former allies in the GOP deserted his budget proposal. Sens. John McCain of Arizona and William Cohen of Maine, both important swing votes on the Armed Services Committee, called for defense cuts about twice as large as those proposed by the White House through 1995.

Mr. Cheney's most controversial proposals, if enacted, would fundamentally change the relationship between the military and the defense industry. One section of his proposed legislation, for instance, envisions a major curtailing of on-site government monitoring at selected major weapons plants.

Instead of "assuming there is a dishonest atmosphere out there," Deputy Defense Secretary Donald Atwood said, the Pentagon wants to test the concept of "reducing the degree of inspections and audits that we put on these contractors." Pentagon critics, including members of the House leadership, are almost certain to keep such proposals from reaching the floor.

Mr. Cheney, who repeatedly said his proposals weren't intended to set up confrontations with lawmakers, tried in other ways to placate Congress. He confirmed that the Navy's Seawolf nuclear attack submarines and Arleigh Burke-class destroyers, which carry a total price tag of about $40 billion, have been included in a previously reported review of the Pentagon's most expensive high-technology weapons.
