Goodyear to Post Sharp Decrease In Quarterly Net

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 04/10/90

AKRON, Ohio -- Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co. will report sharply lower profit for the first quarter, but expects results to improve in subsequent quarters, Tom H. Barrett, chairman and chief executive officer, said after the annual meeting. "We made money" in the quarter, he said, but he declined to make a specific estimate.

However, Harry W. Millis, an analyst with McDonald & Co., Cleveland, who attended the meeting, said he expects the company to report first-quarter net of between 25 cents and 50 cents a share. In the 1989 first quarter, Goodyear earned $94.5 million, or $1.64 a share, on sales of $2.64 billion.

First-quarter profit was reduced by lower tire demand from auto manufacturers, which resulted in temporary plant closings at Goodyear, Mr. Barrett said. "Severe pricing competition in the North American and European replacement markets" also cut into profit, he said.

Mr. Barrett said he doesn't expect Goodyear to close plants in the second quarter, barring a sudden cutback in auto production.

In addition, moves by the Brazilian government to brake inflation in that country are hurting Goodyear's sizable subsidiary in that country, he said. And the company is incurring start-up costs on a tire plant in Canada.

Furthermore, the company's oil pipeline whacked first-quarter earnings by about 50 cents a share, he said. The pipeline's losses weren't reflected in the 1989 first-quarter report.

The pipeline will produce "a quarterly loss of approximately 50 cents per share until it is more fully utilized," Mr. Barrett said at the meeting. "The situation won't get worse," he said, and "there is hope that it could improve earnings substantially by 1992."

The problem, Mr. Barrett said, is that offshore California oil production isn't sufficient to fill the pipeline, which was built to move crude oil from California to refineries on the Gulf Coast.

Mr. Barrett said after the meeting that Goodyear has suspended efforts to sell the pipeline because there's no market for it under present conditions. He said he is hopeful that further drilling by Exxon Corp. and other producers will provide additional offshore crude. Furthermore, some producers, such as Chevron Corp., that currently use tankers to move West Coast crude may be required to switch to the pipeline, he said, because of the danger of tanker spills and other damage to the environment.

At the meeting he outlined a series of actions the company has taken to make its tire marketing more efficient and more effective, including introduction of lower-priced tires carrying the Goodyear brand. "In the U.S. retail operations alone, we have reduced our work force by about 800 people," he said.
