Takeover Lawyer Suggests Delay in Voting by Holders

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/10/90

With proxy fights proliferating, takeover defense lawyer Martin Lipton has a new idea for companies worried about their directors being voted out of office: don't let shareholders vote as often. Mr. Lipton, a partner at Wachtell Lipton Rosen & Katz, suggests that directors ought to be elected to terms of five years, instead of one or three, in what he calls a "quinquennial system." Under his proposal, the annual meeting would be eliminated, and shareholders would only meet every five years.

Currently, most companies either elect all their directors annually, or have staggered boards, with one-third up for election each year. Staggered boards came into widespread use early in the 1980s as an anti-takeover defense.

"We need to strike a balance," Mr. Lipton said in a memo to clients. "A balance that facilitates long-term planning by corporations but does not entrench bad management." With such a system, he added, "directors would run on the corporation's record for the past five years and the corporation's strategic plan for the next five years."

However, Mr. Lipton acknowledged the idea would require changes in state laws, federal securities laws and stock exchange rules.
