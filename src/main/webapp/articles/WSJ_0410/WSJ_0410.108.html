Monday's Markets: Nail-Biting By Investors Sets Tone --- Dow Edges Up 4.95 But Bonds Decline; Trading Is Uninspired

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 04/10/90

Stock and bond prices moved in narrow ranges as investors started a new week worried about first-quarter earnings and a hefty new supply of government debt scheduled for auction this week. The Dow Jones Industrial Average edged up 4.95 points, to 2722.07. But declining issues on the New York Stock Exchange were ahead of gainers and volume was anemic. Long-term Treasury bond prices slipped in thin trading.

While currency traders scrambled to interpret the outcome of the weekend meeting of the Group of Seven industrial nations, neither bond nor stock investors were much influenced by the dollar's gyrations. Instead, analysts said investors in each market had enough to worry about without trying to track the currency.

The stock market's overriding concern remains first-quarter earnings. Last week, bank stocks were hammered by prospects of disappointing quarterly results, providing a preview of what is in store for companies that surprise shareholders with less-than-expected profits. Those stocks rebounded somewhat yesterday after some analysts indicated conditions affecting banks don't warrant the gloomy outlook.

Nevertheless, the stock market probably has some rough going ahead. Although analysts have been steadily revising estimates of corporate earnings lower during recent weeks, investors have reacted mostly with caution, standing pat with their holdings rather than selling.

One indication of the deep uncertainty afflicting investors is that stock prices barely budged in New York after a sharp rally in Tokyo. The Nikkei Index of 225 stocks rose 1119.15, or 3.8%, to 30397.93 in a broad-based rally. The best the Dow Jones Industrial Average could muster in early trading yesterday was a gain of about 12 points, much of it because of computer-assisted buy programs, that faded later in the morning.

Bond prices didn't fare as well. An early plunge in the dollar in Far East trading set the stage for some selling as trading opened in New York. But as the currency rebounded, bond prices recouped some of their losses.

The big factor overhanging the bond market, however, is the auction today of $3.5 billion in Resolution Funding Corp. 40year bonds, followed tomorrow by the sale of $7.5 billion in seven-year Treasury notes. Traders said prices fell because market participants aren't sure how wellreceived the two auctions will be.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose in light trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 115.0 million shares. Declining issues on the Big Board were ahead of gainers 839 to 652.

Bond prices were off. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell a little more than a quarter of a point, or more than $2.50 for each $1,000 face amount. The yield on the issue rose to 8.54%.
