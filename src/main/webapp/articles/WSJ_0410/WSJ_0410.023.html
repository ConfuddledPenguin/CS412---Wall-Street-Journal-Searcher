Letters to the Editor: Private Firms Invented Mail Service in 1840s

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/10/90

Regarding your March 7 page-one article "Letter Bomb: Postal Service Again Asks for Rate Increase as Automation Lags," I was struck by the horrible mistake the nation made back in 1845 when it got rid of the private mail companies. In the 1840s, private mail companies were inventing mail service in America. The government would not deliver letters to and from the post office, so private carriers undertook to do this for a penny for each letter. They innovated the use of letter boxes so letters could be deposited conveniently throughout the city. They started the use of postage stamps so that you could prepay the postage of letters deposited in a box. And they instituted three-times- and then four-times-a-day delivery. Best of all, the private carriers, in competition with the moribund post office of that day, cut the cost of mail delivery between cities, in some cases to one-fifth of the post-office rate. It was a vibrant time of progress in mail service, which was ended only by Congress's giving the Postal Service a monopoly.

Jack R. Sanders

San Diego

I couldn't help but think that a large contributing factor behind the proposed rate increase is the frank; not Postmaster General Anthony Frank, but the free mailing privileges given to every member of Congress.

By making congressmen pay for their mailings like everyone else, the rate increase probably could be cut in half, and simultaneously we would eliminate a large amount of junk mail.

Mark J. Skorcz

Cincinnati
