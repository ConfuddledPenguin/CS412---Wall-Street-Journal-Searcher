Media: Turner Joins Tribune Co. In Venture

  • Author: Glenn Ruffenach and Laura Landro
  • Date: 04/10/90

ATLANTA -- In the latest step bringing cable operators and broadcasters closer together, Turner Broadcasting System Inc. and Tribune Broadcasting Co. entered into a 10-year pact to produce and share both news and entertainment programming. While the cable and broadcasting industries traditionally are adversaries, the two sides increasingly are cooperating in order to improve programming and cut costs.

The Turner-Tribune pact provides for the two companies to collaborate on news, documentaries, specials and original long-form productions, including two miniseries a year. Financial terms of the agreement weren't disclosed.

The two companies said they plan to produce quarterly news specials on a wide range of subjects, including environmental issues, drugs and crime. "Hopefully, there will be an attempt to provide stories with both local and national interest," said Russell Barry, president of Turner Program Services, the world-wide distribution subsidiary of Turner Broadcasting.

The agreement also calls for all Tribune television stations to become CNN television affiliates, in which both companies will be able to share news services and stories.

The two miniseries, according to the agreement, would be produced for Turner's TBS Superstation and the Tribune stations, as well as for general domestic syndication. While Turner previously has worked with independent producers in developing entertainment programming, "This is the first time we've worked with a station group," said Mr. Barry.

The venture isn't the first time that a cable company and a broadcaster have joined forces. In late 1988, General Electric Co.'s National Broadcasting Co. unit and Cablevision Systems Corp. formed a joint venture to develop entertainment programming and regional sports and news networks for cable. NBC contributed its fledgling cable network, Consumer News and Business Channel (CNBC), to the venture and provided capital to Cablevision as well.

The cable industry and the broadcast industry are adversaries on many issues -- in Washington, for example, broadcasters are lobbying hard for re-regulation of the cable industry. But increasingly, as cable reaches more households and needs higher quality programming, both sides are finding it advantageous to team up to produce and distribute programming.

Tribune Broadcasting, a unit of Chicago-based Tribune Co., has used its own station group, the largest in the country, to launch new programming. But the company also sells its programs to other independent and network-affiliated or network-owned stations and has also supplied programs to cable networks, home video and international markets. Its shows run the gamut from "U.S. Farm Report" to "Soul Train."

For its part, Turner held talks this year with both CBS Inc. and NBC concerning possible joint news-gathering operations. Neither set of talks, however, has yielded any agreements.

The companies announced their joint venture after the markets closed yesterday. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, Tribune Co. rose $1.875 to $43.50. In American Stock Exchange composite trading, Turner's Class A shares closed at $44.875, up 12.5 cents.
