International: Top Soviet Aide Sees Opposition To Market Plan

  • Author: Peter Gumbel
  • Date: 04/10/90

MOSCOW -- The Soviet government expects powerful public opposition to its plans to switch to a market economy. But Leonid Abalkin, deputy prime minister in charge of economic reform, says "there is no alternative" if the U.S.S.R. is to remain a major world power. At a news conference, Mr. Abalkin confirmed that the government will soon present a package of measures that includes a sweeping reform of prices, selling off state industries and revamping the nation's banking system. He also said the government may buy consumer goods in the West to ease the pain caused by the measures, but ruled out any quick moves to make the Soviet currency, the ruble, convertible.

The Soviet leadership is expected to announce details of the package this month, and officials have been issuing warnings about painful and unpopular measures. Some labor leaders already say the plans won't be acceptable to the population, which is accustomed to guaranteed employment and heavily subsidized food and consumer goods.

Alexei Sergeyev, an economics professor at the Labor Unions' Higher School, said the government package may include a two-year ban on strikes to head off potential social unrest.

Mr. Abalkin said the government has already taken the "strategic decision" to switch to what he termed a regulated market economy. "The working population will probably resent it, as will much of the "apparat" (bureaucracy)," he acknowledged. "But there's no avoiding the opposition, we'll simply have to face it."

He stressed that the package will differ considerably from the economic "shock therapy" adopted by Poland early this year. The Soviet measures will try to cushion the blows by tying social benefits to inflation, protecting low-income citizens and reforming prices in stages, not all at once. "We want to raise prices but not kill the consumer," Mr. Abalkin said. "We don't want to bring him to his knees."

Although he didn't give details, many of the measures he outlined are similar to ones he proposed last fall. At the time, his advice was largely ignored by the Soviet government of Prime Minister Nikolai Ryzhkov, who opted for a much more cautious economic program that relied heavily on continued central planning.

But Mr. Abalkin said the government now accepted the need to break clearly with the planned economy and move rapidly toward a market system. "It is impossible to set up an efficient economy without using market mechanisms," he said. "Balancing on two stools is impossible."
