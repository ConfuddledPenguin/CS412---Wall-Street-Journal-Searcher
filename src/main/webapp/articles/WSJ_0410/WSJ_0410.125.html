Abreast of the Market: Stocks Finish Mixed in a Session That Is Second Lightest of Year

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 04/10/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks closed mixed in one of the lightest trading days of the year. The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 4.95 points to 2722.07. Broader averages also gained: Standard & Poor's 500-stock index rose 1.29 to 341.37. But 839 issues declined on the New York Stock Exchange while only 652 advanced. Big Board volume totaled 114,970,000 shares, the second-lightest trading day of the year. The lowest volume in 1990 so far occurred March 12, when 114,790,000 shares changed hands.

The slow trading reflected in part the absence of many investors because of Passover and Good Friday observances this week, traders said. U.S. exchanges will be closed on Friday for the latter.

But concerns about first-quarter earnings reports are also keeping some investors away from stocks, said Eugene Takach, fund manager at Eagle Investment Associates, a money-management subsidiary of the First National Bank of Boston.

Goodyear said yesterday it would report lower first-quarter earnings and the stock slipped 1/2 to 35 3/4. The company cited a drop in sales to auto makers, "severe pricing competition" in both North America and Europe, and "a sizable drop" in profit from Brazil.

Investors looking for a place to hide from bad earnings reports bought shares of consumer-products and food companies.

Quaker Oats gained 1 1/4 to 50 3/4. First Boston boosted the stock's investment rating to "buy" from "hold." Elsewhere in the food group, Hershey Foods rose 1 to 33 5/8, Kellogg gained 1 1/2 to 63 5/8, CPC International added 1 1/8 to 70 7/8, and Ralston Purina rose 1 to 85 3/8.

Bank stocks formed another leading industry group. A Heard on the Street column in yesterday's Wall Street Journal suggested that many bank issues were oversold. Also, Charles Peabody, senior bank analyst at Kidder Peabody, raised investment ratings on several bank stocks yesterday, with buy recommendations on J.P. Morgan, Mellon Bank, and Norwest Corp.

"We decided {the bad news} was well washed out," said Mr. Peabody. "A stronger economy in the second half of the year should allow credit quality fears to recede and earnings momentum to pick up."

Some investors remained cautious, however. "I think bank stocks deserve a bounce, but we haven't heard all the bad news yet," warned Richard Pike, bank analyst at Chancellor Capital Management.

J.P. Morgan jumped 1 1/8 to 35 3/4, Mellon Bank gained 7/8 to 25, but Norwest slipped 1/4 to 18 3/8.

Citicorp rose 3/4 to 23 5/8 on volume of 1.5 million shares, Chase Manhattan gained 1/2 to 28 1/8, and Chemical Banking rose 3/8 to 23 1/4, Manufacturers Hanover climbed 5/8 to 31 5/8, and BankAmerica gained 7/8 to 26 3/8 on one million shares.

Regional bank stocks followed suit.

Bank of New York climbed 1 to 31, First Fidelity Bancorp advanced 1/2 to 19, NCNB rallied 3/4 to 36 1/8, and MNC Financial rose 7/8 to 16 7/8.

Federal National Mortgage Association climbed 1 3/8 to 33 3/8 on 1.2 million shares and Federal Home Loan Mortgage gained 1 3/8 to 66 5/8. The concerns about shaky real-estate loans that spurred last week's selling in the banking sector had also buffeted those issues.

PepsiCo climbed 1 1/2 to 66 1/2. The company extended its reciprocal trade agreement with the Soviet Union through the year 2000 and said it plans to boost its sales in the country by upgrading and expanding its operations there.

Coca-Cola, Pepsi's principal rival, gained 1 3/8 to 79 1/4 to contribute to the advance in the industrial average. Other consumer stocks that lifted the average included Philip Morris, which went up 5/8 to 41 7/8; Merck, up 5/8 to 73 3/8, and Procter & Gamble, up 1 1/8 to 69 3/4.

Household International fell 1 1/4 to 49 1/2 on speculation that its first-quarter results would include a write-off related to its commercial real-estate portfolio. The company denied the rumors.

Telecom USA soared 16 1/4 to 38 1/8 as 3.6 million shares were traded. MCI Communications agreed to acquire its long-distance competitor for $42 a share, or about $1.25 billion. MCI eased 1 1/8 to 35 3/4 on the national over-the-counter market.

Windmere, which won a $30 million judgment in an antitrust case against N.V. Philips, added 1 to 8 1/8. Under antitrust law, the damage award will automatically be tripled. Philips slid 1/4 to 21 1/4.

UAL fell 2 1/2 to 161. In reiterating a sell recommendation on the stock, Goldman Sachs analyst Glenn D. Engel said the buy-out offer by the three main unions at United Airlines doesn't have a high probability of succeeding unless the unions can install "a proven management team" or find a substantial equity partner, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

MGM/UA Communications climbed 1 5/8 to 18 1/4 on news that Time Warner's movie unit would arrange a $650 million bridge loan for Pathe Communications' use in its $20-a-share takeover offer for the company. Pathe gained 1/2 to 4 3/4; Time Warner added 7/8 to 93 7/8.

Network Equipment Technologies plummeted 5 3/4 to 15 1/8. After saying last week that earnings for the fiscal fourth quarter ended in March could fall short of the year-earlier 33 cents a share, the company gave its specific forecast for the latest period: about 10 cents a share. Also, the company noted that shipments made in connection with an order found to be invalid would depress its latest results.

Ames Department Stores fell 1/4 to 3 1/4 and traded as low as 2 3/4, a 52-week low, as 4.1 million shares changed hands. The company has delayed its earnings report for fiscal 1990 at least twice; analysts have said uncertainty tied to the delays is behind the stock's fall.

De Soto dropped 1 1/8 to 43 1/8. Sutton Holdings, which is offering $50 a share for the company, said a commitment from Creditanstalt to provide $215 million in financing for its tender offer expired over the weekend.

Amre fell 5/8 to 6 7/8. The company said a management group that holds 56% of its common shares decided against making a planned offer of $8.75 a share for the rest.

Tucson Electric Power slipped 3/4 to 13 after its auditors, Deloitte & Touche, said in a letter to shareholders that the company faces a number of contingent liabilities and other uncertainties.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.33 to 359.68. Volume totaled 12,560,000 shares, compared with 15,060,000 shares Friday. There were 240 issues advancing, 330 declining and 247 were unchanged.

Frederick's of Hollywood gained 7/8 to 15 7/8. The company reported net income of 54 cents a share for the fiscal second quarter ended March 3, compared with 41 cents a share a year earlier.
