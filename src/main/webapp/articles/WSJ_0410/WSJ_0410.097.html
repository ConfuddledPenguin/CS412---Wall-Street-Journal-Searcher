Foreign Exchange: G-7 Remarks Fail to Bolster Sagging Yen

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 04/10/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar rose against the Japanese yen yesterday, shrugging off modest intervention by central banks and a weekend statement of concern about the yen's weakness from the major industrial nations. Most of the big action occurred in early Far Eastern trading, where the dollar bounced like a yo-yo because of differing interpretations of a Saturday communique from finance ministers and central bank heads of the Group of Seven countries. The dollar was mixed against other currencies.

The dollar initially jumped 1.3% to 159.65 yen in very early New Zealand trading, on initial disappointment that the communique wasn't more forceful in supporting Japan's currency. Later in Tokyo, the dollar tumbled as low as 155.70 on Japanese newspaper reports that the G-7 would intervene heavily to boost the sagging yen.

It didn't happen.

In late New York trading, the dollar wound up just 0.4% ahead at 158.23 yen, compared with 157.65 yen late Friday. The U.S. currency was marginally lower at 1.6935 West German marks, compared with 1.6950 marks before the weekend. The British pound fell to $1.6338 from $1.6425 on Friday.

Early Tuesday in Tokyo, the yen quickly gave up gains it had made Monday in Tokyo, plus some. The dollar was trading at 158.08 yen at midmorning, down slightly from the New York close but up sharply from the Monday close of 156.45 yen in Tokyo and even higher than last Friday's close of 156.47 yen. Monday's optimistic expectations of coordinated central bank intervention were gone by the market's opening because such action failed to materialize overnight, and Tokyo traders were much more cautious.

Following a one-day meeting in Paris, representatives of the U.S., Japan, West Germany, France, Britain, Italy and Canada said that the yen's recent decline against the dollar and European currencies has "undesirable consequences." The G-7 also reaffirmed their commitment to economic policy coordination, including "cooperation in exchange markets."

The G-7, however, failed to agree on a Japanese request for a coordinated interest-rate package to support the yen. This package would have included lower U.S. and West German rates, steps that theoretically would reduce the allure of the dollar, the mark and other European currencies tied to the mark.

The Bank of Japan did sell dollars for yen, and the intervention was followed later in the day by the central banks of Britain, West Germany, France and Switzerland and the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. Currency traders weren't especially impressed.

They said the sales -- including those by the Bank of Japan -- were small, looking more like a sign of central-bank solidarity and an attempt to smooth markets than a campaign to push up the yen.

Dealers estimated the combined intervention at a few hundred million dollars, and the Bank of Japan's dollar sales at less than $200 million. That's one-fifth of the estimated $1 billion a day that Japan had been throwing at the rising dollar in past weeks.

Several dealers speculated that Western central banks may have been acting primarily on behalf of the Japanese central bank. "The odds are better than 50-50 that it was Bank of Japan money," said James T. McGroarty, a senior vice president at Greenwich Capital Inc., a Greenwich, Conn., unit of the Long-Term Credit Bank of Japan.

Nonetheless, he said the G-7 communique shouldn't be read as negatively as it has been by many traders. "The joint statement does mean that {the G-7 countries} are concerned," said Mr. McGroarty. "It doesn't necessarily mean they want the dollar at 130 {yen}, but they are concerned about the yen's level and its potential to unsettle world-wide financial markets." Thus, he believes the dollar could decline to about 153 yen.

Richard Witten, head of foreign exchange trading at Goldman, Sachs & Co., isn't so sanguine. "It seems that all the references to Japan and the yen were strained and the product of strained negotiations," he said of the joint statement. "So on its face, reading the communique -- in contrast to other communiques -- shows there is less agreement that markets are incorrectly pricing currencies. And there is less will expressed to make a change in the currency markets."

Since mid-February, the dollar has risen 10% against its Japanese counterpart. Behind the weak yen are a combination of factors, including the recent plunges in Japanese share prices, increased capital flows from Japan to foreign markets and bickering between the Japan's Ministry of Finance and Bank of Japan over whether to raise interest rates.

Confidence in Japan's currency and its monetary authorities also has been eroded as U.S. officials several times rebuffed Tokyo's requests for aid in supporting their currency.

Despite the threat that a weak yen poses for a wider U.S. trade deficit, analysts believe the U.S. is more concerned about domestic inflation. The yen's weakness is viewed primarily as a Japanese problem. That same view appears to be held by West German authorities. A strong dollar helps damp inflationary pressures by reducing import prices, as well as those charged by domestic companies that compete with imports.

Even so, traders don't expect the yen to fall much farther.

After perhaps drifting for a couple of weeks, "the dollar will resume its upward path to the low 160 {yen area}," said Mr. Witten of Goldman Sachs. "Most of the upward movement in the dollar has happened already; there's more, but not a heck of a lot more."

Yoichi Sakaguchi, chief trader at Sanwa Bank Ltd. in New York, said that "people are buying dollars but very cautiously," because they remain fearful of central bank intervention.

He added that although many U.S. banks are focusing on the U.S. currency's positive aspects, "Japanese investor attitude is changing toward the dollar from bullish to cautiously bullish or flat."

Mr. Sakaguchi said that since the April 1 start of the new Japanese fiscal year, "we expected a lot of Japanese interest in buying dollars. But it hasn't occurred, at least so far."

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery dropped $4.70 to $376 an ounce. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.2 million ounces.

In Hong Kong, gold opened Tuesday at $376.69 an ounce.
