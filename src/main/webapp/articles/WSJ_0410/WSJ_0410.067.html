Letters to the Editor: We Don't Spend It Till We Get It

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/10/90

Thank you for conceding the important differences between New York's fiscal problems and those of Massachusetts ("Cuomo's Ghost," Review & Outlook, March 22). In analyzing New York's budget policies, you also acknowledged that New York must address extraordinarily serious social problems, such as AIDS and drug abuse.

With agreement on those important points, we differ only with your commentary regarding the figures you cite.

You stated New York's spending rose by 104% during Gov. Mario M. Cuomo's first seven years in office. That figure is simply inaccurate. The actual increase in New York's spending supported by state taxes during that period, according to the National Association of State Budget Officers, was 69%. That was below the average state budget increase of 71% for the period. Even if federal aid is included, and NASBO didn't include it in either New York's or the other 49 states' figures, New York's growth figure is only 81%.

In addition, U.S. Census figures show that, in 1988, 12 states took in more revenue as a percentage of residents' personal income than New York did.

Furthermore, the increase of 9.3% you cite for this year's proposed budget is misleading in that it includes federal aid. The governor's proposed growth in revenues from state taxes is only 4.49%, a figure below the projected inflation rate.

Finally, we could not agree more with your conclusion that government budgets reflect the political philosophy of chief executives, and we were pleased that you chose to compare Gov. Cuomo with President Ronald Reagan.

President Reagan's philosophy, as reflected in his budgets, was to spend up to $200 billion a year more than you are willing to raise in revenue, and leave the bill for your successors. This philosophy tripled the national debt to $3 trillion during his eight short years in office.

In contrast, Gov. Cuomo's philosophy, as reflected in his budgets, is to raise all the revenue you determine is necessary to meet government's legitimate obligations, and not spend what you do not raise.

Which philosophy is sounder? That's easy. Just ask yourself which one you think President Bush wishes his predecessor had followed.

Tom Conroy

Senior Press Officer to the Governor

Albany, N.Y.
