Ames Stores To Report Loss For Fiscal 1990 --- Company, Hurt by Results At Its Zayre Units, Sees A Deficit of $228 Million

  • Author: Joseph Pereira
  • Date: 04/10/90

Ames Department Stores Inc., hurt by the disastrous results of the Zayre department stores it acquired in 1988, said it plans to close 74 stores and expects to report a $228 million loss for the year ended Jan. 27. Ames, based in Rocky Hill., Conn., said it is negotiating with its banks for a new credit agreement and has hired investment advisers "to assist . . . with a restructuring that will include a capital infusion."

Even though the troubled department-store chain had said it would report a loss for the year, the amount estimated -- which is subject to a final audit -- was larger than anticipated. The company has blamed disappointing sales at its Zayre stores for its lackluster performance.

In fiscal 1989, Ames had net income of $47.2 million, or $1.20 a share, on sales of $3.36 billion.

In heavy trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Ames shares recovered to close at $3.25, down 25 cents, after falling sharply earlier in the day. The loss was disclosed after the market closed.

Ames said the loss will include a charge of about $150 million to cover the costs of closing 74 unprofitable stores, which would reduce the number of stores it operates to 606. The company had posted a loss of $27.7 million, or 99 cents a share, for the first nine months of the fiscal year, indicating that its fourth-quarter loss will be about $200.3 million.

"The operating loss for the year resulted primarily from substantially lower than anticipated sales and gross margins in the former Zayre stores," said Peter Hollis, Ames's chief executive officer. "The profitability of the original Ames stores has been maintained."

When Ames paid $800 million for the 392-store Zayre chain in November 1988, it had high expectations that it could turn the chain around. The cut-price retailer, competing against such chains as Wal-Mart Stores Inc. and K mart Corp., had been battling sagging sales for at least two years prior to the sale.

Ames thought it could do a brisk business if it shed the heavy discounting practice of the former Zayre chain and gave Zayre a new "every-day low price" pitch. Ames closed 74 of the chain's worst stores and spruced up the interior decor of the remaining stores to set it apart from the often disheveled image many Zayre stores presented. With new paint, brighter lighting and quicker computerized cash registers, the stores failed to woo new Zayre customers. The effort, in part was impeded by a souring economy in the Northeast, where much of Ames's business is done.

Still experimenting, Ames last year continued to spiff up its stores, eliminated the Zayre name and rechristened all the stores with the more upscale Ames name. Hope was that the chain would turn the corner last holiday shopping season. To boost financial results, Ames even opened its doors for business on Thanksgiving Day. But the new tactic hasn't yet worked.

Comparable-store sales for December at the remaining units acquired from Zayre were down 13%. Sales at the rest of the chain, however, increased 5%, the company said.

"We were trying to re-educate the Zayre customer but weren't as successful as we had anticipated," said Douglas Ewing, a company spokesman. Results of the fourth quarter and the year have been disappointing to Ames. "The real picture began to unfold as we examined our numbers {in recent weeks}," said Mr. Ewing, "long after we had met with analysts on Jan. 12." "The size of the loss was not known until recently when we started getting details of the full fiscal year. Performance came in well below expectation."

As recently as this month, most analysts had been expecting Ames to report a loss of about $10 million for the year following estimated profit of $12 million for the quarter. Most weren't expecting a $150 million charge.

As for the future, Mr. Ewing expressed confidence that the company would be able to negotiate a new credit agreement with lending institutions later this month. He declined to discuss details or comment on whether Ames was in violation of current lending agreements.

Ames also said it has retained the investment banking firms of Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. and Wertheim Schroder & Co. to help in a restructuring that will include "a capital infusion." The company didn't elaborate.
