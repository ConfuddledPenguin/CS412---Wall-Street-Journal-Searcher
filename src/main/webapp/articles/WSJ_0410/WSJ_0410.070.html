Marketing & Media: Time Warner Pledges to Arrange Half Of Funds to Help Pathe Buy MGM/UA

  • Author: Kathleen A. Hughes and Dennis Kneale
  • Date: 04/10/90

Time Warner Inc. agreed to help arrange financing for about half of the $1.2 billion that Pathe Communications Corp. will need to complete its acquisition of MGM/UA Communications Co. -- even if the debt-laden Time Warner must provide the money itself. Time Warner's Warner Bros. studio said it would "arrange for or provide on a bridge loan basis" a $650 million bank loan to MGM/UA. The sum would be guaranteed by Time Warner, which itself already carries $10.6 billion in debt from its recent merger, and would be secured by the UA film library.

The announcement lessened concern that Pathe, headed by Italian financier Giancarlo Parretti, wouldn't be able to complete the financing for the acquisition. Yesterday, Pathe made the second of four $50 million payments that are required by the purchase agreement.

MGM/UA shares closed at $18.25, up $1.675 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. Pathe, an entertainment company, is offering $20 a share.

"It certainly removes a great deal of the skepticism that the transaction could be completed," said Jeff Logsdon, an analyst at Crowell, Weedon & Co. in Los Angeles. "Now half the foundation is in place, and Pathe has a better-than-even chance of buying the company."

The financial assistance apparently was offered in return for what could grow into a lucrative "support services" agreement for Warner Bros. The studio will handle Pathe and MGM/UA releases for theatrical, television and home-video distribution. Warner would take what some sources said may amount to about half the usual 25% to 30% distribution fee used in most movie-distribution deals, while bearing none of the risks of promoting a film that ends up as a flop.

Moreover, Time Warner also obtained the right to acquire warrants that would amount to a 20% stake in the combined Pathe-MGM/UA company at some point in the future. Specific terms and prices weren't disclosed.

But some Wall Street analysts privately questioned why a company with a large debt burden of its own would risk getting involved in the takeover of MGM/UA, which has been the target of several scuttled merger attempts in the past.

Moreover, Time Warner's involvement may include an additional $200 million -- above and beyond the $650 million in bridge-loan money. In an interview that appeared Monday in Les Echos, France's leading business daily, Mr. Parretti was quoted as saying he expects Time Warner to contribute a total $850 million to the bid for MGM/UA. Executives close to the transaction explained the higher figure by saying Time Warner is expected to provide an additional $200 million for movie production at MGM/UA after the acquisition is completed.

A Warner spokesman had no immediate comment on the $200 million sum. He also countered analysts' doubts about Time Warner's role by saying that the distribution deal would generate substantial cash flow and help Time Warner finances.

In addition, some industry observers noted that by having the $650 million secured by the United Artists film library, Time Warner could pick up the entire film vault for that price -- about half its actual worth -- should the merger go through and the combined companies later collapse.

The Les Echos interview also quoted Mr. Parretti as saying Pathe is currently talking to European and U.S. banks about arranging $300 million in loans for the acquisition. And the story notes that Pathe recently sold British and Dutch theaters for more than $200 million.

The Time Warner announcement yesterday describes a convoluted three-step process for the loan. First, Time Warner would make the loan to MGM/UA, which in turn would acquire certain assets of Pathe Communications. Pathe would then use the money MGM/UA paid it to turn around and acquire all of MGM/UA.

"It's a classic American leveraged buy-out," Mr. Parretti said in the French interview. "You pay with what you buy. I don't understand why all the press is wondering how we will finance the operations."

The announcement seems to end the recent confusion over which studio would be distributing films made by the Pathe Entertainment unit of Pathe Communications. The Pathe unit is headed by Alan Ladd Jr., a former top executive at MGM/UA, and has been working on a string of new movies.

This isn't the first time Warner has backed Pathe, formerly called Cannon Group Inc. Four years ago, Cannon received a $75 million investment from Warner, saving the company from a financial crisis. In return, Warner received certain shares, warrants and options. Moreover, Steve Ross, co-chairman of Time Warner, and Mr. Parretti are friends.
