Politics & Policy: Cosmetics Firms Making Little Effort To Cooperate With FDA, Report Says

  • Author: Timothy Noah
  • Date: 04/10/90

WASHINGTON -- The cosmetics industry is making little effort to comply with the Food and Drug Administration's voluntary regulations, the General Accounting Office said. According to a GAO report, fewer than 40% of cosmetics manufacturers and packers have registered their plants with the FDA, and fewer than 4% filed reports with the agency about product-related injuries. Both figures are slightly below those measured by GAO in 1978, when the congressional watchdog agency last did such a study.

The FDA is authorized by law to oversee cosmetics, but its power over the $18 billion-a-year industry is limited. It doesn't have the authority to approve or disapprove new cosmetics, as it does with drugs. Nor does it have the power to require cosmetics companies to register products and submit information on product-related injuries. Instead, the FDA asks companies to submit this information voluntarily.

"People call up and say, `Why don't you do this? Why don't you do that?'" says Dr. Heinz Eiermann, who heads the FDA's cosmetics division. "All these questions are raised, which are made on the assumption that we have all the authority to get information or require manufacturers to do the testing, which we don't have."

Each year, the FDA receives around 250 consumer complaints about cosmetic products, most of them involving allergic reactions.

The bulk of consumer complaints are made to the companies themselves. Avon Products Inc., which sold 420 million units of cosmetics in the U.S. last year, received 102 complaints -- all allergic reactions. None of these were life-threatening, and all were reported to the FDA, according to a company spokesman.

The GAO found that many cosmetics manufacturers refuse to disclose the results of safety tests to FDA inspectors. But Michael Petrina, vice president for legislative relations at the Cosmetic Toiletry and Fragrance Association, said he knew of no instances where the industry had refused cooperation on "serious safety questions." Mr. Petrina said cosmetics companies are less cooperative when FDA inspectors on routine inspections decide to go on a "fishing expedition."

Mr. Petrina cited a letter sent last year by former FDA commissioner Frank Young praising the industry's willingness to submit data on consumer hazards "of broad public concern."

Rep. Ron Wyden (D., Ore.) disagrees. "This industry seems to be unaccountable," says Rep. Wyden, who headed hearings on cosmetics regulation in 1988 and commissioned the GAO report. Rep. Wyden plans to submit legislation to require cosmetics companies to register with the FDA and disclose safety tests and injury reports.

Mr. Wyden also called for the FDA to review 884 potentially toxic ingredients found in cosmetics. According to the GAO report, industry researchers see "no major safety concerns" with any of them.
