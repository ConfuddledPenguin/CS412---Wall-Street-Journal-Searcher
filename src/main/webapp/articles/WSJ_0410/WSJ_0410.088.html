World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Rise Nearly 4% in Heavy Trading As Yen Surges Amid Mixed Signals on Currency

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli and Masayoshi Kanabayashi
  • Date: 04/10/90

Tokyo share prices soared nearly 4% in heavy trading and the yen strengthened amid conflicting Japanese news reports suggesting Group of Seven countries would seek to bolster the yen. The Nikkei index, an average of 225 key issues, climbed 1119.15 points, or 3.8%, finishing at 30397.93. It was the index's fourth-biggest point gain ever, and extended Friday's 1029.72-point advance. Even though analysts expect the market to turn lower again soon, droves of buyers were in the market. An estimated 800 million shares were traded, off slightly from 838.6 million shares Friday.

At midmorning Tuesday, the Nikkei average was down about 557 points, to 29,840.

Stockbrokers said the main impetus of Monday's rally was a sharp increase in the yen after some national newspapers here reported that finance ministers from the Group of Seven industrialized countries had agreed at a meeting Saturday in Paris to support the currency.

That interpretation of events, implicitly ascribed by the newspapers to sources in the country's Finance Ministry, doesn't jibe with accounts in other Japanese papers or the U.S. and European press. Those stories, quoting a communique from the Group of Seven promising to keep the yen's steep decline this year "under review," indicated that the countries weren't in accord on a plan of action to stop the yen's depreciation.

Some analysts warned that Tokyo's obvious difference of views with the group's other countries threatens to undermine the credibility of Japanese financial policy makers. Already, they have been frustrated in attempts to stem the yen's decline, either by talking the currency lower, or by raising interest rates and intervening in the currency market.

Most market participants in Tokyo hewed to the optimistic Japanese interpretation. Early dollar selling, spurred on by light intervention by the Bank of Japan, was enough to drive the dollar as low as 155.60 yen yesterday morning. In the afternoon, the currency came back a bit to close at 156.45, off from 157.70 late Friday in New York.

"Our reading of the G-7 communique is that it's relatively weak and noncommittal," said Kermit L. Schoenholtz, an economist at Salomon Brothers Inc.'s office here. "The bottom line is that the G-7 statement didn't promise" what the yen needs to recover -- either coordinated interest-rate increases in the seven countries or orchestrated yen-buying by central banks other than the Bank of Japan.

Indeed, Finance Minister Ryutaro Hashimoto undercut those hopes further when he pledged over the weekend to avert further interest-rate increases in Japan. "That just invites further speculative attacks on the yen," Mr. Schoenholtz said.

For the time being, stockbrokers see the yen's appreciation as an opportunity to sell unwanted stocks. "As long as people are willing to buy stock, we are willing to sell," one U.S. broker in Tokyo said.

Many market players expect that kind of profit-taking eventually to bring a halt to the market's recovery. "It's a mere rebound" from the market's dramatic decline of 27% from the start of the year through last Thursday, said Katsumi Nishikido, a senior analyst at Nippon Kangyo Kakumaru Securities Co. He predicts that buying will turn to selling again as the index approaches the 31000-point level.

Others said the yen's direction could set the market tone, and might carry the market below the 30000-point mark again. Hironobu Hagio, a fund manager at Nippon Life Insurance Co., the single biggest investor in the Tokyo market, said the market's direction will depend heavily on the exchange rate.

Investors' attention to earnings outlooks pushed up high-tech shares such as Sony, which advanced 250 yen to 8,650 yen ($55.10). Hitachi was up 40 yen to 1,710.

Also sharply higher were mining and construction shares such as Dowa Mining, which gained 100 yen to 850 yen and Kajima, which increased 50 yen to 1,700.

Some domestic demand-related shares such as railways and real estate companies were among Monday's outstanding winners. Keisei gained 190 yen to 1,740 yen, and Heiwa Real Estate gained 120 yen to 1,530.

Steel shares were actively traded throughout the day. They failed to sustain their morning gains, however, under strong selling pressure in the afternoon. Nippon Steel was down 10 yen at 575 yen, while Sumitomo Metal gained 21 yen to 586.

West German share prices tumbled 1.2% in nervous trading in Frankfurt, as fears about an apparent rift between the central bank and government over East German monetary union frightened investors and triggered a wave of profit-taking.

The German Stock Index plunged 23.98 points to 1923.86, its lowest level since March 22.

Comments last week and over the weekend by Bundesbank officials that indicate the central bank will push interest rates up if the government tries to introduce an even exchange for all East German marks in savings has shaken the market, traders said. To minimize the inflationary impact of the currency conversion, the Bundesbank has suggested a limit of 2,000 marks per person at the 1-for-1 rate and a 2-for-1 conversion rate above that level.

Shares of Daimler-Benz dropped 19 marks ($11.20) to 936 marks ($552.20), leading the market lower. Traders said the decline was triggered by diminishing expectations that Mitsubishi of Japan is preparing to buy a large stake.

Daimler had gained strongly late last week on rumors that Deutsche Bank was planning to sell 10% of its 28% stake in Daimler to Mitsubishi and speculation that the Japanese company would seek to expand that stake through market purchases.

Volkswagen AG slid 9.50 marks to 601.50 marks, Bayerische Motoren Werke dropped six marks to 604 and Porsche dropped 25 marks to 1120.

Machinery-maker shares tumbled, as Linde fell 22 marks to 985, MAN lost 20 marks to 500 marks and Kloeckner-Humboldt-Deutz dropped seven marks to 279.

London shares closed modestly firmer amid light volume, as a number of factors contributed to sluggish trading and allowed the market to drift lazily upward, traders said.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index closed 6.6 points higher at 2227.7. The key index settled just off its intraday high of 2227.9, which it posted in the first hour of trading. The session's low was 2218.8.

The narrower 30-share index was up 2.1 points at 1742.3. Volume was a thin 304.6 million shares, compared with 578.6 million shares Friday.

Dealers noted an absence of fresh features in the day's trading, although a number of ex-dividend stocks provided some movement.

Among companies which went ex-dividend: brewing and distilling concern Guinness lost 16 pence to close at 653 pence ($3.98) a share; Prudential, the insurance and financial services company, shed six pence to 199; General Accident, another insurer, plunged 44 to #10.19 ($6.20) and Reckitt & Colman, the diversified manufacturer, slid 19 pence to #11.50.

Elsewhere, financial services and tobacco conglomerate B.A.T Industries gained 12 pence to 755 on anticipation California state insurance regulators may support Sir James Goldsmith's plans to sell off B.A.T's U.S. insurance assets once he gains control of the company. A decision by California regulators was expected late yesterday.

In other European markets, Paris shares posted their third consecutive record close. Amsterdam shares closed mostly lower and were narrowly mixed in Brussels. Stockholm edged higher, Milan ended mostly higher and Zurich finished easier.

In other Asian and Pacific markets, shares were higher in Wellington and lower in Seoul. Manila's stock market was closed.
