Fairchild Industries Unit Is Charged With Producing Fake Data on Jet Parts

  • Author: Michael Allen
  • Date: 05/11/90

A Fairchild Industries Inc. unit systematically generated fraudulent testing documents for special nuts, bolts and other fasteners used on most U.S. commercial jetliners and military aircraft, federal prosecutors charged. The charges stem from an investigation following a weekend raid in February 1989 of Fairchild's VSI Corp. fastener plant in California by about 20 agents of the Federal Bureau of Investigation and the Defense Criminal Investigative Service. Agents hauled away about 50 boxes of fasteners and documents as part of an extensive inquiry into allegations of fraud in the aerospace-parts industry.

The charges, brought against Fairchild's Voi-Shan testing laboratory in Chatsworth, Calif., and two of its managers, are contained in a complaint filed earlier this week in federal court in Seattle by Assistant U.S. Attorney Bruce D. Carter.

According to the complaint, a phantom "Inspector 11" at the California plant was part of a 15-year scheme to falsify test reports submitted to the government. Affidavits supporting the complaint said the No. 11 inspector's stamp wasn't assigned to any employee, but instead was used routinely to indicate that parts had passed inspection, even when tests hadn't been performed.

In a statement filed in court along with the indictment, Mr. Carter said some inadequate Voi-Shan parts were found, but investigators still don't know if there is any imminent air-safety concern from untested or possibly defective parts used years earlier on aircraft flying today. "No accidents have been traced to Voi-Shan bolts," he said in the statement.

Voi-Shan is the largest of Fairchild's three fastener operations. The unit makes pins and collars, lock bolts, rivets, nuts and other special fastener products. Some bolts made at the plant are intended to bear loads exceeding 200,000 pounds per square inch. Customers include such major aircraft and engine manufacturers as Boeing Co., McDonnell Douglas Corp., Lockheed Corp., General Electric Co. and United Technologies Corp.'s Pratt & Whitney Co. unit.

At Fairchild headquarters in Chantilly, Va., a spokeswoman said she couldn't comment on the charges, because the company is negotiating with the Justice Department to "resolve" the civil and criminal allegations. "We believe a resolution is imminent," she said.

The spokeswoman also said Fairchild is confident that the quality of its products has never jeopardized air safety. She indicated that Fairchild would issue an additional statement today after a court hearing in Seattle on the matter.

According to the charges, the investigation of Voi-Shan was prompted by a complaint from a whistleblower, Thomas Runion, shortly after he left his job in the metallurgical laboratory at the Chatsworth plant.

The complaint also names as defendants James E. Ryan, a former Voi-Shan quality-assurance manager, and Aram Marderian, a former metallurgical lab supervisor for Voi-Shan. If convicted, each faces a maximum sentence of five years in prison and a $250,000 fine.

Neither man could be reached for comment.

The Fairchild unit and the former employees are charged with conspiracy to commit mail fraud, to make false statements to the government and to cheat the U.S. According to court documents, the object of the alleged conspiracy was to hold down costs of inspection and testing in order to boost the unit's profits.

The documents cite dozens of instances in which Voi-Shan allegedly failed to conduct tests of strength, durability and brittleness, and yet produced reports approved by the fictitious inspector indicating that such tests had been properly performed.

Nevertheless, court documents indicate, Voi-Shan allegedly shipped parts that were later rejected by aircraft makers which subjected the products to their own testing procedures. According to an affidavit, Fairchild also shipped some parts that had rusted in a salt-spray test.

Eight other defendants already have pleaded guilty in the case, prosecutors said, and the investigation is continuing.

In a prepared statement, Boeing said its use of Voi-Shan materials "has not compromised the integrity of Boeing products."

Comments from other major Voi-Shan customers weren't immediately available.
