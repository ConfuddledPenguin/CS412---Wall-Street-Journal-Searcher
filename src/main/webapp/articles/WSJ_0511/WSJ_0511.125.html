REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Congress on the Line

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/11/90

The Jim Wright and Keating Five scandals have revealed how some Members of Congress deepened the Savings and Loan crisis by intervening with federal regulators on behalf of suspect thrifts. Federal agencies keep thorough records of their contacts with private lobbyists and officials from the executive branch, but Congress has long resisted any such records of calls that they or their staff make on behalf of constituents. However, Congress's role in the S&L disaster made it impossible for Members to block plans by the three federal agencies charged with regulating the thrift bailout to begin logging contacts with Congress. Last month, the St. Petersburg Times of Florida reported that at least 25 members or aides had contacted thrift regulators by phone or mail since last August.

Many of the contacts were routine inquiries, but other interventions were attempts to obtain a desired result from officials.

Senator J. Bennett Johnston personally called the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency last year to ask that it delay plans to shut down the Security National Bank of Shreveport, Louisiana. A week later regulators closed the bank citing its unsafe lending practices and poor management. Deputy Comptroller Frank Maguire said he viewed Senator Johnston's request as "improper."

Last December, an aide to Senator Tom Harkin "repeatedly suggested" a legal precedent by which regulators could drop a lawsuit against an Iowa bank. After he was told the precedent was not relevant, the aide "persisted" in pressing for the lawsuit to be dropped.

Senator Orrin Hatch twice asked Bill Seidman, head of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., to drop plans for a lawsuit against directors of a Utah bank. "It would mean a lot to me," Senator Hatch wrote. "I know you will do what is right." The interesting thing about this case is that aides to Mr. Hatch told the Times he sent the letters, "knowing full well they would be made public." Public disclosure doesn't automatically put such interventions beyond question, but it at least gets these activities out in the open where it's possible to make distinctions.

Under the current, non-public system, even Members charged with seeing that regulators perform properly have no qualms about intervening in cases when they have a direct interest. The Capitol Hill newspaper Roll Call reported last month that in 1986, Kentucky Rep. Carroll Hubbard, chairman of the banking subcommittee on oversight and investigations, asked thrift regulator Ed Gray to provide financial assistance for First Federal, a Kentucky S&L for which Rep. Hubbard admitted he had done "substantial legal work."

"This institution is special to me for many reasons," he wrote in a letter to Mr. Gray. "Some of my closest friends in my hometown are on the board of directors or are employed at First Federal."

Federal regulators need to be watched. They make mistakes and they have the power to wreak havoc on the lives of private citizens. It is no answer, however, to say that the proper balance against this potential danger is an intervening Congress that is itself immune from some system of checks on its power. After all, it was Congress's actions in the first place that led to the clumsy S&L bailout that many of them are so worried about. At the least, all federal agencies should be allowed to keep public logs of their contacts with Members or their staffs. Voters then could judge when their elected representatives are performing legitimate oversight and when they're simply twisting arms for special interests.
