West German Firms Go Slow on Ventures With East Germans

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/11/90

HANOVER, West Germany -- West German companies have put most business ventures with East Germany on ice until the start of monetary union July 2, executives at major West German industrial groups say. After a flurry of preliminary agreements on joint ventures and investment projects earlier in the year, the companies are waiting for clear financial information from potential East German partners and details on the government's economic program before pushing their plans any further.

"The period between the announcement of a monetary union and its implementation is a difficult time," said Heinz Schimmelbusch, chairman of Metallgesellschaft AG, a diversified metals concern that is particularly active in Eastern Europe. "At the moment, there is almost nothing we can do."

The reluctance of West German companies to make any firm investment commitment in East Germany could brake the arrival of the economic boom that many economists and politicians have predicted. But companies say that once the mark has become the official currency and legal and economic conditions are right, they are planning to move quickly to transfer the needed capital to East Germany.

Managers list a series of uncertainties that stop them from acting before July 2, ranging from the treatment of East German corporate debt to energy prices, wages and business regulations. Most difficult is the absence of clear laws governing corporate ownership, they say.

In the coming weeks, the East German government is planning to pass a series of laws that should create a similar business environment as that found in West Germany, but companies say they aren't willing to commit funds on promises alone.

"The necessary legal basis just doesn't exist yet," said Friedrich Schunder, a senior official at engineering concern Mannesmann AG. "We don't know whether we can hold the majority in a company, we can't buy any real estate, we can't hire East Germans freely, and there is no protection for investments."

Because of these constraints, "we can't adopt any measure that affects the wealth of the concern," Mr. Schunder added.
