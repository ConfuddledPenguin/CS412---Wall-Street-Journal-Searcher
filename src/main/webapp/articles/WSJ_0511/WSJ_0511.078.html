Foreign Exchange: Swiss Franc, Bolstered by High Rates, Strengthens Against Dollar, Other Units

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/11/90

NEW YORK -- The Swiss franc rose against the dollar, pulling the mark up with it, while profit-taking steadied the U.S. unit against most other currencies after sharp losses Wednesday. Large-scale selling of yen for Swiss francs helped the dollar post modest gains against the yen, traders said.

The Swiss unit, which was extending recent gains, remains in strong demand because of high Swiss interest rates, dealers said.

The currency also is benefiting from a market perception of Switzerland as an economically and politically stable country in which to invest. This perception is helping Switzerland draw a healthy portion of the capital that currently is flowing into Europe from the U.S., said Judy Rubenstein, a vice president and trader at Bank of America in Los Angeles.

The Swiss unit is getting further support from Japanese borrowers who need the currency to pay off maturing franc-denominated bonds, Ms. Rubenstein and other participants said.

Traders said that Japanese buying of the Swiss unit late last month brought the franc out of the obscurity it had suffered while the market focused on German unification. However, the franc's original upward pressure against the yen was broadened by a high-profile campaign conducted by the Swiss National Bank on behalf of the currency, they added.

The franc ended the New York day at 112.63 yen, up a full yen from 111.63 yen late Wednesday in New York.

The Swiss National Bank, which sets the Swiss Lombard interest rate, has said repeatedly it will take whatever measures are necessary to keep the franc strong, a policy that has included maintaining comparatively high interest rates.

The Swiss National Bank's Lombard rate stood yesterday at 10.875%, unchanged from its level Wednesday.

The mark also weakened against the Swiss franc, falling to 0.8524 franc from 0.8567 franc in trading late Wednesday in New York. However, the franc and mark tend to move in a roughly parallel direction against the dollar, so some of the Swiss franc's gains against the U.S. unit spilled over to the German currency. Sterling fell to 2.3342 francs from 2.3533 francs.

While the dollar rebounded slightly against most currencies yesterday on some buying of the U.S. unit at its lower levels and modest profit-taking in other currencies, market sentiment on the U.S. currency remains bearish, participants said.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6285 marks, down from 1.6360 marks late Wednesday, and at 156.70 yen, up from 156.50 yen late Wednesday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6795, up from $1.6785 Wednesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Friday, the U.S. currency was quoted at 154.80 yen, down sharply from Thursday's Tokyo close of 156.75 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.6300 marks.

Market worry is focused on the possibility of lower interest rates following Friday's disappointing employment report for April, a concern that has been sharpened by renewed efforts by President Bush and Congress to reduce the federal budget deficit. It's feared that a lower deficit will lead to pressure for lower interest rates.

News that the Treasury's $10 billion of 30-year bonds sold at an average yield of 8.84% came too late in the day to influence trading.

Bearishness about the U.S. currency is such that participants are willing to sell the U.S. unit at the smallest opportunity. This was seen late in the day when traders sold the dollar against the mark following a gain by the Swiss franc against the U.S. currency, said Francoise Soares-Kemp, treasury vice president at Credit Suisse in New York.

"There's a contagion of anti-dollar feeling," in the market, Ms. Soares-Kemp said.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $370.10 an ounce, down $1.80. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.8 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $371.55 an ounce.
