Psychiatrists Hold Mass Meeting As Oedipus Wrecks Mother's Day

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 05/11/90

NEW YORK -- Look who's `conflicted' now: the psychiatrists. The American Psychiatric Association masses here on Mother's Day weekend with a bit of guilt. It's the fifth year in a row that the weighty annual meeting falls on the maternal holiday and the 10th such clash in the past 22 years.

"There have been questions raised," concedes Melvin Sabshin, APA medical director. Complaints began in earnest around 1985, when the group got its first woman president. But "it isn't easy to accommodate" 16,000 people, says Dr. Sabshin. Bookings are made years in advance; few cities can handle such a big group; May is tough -- too early and the weather's nasty, too late and members complain about missing family graduations.

"I've often wondered why they don't examine their collective psyche on this," says the wife of a Midwest psychiatrist. (She prefers anonymity, noting, "I've kept my peace this long.") Her husband is retiring after 40 years, and she'll be with him in New York this weekend. But, "I'm upset we're not going to be home to celebrate with our children and grandchildren."

And well she should be, asserts Dorothy W. Cantor, a psychologist in Westfield, N.J. "There's something sacred about Mother's Day and Father's Day," says Ms. Cantor, who has a general practice and has written about women's and family concerns. From a clinical standpoint, too, Mother's Day "is a big deal," she says.

The holiday can exacerbate any problems between mother and child, says Ms. Cantor. She adds that some of her own patients are "going through terrible things." The main psychologists' group, the American Psychological Association, steers clear of major holidays, holding its annual meeting in the summer.

But the psychiatrists are working their problem through. Next year, they'll meet on Mother's Day in New Orleans. But in 1992, 1993 and 1994, the session will be on other weekends in May.

As in corporate life, much of the change regarding women comes from women themselves reaching positions of authority. "The more you're in a position of leadership, the more what you say gets attended to," says Elissa Benedek, president-elect of the APA. She says all four of her children are along for this year's meeting, although that hasn't always been possible.

"I think it's very likely we'll not be scheduling any more meetings on Mother's Day in the future," says Dr. Benedek.
