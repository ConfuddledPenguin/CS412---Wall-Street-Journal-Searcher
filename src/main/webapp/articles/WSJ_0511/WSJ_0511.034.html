Ford Says Incentives, Certain Other Costs Equal 10% of Its Sales

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/11/90

DETROIT -- Ford Motor Co. said incentives and other variable marketing costs are now running about 10% of its sales -- an extremely high rate that works out to be about $1,000 a vehicle, or $4 billion on an annualized basis. The No. 2 auto maker also disclosed at its annual meeting that it spent $1.2 billion last year on world-wide advertising. About $644 million of that was spent in the U.S., with television getting $255 million of the total, $109 million going to magazines and $38 million to newspapers.

In other matters from the auto maker's second-shortest annual meeting at 1 hour 58 minutes:

-- Ford Chairman Harold A. Poling said government economic reforms in Brazil and labor disruptions in Europe will continue to depress the company's earnings in the second quarter. These two factors hurt first-quarter earnings, which wound up at $506.2 million, or $1.10 a share. In the year-earlier first quarter, Ford earned $1.64 billion, or $3.44 a share.

Mr. Poling also said the world-wide auto industry's capacity to build about 8.4 million vehicles this year, or about 20% more than demand, "will most certainly squeeze margins" for every auto company.

-- Ford "would not take exception" to being chosen as the contract target in negotiations with the United Auto Workers union this summer, Mr. Poling said in a news conference that followed the meeting. The UAW usually picks one of the Big Three auto makers as the target for a pattern-setting contract that it then tries to get from the two other companies.

-- Ford dealers have already taken orders for 100,000 of the redesigned 1991-model Ford Escort and Mercury Tracer subcompacts, Ford officials said. Dealers have taken in about 75,000 orders for the new Ford Explorer sport utility vehicle. Ford has said it expects to sell 500,000 Escorts and Tracers next year, the first full year the cars will be on sale. The auto maker expects to sell 100,000 Explorers this year and 220,000 in 1991.

-- Ford still plans to spend substantially more in the next five years than the record $23 billion it spent in the past five years, including $6.8 billion last year.
