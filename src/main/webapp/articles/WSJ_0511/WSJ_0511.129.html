Thursday's Markets: Stocks Follow Bonds Higher; Dollar Is Mixed

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 05/11/90

Stock prices followed the bond market higher despite the unspectacular reception given the Treasury's sale of $10 billion in 30-year bonds. The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 5.63 points, to 2738.51 in moderately active trading. But the dollar continued its slump against the mark.

Analysts said that although there wasn't strong demand for the $10 billion of 30-year bonds that the Treasury auctioned, it was sufficient to reinforce perceptions that interest rates have peaked. The average yield on the bonds was 8.84%. Last week, yields on the previously issued 30-year bond reached a one-year high of 9.03%.

Traders said that with the auction overhanging the stock market, it has been difficult to gauge the recent strength in stock prices. "Everyone obviously keeps saying, `How's the auction going, how's the auction going?'" says Larry Greenwald, a block trader at Sanford Bernstein & Co.

The risk that interest rates might move higher in coming weeks has kept many stock-market investors on the sidelines recently. They've been afraid that a resurgence in inflation would force the Federal Reserve to drive short-term interest rates higher to slow the economy, and cool corporate profits along with it.

But recent economic statistics indicate that slower growth may occur without any nudging from the Fed. With investors generally eager to purchase Treasury debt, especially the $10.5 billion of three-year notes auctioned Tuesday, much of the anxiety about an interest-rate spurt has eased.

But analysts caution that stock prices aren't likely to head skyward anytime soon. For one thing, investors are still awaiting more evidence about inflationary trends. The release today of the April producer price index, a measure of wholesale inflation, isn't expected to help much. Economists predict it will be up about 0.2%. That's an annualized rate of about 2.5%, but it compares with a 0.2% decline in March. Consumer prices, which have been rising considerably faster than wholesale prices, will be reported next week.

Longer term, analysts point to poor corporate earnings as another drag on stock prices. Although comparisons should improve later this year with year-earlier results, few investors are willing to bet heavily that earnings will rebound sharply.

In currencies, much of the attention was focused on the dollar/Swiss-franc exchange rate, as recent demand for the francs eased slightly. Swiss francs had become highly desirable because of higher interest rates in Switzerland and Japanese purchases of the currency to pay for maturing bonds denominated in Swiss francs. In late New York trading, the dollar was at 1.3880 Swiss francs, down from 1.4025.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose in fairly active trading. Volume totaled 158.5 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where advancers led losers 847 to 601.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading the U.S. currency was quoted at 1.6285 marks and 156.70 yen compared with 1.6360 marks and 156.50 yen.
