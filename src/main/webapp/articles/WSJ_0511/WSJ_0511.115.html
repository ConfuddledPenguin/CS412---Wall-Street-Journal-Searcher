American Savings Bank Liquidates Its Junk Portfolio

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt and David B. Hilder
  • Date: 05/11/90

American Savings Bank acknowledged that it had liquidated its $400 million junkbond portfolio since the end of 1989, concluding an embarrassing episode that may have cost the otherwise healthy thrift $100 million or more. American, which was acquired in December 1988 by Texas financier Robert Bass with government assistance, went on a Texas-size buying spree for junk bonds last spring and summer, only to have Congress ban thrift ownership of the bonds as part of the thrift bailout law in August. At its peak, American's portfolio totaled about $493 million, making it one of the top 10 thrift holders of junk.

"This was probably the most ill-timed major junk-bond ventures of anybody I could remember. The timing could not have been worse," said Kenneth Thomas, a Miami thrift analyst and consultant.

A spokesman for American, based in Stockton, Calif., said the portfolio had all been sold by the end of April. He declined to estimate the financial effect of the sales and had no further comment.

In February, American disclosed that it set aside $65 million in reserves for junk-bond losses in the fourth quarter and said its junk-bond holdings at year end had been reduced to $400 million.

A person close to American said there was "some additional loss, though not huge numbers" subsequent to year end. The person said American "did better than most" in selling its bonds but still suffered.

"Obviously, in hindsight, everybody would have preferred to invest in something better," the person said.

Mr. Thomas, the analyst, estimated that losses totaled in the range of $125 million, or about 25% of the portfolio at its peak, based on prevailing market prices for junk in recent months.

The episode is more of a public-relations disaster than a financial one for American, whose 1989 financial results made it the most profitable large thrift in the country. American had about $16.3 billion in assets at year end. The losses could also spark new criticism of regulators for permitting the junk-bond purchases after the 1988 rescue, analysts said.

"This was clearly their most dramatic mistake so far as their investment in that bank," Mr. Thomas said of the Bass family stake in American. "Everything else seems to have gone very well. They are fortunate they have the capital strength to get out of it."

Most large thrifts who took the plunge on junk bonds haven't been so lucky, with many having failed or in imminent danger of doing so. The largest thrift purchaser, Columbia Savings & Loan Association of Beverly Hills, Calif., which once held roughly $4.5 billion in junk, is currently insolvent and is considered a federal takeover candidate.
