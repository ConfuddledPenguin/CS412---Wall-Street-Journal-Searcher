Marketing & Media: FCC Tightens Broadcast License Rules; Tele-Communications Inc. May Be Hurt

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 05/11/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Communications Commission tightened its rules for awarding broadcast licenses, discarding some of the free-market ideas of the Reagan years and throwing into doubt a request by Tele-Communications Inc. for FCC approval of a new satellite service. On a 5-0 vote, the agency threw out a Reagan-era proposal to award broadcast licenses by lottery rather than the traditional method of conducting hearings to choose the best applicant. It also voted unanimously to broaden the range of misconduct that could disqualify applicants from winning broadcast licenses to include any felony conviction, rather than just those involving fraud, and antitrust violations involving mass-media properties.

Tele-Communications, which lost an antitrust lawsuit in 1985 to a competitor for a cable television franchise in Missouri, could be hurt by the ruling. The Denver cable company currently is asking the FCC to approve a plan to construct and operate a satellite service that would beam video programming directly to roof-mounted satellite receivers. An FCC official said the new policy will be a factor in the decision to grant Tele-Communications a license, but said the agency hadn't decided how it will rule.

Andrew Schwartzman, executive director of the Media Access Project, which is challenging the Tele-Communications application, said the FCC ruling would strengthen his case. But the agency generally goes out of its way to approve new services, even if it sometimes bends its policies to accommodate them. Tele-Communications didn't return calls seeking comment.

Tele-Communications is part of a satellite-broadcast partnership called K Prime Partners, which plans to offer a 10-channel service by the end of this year. Another group, called Sky Cable, plans a more elaborate service in 1993.

Separately, the FCC voted unanimously for a series of proposals to tighten the rules for awarding new broadcast stations. The agency proposed to ban the practice by which competitors for a new broadcast property buy each other out for large sums before going to a hearing.

The Reagan-era policy was meant to quicken the pace by which new licenses were awarded, but sometimes degenerated into a free-for-all in which applications were filed simply to make money during these negotiations. The FCC proposal would limit the amount of such payoffs to the expenses incurred in making an application.

The FCC also said it hoped to reduce the time it took to approve a new license to one year from a current average of three. "Arms-control treaties take less time than the work of this commission," said FCC Chairman Alfred Sikes.

But none of the day's proposals addressed the longstanding criticism of the FCC that it awards broadcast licenses based on trivial differences among applicants. Frustrations over the picayune nature of these inquiries have led to suggestions over the years that the government auction off new broadcast licenses to the highest bidder rather than give them away to those chosen during hearings. But broadcasters have resolutely opposed proposals that they pay for broadcast frequencies they use.
