Banks Take to the Road In Search of Customers

MOBILE BANKS are rolling onto the scene, one of the latest bids to find untapped customers who might want to open accounts.

First National Bank of Jackson, Tenn., for instance, plans to take its new mobile branch -- a converted armored car -- into "areas often ignored by banks, including nursing homes, factory sites and public housing projects," says Chairman Ernest Vickers III. That was enough to win government approval, not to mention the endorsement of the local chapter of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People.

The brightly painted truck, which has regular daily hours, will initially cash checks, set up savings plans and sell certificates of deposit. Eventually, customers will be able to apply for loans and mortgages.

First Citizens Bank & Trust Co. of Raleigh, N.C., is taking a similar route. It now features a mobile automatic teller machine, embedded in the side of a van. Although it won't accept deposits or loan payments, the unit will travel to fairs and other big gatherings to dispense cash to customers who are ATM clients.

Bankers say the cost of setting up and operating a mobile branch is roughly one-tenth of what it costs to build and operate the smallest brick-and-mortar office.

They also want to stress service. Mr. Vickers of First National, in Tennesee, says: "We figured if Federal Express and United Parcel Service are going to folks' front doors to pick up packages, why should bankers demand" that customers come to them?

A State Hopes Pride

And Cash Have Affinity

WHAT'S GOOD for Elvis fans might be good for Rhode Island.

Faced with a $100 million shortfall in this year's $1.5 billion budget, Rhode Island's Legislature passed two separate bills authorizing the state's general treasurer to negotiate with banks and thrifts to establish a Rhode Island credit card. The state would garner a percentage of a fee every time a holder uses the card.

In recent years, the credit-card market has been saturated by such "affinity cards" -- available for everyone from Elvis Presley lovers to sports fans.

State Senator David H. Sholes says a card emblazoned with Rhode Island's seal or a picture of the state's pristine seashore could generate as much as $3 million for state coffers in the first year. Of course, that means some 60,000 of the roughly 850,000 credit cards held by Rhode Island residents must make the switch.

"That's counting on a lot of state pride," says David Watson, vice president of marketing for Cardholder Management Services Inc., a credit-card consulting company. "It's difficult to translate that kind of intangible good feeling into switching credit cards. You've got to offer something more concrete, like discounts."

Indeed, a similar state-sponsored affinity card program was approved in Montana last year, but has gone nowhere for lack of interest.

Will Depositors Flood In

For More Liquid CDs?

SEVERAL BANKS and thrifts are easing restrictions on certificates of deposit, trying to make them more liquid in an attempt to attract investors.

Some investors shy away from CDs because there can be stiff penalties for early withdrawals -- often the loss of both interest and a portion of the principal. Young families, in particular, "have so many big-ticket expenses that can pop up on them," says Paul Jennings, senior vice president of DF Southeastern Inc., a Decatur, Ga., savings and loan. "They want the higher CD rate, but are afraid to tie up the money."

DF Southeastern's new Wait-and-CD, which earns 7.75%, acts as a temporary parking space, says Mr. Jennings. The customer has the option, during the six-month maturity, of switching to a higher-rate CD or withdrawing the funds altogether -- with no penalty. "This is a less-imposing way for younger customers to get their feet wet," he says.

In some cases, the penalties haven't been eliminated, just softened. The Access CD, introduced last month by First of America Bank Corp. in Kalamazoo, Mich., gives customers access to their accounts without penalty. But to get what amounts to an 8% annual rate on their CDs, customers must keep a minimum of $1,000 deposited throughout the two-year term -- otherwise, they will get just 6%.

Odds and Ends

IMAGINE NEVER having to make a last-minute dash for cash before a movie. The American Multi-Cinema Theaters Inc. chain and Los Angeles-based First Interstate Corp. are testing an automated box office to dispense tickets, as well as $20 vouchers for popcorn and other refreshments. Customers would simply use their automatic teller cards. . . . Synergistics Research Corp. in Atlanta is conducting a study to figure out why there is a resurgence in telephone banking. Such services were popular in the late 1970s and early 1980s, but fizzled when banks began offering services through personal computers. Now, the pendulum is swinging back. . . . Sweepstakes are often a no-win proposition for banks. People invariably wander in seeking a chance at an island vacation without ever inquiring about bank services. So First Bank System Inc. of Minneapolis is adding a twist: All players win discounts on various bank products, as well chances at other prizes such as a camera or 1990 Jeep Cherokee.
