Politics & Policy: FAA Reassigns Security Program Chief On Eve of Report on Lockerbie Disaster

  • Author: Laurie McGinley and Judith Valente
  • Date: 05/11/90

The Federal Aviation Administration's security chief was reassigned on the eve of the release of a new report expected to be highly critical of the agency's security program before and after the Lockerbie disaster in which 270 people died. Raymond Salazar, director of the FAA's Office of Civil Aviation Security, next month will take the helm at the Center for Management Development in Palm Coast, Fla., the agency's main management-training facility.

Since the terrorist downing of Pan Am Flight 103 over Lockerbie, Scotland, in December 1988, Mr. Salazar has been under intense fire from relatives of those killed as well as from some members of Congress.

Separately, Pan Am Corp. reached a settlement with residents of the Scottish town of Lockerbie who lost relatives, were injured or lost property as a result of the attack. The bombing killed 11 people on the ground, in addition to all 259 people on the plane.

The new report, by the President's Commission on Aviation Security and Terrorism, is due out next Tuesday. It is expected to admonish the FAA for serious lapses in security and call for major changes in the nation's aviation-security program. It also is expected to urge the Bush administration to take a tougher stand against state-sponsored terrorism.

Two family groups organized after the terrorist attack, Victims of Pan Am Flight 103 and Families of Pan Am 103-Lockerbie, have called repeatedly for Mr. Salazar's resignation, contending that the security program he oversaw was riddled with deficiencies.

FAA officials have defended Mr. Salazar. Yesterday, FAA Administrator James Busey asserted that the report and Mr. Salazar's job-change weren't connected. "We needed a good man for the job and we've got one," he said in a statement.

But within the aviation community, the reassignment, first reported in Aviation Daily, a trade publication, was widely viewed as an effort by the FAA to blunt the commission's report.

The seven-member panel, headed by former Labor Secretary Ann McLaughlin, was appointed in August 1989. It will recommend steps to increase the accountability of government and airline officials involved in security operations.

The commission's April public hearing demonstrated the controversy surrounding Mr. Salazar, as well as the confusion and contradictions surrounding security efforts at the time of the bombing. Officials from the FAA and Pan Am told the panel that the airline hadn't been hand-searching unaccompanied baggage put aboard planes in Frankfurt, where the doomed Pan Am flight originated. The luggage was X-rayed only.

Pan Am said it had received permission from Mr. Salazar to skip the required hand searches -- a contention Mr. Salazar hotly denied before the commission.

Panel members also expressed consternation that airline security in Frankfurt remained inadequate for eight months following the bombing.

Concerning the settlement, attorneys for both sides declined to disclose its terms. The agreement covers 270 claims for damages from ground victims. It doesn't affect lawsuits that have been filed on behalf of the passengers who lost their lives in the disaster.

Attorneys for the ground victims alleged Pan Am failed to take adequate steps to prevent a terrorist attack.

There was no limit to the amount of damages the ground victims could collect from Pan Am, the attorneys said. Passenger claims, however, are covered by international treaties that limit the airline's liability to $75,000 per person. Attorneys must prove Pan Am guilty of gross negligence to win higher awards. British aviation officials are still investigating the incident. Their findings will form the basis for settling passenger claims.

Pan Am said the settlement with ground victims will be covered by its insurers and won't have any immediate effect on its finances.
