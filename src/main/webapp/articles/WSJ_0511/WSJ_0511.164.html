Cummins Engine, Biggest Holder Set Standstill Accord

  • Author: Tom Burton
  • Date: 05/11/90

Cummins Engine Co. reached a standstill accord with Industrial Equity (Pacific) Ltd., formally eliminating any possibility that its biggest shareholder will boost its 14.9% stake. In the pact, IEP, which last summer sparked speculation it might begin an unsolicited bid for the Columbus, Ind., concern, promised not to increase its Cummins holdings or to make any bid or tender offer for Cummins stock.

As part of the plan, both sides agreed to drop bitter litigation born of Cummins's efforts to deter IEP's overtures.

For public consumption, both sides described themselves as good friends.

Bruce Hancox, board chairman of IEP's parent company, New Zealandbased Brierley Investments Ltd., said his concern never had any intention of making a tender offer for Cummins shares and vowed to "pursue a harmonious relationship with Cummins." Brierley is used by Sir Ronald Brierley of New Zealand to make British and U.S. investments.

"We bought our shares of Cummins as an investment and never intended to take any of the actions we have now contractually agreed not to take," said Mr. Hancox.

Privately, Cummins claimed victory.

"Maybe the heat was getting such that IEP had to back off," said one person familiar with Cummins's dealings with IEP. "They apparently just decided that life is too short for all this." Cummins had brought in congressional and regulatory scrutiny of the stock purchases.

In seeking the agreement, IEP had asked for, but been denied, a role on a shareholders advisory committee as part of dropping the litigation, this person said. Instead, Cummins agreed only to "meet with IEP twice a year in Columbus, Ind."

"Of course, we didn't say who we'd have at the meeting," said the person from the Cummins side.

"I think Industrial Equity got a little more than they bargained for," said Eli Lustgarten, an analyst with PaineWebber Inc. "Cummins management has pretty much entrenched themselves."

"We got tired of the litigation," said Rob Sutherland, an IEP director and head of its California office. "We just couldn't find it worthwhile spending months and months in depositions when we have better things to do."

It's also possible IEP lost whatever appetite, if any, it had for more Cummins stock. Cummins traded as high as $65 last July, when IEP more than doubled its stake, but by October the stock had dropped sharply as Cummins's earnings flagged. In composite New York Stock Exchange trading yesterday, Cummins dropped 62.5 cents to $46.50.

IEP is the second big shareholder whose advances the diesel engine maker has turned away. Last July, descendants of Cummins's founding family bought Hanson USA's 8.8% stock holdings to rid Cummins of that shareholder.
