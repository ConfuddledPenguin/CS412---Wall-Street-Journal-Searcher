Heart and blood-pressure diseases caused nearly one million deaths in 1987, almost as much as all other causes of death combined. Nearly one-fifth of these people were under age 65, and 5% were under age 45.

Heart History

A chronology of heart-care breakthroughs:

Early 1900s: Digitalis is the first drug used to treat congestive heart failure by making the heart pump withmore force.

1945: Anti-coagulants are introduced to help prevent blood clots.

1947: Electric defibrillation, the use of an electric shock, restores normal rhythm to a fibrillating heart.

1948: Cardiac catheterization, the process of advancing a thin tube (catheter) into a vein, artery or heart chamber to perform diagnostic studies, is developed.

1952: Diuretics are approved for used in treatment of congestive heart failure and high blood pressure to help the body eliminate excess salt and water.

1954: The heart-lung machine, a mechanical pump that oxygenates and pumps blood during heart surgery, is developed, making open-heart surgery possible.

1955: CPR or cardiac massage becomes an accepted technique to revive an arrested heart.

1958: First external pacemaker is used to pace a baby's heart after surgery.

1959: Three major classes of therapeutic drugs are now available: beta blockers, nitrates and anti-hypertensives.

1960: A pacemaker is successfully implanted.

-- First successful heart-valve replacement is performed with a mechanical valve.

1962: Intra-aortic balloon pump, which temporarily increases coronary-artery blood flow, is introduced.

1968: Coronary angiography allows physicians to examine blood vessels or chambers ofthe heart by tracing the course of dye that's been injected into the bloodstream.

-- Bypass surgery improves the blood supply to the heart by constructing a detour around the blocked part of the coronary artery using a blood vessel from another part of the body.

-- The first heart transplantation occurs.

1969: The first artificial-heart implant takes place. It was used for three days before the transplant of a human heart.

1972: Anti-lipid drugs, to reduce the amount of serum cholesterol circulating in the blood, are introduced.

-- Thrombolytic agents used to dissolve blood clots are introduced.

1973: A genetic defect that raises blood cholesterol levels and causes coronary heart disease is discovered.

1974: Calcium channel blockers are used to lower blood pressure, reduce the frequency of angina attacks and help prevent repeat heart attacks by reducing the heart's workload and increasing its blood supply.

1977: Angioplasty, using balloons or lasers, is used to open an occluded coronary artery.

1980: ACE inhibitors are used to open constricted blood vessels.

1984: A large-scale human experiment proves that lowering blood cholesterol levels reduces the risk of a heart attack.

1985: Implantable defibrillator becomes available to restore normal rhythm to a fibrillating heart.
