Your Money Matters: Pru-Bache Tops Big Firms in First-Quarter Stock Picks

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 05/11/90

Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. moved to the head of the class in stock picking for the first quarter. But Goldman, Sachs & Co. widened its long-term lead in a quarterly study designed to gauge how individual investors would do if they followed their brokers' advice. Investors who bought the stocks recommended by Pru-Bache, a unit of Prudential Insurance Co. of America, eked out an estimated profit of 0.6% for the quarter, according to the joint study by this newspaper and Zacks Investment Research Inc. of Chicago.

People who invested in Goldman's favorites roughly broke even for the quarter, while those who bought the picks of eight other major brokerage houses suffered minor losses (as did the market averages). To estimate the returns of individual investors, the study assumes that a person holds, in equal amounts, each stock highly recommended by a particular brokerage house.

The results show how a hot hand can change quickly. In 15 quarters covered by the study, seven different firms have come out on top for at least one quarter. Pru-Bache was tops in stock picking for the first quarter of 1987, as well as the latest quarter, but has done poorly long-term, ranking 10th for the full 45 months.

Long-term, the study's champion is Goldman Sachs, with an estimated return of 82.1% for the 45 months through March. Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., a unit of Primerica Corp., captured the 12-month crown for the fifth time in a row.

Here's a rundown on how the stocks recommended by the 10 brokerage houses have done, and what sorts of stocks the firms favor now. The firms are discussed in order of 12-month performance.

SMITH BARNEY: With an estimated return of 30.7%, Smith Barney trounced the competition for the latest 12 months. But it stumbled in the first quarter, mostly by recommending Hilton Hotels Corp., which slid 34% as takeover talk fizzled. Smith Barney had been breathing down the neck of Goldman Sachs for the long-term lead, but an estimated quarterly loss of 3.3% leaves it seven percentage points behind. What now? "We're making a big bet on the technology stocks," says research director Alan Benasuli. "IBM and Digital Equipment . . . were the underperformers of the 1980s, and we think they're going to be the leaders of the 1990s." He's also keen on such oil stocks as Mobil Corp., Oryx Energy Co. and Texaco Inc.

GOLDMAN SACHS: While the overall market dropped about 3% in the first quarter, Goldman's Focus List stocks were essentially unchanged, helping the firm strengthen its grip on first place in the long-term standings. Its biggest winner lately has been Maxtor Corp., a San Jose, Calif., maker of computer disk drives, which was up 35% for the quarter. William Kealy, research director, says the firm is sticking for now with a fairly heavy weight on technology and capital-goods stocks. But he says it may go to more defensive issues (those with low price-earnings ratios and high dividends) if it looks like the economy will be weak in 1991.

SHEARSON: The stock pickers at Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. have been getting more defensive. "We're in a trading-range market," says research director Fred Fraenkel. As a result, he says, the firm's analysts and stock-selection committee have been actively pruning, trying to make sure "that we don't keep things on the list that may be risky." In January, a nasty month for stocks, the American Express Co. unit's recommended list had the third-smallest drop (6.4%, vs. 6.3% for Smith Barney and A.G. Edwards). For the quarter, Shearson was No. 4, as losses in issues like Bairnco Corp. (down 45%) and Time Warner Inc. (down 21%) canceled out gains in such stocks as Nike Inc. (up 35%) and Amgen (up 26%).

DEAN WITTER: Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., a unit of Sears, Roebuck & Co., ranked fourth for the latest 12 months, but last for the quarter. "We got hurt with bank stocks" and other interest-sensitive issues, observes research director Manny Korman. Those included Citicorp. (down 19% for the quarter), First Chicago (down 17%) and American Express (down 26%). Nevertheless, Mr. Korman thinks investors should stick with interest-sensitive stocks. The reason: "The market can't go . . . unless you get a break in interest rates." He also likes food, drug and beverage stocks, on the theory that the economy is likely to stay "sluggish" for quite a while.

MERRILL LYNCH: Merrill Lynch & Co. trailed the market averages by a tad last quarter. "I guess we benefited somewhat from an emphasis on technology," says research director Andrew Melnick, "but we were hurt by financials, banks and HMOs." Health maintenance organizations have given Merrill some big gains in the past, but "they're a volatile group," Mr. Melnick notes. "You live by the sword, you die by the sword." Merrill clients made profits last quarter in Landmark Graphics Corp. (up 30%) and OEA Inc. (up 29%), but those who bought Prime Motor Inns Inc. (down 50%) and J&J Snack Food Corp. (down 30%) aren't smiling.

KIDDER PEABODY: Kidder, Peabody & Co., a unit of General Electric Co., beat the market averages with an estimated 2.2% loss last quarter. For both 12 months and 45 months, it ranks sixth. Stefan Abrams, chief investment strategist, likes consumer-product "noncyclicals" -- companies whose fortunes aren't closely tied to the ups and downs of the economy, such as Procter & Gamble or Bristol Myers. He also favors technology stocks -- not only computers (IBM, Digital Equipment, Intel) but also biotechnology and pollution-control issues. Technology stocks, he says, may be a bit risky, but "cost cutting and product cycles are falling into place" and will result in "much more explosive results in the next couple of years."

PAINEWEBBER: Suffering more from an absence of big winners than from any awful losers, the PaineWebber Group Inc. unit PaineWebber Inc. slipped a notch to seventh in the 12-month standings. The firm's list is "eclectic," says research director James Voytko. It has some oil issues like British Petroleum Co. and Texaco; quite a few telecommunications stocks and drug stocks; and some "soft cyclicals" like Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing. For the daring, Mr. Voytko suggests a depressed advertising stock, Saatchi & Saatchi PLC. The mixture reflects what strategist Edward M. Kerschner calls an "Andy Warhol market" -- each stock group has its brief moment of fame.

PRUDENTIAL-BACHE: A good quarter raised Pru-Bache to eighth in the 12-month standings. Long-term it remains last, but the four bottom firms are within about two percentage points of each other. Last quarter it had gains exceeding 30% in Anthem Electronics Inc., Home Depot Inc. and New England Critical Care Inc., while suffering one big loss (First Executive Corp., down 65%). "We still like technology stocks, even though they have performed well in the first quarter," says research director Michael Culp. Among the firm's current technology picks: Anthem, Chips & Technologies Inc., Lotus Development Corp. and Silicon Graphics Inc.

A.G. EDWARDS: Like Dean Witter, A.G. Edwards & Sons Inc. was hurt by the poor performance of interest-sensitive stocks, as interest rates rose in the first quarter. At Edwards, utility stocks are an important part of the list. Research director Terry Dessent says investors shouldn't abandon utilities and bank stocks, which he says have "excellent total-return opportunities." They've "been beat up pretty badly," he says. "We certainly want to buy them when nobody wants them." He also likes selected technology stocks.

RAYMOND JAMES: Wham] Bam] Raymond James customers get lots of action: The St. Petersburg, Fla., unit of Raymond James Financial Inc. has done great picking stocks in up markets, but badly in down ones. For example, the stocks it recommended had the study's worst performance in January (down 14.2%) and the best performance in February and March (up 12.3%). That's the result of a list devoid of such staid groups as utilities, foods and beverages (Raymond James doesn't cover them), and heavy on small stocks and Southeast regional firms. Research director David Henwood says he's looking to add some bigger stocks to give the list stability; he believes corporate earnings and interest rates will continue to be worrisome.
