Credit Markets: Bond Sale May Herald Lower Rates --- Average 8.84% Yield Trailed Expectations As U.S. Ends Auction

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Jones Hayden
  • Date: 05/11/90

NEW YORK -- The Treasury sold $10 billion of 30-year bonds at an unexpectedly low yield of 8.84%, strengthening the belief among investors that interest rates are heading lower. Yesterday's sale completed the Treasury's huge $30.5 billion quarterly note and bond auction, which is closely watched by Wall Street and Washington for hints of the direction of interest rates.

The average yield of 8.84% on the 30-year bonds was the lowest at an auction since last May, when the yield was 9.11%. And yesterday's yield was even lower than the 8.88% average yield on the Treasury's 10-year notes that were auctioned Tuesday. Long-term rates are generally higher than those with shorter maturities, so the discrepancy reflects that rates were falling even as the auction proceeded.

Traders said that a 8.84% yield on the 30-year bond was possible because large institutional investors rushed to lock in current interest rates on their belief that U.S. interest rates are on the way down.

"We had excellent domestic institutional demand. This was a good auction," said John Costas, co-manager of government trading at First Boston Corp. He said, however, that some dealers may have been disappointed that Japanese demand for the 30-year bonds was lukewarm. But at First Boston, which has far more U.S. than foreign customers, "our tenders {bids from investors} were the strongest in six or seven auctions," Mr. Costas said.

Michael Moran, manager of government trading at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Inc., said big investors "are taking a bullish stance in the marketplace." Mr. Moran noted that some portfolio managers at insurance companies, pension funds and mutual funds bought bonds at the auction in an effort to extend the length of their portfolios, a sign that these money managers believe the bond market is ready to rally.

The success of the government's bond auction is likely to extend beyond Wall Street. Since other long-term interest rates often track bond yields, analysts said that some consumer borrowing costs may begin to ease somewhat.

"Mortgage rates are coming down even as we speak," said Paul Havemann, a vice president at HSH Associates, which compiles mortgages rate information. Mr. Havemann said that rates on conventional mortgages, which rose from 9.8% in early January to about 10.6% last week, fell yesterday to about 10.5% and will probably fall further today. Prior to the bond auction, economists worried that bond yields would exceed 9% at the auction, which prompted some mortgage lenders to raise rates. But now, "just about every lender we survey said they are cutting rates," said Mr. Havemann.

Of course, that could change instantly if any new economic indicators show that inflation is accelerating or that economic activity is robust. Some news about inflation is due out today when the Labor Department releases the latest producer price index, a measure of inflation.

Economists surveyed by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report expect, on average, that the PPI rose 0.2% last month. A much higher inflation rate could depress the bond market and push interest rates higher. The PPI declined 0.2% in March and was flat in February.

Although yesterday's 30-year bond sale was considered a relatively good auction, analysts noted that demand was not as great as in the first two legs of the refunding. Nonetheless, the 30-year bond traded slightly higher after the auction, with the yield slipping to 8.81% in when-issued trading activity.

Bond dealers estimated that Japanese buyers purchased between 25% and 30% of the $10 billion of 30-year bonds, slightly less than estimates of Japanese buying in the auctions on Tuesday and Wednesday.

But, "in light of all the fears ahead of the refunding, it seems to have gone pretty well," said Kathleen Camilli, chief economist for Maria Ramirez Capital Consultants Inc.

The government's quarterly bond auction attracted far more interest than expected among individual investors, who showed a hearty appetite for the three-year notes sold Tuesday at an average yield of 8.74% and the 10-year notes sold Wednesday.

But individuals weren't as big buyers of the 30-year bonds. The Treasury said it received $463 million of noncompetitive bids, which come mainly from individual investors, for the 30-year bonds. That was up modestly from $442 million of noncompetitive bids at the previous auction in February.

One measure of the below-average demand by dealers at the auction yesterday was the so-called bid-to-cover ratio. The Treasury received 1.99 bids for every one it accepted at yesterday's auction. That was down from a bid-to-cover ratio of 2.0-to-one in the previous six auctions. But bidding at yesterday's auction was stronger than the last 30-year auction in February, where the government received 1.84 bids for every one it accepted.

Treasury Securities

Treasury bond prices ended modestly higher after the Treasury's 30-year bond sale.

The current benchmark 30-year bond rose more than 1/4 point, or $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield eased to 8.83% from 8.86%.

Short-term interest rates moved lower. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills was 7.72% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.96% while the rate on six-month bills was 7.72% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.12%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended with strong gains.

Traders said the advance reflected a modest squeeze on some dealers, who have been banking on heavy sales of securities by the Resolution Trust Corp.

While the RTC did sell mortgage securities late last week and early this week, the government conservator for failed thrifts wasn't a sizable seller yesterday.

Government National Mortgage Association's 9% securities for May delivery ended at 94 22/32, up 14/32, and its 9 1/2% securities at 97 5/32, up 12/32. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp.'s 9 1/2% issues was quoted at 96 7/8, up 10/32.

Junk Bonds

The junk bonds of Harcourt Brace Jovanovich Inc. ended mixed on news that the publisher has terminated talks for the sale of certain assets.

The news, which was released late Wednesday, was considered negative by most high-yield analysts. However, the market's reaction didn't turn out to be quite so harsh.

Although bids for Harcourt Brace junk bonds fell as much as 3 points in early trading, prices started firming after it became apparent that there were more buyers than sellers.

Harcourt's 13% senior notes due in 1997 finished at 93 3/4, slightly higher than Wednesday's closing levels. The other senior issue of Harcourt junk bonds, the 13 3/4% senior subordinated debentures maturing in 1999, lost about 1/2 point to end at 77 1/2.

Both issues had rallied strongly last month on news that Harcourt was involved in talks with Toronto's Thomson Corp. for the sale of certain assets.

Market participants had bid up the senior bonds on the expectation that if an asset sale took place the company would use proceeds to retire the bonds at a premium.

Peter Karches, head of the high-yield department at Morgan Stanley & Co., said that despite Wednesday's news, Harcourt bonds are still attractive on a fundmental basis.

"People recognize the bonds are trading at relatively cheap levels," said Karches. "It's going to take more fundamental news about the company for the bonds to fall."

Roger Gordon, a high-yield analyst at Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp., said the collapse of asset-sale talks is not critical for a firm like Harcourt Brace.

"The company is not under the gun to sell assets or restructure debt," Gordon said. "Our point of view regarding HBJ's high-yield securities is that the operating cash flow will be more than sufficient to meet debt service obligations over the next couple of years."

Meanwhile, the junk bonds of Interco Inc. were slightly lower on news that the St. Louis-based furniture and shoe maker won't make interest payments due June 15 on its bonds. Instead, the company has proposed that bondholders accept a debt restructuring of nearly $900 million.

Interco's 13 3/4% senior subordinated debentures due 2000 lost two points to end at 26. The bonds are now trading without accrued interest, a sign that the market doesn't expect the company to make future interest payments.

Interco's senior bond issue, the 8 7/8% notes of 1993, were unscathed by yesterday's news, and finished the session unchanged.

In the broader market for junk bonds, speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, prices were slightly firmer.

Investment-grade corporate debt ended generally 1/8 point higher.

Municipal Issues

New York City drew record demand from Wall Street dealers yesterday when it auctioned $900 million of revenue anticipation notes.

The city said it received 100 bids totaling $9.3 billion for the notes, which will be used to help the city meet its financial obligations until it receives funding from the state. The state aid has been delayed because a New York state budget has not been adopted.

Bond dealers said the unusually strong bidding for the notes, which caused yields to come in lower than expected, reflects political rather than fundamental factors.

New York City is currently reviewing its underwriting team. Therefore, firms eager to illustrate an interest in doing business with the city submitted bids at yesterday's auction when they normally might not have done so.

The largest portion of the notes, $400 million, was won by a Merrill Lynch Capital Markets group. The six other groups that won portions of the note deal are Dillon Read & Co. with $200 million; Bear Stearns with $100 million; First Boston Corp. with $75 million; Morgan Stanley & Co. and PaineWebber Inc., each with $50 million; and Goldman Sachs & Co. with $25 million.

Interest rates on the notes, which mature June 29, 1990, ranged from 6.247% to 6.419%.

Traders said that New York City notes normally would come at prices to yield about 6.50%, but a "political bid" pushed the yields lower, they said.

In trading yesterday, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bonds due 2018 rose 3/4 to 97, yielding 7.44%.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese Finance Ministry sold 300 billion yen of 20-year bonds yesterday at a coupon rate of 6.8% and at a price to yield 6.739%.

Although the event represented the first time the Japanese government sold 20-year bonds in a year, traders said that most investors paid little attention to the sale and instead focused on the U.S. Treasury's auction of 30-year bonds.

That's because rates on the U.S. Treasury securities are much higher than rates on comparable Japanese securities. The new 30-year bonds auctioned yesterday had a coupon rate of 8.75% at a price to yield 8.84%.

"It would have been better if the coupon (on the Japanese 20-year bond) was 7%," said a dealer at one of the large Japanese brokerage firms. At that level, he said, the bond sale would have drawn more enthusiasm from investors.

The Ministry last issued 20-year bonds in May 1989, offering 503.2 billion yen of bonds with a coupon rate of 5%.
