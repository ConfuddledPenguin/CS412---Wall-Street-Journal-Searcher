How Stock-Picking By Firms Is Judged In Quarterly Review

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/11/90

NEW YORK -- Within reason, brokerage houses in the Journal-Zacks study of stock-picking prowess may choose the list of stocks on which they will be judged. (Of course, they must specify in advance.) Merrill Lynch, PaineWebber and Raymond James are judged by the stocks their analysts rate "1" on a five-point scale. Kidder Peabody is judged by stocks rated "1"or "2." A.G. Edwards is judged by stocks analysts say to "buy" or "accumulate."

Starting at the end of January, Prudential-Bache began publishing a weekly list giving each analyst's "single best idea." It's judged on that, with sell ideas excluded. (For January it was judged on stocks rated "1" for either short or long term, and no lower than "2" for any term.)

At four firms -- Dean Witter, Goldman Sachs, Shearson and Smith Barney, a stock-selection committee reviews analysts' recommendations and picks a select group of stocks as the firm's official recommended list. Those firms are judged on the committee's list.

The study assumes that an investor buys the recommended stocks, and sells all others, even if they're designated as holds or weak buys. Equal dollar amounts are put into each stock, and portfolios are adjusted monthly to keep the amounts equal. All buying and selling is on the last trading day of each month. Commissions and taxes are disregarded, mostly to make the results comparable to market indexes. Dividends are included.

Brokerage houses are given a chance to check the data for accuracy. All 10 firms did so this quarter.
