Goldman Is Sued Over a Data Base By CPM Group

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 05/11/90

NEW YORK -- Goldman, Sachs & Co. was hit with another lawsuit centering on the investment banking firm's commodity operations. Goldman was sued for $9 million by its former commodity-research group, which is now an independent company called CPM Group. In a complaint filed in New York state Supreme Court in Manhattan, CPM claims that Goldman destroyed a major data base on gold, silver and platinum trends that CPM and Goldman had shared, in what the suit alleges was a "conspiracy and scheme" to hurt Goldman competitors.

The CPM suit is the second to be filed recently that focuses on Goldman's large commodity operations. In December, the former head of Goldman Sachs's oil-futures unit, Leo Haviland, alleged in a suit in federal court in Manhattan that he was forced out of his $600,000-a-year job after he refused Goldman partners' "repeated attempts to extort" him into divulging confidential information. The suit claimed the information was about customer accounts, so that the partners could benefit through Goldman's J. Aron commodity-trading division.

Although unrelated, both the Haviland suit and the new one accuse Goldman of misusing its power to gain advantage in the markets.

Goldman has vigorously denied the allegations in the Haviland suit. And in an answer to the CPM suit filed in state court, Goldman denies CPM's allegations and asks that the suit be dismissed. A Goldman spokesman said: "This is a minor contractual dispute over access to our own library materials, which in any event the former consultant had no right to following the termination of the contract in November 1989."

CPM, run by former Goldman Vice President Jeffrey M. Christian, is a consultant and research firm based in New York. Originally an arm of Goldman, the firm went independent in 1986, and its clients included, until last year, Goldman itself. Early this month, the Silver Institute, a trade group, announced it hired CPM to produce statistical reviews of the international silver market. CPM also advises mining companies.

The CPM suit alleges that Goldman and Goldman partner Dennis Suskind reneged on an agreement to maintain the big precious-metal data base, including research files, periodicals and books. CPM said Goldman was obligated to maintain the data base and allow CPM to use it, even if CPM's consulting agreement with Goldman ran out. But the suit claims the data base was "thrown out and destroyed" without notice.

Mr. Suskind declined to comment on the CPM suit beyond the spokesman's statement.

CPM is seeking $4 million in damages, mostly for lost business, and $5 million in punitive damages.
