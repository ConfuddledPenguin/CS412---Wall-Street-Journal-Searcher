International: East Germans Seek More Concessions That Could Lift Bonn's Cost for Union

  • Author: Terence Roth
  • Date: 05/11/90

FRANKFURT, West Germany -- As desperate East German officials portrayed a rapidly deteriorating economy, 100,000 East Germans protested for more concessions that could significantly swell Bonn's outlays for monetary union, already estimated to reach 60 billion marks ($36.8 billion) annually. East German Premier Lothar de Maiziere told East Berlin's parliament yesterday that East Germany's budget deficit is likely to reach a staggering 120 billion West German marks by the end of this year because of rising welfare costs and business bankruptcies. At least 1.5 million of East Germany's 8.5 million workers will have to be retrained or face unemployment by the end of 1991, according to his report.

Guenther Krause, a parliamentary state secretary and a senior East German negotiator in the monetary union talks, candidly told parliament that "we slowly have to begin thinking about who will pay for these 120 billion."

The apparent appeal to Bonn comes as members in the German-German monetary commission, having agreed on a 1-to-1 mark conversion rate for East German savings and wages, are at loggerheads over support for East Germany's crumbling welfare system. The West German mark is scheduled to replace the East German mark beginning July 2.

Chancellor Helmut Kohl, before the Bonn parliament yesterday, conceded that East Germany's budget and other major problems still defied solution, but he said the July 2 date for monetary union remained intact.

In East Berlin, the Party of Democratic Socialism, the renamed Communist Party, accused Mr. de Maiziere's democratic government of bowing to Bonn's terms for monetary union by not demanding a better financial cushion for East German workers and pensioners. While wages and pensions will be changed into West German marks at a 1-to-1 rate, East German wages and pensions are only a third of West German levels, raising fears of impoverishment when a common German market arrives.

East Germans also could face a doubling in their rents, which are heavily subsidized by the state. The uncertainty boiled over yesterday as workers vented anger over possible job and housing losses, and farmers blocked major highways to back demands for continued price subsidies. Most East German farmers also demand protection from West German competitors, whom they fear will pour in with well-oiled distribution systems once the border is opened to unrestricted trade in July.

Industrial workers downed their tools yesterday in warning strikes to protest subsidy cutbacks planned as East Germany shifts from central planning to a free market. Joined in their strike by East German teachers, the workers fear that their jobs at archaic plants face extinction in open competition with the West.

Citing the government study, Mr. Krause, the state secretary, said only a third of East Germany's industrial enterprises are expected to be able to compete after monetary and economic union. More than half are already operating at a loss, but they could be salvaged with subsidies. The remaining 14% face imminent bankruptcy, the report said.

East German enterprises have a combined debt to the state of about 260 billion East German marks. Monetary union negotiators are still wrangling over how -- and by whom -- the sum is to be settled after July, when impoverished East German enterprises will have to service their debt in hard West German marks.
