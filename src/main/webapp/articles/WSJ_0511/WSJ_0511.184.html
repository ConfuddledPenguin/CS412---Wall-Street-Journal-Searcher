Aura Systems Inc. Expects to Report Narrowed Loss for Fiscal Fourth Quarter

  • Author: Richard Turner
  • Date: 05/11/90

EL SEGUNDO, Calif. -- Aura Systems Inc. expects to report that its loss for the fourth quarter ended Feb. 28 narrowed to between $250,000 and $300,000 from a loss of $552,000 a year earlier, President Harry Kurtzman said. He said fourth-quarter revenue went to about $2 million from $1 million a year ago.

For the full year, Mr. Kurtzman said, the loss widened to about $2 million, or 10 cents a share, from last year's loss of $1.5 million, or seven cents a share, on a surge in revenue to more than $9 million from $2.8 million a year ago.

Mr. Kurtzman stressed that the revenue nearly all came from research-and-development contracts, "almost equivalent to investments," he said. "When most people talk about revenue, they talk about selling something," he added. He said the company would be manufacturing and delivering products before the end of fiscal 1991, and said he expects Aura to be profitable for the year.

Chief Financial Officer Frank Phalen said the company had received a contract from General Dynamics Corp. to develop magnetic devices for an advanced reconnaissance system that contains a commitment of about $1 million but could generate revenue of more than $50 million over the next 10 years if the devices later go into production.

Mr. Kurtzman, in an interview, said he expects revenue to double in the coming fiscal year, though he said two-thirds of it would still be the research-and-development area. The company was founded in 1987.

Mr. Kurtzman also outlined progress in the electro-optical and electromagnetic segments of Aura's business. The company holds two patents on a technology it developed to "isolate" equipment in an electromagnetic field, which eliminates vibration. The process can be used to prevent "jitters" in cameras on aircraft, and Mr. Kurtzman said the process also has applications for vehicle shock absorbers, and stereo speakers.

Meanwhile, government research interest continues, Mr. Kurtzman said. He said Aura is waiting for final governmental approval of a $1.2 million contract with the U.S. Air Force to use the high-resolution display system to test infrared military sensors used in intelligence-gathering.

Mr. Kurtzman said there are potential applications in the entertainment business as well. The company's electro-optical unit has five patents pending on a high definition television system, and he said the company would announce a partnership with an entertainment company soon.

Aura has built a prototype for a projection system that potentially could show films transmitted to movie theaters by satellite or cable through a television signal, removing the need for shipping numerous (and costly) film prints to individual theaters. Aura's system purports to display this television image on huge movie screens with film-like resolution and brightness. Mr. Kurtzman said MCA Inc., Paramount Communications Inc., Sony Corp.'s Columbia Pictures, AMC Entertainment Inc. and other companies have expressed interest in the process.

In the electromagnetic area, Mr. Kurtzman said the company's technology could have applications for elevators in tall buildings, to cut down lateral shaking. It could also be used to prevent vibration and blurriness in film cameras that ride on dollies. Aura is also studying the use of the process in stereo speakers, where electromagnetism can convey low-frequency, or bass, notes much lower than conventional speakers can.

The company also has an advanced computing and a microelectronics division that together generated about $1 million in revenue in the past fiscal year, Mr. Kurtzman said.
