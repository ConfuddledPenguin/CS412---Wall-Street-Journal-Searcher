DEATH IN BANGLADESH

A Unicef-sponsored study in Bangladesh, where diarrhea-related illnesses kill 300,000 children a year, has found that complications from even mild malnutrition can kill children suffering from normally minor diseases.

Unicef said the study found that hypoglycemia, which is an abnormally low concentration of sugar in the blood and a frequent consequence of malnutrition, was an important cause of death in children with no overt signs of malnutrition. Twenty-five percent of young diarrhea patients who died had hypoglycemia, according to the study.

The hypoglycemic children studied had insufficient extra body fat and protein to keep normal blood-glucose levels. "This study illustrated how even mild or moderate degrees of malnutrition can result in children dying from what might have been only a minor illness in a better-nourished child," said Michael Bennish, who led the team that studied more than 2,000 children over eight months.

"Deaths {from hypoglycemia} could have been avoided if the children had been fed during their illness, or if they had been better nourished before they developed diarrhea," Dr. Bennish said.

TELEPHONE INVESTIGATION

The European Community Commission began investigating international telephone agreements to see if there was price fixing in violation of EC competition rules. An EC official said the probe was undertaken due to the growing significance of international communications for the EC economy, the fast growth in telecommunications as well as their rapid technological development and rising costs. The official said the commission was examining whether international phone charges were being set by a few major telephone companies and imposed on all members of the 160-nation International Consultative Committee on Telegraph and Telephone.

TRAINING SOVIET MANAGERS

Finnish companies will train up to 400 Soviet business managers a year, Finland's Trade and Industry Minister Ilkka Suominen announced. The program, to get under way later this year, will concentrate on areas in which Finland has special know-how in order "to further promote bilateral trade" between Finland and the Soviet Union.

SOUTH AFRICAN GOLD PLAN

South Africa's state-aided Council for Mineral Technology said the country should sell more of its gold as jewelry. The council said South Africa had stifled jewelry production with excessive regulations and taxes, ostensibly to earn muchneeded foreign exchange as quickly as possible through bullion exports, and had thus ignored the potential 25% added value to be gained from selling gold as jewelry. South Africa recently announced tax concessions and eased regulations to encourage just such a shift.

TOYOTA ISSUES A DENIAL

Toyota Motor Corp. denied it tried to prevent T. Boone Pickens from placing ads in Japanese newspapers in his battle to assert ownership rights in Koito Manufacturing Co., the Japanese auto-parts maker that is closely linked to Toyota. Mr. Pickens, whose 26% stake in Koito makes him the company's largest single investor, said Toyota tried to block him from publishing the ads -- which mention Toyota -- in Japan's major newspapers. The ads, which haven't yet run in Japan, say that collusive business practices force higher prices on consumers. Several Tokyo newspapers said they had held up the ads and were considering publishing a version that omits Toyota's name.

GERMAN BANK SCANDAL

Three bond traders at West Germany's Deutsche Genossenschaftsbank, the nation's fourth-largest bank, were arrested Wednesday on allegations that they personally profited from bond-trading transactions. The arrests were made at the bank. The three traders were in the bank's domestic bond-trading department, which includes trading of West German government bonds.

NICARAGUAN EXPORTS

Nicaragua has resumed exports to the U.S. now that a 1985 trade embargo has been lifted.

The Latin American nation shipped 18,000 metric tons of sugar on Tuesday from the port of Corinto, part of the 54,328 metric tons Nicaragua can sell to the U.S. this year at a preferred price above the world market. Former U.S. President Ronald Reagan imposed the embargo as a measure against the Marxist Sandinista government. President George Bush lifted it after Violetta Chamorro de Barrios won the presidency in February.

The U.S. had been the biggest buyer of Nicaraguan goods until the embargo began. During the embargo, Nicaragua sold most of its sugar to Soviet-bloc nations.

The U.S. embassy in Nicaragua said the Bush administration is considering "steps to reduce barriers and tariffs and open the market to Nicaraguan exports."
