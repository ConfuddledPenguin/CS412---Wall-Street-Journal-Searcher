Economy: Fed's Greenspan Urges Banks Not to Curb Lending in Reaction to Tight Supervision

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 05/11/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan and other banking regulators urged banks not to curb lending in reaction to tougher supervision. In a private meeting with officials of the American Bankers Association, Mr. Greenspan, along with William Seidman, chairman of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., and Robert Clarke, comptroller of the currency, sought to allay fears among bankers that overzealous regulators would crack down on prudent bank lending.

The regulators denied, however, that the unusual meeting was prompted by concerns of some economists, bankers and their borrowers of a credit crunch that could slow the economy.

"The evidence of that is quite limited," Mr. Greenspan said after the meeting here.

Banking regulators have stepped up their policing of lending practices in reaction to the huge losses on real-estate loans in the Southwest in recent years. But many in the banking industry say that regulators have overreacted by taking such actions as requiring some banks to increase reserves, even for performing real-estate loans. The tougher supervision has been felt most severely in the Northeast.

"I think supervisory overkill and overregulation are at the top of the list of concerns right now," said Ken Guenther of the Independent Bankers Association of America, which represents smaller banks. In a statement, the ABA judiciously said: "All {bankers} agree that the rules are being applied with increased vigor."

Mr. Clarke has particularly been criticized by the industry for tightening enforcement of the rules, and one explicit purpose of the meeting was to stress that the regulators are united in their tough approach.

"This is not like Sherman's army marching across the country in a wave," Mr. Clarke said after the meeting. "We are not telling banks to quit making loans. We are telling banks to be careful."

The regulators did say that real estate has come under particular scrutiny, and that includes stepped-up examinations beyond the Northeast. "We are simply doing targeted real-estate examinations in markets that are soft," Mr. Clarke said. "It's inevitable that there will be some constriction of credit."

Both the regulators and the bankers denied that lending has been choked off significantly. But they both said that some bankers are operating under the perception that all loans will be second-guessed by regulators. Bankers have been particularly reluctant to lend to small businesses, said the ABA.

But the regulators, apparently fearing a greater slowdown in the economy, especially in an election year, took pains to encourage prudent lending. Mr. Greenspan flew back to Washington for the afternoon meeting, following his address earlier in the day to a trade group in Chicago.

One trade group official in the Washington meeting said that Mr. Greenspan told them that, "The nature of banking is risk-taking, and if you have zero loan losses, then you are not doing your job."
