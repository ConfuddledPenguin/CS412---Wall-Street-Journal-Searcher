Heard on the Street: Centex Telemanagement Seen as Vulnerable To Phone Rate Restructuring in California

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 05/11/90

NEW YORK -- A moment of truth is nearing for Centex Telemanagement. The highflying, San Francisco-based telephone service company has captured the heart of Wall Street ever since going public in 1987. Earnings and revenue have risen sharply. Cash on hand has risen to $27 million. Long-term debt is naught.

But developments in California's regulatory environment are casting a shadow over Centex's bread-and-butter business.

Centex is like few other companies. It is part middleman, part consultant, part telephone service manager. In return for a fee, Centex links small businesses to the local telephone company and routes customer calls over any of the various lines and services offered by the local company as well as AT&T, MCI and others.

Until now, Centex has benefited from an anomaly in California's rates. Pacific Bell has a lock on the toll calls within each local telephone area, such as from San Jose to San Francisco. For California's small users, such local toll calls are far more expensive than in other states. In fact, Los Angelenos can talk to New Yorkers more cheaply than to many of their in-state neighbors.

Centex has prospered by buying telephone service at the bulk rates available to large users and offering savings to small businesses. But California is restructuring rates. The state's Public Utilities Commission is expected to open up local toll calls to competition and to cut rates by as much as 30% to 40%.

What's more, Pacific Bell asserts that Centex, among others, rents a type of dial tone service at a price below Pacific Bell's cost. Though Centex disputes this, some of Centex's costs could well be raised. A PUC draft decision on rate restructuring is expected this summer.

David Shantz, a program manager in the PUC's Division of Ratepayer Advocates, which represents telephone users, says, "Centex is what I call a price arbitrager. As toll rates come down, the ability of Centex to compete will diminish."

Amy Newmark, an analyst with Homans McGraw Trull Valeo & Co., sees "a major negative impact." Centex "won't be the only game in town" for customers seeking an alternative to Pacific Bell, and there will be less reason to seek such an alternative, she says.

Peter Howley, an MCI and AT&T veteran who is chairman of Centex, concedes that the changes in California might adversely affect some of Centex's business, but he says that in the long run Centex will benefit from increased competition. Moreover, he asserts that the savings Centex offers on California toll calls are a small part of its appeal.

Although 80% of its billings are in California, Centex is expanding on the East Coast. And Mr. Howley, in general, plays down Centex's appeal as a discounter. "Five years ago, it was easy to show a big saving," he says. Today, as long distance competition heats up, analysts say the saving has probably diminished.

Mr. Howley doesn't dispute this. He says Centex's basic appeal is that it relieves small companies of the burden of picking a carrier and of managing its phones. In a favored phrase, Centex provides a "single point of contact" for a customer's telephonic needs. That is intended to ring nostalgic with those yearning for the simplicity of the Ma Bell era.

Centex also offers various teleservices, such as reports allocating a customer's calls to the customer's various clients. James Hickey, an analyst with William Blair, says, "They are that friendly voice you used to hear when you called AT&T."

Jerry Oliver, a specialist on regulatory matters for Pac Bell, agrees that Centex has carved out a niche as a "telemanager."

But analysts say it costs Centex more time and money to market to clients on the basis of service, rather than on price. And while the company is upbeat about its expansion in the East, analysts say Centex has yet to achieve the economies of scale -- or the profit margins -- that it has in California. Certainly, Centex's profit would seem to be highly vulnerable to regulatory changes in its home state.

Ms. Newmark asserts that "telemanaging" requires no hard assets and little technology or capital. Thus, she wonders why, if the business is so good, Centex has yet to attract much competition. Since the barrier to entry would seem to be low, Ms. Newmark says Centex's stock should trade at a modest multiple of earnings.

Tom Barton, a partner of Feshbach Brothers, which has sold short some Centex stock, betting that it will drop, says, "Centex's real game is rate arbitrage, and this game is over." Mr. Barton could be wrong. But with Centex's stock selling at 23 times estimates for 1990 earnings, investors have a great deal to lose if he is right.
