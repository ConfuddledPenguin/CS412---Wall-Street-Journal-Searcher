MEDICINE & HEALTH (A Special Report): Living Right --- Counting Cholesterol

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 05/11/90

WHAT HAPPENS to coronary risk as the cholesterol count goes up? It depends on where it was to begin with, how much it goes up -- and what kind of cholesterol is involved. Coronary-artery disease is rare in populations with an average of 150 milligrams of total cholesterol per deciliter of blood, a level that researchers consider the practical ideal. (The U.S. average, according to one study: 218 milligrams.)

The incidence of coronary disease rises fairly slowly as cholesterol moves toward 200. Above that level, however, the incidence begins to curve upward sharply, and at 240 milligrams the curve gets even steeper. This means that a large number of Americans are already at high risk. Up to one in four of them has a cholesterol count above 240.

One major complicating factor: The count includes two varieties of cholesterol, called LDL and HDL. Patients with the same total count may have very different proportions of HDL and LDL -- and very different degrees of coronary risk.

Doctors are particularly concerned about patients who show a high level of LDL, which does the artery-clogging, and a low amount of HDL, which is now believed to offer some protection against heart disease rather than contributing to it.

Ideally, say researchers, the "bad" LDL ought to be no more than 100 milligrams and the "good" HDL above 40. Anyone with an LDL count over 160 ought to be on a low-fat, low-cholesterol diet regardless of what the HDL is, doctors say. And anyone with an LDL count of 140 and an HDL of less than 40 ought to consider doing the same.
