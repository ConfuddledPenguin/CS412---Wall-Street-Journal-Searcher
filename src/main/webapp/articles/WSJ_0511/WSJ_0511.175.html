World Markets: Hong Kong Stocks Surge After China Releases Dissidents; Shares in London and Tokyo Firm

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/11/90

Hong Kong's key stock index surged 1.4% in the heaviest trading session in three weeks after news that the Beijing government released 200 people held for alleged involvement in China's pro-democracy movement last year. Elsewhere, London shares were mostly steady to firmer, although the key Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index was dragged modestly lower by a number of big blue-chip decliners. Tokyo stocks ended an initially bullish day only slightly higher, as arbitrage activity and profit-taking in the afternoon erased earlier gains, traders said.

In Hong Kong, the blue-chip Hang Seng Index soared 43.15 points, the best gain in more than two months, to close at 2985.42. The broader Hong Kong Index jumped 28.82, to 1961.32. Turnover shot up to 1.46 billion Hong Kong dollars (US$187.5 million) from Wednesday's HK$1.07 billion.

Properties shares outpaced the rest of the market. They were followed by commercial and industrial issues. Utilities issues posted substantial advances, while banking stocks made modest increases.

After opening unchanged, the market was stuck in tight range-trading in the morning. The impact of the news from China involving the release of the dissidents took hold in the afternoon to vault the market to close just off the intraday high, participants said.

Players said overseas funds were back in force to snap up quality blue chips, while speculative interest in various industrial second liners also heated up.

"Trading momentum improved significantly, and healthy follow-through is almost certain Friday," said Simon Chin, research director at Citicorp Scrimgeour Vickers HK Ltd.

There has been a lot of pent-up buying interest lately, a trader said, pointing to the lack of selling pressure during the recent consolidation period as evidence.

Participants say the market should have no problem piercing the key 3000 level on the blue-chip index Friday, but they differ on whether it can stay above that point. A trader at a major European brokerage firm said it could, and set the next target at the 3050 level. Mr. Chin is less certain and said it remains to be seen, as previous attempts this year to breach that point eventually failed.

In London, the FT-SE 100-share index fell 5.7 points to 2157.0, its intraday low. The index hit a session high of 2169.2, up 6.5, in late morning dealings. At the close, the FT 30-share index was down 4.8 at 1690.8. Volume was 406.3 million shares, down from 425 million shares a day earlier.

In the futures market, the June FT-SE 100 index was at 2194.0, up from 2190.0.

A spate of gloomy corporate news peppered the market -- from profit warnings to poor quarterly results. Dealers noted that the news was reflected in the day's big FTSE 100 decliners.

As well, dealers said that the mixed bag of price action on the day reflected a degree of profit-taking before the expiration of the exchange's current two-week trading account Friday, along with reluctance to aggressively take fresh positions so late in the trading account.

Dealers added that the market has grown increasingly cautious ahead of Friday's scheduled release of British retail price index data, particularly given talk that it will show Britain's inflation rate moving into double-digits for the first time since early 1982.

Among the bearish corporate news that helped undermine the market, automotive-parts maker GKN issued a grim profit statement around midday that clipped early gains. GKN plunged 31 pence, to 366 pence ($6.14) a share, after reporting a decline in pretax profit for the first four months of the year as a result of sagging demand and labor strikes affecting some of its key customers.

Shares took another tumble late in the session, after the release of disappointing first-quarter results from General Accident, Britain's largest composite insurer. General Accident fell 14, to 1026, after reporting an #80.9 million ($135.8 million) pretax loss for the first quarter. It cited winter-storm claims and a deterioration in the underlying business in most of its major markets.

British Petroleum fell 5, to 311. Britain's largest petroleum company said net income dropped 43% on a historic cost basis, with depressed chemical earnings and a drop in value of its inventory countering strong earnings growth in exploration and production.

Tokyo had a bullish opening tied to buying inspired by a stronger yen. But partly because it was expiration day for May options, arbitragers actively liquidated their cash stock against short futures positions in the afternoon.

As a result, the Nikkei index of 225 selected issues closed 34.65 points higher at 30980.26. The index fell 24.97 points Wednesday. Volume on the first section was estimated at 730 million shares, down from 753.5 million shares Wednesday.

In trading in Tokyo Friday, the Nikkei index rose 185.68 points to 31165.94 at the end of the morning session.

Thursday's Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which gained 5.19 points Wednesday, rose 4.06 points, or 0.18%, at 2312.85.

The second-section index, which advanced 46.02 points Wednesday, rose 38.25 Thursday, or 1.03%, to close at 3756.15.

Volume in the second section was estimated at 19 million shares, up from 18.6 million shares Wednesday.

The key Nikkei index gained more than 295 points to its intraday high in the morning, as buyers were inspired to seek out domestic-demand related issues on the back of a stronger yen, according to J. Graham Biggart, head of futures and options trading at Schroder Securities (Japan) Ltd. But the currency's strength induced some profit-taking on high-tech, export-oriented issues, Mr. Biggart said.

Greg Byndy, manager of equity trading at Merrill Lynch Japan Inc., said afternoon activity was dictated by options-related strategies.

Although some futures and options traders didn't expect much arbitrage activity Thursday afternoon, Mr. Byndy said that most had anticipated the selling on the cash indexes, as it usually occurs on options expiration day.

The buying on the strength of the yen, then, was negated by arbitrage selling.

Advancing issues outnumbered declining issues 623 to 348 Thursday.

Profit-taking brought down prices of high-tech shares, steels and shipbuildings, which have mostly been gaining for the past week. Sony closed at 8440, up 20, after trading as high as 8500. Matsushita Electric Industrial fell 40 to 2220 and Toshiba fell 10 to 1070. Nippon Steel fell 15 to 605 and Nisshin Steel fell 12 to 913.

Separately, the Tokyo Stock Exchange disclosed data on arbitrage positions for the week ended May 2. Stocks being held against June stock-index futures totaled 424.9 million shares, up 3.6 million from the previous week. The market value of shares against stock-index futures was 600.17 billion yen ($3.83 billion), up 16.79 billion yen from the week earlier.

In other market activity:

Share prices closed higher in Stockholm and Zurich; lower in Amsterdam and Brussels; and mixed in Frankfurt, Paris and Milan. South African gold stocks closed firmer.

Share prices were higher in Singapore, and were lower in Manila, Seoul, Sydney, Taipei and Wellington.
