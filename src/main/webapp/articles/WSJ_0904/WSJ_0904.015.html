Foreign Exchange: Analysts, Traders Predict Further Fall for Dollar, Citing Weak U.S. Economy, Chance of Rate Cuts

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/04/90

The dollar is expected to falter further against most major currencies this month and much of this autumn. Worsening U.S. economic fundamentals, which could lead to lower U.S. interest rates when rates abroad are rising, will erode support for the dollar, according to 10 economists and traders surveyed by Dow Jones International News Service in New York, Tokyo, Frankfurt and London.

Meanwhile, the crisis in the Persian Gulf hangs ominously over the market, providing a constant source of volatility.

The narrowing U.S. trade deficit and the emergence of problems besetting other economies could provide an undercurrent of strength for the dollar in the long run, economists say. But they disagree on whether these factors will start to turn the dollar around before winter.

By the end of September, the dollar is expected to plumb new depths against the German currency, sinking to 1.53 marks, according to those in the survey. The dollar's all-time low against the mark, 1.5385 marks, was reached Aug. 27.

Despite one bullish analyst's forecast that the dollar will rise to 1.60 marks by the end of September and as high as 1.75 marks by the end of November, the average forecast in the survey calls for the dollar to weaken to 1.51 marks in the next three months, with one estimate as low as 1.45 marks.

The dollar also will weaken to 141 yen by the end of September and to 140 yen by the end of November, according to the average forecast. Forecasts stayed within the 140-yen to 143-yen range for the 30-day period but ranged from 131 yen to 152 yen for the next 90 days. Predictions made about a month ago called for the dollar to be at 1.6045 marks, 148.03 yen and 1.3598 Swiss francs at the end of August and for the pound to stand at $1.8315.

In late New York trading Friday, the dollar stood at 1.5755 marks, 143.79 yen and 1.3084 Swiss francs. The pound was quoted at $1.8905. There was no trading in New York yesterday because of the Labor Day holiday. Late yesterday in London, the dollar was quoted at 1.5837 marks and 143.53 yen. In Tokyo, meanwhile, the dollar finished Monday at 143.55 yen.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday the dollar was quoted at 143.77 yen, down from 143.80 yen at the open but above the 143.53 yen in London late yesterday. New York markets were closed for the Labor Day holiday yesterday.

Since Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait, developments in the Persian Gulf have overshadowed economic indicators as well as events in Eastern Europe. But as the threat of hostilities in the Mideast waxes and wanes, and participants adjust positions accordingly, attention has begun to shift back toward fundamentals.

Most of the economists surveyed made their estimates on the assumption the Mideast conflict will be resolved without fighting. However, even a war there wouldn't necessarily bring an economically shattering scenario, said Akira Suzuki, chief manager of foreign exchange at Mitsubishi Trust & Banking Corp. in Tokyo.

Tim Fox, treasury economist at Midland Bank PLC in London, suggested that the dollar would shoot upward briefly if hostilities were to erupt but would then continue its downtrend.

The dollar's recent emergence as a less attractive haven in an international crisis than its Swiss or British counterparts reflects the bearish view that prevailed before the U.S.-Iraqi confrontation in the Gulf began. This bearishness is expected to persist in the next few months.

Central bank actions will continue driving currency markets, traders and economists say. The dollar suffers from U.S. interest rates that are relatively low in inflation-adjusted terms, and it will probably become even more unappealing as an investment currency, they say. An early easing of rates by the U.S. Federal Reserve is seen as likely, as are further rate increases by other central banks.

Michael Rosenberg, international foreign exchange economist with Merrill Lynch Capital Markets Group in New York, said U.S. fiscal and monetary policy already is shifting away from an anti-inflation stance and toward recession avoidance, a move he said is undermining the U.S.'s credibility as an inflation-fighter.

Meanwhile, the Bank of Japan last week raised its discount rate 0.75 percentage point to 6%. The increase in the rate the central bank charges on loans to financial institutions was the fifth in a series that began in May 1989, when the rate was at a historic low of 2.5%.

"The interest rate gap in favor of U.S. rates will reverse," said Mr. Suzuki.

One economist called 140 yen a firm support level for the dollar and said he expects the dollar to begin edging higher at the end of November.

The mark's outlook depends largely on the state of the German economy as the country completes the process of reunification this fall, traders and economists said. Allan Leslie of Discount Corp. in New York said the process "has to be inflationary," that "it would be unbelievable for it not to have a major inflationary impact."

Rising inflation and a shrinking trade surplus are expected to exert upward pressure on German interest rates. Several economists predict an eventual rate rise by the Bundesbank, although most said the central bank will try to wait until after the all-German elections set for Dec. 2.

Ernest Pullmann, chief foreign exchange trader and vice president for Deutsche Girozentrale in Frankfurt, suggested that despite the shrinking of the trade surplus and high cost of unification, the Bundesbank won't raise rates before next spring.

Uncertainty in the Middle East will continue to provide support to the pound, but as the threat of severe oil shortages wanes, some of that strength could diminish.

Sterling's status as a high-yielding currency could also be undermined by widespread expectations that Britain will cut its interest rates shortly after the pound enters the exchange rate mechanism of the European Monetary System.

When sterling will join the mechanism, however, remains open to speculation, with predictions ranging from as soon as next month to well into 1991 by one account.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery dropped $4.40 to settle at $382.30 an ounce. Estimated volume was a moderate four million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $387.75. an ounce.
