Law: California High Court Bars Release of Tapes In Menendez Case

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/04/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- The California Supreme Court blocked release of a psychologist's tape-recorded conversations with the two sons of slain Live Entertainment Inc. Chairman Jose Menendez and his wife, Kitty. The tapes, due to have been released to prosecutors today, could be a key piece of evidence in the case against the sons, Lyle and Erik Menendez, accused of murdering their parents last year. They have denied the charges.

The court issued a stay pending its review of the brothers' petition to overturn a recent lower court ruling that ordered release of the tapes. The high court will act on the petition before Oct. 26, a court clerk said.

Earlier this month, Judge James Albracht accepted prosecutors' arguments that the brothers allegedly threatened psychologist Leon Jerome Oziel, thereby invalidating the confidentiality of doctor-patient communications. The judge indicated that none of the recorded sessions were privileged because the psychologist had reason to believe that the brothers were a threat to him that should be disclosed to avoid danger.

On Thursday, the ABC television show "Prime Time Live" featured an interview with a woman identified as a former girlfriend of Mr. Oziel's, who claimed the brothers confessed to the murders in sessions with the psychologist last fall. The woman said she overheard the sessions because the psychologist had asked her to listen to them from outside his office, allegedly because he was concerned for his safety. The woman later told police about the sessions and the existence of the tapes, according to the program.

Jose Menendez and his wife were shot Aug. 20, 1989, in their Beverly Hills, Calif. home. Their sons alerted police, saying they had discovered the bodies on their return from the movies. However, the sons were later charged with the murders, and the tapes played a prominent role after Los Angeles County district attorney Ira Reiner said they contained "very important" evidence supporting allegations that the brothers killed their parents to gain access to a $14 million estate.
