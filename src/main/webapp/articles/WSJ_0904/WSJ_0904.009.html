Here's an Instance Where Bankers Don't Bemoan Over-Regulation

  • Author: Ron Suskind
  • Date: 09/04/90

Today, plenty of bankers in white shirts (better to show the tan) are returning to old mail and August phone messages after nice long vacations. At least two weeks. Gotta be two weeks. Or they might get slapped with a regulatory citation. What? Has America's banking crisis come to this: Regulators are now ordering mandatory vacations?

Absolutely. In fact, they've been doing it for years.

"It's banking folk wisdom that, believe it or not, is actually written into our regulatory procedures," says Ellen Stockdale of the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, which regulates all federally insured banks. The two-week policy applies to savings and loans and credit unions as well. The idea, says Ms. Stockdale, is that "in 10 consecutive business days off, if someone's hand is in the till, it will show up. It's hard to manipulate the books when you're on vacation."

That's right. Summertime . . . and the investigating is easy.

The policy "is especially useful in certain types of fraud, like check kites, deposit scams and playing with trust funds," says James G. Richmond, who oversees the Justice Department's probes of savings and loan fraud. "It's not a simple matter of arresting people after their August getaway, but in terms of bank fraud, vacation time is a good place to start."

Whether banks have a branch in the U.S. or are based here, they are governed by U.S. regulatory guidelines. Constantin Phacos, a vice president in the Geneva office of Manufacturers Hanover Corp., was vacationing in Washington, D.C., last fall when a customer's call alerted a bank clerk to accounting irregularities. Mr. Phacos was called home from his vacation and charged with embezzlement, forgery and misappropriation of client funds. He denies the charges, which haven't yet come to trial. Manufacturers Hanover recently took a $50 million charge against earnings to cover his alleged crimes.

"The first thing you should be suspicious of," says Clare Sykes of New York's Banking Department, "is anyone in banking who doesn't want to take a vacation.

"The problem is with Japanese bankers who work here for banks based in Japan," Ms. Sykes adds. "They're used to getting one week, and here they take two. But they always are adamant that we note in our examination reports that it is required {by regulators}. Otherwise their bosses in Tokyo will think they're sloughing off."
