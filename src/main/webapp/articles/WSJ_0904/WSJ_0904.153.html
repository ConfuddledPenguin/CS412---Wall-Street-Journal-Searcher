Purchase of Big Columbia S&L Portfolio Might Be Scrapped if Economy Worsens

  • Author: Robert Sherbin Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/04/90

HONG KONG -- The Canadian and Hong Kong parties that agreed last month to buy Columbia Savings & Loan Association's $3 billion junk-bond portfolio may back out of the deal if the U.S. economy deteriorates further. In an interview, a top official connected with the purchase said the prospective buyers, three groups controlled by Hong Kong billionaire Li Ka-shing and Gordon Investment Corp., a Toronto merchant bank, became concerned about the transaction when the Persian Gulf crisis erupted.

"We are monitoring the situation very closely," said Richard Li, son of the tycoon and managing director of the corporate fund management committee of Hutchison Whampoa Ltd. "We are aware of our options and would exploit our options if necessary."

Mr. Li said his group and Gordon Investment have until Oct. 2 to decide whether to go through with the purchase of the portfolio from the Beverly Hills, Calif., thrift. If the decision were made today, he said, they would go through with the deal. But he said they would back out if it appeared the U.S. economy would weaken to the point where the bond issuers couldn't meet their obligations.

The groups wouldn't face any penalty if they were to scrap the agreement before the deadline, Mr. Li added.

Late last month, the Li group said it would buy half of Columbia's portfolio of junk bonds, believed to be largest held by any U.S. savings and loan association, by taking a 50% stake in a partnership owned by Gordon Investment, which had just agreed to acquire the portfolio. Hutchison, a major local conglomerate, and sister company Cheung Kong (Holdings) Ltd., a real estate group, each agreed to take 20% interests in the venture, with a company controlled by the elder Li taking the remaining 10%.

Together with Gordon Investment, the Li group agreed to pay $300 million to acquire the portfolio and finance the remaining 90% on a non-recourse basis at 10.5% interest. That means that the partnership can drop the portfolio if its losses exceed the down payment and Columbia would have to take the portfolio back.

Mr. Li said the groups have retained a number of U.S.-based investment and legal advisers to help determine whether they should proceed with the transaction, but declined to identify them. He also wouldn't say whether it was possible to modify the agreement as an alternative to pulling out altogether.

The 23-year-old executive said the parties are chiefly interested in determining whether the economic slowdown, which has been aggravated by soaring oil prices, would prevent the 176 issuers from meeting their debt payments, which carry yields ranging between 14% and 18%.

"It really depends on the general economic outlook and how different industries would be affected," he said. "Based on today's outlook, it would be a go, but a lot can happen in the next 30 days."

As well as pursuing the junk-bond portfolio for its investment potential, Mr. Li said the Hong Kong partners are interested in using it to gain expertise on the U.S. market as part of their goal to diversify its investments beyond Hong Kong.

The possiblity that the deal could fall through didn't greatly surprise several local securities analysts.

John Mulcahy, research director at Peregrine Brokerage Ltd., said the prospect of high inflation and slow growth in the U.S. would make it difficult for companies with high-yielding bonds to make good on them. He also said it wouldn't be much of a loss for the Li companies if the deal fell through.

"I'd heave a sigh of relief, quite frankly," Mr. Mulcahy said. "There (are) different ways to acquire an education about the U.S. than this. There are high risks associated with this investment, and junk bonds aren't something one would invest in a Hong Kong conglomerate for."
