Politics & Policy: Crisis in the Mideast Adds an Unexpected Twist To Midterm Elections Already Considered Pivotal

  • Author: David Shribman
  • Date: 09/04/90

WASHINGTON -- Suddenly an unpredictable element -- the Gulf crisis -- has been injected into the fall state and congressional campaigns. For months, candidates have been carefully positioning themselves on the issues of abortion, the thrift crisis, campaign finance and the budget. But as the midterm campaigns get under way in earnest this week, candidates in both parties are wondering how the current crisis will skew November's elections.

"Iraq is the big `X' factor," says Kevin Phillips, the Republican political theorist. "You have to know how many casualties there are. You have to know what happens to hostages. You have to know what happens to oil prices. And you probably have to know what's happening with the stock market."

The political possibilities range from a move to rally-'round-the-leader, which could help Republicans, to an economic recession, which could be devastating to Republicans, to a situation where a standoff or a shooting war overwhelms all other issues.

So far, candidates have approached the Middle East crisis with deliberate caution, lining up behind the president and seldom voicing any second thoughts about the massive deployment of U.S. soldiers and materiel to Saudi Arabia; only in a few scattered states have even the faintest hints emerged of a debate over U.S. policy toward Saddam Hussein. But in the next nine weeks, as the contours of the Gulf crisis become clearer, the issue will surely take on a political tint.

"We have moved into the most volatile two months of an election year in memory," says Paul Maslin, a Democratic political pollster. "It's foolhardy to think that there won't be any impact, but it's foolhardy to predict what the impact will be."

The Gulf crisis becomes a wild card in a campaign that already has carried enormous political implications.

The fall elections will not only determine control of the governor's office in 35 states, including close contests in three critical states -- Florida, Texas and California. They also will play a substantial role in the struggle over abortion rights -- now primarily a state issue -- and will have a direct effect on reapportionment and next year's struggle to redraw the nation's legislative-district lines.

"These elections will have a lot to say about who serves in Congress in the next decade," says Alan Heslop, research associate at the Rose Institute of State and Local Government at California's Claremont McKenna College. "Parties that lose in November lose more than one election, because a party in power can gerrymander itself into retaining a majority for the rest of the decade."

Moreover, the fall elections are an important overture to 1992, when President Bush presumably will seek a second term and when control of the Senate, now held with a 55-45 margin by the Democrats, could be at stake.

Democrats must do well in this fall's elections to buttress themselves for 1992, when they defend 20 seats, including those of 11 freshmen, many of whom won by extremely small margins. The Republicans feel that if they can pick up at least two or three seats, they can win control of the Senate in the presidential election year of 1992.

Meanwhile, as the nation girds for possible war and recession, the candidates are worrying whether their campaign messages might seem out of sync with public concerns.

"The situation is forcing people to reconsider their strategies," says Rep. Beryl Anthony of Arkansas, chairman of the Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee. "The budget and the S&L crisis are off the front page, so a lot of ads and speeches all of a sudden seem out of context."

At the same time, a number of groups that saw the fall elections as a critical juncture are troubled that the public won't get a chance to focus on issues such as abortion. On television, in newspapers and on the street corner, Iraq is crowding out nearly every other issue.

"This crisis is of very great importance, but I'm worried about the distraction it causes," says Kate Michelman, executive director of the National Abortion Rights Action League. "People aren't talking about change at the Supreme Court -- a factor that is going to affect the fabric of our lives for many years to come. And interest in major domestic issues has diminished."

Burke Balch, legislative director of the National Right to Life Committee, believes that the Iraq crisis might help anti-abortion candidates. Abortion-rights activists "aren't going to retain their single-issue voters on abortion -- their supporters are interested in a lot of issues, not only abortion -- but we'll retain ours," he says.

For the time being at least, many analysts feel that deepening public concern about events in the Persian Gulf might benefit incumbents who otherwise were facing the threat of an anti-Washington backlash. Though the crisis has accelerated the economic slide, it has also kept incumbents involved in the summer's major news story in a way that challengers can't match, and it has diverted attention from anti-incumbent issues such as the budget standoff and the S&L bailout.

"People may not be willing to make quite as many changes," says Celinda Lake, a Democratic political pollster. "And there seem to be bigger issues to talk about than whether your representative in Congress took an honorarium or not."

This year's struggles for the governor's office have the potential to overshadow the congressional elections. Nowhere is a gubernatorial race more important than in California, where GOP Sen. Pete Wilson and Democrat Dianne Feinstein, the former San Francisco mayor, are running even in the polls; the winner will preside over reapportionment of the nation's largest state. As a result of this year's census, California is expected to add seven House seats to the 47 it currently holds.

At the same time, races on the state level will determine control of important state legislatures, including the New York state Senate, now dominated by Republicans, and both houses in Pennsylvania, where Republicans control the Senate and Democrats control the House.

In races for national offices, Republicans hope they can knock off Democratic incumbents in Hawaii, Iowa and Rhode Island. They expect to hold almost all their own seats, including in North Carolina, where, in perhaps the nation's hottest race, Jesse Helms is facing a tough challenge from former Democratic Mayor Harvey Gantt of Charlotte.

Little change is expected in the House, where the re-election rate in the past decade was 94.5%. The Democrats hope to add marginally to their 62-seat majority. Republicans take comfort in the argument that the party holding the White House normally loses seats in midterm elections; nevertheless, such a loss would underscore the party's weakness in House races. Even if they held their own, Republicans would return with only 176 seats.

As the campaign season gets under way, Republicans are viewing the president's high support ratings with caution. "Loss of life {in a Gulf war} could make a big difference," warns Linda DiVall, a GOP political consultant. "And when you combine the economic uncertainties, there's real volatility. These factors could cause a lot of short-term surges on voter decisions, and they could either reward or punish the president's party."
