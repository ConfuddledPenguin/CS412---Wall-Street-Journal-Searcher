Apex Oil Emerges From Chapter 11 As Leaner Concern

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/04/90

ST. LOUIS, Mo. -- Apex Oil Co. emerged from Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings, significantly smaller than when it filed for protection from creditors on Christmas Eve, 1987. The closely held concern grew from a tiny oil-trading and transportation operation to become, partly through its highly leveraged 1981 acquisition of Clark Oil & Refining Co., a petroleum conglomerate with annual revenue of about $13 billion.

But after the company had big losses in oil futures trading, and the value of its oil inventory tumbled because of the petroleum market's mid-decade slump, Apex's lenders pulled the plug on vital loan agreements.

Apex cleared its debts with secured lenders in full in 1988, by selling its Clark Oil unit to a partnership composed of Horsham Corp., Toronto, and Apex owners Paul A. Novelly and Samuel R. Goldstein.

Under the court-approved reorganization, Messrs. Goldstein and Novelly will together lend $15 million to Apex. Banque Indosuez of Geneva and Banque Paribas of New York each loaned Apex $15 million in the reorganization.

Apex's unsecured holders will receive as much as $20 million in cash, $20 million in seven-year notes secured by Apex assets to be sold over two years, and $20 million in 10-year notes secured by a portion of anticipated profits from Clark Oil Trading Co. Clark Oil Trading, owned by Messrs. Novelly & Goldstein, isn't associated with the bigger Clark Oil & Refining unit that was sold, nor did it ever come under Chapter 11 protection.

The settlement with unsecured debtholders represents payment of 64 cents to 86 cents on the dollar.

Apex, which said it operated profitably throughout the Chapter 11 period, now plans to focus on its oil trading, oil terminal and shipping operations.
