Gulf Resources' Moves to Acquire Assets In New Zealand Are Concern for EPA

  • Author: David Stipp
  • Date: 09/04/90

Officials of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency said they are "concerned" about Gulf Resources & Chemical Corp.'s decision to invest much of its assets in New Zealand while it potentially faces tens of millions of dollars in liabilities for an environmental cleanup. Separately, New Zealand regulators have begun an investigation of alleged insider-trading violations by Gulf Resources officials in connection with the company's investments there, according to the EPA. Lawrence Mehl, Gulf Resources' general counsel, confirmed that New Zealand authorities had "looked into" possible violations by the company earlier this year. "I am unaware of" current investigations by New Zealand regulators, he added. New Zealand authorities couldn't be reached for comment.

The EPA's worries about Gulf Resources, based in Boston, stem from the company's acquisition earlier this year of a 62% stake in City Realties Ltd., New Zealand, in a transaction valued at about $150 million. "To the extent {Gulf Resources} no longer has assets in the U.S., it may be difficult for the government to collect" payments from it for cleanup of the now-defunct Bunker Hill smelter in northern Idaho, said Allan Bakalian, an assistant regional counsel with the EPA in Seattle.

The 21-square-mile Bunker Hill site, contaminated with lead, zinc and other metals, is one of the nation's largest cleanup projects under the federal government's Superfund program. Gulf Resources, which operated the smelter from 1968 to 1982, is negotiating with the government over its share of the cleanup costs, which could be more than $100 million, the EPA said. Gulf Resources also owes pension benefits associated with the smelter that have been estimated to total as much as $100 million.

As reported, in an agreement last year with the EPA, Gulf Resources amended a reorganization plan that would have made it a unit of a Bermuda concern, potentially exempting it from liability for the smelter cleanup. Among other things, Gulf Resources agreed to give the U.S. government advance written notice before issuing any dividends or common stock or making any loans exceeding $50 million to the Bermuda holding company that was to have acquired it.

But while bargaining with government lawyers last October about its planned transfer of assets to Bermuda, Gulf Resources was quietly negotiating New Zealand transactions that invested much of its assets overseas, said the EPA's Mr. Bakalian. "They never told us they were doing that" until the New Zealand transaction was closed in January, he added. "We're concerned they weren't totally forthright."

Currently, it isn't clear what actions the government may take, if any, concerning Gulf Resources' overseas investments. But if the company refuses to pay its cleanup liabilities, "there are options the EPA has to collect" them even if the concern's assets are invested outside the U.S., Mr. Bakalian said.

In Boston, Gulf Resources' Mr. Mehl said, "I'm not sure I understand the EPA's concern. At the time we signed contracts to buy New Zealand properties last January, we made a very full public announcement."

In another development, San Francisco businessman Donald S. Kennedy confirmed that his D.S. Kennedy & Co. recently raised its stake in Gulf Resources to 24.4% from about 22%. The concern earlier said it may seek control of Gulf Resources, which is now controlled by British corporate raider and real estate investor David J. Rowland.
