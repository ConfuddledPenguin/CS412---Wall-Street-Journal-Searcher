Daily News Unions To Ask Advertisers To Boycott Paper

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 09/04/90

NEW YORK -- New York newspaper unions, locked in difficult negotiations with Tribune Co.'s Daily News, will ask advertisers today to boycott the newspaper. In a related development, the 10 unions are preparing a citywide outdoor advertising campaign, using billboards and city buses to attack what they say is the "anti-labor" stance of Daily News management. The ad campaign is to begin next week.

The call for an advertiser boycott against the tabloid follows months of rancor at the bargaining table. The paper's 10 unions, representing about 2,800 employees, have been working without contracts since March 30. Little progress has been made, and tensions have escalated.

The unions have publicly ridiculed Daily News Publisher James Hoge, while Tribune Co. has prepared extensive contingency plans in case of a strike, with replacement staff from pressmen to reporters ready to step in.

The unions have prepared, too, sending out two letters in recent months to 75 top Daily News advertisers, asking them to suspend advertising in the paper when they are asked. A recent letter said that "we will be boycotting to force the Daily News to abandon its anti-labor strategy."

Large advertisers that ignore the boycott could feel the wrath of the unions, said Deborah Friedman, vice president of Kamber Group, a public relations firm that represents the unions. Uncooperative advertisers will be targeted by union members, who will pass out leaflets outside their stores "telling people what the store is doing to hurt working people," she said. "Hopefully it won't get to that point."

John Sloan, Daily News vice president, called the boycott "a pathetic attempt at disinformation and diversion. It's a misguided campaign of intimidation and threats and coercion against our advertisers."

The Daily News has taken its own steps. After five of the unions authorized strike votes, it advertised last spring for replacement workers, attracting at least 15,000 applicants.

It's unclear how successful the boycott will be, but if it works, it will worsen an already difficult year at the newspaper. The Daily News, like other major metropolitan newspapers, is struggling with steady declines in local ad linage. In the first half of 1990, Daily News full-run ad linage fell 14%. The poor advertising market and costs related to labor strife have led analysts to predict the Daily News could have a loss of as much as $45 million this year on revenue of more than $400 million.

"The unions are directing their efforts toward business people who have the same objectives that we have: to run an efficient operation," Mr. Sloan said. "It's doomed to fail."
