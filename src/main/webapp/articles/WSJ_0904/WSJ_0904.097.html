Adolph Coors Faces Criminal Charges On Water-Pollution Case in Colorado

  • Author: Kevin Helliker
  • Date: 09/04/90

The attorney general of Colorado is investigating charges that Adolph Coors Co., leader of a national campaign to clean up the nation's waters, knowingly hid the fact that it had leaky pipes at a can plant, pipes that allegedly contaminated nearby springs in the 1970s and early 1980s. The investigation means that Coors, which already faces civil charges of water-law violation, could be slapped with criminal charges as well. Both could result in financial penalties.

Already, though, the allegations represent a public-relations disaster. Coors, based in Golden, Colo., has long boasted in its advertisements about the pureness of its beer because it contains Rocky Mountain spring water. And these days Peter Coors, brewery division president, can be seen on national television encouraging viewers to follow Coors in its support of Pure Water 2000, an environmental organization to which the company last year decided to donate $1 million.

The company's credibility as a clean-water crusader isn't helped by the water-pollution charges, acknowledged Coors spokesman Jon Goldman. "We're facing some embarrassment," he said.

The alleged contamination by Coors was first disclosed early this year by a Colorado television station after receiving a tip.

In response, Coors hired an attorney to investigate, and the resulting report confirmed that contamination had occurred, but not to a degree that posed any health hazard.

Coors presented its findings to the Colorado Department of Health's Water Quality Control Division, which conducted its own investigation and in June brought civil charges against the company. The agency contended, among other things, that groundwater contaminated with organic chemicals, including one probable carcinogen, was released from a well under the company's can plant into Clear Creek, a source of water for five municipalities, from 1982 to 1985.

The violations carry civil fines of up to $10,000 a day, and Coors' illegal discharges continued for more than 4,000 days, the agency's report said. In contamination cases of long duration, fines are usually negotiated, or else determined by a judge.

In June, Sandra Woods, Coors vice president for environmental affairs, predicted that within 30 days a penalty would be negotiated that she said would be "very fair to Coors."

But more than two months later, no agreement has been reached, and though Coors officials say they still hope to settle the matter out of court, an investigator for the water agency, Patricia Nelson, said last week that she saw little chance of that. "We are going forward with plans for a formal hearing" before a judge, she said.

Now, with the attorney general having instituted his own investigation, Coors could find itself under attack in two separate legal arenas.

For their part, Coors officials said the attorney general's investigation came as no surprise. "When we brought this problem to the attention of the state we expected these investigations, and we are fully cooperating," Mr. Goldman said.
