International: Emigration in Hong Kong Likely to Jump This Year

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/04/90

HONG KONG -- Emigration from Hong Kong will leap almost 50% in 1990 because of fears of China's takeover in 1997, the British colonial government announced. The government said 62,000 people are expected to leave the territory this year, compared with 42,000 last year, a jump of 48%.

A government spokesman said emigration was expected to level out at about 60,000 people a year, or 1,150 people a week. In 1991, he predicted, 58,000 will leave the colony.

Most of those leaving are members of Hong Kong's well-educated middle class, who have turned this 1,063-square-kilometer patch of islands and peninsula on the southern Chinese coast into an economic powerhouse. Their departure has sparked fears of economic and social problems in Hong Kong once China resumes control of the territory in 1997.

Interest in emigration has skyrocketed since China cracked down on a movement for freedom in Beijing in June of last year. The crackdown convinced many in the territory that China could easily break its promise to maintain the colony's freewheeling economic and social system for 50 years.

London's refusal to give its colonial subjects the right to live in Britain had a role in stimulating emigration.
