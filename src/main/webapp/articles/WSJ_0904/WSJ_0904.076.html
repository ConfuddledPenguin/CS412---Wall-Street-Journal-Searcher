Ensign Bank Said To Be Insolvent; U.S. Takes It Over

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 09/04/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal regulators took over Ensign Bank, a New York thrift controlled by Ted Arison, chairman of Miami-based Carnival Cruise Lines Inc. The Office of Thrift Supervision said Ensign became insolvent because "poor underwriting, inadequate appraisal policies and, to a lesser degree, the softening New York-area real estate market" left the thrift saddled with a high level of nonperforming assets.

As of June 30, Ensign had assets of $1.78 billion, liabilities of $1.79 billion and tangible capital of negative $174.8 million, for a capital-to-assets ratio of negative 14.48%.

A spokesman for Mr. Arison said in a statement that Mr. Arison was "shocked and outraged" by the regulators' actions. Ensign was in the middle of negotiations with federal officials to recapitalize the thrift and to bring it into full compliance with regulatory requirements, the spokesman said.

"There are problems with the bank -- no one denies that," the spokesman said. "But the bank wasn't going to fold." Under plans being developed with regulators, Mr. Arison was prepared to add "substantial amounts of new capital" to the thrift, he said.

Ensign's president and chief executive officer, Alan Master, said the move by regulators was a "terrible and unnecessary loss not only to the American taxpayer but also to the communities served by Ensign and to Ensign's employees."

In 1985, the thrift, which has 19 branches in New York, New Jersey and Florida, began "markedly" expanding its construction, land and commercial real estate lending, the OTS said. Nonperforming assets increased from $16.2 million, or 1.58% of total assets, on Dec. 31, 1985, to $174.5 million, or 9.14% of total assets, on Dec. 31, 1989, the agency said.

The OTS said a whopping $416.7 million, or 22.2% of total assets, were classified as "substandard, doubtful, or loss" as of March 31. Ensign had net losses of $38 million in 1989 and $34.4 million for the first half of this year. Since May, the thrift had been operating under an agreement with the regulators restricting its loan and investment activities.

The institution will remain open under the control of the Resolution Trust Corp., the agency set up last year to sell failed savings and loans.
