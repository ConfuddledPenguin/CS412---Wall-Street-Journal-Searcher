LEISURE & ARTS: Time Off: Diversions and Excursions Sept. 4-17

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/04/90

ART

International Center of Photography Midtown, New York: "Man Ray/Bazaar Years: A Fashion Retrospective" documents the groundbreaking fashion photography of Man Ray, the surrealist artist. The more than 140 photos, many incorporating as props sculpture by Man Ray and BUSY such friends as Brancusi and Giacometti, are distinguished by both technical experimentation and dramatic imagery. Sept. 7-Nov. 25. The exhibit then embarks on a two-year world tour.

For those who prefer a behind-the-scenes perspective, beginning next month there's a 10-lecture series, "The Photographers," with participants scheduled to include the German-born Life Magazine legend Alfred Eisenstaedt. Advance registration required; call (212) 860-1776 for information.

Also premiering at ICP Midtown this week is "The Power to Heal: Ancient Arts & Modern Medicine." Subjects range from Tibetan healers administering pills made from diamonds and rubies, to capuchin monkeys helping quadriplegics in Boston. Through Oct. 14. 1133 Avenue of the Americas at 43rd Street.

National Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C.: "Kazimir Malevich, 1878-1935" -- About 170 paintings, works on paper and architectural models by this Soviet avant-garde artist. Of special interest are Malevich's original set and costume designs for the cubo-futurist opera "Victory Over the Sun," which premiered to boos and a hail of rotten vegetables in St. Petersburg in 1913 and was then suppressed for years in the Soviet Union. Fourth Street at Constitution Avenue NW, Sept. 16-Nov. 4. The show then travels to the new Armand Hammer Museum and Cultural Center, Los Angeles (Nov. 25, 1990-Jan. 13, 1991); and the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York (Feb. 7-March 24, 1991).

Met Life Gallery, New York: "Saving Our Past -- A Race Against Time" -- The World Monuments Fund marks its 25th anniversary with this photographic exhibit examining the key issues, such as pollution, confronting preservationists today. The show highlights some of the WMF's most prominent projects of the past quarter-century: the Church of St. Trophime in France; the Church of St. Ann and the Holy Trinity in Brooklyn, N.Y.; the Citadelle Henry in Haiti; as well as major murals by Diego Rivera damaged in the 1985 Mexico City earthquake. 24 East 24th St., Sept. 17-Dec. 28.

FESTIVAL

The third annual Geraldine R. Dodge Poetry Festival gathers well-known national and international poets for readings, workshops, discussions, storytelling and musical performances, most of which are open to the general public. (Some events are for preregistered high-school students and teachers only.) Participants include Bei Dao and Duoduo, two leading Chinese poets now in exile; Joyce Carol Oates; Native American poet Joy Harjo; and black poet Lucille Clifton. Waterloo Village, Stanhope, N.J., Sept. 13-15. (201) 540-8442.

New York State Big Apple Festival -- A bit of the country comes to the city with this two-day indoor-outdoor event glorifying the Empire State's most-harvested fruit and country life in general. The riverside esplanade at lower Manhattan's World Financial Center will be the site of all kinds of apple-related events: For the kids there'll be an animal petting zoo, hay rides, apple-bobbing and even piglet races. For adults there'll be a wine and cheese garden and a demonstration of grape pressing. For everyone there'll be apple food products, strolling musicians, fiddlers, magicians and the antics of a "Walking/Talking Apple." Battery Park City, Sept. 16 and 17, 11 a.m. to 6 p.m.

MUSIC

Lyric Opera of Chicago: A rare staging of Christoph Willibald Gluck's "Alceste" stars Jessye Norman as the paragon of connubial fidelity and Chris Merritt as the object of her self-sacrificing impulse. The production, by Robert Wilson, is conducted by Gary Bertini. 20 North Wacker Drive. Sept. 14, 18, 22, 26; Oct. 1, 5, 9, 13. (312) 332-2244.

Opera Omaha: The fall festival gets under way with a bel canto rarity, Donizetti's "Maria Padilla," starring Renee Fleming (Sept. 14, 16, 20, 22). Also new is "Golem," a setting by British composer John Casken of the Jewish fable about a Frankensteinoid monster who goes beserk for lack of love (Sept. 15, 21, 23). Filling other evenings with her songs of Weil is Angelina Reaux, who brings her one woman show, "Stranger Here Myself," to Nebraska (Sept. 13, 16, 23). Witherspoon Concert Hall at the Joslyn Art Museum. (402) 342-7107.
