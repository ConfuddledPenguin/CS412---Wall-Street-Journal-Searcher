August Farm Prices On Average Fell 1.3% From July

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 09/04/90

WASHINGTON -- Average farm prices in August fell 1.3% from July, as an across-the-board slump in grain prices more than offset record high prices for beef cattle and milk, the Agriculture Department said. Still, average prices received by the nation's farmers last month were 3.4% higher than in August 1989, the department said.

Wheat prices dropped 20 cents to $2.59 a bushel in August, approaching the lows of mid-1987, and rice prices slipped 19 cents to $6.76 a hundredweight. The decreases reflect the prospect of bumper crops of spring wheat and rice this fall, as well as the price-depressing effects of the enormous winter wheat crop earlier this summer and growing wheat stockpiles.

The loss of Iraq as a major market for U.S. commodities also has hurt food-grain prices, according to agricultural analysts. Before the Persian Gulf crisis, Iraq had been the largest overseas buyer of U.S. rice and it was an important buyer of U.S. wheat, purchasing 1.3 million metric tons last year. A metric ton is equal to about 2,204.62 pounds.

The slide in feed-grain prices last month was almost as steep as the drop in food-grain prices, the department said. Corn prices averaged $2.46 a bushel, down 16 cents from July, and barley prices averaged $2.10 a bushel, down six cents. Sorghum grain fell 29 cents last month to $4.15 a hundredweight, the department said. Department analysts have forecast a 1990 corn harvest of 7.85 billion bushels, up 4% from last year.

Beef cattle prices climbed $1.80 last month to a record $75.40 a hundredweight, the department said. The previous record was $74.60, set in April 1990. Cow prices averaged $54.10 a hundredweight in August, up $1.30 from the previous month, and steers and heifers averaged $78.80, up $2.20. Calf prices jumped $2 to $98.50 a hundredweight.

The surge in cattle prices is "the culmination of relatively tight supplies and strong demand during first half of 1990," said Charles Lambert, economist for the Denver-based National Cattlemen's Association. The industry also views the record as another sign that beef is gaining in consumer acceptance, after years of losing ground to poultry. The industry has lowered the fat content of beef by putting out "a closer-trimmed product," Mr. Lambert said.

Milk industry analysts were somewhat taken aback last month, as the average milk price jumped 30 cents to $14.40 a hundredweight, a record for August, the department said. The previous high was $13.50, set in August 1981. At $14.40, milk was $1.20 higher than a year earlier.

"We were surprised," said James Barr, chief executive officer of the National Milk Producers Federation, mostly because the U.S. hasn't been exporting any powdered milk lately and milk production has been growing steadily in recent months. It was up 5% in July from a year earlier. But unusually strong demand -- particularly for milk and cheese -- continues to drive raw milk prices up, he said.

Meanwhile, hog prices declined sharply, the department said. Hogs averaged $56 a hundredweight, down $4.80 from July. Barrows and gilts averaged $56.90, down $5.30, and sows averaged $48.80, up $1.30.

Vegetable prices rose 6.8% in August, and fruit prices, led by grapefruit, dropped 11%, the department said.
