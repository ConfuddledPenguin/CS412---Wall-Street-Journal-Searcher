RADIOACTIVITY IN POLYNESIA

Radioactivity from French underground nuclear tests at Mururoa atoll is leaking into the Pacific Ocean only a few years after each test, according to a new study by a U.S. scientist.

The findings by scientist Norm Buske of the Washington-based environmental pressure group Search Technical Services challenge French-government claims that such contamination would take hundreds of years to surface at the Polynesian bomb site. The study is based on re-examination of water samples taken by French marine biologist Jacques Cousteau from Mururoa lagoon on June 22, 1987, the day after a nuclear test.

"Water in Mururoa lagoon is contaminated with cesium-134 and cesium-137 leaking from underground nuclear tests," Mr. Buske's study said. "The time required for radioactive leakage to reach the open environment from nuclear explosions under Mururoa is probably less than six years."

Mr. Buske called for a study of the test sites at Mururoa and neighboring Fangataufa atoll to determine the extent of radioactive contamination.

ITALY'S NEW VISA REQUIREMENTS

Italy began requiring visas for Turks and citizens of several North African countries yesterday under a law intended to limit immigration. Algerians, Moroccans, Mauritanians, Tunisians and Turks will all have to have visas, the Foreign Ministry said. In April, Italy imposed visa requirements on citizens of Senegal and Gambia. Italy gets an estimated 100,000 illegal immigrants a year.

A NEWCOMER IN SCOTLAND

Mizuno Corp., a leading Japanese sporting-goods maker, said it would build a golf-club-assembly factory near Glasgow, Scotland. The new plant will start up in December, with production capacity of 240,000 clubs in the first year. Mizuno now sells golf clubs in Europe made in Taiwan and South Korea.

SOVIET MEDICINE SHORTAGE

The Soviet Union is running out of essential medicines, angering consumers already beset by shortages of gasoline, tobacco and food. "As early as next February, the country may run out of drugs completely, because the pharmaceutical industry is crumbling even more rapidly than the rest of the economy," Health Minister Igor Denisov warned. Said the workers' newspaper Trud: "We have already seen tobacco riots. Shall we now prepare for aspirin riots?"

BRAZILIAN INFLATION

Inflation in Brazil was 10.58% in August but showed signs of slackening, the government said. The inflation rate was slightly lower than the 10.97% increase in July, the government statistics bureau IBGE said. However, the final figure disappointed economic policy makers who had predicted inflation of 8.5% in August. The increase was blamed in part on higher fuel prices. Brazil imports about 45% of its oil. Brazil's consumer prices in the past 12 months have risen 4,272%.

GOING AFTER MARCOS MONEY

Philippine President Corazon Aquino swore in a new chairman of the government committee attempting to recover the "hidden wealth" of the late President Ferdinand Marcos. The appointee, David Castro, is the fifth person to head the Presidential Commission on Good Government in the four years since it was created. After the oath-taking, Mr. Castro said he was confident his commission would recover by 1991 a "substantial portion" of the money and property that Mr. Marcos and his associates allegedly took from the Philippine treasury.

WORLD CLIMATE CONFERENCE

Representatives from 75 countries reached agreement last week on the mechanics of global warming but differed over how to combat it.

The four-day United Nations meeting in Sundsvall, Sweden, backed a report acknowledging basic scientific facts about the so-called greenhouse effect, said conference chairman Bert Bolin. "But it has been harder to agree on concrete measures," he said, adding: "Political considerations enter the picture."

The conference report was not immediately published, but officials said there were few fundamental changes to the draft presented by members of the U.N. Environment Program and the World Meteorological Organization. The draft report agreed that burning fossil fuels releases "greenhouse gases," particularly carbon dioxide, that trap heat near Earth's surface and raise temperatures.

Mr. Bolin said a problem facing negotiators was that developing countries, with about 75% of the world's population, emit only 25% of global greenhouse gases. As these nations develop, he said, their emissions will rise steeply unless industrialized nations provide financial aid for environment-friendly technology.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

Delays in Soviet payments for imports from Japan are reportedly worsening after a period of improvement. A Japanese news agency said the delays totaled $518 million as of the end of July, up 16.1% from the month before. . . . Dutch horticulturalists plan to sell flowers grown without pesticides. The Westland flower auction in Naaldwijk would initially set aside an area for selling chemical-free chrysanthemums, but this may be extended to other flowers and potted plants, an industry official said.
