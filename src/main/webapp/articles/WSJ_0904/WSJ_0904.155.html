Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Soft Purchasers' Index Is Expected Today

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 09/04/90

NEW YORK -- The Iraqi invaders begin making inroads into the indicators in what's likely to be a slack August purchasing managers' index due out today. But important jobs data first face a different enemy: downward revisions. The National Association of Purchasing Management's index is expected to hover around 47.4%, the same level as in July, according to MMS International Inc., a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York. But the reading remains well below 50%, indicating a sliding economy. A reading above 50% indicates an expanding economy. In June, the index stood at 51.1%. August's unemployment rate, due out Friday, is also expected to be flat following the previous month's deepening to 5.5%. Furthermore, benchmark revisions are expected to slice further into the softening payroll head count. The week's only other major indicator is construction spending, also due out today and also likely to remain soft.

"The numbers are going to be weak," said Donald H. Straszheim, Merrill Lynch's chief economist, who thinks the purchasers' index may slip a bit further. "All this invasion does is create uncertainty and that causes a lot of businessmen to freeze in their tracks."

Ironically, the jump in oil prices may be a partly positive influence on the August index by pushing up the price component. And, the inventory segment may be plumped by buyers trying to hedge against rising costs for fuel and petrochemical-based supplies.

Some mixed signals came Friday from two smaller, regional pruchasing managers' indexes: Detroit and Chicago. The August Chicago index rose nearly one percentage point to 49.2%. But the Detroit index fell sharply to 44.1% from 52.8% in July -- the largest one-month drop since November 1987, right after the October stock-market plummet. Economists said the diversified Chicago economy probably helped the Windy City index hold up better than the auto-dependent Detroit area. The Detroit purchasers cited the Mideast conflict as a source of pessimism but noted that weakness in the auto industry was a key factor.

"The purchasers' index tells the continuing story of the end of the auto `miniboom,'" said Gary Ciminero, chief economist at Fleet/Norstar Financial Group, Providence, R.I. He and others believe that rising oil prices only add to the mounting economic reasons why consumers aren't likely to rush out to buy new cars, regardless of any incentives. He expects auto makers to cut September and October production levels because of the oil crisis.

But Mr. Ciminero believes that the government's benchmark revisions to important past employment data are potentially far more negative than whatever the national purchasers' index discloses. "We can throw out all these jigs and jags in monthly employment numbers. They were far weaker than we thought," he says.

Downward adjustments in hourly wages may be particularly significant, said Mr. Ciminero. Lower wage levels might ease concerns over wage-push inflation and explain "why consumers are so strapped."

David Wyss, chief financial economist at DRI/McGraw Hill, agrees that the jobs report will be negative. "We're looking for a weak August following a massive drop in July," he said. The consensus calls for an increase of only 75,000 non-farm jobs in August -- excluding layoffs of temporary census workers. Including the layoffs, nonfarm payrolls in August fell by 33,000 jobs, according to the consensus view.

Rising oil prices, coupled with the downturn in construction, prompted economists at Morgan Guaranty Trust Co. to predict an imminent recession. Morgan said in a statement Friday that the U.S. economy appears likely to move into recession over the next few months.
