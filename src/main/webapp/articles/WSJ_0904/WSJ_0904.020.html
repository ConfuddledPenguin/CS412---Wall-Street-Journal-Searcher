International: Brazil Reaches Economic-Reform Pact With IMF

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 09/04/90

WASHINGTON -- Brazil agreed to an economic-adjustment program with the International Monetary Fund, but a few details haven't been determined, officials said, including the exact amount of money the multilateral agency will lend. These details should be settled soon, after IMF Managing Director Michel Camdessus has reviewed the economic program that Brazil is proposing to the fund, the officials said. Brazilian Economy Minister Zelia Cardoso de Mello then is expected to sign a letter to the fund setting out the program, and the IMF in turn would ratify an agreement with that country, including substantial loans. Ms. Cardoso said yesterday she hoped to sign the letter of intent by the end of the week.

The negotiations with the IMF come at a time of growing speculation in Brazil that the government will soon unveil a new temporary wage-and-price freeze to avoid a resurgence of inflation and a deepening of an incipient recession touched off by the tough anti-inflation plan enforced by President Fernando Collor de Mello in March. Mr. Collor has recently suggested that employers and unions join together in a pact to keep wages and prices under control.

Brazil's IMF quota -- the reserves it subscribes to the fund as a member of the agency -- totals just over $2 billion. Under IMF rules, as part of a so-called standby agreement, Brazil can borrow between 40% and 90% of this amount, or between $800 million and $1.8 billion. Brazil is widely expected to borrow about $1.5 billion from the IMF.

Standby agreements are meant to help IMF member nations through temporary balance-of-payments difficulties. Brazil's last standby agreement, which expired at the end of February after 18 months, totaled about $1.3 billion at then prevailing exchange rates.

The amount of money Brazil can borrow from the IMF is critical to its coming talks with commercial bank creditors. The more IMF cash available, the less money recalcitrant commercial banks will have to provide. Brazil is expected to start negotiating with its main commercial bank creditors this fall, probably in October, after it completes negotiations with its foreign government creditors.

The commercial bank talks are expected to be difficult, particularly because of the substantial interest-payment arrears Brazil has built up on its $70 billion of medium-term, foreign commercial-bank debt. Those arrears, which have been accumulating since July of last year, are estimated to total $8 billion. Altogether, Brazil has about $114 billion of gross foreign obligations.

An IMF team just returned to Washington from Brazil. According to wire service reports, Thomas Reichmann, who led the IMF group, said in Brazil that he is "satisfied and impressed" with the government's current economic program, which includes "important and radical measures" that have "a good chance of succeeding."

President Collor's anti-inflation plan includes an 18-month freeze on about half the money circulating in the Brazilian economy, elimination of subsidies, tight monetary policies and reduced trade barriers.
