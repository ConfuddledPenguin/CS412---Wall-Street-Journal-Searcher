Shell Canada Plan to Develop Gas Field Is Cleared by Alberta; Husky Bid Loses

  • Author: Gary Lamphier
  • Date: 09/04/90

CALGARY, Alberta -- Alberta energy regulators approved an 825 million Canadian dollar (US$719.2 million) plan by Shell Canada Ltd. to develop its big Caroline sour gas field, rejecting a rival plan of Husky Oil Ltd. The decision followed weeks of public hearings this spring on the competing proposals, and months of rancorous debate about the environmental impact of developing the Caroline reservoir, which is generally regarded as the largest gas find in Canada in years.

Shell Canada is a unit of Royal Dutch/Shell Group, an Anglo-Dutch concern.

Caroline is expected to produce C$10 billion of natural gas, natural gas liquids, gas condensate and sulfur during its 30-year life. The reservoir's "sour" gas designation stems from its high content of deadly hydrogen sulphide.

"We're delighted with the decision," said Ray Woods, Shell Canada's vice president, resources development. He said site development work will start this fall at Caroline, 75 miles northwest of Calgary, pending final approval from Alberta's minister of environment. Production is scheduled to begin by the first quarter of 1993.

Husky, whose plan called for construction of a 30-mile pipeline to transport Caroline's sour gas to Husky's under-utilized Ram River gas-processing plant, said it will now work hard to bring Caroline into production as soon as possible. "We accept the board's decision and we are now prepared to work with Shell and the other partners," said a spokeswoman for Husky, 43%-owned by Nova Corp. of Alberta. A group of 14 companies holds interests in Caroline, led by Shell Canada, with 62%. Husky owns 12%.

The Alberta Energy Resources Conservation Board -- the quasi-judicial body that issued the ruling -- said that although Husky's plan had some advantages, they were outweighed by those offered by Shell Canada.

Among other things, it said Shell Canada's plan would yield greater economic benefit, reduce safety risks and be more reliable, as Shell Canada plans to process gas from Caroline at a new plant, whereas Husky's proposal depended on upgrading its aging Ram River plant. "While both projects offered substantial benefits, the Shell proposal was the most desirable in terms of overall public interest," it said.

Caroline is expected to have a major impact on Shell Canada's production, cash flow and earnings. "It's an enormously important project to Shell," said Wilf Gobert, an analyst with Calgary-based Peters & Co. The field's daily output is expected to total about 93 million cubic feet of natural gas, 27,000 barrels of natural gas liquids, 17,000 barrels of condensate -- a very light-gravity crude that sells at a premium price -- and 4,000 metric tons of sulfur.

Denis Mote, an analyst with Toronto's Maison Placements Inc., estimates that Caroline will generate for Shell Canada about C$143 million of additional annual cash flow and C$96 million of additional earnings. For 1990's first half, Shell Canada had cash flow of C$289 million and earnings of just C$58 million, on revenue of C$2.53 billion.

Mr. Mote said the Caroline development would also help Shell Canada develop its nearby Bearberry sour gas field more cheaply.
