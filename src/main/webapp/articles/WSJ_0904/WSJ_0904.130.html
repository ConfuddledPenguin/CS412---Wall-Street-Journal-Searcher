Heard on the Street: Ethanol, in Wake of Oil-Price Rise, May Help Fuel the Fortunes of Archer-Daniels-Midland

  • Author: Scott Kilman
  • Date: 09/04/90

CHICAGO -- Archer-Daniels-Midland shares have wavered along with the rest of the stock market since the Persian Gulf crisis raised the specter of an oil-shock recession. But if oil prices stay up, Archer-Daniels might have a silver lining: ethanol.

The Decatur, Ill., processor of agricultural commodities controls about 60% of U.S. ethanol capacity, according to securities analysts. Made primarily from corn, ethanol is the fuel additive that was trumpeted after the 1970s oil shocks as a way to extend gasoline supplies.

The public's interest waned in the 1980s as petroleum prices eased. But the ethanol industry was nursed by a partial exemption from the federal tax on gas.

Now Wall Street expects the ethanol industry's fortunes to rise again.

Even before the Persian Gulf crisis rejuvenated interest in alternative fuels, Congress was mulling whether to require several big cities to use cleaner-burning fuels, such as ethanol blends, as a way to reduce harmful auto emissions.

Passage of clean-air legislation might double the demand for ethanol, figures James G. Joyce, an analyst at Prescott Ball & Turben in New York.

The recent jump in oil prices increases the chance that Washington will extend past 1993 the controversial tax exemption on ethanol-blended gasoline. The exemption amounts to an effective subsidy of 60 cents on a gallon of ethanol.

Without the tax exemption, ethanol isn't cost competitive with gasoline until crude oil prices are at least $40 a barrel, according to an Agriculture Department study.

"This could be the watershed event that could lead to Americans realizing the importance of ethanol," said Clinton O. Mayer III, an analyst at Bear Stearns in New York.

In the short term, ethanol producers are already reaping some gains. Ethanol emulates changes in the price of gasoline. Since Iraq invaded Kuwait a month ago, the wholesale price of ethanol has climbed by roughly 15%, or 20 cents a gallon, in some markets.

Another windfall for ethanol producers is that the price of corn, which is one of the biggest expenses of making ethanol, slipped 6% in August amid forecasts for a good-sized harvest this autumn.

"The people in the ethanol industry are printing money," said Richard T. Gadomski, chief executive of Memphis, Tenn.-based PSI Group of Cos., which builds ethanol plants.

Archer-Daniels probably stands to benefit the most from a boost in the ethanol industry because it is widely seen as the lowest cost producer. It has state-of-the-art facilities and, unlike some competitors, it can switch three mills between ethanol and high fructose corn syrup, a sugar substitute used by the soft drink industry. That flexibility allows it to run its mills at full capacity.

After Archer-Daniels, the ethanol industry gets small fast. According to analysts, the second-largest producer is Pekin Energy, a venture between Texaco and CPC International. Pekin markets about 80 million gallons annually; Archer-Daniels's annual ethanol capacity is 700 million gallons.

It is difficult for analysts to calculate ethanol's effect on Archer-Daniels' bottom line. Ethanol generates less than 10% of its revenue and the company is tight-lipped about its numbers.

According to George S. Dahlman, an analyst at Piper Jaffray & Hopwood, Minneapolis, some investors use the rough rule of thumb that, on an annual basis, the after-tax per-share profit of Archer-Daniels increases by 1.5 cents for every penny-a-gallon increase in the price of ethanol.

An Archer-Daniels official scoffs at the formula, noting that investors can't know the price of the corn the company buys nor of the byproducts it sells.

But analysts are intrigued by the possibilities. Archer-Daniels's annual ethanol capacity works out to about 2.36 gallons for each Archer-Daniels share outstanding.

To be sure, there is reason to be wary about any ethanol play. Any jump in corn prices could eliminate profits, and the Midwest crop is unusually vulnerable to a yield-damaging frost this month because the wet summer slowed its maturity.

Several analysts said oil prices would have to remain high for several months before they consider revising their earnings estimates for the company.

Until then, analysts are recommending purchase of Archer-Daniels for other reasons, such as the prospects for increased exports to Eastern Europe and the Soviet Union, and strong domestic demand for the company's vegetable oil.

Mr. Mayer of Bear Stearns said he is continuing to predict that the company will earn $1.85 a share for the fiscal year through June 1991, up 13.5% from fiscal 1990.
