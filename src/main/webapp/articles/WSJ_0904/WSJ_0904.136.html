What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/04/90

BAGHDAD TOLD nations with citizens trapped in Iraq to send them food. Although Baghdad permitted the weekend departure of several hundred foreigners, including Americans, it then balked at allowing more airlifts, and solicited food shipments for the hostages. The Iraqi government also suspended payment of its foreign debt, conservatively estimated at between $30 billion and $35 billion.

The U.S. is expected to announce a plan to forgive much of the $7 billion in military debt owed by Egypt, a stalwart American ally. And in another move to shore up support for anti-Iraq action, Bush will confer Sunday with Gorbachev. Meetings between the U.N.'s chief and Iraq's foreign minister were fruitless.

A U.S. Air Force F-16 fighter crashed in southern Saudi Arabia during a training mission, but the solo pilot bailed out safely, the Pentagon reported.

---

New Zealand's Geoffrey Palmer resigned as prime minister after opinion polls indicated his ruling Labor Party would be defeated in next month's elections by the National Party. Palmer said he would be succeeded by Foreign Minister Mike Moore, who will be the country's third Labor prime minister in little more than a year.

---

Soviets lined up for bread at stores across Moscow for the first time in years, with many blaming President Gorbachev's policies for shortages of a growing list of products that includes tobacco, paper, cheese and gasoline. The last time Soviets could remember long bread lines was under Premier Khrushchev, ousted in 1964.

A radical economic plan was presented to Soviet leaders Friday, as Gorbachev and Russian republic leader Yeltsin seemed ready to scuttle most of a prior plan that was prepared by Premier Ryzhkov. The new package calls for earlier deregulation of most prices, along with large-scale privatization.

---

The Phnom Penh government said the Khmer Rouge guerrillas want to sabotage a Cambodian peace settlement and are preparing for prolonged civil war. All four factions involved in Cambodia's 11-year conflict are to meet tomorrow in Indonesia to discuss a five-nation plan that would give the U.N. the power to oversee a cease-fire.

---

East and West Germany signed a landmark unity treaty Friday covering the merger of their legal and political systems after the Oct. 3 unification of the two countries. The treaty, which included a compromise on German abortion laws, is expected to be approved by both parliaments following debate over the next few weeks.

---

NASA began its third countdown for space shuttle Columbia, scheduled for liftoff Thursday. Last week, communications problems with the shuttle's $150 million observatory prompted space agency officials to call off a launch planned for Saturday. A fuel leak halted the first countdown in May.

---

The South African government freed 11 African National Congress prisoners, the first of many due for release as a precondition for talks between Pretoria and the anti-apartheid group. As many as 3,500 political prisoners are to be released by the government over the next five months.

---

A study found that exposure to polluted air can provoke life-threatening irregular heartbeats in people suffering from coronary artery disease. The report is further evidence that carbon monoxide -- an urban pollutant emitted in auto exhaust and by industrial smokestacks -- is harmful to the heart.

---

Rebel leader Charles Taylor declared war on a West African peacekeeping force in Liberia, calling it a band of foreign mercenaries. Taylor's insurgent group has been battling the 4,000 soldiers since they arrived in Monrovia 10 days ago. The troops have been welcomed by rival guerrilla leader Prince Johnson and by President Doe.

---

Somalia's president dismissed the East African nation's prime minister and his seven-month-old government as diplomats reported that fighting between security forces and rebels was intensifying in central regions of the country. Siad Barre, who faces severe economic and social problems, gave no reason for his action.

---

About 135,000 public employees began a planned weeklong strike in the Indian-ruled sector of Kashmir to protest alleged brutalities by Indian police. At least nine people were killed in two days of fighting between security forces and Moslem activists.

---

Canadian soldiers encircled a handful of defiant Mohawks inside an alcohol treatment center at the Kanesatake reservation in Oka, Quebec. Earlier, the army bulldozed barricades that were erected seven weeks ago by Mohawks at the reservation, and at a second Indian settlement, in a dispute over a land claim involving a golf course.

---

Ethnic Albanians staged a one-day strike to protest Serbian rule in Yugoslavia's Kosovo province. Tensions have risen since Serbia dissolved Kosovo's government and Parliament in July after ethnic Albanian deputies issued a declaration of independence from Serbia.

---

Japan's Premier Kaifu prepared for Soviet Foreign Minister Shevardnadze's visit to Tokyo today by declaring that Moscow must give back four occupied northern islands before it can hope for friendship and cooperation from the Japanese.
