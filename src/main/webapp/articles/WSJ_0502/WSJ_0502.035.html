What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/02/90

SOVIETS JEERED the Kremlin leadership, disrupting May Day celebrations. Tens of thousands of people in Moscow's Red Square whistled and mocked Gorbachev and members of the Politburo and Presidential Council, turning the traditionally stagemanaged workers' parade into an unprecedented protest against Communist rule. The officials left their posts atop Lenin's Mausoleum, but the demonstration continued for an hour before it was dispersed by KGB security police. The crowd's action was considered the most vivid display to date of public anger at the Kremlin's leadership, underscoring a growing mood of rebellion in the Soviet Union.

Bush will meet tomorrow with the premier of Lithuania, where food rationing began amid Moscow's crackdown on the republic's independence bid.

---

The Senate approved a $3.3 billion fiscal 1990 spending bill providing $720 million in increased aid for Panama and Nicaragua. The measure also included an amendment, backed by GOP conservatives, warning the Soviet Union that new trade pacts would be jeopardized unless accommodations are made on Lithuania.

---

Cheney extended a world-wide spending moratorium on base construction. The defense secretary also listed 207 projects, including Navy "home ports" in Texas, New York and other states, as possible candidates for cancellation. The move affects more than $7 billion in Pentagon contracts.

---

The Energy Department plans to resume producing tritium at the Savannah River facility in South Carolina by late December or early January. The department had closed the nation's nuclear-weapons reactors in 1988 because of safety and management problems, raising concerns of a shortage of the gas used in making nuclear bombs.

---

Former hostage Reed underwent preliminary medical examinations in West Germany, and a special State Department team began questioning the 57-year-old educator about his more than three years in captivity. Reed, who was released Monday in Beirut, was described as "weak and tired." Six American hostages are still being held.

The U.S. said it was making "small gestures" of appreciation to Iran and Syria over Reed's freedom, and there were hints that a release of Arabs jailed in Israel could lead to the freeing of Westerners. U.S. and Iranian aides, meanwhile, met in Holland for scheduled talks on financial disputes arising from Iran's 1979 revolution.

---

About 150 Jewish settlers obeyed an Israeli court order and evacuated a complex they had occupied for almost three weeks in the Christian sector of Jerusalem's walled Old City. An official, meanwhile, said about 10,500 Soviet Jews arrived in Israel last month, the largest monthly figure since a mass Soviet immigration began last year.

---

South Koreans staged May Day strikes to protest a crackdown this week on strikers at Hyundai Heavy Industries shipyard in Ulsan and at Korea Broadcasting Service headquarters in Seoul. Sporadic violence flared as riot police were sent to college campuses, government buildings and industrial sites.

---

The Bush administration agreed in principle to link future U.S. assistance to El Salvador to specific progress toward peace and human rights. The move followed a report Monday by a House Democratic task force, which concluded that a Salvadoran investigation into the killing last year of six Jesuit priests was at a "virtual standstill."

---

The FDA approved the use of irradiation to control salmonella and other bacteria in poultry believed partly to blame for rising rates of food-borne illness. FDA scientists described using radiation as a safe and effective process for, in effect, pasteurizing solid foods.

---

Canadian researchers said the drug THA didn't provide any benefit to Alzheimer's disease patients in a controlled study. Separately, two doctors warned that the costs of caring for elderly people disabled by Alzheimer's, hip fractures and certain diseases will soar as those over 85 rise four to six times by 2040.

---

Cambodian Premier Hun Sen concluded talks with officials in Thailand, saying a cease-fire was a necessary first step toward resolving Cambodia's 11-year civil conflict. He also said that the Khmer Rouge must be part of a negotiated settlement, but stressed that the guerrilla group's older leaders couldn't return to power in Phnom Penh.

---

A Mohawk Indian was killed as tribal factions fought a gunbattle at the St. Regis Mohawk Indian Reservation in Quebec. The 22-year-old man was the first fatality in a nine-month-old dispute over casino gambling on the 14,000-acre reservation.
