Amex Seeks to List U.S. Stock-Index 3-Year Warrants

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 05/02/90

NEW YORK -- The American Stock Exchange wants to list U.S. stock-index warrants that would let investors bet on the market's direction over the next three years. Long-term warrants are the closest thing to a popular new product on Wall Street these days. In particular, put warrants on Japan's Nikkei 225-stock index have attracted wide trading interest among U.S. individuals and institutions. Put warrants rise in value when an index declines, and holders of Nikkei put warrants profited handsomely on the Japanese stock market's recent plunge.

So far, the only stock-index warrants traded in the U.S. have been based on Japanese or British indexes. But subject to Securities and Exchange Commission approval, the Amex wants to start trading three-year warrants based on the 20-stock Major Market Index.

The new Amex warrants would be underwritten by a Wall Street firm or commercial bank. Amex Chairman James Jones says Salomon Brothers Inc., Goldman Sachs & Co., Morgan Stanley & Co., Bankers Trust Co. and PaineWebber Inc. have all shown interest in rolling out the new warrants.

Market professionals said the Amex may find some interest in its warrants, but they added that it is entering a crowded field. The Nikkei warrants achieved much of their popularity because it was difficult for most U.S. investors to find any other way of betting on a Japanese market decline.

With the U.S. stock market, however, investors already have several ways to bet long term on the market's direction. Most Wall Street firms already make an over-the-counter market in long-maturity stock-index warrants and options.

In addition, most professional traders find they can easily simulate a long-term warrant by trading in the stock-index futures market and "rolling" their positions into later-maturity contracts as existing contracts near their expiration dates. And a two-year option on Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index, traded in the Chicago commodities markets, has attracted little interest to date.

Nonetheless, Amex officials seem willing to take their chances. "Traditionally, the Amex has tried to be more flexible and innovative about its new products," says Amex President Ken Liebler. "Some of them are hits, and some aren't. If they aren't hits, we move on."
