Credit Markets: Strong Report by Purchasing Managers Prompts Sell-Off; 30-Year Treasury Ends 3/8 Point Lower

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Andrew Bary
  • Date: 05/02/90

NEW YORK -- A stronger-than-expected economic report triggered a sell-off in the bond market. Bonds got off to a strong start in early trading, with prices rising as much as 3/8 point, or $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. But the gains vanished after the National Association of Purchasing Management said its index of business activity rose in April to the highest level in a year.

The index was 50.2% in April, up from 48.8% in March. An index reading above 50% indicates the manufacturing sector of the economy is expanding, while a reading below 50% indicates it is contracting. The April index was the first showing above 50% since April 1989.

After its release, some market players referred to the NAPM report as the "napalm" report because "it blew the market away," said F. Ward McCarthy Jr., a managing director at Stone & McCarthy Associates.

The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond fell as much as 1/2 point after the report was released, but it staged a partial recovery later in the day and ended 3/8 point lower. Investment-grade corporate bonds ended with moderate losses, while municipal bonds were mixed. Junk bonds, which fell sharply on Monday, were unchanged to moderately higher.

In the mortgage-backed securities market, some bonds were buoyed by unusually heavy issuance of real estate mortgage investment conduits, which are created with mortgage securities. Investment firms created five Remics yesterday totaling more than $2.2 billion, one of the heaviest one-day totals in the seven-year history of the market. And there was talk that at least one more Remic would be issued today.

In the Treasury market, analysts said prices also were hurt by rising commodity prices, which fanned inflation fears. The Commodity Research Bureau's index of futures prices, which includes 21 commodities, rose more than 2 1/2 points in morning trading. The gains were trimmed later in the day, and the CRB index ended 0.65 point higher at 246.46.

On the economic calendar for release today is the March index of leading economic indicators, last month's factory orders and the latest edition of the Federal Reserve's "beige book." The beige book is a summary of economic activity prepared for use at the central bank's next Federal Open Market Committee meeting.

But the next most significant piece of economic news comes Friday when the Labor Department releases the April employment report. The data are expected to set the market tone for next week's Treasury refunding, analysts and traders say.

The Treasury will unveil details of the quarterly auction this afternoon. Most analysts expect a record-size refunding package of between $30.5 billion to $31 billion, up from the $30 billion in the previous refunding in February.

Junk Bonds

After suffering a sizable setback Monday, the junk bond market stabilized in brisk trading activity.

"There's been a reasonable amount of activity across the board," said David Feinman, a junk bond trader at Los Angeles-based Jeffries & Co. Even when prices were falling on Monday, trading activity was far above normal. "The last two days, we've been back in business."

Among the market movers were junk bonds of Ames Department Stores Inc., Trump Plaza Funding and American Standard Inc.

Trump Plaza Funding's 12 7/8% bonds, which tumbled 3 1/2 points Monday, gained 1/2 to close at 82. The gains came in spite of reports that robust business at Trump's Taj Mahal casino appears to be hurting Trump Plaza and Trump Castle casinos. Although the bonds would tend to trade lower on such news, junk bond traders said dealers were snapping the securities up because they have reached levels considered relatively cheap. Ever since Friday's news that casino and real estate developer Donald Trump may sell the Trump shuttle and other major assets, some Trump casino bonds have fallen nearly 10 points.

Ames' 13% senior subordinated reset notes due in 1999, jumped two points to end at 25 1/4. Active buying by vulture funds and investment firms specializing in distressed securities have buoyed Ames bonds ever since the retailer filed for protection under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code last month.

American Standard's 12 7/8% senior subordinated debentures due in 2000, which shed a point Monday, were up 1/4 point at 94 1/4.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Heavy issuance of Remic offerings buoyed Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. and Federal National Mortgage Association securities.

The largest of the $2.2 billion of Remics offered yesterday was a $750.1 million issue from Fannie Mae backed by its 9 1/2% securities and underwritten by Bear, Stearns & Co., the leading underwriter in derivative mortgage-backed securities this year.

There were two large Remics backed by Government National Mortgage Association securities, but those issues had been rumored since late last week and had little market impact.

In trading, Freddie Mac 9 1/2% securities finished at 95 30/32, down 1/32, and Fannie Mae 9 1/2% securities ended at 96 15/32, also down 1/32. But Ginnie Mae 9% securities for May delivery ended at 93 11/32, down 8/32.

Treasury, Agency Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at 94 14/32 to yield 9.03%, compared with 94 26/32 to yield 8.989% on Monday. The latest 10-year notes ended at 96 10/32 to yield 9.05%, compared with 96 17/32 to yield 9.02%.

Short-term interest rates rose. The discount rate on three-month bills rose to 7.94% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.19%, while the rate on six-month bills rose to 8.04% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.48%.

In the agency market, the Federal National Mortgage Association priced a total of $1 billion of four-year and 10-year debentures. Fannie Mae said its $400 million offering of debentures due May 10, 1994, was priced to yield 9.30%, and its $600 million of debentures due May 10, 2000, was priced to yield 9.80%.

Municipals

Actively traded municipal bonds were narrowly mixed in late trading, with some issues off as much as 1/4 point while others were up as much as 3/8 point.

Short-covering aided issues at the start of the uninspired session, but some municipals later weakened along with Treasurys.

In a short sale, investors sell borrowed stock and hope to profit by later buying an equal number of shares at a lower price to replace what was borrowed.

With speculation about possible tightening by the Federal Reserve continuing to grow, municipal bonds may weaken in the near term if Treasurys keep slumping, market participants said.

Among new issues priced was $159.4 million of Minneapolis Housing Redevelopment Authority health care system revenue bonds priced by a Goldman, Sachs & Co. group.

In secondary activity, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 fell 1/4 point to 95 bid, to yield 7.61%.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese government bond market ended weaker as bearish sentiment continued to grow.

"People are adjusting their positions," said a dealer at a Japanese securities firm. "Instead of holding long positions {in this weak market}, it is better to hold short positions."

Dealers noted, however, that trading activity has been light because of the holiday-shortened week. Japan's financial markets were closed Monday and will close again tomorrow and Friday.

Japan's 10-year bond fell 0.14 point to 85.69 to yield 7.435% yesterday.

In Britain, bonds edged up on the latest quarterly survey by the Confederation of British Industry, a trade association.

The poll of 1,232 firms found expectations that manufacturing employment would fall despite a "slight increase" in output in the next four months. It also indicated that business confidence had declined further in response to a drop in domestic demand and capacity utilization.

But activity was muted because of holidays in other European countries. Britain's benchmark 11 3/4% bond due 2003/2007 rose 3/8 to 94 1/2 to yield 12.53%.

Markets in West Germany, France and several other European countries were closed in observance of May Day.
