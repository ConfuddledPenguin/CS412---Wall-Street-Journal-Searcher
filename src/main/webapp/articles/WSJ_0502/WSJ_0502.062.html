Tuesday's Markets: Stocks Gain Due to Data On Growth --- Purchasers' Report Pushes Bonds Lower; Dollar Closes Mixed

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 05/02/90

Evidence of increasingly robust economic growth gave stock prices a little boost while pushing bond prices lower. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 12.16 points to 2668.92 in moderate trading. Long-term bond prices slipped about 3/8 point, or about $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount, while the dollar was mixed. The big May Day holiday in Europe curtailed trading action in both the bond and currency markets.

Investors were caught by surprise when the National Association of Purchasing Management reported that its index of economic activity for April jumped to 50.2% from 48.9% in March. Economists had expected only a tiny increase in the measure of the nation's industrial economy. If the index is below 50% the economy is presumed to be contracting, while readings above 50% show an expanding economy. The April level, the highest in a year, tends to support a view that the economy and inflation are both picking up steam.

The immediate effect of the purchasing manager's report was a modest retreat in bond prices, which had risen slightly in early New York trading. Bond investors have been shaken in recent weeks by the mounting evidence that inflation is accelerating. Accelerating inflation is expected to force the Federal Reserve to boost short-term interest rates sometime soon and the purchasing managers' report will, if anything, advance the Fed's timetable.

While they await a Fed move, bond traders are braced, too, for today's Treasury announcement about next week's big quarterly refunding auction. They worry that Japanese investors won't be aggressive buyers since interest rates in Japan are competitive with rates available here. If Japanese investors shun the auction, yields on the Treasury issues will have to rise to attract other buyers.

But if rising economic activity worries bond investors, it leaves stock investors in a quandary. On the plus side, a more robust economy could spur corporate earnings to higher levels later this year. But the higher interest rates that usually accompany rising inflation increase the cost of doing business. And if the Federal Reserve really cracks down and pushes short-term rates sharply higher to curb inflation, the economy could slip into a recession.

Right now investors are taking a positive view, noting that International Business Machine Chairman John Akers on Monday predicted a "substantially improved" performance this year.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 149 million shares. Advancing issues on the Big Board were ahead of decliners 820 to 679.

Bond prices were lower. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year issue lost 3/8 point, or $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the issue rose to 9.03%.

The dollar barely budged. In late New York trading the currency was quoted at 1.6808 marks and 158.83 yen compared with 1.6796 marks and 158.90 yen Monday.
