Campbell Soup Co. Announces Layoffs In Corporate Staff

  • Author: Vindu P. Goel
  • Date: 05/02/90

Campbell Soup Co. laid off 14% of the staff at its corporate headquarters in Camden, N.J., and said it had eliminated another 5% through attrition since Jan. 1. The cutbacks at the head office continue the streamlining program started by Chief Executive Officer David W. Johnson when he joined Campbell in January.

The maker of food products said a total of 364 positions were eliminated, 267 through layoffs and 97 through attrition. The staff reduction is expected to save about $17 million over the first full fiscal year, which begins in August. All levels of staffing were affected, including senior management.

In an interview, Mr. Johnson said the cutbacks are symbolic of his drive to improve productivity and boost Campbell's earnings growth and return on equity, both of which now rank near the bottom among major food companies. In addition, the changes are intended to make Campbell less bureaucratic and more efficient by eliminating layers of supervisors and reports.

"The headquarters is where you set the drumbeats and the tones," Mr. Johnson said. "If I can get the drumbeats and examples set out here, we can begin to change the vast empire."

Since Mr. Johnson's arrival, Campbell has put its Juice Bowl unit on the auction block and closed several previously targeted plants in the U.S. and United Kingdom. In addition, several top executives have left the company. They include Carl V. Stinnett, president of Campbell's non-grocery business, Steven A. McNeil, head of frozen foods, and Raymond J. Land, category general manager for convenience foods.

Mr. Stinnett, 55 years old, took early retirement and his job was eliminated. Mr. McNeil, 47, left to pursue other interests and will be replaced, Mr. Johnson said. Mr. Johnson would not comment on the departure of Mr. Land, 45.

Mr. Johnson declined to specify which senior executives were laid off yesterday. But he noted that Herbert M. Baum, executive vice president in charge of Campbell's North American operations, now has only 10 people reporting directly to him, about half the number he had before.

Mr. Johnson said the reorganization was necessary to his plan to improve Campbell's financial performance rapidly. "I can't keep sitting back and telling the shareholders to give me another year," Mr. Johnson said. "In my first 12 months, we're going to see the results," he vowed. He said he expected Campbell to be exceeding food-company averages of 15% annual earnings growth and 22% return on equity by the end of the calendar year.

"The score card we're going to be judged on is the quarterly report," Mr. Johnson said.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Campbell closed at $50.75, unchanged.
