Your Money Matters -- Investment Dartboard: Stock-Picking Pros Again Beat the Darts

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 05/02/90

Led by small-stock specialist Jonathan Steinberg, a quartet of investment professionals once again trounced a randomly selected "dart" portfolio in this column's stock-picking contest. Mr. Steinberg, editor and publisher of Individual Investor magazine and son of raider-financier Saul Steinberg, scored a 9% gain with his April selection, Image Entertainment Inc. It followed his victorious pick for March, American Film Technologies Inc., which has risen 58% in the two months since he picked it at the beginning of March.

April's brigade of investment pros averaged a 3.7% gain for the month, while a group of four stocks chosen by tossing darts at the New York Stock Exchange listings fell 12.7%. The Dow Jones Industrial Average sagged 1.9%.

The biggest loser among the dart stocks was Caesar's World, a casino company faced with tough new competition from Donald Trump's Taj Mahal in Atlantic City and Golden Nugget Inc.'s Mirage casino in Las Vegas. The investment pros have now beaten the darts in 14 of 19 monthly outings.

Mr. Steinberg's new pick is Management Co. Entertainment Group, a Santa Monica, Calif., film producer and distributor whose most recent film is "Look Who's Talking." Traded over the counter, the stock ended yesterday at $2.25 a share, bid. Mr. Steinberg recommends purchasing it at any price up to $4.50. "I think in 18 months it will be between $7 and $11," he says.

Last year, Management Co. Entertainment Group acquired Virgin Vision Ltd., a British-based international film-distribution company. Revenue, a mere $34 million in fiscal 1989, will be $330 million in fiscal 1991, Mr. Steinberg figures.

"It is extremely undervalued," he says. "Most production companies trade at one-and-a-half to three times sales. This company trades at about 60% of sales, and sales are growing fast. If it trades at one times sales by the end of 1991, the stock would be at $11."

To finance its growth, the company has taken on about $153 million of debt, Mr. Steinberg notes, including a $72 million bridge loan from Kidder, Peabody & Co. "There's been downward pressure on the stock because of the bridge loan," Mr. Steinberg says. But yesterday, the company and Kidder announced that the bridge loan will be extended on more favorable terms.

Returning to the contest this month is R. Jerry Falkner, director of research for Gulfstream Financial Associates Inc. (a unit of Prescott, Ball & Turben, which is part of Kemper Corp.). At the beginning of February, he recommended Blockbuster Entertainment Corp., then selling for $14.125 a share. Blockbuster ended April at $19.75, a 40% gain that earns Mr. Falkner a return trip.

His pick now: Windmere Corp., a Miami-based maker of small electrical appliances, especially hair-care products. Almost everything that could go wrong for Windmere has gone wrong in the past year, Mr. Falkner says. For starters, many of its factories are in China, the scene of a bloody political crackdown by the Communist regime against pro-democracy demonstrators. Windmere also damaged its credibility on Wall Street by issuing overly optimistic earnings predictions, Mr. Falkner says.

But now, he thinks the stock is "very oversold," having plunged to a recent $5.75 a share from a 1989 high of $29; it closed yesterday at $5.875 in Big Board composite trading. Mr. Falkner would pay up to $7 a share.

Windmere, which has annual sales of about $180 million, recently won a $90 million jury award in an antitrust suit against N.V. Philips, the big Dutch company that Windmere says forced it out of the market for three-head rotary shavers. Philips plans to appeal, but Mr. Falkner says, "We think {Windmere} has a very high probability of receiving that money."

Even without lawsuit proceeds, Mr. Falkner says, Windmere shares are selling below their book value (assets minus liabilities per share) of about $7.50. "We think they lost some market share last year {though the company denies it}, but we think they're recovering," he concludes.

Two new stock-pickers join the fun this month. Lawrence A. Cisneros is president of San Juan Asset Management Inc. in Huntington Beach, Calif. Michael G. Seyfer is president of Starmont Capital Ltd. in Des Moines, Iowa.

Mr. Cisneros suggests buying shares of Service Corp. International, based in Houston, which owns a national chain of some 500 funeral homes and more than 100 cemeteries. The shares sold yesterday for $16.50 in Big Board composite trading; Mr. Cisneros would buy them up to $18.50.

Several things attract him to the stock: Service Corp. is selling off divisions that haven't pulled their weight (prearranged funerals and funeral supplies). It plans to buy back shares in June. And its business is "interest-rate resistant and resistant to economic downturns."

Service Corp. stumbled through hasty and perhaps excessive expansion, Mr. Cisneros says. But now he thinks "management's taken the necessary steps to get it back on track," and he's looking for annual earnings growth of 15% to 20%.

Mr. Seyfer likes Hecla Mining Co., a producer of gold, silver and industrial minerals based in Coeur D'Alene, Idaho. Hecla shares closed unchanged yesterday at $11.625 on the Big Board. Mr. Seyfer says he'd buy at prices up to $12, but wouldn't chase the stock up.

"I think silver under $5 {an ounce} is really attractive," he says. "You have to go back to the late 1970s to see the price much under that." He thinks it's a good sign that Hecla "bit the bullet" and took write-offs on some inactive mining properties last year.

But mostly, he views Hecla as a play on precious-metal prices. As he sees it, a "more normal price" of $5.50 to $6 an ounce for silver, which "may take a while" to achieve, would make Hecla's profits perk up. "The price of silver is reflected in their bottom line very quickly."

For May, the pros will be up against a dartboard portfolio consisting of Borden Inc., Gillette Co., Leggett & Platt Inc. and National City Corp.
