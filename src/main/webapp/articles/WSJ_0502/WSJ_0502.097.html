Marketing & Media -- Advertising: American Express `Portraits' Hit Europe

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 05/02/90

American Express Co. plans to extend its "Portraits" print ad campaign featuring celebrity card holders to a number of European countries. Like the U.S. campaign that features American celebrities such as Ella Fitzgerald and Neil Simon, the European ads will be tailored to spotlight famous personalities of particular countries. An ad for Germany, for example, features Alfred Biolek, a prominent journalist and talk-show host there. "American Express Mitglied seit {card member since} 1980," the ad says.

American Express is rolling out the campaign in Europe as part of a broader effort to beef up its card holder ranks overseas. "All of our efforts are aimed at building an upscale position with the people in the market," says Rick Thoman, president and chief executive officer at American Express International. "Our goal is to own the upscale franchises."

But in its bid to build the business outside the U.S., American Express faces a formidable challenge. The concept of credit is still a relatively foreign idea to many Europeans. Though Americans spend well over 10% of their income with credit cards, many Europeans live in countries where consumers spend only 2% or 3% of their incomes with charge cards. Italians spend just 1.5% of their incomes with charge cards, the lowest percentage in Western Europe.

"Cash is the enemy," admits Mr. Thoman. But he says attitudes overseas toward credit are changing. American Express estimates world-wide spending on plastic will balloon to about $3.5 trillion a year over the next decade from $800 million, with the bulk of that increase coming overseas.

American Express hopes the customized "Portraits" ads, along with targeted television campaigns, will encourage Europeans to apply for its card. The company has raised ad spending overseas significantly, and Mr. Thoman says spending outside the U.S. now accounts for about 40% to 45% of its total ad budget, up from only 15% a few years ago. According to Advertising Age, American Express spent a total of $247.2 million on advertising in 1988.

In the U.S., creative teams at Ogilvy & Mather, a unit of WPP Group, work with American Express executives to develop "wish lists" of celebrities they would like to feature in the campaign. Mr. Thoman says Ogilvy teams in foreign countries are working much the same way, identifying candidates in sports, entertainment and politics.

American Express won't name all of the European celebrities that have been chosen for the campaign. It says, however, that the campaign for France will include Michel Serrault, a French actor, and Christian LaCroix, the clothing designer. The campaign in Germany, which has just begun running, includes portraits of Alwin Schockemoehle, an equestrian gold medalist, and Bernhard Langer, a German golfer.

Although the U.S. campaign has succeeded in large part because it appeals to Americans' thirst for pictures of the famous, American Express and Ogilvy say the interest isn't only an American phenomenon.

"We've found that the campaign has an ability to translate fairly immediately almost anywhere," says Gordon Bowen, senior vice president and creative director at Ogilvy.

All the photos in the international campaign, like those in the U.S., were shot by Annie Leibovitz, a celebrity photographer. American Express says the introduction of the campaign in some instances has been delayed because of Ms. Leibovitz's schedule. The firm says it decided that using a separate photographer overseas would disrupt the continuity of the campaign.

Ms. Leibovitz just finished shooting in France, and other photo sessions are on deck for Italy, Spain, Belgium and Switzerland, among others. The campaign is already running in the Far East and Asia.

Although most stars in the campaigns for countries outside of the U.S. won't be recognized by Americans, some of the ads will run in more than one country. "There are a number of portraits that have relevance across a number of markets," Mr. Thoman says. A portrait featuring classical musician Yo Yo Ma, which was originally created for the U.S., recently ran in Hong Kong.

Breene Is Leaving

Tim Breene, chief executive officer of Della Femina McNamee WCRS's parent company, is stepping down as a result of the company's elimination of its London co-headquarters.

The parent company, Eurocom WCRS Della Femina Ball, had split its headquarters between London and Paris, but will consolidate in Paris. Eurocom WCRS, which itself is 60%-owned by France's Eurocom ad agency, was formed last year when London-based WCRS sold its advertising business to Eurocom.

Mr. Breene had been deputy chairman of WCRS. He will stay on briefly as a consultant to help with the transition and to develop below-the-line, nonadvertising marketing services, said Louise McNamee, president of Della Femina McNamee.

Mr. Breene will be succeeded by Pierre DePlais, who will also remain chief operating officer of Eurocom in Paris. Working with Mr. DePlais will be Jean de Yturbe, who had been chief executive of ad agency HDM's European operations.

Ad Notes. . . .

ACCOUNT SEARCH: Chase Manhattan, New York, is looking for an agency to handle a large assignment advertising its retail banking services world-wide. The account will involve promoting Chase services and products, including credit cards, student loans and mortgages. The account is separate from Chase's estimated $20 million New York branch banking account that is handled at Scali, McCabe, Sloves, a WPP Group agency. Chase said Scali will be among the contenders. It said billings for the account haven't been determined.

EARNINGS: Omnicom Group reported first-quarter net income rose 21% to $10.8 million, or 42 cents a share, from $9 million, or 35 cents a share, in the year-earlier period. First-quarter revenue rose 21% to $274.3 million from $226.2 million in the first quarter of 1989. Omnicom is the parent company of the BBDO, DDB Needham and Tracy-Locke agencies.

ACCOUNT: Panasonic Co., Secaucus, N.J., awarded its $5 million cellular phone account to Levine, Huntley, Schmidt & Beaver, New York.

NEW PRODUCT: Nestle Foods, White Plains, N.Y., named McCann-Erickson, New York, to create advertising for its new Nescafe Mocha Cooler product. The ready-to-drink beverage will be test marketed in Boston and four other cities. Billings weren't disclosed. Nestle Foods is a unit of Nestle S.A.

PRODUCERS: Albright/Labhart, a small Los Angeles ad agency, is producer of the West Coast production of "Eastern Standard," a play that ran on Broadway last year. The shop said it is the first Los Angeles agency to produce a professional theatrical production.

WHO'S NEWS: Gayle Gleckler was named executive vice president and director of business development at MacNamara, Clapp & Klein, New York. She was president and co-creative director at Fearon Gleckler, a tiny New York shop that is closing its doors. One Fearon Gleckler client, MEM Co.'s Fathom men's fragrance, will now be serviced at MacNamara Clapp.
