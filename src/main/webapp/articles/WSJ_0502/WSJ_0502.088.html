Burger King, in Big Blow to Pepsi, Is Switching to Coke

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 05/02/90

In a major coup, Coca-Cola Co. swiped Pepsi-Cola Co.'s biggest fountain customer, the No. 2 fast-food chain, Burger King Corp. In an industry where executives like to characterize market-share battles as "one drink at a time," Coke is taking a big gulp. Burger King, with 5,400 restaurants in the U.S., represents at least $270 million in lost business annually for Pepsi, according to Jesse Meyers, publisher of trade newsletter Beverage Digest. Coke will now have a lock on the nation's two largest fast-food chains, Burger King and top-ranked McDonald's Corp.

Even more important, Mr. Meyers estimates, Burger King accounts for about 18% of Pepsi's fountain business, which was already far overshadowed by Coke's. Pepsi's next three biggest customers are chains owned by its parent PepsiCo Inc.: Taco Bell, Pizza Hut and Kentucky Fried Chicken.

It's that troika, the largest collection of restaurants in the country, that industry executives believe ultimately led Burger King to dump Pepsi. In recent years, Atlanta-based Coke has hammered away at its central argument that fast-food chains shouldn't use Pepsi at their fountain spigots because that, in effect, supports a competitor. In a swipe at Pepsi, a Coke sales brochure for restaurant managers asks: "Is your soft-drink supplier satisfying your customers' hunger instead of their thirst?"

For its part, Burger King considers Pepsi's ownership of rival chains only one factor in its decision to switch, says a spokeswoman for the Miami-based chain, which was acquired by London-based Grand Metropolitan PLC in January 1989. "We looked at the whole package that Coca-Cola offered," she says.

The five-year deal includes a price discount -- Burger King calls it a volume incentive program -- that "rewards incremental volumes of syrup gallons sold over a base level." Figures weren't disclosed. The Burger King spokeswoman wouldn't say whether Pepsi had had a similar plan. The agreement also includes a commitment by Coke to work on what the chain called a state-of-the-art dispensing system to which Burger King would eventually convert. Moreover, in what could be a multimillion-dollar boon for Burger King, Coke will contribute to the chain's cooperative advertising and marketing effort.

Burger King, which already serves Coke products overseas, switched to Pepsi from Coke six years ago. But as part of "vendor reviews" ordered by the chain's new management, Burger King officials approached both soft-drink companies earlier this year looking for a better deal. "We asked them to submit a proposal on what they could offer us for the 1990s," the spokeswoman says.

The loss of Burger King is a major blow to Pepsi's image and the second such loss this year. Wendy's International Inc., which had been involved in a lengthy legal dispute with Pepsi over Wendy's soft-drink contract, said it plans to put Coke in its company-owned restaurants when its Pepsi contract expires at year end.

With the loss of Burger King, Pepsi's share in the soft-drink fountain market drops to about 21% from more than 26%, according to Beverage Digest. Coke's share rises to 65% from about 60%.

"For Pepsi, this is a big morale loss, as well as a revenue loss," says Leenie Ruben, president of Marketing Spectrum Inc., a market research company. "Coke will really be controlling the fountain business with the two largest chains."

A Pepsi spokesman downplayed the loss. "It was not unexpected, given the management changes that Burger King has been going through," he said. He estimated that Burger King accounted for about 2.4% of Pepsi's U.S. soft-drink volume and "nominal" profit.

Coke is getting Burger King's business at a fortuitous time. Sales at the fast-food chain are rebounding. Moreover, Coke's fountain business, which accounts for about one-third of its domestic sales, has been sluggish as a result of softness in the restaurant industry.

The switch to Coke from Pepsi, which is based in Purchase, N.Y., will begin in about 30 days. It may not be completed before the summer in what Burger King said would be a region-by-region conversion. The spokeswoman wouldn't disclose how the chain would announce its change in soft drinks to customers.

Pepsi's contract with Burger King included a 60-day cancellation clause, which Burger King said it exercised yesterday.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Pepsi shares fell 50 cents to close at $65.75, and Coke shares rose 50 cents to close at $76.75.
