American General Proxy-Vote Victory Wouldn't End Pressure to Lift Stock Price

  • Author: Randall Smith and Michael Allen
  • Date: 05/02/90

American General Corp. is likely to remain under pressure to put itself up for sale or take other action to boost its stock price whether or not the Houston-based insurer wins a proxy vote at its annual meeting today. Five out of eight institutional investors surveyed yesterday said they are voting for directors nominated by Torchmark Corp., a smaller insurer based in Birmingham, Ala., that made and withdrew a $50-a-share, $6.3 billion bid for American General.

More ominous for American General Chairman Harold Hook, two of the three institutions backing management's directors said they also favor a Torchmark resolution that the company should be sold. Although nonbinding, a big vote for the measure could keep management under pressure to maximize the company's stock price.

Wall Street stock traders say the outcome of the vote, which may be close, is difficult to predict. Normally, stockholders favor a challenger who has made a credible offer. But Torchmark withdrew its bid to avoid getting ensnarled in state rules limiting insurance takeovers. "This is a hybrid," said one trader.

Separately, a Tennessee federal court yesterday blocked enforcement of an order issued Monday by a Tennessee state official barring Torchmark from soliciting votes for the contest. The federal court news sparked a surge in American General stock, which rose $1.125 a share to $40.125 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

Before the ruling, Torchmark had defied the order and continued soliciting votes, arguing that the order was illegal.

Although only six of American General's 15 directors are up for re-election because the board has staggered terms, traders believe a significant minority of dissident directors can stir up enough trouble to force a sale of the company. To avoid that, American General might pursue either a friendly acquirer or a recapitalization or restructuring to try to boost its stock price.

Just last week, the threat of a proxy victory by a dissident minority slate sparked the $1.9 billion sale of Worcester, Mass., abrasives maker Norton Co. to Cie. de Saint-Gobain, a French glass concern that saved Norton from a hostile bid by BTR PLC, a British conglomerate.

Torchmark is the third major corporation in the last six months to use the one-two punch of a bid and proxy fight to try to break down a target's anti-takeover defenses. Georgia-Pacific Corp. did so in acquiring Great Northern Nekoosa Inc., and BTR used it on Norton.

If the vote is close, or if management is losing, American General may not announce the result for two or three weeks. If Torchmark loses, the result may become known quickly.

The institutions that plan to vote for Torchmark include: the Michigan State Treasurer, with 2.8 million shares; New York State Teachers Retirement System, 1.3 million shares; Capital Research & Management Co. in Los Angeles, with 1.3 million shares at year-end 1989; Brandywine Asset Management Inc. in Wilmington, Del., 600,000 shares, and State of Wisconsin Investment Board, 446,600 shares.

Michigan Treasurer Robert Bowman said he believes the Torchmark nominees "would do the most to enhance the values of American General, either by putting together a sale to Torchmark at $50 a share, or if that doesn't pan out to emulate Torchmark's good record."

Darin Klemm, an analyst at the Wisconsin Investment Board, said, "We don't believe that American General has performed at a satisfactory level, and they haven't shown us a plan to improve this."

Investors that said they planned to vote for American General Management included the Texas Teacher Retirement System, with 1.1 million shares, California Public Employees Retirement System, with 916,000 shares, and another institution that asked to remain unidentified.

Joseph Perrone, chief investment officer of the Texas Teacher fund, said he hopes today's vote will encourage the two companies to explore a friendly merger "to capitalize on each other's strengths."

But another holder, who asked to remain unidentified, objected to Torchmark's tactics in giving American General only 48 hours to consider its bid before withdrawing it. "We don't think the future of the company should be decided on ultimatums like that," he said.

Even though several institutions sided publicly with Torchmark, the outcome remains uncertain because many of the largest holders wouldn't discuss their votes.

For example, some analysts believe Fayez Sarofim, a money manager who owned 6 million shares at the end of 1989, may vote for American General because he is based in Houston, the company's hometown. Mr. Sarofim didn't respond to requests for comment.

Also, an American General employee savings plan holding more than 4 million shares is expected to side with management. American General has 119.2 million shares outstanding.

The largest stock holder, American Financial Corp. with 9.1 million shares, won't influence the outcome because it has an agreement with American General that its shares will be voted in the same proportion as shares it doesn't own.
