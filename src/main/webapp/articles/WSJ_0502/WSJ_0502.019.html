Bush Again Expresses Concern Over Cost Of S&L Bailout; New FDIC Head Sought

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 05/02/90

WASHINGTON -- The White House again expressed concern about the rising cost of the savings-and-loan bailout and said it is beginning the process of picking a new leader of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. to handle it. "We certainly are concerned about additional costs, and it seems clear there will be higher costs than originally estimated," said White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater. "We don't have agreement on how much they will be."

The White House voiced similar concerns several weeks ago, after Congress's General Accounting Office estimated that the bailout's cost would be significantly higher than originally estimated. Subsequently, President Bush met FDIC Chairman William Seidman to discuss the price tag for the bailout. Mr. Seidman also met privately last week with White House Chief of Staff John Sununu, Bush administration aides said.

Mr. Seidman has said he intends to leave his FDIC post before his term expires next year, and Mr. Fitzwater suggested that Mr. Bush hasn't tried to stop him from leaving. The spokesman said that while a firm departure date hasn't been set, the White House has begun internal discussions about a successor for Mr. Seidman. Other officials said it's likely to be several weeks before a firm list of candidates is completed.

Under last year's huge thrift-industry restructuring law, the FDIC was given authority over the thrift deposit-insurance fund and the Resolution Trust Corp., which was created to merge and sell insolvent S&Ls.

Mr. Seidman has irritated Bush administration officials with his outspoken assertions that the S&L bailout probably would be far more costly than the administration's official estimate of $166 billion over 10 years. The FDIC chairman's style has earned him plaudits among Democrats, though, and some of them yesterday urged President Bush to ask him to stay.

Administration aides say the White House has begun increasing its "focus" on the festering problem of failing thrifts. White House aides largely delegated the matter to the Treasury Department after the Bush administration completed the S&L legislation last August.

Since then, though, it's become clear that the administration's cost estimates for the bailout weren't realistic. Last month, the GAO, Congress's investigative arm, said the 10-year cost of the bailout likely will be roughly $243 billion, not $166 billion as the administration initially suggested. The GAO also said the cost over 30 years should reach $325 billion, rather than the $257 billion that the agency itself once estimated.

White House officials say they don't have the time or staff to oversee the thrift bailout, but they seem resigned to devoting more attention to the problem in the next few months.

Mr. Seidman has said many times that he won't stay for his entire term, and Mr. Fitzwater said the White House will be looking for "new leadership there that will carry out an aggressive program" when the chairman leaves.

Mr. Seidman hasn't said precisely when he will be going, but it seems that he will want to stay through the end of June. By that time, the Resolution Trust Corp. hopes to have sold 141 more ailing S&Ls, which would represent a big acceleration. Only about 60 have been sold so far.

Separately, Sen. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio) urged Mr. Seidman to renegotiate some government-backed S&L sales that occurred nearly two years ago. Sen. Metzenbaum said that a recently discovered memo indicates it would have been cheaper to liquidate a group of five Texas thrifts, rather than sell them to New York financier Ronald Perelman.

In a February 1989 memorandum to the president of the Home Loan Bank of Dallas, a lower-level regulator contended that regulators overstated the cost of liquidation by $1.8 billion at the time the deal was made in December 1988. "In my opinion the {agency} has grossly overstated the liquidation cost estimate," said the memo from regulatory analyst Jeff Potter.

The transaction has emerged as the most controversial of nearly 100 government thrift sales struck in 1988. It included about $5 billion in assistance to a newly created thrift, First Gibraltar Bank, over 10 years, as well as nearly $1 billion more in tax breaks for Mr. Perelman's holding company, MacAndrews & Forbes Inc. Renegotiating the deal could involve such things as paying off high-interest government notes, to reduce the cost to taxpayers.
