Senate GOP Offers Plan to Overhaul Election Financing

  • Author: Jill Abramson
  • Date: 05/02/90

WASHINGTON -- Senate Republicans yesterday unveiled their proposals for overhauling the system of financing federal campaigns. Democrats promptly denounced the proposals as insufficient. The Republican campaign-finance package would eliminate political action committees, reduce and require stricter disclosure of "soft money" contributions made to state political parties, restrict contributions from out-of-state individual donors, create a special fund for congressional challengers, and provide lower broadcast rates to candidates.

These proposals, which Senate Republican Leader Robert Dole hailed as an "historic and challenging campaign-reform package," will soon be introduced as legislation by Kentucky Republican Mitch McConnell, who led the filibuster that killed campaign-finance legislation in the Senate two years ago.

Two leading Democrats, Majority Leader George Mitchell of Maine and Sen. David Boren of Oklahoma, said the GOP proposals unveiled yesterday wouldn't stem the flow of special-interest money in politics because they wouldn't cap the overall amount of money candidates can spend in their campaigns. "Without limits on spending there cannot be genuine reform," Sen. Mitchell said.

There has been a partisan logjam on campaign finance for years, with Democrats advocating spending caps and Republicans resisting them. Democrats are also much cooler to the idea of eliminating the role of PACs, and several Democratic senators said that proposals to abolish them would be unconstitutional.

Yesterday, Sen. Boren indicated that Democrats might be willing to agree to the centerpiece of the Republican packagethe abolition of PACs-but only if the Republicans were willing to embrace some form of spending limits.

Democrats, meanwhile, have been meeting for weeks in an attempt to reach consensus on various campaign-finance proposals. Democrats who face re-election in 1992 are seeking an array of transitional provisions to preserve certain fund-raising advantages they enjoy under the current law. But Sen. Boren said the Democrats are close to agreement and hope to unveil their proposals in the next few days.

The question left unanswered yesterday is if there is any real hope that the partisan deadlock on campaign finance can be broken this year. "There's still plenty of room for negotiation," Sen. Boren said.
