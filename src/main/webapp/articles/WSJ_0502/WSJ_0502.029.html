Technology & Health: Doctor Study Finds High Error Rate In Prescriptions

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 05/02/90

Doctors at a big teaching hospital made mistakes in three of every 1,000 drug prescriptions they wrote. Half the mistakes could have caused problems, including fatal reactions, for patients, a team of hospital pharmacists said. The pharmacists, from the Albany (N.Y.) Medical Center Hospital, said they routinely double-check doctors' prescriptions against patients' computerized medical records. They caught mistakes in 905 of more than 289,000 prescriptions written by the hospital's staff and attending physicians in 1987, Timothy S. Lesar and his pharmacy colleagues reported in this week's issue of the Journal of the American Medical Association.

The errors in 522 of the prescriptions could have caused problems for patients, ranging from life-threatening reactions to bothersome side effects, had the prescribed drugs been used as directed, the pharmacists said.

In the most common serious mistake -- 61 cases -- doctors prescribed a drug to which the patient was allergic. Other errors included ordering antibiotics for serious infections for the wrong patient, ordering overdoses of drugs including "blood thinners" to prevent blood clots, underdoses of a drug to combat lead poisoning, and mixing up names of drugs -- such as prescribing azathioprine (an immune suppressant that prevents rejection of transplanted organs) instead of azidothymidine, or AZT, used to bolster the immune system of AIDS patients. (AZT's generic name is now zidovudine.)

Most of the prescriptions were written by doctors on the hospital staff, with first-year residents having the highest error rate -- four erroneous prescriptions per 1,000 written. Doctors in private practice wrote only a small portion of the prescriptions but had the second highest error rate, almost 3.5 per 1,000 prescriptions.
