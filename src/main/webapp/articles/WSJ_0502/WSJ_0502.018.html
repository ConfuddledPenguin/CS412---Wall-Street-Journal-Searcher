Technology and Health: Irradiating Poultry To Curb Bacteria Approved by FDA

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 05/02/90

WASHINGTON -- The Food and Drug Administration approved the use of irradiation to control salmonella and other bacteria in poultry believed partly to blame for rising rates of food-borne illness. FDA scientists described irradiation as a safe and effective process for, in effect, pasteurizing solid foods. As in the heat-pasteurization of milk, they said, zapping poultry with X-rays, gamma radiation or electron radiation greatly reduces, but doesn't eliminate, all bacteria. Thus, although irradiated poultry would be safe longer than untreated poultry, it would still require refrigeration, just as pasteurized milk does.

Despite FDA assurances that irradiation doesn't make food radioactive or expose consumers to radiation, the poultry industry expects consumer resistance to be a problem.

"It would be fair to say at this time that you won't see companies jumping to take advantage of this technology, not unless they feel the consumer will buy the product," said Stephen Pretanik, science director for the National Broiler Council, a trade group for major chicken processors. "There's an element out there that is going to fight this."

Mr. Pretanik said that a consumer-education effort by government and industry is needed to overcome "a lot of misunderstanding and misinformation" about irradiation. Roger Coleman, spokesman for the National Food Processors Association, added, "The issue is consumer acceptance."

FDA approval had been sought by the Agriculture Department, which is responsible for inspecting meat and poultry products, and by RTI Inc., a Rockaway, N.J., company engaged in food irradiation. "This will allow industry a chance to use this new technology if it chooses to," said James Greene, a department spokesman.

The department is particularly concerned about such food-borne pathogens as salmonella and campylobacter, major causes of illnesses. Poultry accounted for almost 10% of the illnesses in U.S. outbreaks that were traced to a food source between 1973 and 1987. FDA scientists estimate that almost every American becomes sick from eating tainted food once every year or so. The illnesses range from a stomachache to an incapacitating, and sometimes fatal, attack.

The FDA already has authorized limited uses of irradiation to inhibit sprouting in potatoes, retard spoilage in produce, and control insects in herbs, spices, grains and other foods.

The latest approval was based in part on studies in which lab animals were put on diets containing irradiated poultry. Irradiation at the relatively low levels approved reduces the number of pathogenic and spoilage bacteria but doesn't sterilize the poultry, FDA officials said. As a result, poultry products would continue to require proper handling and refrigeration by retailers and consumers to prevent surviving organisms from multiplying.
