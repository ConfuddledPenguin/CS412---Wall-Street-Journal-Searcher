Corporate Connection: Flight Facts by Fax

THOMAS COOK, the New York-based travel agency, yesterday unveiled a do-it-yourself booking service for corporate customers.

With the service, customers call into a computer, which then asks a series of scheduling and price questions. The caller responds by pressing the numbers on a push-button phone. The agency then faxes a list of the available flights and fare information from all different carriers to the caller.

The faxed information includes up to seven outbound and seven return flights, to make 49 schedule combinations, complete with prices. The traveler can then call back and book a seat, also by hitting phone buttons.

The service is aimed at business travelers, who often send their secretaries scrambling to gather various flight information. The fax also provides data about on-time performance, flight duration and type of aircraft. Car rentals can be handled as well.

"It's like the ATM for travel," says David Paresky, chairman of Thomas Cook, referring to bank teller machines.

There are shortfalls: You must have an account with the agency and you must have a fax machine at your disposal. The plan also requires that users learn different number codes.

Other agencies hope customers will want to stick with flesh and blood agents. "I've got people who change their minds all the time in the middle of one telephone conversation," says Kathryn Sudeikis, executive vice president of Brennco Travel, an agency in Overland Park, Kan. "How's a computer going to handle that?"

Actually, says Thomas Cook, a push-button code interrupts the system to let you start over.

States Can't Get a Grip

On Air-Fare Direction

MANY STATES would be angry about rising air fares -- if they were sure they were rising.

With deregulation, it's become extremely difficult to monitor ticket prices and balance one market against another, a fact that's led to tremendous confusion among state watchdogs.

Last summer, North Dakota sued NWA Inc.'s Northwest Airlines for $50 million, alleging price gouging, after a state-commissioned study showed fares had soared at the state's airports. But state officials dropped the suit two weeks ago, after a U.S. Transportation Department report reached the opposite conclusion on prices.

Originally, the state claimed fares from North Dakota cities to Northwest's Minneapolis headquarters rose "substantially" between 1986 and 1988. For example, it charged that fares from Fargo, N.D., to Minneapolis surged 56%, while the national average rose only 17% and Northwest systemwide prices inched up only 5%.

But Transportation Department numbers showed the state's air fares are "in line" with national numbers. Slightly red faced, North Dakota dropped the suit in exchange for a promise by the airline that it wouldn't gouge residents.

The government study is "more up to date," says Nicholas Spaeth, North Dakota's attorney general. According to Mr. Spaeth, the federal government's report signaled that state fares didn't change in 1989 while fares across the country rose.

North Dakota isn't the only one baffled. Earlier this year, the California Commission on Aviation and Airports became so confused by piles of testimony and research on air fares that it couldn't issue a verdict on what has happened to California ticket prices since deregulation. Says Christopher Thompson, a consultant to the commission: "It's mind-boggling."

All's Fair in Love

And First-Class Seats

FREQUENT FLIERS are scheming to upgrade their seats to first class.

Free upgrades from coach seats are the hottest awards in frequent-flier programs. As a result, first-class seats have become harder to find. Many airlines won't let fliers upgrade their seats until 24 hours before the flight. By then, many seats already are taken. Frequently, airlines put would-be upgraders on a standby list until the last minute, to make sure there will be room for anyone willing to pay real money for a first-class seat.

To increase the odds that there will be room for them to upgrade their tickets, some frequent fliers are booking phantom travelers in first class. When the seats go unclaimed at the airport, airline counter agents run through their standby list and the schemers can step forward and get a seat.

To get around the 24-hour rule, some frequent fliers book phantom travelers days in advance and then cancel the reservation a few hours before the flight. Then they'll call back to make their upgrade. If all goes well, they'll be told a seat just opened up.

"We've got to do this stuff," says an upholstery salesman aboard a USAir flight to Chicago. "It's a dog-eat-dog world."

Pan Am Belt-Tightening

Shows Up at Mealtime

AMID FINANCIAL woes, Pan Am has launched a cost-reduction drive that's expected to save an estimated $4 million a year. It may reduce some passenger waists as well.

Among the cutbacks: On many short-haul flights, the ailing carrier used to serve soup, a sandwich and dessert. By axing the soup, Pan Am will save about $500,000 a year. On flights between New York and Warsaw, first-class passengers will no longer get caviar.

Not all of the fat to trim is from food: First-class passengers now will get just one hot towel, instead of two.
