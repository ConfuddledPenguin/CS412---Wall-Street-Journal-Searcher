International: Meeting of G-7 Leaders Expected to Seek End to Delays of IMF Capital Increase

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 05/02/90

WASHINGTON -- Financial leaders of the Group of Seven industrial countries plan to meet here Sunday to try to resolve complex issues that are holding up a proposed capital increase for the International Monetary Fund. The sticking points include how to deal with countries that have prolonged overdue payments and how to settle a dispute between France and Britain about their respective rankings in the IMF.

"It's by no means certain that we'll reach an agreement on the quota this weekend, but I think we will," said a U.S. Treasury official. He cautioned, however, that "even when (the G-7) agrees, they may have trouble selling it to the whole membership" of the IMF.

The Group of Seven includes the finance ministers and the central bank presidents of the U.S., Japan, West Germany, Britain, France, Italy and Canada. They are the most powerful shareholders of the IMF and the World Bank. Their meeting on Sunday will be followed by the regular semiannual meetings of the IMF and the World Bank on Monday and Tuesday.

The G-7 also will discuss international financial developments, including the continued weakness of the yen. But in contrast to the group's most recent meeting in Paris, supporting the yen probably won't be a major focus. Japan's currency and stock market have recovered some stability. Moreover, the U.S. Treasury, which plans a refunding of more than $30 billion in U.S. debt next week, is likely to be eager for the meeting to avoid major statements on currencies or interest rates.

The IMF has sought a 100% increase in member contributions, or quotas, which total about $120 billion. But due to opposition from the U.S. and others, the fund probably will have to settle for half that.

The U.S. and, to a lesser extent, Britain, which is currently the IMF's second largest shareholder, insist that the arrears problem be settled before IMF capital is increased. But it could take months to sort out the problem, and that may mean that the capital increase won't take effect until the end of 1991, officials say.

As of Jan. 31, $3.4 billion of payments due the IMF were more than six months overdue; more than $500 million of this amount was more than three years overdue. Eleven countries were delinquent. Sudan, Zaire and Peru are among the most severely indebted to the IMF.

Recently there has been some progress in working out ways of dealing with these arrears, a senior monetary official said this week. Nevertheless, the U.S. is pushing a complex plan to resolve the problem that would involve changing the IMF's charter. It would backstop additional bridge loans to the delinquent countries with gold those same countries have previously provided the IMF to meet their quotas. The additional loans would help the countries work out their arrears, but if they failed to live up to their obligations, their gold would be sold to make good the IMF's claims against them.

The U.S. and 19 other IMF board member countries are proposing using three million ounces of gold, roughly the amount of gold the delinquent countries originally deposited with the IMF. But two other board members -- Japan and West Germany, who will provide the largest part of the bridge loans -- want more gold backing.

Meanwhile, France is asking that it be given an equal rank in the IMF with Britain. Technically, Britain would have a 6% share in the fund after the quota increase, and France would have a 5.1% share, but Paris is asking for parity, with each country having about 5.4% or 5.5%.

There is considerable speculation that some arrangement involving the siting of the new European Bank for Reconstruction & Development in London may persuade the British to cut their quota a little.
