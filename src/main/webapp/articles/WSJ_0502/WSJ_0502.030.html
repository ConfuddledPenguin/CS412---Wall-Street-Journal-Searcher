White House Backs Raising Penalties Levied by OSHA

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 05/02/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration endorsed legislation that would substantially stiffen some penalties for employers' workplace-safety violations. Testifying before the Senate labor subcommittee, Gerard Scannell, assistant secretary of labor, said the Labor Department supports provisions of pending bills that would triple potential civil penalties for safety and health violations and upgrade from misdemeanors to felonies any willful violations that cause death.

Tripling civil penalties, as provided in a bill offered by Sen. Frank Lautenberg (D., N.J.), could sharply escalate fines imposed by the department's Occupational Safety and Health Administration, which Mr. Scannell heads. It would allow fines of $30,000 for each violation; currently, the limit is $10,000 per violation, which, in recent years, has itself resulted in multimillion-dollar OSHA citations against some large employers.

Last month, for instance, the agency proposed fines of $5.7 million against Phillips Petroleum Co., stemming from explosions in October at a Houston-area plastics plant, which killed 23 workers. Earlier, OSHA proposed a record $7.3 million in fines against USX Corp., citing numerous health and safety violations at two plants.

While the citations usually are sharply scaled down in settlements, Mr. Scannell said that since 1986, the agency has issued about 100 citations for "egregious" violations to about 90 employers. He said about two-thirds of those citations have already been settled, "securing swift abatement of hazards," and resulted in about $15 million in fines.

Mr. Scannell also backed changing to felonies from misdemeanors those willful violations that lead to death in order to encourage U.S. attorneys to prosecute such OSHA cases.

Mr. Scannell's testimony also contained the Labor Department's first formal declaration that OSHA enforcement doesn't preempt state and local prosecution of employer misconduct involving workplace health or safety violations. States shouldn't be pre-empted from enforcing criminal laws that deal with murder, manslaughter or assault, he said. But he said that purely regulatory laws, which make it a crime for employers to violate particular health or safety standards, should be pre-empted unless they're part of an OSHA-approved state regulatory plan.

Although OSHA has been working with state and local prosecutors who pursue criminal workplace health or safety cases against employers, the department previously hadn't taken a stand on the preemption issue. The Justice Department, however, has stated that OSHA doesn't pre-empt such prosecutions.
