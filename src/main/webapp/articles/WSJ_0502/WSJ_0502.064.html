Emerson Electric Enters Guilty Plea In Pentagon Case

  • Author: Robert L. Rose
  • Date: 05/02/90

Emerson Electric Co. pleaded guilty in federal court in St. Louis to inflating cost proposals on certain military contracts, and agreed to pay $14 million as part of a settlement with the government. The agreement stems from allegations by the government that at least from Jan. 1, 1983, to Sept. 30, 1987, Emerson's Electronics and Space division submitted "false and inflated" cost and price figures on contracts on which it was the only supplier.

Specifically, Emerson pleaded guilty to four counts of making false statements on government contracts for equipment used to test instruments on military aircraft. In addition to the $14 million civil settlement, which goes to the Defense Department, the company agreed to pay fines totaling $40,000.

The settlement closes a two-year grand jury investigation of the St. Louis maker of electric and electronic products. The investigation became public in February 1988 when a team of Defense Department investigators made a surprise search of the division's Ferguson, Mo., plant.

Investigators were trying to determine whether the company illegally switched military vehicle parts from a U.S. program to vehicles sold to Egypt.

U.S. Attorney Thomas E. Dittmeier, whose office led the investigation, said in an interview the search led to evidence of the cost overestimates. He also said his office found "possible criminal violations" in Emerson's parts transfer practices, but said it allowed the company to plea only to the cost charges as part of the agreement.

Joe Adorjan, Emerson vice chairman, denied in an interview any wrongdoing by the company in the parts transfer area. But he called the guilty plea on the cost charges "a serious event for us."

Emerson said it negotiated the civil settlement to "resolve a number of open issues" with the Defense Department. It also said in a statement that the agreement "is in the best economic interest of shareholders." It said it included the settlement in its second-quarter results announced yesterday, in which it said profit rose nearly 6% to $156.2 million, or 70 cents a share. Sales rose nearly 6% to $1.91 billion.

While it's not about to interrupt Emerson's 32-year string of higher earnings, the plea agreement further tarnishes the company's reputation in the defense business. The Defense Department could still bar the company from contracts, but Mr. Adjoran said in the interview that the company is "optimistic" that won't happen.

Both Emerson Electric and the U.S. attorney said the company corrected the cost-estimating systems before the government investigation began, and that Emerson cooperated in the investigation.

Early last year, the Navy suspended Emerson's Hazeltine unit from competing for and receiving new government contracts. The three-month suspension came after Hazeltine pleaded guilty to conspiracy and other charges in the Pentagon procurement scandal.

In March 1989, a former Hazeltine executive was sentenced to three months in jail after admitting that he conspired to obtain inside information about competitors' bids on a contract.
