Foreign Exchange: Pound Advances Ahead of British Vote; U.S. Unit Is Sidelined in Thin Dealings

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/02/90

NEW YORK -- The pound rose against the dollar, mark and other currencies in a surprising show of strength ahead of this week's British parliamentary elections, which are expected to result in losses for the ruling Conservative Party. Relatively strong activity in pound dealings was the most noticeable example of a trend toward non-dollar transactions, as the U.S. unit idled against the mark and yen in trading bands close to 1.68 marks and 158.80 yen.

Participants said tests of both higher and lower dollar levels against the mark and yen early in the New York day failed to spark decisive movement in the U.S. currency, largely because of unusual market thinness following a holiday Monday in Japan and the observance of May Day in Europe.

In the vacuum left by the dollar, sterling was the star performer of the day, making strong gains against the mark and rising modestly against its U.S. counterpart in defiance of widespread predictions that the pound would fall ahead of tomorrow's parliamentary elections in Britain.

The pound's surprising strength stemmed from confidence that the British government won't relax the country's high interest rates even in the event of a strong opposition showing tomorrow, traders said.

"It's a foregone conclusion" that the Conservatives will emerge from the local elections with some losses, and the pound's resilience suggests the market has already discounted the election results, said Raimund Sargent, vice president and senior dealer at Shawmut Bank of Boston N.A.

Traders said they expect the dollar to begin moving up once some depth returns to the market today.

"If the market (in New York) was at full strength, we would take the dollar higher," said Gabriel Grippo, senior foreign exchange trader at Manufacturers Hanover Trust in New York. Mr. Grippo cited a surprisingly good reading of 50.2% from the April survey of the National Association of Purchasing Managers, well above expectations of 48.9%.

The figure suggests an expansion is taking place in the U.S. manufacturing sector after 11 months of declines in the NAPM survey. The number from March's survey was 48.8%.

Other participants agreed with Mr. Grippo's assessment of market conditions, saying the positive meaning of the NAPM survey hadn't been compromised by other factors such as a 1.4% drop in construction spending during March, the steepest monthly fall since a 1.5% drop in February 1989. March's level was still 3.8% above the level one year earlier. February's construction spending was revised to a rise of 3.2% from an increase of 2.6%.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6808 marks, up from 1.6796 marks late Monday, and at 158.83 yen, down from 158.90 yen late Monday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6445, up from $1.6408 late Monday.

In early trading in Tokyo Wednesday, the dollar was quoted at 158.45 yen, down from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 158.90 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6815 marks.

The pound surged against the mark after reaching 2.75 marks in New York, where technical buying came in to boost it above 2.76 marks. The British unit finished New York trading around 2.7624 marks, above 2.7552 marks late Monday in New York.

The mark was weakened slightly against the dollar and other currencies because of continued uncertainty over the effects of German monetary unification, traders said.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $370 an ounce, up $1.60. Estimated volume was a moderate 2.7 million ounces.

In Hong Kong early Wednesday, gold was quoted at $370.55 an ounce.
