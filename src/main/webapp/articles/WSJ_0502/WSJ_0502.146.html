What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/02/90

MANUFACTURING GREW in April for the first time in 11 months, purchasing managers said. But construction spending fell 1.4% in March, the first decline this year. Both manufacturing and construction have been hurt by the Fed's efforts to battle inflation with high rates.

Stocks edged higher in reaction to the purchasing managers' report, while bond prices weakened. The Dow Jones industrials closed up 12.16, at 2668.92. The dollar drifted lower.

---

Chrysler posted a $71 million profit for the first quarter, surpassing expectations and pushing its stock up 75 cents, to $15.25. Though earnings were down 80% from a year earlier, the auto maker was expected to only break even due to sharply lower production and slumping sales.

---

Coca-Cola scored a major coup against Pepsi-Cola by swiping its biggest fountain client, Burger King. Coke already supplies the nation's largest fast-food chain, McDonald's.

---

The House approved a $1.24 trillion spending plan for fiscal 1991 that Democrats viewed as little more than their opening position for talks with the Senate and White House.

---

Mitsubishi Motors agreed to buy a majority stake in Value Rent-a-Car, becoming the first Japanese maker to align itself with a U.S. car-rental firm. Terms weren't disclosed.

---

American General is likely to remain under pressure to find a buyer or take other action to boost its stock price whether or not hostile suitor Torchmark wins today's proxy vote. The vote is expected to be close.

---

Georgia-Pacific said it is considering selling some assets to help pay off debt from its recent $3.74 billion acquisition of Great Northern Nekoosa.

---

Michael Milken will be subpoenaed by a House panel, which agreed to offer immunity to the former junk-bond king in return for his testimony. A hearing could be held this summer.

---

Carl Icahn isn't expected to win his proxy battle to split USX into separate steel and energy businesses. But he is forcing the company's management to consider some bold initiatives.

---

The S&L bailout's rising cost is causing concern at the White House, which said it is starting to seek a new FDIC chief to handle the problem.

---

OPEC ministers arrived in Geneva for today's start of an emergency meeting, apparently convinced that short-term production cuts are the only way to calm the oil markets.

Gasoline prices aren't expected to decline anytime soon despite the recent 20% slide in crude-oil prices.

---

The American Stock Exchange is seeking SEC permission to list U.S. stock-index warrants that would let investors bet on the market's direction over the next three years.

---

The New York state sales tax may soon be broadly expanded to cover such services as custom computer programming, management consulting and public relations.

---

Group of Seven officials plan to meet in Washington Sunday to resolve issues that are holding up a proposed capital increase for the IMF.

---

Legislation to stiffen penalties for workplace-safety violations was endorsed by the Bush administration.
