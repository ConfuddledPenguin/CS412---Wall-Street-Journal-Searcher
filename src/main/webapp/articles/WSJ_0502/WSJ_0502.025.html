Inside Track: At Three Firms, Insiders Sell Out In Nick of Time

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 05/02/90

NEW YORK -- Talk about a close call. That's what it was for some corporate insiders, according to filings with the Securities and Exchange Commission. These officers and directors sold stock in their companies just weeks before prices fell sharply.

According to the SEC filings:

-- David F. Brown, former chairman of General Development Corp., sold his entire stake in the Miami real estate company in the two weeks before it filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection April 6. Mr. Brown, who resigned under pressure from the company's board March 14, sold 39,059 shares at an average price of $4.40 a share. The stock finished yesterday at $1.375 a share, up 12.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

-- Seven insiders at T. Rowe Price Associates Inc. sold 80,765 shares of the mutual fund between March 6 and March 13. Since then, the price of the Baltimore company's stock has fallen about 16%, chiefly on the company's March 30 announcement that some of its mutual funds held commercial paper issued by Mortgage & Realty Trust, a real-estate investment trust that filed for bankruptcy protection. In national over-the-counter trading yesterday, T. Rowe Price shares closed at $24.25, down 25 cents; the insiders sold at an average share price of $28.71.

-- Eight insiders at Network Equipment Technologies Inc. sold over 164,539 shares in late January and February. They got $28 to $31 a share; the stock's price has since fallen sharply, closing yesterday at $10.625 a share, up 87.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Mr. Brown sold all of his General Development Corp. shares from March 23 through March 30, shortly after he pleaded guilty March 22 in federal court in Miami to one count of conspiracy. On the same day, the company also pleaded guilty in a plea bargain with federal prosecutors to settle charges it defrauded home buyers by charging inflated prices based on improper appraisals.

General Development agreed to pay a $500,000 fine, and under a pending restitution agreement, would pay the home buyers about $80 million to $100 million over 25 years.

Mr. Brown and his attorney declined to comment on the sales. Otis Wragg, spokesman for General Development, declined to comment other than to say that Mr. Brown didn't have to sell his stock as part of an agreement tied to his departure from the company.

Although Mr. Brown's shares, at an average price of $4.40 a share, sold for far more than he would have earned if he had waited to sell them until after the bankruptcy filing, the former chairman probably lost money on the transaction. Some of the shares were acquired through a retirement plan, or through the exercise of warrants provided by the company, but SEC files show Mr. Brown bought slightly less than half of the shares in the open market in purchases from 1986 through 1988, paying $13.38 to $22.65 a share.

The stock of T. Rowe Price has also skidded lower since its insider sales, chiefly on the company's exposure to the problems of Mortgage & Realty Trust. On April 16, T. Rowe disclosed it holds about $65 million of the REIT's commercial paper and notes, and later announced it would take a $4 million charge against second-quarter earnings as a result.

Stephen Norwitz, spokesman for the company, said the strong pace of insider selling in March was "coincidental," adding that Standard & Poor's Corp. didn't downgrade Mortgage & Realty's commercial paper until March 13 -- after most of the insiders had sold -- and that the REIT didn't file for bankruptcy protection until later in the month.

The largest sale was by William D. Gould Jr., a vice president, who sold 30,000 shares March 6 and March 8. The sale represented 36% of his holding in the company, SEC filings show. Stephen Boesel, portfolio manager of the firm's Growth & Income Mutual Fund, sold 21,000 shares, the first time he has sold shares since September 1986. SEC filings show Mr. Boesel retains 21,100 shares, but the company said he holds an additional 100,000 shares that have been omitted in filings to the SEC.

The other T. Rowe insiders sold between 1,260 and 15,000 shares, representing 5% to 57% of their holdings, according to the company.

Mr. Boesel and Mr. Gould declined to comment. Mr. Norwitz said Mr. Boesel sold his shares to purchase a house, while Mr. Gould sold to meet a personal obligation.

At Network Equipment Technologies Inc., Chairman Bruce Smith was one of the biggest sellers, trimming his holdings 50,000 shares Jan. 24 through Feb. 15, according to SEC filings. They show that he retains 179,781 shares. Steve Schlumberger, formerly a company vice president and now on sabbatical, also sold 50,000 shares in February, retaining 75,358 shares, SEC filings show.

The other six NET insiders sold between 393 and 23,000 shares, representing less than 1% to 50% of their holdings in the company, according to SEC filngs.

The company declined to comment on the sales or to confirm the SEC figures.

On April 2, the company announced that its fiscal fourth-quarter results would fall well below expectations. It reported those results April 25. For the quarter ended March 31, it had earnings of four cents a share, down 87% from a year earlier, on an 18% revenue rise.
