GOOD NEWS FOR FRENCH BUSINESS

To help counteract the negative economic effects of the Mideast crisis, France's government is preparing some unexpected good news for businessmen and consumers: Lower taxes next year.

Officials said they also are planning deeper than expected cuts in government spending next year, in an effort to curtail the expected inflationary effects of higher oil prices. The plans are part of the preparation of the 1991 budget, which will be presented to Parliament in the fall.

The planned cuts in business taxes are likely to anger many Socialist members of Parliament, who generally support the government but consider Socialist Prime Minister Michel Rocard too pro-business. The cuts likely also will be criticized by the right-wing opposition, which generally considers the Socialist government's tax and spending cuts too small.

Under the plan, taxes on reinvested business profits are to be cut to 34%, from 37% currently. An earlier plan called for them to fall no lower than 35%.

ALBANIAN DENIAL

Albania denied it is building a wall around foreign embassies in its capital, Tirana, in order to restrict access to them. The official ATA news agency said that "at the request of some embassies themselves some adjustments of the walls and their lattices are being made." It didn't name the embassies. About 5,000 Albanians climbed over embassy walls last month to gain access to foreign missions in a bid to emigrate. They eventually were allowed to leave Albania.

BHOPAL ARGUMENTS END

India's Supreme Court finished hearing arguments yesterday on overturning last year's $470 million settlement between the government and Union Carbide Corp. for the 1984 Bhopal gas disaster. The five-judge panel reserved judgment on the case, United News of India reported. No date was set for pronouncement. The settlement between the government of previous Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi and the U.S. company was ordered by the Supreme Court in February 1989 during the hearing of a $3 billion suit filed against Union Carbide. Victims' groups and the current government appealed against the settlement earlier this year, saying it was too meager.

CZECHOSLOVAK BREAKTHROUGH

Czechoslovak police have trained dogs to sniff out Czech-made Semtex explosive, once a nearly undetectable weapon of terrorists. Prague's daily Mlada Fronta said trained German shepherd dogs can find the plastic explosive hidden in a building, car, luggage or on a person. Training the dogs to sniff out Semtex takes three months, the Communist Party paper Rude Pravo reported.

EUROPE'S IMPERILED FORESTS

Air pollution is taking a toll on Europe's forests, the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe reported. The survey covered 286 million acres in 26 nations, representing two-thirds of Europe's forest area. Spruce, fir and oak trees over 60 years old were the worst hit. Over 15% of all spruces in this age group in 15 countries were suffering moderate to severe defoliation, as were the same percentage of oaks in 15 countries and of firs in seven countries, the report said.

NEW HOTEL FOR BEIJING

Japan's Sapporo Breweries Ltd. and partners will open a hotel in Beijing to coincide with the Asian Games to be held in the Chinese capital in September, a company spokesman said. The first eight floors of the 13-story Beijing Rainbow Hotel will be opened to guests from Sept. 17, the spokesman said. Sapporo has a 60% stake in the hotel, Beijing Tourism Corp. has a 35% stake, and the remainder is owned by the Japanese trading house Tokyo Maruichi Shoji Co.

EAST GERMAN SPORT STRUGGLE

East Germany's training system for swimmers, the most successful in the world, is close to going under, an official of the sport's federation said.

After years of close attention and lavish financing, swimmers face mounting problems in training, medical care and other areas because money has dried up. "The most successful swimming country is disappearing," a federation official lamented. "People are going to other jobs or abroad, years of experience is just flowing away."

The official said "considerable parts of our swimming structure are already broken," including the system for selecting youngsters, cultivating them in training centers and allocating clubs. "The structures are cracking right down to sports medical care," he said.

West Germany's swimming federation has agreed to merge with its East German counterpart. That decision sparked protests among West German swimmers who fear they will lose their places on the team to more powerful competitors.
