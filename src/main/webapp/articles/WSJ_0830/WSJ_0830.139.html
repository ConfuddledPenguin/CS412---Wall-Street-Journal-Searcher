Foreign Exchange: Dollar, Yen Climb as Oil Prices Slide; Pound Is Supported by High U.K. Rates

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/30/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar and yen, the two currencies hit the hardest by expensive oil, rose yesterday on the back of falling petroleum prices. The U.S. and Japanese currencies fared the best against continental European currencies. The pound and other currencies supported by high interest rates held their own.

The biggest loser on the day was the Swiss franc, which was sold off when dealers learned that Switzerland's benchmark Lombard rate had been lowered to 9.375% from 9.5% Tuesday. Last week, the rate was above 10%.

In addition, comments yesterday by National Bank President Markus Lusser convinced currency investors that the central bank will hold firm only on interest rates of long-term securities, and not the shortterm paper favored by currency speculators.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.5595 marks and 1.2900 Swiss francs, up from 1.5563 marks and 1.2867 francs late Tuesday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 143.60 yen, down from 143.73 yen.

The yen posted gains against the mark and Swiss franc. The mark declined to 92.081 yen from 92.354 yen, and the franc dropped to 111.318 yen from 111.704 yen.

In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the dollar was quoted at 143.65 yen, down from 144.35 yen at the close there Wednesday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5599 marks, up from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 1.5556 marks.

The market's movements yesterday also reflected the first attempts by dealers to level positions ahead of month-end accounting and a holiday weekend in North America.

Amid these adjustments, the dollar is enjoying a grace period, said Mark Morris, a dealer in New York for National Westminster Bank. Even as it retains lingering support as a "haven" from Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, the greenback is gaining from the effect that new-found optimism for peace is having on oil prices.

Once dealers are completely free to ignore Baghdad, he said, the dollar will fall once again. Even cheap oil won't save the wobbly U.S. economy, many observers think.

Sterling was trading late in New York at $1.9460, up from $1.9428 Tuesday. The Canadian and Australian dollars ended at 87.63 cents and 83.12 cents, respectively, up from 87.54 cents and 82.97 cents.

In fact, these so-called Anglo high-yielders, which had risen in recent weeks because of their status as petrocurrencies, stood to lose yesterday as the benchmark crude oil futures contract fell $1.96 on the New York Mercantile Exchange to $25.92 a barrel. But interest-rate shopping held these currencies firm.

Meanwhile, speculation about a possible Japanese discount-rate increase heated up as two Japanese daily newspapers reported that the Finance Ministry and Bank of Japan had started discussing such a move Tuesday. Bank of Japan Governor Yasushi Mieno denied any such discussions Wednesday. However, early Thursday in Tokyo, the Bank of Japan raised the discount rate by 0.75 percentage point to 6%.

Analysts had been looking for the Bank of Japan to raise its discount rate by early October, after the central bank's quarterly survey of business sentiment and the second-quarter gross national product report come out in the middle of September.

Jumpei Ishii, deputy foreign-exchange department manager at Mitsui Taiyo Kobe Bank, said the market has factored a rise of 75 basis points in the discount rate into the market. Mr. Ishii himself predicted a rise to 6.25% from the current 5.25%.

The dollar rose against the yen in heavy Asian trading Wednesday, finishing mixed against other currencies. The U.S. currency was last quoted in Tokyo Wednesday at 144.35 yen, compared with 143.20 yen late Tuesday in Tokyo.

Yen selling to close out long positions shook the market in the Japanese afternoon and pushed volume to high levels. The pound joined the dollar in rising strongly against the yen.

The dollar ended European trading slightly higher, in what traders termed a technical consolidation amid quiet dealings. Dollar activity was slack, with most action focused on cross movements in the yen.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $385.30 an ounce, up 30 cents. Estimated volume was a heavy six million ounces.

In Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $386.25 an ounce.
