New Construction Was Down in July, F.W. Dodge Reports

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/30/90

NEW YORK -- Another monthly decline in new construction contracts points to still lower levels of building in the months ahead, according to F.W. Dodge, the forecasting unit of McGraw-Hill Inc. Dodge said setbacks in all segments in July resulted in an annualized construction startup rate of $232.31 billion, down from $244.85 billion in June. The seasonally adjusted rate brought the Dodge index down to 148 from 156 the previous month. The index compares current contracting with a 1982 base of 100.

"Restrictive credit, budget austerity, and a stagnant economy are more than enough reasons for the sorry state of construction in 1990," said George A. Christie, vice president and chief economist for Dodge. Energy inflation is an added impediment to improvement in the near future.

The weakest segment, nonresidential building, dropped 8% to $80.5 billion, as a result of lagging office construction, which fell 23% to the lowest rate in a decade. Nonbuilding construction, including public works and utilities, was down 7% to $42.4 billion, and homebuilding was down 2% to $109.4 billion.

Through seven months of 1990, $143 billion of new construction was started, 8% less than the comparable 1989 period. The most severe regional declines continued to be in the Northeast, down 24%, and the Southeast, down 15% through July.
