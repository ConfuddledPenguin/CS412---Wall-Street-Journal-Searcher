Fluorescent-Bulb Sales Become Electrifying

SALES OF energy-saving fluorescent lightbulbs are brightening so fast that retailers are having trouble keeping some in stock.

One of the most popular is a 27-watt Panasonic bulb that substitutes for 100-watt incandescent bulbs. "We've been out of them 90% of the year," says a spokesman for Energy Federation Inc., Framingham, Mass., a nonprofit concern that sells the bulbs.

Don't expect a low price. The fluorescent equivalent of a conventional incandescent 60-watt bulb retails for about $15. But it lasts 10 times longer, screws into standard sockets, casts about the same color and intensity of light and uses only 15 watts.

Cheaper fluorescent bulbs -- retailing for less than $10 each -- may reach the market within a year, says Amory Lovins, research director at the Rocky Mountain Institute, a think tank in Snowmass, Colo.

Right now, utilities are the biggest customers of fluorescent bulbs; they resell them to foster conservation, which regulators are pressuring them to do. Lighting eats up some 25% of the power generated in the U.S., and fluorescent bulbs could cut the need for power plants costing $85 billion to $200 billion, say Mr. Lovins and other energy researchers in the September issue of Scientific American. Environmentalists also tout the bulbs' potential to cut air pollution from power generation.

This fall, Boston Edison Co. plans to offer customers 300,000 of the bulbs at $3 each, partly door-to-door. Buyers are also thronging lighting stores and other outlets offering the bulbs -- even at retail price, they get back more than their costs through lower electric bills and fewer burnouts.

Buying the bulbs "is not a free lunch," note the Scientific American authors. "It is a lunch you are paid to eat."

Solar-Power Company

Gets Day in the Sun

SOON AFTER Iraqi tanks rolled into Kuwait, the switchboard lighted up at Luz International Ltd.

The Los Angeles company is the biggest player in solar power, and its rows of trough-like solar collectors in southern California produce some 90% of the world's sun-generated electricity. Now people want to know how Luz can help ease the U.S. addiction to foreign oil, says Kathleen Flanagan, a Luz spokeswoman.

The answer: It can't do much. Utilities buying Luz's power use little oil. Still, Luz could realize big gains from higher oil prices, says James MacKenzie, an energy analyst with Washington's World Resources Institute. Utilities burn a lot of natural gas, and gas prices "tend to follow oil prices," he notes.

Thus, the Middle East crisis may close the cost gap between Luz's power and cheaper electricity from natural gas. And the crisis may get more congressmen to vote for solar-power tax credits; Luz has worried that the credits would be discontinued.

Even before the crisis, Luz had almost caught up with the competition in electricity pricing. In a decade, power costs at its new installations fell to eight cents a kilowatt-hour from 24 cents, says Ms. Flanagan.

During the next few years, she adds, Luz expects to shave another two cents off that, putting its power in the same price range as conventional options -- at least in a few sun-drenched states.

Propane Looks Good

To Fuel More Cars

AMONG THE pollution-cutting alternatives to gasoline, propane appears best positioned to benefit from higher oil prices.

Unlike some other alternative fuels like ethanol, propane hasn't needed government subsidies to compete as a vehicle fuel. It's the most widely used alternative fuel, propelling more than 300,000 U.S. vehicles on the road and many off-road vehicles; because of its relatively low pollution, it's often used in forklifts. Some 85% of propane used in the U.S. is supplied domestically as a byproduct of oil and natural gas production; most of the imports come from Canada and Mexico.

Propane, used mainly for heating, is a "much-neglected" vehicle fuel and could be substituted for gasoline faster than other alternatives, says R.F. Webb, an Ottawa energy expert. The facilities to convert vehicles to propane were built up after the 1970s oil shocks and are now "underutilized," he adds. Production of vehicle fuel based on propane could be scaled up "without strenuous effort" to propel about 6.5 million of the approximately 180 million U.S. road vehicles, estimates Mr. Webb. Some 10,000 U.S. retail outlets already sell propane as motor fuel, mainly for trucks and other "fleet" vehicles.

Propane's ability to cut air pollution -- especially the ground-level ozone that plagues big cities -- may soon boost it as a motor fuel regardless of Mideast events. Using propane can halve vehicles' ozone production, according to a recent study at the University of California at Riverside.
