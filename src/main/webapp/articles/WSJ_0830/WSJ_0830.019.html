Sounds Like a Good Way to Have Modern Art Reflect Modern Life

  • Author: Suzanne Alexander
  • Date: 08/30/90

Some folks think modern art is rubbish. Susan Pedicord, on the other hand, thinks modern rubbish should be art. In an effort to improve the environment -- and perhaps nurture budding Picassos -- Ms. Pedicord has opened a store in Montpelier, Vt., to recycle trash as art supplies.

Appropriately named "Restore," the shop offers everything from discarded legal pads, partly used wallpaper rolls and pillow stuffing to clothespin springs, red plastic bottle caps and wood chips. Customers -- mostly artists, parents and kids -- use the materials for such arts and crafts as collages, sculptures, mobiles and toys. Teachers also load up on supplies for use in art classes.

"Most of the materials are perfectly good. They have one little scratch or wrinkle, and they're no longer useful to a company," says Ms. Pedicord. An artist herself, she came up with the idea for Restore three years ago after hoarding leftover scraps from an art project.

Since then, she has persuaded 100 Vermont businesses to channel their scraps to Restore instead of burdening landfills. "Our environmental goal is to get waste out of the waste stream," says Ms. Pedicord. She spends much of her time driving from bottle makers to brush makers to quilt makers -- loading up her small pickup truck with their rubbish. And she also approaches new companies, touring their facilities in search of usable waste.

Vermont artists and kids love the store -- especially the prices. On opening day this month, more than 200 people showed up. At 10-for-a-penny, mailing labels and promotional stickers were among the hot items. "Kids love stickers," explains Ms. Pedicord. But 1990 U.S. Census pencils were also selling fast.

Restore, a unit of Restore Resources Unlimited, a nonprofit, waste-reduction organization, soon will begin exhibiting the work of local artists who've used discarded materials in their pieces. "We wanted to give the art community both access to inexpensive art materials and a new place to exhibit their work," says Ms. Pedicord. In addition, the retailer may sponsor recycled-art competitions at several local schools.

Ms. Pedicord maintains that her store represents "the kind of creative problem solving that will help solve the environmental problems we're facing today." She doesn't address, however, what happens when her customers throw away their art projects.
