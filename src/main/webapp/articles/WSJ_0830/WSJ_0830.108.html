Bush's Nominees to New Housing Board Face Delays Due to Dispute in Congress

  • Author: Joe Davidson
  • Date: 08/30/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush's nominations to the newly created Federal Housing Finance Board are being stalled by a dispute with members of Congress over whether the positions should be filled on a full-time basis. In a letter to the president last week, Senate Banking Committee Chairman Donald Riegle (D., Mich) asked Mr. Bush to withdraw the names of the four nominees if they are unwilling to accept full-time positions. Sen. Riegle said he hadn't scheduled confirmation hearings because the disagreement with the Bush administration, which wants the four to serve part time, hasn't been resolved.

In addition, consumer groups have called for hearings on the nominees, particularly Daniel Evans, who is chairman of the Federal Home Loan Bank of Indianapolis and a longtime associate of Vice President Dan Quayle. Mr. Evans, the Consumer Federation of America contends, may have a conflict of interest because his law firm has represented a bank whose parent company has acquired defunct savings and loans.

The five-member housing finance board was created by last year's thrift-bailout law to regulate the nation's 12 home loan banks, which have 3,200 S&L members. (The secretary of housing and urban development is a permanent member.) The member thrifts buy shares in the home loan banks and get lower interest rates for mortgages.

Chase Untermeyer, director of presidential personnel, said the White House counsel's office is "absolutely vigilant" about potential conflicts of interest. "They would not let anybody out" who even appeared to have a conflict, he added.

Rep. Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas), chairman of the House Banking Committee, argues that a part-time board "where members maintain employment and business interests in the private sector can be the breeding grounds for serious conflict-of-interest situations."

In his letter last week, Mr. Riegle also urged the president not to give the nominees recess appointments, as the White House has considered doing. Recess appointments are made when Congress isn't in session and therefore can't hold confirmation hearings. Recess appointees can hold office until the subsequent session expires, which in this case could be about a year.

Mr. Untermeyer said making recess appointments is "an idea that's in the air," but added there are no plans to make any housing finance board appointments during the current recess.

The Consumer Federation of America has called for the Senate's full confirmation process to examine Mr. Evans's relationship with Bank One in Indianapolis, whose parent company, Banc One Corp. of Columbus, Ohio, has purchased thrifts in Texas, Wisconsin and Ohio. He is a partner in the Indianapolis law firm of Baker & Daniels, which has represented Bank One. Mr. Evans also served as chairman of the Quayle for Vice President Committee in 1988 and as director of the vice president-elect's transition team.

Sen. Riegle has asked the American law division of the Library of Congress to study the part-time, full-time issue. Administration officials say a part-time board would be able to meet its responsibilities fully and would have the flexibility to work as much as is necessary.

Mr. Untermeyer, in an Aug. 13 letter, told the consumer group that the administration believes part-time members could "fully discharge the important responsibilities given it." Part-time status would allow them to work as much as needed, including every day during some months, he said.
