Law -- Legal Beat: Dismissal of Suit Against Rooney Upheld

  • Author: Milo Geyelin and Wayne E. Green
  • Date: 08/30/90

A federal appeals court in Los Angeles upheld the dismissal of a libel suit against CBS commentator Andy Rooney stemming from statements he made during a broadcast two years ago. It is thought to be the first such decision by a federal appeals court since the U.S. Supreme Court ruled last June that statements of opinion are not necessarily immune from defamation suits.

In an April 1988 television broadcast on the CBS newsmagazine show "60 Minutes", Mr. Rooney held up a bottle of "Rain-X," a chemical sealant used to protect cars from rain, snow and ice, and said he spent an hour putting it on his car windshield after receiving a free sample in the mail.

"I suppose he'd like a commercial or a testimonial," Mr. Rooney scoffed, speaking of Rain-X's manufacturer, Unelco Corp., in Scottsdale, Ariz. "You know how they hold the product up like this? It didn't work."

Unelco's suit, filed in May 1988, was dismissed by a federal judge in Phoenix on the ground that Mr. Rooney's comments were protected opinion. But the case was under appeal in Los Angeles when the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in June that commentary is protected only if it is clearly presented as an opinion -- not as objective fact.

Following that standard, the Circuit Court of Appeals in Los Angeles found that Mr. Rooney's comments fell in the latter category. The appeal panel dismissed the case, however, on the ground that Unelco failed to establish that Mr. Rooney's comments were false.

Chicago attorney Robert P. Cummins, who represented Unelco, said the appeal court judges were correct in finding Mr. Rooney's comments potentially defamatory, but said the court's analysis of whether his comments reflected the truth "missed the mark."

"We did not have an opportunity to develop a record that could be objectively scrutinized," said Mr. Cummins, adding that he plans to ask the full appeals court for a rehearing.

The decision was only a partial victory for CBS Inc. "It has always been our belief that these were not statements of fact, but that they were inherently unreliable comments because they reflect Mr. Rooney's personal experience with the product," said Heddy Gold, in-house litigation counsel for CBS in New York.

INCREASES IN COMPENSATION for general counsel appear to slow.

Attorneys who head corporate legal departments got healthy pay increases this year, but a sluggish economy apparently is reducing the size of the compensation boosts.

A recent Ernst & Young survey of 700 U.S. companies found that they are paying their general counsel an average 8% more in salary and bonus this year than in 1989. Last year, general counsel got average increases of about 10%, Ernst & Young says.

"The pay is going up, but it's a far cry from the double-digit increases we saw in the mid-1980s," says Robert J. Berkow, national director of a law firm consulting at Ernst & Young, New York. "It's a reflection of what's going on in the economy."

Ernst & Young made the annual survey in conjunction with the Association of the Bar of the City of New York and the American Corporate Counsel Association, Washington, D.C. Formal results won't be published for about two weeks.

A previously published survey by Of Counsel, a legal trade publication, reported a similar slowing of compensation increases for top legal officers at 170 public companies. It showed an increase of 10.6% compared with 12.5% the year earlier. Some attorneys believe that the slowing trend may continue because of corporate worries about a recession.

"I'd be surprised if it's 10% next year -- real surprised," says Stephen Bokat, vice president and general counsel of the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, Washington, D.C. But other legal experts believe the longer-term pay prospects are strong. "I think the trend for the '90s looks good," says Paul Roy, a vice president of Hildebrandt Inc., Somerville, N.J., a legal consulting firm.

Not surprisingly, compensation appears to vary with the size of a company's legal department. The general counsel overseeing a one or two-person operation received compensation averaging $124,000, the Ernst & Young study shows.

That rose to $420,000 for general counsel supervising staffs of 51 to 99 lawyers and $516,000 for general counsel with staffs of more than 100 attorneys.

RACKETEERING CHARGE filed against company headed by James H. Guerin.

Federal prosecutors in Philadelphia have filed a racketeering charge against Parent Industries Inc., a holding company headed by Mr. Guerin, a military equipment contractor who turned a backyard security and electronics firm in Lancaster, Pa., into a global weapons conglomerate.

The charge, filed as an information in federal court in Philadelphia, alleges that Parent Industries participated in a scheme to create more than $1 billion in phony weapons contracts to inflate the value of International Signal & Control Corp., a maker of hi-tech military hardware that Mr. Guerin started in 1971.

Mr. Guerin sold ISC to Ferranti International PLC, Britain's third-largest military contractor, for $670 million in 1987, but Ferranti has since claimed that it was duped in the deal. Last year, Ferranti wrote off $365 million, more than half its net worth.

A criminal information is an alternative to an indictment and is usually filed after the subject of an investigation has agreed to plead guilty. Last May, Parent Industries agreed to plead guilty to one count of racketeering and pay the government $4.4 million in fines and criminal forfeitures.

Mr. Guerin himself hasn't been charged with a crime. Neither he nor his attorney Joseph Tate, with the Philadelphia law firm of Schnader, Harrison, Segal & Lewis, could be reached for comment yesterday.

Mr. Guerin has been under investigation for two years, but the information is the first criminal charge filed in the case. Federal prosecutors said the investigation is continuing.

HOW LOW may the police fly when searching for drugs?

Above 50 feet if they're searching by helicopter, the Pennsylvania Supreme Court has ruled. The court reversed the conviction of a Bucks County, Pa., man who was spotted through a skylight in his roof growing marijuana in 1985.

The decision is thought to be the first in the country since the U.S. Supreme Court addressed the issue of aerial drug searches last year. But the 15-page decision leaves unclear how low police helicopters can fly in the surveillance of homes and private property of criminal suspects.

Police in the Bucks County case descended to 50 feet from 500 feet and made three passes, rattling windows and shaking the house, to confirm that the plants seen through the skylight were marijuana, according to the decision. The court ruled that the flights constituted an unreasonable search because they "represented a hazard to person and property on the ground."

But the decision doesn't explain what legal standards -- public safety or an individual's right to an expectation of privacy -- should decide the issue. Last year, the U.S. Supreme Court upheld the conviction of a Pasco County, Fla., man who was arrested after local sheriff's deputies flying at 400 feet spotted marijuana growing in his greenhouse. But that case, in which the court was sharply divided, also didn't set limits on how intrusive aerial searches can be.

Assistant Bucks County District Attorney Gary Gambardella said he plans to ask the Pennsylvania high court for a rehearing and may appeal to the U.S. Supreme Court.

VICTIMS OF A CRIPPLING DISEASE sue Kodak over diagnostic dye.

Eastman Kodak Co. and other companies are being sued by 300 plaintiffs who say they were crippled by Pantopaque, a dye used to diagnose back and spinal injuries. The class-action suit was filed in federal court in Newark, N.J.

According to the plaintiffs' attorney, N. Scott Rosenblum, a partner at the Clayton, Mo., law firm Wittner, Poger & Rosenblum, his clients were never told they were risking chronic pain, paraplegia or death from an incurable disease known as arachnoiditis when they allowed doctors to inject Pantopaque into their spinal systems. The plaintiffs are asking for more than $15 million in damages.

A spokesman for Kodak said the company's lawyers hadn't seen the complaint and declined to comment.

Lawyers for the other accused firms, Alcon Pharmaceutical Laboratories Inc., Austin, Texas; Lafayette Pharmaceutical Inc., Fort Worth, Texas; and defunct Lafayette Pharmacal Inc., Indianapolis, could not be reached for comment.

CLASS ACTION SUIT challenges Illinois lethal injection procedure.

Lawyers representing death-row prisoners in Illinois have asked federal Judge James B. Zagel to halt all executions in the state until the courts determine whether an unauthorized chemical, potassium chloride, can be used with two others specified by state statute for lethal injections. The state plans to begin executions on Sept. 12.

Lawyers from the MacArthur Justice Center and the Chicago law firm Sonnenschein Nath & Rosenthal filed a class-action suit on behalf of about 130 prisoners challenging the Illinois Department of Corrections' execution procedures. Sonnenschein partner Charles Altschuler said there is a substantial risk that the proposed injection procedure will "inflict torture" before it kills.

A spokesman for the Illinois State Attorney's office would not comment on the substance of the case.
