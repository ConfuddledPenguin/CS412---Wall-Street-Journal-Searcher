Cotton Exchange Criticized by Agency For Fining a Trader --- Financial Instrument Unit Ignored Testimony, Says CFTC in Angry Opinion

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 08/30/90

NEW YORK -- In a blistering opinion, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission threw out a Cotton Exchange fine against a futures trader for allegedly disruptive behavior. The problem?

It seems that the exchange's tiny Financial Instrument Exchange division ignored testimony that the trader made an offer -- not a bid -- in a disputed trade in the Finex's dollar-index pit.

No matter that the fine against the trader, Gary Fusfield, was a mere $100. Said the CFTC in its harshly worded order: "The magnitude of the exchange's error is not reduced by the relatively trivial size of the fine."

Futures exchanges increasingly have policed their pits for abusive trading behavior following last year's federal probe of futures trading practices at the two big Chicago exchanges. But the Finex case is unusual because of the exchange's apparent blunder -- and because the CFTC tossed out the fine without remanding it to the exchange for further review.

"Because exchanges are under the gun to find violations, it's become a bit of a numbers game," says Thomas Russo, a New York lawyer who specializes in securities and commodities law.

The Finex declined to comment.

The case stemmed from a disputed trade made by Mr. Fusfield in March 1989. James Cahill, Finex floor committee member, accused Mr. Fusfield of bidding -- or seeking to buy futures contracts -- below Mr. Cahill's existing bid in the dollar-index pit, and fined him $100. Such a low-ball bid would have violated exchange rules.

But in an affidavit filed with the exchange, Mr. Fusfield insisted he made an offer -- to sell contracts -- at a price slightly above the bid. Another witness in the pit verified Mr. Fusfield's version of events.

The Finex, however, ignored that testimony in issuing a decision affirming the fine, the CFTC said.

In its opinion, the CFTC called Finex's record of the events "materially misleading," adding that its "reckless disregard for an orderly and accurate procedure has made review of its results impossible, and undermined confidence in that result."

In an unusual twist, the CFTC also refused to remand the matter back to the Finex because it said the exchange's disciplinary procedure was faulty.

Mr. Fusfield, who termed the Finex review a "star-chamber proceeding" in papers filed with the CFTC, said his floor-brokerage business has folded. He now is a computer consultant.
