Heard on the Street: After Sallie Mae's Plunge, Some Analysts Say Fears Over HEAF Losses May Be Overblown

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 08/30/90

NEW YORK -- When will Wall Street stop beating Sallie Mae stock? Until a few weeks ago, the Student Loan Marketing Association, or Sallie Mae, was the darling of portfolio managers, a financial growth stock with a triple-A credit rating. Propelled by earnings increases of more than 20% a year and the market's enthusiasm for growth stocks, Sallie Mae shares rose from around 30 each in early 1988 to a peak of 56 1/2 on July 17.

But less than a week later, this newspaper reported deepening financial difficulties at the Higher Education Assistance Foundation, a privately run firm that had become the largest student-loan guarantee agency in the nation.

Fearing that Sallie Mae might face losses from HEAF's woes, portfolio managers dumped the stock. Sallie Mae shares were trading in the mid-40s within days, and sank as low as 34 3/4 in last week's stock-market rout. Based on yesterday's closing price of 41 5/8, investors have trimmed nearly $1.5 billion from Sallie Mae's market value in less than six weeks.

Several Wall Street analysts who follow Sallie Mae believe the company won't suffer any losses from HEAF's troubles and that the market has punished Sallie Mae far more than it deserves. These analysts are pounding the table to try to lure portfolio managers back into the stock.

"Sallie Mae is one of the strongest financial institutions in the United States," says John E. Keefe, an analyst with Barclays de Zoete Wedd, who believes Sallie Mae's stock price will head back toward 50 by the end of the year. At the heart of Mr. Keefe's recommendation is his belief that whatever happens to HEAF, the Department of Education won't allow Sallie Mae or other holders of student loans to suffer a loss.

Eric I. Hemel, a First Boston analyst, takes a similar view and expresses it with a lack of equivocation unusual on Wall Street. "We see zero, and we mean zero, HEAF-related losses at Sallie Mae," Mr. Hemel declared in a report this month on Sallie Mae. Mr. Hemel and others, including Sallie Mae executives, argue that the government can't allow lenders to take losses on student loans as a result of HEAF's problems, because lenders will then stop making new loans and the program will grind to a halt.

Sallie Mae buys government-guaranteed student loans from banks and thrifts and earns a spread between the interest paid on loans and its cost of borrowing. HEAF is an intermediary between holders of student loans, such as Sallie Mae, and the Department of Education.

If a borrower defaults, the loan holder seeks payment from a guarantee agency such as HEAF, which submits a claim for reimbursement to the government. If too many of an agency's guaranteed loans default in one fiscal year, the government cuts its reimbursement to 90% and then to 80%. HEAF, which guaranteed many trade-school loans, is now in the 80% category.

Lawrence A. Hough, Sallie Mae's recently appointed president and chief executive officer, said in an interview that Sallie Mae owns about $2.5 billion of HEAF-guaranteed student loans, and has loans and loan commitments totaling $1 billion to HEAF and its affiliates. All the loans are backed by more than enough collateral in the form of cash, student loans or receivables from the government, Mr. Hough said. He believes the government will make sure Sallie Mae and other loan holders get 100% reimbursement on their HEAF-guaranteed loans. But even if that doesn't happen, Mr. Hough says Sallie Mae's maximum pretax loss would total between $30 million and $50 million, spread over three or four years.

A concern of analyst Lacy M. Shockley at Smith Barney, Harris Upham, who isn't recommending Sallie Mae, is that the company may have to get into the loan-guarantee business itself to help resolve HEAF's problems. Though Sallie Mae once proposed taking on some guarantee business, Mr. Hough said that proposal was withdrawn and that Sallie Mae won't become a guarantor unless it can be done with "the same virtually zero-risk characteristic that describes our normal business."

Sallie Mae's shares are being hurt, in part, because the resolution of HEAF's troubles and the future size of the studentloan program are political issues unfamiliar to Wall Street. "When political questions enter the valuation equation, many investors just don't want to take the time and they head elsewhere," Mr. Keefe says. "For years, Sallie Mae has been a 90-second story. Now it takes three minutes. In a difficult market, the portfolio manager just doesn't have that much patience."
