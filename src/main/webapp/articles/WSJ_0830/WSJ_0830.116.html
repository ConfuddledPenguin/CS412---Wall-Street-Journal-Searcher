RTC Is Said to Pick J.E. Robert to Run Failed S&L Loans

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 08/30/90

WASHINGTON -- The savings-and-loan bailout agency has chosen an Alexandria, Va., real estate firm to manage $2.4 billion of real estate and loans seized from a Houston thrift, government and private-sector sources said. If a contract to oversee and sell the properties is finalized with the management firm, J.E. Robert Co., it would be the first major asset-management contract awarded by the Resolution Trust Corp. It also is likely to be one of the largest single blocks of assets the agency puts under a management contract.

The package is made up of thousands of loans and properties previously held by University Savings Association of Houston. The government seized the thrift, which was one of the bigger failed savings and loans, and it was sold last October to banking firm NCNB Corp., Charlotte, N.C., for $129 million. NCNB, however, assumed the thrift's deposits and left most of the assets in the hands of the government. NCNB has been managing the properties under an interim contract.

Among the assets are delinquent commercial real estate loans, shopping centers, hotels and land. Under the contract, J.E. Robert would prepare the properties to be sold. In the case of loans, that includes the arduous legal process of foreclosing on the loans and sorting out other claims so that the government can sell the properties.

It wasn't known how much J.E. Robert bid to manage the properties, but generally the asset manager receives an annual fee of about 0.5% of the total value of the properties and then anywhere from 1.5% to 4% of the sales price. Thus, the value of the contract could be $100 million or more if it lasts four or five years.

RTC officials have said they expect to have $20 billion to $30 billion of real estate and real estate loan assets under management contract by year's end. As of June 30, the RTC had total assets of $165.3 billion, but about $120 billion of that was in securities and performing mortgages that the agency finds easier to sell than the real estate and delinquent loans that generally will make up the packages put under contract.

Joseph Robert, president of J.E. Robert, declined to comment.

A spokesman for the RTC also wouldn't comment on whether J.E. Robert had been selected, but he said, "Our asset-management program has been established now, and we expect to be regularly awarding contracts in the weeks ahead."

The agency had a false start with the asset-management program this year after many firms protested that the RTC's process of randomly narrowing the field of bidders was "irrational." The RTC changed the system to try to take the experience of the bidders more into account.

Separately, the RTC said it agreed to sell $379.2 million of residential mortgage loans to Lehman Capital Corp. The portfolio of 5,246 fixed-rate first mortgages, which were assets of Horizon Financial, Southampton, Pa., will be sold for 98.25% of the unpaid principal balance on the day of settlement, expected within three weeks.
