Economy: Leading Index Was Unchanged During July --- Data Confirm the Economy Was Weak Even Before The Persian Gulf Crisis

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 08/30/90

WASHINGTON -- The government's principal economic forecasting index was flat in July, another sign that the economy was precarious even before turmoil in the Middle East jarred American consumers and businesses. The flat reading of the index of leading indicators, a collection of 11 sets of economic statistics that are supposed to foreshadow economic activity six to nine months in advance, followed a 0.1% rise in June and a 0.6% increase in May. Those figures marked slight revisions from last month when, based on preliminary data, the department calculated that the index was unchanged in June and rose 0.7% in May.

Only three of the 11 indicators made positive contributions to the index last month: orders for new plant and equipment, raw materials prices and an index of consumer confidence all rose. The other eight -- including stock prices, unemployment claims, factory orders and building permits -- held the index down.

The leading indicators are the latest in a string of statistics that suggest the economy was in a weak position by July. Mounting evidence of this has increased fears the economy won't be able to survive the higher oil prices prompted by Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait without sinking into a recession.

"While these figures indicate that the economy was already sluggish prior to the Middle East crisis, they do not point to the emergence of a recession. Since this time, however, the sharp rise in world oil prices has made a downturn almost inevitable," said Gordon Richards, an economist for the National Association of Manufacturers.

Private surveys are showing that consumer confidence tumbled in August, suggesting that people may curb discretionary spending in the face of economic uncertainty. That alone, some analysts fear, could tip the economy into recession.

Besides worries over oil prices, the country also faces the prospect of an auto strike later this year. If that happened, the resulting plunge in car production would almost certainly spell recession.

"I think we're right on the edge," said Stuart Hoffman, chief economist at PNC Financial Corp. in Pittsburgh. "I really don't see any growth at all in the fourth quarter."

But some economists don't think a downturn is inevitable. "It's unclear how much shock this is to the economy," said John McAuley, an economist at R.H. Wrightson & Associates, an economic consulting firm in New York. He expects continued slow growth.

Three consecutive declines in the leading indicators are supposed to signal a recession, though the index is notorious for predicting downturns that never occur. After the October 1987 stock market crash, for example, the leading indicators fell for five straight months, but the economy continued to grow at a healthy rate.

In a separate report, the Commerce Department said sales of new single-family homes dropped 2.3% in July following a 4.6% increase in June, which was revised from an earlier estimate of an 8% increase. Sales fell in the Northeast and the West but rose slightly in the Midwest. They were flat in the South.

Nationwide, the average price of a new home fell to $150,500 in July from $152,100 in June. The median price dropped to $121,000 from $127,000.

The home-sales figures are based on incomplete data and are often revised substantially.

The numbers in both reports were adjusted for regular seasonal fluctuations.
