Politics & Policy: Joys of Heat Transfer and Heavy Metal Help U.S. Troops Battle the Boredom

  • Author: John J. Fialka
  • Date: 08/30/90

"You're now in the middle of one of the toughest military missions in modern memory." -- President Bush, Aug. 29

A DESERT AIR BASE, Saudi Arabia -- As President Bush delivered his first message to the troops yesterday, the men in Sgt. William Barrett's tent were listening to heavy-metal rock tapes and trying to keep the blowing sand out of their eyes.

For them, as for many U.S. troops here, it is now a waiting game. The long, sleepless frenzy of unloading planes and setting up camp is over. As the confrontation between the U.S. and Iraq heads into a diplomatic phase, the crucial issue may be which side can best endure what Mr. Bush called the "long, hot days of the Gulf region's cruelest month."

Boredom now looms as the worst enemy. "We just sit around and try to keep up each other's spirits," says 24-year-old Sgt. John Hensley of Fayetteville, N.C.

Sgt. Hensley plays a major role as morale-booster because he has brought along his cassettes. He also has taught his companions a trick that not only is useful but also for a time was the troop's chief form of entertainment. The soldiers wrap plastic water bottles -- which are ubiquitous here -- with gauze. Then they wet the gauze, which is secured to the bottles with shiny duct tape, and set them out on the runway. There, in the 120-degree heat, the desert wind chills the bottles through the magic of evaporation.

The half-dozen men in the tent, all mechanics for their unit's tank-killing A-10 jet fighters, must spend 12 hours a day on the flight line. As the wing's maintenance crew during alerts, they must be ready to prepare the planes -- affectionately known as "the Hogs" -- for what could be one of the first battles of war.

Col. Sandy Sharpe, commander of the 354th Tactical Fighter Wing, which flew here from its home in Myrtle Beach, S.C., worries about what boredom can do to his troops. "They're ready right now to do their job," he says. "I don't think any of them want to sit around for six weeks."

But sit around they may. That's why the tremendous logistical support the U.S. forces are receiving -- from the U.S. and the Saudis -- may become decisive in the Persian Gulf showdown.

The support is beyond what the U.S. has had in any previous war. For example, the mail service here borders on the supernatural. When Sgt. Barrett arrived a week and a half ago, this was a largely empty airstrip, built oversized -- as many Saudi projects were -- for distant future needs. But already, Sgt. Barrett has received five letters from his wife.

Portable showers have been installed. Soon there will be a laundry, a daily hot-meal service and a 500-bed tent hospital. As visiting reporters left this base, a line of flatbed trucks carrying portable outhouses was seen heading north toward the border.

A city of air-conditioned tents has been erected here for sleeping. The Saudis pay the energy bills, and when they heard the airmen were sleeping on cots, they came up with a supply of thick mattresses.

Tonight, the long-awaited MWR -- for morale, welfare and recreation -- tent will be ready to show movies from the U.S.

Most of the men and women here have never seen combat. Col. Sharpe, who saw action during the later stages of the Vietnam War, says the relative handful of veterans remaining are mostly senior unit commanders.

Out on the runway, the long row of twin-engine A-10s have never fired a shot in battle either. They are painted dark green, designed to blend into the shrubbery of Central Europe. The planes' fuselages are built around large machine guns whose uranium-tipped bullets can stitch Soviet-made tanks.

"We'll be right there when their boys meet our boys," says Capt. James L. Baldwin of West Monroe, La., one of the pilots. "We'll be right over them at a couple of thousand feet."

As far as is known here, the Iraqi soldiers who have dug into the table-flat desert over the Kuwaiti border have very little in the way of creature comforts.

"They fought Iran for eight years, but they never had supply lines this long," says one Saudi official. "They have made sure there is very little left in Kuwait, so their lines must go back to Iraq."

Meanwhile, the stiff breeze off the desert flaps the roof of Sgt. Barrett's tent, and the midday sun makes the A-10s so hot that the mechanics must work with gloves on. "We can hang out as long as it takes," Sgt. Barrett says.
