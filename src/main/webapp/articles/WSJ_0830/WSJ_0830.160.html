Commodities: Oil Prices Plunge on OPEC Pact to Lift Output, Perception That Mideast Tensions Are Easing

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 08/30/90

HOUSTON -- Crude oil prices fell sharply following an OPEC agreement to raise production and a growing perception among market participants that Middle East tensions were easing. Ten of the 13 members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries agreed to raise output immediately to help replace the four million barrels of oil taken off the market because of the embargo of Iraqi and Kuwaiti petroleum. Market estimates varied as to how much oil OPEC could quickly put on the market.

Crude oil for October delivery fell $1.96 a barrel to $25.92 on the New York Mercantile Exchange, the lowest level in nearly three weeks. So far this week the contract has dropped about $5 a barrel, or 16%, as financial markets increasingly believe the chances for Mideast hostilities have lessened.

"The crude market weakened on perceptions of a moderating Persian Gulf crisis," said Andrew Lebow, a crude oil analyst with E.D.&F. Man International Futures Inc. He said a number of news reports pointed to the "potential for a peaceful solution."

A number of traders said Iraq's promise to release women and children held hostage, followed quickly by praise from the U.S. government for the move, was one sign of easing tensions. Market participants also pointed to reports that Iraq had made behind-the-scenes peace overtures to the U.S.

And in a market looking to go lower, even news that was several days old was cited as a factor, such as the planned meeting between the U.N. Secretary General and Iraq's foreign minister, scheduled for today.

Among OPEC members, the country that has sizable spare capacity that could ratchet up production quickly is Saudi Arabia. Traders were expecting that country to produce an extra two million barrels a day, with enough output from other OPEC nations to replace a total of three million to 3.5 million of the lost Iraqi and Kuwaiti production.

But some reports on the New York Merc said Saudi Arabia already was producing an extra two million barrels a day and could produce one million beyond that by the end of the year, for a total output as high as 8.5 million barrels daily. Some people remained skeptical, however. "They've been working on projects to upgrade production capacity, but 8.5 million is a very high number," Mr. Lebow said.

Gasoline prices also were off yesterday, in sympathy with crude declines. Gasoline for September delivery finished at 84.72 cents a gallon, down 3.81.

Heating oil fell sharply, with the September contract down 7.88 cents a gallon to 71.64 cents. A sharp buildup in inventories reported Tuesday afternoon by the American Petroleum Institute depressed prices. "Heating oil seems to be in adequate supply right now," one trader said.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

GRAINS AND SOYBEANS: Prices of many corn, wheat and soybean futures contracts sank for the third consecutive day in the absence of any threatening developments in the Middle East. Last week, grain prices rose on speculation that any prolonged climb in oil prices would enable oil-producing nations to import more U.S. grain. "The market is taking out the war premium," said Victor Lespinasse, an assistant vice president at Dean Witter Reynolds, Chicago. Crop prices are also being depressed by good growing weather in the Midwest.

SUGAR: Futures prices eased. The October contract was off 0.42 cent a pound at 11.05 cents and dropped at one point below 11 cents. According to one analyst, the drop was a delayed reaction to the softness that has hit many commodity markets as fears of a war in the Middle East have eased. There are no new developments concerning the current sugar beet crop in Europe, the analyst said. Moderate but steady demand for sugar in the world market from Mideast countries has been curtailed by the crisis in that area, the analyst said.

ORANGE JUICE: Most futures contracts dropped their daily permissible limits. Contract months from November forward declined their daily limit of five cents a pound; November ended at $1.5330. The soon-to-expire September contract, which can rise or fall as much as 10 cents a pound from its previous close, was down as much as eight cents a pound at one point before partially recovering to end 5.95 cents lower at $1.6345. Contributing to selling in the September contract is the fact that Tuesday will be the first day on which notices will be issued to deliver the concentrate against September futures, an analyst said. Concentrate prices have been under pressure because retail demand has slowed in the face of price advances caused by damage to the Florida citrus crop by last December's freeze. The lower prices might help to revive some consumer demand, the analyst said. However, supply concerns have been alleviated by a better-than-expected crop this year in Florida and expectations that new-crop Brazilian supplies will soon be available, which could create further pressure on prices, the analyst said.

COCOA: Futures prices declined. The December contract was off $31 a metric ton to $1,249. Analysts following fundamentals said that rain in the Ivory Coast has eased fears that recent dryness would hurt the crop, although one analyst said the rains were light and scattered and weren't a major factor. "The market fell back because there was little reason for it to have advanced in the first place," she said. John Gambino, a senior futures technical analyst for Merrill Lynch, said that on a purely technical basis, the drop in the December contract to $1,100 a ton in mid-August from $1,560 in May was simply a correction of the market's rally from $960 to $1,560 between last December and May. Now, Mr. Gambino said, "Prices may have bottomed out and could be setting the stage for the renewal of the bull move." The downward trend, he said, "fits into a classic three-wave correction" that he now believes is complete. According to that scenario, he said, the recent low of $1,100 "marks the resumption of the bull market that began last December."
