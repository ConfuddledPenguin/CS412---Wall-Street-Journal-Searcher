International: The Philippines Seeks Flexibility On Debt Terms

  • Author: Lawrence MacDonald and Steven Jones
  • Date: 08/30/90

The Philippines, hard hit by an earthquake in July and the current Mideast crisis, is seeking more-flexible arrangements for repaying its $26.1 billion foreign debt. "There is now a greater need for debt relief," said Jose Cuisia Jr., governor of the Central Bank of the Philippines. "There should be greater flexibility."

Mr. Cuisia said in an interview, however, that the administration of President Corazon Aquino will resist calls for a moratorium on foreign-debt payments. The Philippine Congress has passed nonbinding resolutions asking the president to negotiate a 30-month moratorium, but Mrs. Aquino says she will veto any bill requiring a payment halt, which could ruin the country's international credit rating and hinder foreign trade and investment.

A series of debt-rescheduling agreements has extended repayment periods, but the country's debt burden remains heavy. This year, for instance, payments will eat up 40% of the national budget. One-third of those payments go to foreign creditors.

As an example of the flexibility he is seeking from creditors, Mr. Cuisia said the Philippines wants to conduct another buy-back of commercial-bank debt this year, similar to the $1.31 billion buy-back completed in January. To do so, the government will need permission of the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank to use funds lent by those institutions.

Under a 1989 debt-restructuring agreement, commercial-bank creditors have been given a choice of offering debt to the government for repurchase or of subscribing to new 15-year Philippine bonds. Earlier this year, the Philippines sold more than $700 million of bonds to creditor banks, an amount lower than the government had expected.

Mr. Cuisia said another buy-back would be the "most cost-effective" way for the country to reduce its debt burden. Many foreign commercial banks also favor such action as a way to remove Philippine debt from their books, even at a discount. In the January buy-back, the government repurchased bank debt at 52 cents for every $1 of existing debt with funds from the IMF, World Bank and Japan's Export-Import Bank.

Mr. Cuisia said the amount of commercial-bank debt tendered in the January buy-back exceeded the $1.31 billion actually retired, and he suggested there is at least $500 million and probably $800 million to $1 billion more of debt that could be retired through another buy-back at a similar discount.

New York-based Manufacturers Hanover Corp., the lead bank of the Philippines' 12-bank creditor group, declined to comment on Manila's desire for a second buy-back. But a senior U.S. bank official said creditor banks would be likely to participate in a second such arrangement.

Whatever action is taken to reduce the country's total debt and its payments burden, there is concern both in the Philippines and among foreign bankers that the country may need additional assistance. One reason is the earthquake that struck northern Luzon on July 16, killing more than 1,600 people and causing some $800 million in damages.

Another is the Mideast crisis, which is likely to take a severe toll on the Philippines' balance-of-payments position, especially if oil prices remain near $30 a barrel. The country imports 65% of its energy needs, mostly in oil and oil products. In addition, remittances from Philippine workers in the Mideast are sure to decline.

Before the earthquake and Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait, the government estimated an overall balance-of-payments deficit of $300 million for 1991. "It almost certainly will be much higher than that," Mr. Cuisia said, without providing a new estimate. In the first five months of the year, the balance-of-payments deficit was $58 million.

The Philippines will need new loans to help cover the widening payments gap. Mr. Cuisia said the issue of new lending was deferred during recent meetings with creditors and will be taken up later this year.

In the meantime, the National Economic Development Agency has reduced its outlook for growth in gross national product to 3.8% from 6.5% forecast at the beginning of the year.
