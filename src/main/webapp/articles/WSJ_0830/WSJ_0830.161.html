Credit Markets: Bonds Rally on Strong Bidding at 5-Year Auction And Hints of Easing in Persian Gulf Tensions

  • Author: Kevin Donovan
  • Date: 08/30/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices rallied yesterday, boosted by strong bidding at the Treasury's five-year note auction and signs of less tension in the Mideast. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year note ended nearly 1 1/4 points higher, a gain of nearly $12.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, eased to 8.92% from 9.04% on Tuesday.

Bond prices opened modestly higher on news that the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries agreed to raise output levels. But a late-day rally in the Treasury futures pit at the Chicago Board of Trade spilled over into the cash market and pushed bond prices sharply higher.

The futures market rally was sparked by the release of the government's five-year note auction results, which were better than some analysts had expected.

The $8.56 billion of five-year, two-month notes fetched an average yield of 8.57%. The yield was up from 8.54% at the previous 5-year, 2-month note auction May 24 and was the highest average yield at an auction since May 25, 1989, when the average yield was 8.72%. In when-issued trading activity, the yield on the five-year notes fell to 8.5%.

The bid-to-cover ratio, which measures the number of bids the Treasury receives for each bid it accepts, was a hefty 3.10-to-1, compared with 2.53-to-1 at the last five-year auction.

Noncompetitive bids, a gauge of individual investor interest, were $672 million. That was up from $593 million at the pervious five-year auction in May and the highest since $696 million at the February 1989 five-year note auction.

Those results seemed to indicate investors were warming somewhat to fixed-income securities after weeks of sitting frozen by fear of what the Middle East crisis would do to oil prices and inflation.

And news from and about the Mideast standoff suggested conciliation was more a possibility than had been perceived.

The Iraqi ambassador to the U.S. told reporters Iraq would release all male foreigners, as well as women and children, if the U.S. would assure it wouldn't attack Iraq.

And an article in Newsday said that a purported intermediary for Iraq made a secret offer to evacuate Kuwait if it received guaranteed access to the Persian Gulf and control of an oil field that dips into Kuwait, among other conditions.

Later in the day, the White House dismissed the proposal and said it hadn't had any direct dealings with Iraq.

"There were elements of constructiveness in the air and a sense that investors don't have to build in a worst-case scenario," said Robert Brusca, chief economist at Nikko Securities Co. International Inc., New York.

Fixed-income securities also were helped by the drop in oil prices, which reflected the OPEC announcement. OPEC nations, with Iran dissenting, agreed to boost production to help make up for the embargo against crude from Iraq and occupied Kuwait.

The announcement sent oil prices tumbling. At the New York Mercantile Exchange, crude oil for October delivery settled at $25.92, down $1.96 from Tuesday.

A drop in the Commodity Research Bureau's futures index was also constructive to market tone. The CRB index settled at 234.71, down 3.66 on the day, reflecting widespread price declines in futures markets.

Analysts said soybeans, corn and wheat futures all took a tumble, as did orange juice, sugar, coffee and cocoa futures. The grain futures led the declines, with some corn contracts hitting life-of-contract lows.

On the economic calendar for today is the weekly release of initial unemployment claims at 8:30 a.m. EDT and the release of July factory orders at 10 a.m. EDT.

A Dow Jones Capital Markets Report consensus of economists looks for an increase of 1.5% in factory orders after a 1.5% decline in June.

Treasury Securities

Treasury securities ended higher yesterday, after a rally in the Treasury futures market spilled over into the cash market.

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 98 5/32 to yield 8.92% compared with 96 30/32 to yield 9.04% on Tuesday. The latest 10-year notes ended at 99 18/32 to yield 8.80% compared with 98 26/32 to yield 8.91%.

Short-term rates eased slightly yesterday. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills fell to 7.47% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.70% while the rate on six-month Treasury bills fell to 7.44% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.82%.

Corporate Bonds

Junk bonds ambled along in another day of uninspiring summer trading.

Prices of most go-go bonds, or top-quality, liquid high-yield names, ended the session slightly higher, but junk bonds from retailers and other cyclical issuers continued to come under heavy selling pressure.

Hard hit were junk bonds of R.H. Macy & Co. and Carter Hawley Hale Stores Inc. Macy's 14.50% senior subordinated debentures slipped two points to 59 while the retailer's 14.50% subordinated debentures also slumped two points to end at 46.

Carter Hawley's 12.50% senior subordinated debentures slid two points to 69 while the company's 12.25% senior subordinated notes shed 1 1/2 points to end at 74.

With recession looming, investors have been selling junk bonds of retailers on fears that profits will be squeezed in an economic downturn.

Some other cyclical bonds that were hammered in yesterday's trading: National Gypsum Co.'s 11.375% senior subordinated notes, which fell two points to an offered price of 43. Bids for the company's 14.50% senior subordinated debentures tumbled four points to 36.

Yesterday, Moody's Investors Service Inc. lowered the debt ratings of National Gypsum because the decline in demand for gypsum wallboard prices will continue to erode the company's already skimpy interest coverage.

In the investment-grade corporate debt market, high-grade corporate bonds posted gains with long-term bonds advancing as much as 3/4 point.

Municipal Securities

Municipal bond prices perked up after the doldrums of recent weeks and wrapped up the calm session with gains of 1/4 to 1 1/2 points.

New deals that came to the market this session were well received, traders said.

The top attraction was Puerto Rico's $350 million Public Improvement Bonds of 1990.

The general obligation bonds were priced by a Goldman, Sachs & Co. group to yield from 6.15% in 1991 to 7.735% in 2020.

In secondary dealings, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issues of 2018 ended 1 1/2 points higher at 98 1/2, pushing the yield down to 7.31% from 7.44% at Tuesday's close.

Mortgage- & Asset-Backed Securities

The asset-backed securities market reached another milestone as nearly $26 billion of asset-backed issues have been sold so far this year, exceeding the $24 billion sold in all of 1989. This month alone, nearly $4 billion of asset-backed securities have been issued.

"We really expect asset-backed issuance to remain heavy," said Bruce Mahood, an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co.

That's because issuers, especially banks, continue to step up their sales while investors avidly seek the high-rated securities that offer yields generally 0.55 to 0.90 percentage point higher than short-term Treasurys.

With total issuance of $14 billion, sales of credit-card securities dominate this year's asset-backed market and the trend is expected to persist.

But issuance of securities backed by auto loans may slacken due to slow auto sales. Some automakers "aren't in the market because their auto-loan portfolio has not grown that spectacularly," said Michael Bassett, vice president at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates in Princeton, N.J.

There are just two forthcoming asset-backed deals now being mentioned repeatedly -- both credit-card issues. One is a $300 million offering by Valley National Bank via J.P. Morgan. Valley National is a unit of Phoenix, Ariz.-based Valley National Corp. The other issue is $500 million Signet Banking Corp. via Goldman, Sachs & Co. Signet is based in Richmond, Va.

Another name being bandied about is Baltimore-based MNC Financial Inc., which is seen selling about $500 million of credit-card securities later this quarter or early next quarter.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese bond market eroded further on continued talk of an imminent hike in the official discount rate and a weaker yen.

Two Japanese national newspapers reported yesterday that the Bank of Japan and the Ministry of Finance were negotiating a discount rate hike. The reports, which were denied by government officials, said the discount rate could be raised as much as one full point as early as the beginning of September. The rate, which is what the central bank charges member banks for loans, now is 5.25%.

Bank of Japan Governor Yasushi Mieno denied that the central bank and the Finance Ministry were negotiating another rate increase, but emphasized that the rise in the price of oil was a new factor that the central bank was carefully watching.

Despite Mr. Mieno's denial, many dealers and analysts believe there will be a discount rate increase by October.

"I think they will hike the discount rate," says a dealer at a major Japanese commercial bank. "They only have September to do it in. If they don't {do it in September} they will have missed the right time. . . . I think they are deciding the scheduling."

At the close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the benchmark No. 119 10-year government bond was quoted at a price of 82.18, down 0.55 point on the day, to yield 8.31%. (Foreign bond yields are quoted here in local terms and therefore differ somewhat from yields calculated for these markets by U.S. investment firms, which tend to use the semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets. Using that method, the yield on Japan's No. 119 bond ended at 7.61%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)

The discount rate was last raised in late March by a full percentage point to 5.25%.
