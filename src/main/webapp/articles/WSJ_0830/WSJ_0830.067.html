Letters to the Editor: The Imperial Press

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/30/90

Michael Gartner's spirited defense of what he alleges to be First Amendment rights of the press and his attack on the arguments of the Supreme Court (op-ed page, July 19) suggest why the media generate suspicion and hostility in some quarters. Tattered though it might be by some judicial decisions the cloak of the First Amendment should be the critical shield of a democracy's free press protecting it from chilling winds of self-appointed censors but not used to allow anyone to expose freely unsupported critical allegations just by flashing a press pass.

Claims of untouchable authority in voicing opinion by the columnist/editorialist/journalist/author, who may or may not have any hard evidence, leave the subject of an attack defenseless and, even worse, encourage the growth of sloppy and lazy reporting.

In exercising proper sensitivity to the very real threat to their role the media should not lose sight of their responsibility and the real fears of the citizenry. In a paraphrase of a remark about war, perhaps the freedom of the press is too important to leave to network-news executives.

Nathan Kingsley

Chevy Chase, Md.

---

The press has been able to affect careers and, for that matter, national policy with absolutely no regard for the consequences. The Spanish-American War, the internment of Japanese-American citizens and the McCarthy era are good examples of this.

If an opinion carries the same weight as a fact, then the consequences must be recognized. The court's decision to make people responsible for what they say in no way weakens the First Amendment.

Kurt Franseen

Walnut Creek, Calif.
