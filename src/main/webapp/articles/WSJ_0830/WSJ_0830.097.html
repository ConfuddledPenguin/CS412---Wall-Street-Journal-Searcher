Marketing & Media: Green Cross Issues Environment Seal For Some Products

  • Author: Randolph B. Smith
  • Date: 08/30/90

Green Cross Certification Co. said it issued the first independent environmental seal of approval indicating that products meet a specific standard, such as 100% recycled content. Green Cross said that as a first step, it certified the recycled content of more than 50 paper products, plastic bags and fire logs distributed nationally by six manufacturers. The products will have a label containing the Green Cross seal, and a specific explanation -- such as "made from at least 80% recycled plastics."

Green Cross, a division of Oakland, Calif.-based Scientific Certification Systems Inc., a food-and-product-testing concern, said the labeling program should promote recycling and help clear up confusion over environmental marketing claims. The label won't mislead consumers because it verifies only the accuracy of specific claims and avoids vague product endorsements, such as "environmentally friendly," said a spokeswoman.

However, consumer advocates and some companies caution that Green Cross could increase confusion by adding to the proliferation of competing and conflicting environmental labels. Another group, Green Seal Inc., for example, plans to issue seals of approval next year for products that reduce waste from manufacture through disposal. Some also warn that comparisons could be unfair if competing labels use different definitions for "recycled content" or other standards.

Green Cross said it issued certifications to 40% recycled content paper grocery sacks made by Portland, Ore.-based Willamette Industries Inc.; 100% recycled paper napkins, toilet paper and tissues made by Portland, Ore.-based Pope & Talbot Inc., LaPalma, Calif.-based Orchids Paper Products Co., and Irvine, Calif.-based Confab Cos.; 50% and 80% recycled plastic trash bags made by Peabody, Mass.-based Webster Industries Inc.; and 100% recycled fire logs made by Hillsboro, Ore.-based Green Fields Inc. Green Cross says it will expand the program to glass and additional plastic products.

Most of the companies are smaller producers hoping to increase sales and market shares by promoting an environmental advantage over major brands. "The certification helps establish credibility with consumers," says a spokeswoman for Pope & Talbot, a maker of private-label paper products.

Julie Vergeront, special assistant Minnesota attorney general, says Green Cross should help consumers make informed choices, provided that claims are clear and accurate. She said competition from niche products could force major brands to increase their recycled content.

Green Cross says it independently verifies claims and charges manufacturers only the actual cost, which ranges from $1,000 to $10,000. The program is currently being promoted in four West Coast grocery chains; Green Cross says this will soon expand to chains in the East and Midwest.
