Letters to the Editor: Ethics and Advocacy -- True Test of Character

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/30/90

I read with interest your July 19 "Lawyers Who Switch Sides Draw Ire With Big Checks." It raises questions about the relationship between attorney and client. Jerry Jackson, formerly with the National Wildlife Federation and now with Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom is undertaking the defense of what appears to be a state agency that has been accused of polluting the environment.

While it would be unethical of Mr. Jackson to comment on the particular case strategy absent a release from his client, he could hypothesize as to whether he would "defend" clients who are clearly wrong, or if he felt that his obligations under his public trust as an attorney would either compel him to decline the engagement or ensure that his obligation as an attorney was not in conflict with his client's objectives. This is especially true where the conduct alleged to be in error is continuing.

Barbara Brown, the women's rights attorney now representing corporations in sex-discrimination matters, might have been asked what she would do if, upon independent investigation by her, she found her corporate client had indeed done the dastardly deeds outlined in the complaint. Would she tell her client to settle and pay up, or defend to the bitter end if that is what the client desired?

There is a fine line that separates ethics and advocacy, and many times a substantial fee or career walks along that line with you.

Where one stands on those types of issues is the real test of character.

Howard J. Mellon

Attorney at Law

Columbus, Ohio
