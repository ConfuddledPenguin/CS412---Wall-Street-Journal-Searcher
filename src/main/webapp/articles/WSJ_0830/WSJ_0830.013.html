World Markets: Tokyo Share Prices Decline on Profit-Taking; London Stocks Ease, Amid Thin Global Trading

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 08/30/90

Tokyo stocks retreated on dealer-led selling Wednesday, as profits were taken after three sessions of advances for the Nikkei index. Traders said arbitrage selling added to the decline. In London, share prices closed slightly down, yet far above the day's lows, as sentiment drifted toward a stalemate on the London market's near-term prospects.

Tokyo's Nikkei index of 225 issues was off 815.97 points at 24894.79; on Tuesday, the index gained 569 points.

In Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index rose 34.68 points to close the morning session at 24929.47.

Wednesday's first section volume was estimated at 430 million shares, down from 504.2 million Tuesday.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all first section issues, which rose 42.92 points Tuesday, lost 36.09 to 1911.42 on Wednesday.

The second section index, which jumped 88.55 points Tuesday, eased 4.96 to 3687.38, on volume estimated at 9.8 million shares, up from 9.3 million shares a day earlier.

Press reports that the Bank of Japan was negotiating with the Finance Ministry on raising the official discount rate -- the interest rate on loans to banks -- were viewed as weakening the Japanese government bond market, but traders said this scarcely affected stocks. The Bank of Japan raised the rate 0.75 percentage point to 6% early Thursday, after denying on Wednesday that the talks had been held.

"The market's already discounted a 75-basis-point rise," a trader said before the rate increase was announced. A basis point is 1/100th of a percentage point.

The bulk of the stock market's declines stemmed from dealer selling, traders said, with profit-taking coming on shares that led the three-session rally from Friday. Declines caused by arbitrage selling in tiny chunks throughout the day "complemented" the dealer-led selling, a trader said. Pressure remained on futures indexes, keeping them at a discount to the cash indexes and allowing for limited arbitrage selling.

The Mideast confrontation kept trading thin and oriented for the short term, with only fringe players participating in bargain-hunting, observers said.

Meanwhile, the financial daily Nikkei Keizai Shimbun said Nomura Securities and leading commercial banks, including Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank, Bank of Tokyo and Mitsubishi Bank, were reportedly involved, perhaps unwittingly, in a 1.7 billion-yen ($11.8 million) Colombian drug money laundering scheme. The news hit the market in midafternoon, traders said, but stock prices appeared little affected, continuing lower at a steady pace. Observers also said the likelihood that the institutions will be implicated in the scandal is slim.

Shares that fell as a result of profit-taking included blue-chip leaders such as Sony, down 290 yen at 7,260 yen ($50.55).

TDK lost 140 to 6,060, Kyocera slid 170 to 7,530, Fanuc fell 200 to 5,800, and Hitachi dropped 40 to 1,350.

Nomura Securities fell 80 to 1,710 and Nikko Securities declined 50 to 900, but Yamaichi Securities gained 9 to 960. Daiwa Securities slipped 10 to 1,100.

Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank fell 100 to 1,900, Mitsubishi Bank lost 60 to 1,850 and Bank of Tokyo declined 40 to 1,150.

Nippon Mining was up 3 at 704, Sumitomo Mining rose 10 to 1,300, Dowa Mining fell 1 to 714, and Mitsui Mining rose 25 to 750. Teikoku Oil firmed 50 to 1,350, and Mitsubishi Oil rose 19 to 969.

In London, traders had expected Wall Street to drift downward, but an about-face in early New York trading helped the London market to its flat finish. A lack of genuine buying interest was again a main feature, and traders attributed price fluctuations to market makers covering their positions. "The market is in for a long stalemate," a trader said, adding that volume is unlikely to pick up soon.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index fell 0.4 point to close at 2125.7. The day's low was 2210.1, its high 2128.1. Volume was 297.3 million shares, slightly more than Tuesday's total of 281.6 million shares, which is the low for the year. The FT 30-share index finished up 1.5 points at 1658.9.

Several companies posted interim results that were in line with traders' expectations. Cadbury Schweppes, after reporting a 12% rise in pretax profit, closed at 325 pence ($6.32), up 7 pence.

Ladbrokes PLC, which also recorded a 12% rise in pretax profit, fell 5 to 274. Traders said sentiment was affected by a brokerage firm's downgrading of full-year earnings forecasts by #10 million ($19.4 million) to #340 million.

Pearson PLC, its first-half pretax profit up 15%, was down 4 pence at 690 pence.

W.H. Smith, a diversified bookseller that had a 1.8% decline in pretax profit for the year, fell 5 to 321.

Carlton Communications, downgraded by Barclays de Zoete Wedd on the belief that the strong pound continues to dent its U.S. earnings, fell 8 to 337.

Prices for many oil stocks were firmer, despite an Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries agreement to raise production. The Middle East conflict still remains a potentially bullish factor for crude prices, traders said. Enterprise Oil climbed to 651, up 12. Lasmo Oil shares rose 7 to 481.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Paris and Frankfurt, marginally higher in Stockholm, lower in Brussels, Milan and Madrid, and mixed in Zurich and Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed slightly higher. The Paris climb was a response to gains in the Paris bond market and was fueled by optimism about the oil price outlook, which also prompted Frankfurt's moderate advance. Trading was thin on most bourses.

Stock prices closed lower in Hong Kong, Taipei, Singapore, Wellington and Manila, and higher in Seoul, and were little changed in Sydney, as overall activity was generally thin. Hong Kong stocks slumped 2%. Taipei's main stock index nose-dived 5.57% on profit-taking, which also pushed prices down in Manila. Seoul's rise in prices was the third in a row.
