Litton Profit Falls; More Buy-Backs, Bond Offering Set

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 08/30/90

BEVERLY HILLS, Calif. -- Litton Industries Inc. announced a 2.5 million-share expansion of its buy-back program and a proposed $1 billion note offering, and posted a 2.3% decline in net income for its fourth quarter ended July 31. The diversified technology concern's profit fell to $45.5 million, or $1.91 a share, from $46.6 million, or $1.86 a share, in the year-earlier fiscal fourth quarter. The per-share profit rise reflected a 5% shrinkage in shares outstanding from Litton's continuing repurchase program.

Sales rose 6.8% to $1.41 billion from $1.32 billion.

The company said its Advanced Electronics segment has been hurt by the effects of a tightening defense budget. Operating profit in the segment fell 17%, largely reflecting a slowdown in military demand for spare parts and other equipment in Litton's fiscal fourth period.

Improved results in Litton's Resource Exploration Services business were largely offset by weakness in the Industrial Automation Systems business, which has been hurt by delays in the start of new engine programs by U.S. auto makers, the company said.

Litton's oil-services subsidiary "has experienced accelerating revenues and profits over the past two years in a recovering energy market," said Orion L. Hoch, Litton chairman, who characterized the unit as "an especially promising growth business for Litton." The company's shipbuilding segment also reported higher earnings and sales.

Litton said it expects to raise about $265 million from its proposed offering of $1 billion face amount at maturity of zero-coupon bonds, due 2010. The bonds, which are called liquid yield option notes, are expected to pay a yield of 6.75% to maturity and to be convertible, at Litton's option, into common stock or cash.

Litton said proceeds will be used to retire $440 million of floating-rate subordinated notes. The current rate is about 9.625%. "The transaction will reduce our interest expense and improve our cash flow," Mr. Hoch said. Merrill Lynch Capital Markets, a unit of Merrill Lynch & Co., is the underwriter.

Litton said the boost in its stock buy-back program brings to about three million the number of its shares the company now is authorized to purchase. As of July 31, Litton had 22.4 million common shares outstanding. Litton said it bought back nearly 2.2 million shares in the latest fiscal year ended July 31.

Investors reacted favorably to the announcements. Litton shares rose $1 to close at $75.75 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday.

"Investors are recognizing that Litton has the ability to grow earnings per share, even in a difficult economic environment," Howard Rubel, an analyst at C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc., said. "The earnings weren't bad, even though the company has some defense exposure." Mr. Rubel expects Litton to earn $7.50 a share in fiscal 1991.

For fiscal 1990, Litton's net edged up less than 1% to $178.8 million, or $7.26 a share, from $178.3 million, or $7.05 a share, a year earlier. Sales rose 2.7% to $5.16 billion from $5.02 billion.
