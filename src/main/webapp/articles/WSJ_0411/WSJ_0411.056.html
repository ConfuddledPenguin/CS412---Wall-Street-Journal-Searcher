Bell Atlantic Set To Pay Big Fine In Pennsylvania

  • Author: Julie Amparano Lopez
  • Date: 04/11/90

Bell Atlantic Corp. said its Bell of Pennsylvania phone unit agreed to pay $42 million to settle charges that it used "deceptive techniques" to sell customers more services than they wanted. The settlement puts an end to a two-year lawsuit by the Pennsylvania attorney general and consumer advocate offices, which accused the phone company of selling extra services -- such as call waiting, speed dialing and wire-maintenance plans -- without explaining prices or informing customers that the services were optional, as required by regulations.

"In many cases, packages of optional services were recommended without regard to the consumer's telephone habits or ability to pay," said Attorney General Ernie Preate Jr.

The settlement is one of the largest of its kind and comes on the heels of a U.S. Treasury Department action that suspends Bell Atlantic from receiving future business from the federal government. The department alleges that Bell Atlantic's Chesapeake & Potomac Telephone Co. unit misled the agency on a phone bid.

Under terms of the settlement, Bell of Pennsylvania agreed to pay $36.4 million in refunds to about 1.5 million residential customers, $5 million to a newly established telephone education fund and $450,000 to the state. Bell Atlantic said the settlement isn't expected to have a material impact on earnings.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, shares of Philadelphia-based Bell Atlantic closed at $94.125, down 25 cents.

According to the attorney general and consumer advocate offices, the illegal marketing practices stem from predetermined sales scripts that Bell of Pennsylvania customer representatives were required to use when attempting to sell optional services. In addition, the offices maintain that the representatives were subject to quotas or bonus plans, which were based on the number of optional services they sold.

Agents discovered the practice in 1987 when 32 telephone service inquiries were placed at various Bell of Pennsylvania centers across the state. Of those, there were 23 cases in which sales representatives assigned services without saying they were options.

William Harral, Bell of Pennsylvania's vice president of external and financial affairs, said the sales practices in question were in operation between 1985 and 1988 and have been since suspended. He said that on average, consumers will receive $12 for every optional feature they didn't want. In the refund proposal, the company expects to advertise extensively and will mail notices telling customers they may be eligible for refunds.

Bell of Pennsylvania is the second phone company to be charged with overaggressive marketing practices. In 1986, Pacific Telesis Group's Pacific Bell Telephone Co. unit in California was ordered to refund $35.6 million to customers who were sold optional services they didn't want. Regulators and consumer advocates blame the problem on market deregulation in phone services since the breakup of American Telephone & Telegraph Co. in 1984.
