Economy: Massachusetts Announces Measures To Help Firms Find Willing Lenders

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 04/11/90

BOSTON -- Massachusetts Gov. Michael Dukakis and private-sector leaders announced steps to help businesses find willing lenders, amid rising concern that a credit crunch may be adding to the state's economic woes. The measures are modest. The most concrete is a "credit hot line and clearing house" to match borrowers and lenders. But they add fuel to the debate over whether federal banking regulators, by forcing some of New England's leading banks to make massive provisions for troubled real-estate loans, may inadvertently have caused credit problems for a broad range of small and medium-sized businesses.

The joint news conference in the governor's Beacon Hill chambers also underscores a growing fear in business circles here that the early 1990s could resemble the troubled years before the so-called Massachusetts miracle of the 1980s. With corporate and sales tax revenues down sharply from a year ago, the state is expected to run a deficit of close to $1 billion for the fiscal year that ends June 30. And the state's unemployment rate, which for years was far below the national average, climbed to 5.4% in March compared with 5.2% for the country.

Mr. Dukakis continued to voice optimism about the state's economic health. But, he said, "There is something seriously wrong when businesses that are paying their loans on schedule live in fear of having their credit suddenly pulled."

"Nobody is suggesting our banks shouldn't be following good, solid" credit policies, he added. But banking regulators, "in their concern for the soundness of certain real-estate loans," may be causing banks to tighten up on lending to healthy businesses "that have nothing to do with real estate."

U.S. Comptroller of the Currency Robert Clarke, whose examiners have been the focus of much of the controversy, said in a speech last weekend that charges that his agency is responsible for New England's economic problems are unfounded. The examiners have only forced bankers to recognize already deteriorating market conditions, Mr. Clarke said.

Indeed, some bankers say that skittish bankers -- not Mr. Clarke's examiners -- may be the main culprits behind any unwarranted credit crunch. Alan Morse, vice chairman of the Massachusetts Bankers Association and chairman of U.S. Trust Co., Boston, said many banks in the state remain able and willing to make loans to credit-worthy companies.

"The fact that the regulators have come on so strong {in evaluating real-estate loans} has caused banks to re-evaluate all their underwriting standards," Mr. Morse said in an interview after the news conference. But, while some banks may be requiring more collateral or other added assurances, "that's a far cry from banks ringing up their customers one day and saying `Goodbye, the loan is canceled.' That's only happening where specific banks and bankers are overreacting."

Joseph Blair, executive director of Massachusetts Industrial Finance Agency, said that 25% of 200 companies surveyed recently by the quasi-public agency reported "serious problems" with their working capital. Some have had their lines of credit suspended or renegotiated as fixed-term, rather than revolving, loans. Others have had lower limits placed on the portion of receivables they can use as collateral.

Besides the hot-line service, Mr. Dukakis said further surveys will be conducted and a letter will be sent this week to all Massachusetts banks "urging them to do all they can to take up the credit slack." In addition, the Coalition of Northeast Governors, currently chaired by Mr. Dukakis, will meet with federal credit regulators in Washington "once all facts have been gathered," according to Mr. Dukakis's office.
