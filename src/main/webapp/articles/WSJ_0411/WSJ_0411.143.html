Who's News: Herman Miller Inc.'s Chief of Operations, President Quits Posts

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/11/90

ZEELAND, Mich. -- Herman Miller Inc. said its president and chief operating officer, Edward R. Simon Jr., is resigning, effective June 1, and that another top official is taking early retirement. No successor was named for either man. Mr. Simon's resignation came two years after the furniture maker appointed Richard H. Ruch as its chief executive officer, putting him above Mr. Simon, who has been president since 1986.

In a statement, Mr. Simon, 52 years old, said "the direction of the corporation is best served by reducing duplication and overlapping responsibilities in management. . . . I recognize that my resignation will provide {Ruch} with an opportunity for further alignment of the organization, eliminate confusion and provide a clearer direction for the company."

Mr. Simon wasn't available for comment and the company didn't offer any further details beyond the statement other than to say that its financial position is solid and neither the resignation nor the early retirement was forced.

Herman Miller also said that David L. Armstrong, its senior vice president for sales and marketing, is taking early retirement but didn't specify when. Mr. Armstrong is 54 and has been with Herman Miller 11 years, the company said.

The effect of the two officers leaving is difficult to assess. Mr. Simon worked for Herman Miller for some 20 years, during which he helped establish an internal data processing and information system for the company, led a financial turnaround at a subsidiary, and helped set up a global distribution network. Mr. Armstrong directed the introduction of several new products and served as executive vice president of Herman Miller Research Corp.

Herman Miller stock closed yesterday at $19 a share, down 25 cents in national over-the-counter trading.

In the 1970s and early 1980s, Herman Miller stock was a highflier, riding the crest of a rapidly growing market for office furniture. Then the market for office furniture slowed, causing a "shock" in Herman Miller's earnings and sales growth, said Wallace W. Epperson, an analyst with Wheat First Securities in Richmond, Va.

For the third quarter ended March 3, Herman Miller earned $11.5 million, or 47 cents a share, on sales of $200.7 million. In the year-earlier quarter, net income totaled $10.9 million, or 45 cents a share, on sales of $192.6 million.
