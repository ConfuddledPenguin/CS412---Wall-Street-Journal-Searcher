Tuesday's Markets: Treasury Bonds Hurt; Stocks Rise --- Gain Small as Markets Remain Lackluster; Dollar Moves Down

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 04/11/90

Treasury-bond prices were clobbered in a late sell-off, but stock prices edged higher and the dollar slipped in a continuation of the lackluster trading that has characterized financial markets during the past few days. The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 9.01 points to 2731.08. Volume continued on the light side, and for the second consecutive day the market was mixed, with more issues on the New York Stock Exchange falling than rising.

Long-term Treasury bonds ended moderately lower after being off through much of the day as traders awaited the results of the auction of $3.5 billion of 40-year Resolution Funding Corp. bonds. The dollar fell against the mark and the yen in thin trading.

Analysts said U.S. stock prices remain stable, despite continued volatility in the Tokyo stock market. After two days of gains, the Nikkei Index of 225 stocks plunged 773.25 points, or 2.5%, to 29624.68 Tuesday. Analysts are divided about the impact of Japan's stock-market problems on U.S. stock prices.

Although U.S. money managers continue to be wary of potential disappointments as the rush of first-quarter corporate-earnings reports begins to accelerate, they got a pleasant surprise yesterday when Motorola reported better-than-expected first-quarter results. The stock rose 4 1/4 to 68 1/2 on almost two million shares.

Bond traders said the $3.5 billion Refcorp auction went much worse than expected. The 40-year bonds were priced to yield 8.89%. Traders had hoped for better results after the first auction of Refcorp bonds in January sent prices of long-term Treasurys into a nose dive. Prior to the auction, traders said the Treasury had done a better job of preparing the market for yesterday's sale. Not only was the amount smaller -- $3.5 billion vs. $4 billion in January -- but the timing was more favorable, coming considerably in advance of the Treasury's quarterly refunding auction.

Currency dealers said the dollar's modest retreat in the face of restrained central-bank intervention was mostly because of the sluggish trading in the Passover and Easter holiday week, rather than any fundamental problems for the dollar. They said the intervention by central banks selling dollars for yen has been nothing more than symbolic support for the Bank of Japan, following the weekend meeting of the Group of Seven major industrialized nations.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose modestly in light trading. Big Board volume totaled 136 million shares. Decliners led gainers 740 to 723.

Bond prices slumped. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year issue fell more than half a point, or more than $5 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the issue rose to 8.59%.

The dollar was lower. In late afternoon New York trading the dollar was quoted at 1.6755 marks and 157.60 yen compared with 1.6935 marks and 158.23 yen Monday.
