Technology & Health: AT&T Shrinks Space for `Photonics,' Bringing Light-Based Technology Closer

  • Author: Laurence Hooper
  • Date: 04/11/90

Scientists at American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s Bell Laboratories have sharply shrunk the space taken up by the company's new "photonic" technology, bringing the light-based technology closer to uses in processing information. The development, to be announced today at an optical computing conference in Kobe, Japan, shows that AT&T continues to make important progress in its drive toward superfast data processing based on light rather than electricity.

AT&T said it will unveil an experimental device that combines the world's smallest lasers with a network of tiny lenses and mirrors -- all on a quartz glass disk about one inch in diameter and an eighth of an inch thick. Readily made, the new device can be used to connect conventional computer chips. Since it uses light, the resulting connections carry more information and are faster than ordinary electronic connections, said Tod Sizer, a Bell Labs scientist.

It has been known for some time that communicating with light makes sense over long distances -- that's why telephone companies use fiber-optic cables to transmit phone calls, for example.

But AT&T has faced skepticism about its claims that light is a practical way to communicate between computer chips at distances as short as a few millimeters. And the company has faced downright disbelief about its determination to develop an all-optical computer -- one that would use light not just to communicate, but to do calculations.

The new device should quiet such criticism, Mr. Sizer said. The device uses AT&T's advanced "microlaser" technology and a technique called diffractive optics, in which microscopic lines that act as lenses and mirrors are etched on quartz. This technology, which Mr. Sizer said is far more stable and manufacturable than previous methods, will likely play a large role in the company's optical computer.

Indeed, sources said AT&T is close to developing an all-light computer processor -- one with very limited abilities -- that fits on a piece of quartz the size of an index card.

That compares favorably with its last experimental set-up, unveiled at the end of January: Hailed as a breakthrough, it connected components on a lab bench using an unwieldy collection of precision-mounted lasers, lenses, prisms and mirrors.

The advance evokes visions of a futuristic computer that does its calculations by firing laser beams through a specially manufactured block of quartz -- an image that AT&T's optical computer guru, Alan Huang, has said won't be far from the truth.

For the moment, however, Mr. Sizer said the technology will likely be used to boost the performance of electronic computers, especially supercomputers.

AT&T also said it will demonstrate the nascent technology's first application to its core telecommunications business -- a device that uses light to switch voice, data and video signals -- next week in Atlanta.
