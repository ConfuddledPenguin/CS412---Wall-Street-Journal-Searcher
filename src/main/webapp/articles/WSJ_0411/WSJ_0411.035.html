Technology & Health: Study Uncovers New Evidence Linking Strain on Job and High Blood Pressure

  • Author: Ron Winslow
  • Date: 04/11/90

Health researchers have come up with new evidence that stress on the job may make you sick. Men who hold jobs with high demands over which they have little control are three times more likely to suffer from high blood pressure than men who don't, the researchers report. These workers are also more likely to sustain physical changes to the heart that over time could lead to heart disease.

The study is the first to link job strain and high blood pressure, according to Peter L. Schnall, the lead author, who is assistant professor of medicine at New York Hospital-Cornell Medical College, New York. While it doesn't prove that job stress causes high blood pressure, "it opens the door for trying to understand" the relationship between the two, and that could have profound consequences for the work force -- from the factory floor to the executive suite.

"The study suggests that what's critical is the amount of control you have when faced with a challenging situation," Dr. Schnall says. "If that's correct, one of the things we could do would be to enhance worker control and participation on the job."

Indeed, it suggests that redesigning jobs to relieve strain could improve the health of a company's work force and reduce health costs, among other things. Conversely, failing to mitigate stress's impact could provoke labor problems or increase workers compensation claims.

Further study is required, however, before such definitive conclusions can be made, Dr. Schnall says. The current study is published in today's Journal of the American Medical Association.

Previous studies have linked job strain to coronary heart disease, Dr. Schnall says. While the relationship between stress and high blood pressure has long been suspected, it hasn't been established, in part, the researchers speculate, because of difficulty in developing reliable means of determining blood pressure levels and evaluating stress.

The subjects of the study wore monitors during one 24-hour period to record blood pressure at 15-minute intervals during waking hours and every 30 minutes at night. Taking frequent readings during a person's normal activities yields more reliable measurements than traditional readings in a clinic, the researchers said.

In addition, the study defines job strain in terms of both psychological demands -- excessive work, conflicting demands, insufficient time to work -- and worker control and discretion over job decisions.

"Telling us you work very fast or hard wasn't associated with rise of blood pressure," Dr. Schnall says, nor was lack of job freedom. "Only those folks who told us both things were happening at the same time were having {blood pressure} problems," he says.

The researchers studied 215 men from 30 to 60 years old at seven worksites in New York City. They ranged from clerical workers to executives at a newspaper typography department, a federal health agency, a brokerage house, a private hospital, a sanitation collection and repair facility and a department store warehouse. They were selected from 2,556 potential subjects.

Of the 215, 87 had diastolic blood pressure -- the lower pressure, measured between contractions of the heart -- between 85 and 105 and were considered hypertensive. The other 128 men had normal blood pressure and were considered controls. Women weren't included in the study because their response to job strain could be different and the cost of recruiting enough subjects for reliable data would have been too high, Dr. Schnall said. The research team is doing additional studies that include women, he added.

Among other findings:

-- Men aged 30 to 40 with high blood pressure had significantly thicker heart muscle than those in the control group, an indication of the additional work required of the heart in hypertensive people. While none were at immediate risk of heart disease, Dr. Schnall said, thicker than normal heart muscle is considered an important predictor of heart disease and thus can lead to later trouble if blood pressure isn't controlled.

-- Risk of developing job-related hypertension increases with age. Men between 51 and 60 in high-strain jobs were 5.2 times more likely to have high blood pressure than those in the control group, while those between 41 and 50 were two and a half times as likely to have hypertension.

-- As expected, age, weight and regular consumption of alcohol were linked to high blood pressure. Researchers adjusted their data for these and several other factors in establishing the correlation between job stress and hypertension.
