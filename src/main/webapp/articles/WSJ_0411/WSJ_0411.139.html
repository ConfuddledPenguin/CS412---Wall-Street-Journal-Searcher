Heard on the Street: Ennis Business Forms' Hefty Profits Charm Some Analysts but Others Are Cool to Stock

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 04/11/90

NEW YORK -- Does Ennis Business Forms resemble a fast-moving sports car or an old jalopy that's running out of gas? It depends on whether you're looking at profit or at sales. In terms of profitability, few can hold a candle to the Ennis, Texas, company, which sells business forms mostly to small concerns in rural areas and towns. Its return on equity has been more than 30% in recent years. Per-share earnings have been growing at 20% a year.

But sales growth has been pokey. For the five years through 1989, sales grew only about 4% a year. In fiscal 1990, which ended Feb. 28, analysts estimate sales were about $140 million, compared with $108 million way back in fiscal 1984.

The slow sales growth doesn't bother enthusiasts, who have bid Ennis stock up to a market value of about $317 million, some 2.3 times sales and more than 15 times earnings. Fredric E. Russell, a Tulsa, Okla., money manager, has about 7% of his portfolio in Ennis, owning close to 1% of the company. He admires the company's skill in expanding earnings, thinks its large network of close to 30,000 dealers is underappreciated by Wall Street and expects Ennis to benefit from lower paper prices this year.

Another big Ennis fan is money manager Charles Allmon, who has owned the stock for years in managed accounts and now holds Ennis shares valued at around $4 million in Growth Stock Outlook Trust, a closed-end fund he manages. Ennis "is a classy operator. We've never sold a share," Mr. Allmon says. "They have a great balance sheet, great return on equity and they are buying back shares, which I always like to see. Ennis is going to grow in good times and bad."

Indeed, share buy-backs have been a support for Ennis's stock, which closed yesterday at 24. Early last year the board authorized Ennis to spend $5 million to buy back stock; when that was done, directors in December authorized $10 million more.

What about that slow sales growth? "With a return of 35% on equity, they don't have to grow that fast," Mr. Allmon says. "They're not a hot-shot outfit trying to grow at 15-20% a year. They keep on growing at a steady pace."

Others are less sanguine. Marty McDevitt, analyst with Cleary, Gull, Reiland, McDevitt & Collopy in Milwaukee, gives Ennis a weak buy ("accumulate") rating, but gives stronger buy ratings to competitors Wallace Business Forms, Standard Register and Duplex Products.

David Henwood, director of research at Raymond James & Associates, says Ennis has "the worst revenue growth in the industry." As a result, he says, "I don't think (future) earnings growth or dividend growth will be anywhere near as fast" as past growth was. "I certainly wouldn't recommend it."

In some ways, Ennis is like the Boston Celtics, who are often criticized as too old and too slow, but somehow manage to perform well at playoff time. The slow growth "does worry me," says one money manager who owns the stock. "But Ennis has had problems with slow sales growth for several years now, and it still keeps bringing earnings down to the bottom line."

Kenneth A. McCrady, Ennis's chairman and chief executive officer, says, "I assure you that no one is more concerned than I am about our recent inability to grow sales. We absolutely realize that we cannot continue to grow earnings at a faster rate than sales."

In the past few months, he says, Ennis has revised its incentive plans so that both dealers and plant managers are compensated partly based on sales growth. (Ennis's dealers are all independents, mainly printing shops, independent business-forms dealers and stationery stores.)

In another move to expand volume, Mr. McCrady says, Ennis will experiment with "advertising specialties" such as mugs, T-shirts and other items small businesses can use to publicize themselves. Ennis won't manufacture these, but will buy them from others and use its extensive distribution network to sell them.

"We have no illusions that we can grow sales 20% a year, as we've grown earnings-per-share over the past five years," Mr. McCrady says. And he doesn't think profit margins can be pushed much higher: They're already nearly 23% pretax and more than 14% after-tax. Accordingly, "It's unlikely that we will continue to experience that kind of earnings growth." Nevertheless, he says he thinks Ennis, partly by improving sales, can maintain its unusually high profitability, with a return on equity of 30% or more.
