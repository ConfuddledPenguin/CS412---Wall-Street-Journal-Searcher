Foreign Exchange: Dollar Falls Sharply Against the Mark, Loses Ground Against Other Currencies

  • Author: Caitlin Randall Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/11/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar tumbled yesterday, its sudden plunge sparked by an unconfirmed rumor late in the session that a New York bank's credit rating had been downgraded. While the market rumor was soon shrugged off, it prompted a hefty mark rally as the U.S. unit slipped below the key 1.6850-mark level, dealers said.

"Below that level, a lot of stop-loss orders drove it lower," said Graham Beale of HongKong & Shanghai Banking Corp. in New York, noting that much of the session's action was focused on cross-currency plays.

The dollar ended the session down nearly two pfennigs against the mark and lower against all other major currencies except the Canadian dollar.

In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.6755 marks, down from 1.6935 marks late Monday, and at 157.60 yen, down from 158.23 yen late Monday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6453, up from $1.6338 Monday.

The mark gained against the yen as well, ending the session at 94.08 yen compared to 93.47 yen late Monday in New York.

Traders said volume was thin ahead of the long Easter holiday weekend, spurring rumor-generated deals and increasing non-dollar transactions.

Early Wednesday in Tokyo, trading remained quiet. The dollar was quoted at 157.62 yen by midmorning, slightly up from the New York close but down from the Tuesday close of 158.15 yen in Tokyo.

Douglas Madison, a corporate trader with Bank of America, commented that cross-dealing has increasingly taken center stage as investors grapple with the dollar's next move.

"It seems easier to fine-tune cross-rate relationships than to try and figure out what the dollar may be up to longer term," he said, noting that trouble in Japan's financial markets has again brought crossdealing to the forefront of market activity as Japanese investor capital floods into other currencies.

Indeed, one trader in a Frankfurt-based bank said that in the past few days 70% of mark turnover has been against currencies other than the dollar.

The dollar began the New York day on a firm note, edging gingerly higher throughout most of the session.

Participants said the U.S. unit dipped slightly on an early news report that the Group of Seven industrial democracies issued a blunt warning to Japan that it must raise interest rates to stabilize its financial markets. But the unit quickly retraced its losses.

Traders said the news enhanced the market's perception that the G-7 isn't willing to help prop up the Japanese unit, despite vague promises at a meeting Saturday in Paris to guard against the "undesirable consequences" of a falling yen.

The story, initially reported by the Washington Post, said Japan's reluctance to again boost interest rates was one of several key factors stopping the G-7 from taking more aggressive steps to halt the yen's slide.

Later in the New York session, comments by West German Finance Minister Helmut Haussmann that he expects the mark to firm as a result of the planned economic and monetary unification of the two Germanys lent support to the currency, dealers said.

They noted that dollar-mark trading had been largely sidelined recently as both Germanys haggle over the appropriate exchange rate for a unified currency and the inflationary impact of monetary union.

With so many questions still unanswered about German monetary unification, investors are reticent to expand their mark portfolios, traders said.

And while Mr. Haussmann's comments provided nothing in the way of substantive details, the market reacted favorably to the news.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $375 an ounce, down $1. Estimated volume was a moderate 2.6 million ounces.

In Hong Kong, gold opened Wednesday at $374.95 an ounce.
