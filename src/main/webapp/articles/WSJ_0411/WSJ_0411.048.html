Safety Board Says United Air Accident Reflected Lapses by Boeing, UAL, FAA

  • Author: Laurie McGinley and Judith Valente
  • Date: 04/11/90

WASHINGTON -- Lapses by Boeing Co., United Airlines and the Federal Aviation Administration contributed to last year's catastrophic failure of a cargo door on a United Airlines Boeing 747 over the Pacific Ocean, federal investigators concluded. The National Transportation Safety Board took the unusual step of issuing its findings without having examined the key piece of evidence, the door itself, which is at the bottom of the ocean. Safety officials said they intend to retrieve the door sometime this summer and may amend their conclusions if new evidence arises.

Based on an analysis of the aircraft damage, the board concluded that the Feb. 24, 1989, accident was caused by the "sudden opening of the improperly latched" forward cargo door at 23,000 feet. The explosive decompression that resulted caused several feet of cabin floor to collapse, sending nine passengers to their deaths.

The board said deficiencies in Boeing's door design contributed to the accident. The cargo doors were supposed to be failsafe. But the board found that their locking mechanisms were susceptible to damage.

The current design of the door warning system also makes it possible for ground crews and pilots to receive false indications that the doors are properly latched, safety officials said.

The board cited United, a unit of Chicago-based UAL Corp. for a "lack of proper maintenance and inspection" of the door. Safety officials also said Boeing and the FAA failed to take "timely corrective actions" to fix known problems with the 747s' locking mechanisms.

Boeing knew the locks were susceptible to damage as far back as 1975. In March 1987, damaged and broken locks caused the forward cargo door of a Pan American World Airways Boeing 747 to open slightly in flight. Only two pull-in hooks kept the door from bursting off.

After that incident, the FAA, acting on a recommendation from Boeing, ordered airlines to strengthen the 747 locks. But the FAA gave carriers as long as two years to make the repairs. United hadn't yet repaired the door on the aircraft involved in the accident.

"There were several opportunities for the manufacturer, the airline and the FAA to have taken action during the service life of the Boeing 747 that would have prevented this accident," the board concluded.

The board also noted that United had experienced several problems with the door in the weeks prior to the accident. "We thought it should have prompted some sort of review" of the continuing problem, said Ronald L. Schleede, the chief accident investigator.

Moreover, a clerical error made by United in retyping an FAA order resulted in the airline's failure to conduct required inspections of the door, the board said. In addition, United's "trend analysis program" failed to flag the repeated problems with the electrical system for opening and closing the door, the board said.

The board's findings are at odds with a lengthy analysis submitted by Kevin Campbell, whose son died on Flight 811, that concluded an electrical malfunction caused the door to open accidently in flight. Mr. Campbell's findings were the subject of a Feb. 23 article in The Wall Street Journal. James Kolstad, board chairman, called an electrical malfunction "highly improbable."

Reached at his home in Wellington, New Zealand, Mr. Campbell said: "I cannot agree with the {board} theory of mishandled locks. It simply doesn't take into account all the evidence that was presented."

Some steps are already being taken to prevent a similar catastrophe. Boeing said it is in the process of redesigning the cargo door's warning system. The current warning system isn't tied into the locking mechanism, but is triggered by irregularities in the door's pressure venting system.

In the wake of Flight 811, the FAA ordered immediate repairs of the 747 lock sectors and more frequent inspections.

United said it has changed its own procedures since the accident. The airline said it still hopes the door can be examined.

The door could have been retrieved over a year ago by a private contractor for about $2 million. However, United, Boeing, the safety board and the FAA decided to delay the project when they learned the Navy could do the job for about $200,000.

Flight 811 was en route from Honolulu to Aukland, New Zealand, when the cargo door burst open. Despite damage to the fuselage, wing flaps and two of four engines, Capt. David Cronin was able to guide the aircraft back to Honolulu, saving the lives of 346 people.
