ITC Ruling to Prompt Inquiry Into Imports Of Norwegian Salmon

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/11/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. International Trade Commission ruled that imports of fresh and chilled salmon from Norway may be injuring domestic industry. The ITC's preliminary decisions, made in response to two trade complaints filed by U.S. producers and processors of Atlantic salmon, require the Commerce Department to continue its investigation of charges that these imports violate the U.S. anti-dumping act and the countervailing duty law. These imports total about $94 million a year.

The U.S. producers and processors in Northeastern states, who formed an ad hoc trade group to file the complaints, alleged that the Norwegian government has provided various subsidies to Atlantic salmon exporters. They also charged that Norwegian salmon has been sold at "unfairly low prices" to U.S. buyers.

If the Commerce Department and the ITC issue final rulings against the imports from Norway, the U.S. could assess anti-dumping duties or countervailing duties.

In another decision, the ITC concluded that imports of electro-mechanical digital counters from Brazil aren't harming U.S. makers of these devices, which are used for measuring motor-vehicle mileage and for other purposes. The decision dismisses the complaint involving these imports, which total about $1 million a year.
