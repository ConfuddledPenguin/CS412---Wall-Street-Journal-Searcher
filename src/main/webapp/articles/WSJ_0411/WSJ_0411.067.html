Credit Markets: Auction of 40-Year Bonds By U.S. Is Disappointing

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 04/11/90

NEW YORK -- The Resolution Funding Corp. flopped in its second attempt to sell 40-year bonds, a sign that investors aren't ready for bonds that have extra-long maturities. Refcorp, a new government agency created to rescue the nation's troubled thrift industry, auctioned $3.5 billion of the so-called bailout bonds yesterday.

The sale went badly despite an average yield of 8.89%, which was higher than some analysts had expected and 1/3 percentage point higher than the yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond.

Although the entire issue was sold, bidding was lackluster. One indication of weak demand was the "tail," or the difference between the average yield and the highest bid accepted at the auction. Because there wasn't enough demand at the average yield, the Treasury was forced to accept bids as high as 8.94% to complete the auction.

Shortly after the auction ended, dealers who bought the bonds were forced to cut prices in an attempt to attract buyers. As prices slid, the average yield on the 40-year bonds surged as high as 8.92%.

"This was a rerun of the last auction. It was pretty bad," said James Capra, a senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc. The previous auction, which took place in January, marked the first time the government sold 40-year bonds since the 1950s. That auction was considered a disaster.

The results suggest that "either there's not much demand for 40-year bonds or there's not much demand for Refcorp bonds," said John R. Burrus, manager of trading at CRT Government Securities Ltd.

Many dealers admitted yesterday that they may have overestimated the appeal of 40-year bonds and blamed themselves for the poor results.

The Treasury, however, said it was pleased with the auction because it believes the government saved money by issuing a 40-year bond rather than a traditional 30-year bond. But Treasury officials did admit that there are problems with the 40-year bond auctions.

"To the extent there doesn't seem to be the investor demand the Street had hoped for, clearly, that's a disappointment," said Michael Basham, deputy assistant secretary of federal finance at the Treasury. He said no decision has been made on whether to issue more 40-year bonds.

Lawrence N. Leuzzi, a managing director at S.G. Warburg Securities & Co., said some investors had concerns about Refcorp itself. Refcorp bonds don't actually carry a government guarantee, although interest payments are guaranteed by the Treasury and principal payments are secured by Treasury securities. Still, "the Refcorp name is suffering from uncertainty about how much the savings-and-loan bailout will cost," he said.

The disappointment in the auction spilled over onto the broader bond market, sending prices of most bonds moderately lower late in the day. The benchmark 30-year Treasury fell about 1/2 point, or about $5 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

Dealers had hoped to sell 40-year bonds to insurance companies, pension funds and other large institutional investors that need extra-long maturities to match future claims. Another group of buyers was expected to consist of professional traders eager for another vehicle to bet on swings in interest rates.

Zane Brown, executive vice president at Equitable Capital Management, said life insurance companies, commercial banks and thrifts aren't looking to extend their portfolios to include 40-year bonds. Instead, he said, most are looking to shorten the average maturity in their portfolios.

"Really, the only institutions that are left that might want a 40-year bond are pension funds, and they have plenty of opportunity to buy 30-year bonds," he said.

"I'm not sure that 40-years have a home" in the portfolio of most institutional investors, said Mr. Brown. He said the bonds are better when "stripped" into zero-coupon bonds that have shorter maturities. Those bonds, he said, could then be sold to individual investors for retirement and college-education accounts.

Strips are created when dealers separate the semiannual interest payments from the principal portion of the bond and sell the pieces separately. They also are separated into shorter maturity classes. The principal-only portion, called a zero-coupon bond, is sold at a deep discount from its face value and is considered one of the most volatile securities. Dealers said a 40-year zero might sell for as little as $35 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

Although half of the previous 40-year bond offering was stripped, dealers said the appetite for strips seemed light at yesterday's sale. That's partly because of the flat yield curve. Some analysts say yields on the 40-year bonds may not be high enough relative to shorter-term bonds to make the bonds appealing.

Treasury, Agency Securities

The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond ended at a price of 98 30/32 to yield 8.59%, compared with 99 11/32 to yield 8.55% on Monday.

Short-term rates were little changed.

Despite the poor showing at the Refcorp bond sale, analysts said they expect today's $7.5 billion sale of new seven-year Treasury notes to go smoothly.

In when-issued activity late yesterday, the new seven-year notes were trading at 8.65%, up from a yield of 8.62% Monday and 8.59% Friday.

Corporate Issues

News developments helped drive the junk bond market yesterday, slamming some high-yield issues and buoying others.

Among the notable losers of the day were junk bonds of Ames Department Stores Inc., which plunged nine points on a blizzard of news about the company's distressed financial condition.

By contrast, high-yield bonds of Owens-Illinois Inc. climbed by more than a point on news that insurance companies are liable for $960 million in damage and injury claims against Owens Illinois. The claims stem from the use of the company's asbestos-containing products.

High-yield traders said Ames Department Stores' 13% senior subordinated reset notes, due in 1999, traded at 46, down nine points from Monday's closing price. At one point during the session, bids for the issue fell as low as 42.

The Rocky Hill, Conn.-based retailer said it is estimating a loss of $228 million for fiscal 1990 and is negotiating with its banks for a new credit agreement.

"The operating loss for the year resulted primarily from substantially lower-than-anticipated sales and gross margins in the former Zayre stores," said Ames's chief executive officer, Peter Hollis.

In addition, the company said it has retained Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. and Wertheim, Schroder & Co. "to assist the company in connection with a restructuring which will include a capital infusion."

In the broader high-yield market, junk bonds or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings, finished unchanged.

In the investment-grade corporate debt market, activity was also muted with corporate issues ending the session unchanged.

Meanwhile, junk bonds of Interco Inc. were little changed on news that the furniture manufacturer reported a loss from continuing operations of $17.9 million for the fourth quarter. In the year-earlier period, the company had a loss from continuing operations of $42.2 million.

Market participants said the results were widely anticipated. Interco's 13 3/4% securities finished at 34 1/2, while its zero-coupon bonds were unchanged at 6 1/2.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended 4/32 to 7/32 point lower on a late sell-off following disappointing results of the Refcorp bond sale.

Market attention was focused on a scarcity of Federal National Mortgage Association 9 1/2% securities ahead of the regular April settlement Monday.

The shortage reflects the heavy use of such securities as collateral for real estate mortgage investment conduits that settle later this month. There are about $2.4 billion of these Fannie Mae Remics settling, and production of 9 1/2% securities was only about $1.9 billion last month.

In the pass-through market, Ginnie Mae 9% securities for April delivery ended at 95 27/32, down 5/32; 9 1/2% securities were at 98 4/32, down 7/32; 10% securities were at 100 17/32, down 4/32; and Fannie Mae 9 1/2% securities were at 98 14/32, down 4/32.

The Ginnie Mae 9% issue was yielding 9.73% to a 12-year average life assumption, as the spread above the Treasury 10-year note narrowed 0.03 percentage point to 1.11.

Municipals

Underwriting officials reported that the account is closed on $500 million of California various purpose general obligation bonds sold via competitive bid yesterday.

The California issue, won by a Bank of America NT&SA group, was the largest single issue ever sold by the state. The group's winning bid of 6.845% was about 0.02 percentage point lower than the only competing bid, offered by a Chase Securities Inc. group.

Looking ahead on the calendar, $119 million of Baltimore County, Md., consolidated public improvement bonds will be sold via competitive bid today.

In secondary market trading, municipal bonds ended unchanged to 1/8 point lower in listless dealings. The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was unchanged at 97 3/8 bid to yield 7.40%.

Foreign Bonds

Major foreign government bonds ended lower in quiet trading.

In Britain, analysts said investors sold bonds because of concerns that inflation is accelerating. Inflation fears were rekindled by Monday's news that manufactured goods prices in March were higher than expected.

Britain's benchmark 11 3/4% Treasury bond due 2003/2007 fell 15/32 to 98 1/32 to yield 12.01%, while the 12% notes due 1995 were down 3/8 to 96 22/32 to yield 12.93%.

Japanese government bonds continued to track the value of the yen, which ended lower again against the dollar. The benchmark 10-year bond fell 0.30 point to 86.51 to yield 7.25%.
