Law -- Legal Beat: Panel Delays Corporate-Fine Guidelines

  • Author: Stephen Wermeil and Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 04/11/90

The U.S. Sentencing Commission, unable to muster majority support, put off debate on proposed guidelines that federal judges would have to follow when imposing criminal fines and probation on companies. The delay is a temporary victory for business groups that mounted an all-out lobbying effort against tough guidelines.

Actions by the seven-member commission require at least four votes. As there are three vacancies now, all four commissioners must approve every step. Yesterday, one member, Judge George MacKinnon, effectively blocked action on corporate guidelines by refusing to vote for any proposals until the three vacancies are filled.

His announcement prompted the chairman, Judge William Wilkins, to defer action until next year. The guidelines must be reviewed by Congress.

Andrew Frey, a Washington lawyer who has monitored the commission's drafts for several corporations, said the delay "seems sensible" because more study of the proposals is needed. But John Coffee, a Columbia University Law School professor, said the decision was delayed because "there was no possibility of compromising" between factions favoring tougher and weaker corporate sanctions.

The three vacancies must be filled by President Bush in consultation with Senate Democrats, because the commission must be bipartisan. The White House has nominated Julie Carnes, an assistant U.S. attorney in Atlanta, for one seat.

Another slot is expected to go to Michael Gelacak, a Washington lawyer and former aide to Sen. Joseph Biden (D., Del.). The leading candidate for the third seat is U.S. Judge David Mazzone of Boston, who is supported by Sen. Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.), congressional aides said. The three must be confirmed by the Senate.

CITIZENS' GROUP wins appeal clarifying water-polluter penalties.

The 11th U.S. Court of Appeals in Atlanta overturned a federal judge's dismissal of a water pollution suit against Tyson Foods Inc. The decision clarifies when private citizens' groups can seek civil penalties from water polluters. The lower court had dismissed the case because the Springdale, Ark., poultry processor solved its problem after the suit was filed.

But the appeals court ruled that penalties against Tyson are appropriate because the company was violating federal pollution standards when the suit was filed. It told the lower court to calculate penalties against Tyson for polluting waters at its Blountsville, Ala., processing facility from the time it bought the plant in 1986 until it came into compliance in February 1988.

According to Eric Huber, an attorney for Atlantic States Legal Foundation Inc., an environmental group, the ruling strengthens such group's ability to bring private suits and seek civil penalties under the Clean Water Act. He said Tyson will face as much as $12.6 million in penalties plus attorneys' fees. The penalties will go to the U.S. Treasury.

"We will argue vigorously that nowhere close to the maximum penalty is in order," said H. Thomas Wells, an attorney for Tyson, noting the company acquired the plant from a company in bankruptcy proceedings and acted quickly to draft plans to correct the problem before the suit was filed.

Tyson's only alternative, he said, would have been to close the plant, leaving workers jobless while a new waste-water treatment system was installed.

LEGAL SERVICES CORP.'S departing chief eligible for "burlap parachute."

Terrance Wear, the aggressive, conservative president of the poverty-law grant agency, referred to the issue as a "private matter," adding that the LSC is "not a federal entity." He declined further comment on the sum, $37,750, which is equivalent to one-half his annual salary. The contract arrangement was first reported in the Los Angeles Daily Journal, a legal trade paper.

Mr. Wear last month announced his resignation from the private corporation, effective June 30. The LSC's $321 million budget is entirely funded by the federal government. Although Mr. Wear isn't a political appointee, he was hired by the agency's politically appointed board during the Reagan administration.

LSC board Chairman George Wittgraf said that Mr. Wear resigned at his suggestion. The chairman said he felt that the Bush-appointed board should choose its own administrator. He called the severance agreement a "burlap parachute" rather than a "golden parachute." But Alan Morrison, director of the Public Citizen Litigation Group, a public interest organization, said, "If I were a member of the board, I would have been extremely reluctant to provide any kind of severance benefits."

Severance agreements are standard at the LSC, but more generous than those at the similarly structured Corporation for Public Broadcasting, which receives more than 90% of its funding from the federal government.

According to general counsel Paul Symczak, severance available to CPB officers "doesn't anywhere approach 50%."
