Baker Hughes Enters U.S. Decree, and Now Can Buy Norton Unit

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/11/90

HOUSTON -- Baker Hughes Inc. and Norton Co. said they entered into a consent decree with the Justice Department that will allow Baker to complete the acquisition of Norton's Eastman Christensen unit, probably within a few days. The consent decree essentially is meant to head off antitrust questions.

Baker, an oil-field service company, agreed in December to buy Eastman, a maker of diamond drilling bits and other drilling equipment, for an indicated value of $600 million.

As part of the consent order, Baker will be required to dispose of the diamond drill bit business of its Hughes Tool Co. unit by Aug. 6. If it isn't sold by then, that business will be placed in the hands of a trustee for disposition. The Justice Department had maintained that Baker and Norton were competitors in the diamond drill business.

In Washington, the Justice Department's antitrust chief, James Rill, said that as originally proposed the acquisition would prove "anticompetitive" in the U.S. market for each of three types of diamond drill bits. "The divestiture will ensure that the merger . . . will not raise the cost of drilling for oil and gas in the U.S.," Mr. Rill said.

The transaction calls for Baker to pay $550 million in cash, assume $8 million in debt and issue warrants to Norton for as many as eight million Baker shares at $36.75 each. Norton said it intends to use proceeds to buy back as much as 15% of its common shares, pursue acquisitions and repay debt. Norton makes abrasives, ceramics, plastics and chemical-process products.
