Rhone-Poulenc Unit Suspends U.S. Sales Of Aldicarb Pesticide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/11/90

MONMOUTH JUNCTION, N.J. -- Rhone-Poulenc Inc. said it will suspend the sale of its aldicarb pesticide in the U.S. for use on potatoes because recent tests showed chemical residues on a small number of potato samples. Rhone-Poulenc found the residues while doing a survey for the the Environmental Protection Agency, whose chief interest was possible ground-water contamination. An EPA spokesman said that the residues were found on 10 of 2,600 potatoes tested, and that all 10 came from one of 26 fields tested.

Rhone-Poulenc said it found residues at "higher levels than the company would expect from proper use" of aldicarb. The company said farmers are beginning to plant this year's potato crop, and it wanted to spare them "a needless and damaging `food scare' of the type we've seen in the past."

Aldicarb, sold by Rhone-Poulenc under the tradename Temik, doesn't have any known long-term health effects. The company said that during nearly 17 years of use of the product, it has never had a report of anyone's becoming sick from eating treated potatoes.

The EPA spokesman said residues at the highest levels found in the survey could conceivably cause transient flu-like symptoms but shouldn't create any alarm about eating potatoes. Although death due to direct ingestion of large amounts aldicarb is possible, he emphasized that no deaths have ever been associated with any treated foods.

A Rhone-Poulenc spokesman said the company is still talking with regulatory authorities in other large potato-producing countries about how to proceed there. In the U.S., he said, the company won't sell aldicarb for use on potatoes throughout this planting season and possibly throughout next year's as research into the residue levels continues. The company will continue selling aldicarb, which kills insects, mites and nematodes, for uses other than those on potatoes.

Rhone-Poulenc, the U.S. chemicals unit of Rhone-Poulenc S.A., of Paris, didn't disclose its sales of aldicarb, but the spokesman said cotton growers are a more important market for the pesticide than potato growers. Among potato growers, he said, the product has about 15% of the market.
