JAPAN'S UNION MEMBERS

Union members in Japan increased this year for the first time in eight years, but their share in the total labor force dropped to the lowest level since the end of the war, according to a survey released by the Labor Ministry.

Ministry officials said union membership grew 37,000 at the end of last June from a year earlier to 12.27 million, putting an end to a downtrend that started in 1983. However, union members accounted for 25.2% of the country's entire labor force, down 0.7 percentage point from the previous year and the lowest level in the postwar era, officials noted.

Ministry officials ascribed the decline to an expanding population of workers as a result of Japan's sustained economic growth. The long-lasting economic expansion, now in its 49th month, has brought more women and part-time workers into the labor market, they said. This influx grew faster than the number of workers joining labor unions, they explained.

Ministry officials said, at the same time, that the number of labor unions throughout the country dropped 403 to 72,202 at the end of June.

OECD'S JOBLESS OUTLOOK

The unemployment rate among the 24 members of the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development held steady in October from September at 6.1% of the work force, the OECD said. But the group of industrialized nations said initial data for November suggest that "the recent period of stability has come to an end: The unemployment rate rose in all six countries for which data are available."

EXPANDING BARAJAS AIRPORT

Madrid's Barajas International Airport is to triple its terminal space and add three runways under a 200-billion-peseta ($2.03 billion) expansion plan. Spain's Transport Minister Jose Barrionuevo said the three-phase plan, expected to end in 2030, includes a major increase in parking space and access to downtown Madrid by high-speed train and superhighway. The airport is to accommodate up to 80 million passengers a year; it now handles 16 million, sixth among European airports.

MAZDA PENALIZES EXECUTIVES

Japan's Mazda Motor Corp. will cut the salaries of Chairman Kenichi Yamamoto and 17 other executives by 5% to 10% for 1991's first three months. The cuts were announced one day after the Transport Ministry warned Mazda to pay stricter attention to the safety of its cars. The ministry earlier investigated allegations that Mazda has been replacing defective parts during routine maintenance without recalling the cars or notifying the government. After the warning, Madza notified the ministry that it would recall 3,498 Luce model passenger cars produced between Aug. 8, 1986, and Jan. 31, 1987, because of switch defects. None of the cars went to the U.S.

BRAZIL STUDIES TAX CUTS ON AUTOS

Brazil is studying tax cuts and legal action to force the nation's foreign-owned auto makers to curb "abusive prices," the Economy Ministry said. The move would force the companies "to compete with less expensive and better-made cars," a ministry spokesman said. Imports of foreign cars now are subject to a 40% tax. "We are studying the possibility of lowering this tax or eliminating it altogether," the spokesman said.

RECORD EXPENSE SPENDING

Expense-account spending by Japanese corporate executives totaled a record $36.53 billion between February 1989 and January 1990, according to the annual report of the National Tax Administration Agency. The agency said the figure was 9.4% higher than the previous year's, which had been the record. Company expense-account spending is tax-deductible in Japan.

CHANGING ALBANIA

Albania's state bank has joined a Swiss company to form the first bank with foreign capital in the communist country, the state news agency ATA said.

ATA said the bank would be known as Iliria Bank and would start trading in January in Tirana. It named the partners as the Albanian State Bank for Relations with Foreign Countries and a Swiss company, Iliria Holding S.A. It didn't state their respective stakes or say how much capital was involved.

Earlier this year, Albania's President Ramiz Alia lifted measures imposed by his predecessor, the late Stalinist leader Enver Hoxha, that made it a crime to seek foreign investment or loans for the long-isolated Balkan state.

ATA said the new bank would help attract foreign capital for construction projects in such areas of the economy as light industry, foodstuffs, building materials, electromechanics and tourism.
