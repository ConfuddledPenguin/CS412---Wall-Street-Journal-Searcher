Central California's Citrus Growers Seek Disaster Relief After Cold Wave

  • Author: Chip Johnson
  • Date: 12/27/90

Citrus growers and county agricultural officials in central California's tri-county citrus belt urged county officials to declare the region a disaster area after a week of record low temperatures ravaged the navel orange crop. The declaration of a local disaster is the first step toward seeking state and federal disaster relief.

Growers also encouraged state agricultural officials to lobby the state legislature to place a moratorium on the sale or shipment of all San Joaquin Valley citrus produce this year, similar to past practices in Texas and Florida, in order to protect the reputation of citrus fruit produced in the area.

More than 65 growers, shippers and county officials gathered in the County Agricultural Building to share horror stories about what the coldest week since 1913 had done to the 125 square miles of prime citrus groves at the base of the Sierra mountain range.

Carl A. "Skip" Pescosolido, 54 years old, a co-owner of Sequoia Orange Co., in Exeter, Calif., which farms 5,000 acres of groves in the region, estimated the loss to the company at $16 million. By the end of this week about 310, or 60%, of the 520 employees will be given work furloughs, he said. Next week there will be more layoffs and job reassignments, he added.

"Somebody said it must be the greenhouse effect," said Mr. Pescolido. "If anyone mentions the word greenhouse to me again they're going to get a crooked nose."

A spokesman for the California Citrus Mutual Association, which represents about 800 growers in Fresno, Kern and Tulare counties, said regional citrus crop damage estimates are between $300 and $400 million.

In Tulare County, the largest citrus-growing county in the region, county agricultural officials said about 75% of the navel orange crop was left on the trees and preliminary estimates place losses at between $200 and $250 million.

At the meeting, county officials and independent and corporate growers planned to establish guidelines for inspection of freeze-damaged fruit and to try to assess the widespread damage to citrus crops.

Fresno County agricultural officials said that 90% of the county's annual $70 million citrus crop was still on the trees when temperatures dipped to the mid-teens last week. Across the region, growers attempted to flood groves, warm roots with smudge pots and blow warm air in an attempt to save crops and young trees, which also have reportedly been damaged by the sub-freezing temperatures.

"Old-timers haven't seen temperatures like this since a similar cold spell in 1937, and baby-boom generation growers have never experienced it," said Joel Nelson, the president of the growers association, which is based in Visalia, Calif.

Across the state, lemon, avocado, and valencia orange crops have been damaged. Ventura County agricultural officials place losses from lemon and avocado crops at $100 million, and Los Angeles County, though not as hard hit by the bruising temperatures, places losses at $3 million.
