Politics & Policy: U.S. Attempts to Withdraw Americans From Pro-Iraqi Nations Before Jan. 15

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 12/27/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. is attempting to withdraw as many Americans as possible from four pro-Iraq nations in the Mideast and Africa. American officials fear that if war starts between the U.S. and Iraq, U.S. citizens in those countries could be vulnerable to both Iraq-inspired terrorist attacks and to violence and harassment from local governments or pro-Iraqi mobs.

Separately, a senior U.S. military intelligence officer in Saudi Arabia predicted that Iraq's leader would defy the United Nations demand that he pull his army out of Kuwait by Jan. 15. The U.N. has sanctioned the use of force to oust Iraqi troops after that date. Air Force Lt. Col. Tom Coury, appearing at a military news conference in Riyadh, said, "We presently assess that Saddam Hussein intends to remain in Kuwait for the long haul."

Yesterday, the State Department ordered all "nonessential" U.S. government employees and all dependents of government employees, to leave Jordan and Sudan. It also urged all other Americans to leave the two countries, whose governments sympathize with Iraq, "well before" Jan. 15.

The U.S. already had been evacuating U.S. employees and urging private Americans to leave Yemen, a third ally of Iraq's. Also, the department has authorized -- but not ordered -- the departure of government dependents and nonessential government workers from Mauritania.

The department also has encouraged the departure of dependents of U.S. government employees from Arab countries that are friendly to the U.S. but close to the potential battlefront. They include Bahrain, the United Arab Emirates, Qatar and the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia.

In Saudi Arabia, Lt. Col. Coury said at the first in a planned series of military news conferences there that Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein is spending so much of his scarce resources digging into Kuwait that a peaceful withdrawal is unlikely.

"One of the things you look at as an intelligence officer is how a man is spending his resources," Lt. Col. Coury told reporters. "You're looking at an economy that's embargoed right now, and this man is spending tremendous resources to build defenses in Kuwait and on his western flank," he said. "And that suggests to me that he has all intentions of remaining there."

Lt. Col. Coury also said a chemical-weapons attack on U.S. and allied forces is "likely" if war breaks out, that Iraq has installed Silkworm missiles to strike at U.S. ships, and that it is "very realistic" to expect Iraq to try to attack Israel if war starts.

Meanwhile, President Bush is scheduled to return from the presidential retreat at Camp David today for staff consultations on the Persian Gulf as well as other routine business, the White House said. Aides wouldn't identify with whom the president planned to meet; they said he is expected to resume his vacation in midafternoon.
