REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial) -- Asides: Second Thoughts

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/27/90

Radio Liberty's Soviet/East European Report recently noted a curious exchange between a leading Soviet philosopher, Andranik Migranyan, and the eminent British philosopher Karl Popper. About two years ago, some 100 Soviet philosophers boycotted a speech to the World Congress of Philosophy by Mr. Popper, author of "The Open Society and its Enemies." A year before that speech, Mr. Migranyan made one of the earliest calls for creating a genuine civil society in the Soviet Union. Now, he told Mr. Popper in an interview, the country needs an "iron hand" because "it's impossible to pass over from totalitarianism to democracy at a moment's notice." No doubt some would sympathize with the notion that the Soviet government has to regain control of its disorderly society. However, it's difficult to understand Mr. Migranyan's logic. The Soviet Union is disorderly because the bureaucracy's thousands of iron hands won't let go of what they once held. Giving them more "control" will likely choke the newborn life out of Soviet civil society.
