New York State Proceeds With Plan To Lay Off Workers

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/27/90

ALBANY, N.Y. (AP) -- More than 600 state employees were to put in their last day of work yesterday as the Cuomo administration went ahead with layoff plans to help eliminate an estimated $1 billion deficit in the state's $50 billion budget. Included among the 619 employees to be fired were about 300 prison guards and 300 other prison workers.

James Flateau, Gov. Mario Cuomo's spokesman on criminal justice issues, said yesterday that no problems had been reported at the prisons because of layoffs.

On Christmas Eve, the leadership of Council 82, the prison guards' union, met with Corrections Commissioner Thomas Coughlin to discuss the layoffs amid speculation that guards were considering a strike or some other protest.

Union President Thomas Kennedy said no strike had been called.

While he was reluctant to talk about union sentiment regarding some form of protest to the guard layoffs and other cost-cutting actions, he warned that anxiety among guards was running high. He called for intervention by Mr. Cuomo.

"The last time we saw this type of dissatisfaction. . . ., there was a strike in 1979," Mr. Kennedy said.

At that time, then-Gov. Hugh Carey called the National Guard to run the prisons during a 16-day strike by prison guards upset over negotiations for a new contract.

"We've done everything possible to avert these layoffs, but nobody is listening at the (state) Capitol," Mr. Kennedy said after the Monday meeting.

Mr. Flateau said yesterday that Mr. Cuomo has been involved in layoff discussions. "It's disingenuous to say the governor has not been involved," Mr. Flateau said.

Other state workers putting in their last day yesterday included 12 employees at the state Division of Parole and seven Commission on Correction employees.

In all, 2,000 state workers are to be laid off by the end of the fiscal year on March 31. Mr. Cuomo has promised to eliminate 4,000 additional jobs through attrition and early retirement incentives. Next year, the governor has said, he may have to eliminate 12,000 more jobs.

The state has more than 200,000 employees.

Twenty-three more layoffs will take effect Jan. 2. Those cuts are at the Division of Criminal Justice Services, the Division of Military and Naval Affairs and the Department of State, said a spokeswoman for the Governor's Office of Employee Relations.
