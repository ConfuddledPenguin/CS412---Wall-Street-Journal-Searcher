By 2001, Women to Earn More Ph.D.s Than Men

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/27/90

WASHINGTON -- Women will be earning more doctorate degrees than men by the year 2001, according to an Education Department study. The study, which projects several educational trends into the 21st century, says that the number of men who receive Ph.D.s is declining, while the number of women who receive them is increasing.

The number of male Ph.D. recipients dwindled to 22,627 in 1989, from 26,267 in 1976, while the ranks of their female counterparts grew to 12,752, from 7,797 during that period.

The trend suggests that women, who currently earn roughly 65% of what men do, will soon have tools to close the income gap. But it may also reflect the fact that more men are passing up academic degrees in humanities or the sciences to opt for more financially lucrative degrees in business, medicine or law, according to Christopher Cross, assistant secretary for education research and improvement. The number of men receiving professional degrees today is nearly double that of women, although the gap has narrowed considerably in recent years.

Women already receive more master's degrees than men, a trend that began in 1986 and is attributable partly to the growing number of women attending graduate school part-time while raising a family, Mr. Cross said. Women also outnumber men in overall higher-education enrollment by about one million, another disparity that the Education Department projects will grow.
