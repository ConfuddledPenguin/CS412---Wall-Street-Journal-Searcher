Your Money Matters: When Bond-Buyers Call, It Pays to Stall

  • Author: Tom Herman
  • Date: 12/27/90

Investors are often cautioned to beware of strangers trying to sell them securities over the telephone. But the same warning goes for investors who receive phone calls from dealers offering to buy bonds they already own.

The dealer may be trying to buy the bonds at cut-rate prices to resell directly to the issuer at higher prices.

Even if the dealer is offering what may initially sound like an attractive price, several bond-market specialists advise against leaping at the offer. With a little bit of detective work and comparison shopping, savvy investors may be able to do even better.

Bond issuers often need to buy back some of their bonds to fulfill what is known as a "sinking-fund" requirement. This refers to a provision in the fine print of many municipal and corporate bonds requiring or permitting issuers to buy back some of their bonds at specified intervals before the final maturity date.

The risk for unwary investors was brought into sharper focus by recent charges that a former state-agency official in Kentucky and his father profited from "misuse of confidential information" relating to a sinking-fund program. Several bond specialists say the case involves what are probably the first indictments charging insider-trading in municipal bonds.

The two men, Noble Donald Morse II, who was secretary-treasurer of Kentucky's Infrastructure Authority, and his father, Donald Morse, are accused of making about $7,000 by buying bonds they knew could be resold to the authority at higher prices. They were indicted by a grand jury under a state law in late October.

The Morses have pleaded not guilty. J. Guthrie True, a lawyer representing them, says a motion has been filed to dismiss the charges. He says the Kentucky statute is "unconstitutionally vague, because it fails to define what constitutes confidential information."

Typically, issuers subject to mandatory sinking-fund requirements have a choice of ways to meet them. Often, they can redeem a certain amount of bonds at a specified price -- such as "par," or the bond's face value -- with holders drawn by lot. Or they can try to buy enough of their bonds in the resale market, at lower prices, to fulfill the requirement.

Several municipalities and companies hire specialists to contact bondholders directly. The firm writes letters summarizing the sinking-fund requirement and asks holders if they would be interested in selling, and, if so, to specify a price. After the bids come in, the lowest prices are accepted, usually up to the limit of the sinking-fund requirement -- although sometimes the agency can buy even more than that.

For example, Fiduciary Communications Co. of New York says it is working on a sinking-fund retirement program for several issues of New Jersey's Housing Finance Agency, which subsequently merged with another agency to become the New Jersey Housing and Mortgage Finance Agency. About 2,000 bondholders soon will receive letters asking if they want to sell, and at what price. The letters also will give an 800 number for holders to call.

Fiduciary recently handled a sinking-fund retirement program for Kansas City, Mo., special facility airport revenue bonds issued to help finance a facility for Trans World Airlines. It received 211 tender offers, of which 167 came from individuals, says Amy Winchell, a vice president of Fiduciary.

Large brokerage houses often get involved with sinking-fund redemptions, too. Usually they act independently, trying to make a profit for themselves by contacting large institutions and individual holders to buy back bonds to resell to issuers.

What should investors do if they get a call from someone offering to buy their bonds? First, several traders say, ask the callers why they want the bonds so badly and whether it's because of a sinking-fund requirement.

Frank Scaraggi, an executive vice president at Lehman Brothers, advises: "Ask whoever is giving you the bid, `Are you buying these for an active sinker,' that is, for an issuer that is satisfying a sinking-fund requirement. Then ask for more information, and any reputable broker should give you more, including what they think the sinker will pay."

Even if the offer still sounds good, don't stop there, Mr. Scaraggi and many others suggest. Investors should check the fairness of the price by calling at least two other firms they trust. Prices often vary widely from one firm to another, especially on municipal bonds -- and especially for trades of $25,000 or less.

Ask for any research reports the firms have on the issuer, and whether the issuer's credit ratings have changed since the bonds were issued; these factors can greatly affect the price. Also check the prospectus or official statement for details of the bond's sinking-fund requirements.

In some cases, large bondholders may be able to get more information by contacting the issuer directly. Some treasurers say they are willing to explain the intricacies of their sinking-fund requirements to bondholders -- and even to steer them to major market-makers in those bonds.

"The last thing we want to have is a situation where someone thinks there's no secondary market" for the bonds, says the treasurer of one municipal agency. On rare occasions, he says, the agency might even agree to buy back bonds directly from investors holding large blocks, typically $100,000 or more.

Investors who don't want to worry about the intricacies of sinkers can buy shares of mutual funds and let fund managers worry about it. Or they can stick to buying U.S. Treasury bills, notes and bonds, which don't have sinking funds.
