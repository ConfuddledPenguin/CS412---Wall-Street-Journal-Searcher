Heard on the Street: Carnival Cruise Lines Faces Choppy Waters In Some Areas, but Its Boosters Stay Confident

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 12/27/90

NEW YORK -- If anything in the 1980s caught on faster than Nintendo, Ninja Turtles or junk bonds, it was the recreational cruise. In 1981, 41,000 berths were afloat in North America. The number now is 84,000, with 18,000 more due in the next two years. The chief exponent and beneficiary of this boom has been Carnival Cruise Lines, the biggest North American operator. The question now is to what extent Carnival may suffer from an overhang of berths, which is hitting the market just as higher air fares are raising the cost of air and sea packages, the world is awash in rival amusements such as theme parks and casinos, and the economy seems headed into a recessionary dive.

Miami-based Carnival is, according to travel agents and tour operators, the best cruise operator going. And with the exception of tiny Regency Cruises, Carnival is the only publicly traded U.S. operator. Short of buying or selling an ocean liner, Carnival's stock is the only major way for investors to play the cruise market.

That market appears soft. Travel agents say bookings are holding up, but prices are not. "There is an awful lot of discounting going on," says Jay Lewis, president of Market Scope, a market research firm. "Everybody is feeling it," a tour operator adds.

Howard Frank, Carnival's senior vice president for finance, says "we really haven't seen any softness. Our bookings are holding strong." However, Mr. Frank's tone is that of a man who has, at least, glimpsed the potential for softness. Carnival's brochure prices for 1991 are being held flat. Why? "Uncertainty about what this softer economy will do for us," Mr. Frank says. "If people keep telling you that things are soft, your thinking becomes colored."

On its two oldest ships (out of 15 mostly newer ones) Carnival is already promoting or discounting more heavily than in past years, Mr. Frank says. And Carnival's Bahamas hotel and casino, which according to Mr. Frank "seems more prone to recession," should post losses of $20 million to $22 million for fiscal 1990, ended Nov. 30.

Air fares paid by Carnival will rise an estimated 10% in 1991. Carnival hedges most, but not all, of its fuel costs. And its labor costs, in 1991 as in most years, are apt to rise a bit. The coupling of higher costs with stable prices, Mr. Frank notes, will depress profit margins on cruises, perhaps by a couple of percentage points. The engine of Carnival's growth, in the immediate future, will be more beds. The 2,044-berth Ecstasy will debut in June; three other ships are in construction.

Thus, Carnival will need to continue growing even as the general economy, and in particular, the market for leisure dollars, contracts. Mr. Frank says it will do so. The company has an advantage in name-recognition with travel agents and tourists. "We have a huge reservations center; we make it easy for agents to book on Carnival," he says.

Wall Street agrees. Bear Stearns, Salomon Brothers and Shearson Lehman are bullish on Carnival shares. At 14 1/2, they trade at 9 1/2 times earnings based on Carnival's estimate of $1.50 to $1.55 a share for the recently ended fiscal year. This, to Shearson's Caroline Levy, signals that the stock is "more than discounting the negatives of fuel prices and recession."

But Wall Street in some respects seems to believe that Carnival will sail through the recession essentially unscathed. Steven Eisenberg of Bear Stearns says, "The public is demanding price and value and Carnival's reputation is built on that." He envisions net income rising 13% in fiscal 1991. Salomon, despite the soft market, sees Carnival's cruise revenue growing in 1991 by 15% and profit margins on cruises dropping by only a half-percentage point.

At 14 1/2, Carnival is trading at about twice its book value and it has $1.27 billion in debt, equal to a hefty 57% of capital. Carnival's strengths are real. It may suffer a recession after other cruise lines, and to a lesser extent than others. But given the supply of berths, it seems unlikely that if discounting becomes fierce Carnival won't be hurt.

The secret of its heady growth has been that Ted Arison, Carnival's founder and controlling stockholder, recognized that cruises, once the preserve of the idle rich, could be popularized. Although some of its cruises are pricey, Carnival's passengers, in the main, are what Bear Stearns' Mr. Eisenberg refers to as "Mr. and Mrs. Middle America." If anyone is apt to feel the recession, they will. The most beguiling of bullish arguments, stated by Salomon, is that more supply will lead to more demand. As Salomon puts it, "the fanfare associated with major ship launchings {may} help inflate demand, and to some extent offset a weak economy." But after 10 years of new ship launchings, this may be a slender reed on which to invest.
