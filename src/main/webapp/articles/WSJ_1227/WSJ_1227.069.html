Foreign Exchange: Dollar Finishes Mixed in a Thin Market; Traders Say Greenback Still Has Support

  • Author: Audrey Choi Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 12/27/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar ended mixed in a calm, holiday-thinned market that showed only a mild interest in position-squaring. With Tokyo and New York the only major trading centers active the day after Christmas, there was "just not enough depth to be able to explain specific moves," said David Jaques, senior corporate trader at Barclays Bank in New York.

There was moderate interest yesterday in buying back marks that were sold off heavily following the resignation of Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze late last week. The German currency is believed to be the most vulnerable among major currencies to instability in the Soviet Union.

Nevertheless, the greenback still has firm support, traders said, because of the market's apprehension about the Mideast stalemate. The dollar, which is perceived as a safe investment, traditionally attracts buyers in times of uncertainty.

A statement issued yesterday by Iraqi President Saddam Hussein that the deposed Kuwaiti rulers never again will rule in Kuwait was interpreted as confirmation that he has no intention of complying with the U.N. Security Council's Nov. 29 resolution ordering an Iraqi withdrawal from Kuwait by Jan. 15, said Thomas Palladino, a trader at Oesterreichische Laenderbank in New York.

In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.5368 marks, up from its Tokyo close Wednesday of 1.5330 marks. The U.S. currency was changing hands at 136.25 yen, level with its Tokyo close Wednesday. Sterling was trading at $1.8823, up from $1.8750.

In Tokyo early Thursday, the dollar was trading at 136.85 yen.

Even a report in an Israeli newspaper indicating that U.S. Secretary of State James A. Baker III would likely meet Saddam Hussein in Baghdad on Jan. 9 failed to encourage dollar sellers. The State Department later denied that any date had been set for negotiations. Furthermore, traders noted, even if the two men did meet on Jan. 9, it was hardly feasible that a withdrawal by Jan. 15 could be negotiated.

News that British, Australian and U.S. Marines seized an Iraqi freighter before it entered the Persian Gulf kept the attention of what few participants there were focused on the Mideast.

Concern over the Soviet situation seems to have abated somewhat, allowing traders to buy back some mark positions. After mulling over the consequences of Mr. Shevardnadze's resignation, participants seem to be regarding the mark sell-off as somewhat overdone, traders said.

Among the developments suggesting that panic over the future of Mikhail Gorbachev's rule might have been unwarranted, was news that Mr. Gorbachev won sweeping new powers over a reorganized executive branch as Parliament agreed to create a new vice presidency and cabinet under his direct control.

The focus of the market's political jitters has now shifted to Baghdad rather than Moscow, said Erik Lehtis, assistant vice president at Credit Agricole in New York.

But traders emphasized that the thinness of the market made rate movements unreliable benchmarks, and that the predominant impulse was to ignore the market as much as possible. Traders were reluctant to establish any new positions in the illiquid market and are waiting for Jan. 2 to get a real sense of where the market wants rates to be.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $385.60 an ounce, up $3.10. Estimated volume was 1.9 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $384.88 an ounce.
