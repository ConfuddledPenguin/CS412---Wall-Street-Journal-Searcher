Letters to the Editor: Unfair Portrayal of Realtors

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/27/90

Elizabeth Lesly's Nov. 30 editorial-page article "How Your Realtor Rips You Off" was a tissue of half-truths and innuendo, and has done serious damage to the reputation of the National Association of Realtors, its 800,000 members, and to a profession with an established record of service to the public. The first allegation was that the Boise, Idaho, Board of Realtors "hurled" a local broker {Ralph Colburn} into bankruptcy and "drove him out of business" because he attempted to represent home buyers.

Had Ms. Lesly consulted the board or examined the record, she would have learned that the board's only action was to send a "private" letter to the broker asking that he not disparage the integrity of his fellow Realtors in his advertising. The reporter's charge that the broker was the victim of association opposition to buyer brokerage is false. Far from opposing buyer brokerage, the NAR recognizes it as a primary marketing relationship for the consumer. In fact, 1986 and 1990 NAR surveys indicate that at least one-third of member firms offer buyer-brokerage services. NAR policy guarantees buyer-broker access to Multiple Listing Services.

As regards responsibility for the broker's bankruptcy, it should be noted that when he closed his business a review of court records showed his liabilities of about $14,000 exceeded his assets of about $11,000.

Ms. Lesly's second allegation was that the members of the National Association control the "marketplace," have a monopolistic position, and therefore can charge undeservedly high commission rates. The primary source of her allegation was identified as Paul Roark, a staff attorney of the Federal Trade Commission.

The absurdity of the charge of monopoly is made manifest by two facts: First, out of the 2,210,085 real estate licensees in the U.S., only 800,000 are Realtors. Second, membership in the National Association, and hence, access to MLS, is available to any licensee willing to subscribe to the Realtors' Code of Ethics. We are at a loss to understand how NAR can be accused of being a "monopoly" when everyone licensed to engage in the real estate business is eligible and invited to join.

The irresponsible use of your power and influence has hurt a lot of hard-working men and women in the real estate business, and potentially injured those home buyers and sellers who forgo services of inestimable value to them.

Harley E. Rouda

President

William D. North

Executive Vice President

National Association of Realtors

Chicago

Multiple Listing Services do not have to be a part of the National Association of Realtors. Most sellers do, in fact, know that the commission fees are negotiable. They ask what the fee is. They do negotiate on the fee. Broker fees generally range from 3% to 10% and the full range is used depending on the value of the property and the difficulty in executing a sale. It is fatuous and irresponsible to attempt to mislead the public into thinking the average fee should be closer to 2% of the sale price. The public isn't that stupid. The marketplace is free enough to cause that to happen if it were a reality.

The buyer's broker mode is common. It is not unique as portrayed. I have used that contractual relationship many times. I am a Realtor and I am in a MLS.

Arthur R. Keene

Broker-Owner

Roseburg, Ore.

---

Ms. Lesly strongly infers that buyer-brokering is not allowed by the National Association of Realtors or local real estate boards. I am a past president of the Flint Board of Realtors and my associate, who manages our brokerage department, is the current president. We have been and continue to be very active in buyer brokering, and we are also members of our Multiple Listing Service.

Ms. Lesly obviously did not do her homework. She states, "Under present law both real estate agents in any house-buying transaction are bound to do everything in their power to see that the seller gets the highest price." This is untrue. A broker cannot represent, in most cases, both buyer and seller. However, Broker A who lists a property would obviously be acting as an agent of the seller. Broker B, who acts as an agent of the buyer and is paid by the buyer, would not be representing the seller. Ms. Lesly also incorrectly makes the point that a property placed in the MLS system requires that both brokers work only for the seller's best interest. This is completely wrong.

Robert S. Piper

Piper Realty Co.

Flint, Mich.

Congratulations for being willing to print the truth about damage to consumers due to Realtor monopoly control. Too few in the media have been willing to risk the roar of the Realtor lion.

It's unfortunate you adapted only part of Ms. Lesly's article from the November issue of the Washington Monthly. Taking it out of the context of a well-documented piece will cause many of your readers to miss its import -- that the real estate business in this country is controlled by a multibillion-dollar monopoly that has made itself immune from effective government oversight.

It's also unfortunate that thousands of real estate salespeople who continually stream in and out of the business every year will not recognize that they too are victims as much as the consumers described by author Sloan Bashinsky in 1984 as "Home Buyers: Lambs to the Slaughter." Sad, but true, that even those who remain in the business for any length of time have a general ignorance about the NAR's political machinations, matched only by their naive belief in the NAR-generated image propaganda. Therefore, they participate more or less innocently in the anticompetitive schemes that drive up consumer costs and limit consumer choices.

However, many who have observed the industry from within and without for more than two decades could confirm Ms. Lesly's facts as consistent with their own experiences. Few dare to speak out in fear of reprisal. By and large, the NAR has become a power unto itself, controlling its own membership and the marketplace through proprietary control of the Multiple Listing Services. As the Washington Monthly article shows, NAR's power to intimidate legislators and regulators by winding up its members to do "grassroots" lobbying can thwart efforts at reforms in other areas affecting financial services, appraisal standards and taxation.

The FTC has many cases in its files, but refuses to take any action to protect the rights of brokers to compete or the rights of consumers to choose alternative services that would involve any head-on confrontation with the NAR. The FTC's 1983 Report on the Real Estate Industry revealed that state regulatory agencies function more to protect the industry's self-interest than to protect the public. Yet very few in state government (New York is a notable exception) have dared follow up on that report by taking a hard look at consumer protection and restraint-of-trade issues.

Bernadette C. McTighe

President

Single Agency Realty Association

Rockville, Md.

---

Ms. Lesly must have gotten ripped off by a real estate broker. Every profession has a minority who (in her terms) "rake in" big commissions and receive "hefty broker fees."

I am a real estate broker in a medium-size firm. I work hard. I know the market. Every dollar I earn with knowledge and scrupulous effort. I am one of the leading brokers in my office, and this year will earn, not rake in, $33,000, from 18 separate transactions. Some were effortless, most were arduous.

Yes, there are dilemmas in my work. Although I share in a commission paid by the seller at the close of a sale, when I am with a buyer, working with them day after day after day, I try to perceive what they want and what they can afford, and try to negotiate what is best for them and the seller. It should be a "win-win" situation. I comply with the law and, in writing, notify "my broker" that I will receive payment by "sharing" the sales commission paid to the "listing broker."

The reason we do not have more brokers representing buyers looking for homes (it is becoming standard practice in commercial real estate brokerage) is because most buyers do not want to sign an agreement to work with a broker, and pay them when they buy. Although it might be in the buyer's best interest, we have traditionally done otherwise.

Ruth Weissgarber

San Antonio, Texas

I suspect you will be attacked viciously by the NAR for printing Ms. Lesly's article. But one never needs to apologize for the truth and the facts. That position has cost me, because the ads for which the local Board of Realtors crucified me read:

1) "The real estate buyer without his own agent has no protection in the transaction; he must represent himself just like a car buyer."

2) "The buyer's broker is an ally of the buyer rather than an opponent like he is in most real estate transactions."

Is that bad advertising? I think not. In fact, the complaining broker stated during the "hearing": "An attack of this kind on the industry may have to be tolerated if it comes from the outside, but not from one of our own members."

Talk about a thin skin. The warning they gave me to cease and desist was nothing less than a threat, more like professional terrorism.

Ralph Colburn

Boise, Idaho
