ITT Spreads Cheer To Some Retirees Of Western Union --- Subsidy of Medical Benefits By Ex-Parent Company Is Welcome Holiday Gift

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 12/27/90

NEW YORK -- ITT Corp. is showing Western Union Corp., or what is left of it, that it knows a thing or two about holiday spirit. Cheating bankruptcy and struggling under a mountain of debt, Western Union and its management team, led by New York financier Bennett S. LeBow, told 9,000 company retirees in late November that they would have to make significantly larger payments to their retiree medical fund to keep their benefits intact.

"Payments typically went up two to three times and in some cases more than that, depending on the medical options they had," said a Western Union spokesman. "So if you were, say, paying $55 a month, now you had to pay something like $125 or more."

About 225 of these retired workers were longtime former employees of ITT's World Communications international telex business, which ITT sold to Western Union in 1987.

In mid-December, when one irate former ITT employee complained that ITT had sold its former workers down the river to a company that was killing his benefits, ITT stepped in. It offered to make up 85% of the difference between what the former ITT workers had been paying into their medical plan and the amount that Mr. LeBow and his managers were now demanding -- at least during 1991.

Why? "Our contributions will range from $10 a month for some employees to about $500," said an ITT spokesman. "Western Union said the {retirees} would have to pay so many hundreds of dollars a month to keep the coverage. These were long-service employees and we were concerned that they have the right coverage. It was the right thing to do."

But a Western Union spokesman defended the company's position. "This company has a six-to-one ratio of retirees to active workers. We very reluctantly had to reduce our subsidy to the plan because of the company's impaired financial condition."

Since taking over Western Union in late 1987 in a deal that was touted as a bid to save Western Union, Mr. LeBow and his managers have sold off numerous Western Union operations, fired half its work force and slashed pension and other benefits to those that still work at the almost 140-year-old communications company.

During the same period, however, the LeBow rescue team has prospered with steadily increasing compensation. Mr. LeBow alone has been drawing a $2.4 million annual management fee, including $50,000 per month for overhead expenses. And this year, President Robert Amman and Chief Financial Officer Stewart J. Paperin received raises of 23% and 26%, respectively, not counting bonuses. Their medical benefits remain intact.

Mr. LeBow will no longer draw that management fee under a deal worked out with the company's bondholders as one condition for accepting an exchange offer aimed at restructuring the company's $530 million in junk-bond debt.
