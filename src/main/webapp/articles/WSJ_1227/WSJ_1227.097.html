Law -- Legal Beat: Insurer Seeks Indemnity in Wickes Suit

  • Author: Wade Lambert and Amy Dockser Marcus
  • Date: 12/27/90

Continental Insurance Cos. asked a U.S. court to protect it from having to pay Wickes Cos.' defense costs in securities-fraud cases involving Michael Milken. Continental filed the lawsuit in federal court in Manhattan asking the court to declare that Wickes's general liability coverage doesn't apply either to defense costs or any damages that might be awarded. Wickes is named as a defendant, along with Mr. Milken and Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc.'s brokerage unit, in four civil lawsuits alleging securities fraud.

Continental argues that the insurance doesn't apply mainly because the cases allege wrongdoing by Wickes. According to the lawsuit, the coverage applies only to damages or injuries caused by an accident. "Stock manipulation is the antithesis of an accident," said Barry Ostrager, an attorney representing Continental. "That's someone willfully committing something that is wrong."

The allegations against Wickes stem from stock transactions in 1986 that allowed the company to convert its preferred stock into nondividend-paying common shares and to save about $15 million annually in dividend payments. The lawsuits against Wickes, Mr. Milken and Drexel allege that the conversion was the result of an illegal stock-manipulation scheme. Prosecutors allege that Mr. Milken directed the manipulation of the Wickes stock with the assistance of Ivan Boesky; Wickes wasn't charged with any criminal wrongdoing.

A spokeswoman for Wickes, a Santa Monica, Calif., manufacturer and retailer, declined to comment on the suit by the Continental Corp. unit.

Both Mr. Milken and Mr. Boesky pleaded guilty to felonies related to securities transactions, but the counts they admitted didn't involve Wickes. Coincidentally, Continental's lawsuit was assigned at random to Judge Kimba Wood, the same judge who last month sentenced Mr. Milken to 10 years in prison.

PFIZER LOSES bid to throw out investors' suits over heart valve.

In federal court in Manhattan, Judge John Keenan rejected a motion by Pfizer Inc. to dismiss the class-action lawsuits, which seek unspecified damages. The lawsuits, which have been consolidated, claim that investors from March 27, 1987, to Feb. 26, 1990, were defrauded because Pfizer failed to disclose problems with the artificial heart valve. On Feb. 26, a House subcommittee staff report criticized the New York pharmaceutical company for its level of disclosure. The heart-valve was removed from the market in 1986.

Pfizer argued that the suits should be dismissed for not making adequate claims of fraud. But Judge Keenan, in allowing the suits to go forward, said: "The sources of plaintiffs' allegations, such as the staff report and others, are highly reputable. The allegations, therefore, are not unfounded or speculative."

Judge Keenan also said in his opinion that "Pfizer knew that there were problems with the heart valves, and it knew that suits {by heart-valve recipients} were being and expected to be filed. Whether what was told to investors was sufficient is an issue for trial."

Attorneys for Pfizer couldn't be reached for comment.

PROFESSOR WINS RIGHT to sue over alleged free speech deprivation.

A federal judge ruled that the professor, who espouses the view that blacks are intellectually inferior to whites, can sue officials of City College of the City University of New York for allegedly violating his First Amendment right of free speech.

The professor, Michael Levin, who is white, teaches philosophy at the college. His statements and writings about race have led to protests and demonstrations at the school, where the student body is 39% black, 28% Hispanic, 17% white and 16% Asian. Following the campus protests, the college formed a faculty committee to review the issue of when academic freedom can be restricted and to examine some of Mr. Levin's statements.

Judge Kenneth Conboy ruled that because the committee's recommendations could lead to the revocation of Mr. Levin's tenure, the professor's suit can proceed. The judge didn't rule on whether the college infringed on the professor's rights. Mr. Levin said he has turned down speaking engagements and requests for articles because he is worried about how the faculty committee will react.

In the suit, he also alleged that college officials were unwilling to punish student protesters who disrupted his classes and threatened his safety.

MOST LIBEL CLAIMS against Glamour are thrown out in Foretich case.

A lawsuit by Eric A. Foretich alleged that a Glamour magazine article left the false impression that he had sexually abused his daughter, as claimed by his ex-wife, Elizabeth Morgan.

In throwing out all but one remaining libel claim, U.S. District Judge Gerhard Gesell of Washington stated that it was "crystal clear" that the disputed statements constituted opinions and thus were constitutionally protected. Conde Nast Publications Inc. publishes Glamour.

The judge allowed Mr. Foretich to proceed with one libel claim stemming from a reader's letter. A lawyer for Mr. Foretich couldn't be reached for comment.
