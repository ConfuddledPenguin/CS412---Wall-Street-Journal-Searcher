Pan Am, TWA Merger Talks Grow Rockier --- Icahn Balks on Written Bid, Executive Says Suitor `Not Prepared to Pursue'

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 12/27/90

Merger talks between Pan Am Corp. and Trans World Airlines grew rockier after Pan Am told TWA owner Carl Icahn, in effect, to put up or shut up. In a letter to Mr. Icahn released late yesterday, Pan Am Chief Financial Officer Richard Francis said Pan Am has concluded that TWA "is not prepared to pursue" its Dec. 16 offer to acquire Pan Am after Mr. Icahn balked at putting his offer in writing.

In an interview, Mr. Icahn retorted that he didn't want to put any new offer in writing because he said Pan Am had used an earlier written offer he made last fall to strengthen Pan Am's hand in talks to sell its London routes to United Airlines parent UAL Corp. "In November we gave them a written proposal, and never got the courtesy of a call back," Mr. Icahn said.

Another sore point appeared to be Monday's disclosure that Pan Am has received an offer from Pacificorp, a Portland, Ore., utility, for new financing of the Pan Am Boston-New York-Washington shuttle, with an agreement to allow Northwest Airlines parent NWA Inc. to operate the shuttle.

Pan Am's letter said that if Mr. Icahn wanted to make a written offer, which could also include a separate bid for the shuttle, "We would be pleased to consider it and meet with you if it provides an acceptable basis for further discussion."

In a written reply to Pan Am, Mr. Icahn said, "Once again you have misstated and misunderstood TWA's position. Our offer to shareholders remains in place, subject to usual and customary conditions for a transaction of this type." But he said he remains "ready to meet" with Pan Am.

One of those conditions, Mr. Icahn added, would be any adverse change in Pan Am's fortunes, including a possible Pan Am bankrupcty-law filing for protection from creditors, "a sale of assets for less than fair value, or defaults in major borrowings or obligations."

Pan Am appears to be in constant danger of any number of such changes, particularly in possible defaults of major borrowings. It is in talks with one major aircraft leasing company after failing to make a required payment on 21 wide-body jets.

One key point of dispute was TWA's insistence that any interim debt financing to be provided to Pan Am by TWA be in the form of debtor-in-possession financing.

Although such financing gives the lender a priority position among other creditors, it can only be used in a bankruptcy-law filing seeking protection from creditors.

The problem with that, according to one person familiar with the Icahn-Pan Am talks, is that such a filing could jeopardize any proceeds shareholders receive in the acquisition. "As proposed, it doesn't give any value for the shareholders," that person said.

Mr. Icahn has offered $1.50 a share plus $1 a share in notes or preferred stock for each of Pan Am's 150 shares outstanding. Although the cash offer is valued at $225 million, the notes could be worth as little as $30 million based on the market value of other TWA debt.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Pan Am shares closed at $1.375, up 12.5 cents.
