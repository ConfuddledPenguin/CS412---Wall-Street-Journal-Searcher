Survey Shows Many Manufacturers Were Ready for Recession

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/27/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- A survey of 250 U.S. manufacturers shows that while most think the economy is in a recession, an unusually high percentage nonetheless expect modest profit growth or at least no earnings decline next year. The survey indicates that U.S. manufacturers prepared well in advance for the economic slowdown, according to the firm that commissioned the poll, Grant Thornton, a San Francisco based audit, tax, accounting and management consulting conern.

Companies reduced inventories and receivables, started programs that promised high-yield or short-term paybacks and invested cautiously, Grant Thornton said. As a result, four of ten companies expect increased 1991 profits. Another group, consisting of more than one-third of the respondents, said they expected earnings about the same as 1990. Only one-fourth said they expected declining earnings.

About two-thirds of the companies said the country is in recession, while another 20% said the nation hasn't yet entered a downturn but is headed for one in the coming year. Only eight percent said the likelihood of a recession next year was dim.

Manufacturers seem more concerned with the possibility of inflation cutting into profits than the general downturn. Four of ten respondents said a higher inflation rate was likely, while nearly 50% said the rate won't change over the year. Only 13% said the inflation rate will drop in 1991.

The telephone survey was conducted between Nov. 26 and Dec. 4 by the Wirthlin Group, a national opinion research organization in McLean, Viriginia. The group polled a random sample of U.S. manufacturers with annual sales between $10 million and $500 million. The firm said the survey has a margin of error of plus or minus six percentage points.
