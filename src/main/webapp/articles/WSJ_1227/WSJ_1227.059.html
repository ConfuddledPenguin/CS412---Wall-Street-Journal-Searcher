Marketing & Media: Fame Magazine Halts Publication After Two Years

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 12/27/90

Fame was fleeting. A once-promising celebrity magazine, Fame shut its doors last week after two years. Its founder and publisher, Steven Greenberg, said he decided to close the magazine after estimating that advertising pages for the monthly wouldn't grow in 1991 and that he needed to invest heavily to increase the magazine's circulation.

An executive at the magazine said Mr. Greenberg spent more than $10 million on the start-up and operation of Fame. In an interview, Mr. Greenberg said that in the last year he had been contacted by potential buyers of a part interest in the magazine. There was no sale, however, because no agreement could be reached on the size of needed future investments, he said.

There is a remote chance that Fame could be brought back to life by its editor in chief, Gael Love. Mr. Greenberg said Ms. Love has begun discussions with outside investors who may be interested in buying the magazine.

In a difficult publishing climate, the folding of Fame isn't considered a surprise, particularly because it published independently of any other magazine titles. Most of the magazines that have gone out of business in 1990 published independently or were part of small publishing groups. Fame had a staff of about 45, based in New York and Los Angeles.

"We would have had a market in the 1980s, but the '80s are over," Mr. Greenberg said. "In 1991, many magazines are going to look as thin as pamphlets."

America's seemingly endless fascination with celebrities and movie stars made Fame's magazine category a relatively sturdy one. Fame's recent cover subjects included Warren Beatty, Cybill Shepherd and Tom Hanks, and the last issue in December carried stories on composer Andrew Lloyd Webber's love affairs and a murder in Beverly Hills.

Though Fame was noteworthy for sensational stories, its circulation never exceeded 200,000 after its launch in 1988. Mr. Greenberg said he had expected the magazine to make a profit in 1991, but that plan was derailed by the country's general economic slowdown, which has resulted in sharply smaller advertising budgets for magazines and other media.
