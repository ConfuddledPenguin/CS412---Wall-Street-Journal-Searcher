Samsung Issue Is Seen Sparking Similar Offerings

  • Author: Steven E. Levingston
  • Date: 12/27/90

The successful launch of Samsung Co.'s depositary receipts is expected to usher in a period of low-priced overseas issues by other South Korean companies. The receipts, which were priced two weeks ago at a 15% premium to Samsung's shares, are trading at about a 19% premium. Analysts attribute the success of the launch only partly to the reputation of the company, which is the trading arm of the diversified Samsung group. They say demand was spurred largely by the receipts' low premium compared with Korean issues in recent years.

"It marks a break with the pricing patterns of previous Korean overseas issues," says Melissa Brown, senior analyst with Barclays de Zoete Wedd Securities Ltd. in Seoul. "It will have a serious impact on deals coming to market early next year."

The $40 million issue of depositary receipts was launched in two portions of approximately equal size in the U.S. and Europe. The receipts, which are listed on the Luxembourg Stock Exchange, will give investors access to Samsung shares after the Korean stock market's scheduled opening to foreign investors in 1992.

Investor interest in the issue was reflected in the active trading of the receipts by market makers in London the first week after the launch. Trading has since slackened.

Peter Rigg, managing director of CS First Boston Inc., which shares lead management duties for the issue with Korea's Dongyang Securities Co., says he was pleased by the receipts' initial liquidity. "The issue was extremely well received, as we expected," he says.

Ms. Brown of Barclays de Zoete says investors were cheered when the premium came in at the lower end of the expected range of 15% to 20%. "When the deal was finally launched, there ended up being a little bit more demand than expected because of the price," she says. "So there was a little more trading after the launch."

Some investors were unenthusiastic about the receipts even at the modest price. William Ramsay, investment director of Thornton Management (Asia) Ltd., says his company decided against the receipts. "There are interesting companies to invest in in Korea. Samsung Co. is not one of the them," he says, making a distinction between Samsung Co., the trading company, and other concerns in the Samsung group. "I believe profit growth will be made elsewhere in the group."

Analysts say the price of Samsung's receipts reflects a recognition of the softened market for Korean overseas issues. Because the Korean stock market is closed to foreign investors, vehicles that provide access to shares -- such as convertible bonds and mutual funds -- have traded at sky-high premiums. For example, the Korea Fund, a mutual fund listed on the New York Stock Exchange and investing solely in Korean shares, was trading at a premium of more than 100% last year. Now it is at about 15%.

Investors' rejection of high premiums has ruined some recent issues. For instance, Sunkyong Industries Co., the last company to tap the overseas market, launched convertible bonds in September at a 40% premium. Demand was so weak that the underwriters were forced to swallow most of the issue.

The lessons weren't lost on Samsung. "The (Samsung) issue was priced at a level where there's demand in the market," says an analyst close to the transaction. "There is the realization in Korea that that is what it takes -- that the market has changed."

Several issues that were delayed this year because of the gloomy market are expected to try again in 1991, but their prices are likely to reflect current conditions. "If they think they can come back to the market and ask silly premiums, they'll be disappointed again," says Mr. Ramsay of Thornton Management.

One overseas launch planned for next year is the $100 million Korea Asia Fund, the third mutual fund for foreigners, which was postponed after a failed takeoff in September. Other companies planning to try again to launch overseas issues next year include Kolon Co., Tongyang Nylon Co., and Jindo Co.
