Pennzoil Raises Its Chevron Stake To 9.4% From 9%

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 12/27/90

HOUSTON -- Pennzoil Co. said it raised its stake in Chevron Corp. to 9.4% from 9% and has received a ruling from the Internal Revenue Service that would enable it to buy more shares through May 31 if it chooses. Both Pennzoil and Chevron, based in San Francisco, are oil companies.

In a Securities and Exchange Commission filing, Pennzoil said it acquired an additional 1.3 million Chevron shares for $89 million as of yesterday. Pennzoil began making its latest purchases on Dec. 3, right after the Federal Trade Commission said it would allow Pennzoil to acquire more.

In its previous government filings, Pennzoil said it would use the $2.6 billion of proceeds from its 1988 legal settlement with Texaco Inc. to buy just under 10% of Chevron's shares. The 10% threshold triggers a variety of Chevron's anti-takeover provisions. To date, Pennzoil has spent about $2.2 billion amassing its Chevron stake.

Pennzoil's settlement with Texaco ended four years of litigation in which a court found that Texaco had improperly interfered with Pennzoil's acquisition of a portion of Getty.

For the latest purchases, Pennzoil paid an average price of $69.75 a share.

Chevron closed yesterday at $73.25, up $2, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. Pennzoil finished at $65, up $1.375, also on the Big Board.

Pennzoil said the IRS on Monday granted a company request extending the time for investment of the Texaco settlement under a tax theory Pennzoil hopes will allow it to delay taxes on the money. A Pennzoil spokesman said it asked for the extension "because of delays in obtaining clearance from the FTC for the purchases." The period was to expire at year end but now will continue to May 31.

Pennzoil seeks to qualify its Chevron investment as property "similar or related in service or use to" the contractual interest it had in Getty Oil Co. That agreement was the subject of Pennzoil's litigation with Texaco. On Pennzoil's tax returns, the company has been treating the Texaco settlement proceeds as compensation for the involuntary conversion of property. Under Pennzoil's tax theory, so far untested, such proceeds would be excludable from taxable income if they're used to acquire property similar or related to its interest in Getty.

In its SEC filing, Pennzoil said its tax position "will be subject to review by the IRS in due course." The company spokesman declined to comment on whether Pennzoil views the IRS extension as an indication that the agency would also regard Pennzoil's tax plan favorably.

Pennzoil accumulated the bulk of its Chevron shares in 1989 and has described its stake as "a long-term investment," maintaining it doesn't want to take over the company. But Chevron has alleged in a number of court actions that Pennzoil misstated its intentions and may try to take over the company. So far Chevron hasn't received any support from the courts.
