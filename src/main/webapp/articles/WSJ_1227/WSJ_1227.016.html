Retailing: After a Gray Christmas, Bleak Forecasts

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 12/27/90

The nation's merchants, spooked by the ghost of the bleak Christmas just past, are bracing for tough times in the first half of 1991. For bargain hunters, that is likely to mean a bonanza. "We'll be more aggressive" in promoting special deals, vows William McCarthy, president of marketing at Montgomery Ward & Co. in Chicago. Troubled Sears, Roebuck & Co. also is planning to tout more deals.

But some retail executives fear that, in the current recessionary climate, even big price cuts won't coax consumers to open their wallets. Just listen to Cari Brookbanks, an advertising account supervisor in Chicago. "I'm going to stay out of the stores," she declares, citing a joint decision with her adman husband "to tighten our belts a little bit."

Says Linnette Peterson, a receptionist from Staten Island, N.Y.: "I sure don't plan to get anywhere near the stores for the January sales."

Indeed, the retail slump shows no signs of abating after a Christmas season that, by most accounts, finished on a blue note. Despite widespread discounting, holiday sales slipped below last year's at some stores while others barely exceeded last year's receipts, industry executives say. After adjusting for inflation, the final tallies for the crucial holiday period are expected to be the worst in many years.

The outlook for the first half of 1991 is even grimmer. Consultant Walter Loeb predicts that same-store sales in the first half of the year will decline as much as 4%, further squeezing industry profits.

"If the consumer wasn't shopping for Santa Claus and Christmas, I can't imagine why they would shop in the first half of the year," says Dayton Hudson Corp. Chairman Kenneth Macke.

"This is a difficult economic climate, and it makes running the business a lot tougher," says Julian Taub, a senior vice president at Campeau Corp.'s Bloomingdale's, where holiday revenue was "within a percentage point or two of last year."

Predicts Jack McMillan, president of Nordstrom Inc.: "It's going to be a tough first half."

The wild card is the Persian Gulf crisis. The current standoff undoubtedly depressed shoppers and contributed to the slump in holiday sales. But "if war breaks out in the Middle East, it could be catastrophic," says Arthur Martinez, vice chairman of Investcorp International Inc.'s Saks Fifth Avenue, which matched last year's holiday sales on a same-store basis. "On the other hand, if the dispute is favorably resolved, it could provide a psychological boost."

In any case, most merchants are shrinking inventories, cutting staff and expenses and planning to promote the sorts of basic apparel and household products that typically sell best in a recession.

At K mart Corp., which registered a slight increase in holiday sales, a spokesman says the discount chain is "placing greater emphasis" on low-priced commodities such as toothpaste, paper products and household cleaners, and focusing less on more expensive televisions and stereo equipment. "We're trying to put our inventories where we think it will do us the most good," he says.

The Christmas season provided ample evidence that consumers, fearing a recession and a war, are trading down to lower-priced goods and delaying discretionary purchases.

At Bill's London Transit, a small men's store in Atlanta, manager Philip M. Griffiths says shoppers tended to choose $45 sweaters instead of $1,200 leather suits. Jerry Sheff, owner of The Jewelry Store in Boston, says "customers stayed away from anything costing $100 or more as if it were a pickpocket." At FrontRunners Inc., a Los Angeles active apparel retailer, owner Paul D. Tordella says he sold out of T-shirts for the first time this December and socks were a popular gift, even in the affluent neighborhood of Brentwood.

Tiffany & Co., while reporting a modest but lower than expected sales increase at U.S. stores, says "the average customer purchase declined somewhat." And a hot seller at the Card Factory in Los Angeles was a new $55 model of the Filofax datebook. Last year's big gift was a $99 Mont Blanc pen, now priced at $115 and hardly moving.

Designs Inc., a Boston-based chain carrying only basic Levi Strauss & Co. sportswear, posted a 23% increase in same-store sales for the holiday period. "It could be an indication that, these days, people are looking for the more basic, steadfast product," says Samuel Levenson, assistant to the company treasurer. The chain also discounted aggressively.

Some merchants blame the media for poisoning the Christmas mood. "People have the money," insists Ami A. Peretz, who owns the Vencci men's boutique in Los Angeles. "They're just choosing not to spend it because the news caused people to be concerned about the gift budget. By January or February, I hope people will get a clearer picture of what's happening in the world and start buying again."

But consumers were singing a different tune. "This is a scary, unpredictable time," says Kelly S. Fiand, a college student and Los Angeles waitress who slashed her Christmas budget to $200 from last year's $1,000. "People are worried about the economy and war. . . . Customers just aren't tipping the way they used to."

Says Ms. Peterson, the Staten Island receptionist who intends to stay out of the stores: "I'm kind of worried about next year, since it's been downhill all the way on Wall Street with so many folks getting laid off."

Indeed, most retail industry executives have lowered their expectations and changed their definition of success. "For a while, if you weren't getting double-digit increases just by opening the door, you weren't satisfied," says Robert M. Benham, president of Hallmark Cards Inc.'s Halls and Swansons department stores in Kansas City. "Now, I think it's remarkable that most of us are running even, instead of off 10%. The whole perspective has changed."
