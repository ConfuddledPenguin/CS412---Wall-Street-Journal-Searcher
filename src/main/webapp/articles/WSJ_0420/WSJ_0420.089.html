Strike-Battered Dog Riding Hard Road With Low Octane --- Greyhound Woes Continue; Easter Travel Down 56% From Year-Ago Level

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 04/20/90

DALLAS -- After a disappointing Easter travel weekend, debt-laden Greyhound Lines Inc. continues its struggle to expand its service network and attract enough riders to shore up its strike-battered balance sheet. Just before Easter, the unit of GLI Holding Co. added several hundred new replacement drivers, restored service to 286 locations and offered customers in some cities free tickets if their buses arrived late. But with violence continuing to flare up in the strike by 9,300 members of the Amalgamated Transit Union, Greyhound only attracted about 44% the number of riders it did for Easter weekend a year ago.

"I feel that our ridership is very high," said Greyhound spokeswoman Elizabeth Hale, who said ridership had been hovering between 50% and 60% of pre-strike levels in the days immediately preceding the holiday. She attributed the lower percentage over the Easter weekend itself mainly to the fact that in 1989, Easter coincided with spring break for many college students.

But analysts who follow the closely held company's publicly traded bonds say Greyhound has yet to maintain the levels of ridership and revenue that it needs to break even. "The company has said they need to be at about 70%-plus level to break even," said Charles Mancuso, senior analyst for Moody's Investors Service Inc. "The question becomes how long can they go if they don't achieve that level."

In a recent Securities and Exchange Commission filing, Greyhound acknowledged that a prolonged strike could seriously impair its financial future. Since the strike began March 2, the company has continued to restore scheduled service and add replacement drivers in the face of strike violence.

The company said that, as of last Tuesday, it was running about 72% of its prestrike schedules with 2,903 drivers -- 2,393 of them new hires.

But even if the company succeeds in spite of the strike, some analysts say they are concerned about the long-term impact of negative publicity surrounding the recent violence, the additional debt taken on because of the strike and the problems in rejuvenating Greyhound's charter and package express businesses, both badly battered by the walkout.

"They seem to be winning the strike," says Leigh Walzer, an analyst with R.D. Smith and Co. "The question now is how strong will the company be going forward."
