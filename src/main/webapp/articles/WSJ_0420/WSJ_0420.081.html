Milken Told to Accept Plea Bargain By Noon Today or Face Further Charges

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 04/20/90

NEW YORK -- Prosecutors gave Michael R. Milken a deadline of noon today to accept a plea bargain or face additional charges, according to people familiar with the plea negotiations. Lawyers for the former chief of Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc.'s junk-bond operations have spent nearly four months trying to negotiate an agreement that would enable Mr. Milken to avoid the new charges and the prospect of a lengthy trial. Mr. Milken already faces charges on 98 counts related to insider trading and other alleged securities-law violations.

The government is buttressing its deadline by ordering the federal grand jury that has been investigating Mr. Milken to convene in Manhattan today, individuals familiar with the investigation said. If Mr. Milken decides not to accept the plea agreement, the grand jury could vote on an expanded indictment this afternoon.

Any plea agreement would involve a settlement of civil insider-trading charges brought by the Securities and Exchange Commission in September 1988, as well as the criminal charges -- including racketeering, conspiracy and securities fraud -- handed up by a federal grand jury in March 1989.

Under the settlement terms offered by the government, Mr. Milken would have to plead guilty to six felony counts and pay more than $650 million in fines and penalties, according to individuals familiar with the plea discussions. While a federal judge would decide on the length of Mr. Milken's prison term, prosecutors would recommend a sentence totaling five years for all the counts, the individuals said. A spokesman for Mr. Milken declined to comment.

Lawyers familiar with the case say Mr. Milken would be unlikely to receive as attractive a settlement offer once a new indictment was issued and the government began to prepare for trial. If convicted of racketeering charges at trial, Mr. Milken could face up to 20 years in prison.

While an acceptable plea agreement seemed highly unlikely as recently as last week, Mr. Milken's legal team, led by Arthur Liman, a partner at the New York firm of Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton and Garrison, has been negotiating nearly around the clock since last weekend with officials of the SEC.

Among the major sticking points from the standpoint of the Milken camp was the SEC's insistence that Mr. Milken cooperate in the government's continuing insider-trading investigations, individuals familiar with the negotiations say. The government is understood to have softened the terms of its cooperation requirement.

Individuals who spoke with Mr. Milken as recently as late yesterday said that while some of his attorneys and others recommended that he accept the negotiated pact, Mr. Milken had still not made up his mind about whether to do so. They said Mr. Milken was less concerned about the fines and jail term than he was about having to cooperate with any further investigation. In addition, he was said to be worried about the status of the case against his brother, Lowell Milken, who was also charged in last year's indictment.

Although Michael Milken has directed his lawyers to work out terms of an agreement, individuals who know him well say they wouldn't be surprised if he rejected the pact at the last minute. Before last year's indictment, Mr. Milken's attorneys held a round of negotiations with the government. But after prosecutors relented on certain positions Mr. Milken considered key to a settlement, Mr. Milken rejected the offer in the end, the individuals said.

The latest government deadline was set by Roger Hayes, the deputy U.S. attorney in Manhattan who is supervising the case. Prosecutors last month had told U.S. District Judge Kimba Wood that a new indictment would be handed up no later than this week.
