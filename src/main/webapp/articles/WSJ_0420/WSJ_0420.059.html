Gillette's Net Rose 12% in 1st Period; Payout Is Lifted

  • Author: Lawrence Ingrassia
  • Date: 04/20/90

BOSTON, Mass. -- Gillette Co., citing strong sales of its new Sensor razor and its international businesses, reported healthy sales and earnings gains for the first quarter and boosted its dividend 12.5% The shaving and personal-care products company said net income rose 12% to $87 million from $77.6 million in the 1989 first quarter on an 11% sales increase, to $1.05 billion from $941 million. Per-share net income available to common stockholders dropped 6% to 75 cents a share from 80 cents, however, because of a $14.2 million preferred stock dividend.

Gillette also announced at its annual meeting that it is increasing its quarterly dividend to 27 cents a share from 24 cents, payable June 5 to stock of record May 1.

Gillette said its North Atlantic shaving group posted a 9% increase in sales but a 9% decline in operating profit because of the costs of the Sensor introduction. However, that profit decline was more than offset by improvements elsewhere; in Europe, for example, sales were up 26% and profits rose 9%, the company's Braun electric shaver and small appliance business posted a 15% increase in profits on a 19% sales increase and its Oral B dental care business had a 72% increase in profits on a 33% sales increase.

Sensor got off to an unexpectedly fast start and the company is having a difficult time producing enough of the product to meet demand; the company is speeding up the installation of equipment to increase production capacity. Gillette said it already has shipped nine million Sensor razors since its introduction, a level that it didn't expect to reach until October.

John W. Symons, executive vice president in charge of the North Atlantic shaving group, said that Sensor is helping Gillette achieve its goal of winning over some customers who traditionally use lower-priced, less profitable disposable razors. Early surveys indicate that 74% of Sensor buyers had used other Gillette products, and 26% used competitors' products, he noted. Of that 26%, about half used disposable razors made by other companies.

Mr. Symons noted that the tight supply of Sensor blade cartridges "is easing now and will get considerably better in May and June."

Largely because of Sensor, disposable razors' share of the wet shaving market declined to 41.6% in the January-February period from 42.7% in November and December, Gillette noted. While that is a small shift, Gillette considers it significant because disposables have steadily increased their share of the U.S. market ever since being introduced in the 1970s. "It is a possibility, in fact now a probability" that the long-term trend toward disposables can be reversed, Mr. Symons maintained.

One issue that has made executives a bit "anxious" is that Sensor users seem to be getting more shaves per blade cartridge than they did with other Gillette razors, he said. But whether this is a result of changed shaving patterns or the shortage of Sensor blades isn't clear yet, he indicated.
