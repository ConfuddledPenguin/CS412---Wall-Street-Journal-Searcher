Great American Bank Discloses Rise In Troubled Assets

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 04/20/90

Great American Bank disclosed that its underperforming assets jumped 42% in the fourth quarter, contributing to its previously reported net loss for the period and casting a pall over the thrift's survival plans. On Dec. 31, Great American reported underperforming assets of $645.6 million, compared with $453.4 million on Sept. 30, according to a regulatory filing with the Office of Thrift Supervision. The company's total "classified" assets, a broader measure of actual or potential loan problems, rose 59% to $1.30 billion from $814.6 million at Sept. 30, the filing said.

The increases help explain the $198.9 million, or $8.30 a share, net loss that the company reported on Tuesday for the fourth quarter ended Dec. 31, compared with year-earlier profit of $19.8 million, or 80 cents. The capital-consuming effect of the deficit, and other problems, has left San Diego-based Great American, the nation's eighth-largest thrift with $15.9 billion in assets, on the verge of having to sell or dismantle itself or face possible federal seizure.

On Tuesday, Great American said it was making provision for loan and foreclosed real-estate losses of $271.2 million, but declined to specify the size of its troubled-loan portfolio. According to the OTS filing, the company had loss reserves on Dec. 31 equal to about 19% of its underperforming assets, excluding previous write-downs, and had set aside reserves or taken write-downs equal to about 35% of its total troubled assets.

Analysts said the growth in troubled assets, largely reflecting a disastrous 1986 acquisition of an Arizona thrift, will complicate Great American's efforts to save itself. At year-end, according to the filing, roughly 23% of the thrift's total $2.6 billion of assets in Arizona were considered real or potential trouble. The company must raise $350 million in new capital by year-end as part of a regulator-approved plan unveiled earlier this week to comply with minimum federal capital standards.

A Great American spokesman said the company considers its loss reserves to be adequate. He added: "We are still discussing the bank with a number of qualified investors, and are proceeding with our operations according to our capital plan."

The three biggest California banks -- BankAmerica Corp., Security Pacific Corp., and Wells Fargo & Co. -- are interested in expanding in the lucrative San Diego market and are considered potential bidders for Great American.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Great American slipped 37.5 cents to $3.625.
