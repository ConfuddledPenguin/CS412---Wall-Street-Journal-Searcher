International: Tokyo Relaxes Banking Rules, Aiding Citicorp

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 04/20/90

TOKYO -- Throwing a bone to U.S. critics, Japan's powerful Finance Ministry is easing the way for foreign banks to open branches here -- a move that chiefly benefits Citicorp. A ministry official said the relaxation of its rules is part of a gradual liberalization of financial regulations here. In effect, though, the ministry is granting a special exemption to foreign institutions. But Citicorp's Citibank in particular will benefit, the official said, because it is the only foreign bank aggressively to have sought to expand its retail network.

The move appears at least partly aimed at appeasing foreign critics, especially in the U.S. Congress. The chairman of the Senate Banking Committee, Michigan Sen. Donald W. Riegle Jr., has said that he favors retaliation against Japanese financial firms operating in the U.S. unless U.S. banks have equal access to Japan's market. Japanese banks are the biggest bloc of foreign financial institutions in the U.S.

But international appeasement by Tokyo may backfire at home. Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank Ltd., which acts as the chairman of the Federation of Bankers' Associations in Japan, declined to comment on the relaxation of rules for foreign banks. An official at another of Japan's 12 big commercial banks called the easier terms for Citibank "reverse discrimination," though.

For its part, Citibank, the largest foreign bank in Japan with nine branches and about 200,000 customers, said it now plans to expand its Japanese network by about five branches a year, to a total of about 50. That will give Citibank greater access to Japan's 400 billion-yen ($2.527 billion) retail banking industry without forcing the New York-based bank to pay an exorbitant amount to acquire a Japanese retail bank in order to get in, as Citibank officials had feared they might have to.

"Now we can pace ourselves," said Masamoto Yashiro, Citibank's chief country officer in Japan. "We know what we are getting into every step of the way."

Separately, Japan's Kyodo news agency reported yesterday that Citibank will start dollar-denominated loans next week using bank deposits as collateral, the first such service inaugurated in Japan.

The Finance Ministry's move was largely symbolic. Mr. Yashiro said he still hasn't received formal word of a change in the government policy. But he noted that Citibank already has noticed that its applications for new branches or branch relocations this year have been approved in only two months, down from a normal wait of six months a year ago. "This formalizes our understanding with the {ministry}," Mr. Yashiro said.

The rules, which are administrative, not statutory, prevent banks from opening more than one large new branch or four neighborhood offices every two years.

With corporate banking on a long, slow decline in Japan, banks here have been rushing to expand their retail empires. At the start of this month, two of the country's major banks, Mitsui Bank Ltd. and Taiyo Kobe Bank Ltd., merged to form Mitsui Taiyo Kobe Bank Ltd. the world's second-largest bank, behind Dai-Ichi Kangyo. The new bank has about 700 retail branches. Even with 50 branches in Japan, Citibank won't be a serious competitor to the country's big banks -- and most smaller Japanese banks have niche or regional client bases.

Foreign banks, which hold about 3% of the Japanese banking market, specialize in wholesale, or investment, banking activities, such as money-market and foreign-exchange trading, loan syndications and other corporate financing. "That's all we want to do," a spokesman for Chase Manhattan Bank here said.

Mr. Yashiro said Citibank will continue to focus on those businesses, but hopes to expand its retail client base to include individuals who have international dealings or want to maintain offshore accounts. It began its retail effort about four years ago, when it shifted a group of employees from its sagging corporate banking department to a newly formed retail operation. Early on, it considered buying Heiwa Sogo, a financially troubled mutual bank that eventually was sold to Sumitomo Bank Ltd.

At the moment, Citibank's retail operation in Japan isn't profitable, Mr. Yashiro said. But, he added: "We are heavily investing today so that maybe in 10 years we will become truly profitable."
