Philippines Hurt By Energy Crisis, Coup Rumors

  • Author: Robert H. Reid
  • Date: 04/20/90

MANILA -- An electricity crisis and persistent rumors of a new coup attempt are frightening off foreign investors, threatening the economic recovery that began under President Corazon Aquino. At the same time, inflation has reached double digits. Officials are warning that failure to meet government spending targets will weaken the Philippine peso and drive up interest rates, further threatening capital investment.

Since taking power in 1986, President Aquino has pointed with pride to the revival of the Philippine economy, which has grown 5% to 6% anually the past two years.

But failure to invest in new generating equipment, along with a drought on Luzon island, has brought about a critical electricity shortage. The government has announced a power rationing program affecting more than 300 public and private companies.

The energy crisis has already begun to take its toll by discouraging foreign investors. Philip Chien, president of the Taiwan Chamber of Commerce and Industry in the Philippines, told the Business Star newspaper that most Taiwanese investors here have decided to relocate. Taiwan had been the fastest-growing source of foreign investment here the past three years.

Mr. Chien said some Taiwanese were willing to wait a few months to see if the electricity crisis would ease, but he added: "We don't think it will stabilize."

He said government plans to purchase gas turbines to increase generating capacity would probably drive up electricity rates too high.

Meanwhile, Manila is awash in rumors that military dissidents, responsible for a failed coup in December, are preparing for a new bid to topple Mrs. Aquino.

Despite the arrests of several key figures in the December coup attempt, most of the suspected core group of planners remain at large.

Defense Secretary Fidel Ramos warned that public discontent over rising prices and the power crisis might encourage another coup attempt.

The U.S. State Department, meanwhile, has advised Americans not to travel to eight areas in the Philippines because of continued problems of crime and insurgency. At least eight Americans have been killed in communist insurgency-related incidents in the country in the past four years.
