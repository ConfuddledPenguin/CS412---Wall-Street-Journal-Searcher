Thursday's Markets: Late Sales Push Stocks Down 20.94 --- Bonds Continue Fall But at Slower Pace; Dollar Finishes Mixed

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 04/20/90

Late-afternoon sell programs knocked the wind out an already weak stock market, and bond prices continued their decline, albeit at a slower pace. The Dow Jones Industrial Average lost 20.94 points to close at 2711.94 in moderate trading. Long-term bond prices were off moderately, while the dollar was mixed, rising against the mark but falling against the yen.

Analysts said stock prices remain at the mercy of the declining bond market. As bond prices fall, interest rates rise, providing an incentive for investors to seek safety in cash rather than in stocks or bonds. Once bond prices appear stabilized, the higher rates that many analysts expect are likely to attract more investors to bonds than to stocks.

The erosion of bond prices stems from growing concern that inflation isn't under control and that the Federal Reserve will have to drive short-term interest rates higher if it expects to curb rising prices. Compounding the inflation worries are apparent selling by Japanese investors and the prospect early next month of a $30 billion Treasury refunding auction.

Bond traders said that with little economic news to drive the market yesterday, selling pressures moderated considerably. On Wednesday, long-term Treasury bonds plunged more than a full point, or more than $10 for each $1,000 of face amount. Nevertheless, traders are bracing for yields on long-term Treasury bonds to hit the 9% level in coming weeks. Yesterday the yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year issue stood at 8.84%.

Stock prices were moderately weak all of yesterday, generally tracking bond prices. But computer-assisted sell programs tied to stock-index arbitrage forced prices even lower in the final half-hour of trading. Absent the programs, traders said, the fall in prices that took the Dow industrials down nearly 33 points Wednesday seemed to have moderated.

So far, the downward pressure on stocks has come from the bond market and not from corporate earnings, which many analysts expected to be much worse. While some companies' profits have indeed been disappointing, there have been some positive surprises.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell in moderate trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 152.9 million shares. Declining issues surpassed gainers 968 to 511.

Bond prices were modestly lower. The 30-year Treasury lost about 1/8 point.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the U.S. currency was quoted at 1.6784 marks and 157.20 yen, compared with 1.6722 marks and 158.10 yen.
