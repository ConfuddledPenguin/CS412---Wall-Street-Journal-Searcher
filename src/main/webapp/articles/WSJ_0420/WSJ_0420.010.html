GPU Signs Accord To Buy Electricity From DQE Inc.

  • Author: Bill Paul
  • Date: 04/20/90

General Public Utilities Corp. said it signed a 20-year agreement to buy 500 megawatts of electricity from DQE Inc., parent of Duquesne Light Co., in a deal reflecting the pressing need for additional power in the Northeast. The transaction, valued at more than $630 million, calls for Parsippany, N.J.-based GPU to acquire a 50% interest in a now-mothballed DQE power plant for $150 million. It further calls for the utilities to spend $480 million on a 500-kilovolt transmission line that will carry the power from Pittsburgh-based DQE generating facilities in western Pennsylvania to GPU customers in eastern Pennsylvania and New Jersey.

The first power will be sold in 1994, the utilities said, with the new line scheduled for completion in 1996. GPU said it will pay five cents a kilowatt hour for the power in the first year of the agreement, with the rate rising thereafter. DQE said revenue derived from the agreement will be shared with its customers. In the first year alone, DQE could receive about $185 million in revenue, analysts estimated.

DQE further said it will have the right to use the 50% of the new transmission line's capacity that isn't utilized under the GPU arrangement to sell power to other utilities. DQE Chairman Wesley W. von Schack said he expects "additional business opportunities" in the Northeast by the mid-1990s, when, he said, the need for power will be great, while at the same time new "clean-coal" technology should put DQE's generating facilities in big demand.

GPU Chairman and President Standley H. Hoch said that although the DQE power won't help GPU meet its capacity needs for several years, he expects that other deals GPU has signed with so-called independent power producers should enable the utility to avoid brownouts and blackouts.

The deal generally represents a new way of doing business in the electric-utility industry. Before, when a utility built a new plant or transmission line, that asset would go into a pool that could be shared by all utilities in that region, which in this case is Pennsylvania, New Jersey and Maryland.

"This deal certainly helps GPU, but it won't help all the other power-tight utilities in the region," said Daniel Scotto, utilitybond analyst for Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp.
