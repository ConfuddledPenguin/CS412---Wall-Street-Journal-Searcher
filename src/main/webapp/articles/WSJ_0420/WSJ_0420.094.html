Politics & Policy: Employer Sanctions Show Little Success Halting Illegal Immigration, Study Says

  • Author: Pauline Yoshihashi
  • Date: 04/20/90

LOS ANGELES -- The employer-sanction provisions of the 1986 immigration law have had only a modest effect in deterring illegal immigration, according to a study by the Rand Corp. and the Urban Institute. The joint report comes amid renewed congressional debate over whether to repeal or change the sanctions, which impose civil and criminal penalites on employers who knowingly hire undocumented workers. In essence, the law requires employers to police job applicants by obtaining proof of their legal status.

In their report, the economic and policy research groups estimated that sanctions have reduced the flow of illegal immigrants by less than 20%, thus failing to produce the large declines predicted by some advocates of the law. But if the intent of the law was to curb any increase in illegal immigration, the sanctions could be viewed as successful.

The study also found that sanctions apparently haven't caused a large decline in the availability of illegal workers. The researchers analyzed labor market data to measure wage change in certain occupations that traditionally employ many undocumented immigrants.

"Congress may wish to weigh the cost of continuing the program against its current level of effectiveness," the researchers said. The study was compiled by economists Keith W. Crane, Beth H. Asch and authors Joanna Zorn Heilbrunn and Danielle Cullinane, who analyzed data on border crossings, U.S. Border Patrol apprehensions, labor markets and other indicators.

A congressional review of the sanctions was triggered March 29 by a General Accounting Office study, which found the law has caused "widespread discrimination" against legal job applicants who appear "foreign." A hearing of the Senate Subcommittee on Immigration and Refugee Affairs is scheduled for today in Washington to discuss the issue.

Sen. Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.), the subcommittee's chairman, has introduced legislation to expedite congressional consideration of any changes or termination of the sanctions. Many organizations have called for the sanctions to be repealed, including the American Bar Association, the U.S. Chamber of Commerce and the U.S. Civil Rights Commission.

"Many people saw sanctions as a panacea that would correct all immigration-related problems," said Mario Moreno of the Mexican American Legal Defense and Educational Fund in Washington, one of the groups lobbying for a repeal. "But they're not effective, and Congress is forcing immigration reform on the backs of the Hispanic and Asian communities."

The Immigration and Naturalization Service contends that the law has been successful in reducing illegal immigration. "Have we been able to totally regain control of the border? No, we haven't," said Duke Austin, an INS spokesman. "But the law clearly has been effective."

The INS expects to apprehend about one million persons along the southern border in fiscal 1990, Mr. Austin said. That compares with the 1.7 million apprehended in 1986, the year the law passed.

Other groups and lawmakers such as Sen. Alan Simpson (R., Wyo.), a sponsor of the 1986 law, have argued that sanctions should not be repealed. Some have suggested that more funding be allocated to educate employers about the law, particularly the anti-discrimination provisions.

The Urban Institute is based in Washington, D.C. Rand is based in Santa Monica, Calif.
