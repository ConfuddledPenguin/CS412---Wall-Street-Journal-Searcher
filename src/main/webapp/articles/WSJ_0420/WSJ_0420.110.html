Ford Is Likely to Spend a Record Sum To Advertise Redesigned Escort, Tracer

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 04/20/90

DETROIT -- Even though the redesigned Ford Escort and Mercury Tracer will be unprofitable from the start, Ford Motor Co. will likely spend more money advertising the launch of the subcompacts than it has with any other cars. Ford officials said they will at least equal the record amount they spent to promote the introduction of the Ford Taurus and Mercury Sable midsized sedans in late 1985 and 1986. The Ford officials declined to reveal the size of the ad budgets for the 1991 Escort and Tracer, which are scheduled to start reaching showrooms later this month. But Advertising Age estimates Escort and Tracer ad spending will reach $100 million compared with the $90 million spent on the Taurus and Sable.

Past versions of the Escort have been among the most popular cars sold in the U.S. But Ford has already conceded it will lose money on the new versions of the subcompact, which will have a base price of $7,565.

Nevertheless, the Escort and its Tracer twin are crucial to Ford's continued overall profitability. The reason: Under federal corporate average fuel economy standards, Ford must sell huge quantities of fuel-efficient cars in order to sell all of the large cars, which bring big profits.

"This is the most important launch of a new vehicle in the history of the company in our belief," John B. Vanderzee, advertising manager of the auto maker's Ford division said at a press luncheon yesterday. "It is more important than the first launch of Escort in 1980, the Mustang {in 1965} and Taurus in 1986."

Ford has already invested nearly $2 billion in the development of the new Escort and its state-of-the-art production facilities, which can make about 400,000 Escorts a year.

Ford also plans to use the Escort, which was last year upstaged by the Honda Accord as the best-selling car in the U.S., as a means to attract car buyers who tend to purchase import vehicles.

"We believe we have a legitimate contender for import intenders," said Robert Gillooly, the Ford division's car advertising manager. "We are going after them in a big way."

Similar to the launch of the 1986 Taurus, as much as 75% of the Ford division's car advertising budget will be used to promote the Escort name for the next five months, Mr. Gillooly said.

To reach import car buyers, Mr. Gillooly said Ford is embarking on its "biggest print program ever" by taking four to eight-page advertisements in weekly and monthly magazines. At the same time Ford plans to maintain its strategy of spending two-thirds of its Escort ad budget on television.

The theme of the new Escort ads will be: "A new line of thought. A new line of cars." The television and print advertisements will show the technological and engineering process used to design and develop the new Escorts. The new Escort ads, at least initially, won't dwell on the subcompact's price, which was a focus of previous Escort marketing campaigns.

Neither will the ads mention the role Mazda Motor Corp., Ford's Japanese partner, played in the Escort's engineering. Mazda supplied key parts such as automotive transmissions and some multivalve engines for the sportier GT versions of the car. Ford officials said their studies indicate that there is no added advantage of including Mazda's name in the ads.
