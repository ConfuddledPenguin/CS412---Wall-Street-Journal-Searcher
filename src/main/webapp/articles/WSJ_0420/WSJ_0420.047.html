NASA Can Hardly Wait to Read Next Month's Letters to the Editor

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 04/20/90

Earth to Owen Lipstein] With Earth Day media hype reaching incredible heights, Mr. Lipstein, owner of Mother Earth News, wants to do the same -- by taking his magazine into the heavens. On Sunday, Earth Day that is, a recorded version of the magazine will be beamed into outer space via a satellite transmission from Mount Everest. A team of climbers will broadcast it from battery-powered equipment at Everest Base Camp.

The sonic message in a bottle is taken from the March/April issue of Mother Earth News. It contains many heartfelt messages contributed by the famous and not-so famous about the world's environment and its present condition. Alfred Meyer, Mother Earth's editor in chief, asked an assortment of people to send in messages they would want blasted into space, supposedly to be received by someone or something.

Ann Landers, usually a hopeful sort, was gloomy in her reading: "Our planet is dying," she lamented. Pope John Paul II spoke of a "cosmos endowed with its own integrity, its own internal, dynamic balance." Gonzo journalist Hunter S. Thompson was simply gonzo: "Yesterday's weirdness is tomorrow's reason why." Michael Kramer, a third grader in Stamford, Conn., was more clear thinking: "The rain forest has plants and animals found nowhere else in the world. . . . It also helps us breathe."

Not everyone was so willing to participate. One respected (but apparently xenophobic) scientist refused the invitation to send a message. "He said he didn't want to advertise our presence. Whatever is out there might be hostile," Mr. Meyer says.

Cynics might consider this a mere gimmick by a troubled publisher to grab some cheap publicity. Mr. Lipstein recently ran into a cash squeeze, with the result that he wasn't mailing his magazines to subscribers on time. He eventually had to sell his American Health magazine.

"It's promotional," Mr. Lipstein concedes, "but it's also a profound thought." He certainly knows a good thing when he sees it. The resurging interest in saving the Earth is "reminiscent of the health and fitness boom eight or nine years ago," he says, proclaiming, "It's fortuitous that we have a magazine called Mother Earth News."

And it's fortuitous that "readers" in faraway galaxies will get it for free: They really can't complain if it arrives late.
