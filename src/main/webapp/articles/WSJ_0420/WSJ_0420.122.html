Anchor Savings Sets Write-Down, To Sell Branches

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 04/20/90

HEWLETT, N.Y. -- Anchor Savings Bank, to meet capital requirements, said it will take a $175 million write-down and expects to sell 68 Anchor branches and more than $2 billion in deposits. As a result of the write-down, Anchor reported a loss for its fiscal third quarter ended March 31 of $169.4 million, or $9.87 a share, compared with a loss of $1 million, or 26 cents a share, a year earlier. Excluding the one-time charge, Anchor had a net loss of $3 million, compared with a loss of $7.3 million a year ago.

The write-down is part of Anchor's compliance with a plan approved by the federal Office of Thrift Supervision to eliminate a $331 million capital shortfall. The plan is meant to bring Anchor into capital compliance within two years by selling assets.

Anchor is expected to sell bank branches, mortgage servicing rights, mortgage loans and other assets that could total about $140 million.

The bank will also convert cumulative preferred stock for non-cumulative stock, or an instrument which can be counted as core capital, adding about $157 million in capital.

James M. Large Jr., chairman of Anchor, said under the plan the bank will "refocus on its traditional markets" in northern New Jersey, New York City and Long Island, concentrating on consumer deposits, homeowner mortgages, supporting services.

Mr. Large said Anchor will sell its branches in Georgia, Florida and southern New Jersey. Some branches in northern New Jersey may also be sold.

Under general accounting standards, the anticipated branch sales require the write off of $175 million in supervisory goodwill. The goodwill was created when Anchor acquired branches through a series of government-related mergers in the early 1980s.

After the sale, Anchor will have $8 billion in assets, of which $5 billion will be deposits. The bank, in the last year, has reduced staff from about 3,200 to its current level of about 2,100.
