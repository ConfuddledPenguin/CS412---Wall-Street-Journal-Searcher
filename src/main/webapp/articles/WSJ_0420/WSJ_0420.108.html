Georgia-Gulf's Recapitalization Plan Voted by Holders, Ending Takeover Fight

  • Author: Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 04/20/90

ATLANTA -- Georgia Gulf Corp. said its shareholders overwhelmingly approved its recapitalization plan at a special meeting, ending the chemical company's protracted and bitter battle against a takeover by Dallas investor Harold C. Simmons. The company also reported that first-quarter earnings fell 32% to $37.4 million from $55.4 million, largely due to a drop in prices on its products and resulting lower profit margins. Per-share earnings dropped 28% to $1.52 from $2.12 based on 24.6 million shares outstanding compared with 26 million a year ago.

Revenue in the quarter declined 21% to $251.6 million from $317.8 million the year earlier.

Georgia Gulf said that closing on the financing of its recapitalization, which is the last remaining element of the plan, is expected near the end of next week. If so, the company said the plan will become effective next Friday.

Some 97% of the voting shareholders were in favor of the recapitalization plan. The company said 67.4% of its total shareholders were eligible to vote, based on the Feb. 18 record date.

Under the recapitalization, shareholders will receive $30 in cash, $8.50 principal amount of a new issue of 15% senior subordinated notes maturing in the year 2000, and one share of a new issue of common stock. Through yesterday, shareholders also had the choice, with respect to as much as 30% of their holdings, to receive 5.5 new shares for each share currently held.

Georgia Gulf said yesterday that it was still tallying the final number of shares tendered in that fashion, but noted that a condition of the plan requiring that at least 1.5 million shares elect that choice had been met.

The shareholder approval culminates an eight-month fight by Georgia Gulf to fend off various takeover maneuvers of Mr. Simmons and Houston-based NL Industries Inc., a specialty chemical company that he controls.

J. Landis Martin, president and chief executive officer of NL and a top Simmons strategist, said the group was pleased that Georgia Gulf's plan was approved "and we hope it gets closed quickly." He said the group, which owns 9.6% of Georgia Gulf, will watch how the market values the new stock and debt before deciding whether it will hold on to it or sell.

Mr. Simmons made several offers that were rebuffed by Georgia Gulf's board. In January, Georgia Gulf weighed in with its own recapitalization plan -- but it had to revamp the plan in March because of uncertainty in the junk-bond market.

Mr. Simmons acquiesced earlier this month by allowing his final $45-a-share, or $1 billion, tender offer, which Georgia Gulf had rejected, to expire and he embraced the company's plan.

Regarding the company's lower earnings, Georgia Gulf said it was facing a particularly tough comparison in the first quarter because last year its prices and margins were at historical highs.

Jerry R. Satrum, Georgia Gulf's president and chief operating officer, said that during 1989, especially the first half, "business conditions were robust and pricing reflected spot shortgages for most products." He added that while prices for some products continue to soften, others are higher, and overall volume is "holding at very good levels."

John F. Walker, a company spokesman, said that if the company can sustain its first-quarter performance, "we certainly have room in terms of servicing the debt. We'd be very comfortable at these levels."
