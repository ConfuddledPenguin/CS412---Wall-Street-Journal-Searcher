JAPANESE SHIPS

Japanese shipbuilders signed contracts to build 208 ships with a total gross tonnage of eight million metric tons in the fiscal year ended March 31, an increase of 43% from 5.62 million metric tons a year earlier.

The Japan Shipbuilders' Association attributes the strong performance to bullish sentiment toward the world economic situation and fears by customers that shipbuilders might not have the capacity to meet increased demand for certain types of vessels.

Citing the case of Very Large Crude Carriers, the association asserts 300 VLCCs must be replaced in coming years, but that world shipyards can only build 10 annually.

The Japan Shipbuilders' Association also notes that environmentalist pressure in the U.S. for legislation requiring tankers to be double-hulled could lead to additional new orders for VLCCs.

ENTERPRISE IN CZECHOSLOVAKIA

Czechoslovakia's parliament has approved long-awaited laws permitting private enterprise and the establishment of joint stock companies. The private-enterprise law stipulates that anyone can establish a business without seeking official permission. There are no limits on the number of employees or capital involved in a firm, and private business is allowed in all spheres. The new measures permit the issuing of shares to individuals, groups and companies, and allow the "reasonable" participation of foreign capital in the Czechoslovak economy.

BURMESE ELECTIONS

Western diplomats expect Burma's May 27 elections -- the country's first multi-party polls in 29 years -- to be an army-rigged farce. And Burmese military ruler General Saw Maung has done little to change this pessimistic assessment. In a Wednesday speech at the Defense Ministry in Rangoon, he defended the stringent rules his government has imposed on campaigning and access by opposition parties to statecontrolled radio and television. "We cannot allow a party to do something freely if it affects the freedom of the public," he said.

SINGAPORE RENTS

The cost of renting office space in Singapore has risen 79% since December 1988, according to data compiled by property consultants. Office rent is expected to rise by 20% to 30% in 1990 as available space continues to shrink. New office space, which has been built at an average yearly rate of 1.2 million square feet in recent years, fell to 530,000 square feet in 1988 and 213,000 square feet last year.

CELEBRATING PLASTIC

Plastic, the miracle material hailed as one of the most important inventions since the wheel, has become the subject of a major museum exhibit. London's Victoria and Albert Museum has turned over a whole section to synthetics, ranging from computer keyboards to imitation fruit. While museum officials have tried to exalt the scientific importance of plastic, most visitors appear drawn to nostalgic items, such as cups and plates in ice cream colors, cheap vinyl furniture, ornate sunglasses, and blond Barbie dolls.

EUROPEAN TAXES

The European Community Commission unveiled a two-stage plan aimed at getting EC member states to narrow differences in corporate-tax rates.

The first step would be the adoption by governments of tax legislation to encourage companies to do business across national borders, as part of the EC's plan for a single market by the end of 1992. The second would be to explore how the EC can reconcile differences in national tax systems with the smooth functioning of the unified market.

The EC tax commissioner, Christiane Scrivener, said the commission dropped a 1975 plan to put corporate taxes in a band of 45% to 55%. Instead of standardization, she said, it wants to follow a more pragmatic policy of coordination. "Our aim must be to let companies take full advantage of the single market," she said.
