Bear Stearns Posts 13% Drop in Profit For Its 3rd Quarter

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 04/20/90

NEW YORK -- Even Wall Street's most tightfisted securities firms can't completely shake off the sluggish markets these days. Bear Stearns Cos., renowned for keeping a close eye on costs, posted a 13% drop in net income for the fiscal third quarter ended March 30.

Net fell to $34.7 million, or 34 cents a share, from $41.1 million, or an adjusted 39 cents a share, a year earlier. Per-share results are adjusted for a 5% stock dividend declared yesterday, to be distributed May 31 along with the regular quarterly cash payout of 14 cents a share.

The brokerage and investment-banking firm had quarterly declines of 19% in investment-banking revenue and 15% in commission revenue compared with the year-earlier quarter. The weakness in these areas more than offset strong performances in mortgage-backed securities and corporate bonds.

James Hanbury, an analyst at Wertheim Schroder & Co., said, "It's a typical Bear Stearns quarter -- they're making respectable money in tough times."

Bear Stearns's performance falls in the middle of the pack of earnings reported by Wall Street securities firms. A few firms, including Merrill Lynch & Co. and PaineWebber Group Inc., have managed modest profit gains compared with weak year-earlier quarters; Shearson Lehman Hutton Holdings Inc. posted the biggest quarterly loss ever for a securities firm.

Bear Stearns's results would have been more depressed if it hadn't kept a tight lid on costs. The firm has been able to keep nonpayroll expenses steady for several quarters. As a result, says Diana Keat of Lipper Analytical Securities Corp., "In good times, this is a firm that can really take off."

Revenue for the quarter fell 6%, to $587.6 million, from $623.6 million.

For the nine months, net income plunged 31% to $85.8 million, or 82 cents a share, from $124.8 million, or $1.16 a share, for the year-earlier period. Revenue for the nine months was about flat, at $1.7 billion.

Slowing takeover activity has crimped one of Bear Stearns's bread-and-butter businesses: risk arbitrage, or the trading of takeover stocks. Alan C. Greenberg, chairman and chief executive officer, acknowledged the problems in the risk-arbitrage business but said: "If you're agile enough, you'll still do all right with it."
