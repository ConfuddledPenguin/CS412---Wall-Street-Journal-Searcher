Lockheed Posts 11% Gain in Net, 8% Rise in Sales --- Aerospace Concern Didn't Take Any Write-Downs For the First Quarter

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 04/20/90

CALABASAS, Calif. -- Lockheed Corp., with improved performances from its technological services and aeronautical systems segments, said first-quarter profit increased 11% on an 8% sales climb. Perhaps most importantly, the nation's No. 6 Pentagon contractor had no writedowns for the first three months of 1990, marking the first time in a year that the aerospace concern hasn't been plagued by cost overruns on one project or another.

But it may take some doing to keep the slate clean from here on out. Lockheed is now locked in negotiations with the Navy over terms of production on the P-7 anti-submarine aircraft. And if the government were bent on holding the company to pricing commitments it made earlier, it could "drag the whole corporation down the tubes," said Michael Beltramo, a Los Angeles defense industry consultant.

Even a relatively small write-down on the P-7 would be a blow to Lockheed's chairman, Daniel Tellep, who repeatedly trumpeted during the company's recent proxy fight with Dallas billionaire Harold Simmons that he had finally put such cost overruns behind the company.

For the first quarter, Lockheed earned $69 million, or $1.09 a share, vs. $62 million, or $1.04 a share. The year-earlier results included $4 million in losses at the company's information services businesses, which have since been sold. Sales jumped to $2.25 billion from $2.09 billion.

Across the company, profits tripled to $15 million from an anemic $5 million at the aeronautical systems unit, and rose to $13 million from $9 million in the technological services area. Lockheed said it also benefited from a lower income-tax rate.

Those gains were partially offset, however, by a 5% drop in profits at the company's missiles and space unit; the cost of the proxy contest with Mr. Simmons's company, NL Industries Inc., which ran to about $15 million; and a 90% plunge in earnings to $1 million from $10 million in the electronics segment, where the revenue base shrunk significantly.

But it's the potential for much bigger problems at Lockheed that has the industry alarmed right now. The company already has written off $300 million for the development of the P-7 because its engineers miscalculated how much the plane had in common with Lockheed's old submarine hunter, the P-3.

When it signed the development pact, Lockheed also apparently gave certain target projections for costs of production. Now, with the program having slipped two years behind schedule, it has become clear that there is no way it can stick to those numbers. Some trade publications have even speculated that if the Navy were to exercise its options and press Lockheed, the company could stand to lose nearly $3 billion from the 125-plane accord.

That doesn't seem likely to happen though. Since the beginning of the year, Mr. Tellep has met several times with Navy Secretary Lawrence Garrett to work out a compromise on the P-7. Service officials close to the secretary suggested that the two sides are trying to determine how much it really will cost at this point to manufacture P-7s, how many can be afforded in a tighter budget environment and who would absorb what costs.

Whatever the Navy decides to do, Mr. Tellep has made it clear to Wall Street analysts that Lockheed isn't going to build P-7s at a deficit. He even indicated that the Navy could ultimately decide just to terminate the contract, letting Lockheed off the hook. It's unclear what would happen if the company were to unilaterally default.

Mr. Tellep has declined to be interviewed about the P-7 for the past few weeks.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Lockheed shares closed at $35.875, down 75 cents.

Separately, Lockheed responded to a federal court suit filed eariler this week by Mr. Simmons that challenged the legality and fairness of last month's director elections. Lockheed, in a statement, called the action "nothing more than pure harassment that will continue to divert management attention and corporate resources."
