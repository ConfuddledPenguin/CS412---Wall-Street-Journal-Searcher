Santa Fe Pacific Settles ETSI Case For $350 Million

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 04/20/90

CHICAGO -- Santa Fe Pacific Corp., operating the past year under the cloud of a court judgment that it pay $750 million to a pipeline joint venture, settled the case for $350 million. A settlement with Energy Transportation Systems Inc. had been far from certain. In addition, the final figure came in well below the $400 million to $450 million analysts expected would be required to extricate Santa Fe from the huge judgment, which was the result of an unfavorable ruling in an antitrust case.

In composite New York Stock Exchange trading yesterday, Santa Fe shares jumped $1.75 to close at $21.625, on volume of nearly 900,000 shares.

Not only was the settlement "smart, it was imperative," said First Boston Corp. analyst Graeme Lidgerwood, who added that Santa Fe has been "basically blocked" from progressing with its restructuring plans "until they could put the ETSI litigation behind them."

The settlement, combined with accelerated royalties received under a recent agreement that resolved a separate long-running lawsuit filed by Texas Utilities Co., resulted in an after-tax first-quarter charge of $115 million, Santa Fe said; yesterday the company reported a net loss of $61.4 million, or 39 cents a share.

Under the agreement, the four ETSI partners will receive $150 million in cash "as early as June 1," with a promissory note for the balance. Santa Fe will pay $25 million in 1991 and 1992, and $30 million in each of five succeeding years.

The partners of the failed, Houston-based joint venture will sell the promissory note, rather than wait for the payments. In a release, the companies said Enron Corp. and Panhandle Eastern Corp. will each receive, after deduction of legal fees, $69 million in pretax proceeds. K N Energy Inc., with a lesser stake in the failed venture, will receive about $47 million. The fourth member, a unit of closely held Bechtel Group Inc., with an indicated stake roughly similar to K N Energy's, didn't disclose what it will receive.

In the 1980s, ETSI had planned to construct a coal-slurry pipeline linking the coal beds of Wyoming to Oklahoma and Texas. The project was never built, and the venture subsequently sued a number of railroads -- which profit from hauling coal to the region's utilities -- for allegedly conspiring to block the project. Santa Fe was the only defendant that didn't settle the antitrust lawsuit before trial.

Last year, after a federal judge ruled against Santa Fe, the company ended up owing adjusted damages of $750 million. Although it had appealed the judgment, it signaled repeatedly that it would be amenable to a settlement.

"We continue to believe that we are innocent of the charges in the ETSI case," said Santa Fe's chairman, president and chief executive officer, Robert D. Krebs. But the settlement, he said, is "the prudent course to follow in view of the previous court action and the uncertainty and cost of the appeal and retrial process."

First Boston's Ms. Lidgerwood noted that the spread out payments called for under the settlement will help the company's cash flow. The company this summer probably will replace the $780 million in payment-in-kind debentures it issued under a 1988 recapitalization, she said. The timetable for the planned spin-off of the company's 80%-owned real-estate operation, and the unannounced but widely expected spinoff of its energy unit, may advance as a result of the settlement, she noted.
