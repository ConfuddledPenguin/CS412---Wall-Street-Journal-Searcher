Law -- Legal Beat: Suit Against Fannie Mae Claims Redlining

  • Author: Milo Geyelin and Wayne E. Green
  • Date: 04/20/90

A St. Louis developer is suing the Federal National Mortgage Association, claiming that the agency has declined to finance projects in a section of North St. Louis because it is too blighted. The suit is the first in the nation against Fannie Mae to allege redlining, the practice by lenders of rejecting loan applications in poor neighborhoods. Filed by developer Bruce Ring, the suit seeks $103.5 million in damages against both Fannie Mae and First Interstate Mortgage Co. of Illinois, its local agent in St. Louis.

Fannie Mae is a federally chartered, publicly owned company that stimulates housing development in poor and moderate-income neighborhoods by buying mortgages and refinancing them at reduced rates to qualified developers.

A Fannie Mae spokesman said this is the first time the company has been accused of redlining. "Fannie Mae does not accept or condone or permit redlining in any fashion," said David Jeffers. "All you have to do is look at our underwriting standards and see that is something we take very seriously." The agency says it has provided refinancing for four projects in North St. Louis.

Mr. Ring's allegations are being investigated by Sen. John C. Danforth, (R, Mo.), and Reps. William L. Clay and Richard A. Gephardt, both Democrats from St. Louis. All three have asked Housing and Urban Development Secretary Jack Kemp to investigate.

Mr. Ring applied to Fannie Mae through First Interstate last August to refinance $3.6 million in short-term loans so he could renovate seven run-down apartment buildings in St. Louis. Five of the buildings are in the city's depressed northern section. According to his suit, filed in U.S. District Court in St. Louis, Fannie Mae rejected the entire application after a First Interstate assistant vice-president inspected the properties.

According to the suit, the bank official described two of the properties as "pioneer locations," adding that he "would not personally want to invest in either location." Another location, he wrote, "definitely had some rough areas within a few blocks."

Mr. Ring is asking for $100 million in punitive damages plus $3.5 million, which he says is the difference between his aggregate mortgage payments over 35 years through conventional financing and the 8.1% rate available through Fannie Mae.

Pretrial hearings in Mr. Ring's suit are scheduled for April 27.

COMPANIES MUST DISCLOSE shaky finances to some applicants, a Colorado court rules.

An employer may not have to divulge its financial condition to every job applicant it interviews, the Colorado Court of Appeals said. But full disclosure is required if a company has made statements to an applicant that would create a "false impression" about its outlook and the applicant's future employment prospects.

The decision earlier this month upheld a jury's award of $250,000 in damages to Roberta Berger, who had sued Security Pacific Information Services Inc. for fraudulent concealment arising out of her hiring and termination by the Denver company.

Ms. Berger was recruited by the company, and had moved from New Orleans to Denver to accept a job in January 1986. She was terminated some eight months later, after the computer project she had been hired to manage was discontinued. Ms. Berger contended that in interviews for the job, Security Pacific Information had presented a positive picture of the company and her future, concealing its financial losses and the "substantial, known risk" that her project might soon be abandoned.

The appeals court upheld a jury award that included nearly $92,000 in actual damages, covering lost earnings and losses on the sale of a house Ms. Berger bought in Denver. Punitive damages made up the balance of the award.

Andrew T. Brake, a Denver attorney who represented Ms. Berger, noted that Colorado is a state that permits employers under certain conditions to terminate a worker without cause under a so-called employment-at-will law.

Attorneys for Security Pacific Information couldn't be reached for comment.

CHIEF ATTORNEY IN AT&T antitrust litigation will open his own law firm.

George L. Saunders Jr., a partner with the Chicago law firm of Sidley & Austin, is leaving the firm in June to open his own law firm. He hopes to recruit a small group of "older, successful lawyers" who want to leave big-firm practice, he says.

Mr. Saunders, 58 years old, is not taking any business with him, including American Telephone & Telegraph Co., the client that brought him national attention. Mr. Saunders was AT&T's lead trial counsel in the long-running antitrust litigation brought by the Justice Department and MCI Communications Corp. that led to the breakup of the Bell System.

H. Blair White, a Sidley & Austin partner, says he was surprised Mr. Saunders decided to leave after 28 years at the firm. "It's a very ambitious thing to do in your career at that point," Mr. White says. Sidley partner Howard J. Trienens said Mr. Saunders has been frustrated by having to turn away some business because of conflict-of-interest problems with other clients of the law firm.

Mr. Saunders says he has long contemplated the move. "Maybe you could call it a midlife crisis," he says.

JUDGE CLEARS THE WAY for lawsuit accusing Exxon and Conoco of rigging oil prices.

In federal court in Manhattan, Judge William Conner rejected the oil companies' motions to dismiss the suit, which was filed in 1986 by Transnor Ltd., an oil trader based in Bermuda. The suit accuses Exxon Corp., Conoco Inc. and a Conoco subsidiary of conspiring to depress certain crude oil prices in order to evade taxes in Britain. It seeks damages totaling about $261 million, stemming from losses Transnor says it suffered because of the lower prices.

Transnor accused the companies of forcing down the price of Brent oil, a widely traded North Sea crude oil, by selling cargoes of the oil at below-market prices. Transnor had bought two cargoes of Brent oil, but then refused to accept delivery after the market value plunged. Transnor alleges that after forcing the oil prices down, the oil companies were able to use a trading technique know as tax spinning to increase profit margins on the refined oil products. Britain taxes refinery profits at a lower rate than crude oil profits.

Exxon and Conoco argued that the case should be thrown out on several grounds -- including their claim that a conspiracy is unlikely because they would have hurt themselves by depressing oil prices. Judge Conner, however, said it is plausible that the alleged pricing scheme's tax benefits and other advantages could have offset the companies' lower crude oil revenue.

The oil companies, which previously have said the suit is without merit, also claimed that the case should be dismissed because the Brent oil market is an international market. They argued that a U.S. court has no jurisdiction in such disputes. But Judge Conner said that most of the trading in oil contracts is done in New York and Boston.

A spokesman for Exxon said the company was disappointed that Judge Conner refused to dismiss the case. Officials at Conoco, a unit of Du Pont Co., couldn't be reached for comment.

Edward Swan, an attorney for Transnor, said the trial is set to begin May 21.

BASKIN FLAHERTY suffers Pittsburgh defections.

Half the members of the Pittsburgh office of Baskin Flaherty Elliott & Mannino, a Philadelphia-based regional law firm, have resigned, including name partner and former Chairman Philip Baskin.

Mr. Baskin left last week. About 15 of Baskin Flaherty's 32 lawyers in Pittsburgh also have left to begin their own firm. All of the defections took place amid rumors of dissatisfaction over the direction of the firm.

Baskin Flaherty is developing a more specialized practice in complex litigation, financial services, health law and environmental law, said the firm's President Edward F. Mannino, who specializes in banking litigation, and John M. Elliott, also a name partner.

The Pittsburgh office, said Mr. Elliott, was more oriented toward less sophisticated litigation in domestic relations, personal injury and real estate, which Mr. Elliott said were not compatible with the firm's goals.

"It's a question of how you emphasize things," said Mr. Baskin. "We happen to think that while the new area is very important and should be properly addressed, you don't throw the old things out, too."

Baskin Flaherty is currently filling the positions and opening a seven-lawyer office in Washington D.C.

In Pittsburgh, meanwhile, the firm recently hired Charles Sheehey, former U.S. attorney for the Western District of Pennsylvania, as head of that office's litigation department.
