International: U.S. Won't Say if South Korea Will Be On Pending Unfair Trade Partners List

  • Author: Damon Darlin
  • Date: 04/20/90

SEOUL, South Korea -- U.S. trade officials said they were encouraged after talks with Korean counterparts this week, but they won't say if enough progress has been made to keep Korea off a pending U.S. list of unfair trade partners. Korean news reports Thursday had claimed the U.S. agreed to drop South Korea from the list of unfair traders if Seoul joins the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade's government procurement code. These are international rules that govern government purchases of imported goods. But a U.S. official, in Seoul for several days of trade talks, said that no such promise had been made. She said that President Bush will make that decision based on recommendations from various government departments and agencies.

The 1988 U.S. Trade Act requires the president to list by April 30 each year those countries practicing unfair trade. South Korea is the fourth largest supplier of goods to the U.S. government, but doesn't have clear rules on buying imported goods for government use. "While we were encouraged by the information we heard," the Washington official said, "no one piece of information will determine the outcome."

Even acceptance of the GATT government procurement code won't necessarily end disagreements on government purchases of U.S. products. Japan was an early signatory to the code and the U.S. still faces significant Japanese reluctance to buy U.S. goods.

The trade negotiators also discussed a host of other issues, including implementation of prior agreements. "Generally speaking, we are pleased with the way the agreements are being implemented," said a U.S. official, who asked not to be identified. "There are minor problems, but that is always the case."

The trade officials also are preparing final adjustments to a trade agreement on U.S. beef imports into Korea. The agreement was worked out in Washington last month and a U.S. official said the agreement could be signed next week.

The working-level officials also established an official group to review technical regulations for food safety that affect agricultural imports by both nations. There have been numerous tiffs between the two countries on imported products that may or may not have carried harmful pesticides. Officials agreed to postpone discussions on telecommunications disputes.

The South Korean government also presented complaints to the U.S., including its claims that American tobacco companies market their products unfairly and that U.S. regulations for imported fruit aren't clear.
