Economy: Regional Disparity in Economic Growth Narrowed Last Year, Government Says

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 04/20/90

WASHINGTON -- Disparities in economic growth among different parts of the country narrowed last year as the New England economy slowed and the Rocky Mountain and Southwestern economies accelerated, new Commerce Department figures show. After narrowing for 40 years, regional differences in per-capita income widened significantly in the 1980s. But the new figures support the predictions of some economists that the last decade saw powerful but transitory economic shocks rather than a reversal of the long-running trend.

Per-capita personal income grew by 6.6% in New England last year, a tad better than the 6.5% national average but far slower than the 8% growth to which the region had become accustomed in recent years.

Because New England's economy grew so much faster than that of the rest of the country earlier in the 1980s, however, that six-state region remains the richest part of the U.S. with income of $21,509 per person, more than 20% above the national average of $17,567.

Personal income includes wages, earnings of the self-employed, rent, interest and dividends. It isn't adjusted for taxes, inflation or differences in living costs among regions.

Earnings increases in New England's manufacturing and service industries last year were the smallest since 1975, the Commerce Department said, and construction earnings declined for the first time since that year.

In contrast, economic growth accelerated in the five Rocky Mountain states for the third consecutive year. Per-capita income grew 6.7% in 1989, up from 5.4% in 1988 and 4% in 1987. The region still lags much of the country, with per-capita income of $15,316.

Earnings in service industries in the Rocky Mountain states grew faster than the national average for the first time since 1982. Construction earnings picked up after three years of declines. Farm income also rose. Only in Wyoming did the growth in per-capita income slip below the 1988 rate.

In the four-state Southwest region that includes Texas, per-capita income grew by 6.1%, outstripping the 5.4% growth in 1988 and 3% in 1987. But at $15,231, the region's per-capita income was below that of every other section of the country.

The Southeast has the poorest states, but the region's average is lifted by the prosperity of Virginia and Florida. Mississippi had the lowest per-capita personal income last year at $11,835. Connecticut had the highest at $24,604.
