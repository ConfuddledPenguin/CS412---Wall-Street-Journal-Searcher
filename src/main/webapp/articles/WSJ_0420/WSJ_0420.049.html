International Moves Are Urged to Combat Money Laundering

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/20/90

TOKYO -- International cooperation and financial vigilance must be increased to establish a "minimum standard" to combat money laundering, a task force convened to study the problem said. The Financial Action Task Force called for rapid, tough and practical measures to address the problem as a part of overall international work to stop traffic in drugs. Recommendations include closer monitoring by financial institutions of transactions, more stringent laws and more extensive cooperation by national and international authorities.

But acknowledging both the difficulty of such broad coordination and some disagreement within its ranks, the task force said that "political impetus would also be particularly necessary to crystallize strong coordinated overall international action" to hinder laundering of profits from drug trafficking.

The task force was created at the Paris summit of the Group of Seven in July 1989 to study the money-laundering process, the measures already in place to combat money laundering, and to recommend further action. Fifteen industrialized nations and the Commission of the European Communities participated.

The task force noted that differences among laws and financial regulations of various nations can be exploited by drug traffickers. National measures to fight the problem, then, must be "harmonized" to have greatest impact.

Most signficantly, laws to protect the secrecy of financial institutions should be conceived so they don't interfere with the task force's recommendations, the report said.

It stressed, however, that measures to fight money laundering must not be allowed to inhibit free flow of capital.
