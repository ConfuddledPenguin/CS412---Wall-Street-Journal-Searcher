Marketing & Media: Grumman Considers New Layoffs; Aides To Get `Parachutes'

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/20/90

BETHPAGE, N.Y. -- Grumman Corp. is weighing new layoffs while promising generous severance pay to top executives in the event they lose their jobs following a takeover. John O'Brien, Grumman chairman and president, said after the annual meeting yesterday that the defense company's first-quarter earnings will be flat compared with last year's first-quarter profit of $16 million, or 46 cents a share. That confirms expectations of securities analysts who follow the company.

Mr. O'Brien said the company is reviewing all aspects of its business, including "staffing levels across the board," partly with the aim of cutting expenses. "I have no doubt that we can take many millions of dollars of annual operating cost out of this company without sacrificing anything of business value," he said.

In the proxy statement for the meeting, Grumman also disclosed the new severance packages, known as "golden parachutes," which cover 27 "key employees," including Mr. O'Brien and his top four officers. The plan would pay the employees 2.99 times their average annual base pay for the previous five years, if they are dismissed after a change in control of the company. Mr. O'Brien's base pay in 1989 was $384,375, before a $180,000 bonus.

Mr. O'Brien said the companywide review, aided by the management consultant, McKinsey & Co., could lead to the divestiture of parts of the corporation. "We're examining the businesses we're in and how they fit into our corporate strategy. If there are some that don't fit, we'll divest them," he said.

Mr. O'Brien added that Grumman expects to receive a production contract for the Air Force's Joint STARS flying surveillance and command system next year, which he has previously stated could produce between $6 billion and $10 billion in revenue. He added that Grumman anticipates that its international business will grow by $500 million a year during the next five years.
