Campeau's Proposal To Defer Payments Backed by Big Bank

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/20/90

TORONTO -- National Bank of Canada said it would support a Campeau Corp. proposal to defer its interest payments on 184 million Canadian dollars (US$158.4 million) of 7% convertible debentures. Andre Berard, president and chief executive officer of National Bank, said he would agree to an interest-payment deferral for Campeau because the troubled retailer is "basically sound" and needs time to restructure its finances.

"I will agree to any decent proposal that is to give those people the time and the freedom to restructure and do a better job," Mr. Berard said after a luncheon speech here.

Montreal-based National Bank holds C$60.4 million of the 7 1/2% debentures. It registered them under its name in January along with other Campeau securities when Campeau's chairman, Robert Campeau, defaulted on personal loans by missing interest payments on C$153 million of debt.

Campeau missed interest payments last month on its two series of convertible debentures. At a special meeting June 1, Campeau will ask holders of its 7 1/2% debentures to waive defaults triggered by the missed payments until Dec. 31, 1991.

National Bank has a 35% fully diluted voting stake in Campeau and two seats on its board. Olympia & York Developments Ltd., the real estate concern owned by Toronto's Reichmann family, holds C$46.6 million of the 7 1/2% debentures and all of Campeau's US$260 million of 7% convertible debentures. Sources close to Olympia & York previously said the Reichmanns likely would be willing to support an interest-payment deferral on the debentures.

National Bank's Mr. Berard said Campeau's Canadian properties and its U.S. retailing units, Federated Department Stores Inc. and Allied Stores Corp., are high quality assets even though Federated and Allied are in Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings and the real estate assets largely are pledged as collateral for loans.

"I think if we take the time, if we compromise, if we support a restructuring of that group, we'll end up better three years down the road than we are today," Mr. Berard said.

As for Mr. Campeau's personal financial problems stemming from the bankruptcy-law filing of Federated and Allied and the subsequent plunge in Campeau's stock price, Mr. Berard said the situation was "difficult." He declined to say whether the bank would seek a court order in Ontario enabling it to seize Mr. Campeau's personal assets.

Mr. Campeau is facing a C$50 million lawsuit from National Bank and a C$12.5 million lawsuit from Bank of Nova Scotia related to defaults on his personal loans. Last week, Bank of Montreal obtained a court order requiring Mr. Campeau to repay C$26.7 million outstanding in a personal loan. The order could entitle the bank to seize Mr. Campeau's personal assets.
