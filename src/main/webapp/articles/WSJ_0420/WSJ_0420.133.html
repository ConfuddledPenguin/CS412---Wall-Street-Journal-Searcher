Abreast of the Market: Big Stocks Fall as Investors Leave Firms With Unfavorable Earnings

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 04/20/90

NEW YORK -- Big stocks tumbled yesterday as investors moved out of consumer issues and stocks of companies with bad earnings reports and continued to fret over the sagging bond market. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 20.94 to 2711.94, the second decline in as many days. Stock-index arbitrage sell programs pushed stocks into steeper losses in late trading. Volume totaled 152,930,000 shares on the New York Stock Exchange. That was the most since April 4 when 159,540,000 shares traded, but still below last year's daily average volume of 165,470,000. Declining issues on the Big Board outnumbered advancers 968 to 511.

The 30-stock Dow Average fell 0.77%, while the New York Stock Exchange Composite index fell 0.73%. Big consumer and other non-industrial shares in the Dow average were heaviest hit.

Coca-Cola tumbled 1 7/8 to 77 3/8. A "Heard on the Street" column in this newspaper suggested that shares of Coca-Cola and PepsiCo are overpriced. Pepsi fell 7/8 to 65 1/2.

American Express, another stock in the industrial average, fell 1 1/4 to 27 5/8 on volume of 1.5 million shares. American Express reported a $1.50 a share loss for the first quarter, including charges related to the restructuring of Shearson Lehman Hutton. Shearson Lehman Hutton fell 1/2 to 13.

Among other big stocks in the Dow average were Procter & Gamble, down 1 to 69 1/4, and Boeing, off 3/8 to 71 1/8.

Outside of the Dow industrials, buyers were picking selectively among secondary issues. "If you look outside Procter & Gamble, Coke and Boeing, you can easily notice some demand," said Edward Laux, head of block trading at Kidder Peabody.

PPG Industries, for example, rose 1 1/4 to 44 5/8. The company reported first quarter earnings rose to $1.16 a share from $1.15 a year earlier, and boosted its quarterly dividend to 42 cents from 38 cents.

Yesterday marked a pronounced rotation out of consumer stocks and into shares of companies whose fortunes are tied to the pace of economic activity. Among the so-called cyclical sectors, Ford gained 1/4 to 48 1/4 and General Motors gained 7/8 to 46 7/8.

However, a weak bond market continued to rattle stock investors. The 30-year Treasury bond closed down nearly 1/8 of a point, or almost $1.25 for each $1,000 face amount, after falling more than one point Wednesday.

Peter Canelo, investment strategist at Bear Stearns, said weak bond prices appear to be more of an influence over stocks at this point than first-quarter earnings reports.

While overall earnings appear to be down again, Mr. Canelo said the decline isn't as steep. Quarterly results from a group of 98 companies tracked by Mr. Canelo show an average decline of about 7% from a year earlier, compared with a fourth-quarter drop of about 15%.

Shares of Comdisco plunged 8 1/2 to close at 18 in Big Board trading of 2.6 million shares after the company announced that second-quarter earnings are expected to be as much as 30% lower than the year-ago period. The volume was 30 times its average daily volume. Analysts had been forecasting that net for the quarter would be above the 60 cents a share that the company reported in the year-ago period.

Comdisco executives called the sharp stock decline an overreaction by Wall Street. Comdisco attributed the projected earnings drop to customer jitters over the impending, yet unknown, introduction date of International Business Machines' Summit products. This left Comdisco's customers leary of long-term leases on mainframe computer equipment, and they instead opted for short-term leases during the second quarter ended March 31, the company said.

Silicon Graphics plunged 3 5/8 to 31 3/4 despite reporting that earnings for the fiscal third quarter, ended last month, were almost triple the year-ago figures.

Earnings-related selling also weighed on Reebok International, which fell 1 1/2 to 17 1/2 on a disappointing 7% increase in its first-quarter earnings; Alaska Air Group fell 1 1/4 to 23 3/8, Diamond Shamrock declined 3/4 to 24 1/2, and Charles Schwab dropped 5/8 to 15 1/4.

Conversely, Digital Equipment gained 2 1/8 to 82 1/8 on 1.9 million shares after releasing its earnings for the fiscal third quarter, ended last month. While its net income was down 90% from a year earlier -- largely because of a charge for restructuring -- analysts said the company's operating results were better than expected.

Other issues to benefit from strong quarterly earnings reports included Glenfed, which rose 7/8 to 12 3/4; Oregon Steel Mills, up 1 3/4 to 37 3/4, and Vista Chemical climbed 1 to 4 1/4 in its second straight session of heavy trading amid speculation that Thomson of Canada may be interested in acquiring the company. Harcourt officials weren't available for comment, while Thomson declined comment.

Harcourt was the most active New York Stock Exchange issue on volume of about 2.8 million shares. More than 1.3 million were traded on Wednesday, when the stock rose 1/4 point. The company's 12% preferred stock was also active in the latest session; the stock issue rose to 1/2 to 3 3/8 as 1.5 million changed hands.

Dayton Hudson dropped 1 1/4 to 69 1/2 after announcement of its agreement to acquire the Marshall Field's department-store chain for about $1.04 billion from Britain's B.A.T Industries. B.A.T gained 1/16 to 11 7/8 in American Stock Exchange trading.

Santa Fe Pacific, which agreed to a $350 million settlement of litigation regarding an alleged conspiracy to block construction of a coal-slurry pipeline, climbed 1 5/8 to 21 1/2. A federal court had ordered the company last year to pay $750.1 million in the case.

Raychem gained 1 3/8 to 26 3/4. Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette raised its investment rating on the stock to "buy" from "neutral," even though it lowered its estimate of fiscal 1990 earnings to a range of 40 to 50 cents a share from 60 cents.

Nord Resources dropped 1 to 15 1/8. Shearson Lehman Hutton lowered its earnings estimate for 1990 by 10 cents a share, to $1.20, after learning from the company that it hasn't yet secured any commercial orders for its new high-grade kaolin products.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 2.49 to 350.73. Volume totaled 15,100,000 shares. There were 226 issues advancing and 346 declining.

Texas Air rose 1/2 to 6 5/8 in the wake of a federal judge's decision to name a trustee to manage its Eastern Airlines unit, currently in Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings. The company said it wouldn't appeal the ruling, which also released $80 million in funds from an escrow account.

A.T. Cross Class A slid 1 5/8 to 30 7/8 after estimating that first-quarter earnings declined 40% and sales fell 8% from year-earlier levels.
