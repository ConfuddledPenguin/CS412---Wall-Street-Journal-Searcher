Technology & Medicine: Lotus Net Income Tripled in Quarter On 38% Sales Rise

  • Author: John R. Wilke
  • Date: 04/20/90

Continuing a strong rebound, Lotus Development Corp. said first-quarter profit more than tripled on a 38% sales gain. The big software maker reported net income of $22.8 million, or 53 cents a share, compared with weak year-ago net of $5.3 million, or 13 cents a share. Sales rose to $165.5 million from $119.9 million.

Lotus's first-quarter results were at the high end of analysts' expectations, and its shares closed at $34.375, up $1.125, in active national over-the-counter trading.

Lotus is entering a strong new-product cycle and is expected to show respectable growth this year. But there were some troubling signs in the mix of product sales disclosed yesterday that suggest the recent growth rate will be difficult to sustain.

Lotus, based in Cambridge, Mass., said sales of its flagship 1-2-3 product line were strong but that customers were buying far more copies of the less-expensive version 2.2 than version 3.0, a more powerful product with higher profit margins. The company had hoped for a roughly even split in sales between these two products. Instead, 3.0 amounted to about 24% of total spreadsheet sales while 2.2 accounted for about 70%, with the rest coming from older versions.

Revenue from software-license upgrades also fell during the quarter. "Upgrades are clearly winding down," says David Readerman of Shearson Lehman Hutton, though he expects them to continue to boost results during 1990. One surprise was the strong sales of 1-2-3 versions designed to work in computer networks, executives said.

Lotus said international sales were especially strong during the quarter, particularly in Japan, and accounted for more than half of total revenue for the first time. But domestic sales grew just 9%, according to Richard Sherlund of Goldman, Sachs & Co., a "puzzling slowdown for which Lotus had no good explanation," he said.

Lotus surprised the software industry earlier this month when it said it would merge with Novell Inc. of Provo, Utah. Lotus is the largest maker of personal computer spreadsheets, while Novell is the leader in the software needed to run networks. Lotus has said that the combination of the two companies is a good strategic fit because of the increasing importance of computer networks.

Robert Schechter, Lotus's chief financial officer, said the two companies expect to complete a definitive merger agreement in about three weeks. The agreement is structured as a straight stock swap, valued at about $1.5 billion.

The merger agreement, subject to approval by two-thirds of Novell's shareholders, offers no premium to the Novell holders. Because of this, there have been suggestions that Novell's large institutional holders might try to force better terms for the deal. But analysts were skeptical yesterday that such a move would succeed.
