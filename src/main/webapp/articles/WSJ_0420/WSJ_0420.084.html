Politics & Policy: House Committee Challenges Bush On Budget Deficit

  • Author: Jeffrey H. Birnbaum
  • Date: 04/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Budget Committee approved a deficit-reduction plan that would cut military-spending authority more and Medicare less than initially proposed by the House leadership. The $1.2 trillion plan, approved on a 21-14 party-line vote, is a direct challenge to President Bush. It would set military-spending authority for the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1 at $283 billion, $24 billion less than the president requested. It also calls for fewer cuts in nonmilitary programs than Mr. Bush wants and for a $13.9 billion tax increase.

The panel's chairman, Rep. Leon Panetta of California, and other members of the House Democratic leadership initially had backed a plan that called for $286 billion in military-spending authority. Liberals complained that the figure was too high, and yesterday Mr. Panetta proposed the lower figure, which was approved by the committee.

Though liberals seemed pleased by the change, conservative Democrats were uneasy. As many as 40 conservative Democrats could abandon the budget blueprint when it comes to the floor in the coming weeks; such defections could jeopardize the plan's prospects as few Republicans are expected to support it.

In addition to the changes in military spending, the panel altered the Democratic leadership's proposal so that it would cut less from Medicare. At the request of Chairman Dan Rostenkowski (D., Ill.) of the House Ways and Means Committee, the Medicare cut was set at $1.7 billion, compared with the leadership's $2.1 billion, aides said. The president had proposed a $5.2 billion reduction.

What's more, the panel agreed to add $200 million in new spending, bringing the total for new initiatives in the budget blueprint to $6.2 billion. The extra money would go to child-welfare programs, among other things. Where the extra money would come from remained largely undefined.

Budget resolutions lay out a blueprint for spending and tax legislation by other committees. The current one is designed to reduce the deficit in fiscal 1991 to $64 billion from the projected gap of $100.5 billion that would occur without new legislation.

But more than half the $36 billion in the hoped-for deficit reduction is contingent on yet-to-occur negotiations with the president. The resolution doesn't require the president's signature, but Democrats made clear it will require his acquiescence if it is to be put into effect.

On tax increases, Rep. Rostenkowski is expected to refrain from offering his own plan. That would bog down the tax-writing process. The Ways and Means chairman is said to be considering an attempt to send to the House floor legislation that would increase the gasoline tax without his committee's official recommendation.

The military-spending plan approved by the House panel would provide for $295 billion in actual outlays during the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1, about $8 billion less than the president requested.

In the Senate, meanwhile, Armed Services Committee Chairman Sam Nunn (D., Ga.) announced that he also will push for deeper cuts in military spending than the administration has proposed for the next few years.

Breaking months of silence on specific defense-budget numbers, Sen. Nunn urged removing 200,000 to 225,000 of the more than 300,000 troops stationed in Europe by the mid-1990s. Such cuts would be nearly three times larger than advocated by the administration. Sen. Nunn also wants to save money by keeping largely support units, rather than heavy-tank forces and other front-line combat troops, in West Germany.

Sen. Nunn singled out the Navy for trying to avoid deep cuts by balking at mothballing aircraft carriers and turning over other ships to the reserves. In light of budget pressures and changes in EastWest relations, the Georgia lawmaker said, the Navy should be able to operate with 10 or 12 carriers, compared with the current fleet of 14.

Describing the House Democratic defense number as lower than he would like but still "within the range of responsibility," Sen. Nunn said he will unveil his overall Pentagon-spending blueprint in the next few days.
