Autos: GM Quietly Raises Prices on Options Despite Soft Sales

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 04/20/90

DETROIT -- Despite sluggish consumer demand for many of its cars, General Motors Corp. has quietly raised prices for options that could increase the cost of some of its cars and trucks by up to $100. GM's pricing action follows a similar move last month by rival auto maker Ford Motor Co., and continues a pattern set by GM and Ford last year of raising prices on optional equipment early in the spring selling season. This year, Ford raised the prices for options on most cars by an average of $29. GM officials said they couldn't determine the average increase that would result from its changes.

The price increases will help the No. 1 and No. 2 auto makers boost revenues per car and bolster earnings in a weak market. But they might also be a competitive liability. Officials of Chrysler Corp., Toyota Motor Corp. and Honda Motor Co. said their companies haven't increased prices since the model year began last fall.

Unlike Ford, GM didn't officially announce its increase, but quietly notified its dealers of the changes.

Dealer reaction ranged from frustration to resignation. "We can't sell cars as it is," said Boston dealer Robert Brest, who sells several GM lines. With most rebate programs scheduled to end soon, Mr. Brest figures GM will do something to stimulate showroom traffic. "They must have something up their sleeves."

Other dealers said the increases won't make much difference. "They may as well raise prices when they're not selling cars," said a Pontiac dealer in New Jersey.

Both GM and Ford have been struggling to prop up sales in recent weeks in the face of soft consumer demand. Sales of all domestic and imported vehicles dropped 2.9% in March despite heavy discounting by the Big Three and many foreign car makers. GM's total vehicle sales dropped 1.3% in March, while Ford's fell 6.1%.

A GM spokesman said yesterday the company raised the option prices to recover higher costs for raw materials and health care. "We believe that these are modest adjustments," a GM spokesman said, adding that the company considered what the marketplace would bear before raising prices.

The changes will affect GM's most popular options, such as cruise control, power door locks and power windows. Prices for those items increased $15 apiece. Now the most expensive power door lock system will cost $280, the most expensive power window system will cost $325, and cruise control will cost an average of $210.

GM didn't change prices on air conditioning. Before last summer, the auto maker had discounted some of its options when sold together as a group. But last summer the company halted that practice in most of its divisions.

Price increases on power seats will range from $20 to $55. GM also hit some of its pickup trucks with a $20 rise on optional gasoline engines and a $10 increase on four-speed automatic transmissions.

The changes apply to all of GM's auto divisions, but Cadillac will be the least affected because many of the options are standard on most of its luxury cars.

Because the price increases affect GM cars built after April 2, consumers should pay less for cars built earlier in the year that are still sitting on dealer lots.
