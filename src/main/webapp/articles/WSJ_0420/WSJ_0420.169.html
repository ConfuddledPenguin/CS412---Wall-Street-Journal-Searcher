New Pesticide Policy May Tighten Rules For Processed Foods

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency announced a new policy that could allow wider regulation of cancer-causing pesticides found in processed foods, but it is unlikely to reduce pesticide residues anytime soon. For the first time, the EPA said it might ban uses of pesticides that have long been on the market if their residues in processed foods pose more than a minimal cancer risk to consumers. Up to now, the agency had agreed to enforce a cancer-risk standard on processed foods only for newly registered pesticides; environmental groups said that left out at least 66 pesticides that are suspected of causing cancer in test animals.

While the new policy is a theoretical victory, it is "really a mixed blessing," said Janet Hathaway, a lawyer with the Natural Resources Defense Council, an environmental group. For example, the policy doesn't mean that EPA will launch cancer-risk investigations for the 66 pesticides. EPA officials said their approach will be more gradual; cancer risks in processed foods will be examined at the same time the agency conducts lengthy studies on the general safety of each pesticide now on the market.

Ms. Hathaway also argued that the EPA's minimal-risk standard is too lenient. She said her group intends to go to court to try to force a stricter interpretation of the so-called Delaney Clause of the Federal Food, Drug and Cosmetic Act, which she contends demands that any pesticide posing any cancer risk be banned from food.
