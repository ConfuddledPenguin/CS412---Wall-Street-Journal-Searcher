Waste Management's Overseas Unit, Majority-Owned Concerns to Swap Stakes

  • Author: Jeff Bailey
  • Date: 12/26/90

OAK BROOK, Ill. -- Waste Management Inc. plans to have its fast-growing international business and two other majority-owned companies swap stakes, and the concern could also take the overseas operations public within about one year, the company said. In an interview, James E. Koenig, the parent company's chief financial officer, said the moves are designed to help finance Waste Management International Inc., which is expected to report 1990 revenue more than doubled from last year's $388 million, and to avoid having the three Waste Management-controlled companies needlessly compete in foreign markets.

The two companies each granted an option to swap their common shares for a 15% stake in Waste Management International Inc. are Chemical Waste Management Inc., the 78%-owned hazardous waste handler, and Wheelabrator Technologies Inc., a waste-to-energy and environmental-services company in which Waste Management recently increased its stake to 55% from 22%.

Without stakes in the overseas business, those two companies could be compelled to open their own foreign offices, which would be a costly duplication of effort, Mr. Koenig said. Waste Management is investing heavily to increase its domination over the garbage, hazardous waste and other waste businesses, and keeping its costs down is crucial to maintaining strong profits during this period.

For the first nine months of this year, net income was $489.1 million, or $1.04 a share, after a $24.5 million charge, on revenue of $4.2 billion. A year earlier, net was $409.2 million, or 89 cents a share, on revenue of $3.3 billion.

Marc Sulam, an analyst at Kidder Peabody & Co. in New York, said he expects the international unit to post 1990 revenue of about $900 million, and that revenue will rise to between $1.3 billion and $1.4 billion next year for the unit. The company has been acquiring garbage hauling operations, particularly in Europe, where it hopes 1992 economic unification will allow it to further expand.

The operations' value isn't clear because, although it is growing rapidly, Waste Management International's profit margins are about one-half of those of the company's domestic solid waste business, Mr. Sulam said, in part reflecting cost of doing business overseas. Nevertheless, the unit could be valued at $2 billion or more by the time Waste Management is ready to take it public in 1992, Mr. Sulam said.

Mr. Koenig, who said the earliest a public offering of shares in the international unit could occur is 1992, wouldn't estimate the value of the unit and wouldn't disclose its profits.

Under the plan, Chemical Waste and Wheelabrator wouldn't pursue customers outside of North America, but could share in the foreign growth by owning a stake in the international unit. Waste Management, while operating the companies separately, is trying to get them to share customers and technologies.

Chemical Waste and Wheelabrator's options are to buy the 15% stakes at a 15% discount of Waste Management International's value, to be determined by outside appraisals. They can exercise the option beginning Jan. 1, 1991.

In addition, as part of increasing its Wheelabrator stake to 55%, Waste Management agreed to:

-- Help Wheelabrator obtain an "A" or better rating from major credit rating agencies. Among other things, that could involve Waste Management investing as much as another $200 million in Wheelabrator by buying subordinated debt securities or preferred stock.

-- Sell its still-small medical waste business to Wheelabrator at a 15% discount to its estimated value. The medical waste business has sales approaching $50 million, but could grow rapidly because Waste Management already has garbage hauling contracts with many hospitals.
