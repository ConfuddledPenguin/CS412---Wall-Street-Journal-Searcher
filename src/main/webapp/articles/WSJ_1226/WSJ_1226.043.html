Technology: Attempt to Confirm Link Between Gene And Alcoholism Fails in Federal Study

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 12/26/90

A new experiment by federal scientists failed to support a widely circulated report last spring that a gene related to alcoholism had been found. In the new experiment the scientists found that the putative alcoholism gene wasn't any more common among 40 alcoholics than among 127 nonalcoholics. Moreover, they couldn't find any linkage between the putative alcoholism gene and alcoholism in two families where a tendency to alcoholism appeared to be inherited.

The new study throws a splash of cold water on an experiment reported last April by pharmacologist Kenneth Blum of the University of Texas at San Antonio and alcoholism researcher Ernest P. Noble of the University of California, Los Angeles.

Drs. Blum and Noble reported in the Journal of the American Medical Association that a variation of the dopamine receptor gene was associated with alcoholism. The variation is known as the A1 version of the gene. The gene itself enables a nerve cell to receive molecules of dopamine, one of the chemicals that transmits nerve impulses from cell to cell.

The two researchers last April said they had checked brain cells of 70 deceased people, half of whom had been severe alcoholics and half of whom were nonalcoholics. The A1 version of the dopamine receptor gene was present in 69% of the deceased alcoholics but in only 20% of the nonalcoholics, they reported.

The finding, Drs. Blum and Noble suggested, hinted that the A1 version of the gene may render people more susceptible to alcoholism than people with other versions of the dopamine receptor gene.

The new experiment, reported in this week's issue of the Journal of the American Medical Association, checked blood cells of living individuals for the A1 version of the dopamine receptor gene. It found that 38% of 40 alcoholics possessed the A1 version of the gene, almost half the prevalence seen in the experiment by Drs. Blum and Noble. Of the 127 nonalcoholics 30% had the A1 version of the gene, which was almost the same prevalence seen in the alcoholics.

The new experiment also looked at two families that appeared to be haunted by a tendency toward alcoholism. One parent in each family had begun heavy drinking in adolescence. Of the 10 children in the two families, six had followed their parent into early stage alcoholism and nine had become drug abusers.

In neither family, however, could the researchers find any evidence that the A1 version of the dopamine receptor gene was being inherited along with the tendency toward early alcoholism.

Neither Dr. Blum in San Antonio nor Dr. Noble in Los Angeles could be reached Monday for comment.

The new experiment was reported by David Goldman and his colleagues from the federal National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism in Bethesda, Md.
