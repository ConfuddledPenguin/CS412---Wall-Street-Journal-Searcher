LEISURE & ARTS -- Bookshelf: Divinely Chosen for a Cruel Destiny

  • Author: William McGurn
  • Date: 12/26/90

Dalai Lama means different things to different people. To some it means that I am a living Buddha, the earthly manifestation of Avalokiteshvara, Bodhissatva of Compassion. To others it means that I am a "god-king." During the late 1950s it meant that I was a Vice President of the Steering Committee of the People's Republic of China. Then when I escaped into exile, I was called a counterrevolutionary and a parasite. But none of these are my ideas. To me "Dalai Lama" is a title that signifies the office I hold. I myself am just a human being, and incidentally a Tibetan, who chooses to be a Buddhist monk. With these words Tenzin Gyatso begins "Freedom in Exile: The Autobiography of the Dalai Lama" (Harper/Collins, 288 pages, $22.95). As the title itself implies, the Dalai Lama may be the supreme temporal as well as spiritual leader to the Tibetan people, but legally speaking he is just another another stateless refugee from Communist China. More than three decades after he fled the Land of the Snows and a year after the Nobel Committee awarded him its Peace Prize, the Dalai Lama is telling his story. In so doing he pulls back some of the mysterious curtains on his Buddhist faith and the homeland to which he has never returned.

Born in 1935 to peasant farmers in a cowshed in northeastern Tibet (with his eyes wide open, according to his mother), the Dalai Lama had a normal childhood and even recalls enjoying such unlamalike activities as playing soldier. This normal childhood changed when a search party identified the boy -- not quite three -- as the reincarnation of the 13th Dalai Lama, after he'd correctly identified some of his predecessor's belongings. Shortly thereafter, he was taken from his parents and prepared for the Lion Throne.

The arrival of the Chinese Communists did not immediately fill him with foreboding, he now recalls. The Chinese signed an agreement promising to respect Tibet's traditions and local autonomy (just like they did in the 1984 Sino-British Joint Declaration on Hong Kong). And the notion of creating a sort of Buddho-Liberation Theology was appealing to the teen-ager. "The more I looked at Marxism," he writes, "the more I liked it." Personal audiences with Mao only confirmed his youthful ardor, though the actual destruction wrought by the Chinese soon demonstrated that the Great Helmsman's words were like a rainbow: "beautiful, but without substance."

Now, the relationship between China and Tibet has never been as clear or straightforward as either side insists, though history leans decidedly toward Tibetan claims. A stone monument outside Jokhang Cathedral in Lhasa records the 821 A.D. treaty between Tibet and China and delineates clear boundaries between two separate nations. Beijing dates its claim to the 13th century, when the Mongol Khans established an empire that ruled both China and Tibet ("Dalai Lama," in fact, is a Mongol title). But what really counts for China is that Tibet covers about a quarter of its land mass and serves as a nuclear armament base.

At the time of the invasion, Tibet had only about 8,500 mostly ceremonial soldiers, and the Dalai Lama's urgent appeals to the United Nations never elicited a response largely because Tibet in its geopolitical indifference "had never sought to become a member." These days the Dalai Lama is an internationalist.

Yet for all his bitter personal experience with Sino communism he remains astonishingly open to its premises. Like the current pope, the Dalai Lama seems to think that the problem with Marxism lies not in its anti-human premises but only in the barbaric application of them by overly ambitious (and godless) men. "Some responsibility for the excesses of Communism rest squarely on the West," he writes. Elsewhere he confesses he has still not given up the possibility of an ideological rapprochement. "The two approaches to life {Buddhism and Marxism} are so obviously complementary," he says. "It is sad that people tend to think of them as being in opposition."

He can be similarly confusing about his beliefs. The Dalai Lama is a bit coy about whether he really thinks he is a reincarnation of the other Dalai Lamas. ". . . I have no difficulty accepting that I am spiritually connected both to the 13 previous Dalai Lamas, to Chenrezin {the Spirit of Compassion}, and to the Buddha himself," he writes.

Whether he is in fact a reincarnation of the Spirit of Compassion I am unable to say. I am even less willing to concede his peculiar interpretation of Marxist theory and its potential for good. But these are abstractions, and there can be no doubt that in practice the Dalai Lama has been a consistent champion of freedom. And as a refugee living in an India that would just as soon not have him, as the head of a Tibetan government-in-exile nobody but his own people recognizes, and spokesman for a nation that has suffered so much under communism, he is a living reminder of the heavy price paid by those courageous few who could have gone along -- and didn't.

---

Mr. McGurn is Washington bureau chief of the National Review.
