Credit Markets: Treasury Bonds Fall for Fourth Session in a Row On Growing War Fears, Year-End Profit-Taking

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Lynn K. Adler
  • Date: 12/26/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices fell sharply Monday for the fourth consecutive session in nervous pre-holiday trading. Traders said the increased likelihood of war in the Middle East added to the pressures of year-end profit-taking and looming supply, pushing yields on long-term bonds to the highest levels in several weeks.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell nearly 7/8 point, or $8.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The yield on the benchmark bond, which was as low as 8.08% on Dec. 12, rose to 8.36% from 8.28% Friday.

Shorter-term securities posted smaller declines. Prices of two-year notes, for example, fell just 1/8 point.

Dealers said Monday's losses were exaggerated by the extremely thin Christmas Eve trading conditions. The Public Securities Association suggested a 2 p.m. EST close for the government securities market, and many dealers closed their trading desks an hour or two before that. Financial markets were closed yesterday for the Christmas holiday.

Kevin Flanagan, a money market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., said prices weakened Monday as investors sold bonds to try to capture profits.

"It's the end of the year, so people have closed out their books," Mr. Flanagan said. "They want to cash in and take a portion of the profits" generated during the market's recent 13-week rally.

Bond prices also were hurt by heightened perceptions that the U.S. will end up going to war with Iraq. Fanning their fears were comments from officials in both countries. U.S. Defense Secretary Dick Cheney warned Sunday that "the clock is ticking" quickly toward war. Meanwhile, Iraqi President Saddam Hussein said Tel Aviv will be Iraq's first target if war breaks out in the Persian Gulf, whether Israel joins in the war effort or not.

Some analysts say the market's poor tone might not improve much this week because it will be hit with a large new supply of securities. The Treasury is scheduled to sell a record $12.5 billion of two-year notes today and a record $8.5 billion of four-year notes tomorrow.

In when-issued trading, the new two-year notes were quoted to yield 7.28%, up slightly from Friday. The yield on the new four-year notes was also quoted higher at 7.73%.

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late Monday at a price of 104 6/32, down from 105 Friday.

Three-month Treasury bills, however, moved in the opposite direction as prices rose and rates continued to decline. The average discount rate on three-month bills ended at 6.54% for a bond equivalent yield of 6.72%, down from a discount rate of 6.59% to yield 6.77%.

Six-month bill rates were little changed. The discount rate on six-month bills ended at 6.64% for a bond equivalent yield of 6.94%.

Corporate Bonds

Investment-grade corporate bonds finished with moderate losses as slumping Treasury bond prices weighed on the market.

Long-term high-grade issues registered losses of as much as 5/8 point, but the declines weren't as steep as some of those in the long-term Treasury market.

As a result, the spread, or difference in yields between Treasury and corporate bonds, narrowed by as much as five basis points, or 0.05 percentage point, traders said.

"With the long bond down nearly a point, corporate spreads have definitely tightened," said one trader.

However, Robert Schwartz, senior financial economist at Merrill Lynch & Co., said he wouldn't attach much significance to the tightening.

It's fairly typical, he said, when the Treasury market registers steep losses, for corporate bond movements to lag and the spreads to tighten.

"I would think any price action you are seeing today reflects nominal markups and markdowns rather than any real trading," Mr. Schwartz said Monday.

He said it will be difficult to gauge spreads in the corporate bond market until activity in the sector picks up again after the first of the year.

Municipal Securities

Municipal bond prices ended an abbreviated session unchanged to 5/8 of a point lower, dragged down by escalating concerns about possible war in the Middle East.

Following Treasury issues downward in thin trading, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue due in 2018 was down 5/8 at 98 1/4 to 99 1/4, yielding 7.26%.

While the week's economic data are expected to prove bearish for the economy and positive for the credit markets, Mideast headlines may supersede and continue to dominate the market's tone, traders say.

November durable goods data are scheduled to be released tomorrow, and the leading economic indicators are set for Friday release.
