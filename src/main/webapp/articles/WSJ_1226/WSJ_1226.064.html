Your Money Matters: Monitoring Mortgages for Miscalculation

  • Author: Georgette Jasen
  • Date: 12/26/90

Are your mortgage payments too high? It often feels that way for many homeowners, particularly now, as they try to cut costs in the face of a slowing economy. But for those with adjustable-rate mortgages, it may pay to check: Thousands of mortgage holders may actually be paying more than they should be on variable-rate loans, because the lenders have miscalculated periodic adjustments in the monthly payments.

Unlike fixed-rate mortgages, which carry the same interest rate for the term of the loan, the rate on an adjustable mortgage changes periodically in line with a specific index. A specified amount, often between two and three percentage points, is added to the index to determine the mortgage rate.

Figuring out whether you are being overcharged is fairly easy, once you have all the information. When there is an error, "it's usually a question of using the wrong index or picking the wrong week" in figuring the adjustment, says Paul Havemann of HSH Associates, a mortgage-information service in Butler, N.J.

Most lenders have automated systems to calculate the adjustments; some concede that there could be clerical errors in entering the data.

For borrowers, the hard part of checking the rate on an adjustable-rate mortgage is often determining just what rate is used as the index. While federal regulations require that indexes be publicly available, borrowers still sometimes have trouble tracking them.

"You have to read your loan document very carefully to see exactly what it says," advises Michael Wilson, deputy director of research for the U.S. League of Savings Institutions.

There are indexes based on various Treasury securities, banks' and thrifts' cost of funds, and even on the London interbank offered rate -- or Libor. Some indexes are based on a single date, others are moving averages. Some loans are adjusted monthly, others every six months, annually or every three years.

Besides the index and the frequency of adjustments, the loan agreement stipulates the maximum change in each adjustment period and for the life of the loan, as well as when the new rate is calculated. Lenders generally must notify mortgage holders of the new rate 45 days before it takes effect.

If digging out the papers and looking up interest rates sounds like more bother than it's worth, keep in mind that a recent report from the General Accounting Office, the investigative arm of Congress, said some analysts believe as many as a quarter of adjustable-rate mortgages may have been billed incorrectly. Mr. Havemann of HSH Associates says that between 10% and 20% of the thousands of homeowners who have called in the past few years seeking help calculating their payments have found errors.

Although the GAO said federal law probably doesn't require lenders to reimburse consumers for any overcharges, some financial institutions have already issued refunds. HSH says most lenders probably would give borrowers a choice of having overpayments credited against their mortgage or receiving a check for the full amount. The rate would also be adjusted for future payments.

Here's a look at some indexes and how to find out about them.

Treasury Securities. Interest rates on six-month and one-year Treasury bills and on three-year and five-year Treasury notes often are used for adjustable-rate mortgage indexes, particularly on the East Coast. Some lenders take the rate on a certain day, usually 45 days before adjustment date; others use a weekly average.

Rates and yields on Treasury securities are published in this newspaper and in the business section of many local papers. The Federal Reserve Board also publishes these numbers. Individuals can get on the mailing list for the Fed's release H-15, issued weekly, and G-13, issued monthly, by contacting the publications office of the Federal Reserve in Washington. Mr. Wilson of the U.S. League of Savings recommends getting both. The total cost is $20 a year. (Write to: Publications Services, Federal Reserve Board, 20th and Constitution Ave., N.W., Washington, D.C. 20551.)

Cost-of-Funds Indexes. The 11th District Cost of Funds index, issued monthly by the Federal Home Loan Bank in San Francisco, is more popular among West Coast lenders. The bank has a telephone hotline (415-616-2600) with a recorded announcement giving the index.

The national median cost of funds is a weighted average reported monthly by the Office of Thrift Supervision in Washington. Although the office's toll-free telephone number has been discontinued, individuals can get on a mailing list for the monthly release by calling the thrift agency's public affairs office (202-906-6677).

Libor. The London Interbank Offered Rate, an interbank lending rate used by London banks on dollar-denominated deposits, can vary daily. It is published in this newspaper and in business sections of some local papers.

The various indexes also are available through several computerized data bases.

What happens if after figuring it all out borrowers discover that they are being undercharged? HSH Associates says that in most states the lender probably can't bill mortgage holders for the difference.
