What's News -- World-Wide

  • Date: 12/26/90

GORBACHEV WON parliamentary support for sweeping new executive powers. The Congress of People's Deputies voted to create a new vice presidency and a cabinet under the Soviet leader's direct control, marking a clear victory for the president and for hard-liners. Also, Gorbachev quashed an effort by some legislators to rein in his authority to issue decrees, and he expressed anger at progressives trying to limit his authority. On Monday, the Congress endorsed the concept of a union treaty to hold the country together, and ordered the 15 republics to set dates for holding referendums on the question.

Some of the Western humanitarian aid pouring into the Soviet Union has been stolen, but more than 99% is reaching the people who need it most, the KGB's chairman told the legislators.

Bush was advised by the top U.S. military commander in the Gulf, Gen. Norman Schwarzkopf, that his ground forces won't be fully prepared for an all-out assault against Iraq until February, Pentagon officials said. Meanwhile, Iraqi ambassadors headed home for urgent consultations with Saddam Hussein.

---

A Suriname army chief pledged following a bloodless coup Monday that he would relinquish power to an elected government within 100 days. Ivan Graanoogst, who joined with other officers in overthrowing Suriname's President Ramsewak Shankar, said he wouldn't impose a curfew because "there is no danger whatsoever."

---

A study by federal scientists failed to support a widely circulated report that a gene related to alcoholism had been discovered. In the new experiment, researchers said they found that the putative alcoholism gene wasn't any more common among 40 alcoholics than among 127 nonalcoholics.

---

Salman Rushdie promised on Monday he won't authorize a paperback version of his novel, "The Satanic Verses," and said he doesn't agree with any statements made by characters in the book that cast aspersions on Islam. Rushdie has been in hiding in London since a February 1989 death edict by the late Iranian leader Ayatollah Khomeini.

---

The Israeli army upheld expulsion orders against four Gaza Strip Palestinians but defense lawyers said they would submit an appeal to Israel's Supreme Court. The army said a three-officer appeals panel ruled that the Arabs were senior leaders of an Islamic fundamentalist group involved in a three-year-old Palestinian uprising.

A Soviet envoy in Jerusalem presented his credentials as consul-general to Israel, underscoring warming ties between the Israelis and the Kremlin, which severed diplomatic relations during the 1967 Middle East war. In a similar ceremony in Moscow, an Israeli official presented his credentials.

---

A study found that teen suicide prevention programs in schools may actually stir up depressed feelings rather than help youths who have tried to kill themselves. The report is published in today's Journal of the American Medical Association.

---

Serbia's ruling Communists won a landslide victory in Yugoslavia's biggest republic, according to official results from the first free Serbian elections since World War II, held Sunday. The Communists, renamed Socialists, have lost power this year in elections in four of Yugoslavia's six republics.

---

Romania's former monarch returned to his homeland after 43 years of exile, but faced the possibility of arrest. King Michael, 69, who lives in Geneva, was forced by the Communists to abdicate in 1947.

---

San Francisco's mayor plans to sign a watered-down version of a city ordinance requiring employers to provide health safeguards for employees who use video display terminals. Art Agnos met with business and union leaders last Saturday and obtained general agreement to support six amendments limiting the ordinance's scope.

---

A Kashmiri Moslem group rejected an offer by Indian Prime Minister Chandra Shekhar to negotiate with the rebels, who want to secede from India. But in Punjab, where Sikhs also are fighting for secession, a political leader said he was prepared to talk to the government if he received a formal invitation and a written agenda.

Died: Karl H. Hinke, 84, a former executive vice president of Marine Midland Bank and the creator of what is now known as the MasterCard credit card, Saturday, in East Aurora, N.Y.
