Letters to the Editor -- Milken's Sentence: You Call That Justice?

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/26/90

On Nov. 23 you printed comments from various people concerning "Milken's Sentence: Was Justice Done?" These comments were from important people but not from a guy called "the average American taxpayer" who will again get stuck with the bill to clean up another mess. You did not solicit my comments as an average American taxpayer, but I will give them to you anyway.

Was justice done? Absolutely not. Michael Milken should have received the maximum prison sentence with no hope of parole due to the damage he has done to our economy.

If he is released from prison early and is required to perform community service, what will he do, teach the younger generation how to manipulate stocks and bonds? The only community service he should be permitted to do is to clean the streets of Alaska.

Bob Allen

Newport, Ky.

---

What Mr. Milken did for corporate America was to introduce (through greed) unmanageable corporate debt and siphon off hundreds of billions of dollars from needed capital improvements into the pockets of manipulative investors, arbitragers and attorneys, thereby so weakening corporate structure as to displace hundreds of thousands of workers. These displacements and a loss of confidence in the marketplace have brought about a very real recession. To say that there were no victims is to belie the fact that our entire country was the victim.

M.O. Willis

Danbury, Conn.

---

In my opinion, having lost money in a number of Mr. Milken's manipulations, I think the sentence was not stiff enough. He probably will be sent to a country-club type of prison, like San Luis Obispo or Eglin AFB. He needs to do "hard time." I suggest a prison like Joliet.

George C. Szego

Annapolis, Md.

---

Investor Harold C. Simmons characterized the sentence given as "too strenuous." There is no disputing that Mr. Milken is a brilliant man and contributed greatly to our country's wealth and financial history, but he broke the law, and I believe his sentence was anything but strenuous.

Anyone who is in a position to alter greatly the course of events, as Mr. Milken was, must be above reproach. Michael Milken had control over millions of dollars at any given moment and, whether he realized it or not, controlled the fate of many lives as a result.

How can Mr. Simmons say this is a victimless crime, with more than 40,000 people on Wall Street out of work already and more to come? When is the small investor going to feel it is safe enough to venture back into the market?

Louis P. Sitnik

Silver Spring, Md.

---

It is truly amazing that Mr. Simmons could suggest there were "no victims."

Mr. Milken, through his junk-bond shenanigans, is second only to Congress in causing the savings-and-loan debacle. The taxpayers will pay billions of dollars in the decade ahead to cover the S&L losses. Mr. Milken, through his crimes, has cost innocent stockholders of savings and loans, banks, mutual funds and closed-end funds additional millions. Many insurance companies, college endowment funds and pension funds have fewer assets today than they did a few years ago due to Mr. Milken's greed. Further, the number of jobs lost and careers destroyed because of his manipulation of the markets and destruction of heretofore viable enterprises far outweighs any good he may have done.

No victims? Mr. Simmons must have been emulating Rip van Winkle these past few years.

Arthur J. O'Connor

Sarasota, Fla.
