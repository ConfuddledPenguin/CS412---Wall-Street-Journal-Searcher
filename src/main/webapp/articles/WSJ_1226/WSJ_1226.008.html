Foreign Exchange: Dollar Eases in Light Tokyo Dealings; Mark Firms Against Japanese Currency

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 12/26/90

The dollar weakened in light Tokyo trading Tuesday after having risen Monday in European and U.S. dealings. The greenback was quoted late in Tokyo Tuesday at 135.95 yen, below 136.00 yen late Monday in New York but up from 135.70 yen late Friday in Tokyo. Tokyo markets were closed Monday to honor the Japanese emperor's birthday. U.S. and European markets were closed yesterday for the Christmas holidays.

The U.S. currency also finished in Tokyo at 1.5380 marks, lower than 1.5440 marks late Monday in New York but well above 1.5088 marks in Tokyo Friday.

In Tokyo early Wednesday, the dollar was trading at 136.28 yen, and at about 1.5390 marks.

The main feature of Tuesday's trading was the mark's modest firming against the yen, which traders attributed both to profit-taking in the yen and short-covering of mark positions. The short-mark positions were acquired during the German currency's fall of roughly two yen following the resignation of Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze late last week.

The German currency, thought to be the most vulnerable among major currencies to instability in the Soviet Union, found support at around the 88.20-yen level, where participants began the covering of short-mark positions. To be short a currency means, in essence, betting that the currency will fall.

Late in the Tokyo session, the mark was trading at 88.39 yen, up from 88.08 late Monday in New York but well below 89.93 yen in Tokyo Friday. The mark opened the Tokyo session Tuesday at 88.05 yen.

Meanwhile, traders say the dollar continues to draw support from fears of war in the Persian Gulf, ahead of the approaching Jan. 15 deadline set by the United Nations for a peaceful Iraqi withdrawal from Kuwait. The greenback is also benefiting from concern over possible political turmoil in the Soviet Union. The dollar has traditionally served as a "haven" for investors in times of turmoil.

Soviet Foreign Minister Shevardnadze, long a close ally of President Mikhail Gorbachev, resigned from his post to protest to what he called growing dictatorship in his country.

On Monday, the dollar had gained in thin pre-holiday dealings in Europe and the U.S. as traders troubled by the Mideast crisis and Soviet political instability moved to cover any short-dollar positions. The mark was the market's wallflower as participants abandoned long-mark positions across the board.

"When you look at the political makeup in the Soviet sector and ask who's most susceptible, Germany comes out loud and clear," said Marc Cohen, vice president of corporate foreign exchange at Fuji Bank in New York, citing Germany's proximity to and investments in the Soviet Union.

The Shevardnadze resignation "tacked on a minimum of five pfennigs," or 0.05 mark, to the dollar's trading range, said Jerry Egan, chief dealer at Bank of Boston in Boston. The question now is whether that boost will serve as a catalyst for a larger dollar rally once the market returns to normal after the year-end holidays.

European and U.S. trading institutions mostly operated with skeleton staffs and closed early for Christmas Eve. In these conditions, the effects of each transaction were amplified, causing wider swings in exchange rates than would have been seen in a more liquid market.

Although the market's lack of liquidity draws into question the durability of the dollar's recent gains, many technical traders were calling the rally significant.

Mary Ingrassia, customer dealer at Bank of Tokyo in New York, said that since the U.S. currency broke and remained above 1.50 marks, participants have been viewing 1.55 marks as the next objective. The dollar stopped just shy of that goal Monday, hitting an intraday high of 1.5470 marks.

There was no gold trading on the Commodity Exchange in New York Monday or yesterday. Hong Kong markets were closed Wednesday for a holiday.
