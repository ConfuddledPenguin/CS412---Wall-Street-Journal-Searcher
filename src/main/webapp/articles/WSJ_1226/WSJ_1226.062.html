Technology: San Francisco Mayor To Sign Weaker Law On the Use of VDTs

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/26/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- Mayor Art Agnos plans to sign a watered-down version of a city ordinance that requires employers to provide health safeguards for employees who work at video display terminals. Mr. Agnos met with business and union leaders last Saturday and obtained general agreement to support six amendments that limit the ordinance's scope. The amended rules will be presented to the Board of Supervisors for approval by Jan. 7, said Paul Chignell, a mayoral aide. Even in its more-restricted version, the law, which the mayor said he will sign tomorrow, still is seen as a possible model for future legislation in other cities.

As the use of VDTs has grown, a number of health problems have been attributed to prolonged use of them. Carpal tunnel syndrome, a painful wrist ailment; eye strain, lower-back strain from poorly designed chairs, and concern about the effects of low-level radiation emitted from the displays have been the subject of controversy between computer operators and their employers for a number of years.

The ordinance requires employers to provide workers with adjustable seats, monitors and keyboards; sets guidelines for eliminating screen glare; and requires regular 15-minute breaks for workers and replacement of substandard equipment.

Under the amendments the mayor proposed, companies have four years instead of the originally proposed two years to replace substandard equipment. Companies will be required to retrofit substandard computer equipment within 30 months at a cost of as much as $250 per machine. All new computer workstations purchased within a year of the adoption of the Dec. 17 ordinance will have to meet the new standards set by the ordinance.

Workers at the Pacific Stock Exchange and operators of computer-aided design and display machines -- used in architectural and other design-oriented industries -- are exempt under the new rules.
