And if This Doesn't Do the Trick, They May Call for Squirrel Rifles

  • Author: Ann Hagedorn
  • Date: 12/26/90

Balloons make good targets. Environmentalists have long said balloons that land in the sea choke marine life. Now California says metallic balloons do a number on power lines.

So, starting Jan. 1, Californians who unleash helium-filled metallic balloons will be committing a crime under a new law. First-time offenders will be fined $100, and repeat offenders face as long as six months in jail and a $1,000 fine.

The new balloon law results from the lobbying efforts of California utilities and the state's Public Utilities Commission. Their crusade was inspired by a commission report that fugitive metallic balloons and their streamers cause nearly 1% of California's power outages each year.

Metallic balloons, on the market since the early 1970s, are made of aluminum-coated nylon. They stay aloft longer than latex balloons because the aluminum coating prevents the helium, which has very tiny atoms, from leaking out. Metallic balloons account for about $400 million of the nation's $1 billion balloon industry.

Utility companies were so concerned about the commission's findings that they wanted to ban the sale of the balloons. But the Silver Balloon Association, a Sacramento-based group representing metallic-balloon vendors and manufacturers, convinced legislators that such a ban would devastate thousands of small businesses.

"Squirrels and birds cause nearly 2.5% of the outages," says Silver Balloon Director Lisa Rea. "And automobiles and the weather cause a lot more. But you can't ban squirrels, birds and storms; so they went after balloons."

As of Jan. 1, the sale or distribution of any balloon attached to metallic ribbons or streamers will be prohibited. Metallic balloons must have a weight attached and bear a label warning consumers about the dangers that balloons pose for power lines. And vendors can sell metallic balloons in bunches only if the balloons are attached to an object, like a mug or a key chain.

Still, balloon companies don't seem too deflated.

Alan Harris, a vice president of M&D Balloons, a large U.S. balloon maker, thinks the weighted balloons will be popular. "The new product will prevent a child from losing a balloon in a grocery store with 20-foot ceilings. And there will no longer be a need for people to tie their balloons to their desks or weight them down with a book, only to lose the balloon once the book is lifted . . . We're optimistic."
