Law -- Legal Beat: Big Board Blocked in Bid to Fine Drexel

  • Author: Wade Lambert and Glenn Ruffenach
  • Date: 12/26/90

A U.S. bankruptcy court judge barred the New York Stock Exchange from seeking a record fine of $50 million from Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc. for securities-law violations. The Big Board wants to punish Drexel and its brokerage unit for the six trading-related felonies that the company pleaded guilty to in September 1989. The stock exchange and Drexel were in the middle of settlement talks earlier this year when the company filed for bankruptcy-law protection.

Under bankruptcy law, the exchange can't take action against Drexel without the bankruptcy court's permission. U.S. Bankruptcy Court Judge Francis Conrad, however, sided with Drexel, ruling that the exchange's efforts would result in "the misallocation of scarce resources" that should go to other creditors.

A spokesman for the exchange said: "The New York Stock Exchange is reviewing the decision to determine whether an appeal is appropriate." The exchange was seeking a fine of as much as $25 million each for Drexel and its brokerage unit, Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., which joined the parent company in bankruptcy-court proceedings.

The Big Board claimed that it needed to fine Drexel to send a message to other firms that wrongdoing won't be tolerated. But Judge Conrad, who is presiding over Drexel's bankruptcy proceedings in Manhattan, said: "The public policy reasons for the New York Stock Exchange's desire to go forward in its forum don't appear to accomplish much more than that which has already been accomplished by the U.S. attorney and the Securities and Exchange Commission."

The U.S. Attorney's office in Manhattan prosecuted the case that resulted in Drexel's guilty pleas and a settlement with the SEC. As part of the settlement, Drexel agreed to pay $650 million in fines and penalties. The six fraud charges involved securities transactions from 1984 to 1986.

Judge Conrad also said that allowing the Big Board to proceed would divert Drexel from its main task -- coming up with a bankruptcy reorganization plan. Drexel's "professional attention must remain focused on this critical stage," the judge said.

The stock exchange is continuing to investigate whether to take disciplinary action against individual Drexel employees in connection with the company's guilty plea. Those investigations won't be affected by the judge's ruling.

FLORIDA'S SUPREME COURT endorses restrictions on attorney advertising.

The state's highest court last week agreed to recommendations by the state bar association that will tightly control how lawyers can advertise on TV.

The rules, which take effect Jan. 1, will prohibit lawyers from dramatizing their profession or their services. No music will be permitted in commercials. Nor will slogans or testimonials from clients (real or simulated). And no more than one voice speaking will be allowed.

Supporters of the measures say they want to ensure that the public gets accurate information about attorneys' services and that the legal profession maintains a respectable image.

But opponents say the restrictions are nothing less than an attack on First Amendment rights to free speech. David W. Singer, a personal-injury attorney who spends about $500,000 a year on ads, says the restrictions unfairly dictate how an attorney should best reach potential clients and will result in little more than "cookiecutter" commercials.

Several U.S. Supreme Court rulings have supported attorneys' rights to advertise but have left room for states to impose restrictions.

Some attorneys have characterized Florida's rules as the toughest in the nation. Mr. Singer says a group he helped found, Citizens Against Censorship, plans to fight the measures, either by filing an appeal in federal district court and climbing the federal appellate ladder, or by appealing directly to the U.S. Supreme Court.

"It's not going to end here," Mr. Singer said. "I'm very confident we're going to have the last laugh."

INSURER ISN'T LIABLE for naturally occurring pollution, appeals court rules.

A Colorado appeals court in Denver ruled that West American Insurance Co. of Anaheim, Calif., doesn't have to pay for cleaning up contamination in Red Mountain Creek, in southwestern Colorado.

It is believed to be the first time that a court has ruled that a property owner is not entitled to insurance coverage for pollution damage arising from natural causes rather than intentional activity by the policyholder.

West American sued Frank W. Baumgartner, who owns mining properties in the area, alleging the insurer was not responsible for Mr. Baumgartner's legal defense or potential damages in an environmental suit.

Mr. Baumgartner has been sued by three mining companies that allegedly released hazardous substances into the creek. In the pending suit, the companies allege that acid water from Mr. Baumgartner's land seeped into the creek and contributed to the contamination.

In an opinion late last week, the appeals panel ruled that Mr. Baumgartner's insurance policies do not cover pollution that occurs over a long period of time, regardless of how it was caused.

In a dissent, Judge Sandra I. Rothenberg said that it was unfair to deny coverage to Mr. Baumgartner for the pollution. She said Mr. Baumgartner had not intentionally polluted the creek and that the acid water from his property occurred as the result of a natural leaching process.

Mr. Baumgartner said he would not appeal.

Lawyers for the insurer could not be reached for comment.

Barry Ostrager, a lawyer with the New York law firm Simpson Thacher & Bartlett who represents insurance companies in such disputes, said the case is unusual but that the ruling is consistent with principles applied by other courts.

"Coverage is not available for gradual pollution. This situation is no exception," he said.

FILING AN AMENDED tax return isn't evidence of fraud, appeals court rules.

The decision by the U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals in Manhattan overturns the conviction of James Dyer, former mayor of Danbury, Conn. Mr. Dyer was found guilty last year of filing a false tax return for 1986 after the trial judge told the jury that Mr. Dyer's amended return was an admission of guilt. The jury acquitted Mr. Dyers of six other charges.

In the amended 1986 return, Mr. Dyer included the $10,357 in income that prosecutors charge he was trying to avoid taxes on. Prosecutors claimed that the circumstances surrounding the filing of the amended return indicated that Mr. Dyer knew all along that the original return was incorrect.

But the appeals-court panel said: "An amended return cannot provide an inference of fraud; it can only support an inference of mistake." The court ordered a new trial for Mr. Dyer.
