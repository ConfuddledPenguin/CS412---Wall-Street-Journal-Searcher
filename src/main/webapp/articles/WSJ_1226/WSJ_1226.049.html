Letters to the Editor: Rub a Dub Dub, Too Much for a Tub

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/26/90

In your Nov. 23 page-one article on bathtubs, you report that a certain Hungarian bathtub is made of cast iron two inches thick and weighs a tenth of a ton. Surely it's two-tenths of an inch thick. Otherwise, it would weigh more than a full ton, and would fall through the castle floor. Either you've made a metrics mistake, or they're really building battleships.

Alan Mole

Boulder, Colo.

---

You apparently visited Lilliput and mistook it for Hungary. A two-inch-thick cast-iron bathtub "weighing in at a tenth of a ton" would be about nine inches long, nine inches wide and nine inches deep. Just about right for a Lilliputian.

R.J. Russell

York, Pa.

---

"Nobody makes tubs like this anymore" is an understatement -- nobody ever did.

The article brought back memories of a bathtub factory in Pennsylvania, long closed, where I worked in the early 1950s. Working conditions were arduous, but the job was interesting and fun. At that time, stamped-steel tubs were considered inferior substitutes, and plastic tubs had not begun to appear. Even today, as the article notes, some people, myself included, prefer the rigidity and durability of a good cast-iron porcelain-enameled tub.

Malcolm G. Murray Jr.

Murray & Garig Tool Works

Baytown, Texas

---

(Editor's Note: The tub's thickness in fact is five millimeters, or 0.2 inch, not five centimeters, or two inches. A decimal point apparently was moved in the translation from Hungarian to metric to English.)
