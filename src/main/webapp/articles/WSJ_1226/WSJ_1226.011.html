Enterprise -- State and Local Spotlight: State Legislators Take Closer Look at Managed Growth --- Small Firms Back Efforts to Prevent Backlash Against Development

  • Author: Timothy D. Schellhardt
  • Date: 12/26/90

GROWTH MANAGEMENT elbows its way up the legislative agenda in many states. Although budget and tax woes grab the biggest headlines, two new surveys by the National Conference of State Legislatures suggest that several issues of critical importance to small business are emerging near the top of many lawmakers' wish lists.

Among legislative leaders, interest grows significantly in the issues of land use and how to manage growth. Leaders in California, Florida, Hawaii, Maryland, Oregon, Rhode Island and Washington identified both among their top priorities for 1991. Leaders in Arizona, California, Delaware, Maryland and Pennsylvania say growth management is particularly important for the longer-term interests of their states.

The issues "have been a fairly low profile kind of topic before," notes Doug Sacarto, the NCSL's state-issues director. But lawmakers say that failure to address them "will lead to a backlash to stifle economic development," he says.

In a separate survey, states' legislative-committee chairmen continue to rank a half dozen small-business topics very high for deliberation in 1991. For spurring economic development, efforts to expand tourism and foreign trade rated highest, followed by aid to distressed communities, especially rural areas. Lawmakers in 29 states listed assistance to start-up enterprises as important, and those in 27 states cited efforts to develop high-technology businesses.

Next year's agenda on labor issues apparently again will focus on corralling workers' compensation costs; 40 states ranked that as their top labor issue. Other issues moving up: family-leave policies for employees, job retraining and employee drug testing. The small-business lobby favors a workers' compensation overhaul and generally opposes mandatory family-leave measures.

But interest wanes in helping troubled and mature industries. Only nine states gave a priority label to issues such as plant-closing legislation and state financing to help turn around failing companies.

NEW ENGLAND, Mid-Atlantic states register big gains in black-owned firms.

Nationally, the number of black-owned firms rose 38% between 1982 and 1987, to 424,165 from 308,260, a new Census Bureau report shows. Posting the strongest growth were several New England and East Coast states, while Pacific states showed the slowest growth.

New Hampshire led the nation with an 86% gain, spurred by a proliferation of service-related businesses, though the number of black-owned businesses there remains low at just 229. Wyoming, bringing up the rear, actually had a 10% drop in such businesses, to 81 from 90.

Regionally, New England had a 68% increase in the five-year period, followed by the Mid-Atlantic states of New Jersey, New York and Pennsylvania, with a 50% increase, and the eight-state South Atlantic region, at 47%. The slowest growth came in the Pacific states, with a 23% increase; the Great Lakes states, at 27%; and the South-Central states, at 28%.

California has the most black-owned businesses, 47,728, followed by New York, 36,289, and Texas, 35,725. States with fewer than 100 black-owned firms: North Dakota (the only state without a black-owned manufacturing firm), South Dakota, Montana, Wyoming and Vermont, the survey said.

ONE-STOP SHOPPING: Toledo, Ohio, officials have established a Central Permit Center downtown that speedily issues any of the city's 112 different permits. Previously, people starting a business and other applicants had to go to each department involved. Officials think the center may be bringing in more permit fees because its convenience keeps prospective applicants from giving up in frustration.

CLEANING UP: Arizona, South Carolina and Tennessee have eased procedures allowing laundromats and dry cleaners to dispose of unclaimed items. New Jersey moves to make it easier for these firms to get rid of unclaimed carpets. And Connecticut lawmakers reduce to one from two the number of required inspections of dry cleaners by fire marshals, reports State Net, which tracks legislation.

WARY WATCHDOGS: For three years, members of the Council of Independent Business Owners in Asheville, N.C., have been monitoring city and Buncombe County governing boards. The upshot: The small-business group this month filed a petition for a 10% cut in Asheville property taxes and asked that the city council or Asheville residents vote on the plan early next year. The business owners said their monitoring convinced them "the only way to achieve a more frugal government is through a forced reduction in funding."

STATES SEEK increasingly to link small business to anti-illiteracy efforts.

A tax credit for Mississippi businesses went into effect early this year and Colorado lawmakers are expected to consider a similar measure to be introduced next month. Businesses can write off 25% of any direct costs for providing basic-skills training to employees.

Colorado's comprehensive literacy bill is expected to include several other provisions to tap small-business help, including grants for workplace literacy training. Jim Henderson, a regional small-business advocate at the Small Business Administration, says chances "are good" that some form of literacy legislation will pass.

Mr. Henderson notes that about half the state's lawmakers attended at least one of 15 literacy hearings around the state this fall. A 1985 study placed the illiteracy rate for Colorado adults at 8%, lower than the 13% national average, but Mr. Henderson thinks it's actually higher.

In South Carolina, with a 15% adult illiteracy rate, the governor's office has held talks with business owners to promote literacy efforts.
