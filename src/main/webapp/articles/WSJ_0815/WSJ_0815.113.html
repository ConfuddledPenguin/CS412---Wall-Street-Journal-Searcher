J.C. Penney Net Was Down 15% In Second Quarter

  • Author: Kevin Helliker
  • Date: 08/15/90

DALLAS -- J.C. Penney Co., reporting a larger-than-expected 15% drop in second-quarter earnings, blamed the decline on a weak economy that company officials and industry observers fear is growing weaker. Penney earnings dropped to $83 million, or 63 cents a share, from $98 million, or 74 cents a share, the year earlier. Revenue rose a meager 4.5%, to $3.83 billion from $3.67 billion.

Penney attributed the earnings decline primarily to slack consumer demand -- a problem likely to worsen in light of what's happened since July 28, when Penney's second quarter ended. "Rising prices at the pump mean that consumers have reduced spending power," said Ira Silver, Penney's chief economist.

Not only that, Mr. Silver said, but higher energy prices resulting from the Mideast crisis "clearly affect a lot of things J.C. Penney sells -- synthetic clothes, sneakers, any product with an oil-related content."

With the costs of those products likely to rise, Mr. Silver said the company is in a bind. "There is pressure for price increases" on Penney products, he said. But if, for instance, the company raises the price of a sweater, "our (fear) is, nobody's going to buy it," he said, because "the economy is weak."

Analysts, aware that many retailers experienced soft sales in May and June, expected a decline in Penney's second-quarter earnings, but not of such magnitude. "It was several cents below what I expected," said N. Richard Nelson Jr. of Duff & Phelps, who in response lowered his year-end earnings projections for Penney to $6.25 a share from $6.50.

The market responded by skewering the stock, which dropped $2 to close yesterday at $48.50 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

The economy wasn't entirely to blame. "Dillards, Limited, the Gap, Dayton Hudson, are doing all right," asserted Edward Weller, retailer analyst at Montgomery Securities. But compared with those chains, Penney, which is trying to evolve from a frumpy mass merchant to a trendy specialty retailer, lacks clear focus and a sharp image, according to some observers. "Penney is improving its image, but it still has a ways to go," said Mr. Nelson.

In a weakening economy, completing its repositioning could become all the more difficult. "It's harder to make changes when the economy goes soft," said Mr. Weller, adding that Penney is "caught midstream."

Penney Chairman William R. Howell acknowledged that the company's repositioning contributed to the disappointing quarter. "As we bring our merchandise in line with an appropriate department store mix, we are temporarily giving up some volume in conservative women's sportswear," he said.

But he added that "neither the refining of our merchandise mix nor the costs associated with the development of new specialty businesses would be particularly felt if the economic climate were more favorable."

For the six months ended July 28, Penney said its earnings rose 5% to $237 million, or $1.84 a share, from $226 million, or $1.71 a share, the year earlier. Six-month revenue rose 5.4% to $7.64 billion from $7.24 billion.

Separately, May Department Stores Co. said net income in the fiscal second quarter, ended Aug. 4, was flat with year-earlier results of $78 million. However, earnings per share rose 5% to 58 cents from 55 cents because fewer shares are now outstanding. And earnings from continuing operations -- excluding May's discontinued discount store units -- increased a more robust 10% to $78 million from $71 million a year earlier.

The St. Louis-based retailer said sales rose 9% to $2.19 billion from $2.04 billion a year earlier.

The results, which were in line with analysts' expectations, sent May shares up $1.625 to close at $46.625 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. "Some people are probably selling J.C. Penney and buying May," said Mr. Weller of Montgomery Securities.
