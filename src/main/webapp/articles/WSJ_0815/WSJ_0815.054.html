ZAMBIAN DEMOCRACY

Zambian President Kenneth Kaunda, reacting to growing pressure from pro-democracy activists, launched a campaign against the revival of a multiparty system in Zambia.

Mr. Kaunda, who abolished political pluralism in 1973, accused the pro-democracy movement of receiving funds from outside the country and of abusing the freedom to campaign ahead of a referendum on multiparty rule.

Economic hardship sparked riots in June, and 27 people were killed. Afterward, Mr. Kaunda promised a referendum on the multiparty issue in October but later postponed it until next August.

The pro-democracy movement, which has received powerful church backing, is due to hold its first public rally on Saturday in Kabwe, 125 miles north of Lusaka.

Mr. Kaunda said an extraordinary national council in mid-September would consider proposals by a parliamentary committee on ways to make the ruling United National Independence Party more democratic. These include restrictions on presidential tenure to two five-year terms and open elections for the post.

SOVIET PRESS LAW

The government newspaper Izvestia became the first Soviet publication to register under a new press law guaranteeing freedom from decades of state censorship. Meanwhile, the State Press Committee announced a doubling in the price of many publications, citing increases in the cost of paper, printing equipment and transportation. Book prices are also expected to more than double, officials said.

BRITAIN'S UNSTATELY HOMES

An Englishman's home may be his castle, but a record 14,390 British homeowners had their houses repossessed in the first half this year, double the number of repossessions in the second half of last year, the Council of Mortgage Lenders reported. Mark Boleat, director-general of the Building Societies Association, said continuing high interest rates -- around 15% -- were the main cause of the sharp rise in repossessions. The previous repossession record, 11,700 in the first half of 1987, was related to a miners' strike.

PAKISTANI `BLOOD MONEY'

Pakistani President Ghulam Ishaq Khan issued an ordinance allowing convicted murderers to financially compensate heirs of their victims in exchange for clemency. The order is effective for six months, after which it requires the approval of parliament.

IRAQ'S ASIAN GAMES

Iraq will send athletes to the Asian Games in China, an Iraqi embassy spokesman in Beijing said. The report in a Japanese press agency said there is growing concern that Iraq's presence at the games along with other Arab teams may disrupt the event because of Iraq's invasion of Kuwait. The games are scheduled for Sept. 22 to Oct. 6.

LUMBERING EAST GERMANY

East German farmers and lumbermen, among the workers hardest hit by the massive changes sweeping their country, drove tractors, combines and lumber trucks into West Berlin to demand higher wages, job security and other help from the East and West German governments. Imports from the West have cut demand for East German products. Inefficiency is another bone of contention: "We have such old machines, some of them made before the war," said 20-year-old protester Alexander Witt. "We're supposed to compete in a capitalist system, but it's hard with such old machines that often break down and need repairs and maintenance."

VIOLENT BANGLADESH DOCTORS

Striking doctors trying to force the withdrawal of new medical rules attacked the office of the director of Dhaka Medical College Hospital and threatened widespread violence.

Witnesses said groups of young doctors stormed the office of Director Mohammad Anwar Ali Chowdhury, broke furniture and set fire to his chair. Patients, many of whom have lacked care for weeks, took cover as chanting doctors marched toward the director's room, a witness said.

Medical services in Bangladesh have been at a virtual standstill, except for emergency cases, since July 28, when 15,000 doctors, the vast majority of Bangladesh's practicing physicians, went on strike. They are trying to force President Hossain Mohammad Ershad to withdraw proposed rules requiring specialists to stop private practice and junior doctors to work in villages under rural authorities.

Doctors have denounced the rules as infringing upon their professional freedom, saying they will resign unless the government meets their demands.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

More than 220,000 Czechs who suffered under Communist rule will probably share around $95 million in compensation. Officials said those eligible include people subjected to political trials between the Communist Party's takeover of power in 1948 and its ousting less than a year ago. . . . Students in Shanghai were warned by an official newspaper against using Chinese renminbi, the "people's money," to make intricate origami handicraft figures. The Youth Post said the folding-money fad "shows a lack of respect and also increases the burden on banks."
