Abreast of the Market: Stock Prices Edge Up 0.99 Point As Mideast Tensions Seem to Ease

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 08/15/90

NEW YORK -- Drawing strength from indications that tensions in the Middle East could be easing, the stock market bounced back from early weakness and ended higher despite a late sell-off. However, trading was light as skepticism about whether the Persian Gulf conflict will be resolved anytime soon and ongoing concerns about its economic impact damped interest.

"It's a little bit early to get excited," said Larry Greenwald, a block trader at Sanford C. Bernstein & Co. The market's sluggish pace "indicates that people are waiting to see what the next move is going to be," he said.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which swung within a trading range of more than 30 points over the course of the session, closed 0.99 higher at 2747.77.

Broader measures, which trailed badly Monday as the Dow Jones industrials advanced 30.20, fared a bit better than the 30-stock measure in the latest session. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index gained 0.55 to 339.39, the New York Stock Composite Index rose 0.47 to 186.10 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 0.51 to 314.55.

Advancing issues topped decliners on the Big Board by 852 to 640. Volume amounted to 130,320,000 shares, up from 122,820,000 Monday but well below the totals set earlier this month when prices plummeted. "There's just a lot of waiting and seeing going on here," said Robert S. Robbins, a market strategist and technical analyst at Robinson-Humphrey Co., Atlanta.

Stocks drifted back to their session lows at midmorning, but then started to recover amid news that King Hussein of Jordan was on his way to meet with President Bush a day after holding talks with Iraqi President Saddam Hussein.

Flurries of program buying resulting from stock-index arbitrage fueled the market's rise, traders said. The programs were triggered when index futures rallied to sharp premiums to the cash indexes, allowing program traders to lock in profits by selling index-futures contracts and buying baskets of stocks.

Prices peaked in the final hour of trading as the arbitrage buying ran its course, and the market came under selling pressure near the close. The industrial average erased gains of about 15 points and stood slightly lower just before the session ended.

Mr. Robbins said the market is going through what he called "a smoke clearing" following Iraq's occupation of Kuwait. "We've got more inflation, and we've got the threat of a mild recession," he said.

But he noted that prices rebounded after falling about 10% from their highs -- in line with most of the market's pullbacks in the past few years. "We could get a bounce all the way up to 2850 on the Dow {industrials}," he said.

The Dow Jones Transportation Average fell 11.13 to 967.70 as airline issues were hit with selling, especially AMR, which lost 2 1/8 to 49 on about double its average Big Board composite daily volume.

First Boston analyst Paul Karos told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report he will lower his 1990 and 1991 earnings estimates sharply on AMR, citing weaker-than-expected business at its American Airlines unit. Mr. Karos declined to specify his new estimates.

AMR acknowledged that it has been reducing its expectations for air traffic growth, based on advance booking levels. "We don't know how much" the growth will drop off, said AMR spokesman Tim Smith. Shortly after the close, the company said it would cut certain fares by 15% to 40%; Trans World Airlines and Eastern Airlines lowered their fares Monday.

UAL, the parent company of United Airlines, fell 4 3/8 to 105 3/4 after postponing a planned 5.3% fare increase, designed to offset higher fuel costs. Delta Air Lines dropped 1 7/8 to 57 1/2.

Advanced Micro Devices rose 1/4 to 6 on 1.5 million shares, reflecting speculation that the company may obtain the right to manufacture its own version of Intel's 80386 microprocessor. The companies' dispute over the issue is now in arbitration, and a decision is due next month.

Bear Stearns & Co. analyst Monroe I. Neff cited the potential for Advanced Micro to win the case for cutting Intel's investment rating to "hold" from "buy," according to the investor report. Intel, which also was removed from the firm's list of recommended issues, fell 1 1/4 to 37 and was the most active issue traded on the Nasdaq National Market System.

General Motors added 5/8 to 40 1/8 after reaching a tentative settlement with the United Auto Workers union of a strike at a parts plant in Michigan. The walkout had forced a full or partial shutdown of five GM assembly plants.

May Department Stores rose 1 5/8 to 46 5/8. Other retailing issues also moved higher as the market digested quarterly reports; Tiffany rose 1 1/8 to 39 1/8, TJX climbed 1 1/8 to 15 and Waban gained 3/4 to 7 3/4.

On the other hand, J.C. Penney fell 2 to 48 1/2 following its fiscal second-quarter earnings report, which showed that net income declined 15% from the year-earlier level. Analysts had generally forecast that earnings would be about even with a year ago.

Sotheby's Holdings, whose second-quarter earnings were in line with expectations, added 3/4 to 13 1/4. Net income climbed 26% from a year earlier.

Hanson rose 3/4 to 20 3/8. The British company posted a 27% increase in pretax profit for the nine months ended in June.

Avnet added 1 to 24 1/2 after its board authorized the repurchase of up to one million common shares, representing about 2.8% of its shares outstanding.

Marion Merrell Dow went up 1 7/8 to 31 1/2. The company increased its quarterly dividend by one-third, to 20 cents a share.

Cineplex Odeon, which said there is "substantial uncertainty" about whether a proposed sale of 38 movie theaters for $31.75 million will be completed, fell 1/2 to 3 1/8. Also, the company reported a second-quarter loss that was only slightly narrower than its loss from continuing operations a year earlier.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 1.94 to 339.94. Volume totaled 15,770,000 shares.
