Foreign Exchange: Dollar Posts Its Lowest New York Finish Against Mark on Possible Mideast Peace

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/15/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar suffered its lowest New York finish against its German counterpart yesterday. For the first time since the mark was created after World War II, the U.S. currency was still being quoted below 1.57 marks at the time of day that foreign-exchange dealers consider the end of global trading.

The dollar's worst previous New York close occurred on Dec. 31, 1987, when the greenback finished at 1.57 marks exactly.

The U.S. currency just barely steered clear of its absolute bottom of 1.5615 marks, which it touched briefly in the midst of trading on Jan. 4, 1988. In the late New York afternoon yesterday, the dollar hit its intraday low of 1.5620 marks.

Eager to sell the dollar because of the U.S.'s flirtation with recession, dealers found an excuse with word that Jordan's King Hussein had left for Washington bearing a letter from Saddam Hussein of Iraq. Many participants speculated that the letter contained an offer for negotiations to resolve the Persian Gulf standoff, and they sold the dollar on the possibility of peace.

The dollar has fully retraced, and more, to its level at the end of July, before Iraq invaded Kuwait and investors bought into the U.S. safe haven.

Yesterday's trading session was slipped into history books "without a lot of fanfare," said Susan Stearns, senior corporate foreign exchange adviser in New York for Chicago's Harris Trust & Savings Bank. The dollar certainly didn't tumble and at no point plunged; it was just walked downhill, she said.

But even if the dollar had fallen with a crash, few were around to hear it. Other than the excitement created by testing relatively new territory, everything seemed to be discouraging participation in the currency market.

August is the height of the European vacation season. Those traders that remain in much of Continental Europe will celebrate Assumption today. At the same time, the "O-Bon" season, when Japanese return home, is at its height this week.

Add to that the lingering effects of Monday's blackout in downtown New York. Many large banks and brokerages nearest to the Brooklyn Bridge and the transformer there that caught on fire were still without electricity yesterday.

Among banks unable to operate were Credit Suisse, Lloyds Bank, National Westminster Bank, and Australia & New Zealand Banking Group. Also closed yesterday were two of the U.S.'s largest foreign exchange brokerages: Noonan, Astley & Pearce and Tullet & Tokyo Forex.

Dealing rooms trying to execute trades through a diminished brokerage network were hampered by the loss of the electronic direct dealing network over which about 85% of interbank currency trades are usually executed.

With many of their computer screens blank, dealers were forced to pick up an unfamiliar dinosaur and dial. "People are more reluctant to make prices over the telephone," said Dennis Pettit, New York foreign exchange manager for Long-Term Credit Bank of Japan. Beside not being used to working by telephone, traders missed the automatic transaction record that the electronic system offers, he said.

The result, Mr. Pettit said, was "severely curtailed direct dealing."

All systems were operating normally in the U.S. outside of New York, but said Steve Flynn, a sterling dealer at Bank of Boston, "With most of New York out, no one else wants to play."

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.5645 marks, down from 1.5765 marks when trading came to a halt in New York on Monday. Sterling was trading at $1.8981, up from $1.8830 previously.

The dollar's decline was led by strength in the Swiss franc. Japanese investors were said to be repaying loans they had taken out with the backing of Swiss bond holdings, said Ms. Stearns of Harris Trust. The U.S. unit declined to 1.3040 Swiss francs from 1.3150 francs in New York about midafternoon Monday.

The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 148.78 yen, down from 150.40 yen in New York Monday. Mr. Pettit said the dollar was at first only sold against the Swiss franc and mark, but when yen cross rates became attractive, the Japanese currency was purchased as well.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the dollar was trading soon after the opening at 148.15 yen, down from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 149.90 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5665 marks; in Tokyo Tuesday, it closed at 1.5767 marks.

Robert Mannolito, a New York trader with Commerzbank, said the thin dealings ensured that dollar pricing went in only one direction yesterday. Whenever the situation in the Kuwait-Saudi Arabia frontier seems to settle down, dealers sell the dollar.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $404.80 an ounce, up $7.90. Estimated volume was a very heavy 6.5 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $404.60 an ounce.
