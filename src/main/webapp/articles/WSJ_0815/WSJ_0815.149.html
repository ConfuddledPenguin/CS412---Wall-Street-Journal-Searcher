BP Says Output From Prudhoe Bay May Ease Shortage

  • Author: Allanna Sullivan
  • Date: 08/15/90

NEW YORK -- British Petroleum Co., the biggest producer in Alaska's giant oilfields, said it could quickly boost its output there as much as 10% to offset an oil shortage in the U.S. In an interview, James Ross, chief exeuctive officer of BP America Inc., said that as much as 200,000 barrels of additional daily crude production might be squeezed from Alaska's Prudhoe Bay, the nation's biggest oilfield, and nearby fields.

"We have a little (room) to increase," Mr. Ross said. Such increased Prudhoe Bay production could temporarily replace as much as 25% of the oil the U.S. had been importing from Iraq and Kuwait. The additional Alaskan production could come into play in the fourth quarter, Mr. Ross indicated.

BP made the calculations in response to an inquiry from the Energy Department. Mr. Ross said the question was part of a survey of major U.S. oil producers to ascertain how much additional oil can be produced quickly from flagging U.S. oilfields. Output from Prudhoe Bay began to decline last year.

Mr. Ross said that additional wells could quickly be drilled into existing Alaskan oil reservoirs. But he warned that sustaining such a high level of output for long could do serious damage, including reducing the amount of oil recoverable in the long run.

In addition to sinking new wells, he said, if operators are given permission by the state to burn off natural gas that is normally produced along with oil, output from existing wells could increase. Currently, operators are required to reinject the gas underground to conserve that natural resource. Mr. Ross said environmental restrictions on such flaring would have to be waived.

As for world-wide markets, Mr. Ross said he believes that if only 50% of the four million barrels a day currently lost from Iraq and Kuwait could be made up, supply disruptions probably could be averted. He maintained that sufficient oil inventory exists world-wide to meet needs through November if that much additional production can be found.

But he cautioned: "Any other shock to the supply system would clearly throw us into a supply crisis."

Mr. Ross said believes that Iraq may have more support in the Arab world than is immediately apparent and thus is bracing for a long stand-off in the Middle East. "Anyone believing that this (crisis) will be over within the next couple of months will be in for a considerable shock," Mr. Ross predicted.

He argued that the U.S. and other consuming nations must be ready to head off supply shortages as fast as possible, and noted that he and other industry executives are urging Energy Department officials to prepare to activitate the Strategic Petroleum Reserve quickly if the need arises.

Mr. Ross said he recently unsuccessfully urged Henson Moore, deputy energy secretary, at least to make the necessary procedural preparations immediately. "By getting the administrative machine ready, we would be prepared to inject oil into the market overnight,"he said, adding: "Even small quantities of the oil would have a calming effect on the market."
