Letters to the Editor: Technically, They Don't Know Beans

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/15/90

So now it is the omniscient beanies who are at fault for our lag with the Japanese and Germans ("The Techies' Challenge to the Bean Counters," Manager's Journal, July 16, by Donald N. Frey). The trouble with Mr. Frey's counsel is that he believes the chief executive officer, the man at the top, will make a business successful by his own knowledge. I doubt that is the key to success in today's business world. More than likely the revitalization of the heavy-machinery company, where Mr. Frey was a member of the board, is because the new CEO is aware of the gains that are made when you harmonize skills and practice the art of listening. Give me that sort of leader and it won't make much difference whether he is an auto mechanic, bean counter or rocket scientist. The key is to surround yourself with knowledgeable people, make some effort to see that they have diverse backgrounds and be sure they know they are on the team.

My belief is that the current president of Ford Automotive Group, Allan Gilmour, knows one helluva a lot about working with people even though most of his career and background, including MBA, Harvard, is as a "bean counter." Anyone who says "financial control and measurement is all (he) know(s)" doesn't know diddly. If you are going to run articles such as Mr. Frey's, at least change the heading from the "Manager's Journal" to the "Archaic Manager's Journal."

Richard G.W. Williams

Northville, Mich.

---

Bravo to Mr. Frey for enunciating one of the most important problems in America today. Thankfully, he does not succumb to the common misconception that we lack quality engineering and scientific manpower compared with the Japanese and the Germans. In fact, our engineering and scientific base is second to none. What we lack is a high level of technological literacy in the general population -- including most business executives and political leaders. Because of this, our scientific/engineering class is essentially disenfranchised -- it has nowhere near the influence it should have in business and political decisions. This situation could lead to our inexorable decline as a first-rate economic power.

What we need is full-scale cultural change, and nothing less. Increased technical capabilities will be required not only of CEOs but of politicians, lawyers and the general population.

Marius S. Vassiliou

Thousand Oaks, Calif.

---

Mr. Frey's arbitrary classification of "techies" and "beanies" is false. More than a quarter of the 1990 MBA crop have engineering or technical backgrounds. Obviously, these former techies didn't buy the premise that they were "fast becoming excellent businessmen" while working in a technical capacity like Mr. Frey's "competent engineer." On the other hand, to the lasting benefit of corporate America, "financial types" don't believe that "financial control and measurement is all they (will ever) know" or that their only hope of acquiring even a rudimentary understanding of technology will be through a techie interpreter.

The steady influx of corporate-sponsored German and Japanese students to U.S. business schools demonstrates that foreign companies recognize these beanie academies are providing something of value. Fortunately for the financial staff at Bell and Howell, even Mr. Frey "eventually concluded" that the beanies did "have the company's best interests at heart." Otherwise, we can only assume mass dismissals would have been his sole recourse.

By the way, the preferred form of address is beaner, not beanie.

Daniel A. Mancia

MBA, University of Michigan

James G. Peyton

MBA, University of Chicago Chicago

Mr. Frey puts his finger on an endemic problem in American industry. I would underscore the "essential characteristics." Having worked in two major industrial settings in which strong technical people served as CEOs, I found that the best-laid plans can fail when:

-- The CEO never learns to speak in anything but technical jargon; indeed, takes glee in leaving his or her non-technical people in the semantic dust.

-- The CEO uses emerging technology to leverage a return to old, comfortable products and lines of business well after they are moribund.

-- The CEO not only does not network into the nontechnical company sector, he or she openly disdains "non-techies" for their lack of "insight."

-- The CEO was elevated to his or her position on the basis of technological performance, by "techies," who hold him accountable as a CEO more on science than on the "bottom line" -- until it is too late. They may have never put new technology successfully into practice.

In many cases they may have done so unsuccessfully, but were rewarded nonetheless. The landscape of corporate technology is littered with executives who were rewarded for winning a program that virtually bankrupted the company later.

N.E. Koehler III

Evanston, Ill.
