Inside Track: Several Thrift Officers and Directors Have Increased Their Stock Holdings

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 08/15/90

NEW YORK -- There are some bulls working in the beleaguered thrift industry. Officers, directors and major shareholders at several savings banks and savings and loan associations have substantially increased their holdings of their companies' shares, Securities and Exchange Commission filings show.

Since May 1, the number of insiders buying thrift stocks has outnumbered sellers three to one, according to the SEC filings. All told, insiders at 65 out of 201 publicly traded thrifts have bought shares in roughly the past three months, according to Invest/Net, a North Miami, Fla., company that tracks and analyzes SEC insider filings.

Thrift stocks have been hit hard in recent months by the well-publicized S&L debacle. And tensions in the Middle East, sparking fears of recession and higher interest rates, have driven thrift stock prices down further. Since Dec. 31, the sector as a whole has fallen almost 18%.

"Whenever you have a whole group {of stocks} come down, the fall takes the good ones and the bad ones together," said Robert Gabele, president of Invest/Net. But bargain-hunting investors tempted to follow the example of thrift-industry insiders should use caution: Historically, these insiders haven't tended to be great market timers, Mr. Gabele said.

Indeed, several of the thrift-industry insiders who have bought in recent months have lost money on the purchases, at least on paper.

Investors who are selectively bullish on thrifts argue that, while recession is still only a possibility, there have already been actual economic changes that have benefited the thrift industry. Long-term interest rates have risen more steeply than short-term rates, which is positive for the industry because thrifts fund their operations with short-term money, but generally lend longer term, said Nick Adams, portfolio manager of First Financial Fund, which invests in thrift issues.

Moreover, prices of thrift-industry stocks already have the worst-case scenario built into them, leaving them to some degree insulated from further stock market debacles, some bullish money managers said.

One of the largest recent purchases by a thrift insider was made by David Himick, a director of Heritage Bankcorp. in Taylor, Mich. He bought 32,300 shares in April and June, just about doubling his holdings. He paid an average of $9.32 a share, or $301,036.

"Because of the publicity of the failing S&Ls and their problems," Heritage Bankcorp. stock has fallen drastically, said Mr. Himick. The stock, which reached a high of $14.50 a share last year, closed yesterday in national over-the-counter trading at $7.875, unchanged.

Mr. Himick said he's attempting to acquire a 5% stake in the thrift because "it's very profitable," has complied with all regulatory requirements, and its management is "very frugal, very lean as far as expense accounts." Mr. Himick added that he purchased an additional 6,500 shares in July that aren't yet reflected in SEC filings.

James Large, chairman of Anchor Savings Bank, increased his holdings in the New York City thrift by 24% in May, with the purchase of 5,000 shares, SEC filings show. He paid $1.94 a share.

Mr. Large said he's been buying the thrift's shares regularly because he believes it is in a turnaround situation. "Anchor was a thrift that was short on capital and losing money," he said. But now, as a result of a corporate fix-it effort, "it's humming along awfully nicely."

He said the thrift has trimmed staff to 1,875 from 2,500 employees in the past two years, has "peeled off all of our assets not central" to the primary business of lending money, and will have enough capital to meet federal regulatory requirements by next year.

In its fourth quarter ended June 30, Anchor had net income of $2.15 a share, compared with a year-earlier net loss of $1.75 a share, reflecting gains from asset sales plus improved operations. Yesterday, the stock closed at $1.375, down 6.25 cents, in over-the-counter trading.

At Greater New York Savings Bank, the chairman and four directors increased their holdings in May by a total of 51,147 shares, according to SEC filings. Insiders at the New York thrift paid an average price of $8.03 a share, or a total of $410,710. The largest purchase was made by director Philip Ruppel, who bought 24,100 shares, increasing his total direct and indirect holdings 47%, SEC filings show. Charles Ohlig, chairman, purchased 347 shares, bringing his holdings to 12,808 shares, according to filings. Messrs. Ohlig and Ruppel declined to comment.

In over-the-counter trading, the shares of Greater New York closed at $4, up 12.5 cents, yesterday.

As previously reported, Nathaniel Gross, president of Bell Savings Holdings Inc. of Upper Darby, Pa., purchased 75,000 shares at $1.50 a share on July 12. The $112,500 purchase increased his holdings 47%. In over-the-counter trading, the shares of Bell Savings closed at $1.875, down 25 cents, yesterday.

Three other insiders at Bell Savings who bought shares in the thrift earlier haven't been so fortunate. The three paid an average of $3.08 a share in April and May for 4,100 shares, SEC filings show.
