Columbia S&L Had Loss of $136.5 Million In Second Quarter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/15/90

BEVERLY HILLS, Calif. -- Columbia Savings & Loan Association, sinking deeper into insolvency and possibly into the arms of federal regulators, reported a second-quarter net loss of $136.5 million, or $6.78 a share. Columbia cited four factors: an $88.5 million provision for credit losses on investment securities and loans; a $65 million loss from real-estate investment operations due largely to the declining Southern California commercial real-estate market; a $29.2 million loss recorded with respect to certain corporate facilities, and a $20.9 million write-down in its mortgage-backed securities holdings.

The loss reduced stockholders' equity to a deficit of $352.2 million at June 30. In a statement, Edward G. Harshfield, president and chief executive officer, said the company "will need the continuing patience and cooperation of our regulators if Columbia is to survive." Despite being insolvent, Columbia hasn't been taken over by regulators.

Mr. Harshfield said the latest charges reflect efforts to "stabilize Columbia, to identify realizable values for the assets Columbia holds and to position the association for the sale of its high-yield bond portfolio."

The thrift agreed last month to sell the portfolio of junk bonds to a Canadian and Hong Kong investor group for $3 billion; regulators must still approve the transaction. Declining values of the bonds over the last few months have been mainly responsible for Columbia's insolvency, although Mr. Harshfield said the losses basically ended in the latest quarter.

The loss for the quarter ended June 30 compared with net income of $6.4 million, or 16 cents a share, a year earlier. For the six months, Columbia reported a net loss of $429.9 million, or $21.39 a share, compared to profit of $14.3 million, or 36 cents a share, a year earlier.
