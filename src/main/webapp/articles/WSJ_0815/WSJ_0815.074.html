First Brands' Profits Rose to $4.4 Million In Fourth Quarter

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/15/90

DANBURY, Conn. -- First Brands Corp. reported that increased sales of its plastic bags and automotive care products pushed fiscal fourth-quarter earnings sharply higher, and may improve results in the next fiscal year. First Brands, which said that it is continuing to solicit bids for a sale of the company, reported earnings of $4.4 million, or 20 cents per share, for the quarter ended June 30, up from $863,000, or four cents per share, in the year-earlier period.

The company's total fourth-quarter sales, however, were down to $248 million, from $273.3 million a year earlier.

First Brands, which was created by a 1986 leveraged buyout of several businesses from the Union Carbide Corp., said that strong sales of its Glad plastic bags and wraps and its STP automotive additives more than offset lagging sales of its Prestone antifreeze.

The company's chief financial officer, Donald DeSantis, explained that antifreeze prices had fallen since an earlier shortage of a petrochemical ingredient had eased.

The company's Glad and STP brand products, however, have been increasing in unit volume, sales and profitability, he said.

In late June, the controlling shareholders of First Brands announced that they were seeking a buyer for all or part of the company.

"The process of soliciting bids to purchase the company is continuing, and several parties have expressed an interest," said Alfred E. Dudley, the company's chairman, chief executive officer, and president, in a prepared statement.

For the fiscal year, earnings were $49.6 million, or $2.40 per share, down from $61.0 million, or $3.03 per share, in fiscal 1989. Total sales were $1.01 billion in 1990, down from $1.21 billion in 1989.

The 1989 results, however, were inflated by a $15.5 million gain from the sale of the company's household-products business in Europe. In addition, 1990 earnings were hurt by a $786,000 charge related to the repurchase of senior subordinated debt.
