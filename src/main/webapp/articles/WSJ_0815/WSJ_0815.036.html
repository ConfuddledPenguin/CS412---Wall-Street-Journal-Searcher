Steelmaker Pact Advances Firm's Reorganization

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 08/15/90

PITTSBURGH -- Wheeling-Pittsburgh Steel Corp. reached a tentative agreement with the United Steelworkers union that gives workers 11% of the company and paves the way for the steelmaker to emerge from Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection. The tentative contract reached here represents a victory for both the company and the union. For the U.S.'s eighth-largest steelmaker, the pact is the last major hurdle in its five-year effort to get a reorganization plan approved. This year, the U.S. Bankruptcy Court said it wouldn't confirm a reorganization plan unless a labor pact was ratified with the union and accepted by creditors.

The union, for its part, succeeded in patterning the contract after other accords signed with major steelmakers last year. Thus, it took another step in its goal to return to wage parity among all major producers. During the turbulent 1980s, steelmakers broke with the lockstep wage patterns that had been the industry's tradition by negotiating separate settlements.

The contract is subject to approval of the membership and the union's International Executive Board, which is scheduled to vote Monday. A membership vote hasn't yet been scheduled. The accord has already received the backing of the company's major creditors. The union negotiated the final terms with a group led by New York investor Ron LaBow, the company's largest shareholder and creditor, after the company walked out of talks Sunday.

As part of the agreement, union employees, who were creditors of the bankrupt company, will receive 2.2 million common shares, representing approximately 11% of the reorganized company. The stock is payment for the $26.8 million owed employees in an employee investment program.

Under the contract, union employees will receive an immediate $1.50-an-hour average wage increase representing restoration of past wage sacrifices. In addition, they will receive a 50-cent average hourly wage increase in each of the next three years, a $3,000 signing bonus and a $500 bonus in 1991. The contract also restores one week of vacation, vacation bonuses, five holidays, Sunday premium of time and a half, and incentive rates.

The terms of the new contract will take effect upon confirmation of a new plan of reorganization by the Bankruptcy Court, which is expected to act by the end of September. The contract will expire March 1, 1994.

"There's no question that this is a good contract, one that our members richly deserve after 10 years of sacrifices," said Andrew "Lefty" Palm, chairman of the union's negotiating committee. "There is also no question that the company can well afford this contract."

Union officials said the wage terms are similar to those in contracts signed last year by major integrated steelmakers such as Bethlehem Steel Corp., National Steel Co. and LTV Steel Corp. LTV is also operating under Chapter 11, in which a company is protected from creditor lawsuits while it works out a plan to reorganize. The contract also includes new language, in line with agreements reached last year with other companies, that severely restricts the contracting out of work.

The company and its creditors had balked at many of those terms, deeming them too rich for a newly reorganized company. However, creditors apparently decided that the contract would be less of a financial blow than a strike. The union threatened to strike tomorrow if negotiations deadlocked.

The union hopes to use the pattern when approaching the nation's largest steelmaker, USX Corp., this year in contract talks. USX has already said it doesn't believe in pattern contracts and was undoubtedly hoping that Wheeling-Pittsburgh would negotiate different terms.

In Wheeling, W.Va., Wheeling-Pittsburgh officials said they were pleased an agreement had been reached and looked forward to emerging from bankruptcy-law protection. Once the company emerges, Vice Chairman D. Leonard Wise is expected to take over as chief executive officer, succeeding turnaround specialist William J. Scharffenberger.
