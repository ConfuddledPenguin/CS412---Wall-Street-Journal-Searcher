Advertising: Sears's Glitzy Ads Target Buyers of Stylish Fashions

  • Author: Francine Schwadel
  • Date: 08/15/90

Fashion magazines are known for their alluring advertising. Just flip through the August issue of Vogue. There's a provocative four-page spread for Georges Marciano, a sultry ad for Capezio bags, Mario Valentino's seductive appeal and a pouty-lipped model in an ad for Sears, Roebuck & Co. Sears?

Yup. And it's no mistake.

It's all part of a new shock 'em strategy by the drudge of retailing. Sears wants to grab the attention of women who wouldn't be caught dead in its women's apparel department.

Besides Vogue and Glamour magazines, Sears plans to hype its new look this fall in other women's magazines, notably Cosmopolitan, Mademoiselle and Essence.

This month's Vogue ad introduces an inexpensive sportswear collection designed for Sears by Cathy Hardwick, once a common fixture in department stores. Although the Sears ad isn't as sexy or sophisticated as the Vogue spreads for Bergdorf Goodman, Macy's or Nordstrom, it's a far cry from the typical -- and lifeless -- Sears layout. The message: The giant retailer now sells stylish clothes.

Sears also will be the sole sponsor of a special issue on women that Time magazine plans to publish in October. In 28 pages of ads, Sears will promote women's apparel -- and other things, like its Allstate insurance policies. But Thomas E. Morris, vice president of marketing at Sears, says: "We're not hard-selling product. It is our hope that people reading the issue will say, `Sears understands my life -- how busy I am -- and they're responding to it.'"

Sears needs to attract this younger, more affluent shopper. Its own research shows that its departments appeal to the over-40 crowd with annual household incomes of less than $30,000. To change that profile, Sears is updating its selection and renovating its stores. The plan has been in the works for some time and doesn't reflect Sears Chairman Edward Brennan's new campaign to improve the retailer's merchandising operations.

But Sears, which suddenly views fashion as a way to reverse years of market-share losses and sagging earnings, faces a tough sell. It built its reputation hawking hard goods like Kenmore appliances, Craftsman tools and DieHard batteries mostly to men.

Even people in the Sears camp acknowledge the challenge. "Sears announcing by itself that it has stylish clothes doesn't achieve credibility," says Bruce Beach, a managing director of WPP Group PLC's Ogilvy & Mather unit, which is handling the Sears ads. But, he says, spreads in magazines like Vogue and Time will "force people to think differently about the kinds of fashion that are available at Sears."

J.C. Penney Co., a mass merchant that spent the past decade shedding hard goods to focus on fashion, also started advertising in Vogue to achieve credibility. But skeptics say Vogue may be too much of a stretch for Sears.

Ayer Inc.'s Thomas Hooke, who handles the Penney account, says Sears's fashion statement could further confuse consumers about the store's strategy. He contends that recent Sears ads have sent "a lot of mixed and crossed signals" by touting everything from "everyday low pricing" to national brands in areas like appliances. "The consumer sees Sears with one set of eyes," he says. "They don't differentiate by department."

Agrees Clive Chajet, chairman of image consultant Lippincott & Margulies in New York: "There are so many other Sears messages going out that are contradictory to a fashion image." The Vogue audience, he believes, is probably too upscale for Sears.

But Ogilvy's Mr. Beach defends the strategy, noting that Sears "isn't trying to imitate the Lord & Taylors and Neiman Marcuses of the world," nor is it trying to attract "the purchaser of French first-run fashions." The target audience? "People who are in the market to purchase stylish contemporary but good-value clothes."

Read that cheap. Unlike the $300 threads Saks Fifth Avenue advertises in the current issue of Vogue, Sears is hawking Hardwick pieces priced from $26 to $64. Insiders say the retailer seeks to create a Gap-like appeal. It wants shoppers to think of Sears as a place to buy low-priced but stylish basics. Yet skeptics point out that Gap Inc. succeeds partly because it sells only clothes.

Still, Sears was emboldened by the results of its first major effort last year to target women. The retailer hired an actress who appears to be in her 30s to make its corporate pitch on TV. In the commercials, she talks frankly about why she likes Sears auto and appliance repair services and credit and return policies, among other things.

"We've gotten a tremendous amount of consumer feedback from these spots," says Sears's Mr. Morris. "It's all positive."

Ad Notes. . . .

NEW ACCOUNTS: Procter & Gamble awarded the estimated $15 million account for its Old Spice and Santa Fe men's toiletries and fragrances to Tatham-Laird & Kudner, Chicago. Scali McCabe Sloves, a unit of WPP Group, formerly handled the account. P&G, which acquired the brands in July from American Cyanamid's Shulton Group, said it sought to consolidate its business at an agency currently handling P&G brands. Tatham-Laird already handles several P&G accounts, including Coast, Mr. Clean, Metamucil, Head & Shoulders and Chloraseptic. Scali McCabe had been put on the spot by the Shulton purchase: Sister WPP shops Ogilvy & Mather and J. Walter Thompson create ads for P&G rival Unilever. . . . Hofstra University picked Edward Bird Wilson, New York, to handle its account. Terms weren't disclosed.

STARKIST TOPS VNRs: More than 81 million TV viewers saw footage from StarKist's "Dolphin-Safe Tuna" PR video, making it the most-watched video news release for the first half of 1990. The StarKist release topped the previous record of 80 million viewers for a Drexel Burnham Lambert VNR in 1988, according to Medialink, a Video Broadcasting Corp. unit that transmits the releases to TV stations.

READ THEIR LIPS: A new print campaign for Smirnoff vodka, picking up on President Bush's now-broken campaign pledge, asks consumers to "Read Your Lips." "One sip will tell it all," say the ads, created by McCann-Erickson for the Heublein Inc. brand. "And that's a promise not subject to change."
