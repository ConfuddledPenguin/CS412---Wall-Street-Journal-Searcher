White House Seeks to Arrange Takeover Of Troubled Student-Loan Guarantor

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 07/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration, rushing to calm financial market fears, said it's trying to negotiate a friendly takeover of Higher Education Assistance Foundation, the financially troubled guarantor of federal student loans. Education Department officials said the government has opened discussions with United Student Aid Fund, another large but profitable guarantee agency, about taking over $9.3 billion of HEAF's guaranteed student loans.

"We have had some discussions" with the government, said Susan Macey Thompson, an assistant to the chairman of the student aid fund. But she said that "the characterization of taking over the HEAF portfolio is an extreme one. . . . Everything is really preliminary at this point. The department is looking for some suggestions about what to do." She said that the student aid fund also was talking with other guarantee agencies about HEAF. "We're all trying to sort fact from fiction," she said.

HEAF, a nonprofit corporation based in Overland Park, Kan., recently told the government that it has serious financial problems because of high default rates on some of the loans it has guaranteed. A report of HEAF's difficulties in Monday's Wall Street Journal triggered fears of spreading defaults among the $51 billion of guaranteed student loans outstanding and contributed to Monday's 56.44 point stock market drop.

At a news conference yesterday, Education Department officials went out of their way to lay such fears to rest. "We don't believe that HEAF is the first in a long string of failures of student-loan guarantee agencies," said Ted Sanders, undersecretary of education. He said that five or six of the 55 guarantee agencies may be facing difficulties because of defaults but that their problems were smaller and more manageable.

Officials sought to assure both students and banks that they would continue to have access to the guarantees. "This situation will not affect the ability of students to obtain loans, of lenders to make those loans, or of guarantors to insure those loans," Education Secretary Lauro Cavazos said. Mr. Sanders added, "We intend to seek a solution that does continue to guarantee those loans."

Under the program, when a student borrows from a bank, one of the nation's 55 guarantee agencies insures the loan and the Education Department reinsures the loan.

In most cases, the Education Department pays the guarantee agency 100% of the balance on defaulted loans. But that rate can drop as low as 80% if the guarantee agency's payments on defaulted loans exceed 9% of the agency's insured loans.

At issue in HEAF's case is $300 million in payments to banks for defaulted loans. Because HEAF has a high default rate on the loans it has guaranteed, it stands to receive only 80% of that amount, or $240 million. One question is how much, if any, of this $60 million the government will pay as part of an effort to work out an orderly transfer of HEAF's assets.

"This is not a big federal bailout," said Thomas Scully, an associate director of the Office of Management and Budget. "The liability to the taxpayer is very narrow. It's not a huge budget problem."

A team of investigators from the Education Department and the OMB arrived at HEAF's St. Paul, Minn., computer facility Monday to review the agency's records. It's unlikely that any organization will take over HEAF's assets until the examination is complete, and that could take several days, officials said.

In a statement, HEAF said it's cooperating with the government and "pursuing on its own additional options that would enable us to restore confidence" to the guarantee program. In a separate statement, the Student Loan Marketing Association, one of HEAF's largest creditors, reasserted its belief that it faces "no material risk of loss or other adverse effects as a result of HEAF's financial situation."

HEAF has guarantees outstanding on $9.3 billion of loans that aren't in default, although some of these loans are expected to go sour. Over the years, HEAF aggressively marketed its guarantees to proprietary schools for hairdressers, truck drivers and others that subsequently proved to have high default rates.

The government must decide how much protection it's willing to provide the United Student Aid Fund or others against loss on this portfolio. If the student aid fund doesn't take the entire portfolio, it could be split among other guarantee agencies.

The government also must decide what to do with $2 billion of defaulted loans on which the government has reimbursed HEAF over the years. If a guarantee agency collects from the students, the agency gets to keep 30% of the total. If the government collects, the government gets to keep 100%. The Education Department, which collects 65% of the defaulted loans it goes after, is considering taking over the entire $2 billion.

Student-loan defaults have been a problem for years, despite efforts to tighten the program. For fiscal 1990, the government has budgeted $2 billion to cover the cost of defaults, up from $1.4 billion two years ago.
