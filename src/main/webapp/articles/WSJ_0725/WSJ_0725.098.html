Politics & Policy: Budget Negotiators Hope to Exchange Deficit-Reductions Proposals This Week

  • Author: David Wessel and Michel McQueen
  • Date: 07/25/90

Negotiators at the bipartisan budget talks hope to trade deficit-reduction packages this week, a sign the talks may be about to get more serious, participants said. Participants cautioned that differences on taxes and spending cuts remain wide and that it will be difficult to bridge them before Congress leaves for its August recess.

But an exchange of plans between the Bush administration and congressional Democrats could be a significant step forward. Negotiators also agreed to "a code of conduct" that forbids them from talking publicly about the details of their discussions, another hint that the talks may finally be changing character after more than two months.

Democrats and Republicans both warned there has been no breakthrough yet. House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.) said negotiators were making "incremental progress." His Republican counterpart, Rep. Robert Michel of Illinois, said: "I thought I was a patient person. But I'm getting awfully impatient. Nothing's happened."

President Bush and top congressional Democrats, meanwhile, negotiated a cease-fire in an attempt to save the budget summit, but Mr. Bush promptly took to the campaign trail to criticize Democrats as big spenders.

"There is a general consensus that we need to concentrate on reducing the partisan comments," House Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.) said after meeting with Mr. Bush and other congressional leaders. White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater confirmed that both sides agreed "that they would try to keep the rhetoric under control and really try to make this a productive week."

But within hours, in remarks prepared for delivery to New York Republicans, Mr. Bush said that "to deal with a Democrat Congress is to often face government by gridlock -- with spending skyrocketing out of control, good legislation thrown aside for pork and a budget deficit looming over our children's children."

Earlier in the day, speaking to Pennsylvania Republicans in Philadelphia, the president alternated between his roles as party leader and head of state. In his prepared text, he declared that when the two parties' philosophies on taxes, spending and education are compared, "our side wins."

"As long as spending runs out of control in Congress," he added, "the American people will pay the price."

But he inserted more conciliatory remarks, some written and some ad libbed, expressing hope that the budget deadlock can be broken. "I salute those Democrats in the leadership who are now working with me . . . trying to achieve a bipartisan agreement that we all can live with that would be good for our country."

Mr. Bush is to meet with congressional leaders again this morning, and summit negotiators plan to reconvene this afternoon. The two sides are seeking a package of tax increases and spending cuts to shrink the deficit by $50 billion for the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1.
