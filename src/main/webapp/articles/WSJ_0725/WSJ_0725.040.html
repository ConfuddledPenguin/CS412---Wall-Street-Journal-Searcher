REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Liberate the Lions

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/25/90

The adventures of the Great Circus Bim Bom From Russia have been a high-wire lesson in capitalism. Having made national TV when their U.S. tour went bust, the performers are still holed up in Marietta, Ga., living off the good will of local residents and entangled in the Byzantine complexities of the U.S. legal system. The Bim Bom's adventure started out cheerfully enough. The circus all-stars -- drawn from state circuses throughout the Soviet Union -- arrived here in April for a two-year goodwill tour of the U.S. But before anyone could say glasnost, the circus's Kuwaiti and American backers skedaddled, leaving behind $1.6 million in debts. Pressed by creditors, the circus has filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. Lawyers are arguing about whether this is kosher, given that the circus maybe belongs to the Soviet government. Such litigiousness isn't easy for a bear to bear; the Soviet government disavows responsibility.

So the Bim Bom is hopelessly entangled in a net of suits that would daunt a Houdini. Just when you think it's impossible to squeeze another clown into a little car, another lawyer latches on. Bim Bom manager Yuri Turkin describes this situation as making "an elephant out of a fly." His ursine dancers are fidgety, and its famous wrestling bear now "wrestles with misfortune."

Still, Mr. Turkin looks on the bright side. While in captivity, a lion has given birth to two cubs, and the circus's 18 parrots, which used to speak only Russian, are learning English. But one creditor, a Newburgh, N.Y., transport company, confiscated the circus's animals as hostages. Another creditor took its equipment, which it doesn't have to feed.

A breakaway group, including the Flying Stankeevs, a family of aerialists, followed the star acrobat to Las Vegas, in a so-far-futile search for a stage. Meanwhile, Marietta churches and civic groups have come to the aid of the rest. Though the performers concede that they could go home, leaving the equipment and animals behind, it's not hard to understand why they take their chances with Southern hospitality and the American courts rather than face empty grocery-store shelves back home.

So the cast includes Russian circus folk, American lawyers, Kuwaiti promoters, Southern civic boosters, Northern creditors, bears, lions, parrots and the Soviet government. After looking into the matter, we've decided it would take a year's research to figure out who's conning whom. As a famous American once said, "There's a sucker born every minute."

Still, this looks to us like a good chance to finally make perestroika work. Among the various talents assembled, there should be some way to liberate the lions and bears and let the Bim Bom go back to work.
