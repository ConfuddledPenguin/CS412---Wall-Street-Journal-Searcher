Credit Markets: Bond Prices Fall as Fed Chairman's Promise To Fight Inflation Fails to Convince Investors

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 07/25/90

NEW YORK -- The bond market ended lower again after Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan failed to convince investors that the nation's central bank remains tough on inflation. In part-two of his semiannual economic report to Congress, Mr. Greenspan said the Fed has no intention of backing away from its goal of zero inflation and that central bank policies will eventually bring prices down.

But his words weren't enough to inspire confidence in the battered bond market, which turned overwhelmingly bearish on July 13 when the Fed cut the federal funds rate by 1/4 percentage point to 8%. The rate is what banks charge each other on overnight loans.

"Barely a week after announcing an easing in the face of bad inflation numbers, he's trying to convince us he's shooting for zero inflation. You don't have to be cynical to raise your eyebrows at that," said Robert Brusca, an economist at Nikko Securities Co.

In the view of Mr. Brusca and many other economists, the Fed chairman never fully explained why the central bank cut rates. But the markets interpreted the move -- rightly or wrongly -- as a sign that the Fed is abandoning its fight against inflation and instead wants to stimulate economic growth. And nothing he said yesterday convinced analysts otherwise.

The Fed chairman, "got so much criticism last week for not addressing inflation that he tried to make up for it this time by making a special effort to say that the Fed is still fighting inflation. But the bond market didn't care. They expected him to say that," said Charles Lieberman, a managing director at Manufacturers Hanover Securities Inc.

Norman Robertson, chief economist at Mellon Bank Corp., said that investors' fears of inflation were heightened somewhat yesterday after the Department of Labor reported that its employment cost index, a broad measure of wage inflation, rose 5.4% in the 12 months that ended in June.

"The report shows that while inflation is not getting any worse, it's not getting any better," said Mr. Robertson. "It's really rather startling to realize that the inflation rate has been stuck at {current levels} for the several years," even while "we've had monetary restraint and sluggish economic growth."

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended about 3/8 point lower, a loss of about $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, rose to 8.58%.

Bond prices opened lower in the wake of selling by investors in Tokyo and London. Traders attribute that selling in part to concern that oil prices might soon rise due to heightened tensions between Iraq and Kuwait, both members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries.

Those concerns were underscored by an article in the Washington Post that said Iraq moved 30,000 troops to its border with Kuwait and the U.S. ordered its warships in the Persian Gulf on alert.

Treasury Securities

Prices of Treasury securities ended lower again. The benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 101 21/32, down from 102 on Monday. The latest two-year notes ended slightly lower at 100 14/32, down from 100 15/32 on Monday. The yield on the two-year note ended at 8.10%, up from 8.07% Monday.

Treasury bill rates rose yesterday. The discount rate on three-month bills ended at 7.58% to yield 7.81% while the rate on six-month bills rose to 7.45% to yield 7.83%.

Today, the Treasury will sell a record $11.25 billion two-year notes. Most analysts expect the sale to proceed smoothly. In when-issued trading yesterday, the yield on the two-year notes was about 8.11%, up from 8.06% late Monday in New York.

Mortgage, Asset-Backed Securities

Prices of mortgage securities ended off as much as 3/8 point in quiet trading.

Among secondary issues for August delivery, 9.5% securities issued by the Government National Mortgage Association, the Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. and the Federal National Mortgage Association were all off 11/32.

In the asset-backed market, a unit of Chrysler Corp., Chrysler Auto Receivables Co., sold $1 billion of securities backed by retail installment contracts on new or used autos and light trucks. The offering was priced by Merrill Lynch Capital Markets and was completely sold. A Merrill official said the issue will begin trading today in the secondary market.

The offering was priced at 8.75% at 99 23/32 to yield 8.856%. Expected final maturity is July 15, 1993.

Corporate Bonds

The junk bonds of Trump Taj Mahal Funding Inc. were knocked sharply lower yesterday on speculation that the Atlantic City casino's operator, Donald Trump, may be forced to pay hefty legal claims related to the 1988 sale of Resorts International to Merv Griffin.

Resorts is currently restructuring under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code. As reported in this newspaper Tuesday, a novel feature of Resorts' reorganization plan provides for a trust to be established and funded to pursue for the benefit of Resorts' creditors "all claims the debtors and their affiliates may have against Donald Trump, Trump Taj Mahal Associates L.P.," and other Trump affiliates.

Trump Taj Mahal's 14% first mortgage bonds fell 2 1/4 points to 48 1/4 on the American Stock Exchange while Trump Castle Funding Inc.'s 13.75% first mortgage bonds slipped 7/8 point to finish at 59 5/8.

The junk bonds of R.H. Macy & Co., which managed to outperform the broader market Monday, weakened on profit-taking. The retailer's 14.5% senior subordinated debentures lost 1 1/2 points to end at 74 1/2 while Macy's 14.5% subordinated debentures fell 2 points to end at 63.

In the broader junk bond market, most high-yield speculative-grade securities stabilized after slipping Monday in the face of a sell-off in the stock market.

Municipal Securities

Tax-exempt issues ended unchanged to 1/8 point lower.

In the primary market, a total of 76 bids from 24 underwriting firms were placed for an issue of $1.1 billion New York State general obligation tax anticipation notes sold this session.

All of the notes carry a 6.25% coupon and will be reoffered to yield 5.90%. The notes are rated MIG-1 by Moody's Investors Service Inc. and SP1-Plus by Standard & Poor's Corp.

Underwriters at several of the firms that purchased the notes reported good institutional interest in the notes, along with a smattering of retail buying.

The institutional demand is attributed to the high ratings and the size of the issue.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% of 2018 are off 1/8 point at 99 1/4 to yield 7.25%.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese government-bond market staged a modest recovery yesterday, but dealers said that sentiment remains fundamentally bearish.

Dealers said the market initially was supported by the Bank of Japan buying commercial paper at 7.41%, lower than Monday's commercial paper rate of 7.44%.

That was the second consecutive day that Japan's central bank purchased commercial paper. The market didn't respond favorably when the Bank of Japan purchased commercial paper on Monday, but yesterday's purchase did support the market.

Still, dealers say underlying sentiment remains bearish. "Short-term rates are still high," said a dealer at a Japanese securities firm. "Investors aren't buying in the yen bond market."

The overnight unsecured call money rates rose slightly yesterday to 7.56% and rates on new three-month certificates were unchanged at about 7.7%.

Dealers said reports that Iraq moved several thousand troops close to its border with Kuwait, escalating their dispute over oil policy, caused Japanese bonds to fall temporarily. But the market soon recovered.

At the 3 p.m. JST close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the price of the benchmark No. 119 10-year bond was 85.86, up 0.11 point, to yield 7.445%. (Japanese bond yields are quoted here in local terms and therefore differ from yields calculated for these markets by U.S. investment firms, which tend to use the semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets.)
