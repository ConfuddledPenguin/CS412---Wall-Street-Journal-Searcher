Stotler Begins Dismantling Move As Plan Collapses

  • Author: Jeffrey Taylor
  • Date: 07/25/90

CHICAGO -- Stotler Group Inc. began dismantling its operations after an investor group's conditional offer to buy a stake in the futures brokerage holding company collapsed. Stotler was among the largest independent futures clearing firms in Chicago.

The firm's inability to secure an infusion of money marked the failure of a frantic attempt by its executives to shore up Stotler's troubled finances. It is a major embarrassment for Stotler Chairman Karsten "Cash" Mahlmann, who also is chairman of the Chicago Board of Trade and who owns nearly 10% of Stotler's stock.

"It's really kind of sad," said a CBOT member who asked to remain anonymous.

Mr. Mahlmann was testifying before the U.S. Senate in his role as an exchange official and didn't return phone calls.

Stotler's executives wouldn't explain what caused the company's problems. In a brief announcement, the company said it would give up its clearing memberships on the major commodities exchanges, which cripples its ability to attract business from institutional investors.

Industry officials said the firm's troubles began when regulators questioned the way certain financial data were recorded by Stotler Group and its principal subsidiary, Stotler & Co. As a result of that, the Board of Trade determined that the company's capital levels were below industry minimums.

Stotler's problems worsened as some of its major customers began fleeing to other futures firms. In addition, Stotler was forced to surrender some of its best accounts to competitors. That move was designed to reduce the amount of capital Stotler was obligated to hold to comply with commodities exchanges' minimum requirements.

Exchange members said Stotler is also being weakened by an exodus of some of its brokers to competing firms.

The conditional offer for part of Stotler, widely rumored to have originated in Taiwan and amounted to more than $10 million, collapsed when the investor group's board of directors failed to approve the purchase by Monday morning. Yesterday's announcement of the failed deal pummeled Stotler's stock, which fell $4 to close at $2.50 a share in trading on the American Stock Exchange.

Exchange members said that Stotler also is dismissing employees. Other employees, they said, have begun seeking work elsewhere, both on their own and with the firm's help. "The message seems to be find another spot fairly quickly," one exchange member said.

In its statement, Stotler said it had made arrangements to clear its trades through an omnibus clearing account with another futures firm, Brody, White & Co.

Until a few months ago, Brody White was known as Mocatta Futures Corp. Brody White President Stephen Bergen said his firm was simply acting to clear Stotler's trades and hadn't acquired any part of the company.
