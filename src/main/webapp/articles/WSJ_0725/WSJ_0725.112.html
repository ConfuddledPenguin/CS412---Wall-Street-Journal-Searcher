Morgan Stanley Stock Plunges On Profit News --- Firm Posts Decline of 48% In 2nd-Quarter Earnings; Salomon's Net Falls 53%

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 07/25/90

NEW YORK -- Morgan Stanley Group Inc. stock was hammered as the big securities firm posted a 48% drop in second-quarter earnings, its weakest quarter in nearly two years. Salomon Inc. reported second-quarter profit that was halved from record yearearlier results. Two regional brokers posted mixed results.

Morgan Stanley, dragged down by a 27% drop in investment banking revenue, reported net income of $58 million, or $1.44 a share, down from $112.1 million, or $2.83 a share, a year earlier. The year-earlier results were bolstered by a one-time merchant banking gain.

With some analysts expecting Morgan Stanley to earn as much as $2 a share, the firm's stock was pummeled, falling $6, or 8.3%, to $66.50 in heavy New York Stock Exchange trading. The drop highlights how Wall Street recently has punished companies -- even its own -- for earnings disappointments, analysts said.

"It's a sign of the times," said James Hanbury, an analyst at Wertheim Schroder & Co. Wall Street was thrown, he said, because until now, Morgan Stanley "had always delivered the goods."

Revenue at Morgan Stanley fell 5% in the second quarter to $1.45 billion from $1.52 billion. Trading revenue -- a big earnings driver in the first quarter -- fell 11% to $201.6 million from $226.5 million. Asset management revenue and stock commissions rose modestly.

In a statement, Morgan Stanley officials said operating performance "was affected by the continuing decline in overall volume of investment banking business" and attributed the trading drop largely to "decreased volatility in the foreign exchange market." Much of the firm's first-quarter trading gains stemmed from international stock markets.

The last time Morgan Stanley earned less was 1988's third quarter, when its net was $52.5 million.

For the six months, Morgan Stanley earned $140.2 million, or $3.51 a share, down from $193.3 million, or $4.86 a share, a year earlier. Six-month revenue rose 3.2% to $2.84 billion from $2.75 billion.

Salomon earned twice as much as Morgan Stanley in the second quarter, although it fell far short of its record earnings in the 1989 second quarter. Salomon's net in the most recent quarter slumped 53% to $120 million, or 89 cents a share, from $253 million, or $1.89 a share. Revenue fell 10% to $2.1 billion from $2.33 billion.

Earnings at Salomon's securities unit, Salomon Brothers Inc., were bolstered by trading gains and an improvement in investment banking revenue, particularly from stock underwriting and advisory fees from a handful of merger deals. The securities unit earned $211 million before taxes in the quarter, compared with record pretax profit of $364 million a year earlier.

Salomon Inc.'s Phibro Energy unit rebounded a bit, reflecting stronger refinery margins. Phibro earned $49 million before taxes in the second quarter, a 47% drop from $92 million a year earlier but far above $4 million in this year's first quarter.

Salomon's Philipp Brothers commodity unit continued to perform poorly; analysts say the unit soon will be sharply cut back. Philipp reported a $62 million pretax loss for the second quarter, far wider than the $3 million pretax loss a year earlier. Salomon said "adverse trading results" in agricultural commodities hammered the unit's performance. In past quarters, the villain was nonferrous metals trading.

As a result, Salomon has told analysts it will drastically cut back its commodity inventory and will restructure the unit, resulting in significant layoffs. "If it doesn't completely disband, lots of heads will be rolling," said one analyst who was briefed by the firm. Salomon declined to comment.

For the six months, Salomon reported net of $239 million, up 6% from $225 million in the year-earlier period. Revenue jumped 15% to $4.45 billion from $3.87 billion. Salomon shares closed unchanged at $24.50 in Big Board composite trading.

Two smaller regional firms posted mixed results.

Alex. Brown Inc., Baltimore, reported a 6.7% increase in second-quarter net to $4.5 million, or 29 cents a share, from $4.3 million, or 26 cents a share. Earnings at Piper Jaffray Inc., Minneapolis, fell 8.6% in the second quarter to $3.1 million, or 74 cents a share, from $3.35 million, or 83 cents a share.
