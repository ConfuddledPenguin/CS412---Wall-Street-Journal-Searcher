Politics & Policy: Resources Measure That Cuts Timber Sales Advances

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 07/25/90

WASHINGTON -- A House Appropriations subcommittee approved a $12.8 billion natural-resources bill that would cut federal timber sales in the Pacific Northwest in fiscal 1991 while increasing funding for the Indian Health Service by nearly 27%. Total sales of timber from federal lands would be cut to about 9.26 billion board feet nationally. Within the Northwest, the new 3.2 billion board foot level is an estimated 20% reduction from the current ceiling.

Forest Service road funds would be cut to $151.6 million, and though timber-industry allies blocked deeper reductions sought by the subcommittee chairman, Democratic Rep. Sidney Yates, the action reflects strong environmental pressure to better protect forests and threatened wildlife.

Mr. Yates had proposed cutting sales in the Northwest by 300 million more feet. But the new 3.2 billion-foot level is more than 1.1 billion feet below the annual level of only two years ago.

The northern spotted owl, which the U.S. recently declared "threatened" under the endangered species law, is the most visible symbol of a pitched fight over the fate of the forests, and the bill would provide an estimated $2.2 million to protect the bird, including research and law enforcement funds.

The measure includes none of the $175 million sought by the Bush administration for its "America the Beautiful" tree-planting program. But elsewhere, the bill significantly exceeds President Bush's budget in funding continuing programs to protect natural resources.

Funds for the management of Western range land and other renewable resources under the Bureau of Land Management would be increased by almost 25% from fiscal 1990 levels, for example, and the bill is supportive of a White House initiative to end timber sales entirely in eight so-called below-cost forests where receipts don't match the cost of running the program.

Always a battleground for industry and environmental interests, the underlying measure has drawn increased interest in recent years for its role in providing public funding for the arts. The National Endowment for the Arts would receive an estimated $180 million, and more than $36 million is provided for the Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts here, including $15 million for what amounts to a subsidy to help erase the center's deficit.

Both decisions are sure to provoke controversy. The endowment has become a favorite target for conservatives, who have sought to attach restrictions to its federal grants. In the case of the Kennedy Center, the bulk of the money would still be in the form of a Park Service appropriation to maintain the building and grounds. But critics argue that including the $15 million to ease the current deficit sets a precedent of having the government subsidize operating costs that ought to be met by ticket sales and private donations.

The eclectic nature of the bill makes for an extraordinary array of alliances, and when lawmakers went behind closed doors to resolve their differences, the agenda ran from timber sales to arts to a ban on offshore oil exploration. "Any national security involved?" asked Massachusetts Rep. Silvio Conte, the ranking Republican on the full panel.

"Just personal security," answered Illinois Rep. Yates.

Political security is a factor, too, in construction accounts laced with back-home projects. Park Service construction funding would grow to $225 million, or more than twice the president's request, and an estimated $153.4 million is provided for land acquisition and state grants, an increase of more than 75% over the current fiscal year, which ends Sept. 30.
