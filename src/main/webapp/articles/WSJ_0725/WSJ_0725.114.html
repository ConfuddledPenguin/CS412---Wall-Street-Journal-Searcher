Foreign Exchange: Dollar Is Mixed as Profit-Taking Helps Stabilize U.S. Currency; Pound Declines

  • Author: Charles Tenny Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/25/90

NEW YORK -- Profit-taking helped the dollar stabilize yesterday after the slide it suffered on a 56.44-point drop in the Dow Jones Industrial Average the preceding day. As the industrial average rebounded to post a 17.82 point gain, the U.S. currency finished mixed. Dollar trading was eclipsed by activity in the pound, which dropped unexpectedly against all major currencies, especially the mark.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.6215 marks, down slightly from 1.6220 marks late Monday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 148.78 yen, up from 148.20 yen in New York late Monday. Sterling was trading at $1.8133, down from $1.8255 late Monday.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the dollar was trading at 148.95 yen soon after the opening; it closed at 148.70 yen in Tokyo Tuesday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6258 marks, up from Tokyo's Tuesday close of 1.6260 marks.

The pound's fall, which began shortly after noon in New York, only minutes after the end of the trading day in London, dominated traders' attention on a suddenly busy afternoon.

"The primary activity has been in cross-rate trading," said Richard Witten, vice president and foreign exchange trader at Goldman, Sachs & Co. in New York. He said the market in particular focused on the pound and mark.

Dealers have watched the pound hover mostly between 2.96 marks and 2.99 marks for several weeks, believing that the British government wants to drive the currency to the three-mark level before entering it into the fixed exchange rate mechanism of the European Monetary System.

The pound fell decisively out of that range yesterday, slipping well below 2.93 marks before finding support at 2.9275 marks. Late in the U.S. day, sterling had recovered somewhat to 2.9403 marks.

The suddenness of the pound's decline surprised traders because no special news had reached the markets after sterling's close in London at 2.9541 marks. It had finished in New York Monday at 2.9610 marks.

"Fundamentally, there are no real reasons why the pound should fall," said David Jaques, senior corporate trader at Barclays Bank in New York. But he and others noted that the pound has been attractive only for the high U.K. interest rates that the government has used against inflation. Overall, the country is besieged by high inflation and unemployment and by low productivity, he said.

"A decline like this overall isn't something that should be a surprise" in light of the economy's troubles, said Mr. Jaques. "But the actual timing of this move didn't have any explanation."

Goldman's Mr. Witten noted that the pound's easing against the mark actually began last week, as dealers were drawn toward the German currency on fears of a drop in interest rates in the U.S.

Yesterday, when the pound began to slide in New York, trading volume became "quite, quite heavy," noted Ty Tan, a dealer with Deutsche Genossenschaftsbank in New York.

The dollar ended European dealings weaker against major currencies. One dealer said the Federal Reserve might be tempted to lower interest rates in the wake of Monday's sharp drop in the U.S. stock market.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $367.90 an ounce, down $1.20. Estimated volume was a moderate four million ounces.

In Hong Kong Wednesday, gold opened for trading at $366.45 an ounce.
