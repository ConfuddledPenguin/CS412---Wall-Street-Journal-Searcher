Car Sales Fell For Mid-July After Two Gains

  • Author: Joseph B. White
  • Date: 07/25/90

DETROIT -- The minirally in the U.S. car market fizzled in mid-July, as sales of domestic cars and light trucks plunged 13% from year-before levels. The Big Three U.S. auto makers all suffered double-digit sales declines. Chrysler Corp. had the worst overall performance, as its total sales of cars and trucks dropped 19%. But Ford Motor Co. did little better as its total vehicle sales fell 18.5%, largely because of a 24.6% plunge in sales of its domestic cars.

Meanwhile, most major Japanese auto makers continued to boost sales of their North American-built models. The exception was Nissan Motor Corp., which saw combined sales of its U.S.-made cars and trucks slip 1% in the period.

Overall, sales of domestic cars fell 13.3% to 191,779 vehicles. That amounts to a seasonally adjusted annual selling pace of 6.9 million cars, down from a strong 7.7 million-car pace a year earlier. The latest results put a damper on a market that had seen two consecutive strong periods. Early July sales hit a healthy 7.3 million annual rate, while late June sales surged to an eight-million-car selling rate.

Dealers and company officials speculated that consumers may be balking because of confusion about recent pricing moves by the Big Three. General Motors Corp., Ford and Chrysler all recently announced midyear price increases, and this week began signaling even stiffer price increases for the fall.

"We have people come in and say, `There's a $2,000 rebate, but they raised the price 3%. What's the real price?'" said Longview, Wash., dealer Jim Clary, who has Chevrolet, Jeep-Eagle and Chrysler franchises.

Ford's weak performance in the passenger-car market was largely the result of poor sales of two bread-and-butter models. Sales of the subcompact Ford Escort tumbled 34% from the year before, while sales of the midsized Ford Taurus plummeted 35%. A Ford spokesman said the company saw a sharp reduction in sales to high-volume fleet customers during the period because its U.S. plants were closed for their traditional two-week summer vacation.

But Ford continued to surge in the off-road vehicle business. Ford's new Explorer and its predecessor, the Bronco II, together outsold Chrysler's rival Jeep brand by 10,977 vehicles to 10,152 vehicles during the first 20 days of July. Jeep sales fell 31% during the July 1-20 period.

At GM, total car sales fell 12.5% in mid-July, while light-truck sales dropped 13.7%. Chrysler, meanwhile, suffered a 19.1% drop in car sales and an 18.9% decline in sales of its Jeeps, minivans and other trucks.

Among Japanese auto makers, Honda Motor Co. boosted sales of its domestically produced Accords and Civics 3.2%. Toyota Motor Corp. increased sales of its domestically made Corollas and Camrys 62.6%, in part because of increased production.

Mazda Motor Corp. posted a 70.6% gain in sales of its Michigan-built models, and Mitsubishi Motors Corp. improved sales of the sporty models it builds at its joint-venture plant with Chrysler in Normal, Ill.
