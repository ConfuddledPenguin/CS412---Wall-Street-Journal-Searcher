U.S. Stocks and Bonds Are Losing Their Allure to Global Investors

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit and Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 07/25/90

In the scramble for global investment funds, the U.S. is coming up short. Foreign investors sold a net $4.2 billion of U.S. stocks and bonds in the first quarter -- the latest for which data are available -- according to the Securities Industry Association. It was first time foreigners pulled money out of the U.S. securities markets since the third quarter of 1983.

Moreover, evidence since then points to a continued lack of appetite on the part of foreigners for U.S. stocks and bonds, analysts said. Traditionally, foreign investors have had a bigger influence on U.S. bond markets than on stocks, but the British and Japanese sometimes affect share prices.

The consequences can be significant for the deficit-ridden U.S., which faces increasing competition for investment funds. West Germany and Japan -- the big surplus nations -- have turned inward, and Eastern Europe, with enormous capital needs to finance economic and industrial restructuring, is a new competitor for global cash. Moreover, analysts say Thailand and Malaysia, and eventually possibly even Mexico and Brazil, are emerging as new lures for international investment capital.

"We have become very dependent upon foreign investors in all of our markets. Since we now have less demand for U.S. securities than in past years, this explains why we have not had better and more sustained bond market rallies following positive economic news," said Brian J. Fabbri, chief economist for Midland Montagu, a U.S. securities unit of Midland Bank PLC.

U.S. bond yields, he added, "are too high considering economic growth has slowed and the Fed has eased. Yet, bond yields are much higher now than they were in December. That's because we have had sparse demand for our securities."

What's more, David G. Strongin, director of international finance at the SIA, said these high U.S. rates could damage U.S. economic growth, raise the cost of funding the federal budget deficit and reduce the competitiveness of U.S. corporations.

Led by Japanese investors, who sold a net $3.9 billion of U.S. Treasury securities, foreigners in aggregate unloaded $900 million of all U.S. bonds (Treasurys, agency issues and corporate bonds) in the first quarter after buying a net $19.9 billion in the last quarter of 1989. Overseas investors grew more negative on U.S. stocks, selling a net $3.3 billion following a selloff of $400 million in the last quarter of 1989. Swiss, British and Japanese investors were the major dumpers of U.S. stocks in the first quarter.

Behind this retreat are rising nominal and inflation-adjusted interest rates abroad, falling U.S. interest rates and lately a weakening dollar. At the same time, there's rising interest in European and smaller Asian economies and stock markets.

On a semiannual compound yield basis, 10-year U.S. government bonds currently yield 8.53%. That compares with 11.46% for equivalent British bonds, 8.33% for West German and 6.96% for Japanese bonds. At shorter maturities, foreign fixed-income securities become even more appealing.

"Looking at these factors, an investor abroad has to ask himself: Why invest in the U.S.? There are better opportunities abroad," said Nicholas P. Sargen, an international economist at Salomon Brothers Inc. "Since the beginning of the year, I haven't spoken to a long-term overseas investor who is interested in substantially increasing his dollar bond holdings."

In many ways, the supply and demand for international capital is the reverse of what it was in the 1980s. In the last decade -- especially in the first half of it -- foreign investors were drawn to high U.S. interest rates, an expanding economy, a soaring dollar and a booming stock market.

During the 1980s, the U.S. pulled in more than $800 billion from foreign investors, including direct investment, on relatively favorable terms, said Mr. Sargen. "Japan and Germany were generating huge surpluses, and the funds that used to go to the less developed countries weren't because they weren't creditworthy." That left the U.S. as the prime recipient of foreign cash.

But it's a different world in the 1990s. Bond yields are mostly higher in Europe and rising in Japan, and their currencies are climbing. Several European stock markets have suffered recent setbacks after earlier runups. Still, many long-term investors are attracted to them because of the planned 1992 abolition of trade and investment barriers in the 12 countries of the European Community, and because of the opening of Eastern Europe.

Europe is experiencing its biggest spending boom in two decades. Added to that are the financing needs for German unification -- and more broadly for the reconstruction of Eastern Europe. "At the margin, Europe is the buzzword," said John Morosani, managing director in charge of U.S. equities at S.G. Warburg & Co. in New York.

"You'd think that with the dollar soft, foreign investors would want to buy U.S. equities. From a value perspective, you'd think it would make our stuff look cheaper," he said. But "the observed reality is that foreign investors tend to be more interested in a U.S. market when the dollar is rallying."

William M. Brachfeld, an executive vice president at the U.S. unit of Daiwa Securities Co. in New York, said that "as rates have come down here and Japanese rates have inched up," Japanese sales of U.S. bonds have continued. He added that lately foreign investors' confidence in the Federal Reserve had weakened because of the central bank's apparent policy switch toward attempting to bolster U.S. growth with an easier monetary stance rather than combating inflation.

In fact, the SIA's data show that the biggest buyers -- $2.9 billion -- of U.S. Treasury securities in the first quarter were members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries. Most of them don't take a currency risk when buying U.S. securities, because their major income earner, oil, is priced in U.S. dollars.

Foreigners won't abandon U.S. securities markets. Analysts say they can't afford to, because the U.S. still represents the world's largest single capital market and is regarded as a "safe haven" in times of international tension.

Still, Mr. Sargen of Salomon Brothers said: "There's no doubt the U.S. is going to be hustling for funds with countries that we once didn't have to face as competitors in world capital markets. And surplus countries like Japan and Germany have more investment opportunities in their own backyards."
