World Markets: Wall Street's Monday Slump Fails to Register With Most Markets in Asia and Pacific Region

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/25/90

Wall Street's tremors left Asian-Pacific markets largely unscathed yesterday. And in London, stocks finished narrowly mixed in modest trading as an anticipated early advance on Wall Street failed to materialize.

Investors in Asian and Pacific markets were mostly unperturbed by Monday's volatile trading in New York, which sent the Dow Jones Industrial Average down more than 100 points soon after the market opened.

Although the industrial average ended with a 56.44-point loss, regional markets for the most part followed their own local influences.

Only Hong Kong and New Zealand had sharp falls yesterday. Hong Kong's decline of nearly 2%, the largest drop in the region, was cast in a relatively positive light. The chief explanation was that a correction was overdue after the market's prolonged rally.

"This is a rather useful buying opportunity," said Peter Everington, investment director at Thornton Management (Asia).

New Zealand shares slid 1.6%. Investors were worried not only by the cloud over Wall Street but by the impending disclosure of the Wellington government's budget. Singapore stocks fell 0.7%.

Illustrating its isolation from foreign events, Taiwan's stock market rebounded strongly after plunging the previous day. Its Weighted Price Index surged 5.6%. Likewise, South Korean shares responded to internal influences, falling slightly amid continuing concern over political instability.

In Tokyo, high long-term and short-term interest rates dried up buying interest, traders said.

Prices finished well above midday lows, however, after fluctuating widely at lower levels throughout the day. Traders said thin volume permitted index-linked trading -- including that by arbitragers -- to sway stocks more widely than normal.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the Nikkei index eased another 22.72 points to close the morning session at 31679.74.

In early trading in Tokyo Wednesday, the Nikkei index rebounded 143.44 points to 31845.90.

Reports Tuesday that U.S. warships in the Persian Gulf were on alert as Iraq moved 30,000 troops to its border with Kuwait pressured Tokyo stock prices, traders said. They explained that stocks fall because of weakened bond prices resulting from a belief that the situation might strengthen the dollar.

By the end of the day, most investors concluded that the tension is unlikely to worsen, said Yoshihiro Ito, general manager of stock trading at Okasan Securities. He said Iraq's move appeared to be aimed at pressuring Kuwait to agree to oil-production cuts at the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries meeting that starts in Geneva tomorrow.

Volume on the first section was estimated at 350 million shares, up from 302 million Monday. Decliners outnumbered advancers 774-205, with 144 issues unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index posted a sharper decline than the Nikkei index, falling 25.33 points, or 1.09%, to 2304.59.

The Second Section Index dropped 30.88 points, or 0.71%, to 4329.23. Volume was estimated at 15.6 million shares, up from 13.5 million Monday.

"All the market attention is focused on interest rates," said Hiroyuki Murai, general manager of stock trading at Nikko Securities.

While a broad range of sectors declined, resource shares such as petroleum and mining companies firmed, as they often do when the market loses its focus of trade, traders said.

In addition, petroleum shares attracted investors on expectation that OPEC nations will agree on an oil production cut, traders said.

Nippon Oil gained 40 yen to 1,480 yen (27 cents to $9.97). Mitsui Mining & Smelting was the day's most active issue, finishing at 858, up 30.

Sony and Kyocera, which have been bought heavily this month, received blocks of sell orders from institutions, traders said. Sony was down 80 at 8,720, while Kyocera fell 140 to 8,860.

In London, stocks shot higher from the outset, bouncing back from day-earlier losses that dealers said were overdone. But the gains were scaled back on an uninspired Wall Street start, the dealers added.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index ended up 1.2 points at 2360.9 after hitting a midmorning peak of 2378.1 and a midafternoon minimum of 2351.8.

The Financial Times 30-Share Index eased 0.4 point to 1866.7. Volume was a mild 385.7 million shares, little changed from 369.8 million Monday.

Reuters Holdings plummeted #1.84 to #10.45 ($3.36 to $19.09) on volume of 5.7 million shares.

Although the news and financial information group reported interim results just below the best of market expectations, the stock was clobbered by the company's disclosure that "conditions in financial markets remain difficult and cancellations of Reuter services, which were already high, have recently risen further."

"In the market's current frame of mind, it's in no mood for more setbacks, particularly in regard to service industries," one market professional said. The stock has been a good performer lately and was prone to a setback on any earnings disappointment, he said.

Oil issues rose across the board. British Petroleum gained 7 pence to 340 pence, Enterprise added 14 to 676, Lasmo advanced 14 to 448, Shell Transport & Trading rose 7 to 489, and Ultramar gained 4 to 343.

Water stocks continued to perform well. Against a background of uncertainty over profits, corporate downgrades, currency fluctuations and erratic movements on Wall Street, the sector was seen as one of the most secure and stable in the U.K. market, traders said.

The market's package of 10 water stocks rose 65 to #22.58, Thames Water gained 6 to 228, Anglian Water advanced 7 to 228, Severn Trent added 6 to 210, and Southern Water rose 8 to 207.

Elsewhere, shares closed higher in Paris and Madrid, lower in Frankfurt, Milan, Amsterdam, Stockholm and Zurich, and mixed in Brussels. South African gold stocks closed firmer.
