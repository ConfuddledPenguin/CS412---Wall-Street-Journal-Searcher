Workplace: Postal Service Expects Delays If UPS Strikes

  • Author: Jeanne Saddler
  • Date: 07/25/90

WASHINGTON -- If the Teamsters union strikes against United Parcel Service of America Inc. at the end of the month, the U.S. Postal Service has a message for large shippers: Take a number. Under the Postal Service's plan, anyone with five or more packages to be delivered by truck or priority mail will have to call a toll-free number, make a reservation and then take the parcels to a designated postal facility for delivery. The agency doesn't plan any restrictions on its overnight express-mail service or on shipments of fewer than five parcels.

The Teamsters' contract with UPS expires July 31, and the union has advised its members against accepting the company's final wage and benefit offer. A vote on the contract offer is to be tabulated by July 31, and UPS has told its customers that it can't guarantee its service will continue uninterrupted after that.

"There's no way the United States Postal Service can absorb the operations of UPS overnight," deputy Postmaster General Michael Coughlin said at a briefing yesterday. He said the agency's first responsibility is the 500 million pieces of mail it now handles daily. "But we are a national postal service and we'll try to serve," Mr. Coughlin added.

Postal officials said UPS delivers about 10 million parcels by ground transportation daily, or 20 times the Postal Service's volume for that category. UPS also delivers 300,000 packages overnight, compared with 200,000 delivered by the Postal Service. The agency handles 1.5 million parcels mailed at the first-class rate daily (called priority mail), compared with half a million in that category handled by UPS. Officials said priority-mail package delivery could be slowed by about a day if there's a surge in demand because of a strike against UPS.

Under its labor contract, the Postal Service can hire temporary workers numbering up to 5% of its total work force of about 700,000. Officials said that number of temporaries probably wouldn't be enough if there's a nationwide UPS strike. The agency might have to declare an emergency, which would permit them to hire additional help.

The Postal Service doesn't view the possibility of a strike against a major competitor as a chance to impress the public or pick up new business. "I don't see this as an opportunity. We simply can't handle 10 million extra pieces a day," said Mr. Coughlin, who called picking up new business "a secondary consideration."

The parcel-reservation system, which would give callers a time and place for bringing in their packages, is set to begin operation by early next week. However, the timetable may be moved up a day or more if a strike seems certain. Postal officials will brief major shippers today and will meet with representatives of the trucking industry to enlist their help in carrying the extra volume of parcels.

"We're a national utility. If this comes about, we want the public to understand what we're capable of," Mr. Coughlin said.
