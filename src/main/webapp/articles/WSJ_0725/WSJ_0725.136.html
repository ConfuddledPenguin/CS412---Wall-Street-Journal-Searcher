Deutsche Bank Unit Plans to Purchase Equity Stake in Gleacher's Mergers Firm

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit and Randall Smith
  • Date: 07/25/90

NEW YORK -- Morgan Grenfell Group PLC, a unit of Deutsche Bank AG of West Germany, plans to buy an equity stake in the mergers advisory boutique of Eric Gleacher, former head of mergers and acquisitions at Morgan Stanley & Co. The new firm will be called Gleacher Morgan Grenfell Inc., and both Mr. Gleacher and the Deutsche Bank unit will share expenses evenly, according to people familiar with the transaction. Mr. Gleacher last night confirmed the equity stake, but wouldn't give financial or other details.

With the investment, C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc., the U.S. investment banking arm of London-based Morgan Grenfell, will close its corporate finance and merger departments here. Late yesterday, 36 people in those departments were told they would be out of jobs in two weeks.

People familiar with the transaction said that the investment in Gleacher & Co. was negotiated by Morgan Grenfell's chief executive officer, Michael Dobson, and chairman, John Craven, without the advance knowledge of top executives at C.J. Lawrence here.

The investment in Mr. Gleacher's firm is the latest in a series of alliances between overseas securities firms and U.S. merger advisory boutiques in the past few years.

Nomura Securities Co. of Japan has purchased a 20% stake in Wasserstein Perella & Co. for $100 million, Nikko Securities Co. of Japan paid the same amount for a 20% interest in Blackstone Group, and Yamaichi Securities Co. of Japan paid $101 million for a minority stake in Lodestar Partners.

A veteran of Lehman Brothers, Mr. Gleacher was head of mergers and acquisitions throughout the late 1980s at Morgan Stanley & Co. He led the firm to a bonanza in high-profile takeover advisory business during the heyday of corporate raiders' hostile bids for blue-chip U.S. firms. But he left the firm early this year to form the small boutique.

Mr. Gleacher has since disavowed any interest in expanding to a size that would rival the Wasserstein or Blackstone firms. So far this year, his only major deal as an independent has been to advise ConAgra Inc. on the $1.3 billion acquisition of Beatrice Co.
