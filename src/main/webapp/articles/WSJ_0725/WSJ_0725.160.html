THE NEW YORK STOCK EXCHANGE will halt trading for one hour if the Dow Jones Industrial Average falls 250 points. The Big Board delays program traders' use of its automated order system by five minutes only if stock-index futures fall enough to trigger trading limits at the Chicago Mercantile Exchange. Yesterday's edition misstated the Big Board restrictions. (See: "Trading Halt Braked Steep Stock Slide" -- WSJ July 24, 1990)

---

BOEING Co.'s stock was off $4.75 a share at its lowest point in Monday's composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. A table in yesterday's edition misstated the amount of decline because of a database error.

---

ALLIED-SIGNAL Inc. had completed all its stock buy-back programs as of the end of 1989. In January 1990 it authorized a new 25 million-share buy-back, under which six million shares have been purchased. A table in Monday's edition misstated the status of these purchases.

(See: "Abreast of the Market: Stock Market Loses Vital Corporate Crutch --- Dip in Buy-Backs, Takeovers Cited" -- WSJ July 23, 1990)

---

BB&T FINANCIAL Corp. is a commercial bank based in Wilson, N.C. Yesterday's OTC Focus column incorrectly identified the company.

(See: "OTC Focus: Market Plunges as Sell-Off Continues; Intel Falls 5/8 to 44 1/2 on 3.1 Million Shares" -- WSJ July 24, 1990)

---

ANGELES Corp. said its agreement to acquire a three-year option to buy 22% of its common and other securities from certain former owners of Quinoco Cos. involves an exercise price for the option of $30 million. Monday's edition incorrectly said Angeles agreed to pay $30 million for the option.

(See: "Business Brief -- Angeles Corp.: Pact Set to Acquire Option to Buy Back a 22% Stake" -- WSJ July 23, 1990)
