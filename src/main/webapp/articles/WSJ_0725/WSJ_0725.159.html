Camera Maker Loses Bid for New Barriers On Imported Models

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/25/90

WASHINGTON -- Keystone Camera Corp. lost its bid for higher tariffs and import quotas on fixed-focus cameras using 35-millimeter and 110-type film. The U.S. International Trade Commission ruled 4-0 that such imports, which totaled about $689 million last year, aren't injuring a domestic industry.

Keystone's petition had been opposed by Eastman Kodak Co., Rochester, N.Y., and other camera manufacturers and importers.

ITC Chairman Anne Brunsdale said that Keystone Camera, a subsidiary of Keystone Camera Products Corp., Clifton, N.J., had failed to show that imports supplied by Eastman Kodak and foreign-owned companies have been a "substantial cause" of injury to Keystone.

David Rohr, another ITC member, said the economic difficulties cited by Keystone in its petition resulted more from a "failure" of that company's marketing strategy than from import competition.

Keystone had told the ITC that it had tried to meet stiff import competition in the marketing of its fixed-focus cameras, but would be forced to shift production to foreign plants if the government turned down its bid for import restrictions.

The imports come primarily from Japan and other Asian countries but have been coming in growing volume from Brazil and cross-border assembly plants in Mexico.

Although Eastman Kodak was identified during the ITC inquiry as an importer, Chairman Brunsdale noted that the company is a major domestic camera manufacturer even though it assembles fixed-focus cameras in foreign plants for eventual sale in the U.S.

The ITC will send a report on its investigation to the White House later, but yesterday's unanimous decision against import restrictions means President Bush won't be required to take any follow-up action under U.S. trade laws.
