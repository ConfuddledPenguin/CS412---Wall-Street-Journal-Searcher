Technology & Health: Van Gogh Never Suffered Madness, Doctors Maintain --- Instead, Artist Was Victim Of an Inner-Ear Disorder, New Diagnosis Suggests

  • Author: Ron Winslow
  • Date: 07/25/90

The artist Vincent Van Gogh, a suicide 100 years ago, was never mad but suffered instead from an inner-ear disorder, some doctors now believe. A retrospective diagnosis of Meniere's disease, characterized by severe, repeated attacks of vertigo and ringing of the ears, is offered by researchers at Colorado Neurologic Institute and Swedish Medical Center in today's Journal of the American Medical Association.

For a century, Van Gogh has been portrayed as a mad artist, who sliced off his own ear in a psychotic episode and who had epilepsy. But there's no evidence for that assessment, says I. Kaufman Arenberg, the lead author of a report on the artist. Rather, some 800 letters the artist wrote to his family and friends between 1884 and his death reveal a man "constantly in control of his reason," but in frequent despair as he fought disabling bouts of vertigo, the authors say.

A correct diagnosis probably wouldn't have helped much at the time. Treatments weren't available for either epilepsy or Meniere's disease, Dr. Arenberg says. He didn't speculate about the effect of the disease on Van Gogh's paintings, but thinks it spurred his prolific output. Fear that an attack "would permanently prevent him from painting," Dr. Arenberg says, "was driving him to paint more."

Today, about 80% of Meniere's disease sufferers can manage their symptoms by, among other practices, maintaining a low-salt diet and avoiding caffeine, alcohol, tobacco and stress, Dr. Arenberg says. Surgery is usually required for the other 20%. Dr. Arenberg has designed a shunt to help divert inner ear fluid that contributes to a buildup of pressure in the inner ear. The buildup provokes some symptoms.
