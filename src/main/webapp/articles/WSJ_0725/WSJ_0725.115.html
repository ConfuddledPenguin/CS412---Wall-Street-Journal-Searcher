Nynex Releases Study Involving Purchasing Arm

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 07/25/90

Nynex Corp. released a study it contends shows no correlation between its purchases and attendance by certain suppliers at annual "perverts conventions" organized by a Nynex purchasing executive. The study showed that from 1985 to 1988, companies represented at the weeklong Florida gatherings experienced a compounded annual growth rate in sales to Nynex of 11.3%, compared with 9.9% for other Nynex suppliers, excluding American Telephone & Telegraph Co.

Nynex's New York Telephone unit commissioned the study as part of its $1.4 billion rate-increase request in New York state. The company submitted the study to state regulators yesterday. It hired Miles O. Bidwell of National Economic Research Associates in White Plains, N.Y., to critique evidence presented by Scott Rafferty, a former executive at a small Nynex software unit. Mr. Rafferty, who has intervened in the rate case, found a 67% compounded annual growth rate in sales to Nynex for convention attendees compared with about 3.5% for Nynex suppliers overall.

Mr. Bidwell faulted Mr. Rafferty's analysis for "improper calculation and improper data selection." He criticized Mr. Rafferty's exclusion of companies that did no business with Nynex's purchasing arm, Materiel Enterprises Co., and Mr. Rafferty's decision to start tracking a supplier's sales growth to Nynex beginning with the year the supplier began attending the Florida gatherings. In calculating the growth rate for companies that weren't represented at the conventions, Mr. Bidwell excluded AT&T, a leading supplier whose sales to all of the Bell companies was declining following the 1984 breakup of the Bell system.

Nynex has refused to release any of the underlying statistics or names of companies whose employees attended the conventions. The company has claimed that much of the testimony in the rate case is a trade secret, citing the need for confidentiality in its business deals and relationships.

Mr. Rafferty said Mr. Bidwell "complicates a simple issue and doesn't show his work." By including companies that did no business with Nynex but whose owners or employees were personal friends of convention organizers, Mr. Bidwell "dilutes the statistics," Mr. Rafferty said. The New York Attorney General's office supported Mr. Rafferty's conclusions. "I haven't seen anything so far that makes me change my mind as to the Rafferty analysis," said Charlie Donaldson, a lawyer in the attorney general's office.

The parties, nicknamed the "perverts conventions," were organized by a vice president of the regional phone company's purchasing unit who was fired in 1988. One other Materiel Enterprises employee was fired and seven others were disciplined because of their participation.
