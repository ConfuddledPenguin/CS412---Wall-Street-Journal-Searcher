Greenspan Says Inflation Rate Is Still a Concern --- Fed Chairman's Testimony Suggests Further Easing Of Credit Isn't Imminent

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 07/25/90

WASHINGTON -- Policy makers at the Federal Reserve Board are still dissatisfied with the U.S. inflation rate, Chairman Alan Greenspan told Congress, but he said he is confident that the Fed's policies will bring inflation down. Mr. Greenspan's testimony to a House subcommittee, like remarks he made to the Senate Banking Committee last week, suggested that further easing of credit by the central bank isn't imminent. Earlier this month, the Fed allowed a key interest rate to slip for the first time since December in an effort to "offset" banks' increasing caution in lending.

But yesterday, responding to questions on the overall state of the economy, Mr. Greenspan said, "The inflation rate is higher currently than we would like." He explained: "We don't like to see it where it is because it's very easy to accelerate from here."

The past 12 months have seen a 4.7% increase in the consumer price index, perhaps the most widely used measure of inflation. That is about the same as in each of the past three years, and it is an improvement from the rate earlier this year, when a cold wave drove up food and energy costs and caused a jump in the overall inflation rate.

Last week, however, the Labor Department reported that consumer prices rose 0.5% in June, their biggest increase since March. Mr. Greenspan told the House Banking Committee's domestic monetary policy subcommittee yesterday that the latest price report added "a note of caution" to the inflation outlook. A continuation of inflation at that pace "clearly would be very troubling," he said.

Partly because of that report, renewed worries over inflation have spread through the financial markets recently. A number of investors cited those concerns as one factor behind the stock market's fall Monday.

In testifying before the Senate Banking Committee last week, Mr. Greenspan referred only briefly to the consumer price report, which came out that morning. He said the figures were "higher than we'd like to see" but added that "the core of inflation in consumer prices . . . has moderated in recent months." His statements led some financial analysts to suspect that the Fed was losing some resolve to fight inflation. His comments yesterday appeared to be an attempt to end such speculation.

Responding to questions from subcommittee chairman Stephen Neal (D., N.C.), who has introduced legislation that would require the Fed to pursue policies to virtually eliminate inflation, Mr. Greenspan insisted that the Fed remains committed to bringing about full "price stability."

At the same time, he told the committee that the most recent inflation figures are in many respects "history," and he called the current inflation rate "a lagging phenomenon."

Mr. Greenspan's remarks come at a time when the economy is, by his own words, "very sluggish." Nevertheless he expressed no grave concern that the nation is in danger of falling into a recession.

In fact, he said he is "feeling somewhat more comfortable about the underlying base of the economy," noting that some of the excesses spawned in the 1980s, such as massive leveraging, have abated.

"We've come a long way without tilting into a recession and I think in doing so we have ameliorated some of the imbalances engendered during the mid-1980s," he said.
