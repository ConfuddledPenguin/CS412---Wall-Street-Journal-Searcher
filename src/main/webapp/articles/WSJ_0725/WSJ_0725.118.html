Abreast of the Market: Industrials Rise 17.82 on Strength Of Oil Stocks; Many Issues Plunge

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 07/25/90

NEW YORK -- Energy shares lifted the stock market, but beyond the world of oil there was blood. The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed 17.82 higher at 2922.52. New York Stock Exchange volume was a hefty 181,920,000 shares.

Exxon, Chevron, Texaco and USX were the stilts for the industrial average, providing about 14 points of the overall gain. Driving these and other energy-related stocks was the price of oil; the September futures contract closed six cents higher at $20.56 a barrel yesterday in reaction to signs of renewed conflict in the Mideast.

Energy issues also supported broad averages: Standard & Poor's 500-stock index gained 0.48 to 355.79.

Earnings disappointments and dimmer third-quarter projections again triggered heavy institutional selling in favored, high-priced growth stocks such as Capital Cities/ABC, which tumbled 55 1/2 to 533, a 9% decline.

Capital Cities/ABC reported second-quarter net income of $8.19 a share, about six cents shy of conservative estimates, according to Dennis McAlpine, senior vice president at Oppenheimer & Co. Additionally, the broadcast concern said third-quarter profit may be down from the year-before period, a message heard from many other companies these days.

"The sin of Capital Cities is the sin of anybody who says something about down earnings," said Mr. McAlpine. "And you don't have to say much these days."

Even stocks favored by bargain hunters took their hits: Dow Chemical fell 5 3/4 to 52 3/8 on 3.4 million shares after reporting earnings and tempering analysts' estimates for the second half of the year. Ingersoll-Rand tumbled 3 1/2 to 53 7/8; the company reported second-quarter earnings of $1.03 a share, about six cents below expectations.

"The bottom line is that investors will not tolerate earnings disappointments," said Joseph DeMarco, managing director in charge of equity trading at Marinvest. "The glue of the market has been energy stocks -- they are in the only industry that is reporting good earnings."

Again, traders were astonished by the sell-offs because few buyers seemed to step up after stocks were down as much as 10%. Earnings disappointments lately haven't resulted in mere corrections to fairer valuations, but in assassinations of stocks where institutional buyers walk away and never come back. Paul Hennessey, vice president of trading at Boston Co., said he was contacting portfolio managers yesterday to see whether they wanted to buy some stocks that had fallen sharply.

They didn't. "They fear it will be dead money; the past indicates that the performance isn't there for months" after a stock is taken down, he said.

Despite the carnage, Stefan Abrams, chief investment strategist at Kidder Peabody, said he thinks the growth-stock rally is still in place. What will lead the market higher are a group of consumer non-cyclical shares, such as Procter & Gamble and Coca-Cola Co. Mr. Abrams said investors soon will begin looking at the value of these stocks compared with 1991 earnings estimates and prices will move up.

He said these stocks will have sponsorship from mutual funds that are experiencing a giant inflow of money from investors now. "They will buy what's working," he said. "In the consumer non-cyclical group, the rally is essentially intact."

Exxon rallied 2 1/8 to 50 in Big Board composite trading of more than two million shares. Dean Witter boosted the stock's investment rating to "buy" from "accumulate"; among other things, analyst Eugene L. Nowak cited prospects for a dividend increase in the next six to 12 months and a better performance in U.S. refining and marketing operations.

Texaco jumped 2 3/4 to 62 1/2. Although the company reported that second-quarter earnings dropped 3.6% from a year earlier, the latest results were better than analysts had generally expected. Chevron went up 1 7/8 to 78 3/4; the company reported that earnings climbed 60% in the second quarter.

USX, the parent of Marathon Oil and another of the 30 issues in the industrial average, gained 7/8 to 34 3/8. Mobil rose 2 to 67, Amoco added 1 1/2 to 54 7/8, Royal Dutch Petroleum advanced 2 1/8 to 81 5/8, British Petroleum gained 2 3/8 to 74 5/8, Unocal tacked on 7/8 to 31 and Phillips Petroleum rose 5/8 to 28 3/4.

Baker Hughes rose 7/8 to 29 7/8 on 1.7 million shares, and other oil-services issues turned in even stronger showings.

Schlumberger gained 1 3/8 to 64 7/8, Halliburton jumped 2 3/8 to 53 5/8, McDermott International rallied 2 1/8 to 31 3/4 and Helmerich & Payne went up 1 to 32.

Mesa Limited Partnership rose 1/8 to 4 1/8 in trading that included a block of 2,270,000 units, which represented 2.9% of the partnership's units outstanding. The block accounted for most of the 2.6 million units to change hands.

Student Loan Marketing Association fell 1 3/4 to 48 1/8 as 2.7 million shares changed hands, reflecting ongoing concern about the financial difficulties facing Higher Education Assistance Foundation, a major loan guarantor. Sallie Mae is among the major creditors of the foundation, which broke off merger discussions with another guarantor.

The technology sector, which has stumbled amid several earnings disappointments in recent sessions, extended its downturn. International Business Machines fell 1 1/8 to 114 3/4, Compaq Computer eased 5/8 to 57 3/8, Digital Equipment lost 2 3/8 to 75 1/2 and Motorola dropped 2 to 77 5/8; among smaller issues, Silicon Graphics was unchanged at 32 1/2, Stratus Computer lost 1 to 22 7/8, Network Equipment Technologies went down 3/4 to 8 1/8 and Digital Communications Associates dropped 1 1/8 to 23 1/4.

TCBY Enterprises tumbled 1 3/4 to 13 1/2. The company acknowledged telling analysts that sales for stores open at least one year, which fell 10% for the three months ended in May, remained weak in June, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Based on the same-store sales trend, J.C. Bradford and Robinson-Humphrey both lowered investment ratings on the stock and reduced earnings estimates for fiscal 1990 and 1991.

Wellman advanced 2 1/4 to 24 3/8. Prudential-Bache Securities upgraded its investment rating on the stock to "buy" from "hold," based on its recent decline in price.

Milton Roy added 3 1/2 to 26 7/8 following news that Sundstrand, which holds a 15% stake in the company, submitted a $28-a-share takeover offer.

Bank of New England gained 5/8 to 2 7/8 after rejecting offers for its Maine National Bank unit. The company said it had been unable to reach agreement with potential buyers of the subsidiary on an acceptable price.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.59 to 355.51. Volume totaled 14,430,000 shares.
