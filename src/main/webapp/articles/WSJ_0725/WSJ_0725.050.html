Little Restitution From S&L Fraud Is Expected by U.S.

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 07/25/90

WASHINGTON -- Taxpayers shouldn't hold their breath waiting for restitution from savings-and-loan crooks, nor for fast sales of giant packages of assets from failed thrifts taken over by the government, officials said. Attorney General Dick Thornburgh, testifying before the Senate Judiciary Committee, said it is difficult to get funds from individuals convicted of S&L fraud and ordered to pay restitution. Of $2.5 million in restitution ordered by courts this year, Mr. Thornburgh said $50 had been coughed up so far. Of $3.1 million ordered in 1989, $2,700 has been paid. The department has fared better with the $5.8 million in restitution that courts decreed in 1988: $21,000 has been paid.

Mr. Thornburgh noted ruefully that while he expects the Justice Department to successfully prosecute hundreds of S&L executives, the department is unlikely to win back sums that will make a dent in the cost of the taxpayer-financed bailout, estimated at up to $500 billion with interest.

Mr. Thornburgh also said he expects it will take five years for the department to handle the bulk of the S&L cases and "perhaps begin winding down and transferring some resources to other areas."

Meanwhile, real estate seized from defunct savings-and-loans probably won't be sold in the planned $500 million chunks before next year, the Resolution Trust Corp. said.

Prospective buyers will need a lot of time to examine the big packages of real estate and loans, said Lamar Kelly, the official in charge of asset sales for the S&L cleanup agency. The agency must hire investment advisers as well as firms to prepare and evaluate the bids.

The RTC will provide up to 85% financing for the packages, which will include hard-to-sell loans and real estate. The sales of three bulk packages, part of a pilot project, aren't expected to close until the first quarter of 1991.

The agency also is in the midst of selling $716 million of loans in four packages. The sales don't require government financing and are expected to be completed soon, the agency said.
