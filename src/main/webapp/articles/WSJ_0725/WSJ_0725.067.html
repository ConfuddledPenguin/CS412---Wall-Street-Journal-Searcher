Law -- Legal Beat: Arguments Heard on WPPSS Settlement

  • Author: Amy Dockser Marcus and Ann Hagedorn
  • Date: 07/25/90

A federal judge in Arizona heard arguments on how to allocate money paid in the settlement of litigation stemming from Washington Public Power Supply System's $2.25 billion bond default in 1983. Plaintiffs who bought WPPSS bonds before June 1983 and Chemical Bank, trustee for the estimated 24,000 bondholders, reached approximately $700 million in settlements with the defendants last year. The defendants include WPPSS, several utilities in six states in the Northwest, the Bonneville Power Administration, the state of Washington and various professional firms.

Yesterday, U.S. District Judge William Browning of Tucson heard arguments in New York on how the money should be divided between the various bondholder factions, some of whom oppose the settlements. Plaintiffs' lawyer Melvyn I. Weiss said that by June 1991, when the money is tentatively scheduled to be distributed, the total pot available will be $825 million or more, including interest and money from other pending claims.

Under an allocation plan proposed by Chemical Bank and plaintiffs' lawyers earlier this year, $93.5 million will be put into a fund to be distributed by Chemical Bank to all bondholders. The remainder will be used for attorney's fees and reimbursement of litigation expenses and then distributed to plaintiffs who bought bonds before June 1983.

At the hearing, the main opponents of the allocation plan were bondholders who bought the WPPSS bonds after June 1983, when the Washington State Supreme Court ruled that many of the municipal utilities that were backing the WPPSS bonds didn't have the legal authority to execute their contracts with WPPSS and thus didn't have to pay the debt. They argued that the money allocated to the fund for all bondholders is too small.

Mr. Weiss, of the New York law firm of Milberg, Weiss, Bershad, Specthrie & Lerach, said that those who purchased bonds after the court's June ruling were speculators who knew the risks involved in that decision. "They took a risk and made an investment that didn't pan out. But it's not a total loss for them," he said.

But Robert M. Sedgwick, a lawyer for bondholders who bought bonds after June 1983, said his clients were unhappy with the representation they received from Chemical Bank and feel that all bondholders should be treated the same under the allocation plan.

"They ran risks, but the risk they didn't expect is that the bank would settle this without their consent and agree to a small settlement for their claims," said Mr. Sedgwick of the New York firm of Holtzmann, Wise & Shepard.

But Harold Tyler of the New York law firm of Patterson, Belknap, Webb & Tyler, a lawyer for Chemical Bank, countered: "I don't consider $93 million a bad deal. The fact is we got them money. Better a penny or two than nothing."

Buyers of the WPPSS bonds also have filed a $7.5 billion state-court lawsuit against the state of Washington contending that it's liable in the default because its auditor was negligent in approving the bond sale. Washington contributed $10 million to the settlements in order to resolve the state-court litigation. The plaintiffs in the state-court case and Mr. Sedgwick's clients have filed separate appeals of Judge Browning's approval of the settlements.

The judge said he will decide shortly whether to approve the allocation plan. He hasn't yet ruled on awarding legal fees.

Plaintiffs' lawyers have requested fees of $110 million and reimbursement to Chemical Bank of about $52 million spent on the litigation.

THE RICO CASE against the Teamsters has cost the union $12 million.

The government's suit and its court-ordered supervision of the International Brotherhood of Teamsters has cost the union as much as $8.9 million in legal fees alone, according to the July issue of International Teamster, a union publication.

Among the expenses are about $6 million billed by the New York law firm of Mudge, Rose, Guthrie, Alexander & Ferdon, which represents the union; $2.9 million for "other legal fees and expenses"; $1.7 million for the salaries and supplies of three court-appointed officers and their staffs.

The oversight began in March 1989 as part of the union's settlement of civil racketeering charges brought by the Justice Department to purge the union of alleged mob domination.

Under the terms of the settlement, the Teamsters agreed to broad reforms, including the first democratic election of top officials and the court appointment of an administrator, an investigator and an elections official to oversee the union. The union also agreed to pay the salaries and expenses of the three court-appointed officers and their staffs.

Teamsters in-house attorney James T. Grady said $2.9 million had been spent on legal expenses for defendants in the suit. He added that U.S. District Judge David Edelstein had ordered the union to publish in the International Teamster all reports of the court-appointed officer and all of the judge's opinions in the case. The extra pages for the magazine cost about $1.5 million.

In the recent article -- which bears the headline "Civil Rico Costs Top $12 Million]" -- the leadership appears to be critical of the costs of the court-appointed officers. But, says Edward T. Ferguson, the assistant U.S. Attorney in Manhattan in charge of the case, "The expenses of the court-appointed officers comprise only a very small part of the total expenses the IBT claims."

Lawyers and union members skeptical of the union's leaders say their criticism of the court-appointed officers' costs is largely meant to distract the membership from the high legal fees.

"They've spent $9 million on attorneys' fees in legal efforts to either stop the election or cripple the powers of the election officer," said Ken Paff, a national organizer for the Teamsters for a Democratic Union, a reform group within the union.

Mudge Rose attorney Jed Rakoff said the Teamsters union "has felt that the government and the court officers had departed from the spirit and language of the settlement."

TIME WARNER INC. WINS BATTLE in its crusade to stop Batman knockoffs.

A federal judge in New York ordered Bobtron International Inc., a Los Angeles-based importer of watches, to pay Time Warner $40,000 in damages for infringing the company's rights to the Batman character. Time Warner's DC Comics Inc. unit has owned the copyright to Batman for more than half a century.

Judge Michael B. Mukasey also permanently barred Bobtron from selling watches that bear the Batman logo, character or costume. According to the ruling, Bobtron sold 1,400 Batman watches.

Saul A. Mifhaan, attorney for Bobtron, said the company didn't know whether it would appeal.

Time Warner's 1989 hit movie "Batman" proved to be a boon for knockoff artists who have sold T-shirts, sneakers, watches and other items that carry the caped crusader's bat logo, his costume and other Batman characters.

Time Warner's attorney, Joseph Bainton of the New York law firm of Shea & Gould, estimates that his client has lost at least $10 million from sales of unauthorized Batman merchandise. He says he has reached settlements in about 75 of the 250 copyright infringement cases his firm has brought on Time Warner's behalf.

The Bobtron case is the first that Time Warner has taken to trial. Mr. Bainton says he expects that within nine months all of the remaining cases will be resolved.

OBSCENITY INDICTMENTS THWARTED by federal court in Washington, D.C.

U.S. Judge Joyce Hens Green yesterday barred the Department of Justice's National Obscenity Enforcement Unit from seeking multiple indictments against a major North Carolina distributor of sexually oriented magazines and videotapes.

The distributor, Philip D. Harvey, president and principal owner of a concern called Adam and Eve, markets magazines and videotapes that show adults engaging in sexual acts. None of these involve rape or sexual violence, according to Judge Green's memorandum opinion.

Mr. Harvey sued the Justice Department in March. According to his lawyer, Bruce Ennis, the Justice Department had been threatening for four years to prosecute Mr. Harvey in several jurisdictions for distributing obscene material. According to Mr. Ennis, the agency informed his client in December that indictments were imminent in Utah and Kentucky.

"My client is perfectly happy to stand trial to one case," Mr. Ennis said. "What he can't stand is a nationwide war of attrition." Mr. Harvey couldn't be reached for comment on the ruling.

Justice Department spokesman Doug Tillett said multiple indictments are "an effective means for law enforcement" that the department intends to defend before Judge Green when the matter receives a full hearing in September.
