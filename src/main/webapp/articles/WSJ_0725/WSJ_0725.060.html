No, It's Not a Mistake, and Yes, You Do Have to Pay for Tickets

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 07/25/90

DETROIT -- Weary travelers may need coffee, an aspirin or just some quiet time to themselves when they board an airplane. But do they really want a bank economist's lecture or a stand-up comic's routine? Like it or not, this live entertainment is just what they'll get on Continental Express flights between Detroit and Cleveland for one week beginning Aug. 6. During the "Express Yourself" program, business writers and economists will lecture in the morning, while comedians will perform on the afternoon flights.

Both will have to be brief: The flying time between Detroit City Airport and Cleveland Hopkins Airport is 17 minutes on the 30-passenger commuter planes.

One featured speaker will be David L. Littmann, senior economist and a first vice president of Manufacturers National Bank of Detroit, a unit of Manufacturers National Corp. "Economic Revelation While at Peak Elevation," is the title of his 10-minute talk. It "will be a tale of two cities, if you will," says Mr. Littmann, a discussion of the local economies of Cleveland and Detroit. Mr. Littmann says he has made many presentations before, but never while airborne. Deadpan, he says: "My career must be taking off."

The business experts won't be paid for their services, says Continental Express, but the comedians will receive a yet-to-be-determined fee. Comedy auditions will be held in Detroit on Monday and in Cleveland on Tuesday.

Business-travel experts aren't sure just what to make of all this. "I've heard of news on a screen," says Linda Marion, travel manager at GAF Corp. "But live entertainment on the plane? I've never heard of that before."

The Detroit-Cleveland flights cost $220 round-trip, while restricted-fare tickets can be purchased for as low as $124. Why not simply cut the price instead of resorting to gimmickry? Continental Express, the commuter unit of Continental Airlines Holdings Inc., says it wants to "build awareness" of its service. If the idea catches on, a spokeswoman says, it might be tried on the carrier's other routes.

Refunds won't be given if the lecture is boring or the comedian is a dud. Mr. Littmann, however, says he plans to offer a sort of flight insurance to passengers on his trip. "I will provide cotton wads," he says, for passengers to use as earplugs should they so choose.
