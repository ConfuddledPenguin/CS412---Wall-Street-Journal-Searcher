Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Some Firms Quit 15-Second TV Spots

  • Author: Joanne Lipman
  • Date: 06/20/90

Ever since the big TV networks started allowing 15-second commercials instead of standard 30-second ads, the number of short spots has been spiraling out of control. Quietly, though, some advertisers are starting to abandon 15-second spots, frustrated by messages that are over almost before they begin. A number of advertisers say they've noticed a flattening out, and even a decline, in 15-second spots over the past year.

That trend has caught the eye of Robert Coen, director of forecasting for Interpublic Group's McCann-Erickson. In his annual ad spending forecast last week, Mr. Coen said that during prime-time, 15-second spots peaked last year at 35% of all TV ads and that their number has since slipped to 31%. He said that during daytime, 15-second spots have leveled off in the past year at 41% of all commercials. He expects those levels to remain about the same.

"It's too difficult to really get satisfying information into 15 seconds," explains Roy Grace of Grace & Rothschild, a New York ad agency. "It's an enormous discipline and it's very hard to do really terrific `15s.' It truly is on the way out. Clients don't even talk about them anymore."

Yet the decline masks a split among 15-second commercial users, only some of whom are abandoning the short ads. When the networks first began allowing the short spots in 1984, all sort of advertisers jumped on the bandwagon, especially small advertisers who couldn't otherwise afford network TV prices. Those advertisers appear to be the ones who are abandoning the 15s first, amidst questions about cost and effectiveness.

The nation's biggest advertisers, on the other hand, especially packaged goods companies pushing major brand names, continue to be advocates of the 15-second ads. For them, the 15s, which generally cost half of a 30-second spot, are a relatively cheap way to hammer home their household names. And cost counts at a time when the average prime-time 30-second spot goes for an eye-opening $120,000.

Indeed, at RJR Nabisco's Nabisco Brands brands unit, a spokeswoman says the use of 15-second spots has been on the rise for the past three years, especially for its best-known products, like Oreos and Ritz crackers. It even uses the 15s for brand extensions, like fudge-covered Oreos and peanut-butter Ritz Bits.

"Nabisco's products are very well-established, so a 15-second commercial serves to reinforce the product's message," the spokeswoman says. "People know Oreos, people know Ritz. The basic product is so ingrained in them that with a new variety, we can still get the message across in 15 seconds."

At Omnicom's DDB Needham ad agency, Page Thompson, executive vice president and media director, also doesn't see a decline. One of the agency's biggest clients, Michelin, even started using 15-second spots for the first time this year. The Michelin ad, a shorter version of its long-running baby campaign, "has a lot of equity in it, and it's a very simple message, so we were able to convey that in 15s," he says. "We were able to get a lot more mileage for the campaign."

For the most part, though, major advertisers are leveling off on using 15-second spots, while smaller advertisers are often abandoning them altogether. The networks are partly to blame; in the past year, they've put more restrictions on using the short ads. While policies vary from network to network, some charge a premium for sports or other programs, while others ban or restrict the use of "bookends" -- two 15-second spots for the same product that air with unrelated ads sandwiched in between them.

Advertisers, too, have become disenchanted with a commercial form so short that there's almost no time to grab viewers' attention. The 15-second ads "started out as a way for advertisers who couldn't really afford national media to take advantage of it," says Stacey Lippman, senior vice president and corporate media director for Chiat/Day/Mojo. But competition has heated up so much for those advertisers that "they're willing to forgo the quick hit to stand out from their competitors" with ads that have "a creative, added impact."

At Chiat/Day, known as among the most creative agencies, "I've seen some frustration on the part of creatives in terms of their ability to get a message out," Mr. Lippman adds.

Betsy Frank, a senior vice president at Saatchi & Saatchi Advertising, says she has also seen some decline in 15-second ads. But she notes that they still account for a good third of all commercials, a potentially worrisome amount, considering how cluttered the ad environment is already. Saatchi is about to launch a study of the short spots and, Ms. Frank notes, "we wouldn't be doing this if we felt it was no longer an issue."
