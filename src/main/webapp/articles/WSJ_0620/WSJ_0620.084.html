International: Fourth Japanese Firm Says It Bought Software From U.S. Defense Contractor

  • Author: Jeremy Mark
  • Date: 06/20/90

TOKYO -- A fourth Japanese concern acknowledged purchasing advanced U.S. aerospace software that allegedly was exported without proper licensing. Mitsubishi Research Institute Inc. said it purchased the software package from Plume Technology Inc., a small Los Angeles defense contractor. Last week, a Plume executive, Ronald Hoffman, was arrested for illegally exporting the software, designed for the U.S. Strategic Defense Initiative program.

An institute spokesman declined to say for what purpose the software was acquired. "We bought it because it was interesting," the spokesman said. "That's all we can say." The spokesman didn't know whether the institute dealt directly with Mr. Hoffman.

The institute, an independent entity, is affiliated with the Mitsubishi Group, one of Japan's most powerful industry families. Another member of the group, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Ltd., and two other companies, Nissan Motor Co. and Ishikawajima-Harima Heavy Industries Co., on Monday confirmed purchasing the software. The Japanese purchases were made between 1986 and 1989.

The companies confirm that they purchased the software, known as the Contam system, but insist that they had no knowledge of its use in the so-called Star Wars program. Moreover, they assert that they obtained all necessary import licenses from the Japanese government. The purchasers say they were taken by surprise by the allegations against Mr. Hoffman.

In addition, Mitsubishi Corp., the giant trading house that served as middleman for Mitsubishi Heavy in the purchase, and Kyokuto Boeki Kaisha Ltd., a small trading firm that played the same role for Nissan, also said that they followed normal importing procedures and didn't know the software was restricted. The trading houses and IHI, which purchased the software without a middleman, said they were offered the technology by Mr. Hoffman, who was representing Plume Technology.

"As you know, export permission is the seller's responsibility," said a spokesman for Mitsubishi Corp. "We have no means to check whether they obtained the export permission or not. This is a fact of international trade. We are responsible for the procedures on the Japan side."

Kyokuto Boeki and IHI also asserted it was Plume's responsibility to obtain U.S. export permits, and it wouldn't be possible in Japan to verify whether they were obtained. A spokesman for Kyokuto Boeki added that Plume had requested the necessary documentation for export licenses.

Mitsubishi Heavy and IHI say they are employing the Contam package in the development of the Japan Experimental Module, slated to be part of a space station the U.S. will launch later this decade. Nissan said it is using the software to create simulation models for rocket-engine development.

In an affidavit filed in federal court in Los Angeles, the U.S. government said the Contam software was designed to analyze the exhaust trail of missiles in flight, an important part of SDI research.

In Tokyo, the U.S. Embassy said that "the investigation is continuing in an effort to determine culpability on the part of other persons involved in the scheme."
