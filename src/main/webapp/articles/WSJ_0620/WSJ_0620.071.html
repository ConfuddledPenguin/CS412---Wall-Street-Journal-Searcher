Technology & Health: Motorola Is Expected to Unveil Plans For Satellite System for Cellular Phones

  • Author: Robert L. Rose
  • Date: 06/20/90

Motorola Inc. is expected to unveil plans next week for a new satellite communications system that some industry experts believe could handle cellular phone calls within a few years. Such a move by the Schaumburg, Ill., electronics and semiconductor company would greatly expand cellular telephone coverage, which currently excludes rural areas without ground-based switching systems.

"We're talking about a major change in the technology," said John Pemberton, a mobile communications analyst at Gartner Group, a Stamford, Conn., research firm.

Mr. Pemberton said he believes Motorola plans a "hybrid" system that would use both the ground-based system and what he calls the "sky system." As the user left an area covered by the ground system, the satellite system would take over.

A Motorola spokesman said the company plans to announce Tuesday a "global communications system." He wouldn't elaborate, except to say that the announcement will be made at New York's Hayden Planetarium.

Space News, a trade publication in Springfield, Va., reported in its June 4-10 edition that industry officials told it that Motorola "intends to launch a constellation of dozens of small satellites that would expand vastly its ability to provide continuous mobile telephone service" in the U.S. and abroad.

Mr. Pemberton of Gartner Group said he expects Motorola to eventually launch as many as 60 to 70 small satellites over a number of years beginning as early as 1992, at a potential cost of $1 billion. He said 15 to 22 satellites would be enough to get full coverage of the U.S., and additional ones could be used to expand capacity.

The Motorola spokesman said some of the information in the Space News story was incorrect. He also said numbers describing the size of the communications system have been wrong, but wouldn't elaborate further.

Besides catapulting Motorola into a business dominated by phone companies, a move into satellite transmission of cellular calls could lead to new equipment sales for Motorola. Blanketing the U.S. or other nations with coverage would open markets not currently served by cellular systems.

Motorola already is the world's biggest supplier of cellular phone systems and mobile and portable phones. It cites analysts' predictions that the world cellular market will jump to 100 million subscribers by the year 2000 from about 7 million last year.

Thomas Kurlak, who follows the company for Merrill Lynch & Co., says Motorola now derives 55% of its operating income from its cellular businesses. Mr. Kurlak said a move by Motorola into satellite communications could make it "a new, massive provider" of telephone service over the next 10 to 20 years.

Analysts said Motorola could seek partners to help finance such an effort. Because they could otherwise lose business to a satellite system, analysts said the "Baby Bell" regional phone companies would be ideal candidates for such a partnership.

Dow Jones Professional Investors Report said yesterday that speculation of a possible launch services contract from Motorola was behind recent surge in the stock of Orbital Sciences Corp., It said Orbital wouldn't comment.
