CHERNOBYL'S CONTINUING AGONY

The Soviet republic of Byelorussia appealed for international help to relocate and medically treat more than two million people affected by the 1986 Chernobyl nuclear accident.

Byelorussia got 70% of the fallout from Chernobyl. One-fifth of Byelorussia's more than 10 million people have to be moved from areas contaminated by radiation, said Victor Borovikov, a diplomat from Byelorussia.

Speaking in Brussels, Mr. Borovikov said people were paid by the Soviet authorities to stay in the contaminated zones immediately after the accident, but they now are demanding immediate relocation. "We have no experience in this field, which is why we're asking for international help," he said.

A major relocation problem: Mr. Borovikov said people in uncontaminated areas don't "want to accept the newcomers. They tell their children not to play with the children of the newcomers."

PHILIPPINE AIRLINES SCANDAL

The Philippines' solicitor general has asked Philippine Airlines to investigate "highly scandalous anomalies" that he said may have cost the state-run airline more than two billion pesos ($89.3 million) since 1981. Solicitor General Francisco Chavez, a member of the airline board, said the alleged irregularities included missing airline parts and overpayments to travel agents.

NORWEGIAN BROADCAST STRIKE

Journalists at Norsk Rikskringkasting, Norway's national radio and television authority, began a strike over a wage dispute. During the strike, NRK radio plans to broadcast mostly music and have only four news bulletins daily, instead of hourly newscasts. TV newscasts will be replaced by movies and reruns. Television officials said they would continue to broadcast World Cup soccer games but without Norwegian commentary.

U.S. RETURNS OKINAWA LAND

The U.S. has agreed to return to Japan some land used by U.S. military bases on the Japanese island of Okinawa. A report by the U.S.-Japan Joint Committee on Realignment and Consolidation of U.S. Forces Facilities on Okinawa said land totaling about 2,500 acres in 23 sites will be returned. The schedule for the return is subject to local negotiation and further discussions by the two governments, the Japanese Foreign Ministry announced. The land being returned makes up only 2.5% of the acreage taken up by U.S. military bases on Okinawa.

COMMUNISM IN SOUTH AFRICA

The South African Communist Party said it would relaunch itself officially next month after four decades underground. General Secretary Joe Slovo told the party's first news conference in South Africa since 1950 that its leadership would be presented publicly at a July 19 rally in Johannesburg's Soweto black township. Speaking about the retreat of communism in Eastern Europe, Mr. Slovo said, "A communist party worthy of the name cannot constitutionally entrench its leading role -- it must earn it daily."

CHINA PREPARES FOR A CENSUS

In preparing to count its billion-plus population next month, China has drafted nearly seven million census workers. Hundreds of thousands of centers have been set up to teach the workers how to conduct the world's biggest head count, which begins July 1 and is scheduled to take about 10 days. In addition, millions more Chinese have been mobilized to hang banners in the streets and otherwise popularize the slogan, "The population census -- everyone is responsible."

EAST GERMAN UNEMPLOYMENT

Nearly two million jobs could be lost in East Germany after the currency and economic union between the two Germanys takes effect July 1, Economics Minister Helmut Haussmann said.

But he said job losses would be a short-term phenomenon, as many workers would be quickly retrained for other work. Job losses for East Germans in inefficient state-run industries that were modernized or shut down would be offset by the expected creation of some 500,000 new jobs this year in new small and medium-sized private companies.

"This is a transitional unemployment which will be associated with active training measures," Mr. Haussmann said. During the transition, West Germany would provide start-up aid for a new unemployment-insurance system that would become self-financing when East German workers start paying regular contributions.

When East Germany was under communist rule, unemployment didn't officially exist. But Mr. Haussmann estimated hidden unemployment there at 15% of the work force, saying this was the number of people with artificial or do-nothing jobs in the public sector.

Interviewed in Paris, novelist Mario Vargas Llosa said he is back to writing after his defeat in Peru's presidential election last week. "My compatriots have returned me to literature," he said. . . . Iran has asked Turkey to halt screenings of the U.S. movie "The Naked Gun" because it mocks the late Ayatollah Ruhollah Khomeini.
