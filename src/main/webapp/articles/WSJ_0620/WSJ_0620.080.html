World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Decline 336.42 to 32040.38 As Expectations for Lower Interest Rates Fade

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/20/90

Tokyo stock prices retrenched in light trading as buying interest dried up amid fading expectations for lower interest rates as well as a lack of any other incentives. Meanwhile, London stocks ended a sluggish session narrowly mixed with few fresh developments to inspire investors.

Tokyo's Nikkei index of 225 issues fell 336.42 points to 32040.38 on Tuesday.

At the end of the morning session in Tokyo Wednesday, the Nikkei index was up 140.27 points to 32180.65.

Tuesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 330 million shares, in line with 319.7 million shares Monday.

Decliners outnumbered gainers 813 to 182, while 129 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, dropped 27.10 points, or 1.14%, to 2353.93.

Second-section stocks, which had advanced strongly in the past five trading days, also finished lower yesterday. The second section index lost 33.53 points, or 0.80%, to 4145.06. The section's volume was estimated at 24 million shares, down from 35.9 million shares Monday.

The Nikkei Index opened 130 points lower on discouraged sentiments from the 53-point loss on the Dow Jones Industrial Average in New York overnight, prompted partly by higher interest rates on the U.S. bond market.

The Tokyo market saw some buying by bargain hunters when the Nikkei fell below 32000 points midmorning, but failed to attract further buying interest, traders said.

Participants had thought lower U.S. interest rates might help drag down Japanese interest rates. But such expectations are fading because of better-than-expected reports recently on a series of U.S. economic indicators, observers said.

Hiroyuki Murai, general manager of stock trading at Nikko Securities, attributed yesterday's drop in the Nikkei Index to a setback of small- and midsize capital stocks, which had been performing well while large-capital shares were falling in the past week.

Large-capital shares, meanwhile, extended recent losses. Shipbuilding, steel, construction, trading house and real estate shares were under strong sell pressure linked to margin calls, traders said.

As these sectors were heavily bought during the last week of December, "they are having the hardest period," said Tetsuji Hamazaki, general manager of investment information at W.I. Carr, Sons & Co.

"The biggest damage to the market is the complete withdrawal of institutional investors," said Yoshihiro Ito, general manager of stock trading at Okasan Securities.

Institutions were determined to remain on the sidelines until they see the result of German monetary union, the Soviet Communist Party conference and the Group of Seven nations summit meeting, all of which are slated for July, traders said.

Steel shares continued to decline, with Sumitomo Metal losing 21 yen to 570 yen (14 cents to $3.71), and NKK down 14 yen at 656 yen.

Other notable losers included electric power companies such as Kansai Electric Power, which fell 120 yen to 3500 yen, and mining shares such as Nippon Mining, which plummeted 27 yen to 983 yen.

A few shares that managed to finish higher included Dainippon Pharmaceutical, as potential profits of its anti-cancer agent continued to attract investors. Dainippon gained 50 yen to 3290 yen. Fuji Photo Film was bought for its strong earnings outlook, gaining 90 yen to 4290 yen.

In London, the lack of U.K. economic indicators or major corporate news, combined with the Royal Ascot horse race meeting kept investment interest at a minimum, dealers said.

Although the market opened nearly 25 points down, dealers said there was no outright selling and the lower levels inspired some genuine institutional interest. "Institutions are reluctant to sell any stock so close to the end of the quarter," one market maker said. "The small amount of buying and the reluctance to sell has had a disproportionate effect on the market. It's been very quiet," he added.

By the closing bell, 50 stocks of the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index managed to chalk up gains, while 42 shares ended in negative territory. An additional eight issues were unchanged.

At the final tally, the FT-SE 100 Share Index was down 0.8 point at 2369.7, near the day's best level of 2372.9. The index hit its intraday minimum of 2348.9 not long after the official opening.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index fell 4.5 points to 1907.4. Volume was 408.3 million shares, little changed from 403.4 million issues a day ago.

Additionally, the week's U.K. economic data kept many potential players sidelined, market watchers said. Expected today are U.K. money supply figures, and balance of payments data are due Friday. Dealers were also keeping a close eye on Wall Street after Monday's skid.

The shares of many defense-related companies fell after the British government said Monday it is aiming to cut 3%, or about #600 million ($1.03 billion), from the #21.2 billion budgeted for defense spending in the year to March 31.

U.K. Defense Secretary Tom King Monday announced an immediate ban on new equipment orders pending an examination of where and how additional savings can be met. The Tornado fighters, developed by a consortium including British Aerospace PLC, are the first major casualty.

British Aerospace slipped 14 pence lower to 546 pence, while GEC fell 9 pence to 208 pence, Vickers dropped 3 pence to 233 pence, and Rolls-Royce fell 5 pence to 217 pence.

In other shares, Charter Consolidated, which reported a 12% rise in pretax profit for fiscal 1990, dropped 9 pence to 454 pence.

BET added 7 pence to 259 pence after reporting good full-year earnings a day ago. Several dealers said the shares have been upgraded by a slew of brokerage firms that were cheered by the company's statement that it wouldn't be making any acquisitions this year.

Globe Investment Trust fell a penny to 204 pence after the British Coal pension funds announced it would extend its #1.02 billion bid for the company to Monday after failing to gain control of the company as the previous deadline lapsed.

Elsewhere, stocks ended higher in Frankfurt and Paris, lower in Stockholm, Madrid and Brussels and mixed in Amsterdam, Milan and Zurich. South African gold stocks closed slightly lower.

Also, shares closed lower in Taipei, Sydney, Singapore, Manila, Wellington and Seoul, and were higher in Hong Kong.
