PS New Mexico Gets New Officers; Little Change Seen

  • Author: Carolyn Phillips
  • Date: 06/20/90

Public Service Co. of New Mexico named new top officers, but the two have long histories with the troubled utility, and critics contend they will bring very little change. "They are not new blood," said Ellen O'Brien, a legal assistant for the city of Albuquerque, referring to John Ackerman, a former division head who was named president and chief executive officer, and Ashton B. Collins Jr., a longtime director who was named chairman. "They are just changing seats," Ms. O'Brien said.

Mr. Ackerman, an electrical engineer by training, has worked for the utility for 19 years, most recently as president and chief operating officer of its Gas Co. of New Mexico unit. The 48-year-old automotive hobbyist, who couldn't be reached to comment, was also named to the company's board.

Mr. Collins, 58, is president of Reddy Communications, a closely held firm in Albuquerque that provides research services to electric utilities, including PS New Mexico. He has been one of the utility's directors since 1979.

The two succeed Jerry D. Geist, 56-year-old executive who retired as chairman, president and chief executive, effective with yesterday's board meeting. Mr. Geist, closely associated with the ambitious expansion plans that led to many of PS New Mexico's recent woes, said in a statement, "My departure will facilitate and encourage the resolution of current problems." Mr. Geist was in meetings and couldn't be reached.

Chief among those problems is a passel of disgruntled customers, including Albuquerque, PS New Mexico's home town. The city and other rate payers have berated the utility for 15 years about rates they say skyrocketed when the utility embarked on efforts to diversify beyond regulated businesses and to increase its generating capacity in order to sell energy throughout the Southwest. When the Sun Belt boom fizzled, PS New Mexico was left with a capacity reserve 66% above its peak demand.

"Their eyes have been bigger than their stomach," said Randy Childress, a deputy attorney general for the state of New Mexico. "In a nutshell, this company has been overaggressive."

Many blame Mr. Geist for PS New Mexico's troubles. "Jerry wasn't going to be satisfied with a small electric utility," said Don Hancock of the Southwest Research and Information Center, a nonprofit group that has intervened in PS New Mexico rate cases for rate payers. "He wanted to make an impact" well beyond his service region.

But the state's public service commission has never made Mr. Geist the issue. "Jerry Geist has not become a personality in this as far as we're concerned," said Martin Blake, a commissioner. "He has always been cooperative and tried to shoot straight."

The commission has simply been disinclined to let PS New Mexico recoup its costs for producing power that isn't needed, especially its $1.2 billion investment in the Palo Verde nuclear project. Denied rate increases since 1984 -- and actually hit with rate decreases -- the utility has reduced its work force by one-fifth, suspended its common dividend and taken substantial charges to bail out of its nonutility businesses. The company has even encouraged takeover talk.

Some of his harshest detractors say Mr. Geist apparently left the company he had worked for for 30 years of his own accord. "That board has never forced Jerry to do anything he didn't want to do," said Mr. Hancock.

Mr. Collins, the new chairman, exhorts critics to "stay tuned. The board and management team are strong individuals on their own who will forge a good future" for the company.

He acknowledged that Mr. Geist was chief architect of PS New Mexico's ill-fated expansion plans. But he also said that Mr. Geist "is an extraordinary and gifted leader who will be far more appreciated by the next generation than by this one." He cited PS New Mexico's 15-year-old pollution-control program, which many say sets the pace for the industry, especially given the provisions of the Clean Air Act.
