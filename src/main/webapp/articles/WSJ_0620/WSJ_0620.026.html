Judge Says FDIC Cannot Use Claim In Texas Bank Vote

  • Author: Michael Allen
  • Date: 06/20/90

DALLAS -- A U.S. bankruptcy judge ruled that the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. can't use its $1 billion claim against First RepublicBank Corp. for voting purposes when creditors decide the fate of the company's plan to emerge from Chapter 11 proceedings. The preliminary ruling by Judge Steven Felsenthal increases the likelihood that creditors will approve the plan and cast doubt on some of the legal arguments the FDIC has made in defending itself against a lawsuit filed in U.S. district court here by some creditors.

"This was a stunning victory for us," said Bruce Hendry, a creditor from Minneapolis. "I hope it leads to a settlement with the FDIC, which is the most reasonable route for both parties to take at this time."

The FDIC's $1 billion claim, the largest of several it is asserting against First RepublicBank, stems from a loan the agency made to the company's Dallas and Houston banks shortly before they failed in 1988. The loan was guaranteed by First RepublicBank and a second-tier holding company, IFRB Corp., as well as by other banks in the company's system.

But in his ruling, Judge Felsenthal held that the guarantee amounted to a fraudulent conveyance of assets to the FDIC because the holding company didn't receive an equivalent value in return.

The creditors "would have been far better off if the FDIC had allowed {the Dallas bank} to fail" rather than propping it up with a loan that eventually led all the other banks to fail, too, Judge Rosenthal said.

Although Judge Felsenthal's ruling is preliminary and only concerns the FDIC's ability to vote its claims in the bankruptcy case, attorneys for creditors were cheered by his determination that the FDIC was unlikely to prevail in certain arguments before the district court. In that case, creditors allege that regulators acted illegally in seizing most of First RepublicBank's banks and seek damages that could reach into the billions of dollars.

First RepublicBank's reorganization plan calls for the establishment of a fund to pay the FDIC pending the outcome of the lawsuit. First RepublicBank, which isn't related to First Republic Bancorp of San Francisco, and IFRB list assets totaling $384 million.

An attorney for the FDIC said the agency may appeal the decision.
