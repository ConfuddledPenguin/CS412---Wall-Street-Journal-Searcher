Letters to the Editor: Fear of Inflation Just Doesn't Fly

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/20/90

Both Lawrence A. Kudlow ("What's Good for America Is Good for Bonds," May 9) and Irving Kristol ("Inflation: Almost Never What It Seems," May 16) are right in their editorial-page articles to observe that the conduct of Alan Greenspan's Fed offers no grounds to fear inflation. Yet there is even more evidence: -- The price of gold has not simply been steady, it has fallen to below $375 per ounce. If anything, this measure indicates a slight tightening; 1980s markets were healthy and inflation low with gold at $425 to $475.

-- Exchange rates have exhibited less volatility than at any time since 1973, thanks to both Mr. Greenspan and former Treasury Secretary James Baker's G-7 initiative, causing at least one Journal article to remark they have now become almost "boring." While exchange rates are not the best barometer -- it is possible to have global inflation or deflation with steady exchange rates -- some analysts watch them closely, and they certainly point to no inflation.

Even the money supply, rightly understood, reinforces the case. To find a meaningful monetary aggregate we must exclude currency outstanding -- much of which may be finding its way into East European and other countries where dollars are the "de facto" money. We have this in the Fed's statistics on non-borrowed reserves, an aggregate that, unlike M-1 through M-3 and even the monetary base, excludes the potentially misleading currency.

Non-borrowed reserve growth under Mr. Greenspan: 0.5% since taking office. In fact, reserves stood at $58.4 billion in August 1987; at $58.4 billion in January 1988; at $58.7 billion in January 1989; and $58.7 billion in May 1990. Thus, interest rates of 8.5% on bonds become hard to explain in terms of legitimate inflation fears. Either the market is ill-informed or it simply doubts the Fed will stay the course set by Messrs. Baker and Greenspan.

The remedy for the former is for bond buyers to wake up and take advantage of still-high rates. The remedy for the latter is for the Fed and current Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady to institutionalize all this price stability, gaining what Fed Gov. Wayne Angell has rightly called the critical "announcement effect." Monetary authorities already have done the difficult part of establishing a commodity-backed dollar. Why not the easy part?

Gregory Fossedal

Chairman

Bruce Thompson

Treasurer

Alexis de Tocqueville Institution

Stanford, Calif.
