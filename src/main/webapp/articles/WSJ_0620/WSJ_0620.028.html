Coors's Discharges Polluted Streams, Colorado Unit Says

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 06/20/90

Adolph Coors Co. violated Colorado water law by discharging contaminated groundwater into nearby streams without a permit and without notifying state water authorities, the Colorado Department of Health's Water Quality Control Division said. According to the agency, groundwater contaminated with organic chemicals, including one probable carcinogen, were released from a well under the company's can plant into Clear Creek, a source of water for five municipalities, from 1982 to 1985.

In addition, groundwater contaminated with a petrochemical compound was released into the drainage from 1978 to 1989 from another site near the plant, the agency said.

The discharges stopped when Coors began treating the contaminated groundwater in its own treatment plant. But that also was a violation of state law, says the water agency, because the company didn't get a permit to treat water with those chemicals.

The violations carry fines of as much as $10,000 daily, and Coors's illegal discharges continued for more than 4,000 days. Daily fines are usually levied in contamination cases of much shorter duration, said David Holm, director of the state's water quality division. He said he couldn't estimate what the fine for Coors ultimately might be.

Although no fish kills or other visible impact from the pollution was recorded in Clear Creek during the discharges, "there were very likely days when contaminants would have exceeded water quality standards," said Mr. Holm.

Sandra Woods, Coors vice president, environmental affairs, said the company doesn't believe the contamination ever presented a risk to consumers, employees or Coors's neighbors. She added that the company is certain that no contaminants from the polluted groundwater ended up in the beer. Although traces of trichlorethane, a non-carcinogen, were found in some of the brewing water at the time of the discovery, the amounts were far below government standards. The water quality division said it "is highly unlikely that such chemicals would have survived the brewing process."

The state agency and the company will begin negotiations soon to determine what penalties should be levied. If an agreement isn't reached in informal talks, a formal hearing will be set, Mr. Holm said. Ms. Woods said she thinks the matter will be resolved within 30 days, and the penalties "will be very fair to Coors."
