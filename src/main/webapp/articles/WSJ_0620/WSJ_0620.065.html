Tax Report: A Special Summary and Forecast Of Federal and State Tax Developments

  • Author: Scott R. Schmedel
  • Date: 06/20/90

MUST STATES REFUND $2 billion they don't have to federal-government retirees? In March 1989, the U.S. Supreme Court said Michigan had taxed pensions of federal retirees unconstitutionally while exempting those of state retirees -- but the court didn't say whether the tax treatment must be equalized retroactively by refunds or other action. So the 27 states potentially affected waited to see the outcome of two cases involving refunds of other unconstitutional state taxes. Two weeks ago, the justices reached differing decisions in the two cases, transmitting an ambiguous message to the states resisting pension-tax refunds.

The court has agreed to review another similar case, but lawyers aren't sure that case can produce a definitive ruling. Certainty may have to wait for refund suits to work through state courts to the high court. South Carolina's Supreme Court has rejected refunds; Missouri's has upheld them. But New Mexico has just decided the U.S. Supreme Court hasn't provided grounds for appealing a trial court's award of refunds.

Trial courts have granted refunds in four other states, denied them in one.

STATES FIND IT COSTLY to equalize taxes on federal and state pensions.

Refunds of past taxes still are at issue, but states clearly had to equalize future taxes; 21 have done so. The Federation of Tax Administrators, a state officials' group, estimated that outright equalization could cut states' revenue by $500 million a year; compromises have trimmed that to $90 million. Four states have exempted federal pensions fully, but 11 opted to end full exemption for state pensions and to grant partial exemption to all government retirees.

Three states increased state pensions to offset taxes somewhat. But two others, New Mexico and Utah, dropped state-retiree exemptions and now tax all retirement benefits. Six generous states extended at least partial exemption to all pensioners -- private and government. But one, Virginia, cut back its exemptions this year after projecting that revenue losses from its 1989 legislation would exceed $100 million in 1991; the projected loss now is about $60 million.

IRS CRIME FIGHTERS will consider where to aim their resources in the '90s.

Half these resources long have gone into multi-agency projects to nail organized-crime and drug figures; those programs now include thrift-institution fraud. But total resources have held steady at about 2,800 agents. The IRS and others have questioned whether a larger share of them should be investigating general tax crimes. In 1988, Congress's General Accounting Office said IRS criminal enforcement couldn't be evaluated because it didn't have specific goals.

Now the IRS, the Treasury and the Justice Department are forming a task force to study the allocation of these resources. The group will meet for the first time late in July to formulate purposes and methods. "We need a broad-based study," says Assistant IRS Commissioner Morics. In fiscal 1989, the IRS began 5,417 criminal inquiries, up from 4,899 in 1988. Half the new cases, 2,709, involved drugs and money-laundering, up from 1,430 in 1988.

The group will seek comments from practitioners and others, Morics says.

HE NEVER LOST when he gambled, said Jerold Dean Kling of Scottsbluff, Neb.; that's how he supported simultaneously his wife and three children and his girlfriend and her children. But the Tax Court agreed with the IRS that Kling embezzled a taxable $95,000 in two years from the tire shop where he was the manager.

GENEROUS FOLKS often use charitable remainder trusts: They set aside assets to go to charity when they die, but retain trust income for their lifetimes. Now Revenue Procedures 90-30 through 90-33 describe 16 trust types that meet IRS requirements; the IRS no longer will provide private rulings on whether other forms qualify.

VIRGINIA LANDLORDS can earn personal or corporate state tax credits for 1991 through 1993 by reducing rents by at least 15% for tenants who are disabled or over 62. The credit will equal 50% of the reductions; the annual maximum will be the lesser of $10,000 or the income tax due.

TOUGHER TOOLS for collecting debts are sought by the 93 U.S. attorneys.

These Justice Department officials say they are hampered in collecting federal tax, loan and other delinquencies because federal-court rules require them to operate under the divergent collection laws of the states where they are located. For example, says U.S. Attorney Wortham of Tyler, Texas, five states don't permit garnishment of wages. Also, he says, debtors in Texas and Florida can protect homesteads "worth millions" from seizure, yet those in Idaho have no homestead protection at all.

The U.S. attorneys want to create uniform federal collection rules to bypass the weaknesses in state laws. The Senate adopted their bill last November. But at a House Judiciary hearing last week, Robert Hobbs of the National Consumer Law Center and Henry Sommer of Community Legal Services of Philadelphia said many of the bill's measures are far too harsh.

Its garnishments could put families below the poverty line, Hobbs said.
