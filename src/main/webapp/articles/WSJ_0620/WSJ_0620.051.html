International: Gorbachev Backs New Party Structure In Russian State but Warns Opposition

  • Author: Elisabeth Rubinfien
  • Date: 06/20/90

MOSCOW -- Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev opened a crucial meeting by endorsing a new Russian Federation Communist Party, but assailing those who want to make the new party a stronghold of opposition to his reforms. The meeting of communists in the Russian Federation of the Soviet Union is seen as a preview of next month's important Soviet Communist Party Congress where Mr. Gorbachev is expected to come under attack from the right and left.

Russia is the only one of the 15 Soviet republics without its own party structure. While Mr. Gorbachev yielded to the growing force of Russian nationalism by backing the new party, he also warned that it shouldn't be used to increase the separatist tensions that are straining the Soviet Union. "We must exclude any forms of confrontation between the Russian Federation and the Soviet Union," he said.

Delegates to the Russian party conference voted 2,607 to 61, with 16 abstentions, to adopt the agenda proposed by Mr. Gorbachev. Under it, they will consider the formation of the Russian party and debate the proposed platform of the national congress.

The impetus for Mr. Gorbachev's reluctant endorsement of the party was the emergence of his populist rival, Boris Yeltsin, as the new president of the Russian Federation. Mr. Yeltsin promotes the "sovereignty" of Russia.

The creation of a Russian Communist Party could help Mr. Gorbachev by offsetting Mr. Yeltsin's power. Perhaps as important as any of the words at the first day of the conference, was the scene of Mr. Yeltsin smiling warmly on the dais as he chatted with Mr. Gorbachev.

Earlier there had been rumors that Mr. Yeltsin might resign from the Communist Party at next month's meeting, a move that could foster a split between the party's conservative and democratic groupings. Mr. Yeltsin's plans still aren't clear, but his position on the dais surrounded by Soviet politburo members helped establish an image of the rebel as part of the central leadership.

Prime Minister Nikolai Ryzhkov also occupied a prominent seat at the front of the huge hall in the Kremlin, at times presiding over the meeting. Monday, a senior party official suggested Mr. Ryzhkov as one possible candidate for first secretary of a new Russian party.

Mr. Gorbachev defended a rapid transition to a "regulated market economy" as the completion of unfinished de-Stalinization. The Soviet leader forcefully rejected the arguments of the right in a way that may have a certain appeal in a country where most people aren't sure what a market economy is. He told a nationwide television audience that it is ridiculous to call the movement toward a market a step back toward capitalism. "The market existed long before capitalism," he said.

Mr. Gorbachev's speech was followed by attacks from both sides. Democratic reformists, concerned that a Russian Federation Communist Party will be controlled by conservatives, said its creation should be postponed until basic political concepts could be discussed and voted on by the public. The reformers are seeking to break the party's monopoly over government and turn it into a parliamentary party that would have to compete for power.

Old-line communists assailed the central leadership for perverting the process of establishing a new party. The leadership "has reduced the party to crouching unarmed in the trenches under massive shelling by anti-Socialist forces," said delegate Ivan Osadchiy, according to Tass news service. "Only a Communist Party of the Leninist type" can pull Russia out of its crisis.

Mr. Gorbachev urged the gradual separation of party from state operations. But an ironic glimpse of the current state of reform came when Mr. Gorbachev slipped into old patterns and called for a vote on a motion to order state television to fully broadcast the event.

Only some time later, when radical Leningrad Mayor Anatoli Sobchak took the floor and pointed out that the party has no right to make such an order did Mr. Gorbachev reconsider. Agreeing, he called for another vote to "reflect the reality that all we can do is request."
