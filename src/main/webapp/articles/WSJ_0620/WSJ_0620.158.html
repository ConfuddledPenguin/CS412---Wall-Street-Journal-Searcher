Politics & Policy: House Approves Spending Measure For Energy, Water

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 06/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The House, brushing aside demands for budget cuts, approved a $20.8 billion fiscal 1991 energy and water bill providing for a 12.7% increase from current spending levels and accelerating funding for the cleanup of nuclear-weapons plants. The final 355-59 roll call last night capped a day of defeats for the Republican leadership, which saw many of its own rank and file repeatedly join Democrats to oppose efforts for across-the-board spending cuts.

More than 40 Republicans opposed a final bipartisan effort to impose a reduction of 2%, or $400 million, in the bill, and the debate underscored the immense difficulties that will face budget negotiators if and when they bring back a deficit-reduction package from their budget summit talks with the Bush administration.

"I am really depressed," said Minnesota Rep. Bill Frenzel, the ranking Republican on the House Budget Committee. "They think the deals are being made in the summit and someone else can be a hero for them."

The support summoned for the bill reflects the strength of the energy and water coalition, which still can win support among traditionally conservative Southern and Western delegations even as it now champions more liberal environmental and scientific initiatives.

The measure funds an estimated 33 Army Corps of Engineers construction projects that weren't included in President Bush's budget, and the total of nearly $3.28 billion for cleaning up nuclear plants represents an increase of $488 million from the administration's request and more than $1 billion from fiscal 1990, which ends Sept. 30.

The opposition began at a disadvantage, but the Republicans also appeared hurt by their failure to frame clearer choices. The GOP leadership has yet to offer an alternative budget of its own this year, and in a bill laden with funding for parochial interests, the one Republican amendment targeted at a specific account was offered by a Midwestern lawmaker, who conceded he knew little about the California power provision that a taxpayers' lobby had asked him to oppose.

By contrast, the Democratic leadership appears determined to use the appropriations process aggressively, both to put pressure on the summit talks and to spell out the party's domestic priorities going into the fall elections. On back-to-back procedural votes, Democrats overrode Republican objections to taking up spending bills without a final budget resolution, and by a 276-136 margin, the chamber approved a resolution making the House budget the guide for dividing discretionary spending between domestic and military accounts.

Among major science accounts, the bill provides the full $318 million sought by the administration for the superconducting supercollider. While spending on magnetic fusion would be frozen near current levels, high-energy physics programs would grow almost 6% to $621.2 million.

Reflecting the pressure of Midwestern Republicans on the House Appropriations Committee, funding for biofuels energy programs would rise to $36 million, more than double current levels and 28% above the administration request. Similarly in solar-energy accounts, where Mr. Bush asked for large increases, the bill bids up the president and reflects the political ties of companies in photovoltaic research.

Despite its financial troubles, Lawrenceville, N.J.-based Chronar Corp. is a major force here, for example, with its strong financial ties to the Sheet Metal Workers pension fund as well as investors or projects in the home districts of Appropriations Committee members from California and Alabama.
