Technology & Health: Hospitals Hire Revenue Recovery Firms To Inflate Bills, Senate Panel Claims

  • Author: Walt Bogdanich
  • Date: 06/20/90

WASHINGTON -- A Senate panel is accusing hospitals of hiring firms that inflate hospital bills through questionable billings, while hiding overcharges. The practice, which can contribute to rising health-care costs, will be the subject of a hearing to be held today by the Senate's Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations.

In a report prepared for the hearings, the panel focused on two so-called revenue recovery firms, but it said there are at least 35 such companies operating nationwide.

Hospitals are more inclined to hire revenue recovery firms as their profit margins continue to be squeezed through public and private cost controls, and the added burden of providing uncompensated care for the indigent.

Revenue recovery firms often contract with hospitals to review bills on a contingency-fee or percentage-of-recovery basis -- sometimes 50% of the amount of charges recovered. Because such payment schemes encourage abuse, Big Eight accounting firms said their ethics standards prohibit such arrangements.

The Senate panel's report found that revenue recovery firms "teach auditors to downplay or ignore overcharges," while pressuring employees to find undercharges. One firm required that each auditor find at least $1,225 in undercharges each day, according to the report. Some auditors not meeting their quotas were fired, the report said.

The General Accounting Office, an arm of Congress, reviewed accounts at four Los Angeles-area hospitals that were audited by Manns Co., a revenue recovery firm based in Rolling Hills Estates, Calif. Although Manns uncovered overcharges in 15% of the cases audited, GAO investigators found overcharges were present in 99% of the cases audited, according to Senate investigators.

"It depends on what you call an overcharge," said Dan O'Callaghan, Manns vice president, finance. "We took off $7 million in overcharges in the past two years in our client hospitals."

Revenue recovery employees often conceal their involvement with a hospital bill out of fear that an insurance company will then review that bill, investigators charged. Some revenue recovery employees pose as hospital employees in answering telephone inquiries from patients or insurers, they said.

The Health Insurance Association of America, concerned about the practices of revenue recovery firms, has formed a task force to study the issue. The HIAA complains that hospitals increasingly are erecting barriers to keep them from auditing questionable hospital bills. Some hospitals have begun charging several hundred dollars or more just for the right to audit a bill. The association also complains that hospitals discourage audits through stalling tactics.

Partly as a result of these barriers, insurers rarely review bills under $5,000, the Senate panel said.
