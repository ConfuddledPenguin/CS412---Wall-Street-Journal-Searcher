Tuesday's Markets: Stock Prices Rebound, Gain 11.38 --- Rise Called Reaction To Monday's Loss; Bonds Drift Lower

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/20/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices rebounded slightly from Monday's sharp drop, again on light volume, while bond prices drifted lower. The dollar weakened against most major currencies. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 11.38 points to 2893.56. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices fell about a quarter of a point, or $2.50 for each $1,000 of face amount, in light trading. The dollar fell against the mark and the yen.

Some analysts speculated that the dollar's weakness was partly responsible for bond prices not reacting to a report of yet another decline in housing starts this past month -- further evidence of a sluggish economy -- and declining prices for oil and other commodities. Bonds also were held down by a deluge of newly issued municipal and asset-backed bonds.

The drop in bond prices -- along with more disappointing earnings news and growing conviction that the Federal Reserve Board isn't about to ease interest rates -- helped to hold stock prices to modest gains in tepid trading.

"This is just a reflex rally" following Monday 50-point drop, said Edward Nicoski, technical research chief at Piper, Jaffray & Hopwood in Minneapolis. He noted the gains were confined to blue chips and that the over-the-counter market fell.

What hurt the dollar is less clear. It has been trading in narrow ranges recently in the absence of any startling economic news. Most of the reports showing weakening economic activity have been in line with expectations, which leaves currency traders with little to trade on.

Richard Levine, a Merrill Lynch foreign-exchange broker, said traders are alternately nudging the dollar up to see where they encounter resistance and easing it down to determine support levels. Yesterday, the focus was on the bottom end. "Having failed to take out the overhead resistance level, they're now probing to see how far it should go on the down side," Mr. Levine said.

Traders in any market, Mr. Levine added, are most fearful when their market appears to be drifting along, in no hurry to rise or fall. "A body at rest tends to stay at rest," he noted. "They don't want that."

But Lawrence Leuzzi, a managing director at S.G. Warburg Securities, sees fundamental reasons for some of the dollar's weakness. The yen rose, he said, because of a report that Japan's money supply was growing strongly, as did the nation's output of goods and services in the first period. The mark was aided by a rise in West German bond yields, he said.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose in light trading, with 134.9 million shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange. Advancing issues edged out decliners, 788 to 710.

Bond prices climbed. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 8.47%.

The dollar eased. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6738 marks and 153.55 yen, compared with 1.6758 marks and 153.66 yen Monday.
