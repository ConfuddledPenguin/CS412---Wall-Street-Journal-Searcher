Judge Ends `Minitrial' In Keating's Bid for S&L

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/20/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal Judge Stanley Sporkin concluded an unusual "minitrial" in Charles Keating's suit to recover Lincoln Savings & Loan Association, saying, "It's up to me, and I'm going to ponder it." After 30 days of testimony that looked at a small sample of Lincoln's real estate developments and securities investments, Judge Sporkin has three choices: He can uphold the government's April 1989 seizure of the thrift, return the thrift or order a full trial.

The government and Mr. Keating remained fiercely at odds yesterday as final arguments were heard over Mr. Keating's suit challenging the takeover. Lincoln was the principal subsidiary of Phoenix, Ariz.-based American Continental Corp., of which Mr. Keating is chairman.

Mr. Keating charged that incompetent regulators carried out a "vendetta" against him and smothered Lincoln with unfair regulation because it didn't operate like a traditional thrift. The government says Mr. Keating and his business colleagues crafted sham transactions that allowed them to drain tens of millions of dollars from Lincoln that was paid to American Continental and then to Mr. Keating and his associates in salaries and dividends.

Mr. Sporkin didn't indicate when he plans to make a decision in the case.
