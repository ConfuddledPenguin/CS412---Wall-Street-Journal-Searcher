Goodyear Sees Quarterly Loss; Revamp Cited

  • Author: Gregory Stricharchuk
  • Date: 06/20/90

AKRON, Ohio -- Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co., citing sluggishness in the U.S. auto industry and other factors, said it expects to post a second-quarter loss of as much as $10 million after a pretax restructuring charge of $35 million. About 1,100 employees involved in tire sales and marketing in the U.S. will lose their jobs as part of the restructuring, the company said.

In the 1989 second quarter, Goodyear earned $27.1 million, or 47 cents a share, on sales of $2.8 billion. Results included an after-tax charge of $95 million related to the sale of unused pipe.

Also, the tire maker said it expects 1990 second-half results to about match its disappointing 1989 second half, in which it earned $85.2 million, or $1.57 a share, on sales of $5.42 billion.

On the news, tire industry analyst Harry Millis, of McDonald & Co., Cleveland, reduced his full-year earnings estimate to about $2 a share, excluding any charges, down from $2.55 a share. Mr. Millis said job cutbacks were about double what he had expected.

In 1989 Goodyear earned $206.8 million, or $3.58 a share, on sales of $10.87 billion. Results included a gain of $17.4 million from a tax-loss carry-forward offset by a charge of $105.9 million, primarily from the sale of the company's South African subsidiary.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Goodyear closed yesterday at $33.75, off 25 cents.

Goodyear, which has about 50,000 employees in the U.S., said it's eliminating its five tire sales field offices in an effort to make sales and marketing more efficient and responsive to customers. In April, the company created two new units: one for consumer tires that serves mostly the passenger tire market and the other for commercial tires. Each group has 28 sales regions and 43 sales districts.

McDonald's Mr. Millis said there haven't been any pleasant surprises for Goodyear this year except that sales volume of replacement tires has increased. However, he said, pricing on replacement tires has been weak. Although he said pricing on tires shipped to domestic auto makers has been fairly good, volume has been off. He also said Goodyear has been hurt by a pricing war on tires shipped to European auto makers as well.

Goodyear has other nagging problems. A prolonged strike at its tire plant in Turkey is costing the company about five cents a share each month in lost earnings, Mr. Millis said. The company also continues to be hurt by the carrying costs of its west-to-east crude oil pipeline and startup costs at a tire plant in Canada.
