Southern Co.'s Net Slumped by 43% In First Quarter

  • Author: Rick Christie
  • Date: 05/03/90

Southern Co. reported its first-quarter earnings plunged 43% to $113.1 million, or 36 cents a share, because of higher operating costs and sagging electricity sales. The drop marks the third consecutive quarterly earnings decline for the operator of energy utilities in the Southeast. In the 1989 first quarter, Atlanta-based Southern earned $197 million, or 62 cents a share. Revenue slipped to $1.67 billion in the most recent quarter from $1.68 billion a year earlier.

The earnings slump comes at a time when the beleaguered company faces new federal inquiries into alleged business ties between Southern and the son of the company's chief executive officer, Edward L. Addison, as well as a continuing federal tax investigation into the company's accounting for spare parts.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, Southern closed at $23.625, down 50 cents.

In recent quarters, Southern has been smarting from its Georgia Power Co. unit's continuing rate dispute with the Georgia Public Service Commission over the utility's Vogtle nuclear-power plant. The commission has allowed Georgia Power to recover only $218 million -- over a 10-year phase-in period -- of the company's estimated $413 million in cost overruns at the plant's No. 2 unit alone.

Meanwhile, the latest quarter shows Southern carrying the higher fixed costs for that unit, as well as the No. 4 unit of its Scherer nuclear plant near Macon, Ga. "This was a significant factor in our cost increases," a company spokesman said. "Not only were we not carrying them last year, but we've not received the rates to fully recover the costs." The spokesman didn't specify the amount of the cost increase.

To make matters worse, Southern's kilowatt-hour sales fell 1.2% in the first quarter because of mild winter temperatures, which affected the entire industry. The trend apparently carried over through April, according to the spokesman, which could hurt second-quarter earnings.

The second-quarter results could be further affected by a Mississippi Supreme Court ruling in April that the state's four-year-old Performance Evaluation Plan is invalid. Southern said the plan allowed residential electricity rates for its Mississippi Power Co. unit to be automatically adjusted according to its financial condition and performance.

But if the court's decision to curtail the plan as of April 30 stands, Mississippi Power may have to refund as much as $75 million to customers.

Corrections & Amplifications

A SOUTHERN CO. REQUEST to the Georgia Public Service Commission for a $413 million rate increase was made to recover the company's investment in the No. 2 unit of its Vogtle nuclear power plant, as well as other costs of providing services. In earlier editions it was misstated that the $413 million was for cost overruns at the plant.
