Your Money Matters: Investors May Be Ready for `Prime' Time

  • Author: Karen Slater
  • Date: 05/03/90

Little-known securities called Americus Trust "Primes" may be a good choice for people who want to own stocks these days but are anxious about price declines. Traded on the American Stock Exchange, Primes function like lower-risk versions of 26 blue-chip issues. The Primes allow investors to collect almost all of the dividend income from the related stock and any appreciation in the price of the stock up to a preset maximum.

While Primes generally lag when the market takes off, they suffer less than the related stock when prices collapse. And because Primes sell for less than the related stock, several will deliver solid, double-digit returns over the next two years as long as the related stock prices don't fall below current levels.

"It's a good way for someone to have some participation in the stock market without being quite so nervous," says Tracey Faby, a senior investment officer with Axe-Houghton Management Inc., Tarrytown, N.Y., a money management unit of insurer USF&G Corp. that has been a big buyer of Primes.

These odd-ball securities are the brainchild of Americus Shareowner Service Corp., which sponsored a series of "Americus Trusts" from 1983 to 1987. (No new trusts are being created because of changes in the tax rules.)

The idea was to divide some household-name stocks into a conservative, income-oriented investment and a more-speculative, appreciation-oriented investment. Investors tendered stock to the trusts, and for each share received a conservative Prime and a speculative "Score."

Prime holders get the stock dividend minus an administrative charge of as much as a nickel a year. The holders also get the appreciation in the underlying common up to a preset maximum price, or "termination claim." When the trusts terminate after five years, they receive common shares worth the lower of the then-current stock price or the termination claim.

Price appreciation beyond the termination claim goes to the Score holders. But Prime holders are compensated for accepting that limit because Primes sell for less than the related common.

That discount is central to the Primes' appeal.

The lower price provides "a head start on the stock in terms of performance," explains J. Richard Tashjian, a senior vice president of Sovran Capital Management Corp., a unit of banking company Sovran Financial Corp. Further, Mr. Tashjian says, "the discount represents your reduction in risk."

Consider General Electric Co. Primes, in which Sovran Capital has a big stake. GE Primes are selling at $55.63, a sizable discount from $64 for the company's stock, as of April 30. When the GE Americus Trust terminates in two years, Prime holders will get either the prevailing stock price or the $70 termination claim, whichever is lower.

GE Primes will deliver an annualized return of 10.8% from now to termination if GE's stock price and dividend remain at current levels, according to calculations by Peter Broms, Kidder, Peabody & Co. senior vice president. That return comprises dividend income and the appreciation from the Prime price to the current stock price.

The annualized return will be a heftier 14.7% if the stock rises at least to the termination claim. And it will be 15.6% if the dividend also grows at the rate Kidder expects.

Meanwhile, GE common will have to fall below the current Prime price, which is 13% below the current stock price, for someone buying the Prime now to take a loss at termination.

Among other Primes that offer double-digit returns even without price appreciation, Atlantic Richfield gets high marks from Robert Haber, manager of Fidelity Balanced Fund. Ms. Faby of Axe-Houghton likes Du Pont, General Motors, Johnson & Johnson and Mobil.

But don't buy any Prime if you don't like the company. "You always want to make sure you have faith in the outlook for the underlying common stock," Kidder's Mr. Broms says.

Investors should also realize that the Primes vary greatly in the levels of risk and reward they entail. Some offer limited returns and risk that make them comparable with bonds, while others expose holders to almost all the price fluctuations of the related stocks.

It's all a function of the relationship between the common stock price and the Prime's termination claim.

The Philip Morris Prime is often cited as a good bond alternative. That's because Philip Morris common stock, as of April 30, was trading at $42, far above the termination claim of $27.50. That gives Prime buyers a high probability of collecting the full termination claim when the Philip Morris trust expires in July 1992.

At the April 30 price of $24.50 for a Philip Morris Prime, Kidder figures investors will earn 10.6% a year without dividend growth and 11.7% with the projected dividend growth. That compares with a yield of around 9% on two-year Treasury notes.

Mr. Broms says investors might also want to consider the American Telephone & Telegraph and GTE Primes as fixed-income alternatives. Two Primes that currently do not offer attractive returns as bond substitutes are the Procter & Gamble and Coca-Cola issues.

Meanwhile, at the other end of the spectrum are Primes such as American Express, Eastman Kodak, International Business Machines and Xerox where the common stock price is far below the termination claim. The Primes of such issues sell at only a small discount to the underlying common stock because the termination claim doesn't crimp the appreciation potential of the Prime very much.

Investors interested in those companies should consider the Primes as "a cheaper way to own the same stocks," says Sandy Weisman, a broker with Prescott, Ball & Turben Inc. in Cleveland.

But Mr. Broms advises sticking with the common stock if the price discount of the Primes is less than 5%, as it currently is with IBM and Xerox. For such a small savings, he figures, it's not worth giving up the chance to make a killing if the stock goes through the roof.
