U.S. Borrowing Will Set a Record; S&L Bailouts Cited

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 05/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The federal government plans next week to borrow a record amount for any regular quarterly refunding operation, in part to help finance savings-and-loan bailouts. The Treasury said it plans to sell $30.5 billion of notes and bonds to raise about $12.4 billion in cash and redeem about $18.1 billion in maturing government securities. The borrowing will be $500 million higher than the last two refundings, the government said.

The new securities will consist of:

-- $10.5 billion of three-year notes, to be auctioned next Tuesday.

-- $10 billion of 10-year notes, to be auctioned next Wednesday.

-- $10 billion of 30-year bonds, to be auctioned next Thursday.

The three-year notes will mature on May 15, 1993, the 10-year notes on May 15, 2000, and the 30-year bonds on May 15, 2020.

For the current quarter, the government estimates a net borrowing need of $12.6 billion, assuming a $30 billion cash balance at the end of June. That is a considerably greater borrowing need than for the same quarter last year, when an unexpectedly high level of tax receipts poured into the Treasury's coffers. At a news conference yesterday to announce next week's refunding, Robert Glauber, the Treasury's undersecretary for finance, refused to comment on tax receipts this year.

Mr. Glauber said the $12.6 billion net borrowing requirement for the second quarter includes borrowing to finance the Resolution Trust Corp., the agency set up to manage failing savings and loans, under thrift bail-out legislation.

Including next week's refunding, the Treasury will have raised $2.6 billion of its net borrowing requirement. The remaining $10 billion could be raised through regular sales of three-month and six-month Treasury bills and one-year notes, or through a planned sale of a two-year note in May and a five-year, two-month note in early June, Mr. Glauber said.
