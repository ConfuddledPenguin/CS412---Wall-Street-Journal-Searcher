GM Is Proposing To Boost Pensions Of Top Officials

  • Author: Paul Ingrassia
  • Date: 05/03/90

DETROIT -- General Motors Corp. wants to boost pensions for 3,350 top officials including Chairman Roger B. Smith, whose retirement pay would nearly double to about $1.2 million a year under the plan. The move is drawing fire from the United Auto Workers union, which may make sweeter pensions an issue in this summer's national contract talks with GM and the other domestic auto makers.

The new pension formula, outlined in GM's proxy statement issued last month, is being submitted to shareholders for approval at the company's May 25 annual meeting. The new formula would apply to GM's U.S. executives who retire after Nov. 1, 1989. That includes Mr. Smith, who will retire Aug. 1.

The formula would be sweetened by including salary and bonuses in calculating each executive's pension. Currently, the formula reflects only an executive's base salary. The change would be especially lucrative for top executives such as Chairman Smith, for whom bonus is an important part of total direct compensation.

GM contends that its executive pensions aren't competitive, and that the change would merely bring its pension formula up to prevailing standards.

The proposed change "is consistent with what a number of other large companies do," said a spokesman. "GM had been behind other companies in this area." He added that GM doesn't have to submit the change to shareholders, but the company's directors had decided to do so.

Despite GM's arguments, the timing of the proposed change is sure to complicate the company's contract talks with the UAW. A sizable portion of the union's rank and file wants to make inflation-indexing for pensions a key contract demand. The union's leadership believes the demand isn't realistic and would prefer to focus on job-security issues, but GM's move may bring the pension issue to the fore.

"Later this year we will be bargaining for hundreds of thousands of retired union workers," UAW President Owen Bieber said in a statement yesterday. "In view of GM's largesse to its executives, there will be no excuse for the corporation to be less than fully responsive to the needs of our older members and retirees."
