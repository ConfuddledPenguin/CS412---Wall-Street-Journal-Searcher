Who's News: Saxton is Named Chief of General Housewares Corp.

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 05/03/90

General Housewares Corp. named Paul A. Saxton, the 51-year-old president and chief operating officer, to the additional post of chief executive officer. He succeeds John H. Muller Jr., 65, who remains chairman. The appointment, which was made after the annual meeting yesterday, is effective July 1.

Mr. Muller formed the Stamford, Conn., company almost 23 years ago. He cedes the top operations post just as the firm has closed the sale of its candle unit, substantially completed its effort to focus on the high end of cookware and cutlery, and announced plans to move its corporate office to Terre Haute, Ind., near its main plant.

Housewares is "a very difficult business, and I didn't realize how much when I got in," Mr. Muller said. Fluctuations in interest rates, changes in life styles, plus competition from the U.S. and overseas created roller-coaster results at General Housewares, and the stock price followed suit.

General Housewares' stock price, which through the 1980s ranged from $7 a share to near $30, has only rarely cleared $14 a share since late 1984. The shares were unchanged yesterday at $11 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Now the challenge of strengthening and stabilizing General Housewares' results, through marketing the company's Chicago Cutlery and Magnalite cookware brands, falls entirely to Mr. Saxton.

Mr. Saxton, a former English instructor, came to General Housewares from Allegheny International Inc.'s Sunbeam appliance unit, where he was vice president, sales and marketing. In his 3 1/2 years at Sunbeam, Mr. Saxton said, sales nearly doubled to $228 million, and in September 1987, Mr. Muller hired him to light a fire under General Housewares' cookware business. In August 1989, he was named president and chief operating officer.

When Mr. Saxton joined General Housewares, one problem he identified was that the company offered too many products, confusing the sales force and making inventory control difficult. He pared back the product lines, and in so doing "jettisoned some things near and dear to {Mr. Muller's} heart," Mr. Saxton said.

Mr. Saxton determined to get the company out of mass-merchant, low-priced cookware. "The better place to be was the high end, but I bet on it only so much," Mr. Muller said. And Mr. Saxton brought the sales force in house, replacing independent representatives.

Mr. Saxton also has determined to move the company's marketing efforts away from retail stores and more directly to consumers. He notes that the retailing industry is in financial turmoil, and he suggests that busy consumers are increasingly likely to choose offerings from a catalog or home-shopping television program.
