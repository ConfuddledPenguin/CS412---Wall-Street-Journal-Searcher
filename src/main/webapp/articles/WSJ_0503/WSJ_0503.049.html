Letters to the Editor: A Little Moss on the Rolling Stone

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/03/90

Kudos on editorially deboning the "Mean Greens" at Rolling Stone for their bland hypocrisy-pontificating about the environment while promoting the production of virtually non-biodegradable CDs, LPs and cassettes ("Are CDs Recyclable?" April 18). Your editorial, however, revealed just the tip of the iceberg. In the throes of their ecohysteria, Hollywoodites and the music industry ignore not only their contribution to the solid-waste crisis (not to mention noise pollution), but also their own willynilly squandering of our precious natural resources.

For instance, how many million kilowatts of energy are wasted annually to overamplify today's deafeningly loud rock concerts? Ecologically sensitive balladeers should be blazing a trail for the rest of us by going acoustic, instead of wailing through 30-foot speakers about rain forests and the horrors of nuclear power.

And speaking of nuclear power, how many entertainers check out the source of their juice before plugging in and cranking up? Atomic fission has undoubtedly powered performances by thousands of unsuspecting artistes.

Finally, how much electricity was wasted on public-address systems to exhort the faithful at Earth Day rallies?

Like the Sierra Club, which prints its calendars on unrecyclable paper, Rolling Stone and the rest of the glitterati have found in environmentalism a cause du jour that is painless to preach but much more difficult to practice.

John B. Scanlon

Alexandria, Va.

---

Congratulations for doing battle with the zealots of environmentalism. By the time I finished reading your editorial on Earth Day ("O Holy Day," April 19), I was determined to lend my hand and join the iconoclasts. What could I do? Then I spied the enemy's major icon. Without any hesitation I smashed my six-year-old's globe. Hah]

What next? My gaze fell upon the week's accumulation of The Wall Street Journal. No problem there -- but then my iconoclasm blossomed in full. Why stop with the granolas? There in front of me were the scriptures of another neo-religion run amok. A quick glance assured me that the priests and priestesses were busy at work uplifting the masses to new heights of well-being. Their sermons rolled down from the pulpit: to produce more is good; to consume more is better; to ignore all other purpose of existence is best; unfettered and unquestioned economic growth is the sacrament.

There are zealots of just about every persuasion. The Club of Rome was as wrong as the atomic-energy visionaries of the 1950s. Thankfully, being wrong is not a proscription for speaking out on issues or the Earth would be a very quiet place.

You are quite right that there is a religious-like atmosphere to this issue. The debate is about more than clean air and GNP. It extends to the core questions of how and why we live.

Daniel Flanagan

Ulm, Mont.

---

Everything has its downside, and Earth Day is no exception. I am appalled at what well-meaning environmental alarmists are doing to our children. The environmental problems, and possible solutions to those problems, easily boggle the mind of the most knowledgeable, concerned adult. For example, I have read numerous, convincing predictions that the Earth will perish because it's going to get hotter and equally convincing ones proving that we're headed for another ice age. To little children, these issues are only terrifying fairy stories.

Thanks to a relentless barrage of doomsday predictions, children now believe that the entire world is a hopeless mess. That careless, greedy adults have irrevocably tipped the "delicate balance of Nature" toward destruction.

These unremitting, gloomy messages hit our kids from all sides -- in school and at home over the airwaves. Every nature show on TV must now whipsaw kids by first glorifying and romanticizing Nature and then predicting that adults are on the verge of ruining everything the children have just seen.

Adults just can't seem to resist inflicting their fears and ideology on children. It's sickening. As Pink Floyd sang, "Leave them kids alone]"

Charles H. Small

Newton, Mass.
