Credit Markets: Bond Prices Little Changed in Lifeless Trading; No Surprises in Terms for Treasury Refunding

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Andrew Bary
  • Date: 05/03/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices ended little changed in lifeless trading activity as investors anxiously awaited details of the Treasury's next big bond auction. The announcement, which came very late in the day, had little impact on the market because the terms of the auction were as analysts had expected. The Treasury said it will sell a record $30.5 billion of notes and bonds at next week's quarterly refunding of the federal debt. The government will auction $10.5 billion of three-year notes Tuesday, $10 billion of 10-year notes Wednesday and $10 billion of 30-year bonds next Thursday.

The Treasury also set the next auction for Resolution Funding Corp. thrift bailout bonds for July 10 and said it will announce details of the auction July 3.

In trading yesterday, Treasury bond prices initially fell as much as 3/8 point, or $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount, following news that Federal Reserve Board Vice Chairman Manuel Johnson said inflation was too high.

But Treasury prices recuperated later to end unchanged to about 1/8 point higher.

Mr. Johnson's remarks, which were made to reporters after a speech to executives of the National Automated Clearing House Association, were interpreted by investors as yet another sign that the Fed may eventually raise interest rates in an effort to reduce inflationary pressures.

The comments were especially disturbing considering that the date of the Fed's Federal Open Market Committee meeting is fast approaching. The committee, which is the Fed's policy-making arm, will meet on May 15, and the members will decide whether to change monetary policy.

Fears of Fed tightening seemed to have calmed down a bit later in the day when the Fed released the latest edition of its so-called beige book, a summary of economic conditions prepared for use by policymakers before their May 15 meeting in Washington.

The report indicated that economic activity continues to expand slowly across the country, but not fast enough to allow the Fed room to raise interest rates. Manufacturing, which has been depressed in many parts of the nation, is improving.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

The mortgage securities market was abuzz with talk that the Comptroller of the Currency may soon impose curbs on commercial banks' holdings of certain high-yielding mortgage securities.

Banks have been big buyers of all mortgage securities during the past year because new risk-based capital bank standards favor these securities over many traditional bank investments.

But dealers on Wall Street said the comptroller's office is concerned with the large increase in bank holdings of derivative securities, which are a riskier type of mortgage security. These would include highly volatile securities such as inverse floating-rate, zero-coupon, residual and "jump Z" bonds. The comptroller might also restrict "companion" bonds with original average lives of more than seven years. Companion bonds are relatively high-yielding derivative securities that are created in tandem with prepayment-protected planned amortization classes.

A spokesman for the comptroller said regulations on bank investment in mortgage securities are "in the formulation stage," but nothing has been made final.

Andrew Carron, head of mortgage research at First Boston Corp., said banks would probably be permitted to hold most standard derivative mortgage securities. These would include "plain vanilla" real estate mortgage investment conduits, which are a type of collateralized mortgage obligation.

In trading yesterday, most mortgage-backed securities ended little changed.

Corporate Issues

Ford Capital BV, the Dutch-based finance subsidiary of Ford Motor Co., made its debut in the U.S. credit markets with $400 million of 12-year notes. The notes were priced to yield 9.952%.

Ford Capital is a financing vehicle set up primarily to refinance debt incurred in connection with Ford Motor's $2.52 billion acquisition of Jaguar PLC.

It is unusual for offshore units of U.S. companies to tap the U.S. debt market because their primary purpose is to raise capital through the Eurobond market.

But Peter Milhaupt, director of capital markets at lead underwriter First Boston Corp., said the U.S. market is currently more attractive than the Euromarket for domestic issuers.

Among other things, Mr. Milhaupt noted that European rates have been rising relative to U.S. rates and that traditional Eurobond investors tend to prefer securities that mature in 10 years or less.

Moreover, he said, "The Euromarket has not been able to consistently" absorb certain types of bond offerings. "It is our judgment that for the size, $400 million, and the maturity, 12 years, {Ford} could not get this kind of execution in that market in Eurodollars or for that matter by swapping out of currencies" for dollars, said Mr. Milhaupt.

Although First Boston officials said the deal went well and attracted a broad-based buying interest from institutional investors, some corporate bond traders said the issue was priced a little aggressively.

The non-callable notes were priced at a spread of 0.86 percentage point above the Treasury's 10-year note. A comparable Ford Holdings 9 1/4% issue of 10-year notes is trading at 0.89 percentage point above the Treasury's 10-year note, dealers said. Officials at First Boston said the deal hasn't been completely sold yet.

Junk Bonds

Broad buying interest buoyed the junk bond market, lifting prices of most actively traded bonds.

Most junk bonds, which are high-yield speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings, gained 1/8 and 1/4 point.

Meanwhile, Moody's Investors Service Inc. said junk bonds were the best performing sector of the bond market last month after being one of the worse performing sectors most of last year.

In its latest bond-market performance report, Moody's said junk bonds eked out a small 0.5% total return last month. While that's not a stellar performance, it's far better than the minus 2% total return posted on long-term Treasury bonds and the minus 1.5% total return on investment-grade corporate bonds. Moody's said the biggest losers last month were long-term zero-coupon Treasury bonds, which plunged 8% last month.

In trading yesterday, the junk bonds of Ames Department Stores Inc. were again the major market-mover as vulture funds and other investors in distressed securities continued to swoop down and snap up the retailer's bonds.

Ever since the Rocky Hill, Conn.-based firm filed for protection under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code, its bonds have been gaining ground. Some investors believe the bonds will be valued at higher levels if and when the company emerges from bankruptcy-law proceedings. Ames's 13% senior subordinated reset notes, due in 1999, rose 2 1/4 points to end at 27 1/2.

The junk bonds of R.H. Macy & Co. were unchanged despite news that the retailer has reduced its loss for the third quarter. In a letter to investors, Macy said that despite almost flat sales, it was able to reduce its loss for the quarter ended April 28. In the year-earlier third quarter, the company reported a loss of $64 million, on sales of $1.5 billion.

Macy's 14 1/2% senior subordinated debentures, due in 1998, were unchanged at 70 1/2, while its 14 1/2% subordinated debentures, maturing in 2001, were little changed at 61.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bonds ended at 94 19/32 to yield 9.01%, up from 94 14/32 to yield 9.03% on Tuesday. Two-year notes ended unchanged at 99 23/32 to yield 8.997%.

Short-term rates ended little changed. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills eased slightly to 7.90% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.152%, while the rate on six-month bills was slightly lower at 8.02% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.464%.

Municipals

In a repeat performance of Tuesday's sleepy dealings, municipal bonds finished narrowly mixed in fairly active trading. Some actively traded issues were off as much as 1/4 point, while other issues were up as much as 1/8 point.

Municipals aren't expected to show much life until the path of the Treasury bond market is clearer, traders said.

In light primary market activity, a First Boston Corp. group priced $70 million of Indiana Health Facilities Authority revenue bonds.

In secondary market activity, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was unchanged at 95 bid to yield 7.62%.

Foreign Bonds

News that East and West Germany agreed on terms of monetary union had only a modest impact on the West German bond market.

Under terms of the agreement, most East Germans will be able to convert 4,000 East German marks of savings into West German marks at a 1-to-1 exchange rate. East Germans who are 60 years old and over will be able to convert 6,000 East German marks at the favorable rate, while those 14 and younger will be limited to 2,000 marks. Remaining savings will be converted at a 2-to-1 rate. West Germany said the new official exchange rates become effective immediately.

Investors took the news surprisingly well, considering the West German bond market has been depressed for months because of fears that reunification of the two currencies would result in exorbitant inflation, especially if the exchange rate came in at 1 to 1.

But analysts said inflation concerns already have been priced into the market.

In trading, West Germany's benchmark 7 3/4% bond due in 2000 fell 0.25 point to 92.95 to yield 8.85%.
