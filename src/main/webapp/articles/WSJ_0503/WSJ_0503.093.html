Politics & Policy: Senate Budget Committee Backs Plan To Cut Federal Deficit by $43 Billion

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 05/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Budget Committee rallied behind a plan to reduce the federal deficit by $43 billion by spending less on defense and domestic programs than either the House or the White House has proposed. After weeks of bickering and back-room bargaining, Budget Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.) won support for a $1.23 trillion fiscal 1991 spending plan from all 13 Democrats on the panel as well as from Iowa Republican Sen. Charles Grassley.

Sen. Sasser called the plan "the largest single deficit-reduction package we have passed in more than a decade," and said it demonstrates that Congress can come up with a budget without first negotiating with President Bush. It also demonstrates Sen. Sasser's horse-trading ability: To assemble a majority on his divided committee, he proposed no cuts in farm subsidies whatsoever.

The senior Republican on the budget panel, Sen. Pete Domenici of New Mexico, predicted flatly that Congress won't adopt the tax increases or spending cuts outlined in Sen. Sasser's plan except as part of a pact with Mr. Bush. "There is no way to get from where we are to where we have to be" without a so-called budget summit, he said.

Sen. Sasser said he hopes to bring the budget to the floor of the Senate within 10 days. There it is certain to run into fire from those who would cut defense spending more and domestic spending less, and from others, including influential Sen. Sam Nunn (D., Ga.), who would cut defense spending less.

The committee's budget essentially embraces all the tax increases in the president's budget -- and then some. It calls for $13.9 billion in tax increases plus $6.2 billion in unspecified user fees. The House has a similar tax number, but only $1.4 billion in fees.

For the Pentagon, the Senate plan calls for cutting $13 billion from projected outlays in fiscal 1991, which starts Oct. 1. That's more than the $11.5 billion in the budget narrowly approved in the House this week and far from the $3.2 billion in cuts in President Bush's budget, a figure the administration itself has all but abandoned.

Sen. Nunn, chairman of the Armed Services Committee, is recommending a $10 billion cut. He termed the budget committee's spending cuts "deeper than I believe can be justified by the changes in the threat" from abroad and said they would require sharp cutbacks in troop levels. But Sen. James Exon Jr. (D., Neb.), who described himself as Sen. Nunn's "right-hand man," voted with Sen. Sasser yesterday and said he will do so on the floor.

The Budget Committee also backed a sharp, $30 billion reduction from projected levels in defense spending authority -- essentially the sum to be deposited in the Pentagon's checkbook to be spent over several years. The committee backs $285.6 billion in spending authority, compared with $283 billion in the House budget. Sen. Nunn is calling for $289 billion. The Bush budget proposes $306.9 billion.

The Senate committee's plan would spend substantially less on domestic programs than would the president's budget, which Democrats have criticized harshly. It would cut $3.6 billion from projected spending on nondefense discretionary programs, a category that includes everything from foreign aid to housing assistance, compared with cuts of only $200 million in the Bush budget and an increase of $3.7 billion in the House budget.

Although the total is stated boldly in the plan, the specific cuts aren't. When pressed, Sen. Sasser said the list includes saving $300 million next year, and billions in the future, by terminating a giant atom smasher called the superconducting supercollider.

"There are no gimmicks here," Sen. Sasser asserted. "There's nothing up our sleeve."

But Sen. Domenici noted that Sen. Sasser's plan shows increased spending in almost every government function, and then lumps the proposed cuts together in one big catch-all category at the bottom. Calling the maneuver "ingenious," Sen. Domenici said, "Everyone can say . . . they voted to increase something."

The Senate committee's budget does call for spending more on entitlement programs, such as Medicare and federal retirement programs, than President Bush has proposed. While Mr. Bush would pare $5.5 billion from projected Medicare spending, the committee would cut $2 billion and the House $1.6 billion.

If the Senate backs the Budget Committee's proposal, then the two houses of Congress will attempt to reconcile their differences to come up with a guide for appropriations and tax-writing committees -- unless talks with the White House begin first.

In addition to the $43 billion deficit-reduction plan, the Senate Budget Committee resolution calls for raising $11.1 billion by waiving penalties for Americans who pay their back taxes and by intensifying tax-collection efforts. Sen. Sasser included the amnesty scheme to win support from its author, Sen. Kent Conrad (D., N.D.), and from Sen. Charles Robb (D., Va.), who added the figures together and said he was voting for a $54.1 billion plan.
