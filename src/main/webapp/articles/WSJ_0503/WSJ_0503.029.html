Honestly Now, They Can't Tell Us That They Didn't See It Coming

  • Author: Steven E. Levingston
  • Date: 05/03/90

Newspapers are giving horoscope readers some advice lately, much to the chagrin of astrologers. Taking a tip from a group of self-described skeptics, some 30 newspapers over the past few years have added disclaimers to their horoscope columns. Most sound like this tag found in the Morning Advocate of Baton Rouge, La.: "The horoscope, an entertainment feature, is not based on scientific fact."

That's an outrage to professional stargazers. Syndicated astrologer Sydney Omarr, himself once a wire service reporter, says the disclaimers are "so ridiculous it makes me ashamed of my background in journalism."

Horoscope labeling is the brainchild of Paul Kurtz, chairman of the Committee for the Scientific Investigation of Claims of the Paranormal, a Buffalo, N.Y., group that delights in debunking. In 1985 and again in 1988 -- inspired by Nancy Reagan's cosmic consultations -- the committee sent letters to every daily newspaper in America, urging that editors warn readers not to plan their lives around planet placement.

"If you examine the claims scientifically, astrology is shown to have no evidential support at all," says Mr. Kurtz, a philosopher at the State University of New York at Buffalo.

Agreeing, the St. Louis Post-Dispatch added a disclaimer a few years back. "I don't think we should encourage people they're getting the scoop on their future," says Jim Creighton, features director.

Many papers don't take horoscopes too seriously. Frederick G. Eaton, managing editor of the Daily Telegram in Adrian, Mich., (which runs a disclaimer) recalls how the paper once wasn't able to get the horoscope for a few weeks. Mr. Eaton simply filled the space with some months-old forecasts. No catastrophes were reported.

Astrologers wonder how in the world critics think they know so much. Joan Quigley, the former first lady's astrologer, contends that "astrology is scientific in this sense: It's based on astronomy, the same way a doctor bases his diagnosis on lab reports and the economist bases his predictions on computer-gathered statistics. Only I think astrology is more reliable than those two."

Mr. Omarr finds the disclaimer trend darkly menacing. "Once you permit this kind of thought control and prejudice," he says, "it will go over into other areas. . . . Tomorrow it'll be the bridge column, then the financial column."
