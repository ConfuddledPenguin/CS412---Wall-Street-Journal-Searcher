Marketing & Media: Turner, Act Media To Offer Programs In Supermarkets

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 05/03/90

NEW YORK -- Turner Broadcasting System Inc. is about to give supermarket shoppers something to do besides thumb through tabloids while standing in the checkout line. Turner's Cable News Network and Act Media Inc. are expected to announce today that they will begin offering satellite-delivered news and information programming in supermarkets throughout the U.S., people familiar with the deal said.

The plan is the latest in a series of recent moves by media concerns to find new ways to reach captive audiences in public or semipublic places, such as doctors' offices and schools. Whittle Communications, 50% owned by Time Warner Inc., has a number of print properties it distributes to doctors' offices and has launched an ad-supported TV news program in schools. CNN offers a competing service in schools with no advertising.

But the supermarket venture is expected to rely heavily on advertisers' support. The plan, reportedly dubbed "The Checkout Channel" during its development, is a partnership between CNN and Act Media, a wholly owned subsidiary of Dallas-based Heritage Media Corp. Act Media handles in-store marketing and promotions.

Turner and Act Media, which scheduled a news conference for today, declined to comment. They are expected to announce the number of stores that will participate and the details of plans to sell advertising on the programs.

Turner Broadcasting Chairman and President Ted Turner, speaking yesterday at an industry luncheon in New York, said he was "all for commercials, they pay the bills," although he was opposed to airing them on "CNN Classroom," the Atlanta-based company's 15-minute daily newscast to schools.

According to people familiar with the plan, Act Media will place satellite dishes on top of supermarkets, and programming supplied by CNN will be beamed to television sets placed near checkout lanes. The supermarkets will be supplied with the satellite dishes and television sets.

Act Media approached the three major networks, as well as other cable companies, according to network officials who didn't want to be identified. Network officials were said to be concerned that the supermarket environment might not be the optimum place to promote programming and sell advertising. CNN, by contrast, was said to be eager to exploit the captive audience.

Act delined to comment on any discussions with the broadcast networks.

An official at National Broadcasting Co., a unit of General Electric Co., said Act Media approached NBC Cable in January about the venture. He said the discussions focused on using programming from NBC's joint venture with Cablevision Systems Corp., the cable network Consumer News and Business Channel. "But the investment was enormous, upwards of millions of dollars," the official said, and NBC decided that the financial terms weren't beneficial and the only advantage might be promotional.

Act Media told broadcast networks that they could promote their prime-time programs on the channel, people familiar with the proposal said. But broadcasters were concerned that the TV shows would annoy cashiers, who might turn down the volume. "We were told that it would not be a problem," one network official said. "The TV sets would not have audio buttons."
