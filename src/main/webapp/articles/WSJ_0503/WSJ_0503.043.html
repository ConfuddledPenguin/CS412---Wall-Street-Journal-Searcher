OPEC Postpones Special Meeting On Cutting Output

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 05/03/90

GENEVA, Switzerland -- OPEC postponed the start of its emergency meeting here after informal talks revealed disagreement about how to achieve temporary cuts in oil production. The debate appeared to be over determining which members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries should carry the burden of reduced production this month and next, and the base from which cuts should be figured.

The delay so far isn't viewed as a serious threat to the generally accepted goal of the session, which is to prop up sagging world oil prices with a short-term production cutback.

Informal talks among the oil ministers continued late into the night, and several remained optimistic. "It's just a normal course of developments," Diego Tamariz, Ecuador's oil minister, said in an interview.

The formal session of OPEC's monitoring committee -- but with all 13 nations represented -- had been scheduled to begin yesterday afternoon, after the late arrival of Iran's oil minister. But as the day wore on, it was postponed several times. It has been rescheduled for today.

Several delegates said they still expect to keep the meeting brief. All want a fast agreement -- in two days at the most -- because they hope that will provide a "quick fix" to oil markets that have deteriorated because of OPEC's overproduction.

The ministers remain convinced that only a major short-term cut in production -- one million to two million barrels a day -- can support oil prices, which have fallen 25% so far this year.

However, by embracing that strategy, the ministers have put themselves in an unfamiliar position: Instead of squabbling over how to allocate a rise in production, as often occurs at their meetings, they are arguing about how to allocate a cut.

So far, according to individuals close to yesterday's informal deliberations, the debate over apportioning a cut turns on two main suggestions: that all member-nations reduce output by 5% to 10%; or that the Persian Gulf countries of Saudi Arabia, Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates, all currently producing over their quotas, bear the brunt of the reduction. If every country stuck to its quota, OPEC's total production would drop from the current 23.5 million barrels a day to 22 million, the group's official production ceiling for the first half of this year.

But Kuwait and the Emirates, chronic overproducers, are unlikely to go for strict adherence to quotas. By stubbornly ignoring its past production allotments, Kuwait has been able to force an increase in its official quota to a current 1.5 million barrels a day, and its recent output has ranged up to two million a day. The Emirates also have won special treatment in the past and are now producing two million barrels a day -- nearly double the formal quota.

Oil ministers previously decided they could safely ignore a certain amount of such "cheating," and factored some into their first-half ceiling. The reasoning was that because some other countries couldn't produce all they were officially permitted, net production would still only be around 22.6 million barrels a day. In view of rising demand for OPEC oil, they figured, prices would remain firm.

Now, some of the ministers said, OPEC needs to get back to its projected level. Output of 22 million barrels a day "will be very right," said Fawzi A. Shakshuki, Libya's oil minister. Others suggested cuts to a level even below the ceiling. Asked if he still wants a reduction of two million barrels a day, Issam Abdul Raheem Al-Chalabi, Iraq's oil minister, replied: "We are definitely looking for a cut."

Most outside experts here to track the meeting, including oil executives who are in Geneva to talk with some of the producers, seemed confident that the ministers will strike a bargain, simply because they must if they want to head off further price erosion. "If they walk out of here without an agreement, it's $2 to $4 down" a barrel for oil prices, predicted Oscar Wyatt, chairman of Coastal Corp. The market can't absorb continued high output right now, he explained. "Everybody's storage is full."
