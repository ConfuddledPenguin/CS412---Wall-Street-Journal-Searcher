THE TORIES BRACE THEMSELVES

In local elections across Britain today, Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher's Conservatives are bracing themselves for a drubbing from voters angered by the nation's new so-called poll tax.

National opinion surveys show the opposition Labor Party running up to 23 percentage points ahead of the Tories. Labor could gain an additional 600 local-council seats, giving the party majority control of 24 councils -- including, perhaps, the Tory flagship boroughs of Westminster and Wandsworth in London.

Besides serving as a kind of referendum on the poll tax, the elections also represent the last chance for voters to express their feelings before this fall, when Mrs. Thatcher might face another challenge to her leadership of the Conservative Party. A poor showing in today's elections would further weaken her and possibly strengthen the resolve of Tory politicians eager to succeed her.

One British bookmaking firm yesterday said there is a six-in-five chance that Mrs. Thatcher will leave office before the next general election, which must be called by mid-1992.

MORE STRIKES IN WEST GERMANY

About 68,000 members of West Germany's powerful IG Metall union staged a new round of so-called warning strikes to press their demands for higher pay and a shorter work week, union officials said. Some 200 companies were affected. The total is slightly above union claims of 65,000 workers participating in strikes last Monday and far above the 10 plants hit that day. Yesterday's strikes were concentrated in the North Wuerttemberg/North Baden district and in the state of North Rhine-Westphalia, two of the nation's major industrial centers.

EUROPE'S TRUCK POLLUTION

The European Community Commission proposed tougher emissions standards for trucks that would cut pollution from such vehicles by up to 60%. The plan would bring EC members into line with other European countries -- for example, Switzerland -- and the U.S. Carlo Ripa di Meana, the EC's environment commissioner, said an average of 50% to 60% of goods are carried by road in the community. Trucks can cause 10 times more pollution than other forms of transport, such as rail or ships, he added.

A COSTLY LONDON MUGGING

A London mugger walked away with almost $500 million in negotiable securities yesterday after holding up a messenger at knifepoint on a busy street. Stolen were certificates of deposit worth $200 million and Treasury bills worth $280 million. A Bank of England spokesman said that while the bearer certificates were negotiable, it would be difficult to cash them as the holder would "have to demonstrate that he has good title to the documents." Hand carrying is the usual way such securities are moved around London. However, later this year the Bank of England plans to replace messengers with an electronic clearing system.

STAKE IN AUSTRIAN AIRLINES

Japan's All Nippon Airways Co. increased its equity share in Austrian Airlines to 9% from 3.5%. All Nippon, which had purchased an initial lot of 77,000 shares a year ago, said it took up an additional 157,000 shares for about $65 million. Austrian Airlines offered a total of 400,000 new shares yesterday, aiming to promote privatization of the company, whose largest shareholder is the Austrian government. After the allocation of new shares, the government's holdings will decline to 51.4% from 61.4%.

SOVIET LABOR MOVEMENT

The first Soviet Congress of independent trade unions, in a challenge to the state labor movement, voted to form a council to unite the country's unofficial workers' groups. "The aim is to unite all forces and to guarantee the rights of working people in this difficult economic situation," said a spokesman for the congress, which closed yesterday. The congress also passed a resolution condemning the Soviet leadership for leading the economy to the brink of collapse.

GREECE WANTS CENTENARY GAMES

Greece has multibillion-dollar plans to rebuild much of Athens before 1996, the 100th anniversary of the modern Olympics, in hopes that the capital will be the site of the Games that year.

Among the major projects would be a new international airport, two underground-railway lines and the completion of two main roads.

Notwithstanding those possible attractions, Olympic organizers will need a lot of convincing: Greece is broke and emerging from a long political crisis; and Athens, one of the world's most polluted capitals, suffers from traffic congestion and a poor telephone system.

Greece hopes the emotional argument for staging the Olympics in the country where they originated 2,700 years ago will be too powerful to resist.
