Abreast of the Market: Industrial Average Climbs 20.72, Spurred by Federal Reserve Report

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 05/03/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices remained on the upswing as the Federal Reserve's latest report on the economy spurred buying. The Dow Jones Industrial Average posted its fifth double-digit gain in the last six sessions by climbing 20.72 to 2689.64. The latest gain was the sharpest over that period and brought the average's rise so far this week to 44.59 points, or 1.7%.

Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index went up 2.23 to 334.48 in the latest session, while the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index climbed 1.12 to 183.34.

Advancing stocks outnumbered decliners by 864 to 612 on the Big Board, where activity remained sluggish despite brisk business in the shares of American General, which put itself up for sale.

Big Board volume amounted to 141,610,000 shares, with American General accounting for close to 4% of the total. On Tuesday, 149,020,000 shares changed hands.

Prices rallied at midday following the release of the summary of economic conditions in the central bank's 12 districts, which said the economy continued to expand at a moderate pace last month with "no general upward pressure" on industrial prices.

"Slow growth and moderate inflation levels -- that's exactly what investors like to hear," said A.C. Moore, director of research at Argus Research Corp., who added that the Fed summary confirmed other evidence of improvement in the economy.

News that the index of leading indicators rose 0.9% and factory orders rose 3.8% in March was better than generally expected. On Tuesday, the April survey from the nation's purchasing managers signaled a pickup in the manufacturing sector for the first time since May 1989.

Stocks finished at session highs as the market received a boost just before the close from some program buying related to stock-index arbitrage, in which traders sell index-futures contracts and purchase baskets of stocks in order to lock in profits from differences in price.

However, the latest weekly survey of investment advisers by Investors Intelligence indicated that market sentiment and stock prices aren't moving in the same direction.

As of last Friday, 49.6% of the market letter writers surveyed had a bearish view on stocks, while 17.1% were calling for short-term price weakness. The totals were up from 46.5% and 14.9%, respectively, the previous week. Meanwhile, just 33.3% of the advisers were bullish, down from 38.6%.

Michael Burke, editor of the survey for Chartcraft Inc., New Rochelle, N.Y., said the sharp swing in sentiment stemmed largely from concerns about rising interest rates and poor market breadth, or the relationship between advancing and declining issues.

American General surged 7 1/2 to 47 5/8 in Big Board composite trading of more than 7.1 million shares in response to the company's decision to put itself up for sale, which came in spite of its claim of victory in a proxy fight with its hostile suitor, Torchmark. In addition, the company more than doubled its quarterly dividend to 80 cents a share.

Analysts and takeover-stock traders cited several foreign insurance companies as possible suitors, including France's Axa-Midi Assurances, Nationale-Nederlanden Group of the Netherlands and Japan's Nippon Life Insurance, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Also, Torchmark said it plans to make another offer. Its earlier bid was $50 a share.

Shares of several single-country mutual funds also recorded sharp gains. Taiwan Fund rallied 3 3/8 to 23 5/8, Korea Fund rose 2 1/2 to 19 1/2, Jakarta Growth Fund gained 1 1/4 to 13 3/8, Mexico Fund advanced 1 1/8 to 13 5/8, Germany Fund rose 1 1/8 to 14 1/4, New Germany Fund gained 1 3/8 to 14 1/8, and Future Germany Fund added 1 to 15 1/8.

Traders at the U.S. units of Japanese brokerage firms said Japanese investors, whose buying spurred sharp gains among shares of the country funds early in the year, remain active in them. "We don't seem to be doing too much else," said Thomas Callahan, senior vice president of U.S. equities at Yamaichi International (America).

Union Carbide fell 1/2 to 18 7/8 on 1.6 million shares. First Boston downgraded the stock to "hold" from "buy," based on the company's failure to restructure, and said it should trade at $16 a share, according to the investor report.

Eastman Kodak added 7/8 to 37 7/8. While the company reported that its first-quarter net income dropped 6% from the year-ago level, the latest results exceeded most market forecasts.

General Motors went up 1/2 to 44 3/4 on 1.6 million shares amid the release of first-quarter results by its major subsidiaries. GM's Class E shares, representing its Electronic Data Systems unit, gained 1/4 to 30 5/8. Its Class H shares, representing its Hughes Electronics unit, slid 1/8 to 22 1/4.

Ames Department Stores rose 3/8 to 2 7/8 on 2.6 million shares. A federal bankruptcy judge granted the company interim approval to obtain $25 million in financing and hire Stephen L. Pistner as its chief executive officer.

Rorer Group tumbled 5 1/4 to 64 1/2 in advance of a Friday deadline for tendering the stock to France's Rhone-Poulenc under its offer of $78 a share for a 50.1% stake, the first step in a merger between the two companies.

Halliburton advanced 7/8 to 44 1/8 after reporting that its first-quarter earnings from operations went up 192% from the year-earlier level. Schlumberger, another oil field-services concern, tacked on 3/4 to 51 1/2 on 1.3 million shares.

Control Data declined 1/2 to 18 3/8. The company said it isn't comfortable with analysts' 1990 earnings estimates "above the midportion" of a range of $1 to $1.50 a share.

Xerox dropped 1 1/4 to 46 3/4 amid talk that the company, which took a first-quarter charge of $375 million on its investment in VMS Realty Partners, may cut its dividend. The company denied the rumors.

Fleet/Norstar Financial Group dropped 1/2 to 18 3/4 on speculation that the bank holding company will report today that nonperforming loans will increase to $1 billion as of March 31 from $400 million at the end of 1989. The company earlier had estimated that nonperforming loans would increase to $600 million.

Smith Corona skidded 7/8 to 7. Merrill Lynch lowered the stock's long-term investment rating and reduced its earnings estimate for the fiscal year beginning July 1, according to the investor report.

Windmere rose 1 to 6 7/8. R. Jerry Falkner, director of research at Gulfstream Financial Associates, recommended the issue as "very oversold" in The Wall Street Journal's monthly stock-selection feature, "Investment Dartboard."

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.91 to 344.23. Volume totaled 11,710,000 shares compared with 10,640,000 Tuesday.

Ply-Gem Industries gained 1/4 to 7 1/2 after its board approved the repurchase of as many as 500,000 shares, or about 4.8% of its shares outstanding. Separately, the company posted a first-quarter loss compared with a year-ago profit.
