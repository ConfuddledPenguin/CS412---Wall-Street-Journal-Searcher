International: Brazil Lifts Price Controls On a Handful of Products

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/03/90

RIO DE JANEIRO -- The Brazilian government, in a limited sign of confidence that its tough anti-inflation program is working, lifted price controls on a handful of raw materials and industrial goods. The government also said it wouldn't authorize increases in May for products whose prices remain controlled.

In a related move, President Fernando Collor de Mello asked the government to speed up its studies on allowing imports of foreign cars and letting more foreign auto makers start up operations. The move is a step toward liberalizing the sheltered Brazilian market and a sign of displeasure at the domestic auto industry's production stoppages and high prices since the anti-inflation plan went into effect in March.

In announcing the lifting of price controls, the government didn't list specific products. Instead, it specified which products would remain frozen, implying that all others are now free. Prices that remain frozen include all basic foodstuffs, pharmaceuticals, cars, trucks, appliances, cigarettes, glass, cement, fertilizer, rents, school bills, and electricity, telephone and water rates.

Analysts said the step is a far cry from the near-total lifting of price controls the government envisaged only days ago. This indicates fretfulness that inflation is still a threat, despite government insistence that it has been snuffed out, a claim that many economists and unions contest. The government slapped on price controls on March 16 as part of its sweeping plan to rid Brazil of its runaway inflation, which exceeded 4,800% in the preceding 12 months. The plan's centerpiece is an 18-month freeze on about 70% of the money in circulation in the Brazilian economy.
