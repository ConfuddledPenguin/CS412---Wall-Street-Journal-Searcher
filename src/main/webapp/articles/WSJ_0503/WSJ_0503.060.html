Simon's Venture Sells a Major Stake In HonFed Bank, Hawaii's Biggest Thrift

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 05/03/90

William E. Simon's thrift venture sold a major stake in HonFed Bank, Hawaii's largest thrift, marking an unusual vote of confidence for the troubled industry from an unusual source. The investor, the Kamehameha Schools/Bishop Estate, a wealthy Hawaiian educational trust and landowner, agreed to invest $50 million in HonFed, which has assets of $3.2 billion.

Mr. Simon, Treasury Secretary from 1974 to 1977, and his partners paid $17 million to acquire control of the institution in 1986 as part of a government-supervised transaction.

HonFed has been profitable recently, but, like hundreds of thrifts around the country, it has been scrambling to raise funds to comply with new federal capital requirements.

At Dec. 31, HonFed had an estimated $24.5 million tangible capital deficit and needed about $72 million in new funds to meet federal regulations. The infusion is significant because it is believed to be the first successful capital-raising effort by an ongoing thrift since the new capital rules went into effect in December.

Still, the participation of what is commonly referred to as the Bishop Estate, established in 1884 by the last descendant of Hawaii's 19th century conquering King Kamehameha I, is a two-edged sword for Mr. Simon and his partners.

On the one hand, the money combined with proceeds from pending asset sales, may bring HonFed into compliance with the key federal tangible capital test as soon as June 30, when the now definitive agreement is expected to be completed, officials said. That also is expected be an impetus to HonFed's further growth, which has been complicated lately by its lack of capital.

On the other hand, the investment also will cut into the Simon group's own return on equity investment in HonFed, which last year reached a heady 47%. Terms of the Bishop Estate investment weren't disclosed, although officials said that the new equity investors won't have any voting rights.

The $50 million infusion and planned asset sales also replace a highly touted $100 million public preferred stock offering that HonFed proposed last fall and that officials said they would cancel. Some observers considered that development somewhat embarrassing to Mr. Simon and his investment bankers for the proposed offering, Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co.

Following his service at the Treasury, Mr. Simon became something of a Wall Street darling, amassing an estimated $200 million fortune from well-timed leveraged buy-outs of such concerns as Gibson Greeting Cards and other ventures.

Bert Ely, an Alexandria, Va., thrift analyst, said he figures that the terms of a private transaction such as that with Bishop Estate were less favorable to the Simon group than under a public offering. Nonetheless, he said that such private transactions probably would become the last best hope for capital-shy thrifts because of continuing public jitters about the sick industry.

"We believed that this transaction could be done more efficiently and with a very strong local partner that we knew," said Gerald Parsky, a Los Angeles lawyer and a partner in Mr. Simon's WSGP International, the Los Angeles-based merchant bank that serves as the parent company for the group's myriad financial ventures.

The Bishop Estate, for instance, invested in the Simon group's recent purchase and sale of First Interstate Bank of Hawaii, a transaction that allowed participants to pocket an estimated $20 million profit on a one-year investment of about $15 million.

In a statement, the estate's trustees called its latest venture "an excellent investment opportunity with attractive return potential." The Bishop Estate oversees a private school for students of Hawaiian ancestry and is the islands' largest private landowner with about 338,000 acres, equal to about 8% of the state.

Mr. Simon couldn't be reached for comment.

HonFed reported 1989 profit of $8 million, down from $10.2 million in 1988, although fourth-quarter earnings rose to $3.3 million from $1.2 million a year earlier. The institution has about doubled in size since the Simon group bought in, although last month it chose not to proceed with a planned acquisition of 18 Hawaiian branches from First Nationwide Bank because of capital concerns.

HonFed is just one of three major thrifts that the Simon group controls, and from a capital position, has been the worst off. The others include Western Federal Saving & Loan Association of Marina Del Rey, Calif., and Southern California Savings & Loan Association, Beverly Hills.

The $4.8 billion-asset Western Federal is generally healthy; Southern California Savings, with about $2.3 billion in assets, needs to raise about $35 million. Mr. Parsky said Southern California Savings should be in compliance late next year.

Despite thrift assets currently totaling $10.5 billion, the Simon group has been relatively quiet on the thrift-acquisition front in recent months. Some officials said the group remains committed to expanding itself, however, and sees increasing opportunities as the government steps up sales of insolvent thrifts.
