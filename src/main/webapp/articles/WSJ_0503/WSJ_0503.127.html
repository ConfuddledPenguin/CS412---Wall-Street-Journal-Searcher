Heard on the Street: Fibreboard Proposal to Boost Stock Options, Rights for Company Insiders Raises Tempest

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 05/03/90

NEW YORK -- Expect sparks to fly at Monday's annual meeting of Fibreboard. The tiny wood-products and insulation company has a market value of not quite $40 million, but shareholders are raising a bigtime stink over a board-approved plan to double the amount of options, stock awards and stock-appreciation rights going to company insiders.

When Fibreboard was spun off in 1988 from Louisiana Pacific, its board approved a stock option and rights plan covering 350,000 shares, equivalent to 9% of its 3.9 million shares outstanding. Now it wants to amend that plan by doubling the number to 700,000, or 18% of the shares currently outstanding -- subject to a vote of shareholders.

The meeting will be held in Roxboro, N.C., the site of a small Fibreboard plant that is 2,500 miles from the company's home base in Concord, Calif.

"Management and directors are feasting on the value of the company," says Jery Chambers, a Buffalo, N.Y., investment manager with Courier Capital whose clients own 170,000 shares. "We're going down to scream and yell."

Lawrence Hart, the chairman and the sole management director, says, "The idea is to give an equity stake in the company so our own personal finances are very tied in with that of the company."

However, Fibreboard's managers and directors haven't invested more than a pittance of their own in Fibreboard. Its executive officers and directors had purchased only 7,062 shares, or 0.18% of the company, as of the March 29 proxy statement. Mr. Hart says the total has risen since then, but he won't say by how much.

Graef Crystal, a compensation specialist and professor at the Haas School of Business at the University of California at Berkeley, says "the percentage of shares {in Fibreboard's plan} is awesomely large. The rule of thumb is 5% of the shares outstanding."

Mr. Hart responds that Fibreboard is an unusual case and that compensation is adequate and comparable to that of other "high-risk, start-up companies."

Fibreboard's lumber operations actually are longstanding; it isn't a start-up venture in the sense that a high-tech firm is. But the company does entail plenty of risk.

The company, a former asbestos-products manufacturer, lives under a cloud of litigation. However, a trial court has ruled that Fibreboard's insurers are liable for at least the bulk of anticipated claims. The bottom line is that while Fibreboard isn't yet out of the woods, the asbestos cloud appears to be clearing.

On the positive side, the company owns 117,000 acres of timber that have a book value of $30 million but could be worth $140 million. Last year, it sold 27,500 acres of relatively lower quality timber and fetched almost $1,200 an acre. It also owns a ski resort on the northern side of Lake Tahoe, Calif., which is on the market and could be worth double its $19 million book value.

Those values won't be realized overnight, but they suggest that if the asbestos cloud does abate, Fibreboard's stock, currently trading at 9 7/8, could become much more valuable.

Donald Miller, managing partner of Greylock Financial Partnership, who says he owns 62,000 shares, says the options pave the way for management and directors to reap a small fortune. And, Mr. Miller notes, the options are being proposed at a time when the stock is depressed, down from last year's high of 19 1/4, and trading at 25% of stated book value, which is $36 a share.

That means the exercise price will be cheaper. Some of the shares, however, are in the form of restricted stock awards -- stock that is given away free to managers who hang on for three years.

Such awards, Mr. Crystal says, require of managers that they "breathe in and out on the company payroll." Mr. Hart, who has so far been awarded 35,000 in restricted share rights, says "this gives me an incentive to perform" well enough to stay employed. He also says recruiting managers and directors for an asbestos-plagued company is hard, and compensation levels therefore are justified. Mr. Hart, incidentally, received a 118% increase in salary and bonus last year to $454,000; he also was given a golden parachute plan.

Mr. Miller says there is a larger problem of "corporate governance" at Fibreboard. Fibreboard's four outside directors, who sit on the compensation committee, each receive options on at least 2,000 shares and a $10,000 stipend annually. Mr. Miller questions whether this influences their opinion of Fibreboard's option plan.

Several directors couldn't be reached or wouldn't comment.

E. Judge Elderkin, a Fibreboard director and a partner in a San Francisco law firm that represents Fibreboard, is on the board's compensation committee. He says through a spokeswoman that it is inappropriate for him to respond and refers questions to Mr. Hart.
