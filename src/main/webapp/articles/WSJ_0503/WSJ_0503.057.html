Wednesday's Markets: Late Rally Gives Stocks Modest Gain --- Bond Prices Stabilize; Dollar Ends Mixed, Rising Against Mark

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 05/03/90

Stock prices continued their muted rebound while bond prices stabilized. A late rally helped push the Dow Jones Industrial Average up 20.72 points to 2689.64 in relatively light trading. Long-term bond prices were unchanged and the dollar gained modestly against the mark while slipping against the yen.

Analysts said the stock market's gain, the third in as many days, came from bargain-hunting and the perception that interest rates may be nearing a peak. The most optimistic analysts contend that inflation fears have been exaggerated and that interest rates may soon ease. The Federal Reserve's "beige book," a summary of economic conditions around the nation, tended to support that view yesterday, citing modest economic growth and "no general upward pressure" on industrial prices.

But traders said investors shouldn't read too much into the stock market's performance so far this week. Trading activity has been modest at best, they say, and this week's gains may be nothing more than a temporary bounce from last week's big drop, which saw the Dow industrials lose more than 50 points.

Still, bond traders have been impressed that prices for long-term Treasurys haven't dropped precipitously this week given other economic news that shows the economy picking up steam. They point especially to Tuesday's survey of the nation's purchasing managers, which showed a resumption in economic growth in the industrial sector after nearly a year of contraction. And they're looking to Friday's government report on April employment levels for further clarification about the economy's health.

At the same time, the bond market is facing a potential problem in the form of next week's big quarterly Treasury refunding auction. Yesterday the Treasury said it plans to sell $10.5 billion of three-year notes, and $10 billion each of 10-year notes and 30-year bonds. That's about in line with what many traders expected.

But the question is what role Japanese investors will play. In previous auctions they have been major buyers, but interest rates in Japan have reached competitive levels with U.S. rates. If big Japanese buyers decide to focus more of their purchases in Japan, where they don't face any currency risks, the yield on U.S. bonds could rise sharply from current levels. In major market action:

Stock prices climbed in light trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 141.6 million shares. Advancing issues on the Big Board outnumbered declining issues 864 to 612.

Bond prices were virtually unchanged. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year issue stood at 9.01%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading the currency was quoted at 1.6848 marks and 157.95 yen, compared with 1.6808 marks and 158.83 yen Tuesday.
