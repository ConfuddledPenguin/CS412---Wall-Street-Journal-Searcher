Economy: Leading Index Of Economy Rose in March --- Gauge's 0.9% Gain Follows 1% Decline in February; Factory Orders Increase

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 05/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The government's gauge of expected economic strength rose in March, suggesting the economy will maintain its recent pattern of mild growth. The 0.9% gain in the Commerce Department's index of leading economic indicators was the biggest increase since a 1.6% jump in June 1988. But it didn't make up for a 1% drop in February, and it left the index only 0.3% higher than it was a year earlier.

Economic analysts said the figures allay fears of a recession, but don't point to robust growth. On a more worrisome note, the report offers a bit more evidence to bolster recent reports that inflation may accelerate.

Most of the March increase in the indicators, which tend to foreshadow broad movements in the economy, came in two areas: plant and equipment orders, which were influenced by a big increase in aircraft bookings, and prices of commodities and raw materials.

"There's no sign of a recession but not a lot of signs of strong growth either," said David Wyss, chief financial economist at DRI/McGraw Hill, an economic forecasting concern in Lexington, Mass. The increase in raw material prices, Mr. Wyss added, "may be good for the indicators but it's not good for the economy. It suggests higher prices for goods and more inflation which could have to be choked off" by a rise in interest rates engineered by the Federal Reserve Board.

Separately, the Commerce Department said factory orders, also boosted by the spike in aircraft orders, rose 3.8% in March, to $240.37 billion, after a 1.8% increase in February. The increases almost offset the 5.5% January decline.

Orders for durable goods rose 6.6% to $128.70 billion, while orders for nondurables increased 0.8% to $111.67 billion. An earlier report on durable goods, which are intended to last at least three years, showed a 6.7% increase.

"The manufacturing sector is beginning to recover, but it's not yet out of the woods," said Edward Campbell, senior economist at Brown Brothers Harriman & Co.

Of the 11 statistics that make up the leading indicators index six contributed to the March increase. In addition to plant and equipment orders and raw material prices, they were: new orders for consumer goods, stock prices, deliveries by factories to suppliers, and a drop in claims for unemployment benefits.

Holding the index back in March were declines in building permits, factory backlogs and the money supply. An index of consumer expectations and the average workweek were unchanged. Analysts attribute the drop in building permits to the housing industry's slump, induced by higher interest rates.

"Within the composition we see a housing market that is quite weak and a business sector which is beginning to show signs of bottoming out," said Mr. Campbell. "I have been positive on the economy and this reinforces my positive bent. But it's still a mixed economy."

The leading indicators have risen in four of the past six months, and seven of the past nine, Mr. Wyss noted. However, four of the increases in the past nine months were a mere 0.1%.

Both reports are adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
