International: East, West Germany Reach Accord On Plan for Merging of Economies

  • Author: Timothy Aeppel
  • Date: 05/03/90

BONN -- Sticking close to an outline spelled out by Bonn, the two Germanys agreed to a plan for merging their economies by this summer. Besides setting terms for introducing the powerful West German mark into the East on July 2, the agreement gives the East German government a badly needed political boost on the eve of that nation's local elections, set for Sunday. Both sides wanted to hash out a deal in time for the vote, to give East Germans confidence that the costs of unity won't fall unfairly onto their shoulders.

But while East and West German officials touted the agreement as a solid compromise, it leaves many details unclear and differs little from the opening offer made by Bonn last week.

The plan calls for exchanging East German wages, salaries, and pensions at a favorable 1-to-1 rate, but limits the amount of personal savings to be swapped on par. In accord with Bonn's original proposal, most East Germans will be allowed to exchange 4,000 marks of savings at the 1-to-1 rate ($2,380.80). But East Germans 60 years and older will be allowed to exchange 6,000 marks, while children 14 and under will be limited to 2,000 marks. All other savings will be exchanged 2-to-1.

West German officials say 60% of East German savings accounts have less than 5,000 marks and that this deal would allow families to split their savings among family members for maximum effect.

The special terms for the elderly are meant to signal the West's willingness to cushion the effects of the monetary union, but only in the case of those who really need help. East German pensions are about a third of the level of those in the West, and many East German retirees rely heavily on their savings to survive. Besides offering the higher threshold for the 1-to-1 exchange, the agreement specifies that the East will adopt the West German system of calculating pensions, a move that will lead to a hike in the base level of many pensions as well.

But while both sides agreed on the need to protect the elderly, working out a general exchange rate proved more difficult. The East Germans initially balked at a 4,000-mark limit on the 1-to-1 exchange, arguing that this would make them the paupers in a future united Germany. And in the end, the rate was determined more by political sentiments than by sound economic reasoning.

"East Germans have the idea that their work is just as good as that of their West German cousins, and politically, it's impossible to contradict that," says an official in Bonn's Economic Ministry. "But it simply isn't true."

Productivity levels in the East are only one-third of those in the West, for instance, and many East German factories are hopelessly outmoded.

Moreover, the East German mark is worth only a fraction of the West German mark, so any exchange rate even approaching 1-to-1 is considered extremely generous. Black market traders in East and West Berlin typically exchange the money at a rate of 6-to-1. Yesterday's agreement sets a new official exchange rate for East German marks of 2-to-1. Since the start of this year, the official rate has been 3-to-1.

The agreement, which sets the outlines for a state treaty, differs only slightly from what the West German government put forward as its opening negotiating position. In recent days, West German officials acknowledged that their offer was near the limits of what they could afford without endangering the stability of the mark.

These factors, together with the East Germans' own interest in striking a deal that serves their long-term interests, made it easier to accept the plan.

To help ease the transition, the two sides have agreed to exchange East German company debts -- estimated at 260 billion marks -- at a rate of 2-to-1. Exchanging debts 1-to-1 would have forced most East German firms out of business. As it is, officials on both sides estimate that at least one-third of all East German companies will quickly go bankrupt and unemployment could soon reach one million.

The agreement also will allow rents to be exchanged at the 2-to-1 rate and will give East Germans the chance to buy shares in state-owned enterprises.

The West Germans emphasized that the plan was a conservative solution, a politically necessary stance. The powerful West German Bundesbank -- which initially groused that an overly generous deal would endanger the mark -- has cautiously signed on to the deal, but mainly because it saw few options.

The Bundesbank has cautioned that monetary and economic union, if not carefully structured, could boost West German inflation and interest rates. In keeping with its role as defender of a powerful mark, the bank originally put forward its own plan, which called for an exchange rate closer to 2-to-1.

Some financial analysts note, however, that the final agreement is closer to the Bundesbank's original blueprint than many might think.
