Technology: Commodore Reports Earnings of $200,000 For Its Third Quarter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/03/90

WEST CHESTER, Pa. -- Commodore International Ltd. said heavy marketing efforts essentially wiped out profit during its fiscal third quarter. Still, the battered personal computer maker showed some signs of life. Commodore reported a profit of $200,000, or one cent a share, in its fiscal 1990 third period, which ended March 31. That's a 98% drop from a $12.4 million profit, or 38 cents a share, in the year-earlier period. Sales eked out a 1.4% gain, rising to $213.2 million from $210.2 million.

Most of the decline in profit was due to "substantial marketing investments" in the company's stronghold market, Europe, which drove up operating expenses 28%, to $62.5 million from $48.8 million, Commodore said.

But the company noted that sales of its Amiga computer and a group of machines compatible with International Business Machines Corp.'s PC-AT computer increased "substantially" during the period, more than offsetting a decline in the ancient Commodore 64 family. Indeed, Commodore said the Amiga accounted for almost 55% of its sales during the period. That number should rise again during the coming months, as Commodore rolls out the Amiga 3000, a much-hyped machine aimed at the growing "multimedia" market.

"There's some reason for optimism," said Charles Frumberg, an analyst with Mabon, Nugent & Co. in New York. "My sense is that things are turning up." But Mr. Frumberg added that "it's clear that the ball won't be going over the fence in fiscal 1990."

For the first nine months of fiscal 1990, Commodore said profit fell 92% to $5 million, or 16 cents a share, from $60.2 million, or $1.88 a share, in the year-earlier period. Sales dropped 9% to $689.2 million from $759.4 million.
