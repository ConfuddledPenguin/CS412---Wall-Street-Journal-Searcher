Technology & Medicine: Simulated Sunlight Can Reset Body Clocks, Test Shows

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 05/03/90

Exposing night-shift workers to an artificial "midnight sun" can reset their biological clocks, thereby increasing their performance, an experiment in Boston indicated. The experiment, carried out at the Brigham and Women's Hospital, is the latest effort by scientists to manipulate the rhythms of the human body that are synchronized with the Earth's 24-hour day-night cycle. These natural rhythms, the products of millions of years of evolution, are disrupted when people work at night and try to sleep in the daytime. The result is insomnia in the day and decreased alertness during night work accompanied by a drop in performance and an increase in fatigue-related accidents.

The new experiment found that the biological rhythms of volunteers rapidly adapted to night work if the volunteers worked the midnight shift under simulated sunlight rather than the dimmer light of conventional indoor lighting.

The new experiment, reported in this week's New England Journal of Medicine, was carried out by Charles A. Czeisler and his colleagues at the Center for Circadian and Sleep Disorders Medicine at the Brigham and Women's Hospital, which is affiliated with the Harvard Medical School. Dr. Czeisler also heads a nonprofit organization called the Center for Design of Industrial Schedules, which funded part of the research.

Dr. Czeisler and his colleagues have developed a theory that the body's "clock" that determines the sleep-wake cycle is set each day by a bright light, presumably the rising sun. Last year they reported they could change the "time" on this internal clock by as much as 12 hours by the proper timing of exposure to bright light.

A night worker runs into problems because, after working under indoor lights during the night, he or she goes home and at some point during the day, perhaps upon leaving work, the worker is suddenly exposed to the brightness of the sun, the researchers explained. This confuses the biological clock, and the worker's body never fully adapts to night work.

In their latest experiment, the Boston scientists determined the normal 24-hour biological rhythms of eight healthy young men by monitoring their body temperature, heart rate and the production of several body chemicals during a week of regular bedtimes and wake-up times. During this baseline period, the body temperature usually dropped to its lowest point at about 5 a.m., or about two hours and 40 minutes before the men woke up.

For the next four weeks, the men reported to the laboratory each night shortly before midnight and spent the next eight hours working at a desk under a bank of lights simulating the brightness of the early morning sun. They then went home and slept in a totally darkened room from 9 a.m. until 5 p.m.

By the sixth night of the experiment, the men's biological clocks had changed "time." Their body temperatures reached their lowest points 9.6 hours later than normal. The cycle for the output of various body chemicals also shifted. Tests also indicated that the daily ups and downs in alertness and mental performance changed to later times of the 24-hour day.

By contrast, when the volunteers spent the night working under ordinary indoor lights, there was little change in the biological rhythms, and the rhythms even tended to move back an hour or so rather than forward.

"Although this study is a step toward the development of a practical treatment for maladaptation to nighttime work, a number of important questions remain to be answered," the scientists said. For instance, the most effective timing and duration of the light exposures still have to be determined, they explained.
