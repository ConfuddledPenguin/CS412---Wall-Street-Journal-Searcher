World Markets: Tokyo Share Prices Gain, Reflecting Advances By Domestic Demand-Linked Issues, Traders Say

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/03/90

Tokyo stock prices finished higher, boosted by the pace of strong advances by domestic demand-related stocks such as steels and shipbuildings, traders said. In London, share prices finished higher in light trading, largely led by strength in stock-index futures and mark-ups that supported the underlying shares.

Tokyo's Nikkei index of 225 issues was up 483.81 points at 30173.64, its high for the day. The most recent time the index closed above 30000 points was April 9.

The index gained 105.03 points Tuesday.

Volume remained thin, as most investors avoided largely increasing their positions ahead of holidays in Japan today and tomorrow, traders said.

An estimated 480 million shares changed hands on the first section, improving from 245 million shares Tuesday. Advancers widely outnumbered decliners 773 to 196, while 130 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, also boasted strong performances Wednesday, rising 33.03 points, or 1.49%, to 2247.81. The index gained 8.82 points Tuesday.

The second section index, which added 16.19 points Tuesday, jumped 32.82 points, or 0.93%, to close at 3579.96. Volume was estimated at 10 million shares, up from seven million shares Tuesday.

Almost all sectors finished with gains yesterday as expectations for future advances were fueled by the yen's stability, participants said.

But brokers who had been trying to boost trading in May by establishing electronics shares as a trade focus ended the day in uncertainty, reflecting a rumor spread during the afternoon session.

The rumor, which was said to have been started by a foreign brokerage house, said that TDK's earnings predictions for the fiscal year may be lowered. Share prices of TDK and related companies such as Sony and Pioneer fell sharply during the last hour before bouncing back halfway toward the end of the day.

The market closed without confirming the information on TDK, as the company has been closed all week, extending this week's national holidays.

Shigeru Akiba, head of equity trade at UBS Phillips & Drew International, said that if the rumor is true, many securities companies would have to revise their strategy of promoting the electronics sector as a market leader.

A senior trader at Wako Securities said the market must wait until next week to weigh the impact of the rumor. But yesterday's sell-off of high-priced, high-tech shares didn't involve general investors, and was largely done by dealers who were trading for their own accounts, he noted.

Because dealers had bought these stocks Tuesday, they reduced their holdings ahead of the holidays today and tomorrow, the trader said.

Some participants predicted that stock prices will rise in May, with the Nikkei index approaching 32000 points.

Helped by the yen's stability, investors are gradually regaining confidence on the stock market, despite growing expectations for higher interest rates in the U.S. and West Germany, said Kimitoshi Hasegawa, deputy vice president supervising separate account management at Dai-Ichi Mutual Life Co.

Participants were also encouraged by favorable technical news that the Nikkei index at Tuesday's finish climbed above its 25-day moving average line for the first time this year, indicating that stock prices have turned technically upward from their continuous downward trend since the beginning of this year, traders said.

Winners were spread among large-capital shares such as steel and ship-building companies that rebounded from their Tuesday losses. Construction companies and other public-spending related shares, such as road builders, were also higher.

Sumitomo Metal was up 14 yen at 604 yen (nine cents to $3.80), while Kobe Steel gained 19 yen to 595 yen. Kawasaki Heavy Industries advanced 13 yen to 848 yen and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries rose 16 yen to 939 yen.

TDK finished at 6900 yen, down 160 yen, and Sony was down 130 yen at 8520 yen. But many other electronics shares ended the day firmer or unchanged.

Separately, the Tokyo Stock Exchange said that in the week ended Friday, the balance of margin buying declined 158.8 billion yen to 7.585 trillion yen. The balance of short positions outstanding fell 100.9 billion yen to 612.7 billion yen.

In London, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 share index closed up 19.7 points at 2137.6. The index reached its intraday high of 2145.1 around midafternoon and posted its low for the day of 2121.7 at the start of official trading.

The Financial Times 30 share index ended up 14.5 points at 1678. Volume was 433 million shares, modestly better than the 370.8 million a day earlier.

Several diverse factors, including early firmness on Wall Street, a flurry of bullish corporate results and a shortage of stock helped push the market up, dealers said.

Caution about the U.K.'s economic and political situation -- currently focused on today's scheduled municipal elections, in which Margaret Thatcher's Tory Party is forecast to take a drubbing -- is damping new investor enthusiasm, traders said.

Dealers said the technical bounce that gripped the market at the start of the week has probably run its course. They predicted that the market would most likely come off its current levels shortly and possibly even test the technical support level of 2050.

To stimulate fresh position-taking, "what you need is a little bit of corporate activity" such as concrete takeover bids, one trader said.

Bidding speculation buoyed the insurance sector, as expectations that French group Axa-Midi might try for a U.K. composite insurer following the collapse last week of Axa-Midi's efforts to buy B.A.T's U.S. insurance unit, Farmers Group.

Commercial Union, regarded the most likely candidate for a takeover bid by the French insurer, shot up 11 pence to 455 pence; elsewhere in the sector, General Accident rose 5 pence to #10.13, and Royal Insurance climbed 2 pence to 429 pence.

Favorable results by the Royal Bank of Scotland bolstered banking shares, which have been depressed since Midland Bank released a profit warning last week. Pretax profit for the RBS's first half ending March 31 was up 1.5%, beating analysts' expectations and pushing the share price up 10 pence to 169 pence.

Elsewhere in Europe and South Africa, prices closed higher in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, and Stockholm; and were mixed in Amsterdam and Milan. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Meanwhile, prices closed higher in Sydney, Hong Kong, Manila and Wellington; lower in Taipei; and mixed in Singapore. The Seoul market was closed for a holiday.
