Abreast of the Market: Industrials Decline 5.94 Points; Banking Shares Take a Beating

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 08/02/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks finished only slightly lower, despite a big rout in bank shares. The Dow Jones Industrial Average eased 5.94 to 2899.26. New York Stock Exchange volume surged to 176,810,000 shares, the heaviest since July 24, when 181.9 million shares traded.

Several bank and savings and loan stocks were taken down in aggressive selling yesterday. Traders said the bad news has been mounting, and yesterday the selling intensified in dozens of shares. Among the big losers: Chemical Bank fell 1 to 22; Citicorp slipped 5/8 to 19 3/8; Wells Fargo tumbled 2 5/8 to 65 7/8 and Security Pacific slid 1 to 30 5/8.

A page one article in this newspaper yesterday described how many of the traditional profit centers of big banks are eroding.

Savings and loan associations weren't spared. HomeFed fell 1 1/8 to 10; H.F. Ahmanson dropped 3/4 to 19 and Great Western Financial slipped 1/8 to 15 3/8. "Investors are worried about California real estate," said Nancy Spady, analyst at Morgan Stanley. "Unfortunately, I don't think people are differentiating between commercial and residential lenders." She said she maintains a "buy" recommendation on Great Western Financial and Ahmanson because of their focus on residential real estate lending. She added, "HomeFed's credit-quality problems are linked primarily to multifamily and commercial real estate outside of California."

Traders said the market was full of crosscurrents yesterday. The National Association of Purchasing Management said its July index of business conditions fell to 47.4% from 51.1% in June. The decline ended three consecutive months of increases. A reading below 50 indicates that the manufacturing segment of the economy is generally declining; above 50, that it is generally expanding.

While this weak economic data gave a boost to bond prices, stocks didn't fall significantly. Investors continue to look beyond lagging economic indicators to hopes for stronger corporate profits in the months ahead. Their enthusiasm for earnings strength could be seen in Eastman Kodak, which jumped 4 1/4, or 11%, to 42 3/8, making the stock the largest gainer on the Big Board yesterday.

Kodak earned $1.19 in the second quarter, up from 24 cents the previous year; the results beat the consensus by 20 cents. Many analysts raised their full-year estimates after the report.

Earnings growth is still the dominant theme in the stock market, but investors are searching for stocks in which they can buy growth cheaply.

"I sense a growing nervousness and bearishness," said J. David Mills, portfolio manager at Boston Co. Advisers. He said growth-stock investors are losing confidence that their stocks will continue to be big winners in the second half. "The ability to forecast earnings in this economy is tough," he added.

So while high-profile growers, such as Procter & Gamble, which fell 1 1/4 yesterday to 85 1/2, get notched down day by day, other stocks such as Fund American -- whose earnings outlook is brightening after a five-year spate of stagnant profits -- are turning up. Fund American gained 1 5/8 to 38 1/8 yesterday.

Still, in a slowing economy, big multinational companies are paying out more earnings in dividends, which attracts many investors. Arnold Kaufman, editor of Standard & Poor's "Outlook" newsletter, noted that dividend increases on an S&P universe of some 7,500 stocks this year may be the lowest since 1971, the year President Nixon mandated a freeze on corporate dividends. But while the dividend outlook on S&P's broad universe of stocks is sickly, dividends paid by the 500 S&P companies are expected to grow about 8% this year.

Through July, only 825 companies have increased dividends, down from 1,037 a year earlier. Dividend cuts rose 36.5% for the same period and omissions increased 88.5%, S&P said.

"The weakness in dividends is a direct reflection of poor corporate profits, and it also reflects the increase in corporate leverage," said Mr. Kaufman.

Meanwhile, the recent rally in oil stocks continued as September crude-oil futures rose well above $21 a barrel -- the price target set by OPEC at its meeting last weekin New York trading. Exxon added 1/4 to 52 1/8 on two million shares, Mobil rose 1/8 to 65 3/8, Amoco gained 3/8 to 56 and Atlantic Richfield went up 1 1/8 to 130 1/2.

Utility issues remained on the upswing as well; the Dow Jones Utility Average posted its seventh consecutive advance by rising 2.89 to 212.90. Detroit Edison climbed 7/8 to 29 5/8, Houston Industries gained 3/4 to 34 1/2, Public Service Enterprise Group rose 5/8 to 26 1/8 and Scana added 3/8 to 33 3/4.

Another traditional "safe haven" for investment funds, the precious-metals sector, also fared well. Battle Mountain Gold added 1/2 to 11 7/8, Homestake Mining rose 1/2 to 19 7/8, Placer Dome gained 1/2 to 17 3/8 and ASA Ltd. rallied 1 3/8 to 48 3/4.

However, American Barrick Resources fell 1 1/8 to 21 after saying it doesn't expect the current estimate of gold reserves at a property in Nevada to be "expanded significantly" as a result of continued exploration drilling there. Results to date indicate the site holds about five million ounces of gold, the company said.

UAL fell 4 1/8 to 135 7/8 amid further uncertainty about whether a union-led group would succeed in obtaining financing for its $4.38 billion buy-out bid. Three of the five banks that have been working with the group -- Citicorp, Chase and Chemical -- now have withdrawn from financing talks.

Home Depot gained 2 3/8 to 36 5/8 on 1.3 million shares. Prudential-Bache Securities repeated its buy recommendation on the stock; analyst L. Wayne Hood called Home Depot his "single best idea" in the retailing group. Also, the company said it hasn't experienced a slowdown in business during the current fiscal quarter.

Promus went down 2 3/4 to 23 1/8. C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell lowered the stock's investment rating to "sell" from "buy."

Loews, which posted a 41% drop in its second-quarter earnings, fell 4 1/2 to 99 3/4.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 0.30 to 353.30. Volume totaled 10,880,000 shares.

Stevens Graphics Series A dropped 3 5/8 to 10 1/8. James A. Cole resigned as the company's chief financial officer and was succeeded by David H. Skinner, who previously held that position at Sili-Tex.

Cubic rose 1 1/8 to 19 1/2. The company reported that net income for the June quarter climbed 23% from a year earlier.

Timberland Class A added 3/4 to 10 1/2 after the company reported a profit for the second quarter, compared with a year-ago loss.
