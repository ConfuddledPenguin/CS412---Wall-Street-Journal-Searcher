Credit Markets: Bonds Rally on Purchasing Managers' Report That Suggests Weakening in Industrial Sector

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Silvia Ascarelli
  • Date: 08/02/90

NEW YORK -- Evidence that the nation's industrial core is contracting may be bad news for the economy, but it invigorated the bond market. The National Association of Purchasing Management said yesterday that its index of business conditions declined to 47.4% in July, down from 51.1% in June. The index is designed so that a reading below 50% should indicate that the manufacturing segment of the economy is declining and a reading above 50% should suggest that manufacturing is expanding.

The NAPM report "got the market off to the races," said Steve Ricchuito, chief economist at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Securities Inc. He said the NAPM report, which followed a string of anemic economic indicators, fueled speculation that the economy is in trouble and that the Federal Reserve may eventually come to the rescue by cutting short-term interest rates.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose more than 5/8 point yesterday, which translates into gains of about $6.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The yield on the benchmark bond, which moves in the opposite direction of prices, fell to 8.35%.

Some analysts were surprised that bonds held their gains considering oil prices spiked higher. Often, rising oil prices rekindle inflation fears, which hurts bonds. But some traders explained that bond investors view the rise in oil prices as temporary.

"People looked beyond {yesterday's} price move," said Michael Moran, manager of government trading at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. He said that investors believe the forces pressuring oil prices, "will go away eventually." Prices for oil, as well as food and several other commodity prices, are extremely volatile.

Still, Mr. Moran suggests that oil prices could become a negative factor for the bond market if prices continue to rise, and especially if "some of the oil uptick finds its way into," the producer price index report. The PPI, due to be released next week, is a measure of inflation on the wholesale level.

The bond market didn't blink at news the Treasury intends to sell $32.25 billion of securities next week at its quarterly refunding of the government debt. Many analysts were expecting the refunding to total about $31.5 billion.

But given expectations of a weakening economy, lower inflation and declining interest rates, more supply was looked at by many participants as "an opportunity to buy" bonds said Samuel Kahan, chief financial economist at Fuji Securities Inc., Chicago.

The refunding will comprise a record $11.5 billion of three-year notes scheduled for sale next Tuesday; $10.5 billion of 10-year notes scheduled for sale next Wednesday and $10.25 billion of 30-year bonds to be sold next Thursday.

The Treasury said the auctions would be contingent on legislation raising the debt ceiling, which injects a note of uncertainty that could push rates higher or keep them from falling much further.

When-issued trading will not begin until a debt ceiling bill is passed, which means dealers will be less certain of investor demand for the securities they are underwriting.

In its quarterly refunding news release, the Treasury also projected a massive net market borrowing requirement of between $65 billion to $70 billion during the fourth quarter. That will be a record amount of borrowing.

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's announcement that it will sell a record $32.25 billion of notes and bonds at next week's quarterly refunding of the federal debt didn't move bond prices an inch.

The benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 104 8/32, up from 103 19/32. The yield eased to 8.35% from 8.41%. The latest two-year notes ended at 100 9/32, up from 100 7/32. The yield on the two-year notes fell to 7.83% from 7.86%.

Short-term rates moved moderately lower yesterday. The discount rate on three-month bills fell to 7.43% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.66%, while the rate on six-month bills fell to 7.27% to yield 7.63%.

Corporate, Junk Bonds

Corporate bond investors put aside memories of the Seabrook nuclear power plant's troubled history and eagerly snapped up $211.8 million of secured lease obligation bonds backed by the plant, which is now operating.

United Illuminating Co., a major shareholder in the New Hampshire nuclear power plant, indirectly issued $82.77 million of 16-year bonds priced at par to yield 9.76% and $129.06 million of 30-year bonds priced at par to yield 10.24%.

The transaction was structured as follows: The Seabrook I bonds are considered obligations of United Illuminating. The bonds were issued by Meridian Bank, the owner-trustee in the transaction, which was acting on behalf of a unit of Citicorp's Citibank unit, which invested $38 million for an equity stake in the nuclear plant. In the sale-leaseback transaction, United Illuminating sold a 7% stake in the Seabrook plant to Meridian Bank.

The bonds are secured by United Illuminating's lease payments to Seabrook, as well as by the portion of the Seabrook plant that was sold.

Proceeds from yesterday's debt sale will be used to redeem United Illuminating's outstanding high-coupon debt starting in mid-August.

In the investment-grade corporate debt market, high-grade corporate bonds ended 1/2 to 3/4 point higher.

In the junk-bond market, concerns about the unraveling of the buy-out offer for UAL Corp. damped sentiment in the high-yield market, sending low-rated or unrated speculative-grade securities 1/8 to 1/4 point lower.

Among the junk bonds that registered marked declines were Fort Howard Corp.'s 12 5/8% subordinated debentures which fell 1/2 point to 97 1/2. The bonds started to drop after news that the company's chairman and chief executive is resigning.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds finished flat to 3/8 point higher.

A little new supply came into the municipal market when a Goldman, Sachs & Co. group won the Maryland Department of Transportation's $100 million consolidated transportation revenue bonds, Series 1990.

The bonds were priced to yield from 6% in 1993 to 6.80% in 2005. All bonds were sold by the time dealings finished.

In secondary trading, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securites of 2018 ended 1/8 higher at 99 3/4, with the yield unchanged at 7.21%.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities closed higher.

The issuance of derivative products stalled yesterday, following Tuesday's introduction of $1 billion in new real estate mortgage investment conduits.

"There's no way to keep up the issuance we saw {Tuesday}," said one trader. But, he added, with the market rallying, and with expectations of lower interest rates intact, "we'll definitely see some more deals soon."

Among actively traded issues, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were up 10/32 at 100 23/32. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9% issues were up 15/32 at 97 26/32, and Federal National Mortgage Association 9% issues were up 15/32 at 98 10/32.

Foreign Bonds

The West German bond market set a record in new-issue volume with the sale of eight billion of new 10-year government bonds.

The bonds, issued with a coupon rate of 8.5%, were sold during two days. Three billion marks ($1.89 billion) of bonds were placed Tuesday via the traditional federal bond consortium. Yesterday, 2.9 billion marks of the bonds were sold through a U.S.-style competitive auction and 2.1 billion worth were allocated to the Bundesbank, West Germany's central bank.

Dealers said the bulk of both days' sales ended up in the portfolios of West German banks or were purchased by professional traders for arbitrage accounts.

Other domestic investors showed little interest in the bonds. International investors were light buyers at the auction, dealers said. Japanese investors, who had been expected to buy large amounts, took "very little," according to a trader in the Frankfurt office of a Japanese bank.

In the secondary market, prices of the new 10-year bonds fell as much as 1/2 point, weighed down by the heavy supply, recurrent reports of East Germany seeking more cash from West Germany and a sell-off in the futures pit.

Elsewhere, Japanese and British government bonds ended moderately higher yesterday.
