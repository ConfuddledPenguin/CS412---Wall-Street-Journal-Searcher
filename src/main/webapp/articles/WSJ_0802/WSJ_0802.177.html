Judge Rejects Plan To Redraw District's Lines in Los Angeles

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/02/90

LOS ANGELES -- Federal Judge David V. Kenyon here rejected a county plan to redraw supervisoral district lines that he had earlier found to violate the constitutional rights of the area's large Hispanic population. Judge Kenyon said the plan, supported by three of the county's five supervisors, was a "nonsensical distortion" of existing district lines and "not a good-faith effort" to comply with his earlier order.

After announcing his decision in open court, Judge Kenyon took a short break and then began hearing alternative proposals from the Justice Department, the American Civil Liberties Union and the Mexican American Legal Defense and Educational Fund.

"His {ultimate} decision could be one of our plans, or one he develops himself," said Ramona Ripston, executive director of the ACLU of Southern California. She expects him to adopt a plan as soon as tomorrow.

"We are only a few months away from having results from the 1990 census," said a spokesman for Supervisor Michael Antonovich, who favored the county plan. "The redistricting should be done next year with accurate figures rather than with 1986 {population} projections."

Ms. Ripston countered that the county's "intentional discrimination" has already gone on much too long. "No woman, black, or Hispanic has been elected to the board" in this century, she said.

In June, Judge Kenyon found that the Board of Supervisors illegally drew boundaries in 1981 to separate the county's Hispanic community into three separate districts, thus diluting Hispanic voting strength and maintaining an all-white governing board. He ordered the county to draw up a redistricting plan within 20 days. Supervisor Antonovich said the county would appeal Judge Kenyon's finding.
