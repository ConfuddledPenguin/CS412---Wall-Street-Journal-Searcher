Life Insurer Agrees To Acquire Mutual Fund Company

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 08/02/90

Massachusetts Mutual Life Insurance Co. and Oppenheimer Management Corp.'s senior executives agreed to acquire Oppenheimer Management, a $14 billion mutual fund company, from British & Commonwealth Holdings PLC for approximately $150 million. The acquisition, which will give MassMutual an 80% voting interest in Oppenheimer Management, will set the life insurer up as a major player in the mutual fund business. "We tried growing {a mutual fund business} ourselves, but it's going to take forever before we reach a significant size," said Thomas B. Wheeler, chairman and chief executive officer of Springfield, Mass.-based MassMutual. "But with $14 billion under management, we've achieved the critical mass that we need."

British & Commonwealth was forced to sell its U.S. unit because it needed to raise $2 billion to pay debt. In April, it was forced to write off about $900 million in losses at another subsidiary, Atlantic Computers PLC. In early June, the firm entered "administration," the U.K. equivalent of Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law proceedings.

Oppenheimer and MassMutual are putting up about 50% of the buy-out price and plan to finance the remainder. Oppenheimer's senior managers are contributing $12 million of equity capital for the buy-out.

Some analysts said the $150 million price for Oppenheimer was modest. But Jon S. Fossel, Oppenheimer's president and chief executive, said $7 billion of the company's $14 billion in assets consists of money market funds that Oppenheimer manages for other concerns, including $6 billion for A.E. Edwards & Sons, the St. Louis-based brokerage firm. Management fees for money market funds are usually slimmer and transaction costs are higher than for traditional stock funds, he said.

Mr. Fossel said the accord between the two companies "is a pretty neat marriage." Although Oppenheimer will be an independent unit of the insurance company, he sees some eventual joint activities for the two concerns. Oppenheimer would like to sell variable annuities, and Mr. Fossel hopes to tap into MassMutual's expertise and back-office operations in these areas. MassMutual's 3,500 agents who are licensed to sell mutual funds will have additional funds to sell. Right now, they sell about 26 of Oppenheimer's 50 funds.

Mr. Wheeler said the Oppenheimer acquisition will also put MassMutual on an equal footing with some top life insurers who already own investment management firm or mutual fund companies. He said he expects the Oppenheimer business to generate substantial revenue for MassMutual, which is the 11th largest life insurer in the U.S.

MassMutual has a small family of funds with $350 million in assets. Many of these funds will be eventually folded into the Oppenheimer funds.
