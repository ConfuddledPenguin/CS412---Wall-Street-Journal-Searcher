Letters to the Editor: How `Medical Reviews' Are Hurting Hospitals

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/02/90

Responding to your June 20 article "Hospitals Hire Revenue Recovery Firms to Inflate Bills, Senate Panel Claims," I suggest that the Senate panel is investigating the wrong group. While I would agree that the "revenue recovery" firms described are among the more unsavory elements involved in hospital finance, these firms are used, to my knowledge, by only a very small percentage of hospitals. On the other hand, I believe nearly every health insurer has taken to using the "medical review" excuse as justification to slow down payments. Any hospital that refuses to cooperate with an audit by a health insurer is only defeating itself, since the insurer will not pay the bill until its audit "process" has been satisfied. Accounts receivable in hospitals have risen dramatically in the past few years, and these arbitrary "medical reviews" are a part of the problem. There is, of course, a cost associated with the necessary increase in working capital.

I know of no reputable hospital that would refuse to cooperate with an audit. In some cases it is necessary to have a clinical professional (R.N., technologist, etc.) participate in the audit to explain notations in the medical record. In these cases, it may be appropriate for the insurer to pay for the professionals' time lost from their scheduled duties. Without such reimbursement, nearly every patient account could be subject to groundless examination, all in the name of preserving cash flow.

Finally, if the Senate panel involved thinks shutting down "revenue recovery" firms will add materially to an overall solution of our health-care-cost crisis, the panel itself is a part of the problem.

Arthur W. Nichols

Chief Financial Officer

Cheshire Medical Center

Keene, N.H.
