House Approves Housing Bill That Faces Veto

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 08/02/90

WASHINGTON -- The House, ignoring a veto threat, approved an omnibus housing bill that would significantly increase federal spending for public housing programs. The bill, which passed on a 378-43 vote, would allocate $28 billion for housing programs for fiscal 1991, $10 billion more than the Senate approved last month in its bill.

The White House said yesterday that President Bush would veto the legislation in its current form because it is too costly and doesn't address the administration's concerns over the health of the Federal Housing Administration.

Housing and Urban Development Secretary Jack Kemp argued that the House measure doesn't require adequate up-front insurance premiums for first-time home buyers and that it fails to provide sufficient capital requirements for the FHA.

Under the House bill, $19.4 billion would be allocated for existing nonrural housing programs and $2.1 billion for modernization. The bill also provides $500 million for a new program to subsidize interest rates so first-time home buyers would pay a maximum interest rate of 6%.

In addition, the bill would provide $310 million for a new program to assist public housing residents in buying their government-owned homes, a provision also included in the Senate bill. The House bill also would authorize $300 million for the construction of new rental housing, a provision opposed by the administration.

Another provision of the House bill that differs significantly from the Senate version is the restructuring of the FHA loan program. An amendment introduced by Rep. Bruce Vento (D., Minn.) and Rep. Thomas Ridge (R., Pa.) would phase out over five years the 3.8% up-front insurance premium, requiring instead that a payment of 1.35% of the value of the mortgage be paid at the time of the settlement and that 0.6% be paid over the life of the loan. The Senate measure retains the current 3.8% up-front insurance requirement. The Senate bill also requires that two-thirds of the closing costs be paid up front, a provision not required by the House bill.

In a letter to Rep. Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas), chairman of the House Committee on Banking, Finance and Urban Affairs, Mr. Kemp wrote: "Should a bill containing the Vento-Ridge amendment or a similarly unacceptable proposal reach the president's desk, I and the president's other senior advisers would recommend that it be vetoed."

But Mary Brunette, an assistant to Secretary Kemp, said the administration was hopeful that a compromise could be reached in the conference committee. "The administration would be willing to accept higher spending if the conference report more closely resembles the Senate bill," she said.
