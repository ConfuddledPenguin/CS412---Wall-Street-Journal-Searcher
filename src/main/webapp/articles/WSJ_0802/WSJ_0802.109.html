Mahlmann Quits Top CBOT Post Amid Stotler Woes

  • Author: Scott McMurray
  • Date: 08/02/90

CHICAGO -- Karsten "Cash" Mahlmann, the embattled chairman of the Chicago Board of Trade, resigned suddenly from his post as the top officer of the nation's largest futures exchange. The proud manager, who reveled in the traditions of the country's oldest futures exchange and the influence it commanded, broke a venerable CBOT tradition by stepping down. He is the first Board of Trade chairman to resign in 130 years, the exchange said. The Board of Trade was formed in 1848.

The exchange's directors said that Vice Chairman William F. O'Connor was elected to serve as chairman for the remainder of Mr. Mahlmann's fourth one-year term.

Mr. Mahlmann's resignation yesterday afternoon followed weeks of mounting criticism leveled at the 52-year-old executive for his role as chairman of financially crippled Stotler Group Inc., which has futures brokerage and funds management subsidiaries. Stotler Group's troubles and those of its units have tarnished the reputation of the Board of Trade at a time of increased congressional concern about lax regulation in the futures industry, exchange members said.

Mr. Mahlmann's nickname, earned more than 30 years ago when the young German immigrant started out trading grain in the physical or "cash," as opposed to futures, market, has taken on an ironic aspect in recent weeks. Stotler Group has been driven to the brink of insolvency by the inability of it and its main futures brokerage unit to raise enough cash to meet industry capital requirements.

Perhaps the most serious blow yet to a Stotler unit was delivered Tuesday by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission. The federal regulator charged the company's Stotler Funds Inc. subsidiary with fraudulently misusing $5.5 million in customer money invested in two futures funds it managed. Until then, the cash-strapped Stotler companies had been able to say that no customer funds had been imperiled by their difficulties.

Neither Mr. Mahlmann nor any other individual was named in the CFTC charges.

Mr. Mahlmann couldn't be reached for comment on his resignation or the events leading up to it.

Yesterday morning Mr. Mahlmann resigned from his role as chairman of Stotler Group and its failed futures brokerage unit, Stotler & Co. But in a letter distributed to exchange members he said he was determined to serve out the remainder of his term.

On Tuesday the exchange's board, after telling members in a letter that the exchange had acted properly in its oversight of Stotler, said they unanimously supported Mr. Mahlmann as chairman of the exchange. Some members said privately that the directors should have waited until more facts were known about Stotler's troubles before lining up behind the chairman.

By yesterday afternoon, Mr. Mahlmann appeared to realize that the events surrounding the Stotler parent company and its subsidiaries had overtaken him, and he wouldn't be able to effectively lead the Board of Trade, an exchange director said.

Mr. Mahlmann told the directors that his resignation was "in the overwhelming interest of the Board of Trade," according to an exchange news release.

The fallout from the Stotler companies' troubles is likely to haunt Mr. Mahlmann for months. Regulators are almost certain to take disciplinary action of some sort against Stotler Group, its subsidiaries or both. Company officers could also be named. Civil lawsuits from disgruntled investors in Stotler Group or in the funds managed by Stotler Funds also are likely, regulators say.

Futures regulators say the case may also be referred to the local U.S. attorney's office for possible criminal prosecution.

Mr. Mahlmann has gone to great lengths in recent days to emphasize that he hasn't been involved in the day-to-day management of any of the Stotler companies for more than two years. He began spending nearly all his time on exchange affairs by early 1988, and agreed to relinquish his position as managing partner of the Stotler partnership that was reorganized as Stotler Group when the company went public in August 1988.

Since mid-1989 Mr. Mahlmann hasn't received a salary from Stotler. The Board of Trade made the chairmanship a paying position in 1989 with an annual salary of $250,000.

Stotler's troubles were publicly hinted at July 12, when Stotler Group asked that its stock not open for trading on the American Stock Exchange because of "news pending." Three days later the company, confirming widespread industry rumors, said its Stotler & Co. futures brokerage unit couldn't meet CBOT minimum capital requirements.

The company fell out of compliance with capital rules when a nearly $13 million inter-company funds transfer between it and the parent Stotler Group was disallowed by the CFTC. Futures industry regulations require that brokerage firms maintain a cash cushion equal to 4% of segregated customer funds.

Less than two weeks later Stotler & Co. was forced out of the brokerage business for lack of capital.

Stotler Group last week defaulted on $750,000 in commercial paper payments and said it may not be able to make $7.6 million in debt payments due within 30 days.
