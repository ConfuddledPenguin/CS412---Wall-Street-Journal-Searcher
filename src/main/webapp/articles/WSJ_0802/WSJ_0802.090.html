U.S. Announces New Trade Pact Set With Japan

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 08/02/90

WASHINGTON -- Japan has agreed to give U.S. vendors greater opportunities to sell certain telecommunications terminal equipment and data-transmission services to Japanese customers, U.S. trade officials said. The U.S. negotiators wrung these pledges from officials of the Japanese Ministry of Post and Telecommunications just hours before a deadline set for the White House to determine whether Japan is abiding by a number of telecommunications accords with the U.S. The deadline was set by mutual agreement by the U.S. and Japan. The concessions made by Japan avert threatened trade sanctions against that country, according to the U.S. trade office.

U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills called the Japanese agreement a "major breakthrough," asserting that U.S. manufacturing, financial and other firms that require communications services in Japan "should be able to run those operations more easily and economically."

The latest commitments give U.S. companies the right to sell network channel terminating equipment, or NCTEs, directly to Japanese customers, instead of through common carriers such as Japan's giant Nippon Telegraph & Telephone Corp. as required by current regulations. U.S. trade officials estimate the initial U.S. market share at $150 million annually but much larger in the future.

NCTEs are similar to the modems used to transmit data over telephone lines. These devices enable users of personal-computer networks to exchange digitalized data. Direct access to Japanese customers could stimulate the U.S. sales of NCTEs because of the technical superiority of the U.S. products.

In another major concession, Tokyo has agreed to scrap several barriers to its market for international value-added networks, or IVANs. Such networks are the vehicles through which electronic banking, voice mail, and other communications services are conducted across national boundaries.

The agreement gives U.S. providers of IVANs a shorter wait for approval -- 30 days instead of several months -- and removes the 20% surcharges that typically are levied by common carriers for the lease of their lines. Although the IVANs mostly link customers in the U.S. and Japan, the latest package gives Japan-based U.S. providers the right to service other countries.

U.S. providers currently have a fraction of Japan's $750 million annual IVAN market, but their share could grow sharply as a result of the agreement, U.S. officials predicted.
