Following is an editorial from last Friday's Bangkok Post. It refers to the Asian-Pacific Economic Cooperation meeting held in Singapore earlier this week:

The chief subject of corridor discussions at a conference in Singapore this weekend on the economy of Pacific nations will focus on Southeast Asia's longest-running war without bloodshed. Singapore's tireless dispute with the foreign Press has escalated again. The government has barred reporters from the region's two leading economic publications from covering the Asian Pacific Economic Cooperation conference. By keeping away The Asian Wall Street Journal and the Far Eastern Economic Review, Singapore has assured diluting serious discussion about the region's economy. Delegates, including senior regional and international ministers, will be debating and discussing the Press ban. Indeed, it will be a major topic when U.S. Secretary of State James Baker meets Singapore Prime Minister Lee Kuan Yew.

From a simple assertion of government rights, Singapore's unrelenting pursuit of certain media and correspondents has intensified to this. With their circulations severely restricted, the two publications are effectively barred from the vast majority of the Singapore public and visitors to the Republic. The issue has consumed a large amount of Mr. Lee's time and that of his deputy and appointed successor Goh Chok Tong. The hard-line, no-compromise stance of Mr. Lee's government has made Singapore appear petty in the eyes of many. These certainly include many executives who do business in, or with, the island republic.

In all of this, the Press has become the underdog. Predictably, it has gained much public sympathy, if not outright support. By continuing its public battles with two of the region's leading business publications, Singapore has cast itself in the role of persecutor as well as prosecutor. Neither of these is a role desirable for the Singapore government in this instance.

It is possible, certainly, to consider Singapore's position in the dispute. Mr. Lee and his government maintain they have the right to bar access to reporters of media they consider unfriendly. Of course they do. But the disputes in these cases have sunk to making picayune and obscure points. The fact is that neither publication, for example, has called for the ouster of the Singapore Government, let alone its overthrow. The full-page advertisement {a reprint of an editorial that ran in the Asian Journal July 23 explaining the company's position} in Tuesday's Asian Wall Street Journal left many readers wondering how two eminently sensible organizations -- the newspaper and the Singapore Government -- could be reduced to such bickering.

They can be excused for asking if this is a tempest in a teacup. Mr. Goh has made it clear that reporters from the parent organizations of both publications may cover the weekend conference. Only those employed specifically by their Asian partners are banned. Thus, to many, it will seem that there is neither a crime nor a punishment. The physical barring of reporters is therefore more puzzling than ever.

The Asian Pacific Economic Cooperation Conference is a major international meeting. Current practice in almost every country, and quite correctly so, is to allow the Press to cover such meetings. Singapore neither helps its own case nor, in the end, the publications it seeks to punish. By barring serious economic reporters from a serious economic conference, the Singapore Government succeeds only in appearing oppressive, hardly the judgment it seeks. That the outside world cares little about the specific reasons for such action only further hurts Singapore's image.

Mr. Lee and Mr. Goh, both statesmen of the world class, can perform better than this. Most governments in the world have come to a truce, however uneasy, with foreign newsmen without either giving up their own rights, or forcing reporters to give up their claims to cover news as they and their publications see fit. The Singapore Press dispute has dragged on for years. Instead of seeking impossible redress for events in a past long forgotten by the public, Singapore might well consider calling a truce and looking to the future in this matter. A fine, diplomatic beginning would be for Singapore to make a quick reversal and allow reporters from the two publications to attend this weekend's very important meeting.
