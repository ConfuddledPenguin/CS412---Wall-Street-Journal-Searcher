Foreign Exchange: Dollar Rises Across the Board as Dealers Take Their Profits on Tuesday's Sell-Off

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/02/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar rose across the board as dealers who had sold the currency against the mark Tuesday took profits. But market participants agreed that the day's session represented no long-term reversal in the dollar's fortunes. The great bulk of dollar positions are short, they said, on the belief that the U.S. currency is still headed for record lows against its German counterpart.

In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.5885 marks, up from 1.5869 marks late Tuesday. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 147.18 yen, above 145.80 yen in New York late Tuesday. Sterling was trading at $1.8555, down from $1.8640 late Tuesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the dollar was quoted at 147.05 yen almost an hour after the opening; it closed in Tokyo Wednesday at 146.65 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5840 marks, down from Tokyo's Wednesday close of 1.5938 marks.

The dollar's value in recent days has been edging close to the level of 1.5615 marks briefly seen on Jan. 4, 1988, which was its lowest since the West German currency's creation after World War II.

Supporting their view that the dollar will continue to soften against the mark, currency analysts and traders noted that the dollar's gains were led by purchases with yen. The U.S. currency climbed exactly two yen from its intraday low of 145.60 yen at the start of Australian trading to its day's high of 147.60 yen in the afternoon in New York.

Against the German currency, the dollar peaked at 1.5948 marks just before the European close.

Some U.S. corporations and speculators who tried to sell dollars, however, were unable to get their orders executed before London closed and liquidity dried up, according to Keith Raphael, a Chase Manhattan vice president for foreign exchange. Left on their own, he said, New York market makers had to bring the dollar's price down to find enough buyers for the backlog of sellers.

"Because the pressure on the dollar is still strong, it looks like it'll fall again" today, said Kenichiro Takeda, a senior dealer and trading manager at Mitsubishi Trust in Tokyo.

"The market's frustrated that it can't reach the lows," another Tokyo dealer said, bemoaning the part the Asian market played in interrupting the dollar's decline. "The downside still has a lot of potential for investors to take profits."

But even investors who agree on the dollar's bleak prospects bought it yesterday.

Explained a foreign exchange trader in Paris, "The dollar fell too far too fast {Tuesday} and was due for a correction."

The desire to chalk up profits was strong yesterday even though monthly books had been closed the day before, said Alan Leventen, managing director of financial services for Balfour, Maclaine Futures in New York.

The next opportunity for pocketing gains, he said, will come tomorrow, when the U.S. releases its July employment statistics. Mr. Leventen said profit-taking pressure on any downward tick in the dollar may leave it higher at the end of the day, even if the jobs report is dismal.

In fact, in selling their non-dollar currencies yesterday, dealers and investors ignored added evidence of U.S. economic stagnation.

The July purchasing managers' index fell to 47.4% after three months of gains. A reading below 50% indicates manufacturing is in decline. The index had been expected to read 50.8% for the month.

The June leading indicators index, meanwhile, was unchanged after a revised 0.7% gain in May.

A weaker dollar may be favored by the U.S. Federal Reserve, which is under pressure to spur economic expansion.

"A lower dollar is the equivalent of lower interest rates," Chase's Mr. Raphael said. "I doubt we'll see another easing in credit policy before October. Instead, the Fed will hope weak fundamentals will weaken the dollar enough to improve the trade deficit." The resulting rise in exports would be a welcome boost for the U.S. economy, he said.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $373.30 an ounce, up $3.30. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.3 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $374.75 an ounce.
