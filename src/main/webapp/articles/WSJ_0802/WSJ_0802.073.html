Technology & Medicine: AT&T, Two Bell Firms Plan Technology To Boost Capacity of Cellular Systems

  • Author: John J. Keller and Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 08/02/90

NEW YORK -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co. and two regional Bell companies plan to develop a radio technology that could significantly boost the capacity of cellular radio systems, the companies said. AT&T has signed an agreement with Qualcomm Inc., a San Diego company that pioneered the Code Division Multiple Access, or CDMA, technology for use in cellular radio systems. Nynex Corp. and Chicago-based Ameritech plan to install the systems in their networks.

The plan by three of the industry's biggest players to develop and use the technology is significant because their approval could help make it a standard for the cellular industry and so-called personal communications services of the future.

Personal communications service networks, as envisioned by the telecommunications industry, will allow millions of customers to carry lightweight, pocket-sized phones by the end of this century. But the networks will require enormous transmission capacity -- many times greater than conventional cellular telephone networks. In several major cities, including New York and Los Angeles, these cellular networks are suffering increasingly from capacity shortages.

CDMA systems are said to offer up to 20 times more call-handling capacity than the conventional cellular systems. The CDMA systems assign a special electronic code to each call signal, allowing more calls to occupy the same space and be spread over an entire frequency band. By contrast, the current standard for future digital systems, time-division multiple access, or TDMA, offers three-to-seven times the capacity of existing systems and works by placing each call signal into a time slot and transmitting it within a single frequency.

"We are launching the next-generation digital cellular technology," said Allen Salmasi, vice president of planning and development at Qualcomm. "Basically these agreements will put us on the map as a major supplier of CDMA equipment for the cellular industry." AT&T declined to comment on the agreement.

Nynex Mobile Communications Co., which has the more ambitious plan of the two regional phone companies, said that late next year in New York City it will roll out the nation's first wireless personal telephone service. The company plans to start out small with one new switch in Manhattan. Yesterday, it announced a multimillion-dollar agreement to buy the cellular switching gear from AT&T for an undisclosed amount.

Nynex also said it agreed to buy $3 million of digital telephone handsets from Qualcomm to sell to its customers. The phones are expected to be smaller, lighter and cheaper than most existing cellular phones.

Nynex Mobile, which will use frequencies already assigned to it for cellular telephone transmissions, conducted a trial of the new technology from February to June, and was enthusiastic.

"For densely populated areas like New York, personal telephone service will offer consumers the most flexible, portable and practical personal communications system available," said Robert Keller, Nynex Mobile's chief operating officer. "When PTS is introduced next year, a telephone number will no longer represent a place, but rather, a person."

Like conventional cellular service, personal telephone service will connect with the phone system through radio antennae. However, the antennae will be smaller. Dubbed microcells, the antennae will be connected to a digital network that uses the CDMA technology, which assigns to each call a distinct code that the network can sort. That allows many calls to be transmitted on the same frequency, with each call remaining discrete.

Ameritech Mobile Communications said it will be studying the benefits of CDMA technology but during the evaluation period still supports the current TDMA standard.

"It is critical that we select the appropriate technology to meet customer needs while optimizing that very valuable resource called radio spectrum," said Roger Pachuta, Ameritech Mobile's vice president of network and customer services.
