Economy: Three Reports Show Economy Is Stagnating --- Leading Indicators and New Construction Were Flat; Factory Figures Decline

  • Author: Tuesday Georges
  • Date: 08/02/90

WASHINGTON -- Three new economic reports suggest the U.S. economy remains stagnant. The Commerce Department said its index of leading economic indicators was unchanged in June, following a roller-coaster 0.7% rise in May and a 0.2% drop in April. The department also reported that spending on construction was virtually unchanged during June. And the National Association of Purchasing Management reported a sharp drop in its index of manufacturing health for July.

The latest figures, which follow a string of anemic economic indicators, fueled speculation among some economists that the economy is headed for a downturn. "I expect a mild recession to begin in the fourth quarter," said Chris Varvares of Laurence H. Myer & Associates, an economic research and consulting firm in St. Louis. Other analysts predict the economy will escape recession, but remain very sluggish.

Six of the 11 indicators in the Commerce Department's forecasting index turned down in June. A drop in unfilled manufacturers' orders contributed the biggest decline, followed by a drop in the money supply.

Robert Dederick, chief economist for Northern Trust Co. in Chicago, said he was disturbed particularly by the decline in new and unfilled orders for manufacturers goods. "This is where we were hoping to have strength," he said.

Separately, the Commerce Department reported that new construction reached an annual rate of $447.5 billion in May, barely changed from $447.6 billion in June. An increase in spending on office buildings during the month was offset by a decline in spending on housing.

And in the first hint of the economy's health in July, the National Association of Purchasing Management said its index of factory health plunged to 47.4% in the month, from 51.1% in June and 50.7% in May. The index largely reflected a decline in orders for factory goods, which fell in July after five straight monthly increases. According to the purchasing managers, a reading below 50% indicates the manufacturing economy is shrinking.

"The manufacturing economy started the third quarter poorly, abruptly ending a three-month moderate, but solid, increase in growth," said Robert J. Bretz, chairman of the group's survey committee and director of materials management at Pitney Bowes Inc. "The weakness of new orders in July now casts a shadow over the previous expectations for continued growth in the economy."

The purchasing managers report showed that exports continue to be the one bright sport in the economy. The new export orders index rose to 57% in July from 56.5% in June.

The export figure isn't seasonally adjusted, but other figures in the three reports are.
