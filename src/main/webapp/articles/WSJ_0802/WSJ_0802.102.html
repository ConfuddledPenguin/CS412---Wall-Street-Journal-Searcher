Divorce and Therapy Under the Same Roof

AN AUSTIN, Texas, law firm is offering divorce clients an unusual fringe benefit: free group therapy sessions conducted right in its office.

Boozer & Tull says the weekly meetings are designed to focus attention on the anger and frustration that spouses often bring to divorce cases. "We fix their legal problems, but they go out the door worse off emotionally than they came in," says Jennifer Tull, a lawyer with the firm.

Many law firms have working arrangements with psychiatrists and psychologists, but in-house therapy sessions are unusual. The voluntary meetings are conducted by Larry Bugen, an Austin psychologist paid by Boozer & Tull on a per-meeting basis.

The therapy could also aid lawyers -- by reducing the threat of malpractice suits. "If clients are working out their anger with a group, maybe they won't turn it on me," says Ms. Tull.

So far, one client group has gone through the eight-week course of sessions, and Ms. Tull says the feedback is positive. But Donn C. Fullenweider, president of the American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers, is skeptical. The Houston lawyer thinks psychiatry and law should be kept separate because they have different goals and sometimes end up providing conflicting advice. "When one overlaps the other," he says, "you're bound to have problems."

Testing the Limits

Of Lawyers' Ads

THE DEBATE over lawyers marketing their services on television gets more fuel from a nationwide "advertising cooperative."

The Personal Injury Trial Lawyers Association, based in New Orleans, solicits lawyers to buy memberships in the organization. In return, PITLA finances local, regional and national television commercials promoting the use of member lawyers. Members pay a monthly fee of $500 to $700, plus a pro-rated share of the cost of producing the TV ads.

The commercials instruct potential claimants to call a toll-free number. Staffers refer the callers to lawyers on a rotating basis. Founded in 1986 by marketing executive G. Lenn Brown, PITLA claims a 1990 TV ad budget of about $36 million, up $20 million from 1989. Its commercials, produced by a New Orleans ad agency also founded by Mr. Brown, now run in 230 cities in 49 states, he says.

The association doesn't advertise in Iowa because of the state's tough advertising restrictions, Mr. Brown says. Carl V. Nielsen, executive director of the Iowa Bar Association, says lawyer advertising is permitted, but the bar doesn't allow dramatizations, "And the people who do commercials don't like that." PITLA ads sometimes use actors.

In March, Tennessee regulators blocked the association's commercials, charging that the group is an unauthorized lawyer referral service. The association sued, alleging restraint of trade and First Amendment violations. The two sides subsequently agreed the organization could air its commercials while they try to work out a formal settlement.

The American Bar Association isn't taking a position on the group, but it hears from skeptical lawyers each time PITLA enters a new market. "I can almost gauge {their} marketing efforts by the calls I get," says William Hornsby of the ABA Commission on advertising.

More Companies Bitten

By `Cobra' Lawsuits

COMPANIES ARE facing more suits under a federal law requiring companies to offer group health coverage to former employees.

Enacted in 1986, a section of the Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act, nicknamed Cobra, requires most employers to let workers extend their health insurance for at least 18 months after they have been terminated or have voluntarily left their jobs.

But workers say some employers are not complying with the law, and their complaints are beginning to reach the courts. In the past year, the Labor Department sued Eastern Airlines and Dayton Hudson, charging that they denied Cobra benefits to employees. Both firms made concessions in settling the cases without denying or admitting the allegations. Private suits are pending in Texas, Florida and South Carolina.

Lawyers say some companies inadvertently fail to notify employees of their Cobra rights because the law is complex and relatively new. But other companies resist extending the benefits because they fear it will drive up health-care premiums.

The "big issue," says a Labor Department spokeswoman, is whether an employee was properly informed of the right to purchase extended coverage. Cobra requires employers to give written notice of that right. Another disputed issue is when an employee is considered to have been fired for "gross misconduct." Such employees are exempt from Cobra benefits.

Odds and Ends

NEW MEXICO'S bar association has formed a committee to combat the image of lawyers as greedy and unethical. It isn't easy. "The more you say, `I'm not a crook,' the more people say you are," says committee member Randi McGinn of Albuquerque. . . . A federal court in Lubbock, Texas, is pondering whether cowspeak is a protected trademark. Yet Inc., a restaurant operator, is trying to guard the "Mooo" slogan it uses on "Mooonues," from which patrons order Mooo meals. Yet Inc. sued the Western Sizzlin restaurant chain of Dallas, charging that it misappropriated the trademark with its own too-similar slogan: "Moo."
