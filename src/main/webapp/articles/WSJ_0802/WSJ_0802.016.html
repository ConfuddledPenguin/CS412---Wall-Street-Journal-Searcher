Technology & Medicine: Study Says Antibiotic-Resistant Bacteria Are Common in Infants of 2 Poor Nations

  • Author: David Stipp
  • Date: 08/02/90

BOSTON -- Researchers here found antibiotic-resistant bacteria in almost all infants they studied in two developing countries, suggesting that such bacteria have become a major public health threat world-wide. However, only about half of U.S. infants they studied carried such bacteria -- a surprisingly low prevalence, considering the heavy U.S. use of antibiotics. The finding offers new hope for the fight against antibiotic-resistant bacteria in the developed world, said Thomas F. O'Brien, a co-author of the study and a physician at Boston's Brigham and Women's Hospital.

Doctors are increasingly alarmed about the proliferation of disease-causing bacteria that resist antibiotics. When patients take antibiotics, their internal microbes susceptible to the drugs die off. But if resistant strains of bacteria are present, they quickly grow like weeds in a newly cleared field. Thus, the more antibiotics used, the more antibiotic-resistant bacteria there are. And once a resistant strain arises, it can travel fast -- penicillin-resistant gonorrhea bacteria, for example, have spread internationally in recent years.

Moreover, scientists have discovered that genes conferring resistance can move from one strain of bacteria to another. That means antibiotics are turning benign bacteria that exist in all animals' intestines into reservoirs of antibiotic-resistant genes. Such genes can later be transferred to disease-causing bacteria with devastating results.

In their study, published in today's issue of the New England Journal of Medicine, the Boston researchers found that more than 95% of healthy infants they surveyed in Caracas, Venezuela, and in Qin Pu, China, carried at least one strain of antibiotic-resistant bacteria in their intestines. Some of the infants never had been given antibiotics, an indication that resistant bacteria are ubiquitous in the infants' environments and infect them soon after birth.

Heavy, indiscriminate use of antibiotics in the Third World probably accounts for the finding, said Dr. O'Brien. One reason is poor sanitation, causing high rates of infectious disease. Moreover, "in the developing world, it's a big event to go to the doctor," he said. "Consumers are very oriented to coming home with something to take," which typically is an antibiotic. And if they can't afford to visit a doctor, Third World consumers often get antibiotics from pharmacists or street vendors, Dr. O'Brien said.

The drugs also are used heavily in the developed world -- accounting for around 35% of drug prescriptions for U.S. children under the age of three in 1986, according to one study.

But U.S. doctors typically use them selectively. Laboratory tests on their patients can tell them which antibiotic, if any, will best kill the infecting bacteria. Thus, "we don't so often get the situation where people are banging away at a bacteria with an antibiotic until a resistant strain emerges," said Dr. O'Brien.

When such strains do emerge, U.S. doctors respond with newer -- and usually costlier -- antibiotics that haven't yet spawned bacteria immune to them. But now, "multiresistant" bacteria, which are immune to different antibiotics, are emerging. More than half of the resistant bacteria found in babies the Boston researchers studied -- including the U.S. ones -- were immune to more than one antibiotic.

Still, the fact that perhaps half of U.S. infants begin life without resistant bacteria suggests that many people, with a little care, could avoid such microbes, said Dr. O'Brien. For instance, he said, consumers should avoid taking unneeded antibiotics. And they shouldn't chop meat, which might contain antibiotic-resistant bacteria, on surfaces used to prepare salads.
