Wednesday's Markets: Bonds Rise On Gloomy Statistics --- Utility Stocks, Gold Also Gain; Dow Falls And Dollar Rebounds

  • Author: Tom Herman
  • Date: 08/02/90

Gloomy economic tidings sent investors shopping for bonds, gold and utility stocks. But fears of weakening corporate profits helped depress the Dow Jones Industrial Average 5.94 points to 2899.26. Several broader market indexes also fell slightly.

In the foreign-exchange market, the dollar pulled out of its recent tailspin, climbing sharply against the mark. The dollar also rose against the British pound but managed only a small gain against the Japanese yen. Several traders predict the dollar will resume declining because they expect interest rates to climb overseas and to ease in the U.S.

Speculation about lower U.S. rates intensified early yesterday after the National Association of Purchasing Management's report for July showed more economic weakness than analysts had expected. That report "heightened prospects for lower interest rates" but also "depressed expectations for corporate profits," said A.C. Moore, research director at Argus Research Corp.

"All year, this market has been a tug of war between disappointing earnings, which are bad for stocks, and declining interest rates, which are good for stocks," said Byron Wien, investment strategist at Morgan Stanley. "Right now, the focus of the market is more on earnings than it is on rates."

Treasury bond prices advanced more than 5/8 point, or about $6.25 for each $1,000 face amount, even as the government disclosed plans to sell a record $32.25 billion of new notes and bonds next week. The timing of the auctions depends on when Congress raises the nation's debt ceiling.

While stock prices generally slumped, utility stocks continued to attract investors because utilities are big borrowers and especially sensitive to interest rate changes. The Dow Jones Utilities Average rose 2.89 points yesterday to 212.90, which many traders interpret as signaling lower interest rates ahead.

Gold prices have rebounded recently after a deep slump earlier this year. Traders attribute the turnaround partly to Middle East tensions, the dollar's recent weakness, and the U.S. economy's growing sluggishness. Some analysts also cite growing prospects for another credit-easing move by the Federal Reserve.

In major market activity:

Stock prices fell. Declining issues outnumbered advancers by 777 to 733 on the New York Stock Exchange.

Bond prices rose. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell to 8.35% from 8.41%.

The dollar rebounded. In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 147.18 yen, up from 145.80 yen late Tuesday, and at 1.5885 marks, up from 1.5869 marks.
