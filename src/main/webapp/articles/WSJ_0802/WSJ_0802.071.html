International: U.S. Is Seeking Help From U.N. to Stop Liberia's Civil War

  • Author: John J. Fialka
  • Date: 08/02/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. is trying to get the United Nations to help resolve the crisis in Liberia, where two rebel factions are battling government forces in Monrovia, the country's capital. "We are extremely concerned about the fighting and the bloodshed," said State Department spokesman Richard Boucher, explaining that the U.S. effort is still exploratory, trying to find a way the U.N. might exert its influence to stop the civil war.

Meanwhile, he said, the U.S. is helping private relief organizations distribute 30,000 bags of rice to prevent famine in the war-ravaged country. Other U.S. officials said tens of thousands of Liberians have sought sanctuary in churches and near U.S. installations in the capital, as patrols from government troops and two rebel factions stage sporadic fighting in downtown Monrovia.

As many as 600 Liberians were slaughtered in a Monrovia church on Sunday. Most of the victims are believed to have been members of the Gio and Mano tribes, which are involved in the rebel forces. According to Mr. Boucher, the U.S. has eyewitness accounts that they were shot by soldiers of President Samuel Doe, who is holed up in his fortified headquarters in Monrovia, protected by an elite cadre of troops. Mr. Doe and most of his troops are members of the Krahn tribe.

The fighting began in December, when rebel troops entered Liberia from the north. Since then they have split into two rival factions, the largest of which is led by Charles Taylor, formerly the head of the government's purchasing agency. Mr. Taylor's forces, according to one U.S. official, are believed to include troops who have received training and weapons from Libya.

As the two rebel factions converged on Monrovia, the U.S. evacuated hundreds of Americans from the country, which has a population of 1.5 million. About 400 Americans remain, according to the State Department, and a Marine Corps task force has been positioned offshore in case a further evacuation is needed. So far, according to the State Department, there have been no attacks on embassies in Monrovia, although some of the firefights have occurred in the embassy district.
