Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Tobacco Companies' Sponsorships Of Motor Sports Face Scrutiny

  • Author: Joanne Lipman
  • Date: 05/22/90

Does the televised Winston Cup car race count as advertising? How about the Camel Supercross motorcycle race, aired on ESPN? For legislators attempting to crack down on tobacco advertising, the answer is an unqualified yes. And now, at least one legislator is taking on the televised car, bike and truck races as part of a crusade to curb tobacco advertising.

Rep. Thomas A. Luken (D., Ohio), an outspoken tobacco gadfly and a sponsor of a bill restricting tobacco advertising, plans to launch an investigation into the televised races today, an aide says. In letters being sent to the three major networks and ESPN, among others, Mr. Luken asks for details of their financial relationships with tobacco companies, in order "to understand the extent to which television sports events are in fact sponsored by tobacco companies."

Tobacco foes have long complained about tobacco companies' sports sponsorships, saying the sponsorships are in fact back-door tactics to circumvent a 20-year-old television ad ban. Tobacco ads on scoreboards and billboards in stadiums are frequent targets of legislators and other anti-tobacco activists.

But the critics' complaints so far have had only marginal results, which may be why they are turning to motor sports. Televised motor races are far more obvious in flaunting their sponsors, whose names tend to be plastered across racing cars, helmets, and everything else in camera view. According to the House subcommittee on transportation and hazardous materials, of which Mr. Luken is chairman, a Camel logo was visible 30% of the time on the ESPN-aired Camel Supercross, an indoor motorcycle race. The Marlboro logo was shown 49% of the time during the 1989 Marlboro Grand Prix, according to Mr. Luken's letter.

"This isn't just a baseball stadium where you occasionally see something. This is serious business. It's a matter of degree," says Benjamin Cohen, the Luken aide. He notes that car races frequently include a camera stationed inside one of the race cars, and that a tobacco logo often appears on the dashboard. "These things don't happen by chance," he says. "They happen because people are signing contracts and paying money."

Mr. Luken's initiative comes at a time when public sentiment has clearly turned against smoking. A powerful tobacco lobby was unable to prevent Congress last year from outlawing smoking on most domestic airline flights. More than a dozen bills limiting tobacco in some way are pending on Capitol Hill right now, including Mr. Luken's initiative, which would ban all sports sponsorships and allow only all-text print ads for tobacco products. Next month, Rep. Henry Waxman (D., Calif) is expected to introduce yet another bill, this time one incorporating parts of the various bills already pending.

Tobacco companies, for their part, contend that sponsoring televised races and race cars isn't advertising at all. "It's promotion of our product, just like the Virginia Slims Tennis Tournament is," says a spokeswoman for Philip Morris, maker of Marlboro, among other brands. She adds that "there is no agreement" with the networks under which they are required to show the company's logo during races -- although when the logo is plastered all over the car and its driver, it would be difficult not to show it.

In fact, the spokeswoman says, during the Indy 500, Philip Morris didn't allow an ABC camera in a car with a Marlboro logo on the dashboard. "We didn't want the camera to be focused on the logo," she says. "The law is that there is no television advertising, and we adhere to the law."

At NBC, Richard Gitter, vice president of standards and practices, says that tobacco sponsorships of motor races "isn't advertising that is the kind of advertising contemplated -- and barred -- by the Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act." Mr. Gitter declined comment on the letter, as he hadn't received it.

Another recipient of the letter is to be Charles Mancuso, president of SRO/Pace Promotions, a Lombard, Ill., firm that produces motor sports events, including the Camel Supercross. Mr. Mancuso declined comment on the letter, which he also hadn't received. He added that he didn't know of any relationship between the tobacco companies and the networks that air their races.

Chiat Shop Buy-Out

Chiat/Day/Mojo said it plans to sell a majority interest of its San Francisco office to a group of employees at the office, including its president and chief executive, Fred Goldberg.

Chiat/Day said it was selling the interest because Mr. Goldberg and other managers "wanted the opportunity to own their own business and sought the chance to acquire the agency."

Los Angeles-based Chiat/Day will retain an minority interest in the shop, although it said the San Francisco management group plans to fully own the operation in the future. Terms weren't disclosed.

The San Francisco employees will give the agency an as-yet unannounced new name. It will continue to serve most of Chiat/Day's San Francisco clients, which include Atari and Celestial Seasonings. The office has billings of about $110 million.

Chiat/Day said it hopes to move three clients that it picked up with its merger with Mojo -- Qantas, Australian Tourist Commission and Koala Springs -- to its Los Angeles office.

Ad Notes. . . .

HILL HOLLIDAY: Upjohn Co., Kalamazoo, Mich., awarded its Motrin IB painkiller account to Hill, Holliday, Connors, Cosmopulos's New York office. Susan Smith, managing director of the shop's New York operations, said the account will bill $30 million this year. Ms. Smith said the win represented the office's largest account victory ever. Upjohn introduced Motrin IB last summer with ads created by Avrett, Free & Ginsberg, New York.

WHO'S NEWS: Ira Matathia was named president and chief executive officer of Chiat/Day/Mojo's Canadian agency in Toronto. Mr. Matathia was previously senior vice president and director of client services.

WCRS: Robin Wight was named chairman of London agency WCRS, a unit of Eurocom WCRS Della Femina Ball. Mr. Wight will continue in his position as joint chairman of EWDB.
