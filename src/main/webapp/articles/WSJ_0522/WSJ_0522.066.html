Kidder to Resume Program Trading For Own Account After Six-Month Halt

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 05/22/90

NEW YORK -- Kidder, Peabody & Co., once the biggest program trader on Wall Street, ended a six-month pullback from the practice. The action is the latest sign that the furor over computer-driven trading strategies continues to wane. Kidder, a unit of General Electric Co., was one of six major brokerage firms that last fall stopped doing stock-index arbitrage for their own accounts, amid a public outcry against program trading. Kidder said it resumed the activity after "extensive conversations" with the New York Stock Exchange and its own brokers and clients.

Index arbitrage involves rapid-fire trades of big baskets of stocks with offsetting trades of stock-index futures and options. It is the most common and controversial form of program trading. Critics have blamed such computer-driven trading for big market swings, including the 1987 stock-market crash and the market's sharp plunge Oct. 13.

Kidder is the second big Wall Street firm to resume index arbitrage for its own account. Bear Stearns Cos. announced a similar resumption last December. In the Big Board's latest weekly statistics, Bear Stearns was the most active Wall Street program trader -- with an average 2.1 million shares a day traded in program-trading strategies, almost all of it involving index arbitrage for its own account. (Kidder was the fifth-biggest program trader; most of its trades were index arbitrage for customers.)

Kidder's decision reflects both the big brokerage firm's quest for profits -- the firm had a net loss of $23 million last year -- as well as a reluctance on the part of the Big Board to take an overly aggressive stand against program trading. While the Big Board has publicly cautioned brokerage firms about disruptive program-trading activity, it hasn't been as forceful as program-trading critics would like.

Indeed, with overall trading volume on the Big Board off about 15% from last year's pace, traders are happy for any volume they can get -- even from program trading.

A Big Board "blue-ribbon panel" on program trading and stock-market volatility has been meeting for about five months, but hasn't yet issued recommendations. Because the panel isn't expected to recommend a major crackdown on program trading, brokerage firms apparently don't fear a backlash from the Big Board if they plunge back into program activity.

Letters and phone calls from many of Kidder's 1,400 brokers helped spur Kidder's program pullback last October, after the Dow Jones Industrial Average plunged 190 points Oct. 13.

Charles Sheehan, Kidder's managing director in charge of retail sales, said that two months ago he asked about 100 of the firm's top-producing brokers about their current views on program trading, and "close to 90%" of those responding supported a resumption of program trading by Kidder. "I think they understand what we're trying to do is position Kidder as a leader in developing modern portfolio management techniques," Mr. Sheehan said.

In March, Kidder lost its head of customer program trading, Joseph R. Schmuckler, who joined Nomura Securities Co. to help Nomura's planned plunge into program trading.

Thomas F. Ryan, Kidder's executive managing director in charge of stock trading, said the firm would take a "controlled, responsible approach" to index arbitrage. "We're willing to bend over here" to avoid disrupting the markets, he said.

Kidder said that in its index arbitrage trading, it would at all times sell stocks only on "upticks" (when a stock is rising), and buy only on "downticks." This approach is meant to avoid accelerating a market move already under way. Kidder also said it would make data on its index arbitrage activity available not only to the Big Board but to "other interested parties."

A prominent program-trading critic was disturbed by Kidder's move.

"These guys aren't helping the financial markets; there's no way in the world it could help the small investors," said Charles Wohlstetter, chairman of Contel Corp., a Big Board-listed company. Mr. Wohlstetter has sharply criticized the exchange for its program-trading stance and has led a loose confederation of chief executives campaigning against program trading in Washington.

At Merrill Lynch & Co., the nation's biggest brokerage firm, a spokesman yesterday said, "We continue not to do index arbitrage for ourselves or for clients," and that there aren't any plans for resuming the activity.
