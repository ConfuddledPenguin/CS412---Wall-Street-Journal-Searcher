Short Interest Jumps on Amex And Big Board --- Varity Corp., Texas Air Led Volume for the Month As Trading Set Records

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 05/22/90

NEW YORK -- Short interest for the month ended May 15 climbed to records on the New York Stock Exchange, which posted a 4.9% rise, and the American Stock Exchange, up 2%. Varity Corp., the Toronto manufacturer of farm equipment and automobile parts, led the Big Board short volumes with 13,145,035 uncovered short shares. Texas Air Corp., the Houston airline holding company, continued to lead the Amex, this time with 8,547,761 shorted shares.

The Big Board reported that short interest outstanding on the May 15 settlement date climbed to 644,549,698 shares from 613,933,904 shares at mid-April. On the Amex, short volume reached 71,997,960 shares from an adjusted 70,555,710 shares a month earlier. The latest figures reflect trades through May 8.

At mid-May 1989, short interest on the Big Board was 528,669,482 shares, indicating a 21% rise, while volume on the American exchange was 43,706,386, a 64% jump.

Traders who sell short borrow stock and sell it, betting that the stock's price will decline and that they can buy the shares back later at a lower price for return to the lender.

Short interest is the number of shares that haven't yet been purchased for return to lenders. Although a substantial short position reflects heavy speculation that a stock's price will decline, some investors consider an increase in short interest bullish because the borrowed shares eventually must be bought back.

Fluctuation in short interest of certain stocks also may be caused partly by arbitraging. The figures occasionally include incomplete transactions in restricted stock.

The level of negative sentiment measured by the Big Board short interest ratio rose to 4.61 from last month's 4.19. The ratio is the number of trading days at the exchange's average daily trading volume that would be required to convert the total short interest position.

Some analysts suggest, however, that the ratio has weakened in value as an indicator because options and other products can be used to hedge short positions.

Two bellwether issues on the Big Board were little changed. Volume in International Business Machines Corp., the Armonk, N.Y., computer company, dropped less than 1% to 4,020,829 shares from 4,053,955 shares, while New York oil giant Exxon Corp.'s short volume eased 1.1% to 5,519,394 shares from 5,581,248 shares.
