U.S. Investigating Commodity Sales To Iraq in Mid-80s

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 05/22/90

WASHINGTON -- The Agriculture Department's inspector general is looking into apparent cash rebates and other irregularities in government-guaranteed commodity sales to Iraq, as an offshoot of the $2 billion Banca Nazionale del Lavoro scandal. Department officials who run the export credit-guarantee program have reviewed U.S. commodity sales totaling $720 million to Iraq in the mid-1980s that allegedly were financed with unauthorized letters of credit issued by the Italian bank's Atlanta branch. The administrative review showed at least two "potential problem areas," the department said yesterday.

The inspector general's office has been asked to investigate "after-sales services" said to have been sought by Iraqi officials from U.S. exporters. Services appear to have involved, among other things, providing cash rebates or discounts to the Iraqi government, using favored Iraqi shipping companies, and supplying truck parts or tires as well as additional commodities, according to a department report.

"Iraq has identified several exporters who apparently provided after-sales service," the report said.

The inquiry will also focus on unusually high prices commanded by exporters involved in the commodity sales. Iraq paid sugar prices $15 to $40 a metric ton higher the world-market price for sugar and at least $10 higher for rice and corn, according to the report. The higher prices may have reflected a perception among exporters that Iraq, which was at war with Iran at the time, was "a high-risk market," the report said.

In some transactions, the report added, exporters apparently violated a credit-guarantee program rule by factoring part of the freight costs into the commodity prices.

The inspector general's office will also examine whether the value of the commodity sales was properly reported when export-credit guarantees were sought from the department's Commodity Credit Corp.

The corporation hasn't incurred any losses as a result of the BNL affair, which is being investigated by a federal grand jury in Atlanta as well as banking regulators. The loans guaranteed by the corporation were part of a "clandestine loan operation" in which BNL officials allegedly booked more than $2 billion in unauthorized and unreported loans to Iraq, the report said.

A spokeswoman for Agriculture Secretary Clayton Yeutter said Iraq bought more than $4.5 billion in U.S. commodities with Commodity Credit guarantees since 1983.

The department's administrative review focused mostly on Entrade Inc., a New York subsidiary of Enka Group, a Turkish firm. The Internal Revenue Service alleged that a former officer of BNL's Atlanta branch received a $289,960 kickback from Entrade. The officer, in a suit filed last fall in federal court in New York to block IRS seizure of his residence and bank account, denied receiving any kickback. Entrade has declined to comment.
