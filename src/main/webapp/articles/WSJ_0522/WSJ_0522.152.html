OTC Focus: Market Ends Higher as Cable TV Stocks Lead Advance; Comcast Corp. Gains 1 1/4

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 05/22/90

Utility and technology shares led the over-the-counter market in a climb slightly brisker than the record-setting Dow Jones industrials. The Nasdaq Composite Index gained 4.58 to 452.89, a 1.02% advance, slightly outpacing the 0.9% move in the Dow Jones Industrial Average. The Nasdaq utility index jumped 2.23% and the Nasdaq 100 index of larger industrial issues, including large technology companies, added 1.62%.

Volume remained active at 169.6 million shares, though down from 191.4 million Friday, which was the busiest this year. Advancing shares again outpaced decliners, 1,138 to 820, as 195 stocks rose to new highs, more than triple yesterday's new lows.

"People are trying to get in on this move," said Robert C. King, head of OTC trading in Atlanta for Robinson Humphrey. The skeptical Mr. King said he continues to be surprised by the strength of the Dow industrials, adding that he doesn't expect the market to go much higher. OTC stock investing was "solid, not frenzied," he said.

William Rothe, managing director of OTC trading at Alex. Brown & Sons in Baltimore, said he thinks "all the players are cautious. I think they'd pull back in a minute if adverse news came out."

Cable television shares gained favor as the industry gathered this week in Atlanta for the National Cable Television Association convention. Speculation that regulatory threats will fade also circulated after this newspaper reported yesterday that moves to slap strict new regulations on the industry are losing momentum.

Topping the day's most active list were Tele-Communications class A shares, up 1 1/8 at 15 7/8 on 4.1 million shares. Other cable gainers included Comcast class A shares, which rose 1 1/4 at 14 5/8, and American Television & Communications, which climbed 1 1/8 to 38 1/4.

As expected, shares of Lotus, Novell and Microsoft were among the day's most active after merger discussions between Lotus and Novell collapsed Friday, eliminating a potential challenge to Microsoft's dominance of the software market. Lotus fell 1 to 33 3/4 on 3 million shares; Novell gained 1 7/8 to 42 on 2.9 million shares, amid speculation other buyers might surface for the Provo, Utah, company. Microsoft, due to unveil its long-awaited Windows 3.0 software today, added 2 to 70 1/4 on 2.2 million shares.

Merger and acquisition news continued to fuel some stock gains: Talman Home Federal Savings & Loan Association rose 1 to 6 7/8; Qume added 1/2 to 6 1/2 and Applebee's International jumped 1 3/4 to 18 1/2.

Talman Home Federal, a Chicago thrift, said it accepted an investor group's $10-a-share acquisition offer. Shareholders will receive $5 a share in cash and a senior subordinated debenture of a new holding to be called Talman Bancorp Inc.

Qume, a Milpitas, Calif., maker of IBM-compatible products, announced a merger with an investor group led by the company's chairman and chief executive officer, David Lee, and Wearnes Technology of Singapore. The agreement is valued at $7.25 a share. The company said it may also seek other offers, subject to the payment of a breakup fee to the investor group. Qume also reported net income for the fourth quarter ended Feb. 28 fell to seven cents a share, including extraordinary items, from 17 cents a year earlier.

Applebee, a Kansas City operator of franchised restaurants, said it has received unsolicited acquisition inquiries. Applebee, which hired Piper Jaffray & Hopwood to evaluate its long-term strategy, said no merger or sale talks currently are under way.

Wholesale Club climbed 2 to 22 3/8 after the Indianapolis wholesaler and distributor of general merchandise said it earned 10 cents a share in the first quarter ended May 5, compared to a year-earlier loss of 15 cents a share.

Shares of two biotechnology companies, Amgen, of Thousand Oaks, Calif., and Cytogen, of Princeton, N.J., continued to climb on speculation that products developed by the companies will be favorably reviewed at this week's meeting in Washington of the American Society of Clinical Oncology.

Clinical trials of Neupogen, Amgen's product that stimulates white blood cells, were reviewed yesterday. Amgen leaped 3 3/4 to 73.

Studies of a Cytogen imaging agent used to detect colorectal cancer are scheduled for review today. Cytogen gained 3/4 to 8 3/8.

Synergen tumbled 1 5/8 to 11 after the Boulder, Colo., drug company announced Friday that the Swiss pharmaceutical giant, Hoffman-La Roche, will no longer support Synergen's research on IL-1ra, a product to treat inflammatory diseases such as arthritis. Synergen said it doesn't expect the loss of Hoffman-La Roche's support to hurt its development schedule for the product and added that it hopes to begin clinical trials later this year.

General Parametrics lost 1/8 to 2 after the Berkeley, Calif., maker of computer graphics products suspended its quarterly dividend because of lower second-quarter sales. General Parametrics also reported a loss for the second quarter ended April 30 of nine cents a share, compared with net income of seven cents a year earlier. The company told investors two weeks ago that it expected to report its first quarterly loss since going public in 1986.

Stanford Telecommunications slid 1 to 6. Friday, the Santa Clara, Calif., communications concern reported a loss for the fourth quarter ended March 31 of 31 cents a share, compared with year-earlier net income of 10 cents a share.
