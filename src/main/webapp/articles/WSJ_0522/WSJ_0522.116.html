Big GM Holder Backs Rise in Pensions For Executives, Rejects Smith for Board

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 05/22/90

DETROIT -- One of General Motors Corp.'s largest shareholders said it is voting for a controversial plan to boost executive pensions but against the re-election of retiring Chairman Roger B. Smith to GM's board. The California Public Employees Retirement System's decision not to back Mr. Smith's re-election isn't "necessarily an anti-Roger vote," said Richard Koppes, general counsel for the system, which owns 6.2 million shares, or just under 1% of GM common stock. "Retiring CEOs should retire. They shouldn't be on the board."

That philosophy got an unusual and unexpected boost from the recently retired chairman of Ford Motor Co. earlier this year. Donald E. Petersen surprised industry watchers when he announced he would renounce his board seat when he left Ford on March 1. Mr. Petersen declared that he didn't want to be looking over the shoulder of his successor, Harold A. Poling.

Such a move is highly unusual for chief executives of American companies, however. At GM, for example, it's standard practice for the chairman to retire upon reaching age 65, but remain on the board for five years after that. Mr. Smith turns 65 on July 12, and his retirement is scheduled for Aug. 1.

GM will announce results of the votes on its pension plan and on the election of directors at its annual meeting Friday.

Mr. Smith, who has presided over plenty of controversy during nearly a decade at the helm of GM, seems destined to leave the chairman's post on the same note. GM is proposing to nearly double his pension, to about $1.2 million a year, and that of some 3,350 other executives by including bonuses, instead of base salary alone, in calculating pension payments.

GM said the company's directors had the power to enact the increase without shareholder approval, but decided to put the matter to a vote.

The plan to boost executive pensions at a total cost of $41.6 million a year has already proved divisive, attracting backers and opponents from GM's largest shareholders. The company argues that its executive pensions rank in the bottom 21% of major industrial corporations.

Recently another large shareholder, the state of Michigan, said it would vote its 8.8 million shares against the pension-increase plan. It couldn't be determined yesterday how Michigan voted on Mr. Smith's re-election to the board.

The activist California pension fund earlier this year had asked GM's directors to provide it with detailed information on the process of selecting Mr. Smith's successor. More recently, the fund had said it was leaning toward voting against the pension increase. So the decision to vote for the boost in pensions but against Mr. Smith's re-election as a director is somewhat of a double surprise.

Mr. Koppes said the California fund decided to back the pension-plan provision to support Chairman-elect Robert C. Stempel. "We hope that Stempel can turn this company around," Mr. Koppes said. "And we don't want to make GM less competitive" in retaining executives.

A GM spokesman said it wouldn't be appropriate to comment on any of the voting. "Each individual shareholder reserves the right to vote as they see fit," he said.
