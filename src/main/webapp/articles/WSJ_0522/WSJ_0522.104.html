H.H. Robertson Agrees to Buy Ceco For $150 Million

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 05/22/90

PITTSBURGH -- H.H. Robertson Co., in a move that will double its size, has agreed to buy closely held Ceco Industries Inc. in a cash and stock transaction valued at about $150 million. In a related move, Frontera S.A., a Swiss investment company, has agreed to invest $40 million to help fund the cash portion of the buy-out. In exchange, Frontera will receive roughly 25%, or between 3.3 million and four million shares of Robertson. After the transaction, Robertson, which currently has about 6.5 million shares outstanding, will have about 13.5 million to 14 million shares.

The move will make Robertson a $1 billion concern and open new, but related, markets for the construction company. It will also improve the cash-strapped Robertson's balance sheet, enabling it to more readily secure bank financing. Robertson said it expects to receive within a week a bank financing commitment letter to fund continuing operations.

Under terms of the transaction, Ceco, of Oakbrook Terrace, Ill., will receive $15 million in cash plus between 3.4 million and 4.1 million shares of common stock, valued at about $50 million. Ceco would own roughly 25% of the new company. Robertson will also assume about $85 million of Ceco's debt.

The agreement will be presented to shareholders for their approval in the third quarter. Robertson said its largest shareholder, First City Financial Corp. with a 25% stake, has agreed to support the buy-out. First City, of Vancouver, British Columbia, is controlled by the Belzberg family.

Robertson, struggling in the depressed construction market, posted losses from 1985 to 1988 and has omitted its dividend since 1985. Robertson expects to realize savings from the combination by streamlining duplicate staffs and operations.

Ceco opens new markets not served by Robertson, including overhead doors for residential and commercial markets and side-hinged, wood and steel doors. Ceco is concentrated in the U.S., but Robertson will undoubtedly begin selling those products overseas, giving the company a broader international product mix.

Gregory Drahuschak, an analyst with Butcher & Singer Inc., Pittsburgh, said the backing of Frontera may help Robertson make inroads in Europe. "Traditionally business in Europe is being done on the basis of who you know -- not necessarily what you do," he said. It also gives Frontera's its first major U.S. investment. The Swiss firm has been interested in a friendly investment in the U.S. construction-materials business.

Wall Street had hoped Robertson would be a target of a buy-out, following news last September that a shareholder group including Douglas A. Kass was interested in buying Robertson for $13 a share. Nothing came of the offer, but since that time the Kass group has boosted its stake to 15.4%. Mr. Kass said he met with a Robertson representative regarding the transaction, but didn't have any comment.

Robert Kanters, an analyst with Legg Mason, Inc., Pittsburgh, said he believes the stock fell because Robertson seems to be a less likely takeover target and investors know little about Ceco's balance sheet.

Jack Hatcher, chairman and chief executive officer of Robertson, will retain those positions.
