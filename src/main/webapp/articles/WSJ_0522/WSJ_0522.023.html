Letters to the Editor: Lithuania: Morality vs. Pragmatism

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/22/90

I was so dismayed at the results of your public surveys on Lithuania (page one, April 19). The U.S. public seems to think that supporting Mikhail Gorbachev is more important than the rights of the Lithuanian people to self-determination and that U.S. support of Lithuania would be some kind of interference in Soviet domestic affairs. This confusion can be cleared up by an understanding of Russian history. Conservatives sometimes call the U.S.S.R. the Soviet empire because the Soviet Union is indeed a colonial empire, the same as Czarist Russia in all but name, and the last surviving such empire in the world. Just like the British and the French empires, it acquired much of its territory by military conquest during the 100 years from the late 18th century to the late 19th.

During the era of decolonization in the 1950s and 1960s there was a general public consensus that empires had to be dissolved because most colonies had the right to independence. But partly because that movement was originally leftist inspired, everyone forgot about the Soviet empire.

It is time to recognize the necessity of decolonization. Not because we want to "cause trouble for Gorbachev," but because there is a moral obligation for the West, including the U.S., to support freedom and independence for the captive nationalities of the "Prison of nations."

Eiketsu Hirose

Livonia, Mich.

Congratulations on the thorough and objective reporting of the situation and activities in Lithuania. Your excellent report was perhaps lacking only in some historic perspective to help alleviate the "tradition of xenophobia" of the American people by revisiting the events of the holocaust in Ukraine in the mid-1920s, when some 10 million Ukrainian peasants were starved to death by our onetime ally, Josef Stalin, because of their resistance to agricultural collectivization. This was accomplished by cutting off supplies to the region and by a blockade not too dissimilar to what is now being implemented by our "entrusted" future business partner Mikhail Gorbachev. Several of the Ukraine blockade masterminds and Stalin's henchmen, including Lazar Kaganovich, still reside in Moscow on relatively comfortable pensions.

Ivars Reimanis

Phoenix, Ariz.

---

I fear that the current Lithuanian course of action could derail the reforms taking place and could give Soviet hardliners the chance to retake power. Lithuania, unlike the Warsaw Pact countries, is actually a part of the Soviet Union; our administration disavows so because of the way it came about.

Lithuania was an independent, sovereign, self-governing country until it was invaded and conquered by the Soviet Union in 1940, and subsequently annexed. Today it forms a substantial part of the Soviet Union's western frontier, and is probably indispensable to the Soviets' national-security strategy, at least for a while. The difference and extreme delicacy of this situation might be better seen with a convenient analogy.

Hawaii was an independent, sovereign, self-governing country until it was invaded and conquered by the U.S. in 1893, and subsequently annexed. Today it forms a substantial part of our western frontier and is indispensable to our national security. If native Hawaiians organized a Congress, proclaimed their independence, seceded from the union, and called loudly upon the world for recognition and support, what would our reaction be? What options would we have? I think you see the point.

A more promising course of action for the Lithuanians would be to back down for now, be a "good little state" for two or three years, until the political and economic reforms are on a firmer footing.

Randall P. Shook

Boiling Springs, N.C.

---

I will not believe that 61% of Americans would rather maintain friendly ties with the murderous U.S.S.R. than support freedom for Lithuania. Something must have gone wrong with the poll. I have not found a single person who agrees.

Americans who wave the flag and celebrate our Constitution and the anniversary of our freedom should at least prefer to support freedom for the people of Lithuania, whose bondage the U.S. has never recognized. Those same Americans should at least question friendship with a nation that is guilty of murdering millions of its own and other people, and that has antagonized and opposed the U.S. for more than 40 years. This poll, coming on the heels of Soviet admission of guilt for the Katyn Forest massacre, reveals much about changing American values.

A nation that can find nothing worth fighting for is doomed.

Clement R. Coggin

Ocean Springs, Miss.
