Technology: Baxter to Sell an IV Device That Uses Fewer Needles

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/22/90

DEERFIELD, Ill. -- Baxter International Inc. said it will market nationally a new medical intravenous-therapy device designed to sharply reduce the number of needles used in hospitals. The equipment is aimed at reducing accidental needle-stick injuries that can transmit infectious diseases, such as acquired immune deficiency syndrome and hepatitis, to hospital workers.

The device, called the InterLink IV Access System, is currently being used by more than 30 hospitals and evaluated by about 200 more, Baxter said. It includes a small, blunt plastic tube, called a cannula, that forces medication through a companion injection cap.

The cannula replaces the traditional steel needle in withdrawing drugs from vials or in administering medication into an intravenous-administration set. While the cannula can't be used to inject medication directly through the skin, it replaces most of the needles used in IV therapy.

The system "is a very interesting device. It is the first IV-connecting device that eliminates a needle" and could be used to replace needles in "a very high proportion" of cases, said Janine Jagger, an assistant professor of neurosurgery at the University of Virginia Medical School and an expert in the problem of needle-stick injuries. "It's a very large step forward" in increasing the safety of hospital workers, she said.
