What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/22/90

ANTI-ISRAELI UNREST SWEPT the Mideast in protests over workers' slayings. Israeli soldiers killed three Palestinians and wounded as many as 95 others in clashes sparked by Sunday's massacre of seven Arab laborers by an Israeli civilian near Tel Aviv. Despite curfews imposed by the army, stone-throwing rioters defied the troops in the occupied Gaza Strip and in Israel itself. Hundreds of people were reported injured, including 10 tourists aboard a bus in Jordan who were attacked by a Palestinian gunman. The violence was considered the fiercest since the early days of the anti-Israeli uprising two years ago.

The U.S. said that it was disturbed by the violence in Israel, and that the lack of a peace process in the Mideast increases the likelihood of unrest.

---

India's security forces opened fire on a crowd of about 100,000 Moslems bearing the body of a slain religious leader in the disputed Kashmir region. At least 41 people were killed and more than 200 injured, witnesses said. Government officials blamed "unidentified militants" for the violence.

---

A strike by pro-Moscow forces failed to win widespread support in Estonia as many workers ignored a call to walk off their jobs to protest the Baltic republic's secession drive. In Lithuania, lawmakers drafted a new offer aimed at breaking Vilnius's deadlock with Moscow over the republic's March 11 proclamation of independence.

The Kremlin plans to double food prices as part of economic-restructuring measures that are scheduled to begin next year. A preliminary draft of the economic program, obtained by the Associated Press, calls for a five-year transition to a market economy.

---

Foreign observers in Romania differed over whether Sunday's first multiparty elections in 53 years were free of fraud. Partial results showed interim President Iliescu headed for a landslide victory, with his National Salvation Front winning in parliamentary balloting. But some irregularities were cited by a 60-member monitoring group.

---

Prosecutors in Miami told a federal court judge that the government agreed to pay for Noriega's defense against drug charges. Separately, the U.S. attorneys acknowledged that the deposed Panamanian leader received payments from U.S. intelligence agencies, but disputed a defense figure of $11 million.

---

Bush pledged to continue pushing for an accord with the Soviet Union on a treaty to cut troops in Europe. The issue wasn't resolved during Secretary of State Baker's arms-control talks last week in Moscow. Gorbachev, meanwhile, is sending arms experts to Washington this week to resume pre-summit talks on long-range weapons.

---

The Supreme Court let stand a federal court order issued in 1989 that permanently bars the anti-abortion group Operation Rescue from blocking access at abortion clinics in New York City. The justices allowed a similar ban in Atlanta last week, and there are such orders against the group in a number of cities.

---

A bipartisan congressional group found that 14 of El Salvador's 15 top military officers, including the defense minister, have commanded military units that committed human-rights abuses. The report was released as the House prepared to vote today on a plan to withhold 50% of U.S. military aid to El Salvador.

---

About 80 AIDS activists were arrested at the National Institutes of Health in Bethesda, Md., during a protest calling for more government research into the disease. The demonstration came amid a host of fiscal and political problems at the medical organization.

---

HHS proposed rules to regulate the quality and accuracy of medical tests performed in the nation's clinical laboratories. The proposal, prompted by frequent inaccuracies in Pap-smear results, requires that most labs, including those in doctors' offices, be federally certified.

---

West German Social Democrats urged changes in a newly signed economic treaty with East Germany, a move that could delay the merger of the two German economic systems by July 2. The main opposition party demanded negotiations with Chancellor Kohl on modifications to the pact.

---

Black youths in South Africa erected barricades in Thabong township following a clash between protesters and police that left at least seven blacks dead and about 40 injured. The unrest came as Pretoria and the African National Congress began studying plans for releasing political prisoners.

---

North and South Yemen ratified a merger plan, and a formal declaration of a single Yemeni state was set for today. The combination of the pro-Western North with the socialist South would create the most populous nation in the Arabian peninsula.

---

Gunmen in Colombia killed a senator who was closely allied to presidential front-runner Cesar Gaviria. The Bogota government blamed cocaine traffickers for the slaying in the city of Medellin, saying the shooting was part of a wave of violence that has flared before Sunday's nationwide elections.
