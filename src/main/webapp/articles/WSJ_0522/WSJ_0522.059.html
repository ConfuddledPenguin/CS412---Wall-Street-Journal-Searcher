Raytheon Wins Army Contract Of $414 Million --- Ground-Control Equipment For Italy Would Help Firm Prevent Layoffs

  • Author: Andy Pasztor and Gary Putka
  • Date: 05/22/90

WASHINGTON -- In a move intended to prevent layoffs at Raytheon Co.'s missile-making operations, the Army awarded the firm a $414 million contract to supply certain ground-control equipment to Italy. The unusual contract calls for the U.S to buy support equipment for 20 planned Patriot ground-to-air missile systems in Italy -- even though the Italian government hasn't yet appropriated funds for the missiles the equipment is meant to be used with. The ground equipment is manufactured at the same Raytheon facilities in Andover, Mass., where the company builds Patriot missiles.

Since the Army has proposed phasing out purchases of the missiles during the next few years, Raytheon has pushed hard to win U.S. approval of the contract involving Italy. Without the Army contract, Raytheon has said it would be forced to lay off hundreds of workers.

In response to questions, a spokesman for the Army's missile command said that one of the major reasons for signing the contract was to prevent disruptions or layoffs at Raytheon's missile-making operations. Senior Army acquisition officials previously used similar arguments to defend the Italian contract, and the company itself has used such arguments to garner congressional support to pay for the ground-control equipment.

The Pentagon's decision capped another successful lobbying effort by Raytheon, one of the few major defense contractors that has avoided layoffs from defense belt-tightening. The company had said that approximately 1,200 of the 6,000 workers at its Andover plant could have received pink slips during the next few years unless the Italian project was funded. Some Andover production lines building Patriot equipment for U.S. air defense needs would have stopped working in 1991.

"This helps stabilize our production capabilities as we look forward to future international sales," said a Raytheon spokesman.

Raytheon plans to license production of the missiles in Italy by an Italian consortium. Without the Army's approval of funding for the ground equipment, however, the roughly $5 billion project in Italy would have collapsed.

The U.S. and Italy signed a memorandum of understanding about the Patriot systems two years ago, but Italy still hasn't provided final funding for any of the planned missile purchases.

The Army said 1984 was the last time it approved such a contract to supply ground-control equipment for U.S.-made missiles to a foreign country. That contract involved the West German military.

A person familiar with the Patriot negotiations said part of the Pentagon's decision to pay for the ground equipment was based on fears that Raytheon would raise prices for the equipment for Italy purchased by the U.S if Andover production lines had to be closed down and later reopened.

Late last month, the Pentagon rescinded an earlier decision to cut procurement of Hawk missiles, also made at Andover, saving another 850 Raytheon jobs.

In other contract actions, Rockwell International Corp. received a $10.8 million Air Force contract for B-1B electronic test equipment.
