Surveyed Economists See Expansion Through 1991

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/22/90

WASHINGTON -- The chances of recession have declined within the past three months and the current business expansion will break the postwar record for longevity, according to a survey of business economists. Not only did 82% of the panel forecast no recession through 1991, but two-thirds of the group foresaw no recession at all during the next three years. If that prediction were to materialize, the current expansion -- now 90 months -- would break the February 1961-December 1969 record for the longest postwar period of economic growth.

The quarterly survey of 68 members of the National Association of Business Economists reflects forecasts made in April and May.

Although there will be no recession, the economists say, the outlook for 1990 is for slower economic growth than in 1989. They expect the economy to grow an inflation-adjusted 2% this year, compared with 3% in 1989. For 1991, they expect growth of 2.4%.

The outlook for inflation this year is slightly bleaker, too. The panel is forecasting a 4.7% rise in the consumer price index this year. In a winter survey, they projected a 4.2% inflation rate. In 1989, consumer prices rose 4.6%.

The panel said its forecasters are significantly less optimistic about growth and interest rates than the Bush administration.
