International: Export-Credit Group Set to Discuss Delays In Soviet Payments

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/22/90

LONDON -- The Berne Union -- a group of export-credit agencies from 32 nations -- is moving to review coverage of the Soviet Union in the face of persistent payment delays by Moscow on commercial-trade transactions. The union -- which has 40 members, a quarter from the developed world -- will discuss potential action on Soviet export-credit coverage at its annual meeting starting Sunday in Italy, said a spokesman for Malcolm Stephens, chief executive officer of Britain's Export Credit Guarantee Department and president of the union.

Discussions at the six-day meeting will particularly focus on whether the union should move to hold formal talks with the Soviets about the delays and on remedial actions that may be taken to avoid future payment problems.

Export-credit guarantees haven't been a favored route of import-financing by Moscow, nor have they been affected by payment delays. The notion of formal talks at least signals growing concerns among government-related credit agencies that the situation may deteriorate.

Separately, industry representatives in Tokyo told the Kyodo news agency that Moscow has asked Japanese creditors for a one-year deferment in its payments for recently purchased steel pipes. Kyodo said the creditors rejected the request but said they would accept a deferment of 180 days in exchange for a letter of guarantee. But, the representatives told the news agency, the Soviets "are reluctant to provide such a letter."

London bankers said some Western nations favor using the Berne Union as a medium through which bilateral discussions with the Soviets over remedial action on delayed trade payments can be focused.
