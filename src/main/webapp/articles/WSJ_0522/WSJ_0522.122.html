Procter & Gamble Expects Strong Fiscal Results in '90

  • Author: Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 05/22/90

CINCINNATI -- Procter & Gamble Co. said it expects fiscal 1990 results to be strong, with gains in the laundry, shampoo and diaper categories. Edwin L. Artzt, chairman and chief executive officer, said he expects that detergents will show a 15% gain in volume sold, while shampoos and diapers will climb 12% and 10% respectively. He wouldn't comment specifically about earnings.

In the fiscal year ended June 30, P&G had net income of $1.21 billion, or $7.12 a share, on sales of $21.4 billion.

Speaking to financial analysts, Mr. Artzt said P&G's troubled food and beverage division should "substantially improve." Specifically, he pointed out that P&G has plans to improve Crisco shortening and oil and Duncan Hines baking mixes within a year.

Citrus Hill orange juice remains a problem, he said, noting that part of the trouble has been costs and marketing missteps. "We've lost a bundle there and we don't have it fixed yet," Mr. Artzt said. He likened the eventual turnaround to P&G's success in Japan: "We fixed that one and we can fix this one," he said.

The greatest uncertainty in the food division is P&G's fat substitute, olestra, which hasn't received Food and Drug Administration approval. Mr. Artzt said P&G narrowed the scope of its petition to speed clearance. He declined to be specific but officials noted the narrowing involves concentrating on a couple products that would eventually contain the substitute.

Unlike Monsanto Co.'s Simplesse, which is made from egg proteins, olestra is a more complicated blend that can be used in hot and cold foods. Mr. Artzt said it will take "a couple years" after the FDA approves the substitute until P&G factories can "start pumping it out." Simplesse recently won FDA approval.

"Simplesse is easy to do if you've got enough eggs," he said.

International expansion continues to be a priority. The company is in discussions with joint-venture partners in Eastern Europe and is trying to establish a Moscow office. "We won't go rushing in," Mr. Artzt said. Two concerns he has are finding sources of currency and the lack of an adequate distribution system.

In manufacturing, the executive predicted increased capital spending to expand plant capacity around the world.

In advertising, Mr. Artzt plans to focus more on advertisments than promotions in the future because the balance of spending has "gotten a little out of whack."

One new ad campaign he showed analysts was for Hawaiian Punch, which P&G recently acquired. The campaign reintroduces the familiar "Punchy" character.

Analysts were generally upbeat about Mr. Artzt's remarks. But they lamented the lack of detail in his forecasts. "P&G is philosophically bullish and financially vague," said analyst Hugh Zurkuhlen of Salomon Brothers. "But there's an internal confidence that wasn't there before."
