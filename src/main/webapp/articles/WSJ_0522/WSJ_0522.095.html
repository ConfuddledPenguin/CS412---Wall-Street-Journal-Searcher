Monday's Markets: Stocks Gain 24.77 Points, Hit Record --- Bond Prices Finish With Slight Increase; Dollar Mostly Higher

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 05/22/90

NEW YORK -- Investors, concluding that interest rates have stabilized for the time being, pushed stock prices higher but nudged bonds up only a little. The Dow Jones Industrial Average jumped 24.77 points to a record 2844.68 in active trading. Long-term bond prices rose an eighth of a point, or $1.25 for each $1,000 of face amount. The dollar ended mostly higher after profit-taking trimmed gains made during the morning.

Analysts said investors' fears of higher interest rates and inflation seem to have abated in the past few days. "We think interest rates have peaked, and the market suspects the same thing," said James Solloway, chief economist at Argus Research. Among other things, lower bond yields makes stocks more attractive.

Mr. Solloway said he expects bond yields to fall to 8% or lower by September, which should benefit such interest-rate sensitive businesses as well-run thrifts and utilities, including the regional Bell operating companies.

The perception that inflation is less of a problem was helped by expectations for two economic reports this week. The first, on Thursday, is predicted to show a weaker first-quarter economy than first indicated. The second, on Friday, is expected to show a slowdown in the growth of personal income last month from March.

Leading the stock market higher yesterday were technology issues -- viewed as undervalued and poised for new growth -- and consumer stocks, which tend to do better when inflation slows.

The rally took a while to get started. Stocks opened unenthusiastically because bond prices were down somewhat. "When bond prices got even and started moving ahead a little bit, stocks overreacted, and that seemed to give the green light to institutional money managers," said A.C. Moore, research director at Argus.

Bond prices retreated in the morning because of a rumor that the federal budget surplus in April, the prime tax-collection month, was smaller than forecast. The surplus actually turned out higher than expected, but the news failed to spark more than a mild move higher.

The rebound in stocks also was helped by the dollar's solid gains against the mark early in the day. Peter Canelo, chief market strategist at Bear Stearns, suggested investors may be starting to pay a little less attention to bonds and more to the dollar.

"The dollar appears to be coming back from lows in the past few days," Mr. Canelo said, "and that should help bonds, but stocks already are responding favorably." Stocks held most of their gains even after profit-taking trimmed the dollar's advance.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose in active trading. Volume rose to 166.3 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where advancing issues swamped decliners 997 to 538.

Bond prices gained slightly. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond dipped to 8.68%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6626 marks and 153.72 yen, compared with 1.6477 marks and 153 yen Friday. But the dollar fell against the British pound.
