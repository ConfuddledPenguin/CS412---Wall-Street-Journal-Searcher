Greyhound Lines Hopes to Redeem Portion of Debt

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 05/22/90

DALLAS -- In another effort to stave off a bankruptcy filing, strikebound Greyhound Lines Inc. offered to redeem about $225 million in face amount of debt for about $101.6 million in cash. But how the cash-strapped unit of GLI Holding Co. will finance the offer remains uncertain. J. Michael Doyle, Greyhound senior vice president and chief financial officer, said Goldman, Sachs & Co., its financial adviser in connection with the offering, has held preliminary discussions with several parties but no source of funding has been found. "The financing is not clear," said Mr. Doyle. "With our company in our situation, it's pretty tough."

The debt-burdened bus company has been battered by an 11-week-old strike by 9,400 employees represented by the Amalgamated Transit Union. For the first quarter of this year, Greyhound posted a $55.8 million loss on revenue of $172.5 million. For the same period a year ago, Greyhound lost $14.4 million on revenue of $223.5 million.

Although the company has continued to operate with a replacement work force, Mr. Doyle estimated that the strike will ultimately cost Greyhound between $75 million and $100 million in lost revenue and additional costs for security, legal fees and new driver training.

If Greyhound can find funding for the tender offer, it plans to offer $540 in cash for each $1,000 face value of its 13% senior notes due in 1995 and $275 in cash for each $1,000 face value for up to 85% of its 12.5% senior subordinated notes due 1997. The company is seeking to redeem up to 85% of each issue.

The company said the offer is contingent upon a majority of the face amount outstanding of each of the two debt issues being tendered and upon the holders of a majority of the outstanding notes consenting to the elimination of many of the covenants of the old debt. Greyhound said the offer is also contingent upon the company being able to raise an undisclosed amount of money to "satisfy its immediate working capital needs."

Greyhound has already missed a $9.8 million interest payment, due May 15, on the 13% notes.

With those notes trading at deeply discounted levels in recent weeks, bond analysts said the offer might be attractive to holders who bought the notes at distressed levels. Those who purchased the notes at par value, however, may have a harder time accepting the tender offer.

"They have to make the decision as to whether this is the best they are going to get," said Ken Frankel, an analyst with Drake Securities.

"It's a very disappointing situation all around," said a longtime institutional holder of the Greyhound bonds. "My only regret is that I didn't sell out in December."
