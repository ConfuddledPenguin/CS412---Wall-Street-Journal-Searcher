Cypress Countersues After Patent Lawsuit By Advanced Micro

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/22/90

SAN JOSE, Calif. -- Cypress Semiconductor Corp. filed a countersuit against Advanced Micro Devices Inc., alleging that Advanced Micro attempted to monopolize a key computer-chip market through "fraud, deceit, breach of contract and baseless litigation." The claim, in which Cypress is seeking in excess of $30 million in damages, was in response to an August 1988 patent-infringement suit by Advanced Micro against Cypress and several other chip makers, covering certain specialty chips known as programmable logic devices, used in laptop computers, among other products.

Cypress previously denied the charge and asserted that the patents at issue were invalid and, in any event, covered by a 1983 license agreement between Cypress and Monolithic Memories Inc., which Advanced Micro acquired in 1988.

The Cypress counterclaim, filed here in federal court, alleges that Advanced Micro's acquisition of Monolithic Memories was part of a plan to obtain Cypress technology and monopolize the market in violation of state and federal antitrust law. Cypress also alleges that Monolithic Memories defrauded it as part of the earlier exchange because Monolithic Memories knew that the patent rights it purported to license were being challenged in court.

Cypress, which has been the target of some 20 lawsuits over the past seven years and has settled them all, said the counterclaim was "the first time Cypress has gone on the legal offensive."

"I got a bag of worthless patents, and they got $10 million," said T.J. Rodgers, president and chief executive officer of Cypress, noting that Monolithic Memories received Cypress stock as part of the 1983 transaction. "I want my chips back, and I want my technology back and I want damages."

Advanced Micro, based in Sunnyvale, Calif., called the countersuit "the standard response of a patent infringer" and termed the fraud claim "bizarre."
