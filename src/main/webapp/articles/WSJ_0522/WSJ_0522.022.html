World Bank Considers 2 Loans to China That Could Set Off Protests in Congress

  • Author: Greg Hitt
  • Date: 05/22/90

WASHINGTON -- The World Bank is considering two loans to China that congressional leaders say could set off a new round of protest over Beijing's human-rights policy and endanger support for the Third World lending institution. The loans -- for a $300 million forestry project and a $150 million transportation project -- are scheduled for consideration May 29. Approval of either of the measures, less than a year after the military crackdown in Tiananmen Square, would seriously lessen the chances for passage of legislation needed to replenish the bank's soft-loan window, bank supporters say.

House Banking Committee Chairman Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas) said he favors holding up a bill authorizing a $3.2 billion contribution to the bank's International Development Association, which makes no-interest loans to the world's poorest countries, including China.

A separate bill appropriating the money likely won't be blocked but would face a difficult time in Congress.

"If the World Bank proceeds, it will weaken its position on the Hill," said Rep. Matthew McHugh (D., N.Y.), a member of the Appropriations Committee who supports the bank. "A realistic assessment of the politics here is that those loans will make our job much more difficult."

World Bank lending to China was halted last year after the Tiananmen Square incident, but resumed this spring when the U.S. indicated it would support measures that target basic human needs.

Three loans totaling $140 million have gone toward earthquake assistance, job training and rural development.

U.S. policy is to support loans for "the poorest and most vulnerable people in China," said John Niehuss, deputy assistant Treasury secretary for international policy.

Acting on that policy, the administration opposed the transportation loan when it was up for consideration earlier this spring, organizing enough opposition among the Group of Seven major industrial nations that it wasn't formally put before the bank's board of directors.

U.S. opposition continues on that project, although the G-7 coalition appears to have fractured somewhat, enhancing the loan's chances of approval, congressional and World Bank sources say.

The administration position is less clear on the forestry project. In recent briefings on Capitol Hill, the administration indicated it might support the measure because an argument can be made that it creates jobs.

That view isn't widely shared on Capitol Hill, where dissatisfaction lingers about President Bush's stance toward China. Approval of either loan would almost certainly upset the bank's political base in Congress.

In particular, liberal Democrats, who make up the bank's primary backers in Congress, would face the choice of either sticking with it or opposing it to express displeasure with China's human-rights policy. California's Nancy Pelosi is one liberal Democrat who has already made up her mind.

"If those loans go through, it would say that one year after Tiananmen Square, it didn't make a difference," said Rep. Pelosi, adding that expanded World Bank lending could prompt other countries to resume bilateral aid to China. "Those loans will be subject to very strong scrutiny. They're going to have a fight."

Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell criticized resumption of World Bank lending to China earlier this year. The Maine Democrat has called for a tougher U.S. policy. "Our moral stance has been compromised," he said.
