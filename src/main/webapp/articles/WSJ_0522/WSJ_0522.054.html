Politics & Policy: Sen. Mitchell's Position on Proposed Ban On Assault Weapons Remains a Mystery

  • Author: Timothy Noah
  • Date: 05/22/90

WASHINGTON -- As the Senate prepares to consider a proposed ban on domestic manufacture of military-style assault weapons for civilian use, one senator is conspicuously silent amid the debate: Majority Leader George Mitchell. As is usually the case in gun-control debates, the proposed assault-gun ban generally is dividing Congress along regional and partisan lines. Democrats from the Northeast and Midwest -- including Senate Judiciary Committee Chairman Joseph Biden of Delaware and Sen. Howard Metzenbaum of Ohio -- support a ban; they cite use of such weapons by drug traffickers.

Republicans from the West and South -- such as Sen. Strom Thurmond of South Carolina -- side with the National Rifle Association and other gun-lobby groups in opposing the ban; they argue that the term "assault weapon" is poorly defined in the legislation.

But these dividing lines are far from absolute. The assault-weapon legislation was drafted by Sen. Dennis DeConcini (D., Ariz.), a longstanding NRA supporter from the Southwest. Sen. DeConcini says he acted only after failing to get the NRA to draft language it could accept on the issue.

Then there's Sen. Mitchell. The assault-weapon issue puts the Maine Democrat in an especially awkward bind. As majority leader, he helped Sen. Biden move the larger crime bill, which includes the proposed assault-gun ban, through the slow-moving Senate. But as a legislator who represents a considerable number of Maine sportsmen, he once boasted to constituents that he had a "perfect voting record on issues of concern to the NRA" until he voted to ban plastic guns in 1987.

Sen. Mitchell's position on the assault-gun ban is something of a mystery. According to spokeswoman Diane Dewhirst, the majority leader hasn't issued any statements either on Sen. DeConcini's proposal or on a more restrictive ban proposed by Sen. Metzenbaum. (Despite repeated efforts yesterday to reach him, Sen. Mitchell himself wasn't available for comment.)

Neither Sen. DeConcini nor Sen. Metzenbaum seems unhappy about this apparent neglect. Indeed, both praise the majority leader for assisting their efforts. Sen. DeConcini says he's grateful Sen. Mitchell got the crime bill to the floor; Sen. Metzenbaum says Sen. Mitchell will bring the Ohio senator's more restrictive proposal to the Senate floor as an amendment.

But neither senator is able to guess how Sen. Mitchell will vote on the gun issue. "I don't know where he stands," says Sen. DeConcini. "I'm not sure how he's going to vote," offers Sen. Metzenbaum. The gun lobby is similarly in the dark. "I don't think the senator has made up his mind," offers NRA lobbyist James Baker. "How Sen. Mitchell comes down is not something that I can predict."

Mr. Baker said he hadn't personally lobbied Sen. Mitchell on the issue, but added: "I can assure you that Sportsman's Alliance of Maine has." According to Mr. Baker, that group is "adamantly opposed" to the assault-weapon ban. Spokesmen for the group, based in Augusta, couldn't be reached for comment.

Gail Hoffman, legislative director for Handgun Control Inc., a group that lobbies for gun control, says she spoke to Sen. Mitchell in January about the assault-weapons ban. "I think he found things that we had to say interesting," she says. "But he was very noncommittal."

One reason for Sen. Mitchell's silence may be genuine ambivalence. "I don't believe in gun control," Sen. Mitchell told the Calais, Maine, Advertiser during a 1988 campaign swing.

But after the senator failed to support the NRA on plastic guns in 1987, the NRA sent out a mailing to its members in Maine saying Mr. Mitchell had been fooled by a "media lie" on plastic guns. The charge angered Mr. Mitchell, who told the Maine newspaper that the NRA had made him a target of a "campaign of misinformation."

Then again, Sen. Mitchell's silence may just be the latest demonstration that discretion is the better part of valor. "Mitchell is not buffaloed by them more than anyone else is," says Sen. DeConcini, "but you don't just blow the NRA away as some kind of kooks or something."
