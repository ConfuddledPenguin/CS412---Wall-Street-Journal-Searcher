Bank of Boston Corp. Disbands Division Specializing in Leveraged Buy-Out Loans

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 05/22/90

BOSTON -- Bank of Boston Corp., one of the leading bank lenders for leveraged buy-outs, has quietly disbanded a division that specialized in such transactions. While Bank of Boston has profited in the past from leveraged transactions, the outlook is cloudier for some of the recent deals it has helped finance, such as the acquisitions of G. Heileman Brewing Co., Friendly Ice Cream and Prime Computer Inc.

The action, which bank spokesmen described as part of an ongoing reorganization, underlines a growing wariness by banks toward leveraged buy-outs. Many banks have recently taken a more conservative approach to these loans -- partly because of concerns that a slowing economy could spell trouble for highly leveraged companies, but also because of pressure from federal regulators and worried investors.

Peter Manning, Bank of Boston's chief financial officer, stressed that the bank intends to continue making loans for so-called highly leveraged transactions, despite the dissolution of the bank's acquisition-finance division. The division, which included about 16 officers, made leveraged buy-out loans to midsized companies that didn't fall into the bailiwick of the bank's specialized-loan divisions.

Those specialized divisions will continue to seek acquisition loans in industries that the bank has traditionally served, such as cable television, Mr. Manning said.

But Mr. Manning acknowledged that the bank's decision last fall to tighten its criteria for highly leveraged transactions, had left the acquisition-finance division with "a thinner net" for landing deals. For example, the bank now insists on keeping no more than $50 million of any individual loan, selling off the remaining amount. Previously, the bank would keep as much as $75 million of a loan.

Some leveraged buy-out specialists say that, while many banks have become more conservative about leveraged buy-out lending, the pattern is particularly marked at Bank of Boston. The company, in its first quarter earnings report, noted that it made no new commitments for financing of highly leveraged transactions during the period. Partly as a result, the bank's portfolio of highly leveraged transaction financings shrank to $4.8 billion at March 31 from $5.3 billion at Dec. 31. But the smaller total still represents one of the biggest portfolios in the nation -- especially as a proportion of the bank's total assets of $37 billion.

The acquisition-finance division's officers, as well as its assets, have been reassigned to other lending divisions. Gail Long, who had headed the acquisition-finance division, has since been put in charge of the larger national division, handling corporate loans outside New England, a spokesman said.

Some of the assets from the acquisition-finance division were placed in the bank's loan-workout division, the spokesman said. But he and Mr. Manning insisted that the proportion of troubled loans wasn't notably high, and that the quality of the portfolio wasn't a reason for the decision to disband the division. The division in previous years has produced strong profits for the company, Mr. Manning said.
