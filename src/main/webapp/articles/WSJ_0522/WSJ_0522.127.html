Clinical Lab Tests Targeted For More Federal Scrutiny

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 05/22/90

WASHINGTON -- In a move designed to boost public confidence in medical tests, the Department of Health and Human Services proposed new rules to certify the quality of the nation's clinical laboratories. But the American Medical Association immediately complained that the proposed rules represent a new layer of complex and unnecessary regulation that will drive up the cost of medical care.

The 63-page proposal published in the Federal Register yesterday requires that most labs, including those in doctors' offices, be federally certified. The proposal puts labs into three categories, depending on the complexity of the testing involved and the danger of the disease being screened.

"Complex and high-risk tests, such as Pap smears, or simpler tests with some risk, such as cholesterol screening, will be subject to federal quality and proficiency standards," explained Gail Wilensky, administrator of the Health Care Financing Administration, the HHS unit that oversees Medicare and Medicaid. "Simple, low-risk tests will be on a waiver list, and labs performing only these tests will be exempt from regulation." These simple tests include urine pregnancy tests and certain screening for sickle-cell anemia and infectious mononucleosis.

Dr. James Todd, the AMA's acting executive vice president, said the new rules are "just going to drive up costs unbelievably." He predicted that many doctors will abandon providing lab services to their patients, a move that could reduce services, particularly in rural areas.

The rules were required by the Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments of 1988, which Congress passed after disclosures in The Wall Street Journal of frequent inaccuracies in the results of Pap smears taken to check for cervical cancer, which kills 6,000 women a year.

When adopted, the new rules will cover more than 300,000 labs, a huge expansion of current federal regulations that cover some 12,000 labs. All labs that aren't waived from regulation will be inspected every two years by the HCFA. Labs that fail to comply with federal standards for quality control, personnel qualifications and proficiency testing could lose their certificates and face civil penalties.

Under the law, only labs that are certified or waived from certification will be able to receive payments from Medicare and Medicaid, the federal health programs for the elderly and the poor that pay about 40% of the nation's clinical lab costs.

Dr. Todd complained that the proposals interpret the law too rigidly. "The waiver process is complicated, and the number of tests that can be waivered is relatively small," he said. "They could have applied the law much more conservatively than they have."

The public has until Aug. 20 to comment on the proposals. Mrs. Wilensky said the list of 28 tests subject to waiver was compiled "after careful consultation with the Public Health Service and a host of outside experts. Nonetheless, we are eager to receive additional comments."
