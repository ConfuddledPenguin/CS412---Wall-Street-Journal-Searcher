Heard on the Street: Brazil Fund, With Its Zigzag Performance, Is Just as Much a Bet on Politics as on a Market

  • Author: Stanley W. Angrist
  • Date: 05/22/90

Do the lambada? Why bother when you can wind up equally breathless by owning a few shares of Brazil Fund. Wild zigzags come with the territory for investors in this two-year-old stock fund, which trades on the New York Stock Exchange and invests in Brazilian companies. It offers a reminder that investors ignore at their peril: Owning a country fund is just as much a bet on politics as it is on any particular stock market.

In mid-March, Brazil froze bank accounts as part of an all-out attack on inflation, officially estimated at 1,765% last year. Lo and behold, inflation fell. The government puts it at 3% for the 30 days through April 15, though some analysts thought it was above 40%. Meanwhile Brazil Fund shares are at 10 1/8, up from a low of 8 1/2 earlier this year.

Some investors "must believe the government's plan is going to work," says Michael Porter, who follows publicly traded investment funds for Smith Barney in New York. He notes that the shares in Brazil Fund's portfolio now trade at a modest four times last year's corporate earnings, and only about half book value.

Lest all this sound like an unbelievable bargain, Ed Games, the fund's manager at Scudder, Stevens & Clark in Boston, points out that because of the stern economic measures, it's highly unlikely that there will be any earnings this year for the companies in the fund's portfolio. Hence he isn't optimistic about any further dividend payouts by Brazil Fund this year. The fund, sold to investors in March 1988 for $12.50 a share, has paid a total of $3.99 a share in dividends, including $3.07 Jan. 23 and 12 cents March 30.

For small investors, it's perplexing enough that the shares of a publicly traded fund rarely match the portfolio value -- generally known as the net asset value. And until recently, Brazil Fund added to this quandary by sporting its two different net asset values. One figure showed the effect of a new one-time 8% wealth tax. But the tax was found inapplicable to Brazil Fund, which now quotes a single net asset value of $9.75 a share.

At yesterday's close the shares were at a 3.8% premium to the portfolio value. Sound tempting? Mr. Games notes that during 1989 the fund shares typically sold at discounts of 30% to 55%. At year end, for example, Brazil Fund's net asset value was $18.85 and the shares were 32% lower, at 12 7/8.

Brazil Fund is a reasonable bet now because "a buyer of the fund is getting a portfolio of undervalued assets," Mr. Porter says. Last month the entire Brazilian market was capitalized at about $17 billion; "Does that make sense when IBM is capitalized at about $31 billion?" Mr. Porter asks. Mr. Porter adds that a couple of years ago Mexico Fund was at 4 and now it is at 14 1/4. "Mexico took some of the same steps that Brazil is trying today -- privatization, reducing the public sector and controlling inflation," he says. But he warns that Brazil's stock market is still "vulnerable to further weakness."

In 1989, Mr. Games says, Brazil Fund benefited from spectacular earnings growth for the stocks in its portfolio. The fund's net asset value rose 46% for the year -- to $18.85 share after subtracting the January 1990 distribution -- mostly from gains on investments. Mr. Games, who returned from Brazil last week, says he saw signs that business is picking up. Some companies will survive the transition better than others, he says, but "on balance, there seems to be a lot of confidence in the economy."

Fibreboard said its board rescinded an amendment to the company's employee stock option plan that would have doubled the plan's size.

As previously reported, some shareholders were highly critical of the amendment, which would have raised the number of shares reserved for issuance to 700,000, or 18% of the total now outstanding. But the amendment was narrowly approved at Fibreboard's annual meeting by a vote of 1,080,791 to 1,035,540, with 213,454 shares abstaining.

Lawrence C. Hart, chairman of the Concord, Calif., wood products and insulation company, said "the board and management don't want to expand the option program without broader support from shareholders." Fibreboard said it would "continue to evaluate the use of stock options and other incentives." Donald Miller, a dissident shareholder, said the decision was "appropriate" given that more than half the shares voted no or abstained.
