Economy: Budget Surplus By U.S. in April Was at Record --- Personal Tax Receipts Cited; Surge Was Insufficient To Meet Deficit Target

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 05/22/90

WASHINGTON -- Against the grim backdrop surrounding budget negotiations between Congress and the Bush administration, the Treasury offered a snippet of good news yesterday: A record gush of individual income-tax receipts in April gave the U.S. a record budget surplus during the month. But even the $42.52 billion surplus, larger than many analysts expected, won't be nearly enough to bring the deficit to the $100 billion target set by the Gramm-Rudman law for fiscal 1990 -- or even below last year's $152 billion gap, many analysts said.

Exceeding the Gramm-Rudman requirements, even by the tens of billions of dollars now projected, won't bring any sanctions. The budget process for fiscal 1990, which ends Oct. 30, is past the point where automatic spending cuts kick in. But the huge difference between the target and the actual deficit -- not to mention the likelihood that the deficit will widen rather than narrow this year -- would emphasize the failure of that legislation, and of the government budget efforts in general, to stanch the flow of red ink.

Still, the numbers suggest that the severe shortfall in corporate tax receipts this year, stemming from the slowing economy, hasn't spread to individual income taxes. In April, receipts from personal income taxes reached $74.38 billion, surpassing the $68.53 billion collected last April.

April is a key time to measure individual income taxes, because the filing deadline for individual taxes falls in the middle of the month. Because of the deadline, the Treasury always finishes the month with a bulge of receipts. A quirk of the calendar also helped enlarge the surplus. The government pushed into March about $4.5 billion of military salaries and other payments that were due April 1, which fell on a Sunday.

Given all those special factors, Mark Melcher, director of Washington research at Prudential-Bache Securities, said he doesn't think the big April surplus "is necessarily great news."

April's surplus followed a record monthly deficit in March of $53.34 billion and narrowed the budget gap for the first seven months of the fiscal year to $108.34 billion. That compared with a deficit of $86.98 billion during the comparable period in fiscal 1989.

If the Treasury doesn't do any better than it did in the final five months of fiscal 1989 -- and few economists think it will -- the fiscal 1990 deficit will exceed $170 billion, Mr. Melcher said.

This year, as costs of the government's thrift bailout program have grown, interest rates have risen and corporate tax receipts have trailed expectations; private analysts and even Bush administration officials have steadily enlarged their estimates of the 1990 budget deficit.

Adding to the gloomy fiscal outlook is that a good chunk of the saving-and-loan industry's massive bailout costs aren't reflected in the budget.

The Congressional Budget Office now believes the deficit will reach between $170 billion and $203 billion for fiscal 1990. "Tax collections during the month of April and early May fell short of our estimates by $15 billion," said Paul Van der Water, a CBO analyst.

Kathleen Stephansen, senior economist at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette, while surprised by the size April's surplus, said she saw nothing in yesterday's report to change her opinion that the budget deficit will reach $190 billion this year, almost twice as much as the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law mandates.

Overall, government receipts totaled $139.62 billion last month, compared with $128.89 billion in April 1989. At the same time, the government spent $97.11 billion last month, compared with $88.32 billion in April 1989. The surplus in April 1989 was $40.57 billion.

More than $16.5 billion of the outlays last month were interest payments on the federal debt.
