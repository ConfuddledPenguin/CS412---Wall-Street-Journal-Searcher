High Court Says Minors Can Be Required To Tell a Parent Before Getting Abortion

  • Author: David Shribman and Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/26/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court ruled that states can require most minors to notify at least one parent before obtaining an abortion. Two court decisions, hailed by abortion opponents, will stoke abortion controversies in this fall's elections and could prompt state legislative actions next year to restrict the right to abortion for minors. The decisions also permit a half-dozen states to begin enforcing abortion restrictions on minors that have been blocked by court orders.

By a 5-4 margin, the high court ruled it is unconstitutional for a state to require that a minor notify both her parents without exceptions. By a different 5-4 margin, the justices said Minnesota had corrected this constitutional flaw in its state law by allowing minors to obtain a judge's permission to have an abortion without notifying her parents.

In a separate 6-3 decision, the court upheld an Ohio law that requires notification of one parent or a court procedure. The court procedure requires a minor to specify whether she is seeking a judge's approval because she believes she is mature enough to make her own decisions or because notifying a parent wouldn't be in her best interest. The court stopped short of deciding whether such a bypass is necessary when states impose a one-parent notification requirement.

Abortion-rights activists say such parental-involvement laws are an artifice for curbing abortions rather than a tool for promoting family discussions. They argue, moreover, that forcing some young people to consult with their parents on such a highly emotional subject can cause irreparable psychological damage in a family or even prompt physical harm. They also say provisions forcing teens to discuss the issue with a judge are intimidating and discomfitting.

"Minors were today's victims," says Roger Evans of Planned Parenthood Federation of America.

Abortion opponents said the court ruling would spur legislative action to curb teen abortions. "Given the great public popularity of laws that give parents some say when their young daughters are facing the trauma of a difficult pregnancy, these decisions will encourage state legislators to pass additional laws," said Burke Balch, state legislative director of the National Right to Life Committee.

Lawmakers and activists in Idaho and Montana already were moving to place their state laws in line with the guidelines established by the high court. "We're going to go ahead and make some adjustments," said GOP state Sen. Mark Ricks, a leading abortion opponent in Idaho, where a strict anti-abortion law was vetoed last spring by Democratic Gov. Cecil Andrus.

Public support for abortion rights lessens substantially in cases involving minors, where voters tend to see the issue as more of a family problem than a question of personal rights and reproductive freedom. Abortion-rights activists have avoided making minors' rights the centerpiece of their movement. Nevertheless, Kate Michelman, executive director of the National Abortion Rights Action League, called the two decisions "alarming."

Many abortion-rights activists have avoided a hard line on the question of abortion for minors; NARAL assisted Democrat Douglas Wilder in his successful Virginia gubernatorial race last year despite his support for parental-involvement provisions.

"The court is going through the exact same balancing act that every candidate is going through: They want to be a little pro-choice and a little pro-life," said Douglas Bailey, a Republican political consultant. "If you're a candidate, the safest position to be in is to be pro-choice overall but in favor of some parental notification."

The court's action permits laws to go into effect in at least six states where they had been blocked: Georgia, Illinois, Minnesota, Nevada, Nebraska and Ohio. Variations of such laws are already in effect in Arkansas, Utah and West Virginia.

Parental-involvement provisions are an issue in the Texas gubernatorial race, and controversies are certain to flare in other fall elections, particularly in gubernatorial and senate races in Iowa. The issue probably will be tested in a public vote in Oregon.

The Alan Guttmacher Institute, which formerly was affiliated with Planned Parenthood, estimates that about 183,000 abortions are performed annually on minors under 18 years of age, roughly 11% of the total performed in the U.S. Studies show that about half of the minors who get abortions consult with at least one parent, with three out of four of those under the age of 15 discussing the issue with a parent.

"The young women who feel they can talk to their parents about this situation will do it, and the young women who feel they can't often have good reasons not to," said Ellen Story, associate executive director of the Family Planning Council of Western Massachusetts. "There are abusive family situations; there are cases when the pregnancy is caused by a family member; there are instances when this information could lead to serious health and psychological crises in a family."

The rulings marked the first time that Justice Sandra O'Connor voted to find a state abortion regulation unconstitutional. She joined the 5-4 Minnesota ruling, written by Justice John Stevens, that a requirement for notifying two parents, without an alternative for a court hearing, is unconstitutional. She also was the deciding vote to uphold the two-parent Minnesota law once the court procedure is activated.

In the past, she has acted to stop the court's conservatives from overruling the right to abortion, while joining them to uphold most state restrictions on abortion. But yesterday "was the first time that she has said the constitutional liberty of a woman includes the right to terminate a pregnancy," said Harvard Law School Prof. Laurence Tribe. That may limit how far Justice O'Connor is willing to cut back on the right to abortion.

The decisions showed how divided the court remains over the issue, taking months to try to reach consensus, yet producing nine separate opinions in the two cases.

While the justices said a two-parent-notice law must allow for a court procedure to bypass the parents, they didn't decide whether a one-parent-notice law also must allow a court bypass: They simply upheld Ohio's law, which does provide for a court procedure. That open question, in Justice Anthony Kennedy's majority opinion in the Ohio case, is likely to encourage states to pass one-parent-notice laws without provisions for court hearings, according to both Prof. Tribe and James Bopp of the National Right To Life Committee.

In the Minnesota case, the justices also voted, 6-3, to uphold a requirement that minors wait 48 hours for an abortion after notifying their parents.

In the Minnesota case, Justice Stevens's decision blocking the absolute two-parent requirement was joined by Justices O'Connor, William Brennan, Thurgood Marshall and Harry Blackmun. Justice Kennedy, joined by Chief Justice William Rehnquist and Justices Byron White and Antonin Scalia dissented, voting to uphold the requirement. Separately, Justice O'Connor joined Justice Kennedy's bloc to uphold the two-parent requirement once a court hearing provision is added. The 48-hour wait was agreed to by six members of the court, with Justices Marshall, Brennan and Blackmun dissenting.

In the Ohio case, Justices Blackmun, Brennan and Marshall dissented from Justice Kennedy's decision. (Hodgson vs. Minnesota; Ohio vs. Akron Center for Reproductive Health.)
