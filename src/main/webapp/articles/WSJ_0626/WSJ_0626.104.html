Credit Markets: Treasurys Fall as Worries About New Supply Of Notes and Bonds Push Investors to Shun Bonds

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Kevin Donovan
  • Date: 06/26/90

NEW YORK -- Investors shunned the bond market yesterday, forcing dealers to knock down prices in an attempt to attract buyers. The biggest losses occurred on the longer end of the yield curve. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell nearly one point , or $10 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, jumped to 8.56%.

Short-term securities ended with losses between 1/4 and 1/2 point.

Traders and analysts said institutional investors were noticeably absent from the market yesterday, due in part to concern about the market's ability to absorb a huge amount of new notes and bonds to be sold over the next several weeks.

The Treasury sold $17.2 billion of short-term Treasury bills yesterday and is scheduled to auction $11.25 billion of two-year notes today and $8.25 billion of four-year notes tomorrow.

"The auctions are coming up and there's not much buying," said Charles Lieberman, managing director of financial markets research at Manufacturers Hanover Securities Corp. "The net result is prices go lower."

William Sullivan, money-market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., said that investors are staying away from the market for fear that prices will fall further in the weeks ahead as the Treasury saturates the market with supply.

That could be bad news not only for investors, but also for dealers. Traders' inventories already are bloated and the additional inventory is likely to keep prices lower.

Mr. Sullivan of Dean Witter said that evidence that dealers already hold ample supply of securities is reflected in the firmness of the overnight repurchase-agreement rate compared with the federal funds rate. In a repurchase agreement, a securities firm borrows to finance its inventory, using the securities as collateral. The "repo" rate is generally lower than rates on other collaterized loans made by New York banks. The federal funds rates is the rate banks charge each other on overnight loans.

The repo rate has been close to the federal funds rate for about two weeks, reflecting an abundance of collateral to be financed. An overnight repurchase-agreement rate substantially below the federal funds rate reflects a scarcity of collateral.

Yesterday afternoon the overnight rate stood at about 8.20%, compared with a federal funds rate that fluctuated between 8.1875% and 8.3125%.

Corporate Issues

The junk bonds of Charter Medical Corp. fell sharply on reports that federal regulators are investigating the company for possible Medicare and Medicaid-related fraud.

As reported, officials from the Department of Health and Human Services have been questioning former Charter employees involved in the Medicaid and Medicare reimbursement process for several months.

Bids for the hospital and psychiatric-care company's high-yield debt dried up yesterday. Charter Medical's 14.25% subordinated debentures, due in 2002, were offered at 21, down three points.

The Macon, Ga.-based firm's 14% senior subordinated debentures, which were trading at 101 1/2 at the beginning of the year, were offered at 38, down six points from Friday's closing level.

In recent months, Charter Medical's credit has suffered from a series of confidence blows. The firm posted a $67 million loss during the second quarter after a pretax charge of $35 million for questionable accounting practices.

In the overall junk-bond market, prices slipped modestly in extremely thin trading.

Some traders said a decline in equity prices helped depress junk bond prices which ended between 1/4 to 1/2 point lower. The Dow Jones Industrial Average slipped 12 points yesterday after falling nearly 79 points last week.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 101 29/32, down nearly one point from 102 27/32 on Friday.

The current two-year notes were quoted late at 100 1/32 to yield 8.44%, compared with 100 7/32 to yield 8.35% on Friday. In when-issued trading, the new two-year notes to be auctioned today were quoted at a yield of 8.43%, up 0.09 percentage point from late Friday. The when-issued four-year notes were quoted at 8.54%, up 0.10 percentage point from Friday.

Short-term rates were higher yesterday at the government's regular weekly Treasury bill auction. The average discount rate on three-month bills was 7.78% and was 7.67% on six-month bills. Those rates were up from last week's auction, when they were 7.74% and 7.64%, respectively.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Declines in Treasury bond prices pulled down mortgage securities as much as 3/8 point.

Lesser losses on mortgage securities translated to a slim narrowing in yield spreads, but market participants stress that the spread tightening has done little to break the recent trend of marginal spread changes.

Spread volatility has been virtually nonexistent in recent weeks. Since the start of the month, the late-day spread between Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities and 10-year Treasury notes has been locked in a maximum range of 0.05 percentage point. The same is true for Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities.

Late yesterday, the spread for Ginnie Mae 9.5% securities stood at 1.27 percentage points, 0.02 percentage point narrower than late Friday, with Ginnie Mae 9.5% securities yielding 9.85% and 10-year Treasury notes yielding 8.58%.

Among other actively traded secondary mortgage issues, Freddie Mac 9.5% securities were 11/32 lower at 97 31/32 and Fannie Mae 9.5% issues were off 11/32 at 98 15/32.

Ginnie Mae 9.5% securities for July delivery were off 3/8 at 98 18/32.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds ended narrowly mixed, with most issues steady to 1/8 point lower in ambivalent dealings.

Issues are expected to show only minor price fluctuations as participants await the outcome of this week's Treasury auctions.

In secondary activity, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2020 closed up 1/8 at 97 7/8 to yield 7.37.

Today a Morgan Stanley & Co. group is expected to price $780 million New York State Dormitory Authority consolidated second resolution revenue bonds.

Foreign Bonds

The Canadian bond market was surprisingly subdued yesterday following the weekend demise of the Meech Lake constitutional accord.

In recent months, Canadian bond prices have gyrated wildly due to uncertainty about how such an accord would affect the Canadian economy. The Meech Lake accord was designed to meet Quebec's demand that it be considered a "distinct society" and to defuse independence forces within the province. But the accord died after two provinces failed to ratify it by a Saturday deadline.

Although some investors were bracing for a potential sell-off, the reaction was muted. "The world did not come to an end," said Jim Beqaj, director of global fixed-income at Wood Gundy Ltd., Toronto. "It was actually quite anticlimactic," he said.

The news prompted some selling of Canadian government bonds during overnight trading in Japan. But the bonds recovered somewhat during Canadian trading and ended between 1/8 and 1/4 point lower.

Wood Gundy's Mr. Beqaj said the overnight dip in prices sparked some bargain hunting during North American trading as investors began to focus again on the economic fundamentals, which look encouraging for Canadian bonds.

Michael Gregory, an economist at Royal Bank of Canada, agrees. He said that Canadian bonds are attractive because of the relatively high yields when compared to U.S. government bonds. Mr. Gregory expects the Bank of Canada to loosen interest rates near the end of the summer in response to slowing economic growth and an easing of inflationary pressures.
