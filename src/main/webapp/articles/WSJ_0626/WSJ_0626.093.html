Caterpillar Shares Tumble as Firm Says Profit Will Drop `Substantially' in 1990

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 06/26/90

Caterpillar Inc. shares tumbled after the company disclosed that it expects current-year earnings will be "substantially lower" than those of an earlier year, on flat sales. The Peoria, Ill., maker of heavy equipment said it expects significant "negative short-term effects on profit" from deterioration in the economy of Brazil -- where it operates a unit -- as well as from delays in completing a costly plant-modernization effort, and the effects of a corporate reorganization.

In composite New York Stock Exchange trading yesterday, Caterpillar shares dived $5 to close at $58.50 on heavy volume of almost 1.9 million shares.

In 1989, Caterpillar had net income of $497 million, or $4.90 a share, on sales of $10.88 billion. The company had described its 1990 earnings picture in milder terms, saying only that net would be "under considerable pressure" and projecting a slight sales increase. But its first-quarter net of $99 million, or 97 cents a share, was off 30% from a year earlier, despite a 9% rise in sales to $2.87 billion.

Caterpillar said it expects second-quarter net will be "less than half" the first-quarter earnings.

"Generally speaking," said Wertheim Schroeder & Co. analyst Mitchell I. Quain, the problems Caterpillar cited "appear to be temporary. . . . Most of this is nonrecurring" and doesn't appear to significantly take away from what he continues to see as strengthening "earnings power in the years ahead" for the company.

Still, he noted, Wertheim had been projecting per-share earnings of about $4 to $4.25 for this year, but "now I think you'll see everyone go down to about $3 for 1990."

Caterpillar said the largest reason for the projected downturn this year is what it called a "dramatic decline" in results at its Caterpillar Brasil unit. The unit was profitable through 1989 and the first quarter of this year, but Caterpillar now expects the unit to incur an operating loss for the year. Caterpillar said sales have softened in Brazil since a new government economic program went into effect in March; the government move, by weakening Brazil's currency, also means that the sales which do occur translate into fewer dollars for the parent.

Caterpillar also noted, as it has previously, that the dollar's recent strength vs. the yen makes U.S. exports more expensive, and makes Japanese goods cheaper to U.S. buyers. The company's biggest world-wide competitor is Komatsu Ltd. of Tokyo.

Caterpillar said manufacturing costs will be higher than anticipated through 1990, because of "slippage" in implementing its extensive modernization program. While cost reductions anticipated from the ongoing program will "still be achieved," the point where such benefits exceed outlays for the plan now won't come in the fourth quarter, as the company had expected.

Caterpillar additionally said that it's experiencing "short-lived" inefficiencies associated with a corporate revamping announced in January. That plan, designed to make the company more flexible, established a number of "profit centers" in place of the vertical structure the company had employed. But the company said adjusting to the new plan has produced temporary "disruptive effects."

Caterpillar said all the programs under way, despite the setbacks some have encountered, will "greatly enhance" its long-term competitive posture, and added it will continue to aggressively pursue their implementation.
