House-Senate Panel Clears Bill to Protect Handicapped People

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/26/90

WASHINGTON -- House and Senate conferees approved legislation that would bar discrimination against handicapped individuals on a broad front, including public accommodations and employment. The bill was approved by wide margins in both chambers, and if the conference version passes the House and Senate, it is expected to be signed by President Bush. Known as the Americans with Disabilities Act, the measure would extend to about 43 million disabled persons the kind of protection against discrimination now afforded by a patchwork of state and local rules and in requirements for federal contractors and grant recipients.

The conferees killed an amendment in the House bill that specified that food companies could transfer workers with contagious diseases to jobs that didn't involve handling food. The amendment dealt with employer concerns about employees with acquired immune deficiency syndrome.

Senate conferees ignored a nonbinding resolution passed by the Senate, instructing them to accede to the House amendment. They quoted Health and Human Services Secretary Louis Sullivan that the AIDS-causing virus cannot be transmitted by handling food and that an amendment based on public "fears and misconceptions" would only complicate efforts to control the disease.

The Senate conferees also noted that both the bill and existing law already allow employers to transfer or fire workers who pose a direct threat to the health and safety of others.
