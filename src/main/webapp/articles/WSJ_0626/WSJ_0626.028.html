PRAISE FOR TOKYO

A senior Bush administration official yesterday praised the Japanese government for making progress on all the major issues in talks to cut the bilateral-trade imbalance but stressed that none of the matters had been resolved.

The official's remarks, delivered after the first day of the final round of the so-called Structural Impediments Initiative, contrasted with the harsh rhetoric coming out of Washington in the weeks leading up to the meetings. Last week, U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills said, "Japan's foot-dragging . . . must end."

The most contentious issue is whether Japan will commit to big increases in public spending over the next decade, an act the U.S. argues will shift more resources to Japan's domestic market. A member of the U.S. delegation said the two sides were still "apart" on the issue but "I would not say we are far apart."

A Japanese government official said his delegation didn't increase its proposal from the 415 trillion yen ($2.679 trillion) level given to the U.S. last week. The U.S. has asked Japan to spend 500 trillion yen over the next 10 years on public works.

GLOBAL JET DEMAND

Global demand for commercial jets will total about 12,220 planes for the period from 1990 through 2008, according to a new forecast published by Airbus Industrie. The European consortium said that about 5,500 aircraft of the estimated total would be needed to replace aging jets now in service. The rest would be needed to satisfy traffic growth estimated at an annual average of 5.5% for the period. Airbus estimated firm orders had already been placed for some 3,200 jets by the start of 1990.

AMERICAN EXPRESS IN LONDON

American Express Co. will lease space for a European headquarters from Olympia & York Developments Ltd. in the Canary Wharf office complex in East London. The U.S. travel and financial-services concern said it will take 300,000 square feet of 20 Cabot Square, a 10-story building, in spring 1992. The new building will be the first European headquarters for American Express International, bringing about 1,500 employees under one roof, American Express said. Olympia & York said it now has leasing commitments for more than half the space under construction at Canary Wharf.

BRAZILIAN KIDNAPPINGS

The Brazilian government has proposed drastic measures to put an end to a wave of kidnappings. Kidnappers have seized 25 people in Rio de Janeiro alone this year. "The government must take vigorous and drastic measures to cut off this evil at the root," said President Fernando Collor de Mello in a memorandum to Justice Minister Bernardo Cabral. Measures proposed to the legislature include freezing the assets of victims to stop them from paying ransom and increasing the maximum prison sentence for kidnappers to 30 years from eight.

AIRPORT SECURITY

Japan plans to tighten luggage security checks at its six international airports beginning in October, a Transport Ministry spokesman said. The ministry is negotiating to split costs with a number of airlines that don't X-ray luggage on flights to and from Japan, the spokesman said. Elsewhere, the Geneva-based International Air Travel Association said it opposed a West German plan to levy a $2 security charge on embarking airline passengers. "We firmly believe that the cost of maintaining security at airports . . . should be borne by the state in question," said an IATA official.

JOINT VENTURES IN EASTERN EUROPE

A study by the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development shows that there are more than 5,400 joint ventures with foreign companies in Eastern Europe. But a large number of these, especially in the Soviet Union, are "just on paper and inactive"; and, in general, the joint ventures are noticeably absent from the service sector, an OECD official said. He added that "while many joint projects have had a useful demonstration effect, some countries have to rebuild their legal and economic infrastructure" before many ventures can bear fruit.

KEEPING JAPAN'S WORKERS HAPPY

Many Japanese companies have been cutting working hours in an attempt to hold on to employees in a tightening labor market, the Labor Ministry said.

Ministry officials said a quarterly survey conducted in May showed that 60% of manufacturers have reduced work hours, while 64% of wholesalers and retailers took the same step. The survey, which involved 2,172 companies, also found that 44% of the manufacturers have raised wages and 30% have improved company welfare facilities to hold workers.

The smaller the companies, the more trouble they have trying to find workers or keeping them, the officials said.

According to the survey, 47% of the companies with more than 1,000 people on the payroll have reduced work hours, while 71% of those with between 100 and 299 people reported the same action.
