World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Slide 570.38 Points on Pressure By Index-Linked Trade, Dismay on Outlook

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/26/90

Tokyo's Nikkei Index sank heavily in drab trading to close at the intraday low, pressured by index-linked trading and by widespread unenthusiasm about the near-term outlook for stocks. Meanwhile, London shares rose on a late afternoon surge, as investors were cheered by a positive start on Wall Street.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues plunged 570.38 points to 31124.19. The index lost 392.70 points Friday.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the Nikkei index rebounded 136.50 points to 31260.69.

Monday's volume on the first section was estimated at 300 million shares, off from 350.7 million shares Friday. Losers widely outnumbered advancers 904 to 120, while 100 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section tumbled 40.04 points, or 1.71%, to 2301.02.

The second section index fell 47.16 points, or 1.14%, to 4088.26. Volume in the section was estimated at 12.4 million shares, down from 19.7 million shares Friday.

Insufficient volume has been one of the most detrimental factors in the market lately, traders said.

Japanese securities firms' research groups have estimated that Wall Street needs about 170 million shares a day in order to be profitable, whereas Tokyo needs at least 700 million to 800 million, noted Stephen Leist, manager of Japanese equity sales at S.G. Warburg Securities (Japan) Inc.

"There's just not enough liquidity," one trader at a U.K. securities firm said. "Fund managers have no confidence in this market."

Even if institutional investors had wanted to participate and take advantage of lower prices yesterday, there wouldn't have been enough activity to complete the transactions, the trader noted.

Moreover, although the Big Four securities firms, which usually try to add volume and initiate buying sentiment, were quiet as the weak yen would have made such efforts pointless, traders said.

Institutional money is increasingly flowing out of stocks and into money market instruments, or toward hedges against futures, traders said.

The bearishness is underlined by concerns about global interest rates, partly stemming from uncertainty about German monetary union, observers noted.

"People are afraid that West Germany might increase interest rates, which, if that's the case, will of course not be good for the market. As a result, the market's just going to stay at this level," the U.K. firm's trader said.

At the same time, he noted, baseless chatter on the possibility of another Bank of Japan official discount rate increase and other negative sentiments keep surfacing as participants become more disenchanted with the market.

"With no stories to watch out for, there's really nothing else they can do other than talk," he said.

Electricals were mostly lower. But Hirose Electric rose 160 yen to 7150 yen ($1.03 to $46.16), Kyocera rose 70 yen to 8170 yen, and TDK gained 20 yen to 6800 yen.

Sony fell 120 yen to 8450 yen, Matsushita Electric Industrial fell 30 yen to 2060 yen, and Pioneer fell 30 yen to 6070 yen.

Domestic-demand related issues suffered from the weaker yen. Steel, construction, mining and retail shares fell.

In London, nervousness in the early going on the back of Wall Street's slump Friday had trimmed prices to the day's minimums. But once the New York market opened steadily, London investors breathed a sigh of relief and propelled the U.K. market higher.

In addition, dealers suggested that institutions are still cash-rich and hesitant to sell any stocks. Indeed, as the market slipped to lower levels, traders said fund managers and institutional investors were net buyers of British equities.

"There's a marked reluctance to sell," said one trader. "The perception is growing that the U.K. is still one of the cheapest and best-performing markets on a global basis."

But dealers were quick to warn that participation remained sketchy on the first full day of the new two-week trading account. Moreover, some suggested that a cautious tone would prevail until the end of the week when stock-index futures and options expire.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index gained 20 points to 2398.5, the day's best level and well above the early morning 2361.2 minimum. Some dealers suggested that the index looks set to pierce 2400 today.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index gained 16.4 points to 1929.4. Volume was a calm 379.1 million shares, compared with the 497.8 million issues Friday.

Interest rate sensitive stocks were better on weekend media reports that suggested that U.K. interest rates could be down to 12% by year end from the current 15%. Although some market professionals suggested that this was too optimistic a scenario in the near term, several sectors dependent on interest rates were bolstered.

Among these, builder Tarmac added 11 pence to 284 pence (19 cents to $4.91), cement and building products group RMC gained 12 pence to 689 pence, retailers Burton Group grew 11 pence to 169 pence, Kingfisher rose 7 pence to 372 pence, while Marks & Spencer rose 5 pence to 245 pence.

Dealers said takeover speculation continued to bubble in the composite insurance sector. Commercial Union Assurance added 4 pence to 523 pence, General Accident advanced 11 pence to #11.19, Guardian Royal Exchange rose 5 pence to 246 pence, while Sun Alliance gained 3 pence to 332 pence.

Tobacco and luxury products group Rothmans International, set to disclose year-end results tomorrow, fell 14 pence to 760 pence after strong rises late this past week.

Elsewhere, U.K. controls group Siebe fell 25 pence to 505 pence after saying it was to pay $656.4 million for Foxboro Co., a U.S. supplier of process automation and control equipment.

Elsewhere, stocks closed higher in Paris, Frankfurt, Brussels and Madrid; lower in Zurich; mixed in Stockholm and Amsterdam; and unchanged in Milan. South African gold stocks closed higher.

Also, stocks ended lower in Seoul, Wellington, Manila and Singapore, higher in Hong Kong and Taipei, and little changed in Sydney.
