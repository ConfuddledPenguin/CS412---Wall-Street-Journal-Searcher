Marketing & Media: Top Executives of WPP Publicly Patch Up Rift

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/26/90

LONDON -- One of the advertising industry's best-known spats, between Chairman David Ogilvy and Chief Executive Martin Sorrell of Britain's WPP Group PLC, was publicly patched up. At a meeting of WPP shareholders here -- the first such session at which the two warring executives have shared the podium -- all was amity. The two men, who had clashed last year when Mr. Sorrell's WPP took over New York-based Ogilvy Group, sat side by side in a hotel reception room, warmly greeting WPP shareholders.

When asked, Mr. Ogilvy said of his colleague: "I am very fond of him -- but I don't want him to know." For his part, Mr. Sorrell said their relations have "never been better."

The conflict, triggered by WPP's unwelcome bid for Ogilvy, was noteworthy in the ad industry for its public namecalling. The 79-year-old Mr. Ogilvy was quoted as calling Mr. Sorrell an "odious little jerk." A truce was struck in May 1989 when Ogilvy accepted a sweetened WPP offer of at least $793.8 million, and Mr. Ogilvy was named WPP chairman.

At the meeting, Mr. Ogilvy told shareholders that the company's profits so far in 1990 "are in line with expectations both inside and outside the company." In London, financial analysts forecast WPP pretax profit of between #110 million ($190.4 million) and #115 million, up 46% to 53% from #75 million in 1989.
