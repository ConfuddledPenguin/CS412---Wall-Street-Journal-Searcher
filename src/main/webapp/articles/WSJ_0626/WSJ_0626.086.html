Newly Named Top Chase Bank Officials Plan to Trim Operating Expenses by 8%

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 06/26/90

NEW YORK -- Faced with falling earnings, Chase Manhattan Corp.'s newly named top managers said they plan to slash operating expenses by at least $300 million, or about 8%, over the next six to 12 months. The move could lead to as many as 3,000 layoffs, according to some Chase executives. In a memo distributed yesterday to all officers, Thomas G. Labrecque, Chase's chairman-designate, and Arthur F. Ryan, president-designate, said the $300 million in cutbacks are prompted by a financial climate that is "as difficult as any in the history of the global financial-services industry."

The nation's second-largest banking company hasn't determined how many jobs will be eliminated out of its global work force of 41,600 employees.

The cutbacks are expected to fall across all departments, though European corporate finance has been identified in particular as one vulnerable area because of weak profits. One high Chase officer said, "Nothing will escape the knife."

The last time Chase made a concerted effort to trim its payroll was in 1987, when 1,280 employees took early retirement.

The memo follows last week's announcement that Willard C. Butcher, Chase's chairman and chief executive officer, will retire in September, several months earlier than expected, in order to give Messrs. Labrecque and Ryan a quicker start on slimming down Chase.

In yesterday's memo, which details the first in a series of expected cost-cutting steps, Chase said that it is wiping out its three-pronged organizational structure and instead will have ten business heads reporting directly to Mr. Ryan, formerly head of consumer banking.

In effect, the reorganization demotes Vice Chairman Robert R. Douglass, who moves from head of institutional banking to a new post as senior customer-planning and development officer. In addition, Vice Chairman Richard J. Boyle will no longer oversee global commercial and investment banking, but instead will be senior credit officer. His responsibilities will include commercial real estate lending, a troubled area for Chase and other banks.

Mr. Douglass, a patrician banker with close ties to the Rockefeller family, whose members once ran Chase, will serve in a largely ambassador-at-large role, Chase executives say. Mr. Douglass was responsible for Chase's acquisition of an Arizona bank which last year had a $79 million write-off for bad real estate loans. Mr. Douglass will report to Mr. Labrecque.

As head of credit and real estate, Mr. Boyle is being placed in a sensitive position that he is very familiar with. During the 1970s, Mr. Boyle was instrumental in Chase's work-out of troubled real estate loans, which once again are rising. In addition, Chase executives credit him with dissuading Mr. Butcher from bidding for Texas Commerce Bancshares Inc., a troubled bank that was eventually acquired by Chemical Banking Corp. Mr. Boyle will report to Mr. Ryan.

A number of other Chase executives gain new responsibilities in the restructuring. Michael F. Dacey, head of North American corporate finance, now has securities underwriting, public finance, leasing and domestic institutions businesses reporting to him. Timothy McGiniss, head of corporate finance in Japan, picks up responsibility for the rest of Asia. Robert Murphy, head of Latin American corporate finance, is being given responsibility for developing-country debt.

Donald Boudreau, head of the New York City branch system, added Chase's banks in upstate New York, Ohio, Maryland and elsewhere in the U.S. to his responsibilities. Robert D. Hunter, head of national financial services, will now have the residential mortgage business reporting to him.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Chase shares closed yesterday at $24.125 apiece, up 50 cents.
