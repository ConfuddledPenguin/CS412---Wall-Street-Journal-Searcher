Canadian Markets Rebound After Initial Sell-Off

  • Author: Larry M. Greenberg
  • Date: 06/26/90

TORONTO -- Canadian financial markets recovered from an initial sell-off yesterday following the demise of the Meech Lake constitutional accord. Analysts said a rebound in the Canadian dollar triggered a positive response in the bond and equity markets. They predicted that Canada's relatively attractive interest-rate yields will continue to support the Canadian dollar in coming weeks, overriding lingering anxieties about the future of the Canadian union.

"The wide interest-rate spreads {between Canada and the U.S.} look ever more attractive now that the Canadian debate about the Meech Lake accord has gone out of the air," said Edward Carmichael, an economist at Burns Fry Ltd. in Toronto.

The Meech Lake accord, aimed at meeting demands of mostly French-speaking Quebec and defusing independence forces in the province, failed over the weekend after two provincial legislatures adjourned without ratifying the proposed constitutional amendment.

Yesterday, Montreal was a sea of blue-and-white Quebec flags as a good-natured crowd of about 200,000 watched a parade marking St. Jean Baptiste Day, the French-speaking province's "national" holiday. Lined up 10 deep in places along the three-mile route, Montrealers smiled and waved and at times chanted, "We want a country. We want a country."

Canadian market sentiment was boosted by a report that Standard & Poor's Corp. affirmed its Triple-A rating on Canadian government debt and Double-A-minus rating on Quebec's debt. Moody's Investors Service Inc. also said it would maintain its Double-A-3 rating on Quebec debt.

But Standard & Poor's said it changed its outlook for Quebec's long-term debt to "developing" from "positive" because of political uncertainty. "While S&P expects no change in the relationship between Quebec and Canada in the near term, ongoing discussions of constitutional reform could become increasingly divisive," the rating agency said.

The Canadian dollar began the trading day in Asia, falling swiftly to 84.20 cents from 84.85 cents late Friday in Toronto. But it ended higher at 84.95 cents.

The Canadian 20-year bellwether bond ended down 1/2 point, or 50 Canadian dollars lower for every C$1,000 face amount, to 10.62%. But with U.S. bonds also falling, the Canadian issue improved against its American counterpart, narrowing its premium over the U.S. 30-year bellwether bond to 206 basis points from 209 basis points on Friday. A basis point is a hundredth of a percentage point.

Canadian equities also declined but by little more than the U.S. stocks measured by the Dow Jones Industrial Average. The Toronto Stock Exchange 300-composite index lost 16.56 points to 3474.16 points. Volume was light at 15.5 million shares.
