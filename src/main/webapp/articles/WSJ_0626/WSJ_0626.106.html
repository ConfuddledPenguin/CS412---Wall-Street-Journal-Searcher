Politics & Policy: Bush Trying to Get Talks on Budget Back on Track

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 06/26/90

WASHINGTON -- Democratic negotiators at the budget talks struggled to overcome their differences as President Bush sought to break an apparent stalemate in the negotiations by inviting top congressional leaders to breakfast at the White House this morning. Negotiators from both sides have been saying they need to make significant progress this week if they are to reach a deficit-reduction compromise before September. But chances for that are fading. A negotiating session planned for yesterday afternoon was canceled; Republicans blamed the Democrats and Democrats blamed the administration.

Democrats uniformly want to avoid blame for the tax increase they -- and many Republicans -- expect to be part of any deficit-reduction compromise. But they differ substantially on tactics. Some want to demand that President Bush put a new offer on the table before proceeding, even if that risks breaking up the talks; others are now ready to quietly pursue a compromise with administration negotiators.

The differences among Democrats reflect both the institutional characteristics of the House and Senate and variations in style among the players. Several senators argue for a more confrontational stance. Some House members side with them, but others are more conciliatory.

Senate Finance Chairman Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas) said yesterday that Congress simply should proceed with its budget resolution and appropriations bills so that the administration "will understand that this {the budget summit} is not going anyplace until the president gives us a more substantive offer."

Mr. Bush invited to breakfast this morning the top Democrat and Republican in each chamber -- the same quartet he invited last month to kick off the budget summit -- as well as House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.), who chairs the talks. The invitations followed attempts by Budget Director Richard Darman and Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady to convince distrustful Democrats that the administration sincerely seeks a deficit-reduction compromise rather than partisan advantage.

Meanwhile, Republican congressional negotiators sought to raise pressure on the Democrats. "We're waiting for the Democrats. . . . " said Sen. Pete Domenici (R., N.M.), after meeting with Mr. Bush yesterday. "They are not ready to discuss serious resolution to issues."

Sen. Phil Gramm (R., Texas) added: "We know what we want to do. We want a deal. . . . We know we want one. They're not sure they do."

Mr. Darman, responding to the Democrats' demand for a new offer, last week essentially repeated Mr. Bush's January budget request and proposed another $11.7 billion in new spending cuts. But that apparently did little to advance the negotiations, instead offering Democrats a new target to shoot at.

Democrats promptly reminded administration negotiators that no Republican in the House or the Senate was willing to put the president's original budget proposal to a vote.
