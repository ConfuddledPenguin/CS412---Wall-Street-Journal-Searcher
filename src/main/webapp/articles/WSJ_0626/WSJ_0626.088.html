Abreast of the Market: Industrials Drop 12.13 on Worries Over Profit Reports, Interest Rates

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 06/26/90

NEW YORK -- Rising interest rates and worries about second-quarter profits bumped stock prices lower yesterday. The Dow Jones Industrial Average slipped 12.13 to 2845.05, a decline of 0.42%. Broader averages took a steeper fall: Standard & Poor's 500-stock index fell 3.12 to 352.31, or 0.88%, and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index lost 1.64 to 192.60, a loss of 0.84.%

Volume was light, with 133,100,000 shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues outnumbered advancers, 1,049 to 496.

Several of the year's most popular stocks were knocked down yesterday by fears of weaker earnings. Caterpillar's announcement that profit for the second quarter would be less than half the $99 million, or 97 cents a share, reported for the first quarter started the round of earnings scrutiny. Caterpillar fell 5 to 58 1/2 on 1.9 million shares. In the year-ago second quarter, Caterpillar posted net income of $141 million, or $1.39 a share. Also, the company said its profit for the year would be "substantially lower" than in the previous year.

The Dow Jones Transportation Average fell 16.42 to 1147.81, primarily due to weakness in airline stocks. AMR, parent of American Airlines, fell 1 to 63 3/8; Delta Air Lines slipped 1/4 to 73 3/4. Both airlines have had their second-quarter earnings forecasts revised downward in recent weeks, according to Zacks Investment Research. The second-quarter consensus for AMR fell to $1.90 a share from $2.06 about a month ago. Delta expectations now center around $2.21 a share for the quarter compared with expectations of $2.56 a month ago.

UAL, parent of United Airlines, fell 3 3/8 to 152 in light volume.

"Everybody is focusing on the (fear) that second-quarter earnings are not going to be there," said Edward Shopkorn, director of equity trading at Mabon, Nugent & Co. "People are getting cautious."

Shearson Lehman Hutton lowered its earnings forecasts on 39 companies last week and boosted projections on only 10, said Peter Da Puzzo, the firm's co-director of world-wide equities. With profit expectations coming down and rates going up, "who's to say that this (sell-off) can't continue?" Mr. Da Puzzo said.

The actively traded 30-year Treasury bond fell nearly one full point, or $10 for each $1,000 face amount, taking the yield up to 8.56%. T-bill rates moved higher as the market absorbed a record $17.2 billion weekly sale of three- and six-month Treasury bills. Another $29.5 billion in bills and notes is scheduled for auction later this week.

Tenneco, whose Case IH unit competes with Caterpillar in the heavy-equipment market, fell 1 1/2 to 65 5/8. Analyst Robert Christensen of Mabon, Nugent & Co. told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report the stock's drop appeared to be a "misplaced reaction" to the projection from Caterpillar, as Case "doesn't have the same kind of equipment" or as much exposure in Brazil. Caterpillar said more than half of its expected second-quarter earnings shortfall was tied to the performance of its Brazilian unit.

Earnings-related selling also hurt Armco, which skidded 1 1/8 to 7 3/8 after the company projected a loss for the quarter. Armco said its forecast reflects plans to take about $70 million in second-quarter charges; also, the company said it may not be able to continue paying common stock dividends because of provisions in certain debt agreements.

Philip Morris, which bucked the market's sell-off Friday amid news of its agreement to acquire 80% of Swiss coffee and chocolate maker Jacobs Suchard for $3.8 billion, gained 1 7/8 to 46 5/8 and topped the Big Board's list of most active issues. About 3.2 million shares were traded.

Other blue chips to cushion the fall in the industrial average included International Business Machines, which went up 3/8 to 116 5/8; Merck, up 1/2 to 82 1/4; International Paper, up 3/8 to 51 1/4, and Union Carbide, up 1/4 to 19 3/8.

Foxboro soared 12 7/8 to 51 on 1.2 million shares. Siebe PLC, a British security systems concern, agreed to buy the company for $52 a share.

Chase Manhattan, which said it expects to save $300 million a year through a restructuring that may include jobs cuts and asset sales, rose 1/2 to 24 1/8 on 1.2 million shares. Also, the company eliminated a layer of top management by dropping the posts of group heads for global, institutional and individual banking.

Dividend-related trading swelled volume in three issues: Southeast Banking, which declined 3/4 to 9 3/8 on 2.6 million shares; Commonwealth Edison, which added 1/4 to 32 1/2 on 1.8 million shares, and Repsol, which rose 1/8 to 23 5/8 on 1.9 million shares. Separately, Duff & Phelps lowered its debt ratings on Southeast Banking; the agency linked the downgrading to "severe deterioration" in Southeast's loan portfolio.

J.C. Penney dropped 2 7/8 to 59 7/8 after Kidder Peabody downgraded the stock's investment rating and cut its earnings estimates for fiscal 1991 and 1992, citing indications that its sales so far this month remain sluggish. The company said it expects June sales for stores open at least a year to show a gain in the mid-single digits from last year's level.

Wellman skidded 3 1/4 to 28 1/4. Du Pont, a competitor in the polyester-fiber business, cut the price of its Dacron fiber products by 6% to 8%; the company said it will review the action and "react accordingly" to remain competitive.

Quantum Chemical dropped 7/8 to 17 7/8. Citing polyethylene sales that hadn't met its expectations, Shearson Lehman Hutton lowered its projection of second-quarter earnings.

Honeywell went up 1 3/4 to 98 1/4. The company said a federal district court selected a special master to handle its patent-infringement case against Minolta of Japan, which has been in the courts for more than three years.

Motel 6 rose 1 5/8 to 17 3/4. A published report cited renewed speculation that Kohlberg Kravis Roberts is seeking to sell its 51% interest in the partnership.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.89 to 357.39. Volume was 12,350,000 shares.

Nikkei "put" warrants are active again, following a decline in Tokyo stocks Monday. Yesterday, the Denmark and Salomon Brothers issues made the most-active list at the American Stock Exchange. The Denmark issue gained 5/8 to 10 1/4; an issue from Salomon gained 3/8 to 9 1/8.

Material Sciences rallied 3 3/8 to 13 3/4 following news that it authorized William Blair & Co., a Chicago-based investment banking firm, to explore the possible sale of the company.
