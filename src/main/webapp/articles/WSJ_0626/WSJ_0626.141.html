Technology & Health: Aequitron Recalls Machines to Monitor Respiration and Heart Rates of Infants

  • Author: Scott Kilman
  • Date: 06/26/90

MINNEAPOLIS -- Aequitron Medical Inc. said it is recalling thousands of machines that are used primarily to monitor the respiration and heart rates of infants. The move, disclosed in an earnings report, is the second time in two years that the medical device company has had to recall monitors to fix faulty alarm systems. The monitors are designed to set off an audible alarm if it detects irregular respiration or heart rate.

As in the March 1988 recall, the company said it isn't aware of any deaths or injuries related to malfunctioning monitors. A spokesman for the company said Aequitron detected the latest problems itself.

The spokesman also said the these latest recalls aren't related to an out-of-court settlement it reached earlier this spring with a Corpus Christi, Texas, couple who had claimed that their baby died because an Aequitron monitor allegedly failed. The couple also testified before a congressional subcommittee investigating the medical instruments industry.

The spokesman for Aequitron said the inspector general's office of the Department of Health and Human Services subpoenaed some of the company's monitors last winter and that they were eventually returned to the company. The spokesman said the company isn't aware of the status of any inquiry by the department.

"As far as we know, nothing is going on," he said.

Two years ago, the company told hospitals and families to run the model 9200 Respiration and Heart Rate Monitors on house electrical current instead of batteries because some machines appeared to malfunction when used in a certain voltage range.

This time, the problem seems to be that electro-magnetic emissions can interfere with the monitor. What's more, the respiration alarm on some monitors doesn't produce the steady sound that its designers intended.

The problems involve its 8200 apnea model and its 9200 apnea model. The spokesman said that the company doesn't know how many of the 21,000 monitors are still being used.

In a release about its fiscal fourth-quarter earnings, the company disclosed that it voluntarily decided to alert the users of the monitors after discussions with the Food and Drug Administration.

Aequitron said it is making available to owners a kit that is designed to reduce the sensitivity of the monitors to electro-magnetic interference. The company said it is also telling dealers to return monitors so it can repair the faulty respiration alarm.

The company said it established a $375,000 reserve to cover the expenses of its action.

The one-time charge for the reserve was included in the results the company announced for the fourth quarter ended April 30. Aequitron said it earned $101,200, or two cents a share, compared with a loss of $416,100, or nine cents a share, for the 1989 fourth quarter. Fourth-quarter sales rose 16.5% to $6.5 million from $5.6 million.

Aequitron said in a release that it doesn't know how severely the recalls will hurt its sales. But the company said it has more than enough working capital to weather any temporary revenue decline and that it believes that any loss of market share would be temporary.

The spokesman for the company said the monitors represent about one-third of Aequitron's business.
