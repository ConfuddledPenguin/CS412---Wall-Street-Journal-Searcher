Monday's Markets: Fear of Glut Hits Bonds; Stocks Sag --- Auctions' Size Stirs Worry; Dollar Gains Against Yen, Mark

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/26/90

NEW YORK -- Bond investors drove prices lower as they worried about too many bonds coming to market. The stock market tagged along, with an additional negative nudge from some bad earnings news. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices fell nearly a full point, or $10 for each $1,000 of face amount, in light trading. The Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped 12.13 points to close at 2845.05, also on thin trading. The dollar rose against the West German mark and the Japanese yen.

Roberta D. Olson, fixed-income portfolio manager at Banc One Asset Management Corp., said bond prices were poised for a decline anyway, because they had risen to the point where buyers were starting to show resistance to further gains. "We're in a technical range, and we got something fundamental to make it move down in that range," she said.

In addition to the heavy Treasury auction schedule, the "something" included a jump in commodities prices, mainly grains and gold, which often are interpreted as signaling stepped-up inflation that erodes the value of fixed-income securities. Also, the prospect of huge government-note auctions later this week kept many buyers on the sidelines.

Stock prices attempted an early rally following Friday's sharp decline, with investors looking for bargains among battered blue chips. But the rise in bond yields that accompanies lower bond prices, and which lessens stocks' appeal to investors, cut the gains short.

Adding to stock investors' anxiety were negative earnings surprises. Caterpillar announced "substantially lower" 1990 profit expectations, largely because of a sharp decline in results for its Brazilian unit. Also, a Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst lowered his second-quarter earnings projection for Quantum Chemical, citing weaker-than-expected sales of polyethylene.

In currency trading, the dollar gained against the mark, primarily because traders were dumping marks to buy Swiss francs, attracted by higher Swiss interest rates, said Eric Lehtis, senior dealer at Mitsui Taiyo Kobe Bank. The dollar fell against the franc.

Against the yen, the dollar rose because Japanese investors are worried about signs of rising inflation in Japan, not because they want to own dollars long-term, Mr. Lehtis said. Buying dollars is an easy way to get out of the yen on the way to other currencies.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell in light trading, with 133.1 million shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange. Declining issues far outnumbered advancers, 1,049 to 496.

Bond prices fell. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 8.56%.

The dollar rose. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6770 marks and 155.35 yen, compared with 1.6745 marks and 154.85 yen Friday.
