Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Saatchi Unloads Peterson Consulting Unit

  • Author: Joanne Lipman
  • Date: 06/26/90

Saatchi & Saatchi Co., determined to rid itself of its consulting businesses at any cost, dumped its Peterson Consulting unit -- for which it paid $116 million -- for less than $20 million. Saatchi said it agreed to sell Chicago-based Peterson to the consulting firm's management for an initial payment of just $2 million, with additional payments over the next 10 years of at least $20 million. Saatchi will also take on Peterson liabilities with a net total of $5 million -- meaning that initially, at least, it will in effect be paying Peterson to buy itself back.

"They're giving Peterson $3 million {upfront} to take it. I don't understand the logic," says Richard Sanderson, an analyst with Panmure Gordon in London. "They're actually giving it away."

A Saatchi spokesman readily conceded that the agency won't come close to realizing its investment in Peterson, and that the agency is basically just cutting its losses. Peterson "is a poor, underperforming, loss-making business, and all we're interested in is getting out at whatever cost it takes -- and that is plus or minus zero," he said. Peterson was profitable for the year ended Sept. 30, 1989, but for the six months ended March 31, 1990, it had a loss of #4 million ($6.92 million).

Saatchi stock barely reacted to the news, with the company's American depositary receipts closing off 12.5 cents to close at $4.25 a share in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday. Saatchi shares have already been hammered, reflecting analysts' low expectations; ADRs now trade at less than a quarter of their 52-week high of $17.25.

The paltry take for Peterson, bought in 1987, "wasn't a surprise," says Laurie Goldberger, an analyst with Shearson Lehman Hutton. The price doesn't matter that much because "you don't quite care how {the consulting business} goes away; they've achieved the benefit of getting rid of the downside."

The sale of Peterson, along with that of Saatchi's other consulting firms, has been complicated by the fact that the firms' major assets are their executives. Saatchi has been negotiating management buy-outs with the consulting executives, who hold all the cards because they know their businesses have no value without them.

"The problem is that these are boutique businesses. If Peterson's principals leave, then what are you left with? Air -- and some liabilities," says Greg Ostroff, an analyst with Goldman Sachs. "I was under the impression Saatchi was just looking to get out without any more liabilities."

Peterson executives were obviously well aware of their clout. In a statement, Saatchi said it agreed to the unfavorable deal because it couldn't find an outside buyer and because Peterson senior management's contracts expire at the end of this year. If Saatchi didn't sell to management, Saatchi "might well find that the business ceased to be viable" at year end, the company said. Shutdown costs would be "substantial," it added. Peterson executives declined comment.

The Peterson agreement, which requires shareholder approval, comes just a few weeks after Saatchi finally agreed to sell its biggest consulting firm, Hay Group, back to management. A deal to sell its largest remaining consulting firm, Gartner Group, is expected to be set in the next few weeks. Saatchi also owns three other, small consulting businesses, but two of them -- an employment agency in the U.K. and a real estate consulting firm in the U.S. -- are facing economic downturns in their industries. The Saatchi spokesman said sales of both of those are "on a back burner."

Saatchi has said recently that it expects proceeds of all its consulting firms to total #80 million for the year ending Sept. 30. The Hay sale amounts to #45 million to #50 million. But Mr. Ostroff says the total figure may be elusive, considering the prices Saatchi is eking out in its consulting firesale, and that Saatchi "will probably come up short."

Still, analysts depict the sale as a mark in favor of Saatchi's new chief executive officer, Robert Louis-Dreyfus. When he joined in January, the French executive vowed to get out of the consulting business as quickly as possible -- a feat Saatchi had been unable to accomplish since putting the consulting business up for sale last year.

"It's a business they shouldn't have been in, and now they're out," says Alan Gottesman, an analyst with PaineWebber. "It's quite an accomplishment to be done with the deal."

Babbit & Reiman Gets `Us'

Straight Arrow Publishers awarded its $1 million Us Magazine account to Babbit & Reiman, Atlanta.

Babbit & Reiman replaces Minneapolis agency Fallon McElligott, which continues to handle Straight Arrow's Rolling Stone magazine. Us decided to look elsewhere about a month ago, "when we couldn't seem to agree on a creative approach," said Leslie H. Zeifman, group publisher.

"We had given Babbit & Reiman a two-week window of opportunity to prove that they would deliver a tremendous campaign, and they did," Mr. Zeifman said.

Fallon McElligott had created the magazine's "Definitely Not Us" ads, which sought to peg Us somewhere between People and Vanity Fair, even as critics claimed Us's readership was getting more downscale.

The account is another in a string of wins for four-year-old Babbit & Reiman. The agency won the $1.5 million USA Today account in January, and has also garnered Financial News Network and RJR Nabisco's corporate account.
