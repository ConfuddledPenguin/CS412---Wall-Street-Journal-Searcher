Law -- Legal Beat: Milken Sued for Fraud by Investor Group

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and Wayne E. Green
  • Date: 06/26/90

An investment partnership that Michael R. Milken has admitted defrauding sued the former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. junk bond chief seeking more than $6 million in damages. The suit, filed in federal district court in Manhattan by Finsbury Group Ltd., is the first to result directly from Mr. Milken's guilty plea. The investors in Finsbury, a Drexel-organized partnership that invests in high-yield securities, allege that they were defrauded by Mr. Milken "through a series of artificially rigged securities transactions."

Mr. Milken pleaded guilty to six felony counts in April and agreed to pay $600 million in fines and restitution. Mr. Milken admitted in one of those counts that he, along with former fund manager David Solomon, repeatedly overcharged Finsbury for millions of dollars of junk bonds sold to Finsbury by Drexel between 1984 and 1986.

Mr. Milken admitted to overcharging Finsbury to recoup certain expenses incurred by his Beverly Hills, Calif., junk bond department in connection with the sale of Finsbury shares. Mr. Milken also admitted concealing the illegal price adjustments.

Finsbury claims that the fraudulent activities to which Mr. Milken pleaded guilty cost the partners more than $2 million. Those damages, Finsbury said, include the payment of unjustified fees of more than $1.5 million, direct losses of more than "several hundred thousand dollars" and attorneys' fees. Under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations law, known as RICO, Finsbury is seeking triple damages.

Besides Mr. Milken, the suit also names as defendants Mr. Solomon and Solomon Asset Management Inc. Mr. Solomon became a witness against Mr. Milken last year in exchange for a grant of immunity from prosecution, and resigned from the company he founded.

Mr. Solomon was once one of Mr. Milken's largest customers and managed a high-yield portfolio for the Finsbury fund. Mr. Solomon, who is believed to have provided the government with information about Finsbury, had not been sued before for his activities with Mr. Milken and Drexel.

Finsbury alleges in its suit that Mr. Milken conspired to induce Mr. Solomon to cheat Finsbury by "promising and providing Mr. Solomon and his company with other lucrative deals, fraudulent tax losses and other illegal benefits in the nature of money, kickbacks and gratuities." Mr. Milken also has pleaded guilty to helping Mr. Solomon create fraudulent tax losses.

Although Finsbury alleges that Drexel was "a knowing and active participant in the criminal fraud scheme," Drexel isn't being charged because it is currently operating under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code and is protected from claims by creditors.

Andrew Levander, a New York attorney who is representing Finsbury, wasn't available to comment yesterday. A spokesman for Mr. Milken declined to comment on the suit.

Martin Auerbach, the New York attorney who is representing Mr. Solomon, said: "We do not believe that Finsbury's complaint accurately reflects events as they occurred. However, we also do not believe that newspapers are the right place to set the record straight."

SOUTHWESTERN BELL loses $15 million antitrust case involving phone books.

A federal court jury in Amarillo, Texas, found that Southwestern Bell Corp. illegally tried to monopolize telephone directory advertising in parts of Texas and Oklahoma.

Upholding claims that the St. Louis company violated federal antitrust laws, the jury awarded the damages to two independent telephone directory publishers, Great Western Directories Inc., of Amarillo, and Canyon Directories Inc. in nearby Canyon, Texas.

The ruling is a major boost for independent publishers, who are engaged in an increasingly fierce battle with local telephone companies for lucrative yellow-page advertising. Independent telephone directory publishers proliferated after the 1984 court decree breaking up the Bell system. But in order to compete, they have to buy copyrighted white-page telephone listings from local phone companies. Such listings are needed because they are the most current, and because they provide leads for selling ads in their own yellow pages.

"Independent publishers from across the country have supported this case because their survival depends on their ability to get directory listings at reasonable prices," says Nancy Stone, an attorney in Amarillo for Garner, Stone & Lovell, counsel for the plaintiffs.

Great Western and Canyon contended that Southwestern violated the law by unreasonably raising the price it charged for the listings, which include the names, addresses and phone numbers of subscribers. In 1988, Canyon paid $300.70 for the right to copy 6,200 listings, the suit alleged, but since then it has had to pay $5,000 annually. Great Western's price per listing went from 30 cents to $1, the suit said.

In upholding the allegations, the jury awarded $5 million to Great Western, which serves several cities in Texas and Oklahoma, and $9,400 to Canyon, which serves its local market. Under federal antitrust laws, the damages are tripled.

Ms. Stone said the verdict "will cause a lot of other telephone companies across the country to take a look at what they're doing."

But a spokesman for Southwestern Bell sharply disagreed. "We believe the jury's decision is absurd and we're confident the decision will be overturned on appeal," said Gray Kerrick, senior vice president, corporate communications.

NEW ALLEGATIONS are made in pollution suit against Ashland.

As expected, lawyers representing plaintiffs in an environmental suit against Ashland Oil Inc. amended their complaint, alleging fraud and attempts to obstruct justice.

The complaint alleges that a 1988 letter from Ashland Chairman John R. Hall assuring residents that emissions from a refinery in Catlettsburg, Ky., were harmless was false. The complaint says Art Smith, an air pollution expert employed by West Virginia, testified that if a person were to eat toxic emissions from the plant, as the letter suggested they could do safely, "they would need a stomach and esophagus transplant."

Mr. Smith testified at a trial in May in which a $10.3 million verdict was rendered in favor of four people against Ashland. The plaintiffs are part of 1,200 residents who have other claims pending and are seeking more than $2 billion in damages.

The amended complaint, filed in Kanawha County Circuit Court, claims Ashland tried to obstruct justice by distributing things of value in the county. For example, the complaint alleges that Ashland gave "thousands of dollars in cash awards" to teachers, gave cash to a festival commission and a war memorial and maintained a booth to pass out promotional items. The plaintiffs also claim that Ashland formed, or caused to be formed, a group to launch an offensive against the litigants. After the trial, some of plaintiffs were threatened and harrassed, the complaint says.

Ashland officials couldn't immediately be reached. Earlier the company asked the court to overturn the $10.3 million verdict or to grant the company a new trial, alleging that 297 errors occurred during the trial.

ILLINOIS can't give up its lake bed, judge rules.

A federal judge blocked Loyola University from filling in 18.5 acres of Lake Michigan along the shoreline on Chicago's North Side. U.S. District Judge Marvin E. Aspen, citing an 1892 ruling of the U.S. Supreme Court, said the state couldn't give up its public trust over the lake bed of Lake Michigan.

Upholding the claim of an environmental group, Judge Aspen said the state's sale of 20 acres of lake bottom to Loyola for $10,000 was "a transparent giveaway of public property to a private entity."

The university began driving steel pilings at the site last week and had already sunk $2 million on planning, lobbying and construction commitments for its projected $6 million campus expansion. Jeffrey P. Smith, of Chicago's Gottlieb & Schwartz, represented the Lake Michigan Federation on a no-fee basis.

A Loyola spokesman said the university was "surprised and dismayed" by the ruling but hadn't decided whether to appeal the permanent injunction.
