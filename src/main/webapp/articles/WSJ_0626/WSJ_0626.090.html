Armco Inc. to Post 2nd-Period Loss, May Omit Dividend

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 06/26/90

Armco Inc. said it will post a second-quarter loss and may omit its quarterly dividend, reflecting persistent problems in its reinsurance business. The Parsippany, N.J., steelmaker didn't disclose the size of the expected second-quarter and first-half losses, but said they could violate certain debt convenants and prevent continued payment of the 10 cent-a-share dividend. Armco said its board will examine options, which the company wouldn't identify, to continue payment of the dividend and noted that cumulative preferred stock dividends won't be affected.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Armco common fell $1.125, to $7.375. Still, analysts noted that the company had $640 million in cash at the end of the first quarter and that the dividend announcement, while disappointing, doesn't indicate a liquidity problem.

Armco said it will take a $70 million after-tax charge in the second quarter, including $30 million related to the Armco Financial Services Group companies, which are being liquidated. The bulk of the remaining charges, which were previously announced, reflect a $20 million jury award against Armco in a 1986 breach of agreement suit and a $15 million federal tax assessment.

In this year's first quarter, Armco had net income of $12.9 million, or 12 cents a share. A year ago, Armco had net income of $75.6 million, or 84 cents a share, including a $45 million loss from discontinued operations.

Armco ventured into the reinsurance and financial services business in the 1970s and early 1980s to reduce dependence on the cyclical steel market. But the operations have been plagued by huge claims. In 1984, the company stopped writing new business and began liquidating the bulk of its insurance companies.

The company has taken several insurance-related write-offs since then. However, with this latest charge, the company said it "is confident that the major problems {in its finance and insurance companies} are behind it."

Charles Bradford, an independent steel analyst, said he was encouraged by the company's optimistic outlook. "For the first time, the company is able to say it is confident now that the problems are behind it," he said.

Should Armco be forced to suspend its dividend, it would mark the second time in seven years. In 1984, the company omitted its then 40-cent a share dividend to save cash in the face of major steel and insurance related write-offs. The 10-cent-a-share dividend was reinstated this past year.
