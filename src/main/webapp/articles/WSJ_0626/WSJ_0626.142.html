Melville to Buy Peoples Drug Stores For $330 Million

  • Author: Michael Selz
  • Date: 06/26/90

RYE, N.Y. -- Melville Corp. announced plans to create the nation's fifth-largest drugstore chain by acquiring Peoples Drug Stores Inc. for $330 million. Melville, which owns CVS drugstores, an 811-unit chain in 14 states, mostly in the Northeast, with sales in 1989 of $1.95 billion, agreed in principle to buy Peoples. Peoples, a subsidiary of Montreal-based Imasco Ltd., operates 490 stores in five mid-Atlantic states and the District of Columbia. It had 1989 sales of $1.02 billion. The acquisition would increase the size of Melville's drugstore division more than 50%.

Melville, which also owns specialty retailers of apparel, footwear, toys and household furnishings, said it will finance the acquisition from operating cash flow and existing lines of credit.

At the start of 1989, Peoples was nearly twice its current size. But in three separate transactions, Imasco sold 319 stores to focus on the chain's core mid-Atlantic market. Analysts say the remaining stores were Peoples' best, and the price Melville agreed to pay reflects it. While Melville said it is paying slightly less than book value, analysts say the purchase price, based on Peoples' 1989 operating income of $14 million, is a hefty multiple of 24 times earnings.

"The stores they picked up are the successful core of the drugstores that remained," says Bruce Buckley, editor of Drug Store News, a trade journal. "But it sounds like Imasco got a good price."

"CVS and Peoples make an ideal geographic and strategic fit," said Stanley P. Goldstein, Melville's chairman and chief executive officer. "Both have excellent store locations, with similar customer profiles, and Peoples is positioned in dynamic and growing markets."

Mr. Goldstein, who co-founded CVS in 1963, defended the price he is paying for Peoples. The chain's operations already are improving, he said. "We see this as an underperforming asset."

Robert Huth, Melville's chief financial officer, said "over time, Peoples should add substantially to the growth and profitability of the drugstore divisions."

William Smith, an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., says, "There's definitely room for improvement" in Peoples' operating profit margin, which is less than 2%. By contrast, CVS's operating margin in 1989 was 7.8%. But given Mr. Goldstein's record at CVS, which is highly regarded among drugstore analysts, the acquisition "looks like a good fit," Mr. Smith says.

In 1989, Melville earned $398 million on sales of $7.5 billion. In addition to CVS, its stores operate under the Marshalls, Wilsons, Chess King, Meldisco, Thom McAn, and Kay-Bee Toy names.

Melville expects to continue to operate Peoples stores under its current name.
