LEISURE & ARTS: Time Off: Diversions and Excursions June 26-July 9

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/26/90

ART

New York Public Library: "Portugal-Brazil: The Age of Atlantic Discoveries" -- More than 150 items -- navigational instruments, rare books and letters, early maps, original 15th- and 16th-century prints, oil paintings and illuminated manuscripts -- documenting Portugal's seafaring ways and the encounter of Portuguese and Brazilian cultures in the 16th century. Highlights include the Treaty of Tordesillas (1494), which drew a dividing line in the Atlantic Ocean granting Portugal all land to the east of the line and Spain all land to the west; a letter dated May 1, 1500, to Dom Manuel of Portugal relaying the first news to Europe about Brazil; and the first edition of Sir Thomas More's "Utopia" (1516), which was heavily influenced by written accounts of Portuguese discoveries. Fifth Avenue and 42nd Street, through Sept. 1.

Arthur M. Sackler Gallery, Smithsonian Institution, Washington, D.C.: "Yokohama: Prints From Nineteenth-Century Japan" -- Eighty-five color woodblock prints tracing the development of the city of Yokohama from 1860 to 1890, the period in which the feudal structure collapsed and imperial power was re-established under the Emperor Meiji. Of special interest are those that depict foreign ships arriving in Yokohama harbor; images of foreigners that show a Japanese fascination with Western clothing and furnishings; and prints intended to help Japanese pronounce words in English and other languages. Through Sept. 9. The exhibition then travels to the Asian Art Museum of San Francisco (Oct. 10-Dec. 9), and the Los Angeles County Museum of Art (Jan. 10-March 10, 1991).

Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts, Philadelphia: "Sculptures by Duane Hanson" -- Seventeen life-size and extraordinarily lifelike polyvinyl people sculptures created by this Minnesota-born artist. Among others, you'll meet a tired, beer-bellied traveler leaning on his bags, a couple of older folks seated at a luncheonette table, and two portly tourists loaded down with cameras. Broad and Cherry Streets, through Sept. 23.

FESTIVAL

Caramoor Music Festival, Katonah, N.Y., presents nine weeks of music on the Caramoor estate. Highlights of the event, in its 45th season, are: a rare performance of Robert Schumann's "Scenes From Goethe's `Faust'; world premieres by Nikolai Karetnikov and William Schimmel; a Fourth of July salute to Broadway composers; a mini J.S. Bach festival; and jazz from the Count Basie Orchestra. Through Aug. 19. (914) 232-1252.

Colorado Music Festival, Boulder, celebrates its 14th season with 30 symphony-orchestra, chamber-orchestra and chamber-music/recital performances. Music director Giora Berstein and the 80-member Colorado Festival Orchestra will be joined by such guest artists as conductors Alun Francis and Antoni Wit; violinist Miriam Fried; and pianists Christopher O'Riley and Grant Johannesen. Among the special programs are a Sunday-evening Classic Series featuring the works of Schubert, Mozart, Haydn and Bach; and a Tuesday-evening Celebrity Series showcasing artists and ensembles in solo recital and chamber-music concerts. Most performances at Chautauqua Auditorium, 900 Baseline Road. Through Aug. 3. (303) 449-2413.

MUSIC

Lyric Opera of Cleveland kicks off its Al Fresco Festival season with performances of Rossini's "Cinderella." Joyce Campana sings the title role; Donald Sherrill is Don Magnifico, Cinderella's stepfather; and Fred Love and Howard Carr portray the servant and prince, respectively. Stephen Sulich conducts. June 26, 28 and 30. Also on the schedule: Jerry Bock and Sheldon Harnick's "She Loves Me," with Michael Milton and Nancy Hudson as secret pen pals Georg and Amalia (July 12, 14, 17, 19 and 21); and Mozart's "The Magic Flute" (Aug. 2, 4, 7, 9, 10 and 11). Cleveland Institute of Music, 11021 East Boulevard, University Circle. (216) 231-2910.
