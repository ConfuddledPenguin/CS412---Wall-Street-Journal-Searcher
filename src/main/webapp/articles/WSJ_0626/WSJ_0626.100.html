International: Beijing Permits Fang Lizhi to Leave U.S. Embassy and Travel to Britain

  • Author: Adi Ignatius
  • Date: 06/26/90

BEIJING -- Chinese authorities allowed dissident Fang Lizhi to leave his yearlong refuge in the U.S. Embassy and travel overseas, removing a major irritant in U.S.-China relations. The astrophysicist and outspoken human-rights advocate left Beijing yesterday with his wife, Li Shuxian, aboard a U.S. military plane. The couple, who took sanctuary in the embassy after the brutal suppression of last June's pro-democracy movement, will resettle in Britain, diplomats said. He has accepted a post at Cambridge University.

China's official Xinhua news agency said the pair were given "lenient treatment" so they could seek medical care abroad. Chinese and diplomatic sources said Mr. Fang has a heart ailment.

The freeing of the couple, accused by Chinese authorities of "instigating" last spring's demonstrations, may prompt calls in Washington for improved U.S.-China ties. The Bush administration has argued that allowing the pair to leave was a prerequisite for better relations.

The White House immediately declared, "This humanitarian action is a far-sighted, significant step that will improve the atmosphere for progress in our bilateral relations." Noting that Dr. Fang's second son, who plans to study in the U.S., is still in China, State Department spokeswoman Margaret Tutwiler said, "We have no reason to believe that the son will have any difficulty obtaining permission to study abroad."

The decision to allow the pair to leave doesn't markedly alter the political situation in China. Hundreds of persons arrested after the Tiananmen incident have been released from prison, but hundreds more prisoners of conscience remain, often without being charged. But the release of Mr. Fang is sure to bolster President Bush's argument that his China policy has achieved tangible benefits.

U.S.-China ties have been icy since June 4, 1989, when Chinese troops killed hundreds of demonstrators in Beijing. President Bush curtailed most high-level visits and suspended military aid and some economic assistance. Washington also blocked the resumption of normal lending to China from multilateral agencies.

The Fang release may have been timed by Beijing to ward off further sanctions. Congress is preparing to vote -- almost certainly favorably -- on Mr. Bush's recommendation to retain beneficial trade terms for China. But some lawmakers have threatened an attempt to make future trade ties with China dependent on Beijing's human rights record.

State Department spokeswoman Tutwiler denied "categorically" that the U.S. and China cut any deals trading the Fang release for a lifting of U.S. sanctions against China. Indeed, she said China would have to free other prisoners and stop jamming the Voice of America if it wants "a more normal relationship."

China for months has indicated a willingness to allow the pair to leave, but only under certain conditions, including an admission of guilt as well as a guarantee that they wouldn't engage in anti-China activities overseas. The Xinhua report hinted at a face-saving compromise: It said the two dissidents admitted they had opposed the Communist Party's monopoly on power and ideology, which amounts to a technical violation of China's constitution.

Such a statement enables Beijing to maintain that the pair had repented. At the same time, the pair apparently never admitted any responsibility for last year's demonstrations, the core allegation against them. Xinhua also indicated that the couple agreed that "they will not engage in activities directed against China after they leave the country."
