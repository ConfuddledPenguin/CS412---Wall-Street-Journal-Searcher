What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/05/90

TIMOTHY RYAN WON Senate approval to be the nation's top savings and loan regulator, effectively nullifying a court decision that had derailed the overhaul of the S&L industry. The surprisingly wide margin of 62-37 in favor of Ryan followed a six-hour debate about whether the labor lawyer is qualified for the job. ---

UAL's board meets today to consider a sweetened buy-out bid from United Air's unions valued at over $200 a share, or $4.36 billion. Though several directors appeared to favor the new bid, it faces strong opposition from UAL's chairman, Stephen Wolf.

---

Circle K reached a preliminary accord with senior creditors and its largest shareholder to delay debt payments for seven months, narrowly averting a partial default.

---

Ford agreed to pay $6 million to settle a personal-injury suit involving lap-only seat belts in the rear seats of its Escort model. The huge settlement likely will add impetus to the debate over the safety of such belts.

---

Treasury bonds rallied and stocks fell on rumors that a big institutional investor in the Tokyo stock market was in financial trouble. The Dow Jones industrials closed off 17.34, at 2719.37. The dollar finished mixed.

The Group of Seven industrial nations gather in Paris this Saturday to try to shore up the yen and improve the coordination of economic policy.

---

Baxter International will take a $566 million first-quarter charge to cover plant closings, a 10% cut in its work force, and the elimination of certain lines. The charge will nearly wipe out the health-care products and services firm's profit for the year.

---

U.S. car and truck sales rose 2.9% in late March, helped by hefty cash rebates and some unusual incentives. The gain sparked optimism among auto makers despite continued uncertainty about the economy and rates.

---

Tax-increase options being prepared for the House Budget panel's chairman include raising the top corporate rate and limiting the deduction for state and local taxes.

---

Stock mutual funds generally beat the market for the first time in two years, though they still were down an average 2.31% for the first quarter.

---

Bank of New England plans to cut its work force by 5,600 employees, or about one-third, as expected. But the bank acknowledged it faces further increases in nonperforming assets and loan-loss provisions.

---

Most of Bonwit Teller's retail stores were ordered liquidated by a bankruptcy court, which rejected lastminute appeals to save the chain.

---

News Corp.'s Fox Film unit is joining the growing wave of Hollywood studios and production firms seeking Japanese financial backing.

---

The alcoholic-beverage industry is in a rage over a bill introduced in Congress yesterday that would require health warnings in all ads.

---

A big Avon shareholder, Chartwell Associates, boosted its stake to over 15% from 10%, sources said. Avon's stock climbed $1.25, to $35.25.

---

Tougher job-discrimination rules were approved by the Senate Labor panel. The bill is aimed at countering recent Supreme Court decisions that made job bias harder to prove.

---
