Letters to the Editor: For Yanomani Women, Life Is Doubly Brutal

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/05/90

Your March 21 page-one article "Amazon Tragedy: White Man's Malaria and Pollution Imperil Remote Tribe in Brazil" on the Yanomami tribe in Brazil stirred up lively discussion in my classroom. According to the noted anthropologist Marvin Harris, the Yanomami have been nicknamed "The Fierce People" because they practice wife-beating and female infanticide. Quoting from his book "Cannibals and Kings": "They practice an especially brutal form of male supremacy involving polygyny, frequent wife beating, and gang rape of captured enemy women." Furthermore, it is acceptable behavior for a Yanomami male to trade his wife for food and protection. When the population experiences a shortage of women for childbearing, it is equally acceptable to raid other tribes for them. Our question is: Does such a society deserve protection from the 20th century? Or to put it another way: Are the gold seekers really the "bad guys" in this story as the article suggests?

Perhaps it is the anthropologists who have the most to gain by preserving such a brutal, primitive culture. They depend upon such groups for their research grants. I don't think the Yanomami women enjoy being beaten, raped and traded like objects. In fact, the article stated that the Yanomami, in general, crave the technology the white man is bringing. What's wrong with letting them have it?

Marilyn Faulkenburg

Professor of Managerial Communications

Menlo College

Atherton, Calif.
