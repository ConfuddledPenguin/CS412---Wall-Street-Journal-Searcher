American Electric, U.S. to Share Cost Of Combination Unit

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/05/90

COLUMBUS, Ohio -- American Electric Power Co. said it agreed with the U.S. Department of Energy to share the $660 million cost of building a commercial-scale pressurized fluidized bed combustion unit. The Energy Department will provide $185 million of the cost for the unit, which burns coal more efficiently and with less polluting emissions than standard boilers. American Electric will provide the remaining funds.

The technology hasn't previously been employed in full-scale utility plants in North America, American Electric said. Fluidized bed plants burn pulverized coal in the presence of limestone, which removes most of the sulphur dioxide, and lower operating temperatures reduce emissions of nitrogen oxides. Those compounds are responsible for acid rain and other pollution problems.

Preliminary engineering design for the facility is under way, American Electric said. The study includes a detailed evaluation of repowering a unit at the utility's Philip Sporn generating plant in New Haven, W.Va., with a 330,000-kilowatt fluidized bed combustion unit, or building a completely new facility, the company said.

A fluidized bed unit at the Sporn plant would start commercial operation in 1996, American Electric said, but additional environmental studies would delay start-up of a completely new facility until 1998.

American Electric this year will begin operating a 70,000-kilowatt unit employing the new technology at a mothballed generating plant in Brilliant, Ohio. That unit is a joint project with the Energy Department and the Ohio Coal Development Office, a state agency.
