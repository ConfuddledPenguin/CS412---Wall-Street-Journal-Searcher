Heard on the Street: Sotheby's Art Auction Flop Raises Question Whether Japanese Collectors Are Cutting Back

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 04/05/90

NEW YORK -- The stock of Sotheby's Holdings Inc. took a hit on news that the firm's auction of Impressionist and modern paintings in London Tuesday night flopped. In more than triple the usual volume, Sotheby's stock slid 1 1/4, or 6%, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading, to 21 3/8. The issue for investors is whether the auction, which took in $63 million, well below Sotheby's pre-sale estimate of $75 million to $99 million, was an aberration. Was it, as Sotheby's officials and supporters maintain, a minor-league sale that lacked a masterpiece and included some ho-hum paintings? Or was it the first sign that the Japanese collectors who have been major players in the art market are retreating because of the plunge in the Tokyo stock market?

"The sale was a midseason, minor" auction, said Michael Ainslie, chairman of the firm, from London. Some of the artworks up for bid were not "fresh to the market," and others may have had unrealistically high price estimates. About $35 million of the total spent at the auction came from Japanese bidders, including the $6.1 million for a Marc Chagall painting that broke the artist's record, said Mr. Ainslie.

A Monday-evening London auction by Sotheby's counterpart, London-based Christie's International PLC, had similar dim results. The sales total also came in below the lowest pre-sale estimate and 21% of the artworks, by dollar value, didn't sell. However, some of the most expensive paintings at both auctions were purchased by Japanese collectors. Said Mr. Ainslie, the Japanese "are by no means out of the market."

Neither are some of the institutional players in Sotheby's stock. Michael Gaffney of Security Pacific Investment Managers Inc. of Los Angeles, which has an undisclosed position in the stock, said he isn't selling as the result of "one bad auction."

But a Wall Street bearish on art has been battering Sotheby's shares lately; in early March, the stock was trading at almost four above its current level. Its all-time high was $37, reached Oct. 19.

Ken Heebner of Capital Growth Management, which is affiliated with Loomis Sayles, said he sold more than 660,000 shares of Sotheby earlier this year -- not because he thought the art boom had ended, but because he expects it to end "at some point" and didn't want to try to time the bust.

Is the bloom off the art boom? Not yet, say some art dealers, who blame the failure of the Sotheby's sale on the lack of exciting merchandise and a softening in demand for "second" and "third" tier Impressionist works that sell for a mere $1 million or so. Yet at another Sotheby's auction yesterday in London, of Russian avant-garde paintings from the famed Costakis collection, 76% of the works, by dollar value, didn't sell.

But analysts who had been bullish on Sotheby's stock through its long run-up in 1988 and 1989 aren't changing their tune. They say Sotheby's, which has no debt and now trades at less than 10 times estimated 1990 earnings, remains a good buy. Moreover, Sotheby's is broadening its business beyond the art market with a controversial plan to build a tower of luxury condominium apartments atop its Manhattan auction headquarters.

Salomon Brothers analyst Harvey Katz is maintaining his earnings estimates of $2.25 a share this year -- and recommends buying the stock on weakness. Mr. Katz bases his continued bullishness on three factors: Sotheby's first-quarter auction totals were sharply above last year's levels; Japanese buying is "visible and strong," and some artworks up for auction at Sotheby's blockbuster sales in May are of exceptional quality.

"Wall Street's perception is that the art market is one market, and one bad sale will panic the whole world," said Fred Wintzer of Alex. Brown & Sons, who reaffirmed his buy recommendation yesterday. But the art boom, and Sotheby's profits, are far more broadly based than the weakening Impressionist sector, he says.

But even its boosters agree that there may be booby traps on Sotheby's balance sheet, perhaps most prominently its policy of guaranteeing a minimum take to certain consigners of major art collections, and its willingness to grant loans using art as collateral. Sotheby's loan in 1987 of $25 million to Australian businessman Alan Bond looked dicey until he recently paid it off.

Will the perception that art is less of a sure-fire investment after this week's sales cause buyers to step back from the plate at the Sotheby's May sales? Those major auctions usually account for a sizable chunk of its annual revenue.

"That's the billion-dollar question," said Salomon's Mr. Katz. "But I don't think you can ring the bell and say it's over yet."
