Zephyr Gets You There Slowly, but Scenically

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/05/90

PLAINVIEW, Colo. -- Headlights piercing night, the California Zephyr slowly descends the Rockies toward Denver, far below. "It's like we're circling in an airplane," says Clint Parker, an assistant conductor. But the Zephyr is no 747. Its journey from the West Coast to Chicago takes 51 hours when it's on time -- which is seldom. But the 2,422-mile route offers spectacular views of snow-capped mountains, river valleys, ravines and deserts. No wonder it's Amtrak's most popular train. In the summer, the Zephyr averages 500 to 550 passengers.

From San Francisco, the through fare is $476, including space in an economy bedroom and meals. That compares with the one-way, restricted air coach fare of $498. The stainless-steel cars are the same double-deck Superliners that Amtrak uses on most of its Western trains. The dining car has declined from the old Zephyr days but is much improved from the early 1980s, when microwave cuisine arrived.

Fred Davison, a Mercedes-Benz service director from Downingtown, Pa., says he and his wife are aboard because "flying has got old hat. The romance is coming back into the trains."

The Zephyr reaches the Continental Divide at 9,239 feet while traversing the 6.2-mile Moffat Tunnel. Then it begins the sinuous descent into Denver, where the impressive station recalls another era. Passengers stretch their legs while the train takes on fuel and a mail car.

After snaking through freight yards, the Zephyr opens up to 79 miles an hour as it races through the night toward Chicago. The next day brings views of farms. Some passengers read. James Christen, a student from Naperville, Ill., plays a videotape. Subject: the great streamliners of the 1940s rounding curves and racing across prairies.
