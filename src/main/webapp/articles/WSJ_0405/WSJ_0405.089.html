Bank of New England to Slash Staff By One-Third, Faces New Capital Woes

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 04/05/90

BOSTON -- Bank of New England said it will reduce its work force by 5,600 employees, or about one-third, but the troubled bank-holding company acknowledged that it faces further increases in nonperforming assets and loan-loss provisions. The staffing reduction, which had been expected, includes about 1,700 layoffs. Attrition, consolidations and asset sales will account for most of the rest, the company said.

Lawrence Fish, chairman and chief executive officer, said in an interview that executive vice presidents and senior vice presidents were among those laid off, but he declined to identify individuals or say precisely how many senior officers will be let go.

The company said personnel cutbacks, asset sales and other measures are expected to reduce annual operating expenses by about $300 million when fully implemented.

But the reductions will also carry a big price tag. In its 10-K filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, the company noted that severance costs and other one-time expenses, along with "likely additions to the provision for credit losses . . . may further deplete capital and equity in the future." The company is under orders from federal regulators to improve its capital ratios.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Bank of New England's stock fell 37.5 cents, to $4.25, on volume of 903,300 shares.

In its filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Bank of New England said a continuing review of its loan portfolio is expected to result in an increase in nonperforming assets from the Dec. 31 level of $2.2 billion. It noted that the year-end total didn't include some $1 billion of loans that were still performing at year end, "but where borrowers, in management's opinion, were experiencing some financial difficulty."

The year-end allowance of $1.5 billion for possible credit losses did include "a substantial unallocated provision to reflect" the potential problem loans, according to the filing. Mr. Fish declined to estimate the first-quarter increase in nonperforming assets or loss provisions. Bank of New England's assets currently total $25 billion.

The SEC filing also indicated that the company's capital plan, to be submitted to regulators by tomorrow, could include an exchange offer for all or part of the company's $706 million in long-term debt. Mr. Fish stressed, however, that the initial plan would consist of a broad list of possible measures -- not a final blueprint. He declined to say what form a swap offer for the debt might take, although the filing noted that such an exchange "could have a dilutive effect on shareholders' equity."

A small, New York-based investment banking firm that claims to represent 40 institutional holders of about $200 million, or around 30%, of Bank of New England's long-term debt, said it sent a letter to Mr. Fish yesterday renewing requests for a meeting with bank officials.

"Our group would view an attempt by {the company} to unilaterally demand a compromise of its debt or a conversion into common stock with a great deal of suspicion in view of the total lack of communication {by} the company to date," wrote John Mueller, vice president of Whitman Heffernan Rhein & Co., according to a copy of the letter supplied by the firm. He added, however, "Our group may be willing to make certain sacrifices to make the company financially viable {if we have} an opportunity to evaluate all of the alternatives."

The letter also noted "persistent rumors that a change-of-control transaction, possibly involving regulatory assistance and a compromising of the debt, is being negotiated with a West German financial institution." Bank of New England has declined to comment on such rumors, which have surfaced periodically since January.

Bank of New England, in its filing, said its capital plan may also include the sale of the company's Maine and Rhode Island subsidiary banks. Possible sale of its large Connecticut bank wasn't mentioned.

The company also disclosed that one creditor, Penn Mutual Life Insurance Co., has sued for alleged breach of covenant. Penn Mutual, which holds a $10 million, 14% note due in 1996 from Bank of New England's BNE Massachusetts Corp. subsidiary, has charged in U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania that Bank of New England's asset-sales program breached a covenant concerning the sale of assets. The lawsuit also alleges breaches of information and other covenants, according to Bank of New England, which said it believes it has "meritorious defenses to the claims."

The suit seeks recovery of the $10 million in principal, accrued interest, a $255,000 premium and expenses. Bank of New England said payment of the note is current, with interest having been paid when due. Principal is payable in $1,430,000 installments on each July 15 from 1990 through 1996.
