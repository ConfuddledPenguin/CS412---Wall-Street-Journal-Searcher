Politics & Policy: Job-Bias Measure Aimed at Overturning High Court Decisions Clears Senate Unit

  • Author: John E. Yang
  • Date: 04/05/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Labor Committee approved comprehensive legislation aimed at overturning the effect of several Supreme Court decisions handed down last year that made job bias harder to prove. GOP Sens. David Durenberger of Minnesota and James Jeffords of Vermont joined the panel's nine Democrats to pass the measure on an 11-5 vote after rejecting attempts by Sen. Orrin Hatch (R., Utah) to weaken it.

Sen. Hatch, the panel's ranking GOP member, indicated he would offer other amendments when the bill is considered by the full Senate, probably next month. A similar bill is pending in the House.

"It is no mere overturning of a few Supreme Court decisions," Sen. Hatch said. "It is, in my view, a radical reworking of our nation's basic employment anti-discrimination law."

The measure, the top legislative priority of civil-rights groups this year, is opposed by the Bush administration and conservative policy groups, who say it would result in hiring quotas and reverse discrimination.

Lawmakers voted, 12-4, to defeat Mr. Hatch's attempt to exempt health and safety rules from a provision that would force employers to justify employment practices shown to have a discriminatory impact. Committee Chairman Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.) charged that the change would give employers "carte blanche to adopt practices that have kept women and minorities from fully participating under the guise of health and safety."

The committee also rejected, 11-5, a Hatch amendment that would have expanded the circumstances under which co-workers could challenge court-approved affirmative-action plans agreed upon by employers and minority or women employees.

Last year, Mr. Bush said legislation wasn't necessary to respond to the court's decisions. But after a coalition of Democrats and moderate Republicans introduced legislation in February, the Justice Department prepared a more modest measure that would address some of the same concerns.

The lawmakers' bill has been crafted to avoid addressing a contentious high court ruling that barred the use of racial quotas in state and local government affirmative-action programs.

The measure approved by the committee would:

-- Specify that an 1866 civil-rights law, giving all citizens equal rights to make contracts, covers lawsuits alleging racial harassment on the job.

-- Allow workers to challenge alleged discriminatory seniority plans when those plans affect them, rather than when they are adopted.

-- Force employers to justify employment practices shown to have a discriminatory impact.

-- Protect court-approved affirmative-action plans agreed upon by employers and minority or women employees by permitting only under certain circumstances lawsuits by co-workers claiming reverse discrimination.

-- Make clear that it is illegal to make any employment decisions based on race, ethnicity, gender or religion.

-- Assure victims of intentional bias the right to seek monetary damages.
