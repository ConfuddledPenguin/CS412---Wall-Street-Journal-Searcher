Ottawa Aims to Set Clean-Air Accord With U.S. by Fall

  • Author: John Urquhart
  • Date: 04/05/90

OTTAWA -- The Canadian government hopes to negotiate a clean-air agreement with the U.S. by this autumn that will settle Canada's longstanding grievance over U.S. acid rain pollution, Canadian Environment Minister Lucien Bouchard said. Mr. Bouchard told the House of Commons that the U.S. Senate's approval Tuesday of a comprehensive clean-air bill "moves us much closer to the opening of formal negotiations" for a U.S.-Canada air quality agreement.

He said U.S. and Canadian officials have met frequently in the past year to discuss the framework and "essential details" of such an agreement. He predicted formal negotiations with the U.S. will begin soon and "we hope that it will be possible to conclude an agreement by the fall."

The acid rain issue has been the Canadian government's biggest grievance with the U.S. for much of the past decade. Canada blames wind-carried pollution from the U.S. for half of the acid rain that falls on Canada. Acid rain occurs when gases from smokestacks and other sources combine with moisture in the air and fall to the ground, often hundreds of miles from the pollution source.

The Canadian government claims that acid rain has destroyed fish and other life in at least 14,000 Eastern Canadian lakes. The government says an additional 300,000 lakes are damaged or vulnerable.

The Canadian government has reached agreements with the country's seven Eastern-most provinces requiring a 50% reduction in sulfur dioxide emissions by 1994. Sulfur dioxide, which is emitted by power plants, smelters and other sources, is ranked as the biggest single cause of acid rain.

Prime Minister Brian Mulroney and President Bush are expected to discuss the acid rain issue when they meet in Toronto on Tuesday. Soon after Mr. Bush was elected, he assured Mr. Mulroney that the U.S. administration would take concrete action on acid rain.

The proposed agreement with the U.S. would set targets for reducing acid gas emissions. The Canadian government says that sulfur dioxide emissions in Eastern Canada are already down 40% from their 1980 level.

Wind-carried Canadian pollution accounts for 10% to 25% of the acid rain that falls in the Northeastern U.S., Canadian officials estimate.

The Canadian government and the Carter administration agreed in 1979 to negotiate an accord to control trans-boundary air pollution. But Ottawa wasn't able to negotiate such a pact with the Reagan administration.
