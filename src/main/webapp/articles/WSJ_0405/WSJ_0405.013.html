Technology: IBM Researchers `Write' With Atoms On a Metal Surface

  • Author: Richard L. Hudson
  • Date: 04/05/90

LONDON -- Researchers at International Business Machines Corp. achieved a milestone in ultramicroscopic manufacturing, by "writing" with individual atoms on a metal surface. The researchers used a special IBM-invented microscope to position individual atoms of xenon gas precisely on a nickel plate. They arranged the xenon atoms into patterns -- for instance, tracing out the letters "IBM" in lines one atom wide.

The technological feat, described in today's issue of Nature, a weekly British science journal, is the latest of several recent steps towards the ultimate in micromanufacturing: precise manipulation of individual atoms and molecules. Scientists talk of one day being able to make ultrasmall electronic circuitry, with dimensions of just one atom or molecule. By comparison, the circuitry in today's top-performance computer chips is at least 1,000 times more coarse. In theory, smaller circuitry permits faster, less expensive and more complex computing.

But at the moment, such research is more laboratory curiosity than commercial reality. In their Nature report, the IBM researchers said use of their technique may be limited to certain types of atoms and molecules. Also, the equipment they used is too costly and complex to contemplate commercial applications anytime soon.

The technique, developed at IBM's Almaden Research Center in San Jose, Calif., uses the so-called scanning tunnelling microscope. The device, for whose invention two IBM scientists shared a Nobel Prize in 1987, is normally used in physics and materials laboratories to produce images of individual atoms and molecules. The microscope has a single-atom tip that, when energized by a tiny electric current, can trace the atomic surface contours of many materials.

By simply increasing the electric current passing through the tip, the Almaden researchers converted the device from a "reader" of atomic structures to a "writer." Specifically, the researchers scattered xenon atoms at random over a nickel surface, and then used the microscope's tip to drag each xenon atom into its desired position on the nickel surface. The result was uniform lines of closely spaced xenon atoms on top of the nickel surface.
