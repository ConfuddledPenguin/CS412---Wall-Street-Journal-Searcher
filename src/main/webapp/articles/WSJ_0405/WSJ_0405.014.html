Marketing & Media: Axel Springer Verlag To Expand, Redesign Its Medical Tribune

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/05/90

NEW YORK -- Axel Springer Verlag AG, hoping to build on its foothold in U.S. publishing, will introduce today a redesigned and expanded Medical Tribune, the national newspaper for physicians. The West German publisher bought the Medical Tribune in August from the family of its founder, the late Arthur Sackler, for an estimated $80 million. Medical Tribune was the first acquisition in the U.S. by Axel Springer, one of the largest newspaper publishers in Europe.

Axel Springer, as of this week's issue, converted the publication from a tabloid into a broadsheet and expanded its editorial matter to include life style features as well as clinical news. Published now every two weeks with editions in Japan, France, and West Germany, Medical Tribune will become a weekly in early 1991, said Richard Dauncey, president of Medical Tribune.

"We want to bridge the gap between the physicians' professional and private lives," said Mr. Dauncey. Axel Springer has spent about $8 million revamping the newspaper, which has U.S. circulation of about 125,000. "We go from clinical news to matters of cultural significance."

In a related development, Axel Springer is expected to launch more U.S. publications in the medical field. A consumer magazine called Family Health, now being tested in the U.K. by Axel Springer, may also be launched in the U.S. within a year, Mr. Dauncey said.

Axel Springer is tapping a lucrative advertising market. A large number of publications vie for about $420 million in pharmaceutical and medical-related advertising. The readership is potentially large also, with an estimated 500,000 physicians in the U.S. alone.

But even Axel Springer executives acknowledge they are expanding Medical Tribune during a depressed period. Advertising revenue for pharmaceuticals fell about 30% in the first quarter, said Mr. Dauncey.

"The U.S. publication is an important part of our becoming a world-wide provider of medical news," said Helmut Fluhrer, Axel Springer project manager for mergers and acquisitions. News from "the medical industry in the U.S. is vitally important to other countries," he said.
