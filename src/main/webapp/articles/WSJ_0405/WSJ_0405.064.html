House Bill Urges A Single Agency To Oversee Markets

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 04/05/90

WASHINGTON -- Two House members unveiled a bill to merge stock and futures regulation into a single agency, hoping to replace what they called "an archaic way of regulating financial instruments." The legislation, introduced by Reps. Dan Glickman (D., Kan.) and Dennis Eckart (D., Ohio), is the most sweeping proposal in the debate over who should supervise financial futures.

The congressmen said they would need strong Bush administration support to move this bill through. "This depends on what the administration decides to do," said Mr. Glickman, a member of the Agriculture Committee. The Treasury Department has supported a merger as one of several solutions to what it calls the "regulatory bifurcation."

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission currently monitors financial futures, as well as the rest of the futures industry. But the structure has come under attack from within the Bush administration, including the Securities and Exchange Commission, which regulates the stock and options markets, for creating stock-market volatility and hindering enforcement efforts.

Until now, most discussion about the regulatory split has focused on moving the monitoring of stock-index futures to the SEC. Stock-index futures, which are controversial because they can be used in program-trading strategies, allow investors to bet on the direction of a basket of stocks without buying the actual securities.

The Glickman-Eckart bill would create a single agency, called the Markets and Trading Commission, that would oversee most of the U.S. financial markets. The congressmen contend that merging the agencies could avoid one pitfall that some have feared, the weakening of the CFTC, which could occur if oversight of index futures were shifted to the SEC.

The agency also would control stock margins, which are currently set by the Federal Reserve Board. Futures margins would remain in the hands of the commodities exchanges, but a floor would be set. Margins are the good-faith deposits required for trades.

Chicago Board of Trade President Thomas Donovan has called the merger proposal "worth studying" and said that it's preferable to giving the SEC regulatory control over index futures. Chicago Mercantile Exchange officials have echoed Mr. Donovan's remarks.

A CFTC spokesman immediately attacked the bill, arguing that "we fail to see any need for merging the two agencies."

SEC Chairman Richard Breeden said the agency "wouldn't oppose the merger approach if that's the way Congress determines to deal with the problem of split jurisdiction." But he added that "a merger, while desirable from many perspectives, isn't absolutely necessary to solve the worst problems" of the regulatory division.
