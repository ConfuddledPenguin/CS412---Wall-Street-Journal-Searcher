Politics & Policy: Hubble Space Telescope, Plagued by Problems, May Open the Heavens but Has Cost the Earth

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 04/05/90

WASHINGTON -- The Hubble space telescope, poised to be launched by the space shuttle Discovery Tuesday, promises to be one of science's proudest achievements. It will hunt for black holes and help explain the birth of galaxies. It's also an example of Big Science gone bad.

The $1.54 billion telescope is at least seven years behind schedule, $1 billion over budget and doesn't work as well as expected. The reasons for that are more political than scientific. The National Aeronautics and Space Administration underestimated the telescope's price to win congressional approval. Then the agency drove up the cost and complexity by tying the telescope to the space shuttle, and using the project to stave off layoffs.

"We've been rolled too many times by megascience projects," complains Rep. Bob Traxler, the Michigan Democrat who heads the appropriations panel overseeing NASA. "They develop a constituency and keep going, no matter what the cost." In the past month, for instance, the Energy Department increased the price of its superconducting supercollider one-third to about $8 billion, and NASA engineers warned that the $30 billion space station faces costly design changes.

The space telescope is a case study in how science projects get out of hand. From the beginning, astronomers and NASA managers prized salesmanship as much as science. "A NASA mission gets started by advertising the potential of an idea," says Robert Brown, an astronomer who spent two years as the space telescope's chief scientist. "Then you solve the problems you find."

NASA's biggest problem in the 1970s, when the agency started selling and building the space telescope, was a lack of political support. The agency was desperately trying to line up backing for the space shuttle and fend off attempts to close its big Marshall Space Flight Center in Huntsville, Ala. The space telescope was designed to aid both efforts -- even though the cost to the program proved to be considerable.

Promoting the shuttle was the key. NASA designed the telescope so it could be launched into orbit and serviced by the shuttle, giving the shuttle a prominent role in space science. Shuttle servicing also gave NASA a rationale to slash the telescope's price tag so it would pass muster with Congress. NASA could dispense with plans for costly testing by promising to lug the telescope back from space and fix it on the ground if anything important broke.

"It was a political plus to use the shuttle," says Noel Hinners, a former NASA science official who pushed the telescope through Congress by citing the shuttle's technical merits, too. But years after winning approval of the telescope, NASA dropped its plans to haul the satellite back to Earth -- fearing that the extra handling could harm the instrument.

NASA kept its plans for in-orbit servicing, but that led to other problems. Designing the telescope so it would be safe for astronauts to work on in space increased costs sharply. The Challenger shuttle disaster in 1986 delayed the telescope's launch by three years. Meanwhile, the orbit the shuttle can reach -- about 350 miles above the ground -- is so low that Earth blocks the telescope's view 40% of the time. Overall, the space telescope will spend only one-third of its time making observations, just slighty better than an Earth-bound telescope, which can only work at night.

Internal NASA politics also set back the space telescope. NASA split the project between the Marshall facility, which the White House was threatening to close, and the agency's traditional astronomy center, Goddard Space Flight Center, in Greenbelt, Md. The two centers fought regularly over testing and design issues, say scientists involved in the project, while the telescope fell behind schedule and its cost escalated.

Marshall also was forced by the Pentagon to limit the number of managers it assigned to oversee the telescope. The military feared that too many civilians would learn about technology useful for spy satellites, says Robert Smith, a Smithsonian Institution historian who has written a book about the telescope. As a result of these tussles, says Mr. Smith, NASA managers didn't catch engineering problems early enough.

Now, in some important respects, the telescope isn't as good as advertised. Its ground-based computer system is woefully inefficient, says C.R. O'Dell, a former telescope chief scientist, who estimates that his observing time on the telescope has been cut nearly in half because of the problem. Scientists say the telescope's tracking system isn't quick enough to follow storm systems on nearby planets, which scientists want to study to help learn more about hurricanes on Earth. Its parts are aging.

The project's huge budget also led NASA to rule out smaller missions that it could have launched sooner. If NASA had built a smaller, cheaper telescope, "many of the discoveries that {Hubble} will make might have been made 10 years earlier," writes Freeman Dyson, a physicist at the Institute for Advanced Studies, in the astronomy magazine "Sky & Telescope."

NASA officials believe that the launch of the space telescope will silence the critics. Lennard Fisk, the agency's space-science chief, says shuttle servicing will enable NASA to update the telescope's instruments and repair it if anything goes wrong. Edward Weiler, a NASA space-telescope scientist, says the instrument "represents the biggest leap in astronomical capability since Galileo put a telescope to his eye."

But a little skepticism is in order. In his book, "The Space Telescope," Mr. Smith recounts an exchange a few years back between James Beggs, who then headed NASA, and Rep. Edward Boland, the Massachusetts Democrat who then headed the appropriations subcommittee responsible for the agency.

"The space telescope will be the eighth wonder of the world," boasted the NASA administrator.

"It ought to be at that price," replied the lawmaker.

Hubble Chronology

1946 -- Princeton astronomer Lyman Spitzer proposes orbiting space telescope

1958 -- NASA established

1962 -- National Academy of Sciences recommends a space telescope

1971 -- Early NASA space-telescope studies

1977 -- NASA wins congressional approval of space telescope; cost estimate of $435 million; 1983 launch date

1981 -- Space-telescope mirror completed, one year behind schedule

1983 -- Launch delayed to 1986; cost estimate of $1.18 billion

1984 -- NASA gives up idea of bringing space telescope back to earth for servicing

1986 -- Launch delayed after Challenger disaster

April 10, 1990 -- Scheduled launch of space telescope; cost estimate $1.54 billion
