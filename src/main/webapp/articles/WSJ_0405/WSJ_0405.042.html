Most of the Stores in Bonwit Teller Chain To Be Closed, Bankruptcy Judge Orders

  • Author: Jeffrey A. Trachtenberg
  • Date: 04/05/90

NEW YORK -- A U.S. bankruptcy-court judge ordered the liquidation of most Bonwit Teller retail stores, rejecting last-minute appeals to save the once-proud chain. At a court hearing, Judge Tina Brozman awarded five of the chain's 16 stores to a group led by developers Donald Trump, Alfred Taubman, Phil Palevsky, and Pyramid Cos., for about $21 million.

Three of those stores will be liquidated, as will eight others around the country, putting most of the chain's 1,800 employees out of work. Late last year, Bonwit's sister chain, B. Altman & Co., was entirely liquidated. The two chains' parent, L.J. Hooker Corp., filed for bankruptcy law protection last summer.

Pyramid, a Syracuse-based mall developer, will operate the Bonwit Teller stores in Buffalo and Boston and has acquired the rights to the Bonwit Teller name. In an interview, a senior Pyramid executive said that Pyramid intends to open a new Bonwit Teller store this fall in Syracuse. He estimated Pyramid's cost at $8.2 million plus the assumption of certain employee liabilities.

Mr. Trump has acquired the lease to the Bonwit Teller flagship at Trump Tower in New York for about $5 million. In an interview, he said he intends to close the store and lease it to at least two new tenants. Possible tenants include Chanel Inc. and Tiffany & Co., he said.

"We wanted the lease back because it has another 14 years to run," said Mr. Trump. "I'm very happy, and I have no doubts we'll be able to attract several prestigious retailers."

Mr. Trump said that Mr. Taubman bought back the lease to the Bonwit Teller store at the Mall at Short Hills, which is based in Short Hills, N.J., and that Mr. Palevsky bought the lease to the Bonwit Teller branch in Manhasset, L.I. Both stores also will be closed.

Judge Brozman awarded the Bonwit Teller store in Palm Beach to Frances Brewster, a southeast Florida specialty retailer that currently operates four stores. Frances Brewster will operate the Bonwit Teller store under its own name.

"We're very pleased that the name will go on, but we're quite disappointed that it won't be in more locations," said Elayne Garofolo, senior vice president of marketing at Bonwit Teller. "The employees are taking it as well as one would expect. They are being professional."

An attorney for L.J. Hooker said in court yesterday that former Bonwit Teller President William Ruben had indicated he was attempting to arrange financing to buy l3 stores. However, Mr. Ruben didn't make a formal bid, and Judge Brozman moved the case forward.

"We should have had more opportunity to save this business, because we had the financing," said Irwin M. Rosenthal, a senior partner at Baer, Marks & Upham, and an attorney for Mr. Ruben and his group of investors. "If they would have given us another hour, we could have saved the company and the jobs of its employees. It's tragic and a travesty of justice."

One of the country's oldest retailers, Bonwit Teller was founded in 1898 and soon became one of New York's most fashionable stores. Its former flagship on Fifth Avenue, since demolished, was for years a Manhattan landmark. Bonwit Teller had losses of approximately $30 million over the past three years, according to a spokesman for L.J. Hooker. Although it appeared to be turning around, it foundered last spring when L.J. Hooker stopped providing it with cash for new merchandise.

In addition to Boston and Buffalo, two other Bonwit Teller branches will continue to operate, at least temporarily. L.J. Hooker has reached a licensing agreement with Pyramid that will allow it to operate stores in Hooker-owned malls in Cincinnati and Columbia, S.C., under the Bonwit Teller name. But L.J. Hooker currently is trying to sell the two stores.

L.J. Hooker is owned by Sydney-based Hooker Corp., which is being managed by a court-appointed provisional liquidator.
