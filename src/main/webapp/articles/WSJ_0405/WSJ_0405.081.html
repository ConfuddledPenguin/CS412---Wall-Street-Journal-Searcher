Money Fund Assets Post Largest Drop Since January

  • Author: Georgette Jasen
  • Date: 04/05/90

Yields on money-market mutual funds remained relatively flat in the latest week, but assets posted the largest drop since early January. The average seven-day compound yield slipped to 7.97% from 7.98% in the week ended Tuesday, according to IBC/Donoghue's Money Fund Report, a Holliston, Mass.-based newsletter. Compound yields assume reinvestment of dividends and that the current yield continues for a year.

Assets of the 426 taxable funds tracked by Donoghue's fell by $1.5 billion to $383.6 billion. The previous decline in assets was less than $900 million in mid-March. Assets dropped by $3.6 billion in early January, although that was attributed to seasonal factors.

The latest week's drop was across the board, said Brenda Malizia Negus, editor of the newsletter, affecting general purpose funds, broker-dealer funds and funds open only to institutions. But Ms. Negus called the decline "not statistically significant," noting that it was well under 1% of total assets.

Although the deadline for paying income taxes is less than two weeks away, Ms. Negus said it's a bit early for that to be a significant factor in money-fund assets. The latest totals reflect checks written a week ago or more.

Assets rose steadily last spring as yields reached their recent highs. There was a falloff of about $435 million at the end of April, which analysts then attributed to an attractive stock market. Total money-fund assets have increased about $80 billion since then.

The latest money-fund yields reflect generally unchanged interest rates. Treasury bills sold at Monday's auction, for instance, yielded just two-hundredths of a percentage point less than the week before. Yields on bank certificates of deposit also were little changed in the latest week.

Average maturity of the funds' investments, which include short-term Treasury securities, CDs and commercial paper (short-term corporate IOUs), remained at 45 days. Maturities are thought to indicate which way portfolio managers think rates are headed; longer maturities permit them to lock in relatively high yields when rates are falling just as short maturities allow them to capture rising rates sooner.

The average seven-day simple yield of the taxable funds slid to 7.67% from 7.69%. The average 30-day simple yield edged up to 7.67% from 7.66% and the average 30-day compound yield rose to 7.97% from 7.95%.

Yields on tax-free funds rose. The average seven-day compound yield of the 220 tax-exempt funds tracked by Donoghue's rose to 5.57% from 5.49%. That's equal to 7.74% for an investor in the 28% tax bracket and 8.31% in the 33% bracket.

Assets of the tax-free funds fell by just under $100 million to $77.3 billion, while average maturity remained at 46 days.
