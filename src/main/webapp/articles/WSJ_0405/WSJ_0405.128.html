Wall Street Grows Treacherous for Analysts Who Speak Out

  • Author: Ellen E. Schultz
  • Date: 04/05/90

NEW YORK -- Peter Sidoti, former health-care analyst at Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., recalls the fireworks when he wrote a negative report about one of the firm's investment-banking clients. The corporate-finance department "went to Mike Milken, who called up the research director and gave him hell for jeopardizing business with a corporate-finance client," he says. While his boss stood up for him, Mr. Sidoti says, in the end the report wasn't issued.

Wall Street securities analysts have always had to fight pressures to inflate their opinions of the companies they follow. But these pressures are mounting as Wall Street struggles to survive the recession gripping the securities industry.

To some extent, the pressures come from outside -- as in the recent case of a Janney Montgomery Scott Inc. analyst fired after Donald Trump threatened to sue because of the analyst's comments.

But analysts are also feeling the heat more than ever from within their own firms. Brokerage houses insist they maintain a "Chinese Wall," completely separating their corporate finance and research departments. Yet at time of belt-tightening on Wall Street, when a number of firms have been shrinking research staffs, many analysts worry that an inappropriate opinion can land them in the frying pan.

"It's increasingly common that the investment-banking division would get analysts fired," says Barry Greenfield, portfolio manager of Fidelity Investments' Fidelity Fund. "I've seen some research departments on Wall Street fall apart in the past few years."

Robert Diener, president of American Health Properties Inc., a real-estate investment trust, says that some investment bankers have "implied that ratings {on his company} would rise if we did business with them."

Some investment professionals say that analysts face less pressure at firms that lack a strong corporate-finance business. Even where the pressures do exist, many analysts do manage to withstand them. But enough don't that at least some of the people who use their reports are dubious about their opinions.

"The first thing I look at is whether the firm is the investment banker for the company" the analyst is recommending, says Todd Schubert, an analyst with USAA Investment Management. "I'm a little skeptical and cynical at this point."

Whatever the reasons, brokerage houses make many more "buy" recommendations than "sell" recommendations. A tally of Wall Street recommendations on 1,500 companies by Zacks Investment Research Inc., in Chicago, shows that 44.6% are "buys" or "strong buys," and 45.6% are "holds." Only 9.7% are "sells" or "strong sells."

Mr. Sidoti's experience at Drexel, which happened in 1987, illustrates the internal pressures on Wall Street analysts. He says there was "a lot of yelling and screaming" when he wrote the negative report on the firm's corporate-finance client. He declines to name the company, but says the report "was rewritten to make it more palatable." But not even that was enough to head off intervention by junk-bond king Michael Milken, he says.

Mr. Sidoti is currently at County NatWest Securities USA, whose president and chief executive officer, Arthur Kirsch, was Drexel's research director at the time of the incident. He "stood up for me," Mr. Sidoti says.

"Clearly, we did work with the corporate-finance department, but we went out of our way to maintain independence," says Mr. Kirsch, who confirms the incident.

John Katz, who was associate research director at Drexel, is more blunt: "Milken was strongly supportive of his clients, and did a hell of a job for them."

The research department "did our darndest to resist" pressure from corporate finance, says Mr. Katz. "Sometimes we won, and sometimes along the way compromises took place. I've certainly seen other research materials go out that made me throw up."

Still, he says, "We made it clear when we had a bias, when we had an underwriting relationship." As a result, he says, the thinking was that institutional investors using the Drexel research reports had a red flag, "and unless they were completely stupid, they'd do a double check with someone with less involvement."

A spokesman for Mr. Milken denies the incident. A spokesman for Drexel, which is in liquidation, declines to comment.

The sort of pressures encountered by Mr. Sidoti have become more common as Wall Street firms, faced with depressed retail-brokerage business, junk-bond jitters and a sharp falloff in merger-and-acquisition activity, fight to hold onto a piece of the shrinking underwriting pie. "Everyone's trying to pay the rent right now, so the pressure's greater," says Kenneth Statz, a managing director at Chancellor Capital Management.

In November, Morgan Stanley & Co. chemical analyst Paul Leming found himself in trouble when Occidental Petroleum Corp. disagreed with his negative comments. Among other things, Mr. Leming observed that the price of Occidental shares rose when 91-year-old Chairman Armand Hammer underwent surgery for a heart pacemaker and suggested that Morgan Stanley customers be ready to sell if Mr. Hammer died.

Mr. Leming declines to comment, but people familiar with the situation say that Occidental Petroleum officials complained to Morgan Stanley and that the report was amended. Mr. Leming subsequently stopped covering Occidental and has since been promoted to principal at the investment banking firm. Morgan Stanley declined to comment, as did Occidental Petroleum.

Even if the corporate-finance side is quiescent, analysts can still get their arms twisted. "The pressure is always there" to give favorable ratings, says Jack Rivkin, director of research for Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc.

Most common, he says, is a company's "explicitly or implicitly threatening to withdraw {investment banking} business." Intimidation also arises "when you run into a company run by an egomaniac, or when you run into a small company."

When a Shearson analyst downgraded one stock, Mr. Rivkin remembers, the company's chief executive wouldn't speak to him for four years -- and then, only after the analyst upgraded the stock.

Jonathan Gray, who follows savings and loans for Sanford C. Bernstein, recalls that when he put out negative reports on Financial Corp. of America, "the company tried to shut me up with the implied threat of a lawsuit." But, he said, the chairman of his firm backed him up. Soon thereafter, FCA went bankrupt.

Whatever the source of the pressure, Wall Street research chiefs say it is essential to resist. If an analyst comes to be "viewed as a mouthpiece for investment banking, their career is over," says Michael Culp, director of research at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. As far as he is concerned, "The best mark of an analyst is having two managements that want you dead."

Still, analysts sometimes find it possible to skirt the issue. More and more, brokerage firms are switching from a simple buy-hold-sell rating system to one with as many as six layers that may include: strong buy, buy, outperform, attractive, hold, accumulate, neutral, long-term attractive, underperform, sell and strong sell.

Ostensibly, the gradations make it possible for analysts to express subtle differences in their feelings about companies. But some analysts and institutional investors say the new ratings can also allow considerable wiggle room.

As for making an outright sell recommendation, some analysts joke that it would be practically heretical. Besides, says one analyst, "a `buy' is more sexy." Brokers "can call everyone who owns {the stock}, and everyone who doesn't."
