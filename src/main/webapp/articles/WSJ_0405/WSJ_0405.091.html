Corporations' Bonds in Default Rose in Quarter

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 04/05/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar amount of corporate bonds defaulting in the first quarter quadrupled from a year ago, the latest indication that junk bond default rates could reach record levels this year. Municipal bond defaults increased 37% in the first quarter, but the rate for the entire year may be lower.

Those were among the findings in a report issued yesterday by the Bond Investors Association, a bondholders organization based in Miami Lakes, Fla.

The report said that 13 companies defaulted on 38 bond issues totaling $5.7 billion for the three months through March. That's up from the $1.3 billion in defaults by nine companies that occurred in the first quarter of 1989.

The latest default figures included $3.5 billion of bonds issued by companies controlled by Canada's Campeau Corp., the retail and real estate company that operates the struggling Allied Stores Corp. and Federated Department Stores Inc. in the U.S.

"In light of the first-quarter results and given the amount of issues on the brink of default, we feel our prediction of $15 billion in defaults for 1990 (may be) optimistic," said Richard Lehmann, president of the Bond Investors Association. He said more defaults are expected from "poorly structured businesses trying to support impossible debt loads."

The report also measured municipal bond defaults, which were said to have totaled $276.1 million. Mr. Lehmann said that municipal bond defaults for the entire year, however, could actually come in lower than last year.

But that outlook could change depending on one corporation: First Executive Corp., the big California insurance company that is one of the largest holders of high-yield junk bonds.

First Executive, which recently reported an $835.7 million fourth-quarter loss largely because of the dwindling value of its junk bond portfolio, provided guaranteed investment contracts as backing for more than $1.7 billion of municipal bonds.

"These bonds would be at risk should (First Executive) be seized by the California insurance commissioner," Mr. Lehmann said. "That could make 1990 a record year for municipal bond defaults," he said.
