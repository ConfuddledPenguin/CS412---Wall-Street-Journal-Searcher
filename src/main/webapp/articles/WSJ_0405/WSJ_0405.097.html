Wednesday's Markets: Bonds Surge As Tokyo Rumors Fly --- Fears of Troubles Facing Big Investor In Japan Are Cited

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 04/05/90

Rumors that a big Tokyo stock-market player was in financial trouble sent investors scurrying to the safety of U.S. Treasury bonds. Long-term Treasury-bond prices ended up more than one point, or more than $10 for each $1,000 of face amount, after having climbed 1 3/4 points as the unconfirmed rumors spread in midafternoon New York trading. The rumors sent the Dow Jones Industrial Average into a mild decline. The average fell 17.34 points to 2719.37 in moderate trading. The dollar was mixed.

The rumors about the inability of a big institutional investor to raise cash to support stock positions bought on credit came as worries intensified about the effects of further declines in the Tokyo stock market. Although U.S. investors have managed to shrug off the Tokyo market's slump, they are edgy about how the mounting financial troubles in Japan will be resolved.

Speculation among stock traders was that the Nikkei Index of 225 stocks could plunge between 1000 and 3000 points, or more than 10%, if a big Japanese institutional investor really is in trouble. The pace of selling in Japan hasn't yet taken on panic proportions-volume has been relatively light-but a financial crisis among one of the big market players could change that, especially among individual investors who are being asked to put up more money for stocks they bought on credit.

The rally in bond prices surprised many traders who looked for a quiet day leading up to the Treasury Department's disclosure of the size of Resolution Funding Corp.'s 40-year bond auction next week. The decision to offer $3.5 billion in 40-year bonds relieved traders who were worried that the Treasury would attempt another $5 billion bond sale, like the one in January that resulted in a two-point drop in long-term Treasury bond prices.

Bond investors also were holding back waiting for Friday's government report on employment, a key economic indicator. Economists expect the report to show that 160,000 to 180,000 jobs were created last month. While investors initially viewed that many new jobs as indicative of a strengthening economy, which would be bearish for bond prices, opinion has shifted to essentially neutral.

Currency traders said the dollar's modest retreat against the yen doesn't indicate a peak has been reached. Instead, they said traders are waiting for the results of this weekend's meeting of the Group of Seven major industrial nations.

In major market action:

Stock prices slumped in moderate trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 159.5 million shares. Declining issues on the Big Board were ahead of gainers 918 to 596.

Bond prices rallied. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year issue slid to 8.51%.

The dollar was mixed. In late afternoon New York trading the currency was at 1.6995 marks and 158.95 yen compared with 1.6979 marks and 159.79 yen Tuesday.
