What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/05/90

GORBACHEV DENOUNCED Estonia's drive for gradual independence. In a telephone call to the president of the Baltic republic, the Kremlin leader criticized a resolution declaring Estonia an occupied state and said Tallinn's call for step-by-step secession was invalid. The Estonian official said Gorbachev threatened "similar measures as those taken in Lithuania." In Vilnius, Lithuanian lawmakers approved a resolution accusing the Soviet Union of using the army in an attempt "to overthrow" the government of the republic, which proclaimed its independence three weeks ago.

Secretary of State Baker opened talks in Washington with Moscow's Shevardnadze, who pledged a "wise and fair" resolution to the situation in Lithuania.

---

ISRAEL'S PERES CLAIMED enough support to form a governing coalition.

The Labor Party leader, who has been negotiating with small political parties since the March 15 collapse of Prime Minister Shamir's government, said he had enough backing in the Knesset to form a ruling coalition. He requested a special session of the parliament to approve the new government. Peres, who supports U.S. proposals for Mideast peace talks, refused to name the new coalition members, and radio reports questioned whether he had the needed votes.

Ex-President Carter, who met in Paris with PLO leader Arafat and France's Mitterrand, blamed leaders in the Mideast for stalled peace efforts.

---

Bush held talks in Washington with West Germany's Genscher, who predicted that the Germanys would be reunited next year. The foreign minister also said the Soviet Union eventually may agree that a unified Germany should be in NATO. In Bonn, West Germany said it wants a pact on monetary union with East Germany by next month.

East German political parties neared formation of a non-Communist government. Officials agreed to ask Christian Democrat leader Maiziere to try to build a coalition, and the legislature is expected to approve the move when it convenes today.

---

The House approved a bill requiring the Justice Department for the first time to collect and publish statistics on so-called hate crimes, those that are motivated by prejudice based on a victim's race, religion, national origin or sexual orientation. The measure, already passed by the Senate, goes to Bush, who is expected to sign it.

---

Mandela said the African National Congress is committed to talks with South Africa's government, even though the group canceled exploratory discussions that had been set for next week. He said his meeting today with President de Klerk would help determine whether both sides are prepared for power-sharing negotiations.

---

El Salvador's government met in Geneva with representatives of the country's leftist rebels for U.N.-sponsored talks in a renewed effort to end a 10-year-old civil war. Both sides agreed to seek a cease-fire as an "initial objective" toward a negotiated settlement, but the guerrillas linked acceptance of such a truce to political changes.

---

China's Li asserted that the country's leadership is "united and strong," and that the public doesn't want a renewal of last year's student-led protests. The premier, in his first comments since Beijing's crackdown on dissent, also said that former Communist Party chief Zhao was being investigated over his stance during the protests.

---

Lebanon's rival Christian forces battled in Beirut after Lebanese Forces chief Geagea urged Syrian-backed President Hrawi to send army troops into the country's Christian enclave to end two months of fighting. Maronite Catholic clerics, meanwhile, attempted to forge a truce between Geagea's militia and forces loyal to Gen. Aoun.

---

Colombia's President Barco said the suspected leader of the Medellin-based cocaine-trafficking operation won't be extradited to the U.S. if he surrenders to authorities. It was the first time that Barco, who guaranteed a fair trial for Pablo Escobar, has made such a pledge since ordering a government crackdown on drugs last August.

---

Khashoggi was portrayed in opening arguments in U.S. court in New York as a naive businessman who kept in contact with the Marcoses in 1986 because he was promised a part of a shipload of gold. Prosecutors contended that numerous phone calls by the Saudi financier to the couple were part of an effort to cover up alleged racketeering.
