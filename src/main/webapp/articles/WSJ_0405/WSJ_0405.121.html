Politics & Policy: Senate Panel Seeks To Trim Spending At Military Schools

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 04/05/90

WASHINGTON -- Congressional budget-cutters have begun looking for ways to scale back spending by the three military academies and reduce the number of college students enrolled in officer-training classes nationwide. Such cost-cutting ideas, aimed at the military's time-honored way of choosing and training its future leaders, emerged yesterday during a Senate Armed Services subcommittee hearing. Once considered to be almost heretical and political folly by Pentagon supporters, the proposals circulating on Capitol Hill show how far the debate over defense spending has shifted in recent months.

"We know that current demand for new officers is less than was forecast four or five years ago," said subcommittee chairman Sen. John Glenn (D., Ohio), adding that heftier troop cuts are inevitable by the mid-1990s. Budget pressures require the Pentagon to "improve the efficiency as well as the academic vitality of the academies," Sen. Glenn said, noting that Congress has an obligation to carefully review how the institutions manage those changes.

Budget cuts involving the academies are still preliminary, and the details won't become clear until the Pentagon completes its own personnel review later this year. But members of the subcommittee, conducting their first detailed oversight hearing on the subject in more than a decade, grilled the superintendent of each of the service academies about everything from maintenance and training costs to faculty-student ratios.

"We're going to have to do something about {newly commissioned} officers coming through the system as though nothing has changed" in East-West relations, said Sen. James Exon (D., Neb.), usually a strong supporter of the Pentagon.

So far, the military has avoided committing itself to major changes. "There are no sacred cows" in the effort to cut costs, Christopher Jehn, the Pentagon's top personnel official, assured lawmakers.

But other witnesses acknowledged that current Pentagon plans envision that the size of the class entering each of the three service academies -- West Point, Annapolis and the Air Force Academy in Colorado Springs -- will remain basically flat this fall. As a result, the largest and most immediate cuts in new officers are expected to come from the 850 or so colleges and universities that offer officer-training programs.

Whatever the outcome, lawmakers are certain to think hard about making changes in programs that for decades have been among the most popular and jealously guarded portions of the defense budget. Sen. Glenn, who clearly used the hearing to send an early-warning signal to the military bureaucracy, nevertheless assured the witnesses that his goal was to impose tighter financial controls on the academies rather "than to close them down or to reduce their role as the bedrock source of military officers."

Increased congressional scrutiny, however, already has embarrassed the academies. A General Accounting Office report released by the subcommittee concluded that the Pentagon may have tried to hide millions of dollars paid to run the facilities, while failing to adequately audit and supervise their financial affairs.
