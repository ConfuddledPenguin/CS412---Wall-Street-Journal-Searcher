Foreign Exchange: Yen Climbs Against Dollar as Dealers Take Cautious Steps Before G-7 Meeting

  • Author: Randolph Walerius Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/05/90

NEW YORK -- The yen got a boost from currency dealers covering short positions amid suspicion that the Group of Seven industrial nations is laying a trap for those who dare take positions against the Japanese currency. Though most dealers said they doubted the G-7 will agree to intervene at its talks Saturday, some speculated that the group is trying to lull them into complacency with comments suggesting intervention is unlikely. They say the G-7 might be planning to surprise them next week with heavy dollar sales for yen. G-7 nations include the U.S., Japan, Britain, West Germany, France, Italy and Canada.

In other dealings, the dollar was mixed, with trading confined within a narrow range. In late afternoon New York trading, the dollar was at 1.6995 marks, up from 1.6979 marks, and at 158.95 yen, down from 159.79 yen late Tuesday. Sterling was at $1.6401, up from $1.6357.

In Tokyo early Thursday, the dollar was trading at 158.18 yen, down from the opening price of 158.32 yen and down from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 158.90 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at around 1.6985 marks, up from the opening quote of 1.6980 marks and up from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 1.6971 marks.

Comments by Japanese Finance Minister Ryutaro Hashimoto overnight were a green light for traders to sell yen, but they didn't, said Albert Soria, first vice president and foreign-exchange manager at Kansallis Osake Pankki in New York.

Mr. Hashimoto was reported as saying that capital flowing out of Japan wasn't the cause of the yen's current weakness and that he wasn't considering regulating such capital flows. He also said the G-7 wouldn't focus only on exchange rates at the meeting Saturday.

Many observers blame the yen's decline on the heavy flow of Japanese capital abroad for investment use. Some also wonder why Mr. Hashimoto seems to be trying to deflect attention from any discussion of exchange rates Saturday.

Mr. Soria said there was a certain amount of skepticism about the comments. On the surface, it looks as if nothing will come out of the meeting, but the market is apprehensive about selling the yen, he said, adding: "Is he setting us up?"

But Baruch Deutsch, a dealer at Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc., in New York, said the dollar is still close to its recent high at around 160 yen, an indication that most traders don't expect intervention.

Mr. Deutsch, who predicted an accord on coordinated dollar sales would emerge from the meeting, said the greenback is contained more by the difficulty of breaking the psychologically important 160-yen level. Trade talks between the U.S. and Japan seem to be going well, he said. The U.S. will reward the Japanese for their trade concessions and for Japan's previous help in supporting U.S. policy on the dollar, he said.

In Tokyo, Yoichi Itoh, chief corporate-service officer with Sumitomo Trust & Banking, said, "I think the U.S. might judge that higher Japanese interest rates might force U.S. interest rates up, and that some kind of currency stability will be necessary to stop Japanese interest rates from rising further." Higher Japanese interest rates are generally thought of as another way to lift the yen because they would make yen-denominated investments relatively more attractive. But when the Bank of Japan raised its discount rate to 5.25% from 4.25% two weeks ago, the yen plunged, largely because the move was widely expected and discounted.

Sterling recovered another piece of ground that was lost in a sharp tumble when trading opened in the Far East Monday. Mr. Soria said the pound was benefiting from high yields available in Britain. Everyone went short sterling after the violence in Britain during the weekend, he said, adding that the short-covering is still evident.

Linda McLaughlin, vice president at Chemical Bank in New York, said traders are trying to fill in the gaps between $1.6455, where sterling was at late Friday in New York, and around $1.6250, the level it fell to shortly after the opening in New Zealand Monday.

The market was talking about the pound going to $1.66 before the weekend violence, she said. Nobody knows what to do or where sterling is headed until the market fills in the big gap by moving the pound up to around $1.6450, she said. Once that is filled in, there will be a basis from which to move, said Ms. McLaughlin.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $377.10 an ounce, up $1.80. Estimated volume was a light 2.4 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Thursday, gold didn't trade due to a public holiday.
