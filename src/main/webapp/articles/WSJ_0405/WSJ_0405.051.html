Chicago Exchanges Will Raise Margins On Index Futures

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/05/90

CHICAGO -- The Chicago Mercantile Exchange and the Chicago Board of Trade, citing volatility in foreign stock markets, raised margin requirements for their stock-index-futures contracts, effective Monday. A Merc spokesman said it was the first time the exchange was raising margins in response to movements in foreign markets rather than volatility in one of its own contracts. Sharp price declines in the Tokyo stock market in recent weeks have led some investors to fear that a "spillover effect" may eventually be felt in U.S. markets.

Futures margins are good-faith deposits required of traders to ensure they can honor their trading obligations in the face of anticipated day-to-day volatility in the price of a particular contract. When daily price moves become more volatile, margins typically are raised to account for the increased risk.

The futures industry has been attacked by certain critics for its relatively low margin levels compared with the stock market, where margins are 50% of a stock's value. A margin payment in the stock market is an actual downpayment toward the purchase of a stock, rather than a good-faith deposit. In both markets, margin money represents the amount of upfront cash a speculator needs to bet on the near-term direction of stock or futures prices.

The Merc, whose Standard & Poor's 500 stock-index-futures contract accounts for more than 75% of all stock-index-futures trading, raised margins for speculators trading S&P 500 futures to $20,000 a contract, or nearly 12% of the contract's face value. The previous speculative margin had been set at $17,500, or slightly more than 10% of the contract value.

Trading in the Board of Trade's Major Market index accounts for a much smaller share of stock-index-futures trading volume. That exchange raised speculative margins effective Monday to $20,000 a contract, or nearly 15% of contact value, from $13,000, or 9.5% of the contract value.
