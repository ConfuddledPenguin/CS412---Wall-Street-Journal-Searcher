UAL Directors Meet Today to Consider Bid Valued at More Than $4.36 Billion

  • Author: Judith Valente and Randall Smith
  • Date: 04/05/90

The board of UAL Corp. meets today to consider a new higher union bid for United Airlines valued at more than $200 a share, or $4.36 billion, in what could be the last chance to stave off the start of hostilities in a proxy fight. Although some directors seemed inclined to approve the labor bid, that outcome wasn't a foregone conclusion because of stiff opposition expected from UAL Chairman Stephen M. Wolf at today's meeting.

"There could be open warfare on the board," said one person familiar with the situation. The board was due to meet at 10:30 a.m. CDT in Chicago, UAL's headquarters city. However, it was understood that the board's lawyers were prepared to tell directors that there would be no legal problem in accepting the bid.

The group of United pilots, mechanics and flight attendants boosted the value of its previous offer of roughly $4 billion, or $185 a share, for a 75% stake in the airline by adding a proposed sale of United's 50% stake in its Covia computer reservation system.

Some people involved with the revised bid said it could be valued at $200 to $210 a share, or $4.36 billion to $4.58 billion, enough to meet what was believed to be the board's minimum acceptable price of $200.

UAL's stock rose $1.75 to $166.50 a share in composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange.

If the board rejects the revised offer, Coniston Partners, UAL's largest shareholder with an 11.8% stake, is poised to begin campaigning for votes to oust the directors at UAL's annual meeting April 26. Coniston is supporting the union buy-out effort.

The biggest stumbling block to the board's acceptance of the bid was said to be opposition by Mr. Wolf and directors loyal to him. Mr. Wolf faces the likely loss of his job if the union group can complete the acquisition. The unions have already begun interviewing other executives as possible replacements for Mr. Wolf, who bore the brunt of the blame for the collapse of an earlier labor-management buy-out effort.

Mr. Wolf is understood to be questioning the value of concessions the unions have promised to make to help obtain financing for the current offer. Those concessions are said to start at $300 million annually, with a cumulative value of $2 billion over five years. Mr. Wolf has also criticized the way the unions have treated the company's non-union employees in the proposal.

The previous union offer would give UAL shareholders $150 a share in cash, $25 a share in 15% ten-year notes, and an equity stake in the company totaling 25%. Analysts valued that offer at $180 to $190 a share.

Sources confirmed a report in Investor's Daily that the new offer incorporates the proceeds from the 50% Covia stake, valued at $500 million or more. The directors had threatened to try to compete with the earlier union bid by selling such assets as Covia and paying shareholders a special dividend.

If the board approves the offer, that could begin to bring an end to a prolonged period of uncertainty that has gripped the nation's second largest airline ever since the collapse last October of a $6.79 billion, $300-a-share labor-management bid.

Coniston, which is supporting the union effort to acquire the company, stepped into the picture by buying stock in the wake of the bid's collapse after the UAL board said it wanted the airline to remain independent. Coniston has been waging a slow-moving campaign to reassemble a transaction ever since.

The new proposal still faces significant uncertainties, chiefly whether banks will be willing to make loans for an acquisition controlled by an unwieldy trio of unions that hasn't yet identified the executives it plans to install to manage the airline.

Because the unions lack the backing of an equity investor with substantial financial resources, bankers might reasonably wonder how they could get the unions to make necessary changes, including pay cuts or layoffs, if the airline encountered financial setbacks.
