Raychem Plans To Take a Charge For Its 3rd Period

  • Author: Carrie Dolan
  • Date: 04/05/90

MENLO PARK, Calif. -- Raychem Corp. said it would take a charge of up to $90 million in its third quarter, which will cause a loss for the fiscal year. The charge will cover severance payments, management consolidations and certain expenses associated with divestitures. Raychem said excluding the one-time charge and taxes, results in the quarter ended March 31 were at about the break-even level, reflecting a larger-than-expected drop in its defense business. Sales were about 5% above the year-earlier quarter's level of $270 million "in constant currency," Raychem said. A spokesman said that since about 60% of sales come from international markets, currency fluctuations could change final sales results.

Raychem makes aerospace, automotive, electronics and telecommunications products. Its stock closed yesterday at $27.75, down 25 cents, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

In the year-earlier third quarter, Raychem took a one-time charge of $10.7 million for certain programs intended to "improve corporate efficiency." In that quarter, Raychem posted net income of $2.4 million, or seven cents a share fully diluted, down 92% from a year earlier.

Raychem has seen several quarters of declining profits, which the company has blamed, in part, on losses from its Raynet unit. That unit, which designs fiber-optics systems, is still developing products and markets. A spokesman said Raynet is expected to cost the company about $60 million this fiscal year, ending June 30. However, Raychem said it "remains fully committed" to the Raynet unit, where technology trials now under way are showing "encouraging results."

The company will cut its work force by 8%, dismissing 900 workers to reduce staff to about 10,500. Raychem also said it will consolidate certain management and administrative jobs, and sell undeveloped land in Menlo Park, Calif. Raychem said it plans to acquire manufacturing sites in other, less-expensive parts of the U.S.

In addition, Raychem plans to sell its Advanced Packaging Systems division, which makes products to enhance communications among integrated circuits, and much of its shape memory alloy business, which makes metals for the aerospace industry.

Raychem said the moves are intended to reduce ongoing expenses but a spokesman wouldn't estimate how much money will be saved. Raychem said once the moves are implemented, it expects the costs to be recovered over two to three years.

For the fiscal year ended June 30, 1989, the company posted net income of $36.3 million, or $1.02 a fully diluted share, on sales of $1.08 billion.
