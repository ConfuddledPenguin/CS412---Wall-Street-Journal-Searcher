Accord Is Sought To Settle Lawsuits On Hammer Art --- Lawyer Says Shareholders Would Be Contributing To `National Treasure'

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/05/90

WILMINGTON, Del. -- A lawyer defended Occidental Petroleum Corp.'s planned outlays of $95 million for a museum to house Chairman Armand Hammer's art collection as akin to the purchase of the Louisiana territories and Alaska. William Prickett told the Court of Chancery in Delaware that for "pennies per share," Occidental shareholders are contributing to "a national treasure."

Mr. Prickett, a leading lawyer for a group of plaintiffs proposing settlement of class-action suits with Occidental over the museum plans, urged the court to approve the pact as best for Occidental stockholders, the company and the public. Occidental, based in Los Angeles, has interests in oil, pipelines, chemicals, and meat packing.

The settlement calls for, among other things, a $60 million cap on direct costs of the museum, provides for outlays for museum support and sets a limit on future Occidental donations to Hammer-related charities. It also includes $1.4 million in fees plus expenses for plaintiffs' lawyers involved in the pact, which was reached by one set of litigants in the case but which is opposed by others.

Mr. Prickett, who has in the past defended the much noted proposed legal fees, backed away from the figure yesterday, asserting that whatever amount is set by Vice Chancellor Maurice Hartnett in the case will be acceptable.

Opposing the proposed settlement, Susan E. Henricksen, a California deputy attorney general, called the museum "a waste of assets" that amounts to "a gift to Dr. Hammer." Ms. Henricksen, appearing on behalf of the state's public pension funds, which hold about two million shares of Occidental, argued that the size of Occidental's outlays is so enormous "as to raise reasonable doubt as to whether it is entitled to protection" under a standard of Delaware jurisprudence known as the "business judgment" rule, which protects certain decisions by corporate managements and directors.

Occidental has argued that a special committee of outside directors approved the plan, removing it from assertions that Dr. Hammer caused corporate funds to be spent for his benefit.

Vice Chancellor Hartnett reserved decision on the proposed settlement.

The museum, currently under construction in the Los Angeles suburb of Westwood, is scheduled to open in November, with a retrospective exhibit of the works of Soviet painter Kasimir Malevich.
