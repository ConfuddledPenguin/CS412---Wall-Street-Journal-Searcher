Economy -- Tracking the Economy: July Jobs Report Isn't Expected to Budge Economic Forecasts Out of the Basement

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 07/30/90

NEW YORK -- The July jobs report -- the first official reading on the third quarter isn't likely to budge economic forecasts out of the basement. Many economists began knocking down third-quarter growth estimates to the lowest single digit after Friday's report that real gross national product limped along at an annual rate of 1.2% in the second quarter, after adjusting for inflation. The payroll employment report, scheduled for release Friday, and the July purchasing managers' index, to be posted Wednesday, are expected to reflect continued economic anemia.

Most of the week's other reports round out June data. The calendar includes June personal income and personal consumption, leading economic indicators, construction spending, manufacturing orders and shipments. Other coming July statistics include agricultural prices and auto sales.

The economy "looked like it might be caving in in the second quarter," Allen Sinai, chief economist at Boston Co., said. He expects the employment and purchasers' data "to collateralize" the weakness. What's more, Mr. Sinai says he believes inflation is still entrenched, raising the specter of "stagflation."

Mr. Sinai expects that about 85,000 new jobs were added to non-farm payrolls in July, after taking into account the loss of many temporary census workers. Factory jobs probably dropped 15,000 to 20,000, he estimated. "That kind of employment report would suggest real GNP between flat and 1% growth" for the third quarter, he said.

The consensus view is somewhat firmer, calling for job creation of about 120,000 positions after accounting for census workers, according to MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York. Factory payrolls are thought to have dropped by about 10,000, for an overall unemployment rate of 5.3%, compared with 5.2% in June. Overall in June, about 97,000 jobs were created, while manufacturing payrolls shrank by 27,000.

Donald Fine, chief market analyst at Chase Manhattan Bank, shaded his third-quarter outlook lower after Friday's GNP report. Now he expects a growth rate between 1% and 1.5%, compared with the 1.5% he saw earlier. "It's not recession, but we're skating on thin ice here," he said.

Economists are keeping a close eye on the manufacturing sector, which recently began to grow modestly. It seems much of the factory activity in the second quarter stemmed from inventory-building. Now the question is whether those inventoried goods will be purchased, generating further demand, or whether stockpiled goods will languish.

The July purchasing agents' index, which gauges manufacturing demand, is expected to weigh in at 50.8%, down a bit from 51.1% in June. A reading exceeding 50% suggests a manufacturing economy that is generally expanding, and a level below 50% suggests contraction.

The government's main forecasting gauge, the leading-indicators index, is expected to be unchanged for June after a 0.8% gain in May. Stock prices, sensitive-materials costs, building permits and vendor performance are expected to contribute positively to the 11-component index. But weak durable-goods orders and declining consumer sentiment are thought to have offset gains in other segments.

June personal income and consumption data, to be released today, are expected to be firmer than those for May. The consensus view calls for income to have risen 0.4%, compared with the 0.3% increase in May. Consumption is expected to have risen 0.7%, largely because of a pickup in auto sales in June after a flat May reading.

The construction industry is expected to show continued weakness. June construction spending is likely to have dropped for the third consecutive month, with the consensus expecting a decline of 0.6%. May construction outlays were off 4%.
