Marketing & Media: New Ms. Magazine Makes Its Debut, Minus Advertising --- Price Rises but Experts Doubt Its Chances of Survival; Adding a Literary Flavor

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 07/30/90

NEW YORK -- Defying the skeptics, Ms. magazine -- sans advertising -- has returned from the dead, but its longevity is anything but assured. The new Ms. emerges today on newsstands and at Waldenbooks and B. Dalton bookstores nationwide. The premier issue starts with a bang: a long essay entitled "Sex, Lies & Advertising," by Ms. founder Gloria Steinem exploring how women's magazines shamelessly cater to advertisers. Lang Communications Inc., publisher of the feminist magazine, suspended publishing Ms. in December 1989, after large declines in advertising and costly attempts by past owners to increase subscribers.

To compensate for its lack of advertising, the new Ms. must now survive purely on circulation revenue from newsstand sales and subscribers. On newsstands, its cover price is now $4.50 -- up from $2.25. Ms.'s annual subscription rate of $40 a year makes it one of the industry's most expensive magazines. But readers who act immediately can take an introductory "founding subscriber" rate of $30. Old Ms. subscribers will be able to pay $25 a year.

Foregoing advertising allows Chairman Dale Lang to operate Ms. on a much smaller budget, since the magazine can do without expensive direct mail campaigns and has no need for a salaried sales staff. But industry experts continue to question whether Ms. will find enough readers at the expensive subscription rate to survive. Only a handful of successful magazines, such as Consumer Reports, don't accept advertising.

Mr. Lang, an entrepreneur who dabbles in television as well as publishing, last year promised to relaunch the magazine without advertising and to let the magazine resume its more vigorous feminist stance, which it had softened in recent years. Ms., which was founded in 1972, floundered in the late 1980s as the feminist movement waned and much of the editorial content of Ms. was imitated by other women's magazines. The new Ms. boasts a decidedly more literary air: Its contents include poems, long essays and photojournalism.

Helped by heavily discounted subscriptions, Ms. had reached a circulation of 550,000 by its last issue. A Lang spokeswoman said the magazine can still cover its costs with fewer than 100,000 subscribers.
