Grumman Says 2nd-Period Net Gained Sharply --- An Increase of 28% or More Is Estimated, Exceeding Analysts' Expectations

  • Author: Charles W. Stevens
  • Date: 07/30/90

BETHPAGE, N.Y. -- Grumman Corp.'s second-quarter earnings sharply exceeded analysts' expectations, and profit in the second half should be "significantly" higher than a year earlier, Renso L. Caporali, chairman, said. In an interview, Mr. Caporali said that when Grumman reports second-quarter results this week, probably Wednesday, the figures will show that net income rose at least 28% to between $25 million and $30 million, or 75 to 80 cents a share.

In the year-earlier period, Grumman earned $19.5 million, or 56 cents a share.

Grumman this year has taken a number of steps to handle the expected decline in U.S. defense contracts, including offering early retirement to several thousand employees. But even if the early retirement program is a success, Mr. Caporali said, further layoffs may be necessary.

Securities analysts who follow the company have predicted Grumman's second-quarter earnings will be about unchanged at between 50 and 60 cents a share.

Second-quarter sales rose about 20% from $881 million a year earlier, Mr. Caporali said, indicating sales in the area of $1 billion.

Mr. Caporali attributed the improvement primarily to the beginning of full production of the F-14D fighter for the Navy this year. "We're pretty much in full swing" with the assembly of the plane, he said.

The second-quarter earnings growth also stemmed from increased sales of aircraft parts to Boeing Co. as a result of Boeing's own sales boom, higher revenue and profit in Grumman's data systems business, and improved margins in the production of delivery vehicles for the U.S. Postal Service, Mr. Caporali said. He said he believes the higher margins in the postal vehicle business were linked to an employee profit-sharing plan begun in that operation. "We're going to try to look at applying it elsewhere" in the company, he said.

In addition, Mr. Caporali said interest expense fell $3 million in the first six months from the 1989 first half, with the decrease coming primarily in the second quarter. Debt outstanding dropped to $890 million as of June 30 from $950 million at the end of 1989.

Receivables and inventories fell to $1.6 billion June 30 from $1.78 billion at the end of the year, he said.

The backlog fell to $7 billion at June 30 from $7.4 billion a year earlier, mainly because of the delivery of postal vehicles as part of a five-year, $1 billion order for almost 100,000 vehicles.

Mr. Caporali, formerly Grumman's vice chairman, technology, took over the duties of chairman, president and chief executive recently after the resignation of John O'Brien. The company's board is searching for candidates to fill those positions; Mr. Caporali is among those being considered.

Mr. O'Brien resigned amid reports that his name had arisen in a federal grand jury investigation of Pentagon procurement fraud. Mr. Caporali has declined to discuss those reports, but in the interview he said Grumman has been told the company isn't a target of the investigation. Mr. O'Brien wasn't available for comment.
