Transport Bill Would Bar Trucks' Radar Detectors

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 07/30/90

WASHINGTON -- Interstate truckers and bus drivers would be barred from using radar detectors under federal rules proposed in a more than $28 billion Senate transportation bill incorporating major increases in funding for the Northeast rail corridor in fiscal 1991. The Transportation Department would retain discretion to draft the proposed rules, but the spending measure virtually dictates that the ban take effect within a year unless Transportation Secretary Samuel Skinner determines that the prohibition wouldn't improve highway safety.

Radar detectors already are prohibited by a few states, including Connecticut and Virginia. But the proposed federal ban would be far more sweeping and would apply to all drivers of commercial motor vehicles. The proposed rules would take the form of amendments to federal motor carrier regulations, and violators would be subject to civil fines or criminal penalties, according to a legislative report filed by the Senate Appropriations Committee.

The underlying measure, approved by the full committee last week, rejects proposed cuts in rail and mass transit and represents a nearly 5% increase from the Bush administration's transportation budget for the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1. While trimming the larger House-passed bill, New York and New Jersey senators added almost $150 million for the Northeast rail corridor, and the total figure, $165 million, represents a more than sixfold increase in current aid.

Coast Guard acquisition and construction accounts would be increased by $40.4 million from the House-passed level. And while cutting the projects of House lawmakers, senators salted away hundreds of millions for endeavors of their own. More than $160 million -- none of which was in the administration's budget -- was secured for highways backed by Senate Appropriations Committee Chairman Robert Byrd (D., W.Va.).

The proposed Coast Guard budget relies on the transfer of $300 million from the Pentagon, and the military's fights over weapon contracts can spill over into Coast Guard decisions on buying its equipment.

General Motors Corp.'s Allison Gas Turbine division and Turbomeca Engine Corp., a Texas unit of Labinal Corp. of France, have a competing interest in building replacement engines for the Coast Guard's HH-65 Dolphin helicopter, for example. The dispute is driven partly by Allison's stake in the development of its T-800 engine for the Army's costly new light helicopter.
