The Outlook: Midwest States Show Surprising Pep

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 07/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The economic party's over on the East Coast and much of the West. But look at the wallflower Midwestern states. They haven't wilted yet. As the nation's long economic expansion in the 1980s stretched into a bicoastal boom, America's heartland missed out on some of the greatest merrymaking -- real estate appreciating at more than 25% a year, government dollars gushing for big defense contracts. But while the East and West coasts raked in most of the party favors, the Midwestern industrial states quietly picked themselves up from the devastation of the last recession, evolving into more diverse, resilient economies.

Now comes the test for the new Rust Belt.

As the nation edgily eyes the likelihood of a prolonged economic slowdown or even downturn, will the big industrial states finally defy economic tradition? Will they escape the hard fall that prior recessions have guaranteed?

The latest evidence suggests that this time things could be different indeed. While the U.S. economy has slowed markedly over the past year and while national manufacturing employment has declined month after month, the unemployment rates of Ohio, Indiana and Illinois remain around the national average -- near full employment by many economists' standards.

In May the jobless rate in Indiana, which has a big auto-parts industry, was 5.2% of the civilian work force while the nation's was 5.3%. Illinois's was 5.4% and Ohio's 5.6%. Only Michigan, center of the battered auto industry, had a worrisomely high level of unemployment, 7.1% of the work force, the third worst in the nation.

By contrast, in the early 1980s state unemployment rates in the region shot past the nation's by a nearly two percentage points. Ohio's jobless rate was more than 12.5% in 1982, while the U.S.'s was at 9.7%. Illinois' was 11.3% and Indiana's 11.9%.

Or take housing -- widely believed to be in a recession nationwide but still growing in the Midwest. New residential construction, measured by square foot of floor space, is down about 5% in the U.S. from last year, but it's up by about 5% in the district served by the Chicago Federal Reserve:

Northern Illinois, Iowa, the lower peninsula of Michigan, most of Indiana and southern Wisconsin. Nonresidential square footage is down about 7% nationwide but is up about 14% in the region.

Even manufacturing, though clearly softer than a few years ago, isn't as bad off as it could be in the current economic climate. Booming exports are keeping factories churning.

"We are competing in an international manufacturing marketplace that we couldn't have competed in successfully a decade ago," says Michael Bryan, an economist at the Cleveland Federal Reserve Bank. "Ten years ago it was all manufacturers could have done to get some government relief from foreign competition; now they seem to be seeking it out."

The Midwest's relative health in the face of softness in the national economy, particularly its manufacturing sector, in part reflects its new diversity. No matter what happens to manufacturing, fewer people in the region are tied to it.

An increasing portion of the population works in health care, financial services, legal services, data processing and other service jobs. In the Detroit metropolitan area, the portion of people working in manufacturing has fallen to 23% from nearly 35% in 1978, though many of the services are still dependent on the auto industry.

In Ohio, the portion of the nonagricultural work force employed in manufacturing, for example, has dropped from 36.3% in 1970 to 22.8% in 1990. In Youngstown, a heavy manufacturing center whose closed steel factories and long unemployment lines were the emblem of devastation in the last recession, nonmanufacturing employment grew 3.5% in the year ended in April, which was higher than the gain nationwide.

"It's a lot more diversified, more viable economy than before," says Theodore Tung, chief economist at Cleveland National Bank. "That doesn't mean Ohio isn't going to be affected by national trends, but I would say it's much less vulnerable to a cyclical downturn than before."

But while the real estate and defense booms largely bypassed the Midwest, so too may the busts. "For a long time we complained about not getting our fair share of defense contracts, but now it's starting to look like it's not that bad a thing," says David Allardice, an economist at the Chicago Federal Reserve.

Mr. Allardice adds: "We're no boom, no bust -- just kind of Midwestern blase, business as usual. Business is slowing, without major concern."
