Two EC Commissioners Are at Odds In Dispute Centered on Sara Lee Unit

  • Author: Martin du Bois
  • Date: 07/30/90

BRUSSELS -- Sir Leon Brittan, the European Community's commissioner in charge of competition, is embroiled in a bitter battle with External Relations Commissioner Frans Andriessen over the role the commission should play in policing mergers and acquisitions. The dispute is centered on the fate of the Dutch coffee maker Douwe Egberts N.V. But it has broader implications for competition policy in the EC single market planned for 1992.

The two commissioners disagree about whether Douwe Egberts's acquisition of a rival coffee maker in February 1989 gave the company an unfair market position and whether the company should be forced to divest itself of all or part of the acquisition. Douwe Egberts is a subsidiary of Sara Lee Corp., a Chicago-based food and consumer-products company.

Mr. Andriessen argues that Douwe Egberts's market share of about 67% in the Dutch, Belgian and Luxembourg coffee markets doesn't endanger competition in the EC and that the commission shouldn't bother with intervening in such a small part of the overall EC market.

On Sir Leon's side, however, officials say that by acquiring Van Nelle Holdings N.V., Douwe Egberts "created a position which is virtually unassailable." Douwe Egberts's near-monopoly makes it almost impossible for another company based elsewhere in the EC to grab a substantial share of the Benelux market, they argue.

Mr. Andriessen, a Dutchman, has received support from several other commissioners who also come from the EC's smaller countries. They argue that companies in the smaller nations should be allowed to build up sufficient size and local market share to compete on a communitywide basis. Commission President Jacques Delors, who currently is siding with Sir Leon, wants the debate resolved before any decision is taken on Douwe Egberts.

Some EC officials suggest Mr. Andriessen is taking sides because of his Dutch nationality, though others dismiss that suggestion. "He has been taking a very nationalistic stance on this issue," says one official.

The dispute comes at an awkward time for Sir Leon, less than two months before the commission's new merger-control powers come into force. On Sept. 21, the commission will for the first time have the power to block large anticompetitive mergers before they happen.

"We're all a bit in a state of confusion now," says a commission spokesman.

Some lawyers and officials are surprised that Mr. Andriessen is challenging Sir Leon on the Douwe Egberts issue. They say that Sir Leon had appeared to have a clear-cut case.

"I don't think there should be any controversy here," says Frank Fine, a lawyer with the British firm of Frere Cholmeley. "It would be a mistake {for the commission} to drop a case like this where the market share is so strong."

In any case, the dispute sets a bad precedent, lawyers and EC officials say, because the new merger-control system won't function if the commission can't stay free of the kind of political infighting that is happening with Douwe Egberts. Under the new merger-control rules, the commission will have to make decisions within tight deadlines.

"It's a rather bad signal at a time when one might expect that the commissioners would defend a common position in an area as important" as merger control, says Alain Vanderelst, a lawyer with the Brussels firm Van Bael & Bellis.

The commission's investigation of Douwe Egberts for "abuse of dominant position" was officially launched more than a year ago, in May 1989. Commission officials have been trying to negotiate an amicable solution with the company, but talks have broken down. The current dispute means that a decision probably won't be reached until after the commission's August holidays, but Sir Leon is expected to press on with the case.

"Brittan is doing his job, showing himself as an enforcer {of EC competition law} that has to be reckoned with," Mr. Fine says.
