FDA Halts the Sale Of Cal-Ban Diet Pills

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/30/90

WASHINGTON -- The Food and Drug Administration halted the distribution of Cal-Ban 3000 diet pills after receiving numerous reports of the pills swelling in the throats of consumers and causing injuries. Health Care Products Inc., Lutz, Fla., was directed to recall all Cal-Ban 3000 tablets and capsules on the U.S. market as the FDA moved toward banning more than 100 diet-product ingredients, including guar gum, sea kelp and karaya gum, that have never been proven effective.

The agency advised consumers against taking the Cal-Ban products after investigating 19 reports of throat obstructions. Cal-Ban's active ingredient is guar gum, a complex sugar that swells when wet and can create a sense of fullness in the stomach. But if Cal-Ban pills become stuck in the throat or esophagus, they can swell and cause obstructions, according to Peter Rheinstein, director of the FDA's office of drug standards.

Ten dieters on Cal-Ban have been hospitalized, and one person died of a blood clot that reached the lungs a week after undergoing surgery for an obstructed throat, Dr. Rheinstein said.

On July 18, Florida health officials ordered Health Care Products, which also does business as Anderson Pharmacals, Tampa, Fla., to stop selling Cal-Ban 3000 on the strength of 50 cases involving injuries. The FDA plans to look into those cases. The agency is investigating similar diet products to determine if they pose the same health hazard as Cal-Ban.

The agency has come under pressure from Congress to crack down on the over-the-counter sale of ineffective weight-control products. The FDA announced in May that unproven diet-product ingredients will be taken off the market under a rule proposal to be published this fall.

An FDA spokeswoman said guar gum is approved for use in such foods as cheese, salad dressings and ice cream. She said so little is added that it doesn't pose any health risk.
