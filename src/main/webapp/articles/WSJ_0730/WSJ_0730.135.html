Law: McMartin Trial Ends in Deadlock, Case Is Now Dead

  • Author: Ann Hagedorn
  • Date: 07/30/90

A California judge declared a mistrial in the second McMartin Preschool child-molestation trial when jurors became deadlocked after deliberating three weeks in the case of former teacher Raymond Buckey. Shortly after Judge Stanley Weisberg's decision, Ira Reiner, Los Angeles district attorney, said he would not try Mr. Buckey a third time. Jurors later said they were leaning toward acquittal on six of the eight counts against him.

"The McMartin case is over," said Deputy District Attorney Joseph Martinez at a news conference.

It was the end of a controversial case that began in Manhattan Beach, Calif., in August 1983 and resulted in the longest and costliest criminal trial in U.S. history. Mr. Buckey spent nearly five years in prison.

"No one has won in the McMartin case," Mr. Buckey told reporters. "Now I would like to be just left alone. . . . This is not the kind of life I would have planned out."

Hours before Judge Weisberg stopped the trial, the jury asked him to allow them to review the only two verdicts they had so far submitted. The two verdicts had not been made public. The judge granted their request and only minutes later jurors announced their impasse.

In interviews with reporters the jurors said the two sealed verdicts had initially been unanimous votes for acquittal. On only one count was there a majority vote for a guilty verdict.

Mr. Buckey's ordeal began seven years ago when a woman, now deceased, told local police she believed her son had been molested at the Virginia McMartin Preschool.

In January, after a 28-month trial and three months of deliberations, another jury acquitted Mr. Buckey, 32, and his mother, Peggy McMartin Buckey, on 52 out of 65 counts of child molestation.

The acquittals provoked a wave of protest among parents of the allegedly molested children and victims rights groups. In February, Mr. Reiner, at the time a Democratic candidate for state attorney general, announced he would retry Mr. Buckey on eight of the deadlocked counts.

Those charges involved three girls, now teen-agers, who attended the preschool between 1979 and 1983. Defense attorney Danny Davis called the move for a retrial "banal stupidity" and accused the district attorney of using the case for political purposes.

According to the Los Angeles County auditor, from August 1983 through May 1990, the case cost California taxpayers almost $14 million.

Mr. Buckey has never deviated from his claims of innocence.
