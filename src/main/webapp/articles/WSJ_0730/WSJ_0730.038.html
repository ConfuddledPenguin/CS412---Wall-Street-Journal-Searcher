SOVIET-ALBANIAN TALKS

Albania is holding talks with the Soviet Union on renewing diplomatic ties, the Albanian news agency ATA reported yes terday.

The agency said a Soviet foreign-ministry delegation arrived in Tirana Satur day to discuss the normalization of rela tions between the two countries.

The Soviet Union broke off diplomatic relations with Albania in 1961 as a result of a split with China, which then became Albania's chief communist ally. Albania, the last hard-line communist state in Europe, also has no relations with the U.S.

In Albania, the government proposed that agricultural workers be given larger plots of land to farm individually, the ATA news agency reported. Plans to allow a measure of private farming were announced several weeks ago, but the ATA report was the first to contain details.

FOREIGN INVESTMENT IN VIETNAM

Oil and gas projects accounted for most of the $245.6 million in new investment in Vietnam in the first six months of this year, the official Vietnam News Agency said; of that total, $197 million came from abroad. The report said Vietnam granted licenses for 54 foreign-investment projects in the first half of the year. Four projects involved oil and gas, but they accounted for 73% of the capital. Other projects involved tourism, services, fishing, agricultural processing and consumer goods, the report said.

NERVOUS NORWAY

Norway's Environment Minister Kristin Hille Valla urged West Germany, the Netherlands and Spain to refrain from exporting their radioactive waste to a disposal and reprocessing site at Dounreay, Scotland. She said a radiation leak from the northern Scotland site could contaminate Norway, neighboring countries and fish stocks. She cited a 1987 United Nations report urging nations using nuclear power to dispose of waste within their own boundaries. The Dounreay plant reprocessed such nuclear fuels as enriched uranium until the early 1970s. It is due to reopen and possibly expand, said a Norwegian Environment Ministry official.

THAILAND'S EXPORT GROWTH

Led by strong growth in industrial products, Thai exports in the first half of 1990 were 15.5% higher than the same period last year, an official of the Department of Business Economics said. A report from the department said the strongest industrial exports were computers, integrated circuits, shoes and garments. The report projected that exports would total $23.9 billion for the year, compared with last year's $20.2 billion.

NINJA TURTLES ABROAD

The Beecroft Public School in Sydney, Australia, has banned games or play related to "Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles." "The children have been fighting in the playground and picking up sticks to use," a school official said. "When you ask them what they are doing, they say they are just doing Ninja Turtles." And in Leeds, England, two firemen had to be rescued from a sewer Friday after trudging through water in search of children imitating the Ninja Turtles. The children were already above ground by the time their exhausted would-be rescuers radioed a colleague to come and get them.

THEFT IN THE PHILIPPINES

Philippine Defense Secretary Fidel Ramos accused politicians of stealing relief goods intended for victims of the July 16 earthquake. Canned milk, sardines and blankets sent by foreign donors were not reaching victims of the quake and were being diverted to supporters of local politicians in areas unaffected by the quake, Mr. Ramos said. Two truckloads of relief goods were stolen before they could reach the northern city of San Jose in the devastated province of Nueva Ecija, rescue officials said.

KEEPING POLAND POLISH

A motion to bar sale of Polish government-owned real estate to non-Poles was approved by a 224-136 vote with seven abstentions in the lower house of Parliament.

There is widespread concern among Poles that their country will be "bought out" by Westerners -- particularly Germans -- seeking relatively inexpensive properties in Eastern Europe.

The law doesn't apply to privately owned land, which currently can be sold to foreigners with the permission of the Interior Ministry. Such sales were strictly limited under the former communist regime and are still infrequent, with 80% of the approvals going to foreigners of Polish origin, Interior Minister Krzysztof Kozlowski told Parliament.

Last week, the Polish Senate gave final approval to legislation that would permit transferring 80% of the nation's state-owned industry to private investors.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

The Soviet Union plans to abolish its censorship organization, Glavlit, which for decades was the guardian of the Communist Party line in literature and the arts, a Soviet newspaper said. Komsomolskaya Pravda said Glavlit will be replaced by Guot, an organization concerned with guarding state secrets. . . . In Britain, Queen Elizabeth II won a high court injunction banning publication of "Courting Disaster," a book by a former royal clerk. The book reportedly describes heavy drinking and illicit sex among royal employees and includes a picture of the queen in bed.
