GNP Commodities To Move Accounts To British Firm

  • Author: Jeff Bailey
  • Date: 07/30/90

CHICAGO -- GNP Commodities Inc., facing expulsion from the futures industry, is transferring its customer accounts and employees to a British futures firm and plans to cease operation. GNP and its chairman, former Chicago Mercantile Exchange Chairman Brian P. Monieson, were ordered banned from the industry in May by an administrative law judge at the Commodity Futures Trading Commission. Though the expulsion is on hold while he and the firm appeal the ruling to the CFTC commissioners, some GNP customers had been taking their business elsewhere and others were uneasy with the situation, Mr. Monieson said.

The CFTC case against Mr. Monieson and GNP, both accused of failing to control allegedly renegade commodities salesmen, could be a watershed in futures regulation. The CFTC has, in effect, shown that it can put out of business a powerful industry insider over the alleged wrongdoing of some brokers working for him.

In recent months, other futures clearing firms have been reducing or getting out of their so-called introducing broker sales business because of the fear that they too could be severely sanctioned for the actions of far-off salesmen. Such sales offices often operate independently of their clearing firm.

Dennis Klejna, CFTC enforcement chief, declined to comment on the case other than to say that the agency will pursue the expulsions against GNP and Mr. Monieson regardless of the transfer of business.

The transfer of the accounts and GNP's 110 employees to E.D.& F. Man International Futures Inc., London, was agreed to late Thursday night and is to become final this morning, Mr. Monieson said.

GNP will become an inactive futures clearing firm. Mr. Monieson said he will devote his time to the appeal of the expulsion order, and to his personal trading.

He wouldn't disclose terms for the transfer of GNP's accounts, which included some big institutional traders and exchange-floor traders as well. None of GNP's liabilities, which include the CFTC case and some civil litigation against the firm, was assumed by E.D.& F. Man. Before the CFTC enforcement case became publicly known, Mr. Monieson had been close to selling GNP for as much as $40 million.

E.D.& F. Man International is a subsidiary of E.D.& F. Man Ltd., a closely held trading and money-management firm based in London.

The CFTC administrative law judge, George H. Painter, specifically found that, after being warned by other GNP employees, Mr. Monieson failed to promptly stop some salesmen from bilking customers. The salesmen, one of them a close friend of Mr. Monieson, were accused of so-called trade allocation -- initiating trades without the required customer account number and then keeping the winning trades for themselves and stuffing the losers in customer accounts.

The two brokers, Norman Furlett and Ira P. Greenspon, were also ordered banned from the futures business and fined $75,000 each. They denied the charges. Mr. Monieson and GNP were fined $500,000 each. Mr. Monieson denied knowing about the alleged abuses and said "no firm could survive" the supervision standard he was being held to.
