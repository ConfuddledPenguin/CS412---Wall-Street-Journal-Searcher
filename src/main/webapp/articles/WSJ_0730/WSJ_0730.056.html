Abreast of the Market: Some Neglected Stocks Find New Life --- Insurance, Food, Metal Groups Cited

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 07/30/90

NEW YORK -- The pummeling of many growth stocks during this current spate of earnings reports has been ugly to behold. But not all money managers are aghast. Many companies whose stocks were beaten down long ago to cheap multiples of annual per-share profit are surprising investors with better-than-expected second-quarter earnings. To see such "low P/E" industry groups as aluminum and insurance earn more than analysts predicted is enough to bring tears of joy to value investors' eyes. With earnings expectations driving the stock market this year, these forlorn owners of stocks that usually respond to healthy economic growth have watched their portfolios lag the market, while portfolios filled with blue-chip shares of tried-and-true growth companies raced ahead.

Now, however, the tables may be turning. Last Monday, investors said enough to hyped-up earnings expectations and took two steps back from the market. The Dow Jones Industrial Average tumbled 105 points in about an hour, then recovered to post a 56.44-point loss. There's no question that earnings growth is still the stock market's motor. But what may be changing is the kind of stocks investors are looking to for growth.

Whether money managers begin paring back positions in such double-digit earnings growers as Coca-Cola and Wal-Mart Stores and begin reaching down to cheaper, neglected shares of slower-growing companies depends largely on their economic outlook.

Data released Friday morning showed the economy growing at a annual rate of 1.2%. That report shook up the stock market, contributing 22.28 points to the Dow industrials' loss of 62.63 points for the week. If anything, this weaker-than-expected growth bolstered the convictions of growth stock managers, who are betting that the companies in which they own stakes will increase profits faster than those whose fortunes are tied to the economic cycle.

But economic growth between 1% and 2% is turning out stronger-than-expected earnings in many neglected sectors of the stock market. If the economy can sustain this slow-but-steady pace, investors may begin to look at some cheap stocks that are entering stronger earnings cycles.

Copper and aluminum stocks, for example, which fall into the nonferrous metals industry group, earned more in the second quarter than analysts expected. "Better product pricing is materializing despite economic weakness," said Joseph J. McAlinden, director of research at Dillon, Read & Co. The coal industry also beat analysts' earnings expectations.

If the economy does slow further, some strategists think the Federal Reserve will take its foot off the monetary brakes and drive interest rates lower to avoid recession. Reflation, as this policy is called in some quarters, is an emerging theme in the stock market these days, possibly because it's essential to the case of investors who foresee stepped-up earnings growth for many cyclical companies.

"To the extent that the Federal Reserve stimulates more, you have tremendous upside potential" in cyclical shares, said Stefan Abrams, chief investment strategist at Kidder, Peabody & Co. "Nobody is valuing these shares for reflation" -- at least not yet.

If the economy lapses into a recession, which many strategists think is possible, high-flying growth stocks may be hurt more than cyclical shares. For all their talk about global earnings power, investors have been digging into earnings reports to see how much came from the U.S. -- and shivered. It was McDonald's disappointing performance in U.S. stores that caused the stock to drop 12% in two days last week. Meanwhile, many cyclical stocks are already trading at prices that appear to anticipate a recession.

Positive and negative earnings surprises reflect analysts' exaggerated fears and hopes. So far in this period of profit reports, negative earnings surprises, on average, were 61.6% below the average estimate of at least three analysts. Positive surprises averaged 28% better than the forecasts.

Zacks Investment Research said the overall earnings disappointment for the 750 companies that are in the Dow Jones Equity Market Index was 4.5% in the second quarter, compared with 3% in the first quarter. However, the number of negative surprises dropped to 51.5% in the second quarter from 54% in the first quarter, said Ben Zacks, executive vice president.

For a value investor, the trick is to find stocks that are at the trough of their earnings cycle, just before results start heading up again.

Positive earnings surprises in aerospace, securities brokers, and container makers don't reflect a profit turnaround, analysts say. Defense contracts will get slimmer; Wall Street is still weathering a drought in investment banking fees, and the paper packaging industry can still produce more than it can sell, analysts say.

But the positive surprises in the insurance, nonferrous metals, oil and food industry groups may portend accelerating earnings growth.

Life insurance firms seem headed toward higher earnings again by cutting back on sales incentives and keeping more investment income for themselves. "A value investor with some degree of patience should be accumulating" insurance stocks, said Denis Callaghan, managing director at Alex. Brown & Sons. Earnings aren't rising yet, he cautioned, but the rate wars are over, and earnings should be climbing unmistakably by late 1991. He recommends Chubb and Marsh & McLennan.

Earnings of food retailers should also be stable to better, said Edward Comeau, analyst at Oppenheimer & Co. "You have a whole array of highly leveraged companies out there. It is not in their best interest to get aggressive" and cut prices, Mr. Comeau said.

Among metals stocks, Phelps Dodge's second-quarter earnings of $3.30 a share beat analysts' expectations by nearly 15%. Analysts figured that slow economic growth would drive the average price of copper below $1 a pound this year, but prices have averaged above $1.

"The economy is expanding fast enough to create an environment where copper, aluminum and insurance companies are able to raise prices," said Mr. McAlinden. Inflation remains stuck between 4% and 5%, even though economic growth is slow, because the unusually long eight-year expansion has created shortages in labor and other resources, he added.

Said Kidder's Mr. Abrams, "If investors come to believe that we are in a recession, these {cyclical} stocks don't have much downside {risk}, and they will be good performers because step A is recession, and step B is reflation" by the Fed.

But without renewed inflation to help boost product prices, many of these stocks may just muddle along with mediocre earnings in a 1% to 2% growth economy, Mr. Abrams cautioned. For now, he recommends that investors stick with a portfolio that's two-thirds growth stocks. "For the very long term, that's where the big money has always been made," he said. "Sam Walton {chairman of Wal-Mart} didn't get rich trading the cyclicals."

Friday Market Activity

The Dow Jones Industrial Average tumbled 22.28 to 2898.51, largely because of weaker-than-expected second-quarter economic growth and increased recession fears. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange was a moderate 149,070,000 shares.

The industrial average's loss for the week, which started with a 56-point drop Monday, was 62.63, or 2.1%. Standard & Poor's 500-stock index fell 2.3% for the week, while the Big Board's composite index lost 2.2% and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index dropped 2.4%. A steady stream of disappointing second-quarter earnings reports was the main influence.

Friday's preliminary report on second-quarter gross national product -- the nation's output of goods and services -- reinforced the notion that the economy may be slowing too much. Investors were concerned not just because GNP growth was less than consensus expectations -- 1.2% as opposed to 1.8% -- but also because the figure included a decline in consumption and a rise in inventories. Many consumer stocks were hit especially hard.

Investors clearly are refocusing on how weak economic growth might hurt profitability, rather than on how it might lead to lower interest rates, which can help corporate profits, said David Bostian, chief investment strategist at Jesup, Josephthal.

Walt Disney added to investors' anxiety about earnings. The company's second-quarter profit rose 23%, which was slightly lower than analysts' expectations. The stock dropped 5 to 116.

McDonald's, which started the week with an earnings-related sell-off, lost 7/8 to 31 3/4.

Philip Morris continued to stumble on news earlier last week suggesting tobacco companies aren't immune to product-liability lawsuits. The stock fell 5/8 to 46 5/8.

Wal-Mart Stores dropped 1 to 31 5/8. Elsewhere in the consumer sector, Anheuser-Busch dropped 1 1/2 to 42 3/8; Johnson & Johnson fell 1 to 72 3/8; Procter & Gamble slid 3/4 to 86 1/8; Woolworth lost 3/4 to 31 1/2, and Toys "R" Us fell 1 3/8 to 29 3/8.

International Business Machines fell 1 3/4 to 111 3/8. SoundView Financial Group downgraded the stock's investment rating to "sell" from "hold" and lowered its 1991 earnings estimate, saying it expects IBM will have problems making the transition to a new line of mainframe computers in the fourth quarter.

Other technology issues also declined: Compaq Computer fell 2 1/8 to 55 3/4; Digital Equipment lost 1 1/4 to 74 7/8; Unisys slid 3/4 to 11 1/8; Cray Research slipped 7/8 to 43; and Hewlett-Packard fell 5/8 to 44 1/8.

Oil stocks, on the other hand, generally held firm amid news that the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries agreed on a target price of $21 a barrel, up from $18, for crude oil. Exxon rose 3/8 to 49 1/4, Chevron rose 1/4 to 77 1/4, and Amoco rose 7/8 to 54 1/4.

Dividend-related trades swelled volume in the two most active Big Board issues: Merrill Lynch, which fell 1/8 to 23 1/2 on 3.2 million shares, and Citicorp, which declined 1/2 to 20 3/8 on 2.9 million shares.

Boeing advanced 1/8 to 59 1/4 on a better-than-expected earnings report.

Mylan Laboratories skidded 2 1/2 to 19, extending its slide in the wake of a weaker-than-expected quarterly earnings report.

Pfizer added 1/4 to 74 3/8 on news that a Food and Drug Administration panel set a tentative date for review of Sertraline, the company's anti-depressant drug.

Northrop fell 1 3/8 to 14 1/8 amid reports of a Justice Department recommendation that the company be barred from making weapons for the U.S. military. Northrop has pleaded guilty to fraud charges.

Grumman gained 3/4 to 15 1/2 on a report that the company expects to report strong quarterly earnings.

Milton Roy advanced 1 3/4 to 28 5/8 after its board agreed to hold discussions with Sundstrand, which holds a 15% stake in the company and has offered $28 a share for the rest.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index slid 0.84 to 355.49, extending its loss for the week to 1.4%.

Air & Water Technologies Class A tumbled 3 1/8 to 24 3/4. Alex. Brown & Sons and Kidder Peabody both lowered investment ratings and cut earnings estimates on the company's 83%-owned Metcalf & Eddy unit. On the over-the-counter market, Metcalf & Eddy fell 5 1/2 to 20 1/2.
