Council Says Social Security Surplus Shouldn't Be Included in Budget Target

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon and David Wessel
  • Date: 07/30/90

WASHINGTON -- To highlight the true size of the budget deficit, the government shouldn't count the growing Social Security surplus in deficit-reduction targets, a federal advisory panel said. The panel, the Advisory Council on Social Security, also warned against reducing Social Security taxes as the surplus builds.

The Social Security surplus is expected to be about $63 billion in the fiscal year ending Sept. 30. Excluding this surplus from budget calculations would increase this year's deficit to about $224 billion from the current estimate of $161.3 billion and intensify the difficulty of balancing the budget.

"The council believes that the growing Social Security reserves shouldn't routinely be used simply to offset large operating deficits in the rest of the federal budget," the panel said in an interim report. The secretary of Health and Human Services appoints such a council every four years to review the soundness of the Social Security and Medicare trust funds.

The panel said it's appropriate for the Social Security trust fund to be building a surplus in anticipation of the retirement of the large baby-boom generation starting around 2010. It cautioned against reducing the current payroll tax rate of 6.2%, which is paid both by workers and employers on income of up to $51,300 this year, as suggested by Sen. Daniel Patrick Moynihan (D., N.Y.).

The council said the long-term health of the Social Security system depends on boosting the nation's productivity, or output per hour worked. The nation should strive to eliminate the budget deficit -- and ultimately to run a budget surplus -- to boost savings and investment, the foundation of higher productivity, the advisory council recommended.

This long-term goal is widely shared, although successive Congresses and presidents haven't yet shown the political will to achieve it. However, the current budget summit is likely to take the Social Security surplus out of the deficit calculations.

Negotiators at the budget summit talks, meanwhile, continued to be more preoccupied with partisan maneuvering and tactics than with reaching a deficit-reduction compromise. Republicans -- stung by publicity and criticism surrounding a draft deficit-reduction plan they haven't offered to Democrats yet -- are now threatening to block the start of the congressional recess unless Democrats put forward a proposal next week, congressional aides said.

Democrats haven't decided how to respond yet. But negotiators said any plan they put forward at this stage is likely to rely more on deep cuts in defense spending than on cuts in benefit programs and also will call for increasing taxes on upper-income taxpayers. One possibility being considered by Democrats is to impose a new tax on luxury items.

As previously reported, the draft Republican plan would raise taxes on beer, wine and liquor and limit the deduction for state and local income taxes to $10,000; it also would cut defense spending about $7 billion and reduce spending on government benefits programs $16 billion. Republican aides cautioned that the plan may change before it is formally offered.

President Bush is expected to meet again today with top congressional leaders. The timetable for the budget talks is likely be the main item on the agenda. Democrats last week proposed to call the summit negotiators back early from the August recess to try to complete a package, but the White House rejected that. One possibility that both Republicans and Democrats are discussing is for the entire Congress to vote on firm deadlines for the budget negotiators before leaving town -- perhaps in the resolution that begins the recess.
