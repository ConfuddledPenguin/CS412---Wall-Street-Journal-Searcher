Politics & Policy: Nation's Governors Express Resentment On Issue of Underwriting Thrift Bailout

  • Author: David Shribman
  • Date: 07/30/90

MOBILE, Ala. -- The nation's governors, always skeptical of Washington, came together for their annual meeting here with an unusually harsh and pervasive sense of resentment toward the federal government's handling of the thrift crisis. The executive committee of the National Governors' Association approved a resolution to create a committee to examine the causes of the S&L crisis and to recommend ways to "stop the drain on the taxpayers' money." The resolution, which is expected to win approval by the entire group of governors, calls for a review of the "regulatory failures that caused this unprecedented disaster." The resolution also asks for "decisive action" to prevent a repetition of this crisis.

Underlying the anger is a strong regional division, with leaders of farm states expressing hostility over the prospect of helping to underwrite the thrift bailout just a few years after a farm-credit crisis left their states economically devastated.

"We didn't have any S&Ls that went broke in our state, but we led the nation in farm bankruptcies in 1986," said Republican Gov. George Mickelson of South Dakota. "Nobody bailed us out."

And though the governors divided along predictable lines over whether the entire nation should bare the burden of the thrift bailout, there was virtually unanimous sentiment that the S&L problem was skewing priorities and diverting money.

"We're looking at resources that we desperately need and see them being dished out to deal with this problem -- with no real sense that we have a tourniquet to stop the hemorrhaging," said Democratic Gov. Richard Celeste of Ohio, who had his own thrift crisis in the 1980s and who is a leading voice of resentment against Washington now. "Part of this feeling is regional sentiment, but there's a real sense that Head Start and other programs for children and the poor will pay for this."

The governors acknowledge that, like many initiatives pressed at conferences such as this, any action they take would have little effect and in any case would be binding on no one.

"There is not a lot we can do as governors," said GOP Gov. Stan Stephens of Montana. "The fate of these kinds of resolutions is well known. Nothing much happens."

Republican Gov. Garrey Carruthers of New Mexico dismissed the resolution as a "feel-good" gesture, but Democratic Gov. James Blanchard of Michigan characterized it as a "vote of no confidence on federal action thus far."

One of the major concerns of the governors is to have some voice over whatever proceeds are reaped, now or in the future, from the costly efforts to get the thrift industry back on its feet.

"Maybe we can generate some money that will go back and pay for some of this," said Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, president of the Democratic Governors' Association. Mr. Clinton's state, like many in the South, was hit particularly hard by the thrift crisis. But he argues that Arkansas and others were "just hit-and-run victims" rather than the perpetrators of the crisis.

That argument hasn't mollified farm-state governors, who, despite congressional action to ease the farm credit crisis of the 1980s, still are resentful over what they consider a lack of national resolve over their problem.

"This farm-state governor is madder than hell to be asked to pay for this," GOP Gov. Terry Branstad of Iowa said of the thrift bailout. "I don't like the idea of paying the bills for the extravagance of people involved in the S&L crisis. We've been asked to pay the bill for people who make stupid decisions."

Mr. Branstad's remark stoked the issue, which other Republicans hoped would remain under the surface and not flare into a public disruption. But some Democrats considered Mr. Branstad's arguments partisan in their own right. "The Branstad comments," said Democratic Gov. Clinton, "will be good politics in an election year in Iowa."
