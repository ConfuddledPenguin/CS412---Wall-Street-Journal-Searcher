Banks Cut Commercial Paper Borrowing

  • Author: Robert Guenther and Tom Herman
  • Date: 07/30/90

NEW YORK -- As investors grow more nervous about the banking industry's health, many banks are cutting back their borrowing in the commercial paper market. While banks apparently have been able to line up alternate sources of money without disruption, the fact that the world's largest market for short-term money is less hospitable to some banks is another sign of the increasingly difficult environment banks are facing, analysts say.

Commercial paper, which plays a relatively minor role in bank funding, is basically an unsecured corporate IOU that is supposed to be repaid within a short period of time, typically before 90 days. The popularity of commercial paper issued by corporations to meet short-term borrowing needs surged in the 1980s; currently, more than $550 billion of commercial paper from all types of borrowers is outstanding.

Money market mutual funds are the biggest holders; by some estimates, money funds hold about 40% of the total amount outstanding.

But many banks increasingly are concerned that sales of commercial paper could dry up on short notice, leaving them scrambling to raise funds elsewhere. That danger has increased, analysts say, as credit-rating agencies have downgraded the ratings on many banks' debt. "Commercial paper buyers can cut and run at any time," says Joseph Spalluto, senior vice president for fixed-income sales at Keefe, Bruyette & Woods Inc., a securities firm.

"Banks are reducing their dependence on commercial paper, and one reason is a major buyer of commercial paper -- the money market funds -- are limiting their purchases" to top-rated paper, says Stephen W. Joynt, executive managing director at Fitch Investors Service Inc., a rating agency. Even among top-rated commercial paper, money funds are being "more selective" about what they buy, he adds.

Citicorp, which had its debt rating downgraded by Moody's Investors Service Inc. this spring, cut its average commercial paper borrowings in the first quarter 30% to about $6.9 billion from $9.9 billion a year earlier. The U.S.'s biggest banking company says it has gathered more consumer deposits, usually a cheaper source of funding, to reduce its appetite for commercial paper.

Edison, N.J.-based Midlantic Corp. has sliced its commercial paper borrowing to $170 million from a peak of about $225 million. Donald Ebbert, a senior vice president, says, "We are very clearly trying to reduce our commercial paper outstandings to zero over the next 60 days."

Privately, banking regulators, including the Federal Reserve, have encouraged banks to reduce commercial paper borrowings, according to individuals close to banks, in favor of longer-term and less-volatile sources of money like consumer deposits or the sale of long-term bonds.

The movement away from commercial paper is part of a broader trend among banks in favor of longer-term funding that can't disappear on short notice and of keeping greater amounts of cash on hand.

In January 1989, the Federal Reserve quietly stopped publishing data on banks' commercial paper outstanding. However, in December 1988, bank commercial paper outstanding stood at $44.4 billion. According to statistics compiled by Moody's, commercial paper outstanding among a group of the U.S.'s largest banks fell to about $22.7 billion at the end of March from $30.8 billion a year earlier.

Banks issue commercial paper at the parent holding-company level, not the bank, usually to fund nonbank subsidiaries. Bank commercial paper isn't covered by federal deposit insurance.

Commercial paper defaults are rare, but when they hit, they can send shock waves throughout the financial markets. Many investors still recall vividly the financial wreck in 1970 of Penn Central Transportation Co. The company ran out of cash and defaulted on its commercial paper borrowings, frightening many investors away from the market.

Prices have also been a factor in some banks' retreat from the commercial paper market. Since March, when banks reported generally poor results, the differential in commercial paper rates paid by top-rated banks and lower-rated banks has widened to 0.30 percentage point. That compares to a 0.15 differential between top-rated and lower-rated industrial issuers. This is a sign that lower-rated banks are having more difficulty raising funds cheaply in the paper market.

Indeed, says Mr. Spalluto of Keefe, Bruyette & Woods, "The concerns of the commercial paper market are filtering over to longer-term debt issues. People are keenly aware of the role that commercial paper plays in liquidity."

So far, banks have been able replace their commercial paper borrowings by several means. Midlantic has agreed to sell its leasing subsidiary, thereby eliminating its need for commercial paper. Others, like Baltimore-based MNC Financial Corp., have bundled portfolios of credit-card loans into securities that were sold, thereby reducing the amount of assets that need funding.

Depositors of beleaguered savings and loan associations apparently have been a source of replacement funding. Federal Reserve figures show that in 1989, time deposits of less than $100,000 at commercial banks grew 17.7%, far faster than other types of bank liabilities.

Nevertheless, banks like Citicorp don't intend to drop out of the commercial paper market entirely. "We like to keep our options open," said a spokesman. Commercial paper provides banks with a quick source of relatively inexpensive funds. Top-rated Banc One Corp. earlier this year sold $700 million in commercial paper without any snags to fund its acquisition of a Texas bank. Banc One, based in Columbus, Ohio, has since replaced the paper with long-term funding through sales of stock and bonds.

George R.L. Meiling, Banc One's treasurer, said: "Commercial paper is an accordion that expands and contracts with our needs. We use it as filler to tide us over lumps in funding."
