f As World Trade Talks Recess, Officials Have Yet to Tackle Many Tough Issues

  • Author: John T. Norman Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/30/90

WASHINGTON -- Until now, the world trade negotiations involving 105 nations in Geneva have been uneventful. And negotiators, working under a Dec. 3 deadline, haven't yet tackled the many issues concerning the future shape of the world's trading system.

Instead, in Geneva and elsewhere, officials of major trading nations and others have been playing what Arthur Dunkel, the director-general of the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade calls a game of "hide-and-seek."

At the conclusion of a meeting of trade negotiators Thursday, Mr. Dunkel warned that such tactics pose a serious risk that the Uruguay Round of world trade talks, launched in Uruguay nearly four years ago, might fail.

"None of us can afford the disruption in the flow of goods, services and investment that failure would entail," said the former Swiss diplomat, who isn't given to exaggerated comments on trade issues.

"We must," he said, "have a radically changed and more urgent approach to the task before us."

The reports of negotiating groups in many different sectors of the trade talks represent a "compendium of positions, rather than draft agreements," Mr. Dunkel told high-level representatives of GATT member countries, in proposing a series of deadlines for resolving differences over the next four months.

There are, said Mr. Dunkel, only about 15 "working weeks" remaining to wrap up agreements on a new set of international trade rules covering agriculture, services, textiles, GATT disputes-settlement mechanisms and many other issues.

By late September or early October, the most ambitious effort since the end of World War II to write new international trade rules may have to be scaled back in order for trade ministers to proclaim the Uruguay Round a "success" late this year.

In Geneva last week, there had appeared to be a strong and growing sense of frustration about the current state of affairs in the Uruguay Round talks, yet not enough hand-wringing to upset the traditional August vacations of government officials in many countries.

The U.S., the European Community and other participants in the world trade talks are slated to resume negotiations in the week of Aug. 27 on the toughest unresolved problem, agricultural reforms. But a senior U.S. official predicted the "big fight" on that could come in Geneva during the month of September.

Without an agreement on agriculture, involving commitments by the industrial nations to reduce their farm-export subsidies and open markets, the prospects are that many other commodity-exporting countries will conclude that the Uruguay Round was a failure.

Some representatives of developing nations had anticipated that this "big fight" would take place last week in Geneva, with hard bargaining and "real results." Rubens Ricupero, of Brazil, complained that last week's meeting in Geneva was largely a waste of time, with delegates representing Third World nations having "nothing to do but stand around."

Despite the uncertain prospects for the world trade talks, some veterans in the international trade bureaucracy say it isn't unusual for the situation to look pretty grim several months before the wind-up of such negotiations.

Almost everything will fall in place, they predict, in the last few weeks of the negotiations in Geneva or during the windup meeting of trade ministers in Brussels in early December.
