Abreast of the Market: Blue Chips Ease Amid Wariness, But Broader Market Edges Up

  • Author: Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 06/01/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks eased yesterday as the closing bell approached, with investors backing away from the market ahead of today's economic reports for May. But while blue-chip stocks ended lower, the broader market held onto fractional gains. U.S. employment figures for May will be released at 8:30 a.m. EDT today, and the purchasing managers report for the month also is to come out. Both reports will be scanned for signs of continued softening in the economy, adding credence to the theory that inflation is under control and interest rates have peaked.

After sharp gains in the stock market last month, however, many market players turned cautious yesterday. Any sharp deviation in the data from expectations could spark a round of profit-taking, analysts said.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average slipped 1.90 points to 2876.66. The blue-chip index opened lower, as traders reacted to weaker prices overseas. While the DJIA soon recovered, the day's gains evaporated late in the session.

Tonka crashed early, losing more than 30% of its value with a 3-point decline to 7. Trading in the issue opened late, as sellers rushed to dump the stock after the company's announcement late Wednesday that it expects to report a "significant loss" for the second quarter, on lower sales. The company also said the loss could result in violations of some covenants in its bank loan agreement.

Meanwhile, the broader market ended higher, with Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index inching ahead to yet another all-time high. The index has climbed nearly 10% from its late-April low, yesterday adding 0.37 point to 361.23.

The New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 0.19 point to 196.94, while the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 0.31 point to 335.98.

Though the market's altitude is making some investors queasy, Guy W. Courtney, senior vice president of David A. Noyes, is undaunted. "I'm not nervous at all," he said. "This market has got plenty of upside left in it."

Advancing issues led decliners, 801 to 682, and 534 stocks ended unchanged. Big Board volume totaled about 165.7 million shares, down from Wednesday's robust 199.5 million shares. Yesterday's volume was bloated by a huge block of Armstrong World Industries and dividend-related trading in Texas Utilities and other stocks, however.

Steven A. Kroll, president of SLH Asset Management, Shearson Lehman Hutton's money management operation, said that while the market paused ahead of today's data, stocks won't begin "a new leg of the bull market" until the Federal Reserve has eased credit. Until then, Mr. Kroll believes, stock prices will remain locked in a trading range with a ceiling at about the 2900 level on the Dow industrials.

Many market watchers expect today's employment report to show a strong increase in job growth for May, with the nation's nonfarm payroll having increased by 253,000 employees. This anticipated increase includes newly hired census workers, however, meaning that actual job growth is estimated to have been a more modest 135,000.

Stronger job growth could cue some investors to sell stocks. Edward J. Laux, head of block trading at Kidder Peabody, said the DJIA could drop 70 to 100 points if the number is disappointing. As for any gains, Mr. Laux sees room for only about a 30-point move by the Dow industrials.

Among the blue chips, Procter & Gamble rose 5/8 to 82 5/8 yesterday. Morgan Stanley has repeated its buy recommendation on the stock.

International Business Machines eased 7/8 to 120. The stock opened lower, following weakness in Europe because of concerns that Hitachi of Japan will soon unveil a mainframe computer that will be faster than IBM's competing models. Hitachi is scheduled to introduce a mainframe product next Wednesday.

Data General added 1 1/4, or 11.5%, to 12 1/8. The company said Smith Barney, Harris Upham raised its rating on the stock, after analysts met with Data General executives Wednesday, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

But L.A. Gear continued to tumble, closing off 4 3/8 at 39 1/4, on volume of more than 2.2 million shares. Profit-taking has hit the issue since Kidder Peabody cut its rating on the stock earlier this week, citing the recent sharp rise in the shares' price. The stock lost 20% so far this week.

Philip Morris led the most-active list, on volume of more than 2.7 million shares. The stock eased 3/8 to 42 1/8.

Westinghouse Electric jumped 1 3/8 to 36 3/8. The company said it will begin a buy-back of as many as 20 million common shares in the next 12 to 18 months. Westinghouse has about 294 million shares outstanding. Separately, Westinghouse's board voted to exempt the company from coverage under a recently passed Pennsylvania anti-takeover statute.

Armco rose 7/8 to 8 3/4, after Goldman Sachs upgraded its rating on the stock, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Ashland Oil dropped 1 1/4 to 38 3/8, after the company was ordered to pay $10.3 million in damages to residents near its Catlettsburg, Ky., refinery. The residents had sued Ashland, complaining that emissions from the plant had damaged their health and property. Ashland said it plans to appeal the award.

Pfizer gained 2 1/4 to 65 7/8. A judge dismissed all claims against the company in a lawsuit stemming from the sale of faulty heart valves marketed by Pfizer. A consumer group brought the suit in March, seeking to require Pfizer and Shiley, maker of the valves, to communicate directly with all recipients of the valves.

Commonwealth Edison slid 1 1/4 to 31 1/2. The Illinois Supreme Court has ruled that the utility should refund more than $300 million to customers. The revenues were collected under a rate increase awarded by the Illinois Commerce Commission. In its ruling, the court said the commission acted without jurisdiction.

First Bank Systems dropped 1 to 14 1/2. The company said it will sell 12.6 million newly issued shares in a private placement to an investment group. M/A-Com rose 1/2 to 5 1/4, after it definitively agreed to sell its government systems unit to Titan Corp. Titan closed unchanged at 2 3/8.

Air Products & Chemicals rose 5/8 to 56 3/4. The company said it agreed to sell its polyvinyl chloride buisness to a unit of Pacific Western Extruded Plastics for an undisclosed amount.

Florida Progress eased 1/2 to 36 3/8, on volume of 1.1 million shares; much of the trading took place on the Philadelphia Stock Exchange.

Analysts identified Hasbro, the largest U.S. toy maker, as a likely bidder for Tyco Toys, which said Wednesday it received an unsolicited acquisition approach from a party that wasn't identified. Tyco declined to elaborate yesterday. Hasbro wouldn't comment on the report, though an official said the company remains interested in potential acquisitions. Tyco shares jumped 2 3/4 to 27 3/8 in national over-the-counter trading, with more than 1.3 million shares changing hands. Hasbro's shares also advanced on heavy volume, closing up 3/8 at 21 1/4 in American Stock Exchange trading of 617,900 shares. Paul Valentine, an analyst with Standard & Poor's Corp., said that Tyco "may be trying to put itself into play," and that the stock "has had a great run" since going public at $6 in 1986.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.15 point to 363.06. Amex volume totaled about 15.9 million shares, down slightly from 16.3 million shares a day earlier. Advancing stocks led those retreating, 291 to 269, and 264 issues were unchanged.

Thermo Process Systems fell 1 to 13 1/2. Yesterday's "Heard on the Street" column in The Wall Street Journal said the stock is getting pricey, after appreciating 72% since the start of 1989.

Heritage Media added 1/2 to 5 3/8. Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette initiated coverage of the stock with a "buy" recommendation, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Brown Group gained 2 1/2 to 27 1/8. The company reported first-quarter net income of 51 cents a share, up from 42 cents a year earlier.

Sanifill surged 1 1/2 to 19 3/8, after Tucker Anthony raised its earnings estimates for the company and repeated a "strong buy" rating on the stock.
