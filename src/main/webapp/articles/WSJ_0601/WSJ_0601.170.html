Swiss Central Bank Ends Hopes for Easing Of Monetary Policy

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/01/90

ZURICH -- The Swiss National Bank effectively dashed hopes that it might ease its restrictive monetary policy this year, reiterating a commitment to firm interest rates and a strong Swiss franc. The statements at the central bank's annual news conference came as no surprise to the markets. But the remarks underscored the Swiss National Bank's intention to turn a deaf ear to domestic observers who would like to see rates begin to ease as early as this summer to preclude any chance of a recession.

Central bank President Markus Lusser, citing continued inflationary pressures in the robust economy, said "we therefore see no reason to modify fundamentally the direction of our monetary policy in the near future."

He repeated the central bank's forecast that inflation will fall sharply by the end of the year after reaching an expected 5.5% annual pace in May. With the help of the strong franc, the earlier fears of 6% annual rates in the summer months may be avoided, he added.

Nevertheless, Hans Meyer, vice president of the central bank, ruled out the possibility that this will usher in lower interest rates in the autumn. "I don't see any significant change for the rest of the year," he said.

Officials also emphasized the positive contribution to monetary policy objectives made by the recent strength of the Swiss franc, which helps to reduce the inflationary impact of imports.

"The evolution of the Swiss franc has been a lot more favorable than we could have hoped for several months ago," Mr. Lusser said. He added that he "could live" with a slightly firmer franc and doesn't see a danger now that the currency may get too strong.
