First Bank System Is to Sell 16.7% To Investor Group

  • Author: Jeff Bailey
  • Date: 06/01/90

First Bank System Inc. agreed to sell a 16.7% stake to an investor group for $175 million in a move to increase its capital base and speed the banking concern's recovery. At the same time, John F. Grundhofer, First Bank's new chairman, president and chief executive officer, said that, following a "thorough review" of the loan portfolio, a $390.6 million reserve for loan losses was "adequate." One of the big uncertainties hanging over First Bank's depressed stock price since Mr. Grundhofer was hired four months ago has been whether he would make another big addition to the reserve after going through the loan files.

Mr. Grundhofer said yesterday that he hopes his positive review, along with the commitment of a Lazard Freres & Co.-sponsored investment fund to buy a big stake in First Bank would begin to re-establish the company's credibility.

First Bank has been hurt by big bond and loan losses in recent years, as well as from a management strategy that didn't emphasize the Minneapolis-based concern's big market share in consumer banking in the upper Midwest.

The Lazard fund, Corporate Partners, would put up $145 million, and one of the investors in the fund, the State Board of Administration of Florida, would put up $30 million. Together, they would buy 12.6 million newly issued First Bank common shares at $13.875 each; that price was agreed during a due-diligence period when First Bank's average price was $13.50, the company said.

Yesterday, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, First Bank common closed at $14.50, off $1, on volume of 334,200 shares.

The sale would increase First Bank's shares outstanding to about 75.4 million from 62.8 million, but because of tax benefits the company has and its relatively modest profit, the higher number of shares wouldn't immediately cut into per-share profit much. "A few cents a share this year," said Richard A. Zona, chief financial officer, adding: "It will be more as the earnings pick up."

First Bank had first-quarter profit of $28.5 million, or 37 cents a share.

First Bank said Lester Pollack, the Lazard partner who manages the fund, approached the banking concern about the investment. Another so-called friendly, minority stake fund also approached First Bank, but it preferred the terms offered by Mr. Pollack.

A public sale of stock didn't seem feasible. "Most of our investment banking friends were not very optimistic," Mr. Grundhofer said.

The private placement of the stock is subject to Federal Reserve Board approval, which First Bank expects could take about 45 days.

As part of the investment, the Lazard fund would designate a nonvoting "observer" to First Bank's board. The fund couldn't raise its stake beyond 150% of what it would be under this investment, couldn't launch a proxy fight, and couldn't sell more than a 5% stake to another single investor.

The investors also would get what Mr. Grundhofer called "disaster scenario" warrants that would help protect their investment should First Bank's stock, or its net worth, not live up to expectations, or should its dividend be cut or omitted.

The money will boost First Bank's common equity-to-assets ratio to 4.8% from 4%, as of March 31, on a pro forma basis. That will make it a lot easier for the company to expand its consumer banking business, which is Mr. Grundhofer's priority. He said the stock sale was better than having to sell some businesses to raise capital.
