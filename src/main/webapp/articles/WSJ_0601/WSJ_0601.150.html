Speculators Focus on Denmark Bourse As EC Unification in '92 Draws Near

  • Author: Debra Marks Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/01/90

COPENHAGEN -- Although not traditionally known as one of Europe's peppier exchanges, the Danish stock market could be the scene of some clever bidding ahead of European Community market unification at the end of 1992. But while takeover moves are pushing the Copenhagen bourse higher, individual investors may find it hard to capitalize on the speculation.

Foreign companies, and especially those of Denmark's non-EC neighbors to the north, are expected to show increasing interest in Danish companies in coming years, with Denmark acting the role of bridgehead into the single European market, some analysts suggest.

"Investors are looking strategically at Denmark," said Claus Anderson, assistant general manager at Bikuben Bors Borsmaeglerselskab AS, a bank-owned broker and member of the Copenhagen Stock Exchange. "Swedes, Norwegians and Finns are very interested in the Danish market as a way into 1992. . . . "If you can't be there directly, you can be there by buying Danish companies."

The bidding already has sent the Danish market motoring higher. Last year, the 300-stock Copenhagen Stock Index gained a hearty 33%. It advanced a more modest 10% during the first four months of this year, held back, market observers say, by rising Danish interest rates, which are closely linked with West German ones.

But Bikuben Bors said he expects prices to rise further and the rising prices to spur more new listings on the Copenhagen exchange. While only 31 companies listed their shares on the exchange during the first half of the 1980s, 72 did so in the second part of the decade.

"There's no doubt that more companies will come to the Danish Stock Exchange this year and next as they see the common market in 1992 as a business opportunity," said Carsten Nielsen, chief of block trading at Unibors Borsmaeglerselskab AS in Copenhagen.

As the only Nordic country belonging to the 12-member arrangement, Denmark already has attracted more than just a curious glance from some large Swedish and Norwegian companies.

During the past year, Finland's Kemira Oy bought the fertilizer unit of Superfrost AS; Sweden's Nobel Industrier Sverige AB bought paint-maker Sadolin Holmblad AS; Norway's Nora Industrier AS netted Danish Fancy Food Group AS; Sweden's Hexagon AB bought a slew of small Danish food companies, and Foervaltnings AB Ratos of Sweden bought Dahl Invest International AS, a subsidiary of building-products maker Broedrene Dahl AS.

A number of strategic alliances have been forged between Danish concerns and others in Europe, giving small to medium-sized Danish companies a chance against bigger players on the post-1992 stage.

Dutch electronics giant N.V. Philips Gloeilampenfabriken in May bought a 25% stake in advanced audio-video designer and manufacturer Bang & Olufson AS for 340 million Danish kroner ($53.2 million).

Also, Britain's Blue Circle Industries PLC, a cement maker, bought a 50% interest in Denmark's only cement maker Aalborg Portland for 980 million kroner.

But some say the EC link is tenuous at best. "The euphoria is a bit overdone," said Claus Gregerson, an institutional salesman at Stockholm-based brokerage Alfred Berg UK Ltd. "There's a big tradition for cross-border trade in Scandinavia but the same doesn't hold for Denmark."

For stock-market investors, tapping into Danish takeovers is made difficult by the exchange's makeup. Although Denmark adopted a screen-based trading system two years ago, eliminating dealing on the stock-exchange floor, there are huge obstacles that make quick and easy settlement difficult. Because of its substantial delivery problems, the Danish market is "diluting potential foreign interest by having an inflexible system which keeps a lot of people from thinking about investing there," said Mr. Gregerson.

Part of the problem, say some Danish market watchers, is the inability in practice to deal in short positions or take up a role similar to the market makers and specialists in London and New York. Because there are no guaranteed sellers, getting hold of securities to cover short positions can be costly, traders note.

In addition, takeover opportunities among Danish companies are severely limited because many Danish businesses are protected by two-tiered share capital, making it virtually impossible to obtain a controlling interest in a company by buying up only one class of its stock.
