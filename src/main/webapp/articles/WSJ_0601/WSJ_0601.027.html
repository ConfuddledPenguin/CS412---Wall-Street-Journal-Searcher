Marketing & Media: Sale of Saatchi & Saatchi's Hay Unit Appears Imminent, at a Reduced Price

  • Author: Amanda Bennett
  • Date: 06/01/90

Saatchi & Saatchi Co.'s long-awaited sale of its Hay Group unit finally appears imminent -- at a price far below what it once hoped to fetch for the consulting company. People close to Hay confirmed that a management buy-out of the Philadelphia-based firm that specializes in compensation, employee benefits and human resources planning would be announced as early as next week. The price is expected to range between $85 million and $95 million.

Although such a management buy-out has been rumored repeatedly over the past few months, an executive familiar with Saatchi's plans said negotiations were finished and all that remained was for Hay to complete financing and legal details. Both Saatchi and Hay declined to comment.

The sale of Hay to its own managers would nearly complete Saatchi & Saatchi's withdrawal from the world of management consulting, a field it once vowed it would dominate. What's more, even in retreat, the troubled British advertising giant fell short of its own expectations.

The company last June told analysts it hoped to bring in between $393 million and $550 million from the sale of its stable of a dozen or so consulting businesses. But, including the Hay sale, Saatchi's major consulting companies have brought only about $132 million: Saatchi late last year sold its interest in its Information Consulting Group unit to McKinsey & Co. for $12 million, while the sale of a small consulting company in France, one in Britain and of Cleveland Consulting Associates in the U.S. fetched $25 million total. Of major units, only Gartner Group, a company smaller than Hay, remains unsold.

The executive familiar with Saatchi's situation said Saatchi had been discussing continuing to hold a mortgage of about 20% of the Hay purchase price, and that it was understood that a third, British investor, might assume an equity position in Hay.

One analyst said a final sale would be positive for all concerned. "This whole episode is best closed," said Alan Gottelsman, an analyst with PaineWebber. "This should have happened a year ago."

A person close to Hay suggested that following the buy-out Hay would revert to its pre-Saatchi structure of ownership by about 120 key principals. Hay has about 1,000 employees in the U.S.

The buy-out will leave Hay's managers with a big task. Hay "has really suffered over the past two years," says David Lord, managing editor of Consultants News, an industry publication, which ranks Hay 18th among U.S. consulting companies. The publication estimates Hay's 1989 revenue at $210 million, about flat from the year earlier.

While Hay doesn't disclose specific revenue figures, a company spokesman acknowledged "relatively flat" revenue, attributing it to "an industrywide phenomenon." Hay confirmed that it is closing two of its 20 U.S. offices and laying off an unspecified number of consultants. "The current cutbacks in professional and administrative staff are part of a normal adjustment process," the spokesman said.
