Junk Buy-Backs Take Off; Issuers Spring at Discounts --- Often-Secret Moves Can Boost Earnings

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 06/01/90

NEW YORK -- Corporate buy-backs of junk bonds hit a record $2.5 billion during the first quarter and are expected to set new highs for the year. Dozens of companies that sold junk bonds to investors now are rushing to buy them back at a steep discount. Traders say that the surge in buy-backs is one reason -- along with falling interest rates -- for the recent modest rally in the sagging junk market.

The buy-back trend started to sizzle after the junk-bond market went into a tailspin last September. Since then, prices of some high-yield, high-risk bonds have fallen as much as 40%, creating opportunities for companies that have access to large amounts of cash to buy back their debt on the cheap.

Tom Mandel, a portfolio manager at 1838 Investment Advisors L.P., believes that corporate treasurers are among the biggest buyers of junk bonds. "It's clearly a big help to the market. People at least have bids for their bonds, whereas before they could easily have not had any. You're not always getting a fair price, but at least you're getting something."

Joseph Bencivenga, director and manager of high-yield research at Salomon Brothers Inc., said the buy-backs have provided a firm floor under the depressed junk market.

Most of the $2.5 billion of junk bonds that corporations bought back in the first quarter were repurchased informally in the open market, according to statistics compiled by First Boston Corp. But the figures also include bonds that were formally "called" or retired.

The buy-backs are great for the issuers, which get to retire their debt at a discount to face value in transactions that can boost earnings. In recent months, traders have spotted Burlington Industries Inc., Mark IV Industries Inc., Coltec Industries Inc., Levi Strauss & Co. and many others actively buying back their bonds.

But don't expect to hear much about the trend. Traders said the buy-backs are often kept secret, at least long enough for the companies to buy the bonds while prices are still cheap. That's very different from the stock market, where companies routinely announce stock repurchase programs. But then, the purpose of buying back stock is often to boost prices, whereas bond buy-backs are designed to retire debt cheaply.

"We have such an illiquid market, that as soon as a company announces it's buying back debt, prices jump," said Kingman Penniman, senior vice president at McCarthy, Crisanti & Maffei, a Xerox Corp. unit. "Once you advertise that you're buying, nobody wants to sell at low prices."

On and off speculation that RJR Nabisco Inc. is considering buying between $1 billion and $2 billion of its junk bonds have kept prices of those bonds gyrating wildly in recent months.

Burlington Industries, a textile and apparel maker, quietly repurchased junk bonds with a face value of $260 million for just $200 million -- a $60 million discount. Like many junk-bond issuers, Burlington took out a bank loan to get the money to buy back its bonds. Banks are often willing to make such loans to corporate clients because the buy-backs improve a company's financial health, investment bankers say.

Burlington said the transaction resulted in a $14 million net extraordinary gain to first-quarter earnings. Burlington, which reported a net loss for the quarter, purchased the bonds between January and March.

There are signs, however, that bondholders are starting to balk at the low-ball bids received from companies.

"One thing that may slow down repurchases is bondholders themselves, if they feel they are not being adequately compensated," said Salomon's Mr. Bencivenga.

On Wednesday, for example, Michelin & Cie.'s Michelin Finance unit said it was unsuccessful in its attempt to buy back the junk bonds of its Uniroyal Goodrich Tire Co. subsidiary. Although Michelin offered to pay bondholders premiums over current market prices, junk-bond traders said investors held out for more money.

The Michelin transaction differs from many recent buy-backs in that the French tire maker made a formal, public offer to investors.

Most companies, however, quietly buy up their junk bonds. Often, the buy-backs are initiated by traders on behalf of large institutional investors that have been unable to sell their bonds. Some money managers say they contact junk-bond issuers directly and negotiate buy-back agreement without the help of Wall Street.
