Kollmorgen Appears Winner in Proxy War Waged by Vernitron

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/01/90

SIMSBURY, Conn. -- Kollmorgen Corp., a maker of motors and electro-optical instruments, appears to have won a proxy fight for control of the board waged by hostile suitor Vernitron Corp. But Vernitron said that Kollmorgen won the fight only because of the votes from some convertible preferred stock Kollmorgen sold to an investor group in March. The vote was held yesterday at the annual meeting.

Vernitron's chairman and chief executive officer, Stephen Bershad, has said the company would drop the battle if it lost the proxy fight. But Mr. Bershad said yesterday that Vernitron is "evaluating all (its) options," and will continue the litigation pending between the two companies.

Vernitron, a closely held Deer Park, N.Y., manufacturer of motors and controls, that has pursued Kollmorgen for 17 months, now holds 6% of that company.

At one point, the companies had a definitive agreement under which Vernitron would pay $25 a share, or $320 million, for Kollmorgen. Vernitron withdrew from the transaction a week before it was to close, citing "adverse changes" in Kollmorgen that it didn't expect. The transaction's collapse led the companies to sue each other. Since then, Kollmorgen has announced it would restructure, a move that includes sales of assets.

Most recently, Vernitron proposed to buy the 94% it doesn't own of the now-smaller Kollmorgen. Vernitron said it would pay $15 a share in cash, or in a unit consisting of one common share of the merged Kollmorgen-Vernitron plus a share of the proceeds from a sale of Kollmorgen's electro-optical business. Vernitron would pay cash for as much as 50% of the shares on a pro-rata basis.

"The fact is that the majority of the common shareholders appear to have voted for at least four of our directors," Mr. Bershad said. "If you take out the preferred and the shares owned by (Kollmorgen) management and their families, then they didn't get many votes at all. And for them to consider that an endorsement of any of their policies is to misconstrue the facts."

John Youngblood, Kollmorgen's chairman and chief executive, said that the company is pleased that shareholders "have spoken so clearly and decisively." He added that Kollmorgen is "confident that without the continued disruption of Vernitron's activities," the company's restructuring will benefit all shareholders.

Mr. Bershad said, "I would like to see some form of combination. Whether that's possible, I don't know."

Final results of the balloting will be available in about a week.
