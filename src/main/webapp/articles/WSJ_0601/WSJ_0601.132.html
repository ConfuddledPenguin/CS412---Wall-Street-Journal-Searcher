Boeing Co. Gets Order by China For 33 Aircraft --- Value Is Put at $2 Billion; Chinese Take Options For an Additional 36

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 06/01/90

China's Civil Aviation Administration ordered 33 Boeing Co. airplanes valued at $2 billion and took options on 36 others, marking the second big booking for the world's No. 1 jet maker in the past week. "It seems like the good orders just won't stop," said Jerry Cantwell, an aerospace analyst who follows Seattle-based Boeing for Wertheim Schroder & Co. "It seems like the stock won't stop going up either."

Indeed, Boeing shares closed in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday at $82.375, up 37.5 cents.

The stock had climbed more than a point earlier in the day, but it fell back as the entire market retreated. Mr. Cantwell noted that details of the Chinese order weren't disclosed until just minutes before the final bell, meaning the Big Board might not have had time to react to the latest good news. Boeing's high for the past 52 weeks is $82.75, reached last week.

The firm orders consist of five 747-400 four-engine jumbo-jets, which cost about $130 million apiece; one 747-200F freighter; four 767 wide-bodies; 10 757 narrow-bodies; and 13 737-300 twinjets.

The aviation authority oversees the purchasing of planes for China's airlines, and the aircraft ordered will be used by Air China, Guangzhou CAAC, China Southwest Airlines and other carriers.

The agency has picked PW4000 engines, a new, powerful model made by United Technologies Corp.'s Pratt & Whitney unit, to propel the 747-400s and 767s. And a Pratt & Whitney JT9D-7R4G2 will be used on the freighter. That order is valued at about $250 million.

Rolls-Royce engines were tapped for the 757s, and engines made by CFM International, a joint venture of General Electric Co. and Snecma of France, will be used on the 737-300s.

Although Boeing has, as a matter of policy, ceased providing delivery dates for its planes, Pratt & Whitney said the first aircraft will begin being shipped to Air China in the first quarter of 1992. That is earlier than when many other Boeing customers are set to receive their jets, and there has even been some speculation that Boeing earlier this week lost a Mexicana Airlines order to rival Airbus Industrie because its delivery schedule is stretched so far.

Boeing has been negotiating seriously with the Chinese for the past eight months, and the company said the purchase of the 69-plane package had nothing to do with President Bush's decision last week to renew most-favored trade status with that country. Outside experts concurred, explaining that such a designation is designed to give China low tariffs on its exports to the U.S.

It wasn't immediately clear how many other companies were in the running for the Chinese order. McDonnell Douglas Corp. has a good relationship with the nation, whose airlines have ordered MD-11 tri-jets in the past and which actually assembles some MD-80s in Shanghai.

A spokesman at McDonnell Douglas's transport aircraft division in Long Beach, Calif., said he didn't know whether the firm had actively bid against Boeing for the latest order. But one source said the company may not have been "in a particular campaign on this one."

Boeing, for its part, stressed its own longstanding relationship with the Chinese. Since 1972, the nation has purchased 101 Boeing airplanes, including three additional 757s last October.

The company's orders for the year for all models now stand at 187 valued at about $16.2 billion. Only a week ago, Japan Airlines ordered 20 747-400s valued at $3.1 billion and placed options for 34 others.
