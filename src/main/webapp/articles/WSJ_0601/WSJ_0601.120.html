Westinghouse Will Buy Back 20 Million Shares

  • Author: Gregory Stricharchuk
  • Date: 06/01/90

PITTSBURGH -- Westinghouse Electric Corp. said it will buy back as many as 20 million common shares, or roughly 7% of its shares outstanding in the next 12 to 18 months. The broadcasting, financial services and power generation concern also said directors voted to exempt Westinghouse from the protection of Pennsylvania's anti-takeover law that went into effect on April 27. The move apparently is aimed at making Westinghouse stock more attractive to institutional investors even though the large company isn't considered a likely takeover target.

In addition, Westinghouse said it sold its combustion control business to Rosemount Analytical Inc., of Minneapolis. Terms weren't disclosed. Rosemount is a unit of Emerson Electric Co., of St. Louis, Mo., a maker of measurement, control, analytical and valve products.

Westinghouse also said it is part of a consortium that was awarded a $358 million contract to build a coal-fired power generating unit for Old Dominion Electric Cooperative and Virgina Power. Other members of the consortium are Combustion Engineering Inc., H.B. Zachry Co. and Black & Veatch Engineers-Architects.

The contract calls for a 393-megawatt pulverized coal unit to be built at the Clover Power Station in Halifax County, Va.

Westinghouse stock rose $1.375 to close at $38.375 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

Analysts who follow the company said the announced buy-back of more than $700 million of stock all but ends speculation that Westinghouse might want to launch a bid for Sundstrand Corp., an aerospace-parts concern based in Rockford, Ill.

Westinghouse's top officials have declined to comment specifically on the Sundstrand rumor. But in a story in this newspaper earlier this week, the officials cast doubt on whether Westinghouse would undertake a more than $2 billion acquisition that would dilute earnings.

Sundstrand on Wednesday tumbled $4.25. Yesterday the stock closed at $74, down 12.5 cents in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

Westinghouse said it considers its stock an attractive investment. Initial funding for the buy-back will come from existing cash, internally generated funds or through the issuance of short-term debt.

Separately, the company said it is negotiating a contract to build and operate a waste-to-energy plant in Waukesha, Wis.

The plant will process as many as 600 tons of municipal solid waste daily to fuel a 20-megawatt power plant. Power from the plant will be sold to Wisconsin Electric Power Co. Construction will begin next year with commercial operation scheduled to begin in early 1994.

Waukesha County will finance the plant with project revenue bonds, Westinghouse said.
