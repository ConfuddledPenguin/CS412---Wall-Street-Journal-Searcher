United Asset Management Expects Net, Revenue for Second Quarter to Increase

  • Author: Gary Putka
  • Date: 06/01/90

BOSTON -- United Asset Management Corp. expects second-quarter earnings and revenue to be about the same as the first quarter, when the company reported net income of $4.3 million, or 26 cents a share, on revenue of $40.1 million. If results match the first quarter, UAM would be posting gains above the 1989 second quarter. In that period, the company had net income of $3.6 million, or 23 cents a share, on revenues of $35.6 million.

UAM President Norton Reamer, in an interview, said he expects assets under management to reach a record high at the end of May, of about $39 billion, up from $37.7 billion at March 31.

Net asset growth is coming from market appreciation, and not new invested funds. Mr. Reamer said customer cash flow -- or new cash added vs. cash taken out of portfolios -- was negative so far this year, but only by about 1% to 2% of portfolio assets.

"We're bucking the trend" of greater losses in the rest of the industry, Mr. Reamer said. Because pension plans have appreciated to a size larger than that required to pay future benefits, employers have been net withdrawers from plans for several years. UAM's portfolios are 60% invested in stocks and 40% in fixed-income securities.

Mr. Reamer said he returned recently from Japan, where he has been exploring UAM's interest in forming a joint money-management venture with Japanese financial concerns. UAM, which has acquired 22 money-management concerns since 1983, also is shopping for acquisitions, generally among firms managing at least $300 million, in the United Kingdom and on the European continent. Another sector where UAM hopes to make acquisitions is U.S. money-management concerns that specialize in foreign stocks and bonds or domestic real estate, Mr. Reamer added.

"Our goal is to manage all asset classes in all parts of the world," Mr. Reamer said. Acquisitions that would bring in foreign clients and investments to UAM are a priority now, he said because of "huge amounts of capital being generated in Japan," and the desire by institutional investors globally to expand the geographic diversity of portfolios. UAM currently has negligible assets under management.

"Britain is the most logical shopping place," the UAM president reckons, because a British purchase would kill two birds with one stone -- bringing in new foreign customers and assets invested outside the U.S. But UAM also has found strong German and French bank interest in U.K money-management concerns, bidding up prices for potential buy-out candidates in London.

Mr. Reamer said he thinks UAM is capable of acquiring a money-management concern with as much as $15 billion in assets -- which would add nearly 50% to the company's current portfolios. Asked whether he thought UAM would make an acquisition before the end of 1990, he said "it would be normal to expect that we would. We have a great appetite for deals."

UAM wants to "strike an alliance" with a Japanese partner, perhaps a trust bank or insurer, that will enable UAM to manage a U.S. equities portfolio for the partner's customers. Mr. Reamer said he believed that buying a Japanese money manager isn't possible or desirable because of heavy market concentration and cultural resistance to foreign ownership. But he said that Japanese investors are beginning to show greatly increased interest in the U.S stock market, providing opportunities for U.S. managers.

In addition to its interest in getting foreign customers and assets, UAM also continues to shop for U.S. money managers. Mr. Reamer said many U.S. banks, under regulatory pressure to raise capital, currently are interested in money-management divestments. He said UAM had reviewed several for purchase, but declined to identify them.
