Farm Prices Post 2% Increase For Last Month

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 06/01/90

WASHINGTON -- Agricultural prices in May rose 2% from the previous month as strong demand for pork and tight supplies sent hog prices soaring to their second-highest level ever, the Agriculture Department said. Hog prices hit $60.50 per 100 pounds in May, up $6.70 from April and up $18.90 from a year earlier, the department said. The record high of $62.10 came in August 1986.

The surge in prices received by hog producers portend a 15% jump in retail prices for bacon and other pork products by year end, economists said. Retail pork prices in the first four months of the year were already up 9% from the year-before period.

The near-record prices reflect, among other things, a 15% drop in pork imports and a 26% jump in U.S. pork exports during the first quarter, a 25% decline in cold storage of pork from a year ago and a slight slowdown in hog slaughtering.

"I can remember only one other time in the last 25 years when we had these many price factors go positive for higher prices at one time," said Glenn Grimes, a University of Missouri agricultural economist and consultant to the National Pork Producers Council.

As a result, prices for barrows and gilts rose $7 to $61.20 per 100 pounds in May, while sows rose $2.90 to $51.60 per 100 pounds, the department said.

"The probabilities are very high for hog prices to stay very strong through at least August," said Mr. Grimes. "We think the odds are high that retail prices will go up somewhere in the range of 15%."

Overall farm prices in May were up 3.4% from last year, according to the department. Beef cattle prices averaged $74.30 per 100 pounds, down 30 cents from April. Prices for steers and heifers were also down 30 cents, while cows rose 50 cents.

Corn prices averaged $2.66 per bushel in May, up 15 cents, and sorghum grain averaged $4.10 per 100 pounds, up 21 cents. Barley prices slipped one cent in May to $2.21 per bushel, while oats were unchanged at $1.44 per bushel, the department said.

Egg prices averaged 51.2 cents per dozen, down 13 cents from April, the department said. Broiler prices rose two cents per pound in May to 35.2 cents, and turkeys edged up 1.2 cents per pound to 38.2 cents.

Milk prices averaged $13.20 per 100 pounds in May, down 20 cents from the previous month, but up 90 cents from a year earlier, the department said. Fruit prices, led by oranges and grapefruits, climbed 3.6% in May, while vegetable prices, led by lettuce and tomatoes, rose 3.4%.
