Treasury Suggests Home Loan Banks Cut Payouts to S&Ls

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/01/90

WASHINGTON -- The Treasury Department recommended that the Federal Home Loan Bank system consider reducing the dividend payments it makes to savings and loans because the system may face some losses on advances to insolvent thrifts. Thrift industry groups immediately criticized the suggestion, arguing that the dividend payments are important to the struggling industry's earnings. Last year, the home loan banks paid about $800 million to $900 million in dividends to solvent thrifts, Martin Regalia, an economist with the National Council of Savings Institutions estimated, while those thrifts overall had losses of $86 million for the year.

"Clearly the dividends are an important contribution to keeping the industry healthy," he said. The government is in the process of closing and selling off hundreds of insolvent savings and loans. To obtain advances from the home loan banks, thrifts are required to buy stock in the banks, which, in turn, pay dividends back to the thrifts.

The Treasury's suggestion came in a study of government-sponsored enterprises. Though the home loan banks haven't lost money on an advance to any thrift, the department said that "certain banks have exposure to failed and failing thrifts." The study suggested reducing the dividend payments to increase the home loan banks' capital or for establishing a "modest" loan-loss reserve.

A spokesman for the Federal Housing Finance Board, the Department of Housing and Urban Development agency that oversees the home loan bank system, said the need to increase retained earnings has to be balanced "against the need to maintain a market rate of return on members' invested capital."
