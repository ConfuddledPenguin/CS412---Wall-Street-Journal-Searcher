Economy: Factory Orders Dropped in April After Two Gains --- Decline of 2.3% Was Linked To 4.3% Fall in Bookings For New Durable Goods

  • Author: Tuesday Georges
  • Date: 06/01/90

WASHINGTON -- Factory orders fell sharply in April after two months of growth, the Commerce Department reported, a new sign of weakness in U.S. manufacturing. Pushed down by a 4.3% plunge in durable-goods bookings, orders for manufactured goods fell 2.3% in April, after increasing 4% in March and 1.8% in February.

The drop in durable-goods orders centered in transportation equipment, especially motor vehicle and aircraft. Transportation-equipment orders fell 14.7% in April, to $31.68 billion, following a 24.1% surge in March.

Economists said the factory-orders report is just another indication that the manufacturing sector is fairly sluggish, specifically in the automobile industry.

"What this report reflects is a continuing softness in the manufacturing sector, with a concentrated weakness in the auto industry," said Roger E. Brinner, chief economist at DRI/McGraw-Hill.

Agreeing with Mr. Brinner, Priscilla Trumbull, industry analyst at WEFA Group, said the "auto and aircraft sectors are showing some bumpy patterns . . . which mean that manufacturing is not really sliding, but it is not growing either."

While many economists agreed that the April figures reflect a problem in the automobile industry, they said the drop in aircraft bookings simply means orders were slow in that particular month. The aircraft industry has been one of the few strong spots in U.S. manufacturing.

"Aircraft orders will definitely bounce back . . . within the next month," said Mr. Brinner. "The only real problem at this point is a problem in the automobile industry, and this is nothing new."

The April report said overall factory orders totaled $235.60 billion.

While most analysts focused on the steep decline in durable-goods orders -- which was slightly more than an earlier government estimate of 4.1%-nondurable-goods orders also failed to improve. They were about unchanged in April at $112.2 billion.

Durable goods are products such as computers, cars and other big-ticket items expected to last at least three years.

Economists said they wouldn't be surprised if the sluggishness in manufacturing led to a further increase in unemployment.

"Unemployment will be somewhat shaky. . . . We definitely will see some continued layoffs, especially in the defense industry. But I don't think we will see widespread unemployment," said Ms. Trumbull.

Ms. Trumbull said she expects a small increase in factory orders throughout the rest of the year.

Manufacturers' inventories edged up 0.1% to $372.28 billion in April, following a 0.4% drop in March and a 0.3% decline in February.

April shipments decreased 1.2% to $234.35 billion, following increases of 1.2% in March and 3.4% in February. Unfilled orders inched up 0.2% to $517.68 billion, following a 0.7% rise in March and a 0.5% decline in February.

All the figures are adjusted for seasonal variations.
