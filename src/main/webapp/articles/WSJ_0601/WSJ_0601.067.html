Thursday's Markets: Stocks Mixed; Bonds, Dollar Post Advances

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/01/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices turned in a mixed performance, as investors took profits in blue-chip issues that have led the recent rally and then bid for stocks that are catching up. Bond prices, meanwhile, extended this week's string of gains by drifting higher in light trading, and the dollar rose.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average eased 1.90 points to close at 2876.66 in active trading, but the broader Standard & Poor's 500-stock index nudged ahead to a third consecutive record close, up 0.37 at 361.23.

Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices rose more than an eighth of a point, or $1.25 for each $1,000 face amount. The dollar gained against most other major currencies on concern over instability in the Mideast and questions about how a reunified Germany would be aligned politically.

Economic indicators are strengthening the case for slow but stable growth in the U.S. economy and low inflation, both of which tend to make fixed-rate investments such as bonds attractive. But slow growth -- rather than a recession -- is also relatively good for stocks. That's why investors haven't been eager to jump off the blue-chip bandwagon; the Dow Jones industrials have posted new highs in eight of the past 13 sessions.

"I like the market," said Philip Roth, chief technical analyst at Dean Witter Reynolds, "but I don't rule out a correction for a few days." Over the summer, however, he expects stocks to move modestly higher. One reason is a decline in commodity prices, a further sign that inflation need not be feared. As a result, interest rates should fall, meaning bond yields will drop, which in turn should lure more investors into stocks.

But David Bostian, chief investment strategist at Jesup & Lamont, said stock market investors may discover that growth is slowing faster than they thought. "Investors' perceptions have become divorced from the economy," he warned.

Bond prices fell in early trading in reaction to regional surveys of purchasing managers that suggested the manufacturing business is improving. But then dealers began buying back bonds to replace those they had sold, in effect betting that prices would fall-which drove prices higher.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell. Trading was moderately active, with 165.7 million shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange. Advancing issues outnumbered decliners, 801-682.

Bond prices climbed. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 8.57%.

The dollar gained slightly. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6965 marks and 152.10 yen, compared with 1.6830 marks and 151.45 yen late Wednesday.
