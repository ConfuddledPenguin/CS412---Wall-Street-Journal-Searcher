Developing Asian-Pacific Stock Markets Show Fast Growth, Strong Performance

  • Author: Russel G. Todd
  • Date: 06/01/90

HONG KONG -- Developing stock markets in the Asian-Pacific region grew faster and performed better than established markets over the past five years, according to the International Finance Corp.'s latest survey of emerging equity markets. The report also shows that manufacturing and financial stocks dominate trading in the region's emerging markets, and that some of these markets are among the most active and volatile in the world.

The new edition of the IFC's annual emerging-market fact book shows that the region's developing exchanges have turned in impressive results over the period. The IFC Asia index, which tracks stocks in Malaysia, South Korea, Taiwan and Thailand, shot up 558.4% from the end of 1984 to the end of 1989.

By comparison, Standard & Poor's index of 500 U.S. stocks rose 251.9% in the period. The Europe, Australia and Far East index, which is calculated by Morgan Stanley Capital International Perspective and covers developed markets outside the U.S., rose 474.1%. (All the indexes are calculated in terms of dollars and are expressed in terms of total return, including stock-price rises and dividend payments.)

The IFC, the finance arm of the World Bank, maintains one of the largest data bases on emerging stock markets. The organization also helps develop and underwrite international investment in emerging markets; in Asia, it has been instrumental in launching numerous single-country mutual funds, such as the Korea Fund, listed on the New York Stock Exchange.

The advance of Asian-Pacific emerging markets during the past half-decade has been even more impressive in terms of the growth in capitalization. The combined capitalization of seven developing markets -- India, Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, South Korea, Taiwan and Thailand -- jumped 850%, to $485.24 billion. The capitalization of the region's developed markets -- Japan, Hong Kong, Singapore, Australia and New Zealand -- rose 338%, to $4.66 trillion.

The giant Tokyo market accounted for much of the developed markets' increase. Its capitalization jumped 363%, to $4.4 trillion, though losses in 1990 have trimmed the gain. The value of shares on the Singapore market rose 225%, to $35.9 billion. Market capitalization in Australia and Hong Kong each jumped about 125%, to $136.6 billion and $77.5 billion, respectively. New Zealand's market value grew the least, rising 53.9%, to $13.5 billion.

Among the developing markets, Taiwan's capitalization skyrocketed 2,172%, to $237 billion. The much smaller Indonesian market jumped nearly as much, rising 2,049%, to $2.51 billion; most of the increase came in 1989 as the Jakarta market was opened to more foreign investment.

The capitalization of three other emerging markets each rose more than 1,200% during the five years: South Korea, to $140.95 billion; the Philippines, to $11.97 billion; and Thailand, to $25.65 billion. Capitalization of the Malaysian market rose 145.5%, to $39.84 billion, while that of India rose 90%, to $27.32 billion.

Emerging markets in the region also outpaced developed markets in terms of new listings. Combined listings on the five developed markets grew 12.9% during the five-year period, to 4,016 issues. New Zealand's market was the only one that lost issues over the five-year period; in 1985, 317 issues were listed, but that figure fell 23.7%, to 242 issues, at the end of 1989.

Among the seven developing equity markets, new listings jumped 40.4%, bringing the total to 7,438 issues. The number is distorted by India's estimated 6,000 listed issues at the end of 1989; foreigners currently are barred from investing in Indian shares.

Three markets that have attracted growing foreign investment -- Indonesia, Malaysia and Thailand -- had mixed results in their efforts to bring new issues to market. In the five-year period, the number of issues on the Bangkok exchange rose 75%, to 175; in neighboring Malaysia, listed issues climbed only 13.1%, to 251 companies. Listings on Indonesia's market jumped 154% in 1989 alone. The market had only 24 listed issues from 1985 through 1988, but a further 37 companies were listed last year as the government encouraged the market to expand.

The number of listed companies in South Korea grew 83%, to 626, while Taiwan's listings rose 42.5%, to 181. In the Philippines, the number of listed shares rose just 4.3%, to 144.

According to the IFC report, financial and manufacturing shares dominate the region's emerging exchanges. At the end of 1989, a broad sector that includes banks, real-estate and insurance shares accounted for 44.8% of combined capitalization; bank shares alone accounted for 27.4%.
