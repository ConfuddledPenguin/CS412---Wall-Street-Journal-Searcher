Meyer Will Leave Rockefeller Fund To Oversee Harvard's Endowment

  • Author: James A. White
  • Date: 06/01/90

NEW YORK -- Jack R. Meyer, the Rockefeller Foundation treasurer picked to oversee Harvard University's $5 billion endowment, is careful not to confuse his investing skills with the bull stock market. When Mr. Meyer joined the Rockefeller Foundation in 1983, a decade of inflation had cut the real value of the foundation's assets in half. In the following seven years, the foundation's assets more than doubled to $2.1 billion, even while the foundation gave away $500 million in grants.

Mr. Meyer attributes that record to "a lot of good luck and a little skill" during history's greatest bull market run. But Mr. Meyer also did far better than other institutional investors during the run-up: the foundation earned an average of 19.5% a year on its assets in the five years ended Dec. 31, compared with 16% a year for the average institutional fund during that period.

As expected, the 45-year-old Mr. Meyer was named to become president of Harvard Management Co., the investment arm that manages Harvard's endowment, the biggest of any private university in the U.S.

He will assume the post Sept. 1, succeeding Walter Cabot, who has been Harvard Management's president since its formation in 1974. Mr. Cabot will remain with Harvard Management "on a part-time advisory kind of role in the private placement area," according to Harvard University Treasurer D. Ronald Daniel.

Mr. Daniel rejected speculation that Mr. Cabot, who missed several months of work in 1989 because of a heart attack, was being forced from his post following subpar investment returns last year, when the Harvard endowment missed much of the stock market's 31.7% increase. "If he had not had that heart attack, he would still be there" as president, Mr. Daniel said of Mr. Cabot.

Mr. Meyer has a reputation as an aggressive money manager who led the Rockefeller Foundation into areas such as short selling and index arbitrage. He said he is leaving the foundation for the professional opportunity offered by Harvard Management, which has more than double the assets and 15 times the investment staff of the Rockefeller Foundation.

"Harvard Management is not broken, so it isn't my job to fix it," said Mr. Meyer. "It's clear they didn't have a good 1989, but they are solidly in the first quartile {of returns for institutional investors} over the last five years," he said.

Harvard Management was a pioneer among big institutions in nontraditional investments, spending more than $1 billion, or 20% of the overall fund, on such areas as venture capital, oil wells and leveraged buy-outs. Its $70 million commitment to the buy-out fund of Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., is the largest by any endowment.

These private investments, whose true value can be difficult to determine, have been controversial. "I'm pretty uneasy about having 20% of the endowment in illiquid investments," said money manager John Train, a Harvard alumnus whose book, "The New Money Masters," includes a chapter on Harvard Management.

Mr. Daniel said Harvard Management looked at hundreds of candidates for its presidency before a list of four finalists was prepared. The job is considered one of the most prestigious in the money-management industry. Among those turning down a request to apply was Peter Lynch, the famed portfolio manager who is retiring from Boston-based Fidelity Investments.

Mr. Meyer, a graduate of Denison University and Harvard Business School, was New York City deputy comptroller before joining the Rockefeller Foundation. His duties included overseeing $20 billion of city pension funds.

Previously, he worked at the Wall Street firms of Lionel D. Edie & Co. and Brown Brothers Harriman & Co.
