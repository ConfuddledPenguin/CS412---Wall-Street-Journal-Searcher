SEC Chairman Sounds Warning On Stock Futures

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 06/01/90

WASHINGTON -- Securities and Exchange Commission Chairman Richard Breeden said that the current debate over stock-index futures is similar to the dispute over the savings and loan crisis and that "people have been warned." Mr. Breeden, who is battling with the Commodity Futures Trading Commission for regulatory control over the commodity-exchange products, also contended that the U.S. regulatory structure is "not a strongly successful model that the whole world tries to emulate. We are losing market share {in stock-index futures} at a very rapid rate." He added: "Volume is down in the last three years {in index futures}. It's not this rapidly growing product."

The arguments, in an interview, were some of the strongest to date by Mr. Breeden, who has stepped up his rhetoric in an effort to win support for a Treasury Department proposal to shift jurisdiction of index futures.

Citing the thrift crisis, Mr. Breeden said, "People have said, `Why didn't somebody warn us?' Well, here you have a bunch of people warning." Specifically, he argued that the leverage created by low margins in the futures industry causes excess volatility. Noting that margins on index futures stood at 2.2% before the stock market's plunge last Oct. 13, when the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 6.9%, he said, "Even thrifts don't have 97.8% leverage."

He said that Congress should move to create federal oversight of futures margins, which currently are set by the commodity exchanges and can be changed by the CFTC only in specific emergencies. Those exchanges are dependent on volume encouraged by lower margins, Mr. Breeden said. "We have thought that is something very prudent to try to correct."

Moreover, he argued that the securities markets have been more innovative in the past decade than the commodities markets, largely aided by regulators. He cited off-board trading and upstairs trading in the securities markets, while commodities markets can only trade by the open outcry method.

Those who argue that innovation has been only in futures are offering "a complete mischaracterization," Mr. Breeden said.
