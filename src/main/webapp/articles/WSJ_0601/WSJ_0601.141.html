Greyhound Union Is Seeking Advice On a Possible Bid

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 06/01/90

DALLAS -- With Greyhound Lines Inc. scrambling to avoid bankruptcy, its striking employees have enlisted a prominent investment adviser to organize a union bid to buy the troubled bus company. Brian M. Freeman, who recently guided a proposed union buy-out of UAL Corp., sent a letter Tuesday to Greyhound Chairman Fred Currey asking him to discuss a possible purchase of the strike-hobbled unit of GLI Holding Co. by Amalgamated Transit Union members.

Mr. Freeman declined to discuss the union proposal but in the letter to Mr. Currey, he called " 100% employee ownership" the only "feasible and practical" means of assuring the company's long-term viability although he indicated that new investment by a third party could also be an acceptable "variant." He indicated the union would proceed with a proposal regardless of the company's response.

Saying that the company isn't for sale, Greyhound officials said they don't plan to discuss the proposal with Mr. Freeman.

"We are surprised the group that has brought about the company's current financial crisis through an unprecedented wave of violence now proposes to profit from its own criminal activity by buying the company," said P. Anthony Lannie, Greyhound executive vice president.

The ATU, which represents about 9,400 Greyhound employees, went on strike against the bus company last March 2. Strike-related violence led to a $55.8 million loss in the first quarter, compared with a year-earlier $14.4 million loss.

Staggered by the losses, Greyhound has missed $5 million in lease payments due April 30 and a $9.8 million May 15 interest payment on its 13% senior notes due in 1995. The company has said it may have to file for bankruptcy protection if it cannot complete a financial restructuring.

A union buy-out bid would be complicated without the company's cooperation. A majority of the stock, which is not publicly traded, is owned or controlled by an investor group led by Mr. Currey.

Holders of the company's publicly traded notes are said to be grumbling over a recent tender offer from the company to redeem about $191 million in face amount of debt for about $86 million in cash. Although the tender offer is the lynchpin of its financial restructuring, Greyhound says it hasn't found a source to finance it.

The union, which has held informal discussions with at least one bondholder, could make a counter offer but some bondholders questioned whether the ATU could find financing if the company cannot. "Nobody on the street thinks they'll get the money," said Robin Rodriguez, chairman of Anglo American Investors Services Corp., a holder of Greyhound bonds.

Mr. Rodriguez said he has hired an attorney and begun organizing other bondholders with an eye toward forcing Greyhound into Chapter 11 if the company does not improve its tender offer.
