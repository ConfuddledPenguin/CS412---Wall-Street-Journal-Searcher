Technology & Health: Court Dismisses Suit Against Pfizer Over Heart Valve

  • Author: Michael Waldholz
  • Date: 06/01/90

NEW YORK -- A California court dismissed a lawsuit that would have forced Pfizer Inc. to notify about 60,000 people that they had received a potentially defective artificial heart valve. In March, Public Citizen, a consumer group associated with Ralph Nader, filed a lawsuit in state court in Los Angeles asking that Pfizer's Shiley subsidiary be required to contact valve recipients, advise them of the valves' history of defects, and pay for medical consultation and psychological counseling. Pfizer has argued that the recipients should be told about the valve by their doctors.

The ruling, by Los Angeles superior court Judge Ronald M. Sohigian, is the latest in a series of lawsuits related to the Shiley-Bjork convexo-concave heart valve that have cast a shadow over Pfizer, raising questions about the size of its financial liabilities stemming from the valve.

Pfizer's announcement of the court's decision buoyed the company's shares, which closed at $65.875 a share, up $2.25, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday.

Pfizer said the court ruled that the U.S. Food and Drug Administration regulations pre-empt California state law regarding medical devices. Although the FDA has ruled that patients should be advised about the valve's potential problems by their doctors, it is holding hearings to consider whether to change its position.

Pfizer has told the FDA that 394 of 85,000 heart valves it sold world-wide between 1978 and 1986 are known to have failed because of the fracture of a particular part. The flaw has resulted in 252 reported deaths. The two models in question are Shiley's 60-degree and 70-degree convexoconcave Bjork model heart valves. The FDA banned the use of one model in 1986; the company also stopped selling the other model.

In its suit, Washington-based Public Citizen charged that despite the problem, neither Shiley nor Pfizer has directly alerted recipients of the valves or their personal doctors. The consumer group said people ought to be advised of the symptoms of a failing valve in order to have it treated quickly.

Brian Wolfman, a Public Citizen lawyer, said the court's ruling was "wrong," noting that California law can require direct patient notification despite the FDA position.

He said the group will appeal the court's decision.

Steven Kany, a Pfizer lawyer, said the company believes that most, perhaps all, valve recipients already know about the valve's problems from their doctors, or from news stories and advertisements by product-liability lawyers. Moreover, "Public Citizen knows that we don't have the names of about half the people who received the valves," Mr. Kany said, "That is known by the doctors who implanted the valves or treat recipients."

The issue of notification may become more important pending the outcome of another lawsuit by a woman who is seeking damages from Pfizer due to anxiety about the valve's performance. In early May another California court ruled that the recipient can sue Shiley even though her valve hasn't failed.
