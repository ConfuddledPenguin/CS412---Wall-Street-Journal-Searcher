Chrysler Gives New Duties to Miller, Lutz, Latest Top Contenders to Succeed Iacocca

  • Author: Melinda Grenier Guiles
  • Date: 06/01/90

HIGHLAND PARK, Mich. -- Chrysler Corp. has expanded the duties of two men who apparently become the leading candidates to succeed Chairman Lee A. Iacocca, although the timing of the 65-year-old chief executive officer's retirement remains as ambiguous as ever. The moves, which constitute Chrysler's fifth management restructuring since 1985, came in the wake of Vice Chairman Gerald Greenwald's abrupt resignation to lead a proposed employee buy-out of United Airlines. The action divided Mr. Greenwald's duties between Robert S. Miller Jr. and Robert A. Lutz.

Mr. Miller, 48 years old, becomes vice chairman, a title he previously held from 1985 to 1988. In addition to supervising all of Chrysler's financial activities and the office of the general counsel and secretary, he now adds responsibility for Chrysler Financial Corp., Chrysler Technologies and international operations. He will report to Mr. Iacocca; he previously reported to Mr. Greenwald.

Mr. Lutz, 58 and president of Chrysler's automotive operations, assumes responsibility for quality and productivity, along with Acustar, Chrysler's parts-making subsidiary. He also will report to Mr. Iacocca. He had reported to the Office of the Chairman, which consisted of Mr. Iacocca and Mr. Greenwald but now has been dissolved.

Succeeding Mr. Miller as chief financial officer and executive vice president of finance is Jerome B. York, 51 and previously vice president and controller. Mr. Greenwald's departure didn't generate much excitement on Wall Street, even though he's the third top Chrysler official in as many weeks to announce his resignation. Mr. Greenwald also was considered Mr. Iacocca's most likely successor. In moderate composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Chrysler stock fell 25 cents to close at $15.375 a share.

"The loss {of Mr. Greenwald} isn't changing anything," said Philip K. Fricke, an auto analyst with Prudential-Bache Research. "There's no sign his departure signals a change in course or anything different in product development."

Analysts also noted that Chrysler's shares already have been deeply discounted for bad news. "It would take an earthquake in Highland Park for the stock to get much lower," said Scott Merlis, an auto analyst with Morgan Stanley & Co.

Mr. Greenwald said the United Airlines unions first approached him about six weeks ago, but he didn't seriously consider their offer until they assured him that the company they will own if their buy-out succeeds will be "run in a normal way" with a majority of "independent directors."

He made his final decision early Wednesday morning, "after spending a day and a half privately" with Mr. Iacocca in the chairman's Tuscany, Italy, villa. The two men then flew back together on a Chrysler corporate jet.

Mr. Greenwald held news conferences in Detroit and New York yesterday, but he didn't disclose much about what he and Mr. Iacocca discussed. He did say Mr. Iacocca asked him if "there was uncertainty in my mind" about Mr. Iacocca's plans to retire, and was such uncertainty "influencing my decision, and I said absolutely not and we left it at that."

In response to persistent questioning from reporters about whether Mr. Iacocca will retire when his contract with Chrysler expires in October 1991, Mr. Greenwald said: "Frankly, I really didn't want to get in that close on that subject with Lee."

Mr. Greenwald said there's "no operating assumption" among Chrysler's top executives about when Mr. Iacocca will actually leave, but he insisted this hasn't created any problems. "Nobody inside that team is trying to outguess who's going to go where, when and how. . . . Maybe this is a little hard for you to believe, but there isn't that kind of speculation going on inside our company."

Mr. Greenwald said he sees himself pursuing opportunity rather than fleeing frustration. "There's been some speculation that I didn't want to wait for the day when I'd {become} CEO at Chrysler," Mr. Greenwald said. "That really didn't enter into it at all. . . . I've always felt if I performed well that the time would come when I would get the chance to run Chrysler." However, the prospect of leading the world's largest employee-owned company "was just too much a challenge, or a temptation, to say no to."

People close to Mr. Greenwald, though, say he was growing impatient because Mr. Iacocca hasn't ever made it clear, despite his contract, when he'll retire.

When Mr. Greenwald returned to Chrysler headquarters late Wednesday afternoon, the buildings were abuzz with rumor. Officially, Chrysler's spokesmen had been saying since last Friday that Mr. Greenwald was visiting a sister in California, and that there was "nothing to" rumors of his departure.

Mr. Greenwald apologized for the deception yesterday, but said it was unavoidable. "It wasn't until 10 days ago that I took this really seriously," he said.
