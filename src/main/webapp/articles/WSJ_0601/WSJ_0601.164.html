Credit Markets: Bond Prices Post Modest Gains After Weak Start Spurred by Rise in Regional Purchasing Indexes

  • Author: Andrew Bary and Susan Scherreik Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/01/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices ended modestly higher in quiet trading as investors braced for today's release of the May employment report. "It's the calm before the potential storm of the employment report," said Robert T. Falconer, a senior vice president at Aubrey G. Lanston & Co. "Everyone is gun-shy."

Surprises in the employment data, considered the most important of the government's monthly statistics on the economy, often roil the bond market.

That was certainly the case when the much weaker-then-expected April jobs report issued on May 4 ignited a bond market rally that lifted the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond 1 1/2 points, or $15 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

What little activity there was yesterday generally involved position-squaring, traders and analysts said.

"There's been a last-minute adjustment of positions in anticipation of the employment report," said Charles Lieberman, a managing director at Manufacturers Hanover Securities Corp.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose more than 1/8 point. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, eased to 8.57%.

Bonds got off to a weak start after increases in the indexes of regional surveys of purchasing managers in Chicago and Detroit raised speculation that the national survey, also due out today, would show more strength in the manufacturing sector of the economy than many economists have forecast.

Analysts said market participants were especially concerned about the report by the Purchasing Management Association of Chicago. Its index of business conditions in May rose to an unadjusted 56%, compared with 54.2% in April. On a seasonally adjusted basis, the index was 55.3%, compared with 50.2% in April.

"People thought that the surprisingly strong Chicago purchasing management survey might transfer to the national figure," said Christopher Rupkey, a financial economist at Mitsubishi Bank.

Meanwhile, the Detroit Purchasing Management Association index rose to 48.8% in May from 45.6% in April.

The national purchasing agents' survey is closely monitored by the bond market because, like the jobs report, it provides one of the first readings of how the economy performed in a given month.

After the initial flurry of selling on the regional reports, "there was no follow-through to the downside," a dealer of government securities said. As a result, players who have established short positions, in a bet that prices would decline further, then covered those shorts, he said.

A consensus of economists polled by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report expects an acceleration of employment growth in May, with nonfarm payrolls seen having increased 253,000, with a gain of 135,000 seen if census workers hired in the month are excluded.

The May civilian unemployment rate is expected to have been unchanged at 5.4%.

In April, nonfarm payrolls grew a paltry 64,000, with payrolls decreasing 14,000 if census workers hired that month are excluded.

Meanwhile, the index of the National Association of Purchasing Management is expected to have declined to 49.5% in May, according to the average of estimates of economists surveyed by Capital Markets Report. A reading above 50% indicates an expanding industrial sector, and a reading below 50% suggests the sector is contracting.

Corporate Issues

Consolidated Rail Corp., the Philadelphia-based freight railroad, drew a sell-out crowd when it offered $250 million of notes.

The sale of the 10-year notes, which were priced to yield 9.785%, marked the first public debt offering by Conrail since its recapitalization earlier this year.

"We were very pleased to get it done at these spreads without any event-risk protection," said treasurer John McKelvey.

Strong investor demand prompted underwriters led by Morgan Stanley & Co. to boost the offering from $200 million.

About 50 accounts participated in the deal, and investment bankers said the notes were snapped up primarily by institutional buyers such as pension funds and mutual funds.

The noncallable securities were priced at a spread of 1.15 percentage points above the Treasury's 10-year note. Proceeds from the offering will be used mainly to refinance commercial paper borrowings incurred in connection with the company's $1.1 billion self-tender offer.

Mr. McKelvey said Conrail is likely to tap the market with at least $500 million more in new debt later this year.

In trading, most actively traded investment-grade corporate bonds ended unchanged to slightly stronger.

In the junk bond market, high-yield bonds of R.H. Macy & Co. weakened significantly as investor skittishness about the giant retailer mounted.

Macy's 14 1/2% senior subordinated debentures due in 1998 were quoted at a bid price of 65, down three points from Wednesday, while the 14 1/2% subordinated debentures maturing in 2001 ended at a bid price of 54, after trading as high as 58 Wednesday.

In the broader high-yield market, junk bonds finished about 1/4 point higher on light volume.

Junk bonds of Bally Manufacturing Corp. continued to strengthen after surging five points Wednesday. Bally's 13 5/8% subordinated debentures, due in 2001, added three points to close at 61.

Bally Manufacturing bonds began climbing Wednesday after the company announced an exchange offer under which bondholders would receive 57 shares of Bally common stock for each $1,000 principal amount of debentures tendered.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Slightly firm prices were being quoted for most mortgage securities.

The focus of the Government National Mortgage Association market was a Public Securities Association decision yesterday afternoon to restrict trading in high-coupon Ginnie Maes with rates of 11% and higher issued from Jan. 1, 1989, to July 1, 1990, effective in the next session.

This move is intended to stem the issuance of so-called live-free mortgages, which have soured the entire $50 billion market for high-coupon mortgages.

Certain mortgage originators have been issuing mortgages to homeowners at above-market rates for refinancing purposes, agreeing in advance to refinance the mortgages at lower rates.

For the investor, the unusually quick prepayment rates often resulted in losses on the securities they were willing to pay premiums for in anticipation of prepayments later on.

Under the reported agreement, the bond trade group has deemed these high-coupon Ginnie Maes ineligible for delivery for "to-be-announced" trades, which are standard mortgage securities trades in which the buyer doesn't know the exact pool he will receive at settlement.

Meanwhile, Ginnie Mae President Arthur Hill sent a letter to mortgage securities market players stating that high-coupon loans originated with these pre-arranged refinancing provisions aren't eligible for inclusion in Ginnie Mae mortgage securities pools effective immediately.

Ginnie Mae 9 1/2% securities for June delivery were up 3/32 at 98 19/32.

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at 101 24/32, up from 101 18/32 on Wednesday. The latest 10-year notes ended at 101 25/32 to yield 8.59%, compared with 101 22/32 to yield 8.60%.

Short-term rates were little changed to modestly higher. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills was little changed at 7.76% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8%. The rate on six-month notes rose to 7.74% to yield 8.15%.

Municipals

Advancing to mostly unchanged levels after slim declines on new economic data, municipal bonds were flat to up 1/8 point in thin late trading.

New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was unchanged at 98 bid to yield 7.36%.

Some municipal issues had traded down about 1/8 point after the release of the Chicago Purchasing Management Association's index for May, traders said.

In addition, news that President Bush and Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev may agree on the major elements of a strategic arms reduction treaty today, buoyed the market, some traders said.

In slim new issue dealings, a $50.9 million issue of New Jersey certificates of participation was won by a Dillon, Read & Co. group. The bonds were priced to yield from 5.90% in 1990 to 6.65% in 1997.

No large municipal issues are expected to be priced today.

Foreign Bonds

Japanese government bonds ended stronger on news that Japanese wholesale prices declined.

The Japanese government said wholesale prices fell 0.4% in the second 10 days of May from the first 10 days. Traders said most buyers Thursday were dealers.

The price of Japan's benchmark 10-year government security rose to 88.14 points to yield 6.94%. (The yield is calculated in local terms, but it would be 6.56% if calculated by the semiannual compound measure used in the U.S.)

In Britain, government bonds ended moderately lower as speculation faded again about an imminent entry by Britain into the European Monetary System's exchange rate mechanism.

Prices had surged Wednesday on renewed speculation that British authorities were about to make an announcement concerning the mechanism, which is the European Community's currency stabilization arrangement. But in comments made on Wednesday, Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major didn't say anything regarding the country's entry into the arrangement.

In Frankfurt, West German Bundesbank President Karl Otto Poehl also discouraged expectations. He said "Britain can't and shouldn't participate in the EMS" because of its high inflation and balance-of-payment problems.

Britain's 11 3/4% bond due 2003/2007 fell 9/32 to 99 27/32 to yield 11.76%.
