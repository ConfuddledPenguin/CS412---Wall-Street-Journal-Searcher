Marketing & Media: U.S. Booksellers, Heading to Convention, See Strong Sales and More Best Sellers

  • Author: Meg Cox
  • Date: 06/01/90

Publishers and bookstore owners heading to the annual convention of the American Booksellers Association, which starts today in Las Vegas, predict strong sales this fall and plentiful best sellers after a year that was disappointing for many. "Our sales were soft for most of last year, but I think you're going to see a pretty upbeat community of booksellers and publishers," says Ronald Jaffe, senior marketing director for the Waldenbooks unit of K mart Corp. "It's like night and day. Our sales are terrific right now, and from what we've seen about new books coming out this fall, we're very excited."

"I think the doom and gloom has receded," says Brigitte Weeks, editor in chief of Time Warner Inc.'s Book-of-theMonth Club. "People are feeling more secure, and there are going to be some really big books this fall, including new novels by Jean Auel and Colleen McCullough. There are some really classy, important books coming out, too, from authors like John Updike and Gabriel Garcia Marquez."

At this annual gathering, hundreds of publishers of all sizes tout their forthcoming books to thousands of bookstore owners, hoping to boost sales for the fall season, the biggest in publishing. "We use this as a forum, to find out what kind of season to look forward to," says Bernard Rath, executive director of the association. Although many New York publishers complained that their high-brow industry is meeting in a low-brow town, attendance is expected to be about 25,000, says Mr. Rath, slightly ahead of last year's convention in Washington, D.C.

The association recently said bookstore sales jumped 63% between 1982 and 1987, and sales reportedly rose about 11% in 1989. However, a recent report by the American Association of Publishers showed only a 4.1% increase in sales of adult hardcover books in the first quarter of 1990. Various surveys predict that growth will be better than that in the next few years, but not as strong as during the boom years of the mid-1980s.

Despite a consensus among publishing executives that this fall will bring more blockbusters than last year's disappointing lineup, there are still some jitters in the industry after tumultuous times. During the past six months, several big publishers took substantial write-downs against earnings, several small publishers were put up for sale and an earlier wave of big mergers was followed by consolidation and staff cuts at many houses. At the same time, growth of the big bookstore chains was slowing down.

"I'd say the mood is optimistic but far from euphoria," says David Godine, a small, independent publisher based in Boston. "You can't look around and say this industry is in terrific shape."

"We're fairly bullish as we head into the summer, but I'm cautious," says Jack Hoeft, president of Bantam Doubleday Dell Publishing Group, a unit of Bertelsmann AG. The company's Doubleday unit had an especially difficult year in 1989 -- racking up losses, switching top management and cutting back the list of titles sharply. "The cautious part is because I'm getting some reports from customers that the bookselling market in general is still pretty soft," says Mr. Hoeft.

"It has been a sobering time, but there is an optimism again," says Joni Evans, publisher of Random House, which is owned by the Newhouse family's Advance Publications Inc. "We all feel a bit like Gorbachev: Things are better, but at any moment, they could take a wrong turn." Ms. Evans said that, as part of a new conservatism in publishing, "the bubble has definitely burst on advances."

Allan Lang, an agent and consultant, adds, "Sales and marketing and editorial departments are being trimmed all over the industry. Publishers aren't publishing fewer titles, but they are publishing smaller initial print runs and paying for fewer author tours."

Nevertheless, booksellers and publishers are ready to celebrate in Las Vegas, with dozens of author lunches and lavish cocktail parties at local casinos and mansions. "The mood will definitely be good: This is always a happy convention," says Mr. Lang. "Publishers are eternal optimists, or they couldn't remain sane in this business."
