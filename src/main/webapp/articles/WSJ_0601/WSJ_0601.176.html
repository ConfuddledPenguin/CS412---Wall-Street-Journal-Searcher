First Federal Bancorp Says Regulators Curb Its Lending Activities

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/01/90

PONTIAC, Mich. -- First Federal Bancorp Inc. said the federal Office of Thrift Supervision has placed strict limits on the thrift's lending operations because of its inability to meet new capital requirements. First Federal said a group of investors whom it identified only as current shareholders has offered to infuse $40 million in cash into the company. In return, it said, the investors want newly issued First Federal shares. The transaction is designed to enable First Federal's operating units to meet the federal capital requirements, the thrift said.

However, the transaction would "significantly dilute" current holders' stakes, the company said. First Federal has 3.5 million shares outstanding. The company's stock closed at $2.25 a share, up 12.5 cents, in American Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday.

First Federal said it will continue to explore ways to improve its capital position, including the possibilities offered by the cash infusion.

The restrictions imposed by regulators bar First Federal from increasing its assets and forbid it to sell or pledge assets or to make any new loans or investments without prior U.S. approval. But First Federal may use assets to maintain adequate liquidity for current obligations, and it may make certain consumer and home loans.

The restrictions apply to First Federal's chief operating subsidiaries, First Federal Savings Bank & Trust, based in Pontiac, and Citizens Federal Savings Bank, Jacksonville, Fla.

Federal authorities imposed the restrictions Wednesday after First Federal failed to meet new U.S. guidelines for thrifts' ratios of capital to assets.

In the quarter ended March 31, First Federal had a loss of $15.2 million, or $4.20 a share.
