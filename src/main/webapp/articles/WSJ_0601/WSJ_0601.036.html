Letters to the Editor: Tracking Leads to Happier Trails

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/01/90

What an example of killing the messenger. Your April 23 article "`Tracking' of Minority Pupils Takes Toll" shines the spotlight on the outrageous differences in percentages of white students vs. minority students in high-achievement classes in schools throughout the country. The article deduces (I assume a reflection of the Rand Study deduction) that, because there are so many more minority students in low-achievement groups, tracking should be abolished because it is racist policy. But the tracking is only the barometer, not the cause, of the discrepancies among students' ability to learn.

You point with horror to the fact that in many schools tracking of students begins as early as kindergarten. As a parent involved in an integrated public-school system for eight years, I feel qualified to report that the differences in a child's ability to learn are every bit as pronounced in kindergarten as they are in high school.

By eliminating tracking or by trying to make tracking the villain of a painful scenario, what do we accomplish? Education designed to serve the lowest common denominator?

Instead, let us address all of our energies and resources toward the soul of the problem. Let us commit ourselves to early-childhood programs, mentoring programs, programs for teen-age parents and dropouts, and every other creative approach available to combat the conditions that are keeping minority children prisoners of their own fates.

Nancy Butler Brown

Denver

---

I spent 30 years teaching students ages 11 to 15. In my opinion, the main reason for poorly educated students is the propensity of the educational bureaucracy for mixed-ability classes. Forcing students to take a course when they do not have the prerequisite knowledge is a cruel and insidious way of perpetuating segregation. Yet it happens daily in the name of desegregation.

Until the people who control our schools learn to use objective measures of achievement (not ability) and to group students accordingly, we will continue to have such problems as low self-esteem and a high dropout rate.

Claudine Miller

La Jolla, Calif.
