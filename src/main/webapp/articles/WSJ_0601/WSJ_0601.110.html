Politics & Policy: Bush, Gorbachev Fail to Settle Differences Over Germany on the First Day of Summit --- New Dispute Concerning Trade Treaty, Lithuania Threatens Grain Deal

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg and Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 06/01/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush and Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev failed to settle deep differences over Germany on the opening day of their summit meeting, and a new superpower dispute emerged that threatens to torpedo a lucrative U.S.-Soviet grain deal. As the summit opened yesterday, Soviet negotiators hinted they might not sign the five-year pact to buy American grain, apparently in an effort to get President Bush to sign a new U.S.-Soviet trade treaty. Mr. Bush has said he is reluctant to sign the new trade treaty because of Moscow's continuing resistance to Lithuanian independence. Both agreements were negotiated earlier and await only the leaders' signatures.

Late yesterday, U.S. officials said the chances of signing either treaty were poor -- unless Messrs. Bush and Gorbachev themselves can break the logjam by this afternoon. The president said trade would be on the agenda today.

The grain and trade dispute was carried out behind the scenes of the summit and wasn't referred to by either leader or their spokesmen. The public focus of the summit's first day was a spirited debate the two leaders held on the more important matter of German unification and whether a united Germany should join the North Atlantic Treaty Organization.

Mr. Gorbachev caused a stir by suggesting the session had produced progress on the German issue, and the two presidents issued a statement saying they had assigned Secretary of State James Baker and Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze to discuss the matter further.

But American officials close to the session said neither side put forward any fresh schemes for resolving the German problem. They said Mr. Gorbachev repeated earlier suggestions that a united Germany could belong to both NATO and the Warsaw Pact and that full German sovereignty be deferred. Mr. Bush repeated U.S. assurances, already conveyed by Mr. Baker, that NATO will change into a more political grouping and that the West will support a cut in German armed forces in later negotiations.

In addition, the Soviets delivered a letter to the Bush administration repeating a proposal that the 35-nation Conference on Security and Cooperation in Europe be transformed into a full-blown security system that might replace NATO. The U.S. has repeatedly rejected that idea.

The U.S. officials said the decision to ask the two foreign ministers to discuss Germany further was a face-saving measure adopted to imply that more progress was possible. But the two presidents conceded Germany wouldn't be settled at this summit, and no Baker-Shevardnadze meetings specifically on Germany were scheduled as of last night. Mr. Bush plans to call West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl today to reassure him that no deals were cut.

On the trade issue, American officials said Mr. Shevardnadze and other top Gorbachev aides emphasized repeatedly in yesterday's meetings that a signed trade treaty was of special political importance to Moscow, even though it wouldn't result in an early expansion of trade.

The trade pact would be a valuable political symbol for Mr. Gorbachev, who needs to demonstrate at home that he can improve economic relations with the West. But failure to conclude the pending five-year grain deal would have a much greater immediate economic impact, casting doubt on whether U.S. farmers will be able to sell Moscow 10 million metric tons or more of grain annually, as planned.

Today, the two superpower leaders are expected to sign a raft of other treaties and agreements. The most important of these is a statement setting out the major elements of a strategic nuclear-arms control pact to be signed later this year and establishing goals for a further nuclear-arms treaty to be negotiated, starting immediately thereafter.

In addition, the two leaders will sign treaties to slash into their chemical-weapons arsenals and set up rules for verifying curbs on nuclear testing. Other officials will sign accords increasing air and maritime links and student exchanges.

Right from the summit's opening, Mr. Bush coupled progress in superpower relations to the U.S. plea that Moscow start negotiating an independence deal with Lithuania.

"The agreements we've reached cannot cause us to lose sight of some of the differences that remain," he told Mr. Gorbachev minutes after greeting the Soviet leader on the White House lawn. "Lithuania is one such issue. We believe that good-faith dialogue between the Soviet leaders and representatives of the Baltic peoples is the proper approach, and we hope to see that process go forward."

Mr. Gorbachev appeared bitter about Lithuania's decision to declare independence from the U.S.S.R. At a Soviet Embassy luncheon, he told a group of U.S. intellectuals and film stars: "Only in Lithuania, they can overnight decide a question about seceding from the Soviet Union, and after that they are at a loss what to do, and we are at a loss what to do."

The Soviets badly want better trade ties with the U.S. "It's quite unnatural that we, being almost the two biggest powers in the world with a huge economic and scientific and technical potential are having so little in trade and cooperation," said Arkady Maslennikov, President Gorbachev's spokesman.

The Soviets are arguing that their Lithuanian policy is an internal Soviet matter and therefore shouldn't interfere with U.S.-Soviet cooperation. Beyond that, they are asserting that, contrary to U.S. charges, the Kremlin isn't blocking Lithuania from seceding from the Soviet Union but only insisting that it do so through the slow process outlined in Soviet law.

Those arguments aren't enough to satisfy the U.S. Yet Mr. Bush has enough sympathy for Mr. Gorbachev's position that he has tried to prevent it from disrupting superpower cooperation on broader political and arms-control matters. But Mr. Gorbachev, by balking on the grain agreement, appears to be trying to demonstrate that a failure to cooperate on economic matters can harm the U.S. as well as the Soviet Union.

U.S. officials believe Moscow has chosen to use the grain deal as a weapon because the Soviet harvest has been good and U.S. farmers can be expected to press Washington to save the deal by signing the trade pact.

Even if the trade agreement were to be signed, it would be more of a political prize than an economic one for the beleaguered Mr. Gorbachev. Administration officials say President Bush could sign the pact but refrain from sending it to the Senate, which has warned that it won't be ratified until the Lithuania crisis is settled.

What's more, the treaty merely would establish the legal framework to permit increased trade. Unless it is accompanied by the granting to the Soviets of most-favored nation tariff treatment, it will have little impact.

However, Mr. Bush won't grant such treatment to Moscow until the Soviet Legislature gives final passage to a liberalized emigration bill. The bill was supposed to come to a vote yesterday in Moscow, but has been removed from the Soviet legislative calendar, and U.S. officials don't expect it to be passed during the summit, despite continuing rumors to the contrary.

The grain accord, which is to take effect Jan. 1, would increase the minimum amount of U.S. wheat, feed grains, soybeans and other commodities that Soviets would buy from the U.S. each year and would give the Soviets greater flexibility in their purchases.

Among other things, the new deal calls for an 11% rise in Soviet purchases to 10 million metric tons of grain a year from about nine million currently; of this amount, at least four million tons would have to be wheat and another four million tons would have to be corn and other feed grains. For the first time since the inaugural 1973 agreement, barley and sorghum grain would count as feed grains.

In addition, the new accord would allow the Soviets to buy as much as 14 million tons of grain a year without having to clear it with U.S. officials beforehand. The previous limit was 12 million tons.

The Soviet Union is the largest foreign market for U.S. grain.

Bush administration officials said that even if a grain agreement isn't signed at this summit, it could be completed later this summer if the impasse over Lithuania and the broader trade treaty is broken.
