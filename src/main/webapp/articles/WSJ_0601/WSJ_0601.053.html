Eurotunnel's Lenders Consent to Provide Additional Backing

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/01/90

LONDON -- Eurotunnel PLC may be seeing a glimmer of light at the end of the tunnel. The Anglo-French consortium said its four lead banks agreed on terms to lend an additional #2 billion ($3.39 billion) -- needed to cover still higher costs for the beleaguered cross-channel link. In exchange for increasing Eurotunnel's #5 billion credit lines to #7 billion, the banks insisted that the company arrange standby underwriting for a #530 million equity rights offering planned for this fall.

The new financing arrangements clear a hurdle toward completion of the tunnel project, which has been beset by revised cost estimates, reworked funding arrangements and disputes with its contractor consortium. Eurotunnel's protracted difficulties have made investors skittish. Some skepticism undoubtedly will linger until the latest financing proposal wins approval this summer from the full consortium of 208 banks backing the project.

"There have been a lot of doubts (about Eurotunnel) and there will continue to be doubts as cost figures are revised," said William Beazington, an analyst at Shearson Lehman Hutton Securities in London. But with extra funding apparently lined up, he added, "I might guardedly say that now is the time to start considering buying" Eurotunnel stock. In late trading on London's Stock Exchange yesterday, Eurotunnel shares were off five pence at 543 pence ($9.21).

Eurotunnel yesterday raised the project's estimated cost to #7.66 billion, citing higher inflation, higher interest rates and lower projected revenues. That's up from its April estimate of between #7.5 billion and #7.6 billion -- and way above Eurotunnel's projected total cost of about #4.9 billion set in 1987. Eurotunnel officials also said the project likely won't pay a dividend until 1999, four years later than initially expected.
