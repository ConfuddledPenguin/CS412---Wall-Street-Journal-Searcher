Commodities: Soybean Futures Prices Fall on Profit-Taking After Run-Up; Weather Forecast Is Also Cited

  • Author: Scott McMurray
  • Date: 05/01/90

Soybean futures prices declined on profit-taking, following a sharp run-up in price last week. "There was massive commodity-fund buying last week; we were due for a respite," said one analyst. Many commodity fund managers follow price trends, so that their purchases on the heels of rising prices fuel subsequent rallies.

Adding to the slump yesterday was a 30-to-90-day weather forecast, released after the close of trading Friday, that predicted moderate temperatures and normal moisture levels across many U.S. growing regions. Some speculators last week were buying soybeans on the belief that the forecast would call for hot, dry weather, possibly leading to a drought-induced crop shortage this year and higher crop prices, analysts said.

Soybean futures for July delivery slid 2.5 cents a bushel to close at $6.4850 a bushel on the Chicago Board of Trade.

Export demand and favorable weather forecasts buoyed corn futures prices slightly. July corn futures gained 0.25 cents a bushel to close at $2.83 a bushel on the Board of Trade.

The outlook for potential frost in certain winter-wheat growing regions in coming days sent wheat futures prices higher. Wheat futures for July delivery advanced 4.75 cents a bushel to close at $3.4575 a bushel on the Board of Trade.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

ENERGY: Crude oil contracts were generally higher, though trading was thin ahead of tomorrow's meeting of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries. The market opened strongly on anticipation that OPEC may agree to cut back its production in May and June. Prices were then knocked down by waves of selling, before they recovered late in the session. Crude oil for June delivery actually ended unchanged, at $18.54 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange, but outer-month contracts finished higher. July crude, for instance, wound up seven cents higher, at $19.27 a barrel. Heating oil rallied while spot prices for gasoline plunged, both mainly for technical reasons. The expiring May heating oil contract gained 2.79 cents a gallon to 56.53 cents. Gasoline for May delivery fell 0.95 cent to 60 cents a gallon.

COPPER: Copper for May delivery fell 2.7 cents a pound, to finish at $1.1690. Futures prices started off weak, recovered and then settled back again. The April contract had expired Thursday at a very high price because of tight supplies, and "after an expiration like that, there is a tendency for the market to pause and for prices to sag," said Robert Green, president of Connecticut Minerals & Metals Inc., a Darien, Conn., metals consulting and trading company. April's final price of $1.35 a pound last week was 15 cents a pound above the May contract's close, one of the biggest premiums traders could recall. John Gross, president of J.E. Gross & Associates Inc., a New City, N.Y., consultant to the copper industry, said that when a spot month expires at a premium, the next month usually rises to pick up the firmness or a good part of it. "That didn't happen this time because the April premium over May was so large," he said. Meanwhile in Peru, a miners' and smelter workers' strike continues at Southern Peru Copper Corp. despite recent indications that union and management may be coming closer together, another analyst said. "Management has made offers but none have been accepted," he said. There has been talk that a national miners' strike might be called next Monday if the strike at Southern Peru Copper -- a walkout that began March 12 -- isn't settled by then.

PRECIOUS METALS: Futures prices rebounded from early declines but ended mixed. June delivery gold was off $2.60 an ounce at $371.40. At the day's low in early trading it dipped to $367.70, just 70 cents above its life-of-contract low. July silver ended higher, with a gain of 1.5 cents an ounce at $5.0150, after dropping to a new life-of-contract low of $497.00 (the previous low was $497.70.) July platinum declined $1.50 an ounce at $474.10. At one point, July platinum traded as low as $467.00. The early declines in precious metals appeared to be influenced by active selling earlier in Europe, according to Ian MacDonald, vice president, Credit Suisse, New York. Many European traders will be observing May Day today. Platinum was weaker than gold mostly because platinum is a thinner market and tends to exaggerate price movements more than gold, Mr. MacDonald said. Gold's rebound was touched off by demand from Europe and the Middle East when the metal dropped to its low for the day, he said: "It was apparent that, as the June gold price approached the $366 level, new demand was attracted."
