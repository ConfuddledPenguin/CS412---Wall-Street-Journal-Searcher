Heard on the Street: Bank of New York, Bound by Merger Pact With Northeast Bancorp, Hurls Accusations

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 05/01/90

NEW YORK -- Bank of New York Co., acting like a prospective bridegroom with a serious case of wedding-bell blues, is bickering ever louder with its fiancee of the past seven years, Northeast Bancorp. Yesterday Northeast shares plunged 4 7/8 or 15% to close at 28 1/4 as Bank of New York accused the Connecticut company of breaking their merger pact by failing to maintain accurate records. Unless the problem is corrected within 60 days, Bank of New York said, it will terminate the deal.

Poppycock, responded Northeast, which said its books are in order and accused Bank of New York "of a transparent attempt to avoid its contractual obligations."

Up to now, some investors have been hanging on to Northeast, believing that Bank of New York Chairman J. Carter Bacot may simply be jawboning for a better price rather than trying to kill the merger. But sentiment is growing among analysts that Mr. Bacot really wants out of this deal.

As Felice Gelman at Dillon Read put it: "It doesn't sound like they're negotiating to me. Bank of New York has made it really tough for Northeast to conclude this transaction."

Nobody believes that Mr. Bacot, a black-belt penny-pincher, is about to pay anything approaching $84 a share (about $500 million) for Northeast, the parent of Union Trust Co., Connecticut's third biggest commercial bank. Yet on paper at least, Bank of New York is obligated to pay 250% of Northeast's book value, which was $33.53 a share on March 30. The merger accord was stalled until Connecticut recently changed its banking laws.

Mr. Bacot is a tightfisted guy. He had his Morgan Stanley advisers pick up the tab for the champagne that flowed after Bank of New York won a takeover fight for Irving Bank in 1988. He personally reviews all of the bank's charitable contributions in excess of $50. Yet some investors assumed the merger accord was ironclad and bid up Northeast shares as high as 72 last year. The shares later slumped as Northeast was wracked by losses in the fourth and first quarters, reflecting heavy additions to reserves for possible loan losses.

Recent changes in bank regulation have left Bank of New York a potential escape hatch. Banks can't any longer count good will -- an intangible asset arising from acquisitions -- as part of equity. Hence, the suitor would have to replace with new capital any cash it paid in excess of Northeast's book value. Raising capital by issuing new shares would seriously dilute the interests of Bank of New York's current holders. Bank of New York can scuttle the deal if regulators impose "materially burdensome" conditions on it. The suitor could argue that a new stock issue was burdensome.

So far there's no sign that Northeast is caving in to Mr. Bacot's hardball tactics. Northeast yesterday reiterated that it "will pursue all available legal remedies if Bank of New York doesn't fulfill its contractual obligations."

Even before yesterday, Bank of New York was arguing that Northeast's Dec. 31 book value was really only $12.31 a share based on the outlook for its real estate loans. Northeast scoffed. Bank of New York has even suggested that Northeast seek other merger partners, an ironic turn because Northeast did get a 1986 bid -- believed to be from Fleet/Norstar Financial Group -- that prompted Bank of New York to boost its price to 250% of book value from 180%.

Behind all the posturing, some analysts suspect Mr. Bacot still wants Northeast. But if so, at what price? If not, will somebody else buy it?

Union Trust has the premier retail banking franchise in New York's well-heeled Connecticut suburbs, and would complement Bank of New York's major presence in neighboring Westchester County. The Irving acquisition hasn't sated Mr. Bacot's appetite. In a development not previously reported, Bank of New York early this year had serious discussions about acquiring New Jersey's First Fidelity Bancorp. Mr. Bacot was said to be keenly disappointed when First Fidelity's board broke off talks and hired a new chief executive officer.

A bank merger specialist without ties to either party estimates that Mr. Bacot could pay $30 to $40 a share in cash and securities without hurting his holders severely. On the other hand, buyers aren't paying 250% of book value for banks anymore, particularly in New England where real estate losses are growing. Merger experts say a price of 150% to 180% of book value is more in line with today's market -- with payment in securities rather than cash and perhaps a guarantee against bad loans exceeding a certain level.

If another bidder emerges, analysts expect it to be foreign. They expect no sudden largesse from Bank of New York. Says Lacy Shockley at Smith Barney: "I wouldn't want to fool with Carter Bacot after seeing how incredibly foxy he was on the Irving bid."
