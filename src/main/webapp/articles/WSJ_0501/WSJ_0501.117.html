Clamor Builds For Automating Treasury's Sales

  • Author: Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 05/01/90

NEW YORK -- Momentum is building for Uncle Sam to automate the huge auctions of U.S. Treasury securities. For the past 60 years, the sales of Treasury bills, notes and bonds have been conducted mostly by hand by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, on behalf of the U.S. Treasury.

This quaint procedure is increasingly irritating many of the Fed's 44 primary dealers, the elite group of securities firms that must bid at every Treasury debt sale in exchange for the privilege of trading directly with Fed.

These dealers say the sales could easily be computerized, providing results in minutes, instead of hours. The speedier sales not only would help dealers avoid costly mistakes, they say, but also would save the Treasury millions of dollars annually in borrowing costs and lessen auction-day turmoil in the world's largest bond market.

"It's an antiquated system and it ought to change in this day and age," says E. Craig Coats Jr., a managing director at First Boston Corp.

The Fed, as the Treasury's fiscal agent, conducts the sales of Treasury securities, which totaled $1.25 trillion last year, to fund the federal deficit. Tomorrow, the size of the quarterly note and bond auction next week will be announced, and it is expected to total about $30 billion.

Dealers say they have periodically pleaded with Treasury and Fed officials to automate auction procedures, which have changed little since three-month Treasury bill sales started in 1929. Their requests prompted officials two years ago to quicken existing procedures by encouraging the clerks involved in sorting and counting the bids to work faster. That effort shaved about 40 minutes off the wait for auction results.

But even so, winners aren't notified of purchases until as long as three hours after the 1 p.m. EDT bidding deadline. The reason is that after the deadline has passed, the steel boxes containing the bids are brought to the eighth floor of the New York Fed for processing by hand. The preliminary tally is then faxed to the Treasury for final compilation.

Traders envision computerized auctions that would enable them to type in their bids on a keyboard and send them directly to the New York Fed, just as the World Bank's trading desk in Washington already does.

Bidders are concerned about market risk because auction days -- especially those that involve infrequently offered securities -- are among the most volatile days for the U.S. bond market. The anxious wait for results breeds rumors that can whipsaw prices of more than $100 billion of securities that trade daily.

For example, consider the Treasury's attempts earlier this year to sell 40-year bonds for the first time since the 1950s. The auctions of $5 billion of these securities in January and $3.5 billion in April by the Resolution Funding Corp., the agency created to fund the federal government's rescue of the thrift industry, drew considerable interest because of their novelty.

Around the bidding deadline at both auctions, rumors surfaced about robust demand for the new "bailout" bonds, which lifted the entire bond market. But results issued three hours later showed that those rumors were false, and bond prices fell a full point late in the afternoon on the day of the first sale and half a point following the second auction. "By having timely results, you would have avoided those market slides," contends Joseph Liro, a money market economist at S.G. Warburg Securities Co.

The latest attempt to lobby for electronic auctions began in February when a trade group representing primary dealers met with New York Fed officials and offered to shoulder some of the costs of automation. The discussions initiated by the Public Securities Association were widened early last month to include senior staff at the Treasury. The result is that the Treasury and the New York Fed have agreed to study the possibility of computerizing the auctions.

The dealers are eager to move fast. "I would hope by the fall we would have a working, automated auction system in place," says Thomas Pura, chairman of the PSA's primary dealer committee and a partner at Goldman, Sachs & Co.

But Michael Basham, deputy assistant secretary of federal finance at the Treasury, cautions, "It's at least a year away from implementation." The typically tight-lipped New York Fed allows through a spokesman that "we are at a highly preliminary point."

Well, says Mr. Pura, the effort "is tentative, but the important thing is that we have the dealers, the Fed and the Treasury working together. The biggest hurdle has been overcome."

While all of the Fed's 12 regional banks and 25 branches accept bids for the auctions, the New York Fed typically collects 80% to 90% of the competitive offers because it is close to the nation's largest brokerage firms and commercial banks.

Until auctions enter the computer age, the Treasury will pay a hefty premium to finance its borrowing, many automation proponents assert. That is because bidders usually add a little extra to the interest rates they offer to pay on the securities to compensate for the risk involved in waiting for results, they say. "It's almost like a bit of an insurance premium," says Mr. Liro of S.G. Warburg.

In a volatile market, where prices can move a full point in minutes, or $10 for every $1,000 face amount of a 30-year Treasury bond, there is a chance that prices of the securities offered at the auction will have edged lower by the time results are announced. That trims the returns to dealers, who resell most of these securities to customers or in the secondary market. Of course, the opposite sometimes occurs and prices move higher during the wait, earning the firms a better-than-expected profit.

But whether bidders win or lose, it is the Treasury that pays for their risk, those who favor automation say. The auctions are "a costly process for the Treasury and a difficult one for dealers," says James R. Capra, a senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc.

Mr. Capra says the premium built into auction bids is about what a broker would charge a client for a very short-term option to sell bonds at a fixed price. In this case, the broker's "customer" would be the Treasury and the "option" would give the federal government the right to sell securities late in the afternoon of auction day at the market price of three hours earlier.

On the day that the Treasury last sold 30-year bonds, Feb. 8, the cost of a three-hour option to sell a 30-year bond was 1/4 point, or $2.50 for a $1,000 face amount, according to data of GFI Group Inc., a broker of over-the-counter Treasury bond options. That option cost twice as much as on a typical day because the bond sale made the market jittery. Using Mr. Capra's estimate of bidders' "cushion," the hidden cost to the Treasury for the $10 billion of 30-year securities sold in February was $25 million.

With automation, "hundreds of millions of dollars could be saved over the years by the U.S. government," says Mr. Pura of the securities group.

The Treasury's Mr. Basham concurs: "Even if it saved a fraction on the $1.25 trillion of securities that are auctioned each year, that's a substantial savings."

Not everybody agrees with these projections. Some dealers say the auctions are so competitive that there is little, if any, risk premium. "The savings would be minuscule," says Scott E. Pardee, co-chairman of the U.S. unit of Yamaichi Securities Co. and a former senior official at the New York Fed.
