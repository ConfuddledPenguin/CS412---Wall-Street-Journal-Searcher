Technology & Health: EPA to Issue Rules to Reduce Amount Of Dioxin Released in Making of Paper

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 05/01/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency said it will issue regulations aimed at reducing the amount of dioxin that gets into water and soil through the manufacturing of bleached paper. At the same time, however, the agency said the health risk posed by dioxin in paper products, such as coffee filters, milk cartons and disposable diapers, is "no cause for alarm." Instead, the agency referred the food-packaging issue to the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, which will work with the paper industry to bring these levels down still further.

Dioxin, a highly toxic chemical and probable human carcinogen, is a byproduct of the chlorine bleaching process, which is used to improve products' purity, strength, absorbency and brightness. The EPA's action, announced at a press conference yesterday, was required under a consent decree between the agency and two environmental groups, the Environmental Defense Fund and the National Wildlife Federation.

Red Cavaney, president of the American Paper Institute, estimated that changes needed to reduce dioxin contamination will cost about $20 million for each of the 104 mills nationwide that use the bleaching process. Noting that the industry has already cut dioxin levels considerably, however, he said that much of the improvements have already been made.

Environmentalists are pushing the industry to limit the amount of bleaching it does and to avoid using chlorine, wherever possible. The EPA's action "puts the industry on notice that they've got to keep moving away from the chlorine-bleaching process," said Karen Florini, a senior attorney with the Environmental Defense Fund.

The EPA said it was most worried about the risk to the small number of generally low-income people who eat large quantities of carp, catfish and other fish caught in waterways downstream from paper mills. Deputy EPA Administrator Hank Habicht said the cancer risk, which could be greater than one in 1,000 for some subsistence fishermen, is a "basis for continued aggressive action."

Working with the states, the agency is trying to identify "hot spots" where fishing advisories or bans should take effect.

Paper mills also are being required to meet new limits on allowable effluent discharges. In addition, the EPA is developing new guidelines, expected to be proposed in 1993, aimed at preventing the dioxin pollution from being created in the first place.

Soil contamination will be addressed through new regulations on dioxin-contaminated waste-water treatment sludge from pulp and paper mills. Mr. Habicht said about 5% of such sludge is used as a soil conditioner. The agency also said it will establish guidelines for managing landfills and sludge lagoons that contain sludge from mills.

On packaging, the paper industry said it has already reduced dioxin in milk cartons to levels that are almost undetectable. At yesterday's news conference, the FDA said it would confirm the milk-carton results and work with the paper institute to make similar changes in other food packaging.

Mr. Habicht said the risk from the vast majority of nonfood products such as diapers and tissue was "not a source of regulatory concern." EPA officials, noted, however, that reducing dioxin contamination in other areas would cut the levels in nonfood products as well.
