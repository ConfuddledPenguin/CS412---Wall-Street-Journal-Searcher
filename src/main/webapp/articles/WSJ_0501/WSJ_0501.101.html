Torchmark Told To Halt Battle With Insurer --- State Order on Proxy Fight With American General Faces Legal Challenge

  • Author: Michael Allen and Rick Christie
  • Date: 05/01/90

The Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance ordered Torchmark Corp. to halt its proxy battle with American General Corp. The order requires Torchmark, a Birmingham, Ala., insurer, to stop soliciting proxies unless it obtains regulatory approval, a lengthy process that would be impossible to complete before American General's annual meeting tomorrow. A spokeswoman for the department said Torchmark would risk possible felony charges if it violates the order.

But some takeover specialists, while conceding that the decision injects a new element of uncertainty into the proxy fight, predicted Torchmark would obtain a court order allowing it to continue.

Although American General shares plunged in the early afternoon, they closed at $39, down only 62.5 cents in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. Torchmark shares closed on the Big Board at $43.625, up 25 cents.

Torchmark said it "strongly disagreed" with the ruling and plans to immediately challenge its validity in court. Torchmark added that its Tennessee counsel advised it that it is free to continue to solicit proxies and that the Tennessee order isn't effective until approved by a court.

American General, based in Houston, said it is "gratified" and added that it plans to hold its annual meeting tomorrow as scheduled.

The Tennessee decision, rendered by acting commissioner Joe Huddleston, supported an earlier opinion by the department's legal staff that Torchmark's actions constituted an attempt to obtain control of American General without proper regulatory clearance. Mr. Huddleston also ordered Torchmark to drop its efforts to pass a nonbinding shareholder resolution asking American General's board to put the company up for sale.

Torchmark has argued that its efforts wouldn't enable it to control American General, in part because it only nominated five people for the insurer's 15-member board. Torchmark also describes its nominees as "independent." Torchmark, which previously made a $50-a-share, or $6.3 billion, offer for American General in late March and later dropped it, said it will make another takeover proposal if it wins the proxy fight.

But Mr. Huddleston, after hearing two days of testimony, found that the department and American General "have established by a preponderance of the evidence that Torchmark will have control" of American General after the proxy solicitation.

The decision brought to light new details of Torchmark's bid for American General. According to the acting commissioner, Torchmark requested its investment banker, Morgan Stanley & Co., to evaluate a possible merger with American General in late January or early February. Dubbed "Project Patton," the takeover strategy was approved by Torchmark's board Feb. 26, Mr. Huddleston said. In mid-March, Torchmark developed a two-prong attack, the acting commissioner found: Seek first to negotiate a merger, and if that failed, launch a proxy contest.

Separately, Torchmark said an Indiana Superior Court judge issued a preliminary injunction delaying a hearing on the proxy solicitation before the Indiana insurance commissioner. Judge Richard L. Milan delayed the hearing, scheduled for today until Monday. In its lawsuit, Torchmark had alleged that Indiana's insurance commissioner abused the office's discretion and violated due process.
