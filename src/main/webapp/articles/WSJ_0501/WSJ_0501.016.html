Who's News: New York Merc Appoints Davis Of CFTC to Post

  • Author: Stanley W. Angrist
  • Date: 05/01/90

NEW YORK -- The New York Mercantile Exchange named Robert R. Davis, a member of the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, as its senior vice president for planning and development, effective May 21. Mr. Davis, 41 years old, who was appointed to the CFTC by President Reagan in 1984, resigned as a commissioner effective yesterday.

Until his resignation, Mr. Davis was chairman of the CFTC's financial products advisory committee. He initiated the revision of CFTC regulations on hedging and risk management. He also reviewed and analyzed off-exchange derivative instruments, such as swaps, for the CFTC.

R. Patrick Thompson, president of the New York Merc, said Mr. Davis "brings to our exchange broad-based expertise in international finance, strategic planning and negotiating, and economic research and policy development, as well as a deep understanding of the current regulatory environment." The New York Merc is the nation's third-largest futures exchange and the largest market for trading oil and other energy futures contracts.

In an interview, Mr. Davis said the chief problem facing all futures exchanges today is competition. "When I joined the CFTC," he said, "the exchanges had no competition; today, they must compete with foreign futures markets and off-exchange products." He noted that three of the 11 largest futures contracts based on volume now trade on foreign exchanges. Mr. Davis also noted that while many of the off-exchange products are complementary to exchange contracts, some of them are directly competitive.

According to Mr. Davis, futures exchanges must play a role in a world that is moving toward 24-hour trading for certain contracts. "Maybe screen {electronic} trading will be the most effective way to offer that service, but right now that remains to be determined," he said. "For competitive reasons, exchanges must remain the lowcost producers."

Prior to joining the CFTC, Mr. Davis was senior economist for the Congressional Joint Economic Committee. Before that, he served as a vice president and economist at the Harris Trust & Savings Bank in Chicago.
