Who's News: Ames Brings in Pistner as Chief to Turn Firm Around After Chapter 11 Filing

  • Author: Joseph Pereira
  • Date: 05/01/90

Ames Department Stores Inc., which last week filed for bankruptcy-court protection, hired retailing consultant Stephen L. Pistner to rescue the discount chain from the financial misfortunes of its Zayre stores. Mr. Pistner, 58 years old, was named chief executive officer and to the "office of chairman," a newly created post he will share with James Harmon, who continues as chairman. As chief executive, Mr. Pistner succeeds Peter B. Hollis, 46, who resigned. Mr. Pistner's appointment is subject to approval by the U.S. bankruptcy court.

Styled a turnaround artist, Mr. Pistner helped reverse problems at variety-store operator McCrory Corp., department-store chain Montgomery Ward & Co. and at Dayton Hudson Corp.'s Target discount chain since the late 1970s. "He has the reputation of a contract executive," said Chriss Street, bankruptcy specialist at Seidler Amdec in Los Angeles. "He comes in, turns the company around and leaves."

But the hard-driving executive, once known for flying planes under Mississippi River bridges, will need to pull some stunts to pull Ames out of a tailspin.

Thursday, Ames filed for protection from its creditors under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code, after disclosing that it expects to report a $228 million loss for the year ended Jan. 27, largely because of problems at former Zayre operations Ames acquired in 1988. The filing, along with $250 million in new financing, allows Ames to buy badly needed merchandise from its suppliers and continue to operate while trying to work out a plan to repay its debts.

Mr. Pistner will attempt what Mr. Hollis, who himself was described as a merchandising artist, was unable to accomplish -- restoring profitability to the 680-store chain with annual revenue of more than $4 billion.

"It's going to take some work," admitted Mr. Pistner, who said he began looking at Ames's problems eight days ago. He said he has given the task "some thought," but added it would be premature to announce any strategic plan. "Basically, it's a question of how to reposition the company. But that's my skill," he said. "I specialize in turnarounds."

Ames's problems stem in part from poor marketing decisions at the old Zayre stores. It tried to change the Zayre philosophy of attracting discount hunters by advertising bargains -- and even selling some merchandise at a loss -- in hopes of getting shoppers into the stores to buy other goods as well. Ames instead switched to an "every-day low price" strategy. But many of Zayre's old customers who had been programmed to buy heavily discounted items went elsewhere. While sales at the old Ames stores continued to grow in the 5% range, this was more than offset by the double-digit declines at its Zayre units.

Analysts note that Mr. Pistner must choose to continue on the path already set by Mr. Hollis or revert to Zayre's one-time successful formula of promotions. "It's like navigating a boat in mid-stream," said Mr. Street.

Ames's new chief also faces another challenge -- all his moves must be scrutinized by the bankruptcy court and lawyers representing various interests, and funds to make changes will be limited. "This already monumental assignment has to be done with one hand tied behind his back," said Wayne Hood, retail analyst for Prudential Bache.
