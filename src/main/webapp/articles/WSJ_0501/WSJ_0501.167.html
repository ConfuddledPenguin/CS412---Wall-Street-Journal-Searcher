New England Banks and Thrifts Face Wave of Insolvencies in Next Few Years

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 05/01/90

BOSTON -- New England, which had little more than a dozen failures of banks and thrift institutions in the past two decades, faces a wave of insolvencies that could surpass that total in the next couple of years, according to bankers, securities analysts and regulators. The first edge of the wave arrived Friday, as federal thrift regulators took control of Home Owners Savings Bank in Burlington, Mass., and a smaller Massachusetts bank disclosed that it is seeking financial assistance from the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.

Regulators privately acknowledge that they will soon be stepping in at more New England institutions.

At least a dozen more banks from Connecticut to Maine have seen their capital shrink to perilously slim levels in recent months, and even more could find themselves tottering at the brink of insolvency if the region's anemic real estate market doesn't pick up soon, analysts say. Gerard Cassidy, a bank analyst at Tucker Anthony Inc.'s Portland, Maine, office, predicts "a minimum of two dozen bank and thrift closures over the next three to four years, when this {real estate glut} finally washes out."

Some companies have issued warnings to shareholders in recent weeks. One Bancorp, a Portland company with $2.3 billion in assets, told investors that its "ability to preserve any value in its securities would remain in doubt," unless it carries out an ambitious capital-recovery plan. Eliot Bank, Boston, reported a $13.8 million deficit in shareholders' equity and warned that "if additional capital is not raised, it is expected that . . . regulatory agencies would take such actions as they consider appropriate, including the possible appointment of a receiver for the bank."

Bank of New England Corp., one of the region's largest commercial banks with more than $20 billion in assets, is working on a plan to recover from a $1.1 billion loss last year. But company officials have acknowledged that the plan's success may hinge on the health of the New England economy.

Veribanc Inc., a Wakefield, Mass., company that rates the financial condition of banks, placed 24 Massachusetts banks in its "red" category in last year's fourth quarter, up from two in the 1989 first quarter. The classification covers institutions with equity of less than 3% of assets, or with a net loss such that it may become insolvent in less than a year if the loss continues at the same rate. In New Hampshire, red-rated institutions jumped to 13 from zero last year, and the number in Connecticut climbed to eight from one.

That isn't to say that New England faces anywhere near the problems that the Southwest faced in the 1980s. Despite growing concern about a possible credit crunch in the region, most banks remain fairly well capitalized. A study by the Boston office of Grant Thornton, the accounting firm, found that average equity capital for Massachusetts banks with assets from $25 million to $500 million was 8.98% of assets, still above the 8.65% for comparable banks nationwide. And, so far at least, only scattered incidents of executive fraud have been uncovered in New England.

But after long enjoying a reputation as among the soundest and most conservative in the nation, many New England financial institutions now find themselves paying the price for recklessly aggressive commercial real-estate lending in the mid-1980s. Many of the region's mutual savings banks cashed in on Wall Street's bull market and New England's new-found cachet by converting to stock ownership and issuing shares during those years. With real-estate values soaring throughout the region, most promptly raced to lend the proceeds to developers.

But as the economy cooled in the past two years, scores of developers were driven into bankruptcy and banks found themselves trying to collect on unsold condominiums and half-vacant office buildings, structures that in some cases are now valued at half the original appraisals.

Few were as aggressive -- or as controversial -- as Home Owners. After converting from mutual to stock ownership, the company bought up mortgage-banking firms and savings-and-loan associations around the country. It also invested heavily in arcane mortgage-related assets whose estimated value depended on assumptions about interest-rate movements and other factors. The company's assets soared to more than $4 billion from $547 million in 1984. But critics called it a house of cards built with 1980s accounting gimmickry.

Whether or not those charges were true, it was apparent by last fall that Home Owners would have a hard time surviving under the new, more conservative thrift regulations. Albert Holgerson Jr., who led the company throughout the 1980s, was forced out by federal regulators. Remaining company officials said they would try to turn back the clock, returning to the plain-vanilla business of taking deposits and making loans. But a loss of nearly $130 million in the quarter ended Sept. 30 left the company badly crippled.

The Office of Thrift Supervision said it had placed Home Owners under government conservatorship because it "was operating in an unsafe and unsound manner." The agency also noted that the company had negative tangible capital amounting to $106.3 million at Dec. 31.

Meanwhile, Home National Corp. of Milford, Mass., said it expects a $4.8 million first-quarter loss to reduce its shareholders' equity to a deficit of at least $4.1 million. The company, which has $486 million in assets, said its Home National Bank subsidiary has asked the FDIC for a capital injection that would enable it to continue operating, a form of government assistance that has been granted to some banks in Texas and Oklahoma.
