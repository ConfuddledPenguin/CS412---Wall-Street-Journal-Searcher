How Quarterly Study Evaluates Brokers' Allocation of Assets

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/01/90

NEW YORK -- The quarterly Wall Street Journal-Wilshire Associates study of asset allocation includes the 10 brokerage houses with the largest number of registered representatives. Some companies publish more than one set of asset-allocation recommendations. Within reason, they may pick which set they want to be judged by, provided they specify in advance. Two companies, PaineWebber Inc. and Goldman, Sachs & Co., recently changed the asset-allocation blend tracked in the study.

PaineWebber formerly used a theoretical asset-allocation model developed by Edward Kerschner and Charles Pradilla. Its neutral reading calls for a 33.3% position in each of three assets, stocks, bonds, and cash. As of Jan. 31, PaineWebber asked to be judged, instead, on its advice for a typical balanced portfolio, in which stocks normally have heavier weight.

Goldman Sachs, previously judged on its advice to large pension funds, has asked to be judged as of March 31 on its advice for a smaller, aggressive pension fund. Strategist Steven Einhorn said he believes this advice more closely approximates what individual investors should follow.

Other companies with more than one asset-allocation blend are tracked as follows:

Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. is judged on its recommendations for a "small, aggressive fund"; A.G. Edwards & Sons Inc. on a "growth and income" portfolio; Kidder, Peabody & Co. on an "individual balanced" portfolio, and Raymond, James & Associates Inc. on advice to "young urban professionals."

The study disregards the cost of trading, mainly to make the results comparable to market averages. In practice, of course, people who often adjust their investment blends may incur high commissions.

Each house's advice is mirrored in a theoretical portfolio. Holdings are adjusted whenever the advice changes, and also on the last day of each month, to keep assets in the recommended ratio.

Investments are assumed to do as well as the following indexes: the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index for stocks; the Merrill Lynch corporate and government master bond index for bonds; 90-day Treasury bills for cash; the National Council of Real Estate Investment Fiduciaries/Frank Russell Co. index for real estate; and the average monthly price of gold for tangible assets.
