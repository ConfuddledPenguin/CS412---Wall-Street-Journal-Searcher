What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/01/90

MOSLEM MILITANTS FREED an American captive, the second in eight days. Kidnappers in Beirut, putting pressure on Bush to reciprocate with a goodwill gesture toward Iran, released Frank Reed, who had been held since September 1986. The 57-year-old educator, who called his captivity "lonely" and "boring," headed to West Germany after a stop in Syria. Robert Polhill, freed April 22, delivered a message from his captors to Bush, who hailed Reed's release and thanked Iran and Syria. The president also said he'd have "no objection" if Israel frees a Shiite Moslem cleric, as Tehran has demanded.

The State Department issued its annual report on terrorism, which accused Iran of being the "most active state sponsor" of terrorism last year.

---

A former HUD official told a congressional panel that the housing agency under Secretary Pierce operated "basically on political favoritism." DuBois Gilliam, once a deputy assistant secretary who currently is serving an 18-month prison term for bribery, said Pierce personally made decisions on project approvals.

---

NASA engineers untangled a cable on one of the Hubble Space Telescope's main antennas, a move that could allow at least limited operation of the orbiting device. Ground controllers hadn't been able to communicate with the telescope since its launch Wednesday by the space shuttle Discovery.

---

A House Democratic task force said El Salvador's inquiry into last year's killings of six Jesuit priests has come to "a virtual standstill." The report, which questioned whether full justice would be done, is expected to influence efforts to reduce aid to the government of President Cristiani.

---

A citizens' group in Latvia demanded that the Baltic republic emulate Lithuania and proclaim its independence from the Soviet Union. The group, claiming to represent hundreds of thousands of Latvians, termed the republic "an occupied country." The call came as the legislature prepared to meet this week to discuss secession.

About 20,000 Lithuanians gathered to mourn a man who burned himself to death in Moscow last week to protest the Kremlin's efforts to end the republic's independence bid. Officials in Vilnius, meanwhile, were quoted as saying the republic was prepared to suspend its secession drive if Moscow agrees to open negotiations.

---

West German leaders attempted to overcome Kremlin opposition to a united Germany's membership in NATO, saying Bonn wouldn't overlook the Soviet Union's "justified security interests." The assurances came as the two Germanys and the four World War II victors prepared to open unification negotiations Saturday in Bonn.

---

South Korean riot police stormed the headquarters of the Korean Broadcasting System in Seoul, crushing 19 days of demonstrations by workers that have disrupted state radio and television networks. In the southeastern city of Ulsan, shipyard workers clashed with riot police for a third day, and labor unrest spread across the nation.

---

Bush conferred in Washington with Panama's President Endara and announced the nomination of the first-ever Panamanian to manage the Panama Canal. The president, who hadn't met with Endara since the U.S. invasion of Panama last December, also chastised Congress for moving slowly on aid for the Central American nation.

---

The EPA will issue rules aimed at reducing the amount of dioxin that gets into water and soil through the making of bleached paper. The agency said, however, that the risk posed by dioxin in paper products is "no cause for alarm," and referred this area to the FDA.

---

The Supreme Court let stand a ruling by a federal appeals court in New York that abortion-rights groups lack standing to challenge the tax-exempt status of the Roman Catholic Church. The justices also let stand a lower-court ruling that allows random drug testing of Transportation Department employees who have safety-related jobs.

---

Israel's Herzog called for changes in the nation's electoral system and criticized politicians for showing a "total contempt for the principles of democracy." The president's Independence Day address came amid political divisions that have blocked formation of a coalition following the March 15 collapse of Prime Minister Shamir's government.

---

South Africa's foreign minister said the country's blacks couldn't push the white minority too far without risking a backlash that could destroy the nation. His remarks came as the Pretoria government and the African National Congress prepared for discussions this week aimed at exploring the possibility of black-white power-sharing.

---

Authorities in China announced the lifting of martial law in Tibet's capital. The move came nearly 14 months after the restrictions were imposed in Lhasa to quell three days of nationalist rioting that left at least 16 people dead and more than 100 injured.

---

The U.S. resumed diplomatic relations with the Marxist government of South Yemen after a break of nearly 21 years. The country has had close ties with the Soviet Union. In announcing the move, the State Department said Aden has begun liberalizing its economy and political system.
