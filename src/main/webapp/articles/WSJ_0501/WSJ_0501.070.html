S&L Cave-In Brings Minority Opportunities

THE SAVINGS and loan bailout may give people of color a bigger presence in the thrift business.

An amendment to last year's Financial Institutions Reform, Recovery and Enforcement Act requires federal agencies to help blacks, Hispanics and Asians increase their presence in the restructured S&L industry.

John Harshaw, president of the American League of Financial Institutions, a Washington-based organization of minority-owned S&Ls, says that new bidding rules for the government's S&L fire sale will keep failed minority-owned thrifts in minority hands. Although minority investors haven't succeeded in buying any of the 57 non-minority thrifts the government has sold, Mr. Harshaw expects minorities will ultimately pick up 10 of the approximately 354 S&Ls in U.S. hands.

The bailout also creates opportunities for minorities in legal, accounting and other services. The Resolution Trust Corp., created to sort out the thrift mess, recently set a 3% price advantage for minorities and women bidding for non-legal professional work.

Resolution Trust expects to spend up to $500 million on outside legal services in 1990. In 1989, less than $2 million of the $99 million spent on outside legal fees went to minority or women-owned firms. But a Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. official insists a new reporting system will soon show an increase in fees paid to minority lawyers.

Another Magazine Aims

For Asian-Americans

THE OWNERS of A.D. magazine, set to premiere next month, want the publication to be a vehicle for a new Asian-American consciousness.

It won't be easy. There are unique obstacles to the success of A.D. -- which stands for Asian Descent. The Asian population in the U.S. grew 70% to about 6.5 million between 1980 and 1988, according to a Census Bureau report. Yet most of the growth came from immigrants, who prefer Asian-language media, says New York advertising consultant Joseph Lam.

Moreover, there's a traditional reluctance among Asian-Americans to openly address issues of racial and cultural identity, which A.D. will emphasize. Audrey Chin, a financial analyst working on A.D.'s business plan, concedes Asians are "very quiet" about such issues, including discrimination. Still, she believes A.D. will provide "a strong voice."

A.D. will target urban East Coast Asian-Americans. Two similar magazines, Jade and Rice, have failed in the past three years, and a third, Transpacific, has "just begun to scrape the black" after four years, says Publisher Tom Kagy. To make it, A.D. will have to convince skeptical advertisers that there are many upscale young Asian-Americans who feel the way editor Jeff Yang does: "Each of us once tried to be the same, to fit in," he says. "But eventually we realized that you can't escape the role of color in this country."

Seed Clouds Spread

Over Engineering Pool

CORPORATE CASH is starting to flow to the nation's fastest-growing pool of engineering talent -- black and Hispanic students.

General Electric Co. has committed $35 million over 10 years for minority students and faculty. And General Motors Corp. has almost doubled its support for minority engineering students since 1984 to $1.9 million last year. "We're trying to make sure we have enough engineering talent," says Richard O'Brien, a GM personnel official.

Meanwhile, the National Action Council for Minorities in Engineering, which distributes corporate funds to schools and minority engineering students, says it's running $500,000 ahead of last year's $5 million budget.

In 1989, blacks received only 2,122, or 3%, of the 68,291 engineering degrees awarded, about the same share as Hispanic students. On the bright side, U.S. engineering school enrollments turned up last year after a seven-year slide, with black and Hispanic students accounting for more than half the increase.

Of course, some professionals believe corporations haven't done enough. "Imagine at one point there was no money running," says Tyrone Taborn, who publishes three magazines for black and Hispanic engineers. "Now there is a trickle."

A Departure From Norm

In Airport Business

AT THE NATION'S airports, minority entrepreneurs are quietly making business connections.

In 1980, the Transportation Department began requiring that more airport concessions be owned by minorities and women because a study had found that neither of these groups owned businesses at the top 21 airports. "It was almost a closed industry, serviced by a handful of vendors," says Clark Sharp, a Federal Aviation Administration civil rights officer.

Now, airport authorities must build minority participation into the bidding process for any new or amended concession lease.

Marriott Corp.'s Host/Travel Plaza's division, a major concessionaire, has had to give up some ground. So Host has been expanding the role of minority partners. "We've tried to work out ways to give minorities meaningful participation opportunities," says a Host spokesman. "It's one of the facts of business life today."

The rule helped Patrick Jones, a black Chicago venture capitalist, snap up three car rental outlets, at airports in Pittsburgh, Orlando, Fla., and Charlotte, N.C. One of the six rental concessions at each of the airports was reserved for minorities or women. "It's a sleeper," Mr. Jones says of the little-publicized policy.
