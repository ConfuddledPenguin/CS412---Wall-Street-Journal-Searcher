Detritus of America Helps Shape A Modern-Day Ashcan School

  • Author: Carrie Dolan
  • Date: 05/01/90

To some, San Francisco's solid-waste transfer station is just a dump that handles 2,000 tons of trash a day. To William Wareham, it's an inspiration "to another side of my creative direction. It's energizing." Mr. Wareham, an abstract metal sculptor, recently became the dump's first artist-in-residence.

"It's an absolutely wonderful way of dealing with the problem" of too much rubbish, says Claire Isaacs, the city's director of cultural affairs, who sees a "natural partnership" between art and trash.

Artists will be paid as much as $2,000 a month for three-month stints at the dump, owned by Norcal Solid Waste Systems Inc. Their creations will be made exclusively from materials plucked from the site, and the finished works will be displayed there. Norcal's Jacquiline Tripp, who organized the program, says the artists' efforts will "enhance the environment for employees" and raise awareness about recycling. Norcal may someday have a "complete museum on the property" to showcase the creations, and it hopes to "develop markets for this artwork."

The program "brings private industry into a patronage relationship with an artist in a very organic way," says Mierle Laderman Ukeles, unpaid artist-in-residence for New York's Department of Sanitation since 1978. The Norcal stipend, she says, is "a mark of respect. It shows the artist is taken seriously." She likens Norcal to "the Medicis."

"An artist never had it so good to be located at the dump," says Jo Hanson, a conceptual artist who helped arrange the San Francisco program. "You have all the materials available." She once held an exhibition at City Hall of "things I collected while cleaning up my sidewalk and street."

Mr. Wareham, 48 years old, works at a studio on the site. He says he is only occasionally distracted by the "odor of garbage decomposing on just the other side of my wall. The public, he says, often "confuses junk and garbage." He works only with junk -- pieces of old stoves, car parts, crushed water heaters and the like -- though he's sure "some conceptual artist will be hot to work with garbage. Somebody will want to do a monument of decomposed food. I don't."

His current assignment may be a perfect fit, Mr. Wareham says. "Artists love pre-used material with history in it," he says. And some people "think all abstract art is junk, anyway."
