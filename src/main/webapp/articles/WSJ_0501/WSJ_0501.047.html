Vernitron Revives Bid for Kollmorgen, Promises $15 a Share

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/01/90

NEW YORK -- Vernitron Corp. resurrected its bid for Kollmorgen Corp., even though the two companies are suing each other over a previous, collapsed merger agreement. Vernitron, a closely held motors and controls company based in Deer Park, N.Y., said it is prepared to offer $15 a share for Kollmorgen if it wins a proxy contest to control Kollmorgen's board.

A successful proxy solicitation and takeover offer would win Kollmorgen at about half the price Vernitron was willing to pay last year for the motors, controls and electro-optical company.

The circumstances of Vernitron's abandoned bid last September, just a week before it was to close, are in dispute. Vernitron has asserted that Kollmorgen's business "deteriorated dramatically" before the acquisition was to take place, and that Vernitron became aware of other factors that caused it to terminate the merger.

Kollmorgen, based in Simsbury, Conn., asserts Vernitron violated its merger agreement.

A "standstill" agreement and certain court orders preventing Vernitron from soliciting proxies or taking other steps to arrange a purchase of Kollmorgen expired Sunday.

Vernitron, which holds a 5.54% interest in Kollmorgen, said that if its proxy solicitation is successful, it will seek to auction off the company. Vernitron will be among the bidders, offering shareholders either $15 a share in cash or a unit of one share of common in a merged company and the right to receive a share of proceeds from sale of Kollmorgen's Electro-Optical division.

At $15 a share, the new Vernitron offer would give the Kollmorgen shares an indicated value totaling $160.5 million, or about half the $320 million Vernitron agreed to pay to acquire a larger company just a year ago. Earlier this year, Kollmorgen sold its circuit board business and other assets for about $50 million.

Stephen Bershad, Vernitron chairman and chief executive officer, said Vernitron decided against making a hostile tender offer because of Kollmorgen's shareholder rights plan, and because of a strict New York anti-takeover law. He said Vernitron has "already spent about $18 million on this transaction," and that it plans to spend an additional $2 million. But he said Vernitron would give up if it loses the proxy fight.

John L. Youngblood, Kollmorgen's chairman, president and chief executive, said directors would review the latest proposal.
