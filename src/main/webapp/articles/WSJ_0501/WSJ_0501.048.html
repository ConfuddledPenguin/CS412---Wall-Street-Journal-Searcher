World Markets: South Korean Stocks Log Biggest One-Day Plunge, Index Lowest Since Late '88; London Shares Off

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/01/90

SEOUL, South Korea -- Share prices on the Korea Stock Exchange recorded their biggest single-day plunge on Monday, partly because of nervousness about labor unrest following violence at South Korea's biggest shipyard over the weekend. The Tokyo stock market was closed for a holiday. London share prices closed mostly lower in lackluster dealings.

In Tokyo Tuesday, the Nikkei index of 225 selected issues fell 45.21 points to close the morning session at 29539.59.

In South Korea, protests by investors crippled operations at stock trading offices in Seoul and at least six other cities. Investors angry about the government's refusal to support the sliding market forced several provincial brokerage firms to keep their doors locked.

The Korea Composite Stock Price Index dived through the 700-point barrier in early trading and closed at 688.66, down 31.71, or 4.4%, from Saturday. Trading was weak, with volume of 4.4 million shares valued at 63.7 billion won ($90.7 million). Losers far outnumbered gainers, 750 to two, while nine issues closed unchanged. About 654 stocks fell to their daily limit lows; the government sets a daily maximum for each stock's price movements.

The index's closing level was the lowest since Oct. 13, 1988, when it hit 685.71. This year, the index has tumbled 219.93 points, or 24%, and it was near the low limit Monday.

Brokers said the main reason for the decline was weekend comments by economic planners that the government wouldn't prop up the market. Bearish sentiment was exacerbated by labor unrest in the industrial city of Ulsan, where tens of thousands of Hyundai Group workers on Monday stayed away from work to protest the police storming of the company's main shipyard on Saturday to evict strikers. Ulsan was reported paralyzed by violent protests. About 15 branches of securities companies in the southwestern city of Kwangju refused to open their doors on Monday, the Yonhap news agency reported. Yonhap said work stopped at seven brokerage firms in Musan, in the southeast. Three people were arrested on Monday in the southern port of Pusan, after causing serious disturbances at securities branches last week. One of those arrested led 150 investors into a branch of Cheil Securities in the city. They screamed for President Roh Tae Woo to be removed and damaged the property.

Tension was growing in other parts of the country, too, as a major dissident labor federation plans to hold nationwide general strikes on the Tuesday workers' holiday.

The stock market's plunge and the unrest forced President Roh to convene an emergency meeting of his economic ministers Monday night to review the nation's growing economic crisis, news reports said. Mr. Roh met with economic advisers to draft measures to curb inflation and spiraling land speculation, and ordered formation of a special team to consider ways to stabilize the real estate market, including those that may encourage securities and insurance companies to dispose of their land holdings.

In London, stocks were lower, though they shrugged off bearish sentiment that had considerably damped the market early in the session to post a gradual climb from midday minimums.

Heartening first-quarter results from the British industrial bellwether Imperial Chemical Industries PLC, as well as some selective bargain-hunting, perked the market up, dealers said. But traders said a bearish mood still hangs over the market. With economic and political worries, including local government elections Thursday and a likely somber quarterly report from the Confederation of British Industry on Tuesday, "Sentiment still doesn't feel too good," a dealer said.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 30-share index was off 5.1 points at 1653.6. Volume, on the first day of a new trading account, was a dismal 363.3 million shares, compared with 536.7 million on Friday. The 100-share index finished down 3.2 points at 2103.4, slightly below its intraday high of 2104.6 and well above the day's low of 2084.4, reached in the first half-hour of trading.

ICI shares ended up 20 pence at #10.80 ($17.66), after the company said its pretax first-quarter profit slipped to a better-than-expected #414 million ($677.1 million) from #442 million a year earlier. "That helped pull the market all the way back up," a dealer said.

In other earnings-related developments, the textile and thread concern Tootal Group gained a penny to 87, after posting a decline in full-year pretax profit to #35.7 million, at the bottom of analysts' expectations, from #42.3 million.

Oil stocks fared poorly. A trader said the sector is susceptible to stock market movements and isn't "very resilient." Burmah, which began trading ex-dividend Monday, lost 19 to 560; Enterprise shed 9 to 555, and Lasmo fell 15 to 559.

Among brewers and distillers, Whitbread slipped 6 to 364, after County Natwest recommended a switch from Whitbread into Bass, Britain's largest brewing concern, which rose 20 to 935 and became one of the day's biggest gainers.

Rothmans International jumped 10 to 645. Royal Insurance fell 14 to 424. The property company Hammerson sank 15 to 712, pushed down both by the market's tone and the property sector's difficult conditions.

The electronics concern STC shed 11 to 234 on profit-taking, dealers said. And Ranks Hovis McDougall, a food company, slid 9 to 364; a trader cited a shift in speculation that the company has been targeted for a hostile bid by a Sir James Goldsmithled consortium, after the entrepreneur gave up his quest for the tobacco and financial services conglomerate B.A.T Industries last week.

Elsewhere, share prices closed lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Stockholm and Milan, and were mixed in Brussels. The Amsterdam market was closed for a holiday. South African gold stocks were down.

Stocks finished lower in Sydney, Hong Kong, Singapore, Manila, Wellington and Taipei. The Sydney slump took the main stock-price index down for the seventh consecutive session. Hong Kong stocks fell 1.2% and Taipei shares tumbled 3.86%. Manila's lower finish marked its ninth consecutive decline. The Wellington index fell 1.7% to a two-year low.
