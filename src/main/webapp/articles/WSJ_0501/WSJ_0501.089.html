LEISURE & ARTS: Time Off: Diversions and Excursions May 1-14

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/01/90

ART

National Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C.: "The Passionate Eye: Impressionist and Other Master Paintings From the Collection of Emil G. Buhrle" -- The first exhibition in the U.S. devoted to the collection of this Swiss industrialist (1890-1956) includes 85 oil paintings and works on paper by Italian, Dutch, French and Spanish artists. Represented are early masters such as Frans Hals and Canaletto; impressionists and post-impressionists such as Manet, Monet and Gauguin; and 20th-century artists such as Kandinsky, Matisse and Picasso. Fourth Street at Constitution Avenue NW, May 6-July 15. The show then moves to the Montreal Museum of Fine Arts, Aug. 3-Oct. 14; the Yokohama Museum of Art, Japan, Nov. 2, 1990-Jan. 13, 1991; and the Royal Academy of Arts, London, Feb. 1-April 14, 1991.

National Museum of American History, Smithsonian Institution, Washington, D.C.: "Information Age: People, Information & Technology" -- This $10 million, 14,000-square-foot show, on indefinite display, examines how information technology has changed our society in the past 150 years. A complete computer network ties together interactive work stations, video, films and radio recordings. More than 700 artifacts are displayed along with graphics and objects from the museum's collections -- the Morse telegraph, components of Bell's first telephone, early personal computers. 14th Street and Constitution Avenue NW.

National Museum of Women in the Arts, Washington, D.C.: "Elisabeth Frink: Sculpture and Drawings 1950-1990" -- Sixty sculptures and 25 drawings by this British artist, best known for her monumental cast bronze portrait heads, male figures, birds and animals. 1250 York Avenue, through July 4.

Baltimore Museum of Art: "Weston's Westons: Portraits and Nudes" -- More than 100 vintage prints by Edward Weston (1886-1958), focusing solely on Weston as a figurative photographer. The nudes include the little known "Male Torso" (circa 1918); the highly abstract prints of the 1920s and '30s; his '30s nudes on the sand dunes at Oceano; and his last, satiric works, such as "Civilian Defense" (1942). Art Museum Drive, through June 10.

China Institute in America, New York: "Clear as Crystal, Red as Flame: Later Chinese Glass" -- Almost 70 pieces form a comprehensive survey of the art of Chinese glassmaking from the 14th to 19th centuries. The pieces range from clear glass vessels to trompe l'oeil imitations of jade, turquoise, porcelain and other precious materials. 125 East 65th Street, through June 16.

DANCE

Tomov Folk Dance Ensemble -- Authentic dances from Yugoslavia and the Balkans performed by some 40 dancers, singers and musicians. The troupe, in anticipation of its performing tour of southern Yugoslavia this summer, highlights the many dances of Macedonia. Riverside Church Theater, Riverside Drive at 122nd Street, New York. May 12 at 2:30 and 7:30. For tickets, write the Folkdance Foundation, 66-12 48th Ave., Woodside, N.Y. 11377.

MUSIC

Metropolitan Opera: Sharon Sweet, a newcomer to the company, sings Leonora in Verdi's "Il Trovatore." Franco Bonisolli sings her troubadour. Lincoln Center, 65th and Broadway, New York. May 2 and 5.

Otowa Yurikago Kai, Japan's leading children's chorus, makes its American debut at Carnegie Hall as part of the 30th anniversary celebration of the sister city relationship between Tokyo and New York. The 30 children who've made the trip range in age from three to 17 years. They will performe ancestral and contemporary Japanese folk songs, with half the proceeds going to the United National Children's Fund. 57th Street and Seventh Avenue, (212) 247-7800.

ORRA, a music organization devoted to producing and performing works of contemporary composers, presents "Rimshot," a new satirical opera by Russell Currie about the life of a ficticious rock drummer, Joe Rimshot. Based on the play "Best" by Scott Speed, the show explores the elusive nature of fame and popularity in the music biz. The libretto is by Ron Singer, Jennifer Johanos directs, and the Orra Chamber Ensemble provides the accompaniment. Pace Downtown Theater, Schimmel Center for the Arts, Pace University, Pace Plaza, New York. May 10-12. (212) 620-4294.

Opera Delaware presents Mozart's "The Abduction From the Seraglio," set in exotic 18th-century Turkey. It stars Evelyn de la Rosa as Constanze and tenor James R. Longacre as Belmonte. Stephen Lord conducts and Sally Stunkel directs. Grand Opera House, 818 Market Street, Wilmington. May 5, 11 and 12. (302) 652-5577.
