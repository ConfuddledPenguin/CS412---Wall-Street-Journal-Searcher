Monday's Markets: Late Rally Gives Lift To Stocks --- Bonds Gain Modestly In Sluggish Trading; Dollar Ends Mixed

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 05/01/90

A late blue-chip rally driven by computer-assisted program trading lifted stock prices out of the doldrums. The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 11.71 to 2656.76 in very light volume. Bond prices posted moderate gains in sluggish trading with the yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year issue slipping below the psychologically important 9% level. The dollar ended mixed.

The pace of trading was lethargic across financial markets, partly because of holidays in Japan and in parts of Europe. Japanese investors have been major players in the U.S. bond market and their absence tends to keep other players on the sidelines. And with the bond market nearly moribund, stock investors are reluctant to make major moves as well.

In any case, analysts said the level of uncertainty about the direction of the economy, interest rates and inflation is keeping a lid on market activity. Last Friday's report on first-quarter gross national product, which showed the economy growing at an annual rate of 2.1% but inflation leaping upward at a 6.5% annual rate, merely exacerbated the confusion. While the stock market would ordinarily welcome the seemingly modest rate of economic growth as a boon to flagging corporate earnings, the inflation data hint that higher interest rates may be just around the corner. Higher rates increase the cost of doing business, taking a toll on earnings, as well as cool the economy.

In the bond market, traders are debating not if but how soon the Federal Reserve will push short-term interest rates higher in an effort to curb inflation. Many economists contend that the first quarter's inflation was merely the lingering effect of the December 1989 freeze that drove food and fuel prices up sharply. But others believe that inflation is becoming embedded in the economy, such as in stepped-up wage increases. They are hoping for strong measures to bring price increases to heel and they point out that the sooner the Fed acts to drive up short-term interest rates, the more confidence investors will have in the long-term economic outlook.

Indeed, if the Fed were intending to raise interest rates, next week's big quarterly Treasury refunding auction would provide the perfect opportunity. With an estimated $30 billion or more in Treasury debt likely to be sold, analysts reason that the Fed isn't apt to wait until after the auction to push rates up because such action would undermine investors' purchases.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose in light trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 122.8 million shares. Declining issues were ahead of gainers 749 to 732.

Bond prices rose modestly. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond gained about a quarter of a point, or about $2.50 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the issue slipped to 8.989%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading the currency was quoted at 1.6796 marks and 158.90 yen compared with 1.6775 marks and 159.30 yen Friday.
