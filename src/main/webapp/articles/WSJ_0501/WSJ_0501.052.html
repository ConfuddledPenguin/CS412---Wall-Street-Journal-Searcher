Law: High Court Says Former GM Workers May Sue Company in Rehiring Dispute

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 05/01/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court let stand a ruling that allows 32 former employees of General Motors Corp. who were laid off to sue the company for alleged fraud for refusing to consider rehiring them. Without comment, the justices left intact an August 1989 ruling by a federal appeals court in New Orleans that the former employees may sue for damages under state law and aren't restricted to filing complaints with the National Labor Relations Board.

The 32 workers were employed at GM's Packard Electric Division plant in Clinton, Miss. In 1983, the company announced layoffs and agreed with the union to give workers the choice of receiving severance payments or retaining their seniority and rehiring rights.

Under the agreement, the 32 workers chose severance payments. When GM began hiring again in 1985, the workers applied for jobs. They said they were told that not only had they given up their seniority rights but that they weren't eligible to be considered for jobs, even with a pool of general applicants.

When they sued GM, the company argued that the issues should be handled by the NLRB and decided under federal labor law. The appeals court rejected GM's arguments, ordering a trial, and the Supreme Court yesterday refused to hear GM's appeal.

GM said it is "truly disappointed" at the court's action. John Maciarz, a GM spokesman, said, "We plan to continue to pursue the case vigorously." (General Motors Corp. vs. Wells)

Court in Brief

In other action yesterday, the Supreme Court:

-- Ruled, 9-0, that the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey, which operates airports, trains and other facilities for the two states, may be sued for damages in federal court by a worker who was injured on the job. (Port Authority Trans-Hudson Corp. vs. Feeney)

-- Let stand a ruling by a federal appeals court in Washington that allows random drug testing of Transportation Department employees who have safety-related jobs. The policy was challenged by aviation and railroad safety inspectors, highway safety specialists, aircraft mechanics and other government workers represented by the American Federation of Government Employees.

Transportation Secretary Samuel Skinner said the court's action was a victory for a "safe and drug-free transportation system." (AFGE vs. Skinner)

-- Let stand a ruling by a federal appeals court in New York that abortion rights groups lack standing to challenge the tax-exempt status of the Roman Catholic Church. The lawsuit was filed in 1980, arguing that the anti-abortion activities of the Catholic Church were highly political in nature and should lead the Internal Revenue Service to take away the church's tax-exempt status. (Abortion Rights Mobilization Inc. vs. U.S. Catholic Conference)
