PepsiCo Net Rose 10% in 1st Quarter On 24% Sales Gain

  • Author: Rick Christie
  • Date: 05/01/90

PepsiCo Inc. reported a 10% increase in first-quarter net income, despite continued expenses from earlier acquisitions. The soft-drink, snack-food and restaurant concern's earnings rose to $181.9 million, or 68 cents a share, from $165.1 million, or 62 cents a share, a year earlier. The latest quarter's earnings were diluted by a higher-than-anticipated 12-cents-a-share expense from last year's acquisitions, the company said.

Sales of the Purchase, N.Y.-based company rose 24% to $3.68 billion from $2.96 billion last year.

Though some analysts expected Pepsi to earn closer to 70 cents a share in the period, Pepsi Chairman and Chief Executive Officer Wayne Calloway said, "This is an excellent start on what should be another year of record profits for PepsiCo."

Most analysts seem to agree, pointing to Pepsi's hefty 22% jump in operating profit, excluding acquisitions. "You can't ignore the underlying strength of the operating businesses," said George Thompson, an analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "We should see some pretty strong profit comparisons throughout the year."

Pepsi's stock rose $1.75 a share yesterday, to close at $66.25 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Overall restaurant earnings jumped 39% in the first quarter. Pizza Hut and Taco Bell continued to turn in strong performances of 37% and 48% growth in operating profit, respectively. The big surprise came from a revitalized Kentucky Fried Chicken, which posted a 37% increase in operating profit, as promotional menu pricing and its new "Hot Wings" product boosted customer traffic.

"These days, fast-food restaurants are competing more and more on the basis of price, and Pepsi is leading the pack in that regard," said Roy D. Burry, an analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co. "Its {restaurant} profits could outpace the other two businesses this year."

Meanwhile, Pepsi's soft-drink operating profit rose 27% to $151.5 million. The company said both the Diet Pepsi and Mountain Dew brands showed double-digit growth in the quarter. International soft-drink earnings were down, however, from an unusually strong 1989 first quarter. In addition, the company incurred seasonal losses in two bottling operations recently repurchased in Greece and Japan.

Excluding last year's acquisition of Smiths Crisps and Walkers Crisps, Frito Lay's international profit jumped more than 50%. This offset slower domestic growth, which was hampered by higher advertising and marketing expenses. In addition, sales of Doritos declined from last year, because the previous year's potato shortage boosted sales of corn-based products.

Separately, Kentucky Fried Chicken's franchise association proceeded with a lawsuit against the company. It alleges that Kentucky Fried Chicken is violating renewal rights under the existing contract, and that its proposed new contract takes away certain franchisee rights, including territorial and other protections. In response to the lawsuit, Kentucky Fried Chicken President and Chief Executive John M. Cranor said, "To be competitive, we need a new contract that matches the specific performance requirements already found in the contracts currently being used by our major competitors."
