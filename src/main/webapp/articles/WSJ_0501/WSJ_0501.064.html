Economy: Personal Outlays Rose by Modest 0.4% In March; New-Home Sales Dipped 5%

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 05/01/90

WASHINGTON -- Personal spending increased by a moderate 0.4% in March, the Commerce Department reported, as consumers paid higher prices to keep up with inflation. Personal income jumped 0.8%, but much of that stemmed from a boost in subsidies to farmers; otherwise, the increase was 0.5%.

Adjusted for inflation, March personal spending dropped by 0.2% after increasing 0.2% in February and remaining unchanged in January. However, analysts said the low numbers don't necessarily mean weakness, as they follow a large 0.8% increase in December. Several analysts said they expect inflation-adjusted consumer spending in the second quarter to show monthly increases of 0.2% or 0.3%.

"I'm still looking for an upward trend in consumer spending," said Edward Yardeni, chief economist at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "The key to consumer spending has always been jobs, and they are still expanding."

In another report, the Commerce Department said sales of new homes fell 5% in March following a revised 1.5% drop in February. The March tumble left new-home sales at an annual rate of 574,000, down from a February rate of 604,000. The February drop was originally reported as a 3.1% gain. Figures in both reports are seasonally adjusted.

"The housing sales numbers look pretty awful," said Nariman Behravesh, senior vice president at WEFA Group Inc., an economic-forecasting company in Bala Cynwyd, Pa. "We're seeing further bites in the market because of higher interest rates, especially for longer-term loans."

With the increase in personal income outstripping personal spending, the savings rate increased to 5.8% after rising to 5.3% in February and to 5.2% in January. The savings rate is personal savings divided by disposable personal income.

On an inflation-adjusted basis, the biggest drop in personal consumption was a 1.2% decrease in spending on durable goods -- items intended to last three years or more. Spending in that category dropped 2.7% in February. The Commerce Department said lower auto sales caused both declines. Higher auto sales spurred by discounting fueled a 6.5% increase in durable-goods spending in January.

Purchases of nondurable goods also dropped, falling 0.9% after a 0.7% increase in February. Meanwhile, spending on services increased 0.6% in March after a 0.7% rise the previous month.

The 0.4% increase in personal spending before inflation adjustment followed a revised 0.6% increase in February. The February increase had originally been reported as 0.4%. The 0.8% personal-income growth in March followed a revised 0.8% increase in February, originally reported as 0.9%. The February figure was also affected by farm subsidies; without them the rise would have been 0.6%.

"We've seen continued fairly rapid growth in income," said Jay Woodworth, senior domestic economist at Bankers Trust Co. "The numbers continue to make a strong case that the Federal Reserve may need to reconsider interest-rate policy" and raise rates slightly to head off inflation, he said.

Mr. Behravesh concurred, saying WEFA Group expects the Fed to push the federal-funds rate to about 8.5% later in May and possibly as high as 9% by the end of the summer. This key interest rate, which is what banks charge one another on overnight loans, is currently hovering around 8.25%. "There's a good deal of underlying strength in the economy, and inflation isn't going away," Mr. Behravesh said.
