Who's News: San Diego Thrift Selects Kemper To Succeed Luce

  • Author: Chip Johnson
  • Date: 07/02/90

Great American Bank said it replaced longtime chairman and chief executive officer Gordon Luce, a move that analysts said reflected stepped-up regulatory control of the troubled San Diego thrift. Great American selected Robert L. Kemper, 61 years old, a former Wells Fargo & Co. executive, most recently affiliated with the New York merchant banking firm of Gregory & Honemeyer. Besides succeeding Mr. Luce as chairman and chief executive, Mr. Kemper will also assume the post of president, succeeding Roger K. Lindland. Mr. Lindland's future wasn't clear, although a Great American trustee said he would continue as a consultant. The moves are effective today.

Great American, the nation's eighth largest thrift, has been ordered by federal regulators to raise $350 million by year end or face seizure by the government. Analysts doubt the company's ability to do that, although officials have indicated they are trying to sell much of Great American's lucrative Southern California branch network as a way of raising the desperately needed cash.

The move to replace the 64-year-old Mr. Luce, who announced in May that he would be stepping down sometime this year, suggested to some analysts that Great American's fate may soon be resolved. "You could call it a quasi-conservatorship or a very quiet conservatorship," said Allan Bortel, an investment banker specializing in thrifts with Sutro & Co., San Francisco. "It's the regulators' way of saying `we would like to raise $350 million, but they know it's not possible.'"

Mr. Kemper wasn't available for comment. He contended in a statement that he was "looking forward to addressing the issues facing the bank and working with its shareholders and customers."

His hiring followed what Great American called "an extensive interview process." The company had earlier tried to lure James Cirona, Federal Home Loan Bank of San Francisco president, as a successor to Mr. Luce, but apparently couldn't reach financial terms, Great American previously indicated.

For Mr. Luce, a former member of Ronald Reagan's informal group of "Kitchen Cabinet" advisers, the changing of the guard is a rather swift fall from grace. He joined the company in 1970 and built it into one of the nation's biggest thrifts, only to have its financial condition shattered over the past year by the effects of a 1986 venture into Arizona real estate.

Great American said Mr. Luce continues as a trustee.
