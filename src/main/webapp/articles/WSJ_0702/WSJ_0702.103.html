Law -- Legal Beat: Rite Aid, Its President, Go to Trial On Charges of Attempted Bribery

  • Author: Randolph B. Smith and Wayne E. Green
  • Date: 07/02/90

Rite Aid Corp., the nation's largest drugstore chain, and its president will go to trial today in Cleveland on charges that they tried to bribe a member of the Ohio Board of Pharmacy. Attorneys for the company say a judge's ruling last week that some taped conversations of Rite Aid president Martin L. Grass can't be used as trial evidence was a key victory.

Mr. Grass and the company are charged with paying a member of the Ohio Board of Pharmacy $33,000 to resign as part of an alleged scheme to pack the board with people more friendly to Shiremanstown, Pa.-based Rite Aid. The company insists the payment was a legitimate buy-out of an existing consulting contract with the board member, Melvin T. Wilczynski.

Last week, Cuyahoga County (Ohio) Common Pleas Court Judge Lillian Greene ruled that pharmacy-board investigators had no authority to tape conversations between Mr. Grass and Mr. Wilczynski because bribery doesn't fall under the board's jurisdiction. The judge suppressed three taped conversations but said a fourth tape and a videotape of the payment could be used at the trial. The two tapes were made by Cuyahoga County sheriff's officers.

John Jeffries, professor of law at the University of Virginia, called the suppression of the tapes "very surprising" and "hostile to the prosecution." He says such taping is legal under federal law because one party to the conversations, Mr. Wilczynski, consented.

Rite Aid attorney George J. Moscarino, of the Cleveland law firm of Jones, Day, Reavis & Pogue, says the ruling will help prove Rite Aid's innocence. "The ruling finally enables us to show the tremendous bias and outrageous misconduct of the pharmacy-board investigators," Mr. Moscarino says. He contends board investigators had a grudge against Rite Aid and "venue shopped" after a prosecutor in another county refused to bring the charges.

Assistant Cuyahoga County Prosecutor William R. Caine, who brought the case against the company, declined to comment.

But others close to the case said the suppressed tapes aren't crucial to the prosecution. They say prosecutors can rely on the videotape of an April 27, 1989, meeting in a Cleveland airport hotel room. The tape shows Mr. Grass handing Mr. Wilczynski a corporate check and six copies of an undated resignation letter to Ohio Gov. Richard Celeste, which Mr. Grass instructed him to sign. On the tape, Mr. Grass said that Gov. Celeste had agreed to appoint a Rite Aid official as Mr. Wilczynski's successor on the board.

A spokesman for Gov. Celeste vigorously denied an agreement.

LENDER LIABILITY LIMITED in some environmental cases by Colorado statute.

The law, which took effect July 1, is designed to shield financial institutions in situations where they have foreclosed on loans to companies that own or operate property contaminated with hazardous substances. Because lenders acquire such properties in foreclosure, they're increasingly named as defendants in environmental damage suits filed by adjoining landowners and others who contend their property values have been hurt by the pollution.

The Colorado law exempts lenders from these so-called third-party claims if the contamination occurred before the lender acquired the property. Even if it occurred afterwards, a lender may be exempt if it had the property properly inspected before foreclosing and didn't "knowingly and recklessly" cause the pollution.

Colorado is the first state to impose such limits on lender liability, but the law is attracting considerable attention from other states, says John Byrne, senior counsel at the American Bankers Association in Washington, D.C. "It's a positive change," says Mr. Byrne.

Some environmentalists had contended that the law unfairly limits a plaintiff's right to sue. But Colorado bankers countered that it will help small businesses that handle hazardous materials. They said banks were refusing to make loans to such companies because of the prospect of litigation if the lender had to foreclose. "This law eliminates some of the uncertainty," says Jac K. Sperling, an attorney with Denver-based Fairfield & Woods, counsel for the Colorado Bankers Association.

However, Mr. Byrne notes that the law doesn't restrict a lender's liability for cleanup costs (as distinct from property-damage costs) at a site owned by a company to which it lent money.

Last May a decision by the federal appeals court in Atlanta apparently expanded the potential cleanup liability of financial institutions.

FREE-SPEECH PROTECTION for fund-raisers eroded in New York ruling.

Philanthropists who feel deceived by fund-raising campaigns may sue charities in New York for fraud and misrepresentation without having to prove the charities intended to deceive givers. The decision was part of a state appeals court ruling in a case brought by several American Jewish donors against the Jewish National Fund.

The donors filed the suit in September 1988, claiming that the fund had violated New York consumer protection laws by leading them to believe that donations aided tree planting in all of Israel, including the territories occupied after the 1967 war with Arab countries. The fund, however, only plants trees in land held before the war.

The charity challenged one part of a lower court ruling, which ordered it to state in its literature that it does not plant trees in occupied territories. The plaintiffs challenged another part of the lower court ruling that dismissed the claim that the fund had violated New York consumer protection laws.

In a 4-1 decision, the Appellate Division of the state Supreme Court reinstated the plaintiffs' consumer law claim and upheld the injunction (requiring explanation of where the trees will be planted) imposed by the lower court. "While the solicitation of charitable donations is a form of protected speech, it is subject to narrowly drawn government regulation designed to protect the public from fraud or other deceptive practices," the court ruled.

The dissenting judge wrote that "the leading case on charitable solicitation . . . makes it clear that such solicitations involve speech protected by the First Amendment."

The plaintiffs' lawyer, Howard Rubin of Goldman, Drazen, Rick, Steiner & Rubin, says the ruling means that anyone who gives money based on statements calculated to persuade now has the right to sue for fraud.

The fund's lawyer, Stephen Wagner of Anderson Kill Olick & Oshinsky, says he will probably appeal. If upheld by the Court of Appeals, the highest court in the state, the ruling will force all advertisers -- not just charities -- to screen all advertising and campaign copy closely to weed out statements that might be construed as deceptive, regardless of the intent, Mr. Wagner said. Such a decision might persuade courts in other states to construe their consumer laws in the same broad manner, he said.

"It will be a very grave restriction of the First Amendment rights" of anyone who advertises, Mr. Wagner warns.

JUDGE DENIES UNUSUAL REQUEST in libel suit.

Federal Judge Edward S. Northrop of Baltimore ruled last week that National Life Insurance Co., a Montpelier, Vt.-based insurance company, isn't entitled to receive the names and addresses of people who received copies of allegedly defamatory materials.

National Life had sought the information in connection with a suit it filed in May seeking $13 million in damages against Phillips Publishing Inc., a Potomac, Md.-based publisher of consumer and trade newsletters and magazines, and Richard E. Band, a financial analyst who edits a Phillips publication called Richard E. Band's Profitable Investing.

The suit alleges that in a series of special reports, articles and promotional materials, Mr. Band made false and defamatory statements about the company's financial stability that damaged its reputation and business. The Wall Street Digest Inc., a Sarasota, Fla.-based financial newsletter publisher that reprinted one of Mr. Band's articles, is also a defendant in the suit.

The defendants deny the allegations.

National Life sought the names of subscribers and the approximately one million people who recieved promotional material containing Mr. Band's findings. Profitable Investing has 2,400 subscribers; the Wall Street Digest has 25,000.

Theodore Sherbow, a lawyer with the Baltimore firm of Weinberg & Green who is representing National Life, said the request was an attempt by the company to engage in "self-help" to correct the false information, as urged by former U.S. Supreme Court Justice Lewis Powell in another libel case.

But Bruce W. Sanford, in the Washington, D.C., office of Cleveland-based Baker & Hostetler, attorneys for Phillips and Mr. Band, said National Life's request would have resulted in "the court helping them engage in a war of public opinion rather than a war of litigation."

In his ruling, Judge Northrop wrote that the information didn't seem relevant to National Life's case at the present time, but said the company could resubmit its request at a later date.

FORMER CONGRESSMAN'S extortion conviction in Wedtech scandal is overturned.

A federal appeals court reversed the convictions of former U.S. Rep. Robert Garcia, of New York, and his wife, Jane Lee Garcia, who were convicted in October. Mr. Garcia, a Democrat who had represented the Bronx for six terms, resigned earlier.

The court ruled that insufficient evidence may have been presented to prove that the Garcias committed extortion. The case stemmed from payments and loans that Wedtech Corp., a defunct defense contractor in the Bronx, made to the Garcias in return for favors by the congressman.

The appeals-court panel said prosecutors failed to prove that Wedtech made the payments out of fear of economic loss, one of the legal definitions of extortion. Instead, the appeals court said, "By paying the Garcias, Wedtech was purchasing an advocate, not buying off a thug."

A spokesman for the U.S. attorney's office said no decision has been made on whether to appeal the decision.

THE SENATE CONFIRMED three new members for the U.S. Sentencing Commission, giving the panel a full complement of seven. The new members are Julie Carnes, a former assistant U.S. attorney in Atlanta, David Mazzone, a federal judge in Boston, and Michael Gelacak, a Washington lawyer and former Senate aide. The commission establishes guidelines for criminal sentences in the federal courts.
