Quarterly Review of Underwriting: Underwriting Fees in 1st Half At 5-Year Low

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 07/02/90

NEW YORK -- When it comes to underwriting stocks and bonds, more is less these days on Wall Street. Despite a rising volume of new issues, securities firms' underwriting profits in the first half plunged to their lowest level in five years. Fees that firms earned from underwriting new securities so far this year amounted to a paltry $1.2 billion, according to Securities Data Co. That's down 17% from a year earlier -- even though the volume of new issues actually rose 13% in the same period.

The reason is that Wall Street firms are making less than ever on each stock or bond issue they help bring to market. The fee that securities firms raked in for each issue -- an average of $399,000 -- is down 44% from a year earlier. In 1985, the last time total underwriting fees were so lean, Wall Street firms made an average of $1.1 million an issue, Securities Data says.

"This is just a dramatic drop-off," says Francine Wissak, a Securities Data analyst. The shrinking fees per issue should throw cold water on "anyone who says they're doing deals and making money and that things are coming back to normal levels," Ms. Wissak says.

The deepening slump in underwriting profits is likely to lead Wall Street firms to cut staffs further and pay many of the remaining employees less, some industry executives argue. Even as underwriting fees slump to five-year lows, salaries remain far above 1985 levels.

"We've got to be more rational on compensation," says Robert Downey, a partner at Goldman, Sachs & Co. and chairman of the Securities Industry Association, a trade group. Wall Streeters "can have damn good careers," Mr. Downey says, "but they're not going to have instant gratification."

The end of Wall Street's fat-fee era reflects the collapse of the junk-bond market, once a big money maker for investment banks. Just seven new junk-bond issues were brought to market in this year's first half, with fees totaling a mere $16.2 million. This is down from $462.4 million in fees for 69 issues a year earlier, Securities Data says. Throughout the late 1980s, junk bond fees averaged $450 million every six months.

Despite lower fees paid by issuers, some securities executives maintain that underwriting profitability has rebounded in other areas. They say that's partly because firms are widening the underwriting "spread," or the difference between the price a securities firm pays to buy an issue of stock or bonds from the issuing company and the slightly higher price the firm charges investors.

"In the past, much of the spread has been given up to the investor, and investment bankers haven't kept it," says Roger Vasey, senior vice president of Merrill Lynch & Co.'s global debt markets division. "Now the trend is in favor of retaining a bigger percentage of the spread on deals, although we're working harder for it."

Still, there's currently nothing in Wall Street's hopper to replace the once-lush revenue flowing from high-yield junk bonds. For instance, the hottest area of the underwriting landscape right now -- mortgage and asset-backed securities -- generates razor-thin fees for securities firms, industry executives say.

One area that's making up ground is fees from underwriting issues of common -- both initial public offerings and issues by companies with stock outstanding. With the stock market rising to near-record levels and many companies trying to reduce debt levels by selling equity, fees earned from underwriting stocks surged 35% to $709.3 million in the first half, Securities Data says.

Some analysts expect the stock resurgence to continue. "As companies de-leverage going into the '90s, you should have more issuance of stock to strengthen {the companies'} capital structures," says James Hanbury, an analyst at Wertheim Schroder & Co.

Goldman Sachs was swept up by the rising equity tide. Goldman ranked No. 2 in total underwriting fees for stocks and bonds for the six months, earning $171.7 million in fees, nearly two-thirds of it from stock issues. Merrill Lynch, with $172.6 million in fees, remained on top, underwriting 29 more stock or debt issues than Goldman.

Initial public stock offerings, although not booming, continued to rake in the fees. Securities firms' fees from IPOs totaled $457 million, a 15% jump from $396 million in the first half of 1989. Although IPOs account for 40% of all underwriting fees, they're still far below the levels seen just a few years back. In 1986's fourth quarter alone, IPOs generated $541 million in fees for Wall Street, surpassing this year's entire first-half level.

Alex. Brown & Sons Inc., helped by heavy IPO volume, jumped to the No. 3 fee spot for total underwriting fees. The Baltimore-based brokerage firm earned $114.3 million in fees through 22 offerings -- a big jump from its $63.9 million and its No. 9 ranking in last year's first half. Much of the increase stems from a threefold jump in IPOs underwritten by Alex. Brown.

"The markets have been more receptive," says Richard Franyo, an Alex. Brown managing director. "But you've got to be a bit worried that as the markets get a little overcooked, it could end."

Salomon Brothers Inc. ranked No. 4, posting a 65% jump in underwriting fees for the period. But First Boston Corp. and Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. lost ground. First Boston, which has shied away from the junk market recently, made just $89.5 million in total underwriting fees, down 22% from a year earlier. Shearson Lehman slipped to the No. 8 spot from No. 5, earning $78.6 million. Shearson was hurt in part by uncertainty stemming from the firm's well-publicized financial and management turmoil.

Missing in action is Wall Street's fee leader for 1989's first half. Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. earned $260 million from underwritings, with junk bonds accounting for 94% of its fee total. Of course, Drexel is out of the picture now; its parent company filed for bankruptcy-law protection in February after a cash crunch caused by the collapse of the junk-bond market.
