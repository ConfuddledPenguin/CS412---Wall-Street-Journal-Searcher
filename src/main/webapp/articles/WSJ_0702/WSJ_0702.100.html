Great Western Acquires Remains Of Miami Bank

  • Author: Charles McCoy
  • Date: 07/02/90

It pays to be lucky in business, as James F. Montgomery once more has been reminded. Mr. Montgomery is chairman and chief executive officer of Great Western Financial Corp., which became the biggest thrift in Florida's lending market with its acquisition of the remains of Miami-based CenTrust Savings Bank from the Resolution Trust Corp. Great Western, based in Beverly Hills, Calif., paid about $86 million to pick up 71 branches and $5 billion of deposits formerly held by CenTrust. Mr. Montgomery said Great Western intends to sell assets it also acquired in the transaction, reducing its ultimate outlay to about $60 million. The government threw in $1.7 billion, tying CenTrust with Western Savings Association, Phoenix, Ariz., for the distinction of being the most expensive single thrift failure to date.

Great Western's $60 million bill contrasts with the $150 million Mr. Montgomery agreed to pay for certain CenTrust branches and deposits last year, before the extent of the rot at CenTrust became clear. CenTrust, laid low by the extravagant ways of its chairman, David L. Paul, was seized by the government early this year, enabling Great Western to scrap its previous accord and ultimately to save tens of millions of dollars. That also raises the question of whether regulators could have saved the taxpayers money by moving more quickly to rein in CenTrust before the value of its assets shriveled so much.

"Some time has passed since our first agreement, and the assets have deteriorated," noted Mr. Montgomery. "I can't second guess the government. But sometimes things work out to where you can pick up things a little cheaper."

Great Western also on Friday acquired 18 Florida branches of defunct Gibraltar Financial Corp.'s Gibraltar Savings Bank from the RTC. The two transactions will give Great Western 150 Florida branches and particularly strenghten its presence in Miami and Orlando, two of the country's more bristling banking markets. Great Western also got a toehold on Florida's prosperous West Coast, entering for the first time Tampa and St. Petersburg.

Mr. Montgomery said that about $2.1 billion of the $5 billion in CenTrust deposits acquired from the government are retail deposits, with the remainder being more volatile brokered deposits. On that basis, Great Western's purchase price was roughly 3% of retail deposits, which is the middle range of prices paid for large thrift branch systems bought from the RTC.

Brokered deposits are large sums of money placed by Wall Street money managers, who move the deposits swiftly from institution to institution in search of the highest interest rates.

Mr. Montgomery said he expects the brokered deposits to leave the institution when Great Western trims the high rates CenTrust had been paying to attract deposits. In all, Mr. Montgomery said, the two transactions are expected to eventually leave Great Western with about $6 billion in Florida retail deposits.

Great Western has $40 billion of assets and about 1,000 offices nationwide. The transactions were disclosed after the market closed on Friday, when its stock closed at $18, off 12.5 cents, on the New York Stock Exchange.
