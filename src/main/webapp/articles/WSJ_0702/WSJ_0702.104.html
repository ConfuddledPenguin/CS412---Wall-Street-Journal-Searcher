Family Leave Bill Vetoed; Override Believed Unlikely

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 07/02/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush vetoed a bill that would guarantee workers leave from their jobs of as much as 12 weeks a year for childbirth, adoption or serious family illnesses. Mr. Bush, whose veto was expected, said he opposes the legislation because of its "rigid, federally imposed requirements." Noting that U.S. companies face their "stiffest competition in history," he said employers need flexibility in offering benefits to workers.

The veto was the president's 13th since he took office, and is likely to be sustained by Congress, as were his previous 12 vetoes. But Democrats seem certain to seize on the veto as a campaign issue. They noted that Mr. Bush pledged in his 1988 election campaign to protect women's jobs, though he didn't specify whether he favored any federal family-leave requirement.

"No working mother should be forced to choose between the child she loves and the job she needs," Sen. Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.), chairman of the Senate Labor Committee, said. "The president might as well try to veto motherhood."

Sen. Christopher Dodd (D., Conn.), the measure's chief sponsor, has said the Senate, which approved the bill by a voice vote earlier last month, is within two or three votes of the necessary two-thirds margin to override the veto. But in the House, "It's going to be a tough road" to win enough support for an override, said an aide to Rep. Curt Weldon (R., Pa.), a leading House sponsor of the bill. The House passed the bill in May by a 237-187 margin, 50 votes short of override strength.

"I do not want Republicans to be blamed for killing the Family and Medical Leave Act," Mr. Weldon said of the bill.

Rep. Marge Roukema of New Jersey, another Republican who backed the bill, said in an interview that she was "stunned and dismayed" by the speed of the veto of a bill offering a "minimum job benefit," and noted that Mr. Bush declined to meet with some GOP members of Congress on the issue. The president "has signed mandates before," she said. "I don't know why he has a blind spot on this one."

While women's and family-oriented organizations decried the veto, business groups applauded it.

"Mandated parental leave is not only an anti-business idea, but . . . it discriminates against those unable to take advantage of the leave, discriminates against those forced on overtime because of its requirements, and discriminates against those who would prefer other fringe benefits, which would be eliminated if this measure were enacted," said John Sloan, president of the National Federation of Independent Business, a small-business group.
