Bank of Boston Sets Holder Rights Plan To Block Takeovers

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/02/90

BOSTON -- Bank of Boston adopted a stockholder rights plan, adding to its defenses against possible takeovers attempts. The plan's many provisions are triggered if any person or group buys 15% or more of the common shares. At that point, existing stockholders have the right to purchase newly issued shares at "a substantial discount," diluting the position of anyone attempting an unwanted takeover, company officials said.

As with the other New England banks, Bank of Boston has reason to be defensive, analysts say. Its first-quarter profit fell to $44 million, less than half of what it was in the quarter a year ago. Bank of Boston's return on assets of 0.46% and return on equity of 8% are well below what many major banks earn, said James Moynihan, an analyst with Advest Group Inc.

"Given the decade we've just gone through it makes sense to be defensive," says Bank of Boston spokesman Wayne J. Taylor, "to be protective of the long-term share value of the company."

Bank of Boston officials have lobbied against a bill passed Friday by the Massachusetts Legislature that opens the state to nationwide interstate banking. (The bill now awaits signing by the governor.) Executives have said they will opt for a provision in the bill allowing banks to not participate in interstate banking until 1992.

Analysts point out that these defensive moves are more for future protection than for any current threat.

"At this point, who would want to buy it?" says Carol Berger, an analyst with C. J. Lawrence, noting the general economic problems in New England. "It'd be like bidding into a blind pool of risk."
