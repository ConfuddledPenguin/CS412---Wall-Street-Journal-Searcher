Arco Hits Snag In Acquisition Of Carbide Lines

  • Author: Richard Koenig
  • Date: 07/02/90

The Federal Trade Commission took a step toward blocking the $220 million acquisition of certain assets of Union Carbide Corp. by Arco Chemical Co., but the companies said they believe they can still resolve antitrust issues. Resolving those issues may be particularly important to Arco Chemical, because it has already made full payment to Carbide. And, according to both companies, the payment is nonrefundable under the terms of their agreement, reached last September. The companies added that if the deal doesn't go through, Arco Chemical has rights to the proceeds from a subsequent sale of the assets.

The step taken by the FTC was to authorize its staff to seek a preliminary injunction rescinding Arco Chemical's purchase of Carbide's urethane polyols and propylene glycol businesses. Urethane polyols are the main raw material for polyurethane, which is used in foam cushions and packaging. Propylene glycol is used in products including fiberglass, paints and cosmetics.

The FTC said it was seeking a recision, as opposed to a barring of the transaction, because it "had reason to believe that beneficial ownership" of Carbide's assets had already passed to Arco Chemical. Marc Schildkraut, an FTC staff member, declined to say exactly what constitutes beneficial ownership in the view of the commission. But he acknowledged that the up-front payment by Arco Chemical is part of the issue.

"An up-front payment is unusual," said Mr. Schildkraut. Asked whether the payment could be returned to Arco Chemical by court order, he said, "That's something that will be up to the judge." The companies haven't commented on the reasons for the terms of the agreement.

The FTC also alleged that the companies failed to make timely premerger notification filings to antitrust agencies. The commission didn't go into specifics, and the companies denied the charge.

A spokesman for Carbide, based in Danbury, Conn., said it continues to operate the businesses in question and to collect the revenue from them. Upon closing of the sale, he said, Carbide is to pay Arco Chemical any "surplus cash flow" accumulated during this interim period, and Arco Chemical is to compensate Carbide for any "negative cash flow."

Carbide has already accounted for the up-front payment, having taken in the 1989 third quarter a gain to earnings of about $62 million.

Arco Chemical, based in Newtown Square, Pa., said in a statement that it is still negotiating with the FTC's staff and that the commission's action "was intended only to authorize litigation in the event the matter cannot otherwise be resolved."

All the parties to the negotiations declined to specify the antitrust issues.

Arco Chemical added that it is confident the courts will allow the acquisition to proceed if the FTC's action results in litigation.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Arco Chemical closed at $40.75, unchanged. The company is 83%-owned by Atlantic Richfield Co., Los Angeles. Carbide shares closed on the Big Board at $19.25, down 12.5 cents apiece.
