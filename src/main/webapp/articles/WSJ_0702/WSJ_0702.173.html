Treasury, in Switch, Finds No Glitch in Mexico Bonds

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/02/90

WASHINGTON -- After recently saying that its discovery of a glitch in accounting for zero-coupon bonds sold to Mexico had caused an immediate jump of $27 billion in debt subject to the federal debt ceiling, the Treasury has concluded that there was no glitch. A Treasury spokesman, acknowledging "some confusion" in statutory interpretation, said the department's lawyers have now determined the initial interpretation was the correct one. The ruling involved $30 billion in zero-coupons bonds sold to Mexico earlier this year to help that country alleviate its debt problem.

The latest shift, however, doesn't alter the Treasury's need for an increase in its current $3.12 trillion debt limit before Congress starts its scheduled August recess. Without an increase before the recess, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady warned Congress in a recent letter, "it is highly likely that default would occur before Congress returns in September."

Mr. Brady stressed that debt ceilings estimates are subject to greater-than-usual uncertainty now because the Resolution Trust Corp. is borrowing working capital from the Federal Financing Bank, an entity within Treasury. His letter didn't mention the zero-coupon bonds.
