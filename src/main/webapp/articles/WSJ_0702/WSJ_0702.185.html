Law: RICO Law Applies To Utility Concerns, Appeals Court Rules

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/02/90

NEW YORK -- A federal appeals court ruled that state-regulated utilities can be sued under the federal Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations law. The decision by the U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals in Manhattan reversed part of a ruling by U.S. District Judge Jack B. Weinstein, who overturned a RICO jury verdict against Long Island Lighting Co. in 1989.

The appeals court upheld other aspects of Judge Weinstein's decision, however, and agreed that the verdict should have been overturned. The case dealt with allegations that Lilco lied to New York state regulators to obtain higher rates to finance the Shoreham nuclear power plant. The closed plant on Long Island is now owned by New York state.

The appeals court said that utilities can be sued under RICO, although the law -- and its legislative history -- doesn't directly address the issue. Judge Weinstein had ruled that applying RICO in cases involving utilities would be an intrusion of federal law in areas historically controlled by state governments.

The appeals court said, "While ambiguities as to what conduct constitutes a violation may lurk in the RICO statute, the statute isn't ambiguous as to whether its proscriptions extend to a state-regulated public utility." Since a public utility is an "entity capable of holding a legal . . . interest in property," as RICO requires, it falls under the scope of the law, the court said.

In the decision, the appeals court also upheld a related settlement that Lilco reached with ratepayers in 1989 to reduce electric rates by $390 million over ten years. The appeals court rejected claims that the judge improperly approved too small an amount.
