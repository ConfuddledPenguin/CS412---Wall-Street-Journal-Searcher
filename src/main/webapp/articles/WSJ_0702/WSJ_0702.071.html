Beverage Firms To Post Higher Quarterly Profit --- Coke to Report an Increase Of About 18%; Pepsi, Anheuser at Least 10%

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 07/02/90

The big beverage companies will post higher second-quarter earnings, on the continued strength of low-calorie drinks and on the successful introduction of several new beers. Coca-Cola Co. is expected to post an earnings gain of about 18%, analysts estimate. PepsiCo Inc. and Anheuser-Busch Cos. are seen having increases of at least 10%. Adolph Coors Co. may report a 20% boost in earnings. After a gain from the sale of an Ohio business, bottler Coca-Cola Enterprises Inc. is also expected to report higher net income.

Analysts expect Coca-Cola, which generates the bulk of its earnings abroad, to show a 6% to 7% increase in concentrate shipments overseas. That's below the company's typical goal of 8% to 10%, but analysts noted that much of the decline resulted from continued economic deterioration in Argentina and Brazil. Outside those countries, Coca-Cola's overseas shipments rose 9%, estimates Emanuel Goldman, an analyst with PaineWebber Inc. The second-quarter results compare with a strong period a year earlier, in which shipments increased 15%.

Coca-Cola is expected to show strong volume gains in Europe, a critical geographic stronghold for the company, particularly in France and Spain. Coca-Cola has made a big push in France since it acquired the bulk of its French distribution business last year. Separately, Coca-Cola said its British joint venture opened a new plant in Wakefield, England, on Thursday. The plant is the largest soft-drink production facility in Europe.

In the U.S., Coca-Cola's volume, measured in gallons, is expected to inch up only 2% to 3%, analysts estimate, while PepsiCo is expected to post an increase of about 5%. Coca-Cola, which derives about one-third of its volume from fountain accounts, has been hurt by softness in the restaurant industry. But PepsiCo has been able to capitalize on the fountain business because PepsiCo's own restaurants have been outperforming most others, says Martin Romm, an analyst with First Boston Corp.

Additionally, analysts said, PepsiCo benefited from stronger sales of recently reformulated Diet Pepsi and from a smoother execution of its summer marketing programs, including the sale of specially designed Pepsi cans, called "Cool Cans." Coke has had a rougher ride. Earlier this month, amid bad publicity, the company abandoned its MagiCans promotion. Some of the cans, which contained currency and coupons redeemable for prizes, proved to be defective. Mr. Romm said he believed the promotion had a "modest" impact on volume. But a spokesman for Coca-Cola said the MagiCans promotion had "no negative impact. If anything, MagiCans contributed to the up side" of sales, the spokesman said.

In the second quarter, Atlanta-based Coca-Cola will earn 58 cents to 60 cents a share, according to analysts' estimates, compared with 49 cents, adjusted for a stock split, a year earlier.

PepsiCo, of Purchase, N.Y., will earn about $1.10 to $1.15 a share in the quarter, despite an estimated four cents a share in earnings dilution from acquisitions. The higher earnings, boosted by stronger restaurant and snack-food sales, compares with the $1 a share PepsiCo reported a year earlier.

Discounting a gain and certain one-time charges, Coca-Cola Enterprises apparently will show another decrease in operating earnings. The company's earnings have been disappointing for several quarters because soft-drink price increases haven't covered the bottler's rising costs for marketing and raw materials.

Several analysts expect the bottler's per-share earnings from operations to be about 21 cents to 24 cents. Those figures don't include a gain from the sale of an Ohio bottling property and certain charges that, taken together, will result in a net per-share gain of 15 cents. In the year-ago quarter, Coca-Cola Enterprises reported per-share net income of 26 cents. A company spokesman noted that that figure included about three cents in income related to the sale of property and a repurchase of bonds.

In the quarter, Anheuser-Busch, St. Louis, is expected to report earnings of 90 cents to 92 cents a share, compared with 82 cents in the quarter a year ago. The company's beer shipments increased a moderate 3% or so, as the brewer boosted sales with new products, including Bud Dry and Busch Draft Light. Mr. Romm said Anheuser-Busch continued to enjoy sales growth at the expense of smaller competitors.

Coors, of Golden, Colo., is expected to report that earnings increased to between 55 cents and 60 cents, from 46 cents a year earlier. Led by sales of Keystone, Keystone Light and Coors Light, Coors is expected to show an 18% jump in beer shipments in the quarter.
