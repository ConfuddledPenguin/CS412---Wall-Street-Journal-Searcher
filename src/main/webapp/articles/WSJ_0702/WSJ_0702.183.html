Letters to the Editor: Still an Opportunity Waiting to Be Found

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/02/90

In regard to your May 10 editorial "Lost Opportunity" about the Senate's failure to approve a capital-gains tax cut last November, I do not wish to quarrel with the excellent sense of your editorial in the slightest, but why call this a lost opportunity? Although the federal deficit is many times larger and the crises we face in social wars far more evident than they were in 1985, I do not understand why the opportunity to lower the capital-gains tax is any less. Particularly if the American people are united in demanding it. I've been a long-term investor in both real estate and the market since 1943, and I owe much of my success to the more sensible tax code in place before 1986. The main benefit to America is the unquestionable rise in revenue that would ensue if the capital-gains tax were reduced. If longterm investors were to be confronted with the bargains they would have from a lower tax on sale of appreciated assets, the effect would be enormous. Perhaps the reduction of the federal deficit, as important as it would be when accompanied by the natural reduction in inflation, is not as significant to America's future as the break middle-income investors would get from longterm appreciation.

Middle-income people have virtually no other way to save up for the enormous costs of college education for their children. And the housing and construction industry, now in a predicament, could be given a shot in the arm if middle-income folks could invest in family homes. If demand for homes rises, the relative cost of mortgages goes down. And every industry would benefit from a renewed lower tax on capital gains. I think this is still very much worth fighting for.

Theodore W. Georges

New York
