Technology: Pressure Rises For New Accord On Cut in CFCs

  • Author: Richard L. Hudson
  • Date: 07/02/90

LONDON -- The ink is barely dry on a world ozone-protection agreement reached here, but political pressure for tougher measures is already mounting. The United Nations-sponsored pact calls for the developed world to ban chlorofluorocarbons and many other ozone-destroying chemicals by the year 2000. The developing world has an extra decade to comply. The accord, expected to cost the U.S. economy about $4.5 billion, is aimed at halting chemically caused damage to the atmosphere's natural layer of ozone gas, which helps block the Sun's harmful ultraviolet rays.

But the London agreement won't be the last word on the subject. Even as the 10-day meeting ended Friday, officials from West Germany, Australia, Scandinavia and other "green"-minded nations were pushing for tougher measures -- including a ban by 1997, instead of 2000. And environmentalists and some leading ozone scientists immediately denounced the accord as inadequate to protect the ozone layer.

"Within five years' time, they {world governments} will realize they have to go a lot faster" to end use of CFCs, said Joseph C. Farman, a British scientist whose discovery of an "ozone hole" over Antarctica in 1985 touched off the global alarm over CFCs. The accord, Mr. Farman said, will still allow a 33% rise over the next decade in the atmospheric concentration of ozone-destroying chlorine compounds -- with unpredictable results for human health.

The prospect of tougher curbs worries many chemical-industry officials, who say even the 2000 deadline will be difficult to meet. "It's crystal ball-gazing," said Michael Harris, a lobbyist for Britain's Imperial Chemical Industries PLC. The accord "is an act of faith that somehow or other our industry will find the answers to meet that timetable."

The world's $2 billion-a-year production of CFCs and related chemicals are used in refrigerators, air conditioners, foam insulation, spray cans and industrial solvent compounds. Many of these uses can be avoided; for instance, nations can easily replace CFCs with hydrocarbon gases as spray-can propellants, as U.S. industry did by law in 1978.

But industry officials say that at least 40% of today's CFC uses will depend for substitutes on special ozone-friendly chemicals that the industry is only now developing, many of which won't be ready until the middle of the decade.

Most of the measures adopted in London -- which must still be ratified by each nation's legislature -- are already in U.S. House and Senate drafts of the Clean Air Act in Congress.
