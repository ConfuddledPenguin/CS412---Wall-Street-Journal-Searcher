Letters to the Editor: Chemical Reaction In the Cornfields

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/02/90

In your May 30 page-one article "Seeds of Change: Farmers, Eying Costs and the Environment, Limit Use of Chemicals" you are generally on the mark with the conclusions that farmers are trying to apply fertilizer and chemicals more effectively. There has been a great deal of emphasis on the appropriate application rate of fertilizer, particularly nitrogen fertilizers, that may lead to high nitrate levels in the water supply if applied indiscriminately. However, your suggestion that fertilizer use per acre on corn dropped 8% last year due to this phenomenon is subject to other interpretation. For example, the drought in 1988 reduced yields and fertilizer uptake in many areas. As a result, there was a substantial amount of fertilizer carry-over from 1988 to 1989, the most likely reason farmers applied less fertilizer in 1989. This may also be the reason some farmers were able to slash their fertilizer use substantially and still obtain high yields.

I also take exception to the statement that "many farmers were afraid of putting something into the ground that could knock them dead if they inhaled it," in reference to anhydrous ammonia. Having applied anhydrous ammonia fertilizer on many acres as a young man, I can assure you that inhaling it does not knock anybody dead. If it did, this is a ghost composing this letter. As with other gases, if it is in such heavy concentration that it replaces the oxygen, one could be asphyxiated from the lack of oxygen.

Terry Francl

Economist

Markets and Finance

American Farm Bureau Federation

Washington
