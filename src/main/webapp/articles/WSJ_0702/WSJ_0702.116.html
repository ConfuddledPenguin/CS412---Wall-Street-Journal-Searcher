Abreast of the Market: Growth-Stock Managers See New Gains --- `Value' Investors Have Other Ideas

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 07/02/90

NEW YORK -- Growth was golden in the first half of 1990. The growth-stock managers who are having a stupendous year with stocks such as Compaq Computer, Merry-Go-Round Enterprises and Home Depot -- all up more than 20% in the second quarter -- predict more of the same in the second half.

Not so, protest the "value" investors, whose hunt for bargain-priced stocks has saddled them with sizable holdings of lagging bank, auto and basic materials issues. They predict a sharp second-half advance for shares of companies whose fortunes are linked to the economic cycle.

Who's right will depend largely on which way the economy swings. Right now, investors and economists are divided over whether economic growth will speed up or slow further. Polarized thinking on this topic is one reason why the Dow Jones Industrial Average has drifted between 2850 and 2900 over the past two weeks.

But scanning the list of winning stocks and industry groups leaves little doubt about the dominant investment theme so far this year. "The thing that's driving the stock market right now is earnings momentum," says Byron Wien, chief U.S. investment strategist for Morgan Stanley. "What's not driving the market is value."

The software, communications and semiconductor industry groups are full of companies growing earnings at 15% a year or better. Winning stocks such as Storage Technology, up 189.4% so far this year, have impressed investors with their unique products, dominant market shares and their leap-frog earnings advances in a time of slowing economic growth.

But value investors, who specialize in sniffing out stocks trading at discounts to their worth, say the game isn't over yet.

"There is more than one way to skin a cat in this market," says Mr. Wien. "Everybody is focusing on growth stocks, but an investor could make it in cyclical stocks, too." Mr. Wien is a growth stock enthusiast, but he also has one third of his portfolio dedicated to economically sensitive issues, such as Ford and Fluor.

No doubt, cyclical stocks are cheap, a sign that most investors expect more slow economic growth. Aluminum Co. of America, for example, is trading at a ratio of price to per-share earnings of about 7, compared with 16.52 for Standard & Poor's 500-stock index.

But "if the economy picks up, cyclical stocks will do better than the growth stocks," says value investor Paul Lesutis, a director at Provident Capital Management.

Sanford C. Bernstein & Co. economist Giulio Martini expects the economy to snap back with 4.5% growth in the second half of this year from about 2% in the first half, with much of strength coming from a rebuilding of inventories by all kinds of companies, such as auto and chemical manufacturers.

"When the economy turns out to be stronger, the underestimation of earnings will be greatest for those companies that are sensitive to the economy," Mr. Martini says.

First-half weakness was related to inventory liquidations, Mr. Martini argues. "Once you get stimulation on the production side, you will have more income creating more consumer spending," he says. He expects the unemployment rate to drop to 4.3% a year from now compared with slighty more than 5% currently, and inflation to rise to more than 6% from about 5% currently. The Federal Reserve could even raise interest rates later this summer, Mr. Martini predicts.

Some investors are already second-guessing growth stocks, not so much because of their investment style but because these stocks may not meet their high earnings expectations. At the end of the second quarter, money managers showed a preference for stocks such as Procter & Gamble that are indeed growth companies but are less prone to negative earnings surprises.

The rotation in leadership of stocks in the Dow Jones Industrial Average tells the story best. Procter & Gamble and Merck, two blue-chip consumer stocks, accounted for 22.1% and 19.7%, respectively, of the Dow industrials' 173.48-point gain in the second quarter, according to Birinyi Associates, a stock research firm. But for the first half, technology bellwether International Business Machines accounted for 37.1% of the Dow industrials' 127.49-point gain, Birinyi says.

"The market is terrified of earnings disappointments currently," says Kent Simons, general partner and fund manager at Neuberger & Berman, which is why "investors are running to Procter & Gamble, PepsiCo, Coca-Cola Co. and McDonald's."

Mr. Simons and his partner Larry Marx say that many growth stocks -- be they technology or consumer issues -- are so expensive that they are now riskier than some of the shares of basic industrial companies that aren't growing earnings as fast. "There is earnings risk in Alcoa, but it is arguable that there is more risk in the price of Johnson & Johnson" because earnings expectations for the drug stock are high, says Mr. Marx.

Growth stock investors expect slow economic growth, a mild drop in interest rates and declining inflation this year. Lower interest rates will make bonds less competitive, enhancing the relative appeal to investors of the high-growth companies -- especially if the earnings of cyclical companies deteriorate further.

Michael Strauss, chief economist for UBS Securities, expects the economy to slow to such an extent that bonds will be a better investment than stocks.

"In the second half we will get a major slowdown in consumer spending," he predicts. Mr. Strauss foresees the economy growing at less than 1.5% in the second half. He expects unemployment to rise, weak sales, and, naturally, poor corporate profits. Mr. Strauss says the consumer has lost his punch. And the performance of some retail stocks recently shows those fears. J.C. Penney was down 11.5% last quarter to 60 5/8.

But it is precisely in such an economic environment that companies with dominant products continue to earn more than concerns subject to the economic cycle. "I continue to believe they (growth stocks) will maintain leadership," says Michael Schonberg, a growth stock investor and managing director at Chase Investors Management Corp.

Mr. Wien of Morgan Stanley says the debate between the growth stock managers and the value investors will be settled this summer. Meanwhile, investors should fasten their seat belts because changes in sentiment are likely to be explosive.

"Precarious balances don't endure forever," says Mr. Wien in a recent report. "The factors producing the standoff are too powerful. Something will change."

Friday's Market Activity

The Dow Jones Industrial Average edged 1.98 points higher to 2880.69, helped by further gains in bond prices and by portfolio managers seeking to "windowdress" their holdings for quarter-end reports. For the week, the industrials rose 23.51, or 0.8%.

In the second quarter, the industrial average gained 173.48 points, or 6.4%, while the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index gained 5.5% and the New York Stock Exchange composite index advanced 4.6%.

The industrials were up as much as 16 points Friday before a late wave of futures-related selling.

L.A. Gear, on the upswing since issuing quarterly results Wednesday that were in line with expectations, rose 1 3/8 to 30 3/8.

Ferro climbed 4 3/8 to 25 1/2 in response to news that an investor received federal clearance to acquire as much as 15% of its common.

Wells Fargo rose 2 3/4 to 79 on a report that investor Warren Buffett had a stake in the company. Wells Fargo and Berkshire Hathaway, Mr. Buffett's holding company, declined to comment.

Motel 6, the most active Big Board issue, fell 1 3/8 to 16 3/8. A block of 2.2 million partnership units changed hands in midafternoon.

Gannett fell 1 1/4 to 38 1/4. An analyst said advertising data for May showed that linage at the company's newspapers remained weak.

Campbell Soup gained 3/4 to 57 1/4 on news of restructuring plans. MGM/UA Communications fell 5/8 to 17 1/4 after Pathe Communications said it had reached an impasse on financing its offer for the company. Chiquita Brands International fell 3/4 to 26 on a report that the company's employees in Honduras went on strike.

Coca-Cola Enterprises rose 1 to 16. There was speculation that Coca-Cola Co., which owns a 49% stake in the bottler, may soon bid for the rest.

First City Bancorp. of Texas went down 2 to 26 1/4. The bank forecast a decline in second-quarter earnings.

TransTechnology slid 1/2 to 6 5/8. The company suspended its quarterly dividend and posted a loss on continuing operations.

Bairnco, which said it expects to report "significant" second-quarter losses, dropped 1/2 to 6 1/2.

The Amex Market Value Index rose 2.64 to 361.21, leaving it up 0.5% for the week but down 0.1% for the quarter.

Hasbro fell 7/8 to 18 3/8 in Amex trading. The company said second-quarter earnings will be down more than 50% from a year earlier.

Biomagnetic Technologies rose 1 3/4 to 6 1/8 after PaineWebber upgraded the stock's rating. Lionel went up 3/8 to 2 3/4 on news that an investment firm was considering moves to improve shareholder value.
