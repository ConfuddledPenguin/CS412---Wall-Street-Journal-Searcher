U.S. Middle Class Squeezed in 1980s, House Liberals Say

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 07/02/90

WASHINGTON -- The American middle class was squeezed during the 1980s, while the rich prospered, according to an analysis by influential House liberals that previews the coming debate in Congress over what taxes to raise. "They {the middle class} didn't come to the party," the Democratic Study Group concluded in a report prepared for release today. "Don't send them the bill."

With President Bush's "no new taxes" pledge now history, many Democrats are focusing more on the nature of the taxes to be increased as part of a deficit-reduction compromise. The DSG last week released a letter signed by 134 members of Congress who vowed to oppose any compromise that adds to the tax burden on working- and middle-class families rather than the very wealthy.

The Bush administration and many congressional Republicans are talking about higher consumption taxes of various kinds -- for instance, on cigarettes, alcohol, gasoline, energy. They oppose raising income-tax rates on upper-income taxpayers. With President Bush determined to cut the capital-gains tax, which would benefit upper-income taxpayers, Democrats are resolved to offset that by raising some other taxes paid by those same people.

"People who should pay are the ones who benefited from the Reagan party," Rep. Thomas Downey (D., N.Y.), a member of the House Ways and Means Committee, said last week.

On NBC News' "Meet the Press" yesterday, New York Gov. Mario Cuomo said any tax changes should balance the tax burden. "This isn't a question of sacrificing the rich, it's getting the rich to catch up with the middle class and the poor," said Mr. Cuomo. "You've already taxed {the poor and middle class} by cutting off the states, by forcing us to tax gasoline, real estate, sales taxes. That's the poor people, that's the middle class that get hit disproportionately."

The DSG report notes, as other analysts have, that wages failed to keep pace with inflation during the 1980s. Because more and more women went to work, inflation-adjusted median family income did rise, though.

But, the DSG argues, families that sent second wage-earners to work may not have seen their standard of living rise. "A family which requires two earnings is less well off than a one-earner family with the same income," it argues.

Based on Bureau of Labor Statistics data gathered during the early 1980s, the group says child-care and other work-related expenses consume between 20% and 30% of the added income. As a result, the net contribution of second earners wasn't sufficient to offset fully the general decline in wage levels, the group said.

Two-earner families saved less and were slightly less likely to own a home than single-earner families with the same family income, the DSG said.
