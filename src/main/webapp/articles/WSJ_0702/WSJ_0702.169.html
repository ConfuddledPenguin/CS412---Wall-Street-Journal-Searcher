Judge Upholds Ban On ABC Broadcast Of Games in Cuba

  • Author: Wade Lambert
  • Date: 07/02/90

NEW YORK -- A federal judge ruled that the U.S. can prohibit Capital Cities/ABC Inc. from broadcasting the 1991 Pan American Games in Cuba under the federal Trading With the Enemy Act. The Treasury Department had refused to allow the broadcast, claiming that ABC's agreement to pay Cuba $6.5 million for exclusive rights to televise the event violated the law. The Treasury Department told the network it would allow the broadcast only if no substantial amount of money was paid to Cuba.

ABC sued the Treasury Department in federal court in Manhattan in December, alleging that the prohibition violated the network's freedom of speech and was an illegal attempt to restrict news coverage.

CBS Inc. and General Electric Co.'s National Broadcasting Co. network filed a friend-of-the-court brief supporting ABC.

Judge John Sprizzo, however, said the restrictions were constitutional and dismissed ABC's lawsuit. He said that in this case constitutional concerns about free speech were outweighed by the executive branch's right to conduct foreign policy.

A. Douglas Melamed, an attorney for ABC with Wilmer, Cutler & Pickering in Washington, said, "While no decision has been made, it is likely we'll appeal."

To win rights to broadcast the sports event, which is held in a different member nation every four years, ABC agreed to pay the Pan American Sports Organization $8.7 million, with the understanding that 75% of the money would be passed on to Cuba. The network said that the Treasury Department's proposed alternatives, which called for paying only minimal travel expenses in Cuba, were unacceptable to the Pan American Sports Organization, which includes a group representing the U.S.

The judge rejected arguments that the broadcast agreement was protected by a 1988 law adopted by Congress to exempt informational materials from trade embargoes. The judge said the law's legislative history was too vague to determine whether such agreements were protected, and he left it to the agency to interpret the law.

Floyd Abrams, an attorney for CBS and NBC in their friend-of-the-court brief, said the 1988 law was meant to include such broadcasts, and Congress's intent in passing the law will be an important issue if the case is appealed.

ABC also argued that the Treasury Department was misapplying its own regulations because the restrictions allow newsgathering activities to proceed without prior approval. But Judge Sprizzo said the agency has the right to decide whether the broadcast agreement fell under the category of news gathering.

Mr. Abrams said he was also concerned about why the Treasury Department would take a hard line against the broadcast. "At a time of cooling rhetoric around the world and a decrease in international tensions, it is difficult to understand why First Amendment interests must be trumped by Cold War ones," he said.
