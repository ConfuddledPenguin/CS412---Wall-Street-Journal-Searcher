Credit Markets: Junk Bonds Ride a Long Comeback Trail

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 07/02/90

NEW YORK -- Junk bonds, the sorriest securities in the fixed-income market just a few months ago, are climbing out of their deep hole. The high-risk, high-yield bonds posted a total return of 4.31% in the second-quarter, according to Merrill Lynch & Co. Statistics compiled by First Boston Corp., which unlike Merrill Lynch includes in its study highly volatile zero-coupon bonds, said junk bonds posted a total return of 5.74% in the second quarter. Only convertibles, which are bonds that can be exchanged for stock, outperformed junk during the second quarter, thanks largely to a soaring stock market.

While the returns for junk bonds are respectable, they are far from spectacular considering investors could have gotten similar returns by investing in long-term Treasury bonds. Long-term Treasurys posted a total return of 4.30% in the second quarter after a negative 4.02% return in the first quarter. And investing in Treasurys, which are considered the safest securities in the world, doesn't create the nail bitting, sweaty palms and sleepless nights that have come with investing in junk.

Still, junk's second-quarter performance represents an important turning point for a market that nearly collapsed last September. Junk bonds posted negative total returns (interest payments plus price changes) in this year's first quarter and the fourth quarter of 1989.

The current junk-bond recovery could come to a screeching halt if the economy contracts or if interest rates rise. And with prices of most middle-tier and lower-tier junk bonds trading at substantial discounts to their face value, prices have a long upward climb before they are anywhere near full face value.

"We are not ready to call it a huge, dramatic rally, but you will see better overall performance," said Joseph C. Bencivenga, director and manager of high-yield research at Salomon Brothers Inc. He said that while prices were bid higher for many junk bonds, trading was active in a limited number of issues.

But Sam DeRosa-Farag, an analyst at First Boston Corp., said that technical indicators for junk appear bullish. The advance-decline ratio, for example, shows marked improvement. Mr. DeRosa-Farag said that of the 352 junk-bond issues he followed in the month of June, 4.6 junk bond issues advanced for each one that declined.

Another sign that sentiment is improving, he said, is that buying has moved from the "higher quality" junk to middle-tier and lower-tier bonds.

"The best paper always pops first," Mr. DeRosa-Farag said. "Then, as sentiment improves, people start to venture down the capital structure and begin to look at the lower tiers." Stronger purchases of middle-tier and lower-tier junk bonds may explain why B-rated junk bonds posted a total return of 8.12% in the second quarter, outperforming junk bonds that had higher credit ratings.

Top-tier junk bonds, such as those issued by Kroger Co. and Duracell Inc., have little room for capital appreciation because they already trade at premiums to their face values.

The turnaround in long-term Treasurys represents yet another about-face for a market that has shifted gears several times this year.

Treasury-bond prices fell sharply in January when rising interest rates around the world pulled bond yields higher. Treasurys fell further in April on stronger-than-expected economic data and fears of higher interest rates.

But since May, the market has been rising on growing speculation that the economy is growing weaker and, more recently, on optimism that efforts are underway to cut the size of the U.S. budget deficit. These trends are considered good for bonds because a weaker economy and smaller deficit could prompt the Federal Reserve to lower interest rates.

James Kochan, chief fixed-income strategist at Merrill Lynch, said the factors that fueled the bond market's rollercoaster performance during the first half of 1990 remain in place. This makes bond-market forecasting extremely difficult.

"The market begins the second half under the influence of two opposing forces -- a soft domestic economy that could prompt Fed easing and booming economies overseas that could spark central-bank tightening," Mr. Kochan said.

By far the best performers in the second quarter were zero-coupon bonds. Zeros, which pay no interest and are sold at a deep discount to face value, racked up a total return of 12.05% in the second quarter, according to First Boston.

Zeros are extremely volatile and their prices tend to surge when the interest rates fall and plummet when interest rates rise. The securities are generally three times more volatile than cash-paying bonds with similar maturities.

Friday's Market Activity

Bond prices ended with gains for the fourth straight session on growing optimism that the federal budget deficit will soon be reduced.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose 1/2 point, a gain of about $5 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The bond's price was quoted late Friday at a price of 103 23/32 to yield 8.40%.

The bond market began to advance on Tuesday, lifted by news that President Bush had said that tax revenue increases are needed to shrink the budget deficit. On Friday, Mr. Bush reiterated his stance and said that higher taxes are a "necessary step to get stalled budget negotiations moving."

In the corporate bond market, investment grade bonds ended 1/4 point higher, while junk bonds were little changed.

Among the notable exceptions to the junk market's quiet tone were the bonds of Stone Container Corp. and Charter Medical Corp., which posted strong gains, and those of Carter Hawley Hale Stores Inc., which continued to weaken.

Stone Container's debt securities climbed on news that the company will use $65 million from the sale of timberland to repay debt. Stone Container's 11.5% senior subordinated notes, maturing in 1999, advanced 3/8 point to end at 93 3/8. The gain in Stone Container bonds comes amid reports that second-quarter earnings for paper producers will be lower than year-earlier results.

The bonds of Charter Medical advanced despite a credit-rating downgrading by Moody's Investors Service Inc., which cut Charter Medical's senior debt ratings to single-B-2 from single-B-1, and its subordinated and junior subordinated debentures to Caa from single-B-3. Charter Medical's 14% senior subordinated debentures, due in 2000, finished 1/2 point higher at 39.

The junk bonds of Carter Hawley Hale Stores continued to slip as worries about softer retail sales nagged bondholders. Carter Hawley Hale's 12.25% senior subordinated notes, maturing in 1996, fell two points to 84.
