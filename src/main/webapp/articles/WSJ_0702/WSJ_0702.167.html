You Can Be Sure There Isn't Any Understudy Waiting in the Wings

  • Author: Judith Valente
  • Date: 07/02/90

Admit it, frequent fliers. You tune out when the pilot tunes in to give his customary spiel to passengers. But then, you've probably never been aboard Capt. Michael Berry's flights. The Pan Am pilot's announcements are, well, different. His motto: "Flying is serious business, but it doesn't have to be dull."

The 28-year veteran flies mostly out of New York's John F. Kennedy Airport. Given the usual delays there, his unsuspecting passengers probably don't think too much of it when he briefs them on the time of their "escape from New York."

But, when the seat-belt sign is turned off, the tall, husky pilot turns on. He reminds passengers they are free to walk about. "As a matter of fact, if you feel an overwhelming urge to disco dance in the aisles, you may now do so," he adds, in his lush Louisiana drawl.

En route updates go something like this: "Well, we're passing directly over the Piggly Wiggly supermarket in downtown St. John's, Newfoundland. . . ."

"I've put Piggly Wigglys everywhere," says the 50-year-old pilot. But once, over the Caribbean, he announced the flight was "180 miles due west of the Piggly Wiggly located on the island of Bermuda." A passenger, who took him too seriously, later told him Bermuda's only Piggly Wiggly had become a Safeway. Now he uses "the Snappy Shine Car Wash."

Over Washington, D.C., he told passengers some heavy turbulence was being caused by "thermal variations in the atmosphere -- a fancy term for hot air rising." Then he added: "As you might expect, hot air is always a problem in the Washington area." A flight attendant sent the item to Reader's Digest, which paid her $300 to publish it.

His Pan Am Shuttle passengers get this Capt. Kirk-ish welcome: "These are the voyages of Pan Am Clipper Ship First Prize. Its one-hour mission: to explore a strange new world. To boldly go where only this group of carbon-based terrestrials wanted to go this hour -- the Big Apple."

Elaine Copland, a New York travel agent, found Capt. Berry's approach "a very welcome change" during a flight from Europe. Passengers applauded when the pilot emerged from the cockpit.

The flight had arrived early, and Capt. Berry apologized that "there can be no refund for flying less time than scheduled." Then he gave his trademark farewell: "Ladies and gentlemen, you're on your own again. Let's be careful out there."
