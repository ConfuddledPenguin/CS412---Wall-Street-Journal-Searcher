International: Lithuania Agrees To Suspend Act Of Independence

  • Author: Elisabeth Rubinfien
  • Date: 07/02/90

Lithuania gave Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev a gift by temporarily freezing its act of independence in time for the start of the Soviet Communist Party congress today. Mr. Gorbachev is returning the favor by turning the gas pumps back on. The breakthrough opens the door to negotiations that Lithuanian Prime Minister Kazimiera Prunskiene predicted could lead to independence in two or three years. Lithuanian radicals fear a Soviet trick, but the Parliament accepted Mrs. Prunskiene's logic that "you can only have talks with an independent country, so the very fact of having talks shows we're already a little independent."

Lithuania's moratorium will last for 100 days from the start of talks and can be extended or terminated at Lithuania's will. If the negotiations break off or if the Lithuanian Parliament is kept from its business, the moratorium will automatically cease. The leaders of Lithuania, Estonia and Latvia, the three Baltic republics, agreed Saturday to seek joint talks with Moscow on independence, Reuters reported.

The timing of Lithuania's announcement gave Mr. Gorbachev a badly needed victory. But the decision stemmed more from diplomacy than desperation; the Kremlin's two-month-long blockade was singularly unsuccessful at disrupting daily life or undermining spirit in Lithuania.

As the Parliament debated the dangers of compromise, Vilnius felt more like a summer resort than a city under siege. The blockade had forced some 50,000 people out of work, but most took long paid summer holidays, and the reality of unemployment hadn't hit home. Gas shipments had long since stopped, but the roads were full of cars fueled by gasoline brought in privately from Poland and Byelorussia and sold on the black market. Shops were full and there were few lines.

The blockade of gas and vital raw materials was Moscow's key instrument in trying to force Lithuania to renounce its drive for independence. It did take a toll: Hundreds of factories curtailed operations and 10 plants, including a major oil refinery, were shut down. People lived under strict rationing for months.

But the blockade also strengthened the confidence of many Lithuanian's in their ability to make it without Moscow. "First we thought we couldn't stand the blockade more than a month, but now after two months we realize we can survive these conditions," said Povilas Gylys, an economics professor and official of the independent Lithuanian Communist Party.

Indeed, Mr. Gorbachev seems to have understood the blockade strategy wasn't working as planned. After returning from his visit to the U.S. in May, he backed down from his refusal to negotiate unless the republic revoked its March 11 declaration, and proposed a freeze instead.

"Both sides can interpret a moratorium as their own victory," Lithuanian President Vytautas Landsbergis told the Parliament last week. "They can say it's not independence, and we can say it's the first step toward independence."
