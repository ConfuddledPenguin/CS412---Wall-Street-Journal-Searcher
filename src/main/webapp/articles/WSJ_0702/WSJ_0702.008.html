Foreign Exchange: Dollar Is Expected to Rise Against Mark In July Due to Effects of German Union

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/02/90

The dollar is expected to show gains against the mark in July, but remain steady against the yen, according to economists and traders. But the "German question," of whether West Germany can swallow East Germany without indigestion, likely won't be answered until late summer, they say. They do believe the mark will survive, but they say it won't be without a fight.

At the end of July, the dollar will gain three pfennigs to 1.691 marks, according to the average forecast of 10 economists and traders in New York, London, Frankfurt and Tokyo surveyed by Dow Jones International News Service on the eve of the German economic and monetary union, which was completed yesterday. The average forecast for the end of September is 1.712 marks to the dollar.

The dollar-yen exchange rate is expected to remain exceptionally steady, while the dollar is seen rising against the British pound and the Swiss franc, in part because the latter currencies are expected to shadow the mark's movements. The dollar is forecast at 154.30 yen at the end of July and 153.40 yen at the end of September.

Late Friday in New York, the dollar was quoted at 1.6610 marks, below 1.6730 marks late Thursday, and at 152.20 yen, below 153.15 yen late Thursday.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the dollar was trading at 152.15 yen; it closed Friday in Tokyo at 152.85 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6574 marks, down from Friday's Tokyo close of 1.6713 marks.

On Friday, Ingo Kuller, chief trader at Westdeutsche Landesbank Girozentrale in Duesseldorf, cast the two most bullish projections for the dollar against the mark in the belief that the dollar remains undervalued against the mark and is overdue for a rally.

Since the Berlin Wall was breached on Nov. 9, the dollar has risen about 10% against the mark, with most of the gains made in the first three months.

Mr. Kuller cautioned, however, that the effects of monetary union with East Germany make all forecasts extraordinarily difficult. "Who knows how the markets are going to react to monetary union?" Mr. Kuller mused, voicing a common uncertainty.

Ernst Pullmann, chief trader for Deutsche Girozentrale Deutsche Kommunalbank in Frankfurt, said the uncertainties and surprises coming out of East Germany "won't have as large an impact as some people think." Mr. Pullmann expects the mark to resume its long-term ascent as the summer progresses.

Japanese investors, Mr. Pullmann predicted, "will show up as much stronger investors," he said. "The Japanese are going to need marks to invest in East Germany. The recent firmness of the yen against the mark will prove to be only temporary."

The key question in economists' minds is how East Germans will react to having their pockets full of West German marks. The conversion of East German currency to West German currency boosted the money supply by about 10% overnight, and some believe East Germans will go on a buying spree, spurring inflation. One U.S. foreign exchange dealer evoked the image of kids in a candy store.

Many economists, however, say the prospect of massive unemployment in East Germany will spur more saving than spending.

Analysts who focused on interest rates were in agreement that German rates aren't headed down, while U.S. rates quite possibly are.

The wild card in the U.S. rate-easing scenario, according to Jerry Egan, chief dealer at the Bank of Boston, is the release Friday of U.S. employment data for June. "If the employment data show a weak number, there will be a lot more talk about easing," Mr. Egan said, but he doubted whether the Federal Reserve would make a move before autumn.

Impetus for a looser monetary policy could also come from an ambitious deficit-reduction proposal, which U.S. negotiators promised their Japanese counterparts in trade talks completed last week in return for Japanese concessions. U.S. President George Bush, in a news conference Friday, said a deficit-reduction pact likely would speed economic growth despite any new taxes, paving the way for the Federal Reserve to ease interest rates.

Most of the survey's respondents said the dollar's exchange rates with sterling and the Swiss franc will be dominated by those currencies' relationships with the mark.

At the end of June, the average forecast put the pound at $1.7160, below $1.7485 late Friday in New York. In 90 days, the pound is expected to be worth $1.6975.

"Always think in terms of the cross," said Eric Nickerson, vice president and economist at Bank of America in San Francisco. "The sterling-dollar is going to fall more out of the cross than from any mood regarding sterling-dollar directly," he said. The reason is sterling's probable entry in the exchange rate mechanism (ERM) of the European Monetary System (EMS) this year.

Geoffrey Dennis, an economist with James Capel & Co. Ltd., said sterling's strength rests "almost solely" with the market's perception that Britain is about to put the pound inside the ERM.

The pound now ranges close to where the market thinks Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher wants it to enter the ERM, leading some observers to believe the entry will come as early as July and no later than October.

The Swiss franc is seen slipping against the dollar but gaining on the mark. In 30 days, the forecast calls for the dollar to trade at 1.4318 francs, above 1.4150 francs late Friday in New York. In 90 days, the dollar would trade at 1.4490 francs, the survey predicted.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery jumped $5.80 to $357.90 an ounce. It was the highest close since June 5. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.6 million ounces.

In Hong Kong early Monday, gold was quoted at $357.45 an ounce.
