Politics & Policy: White House Will Try to Curb Soviet Space Trade, Help Boost Oil Production

  • Author: Bob Davis and Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 09/07/90

WASHINGTON -- The White House said it will try to restrict sharply the Soviet Union's commercial space launch business, but the Commerce Department offered to help the Soviets increase their oil production. The separate and seemingly contradictory moves reflect the administration's ambivalence about aiding economic development in the Soviet Union in the wake of the Cold War.

The administration, which only two weeks ago for the first time approved a plan in which Soviet rockets would be used to launch Western satellites, issued a statement spelling out its commercial space policy. The policy would allow Moscow to sell rockets, but prevent it from managing actual launches.

In coming negotiations with Moscow, the U.S. will insist that the Soviets drop plans to launch Western commercial satellites from Soviet launch pads; instead, Washington will require that Soviet rockets be launched by westerners from a single site outside the Soviet Union.

The Soviets also would have to agree to requirements aimed at preventing them from obtaining the technology used in the satellites it launches. Details weren't spelled out in the declassified version of the space policy that was made available to the public, though.

Further, a White House official said the administration would set a "goal" of requiring the Soviets to sell their launch services at market prices.

The U.S. believes it has a strong hand in negotiating tough conditions on Soviet launches because Washington must give its approval for the launch of satellites using U.S.-made parts. Nearly all communications satellites use such parts. "We have an historic opportunity to cut a deal {because} the Soviets are so eager to enter the market," said a White House official.

Frank Gaffney, president of the Center for Security Policy, a conservative Washington think-tank, said the policy would hurt U.S. rocket companies, which already face tough competition from Arianespace, the French rocket firm. "I wouldn't do anything to facilitate the Soviets' getting into the market," Mr. Gaffney said. "It's a real cannard to suggest that there's any way to insure {Soviet} services are fairly priced."

The administration plan would limit Soviet launches to a proposed space port in Cape York, Australia, which would be operated by USBI Co., a subsidiary of United Technologies Corp. A spokeswoman for USBI, noting that the White House two weeks ago endorsed its plan to use Soviet rockets to launch satellites from the site, said the company expects the State Department to issue an export license allowing it to run the space port within a few weeks. USBI said the first launches using Soviet rockets would take place in 1995 or 1996.

An administration official said that the Soviets objected during preliminary talks to the proposed U.S. restrictions. The U.S. plans talks concerning the Soviet Union and other space issues with the European Space Agency starting Sept. 24. Currently, several U.S. companies and Arianespace control nearly all the world's commercial launch business.

At the same time the White House was talking tough about launch business, Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher said the U.S. is offering the Soviets technology that can help them reverse the decline in their oil production. He said the know-how can be provided by a number of energy-related companies whose chief executive officers he is taking to the Soviet Union tomorrow.

"We're doing this for the benefit of both countries," Mr. Mosbacher told reporters here.

For instance, the commerce secretary said U.S. experience in "fracturing" -- injecting certain chemicals into an oil reservoir to increase its porosity and flow -- could be applied to Soviet fields with potentially quick results.

The American Petroleum Institute reports that Soviet oil production has declined from 11.7 million barrels a day in 1987 to about 11.2 million barrels a day this year as a result of inadequate investment and oil-field exhaustion.

Among the energy firms represented in the U.S. business delegation are Chevron Corp., Atlantic Richfield Co., Texaco Inc., and Dresser Industries Inc.
