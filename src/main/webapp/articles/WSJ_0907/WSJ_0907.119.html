Insurers' Net Plunged 21% on Higher Losses During the First Half

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/07/90

U.S. property/casualty insurers saw net income plunge 21% to $5.2 billion during the first half of 1990 as lower premium rates and higher underwriting losses continued to plague the industry. Because of the price war that has gripped the property/casualty industry for nearly four years now, premiums grew just 3.8% in the first half to $105.3 billion. However, underwriting losses jumped 29% to $10.6 billion from $8.2 billion in the 1989 first half.

Pretax operating profit fell 23% to $5.1 billion from $6.7 billion a year earlier.

Sean Mooney, an economist with the Insurance Information Institute, said the financial results for the property/casualty insurers deserve a "C-minus" because of decreasing after-tax profits and relatively low rate of return. "The industry continues to muddle along, with no clear sign of a financial upturn."

He said the second-quarter results were pretty telling, when net fell 3.6% to $2.7 billion for the industry. Relative to premiums, after-tax earnings in the second quarter stood at 5.1%. That means that for every premium dollar, the industry's profit was five cents. Mr. Mooney noted that such results were lower than the profit margin for most industries of seven cents on the sales dollar.

The industry data, compiled by the Insurance Services Office and the National Association of Independent Insurers, includes results for 96% of the property/casualty companies in the country.
