Technology: Scientists Find Gene That Causes a Form Of Cardiomyopathy

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/07/90

BOSTON -- Researchers here reported the discovery of a faulty gene that causes an inherited form of cardiomyopathy, a disease that thickens the walls of the heart and can cause sudden death. Cardiomyopathy recently came to national attention when it caused the death of Loyola Marymount University basketball star Hank Gathers, who collapsed March 4 during a game. The disease, which can stem from viral infection, alcohol abuse and other factors besides faulty genes, can cause irregular heartbeat, decreased cardiac pumping power and heart failure. The cause of Mr. Gathers's cardiomyopathy hasn't been identified.

The latest finding pinpointed the gene behind a rare, inherited form of the disease and should enable scientists to develop a genetic test to diagnose it early in such cases. That may enable doctors to mitigate the disease with drugs -- there presently is no cure for it. Researchers also hope the finding eventually will help them develop better treatments for cardiomyopathy.

An international team of scientists led by Harvard Medical School researchers Christine and Jonathan Seidman reported that the gene responsible for the inherited cardiomyopathy produces a heart muscle protein called the myosin heavy chain. Myosin plays a central role in muscle tissue's ability to contract. "It was very much a surprise that the myosin gene" was involved in the disease, said Christine Seidman, a cardiologist. "One doubted that a molecule as critical as that (could be faulty) and still permit survival."

The researchers, who reported their discovery in today's issue of the journal Cell, found that different families in which inherited cardiomyopathy occurs have different defects in the myosin gene. The genetic basis of the disease is further complicated by recent findings that show genetic defects not involving myosin can also cause cardiomyopathy, Dr. Seidman said.
