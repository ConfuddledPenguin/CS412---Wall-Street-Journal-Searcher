Foreign Exchange: Dollar Plummets Further Against Yen As a Weak U.S. Jobs Report Is Expected

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/07/90

NEW YORK -- For the first time in eleven months, the dollar fell below 141 yen and stayed there. The greenback was quoted in late New York trading at 140.70 yen, off one yen from late Wednesday and its worst North American close since Oct. 2, 1989, when it ended at 139.70 yen. Until yesterday, Wednesday's New York close had been the dollar's lowest for 1990.

In early trading in Tokyo Friday, the dollar was quoted at 140.45 yen, down from 141.65 yen at the close there Thursday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5558 marks, down from Thursday's Tokyo close of 1.5566 marks.

"The bottom just dropped out of" the dollar, said Javier Santalla, a New York trader for Nippon Credit Bank. "There was very little fanfare."

Mr. Santalla and others insisted that the day's activity was yen-based and not dollar-based. Though some speculators got a head start on the dollar-selling they expect to be spurred by a weak U.S. employment report today, the dollar fell only slightly against the mark. It actually gained against recent strong performers, such as the Swiss franc and Canadian dollar.

The U.S. currency was changing hands in late New York trading at 1.5550 marks, down from 1.5585 marks late Wednesday in New York. It climbed to 1.2975 Swiss francs from 1.2960 francs. Sterling was trading at $1.9125, up from $1.9043.

The U.S. and Japanese currencies are moving in opposite directions along the lines of each nation's interest rates.

The cost of Japanese funds, especially at the long end of the yield curve, has continued to rise following the Bank of Japan's decision last Thursday to raise its discount rate to 6% from 5.25%. Though at the time Bank of Japan Gov. Yasushi Mieno said the market shouldn't expect another tightening in the near future, the central bank's refusal since then to add enough liquidity to keep rates down has fueled doubts.

The result, Mr. Santalla said, is "a very strong case to be in yen."

U.S. rate watchers, meanwhile, are already counting on one round of easing by the U.S Federal Reserve and think a second may follow right on its heels if today's jobs report is particularly lame.

Already incorporated in dollar rates, said William Sterling, an economist at Merrill Lynch, is a Fed move to push the federal funds target rate down a quarter point to 7 3/4% from the currently assumed target of 8%. The only question is one of timing. A weaker-than-expected report could provoke an immediate response, he said.

A really bad number, Mr. Sterling said, could launch "the beginning of a series of moves" to ease credit. That, in turn, would "heighten concern that the U.S. is willing to pursue a policy of dollar depreciation to invigorate the economy."

Another cloud over the dollar is the federal budget deficit. At a rally in Topeka, Kansas, yesterday, President Bush pledged to eliminate the budget gap in five years -- or two years later than the time frame set by the Gramm-Rudman-Hollings law.

After breaking below 141 yen in early European trading, the dollar rose above that level as New York began trading before declining again.

Technical charts signaled a dollar-buying opportunity at 140.60 yen, and kept the greenback from falling further, said Susan Stearns, a senior corporate advisor in New York for Chicago's Harris Trust & Savings Bank.

Though yen-buying dominated the day, the Japanese currency rose only marginally against its German counterpart. Less in favor than the yen, the mark is nevertheless being supported by a psychological floor at 90 yen and expectations that the Bundesbank will match the recent Japanese rate increase.

The mark closed New York trading at 90.482 yen, down from 90.921 yen late Wednesday.

The Canadian dollar suffered from expectations of interest-rate easing in its home country, falling to 86.48 U.S. cents in New York from 86.77 cents Wednesday.

In Europe yesterday, the dollar ended little changed from where it began the European trading day, as dealers said participants awaited developments on several fronts.

In Asia, the dollar ended active dealings mostly lower as a brief flurry of covering short dollar positions was overcome by bearish sentiment toward the U.S. economy. The U.S. currency finished Thursday in Tokyo at 141.65 yen, below 142.30 yen late Wednesday.

News about an American in Kuwait being shot added momentum to a round of short-covering that had gotten under way after the dollar dropped to 141.25 yen in early Asian trading.

But after the wave of short-covering lifted the dollar as high as 142.45 yen, continued pessimism about the U.S. economy helped prompt dollar sales by life insurance companies and other institutional investors, pushing the U.S. currency back down.

Separately, on the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $389.70 an ounce, up $3.60. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.7 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $390.25 an ounce.
