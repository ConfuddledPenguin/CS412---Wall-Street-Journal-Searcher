Daimler-Benz Aerospace Lines Coming of Age

  • Author: Susan Carey
  • Date: 09/07/90

FARNBOROUGH, England -- Juergen E. Schrempp, the 45-year-old chairman of Daimler-Benz AG's Deutsche Aerospace AG unit, has spent eight months stitching four disparate West German aerospace companies into a cohesive whole. Now, his handiwork is on display to the world.

Deutsche Aerospace is here at the big biennial show taking up at least half the space in the German pavilion with its airplanes and a plush hospitality chalet.

It is a heady time for the four companies involved, each of which is at least 50%-owned by Deutsche Aerospace, which was establshed in May 1989 to embrace Daimler's aerospace activities. Placed together, Deutsche Aerospace's product range is vast and its potential appears enormous.

The four companies involved are: Dornier G.m.b.H., a maker of small aircraft; Motoren & Turbinen-Union G.m.b.H., known as MTU, which makes engines; defense contractor Messerschmitt-Boelkow-Blohm G.m.b.H., known as MBB; and Telefunken Systemtechnik G.m.b.H., a maker of defense electronics gear.

Deutsche Aerospace is participating in the building of Airbus Industrie jetliners, engines from United Technologies Corp.'s Pratt & Whitney unit in the U.S., a proposed Eurofighter project, radar systems, the Tornado fighter, medical systems and helicopters. Besides MBB, Airbus Industrie's partners are Aerospatiale of France, British Aerospace PLC and Construcciones Aeronauticas S.A. of Spain.

"If you're not one who can say, `I can do everything,' you don't get the juicy piece of the steak," Mr. Schrempp says.

But the banquet hasn't quite begun. Although mighty Daimler-Benz has made progress in reorganizing its new aerospace acquisitions, the project is far from complete. Until it is, Deutsche Aerospace will find it difficult to bite into the meaty military, airliner and helicopter projects the world's aerospace concerns are increasingly doing in partnerships.

Nevertheless, Mr. Schrempp says, the continuing work means "we are bringing much more to the table" than when the four companies were small, lacking in resources and participating in industrial ventures only on an ad hoc basis.

Today, "there is no company in the world that is going to make a plane on its own," he says. "But we want to take a lead" in such partnerships. And only as the larger, richer and more-focused Deutsche Aerospace can German industry approach such ventures as equals of U.S. defense companies or, closer to home, of British Aerospace, Aerospatiale S.A. and Aeritalia S.p.A.

Hence the announcements at the show that Deutsche Aerospace is talking to partners about joint helicopter projects in Europe and the U.S. and about a European regional jetliner. The company also reiterated that its wide-ranging discussions with the Mitsubishi Group of Japan are continuing.

But it is a big job to amalgamate four companies and to strive for synergies with Daimler-Benz, which itself has been reorganized. "We have made a good start but we aren't finished," Mr. Schrempp says. "At some levels, we are very thin. Elsewhere, we have to reduce the bureaucracy. We have to restructure the production side. Dornier and MBB were fierce competitors. Now, they have to be friends."

On the organizational chart, the plan is to regroup activities among the four companies by functions. Thus, Dornier's airplanes and MBB's participation in Airbus will be grouped into the aircraft division, while pieces of various companies' satellite and space activities will be funneled into the space division. The other two divisions are defense systems and propulsion systems.

The cleanup exercise also means minority holdings in two Deutsche Aerospace companies must be sorted out. The state of Hamburg recently swapped its 7% stake in MBB for a piece of Deutsche Aerospace, Mr. Schrempp said. But Bremen and Bavaria still hold 24% of MBB. Bremen has said it wants to sell its shares, but a sale or swap will have to be organized with Bavaria. Likewise, Deutsche Aerospace is in talks with members of the Dornier family -- who hold 18% of the voting rights and 46% of equity in Dornier -- to win the freedom to manage the company.

There are other details to attend to. At the end of 1996, Deutsche Aerospace must buy Bonn's 20% stake in Deutsche Airbus, the MBB unit that participates in Airbus Industrie. But that sale is conditional on a number of things, including Airbus becoming a corporation instead of a murky grouping of industrial interests.
