Letters to the Editor: Not All Wealth Transfers Head South

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/07/90

I was fascinated to read Reps. Howard Wolpe (D., Mich.) and Frank Horton (R., N.Y.) bemoaning the "transfer of wealth" from the Northeast and Midwest to the Southwest, which they perceive taking place as a result of the federal bailout of the S&L mess ("The S&L Bailout: Looting the North for Texas' Benefit," editorial page, Aug. 14). Apparently they believe in the integrity of the union only in those cases when their respective constituencies stand on the plus side of the federal ledger. If Messrs. Wolpe and Horton are truly concerned with balancing these types of regional transfers, perhaps their analysis should begin with their regions repaying the Southwest the tens (if not hundreds) of billions of dollars that flowed to the Northeast and Midwest by virtue of federal price controls on oil (1973-80) and natural gas (1954-present). These misguided regulations not only encourage a massive waste of valuable and nonrenewable natural resources and subsidized imported oil, but also represent a "transfer of wealth" as real as any government insurance or transfer-payment program.

It is highly unlikely that Congress had any regional bias in mind when it handed the keys to the federal treasury to the hustlers and con men by so liberalizing our banking laws. Therefore, its members should not now be heard to complain that their respective states didn't attract their share of crooks.

At least in that one regard federal price controls differ from the S&L bailout: Price controls were designed to loot one region of the country for the benefit of the others.

Michael L. Pate

Tulsa, Okla.

Texas may indeed be the barn door through which the majority of S&L dollars have flown, but the door was unlocked and opened wide by ill-conceived legislation sponsored and passed by Messrs. Wolpe and Horton's fellow wise men in Washington.

J. Potasznik

Mesquite, Texas

---

The authors assume a loss -- looting and benefit that may be misplaced -- while overlooking another under their noses.

Taxpayers are, to be sure, paying for losses at today's depressed price levels, and are asked to finance those losses over 30 to 40 years. But the true wealth transfer may well belong to asset buyers who benefit from cyclical recoveries in asset values. This is attributable to Texas only if the buyers are from Texas.

The Commerce Department report on Texas's resurgence during the 1990s, to which the authors refer, suggests that the "glut" of property dampening real-estate values today is setting the stage for a large wealth transfer to buyers to be realized tomorrow. But it is in the nature of Washington politics to be mesmerized by yesterday's problems.

Stuart D. Root

Pelham, N.Y.

---

I think the situation will be healthy for Northeastern states in the long run. The Northeast is so socialistic, backward and crime-infested that like Russia it will have to go completely broke before returning to normal capitalism and some semblance of social order. (Been to New York City lately?) So perhaps Texas is doing the Northeast a favor by drawing money away and helping to speed up that "going broke" process that will lead to true Northeastern economic and social reform. Bitter medicine but necessary. Bravo for Texas.

Jack Pearson

Seattle
