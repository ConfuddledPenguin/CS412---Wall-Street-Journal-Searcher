Credit Markets: Bond Prices Hold Firm Despite Rising Oil Prices As Traders Await Today's Employment Report

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Robert Sherman
  • Date: 09/07/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices barely budged as investors again shrugged off rising oil prices and braced for today's potentially market-moving employment report. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended 1/8 point higher, a gain of just $1.25 for bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, fell to 8.95% from 8.96% on Wednesday. Short-term securities ended unchanged to slightly lower.

But trading was very light. "Everyone is waiting for the employment report," said Philip Braverman, senior vice president and chief economist at DKB Securities Corp.

"There was barely any movement in price and barely any activity on the trading floors," said Kevin Logan, chief economist at Swiss Bank Corp. of America.

The employment report has become one of the market's most potent economic indicators, with the ability to dramatically sway bond market sentiment and cause wild gyrations in bond prices. Because of this potential, trading often slows ahead of the report's release.

Among other things, investors hope to find clues about the future direction of interest rates and Federal Reserve monetary policy. There is widespread speculation that if the report shows clear signs of economic weakness -- rising unemployment or slow jobs growth -- the Federal Reserve may reduce short-term interest rates in an effort to stave off an economic recession.

But if the jobs report is ambiguous, the future direction of interest rates remains uncertain.

A Dow Jones Capital Markets Report consensus of economists expects nonfarm payrolls rose 83,000 in August, excluding the layoff of census workers, in marked contrast to the growth in payrolls early this year and in 1989.

With the possibility of the Fed easing dominating market talk, other events had little impact. The market largely brushed aside a rise in oil prices. October oil futures ended the day at $31.43 a barrel, up $1.66 at the New York Mercantile Exchange.

Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan's comments also failed to inspire trading interest. In a written response to questions from the House Banking Committee, Mr. Greenspan said that if credit conditions tightened further, the central bank "might find it appropriate to adjust policy."

Meanwhile, the World Bank plans to sell its third global bond issue in the next few weeks, with an offering that will total between $1.5 billion and $2 billion.

As with the prior issues, the new securities are designed to trade globally 24 hours a day and will be underwritten and distributed simultaneously in Europe, the U.S. and Japan.

A group of 15 investment dealers will bring the issue to market, with West Germany's Deutsche Bank Capital Markets Group and the U.S.'s Salomon Brothers Inc. serving as joint lead managers.

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 97 27/32, up from 97 22/32 on Wednesday while the yield eased to 8.95% from 8.96%. The latest two-year notes ended at 100 4/32, down slightly from 100 5/32 on Wednesday while the yield rose to 8.04% from 8.02%.

Short-term rates were little changed. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.39% for a bond equivalent yield of 7.61% while the rate on six-month bills ended at 7.33% to yield 7.70%.

Junk, Corporate Bonds

The Resolution Trust Corp.'s liquidation of nearly $30 million in junk bonds did little to rattle the high-yield market.

Most junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, ended unchanged to about 1/4 point lower.

A spokesman for the RTC, the government agency set up to bail out the nation's troubled thrifts, confirmed that the agency was in the market selling about $30 million in high-yield high-risk securities.

Although the spokesman declined to specify which bonds the RTC liquidated, market participants said they were high-quality, liquid issues. "They were all names the market wanted to buy, and were absorbed easily by the market," said one high-yield trader.

Among the names on the RTC's bid list were junk bonds of Duracell Holdings Corp., Coltec Industries and Playtex Family Products, market sources said.

Elsewhere, in the high-yield market, junk bonds of Burlington Holdings Inc. continued to weaken on concerns that the textile maker will be financially squeezed if a recession materializes.

Burlington's 14.25% subordinated debentures, due in 1999, were quoted at 67, down two points, while the company's 13.875% subordinated debentures, due in 1996, were quoted at 72, down two points.

Municipal Issues

Municipal bonds ended unchanged to 1/8 point higher in very quiet trading.

The market for new issues was moderately busy, with more than $402 million of new debt priced.

The most talked about issue yesterday was an $84 million offering by the New York City Industrial Development Agency on behalf of American Airlines Inc. via a group led by Bear Stearns & Co. The 29-year and 30-year bonds were priced to yield 8.06% and 8.07%, respectively. American Airlines is a unit of AMR Corp.

Analysts said yields for the bonds were relatively high for an issue that is largely tax-exempt, a reflection of the market's trepidations about the long-term prospects of the airline industry. Still, the attractive yields lured large numbers of both institutional and individual investors and allowed underwriters to reprice the bonds and lower the yields from initial levels of 8.12% and 8.13%.

Although the bonds are subject to alternative minimum tax, a manager at Bear Stearns said the bonds are triple-tax-exempt for residents of New York state. For investors whose income from the bonds would be fully tax exempt, the yields on the American Airline bonds would translate into a taxable equivalent yield of over 11%.

"The good news is that the yield is very attractive," said Bruce Whiteford, manager of fixed-income trading at Chase Manhattan U.S. Private Banking. "But given the long maturity, uncertainty of where interest rates are going and concerns about the airline industry, these could be very volatile bonds," he said.

Mr. Whiteford estimates that if interest rates rise one-half percentage point, the price of these bonds would fall about five points, or about $50 for each $1,000 face amount.

The proceeds from the bond sale will be used to refurbish and rebuild certain areas at New York's LaGuardia and Kennedy airports.

Among benchmark issues yesterday, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bond due 2018 were unchanged at 98 1/2 to yield 7.31%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities showed slight gains in late trading.

Government National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% issue for September delivery was quoted at 98 23/32, up 4/32. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp.'s 9 1/2% issue and Federal National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% issue for September delivery both were up 3/32, at 98 8/32 and 98 26/32, respectively.

The primary market experienced a slight upswing in real estate mortgage investment conduit, or Remic, issuance.

Citibank N.A., a Citicorp unit, announced a $350 million Fannie Mae 90-121 issue, backed by Fannie Mae's 9 1/2% securities. Nomura Securities announced a $300 million Fannie Mae 90-122 issue, also backed by Fannie Mae's 9 1/2% issue.

First Boston Corp. announced First Boston Mortgage Securities Corp. 1990-1, a $137 million Remic collateralized by whole loans.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese bond market, already reeling from a renewed rise in oil prices and higher short-term interest rates, deteriorated further yesterday on news that a U.S. citizen was shot in the Middle East.

The yield on the benchmark No. 119 10year bond rose to nearly 8.3%, but eased late in the day to end at 8.24%. The price of the benchmark issue closed at 82.50, down 0.37 points. (When calculated using the semi-annual compound method to compare yields across markets, the yield on the No. 119 bond ended at 7.56%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)

The State Department said an American citizen was shot and wounded in Kuwait City by Iraqi police while trying to evade capture at his apartment. News of the shooting weakened the Japanese stock market and lifted the dollar.

Major European bond markets ended higher in light trading.
