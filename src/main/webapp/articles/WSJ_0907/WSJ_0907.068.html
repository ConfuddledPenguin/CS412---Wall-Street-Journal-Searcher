THE TOKYO CONNECTION

South American drug rings for the first time have been caught using a Tokyo connection to launder their cash, Japanese and U.S. officials said.

Police have concluded that a Colombian drug syndicate transferred about $1.8 million through a Japanese bank branch in Hong Kong and a Swiss bank branch in Tokyo. Japanese newspapers quoted police as saying the money, which ended up in a Colombian account at a U.S. bank, was intended for use by a drug syndicate controlled by a reputed member of the Medellin drug cartel.

Authorities disclosed the transaction on the arrival in Tokyo of U.S. Attorney General Dick Thornburgh, who is in Asia to discuss law-enforcement issues with other attorneys general in the region.

Mr. Thornburgh said the case "made the point very graphically" that "any modern, efficient banking institution is fair game for the process of laundering these billions of dollars of drug proceeds."

ARGENTINE SALE IS SUSPENDED

An Argentine judge ordered the suspension of the sale of the state-owned airline Aerolineas Argentinas to a consortium led by Spain's Iberia because the proposed buyers made the down payment by letter of credit instead of cash. Administrative Law Judge Oscar Garzon Funes gave the Public Works Ministry eight days to explain why it accepted the letter of credit; he said he would then decide whether to annul the sale. The consortium agreed last July to buy 85% of Aerolineas for $260 million and the retirement of $2 billion of Argentina's foreign debt.

A POSTPONEMENT IN CHINA

China has pushed back the opening of its first futures market to Oct. 12, representing the latest of a long series of delays. William Grossman, managing director of the Chicago Board of Trade's Asia-Pacific office, said in Tokyo that Beijing decided to postpone the September opening of the wheat-futures market in Zhengzhou, the capital of Henan Province, because of conflicts arising from the Asian Games, which begin in China later this month. China, is using the CBOT as a technical adviser.

GERMAN-SOVIET TRADE

West Germany will reportedly guarantee delivery agreements of East German companies to the Soviet Union. The Berliner Morgenpost, a West German newspaper, said that an agreement being worked on would require both sides to dismantle or remove "trade barriers of every type" in a step-by-step fashion. The report said Bonn would even guarantee support for East German companies that are in precarious financial condition if they supply important goods to the Soviet Union.

TAIWAN'S GOLD IMPORTS

Concern over the Persian Gulf situation and worries over rising inflation sent Taiwan's imports of gold and coins soaring in August, bullion dealers said. Finance Ministry statistics showed that gold imports totaled 13.24 tons valued at $163.6 million in August, tripling the 4.27 tons valued at $51.4 million imported in July. Switzerland was Taiwan's biggest gold-bar supplier, shipping 8.13 tons of gold to Taiwan in August.

A DOG REGISTER FOR THE EC?

The European Parliament's environment committee wants a register set up for the European Community's millions of dogs. Registering pets could help identify strays and control the spread of rabies when border checks are abolished, parliament member Anita Pollack argued. Before entering the EC, foreign dogs would have to undergo blood tests to certify they are rabies-free. The register wouldn't replace the six-month quarantine in the United Kingdom and Ireland.

CONDEMNING ANTI-SEMITISM

Roman Catholic leaders condemned anti-Semitism and racism as they closed a Catholic-Jewish conference in Prague that looked at growing anti-Semitic incidents in Eastern Europe.

A statement issued during the four-day meeting said delegates from 16 nations gave special attention to "recent manifestations of anti-Semitism" in former Eastern bloc nations since the fall of tightly run communist societies.

The statement came at the end of the first formal meeting in five years of the Pontifical Commission on Religious Relations with the Jews and the International Jewish Committee on Interreligious Consultations. The conference's plan to fight anti-Semitism includes deleting racially or religiously divisive material from textbooks and establishment of courses for priests to counter anti-Jewish sentiment.

The plan also called for swift translation and dissemination of recent Vatican statements on Catholic-Jewish relations.
