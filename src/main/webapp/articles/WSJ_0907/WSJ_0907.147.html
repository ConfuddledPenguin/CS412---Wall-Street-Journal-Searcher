Skis Rossignol Plans to Acquire U.S. Golf Concern

  • Author: Peter Goldstein Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/07/90

PARIS -- Skis Rossignol S.A., plagued by mounting losses after three winters of poor snow conditions, plans to buy a U.S. golf company in an effort to pull itself out of the red through greater diversification. Rossignol General Manager Jean-Jacques Bompard said in an interview that the company hopes to present to the annual meeting Sept. 19 a finalized agreement to buy a small but aggressive U.S. golf concern for about 100 million francs ($19.1 million) to balance its ski-based product line.

He said the unidentified target isn't yet a major player in the U.S. golf market, but that Rossignol thinks its technical and marketing acumen can propel it into the big leagues. "The talks are well under way. I expect a decision in the next 10 days," said Mr. Bompard, noting that Rossignol was attracted to the company because of its "very competitive line of high-quality equipment."

This would represent an initial step by the world's leading ski maker to face up to anticipated losses of 85 million francs for the fiscal year ending March 31, 1991, following a 9.3 million franc deficit in fiscal 1990. Revenue, meanwhile, is expected to slip to 1.39 billion francs in fiscal 1991 from 1.47 billion francs a year earlier.

A lack of snow in the Alps and the western U.S. for three consecutive seasons, combined with the French franc's recent strength against the yen and the dollar, has caused most of the damage.

Grenoble-based Rossignol has begun a three-year, 100 million-franc modernization program at two main ski factories to try to cut costs and improve quality. The plants have combined annual production capacity of one million pairs of skis.

Mr. Bompard said both programs will be self-financed, drawing in part on resources derived from a 100 million franc capital boost in November 1989 that helped reduced the group's debt-to-capital ratio to 47.5% from 95% two years ago.

However, doubts about Rossignol remain in the French investment community. Stock analysts aren't confident about management's plans to expand summer-sports activities beyond the minor tennis operations that currently account for about 6% of revenue.

Rossignol has made tennis rackets that carry its name since 1977 and sells an average 350,000 rackets a year, well below sales of leading brands Dunlop, Kennex, Prince, Wilson and Head.

Analysts question to what extent the company's balance sheet will benefit, citing the example of sporting goods counterpart Salomon S.A., which gets 28% of its revenue from its Taylor-Made golf operations. Salomon had a loss of 91 million francs in the fiscal year ended March 31, prompting work force cutbacks scheduled to start this month. Even though golf sales grew 45% in the quarter ended June 30, its total revenue was off 26%.

But golf or no golf, analysts say, Rossignol will remain hostage to the foreign-exchange market. As long as the franc holds firm, sales figures will suffer.
