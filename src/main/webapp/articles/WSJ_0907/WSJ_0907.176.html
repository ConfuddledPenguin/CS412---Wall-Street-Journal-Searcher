Heard on the Street: `Case of the Hairline Cracks' on Compaq's PCs Is Just One of Several Woes Depressing Stock

  • Author: Jim Bartimo
  • Date: 09/07/90

A company declaring a stock split usually hopes to boost its shares by making them seem more affordable. But a recent split hasn't done a thing for Compaq Computer. The personal computer maker, long a Wall Street favorite, split 2-for-1 in early July. The shares were above a split-adjusted 61 1/2. But as the weeks rolled by and both Dean Witter and Oppenheimer & Co. lowered their opinions to "hold" from "buy," the stock has plummeted 34%, to 45 1/2.

Technology stocks are always prone to zigzags, of course, and the Mideast crisis is reason enough for a drop. But that alone doesn't explain what's been happening to Compaq. Last Friday, for instance, the Houston Chronicle reported that some of Compaq's notebook-sized laptop computers were developing hairline cracks after brief use.

It was a blow for quality-conscious Compaq, which says this is the first widespread defect it has ever had in a product it shipped to market. Word of the potentially damaging report even reached Benjamin C. Rosen, Compaq's chairman, on the golf course.

"The cracked cases are an old story," insists Daniel C. Benton, a Goldman Sachs analyst. A similar report surfaced six months ago. Yet a Compaq spokeswoman doesn't blithely shrug off the story. "We originally believed it was just one lot," she says, "but now we're not so sure."

Houston-based Compaq took another hit Wednesday, as semiconductor analysts lowered estimates for chip companies and cited a slowdown in demand for chips from personal computer makers.

Goldman Sachs and Merrill Lynch have been projecting profit of $5 a share for this year, up from a split-adjusted $3.88 a share in 1989. They have continued to recommend the shares. Edmund Spelman of Oppenheimer thinks their projections may be "too aggressive," however, and consensus per-share estimates by analysts are $4.66 for 1990 and $5.53 for 1991, according to Thomson Financial Networks.

Analysts continue to praise the company's management and high-performance product line. A question mark is Systempro, an important and costly new class of computer for business users announced last fall. "A lot of earnings projections are pegged to Systempro sales," says analyst Eric C. Zimits at Rauscher Pierce Refsnes.

Compaq has said it expected to sell only a few thousand units in the first year, and is right on target so far. Yet some analysts conditioned by Compaq's past triumphs expected much more, perhaps as many as 25,000 sold by year end. "The Systempro makes Wall Street nervous," says Rick Martin of Prudential-Bache.

Compaq also now faces serious price competition from Dell Computer, an Austin, Texas, mail-order computer firm with a modest following in data-processing circles.

Last week, Dell announced two Systempro lookalikes, including a high-end 425TE that runs about $12,000 compared with Compaq's Systempro at $17,000 or higher. Speculation persists that International Business Machines soon will announce a powerful personal computer much like Systempro and priced competitively.

Once Systempro stirred concern on the Street, ghosts began looming around every corner. Analysts even fretted about Compaq's 21% share of Conner Peripherals after word spread that disk-drive maker Quantum Corp. of Milpitas, Calif., would soon offer a drive that could erode Conner's market share.

Some analysts also brood about a laptop that IBM is said to be introducing to challenge Compaq's best-selling Compaq LTE. Although Oppenheimer's Mr. Spelman says "IBM's bark is usually worse than its bite" in the personal computer market, he believes IBM has finally "gotten its act together" and could pose a threat.

Compaq is expected to score points with a new laptop of its own in the next few weeks. Enthusiasm for that introduction has been tempered by concern over an onslaught of Japanese competition. "The {profit} margins on laptops are going to be coming down," predicts Eugene G. Glazer of Dean Witter Reynolds.

Foreign markets have been a bragging point for Compaq in recent years. Yet one analyst believes that a seasonal third-quarter slowdown in European sales will hit Compaq harder this year than in the past; it got 45% of 1989 sales from Europe, compared with 39% a year earlier.

Analysts' misgivings come in the midst of a slowing U.S. computer market, which does little to soothe Wall Street's itchy trigger fingers. "The PC business is headed into a period of restructuring that will last a number of years," says Mark D. Stahlman, analyst with Alex. Brown & Sons.
