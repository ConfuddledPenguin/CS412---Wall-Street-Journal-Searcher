Who's News: Daiwa Appoints John Carlson as Head Of Asset-Backed Securities Unit in U.S.

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 09/07/90

The U.S. subsidiary of Japan's Daiwa Securities Co. said it named John H. Carlson, former president and chief executive of Security Pacific Corp.'s securities unit, as an executive vice president and head of a new asset-backed securities unit. Asset-backed securities are fixed-income securities whose principal and interest payments are supported by assets such as mortgages and credit cards.

In the past few years, the market has grown at breakneck speed, particularly as commercial banks and finance companies have sought to unload assets to profit quickly from loans they have written, free up funds for other uses and meet new international capital guidelines. The mortgage-backed securities market totals more than $800 billion. By the end of last year, the global market for non-mortgage asset-backed securities had grown to $62 billion from a negligible amount in 1985, according to IDD Information Services.

Saying that Mr. Carlson was always his prime candidate for the asset-securitization job, Stephen W. Bradley, co-head of Daiwa's capital markets operations, added, "John has intimate knowledge of the product, from the origination point through the structuring and analytical stages to actual sales."

Mr. Carlson, 40 years old, said he enjoyed working at Security Pacific, which he joined in 1987 from Merrill Lynch & Co. "However," he said, "my background is in the securities business; I came to SecPac in anticipation of significant Glass-Steagall reform which hasn't materialized, and Daiwa is a global securities firm." For years, big money-center banks have tried to persuade Congress to abandon or change the Glass-Steagall Act separating commercial and investment banking.

At Security Pacific, Mr. Carlson was jointly responsible for a unit with $100 million in capital and more than $300 million in annual revenue. His unit also co-managed the bank's first residential collateralized mortgage obligation and a $750 million credit-card-backed transaction.

Mr. Carlson said he was attracted by the chance to work for Mr. Bradley, with whom he had worked at Merrill Lynch, and by Daiwa's reputation. "My intent is to develop an asset-securitization business leveraging off Daiwa's strong reputation and market share in Europe and the Far East," he said.

He said he plans to begin originating asset-backed securities both in the U.S. and overseas as well as using securitization techniques in helping Daiwa's corporate clients to restructure their balance sheets by reducing their debt-service payments.

In jumping into asset-backed securities, Daiwa, to some extent, is playing catch-up with big U.S. firms. While Daiwa has distributed these securities and advised clients in the U.S., Europe and Japan on issuing them, it hasn't yet originated any.

In Mr. Carlson, Daiwa is hiring someone with an eclectic background that includes graduate studies in meteorology, competitive sailing and a bit of entrepreneurship.

He nearly completed a doctorate in meteorology at Massachusetts Institute of Technology. "I wrote seven of 10 chapters on the genesis and persistence of droughts in the continental U.S. and left for the Street," he said. At MIT, Mr. Carlson sold long-term weather forecasts to commodity firms and a "permafrost model to the U.S. Army for cold-regions research engineering laboratories." That, he added, was "to keep from starving in graduate school."

An avid sailor, Mr. Carlson recalls a race from Bermuda to Marblehead, Mass., in which his boat, after losing its drinking water, confronted 20-foot waves and 40-knot winds. "It was a good preparation for Wall Street," he said. "It was when I was a kid and didn't know any better."
