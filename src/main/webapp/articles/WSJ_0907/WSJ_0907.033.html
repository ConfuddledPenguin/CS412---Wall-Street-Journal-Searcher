Letters to the Editor: Job Discrimination Hits the Symptomless

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/07/90

I was distressed to read your July 9 article "Genetic Testing May Mark Some People as Undesirable to Employers, Insurers." Job discrimination against people with polycystic kidney disease is so unnecessary. It is wrong to deny a person a job because of a genetic test for a disease that may never cause symptoms. The individual is denied the important role of participating in the work force and the work force is denied the advantage of a multitalented large group of employees.

The article mentions that this discrimination may result before the course of the disease is determined, inasmuch as debilitating symptoms might not even occur until a person reaches late middle age. This is an important fact because only 50% of people with polycystic kidney disease reach the stage of renal failure. The advanced technologies of dialysis and transplantation may even give those with kidney failure the benefit of participating in the work force until retirement.

Since 1982, the Polycystic Kidney Research Foundation has promoted research into the cause, cure and treatment of polycystic kidney disease. Currently, more than a dozen major medical centers in this country and abroad are involved in research projects related to this disease. It is my sincere hope that this common genetic illness will be conquered within the decade of the '90s.

Joseph H. Breuning

Chairman

Polycystic Kidney Research Foundation

Kansas City, Mo.
