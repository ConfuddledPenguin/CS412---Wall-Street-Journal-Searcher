Oil Industry Maps Strategy to Combat Spills, Leaving Alaska Out of the Picture

  • Author: Allanna Sullivan
  • Date: 09/07/90

After a year of study, the oil industry yesterday unveiled a detailed $800 million, five-year program to improve its ability to fight catastrophic oil spills off the U.S. coast. But the plan, which will create five regional response centers nationwide, doesn't provide for such a center in Alaska, the site of the worst oil spill in U.S. waters. And that leaves Alaskan officials cold. "It's ludicrous not to locate one of the primary response centers in Alaska," said Terence O'Malley, spokesman for Alaska Gov. Steve Cowper. "Alaska, which provides 25% of U.S. oil production, has more coastline than the rest of the U.S. combined."

The program creates two organizations. The Marine Spill Response Corp., a privately financed non-profit group, will be responsible for cleaning up any huge oil spills occurring in the lower 48 states. The Marine Preservation Association, composed of twenty oil companies and a pipeline company, will use the services of the Response Corp. and provide the funding for the organization. The cost to the membership, which is expected to grow to include terminal and shipping operators, will be based on the quantities of oil they handle in the waters covered by the plan.

The newly created agencies replace the Petroleum Industry Response Organization, formed by the industry several months after the tanker Exxon Valdez rammed an underwater reef in Alaskan waters last year, causing the largest oil spill in U.S. history. At that time, the industry had projected that creation of a nationwide response network would cost $250 million and take about a year. Officials of the Response Corp. now project it will take about 30 months to get the centers established and equipment in place.

The new estimates project sharply higher costs to get the new program off the ground, because "the industry initially reacted in crisis mode," said G. Stephen Duca, vice president of readiness for the Response Corp. "It hadn't had the opportunity to check the prices of things when it made the projection."

For example, Mr. Duca said, the industry had originally assumed that there were enough existing vessels and barges for their needs. However, when they checked, they found that few were available for charter or lease, so 16 vessels and 22 barges will have to be built. Of the $800 million allotted, $315 million will go toward the construction of boats and purchase of equipment.

The Response Corp., which will employ nearly 400 people and be based in Washington, is being designed to manage large spills of as much as 216,000 barrels. It would not respond to small spills unless local contractors and cooperatives responsible for handling them are unable to.

The plan puts regional centers in the New York-New Jersey area; Port Everglades, Fla.; Lake Charles, La.; Port Heuneme, Calif.; and Seattle. Each regional center will have four to six areas where equipment and sometimes vessels and personnel will be located.

Alaskan officials say the plan is exactly what's needed to combat huge spills, and with Alaska's vast coastline, those officials want to be part of that program. But, according to Mr. Duca, some state regulations passed earlier this year stand in the way of a regional center in Alaska.

Mr. Duca said that the industry found the liability issue in Alaska especially troubling. Alaska's oil-spill laws now have a clause that provides for damage claims without proof of gross negligence. It also demands that cleanup crews be able to respond within 72 hours to spills 50% bigger than the Exxon Valdez, he said. "Nobody can reasonably meet a 72-hour deadline for a 300,000-barrel spill," Mr. Duca said.

In an announcement, officials of the Response Corp. said that they intend to have "an appropriate response and cleanup role in Alaska." But they added that the role "is not yet fully defined."

Recently passed federal oil spill legislation limits liability but doesn't pre-empt some state laws -- including Alaska's -- that don't limit liability. Mr. Duca said the new corporation is currently holding talks with some of these states in efforts to have them adjust their regulations to the new federal standard. "We need constructive dialogue," he said.

Currently, Alaska must depend on cleanup plans implemented by Alyeska Pipeline Co. And in case of a severe crisis, the state could get additional assistance from the regional center to be established near Seattle. But "Seattle is pretty far from Prince William Sound," said Gary Hayden, deputy commissioner of the Alaska Department of Environmental Conservation.

Alyeska, which is responsible for handling spills in Prince William Sound only, has beefed up its response capability since its poor performance during the Exxon Valdez disaster. But asked whether Alyeska's ability to respond to a giant spill is now sufficient, Mr. Hayden said, "Not today, it isn't."
