Iraq May Owe Italian Firms Total Of $11.89 Billion

  • Author: John Perrotta Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/07/90

ROME -- Italian companies' debt exposure to Iraq could total as much as 14 trillion lire ($11.89 billion), among the highest in the world, the Foreign Trade Ministry said. About half of that figure represents the debt exposure of SACE, Italy's export-credit guarantee agency, state-owned Banca Nazionale del Lavoro and Cantieri Navali Italiani S.p.A., known as Fincantieri. The latter is part of Istituto per la Ricostruzione Industriale, or IRI, Italy's sprawling state holding company.

"We are surely among the countries most exposed to the Saddam regime, but that fact does not restrict the (Italian) government in any way. In times like these, political interests prevail over economic ones," Italian Foreign Trade Minister Renato Ruggiero told participants at an Aspen Institute conference last week in Venice.

SACE stands to lose $1.87 billion, while BNL is owed as much as $2.8 billion, and Fincantieri $1.32 billion.

SACE's exposure is in potential insurance claims. A spokesman said that almost all the claims materialize, if at all, in two to six years.

BNL's credits largely are the result of loans in the form of letters of credit issued from its Atlanta, Ga., branch that weren't authorized by the head office.

Fincantieri's credits stem from a 1980 order to provide Iraq with a fleet of 11 warships -- one logistical-support ship, four frigates and six corvettes, a company spokesman said. Fincantieri, which is based in Trieste, subcontracted much of the work to other companies.

Fincantieri shared 61% of the value of the 1980 contract with Selenia-Industrie Elettroniche Associate S.p.A. and Elettronica San Giorgio Elsag S.p.A., both of which also are members of the IRI group. Fincantieri subcontracted 28% to Oto Melara S.p.A. and Breda Meccanica Bresciana S.p.A., both units of state holding company Ente Partecipa zioni & Finanziamento Industria Manifatturiera, known as Efim.

Fiat Aviazione S.p.A. and several other private concerns were awarded the rest of the contract.

In 1986, Iraq paid $1.32 billion -- about half the contractual amount -- for three ships.

After the end of the Iran-Iraq war, Rome and Baghdad reached an agreement in 1989 on delivery of all the ships. But partly for bureaucratic reasons, one still sits in the port of Alexandria, Egypt, while the other 10 are in the Italian port of La Spezia.

So far, the Fincantieri spokesman said, the cost of maintenance of the ships and lost interest on the balance have reached L150 billion.
