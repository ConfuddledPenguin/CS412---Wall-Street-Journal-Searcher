International: Bush and Gorbachev Will Use Summit to Display Their Unity -- After Spelling Out Mideast Aims

  • Author: Peter Gumbel and Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 09/07/90

The meeting between President Bush and Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev Sunday in Helsinki is intended to send a strong message to Iraq that the new-found Washington-Moscow unity isn't about to crack. The meeting "is a fairly dramatic way of signaling that things are getting tougher and tougher for Saddam Hussein," says a senior Bush administration official involved in preparing for the talks. "It should remove from his mind any thoughts that he can split the U.S. and the Soviets on this one."

But officials in Washington are well aware that Mr. Gorbachev will first need some explanations of American intentions in the Persian Gulf. They say Mr. Bush may advise Mr. Gorbachev that a small U.S. presence could remain indefinitely as part of a planned new security arrangement in the region. The State Department is already contemplating a Soviet role in any such arrangement, U.S. officials say.

For his part, Mr. Gorbachev is likely to explain why Soviet military specialists still are stationed in Iraq. In public, the Kremlin has insisted that the 160 specialists weren't involved in Iraqi preparations for the Kuwait invasion and will be withdrawn when their contracts expire. In private, however, Mr. Gorbachev seems embarrassed by the controversy they are causing and reportedly raised the issue at a recent meeting of his Presidential Council. Now that most Soviet civilians have been evacuated from both Iraq and Kuwait, the advisers may be pulled out rapidly, Soviet officials say.

Behind such seemingly minor details, some Soviet analysts detect a bigger discrepancy in the longer-term aims of Washington and Moscow. Both sides agree on the immediate goal of getting Saddam Hussein out of Kuwait. But beyond that, "I don't expect the positions {at Helsinki} will be identical," says Andrei Kortunov, an analyst at Moscow's U.S.A. and Canada Institute. "Objectively, the interests of the Soviet Union and the U.S. are a bit different."

Mr. Kortunov and others in Moscow contend that while the U.S. wants to dislodge Saddam Hussein in favor of a more pro-Western leader, the Kremlin is worried about the effects of military upheaval in the region. It doesn't want to send the wrong signals to other Soviet allies in the Middle East, such as Libya and the Palestine Liberation Organization, for fear they may become more radical. And officials point out that Moscow and Baghdad are still bound by a 1972 treaty of friendship and cooperation, with Iraq owing the Soviets more than $6 billion.

"The preferable option {for Moscow} is a compromise involving Iraqi withdrawal from Kuwait that allows Saddam Hussein to save some face publicly, not a total collapse of the regime," Mr. Kortunov says. But if it became a question of siding with Iraq or siding with the U.S., Mr. Gorbachev wouldn't hesitate to choose the latter. "Ultimately, too much is at stake," Mr. Kortunov says. "Gorbachev would yield."

Iraq is seeking to exploit any differences that exist between Moscow and Washington. After meeting Mr. Gorbachev in the Kremlin, Iraq's foreign minister, Tariq Aziz, yesterday described the talks as "constructive, cordial and frank," though he admitted there were differences. Unlike the U.S., Moscow is prepared to give Iraq a hearing, Mr. Aziz said. "Our Soviet friends are attentive. I appreciate their listening to us."

The U.S. may end up paying a price for continued close Soviet cooperation over the Persian Gulf crisis.

After more than a year of aborted efforts, Moscow is now on the verge of reforms in its creaky economy that would abandon central planning for a market system. Soviet economists say such a plan can only succeed if the West provides aid during a difficult transition period. But so far, only West Germany has provided cash. Most other Western nations, including the U.S., have been lukewarm at best.

Washington is hinting that it may now be willing to do more. "I think it's fair to say that the Soviet cooperation in the Gulf has impressed us to the point that we are even more interested in being supportive economically if we can be," Marlin Fitzwater, the White House spokesman, said this week. Although the U.S. still worries that the Soviet economy isn't yet in a position to absorb much aid, Mr. Fitzwater said Moscow's stance in the Gulf crisis, "clearly moves us in the direction of being more helpful."

Mr. Gorbachev has some other cards to play apart from the Gulf. Two arms-control agreements, one on strategic nuclear forces and one on conventional weapons, are supposed to be signed later this year. But the negotiations in Geneva and Vienna have been going slowly in recent weeks, bogged down by technical details. Some Western analysts in Moscow say that a pledge of U.S. aid would increase the Kremlin's good will and help speed up the conclusion of the talks.

The Helsinki summit should also provide both presidents with the opportunity to sketch out their ideas for future arrangements to safeguard world peace.

U.S. officials say much of Mr. Bush's focus will be on a new theme emerging in Washington that Saddam Hussein has unwittingly forced the superpowers and others to start constructing the post-Cold War world order. Mr. Bush is convinced that collective action, involving both the U.S. and the U.S.S.R., can squelch strongmen like Saddam Hussein, not only in the Mideast but elsewhere, the officials say.

Such thinking is likely to strike a chord in Mr. Gorbachev, who has long argued for collective steps to strengthen international security. But here too, there are differences. Mr. Gorbachev favors making as much use as possible of the United Nations. The U.N. played an important role in the Gulf crisis, approving sanctions against Iraq and the use of force to implement them. But Secretary of State James Baker said this week the U.S. would prefer to avoid a U.N. military command in the Gulf, an idea the Soviets have advanced.

Instead, Mr. Baker floated the idea of setting up a permanent Mideast regional "security structure" to keep the peace. This would house the small residual American force that the White House envisions staying on when the Gulf crisis abates, and possibly a Soviet component. U.S. officials say Mr. Bush may seek Mr. Gorbachev's reaction to the idea in Helsinki.

For their part, the Soviets have again raised the idea of holding an international Mideast conference to sort out regional issues such as the Arab-Israeli conflict, Lebanon and the fate of the Palestinians, as well as the Kuwait crisis.

The Soviets deny that they are seeking to link the Gulf to other Mideast regional problems, as Iraq proposes. Nonetheless, Israel continues to reject the idea of an international conference, and Washington is cool to the idea.
