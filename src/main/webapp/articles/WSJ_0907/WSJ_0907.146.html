Law -- Legal Beat: Ex-Thomson McKinnon Director Is Indicted in Insider Trading Case

  • Author: John R. Wilke and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 09/07/90

Charles H. Howard III, former director and first vice president of defunct Thomson McKinnon Securities Inc., was indicted by a federal grand jury on conspiracy, obstruction and perjury charges in connection with a broad Securities and Exchange Commission investigation of insider trading in New England thrift stocks. Mr. Howard, 45 years old, was once a star performer for the firm and a key player in the hot market for New England thrift stocks in the mid-1980s. Thomson McKinnon Securities filed for bankruptcy-court protection last year.

The Justice Department said the charges are related to the SEC's 1987 investigation of alleged insider trading in connection with the public announcement that Suffield Savings Bank, Suffield, Conn., planned to acquire Coastal Savings Bank of Portland, Maine.

The investigation touched off a broad probe of insider trading in New England bank stocks, and resulted in additional charges of securities violations by Thomson McKinnon Securities. The firm paid $320,000 in fines last year to four New England states to settle charges that Mr. Howard and others sold securities without licenses.

The investigations also led to a blizzard of lawsuits and to the conviction in New Haven of Robert C. DiGennaro, Suffield's former chairman and chief executive, on insider trading and other charges.

According to the indictment of Mr. Howard, announced by U.S. Attorney Wayne A. Budd in Boston, Mr. Howard conspired with others to conceal that investors had purchased Coastal stock prior to the announcement of the merger on the basis of inside knowledge. The indictment also charges that he tried to obstruct the SEC's investigation and perjured himself in testimony before commission investigators.

Mr. Howard made his name mainly as a financial adviser to small New England savings banks, many of which converted from mutual to public ownership in the mid-1980s. During much of the bull market, New England thrift stocks were star performers, and Mr. Howard was their most visible and flamboyant advocate.

He not only helped the fledgling public companies drum up interest in their shares, he helped invest the capital they raised in the public offerings. In many cases, the banks he advised invested large amounts in each other.

But things began to sour two years ago, as federal and state investigators poked into irregular trading in New England bank stocks and many of the banks sustained steep portfolio losses.

Mr. Howard couldn't be reached for comment. But his attorney, Steven M. Gordon, said Mr. Howard "has known that he was a target of a federal investigation for a number of years." He said that Mr. Howard is "in some respects relieved that he will now be provided with the specifics of the allegations against him and be permitted to fully contest the charges." He declined to comment further.

NEW CHARGES are filed in Combustion Engineering insider-trading case.

The Securities and Exchange Commission filed an amended civil suit making new allegations in the case involving Asea Brown Boveri Group Ltd.'s takeover last year of Combustion Engineering. The amended complaint, filed in federal court in Manhattan, names Christian Norgren, a former director of ABB, as a defendant and accuses him of tipping off a friend and a business associate about plans for the $1.6 billion takeover.

Mr. Norgren, a Swedish citizen living in Liechtenstein, resigned as a director of ABB, a Swedish-Swiss power-generation company, last November, a few days after the initial SEC suit was filed in the case. Mr. Norgren also was fired as president of Bank in Liechtenstein. He is part owner of Finacor Anstalt, a Liechtenstein financial-services firm charged in the SEC suit of making illegal profits of $2.5 million on the alleged insider trading.

The SEC accused Mr. Norgren of purchasing Combustion Engineering common stock and call-option contracts through Finacor just days before the $40-a-share offer was announced on Nov. 13. In the amended suit, the SEC said Mr. Norgren recommended the stock to a friend as "the chance of a lifetime." In the suit, the SEC said that the friend sold the securities at a small loss the next day after he learned from Mr. Norgren that the recommendation was based on inside information. The SEC said the friend warned Mr. Norgren that trading on such information is illegal in the U.S.

Mr. Norgren couldn't be reached for comment. Finacor said the amended complaint "represents an unwarranted effort to expand the charges in this case." Finacor's attorney, Thomas E. Engel of New York, said that the SEC had "frustrated" Finacor's efforts to rescind the trades and has "refused to consider any reasonable resolution" of the matter.

In connection with the alleged insider-trading scheme, the SEC also filed a civil suit yesterday against Howard Marguleas, a businessman in Rancho Mirage, Calif. Mr. Marguleas agreed to settle the suit, without admitting or denying the allegations, by instructing his brokerage house to turn over to the SEC $229,000, which the SEC said was illegal profits from the alleged insider trading. The money was held by the brokerage house after Mr. Marguleas tried to reverse the transactions.

The SEC alleged that Mr. Norgren told Mr. Marguleas about ABB's planned offer during a business meeting in New York on Nov. 7, 1989. The SEC said in the suit that Mr. Marguleas agreed to buy the securities on Mr. Norgren's behalf, but then tried to reverse the transactions after the takeover was announced.

Mr. Marguleas said he tried to reverse the transaction after having second thoughts about its legality. He said he voluntarily reported the trading to the SEC and that he has cooperated fully with its investigation. The SEC sought no fines or injunctions against Mr. Marguleas.

JUDGE REFUSES TO DISMISS obscenity charges in Mapplethorpe exhibit case.

Cincinnati's Contemporary Arts Center and its director, Dennis Barrie, previously were indicted on charges of violating Ohio's obscenity law in April by displaying sexually graphic photographs in an exhibition of works by the late photographer Robert Mapplethorpe. They sought dismissal of the charges on grounds the material isn't obscene as defined under constitutional law.

But Hamilton County Municipal Judge F. David J. Albanese rejected the argument, saying it is up to a jury to decide the obscenity issue. He ordered the defendants to stand trial Sept. 24, the same day they will be tried on a related misdemeanor charge stemming from the Mapplethorpe exhibit.

Judge Albanese also upheld a prosecutors' motion to limit the evidence in the obscenity case to five photographs described in the indictment. The defendants had contended that the five pictures must be considered in the context of the entire exhibit, which included photographs that weren't sexually oriented. But Judge Albanese ruled that "each photograph has a separate identity."

Marc Mezibov, an attorney in Cincinnati for the defendants, said, "We're certainly not pleased" with the ruling.

He said the judge is expected to issue guidelines clarifying his opinion and that a decision on whether to appeal won't be made until then.

CARPENTERS UNION in New York is sued for alleged ties to organized crime.

The U.S. Attorney's office in Manhattan filed a civil lawsuit under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act accusing the leaders of the carpenters union of running a protection racket that collected payoffs from contractors.

The suit claims that union leaders and organized-crime families extorted the payments in exchange for guaranteeing labor peace. The suit, the result of a joint federal, state, and city investigation, says some contractors were allowed to get around union work and safety rules in return for the payoffs.

The suit asks that an independent trustee be appointed to run the 30,000 member union, the largest construction union in New York City.

"Union members' rights have been systematically traded for secret payoffs," says the suit, filed in federal court in Manhattan. "Those who opposed this corrupt state of affairs -- whether employees or union members -- have been intimidated into silence by violence, threats, economic coercion, and the known ties between corrupt union officers and organized crime."

The defendants include the governing body of the union, four union officials and six alleged members of the Genovese organized-crime family. The suit doesn't specify how much the union leaders and the mob allegedly received from contractors.

A spokesman for the union declined to comment on the allegations.

DISTRIBUTOR WINS $6 million in damages in a vertical price-fixing verdict.

A federal jury in Atlanta awarded $2 million in damages, to be trebled under antitrust law, to DeLong Equipment Co., an Atlanta distributor of metal-polishing agents, in a price-fixing case. DeLong alleged that it was terminated as a distributor of a polishing agent made by Washington Mills Abrasive Co., North Grafton, Mass., because DeLong wouldn't join in a scheme to fix the price of the material sold to the Pratt & Whitney Aircraft division of United Technologies Corp. Pratt & Whitney used the material in making jet-engine parts for the U.S. military and others.

DeLong alleged that Washington Mills relabeled generic polishing material as "special" and doubled its price.

An attorney for Washington Mills said the company will ask the judge to set aside the jury verdict.
