Commodities: Gold Prices Advance as Diplomacy's Failure To Resolve Persian Gulf Crisis Raises Tensions

  • Author: John Valentine
  • Date: 09/07/90

NEW YORK -- Gold futures prices firmed as traders, watching diplomatic moves fail to settle the Persian Gulf crisis, concluded that chances of a military confrontation are rising. The most active gold contract, for December, ended near its high for the day with a gain of $3.20 an ounce at $396.20. December silver was pulled up by gold and rose 3.3 cents an ounce to $4.87. October platinum was off $2 an ounce at $482.20.

"Two weeks ago, the world was braced for a military clash in the Middle East; but when it didn't take place, hope grew that there would be a diplomatic settlement," said William O'Neill, senior futures strategist for Merrill Lynch. "Now that hope, too, is fading away, and traders are becoming nervous once more."

A major reason that gold isn't reacting more strongly to the Mideast crisis, Mr. O'Neill said, is that there is little investor demand for bullion.

"There was very little Far Eastern buying interest for gold bullion early in the year, but there was a pickup from Taiwan in August," he said. "Japan wasn't much of a buyer, even in August, and European demand has been even more lackluster. Without purchases of bullion in the cash market, prices have little chance of advancing significantly and, even if they do, of holding onto their gains."

The continued strength in crude oil was a price-firming factor in gold, said Greg Klochkoff, vice president of Bank of Boston in Providence, R.I.

"Traders are also watching the headlines about the Middle East and the White House reaction to the whole situation," he said. Mr. Klochkoff said traders who previously sold gold contracts short in anticipation of falling prices might decide to cover, or buy back their positions, today so as not get caught off base by any weekend developments in the Middle East.

"If anything happens, it would most likely be something that would make prices go up," he said. "The short-covering could firm prices, but by late in the day {today} that demand should fade, and prices could fall back somewhat."

Jeffrey Nichols, president of American Precious Metals Advisors Inc. in Toronto, said in his latest review of the precious metals market, released yesterday, that consumer confidence in the U.S. is dropping. He said a recession in the U.S. and a slowdown in the world economy could severely affect buying of gold jewelry -- which is one of the most discretionary and most postponable of all consumer purchases but is the backbone of the gold market.

The world jewelry industry last year consumed an estimated 58.2 million ounces of gold, Mr. Nichols said, far exceeding supplies from Western world mine production of about five million ounces. The jewelry demand also absorbed more than 70% of all supplies entering the market from scrap and from sales by Communist nations as well as from forward sales and gold borrowings by mining companies, he said.

"Since the lion's share of jewelry sales occur during the fourth-quarter holiday shopping spree, the state of the consumer during the next few months will have a disproportionately large bearing on how the year shapes up for jewelry manufacturers," Mr. Nichols said.

As for the price of gold itself, it has "performed so poorly for so long that Western investors will need to see evidence of a sustainable bull market before returning with confidence," he said.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

GRAINS and SOYBEANS: Prices gained ground for a second consecutive day on rumors that the Soviet Union will be granted emergency export credits after the Bush-Gorbachev summit meeting this weekend. The Bush administration so far hasn't given any indication whether such credits are in the cards. The latest six-to-10-day forecast from the National Weather Service, which calls for hot, dry weather across most Midwestern and Southern growing regions, also is likely to lend support to futures prices, analysts said. Soybean futures for November delivery advanced 5.25 cents a bushel to close at $6.3175. December corn futures gained 1.75 cents a bushel to close at $2.3775, and December wheat futures rose 1.75 cents a bushel to close at $2.825.

SUGAR: Futures prices rose. The October contract advanced 0.47 cent a pound to 11.24 cents. A price-firming factor was an announcement by Brazil late Wednesday that it would export only 550,000 tons of raw sugar this season, according to Judith Ganes, analyst for Merrill Lynch. That amount is down from expectations in the sugar business that Brazilian exports would be around 650,000 tons, and from its exports last year of more than 700,000 tons, Ms. Ganes said. Brazil's sugar season runs from June to May. Brazil appears to be holding back some cane supplies as a precaution so it can use them to make alcohol fuel for automobiles to help combat the crude oil price surge triggered by the Mideast crisis, Ms. Ganes said. Also, there is concern about immediately available supplies of raw sugar in the world market, she said. The present time represents a lull in the market before the receipt of supplies from major growers such as Europe, where the harvest is in progress, and Thailand, where harvesting will reach its peak in December, according to Ms. Ganes. Another possible price-firming factor was speculation that the U.S. import quota may be raised next week, she said. Currently the quota for the 21 months ending Sept. 30 is 3.2 million tons.

COPPER: Futures prices again advanced. The December contract was up 1.20 cents a pound to $1.2340. Buying was mostly technical, according to one analyst, who said the New York market was also reflecting gains in London that were prompted by production problems in Zambia, a major copper supplier to Europe. The miners' strike in Peru, which was in its 21st day, was also a price-firming factor, the analyst said.
