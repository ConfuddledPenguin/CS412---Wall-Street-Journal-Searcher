Foreign Exchange: Sterling Climbs and the Yen Plummets As Iraq's Threat Pushes Up Oil Price

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/25/90

NEW YORK -- The pound rose and the yen fell as the currency markets reacted to a jump in oil prices. The dollar gained against its Japanese counterpart but fell against other major currencies.

In late New York trading, sterling was quoted at $1.8810, up almost four cents from $1.8435 late Friday in New York. The dollar was changing hands at 137.35 yen, up from 136.65 yen in New York late Friday.

The U.S. currency was also quoted at 1.5590 marks and 1.2978 Swiss francs, down from 1.5785 marks and 1.3190 francs.

In Tokyo early Tuesday, the dollar was trading at 136.52 yen, down from 137.33 yen at the close there Friday, the last day markets were open in Japan.

Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5565 marks, down from Friday's Tokyo close of 1.5764 marks.

On Monday, the markets paid more attention to news from the Middle East than to a statement issued over the weekend by the Group of Seven -- the financial leaders of the seven major industrialized nations. Currency prices followed oil prices, which soared after Iraqi President Saddam Hussein threatened to attack Israel and bomb Saudi Arabian oil fields unless the United Nations embargo against Iraq is lifted.

The benchmark crude-oil futures contract on the New York Mercantile Exchange rose above $39 a barrel before closing at $38.25 a barrel, up $2.82 on the day.

Because of its oil fields in the North Sea, Britain is a net exporter of oil, though a small one. Japan, on the other hand, imports 95% of its petroleum needs and is considered the most vulnerable among major nations to higher oil prices.

The pound is called a petrocurrency or a petroneutral currency and generally moves in the same direction as oil prices, while the yen is tagged a petrodependent currency and moves against oil prices. Direct trading between the two units is being called the "petro cross," because it is the most sensitive to oil markets. The pound shot up to 258.36 yen in New York yesterday from 251.91 yen Friday.

Reflecting the nervousness of global investors, the price of gold jumped. Gold is generally seen as a haven for investors in times of turmoil. On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery rose $13.60 an ounce to $403 an ounce. Estimated volume was a heavy five million ounces.

Stock markets suffered around the world, with New York's Dow Jones Industrial Average shedding 59.41 points, or about 2.4%, to 2452.97 -- its lowest level since July 3, 1989.

Meanwhile, the G-7 seemed to give dollar sellers the go-ahead after its meeting Saturday in Washington, but the market was overloaded with other influences and didn't really take the officials up on it.

In their concluding statement, the financial authorities of Britain, Canada, France, Italy, Japan, the U.S. and West Germany said that, following the yen's appreciation since their last meeting, exchange rates are now "broadly in line with continued adjustment of external imbalances."

The yen has gained 7.4% against the dollar since July, when the G-7 met in Houston. In theory, a strong yen would help close the U.S. trade gap with Japan because it makes Japanese goods more expensive.

Initially, the G-7 statement moved some market participants to sell dollars from their homes. Although the Tokyo markets were closed in celebration of Autumnal Equinox Day, other Asia-Pacific dealers said they were surprisingly busy.

"What we're seeing is New Yorkers calling on a Sunday afternoon wanting to sell dollars," an Auckland dealer said.

But the dollar's downward momentum was broken in New York when French Economics Minister Pierre Beregovoy told reporters in Washington that the G-7 "would have to consult and decide what to do" if the dollar continued declining.

However, most of the market's attention was focused on oil prices and yen cross trading. The Japanese currency fell as investors sought to cover long, or overbought, yen positions. These investors had bet on a G-7 statement even more supportive of the yen's appreciation than what they got, and they hadn't foreseen the leap in oil prices.

The pound, meanwhile, rode into the session on a raft of short, or oversold, positions after its decline of more than three cents Friday. Dealers looking to take profits on that decline found excuses even before oil prices took off.

During the weekend, U.K. Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major ruled out an early interest-rate cut. Yesterday, the British government reported the U.K. trade deficit for August at #1.1 billion, below expectations of a #1.4 billion shortfall.

The dollar ended European dealings broadly weaker. Action was thin early in the day because of the holiday in Japan. But traders said dollar selling accelerated in the European afternoon, when U.S. markets opened.

In early trading in Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $401.35 an ounce.
