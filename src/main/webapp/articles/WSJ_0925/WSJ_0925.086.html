Economy: Fed's Stinginess Aggravates the Threat Of a Long Recession, Economists Say

  • Author: Alan Murray
  • Date: 09/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Reserve's steady grip on credit is reducing any chance of skirting a recession and could prolong one if it occurs, according to Bush administration and private economists. The Fed's interest-rate policies haven't changed since midsummer. But economic statistics released since then suggest the economy is grinding to a halt. Employment growth is the slowest it has been since the 1982 recession. The housing and construction industries are in a deepening slump. Consumers, after borrowing and spending heavily throughout most of the 1980s, seem to have run out of steam.

Concerns about the economy, and the Fed's reluctance to ease rates, is also causing the stock market to plummet. Yesterday, the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at 2452.97, off 59.41.

"Recession seems very likely unless something changes very promptly," says Robert Dederick, chief economist at Northern Trust Co. in Chicago. An easing of short-term interest rates by the Fed might prevent or cushion a recession, he said.

Administration officials express growing frustration at the Fed's unwillingness to move. Holding interest rates steady in the face of a weakening economy, they argue, amounts in effect to a tightening of credit.

But Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan expresses concern about rising oil prices, a plummeting bond market and signs of inflation in areas other than oil. As a result, he continues to hold credit firm.

At a meeting late last month, a majority of the members of the Fed's policy-setting committee leaned in favor of a modest reduction in rates, according to officials who attended that meeting. The committee apparently gave Mr. Greenspan the leeway to make a modest cut in short-term rates if conditions allowed it.

But the Fed chairman so far hasn't used that discretionary authority, causing considerable grumbling among top administration officials. And in testimony to Congress last week, Mr. Greenspan gave little reason to expect an easing soon.

His tough anti-inflation stance received some moral support this weekend from the finance ministers and central bankers of the seven leading industrial nations, who met in Washington. The group largely rejected the arguments of Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady that lower interest rates are needed, and instead called for "stability-oriented monetary policies." Most of the six other nations continue to grow at a solid pace, and thus face a greater risk of inflation than recession.

Some administration officials speculate that Mr. Greenspan may be delaying any easing move to put pressure on the budget negotiations between the White House and Congress. Negotiators are aiming for a budget agreement by the end of the week.

Mr. Dederick argues that Fed officials also know that even if there is a recession, they won't get the blame this time. "It's not the Fed that shocked the economy," he says, "it's Saddam Hussein."

Mr. Greenspan spoke yesterday to the National Association of Business Economists but gave no clues to his thinking on current policy.

Lee Hoskins, president of the Cleveland Federal Reserve Bank and a voting member of the Fed's policy-making Open Market Committee, also spoke to the business economists yesterday and underscored his anti-inflation views. He said he favors a tight policy that would prevent the price level from deviating by more than two percentage points from its base line. "When prices deviate from the base line level, the Fed should act to bring prices back in line," he said.

"Critics contend that events like the Persian Gulf conflict and the consequent oil-price shock make achieving price stability impossible," he said. "What these critics fail to recognize is that an economic environment where price stability is credible and expected is considerably different than the environment in which we have lived over the past two decades or so."

Mr. Hoskins, along with Fed Governor Wayne Angell, opposed any tilt toward easing at the August meeting of the Fed's policy committee.
