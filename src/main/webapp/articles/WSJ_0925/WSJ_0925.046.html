Anyone Who Can Sell Green Eggs And Ham Is Worth Listening To

  • Author: L.A. Winokur
  • Date: 09/25/90

Add another familiar name to the list of management mavens: Theodor S. Geisel. S as in Seuss. As in Dr. Seuss.

The children's author who put "Green Eggs and Ham" on practically every kid's breakfast plate is being served up as recommended fare to grown-up managers. Cynthia Eisenhauer, for one, urges her staff at the Iowa Department of Employment Services, where she is director, to savor the wisdom in the 86-year-old author's most recent book, "Oh, the Places You'll Go]"

Here is one such morsel:

You have brains in your head.

You have feet in your shoes.

You can steer yourself

any direction you choose.

"It's inspirational and encourages people to set high standards and work to achieve them without being discouraged," exclaims Ms. Eisenhauer.

Carolyn B. Elman, executive director of the American Business Women's Association, agrees. She has been pushing the book to her group's 100,000 members. Unlike many how-to-succeed books, she contends, "this book lets you know everyone fails at some time and that you have to pick yourself up and just go on." Dr. Seuss's prescription:

Out there things can happen

and frequently do

to people as brainy

and footsy as you.

And when things start to happen,

don't worry. Don't stew.

Just go right along.

You'll start happening too.

Nearly 800,000 copies of the book have been sold since it was released early this year, reports Random House, which is marketing it "as a book for anyone starting out on a new adventure in life," says a spokeswoman. Described on its jacket as a graduation speech for "out-starting upstarts of all ages," the book has been on the New York Times fiction best-seller list for more than 30 weeks.

Like Grinches, some big-name management gurus are giving their newest colleague the brushoff. "I haven't gotten to it yet," says Peter Drucker, who suggests he prefers Shakespeare to Seuss as a management tool.

What does Dr. Seuss say about all this? "While I had no intention of speaking directly to managers when I wrote the book, I'm pleased when readers get something beyond what I intended."
