U.S. Clears Up to 10% Rise In International Air Fares

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. Transportation Department, as expected, approved passenger fare increases of 5% to 10% on overseas flights, starting Oct. 1. Most international fares would increase 7%. The department yesterday said it was sanctioning the increases because the airline industry is facing rising jet fuel prices, prompted by Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait.

A government official said the increases aren't unlawful or injurious to competition.

Unlike on domestic routes, carriers must get government approval before they can raise or lower international fares, which are regulated mostly by bilateral agreements. The increases are awaiting government approval from other countries.

Carriers are turning to fare increases as a way of generating extra revenue at a time when the higher cost of fuel is prompting layoffs, fleet reductions and lower earnings forecasts.

The department approved a 7% fare increase on most international fares. On flights within Europe, ticket prices will rise 5%. It sanctioned a 6% boost on fares to Ireland and the United Kingdom and an 8% increase on flights from Canada. One of the increases allowed is a 10% rise on flights from Greece.

The increases will last for six months to cover fuel costs. The department can allow extensions or other fare increases after these have expired.

A war risk surcharge was also approved for flights mostly in the Middle East. The carriers may determine the terms of the surcharges. In addition, cargo rates will be increased 6% to 10% starting Oct. 1.
