PS New Mexico Ends Attempts To Sell Itself

  • Author: Patricia Ann McKanic and Carolyn Phillips
  • Date: 09/25/90

Public Service Co. of New Mexico, abandoning attempts to sell itself, unveiled a business plan that embraces much of the controversial strategy associated with its former chairman. The beleaguered utility, based in Albuquerque, also said it ended talks with a group of investors led by Wheeler Peak Capital Corp. after the two sides failed to reach a compromise on a confidentiality agreement.

Under its new strategic plan, PS New Mexico will pursue previously untapped markets, use recently approved economic development rates to market its excess capacity and attempt to sell all or portions of certain excess power generation facilities. The company also said its board authorized it to find more opportunities for selling power outside its service area.

"The board is willing to authorize additional expenditures to increase capacity to reach markets in the west, particularly California," John Ackerman, president and chief executive officer of PS New Mexico, said in an interview.

Like many utilities in the Southwest, PS New Mexico aggressively overbuilt its capacity in anticipation of the widely expected Sunbelt boom that never happened. That excess capacity and an ill-fated diversification effort pushed the utility to the brink of financial disaster, forcing it to suspend common dividends, cut its staff by 20% and sell off non-utility assets. Last spring, it hired Morgan Stanley & Co. to help it decide by summer's end what to do to pull itself out of its financial morass.

"Management and Morgan Stanley have conducted a very thorough analysis of alternatives," Mr. Ackerman said in a statement. "We believe that the strategic plan presents the best option available to the company."

The plan also harkens back to strategies laid out by Jerry D. Geist who resigned as chairman and president of PS New Mexico in June, saying he was too closely associated with the problems that led to the utility's woes. Mr. Geist, architect of PS New Mexico's diversification efforts, also had championed marketing power throughout the Southwest.

The same obstacles the utility faced in selling itself outright may impede efforts to sell its energy, analysts said. Foremost among those is the scarceness of buyers, generally Southwest utility companies, who aren't already themselves grappling with excess capacity problems.

In July, Wheeler Peak, an Albuquerque venture-capital company run by a former PS New Mexico executive, floated the idea of acquiring the utility for $12 a share and equity in the continuing company. Its partners in the transaction included Southwestern Public Service Co., a Southern Union Co. affiliate, and Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Inc., a unit of Equitable Life Assurance Society of the U.S.

PS New Mexico closed yesterday at $9.875, down 37.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.
