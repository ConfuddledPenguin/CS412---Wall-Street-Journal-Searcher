Letters to the Editor: Who's All Wet on Wetlands Issue?

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/25/90

Your Aug. 27 editorial "EPA v. Private Property" was very timely for us here in Chatham County, Ga. Under the new guidelines for wetlands determination, it has been estimated that 85% of the undeveloped land in Chatham is now wetlands. Consequently, use of this land has come to a screeching halt. There have been numerous meetings with the government agencies concerned and they have come up only with evasive replies. I own a tract of land that was originally granted by King George II in 1758. The only restrictions in the grant are that "all White Pine trees if any . . . and . . . one Tenth part of Mines of Silver and Gold" are reserved for the King and his heirs. Also, certain developments must take place in a given time frame. This includes that for "every fifty Acres . . . do Clear and drain three Acres of Swamp or sunken ground or drain three Acres of Marsh if any such contained herein. . . ."

Our forefathers threw off this yoke of oppression and tyranny for freedom and rule by the people. Now this new government has declared 95% of my land as wetlands coming under its jurisdiction and control. I may do nothing in this area without applying for permission, at which time the government will decide what I may or may not do and how it is to be done, if at all.

There is now in this county a backlog of more than 3,000 requests before the government for wetland permits, delaying infrastructure projects, economic development efforts and use of property by private citizens. Comparing these current rules and regulations with those of the oppressive king, I am starting to wonder if we really do have more freedom than before. Was our Revolution really a success or did we just trade one House of Lords for another?

By the way, there are no white pine trees on the land and I'm still looking for the silver and gold.

William I. Stemwell Jr.

Savannah, Ga.

---

In addition to your usual ranting and raving about environmental regulation, your editorial proclaims that "private property rights lead to a cleaner environment because they imply individual responsibility." Has it occurred to you that the unchecked exercise of "private property rights" led to many of the thousands of abandoned toxic-waste sites now being addressed by the federal Superfund law and similar state statues?

Also, please describe something desirable about the relentless assertion of "private property rights" now ravaging the South American rain forests.

And please note that "hoi polloi" is not correctly proceeded by the article "the."

J.B. Ruhl

Austin, Texas

---

In your Aug. 20 editorial "Whose Wetlands Mistake?" you have tried to characterize the federal wetlands regulatory program on the basis of a single event. If prosecutors played fast and loose with John Pozsgai, who was convicted of turning a wetland into a dump, then Mr. Pozsgai should be granted relief and the prosecutors curbed. That's not editorial material. Your statement that this is an example of arrogance on the part of the environmental community is, and it's wrong.

The environmental community's use of the full weight of the law is a cornerstone available to all members of our society. Your condemnation of its use by the environmental community is unworthy of a paper of your stature. The national abuse of wetlands and its serious adverse consequences to public health, welfare and safety have been well documented. If your staff is among those who still believe that environmental concerns are an exercise in exaggeration, I recommend an assignment to Eastern Europe or China to see firsthand, as I have, the human misery brought about by that mind-set.

Joseph S. Larson

Professor and Director

Environmental Institute

University of Massachusetts at Amherst

Amherst, Mass.
