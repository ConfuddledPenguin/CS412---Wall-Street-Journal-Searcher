Tobacco-Liability Plaintiff Wins; No Award Is Made

  • Author: Alix M. Freedman
  • Date: 09/25/90

NEW YORK -- Jurors in a closely watched tobacco-liability case found in favor of the plaintiff but didn't award any damages. The decision, reached in Oxford, Miss., appeared to signal a victory for American Brands Inc. The case was widely predicted by tobacco experts to be favorable for the plaintiff. However, as of late last night, people familiar with the trial said that Judge Eugene Bogen hadn't dismissed the jury, indicating that the 9-3 verdict in favor of the plaintiff wasn't final.

Nathan H. Horton, a housing contractor, died of lung cancer in 1987. His family charged American Tobacco with "marketing a defective and unreasonably dangerous product."

American Tobacco, a unit of American Brands, has denied any responsibility for Mr. Horton's death, but the case raised hopes among anti-smoking forces. Mississippi state law is more favorable to plaintiffs than most other states. Under its so-called comparative-negligence law, the jury could have awarded damages to the Horton family even if it had found the tobacco company bore only 1% of the responsibility for Mr. Horton's death.

"What jurors are saying is that they didn't like what tobacco companies did but they don't think smokers deserve to be paid for what they did," said Victor Schwartz, a tobacco-liability expert at the firm of Crowell & Moring, of Washington, D.C. "There is no psychological negative in this for the industry -- the only negative is when they have to pay." Mr. Schwartz added that "if ever the plaintiffs were going to win it was this one" because the liberal Mississippi law was favorable to plaintiffs.

But Edward Popper, associate professor of marketing at Bryant College, Smithfield, R.I., and a plaintiff's witness, said, "For the jury to have found for the plaintiff, they had to determine the product was unreasonably unsafe and that to some extent Mr. Horton was unaware of that."

American Brands, which has been under a gag order, said it couldn't comment on the outcome of the case. The plaintiff's attorneys couldn't be reached for comment.
