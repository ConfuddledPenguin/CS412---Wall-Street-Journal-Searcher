Rodamco Suspends Share Redemption Amid Big Outflows

  • Author: Bob Hagerty
  • Date: 09/25/90

Rodamco N.V., a $4.5 billion international property investment fund based in Rotterdam, the Netherlands, suspended its longstanding policy of redeeming shares in the face of a massive outflow of funds. The move stunned investors, and several Dutch analysts predicted that Rodamco's share price will fall at least 10% to 20% as a result. Because Rodamco will no longer buy its own shares at approximately book value, investors wanting out will have to sell them at the prevailing stock market price.

Some analysts said the sudden policy change will hurt the image of the entire Robeco Group, of which Rodamco is one of the largest funds. Robeco Group, the biggest independent fund manager in Europe with the equivalent of nearly $24 billion under management, has shown lackluster investment results over the past several years. Meanwhile, executive turnover has been high at the group amid a reorganization orchestrated by management consultants McKinsey & Co.

Rodamco shares were suspended from trading on the Amsterdam Stock Exchange yesterday and today to give investors time to digest the news. Andre Mulder, an analyst at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Nederland, said Rodamco shares might fall to between 60 guilders and 65 guilders when trading resumes Wednesday. Rodamco closed Friday at 73.30 guilders.

Rodamco has "destroyed its image of being a very stable property fund," Mr. Mulder said. "They will have extreme difficulty getting investors' confidence back."

Rodamco officials insisted they had no choice. The fund has repurchased more than 20% of its shares over the past nine months at a total cost of about 2.3 billion guilders ($1.29 billion). Investors have been bailing out to take advantage of higher yields on money market investments, and many are worried about Rodamco's heavy exposure to the weak British and U.S. real estate markets, which account for about 70% of the fund's assets.

The heavy repurchases have cut deeply into Rodamco's cash. Had the trend continued, the fund said, it would have been forced either to borrow heavily or sell "a potentially large number of property investments in difficult markets."

A Robeco spokesman acknowledged yesterday that the company could end redemptions at any time for its other main offerings, the Robeco and Rolinco equity funds and the Rorento bond fund. But he said such a move was "very unlikely" given that those funds trade in marketable securities, which can easily be cashed in on short notice, unlike real estate.

Through August, Rodamco produced total returns on an annual basis of 5.25% over the past two years and 8.31% over five years, according to Theodoor Gilissen Investment Research of Amsterdam.

For Robeco, an international equity fund, the returns were 1.88% over two years, putting it near the bottom among 14 Dutch-based international equity funds tracked by Gilissen.
