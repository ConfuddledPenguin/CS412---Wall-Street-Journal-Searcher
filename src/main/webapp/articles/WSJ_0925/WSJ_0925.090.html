Lockheed's Board Votes to Amend Poison-Pill Plan

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 09/25/90

CALABASAS, Calif. -- Lockheed Corp., saying it wants to "address concerns expressed by some of our shareholders," softened its poison-pill plan to allow for a specific type of "qualifying offer" that wouldn't be subject to any of the plan's anti-takeover measures. But at the same time, the defense contractor's board spurned proposals from financier Harold Simmons to amend or redeem the pill in a way that would permit one of his companies, NL Industries Inc., to buy 10 million additional shares of Lockheed common stock from the employee stock ownership plan or on the open market.

Either of Mr. Simmons's proposals, Lockheed noted, would effectively lay 35% of the company's stock in the hands of the Dallas investor, who led an unsuccessful proxy battle for control of Lockheed earlier this year. Mr. Simmons, through Houston-based NL, holds a 19.8% stake in Lockheed.

Under previous terms of the poison pill, known officially as a shareholder rights plan, provisions diluting a suitor's stake would be triggered as soon as his investment rose above 20%. Now, under the so-called "chewable pill" that Lockheed adopted yesterday, the company would suspend those provisions as long as:

-- The bid for the company is a fully financed, all-cash offer for all shares outstanding.

-- Owners of a majority of the shares outstanding (excluding the offerer's) accept the bid.

-- The offerer provides and updates "a written fairness opinion" from a nationally recognized investment banking firm.

-- The offerer commits to a second-step transaction providing equal treatment of all shareholders on the same terms and price.

-- The offer remains open for at least 60 business days.

The California Public Employees' Retirement System, a major institutional holder that has encouraged Lockheed to amend its poision pill, said yesterday that it was encouraged by the new action. "It looks like they're addressing some issues that we had raised," said DeWitt Bowman, chief investment officer for the fund.

But NL was hardly mollified by the board's move. Instead, J. Landis Martin, NL's chief executive officer, attacked the decision about the 10 million shares as confirming "to me that (management) is afraid to death of losing the ESOP vote." He declined further comment.

NL has sued Lockheed in federal court in Los Angeles, claiming that the employee stock ownership plan, while it's supposed to be administered by an outside trustee, is really executing "a creeping, management-led, employee buy-out of the company." Lockheed counters that the employee stock ownership plan is run independently and provides "motivational incentives to a broad base" of employees and takes advantage of "significant tax and economic benefits."

In a letter to NL dated yesterday, Lockheed Chairman Daniel Tellep stated that the board had extensively reviewed NL's proposals concerning the 10 million shares, but rejected them unanimously "as contrary to the best interests of Lockheed and its shareholders." He went on to describe the NL proposal, which offered to buy shares from the employee stock ownership plan at $35 each, as "nothing more than an attempt to acquire control of Lockheed without offering a fair price to all shareholders."

"The NL proposal provides no significant economic benefit to the ESOP or other Lockheed shareholders and could be economically detrimental to Lockheed," Mr. Tellep said. "Since last spring's annual meeting, we have met several times with Mr. Simmons to brief him on our performance and plans. Clearly, Mr. Simmons recognizes the underlying value of Lockheed stock, and he is attempting to take advantage of the current industry and market environment, contrary to the best interests of other Lockheed shareholders."
