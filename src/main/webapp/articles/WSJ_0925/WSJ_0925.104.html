Law -- Legal Beat: TWA Workers to Get Back Pay in Ruling

  • Author: Milo Geyelin and Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 09/25/90

A federal appeals court panel in Chicago cleared the way for 3,000 Trans World Airlines Inc. employees to collect what their lawyers say could be "tens of millions of dollars" in back pay and damages stemming from their termination by the air carrier after a three-month strike in early 1986. TWA violated a Labor Department regulation when, after it fired the employees, it refused to notify them immediately of their eligibility for preferred hiring status by other airlines, the court said.

The notification, known as a "designated rights letter," was an administrative vehicle used by the department to implement the Employee Protection Program of the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978. The employee-protection provided by the act expired in 1988.

With the letter, employees could have received preferential treatment over other candidates for jobs with other airline companies. TWA didn't send the notification letter until February 1987.

Each flight attendant will seek an average of two years' back pay, to cover the period from May 17, 1986 -- when they ended the strike and offered to return to work -- till they were rehired by TWA.

The Seventh U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals, affirming a lower court ruling that found TWA liable, remanded the case to the trial court for a hearing on damages.

An attorney for TWA, which is based in Mount Kisco, N.Y., said the company was reviewing the decision.

PARENT INDUSTRIES pleads guilty to racketeering in weapons case.

Parent Industries Inc., a holding company owned by James H. Guerin, filed the plea in federal court in Philadelphia yesterday, a month after federal prosecutors charged that the company participated in a scheme to create more than $1 billion in phony weapons contracts.

Mr. Guerin, who has not been charged with a crime, was the creator of International Signal & Control Corp., a global weapons conglomerate based in Lancaster, Pa., that he started in 1971 as a backyard industry.

Parent, the holding company for all of Mr. Guerin's assets, pleaded guilty to padding and falsifying weapons contracts to inflate the value of International Signal & Control prior to its sale in 1987 to Ferranti International PLC, Britain's third-largest defense contractor, for $670 million.

Under the terms of the agreement, Parent will forfeit at least $4 million and pay an additional $2.4 million in fines. Mr. Guerin remains under investigation.

In a plea memorandum filed by federal prosecutors, Mr. Guerin is alleged to have "directed the financial fraud." His attorney in Philadelphia, Joseph A. Tate, said Mr. Guerin and Parent are cooperating with prosecutors.

DENTISTS ARE CONVICTED in unusual price-fixing case.

Three dentists were convicted in Tucson, Ariz., on charges of conspiring to raise and fix fee amounts paid by their patients, the Justice Department said. The case involved the first criminal antitrust indictments and convictions of medical practitioners in 50 years, according to Robert Bloch, the prosecutor who supervised the case.

The government in recent years has focused increased attention on alleged antitrust violations in health care. Previous cases have involved civil infractions related to such things as hospital mergers. More criminal charges could emerge from federal grand jury investigations under way in Boston and in Savannah, Ga.

The dentists in the Tucson case were found guilty of conspiring to raise "co-payment" fees charged to members of four prepaid dental plans. Co-payments are the extra amounts that plan members must pay for certain non-routine procedures, such as root-canal work.

Convicted Sept. 20 in Tucson were Aaron Alston, Ronald Walker and Richard Meyer. The dentists couldn't be reached for comment. Stephen Weiss, Dr. Alston's lawyer, said a motion has been filed requesting the judge to reverse the jury's verdict "for lack of sufficient evidence." If the motion is rejected, as typically happens, the three dentists "in all likelihood would appeal," the lawyer added.

Mr. Bloch said that the three men convicted were the central figures in a conspiracy involving more than 30 dentists and affecting 80,000 dental-plan members. The price collusion began as early as May 1987 and continued until February of this year, according to the government.

The defendants face possible prison terms of as much as three years each, as well as individual fines of hundreds of thousands of dollars. Sentencing is scheduled for Dec. 17.

FEDERAL TRADE COMMISSION official passes through revolving door.

Terry Calvani, a commissioner for seven years at the FTC who briefly served as acting chairman during the mid-1980s, will become a partner in the Washington law office of San Francisco-based Pillsbury, Madison & Sutro.

Mr. Calvani, whose term expires today, will begin work at the 40-lawyer office tomorrow. An expert on antitrust issues, Mr. Calvani said that he will advise clients on antitrust matters pending at the FTC, and that he expects to represent clients before the commission as soon as the law will allow. In most instances, federal officials are permitted to represent clients before their former agencies after a one-year grace period.

The White House said last week it would nominate Roscoe Burton Starek, White House deputy director for personnel, to fill Mr. Calvani's seat. Before going to the White House, the 42-year-old Mr. Starek worked for seven years in various capacities at the State Department, including the post of deputy assistant secretary for policy and counterterrorism. He also held several legal positions on Capitol Hill in the 1970s.

The post at the FTC requires Senate confirmation.

TOBACCO ANTITRUST APPEAL: Brooke Group Ltd., formerly Liggett Group Inc., sought reinstatement of a $148.8 million jury award in its predatory price discrimination case against Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp. After an eight-month trial, the jury in Greensboro, N.C., had found that Brown & Williamson competed illegally in the market for low-priced, generic cigarettes. Brown & Williamson argued at trial and in its motions to the judge that its price-cutting benefited, rather than harmed, competition. Brooke is appealing a federal trial judge's decision last month overturning the verdict.

BOSTON MERGER: Gaston & Snow, a 305-lawyer corporate firm, will merge with Csaplar & Bok, which represents institutional investors, among other clients. All of Csaplar's 17 partners and 30 associates and staff attorneys are expected to join Gaston & Snow, effective Oct. 1. The new firm, based in Boston, will be called Gaston & Snow.
