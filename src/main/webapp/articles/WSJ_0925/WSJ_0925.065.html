Credit Markets: Bonds Plunge Pushing Yields to Highest Levels Since March 1989 as Result of Soaring Oil Prices

  • Author: Tom Herman and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 09/25/90

NEW YORK -- U.S. government bond yields rose to their highest levels since March 1989 as soaring oil prices stung investors around the world. Long-term Treasury bonds now yield about 9.2%, up from 9.12% last Friday. Bond prices, which move in the opposite direction of yields, fell 5/8 of a point, or $6.25 for each $1,000 face amount. Bond prices also slumped in Germany and the U.K. Traders fear higher oil prices will lead to significantly higher inflation, the archenemy of fixed-income investments. Many investors also fear higher inflation will torpedo hopes for credit-easing moves anytime soon by the Federal Reserve and other major central banks. Thus, more investors are shying away from long-term bonds and turning instead to short-term Treasury bills, noted for safety and security.

"We're still fighting the same battles: tensions are escalating in the Middle East, oil prices are up sharply, and there's no agreement" on how to cut the U.S. government's huge budget deficit, said Steven Saslow, executive managing director at Carroll McEntee & McGinley Inc. "So buyers are on the sidelines." Treasury bills are increasingly popular among investors, he said, "because money is seeking a safety net, and the safest instruments are Treasurys."

Investors are especially leery of bonds and stocks issued by major New York banks. For example, prices tumbled yesterday on bonds of Citicorp, Chemical Banking Corp. and Manufacturers Hanover Corp.

Fears are widespread that Chase Manhattan Corp.'s recent dividend cut and its decision to reduce its staff by 5,000 employees could signal troubles at other large banks. Several traders cited an article in The Wall Street Journal yesterday that quoted a banking industry executive as saying that a high-ranking official at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York had told him Chase isn't the weakest of the New York money-center banks.

The Fed official was said to be surprised that it was Chase, and not another bank, that was getting so much negative attention in the stock market. The official didn't identify the other bank or explain his criteria for weakness. Yesterday afternoon, a Fed spokesman said the Fed would have no comment.

However, several bond dealers said trading in bank bonds yesterday was very light, allowing even relatively small trades to produce large price swings. Some traders also argue that investors are overreacting to the Chase news, and that yields on bank bonds already more than compensate investors for the perceived risks.

"The rumors in the market today are patently ridiculous, and I don't think it is responsible to give them any credence," said Michael Callen, sector executive in charge of Citibank's wholesale banking division for the U.S. and the rest of the developed world. "We have a very different profile than other banks in New York, and we have very different types of earnings streams." Mr. Callen also reiterated his comment in yesterday's Wall Street Journal that "no one around here is talking about a dividend cut."

Whatever the case, the price of Citicorp's 9.75% subordinated capital notes, due in 1999, fell about 2 1/4 points to 89 5/8. The yield jumped to 11.65% from 11.15% Friday.

Chemical Banking's 9.75% subordinated capital notes, due in 1999, fell to 75 7/8, down one point from Friday, to yield 14.75%, up from 14.50%. Manufacturers Hanover's 8.50% subordinated capital notes, due in 1999, now yield 12.70%, up from 12.50% late Friday.

However, Chase Manhattan bonds, which have fallen sharply recently, held steady yesterday even as the company's stock dropped sharply again.

Elsewhere in the credit markets, prices of junk bonds, those issued by companies with low credit ratings or no ratings at all, fell sharply. Among the hardest hit were bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc., Carter Hawley Hale Stores Inc., Ralphs Grocery Co. and Marriott Corp. Traders said the stock market's sharp drop yesterday unnerved junk-bond investors already worried that the economy's weakness will hurt companies struggling to cope with heavy debt burdens.

Treasury Securities

Investors reacted to higher oil prices by shunning long-term bonds.

Among actively traded issues, the government's 30-year bonds were quoted late yesterday at a price of 95 20/32, down about 5/8 point. The yield rose to 9.17% from 9.12%.

Traders say the 9.17% yield late yesterday was the highest closing level of any day this year and also the highest closing level on a comparable Treasury issue in at least a year and a half. Several other long-term issues that are less actively traded now offer even higher yields than the 30-year issue. For example, some Treasury bonds now yield about 9 3/8.

Today, the Treasury will sell new two-year notes. Trading in the "when-issued" market yesterday, those notes were quoted at a yield of 8.17%, up from 8.10% Friday. The yield on the new four-year Treasury notes, to be sold tomorrow, rose to 8.54% from 8.48%.

"The market got killed" because Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein "was rattling sabers," said David Resler, senior vice president at Nomura Securities International Inc. "A lot of the movement {in Treasury bond prices} was off of oil and the heightened uncertainty about what's going on in the Middle East."

Corporate & Junk Bonds

Corporate bond prices slumped, with junk bonds posting especially large declines.

Among the biggest decliners were junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc., Carter Hawley Hale Stores Inc., Ralphs Grocery Co. and Marriott Corp.

RJR Nabisco's 17.375% convertible payin-kind debentures, due in 2009, finished down 1 7/8 points to close at 109 5/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely-held RJR issue, the 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, ended 2 1/8 points lower at 85 3/8. RJR pay-in-kind bonds pay interest in the form of additional bonds instead of cash.

Meanwhile, junk bonds of Carter Hawley Hale continued to plummet. Carter Hawley's 12.25% senior subordinated notes, maturing in 1996, finished down six points at 57 while its 12.50% senior subordinated debentures, maturing in 2002, ended down three points at 51.

Prices of bonds issued by Carter Hawley and many other retailers have been hammered in recent weeks by fears that consumer spending will be light this Christmas because of the economy's weakness.

Elsewhere, in the high-yield market, Ralphs Grocery's 14% senior subordinated debentures, due in 2000, dropped five points to 77.

Marriott's 9.125% senior notes, due in 1995, fell five points to 91, while its 9.375% debentures, due in 2007, finished down four points at 92 1/2.

Meanwhile, prices of many investment-grade corporate bonds also slumped, with some actively traded issues falling about 1/2 point. Larger declines, though, came on bonds of several large New York banks.

Municipal Bonds

Actively traded municipal bonds posted losses of 1/2 point to one point in a fairly active session, suffering the same fate as the other bond markets as oil prices surged.

The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bond due 2018 closed one point lower at 95 3/4, boosting the yield to 7.55% from 7.47% late Friday.

No sizable municipal issues of more than $50 million were priced yesterday.

Mortgage & Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities fell 1/8 to 3/8, also hurt by soaring oil prices.

Government National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% securities for October delivery fell 1/4 point to 97 21/32, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp.'s 9 1/2% gold participation certificates fell 7/32 to 98 5/32, and Federal National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% securities fell 7/32 to 97 31/32.

In the asset-backed market, Morgan Stanley & Co. and Norwest Investment Services Inc. priced $400 million of creditcard certificates at 99.9375 to yield 9.11%. The securities are collateralized with Visa and MasterCard receivables sold by Norwest Bank Iowa, a unit of Norwest Corp.

Separately, Merrill Lynch Money Markets Inc. has set up a "special purpose corporation," called Hamilton Funding Corp., to finance the purchase of short-term, money market securities off balance sheet. The maximum size of this "asset-backed commercial paper program" will be $1.5 billion. Hamilton yesterday issued just under $200 million of commercial paper and hopes to issue between $500 million and $1 billion by Friday.

Foreign Bonds

German and British government bond prices fell yesterday, hurt by higher oil prices and fears of additional turmoil in the Middle East.

Bonn's 8 1/2% issue due 2000 dropped 0.38 point to 96.19 to yield 9.095%. British government bond prices also ended lower. Britain's 11 3/4% bonds due 2003/2007 fell 1/2 point to 99 25/32 to yield 11.76%.

However, shorter-term British bonds were little changed. For example, the U.K.'s 12% bonds due 1995 fell only 3/32 of a point to 99 28/32 to yield 12%. And the 11 3/4% bonds due 1991 were unchanged at 99 1/8 to yield 14.32%.
