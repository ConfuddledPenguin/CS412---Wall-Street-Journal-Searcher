Commodities: Gold Futures Post Largest Gain in Over 3 Years As Mideast Tension Rises and Oil Reaches Highs

  • Author: John Valentine
  • Date: 09/25/90

NEW YORK -- Gold futures posted the largest one-day gain in more than three years as Iraqi President Saddam Hussein raised Mideast tensions another notch and oil prices boomed to record highs. The most-active December gold contract ended with a gain of $13.80 an ounce at $408.30, just under the high of the day of $409. It was the largest one-day gain since April 14, 1987, when the April contract also advanced $13.80 to $450.90, according to the Commodity Exchange in New York. It was the first time the December contract was above $400 since Aug. 24, when it settled at $423.20, the exchange also noted.

The gold rally pulled up silver and platinum prices, which had been laggards in recent trading. December silver rose 12.5 cents an ounce to $4.9950. October platinum was up $8.40 an ounce at $460.70.

"This is a very uncertain market," said Sharon Ziemian, vice president of Citibank. "The market is turning on the perception of Middle Eastern problems and the price of oil, depending on whatever military problems develop there."

War jitters were triggered by bellicose statements by President Hussein over the weekend, and resulted in a record futures price for crude oil at $39.20 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange. In Europe, Brent crude was reported to have traded at $40 a barrel, according to analysts.

In gold, as a result, there was heavy buying of contracts that had been previously sold by traders in the expectation of lower prices, analysts said. As December gold thrust above $400, the advance was accelerated by waiting buy orders that were triggered by prices rising through that area, they said.

One analyst said a contributing factor to the strong rise was that producers hadn't sold into the rally as has been the case in recent years, stifling advances. However, he noted, "If we get another rise of this kind, it will certainly encourage them to become active sellers."

Another analyst observed that last week there was some hope of a diplomatic resolution to the Middle Eastern crisis, "but those hopes were destroyed by President Hussein's comments."

Peter Cardillo, director of commodity research for Jesup Josephthal & Co., said that gold has a strong technical base and that December could top $425. That price has been a strong resistance level as far back as March; since July four attempts to go above $425 have been unsuccessful. "The current advance can even go as high as $435 or $440," he said.

According to Ken Gettinger, vice president of Union Bank of Switzerland's New York office, "President Hussein's comments apparently convinced traders that some kind of military action is inevitable, with the only question as when it is going to happen."

Mr. Gettinger said that along with the record prices in oil, a sharp drop in the stock market also contributed to gold's strength.

"Traders are speculating that the December price might hit $410, and that there's no telling how high it can go if there's military action," he said.

Giving the market a sound underpinning, one analyst said, was that demand for gold bullion had picked up in Europe for the first time in a while.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

GRAINS and SOYBEANS: Mounting concern about the size of the Soviet grain harvest, and rising inflation fears, boosted futures prices. Harvest delays are continuing in the Soviet Union because of bureaucratic inefficiencies and shortages of fuel and tractor spare parts. The delays are causing analysts to boost their estimates of tens of millions of bushels of grain that are likely to rot in the fields this season, and predict that the Soviets will receive some form of export grants from the U.S. in the near future. Meanwhile, sharply higher gold and crude oil futures prices rekindled concern that the inflation rate would rise at a brisker pace than in recent months. Grain and soybean prices typically advance during inflationary periods. Wheat futures for December delivery gained five cents a bushel to close at $2.7525. December corn futures advanced three cents a bushel to close at $2.2875 and November soybean futures added two cents a bushel to close at $6.1350.

COFFEE: Futures prices declined. The December contract ended 3.5 cents a pound lower at 94 cents. According to one analyst, Brazil, at an International Coffee Organization meeting in London over the weekend, left the door open for further negotiation on the restoration of quotas and prices in the International Coffee Agreement, but wasn't ready to engage in negotiations now. The agreement expires in September 1991, and Brazil was agreeable to extension of the current administrative agreement for one year, the analyst said. Apparently there were some traders who were harboring hopes that negotiations might begin on economic clauses of the agreement and were disappointed when that didn't happen, according to the analyst. However, she said, this is the season when roaster demand for green coffee picks up, which could help prices to level off and even recover somewhat.

COTTON: Futures prices dropped sharply. The soonest-to-expire October contract, which has no trading limits, was off by 4.09 cents a pound at one point under heavy selling before partly recovering to end at 73.70 cents, down 1.95 cents. The decline was accelerated as the price dropped below key support levels. The December, March and May contracts all were down by their daily limit of two cents a pound at one point. December settled at 71.14 cents, down 1.90 cents. The October contract actually started off with a gain of 0.25 cent, said Ernest Simon, cotton specialist for Prudential-Bache, but then the boom in oil prices and the drop in the stock market created concern that the already slumping economy could get worse. "This would translate in fewer textile sales and a drop in demand for cotton," Mr. Simon said.
