Administration Prepares for Layoffs As Budget Talks Remain Unresolved

  • Author: David Wessel and Jackie Calmes
  • Date: 09/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration is preparing to furlough thousands of federal workers -- from air traffic controllers to White House spokesmen -- beginning next week unless budget negotiators reach a last-minute accord. The five congressional and three administration negotiators met again last night. Before the meeting, Democratic leaders suggested there might be an accord, at least in principle, by midweek. Administration officials, however, emphasized that substantial differences remain, though they acknowledged that negotiators are making progress.

In what amounts to a high-stakes game of chicken, the White House yesterday renewed its vow to veto any congressional attempt to delay the automatic spending cuts slated to begin Monday, the first day of the new fiscal year.

If President Bush doesn't flinch, the impact will be immediate -- and not just on the paychecks of federal employees who will be told to stay home a day or two each week. "It's seven days to Armageddon," Rep. Steve Gunderson (R., Wis.) warned on the House floor.

The Federal Aviation Administration, for instance, says that its plan to furlough controllers 2 1/2 days in the first two weeks of October will force the cancellation of 3,000 domestic flights a day. The FAA had been saying that the cancellations would begin Monday, but after meeting with airline industry representatives yesterday, the agency said it is postponing the cancellations until 3 a.m. next Thursday. Flights will be delayed as necessary Monday, Tuesday and Wednesday of next week.

Takeoffs and landings at 40 airports will be restricted, the agency says. At Chicago's O'Hare Airport, the busiest airport in the nation, no more than 70 landings an hour will be allowed. But at the peak hour from 8 a.m. to 9 a.m., 98 arrivals are scheduled -- so the FAA says 28 flights must be canceled. Airlines are to notify the FAA by noon Friday what flights are to be canceled.

In many other agencies, the initial impact of spending cuts won't be as drastic. Although plans won't be final until later this week, the Social Security Administration is now planning to work six-hour days instead of its usual eight-hour days Monday through Thursday and close all of its offices across the country for the day on Oct. 5, according to Kevin Moley, assistant secretary of health and human services for management and budget. But Social Security still expects to be able to deliver benefit checks on time; benefits themselves are exempt from the spending cuts.

And in some corners of the bureaucracy, the cuts will have little instant impact. At the main offices of the Treasury, typical of many agencies, furloughs won't be ordered next week. A spokeswoman said employees will have to take off two days without pay during the two weeks beginning Oct. 8 -- assuming nothing has changed by then.

Unless the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law is altered, the government must reduce spending by an estimated $85.4 billion in fiscal 1991 to meet the law's $64 billion deficit target. With about three-quarters of all federal spending exempt from the cuts -- including military personnel -- the remainder will be hit hard. The White House budget office says spending by affected agencies must be slashed by 32.4%, six times the cuts imposed last year when Congress missed the budget deadline. "Every other year, this exercise has been at the margins. This year it is meat and potatoes," said Mr. Moley of HHS.

The spending cuts are so deep that Congress and the president are certain to prevent them from lasting all year. But administration officials insist they will let them take effect next week to increase the pressure on Congress to reach an agreement. "We think those are an important discipline," White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said yesterday.

Congress is contemplating incorporating a provision that would delay the Gramm-Rudman cuts until Oct. 13 or Oct. 20 into an emergency temporary spending measure that must pass by Oct. 1 to keep the government running. Republicans are mobilizing to defeat that bill. And Mr. Fitzwater reiterated previous warnings that such a measure would be vetoed if it waives the Gramm-Rudman spending cuts. But he added: "We'll wait and see what the situation is when we get there."

Congressional and administration officials expect that the spending cuts would be waived, or at least delayed, if negotiators reach an agreement soon -- even though the agreement won't be enacted into law for weeks.

Federal agencies have been issuing furlough notices for weeks and drafting contingency plans, but in many corners of the bureaucracy, managers confess uncertainty about how the cuts actually will be executed.

Some agencies can absorb the spending cuts for the first few weeks without furloughs -- by deferring purchases, delaying travel and leaving vacancies unfilled. But the White House budget office has limited agencies' flexibility and issued stern warnings about the criminal penalties that managers face if they expend unappropriated funds.

Under the Gramm-Rudman rules, small agencies that spend nearly their entire budgets on personnel are hit particularly hard. The office of the U.S. commissioner to the Delaware River Basin Commission must cut $67,000 from its $206,000 allocation for salaries and expenses. Since the commissioner is exempt, the only other employee, staff assistant Barbara Cranford, has been instructed to take off 255 days of the 260-day federal work year. She is planning to work five days next week and stay home after that.

"You're sort of numb," said Mrs. Cranford, who has worked for the federal government for 35 years, 22 of them at the commission. "You sort of hope that Congress wakes up. They expect civil servants to be responsible, and we hope Congress will be responsible."
