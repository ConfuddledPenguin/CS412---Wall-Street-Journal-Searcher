Stamford Capital Dissolves Boutique Of Lehman Alumni

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 09/25/90

NEW YORK -- A fledgling investment boutique of Lehman Brothers alumni folded, and several top partners joined another Wall Street firm headed by former Lehman executives. The dissolution of Stamford Co. stemmed from a sudden pullback of capital by its parent, Stamford Capital Group Inc., amid the deep slump in the investment banking business.

Launched last year by Sheldon S. Gordon, former vice chairman at Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc., Stamford was modeled after Wall Street's traditional investment banking partnerships. But trading losses and a slowdown in merger activity and other traditional investment banking activities stung the firm badly.

The demise of the firm -- its core partners were all former executives of Lehman Brothers, which was bought by Shearson in 1984 -- is a vivid reminder of the difficulty facing Wall Street's investment banking boutiques these days.

"The real lesson here is that it has to be the right idea with the right people at the right time," Mr. Gordon said, adding that he will concentrate now on private investing. "We had the right idea with the right people, but the timing was poor."

Several of Stamford's top partners, meanwhile, have jumped to another securities firm with strong Lehman ties: Primerica Corp.'s Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. They include James Boshart, who was hired at Lehman Brothers 20 years ago by Lewis Glucksman, who now is a Smith Barney vice chairman. Mr. Boshart will head Smith Barney's corporate finance operations.

Clifford Goldman, Stamford's former chief financial officer and another former Lehman executive, will become a managing director supervising Smith Barney's administration. Mr. Goldman initially will focus on the financial reporting of the firm's businesses, a Smith Barney spokesman said.

Another senior Stamford partner, Judah Frankel, will join Smith Barney as a managing director in its mortgage-backed securities department. Several other former Lehman executives have left Stamford in the past several months, as the firm's fortunes have sagged.

Meanwhile, some of Stamford's business lines are likely to be bought soon by senior management as separate entities, people familiar with the firm said. For example, Stamford's corporate finance and mergers group, which focused on companies involving transactions valued at between $15 million and $150 million, is likely to be bought out by a small group led by Arnold Hoffman, another former Lehman executive who ran Stamford's middle-market merger activity, the people said.

A planned partner in the new entity, to be called Middle Market Group L.P., is Shearson Lehman Brothers itself. Shearson's interest stemmed from a joint venture worked out with Stamford last year, in which each firm received revenue from certain transactions involving midsized companies.

A spokesman for Shearson, a unit of American Express Co., said, "We'll continue to have an interest in the middle-market group."
