Abreast of the Market: Industrials Tumble 59.41 Points On Economic Worry, Oil Price Rise

  • Author: Craig Torres and Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 09/25/90

NEW YORK -- Pessimism about the economic outlook and a sharp jump in oil prices sent stock prices to their lowest levels in 14 months. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 59.41, or 2.36%, to 2452.97, the fourth consecutive slide and the lowest level since July 3, 1989.

Crude oil for November delivery closed $2.82 higher at $38.25 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange yesterday, following new threats of aggression from Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein.

Trading wasn't especially heavy on the New York Stock Exchange, where 162,210,000 shares changed hands. But the damage to individual stocks was steep. Only 233 stocks advanced on the Big Board, while 1,423 declined.

Money managers and traders alike stared grimly at the indiscriminate selling of some of the year's favorite shares. So-called recession-proof sectors, such as pollution control, food and beverages, slid easily into losses as buyers remained cautious and reluctant. Traders said some money managers are simply getting out of stocks, not trying to hide in the "safe" groups any more.

Waste Management, a favorite this year, was down more than a point during the session, but late bargain-hunting pared the issue's loss to 3/4, to 32 3/4. Boeing, another popular issue, tumbled 2 7/8 to 40 3/8, despite news that a division of Lufthansa had placed a large order for jets; the stock was also downgraded by analysts at Lehman Brothers and First Boston, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Cyclical stocks also took a drubbing. It was an especially bad day for several metals issues. Copper producer Phelps Dodge tumbled 4 5/8 to 53 3/4; Reynolds Metals fell 3 7/8 to 63 1/2. Aluminum Co. of America fell 3 3/4 to 63 3/4. Stories in yesterday's Wall Street Journal and this week's Barron's magazine note that aluminum prices could drop swiftly if the world economy continues to weaken. Also, Lehman Brothers and PaineWebber downgraded the industry group, while aluminum prices fell on commodity exchanges.

"There are no places to hide in a bear market," said Ronald Sloan, executive vice president at Siebel Capital Management Inc. "The only way to preserve capital is not to own stocks."

The widespread selling pushed broader averages to the lowest levels in 17 months. Standard & Poor's 500-stock index fell 6.73, or 2.16% to 304.59, the lowest close since April 17, 1989; the New York Stock Exchange composite index fell 3.52, or 2.06%, to 167.51, the lowest close since April 13, 1989.

Computer-guided sell programs played only a marginal role in the market's decline. The Big Board's "uptick rule," which hinders many forms of program trading, kicked into effect when the Dow industrials were down more than 50 points shortly before 11 a.m. EDT.

American Express dropped 2 to 20 3/4 on trading of slightly more than one million shares. This newspaper reported yesterday on an effort by American Express to smear rival banker Edmond Safra.

Chase Manhattan topped the most-active list, with a 1 5/8-point loss to 11 1/8 on volume of more than five million shares. On Friday, Chase announced plans to take hefty third-quarter charges for a restructuring and possible loan losses and cut its dividend by more than half.

Among other bank stocks following Chase lower, Citicorp dropped 1/2 to 15 1/4 on volume of 2.4 million shares and Chemical Banking fell 1 7/8 to 15 1/8. Bankers Trust slid 2 to 32 1/4, Continental Bank lost 5/8 to 8 1/4, Mellon Bank lost 7/8 to 18 7/8 and Wells Fargo eased 1 3/8 to 49 7/8.

UAL fell 8 1/2 to 92 1/4. The United Airlines Pilots Association asked its members to express their support for the union-led buy-out effort, in order to counter opposition by UAL's chairman, Stephen M. Wolf. In a letter to its members, the union claims that Mr. Wolf has advised the UAL board that the buy-out isn't supported by rank-and-file union members.

The union also indicated that if the buy-out effort fails, it is prepared to return to the "traditional labor-contract negotiating table" with the possibility of a strike if its demands aren't met by top management.

Computer Associates went down 1/2 to 4 7/8 following a critical article on the company's founders in Sunday's edition of the New York Times.

Corroon & Black added 1/4 to 29 1/2 after declaring a $2.50-a-share special dividend. The dividend will be payable 15 days after the completion of the company's stock-swap merger with Willis Faber. On Friday, Aon Corp. withdrew a $40-a-share offer for Corroon & Black at Corroon & Black's request.

American Brands fell 1 3/4 to 66 1/4. A Mississippi jury is expected to decide shortly whether an American Brands unit bears any responsibility for the lung-cancer death of a Lexington, Miss., man in a closely watched case. While most analysts expect the jury to rule in favor of the company, product-liability suits have caused short-term volatility in tobacco stocks in the past.

Marriott dropped 1 1/2 to 10 7/8, its lowest level in at least eight years. Lehman Brothers and Wertheim Schroder made cautious comments on the company yesterday, noting that the hotel chain holds a sizable real estate portfolio that it would like to sell. Last Tuesday, Prime Motor Inns filed for reorganization under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code, largely as a result of slumping real estate prices. Prime Motor closed unchanged at 11/16.

Yesterday's short list of winners was crowded with gold stocks. Hecla Mining gained 5/8 to 10 3/4; Homestake Mining rose 1 1/4 to 21 5/8; ASA Ltd. jumped 2 5/8 to 50 1/2; Placer Dome went up 1 to 18 1/2; Newmont Gold rose 3 1/8 to 46 1/2; Coeur d'Alene Mines gained 1 to 28 1/2, and Newmont Mining added 2 1/8 to 44 1/8.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 3.17 to 312.84. Volume totaled 14,050,000 shares.
