Politics & Policy: Bush Reasserts U.S. Won't Negotiate With Iraq Till Withdrawal From Kuwait

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 09/25/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush again drew a clear line against any negotiations to end the showdown over Iraq's occupation of Kuwait. In an occasionally contentious session with representatives of Arab-American groups, Mr. Bush was pressed to negotiate with Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein as he has with other world leaders. At one point, Mr. Bush retorted: "No negotiations -- withdrawal totally from Kuwait."

Mr. Bush said that discussions with Iraq's leaders might be possible once Iraq fully complies with United Nations Security Council resolutions demanding unconditional Iraqi withdrawal. But he reasserted that the U.S. won't "yield one inch" on its demand for an unconditional withdrawal.

The president, also pressed about depriving Iraqi children of food through the U.N. trade embargo on Iraq, declared more openly than in previous statements that he is willing to let shipments of food reach Iraq if international relief organizations supervise their distribution. "We want to get the food to those that need it," Mr. Bush said, but the U.S. is "much less interested in feeding Saddam Hussein's army at this point."

Meanwhile, in an important indication that one key link in the trade sanctions may be holding, Iran announced that it had arrested 29 people for trying to smuggle food into Iraq. Iran's official news agency said that two groups of smugglers were handed over Sunday to a court in the Kurdish city of Sanandaj, 50 miles from the Iraqi border, and that officials seized rice, flour, sugar, vegetable oil, dates, raisins, powdered milk and tomato sauce.

U.S. officials, while maintaining that they expected Iran to honor the sanctions, have been looking for such tangible evidence that the country would spurn overtures from Iraq that it help to circumvent trade sanctions.

The Iranian announcement came as Syrian President Hafez Assad, who is sending thousands of troops to Saudi Arabia to deter further Iraqi moves, extended a visit with Iran for talks apparently aimed at solidifying Iranian cooperation with the sanctions.

Mr. Assad, who met U.S. Secretary of State James Baker earlier this month in Damascus to coordinate actions against Iraq, held his third meeting in three days with Iranian President Hashemi Rafsanjani yesterday. He had been scheduled to leave after that, but Iran's official news agency said he is remaining for more talks.

President Bush's remarks ruling out negotiations with Iraq matched in tone Baghdad's unyielding declarations made Sunday asserting that the seizure of Kuwait is permanent and threatening a widespread Middle East war if Iraq is economically "strangled." Moreover, the Iraqi news agency quoted Saddam Hussein yesterday as saying that he would fight "for 1,000 years" to keep Kuwait.

Mr. Bush's appearance before Arab-Americans was scheduled in part so the president could condemn prejudice and acts of hatred aimed at Arab-Americans. But while the audience enthusiastically applauded those remarks, some seemed to question his unbending stance toward Iraq, and Mr. Bush responded firmly and at times testily.

When one questioner voiced concern that the U.S. was pledging to restore an unelected monarchy in Kuwait rather than bring democracy to that state, Mr. Bush retorted: "The objective is to see that naked aggression does not pay off, sir . . . Iraq is no model of democracy, nor was Kuwait. That isn't the question here -- the question is international law and respect for one's neighbors." Many in the audience applauded, indicating support for Mr. Bush's stand.

Mr. Bush complained bitterly that Saddam Hussein isn't allowing humanitarian food shipments to Iraq to be distributed by international relief workers. He asserted that Iraq's stance, rather than the international sanctions, is keeping food from thousands of Asian refugees trying to flee Iraq and Kuwait.

Separately, the Bush administration continued trying to persuade Congress to approve the president's request to forgive almost $7 billion in Egyptian military debts to reward Egypt for its stand opposing Iraq's occupation of Kuwait. The request faces opposition. Some lawmakers are considering inserting in a short-term spending bill a waiver of an Egyptian debt payment due Oct. 2.
