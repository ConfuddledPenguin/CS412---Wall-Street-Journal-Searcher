Oil Prices Hit Highest Levels In a Decade --- Some Crudes Soar $3 a Barrel In Wake of Iraq's Threat Against Mideast Fields

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 09/25/90

Panic buying in the wake of Iraqi threats against Middle East oil fields drove petroleum prices to the highest levels in a decade. The run-up of more than $3 a barrel for some crudes added to the prospect of inflation, depressed the U.S. stock market and increased support for tapping into the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve.

Only a month after breaking the $30 a barrel mark, several crudes surged beyond $39 a barrel in trading with the U.S. futures price reaching $39.20 briefly. Some North Sea oil was valued at $40 a barrel on European spot markets in late trading, although details of transactions at those levels weren't confirmed.

Even though it dropped back from the high, crude traded on the New York Mercantile Exchange posted a futures record. Crude oil for November delivery was up $2.82 a barrel to settle at $38.25. That was the highest price for this type of oil, a light low-sulfur crude, since West Texas Intermediate, the U.S. benchmark, reached $39.80 a barrel in the cash market in March 1980.

Fuels reflected the escalation in crude oil prices. In U.S. futures trading, heating oil and unleaded gasoline for October delivery rose nearly seven cents a gallon each to settle at $1.04 a gallon each.

The oil-price surge followed a weekend statement from Baghdad by Saddam Hussein that he would strike at Middle East oil fields before he would let U.S.-led sanctions "strangle" Iraq. Although the Iraqi president has issued warnings against a U.S. attack on Iraq, his new remarks were interpreted as indicating he might strike first.

Iraq failed in its eight-year war with Iran to halt that nation's oil flow. Oil and military analysts questioned whether Iraq could be any more successful in destroying Saudi Arabia's well-defended installations if it tried.

Still, Saddam Hussein's statement advanced war fears another notch and kept alive the possibility of major disruptions of the flow of oil from the Middle East, which holds the bulk of the world's reserves. As a result, the new threats were quickly reflected in higher petroleum prices around the world as trading opened yesterday.

"The market is very, very jittery," said Peter Beutel, director of the energy group at Pegasus Econometric. "People are afraid we are going to see a war in the Middle East."

Few oil analysts were bold enough to forecast the direction of the oil market after the rapid escalation of recent days. Several suggested that $40 is no longer out of reach and that $65 may well be the likely target, as some studies have predicted, if war breaks out.

Most private and government energy officials continued to maintain that the industry's supply-demand fundamentals don't justify the current levels of pricing. Barring an extremely cold winter or a disruption, oil supplies should be ample, they said.

"The market is anticipating an awful lot," said Peter Gignoux, manager of the international energy desk in London for Shearson Lehman Brothers. "Without hostilities, we are totally, fully priced."

Most oil economists agreed that oil market participants are clearly reacting to the words of Saddam Hussein. "The market now is suggesting we are on a war footing," said Lawrence Goldstein, president of the Petroleum Industry Research Foundation, "and a war premium has been built into the {pricing} system."

The Petroleum Industry Research Foundation, a non-profit organization that receives some financing from oil companies, is a leading supporter of opening up the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve to help damp the escalation in oil prices. Its position is that the artificially high prices damage economies.

"We believe it's time to release SPR barrels" to prevent further deterioration of the U.S. economy, said Mr. Goldstein.

Under the law that established the reserve, President Bush must make a "finding" that there is justification for commercial releases of oil from the reserve because of an oil shortage. But proponents of opening the reserve contend that the evidence of supply restraints won't show up until long after the shortage has begun.

A test of the system is planned for next month, however. Recent legislation approved by Congress to establish a new target of one billion barrels of oil for the reserve, which contains slightly under 600 million barrels, would permit withdrawals, totaling as much as five million barrels, in such a test.

"It could have the same psychological effect on the market that a commercial release would have," Mr. Goldstein said.
