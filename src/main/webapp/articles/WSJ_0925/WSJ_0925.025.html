Street Trades In New Market Of Bank Debt

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 09/25/90

NEW YORK -- For sale, cheap: bank loans that were made to some of America's most debt-ridden companies. Gung-ho Wall Street traders, who a few years ago created a market in Brazilian, Mexican and other Third World bank debt, now are turning their attention to bank loans to leveraged U.S. companies.

Most of these loans were made to finance leveraged buy-outs, in which acquisition debt is paid with funds generated by the acquired company, and other takeovers. The borrowers range from Ames Department Stores Inc. to Zapata Corp. Recent market quotes for such bank loans vary from 20 cents to 70 cents for each dollar of face value.

The traders' opportunity may become commercial banks' embarrassment. This is because commercial banks generally value their corporate loans at 100 cents on the dollar, unless the borrower stops paying interest. Banks seldom mark down their loan portfolios to market value to reflect any preliminary deterioration in credit quality, though regulators recently have been pressing banks to make some adjustments.

As loan traders start to price some leveraged-company debt at big discounts to face value, "That's going to raise questions about banks' carrying values of loans," James McDermott, head of bank-stock research at Keefe, Bruyette & Woods, said. "To the extent that {corporate bank-debt trading} becomes a viable market, it will contribute to psychological pressure to mark these loans to market."

Overall trading in distressed corporate bank loans totals a modest $100 million a month, dealers estimated. But "Business is really picking up," said Tom Hendrick, a loan trader at Continental Illinois Corp.

On Wall Street, the Salomon Brothers Inc. unit of Salomon Inc. and Goldman, Sachs & Co. recently set up departments to trade leveraged-company bank loans. They are following in the steps of the market pioneers Oppenheimer & Co., R.D. Smith & Co. and the Bear, Stearns & Co. unit of Bear Stearns Cos. Even some banks, such as Citicorp and Bankers Trust New York Corp., are building up specialties in trading these loans.

The potential pool of tradable bank loans is huge. As of June 30, the 25 largest U.S. banks held $69 billion of what regulators call "highly leveraged transaction" loans, according to a Morgan Stanley & Co. analyst, Arthur Soder. Most of these loans were booked in the late 1980s leverage binge, often with the lure of big upfront fees for the banks that made them. The loans generally pay interest of 1 1/2 percentage points above the prime lending rate.

While many of banks' leveraged loans have been made to relatively healthy companies, such as Time Warner Inc. and RJR Nabisco Inc., analysts estimate that 5% to 10% are to companies that have tumbled into financial distress. Some of the ailing companies have defaulted on their bank debt; others continue to make interest payments but may face financial restructurings to sort out their overall debt obligations.

As a result, traders say, a two-tier trading market has developed. Healthy bank loans routinely are swapped among banks at 100 cents on the dollar, or close to it. Troubled-company bank loans, however, are increasingly quoted by Wall Street traders at big discounts to face value. Buyers of these loans tend to be so-called vulture funds that seek profits by investing in troubled companies at cut-rate prices.

Selling off corporate loans at a discount to face value is "a fairly new and revolutionary concept for banks," said Steven Miller, an analyst at Loan Pricing Corp. who edits "The Loan Investor" newsletter. Both U.S. and overseas regulators, however, are leaning hard on banks to rein in their leveraged lending. This has prompted some regional and foreign banks to take losses on bad loans and get out, Mr. Miller said.

Trading distressed bank debt isn't easy. Most loans are encumbered with phonebook-sized supporting documents, said Christopher Mackey, an analyst at Halcyon Distressed Securities Fund. Switching ownership of a loan can take weeks. Typical transaction sizes are $5 million or $10 million, putting the market out of reach for most individual investors.

As is true of any small market, potential sellers and buyers often are so far apart in their proposed prices that trades can't occur. When trades do close, Wall Street firms or other middlemen typically collect fees of 1% of the loan's face value. This is a much richer haul than ordinary stock and bond trading provides for middlemen.

At cheap enough prices, some vulture funds see opportunity in buying even the diciest bank loans. Halcyon bought some Zapata bank debt at 35 cents on the dollar earlier this year, Mr. Mackey said, even though the energy company incurred big losses in the late 1980s and missed interest payments on its bonds last year. Zapata's bank debt recently climbed to 60 cents on the dollar, as surging oil prices have brightened the company's outlook.

"A lot of people who historically played in junk bonds might end up in this market," said Joseph Calhoun, a bank-loan trader at M.J. Whitman & Co., a New York brokerage firm. Bank debt generally is the most senior claim against a company, making it most likely to keep some of its value if a company gets in trouble, Mr. Calhoun said. In contrast, high-yield junk bonds carry only a subordinate claim on a company's assets, making them far riskier for investors.

For banks, the danger is a replay of the same pressures for portfolio markdowns that occurred a few years ago with their Third World loans, Keefe Bruyette's Mr. McDermott said. When trading of less-developed-country loans got started in the mid-1980s, big commercial banks argued that traders' prices were far too low. Bankers insisted that market prices didn't reflect the real "value" of their loans to Argentina, Brazil and the like.

But as Third World debt trading grew, Mr. McDermott said, banks gradually stopped insisting that these loans were valued at close to 100 cents on the dollar. Starting in 1987, big U.S. banks began taking loan-loss reserves that eventually approximated the market's cut-rate prices for such loans.
