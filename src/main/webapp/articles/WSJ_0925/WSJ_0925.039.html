Politics & Policy: IMF to Assist Countries Hurt By Gulf Crisis --- Panel Agrees to Lend More To Nations That Adopt Sound Economic Plans

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 09/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The International Monetary Fund's policy-making panel agreed the agency should promptly lend more to countries hurt by the Persian Gulf crisis, provided they are adopting sound economic adjustment policies of their own. But the panel, the so-called interim committee, said the increased lending can be done through existing lending mechanisms, and it didn't recommend any specific new fund for lending to countries hard-pressed by higher oil prices.

Canadian Finance Minister Michael Wilson, who heads the interim committee, said the panel's agreement "provides a framework" for the IMF to provide help "on an expedited basis."

IMF managing director Michel Camdessus said that the increased lending would be evident by early next year. But he added, "We won't put money down the drain. Help will go to countries with good adjustment policies."

Messrs. Wilson and Camdessus spoke at a joint news conference yesterday after the release of the Interim Committee's statement, which sets out IMF policy for the coming months. President Bush is scheduled to address the opening session of the annual meetings of the full IMF and World Bank here today.

The interim committee, which normally convenes prior to the IMF and World Bank's annual and semiannual meetings, warned that the recent rise in oil prices "may contribute to cost-price pressures and moderate growth, particularly in oil importing countries." It cautioned against attempts to cushion consumers through subsidies, price controls or higher wages, and advised that oil prices should be passed on quickly to consumers.

The interim committee indicated that the IMF will use several existing lending mechanisms to help countries damaged by higher energy costs. It will employ its Enhanced Structural Adjustment Facility, from which it lends to the very poorest countries at subsidized interest rates. It also will increase standby loans and use of its so-called compensatory and contingency financing facility.

The IMF committee hasn't given estimates of the additional lending it will do, but Mr. Camdessus said he expects the organization's existing resources to be "adequate" at least until March 1993, assuming the $65 billion capital increase recently approved by the organization's members is completed soon.

While it praised Saudi Arabia, Japan, the European Community and Japan for pledging to help severely affected Mideast countries, the IMF's policy-making body still is looking to some of the Fund's richer members for further help. It said that it hoped "all members that are in a position to do so will collaborate" in this assistance. Last week, the IMF's managing director discussed the likelihood that the agency might act as an intermediary between oil-rich countries and those suffering balance-of-payments difficulties.

Treasury Undersecretary for International Affairs David Mulford is scheduled to head an international meeting Wednesday to discuss how to coordinate international plans to lend between $13 billion and $16 billion to Egypt, Turkey and probably Jordan, three countries most severely affected by the Persian Gulf crisis.

The IMF already is producing preliminary estimates of the damage to the "front-line states." In an internal memorandum, Jacques de Groote, the IMF's director for Turkey and several other countries, said the crisis "will cause an annual loss to Turkey of about $5 billion."

Mr. de Groote's memo lists measures Turkey has taken since the crisis. These include big rises in prices for gas and diesel; higher sales and import taxes; sales of public land and other revenue-raising efforts. Because of this prompt action, and with "appropriate" foreign financing, Turkey "will be able to restore a viable balance of payments position, reduce inflation, and maintain relatively high real income growth," Mr. de Groote concludes.

Despite what Mr. Camdessus called the "mini-oil crisis" that has followed Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait, the interim committee -- tracking the statement released Saturday by the seven major industrialized nations -- said economic growth is still continuing "albeit at a slower pace."

It also delivered a strong message calling for completion of the current round of international trade talks being held under the world trade treaty, the General Agreement on Tarriffs and Trade. It said a new accord would strengthen the world economy and ensure the success of reforms in eastern Europe and in developing nations.

Mr. Bush is expected to focus his remarks today on arguing that there is an urgent need for more progress in the GATT talks.
