SWISS ANALYZE NUCLEAR VOTE

Although Swiss voters in a weekend referendum narrowly rejected a proposal that the country's five atomic power plants be dismantled within the next 30 to 40 years, the Socialist Party, which had backed the proposal, hailed the vote as marking a "turning point" in the country's energy policy.

According to the final official result, 52.9% voted against the shutdown. The Socialists blamed the narrow rejection of the shutdown proposal on an expensive publicity campaign by the utility industry. The Socialists also noted that 54.6% approved a related draft constitutional amendment that bars construction or licensing of new reactors until the year 2000.

The Swiss government, which had opposed both initiatives, also voiced satisfaction at the results. It said the vote proved that most Swiss felt the country can't renounce nuclear power.

Environmental activists, noting that Switzerland is a net exporter of electricity, say that increased energy efficiency and alternative energies would permit a nuclear shutdown.

LATEST SHORTAGE IN CUBA: PAPER

Cuba, blaming shortages in Soviet supplies of newsprint and paper pulp, said it was cutting back its national state-run press by turning two dailies into weeklies and suspending an armed-forces newspaper. The official newspaper Granma, representing the views of the Communist Party, would remain as the only national daily from Monday to Friday, with a Saturday edition for Havana only.

MORE FOREIGN INVESTMENT IN CHINA

Foreign investment in China rose for the third consecutive month in July after the decline experienced between January and April, according to semi-official news reports. The China News Service reported the country approved 632 new foreign-investment projects in July with total contracts valued at around $536 million. Actual investment during the month totaled $284 million, according to the Ministry of Foreign Economic Relations and Trade.

CAR SLUMP IN SPAIN

Spanish car sales are seen skidding 10% in 1990. In a survey of car makers conducted by the magazine Economia, industry leaders attributed the drop in sales to Spain's strict credit restrictions, a stiff value-added tax and the Gulf crisis. "The Persian Gulf crisis has provoked soaring oil prices and concern that we're heading for a recession . . . all of which implies a decline in consumption," one auto official told Economia.

BRITAIN'S POLLUTION

The environmental group Greenpeace termed Britain "The Dirty Man of Europe" and called for policy changes on air, water and waste. Greenpeace said in a report that in the past year Britain had made "some green gestures" but that the nation was still the largest manufacturer of ozone-destroying chlorofluorocarbons in Europe and faced more legal actions from the European Community over water and air pollution than any other country. The report was called "political pollution" by Environment Secretary Chris Patten, who today will present a policy document on the environment.

CHILE LIFTS `FIDDLER' BAN

Chile's government lifted a 16-year-old ban on the film "Fiddler on the Roof," suppressed by the former military regime of Gen. Augusto Pinochet for alleged Marxist content. The current civilian government said it lifted the ban "because the nation has started a time of real implementation of the principles of democracy and freedom."

AUSTRALIA'S ASBESTOS PROBLEM

Health experts say Australia faces a rising tide of asbestos-related deaths that will not peak until the first decade of the next century. The nation already has the world's highest rate of mesothelioma, a fatal cancer of the lungs, heart and abdomen caused by inhaling asbestos fibers.

James Leigh of the Australian Mesothelioma Register says 1,900 people have been diagnosed as having mesothelioma in the past 10 years, and the incidence of the disease is growing rapidly. So far this year, 167 people have already registered, compared with 214 in the whole of 1988 and 270 in 1989.

"It's been quite a big jump from 1988 to 1989 and the curve of incidence doesn't predict a flattening off until the year 2010," Dr. Leigh said.

"Over the years, millions of (Australians) in many occupations have been exposed to the asbestos fibers" that cause mesothelioma, said David Ferguson, one of the country's leading experts on diseases caused by exposure to asbestos. "Blue asbestos was used in an estimated 3,000 Australian products."
