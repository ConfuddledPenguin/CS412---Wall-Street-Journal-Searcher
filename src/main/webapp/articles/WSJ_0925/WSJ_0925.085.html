Marketing: BankAmerica and Barclays To Link Traveler's Checks

  • Author: Stephen Kreider Yoder
  • Date: 09/25/90

BankAmerica Corp. will join forces with London's Barclays PLC in an assault on American Express Co.'s grip on the lucrative traveler's check business. The two companies hope the merger will capitalize on banks' reluctance to sell checks bearing the name of a competing bank or financial institution -- such as American Express. And the new operation's combined size could give it a boost. "It's our intent to challenge for leadership in the traveler's check business," said Gordon DeLang, senior vice president of BankAmerica's BA Checque Corp.

But American Express, although its share has slipped, has long been the dominant player in the market, and many analysts doubt that the new combination will have much effect on it. Estimates of American Express's share of the world-wide market range from 39% to close to 50%. In addition, American Express checks carry a cachet with consumers that other companies haven't been able to match.

American Express issued $24.1 billion in traveler's checks last year. Mr. DeLang wouldn't disclose BankAmerica's or Barclay's check sales, but said he expected the Interpayment venture to do more than $12 billion in sales in 1991.

BankAmerica, based in San Francisco, said it agreed to acquire an equity position in Barclays's Interpayment Services Ltd. unit and to fold its traveler's check operations into the company, which will then issue both banks' traveler's checks. Moreover, Interpayment Services will provide clearing services for other banks who decide to issue traveler's checks under their own names. The venture also will issue checks under the brands of BankAmerica, Visa and MasterCard.

The deal also could benefit from banks' concerns about selling American Express checks when they sell Visa or MasterCard credit cards, said Spencer Nilson, publisher of Nilson Report, a newsletter on the credit and traveler's check industry. "The banks don't want (to work with) a competitor to their credit cards," he said.

The venture, said Mr. Nilson and other analysts, is a sign of the times for the traveler's check industry, where profits are being squeezed as consumers begin to use automated teller machines as substitutes. World-wide traveler's check sales will increase about 7% percent this year, to $58.5 billion, Mr. Nilson said.

"The business is still profitable but less profitable and it's growing slower," said Mr. Nilson. "There's going to have to be more consolidation -- more of this kind of thing."

By joining forces with Barclays, BankAmerica taps into Barclays strength in markets such as the U.K. and Canada. While Bank of America issues only U.S. dollar checks, Interpayment issues checks in the currencies of countries such as Great Britain, Germany and France and has reciprocal agreements with issuers of checks in Australian, New Zealand and Italian currencies.

Barclays, the world's largest issuer of Visa checks, will benefit from BankAmerica's strength in Europe and Asia and will gain access to BankAmerica's service organization. The Interpayment joint venture will move its headquarters to New York from London, Mr. DeLang said. The banks expect to complete the venture in the fourth quarter of 1990, pending regulatory approval, he said.

American Express executives expressed confidence yesterday that the joint agreement between BankAmerica and Barclays won't have a significant impact on its traveler's check revenue.

According to Michael Goldstein, a senior research analyst with Sanford C. Bernstein & Co. in New York, American Express will earn about $120 million in pretax profits this year from its traveler's check operations, about the same amount it did in 1989.

"This is a slow growth business, but as interest rates rise, it becomes more interesting because of the float," said Mr. Goldstein. The float -- the period during which the checks remain uncashed and the issuer has use of the money -- accounts for much of the profits American Express earns from traveler's checks. "The same thing happened in the early 1980s, when interest rates were high," said Mr. Goldstein.

Mr. Goldstein noted that many of the opportunist companies that leaped into the traveler's check industry have since closed or merged their operations. "I don't think this newest development is going to hurt American Express in any measurable way," he said. "American Express has a strong brand franchise with adequate distribution. It has weathered challenges in the past, and will probably do so again now."

Mr. Nilson, the newsletter editor, said consumers aren't likely to shift check-buying habits because of such mergers. "It isn't going to change the consumer's perception," he said.
