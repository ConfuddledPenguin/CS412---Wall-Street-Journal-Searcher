Senate Clears Bill Designed to Protect Retirement Plans

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 09/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate overwhelmingly approved legislation aimed at restoring protection of older workers' retirement benefits. The Senate voted 94-1 for the measure, after leading backers and former opponents worked out a compromise. That agreement between Howard Metzenbaum of Ohio and David Pryor of Arkansas, Democrats pushing the bill, and Republicans Orrin Hatch of Utah and John Heinz of Pennsylvania attempted to meet employers' concerns that earlier versions would lead to costly benefit packages and make it difficult for the employers to offer early retirement to older employees.

Senior Bush administration officials had recommended that the president veto an earlier version, but a White House spokeswoman yesterday said that the administration hadn't reviewed the compromise and didn't yet have a position on it. The House hasn't yet moved ahead with its version of the bill, and employers want to prevent the House from going beyond the Senate compromise measure in assuring retiree benefits.

The Senate compromise measure would bar employers from discriminating against older workers in providing such benefits as pensions and disability payments. It eliminates a provision that would have penalized employers retroactively, for benefit plans that don't comply with the bill but would have been in effect before its enactment.

The agreement also allows employers to avoid discrimination against older workers by combining benefits, rather than considering each benefit separately. Non-union employers would be given 180 days, instead of 60 days, to comply with the new requirements.

The legislation is designed to overturn a 1989 Supreme Court ruling that gave employers wide leeway to restrict retirement pension and health benefits. The ruling reversed an appeals court decision that had favored June Betts, who had to retire at age 61 because of illness after six years as an Ohio speech pathologist. Because she was over 59, the state denied disability in accordance with state law.

The high court decision was one of several rulings that civil-rights groups said rolled back much of the gains they had made over the years in winning legal protection for workers' rights.
