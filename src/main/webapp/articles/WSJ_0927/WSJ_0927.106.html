USAir Suspends Quarterly Payout, Defers Deliveries

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani
  • Date: 09/27/90

USAir Group Inc. suspended its quarterly dividend and deferred delivery of 16 new Boeing Co. jets for several years in an effort to get its balance sheet and growth plans in order. The Arlington, Va., airline holding company said its board decided yesterday to suspend its quarterly three cents a share dividend for "an indefinite period."

Industry analysts have expressed concern about USAir for some weeks as its already-huge operating costs have swollen in the wake of skyrocketing jet fuel costs, its second-largest expense after labor.

The company is considered more vulnerable to an economic downturn because of existing operational and customer service problems. Those largely resulted from difficulties merging with Piedmont Aviation Inc. last year.

In a statement, USAir Chairman Edwin I. Colodny said the board decided to suspend the dividend "in light of the company's recent financial performance and the poor outlook for the nation's economy and its effect on air travel."

Mr. Colodny said the company would be "conserving funds to meet the company's ongoing capital needs."

USAir yesterday also released details of how it plans to cut its capital plans. USAir said it is delaying delivery of 14 Boeing 737s and two 767s that it was supposed to start getting next year. The 737 is a narrow-body, mid-range plane. The 767 is a wide-body, long-range plane.

Of the 16 planes that it's deferring, USAir said it will postpone delivery of one 767 to 1994 from 1992. The other 767 is being delayed to 1993 from 1992.

Delivery of the 737s is also being delayed to 1993 and 1994 from 1991 and 1992.

USAir executives foreshadowed the delivery delays several weeks ago when they moved to cut the airline's capacity growth to a maximum of 2% from 6%, as compared to this year, and lay off 3,600 employees, or 7% of its work force of 54,000. Many employees say morale at the company has slumped in recent days as a result of the moves.

As a precursor to the capacity reduction, USAir in June laid plans to postpone delivery of another dozen Boeing 737s to 1995 from 1991 and 1992.

The company yesterday indicated it is willing to commit to more planes some years down the road. It said agreed with Boeing to convert 10 existing options for 737s into firm orders that would be delivered in 1994.

Before the suspension of its dividend, USAir's payout was already low relative to its rivals. UAL Corp., parent firm of United Airlines has a quarterly dividend of 25 cents a share. Even cash-strapped Pan Am Corp., which owns Pan American World Airways, had a larger quarterly payment of 10 cents a share.

In his statement, Mr. Colodny acknowledged that the company's dividend was "comparatively modest."

Because its dividend is only three cents a share, it doesn't appear as if the suspension will save the company a lot of money. USAir had 44.5 million shares outstanding as of June 30.

USAir announced its actions after the close of trading yesterday.In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, USAir shares closed at $16, down 50 cents.

The company said it's also putting up for sale 28 old Boeing 727-200 planes and 21 Boeing 737-200s between now and 1992. Those aircraft are considered fuel-guzzlers that would cost USAir a lot of money to operate if fuel costs continue to rise.

In the weeks since Iraq invaded Kuwait, industry observers have said that they expect carriers such as USAir to shelve their less-fuel efficient planes in an attempt to combat rising fuel costs.

A suspension of dividends wasn't one of the strategies, however, that Wall Street analysts expected airlines to use to combat their cost squeeze. Pan Am, for example, has already taken the anticipated course of reducing capacity and laying off employees.
