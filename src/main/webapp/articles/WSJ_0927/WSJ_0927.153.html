McDonnell Douglas Accused by Dingell Of Poor Managing

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 09/27/90

LONG BEACH, Calif. -- Sloppy management has pushed the price tag of McDonnell Douglas's C-17 military cargo plane to more than $400 million each, the chairman of a House subcommittee on oversight and investigations charged yesterday. Rep. John Dingell (D., Mich.), who leads the Energy and Commerce Committee panel, said in a letter to Rep. Les Aspin that cost overruns at the company's sprawling factory here show there is a "need for action to protect the taxpayers' interest," and he called on the chairman of the House Armed Services Committee to conduct a joint hearing into the matter.

While blasting the nation's No. 1 defense contractor for a "management collapse," Rep. Dingell said the Air Force has advised his staff that "the only mechanism left to get {McDonnell} Douglas's attention was either to suspend or significantly reduce progress payments on the C-17 program."

A spokesman at McDonnell Douglas's St. Louis headquarters acknowledged that the Air Force has halted all progress payments on the C-17 since July, pending the completion of a cost-performance review. But he disputed the $400 million-a-copy number, saying that it represented only the first planes off the line; the real "flyaway" cost of a single C-17, he contended, is about $120 million, based on the 120 aircraft the Pentagon says it wants to buy.

In his correspondence, Rep. Dingell cited internal McDonnell Douglas reports from April, which showed that the company's estimate to complete full-scale engineering and development work on the C-17 had climbed more than $836 million above its earlier projection. He added that "more disturbing, the situation appeared to have been deteriorating" as time went on.

The lawmaker, chiding the company for "an absolutely appalling picture in factory labor performance," also singled out a report that showed McDonnell Douglas "was spending 25.8 assembly hours to produce work its own industrial engineers determined should take one hour." In May 1989, he noted, the ratio of actual hours on the project to so-called "should take" hours was 5.15; a year later, the figure doubled. "Some learning curve]" said Mr. Dingell.

But a spokesman for the company here downplayed the Dingell letter, noting that an $836 million cost overrun would still leave McDonnell Douglas some $300 million below the contract ceiling of $4.9 billion. After that crucial benchmark is reached, financially strapped McDonnell Douglas would have to absorb all expenses on its own, without any government reimbursement.

Most importantly, the spokesman indicated that the company is still below the ceiling level, and expects to remain there until it completes the pact. The company, he reiterated, has met every milestone on the C-17 program since November 1989.
