Southland Says Bankruptcy Filing May Be Possibility

  • Author: Kevin Helliker
  • Date: 09/27/90

DALLAS -- Southland Corp. said it may seek bankruptcy court protection if bondholders don't approve by Oct. 22 a debt-swap offer needed to complete a $430 million bailout by the company's Japanese partners. The bankruptcy threat came as part of an announcement that the debt-swap offer, which was to expire at midnight Tuesday, would be extended for a fourth time, to Oct. 22, as well as sweetened in a way designed to attract minority holders.

Ito-Yokado Co., operator of the company's 7-Eleven stores in Japan, also extended to Oct. 22 its offer to buy 70% of Southland for $430 million, cash desperately needed by the debt-laden convenience store operators.

To complete the bailout, Southland needs tenders from bondholders representing 95% of each of five classes of notes. But Tuesday night it remained far short of that. Approvals from one class, the 16.75% notes, remained as low as 45.6%.

To get around the 95% approval requirement, Southland said it is considering filing a prepackaged bankruptcy plan. In such a case, a company enters court with a reorganization plan already approved by 50% of its bondholders representing at least two-thirds of its debt. If the court approves the plan, minority holdouts are legally bound to it.

Southland predicted that it could complete a prepackaged bankruptcy within six months. It also said that Ito-Yokado "presently" was willing to consummate its bailout "within the context of a prepackaged reorganization," if necessary.

Southland said it amended the debt-swap offer to include a cash-election option for holders of less than $300,000 of its notes. The option gives such holders the right to trade their existing notes for cash instead of new debt. For instance, for each $1,000 principal amount of Southland 16.50% notes, a holder could receive $227.87 in cash. Such holders would still participate in the proposed transfer of 25% of Southland common stock to bondholders. Observers say the cash option is designed to bring hard-to-find minority holders out of the woodwork.

In July Southland, operator of about 6,700 7-Eleven and other convenience stores in the U.S. and Canada, went into default on debt taken on in its $4.9 billion 1987 leveraged buy-out. Under its proposed bailout plan, Ito-Yokado would get 75% of the company, bondholders would get 25% and the founding Thompson family of Dallas would see its stake shrink to 5%.
