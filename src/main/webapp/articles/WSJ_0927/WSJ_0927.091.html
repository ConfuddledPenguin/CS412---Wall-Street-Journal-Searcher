Wednesday's Markets: Nikkei Rout And Oil Rise Hurt Stocks --- Industrials Drop 25.99, Almost Wiping Out Tuesday's Price Gain

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/27/90

NEW YORK -- Investors knocked down stock prices, spooked by rising oil prices and a rout overnight in Japanese stocks. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 25.99 to 2459.65 in moderate trading. Longterm U.S. Treasury bond prices were flat in light activity. The dollar was mixed, also in thin trading.

Even though oil prices overseas were lower before U.S. stock markets opened yesterday, the mood among traders was negative because of the sharp drop in Tokyo stocks. The Nikkei index plunged 4.75% on Mideast jitters.

The decline in U.S. stocks nearly wiped out Tuesday's 32.67 point gain in the Dow industrials. "Everyone is looking to get out now," said Christopher Pedersen, director of trading at Twenty-First Securities. "People are looking for 2200" on the Dow, he added.

Mr. Pedersen said another depressant for stock investors was a report in this paper suggesting that investors shouldn't take it for granted that the Big Three auto makers' current dividend levels are sacrosanct. "That's not very comforting," he said.

Another blow to stocks was a sharp reversal in oil prices soon after trading began. Crude oil futures for November delivery ended the day up $1.14 a barrel at $38.67, after being down briefly as much as $1 from Tuesday's close. Peter Beutel, oil futures analyst at Pegasus Econometric Group, said the initial oil price decline -- following a drop the previous day -- was halted by some investors who saw the dip as a buying opportunity.

Oil prices kept moving higher on U.S. Defense Secretary Richard Cheney's comments to a gathering of economists that the embargo may be pushing Iraq to the brink of lashing out militarily. Also, a spate of unconfirmed war rumors ran through oil trading pits -- including one that Iraq had given the U.S. 48 hours to leave the Mideast.

While current oil supplies appear adequate, Mr. Beutel said that traders are justified in bidding prices as high as they are, since war remains a strong possibility, and an Iraqi attack on Saudi Arabian oil fields is likely if hostilities break out. "Saudi oil can't be replaced," he said. "The only people with spare capacity on that level are Iraq and Kuwait."

While bond price movements were shallow, said Raymond W. Stone of Stone & McCarthy Research Associates, those movements nevertheless followed the opposite path of oil-up in the morning on lower oil prices, down in the afternoon on higher oil.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell. Volume was 155.6 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues overwhelmed advancers, 1,119 to 421.

Bond prices were flat. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond stood at 9.12%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency fell to 1.5650 marks from 1.5725 marks and rose slightly to 136.84 yen from 136.72 yen Tuesday.
