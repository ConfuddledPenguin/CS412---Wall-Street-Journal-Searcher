Economy: Poverty Rate Eased Slightly Last Year As Gap Between Rich and Poor Widened

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 09/27/90

WASHINGTON -- The percentage of Americans living in poverty continued to decline in 1989, but the disparities between rich and poor continued to widen, the Census Bureau said. More than 31 million Americans, or 12.8% of the population, lived in poverty last year, according to the bureau -- a statistically insignificant drop from 13% in 1988, but a continuation of a six-year decline from 15.2% in 1983.

Yet the improvements have come during the longest peacetime economic expansion in the country's history, and some analysts believe progress should have been far greater.

"After seven years of economic recovery, the poverty rate was higher in 1989 than in any year of the 1970s, including the deepest recession years of that decade," said Robert Greenstein, director of the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, a nonprofit organization that researches poverty issues. In the recession year 1979, for example, the poverty rate was 11.7%.

"With the economy now running out of steam and a new recession threatening, the poverty rate is likely to start rising again," Mr. Greenstein said.

The bureau's report also showed that the spoils of the country's wealth were being shared less equitably than they were the previous year and over the past few decades. The richest fifth of American households held 46.8% of the nation's income last year, compared with 46.3% in 1988, 44.2% in 1979 and 43% in 1969.

The poorest fifth of American households, meanwhile, claimed 3.8% of the nation's income in 1989, unchanged from 1988, and a slightly smaller share than the 4.1% in both 1979 and 1969.

The figures were based on a survey of 60,000 households. The goverment's poverty threshold -- three times Agriculture Department estimates of what a family spends on food -- was $12,675 in 1989 compared with $12,091 in 1988.

As in the past, far more black Americans than whites lived in poverty. More than 30% of blacks fell below the poverty line in 1989, a slight improvement from 31.3% in 1988, while 10% of whites were below the threshold, compared with 10.1% in 1988. Hispanics also were disproportionately poor; 26.2% lived in poverty last year, compared with 26.7% in 1988.

Daniel Weinberg, the Census Bureau's chief of housing and economic statistics, speculated that one reason black poverty rates are so high is that "a large proportion of them live in female-headed families."

The department also said:

-- After adjusting for inflation, median household income rose 1.3% in 1989, to $28,910. Per-capita income grew 2.2% to $14,060.

-- Male earnings fell 1.8% last year, after adjusting for inflation, while female earnings rose 1.8%.

-- Four times as many children as elderly Americans lived in poverty.
