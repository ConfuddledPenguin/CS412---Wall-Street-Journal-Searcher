Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Putting It `Together' Is New Xerox Theme

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 09/27/90

The top brass at Xerox Corp. have convinced themselves that the copier maker has been transformed into "the Document Company." Now they have to convince the rest of the world. On Monday, Xerox will begin an advertising campaign that attempts to broaden the company's image beyond the product that is synonymous with its name. Some of the ads also will pitch a product that hasn't been introduced yet, known internally as "Xenith," that Xerox is betting its future on. The new machine is a do-it-all contraption that combines computing, scanning, faxing, copying and other capabilities to create high-quality reports.

The new ad campaign, with the theme "Putting It Together," features shots of office workers preparing and using such documents. The Broadway show tune "Putting It Together," from Stephen Sondheim's "Sunday in the Park with George" musical, plays in the background of all the ads. The spots finish with the line: "Xerox. The Document Company." The campaign is the first developed for the company by Young & Rubicam, the agency that Xerox chose in July to replace Backer Spielvogel Bates on its $25 million account.

"We're aligning all the energy of the company to focus not on our products, but on the product of our products, which is a document," says Len Vickers, senior vice president of world-wide marketing at Xerox. "The document is going to be a big issue in the 1990s."

But Xerox faces a stiff challenge in changing its image from the familiar to the slightly more complex. Marketing consultants say they aren't sure exactly what "The Document Company" means. "Maybe a document is something more than just a copy, but you really have to think hard to make a meaningful distinction," says Clive Chajet, chairman and chief executive officer of Lippincott & Margulies Inc., an image consultant.

Xerox, though, insists that there's a difference, and that the positioning is right on target. In a bid to help business executives understand, Xerox is adopting almost a bang-'em-over-the-head strategy by running many lengthy 60-second TV ads that pitch the process of putting together documents "bit by bit . . . piece by piece," according to the Sondheim lyrics.

"The kind of executives we are talking to aren't walking around thinking `I've got document problems,'" Mr. Vickers says. "But they have business problems for which document solutions are the answer," he adds.

One newspaper ad asks the question: "How much are you spending on documents that never get read?" The ad pitches the importance of creating "business documents that are inviting and involving . . . documents that truly reflect the intelligence and uniqueness of your thinking."

Some of the TV spots are more exotic. One features art restorers at work fixing a fresco on the ceiling of an Italian chapel. They're shown looking at maps of the original work. In another ad, astronauts are shown heading out to the launch pad while they check out last-minute documents and schedules. In yet another, a weight lifter in a wheelchair notes his progress on a weight-lifting chart on the wall.

Eric Weber, vice chairman and creative director at Young & Rubicam, concedes that the perception of Xerox as just a "copier maker" is still alive. "But just by saying `Putting It Together,' we began dramatically changing that perception" in consumer focus groups, he says. The company plans an aggressive ad schedule, with TV, radio and print ads scheduled throughout the fourth quarter.

The new ads differ markedly from early Xerox ads, many of which won accolades for their use of humor. One famous Xerox pitchman was Brother Dominick, a monk who won the approval of his superiors by making quick copies with a Xerox machine. And yucks also abounded in a spot in which a nerdy football player named Kolodny handed copies of a game plan to his teammates on the field. Most recently, Xerox ran ads centering on its Malcolm Baldrige National Quality Award.

There are hints, though, that Young & Rubicam may eventually lead Xerox back to its irreverent roots. One ad still being shot features a group of corporate workers toiling late on an important business proposal. But when it's discovered that the firm's eccentric CEO will be giving the presentation, the workers frantically use the Xerox equipment to enlarge the type and make other adjustments. The next day, the young workers watch through a two-way mirror as the CEO does a commendable job.

In any event, Xerox says it's committed to the "Putting It Together" campaign for the long run. "It's a 10-year idea or longer," Mr. Vickers says. "We're not going to change our tune on this one. We've made our bet . . . and this is it."

Ad Age Names Top 100

Philip Morris retained its position as top U.S. advertiser in 1989, with $2.07 billion in spending, according to a report by Advertising Age magazine.

Procter & Gamble, at $1.79 billion, finished second to Philip Morris for the third consecutive year. Sears, Roebuck edged out General Motors for the No. 3 spot this year, spending $1.43 billion. General Motors fell to No. 4, at $1.35 billion.

Total spending by the top 100 national advertisers, at $33.9 billion, was up 6.4% from 1988. The rate of spending growth slowed slightly from 1988, when spending increased 6.7% over the previous year.

The top four advertisers -- the only ones to break the $1 billion spending mark -- accounted for nearly 20% of the top 100 total. Likewise, the top 100 spenders accounted for more than three-fourths of total U.S. ad spending.

Network TV remained the most popular ad medium, accounting for 21.4% of the top 100's overall ad spending and growing 2.7% to $7.2 billion. But its dominance among national measured media is being eroded by jumps in syndicated TV and cable TV, which grew 43% and 27.4%, respectively.

Ad Notes. . . .

BOZELL MERGER: Bozell, New York, said its agency in Italy merged with Testa Pella Rossetti of Milan. The new agency, called Bozell Testa Pell Rossetti, will have billings of about $105 million. Under the agreement, Bozell has majority control under an equity partnership with TPR's principals. Bozell's Tullio Cottinini will be chairman of the new agency.

TOBACCO BAN: Two small Michigan newspapers have banned national tobacco advertising. The Daily News and the Carson City Gazette, both owned by Greenville News of Greenville, Mich., joins a small group of other newspapers across the country that don't accept tobacco ads.
