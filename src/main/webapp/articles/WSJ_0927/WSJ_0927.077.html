Enterprise: Franchising's Prospects Appear Bright, Specialist Says --- Patrick Boroian Expects Floodgates To Stay Open Even With a Recession

  • Author: Jeffrey A. Tannenbaum
  • Date: 09/27/90

Franchising will keep booming, even though franchising failures are more common than most people think. If a recession develops, it will only increase the rush into franchising. That is the forecast of Patrick J. Boroian, president of Francorp Inc., a franchise development and consulting firm based in Olympia Fields, Ill. An M.B.A. graduate of DePaul University, Mr. Boroian became a management consultant and specialist in franchising.

Typically, franchisers license their names and know-how to investors, called franchisees, in exchange for initial fees, ongoing royalties and, often, contracts to supply products and services. Francorp's clients mostly are prospective franchisers, whom the consulting concern helps to create and market franchise programs.

In a recent interview, Mr. Boroian talked mostly about prospects for potential franchisees and franchisers.

Q. If there is a recession, how will it affect franchising?

A. To the extent that it hurts retail sales, it obviously will be negative. But paradoxically, it may have some positive effects too. More companies won't have the capital to expand with company-owned units, and that will make more franchises available. And, of course, middle-management personnel of more companies will be let go, and the sheer number of prospects to buy franchises will rise.

Q. Which sectors of franchising are especially attractive, or unattractive, to investors right now?

A. Some of the old-line industries that have franchised -- hotels, fast-food restaurants, car-rental agencies -- are reaching near-saturation levels, and it's tough for franchisees to make a good living. Investors don't perceive those saturated industries as businesses of the future.

On the other hand, some of the "information age" franchises, business services and professional services are doing well. We're also seeing aspects of distribution -- sales forces, delivery routes -- being franchised and doing very well. Advantage Refreshments System in Los Angeles is a distributor of vending machines that fired its entire sales force and sold franchised sales territories. The result has been higher sales and reduced overhead costs.

Q. There's still a flood of offerings?

A. Yes. That's largely because it is far less expensive to franchise a business successfully today than it was, say, 10 years ago. Prior to 1975, there were 15 states that had franchise registration requirements, and they were all different. Franchising was generally a very expensive proposition just from a legal point of view. Now uniform disclosure documents are accepted in all the states. That opened the floodgates for new franchises. The total cost for becoming a franchiser can run as high as $200,000 or more, but more often it can be done for $100,000 to $200,000.

Q. Who are the new entrants?

A. What we typically see is a small company that has built up one or several outlets. They may or may not be performing well financially.

Q. Don't investors tend to shun unprofitable franchisers?

A. Generally, yes. But 85% of the franchisers out there do not provide an earnings claim. So the first few people who buy into such a franchise are buying on faith. It's like buying a stock that's relatively unknown: If you buy the right one, it can go to the moon. But there's also a higher chance of failure.

Q. In general, how risky is it to become a franchisee?

A. The Department of Commerce has said about 5% of franchisees go out of business in any given year. What that statistic doesn't reveal is the number of franchisees who have failed, but who have been bought out, either by another franchisee or by the franchiser. The real number of failures every year is probably 10%.

Q. How do you know if you are paying too much in royalties?

A. You're overpaying a royalty if it doesn't leave you a competitive rate of return after all your expenses. But you can't just look at the price. You can't just say 8% is too high and 3% is too low. It depends what you're getting back in support -- in advertising, new products, consulting advice. In the temporary-help business, the franchiser takes 50% of gross profit. On the surface that sounds exorbitantly high. But those franchisers finance the payrolls of the franchisees.

Q. How can investors tell if a franchise offering is sizzle or steak?

A. If an opportunity sounds good, you owe it to yourself to visit the franchiser's headquarters and meet the management. Also, by law you receive an offering circular, containing a lot of detail about the franchise. That document should be reviewed by a CPA and by a lawyer who looks at fairness and enforceability. Most important, you should do some due diligence and talk to some existing franchisees to find out how they're doing.

Q. How much capital is needed to become a franchisee?

A. The cheapest franchises, which may be home-based, can be as low as $10,000 -- total investment. You can go up as high as $7 million or $10 million. Most franchises are in the $50,000 to $250,000 range; the average is about $140,000. Generally, up until the recent credit crunch, you could get into a franchise with about 20% or 25% of that in cash. Today, however, franchisees are needing to come up with about 40% in cash. The rest can be financed by a bank, providing you're willing to give personal guarantees and pledge personal assets as collateral.

Q. Besides money, what does it take to be a successful franchisee?

A. Most franchisers require the full time and attention of the franchisee. Obviously, that is not always practical; someone who might buy five (hamburger) franchises for $1.5 million apiece isn't very likely to stand behind the counter and flip burgers. In those cases, there are financial partners and operating partners. But the operating partners must make at least 10% of the investment with their own money; that's what creates the motivation. If you look at franchisee failures, many times they're due to an absentee investor, someone who has the money but is not in the trenches. You build a successful business a brick at a time, a hamburger at a time.
