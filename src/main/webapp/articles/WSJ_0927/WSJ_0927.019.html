Midwest Shakes, Rattles, Rolls, But May Really Boogie on Dec. 3

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 09/27/90

A scientist predicts that a major earthquake will strike the Midwest in early December. Yesterday, the area was rattled by its biggest quake in 14 years. Coincidence? Read on.

At 8:19 a.m. CDT yesterday, a moderate earthquake shook parts of Arkansas, Illinois, Indiana, Kentucky, Missouri and Tennessee. The quake, which measured about 4.7 on the Richter scale, broke windowpanes, cracked plaster, and toppled a chimney in southern Illinois.

The quake was centered on the New Madrid fault, a 120-mile jagged formation that runs from Illinois to Arkansas. In the early 1800s, the area had some of the worst quakes ever on this continent. Still active today, the New Madrid fault has 150 small earthquakes a year.

But what rattled nerves more than usual yesterday is what's known as "The Prediction," which for several months has been the talk of cities and towns all along the Mississippi Valley. Iben Browning, a New Mexico scientist and business consultant, has said strong tidal forces around Dec. 3 will produce a greater-than-normal chance that the New Madrid area will have at least a magnitude 7.0 quake -- about as big as the one that devastated California's Bay Area last October.

Don't laugh: Mr. Browning predicted the Bay Area quake one week before it happened, say people who heard him speak at the time.

The Dec. 3 forecast, which has been dismissed as absurd by the country's top seismic experts, has nonetheless spurred thousands of people to cram into earthquake-survival seminars. Some schools and businesses plan to close in early December.

Was yesterday's quake a foreshock for the Big One? "There's no way a seismologist can know," says Arch Johnston, an earthquake expert at Memphis State University, which had three secretaries tied up all yesterday morning answering calls from media and concerned Memphians. Adds Gerald Wierczorek, a spokesman for the U.S. Geological Survey, "We don't think there is necessarily any correlation with the prediction." Mr. Browning couldn't be reached.

The quake certainly didn't help already jittery nerves. In St. Louis, a TV business reporter aired a special segment yesterday on buying earthquake insurance. In New Madrid, Mo., the fault's namesake, a spokesman for the school district says plans under consideration to close in early December are all but locked up now.
