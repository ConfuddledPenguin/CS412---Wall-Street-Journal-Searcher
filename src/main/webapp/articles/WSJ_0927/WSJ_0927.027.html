Letters to the Editor: Environmentalists Need Sound Economic Base

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/27/90

I join Tim W. Ferguson in applauding Walter Minnick's argument that our nation's efforts to achieve environmental goals should be built on sound economic policy ("Business World," Sept. 18). Economists know that protectionism promotes inefficiency and waste. Environmentalists should heed Mr. Minnick's warning that a domestic timber industry propped up by trade restrictions and other government subsidies won't "save" old forests -- or new forests -- for long. Unfortunately, Mr. Ferguson has reported one very important statistic incorrectly. U.S. forestry companies do not rely on public lands for the bulk of their timber supply. According to the Agriculture Department, the national forests now account for 13%, and all other federal and state lands account for 7%, of the annual wood harvested in the U.S. Nevertheless, each year up to one-half of Forest Service timber placed on the market is sold at prices that do not recover the government's costs of growing and selling the trees. In 1989, such below-cost timber sales cost federal taxpayers at least $365 million.

This strange outcome reflects the fact that Forest Service managers are rewarded for gross timber receipts, acres logged and local jobs "created," not for habitat protected or taxpayer dollars saved. Until these perverse incentives are changed, the Forest Service will continue destroying the environment and undercutting investments in private-sector forestry.

Peter M. Emerson

Fairfax, Va.
