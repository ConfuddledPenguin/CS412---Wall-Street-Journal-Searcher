Study by Panel On Integrity Tests Proves Inconclusive

  • Author: Gilbert Fuchsberg
  • Date: 09/27/90

A long-awaited congressional study failed to resolve whether written integrity tests can reliably predict dishonest behavior. The move effectively offered a reprieve to thousands of employers who want to keep using the controversial tests. The study, by Congress's Office of Technology Assessment, questioned the credibility of research supporting the tests but didn't dismiss it. The result: a declaration that available evidence isn't conclusive.

Because it isn't overtly negative, the finding deprives foes of the exams of ammunition and appears to lessen the potential for an outright ban. Integrity test makers have lobbied for months against an unfavorable finding, fearing federal limits comparable to those passed two years ago against most lie-detector tests.

While hedging on the validity issue, the report urged "further government attention" to concerns arising from intrusive test questions, a lack of safeguards against the misuse of test results and the fact that many honest people are rejected from jobs because they fail the exams. The report also urged further study to insure that the tests don't discriminate against minorities and women.

"Assuming even a modest error rate, widespread use of integrity tests could deny opportunity to many individuals," the study said. It cited data showing that the number of people "incorrectly identified" as dishonest on some tests range as high as 97%.

Such findings drew praise from the National Employment Lawyers Association, a workers' rights group, which said integrity tests should be banned because they can "smear honest job applicants."

But a group of the largest publishers of the tests, who maintain that the exams are more effective than alternative screening methods, assailed the study as "inaccurate and without scientific merit." They termed supportive comments from some experts in the report as "positive aspects," but repeated previously reported assertions that the technology office distorted data to bolster an anti-testing bias.

Officials of the office have denied the charges but may face close scrutiny when lawmakers hold hearings on the issue early next year. Legislative proposals to protect the privacy, legal rights and job chances of people who take the tests are probable, congressional aides said. They wouldn't rule out efforts to ban the tests but judged them less likely, given the intensity of the publishers' efforts as well as complex and conflicting evidence about whether the tests work.
