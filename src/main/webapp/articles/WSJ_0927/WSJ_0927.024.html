Letters to the Editor: Wait for the Real Exxon Salmon

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/27/90

Your Sept. 4 editorial "Salmon Survive Oil Spill" cites this year's strong return of pink salmon to Prince William Sound, Alaska, as conclusive evidence that last year's massive oil spill by the tanker Exxon Valdez had little impact on the area's salmon. Unfortunately, editorial writers often reach conclusions that scientists wouldn't touch with a 10-foot pole. While the pink-salmon return is encouraging, the scientists we talked to warned against drawing any conclusions until more information is gathered. Those scientists argued that we must first understand what role the various parts of Prince William Sound's complex ecosystem might have played in producing the large return.

For example, as televised news reports repeatedly showed, the heavy crude oil killed vast numbers of sea birds and marine mammals. It is these same populations of birds and mammals that prey on salmon fry and smolts. Remove the predators from the food chain and the prey flourish. Clearly, if a greater number of juvenile salmon survive to adulthood, then there are more adults to return to their spawning rivers and streams.

Your editorial appropriately argues against "the politics of hysteria." Even more destructive, however, are public apathy and misplaced confidence that could result from simplistic -- and perhaps incorrect -- conclusions.

James W. Fullilove

Associate Publisher and Editor

National Fisherman magazine

Rockland, Maine

You were factually so far off that I wonder what you were thinking about. Salmon hatch way upriver, which was probably not in water reached by the Exxon oil spill. And most salmon stay out for more than a summer or even a year. Most of them caught this year should have been out in the ocean when the spill occurred. Although it is interesting that a large number came back through the spill area after a good deal of cleanup, the effect of the spill itself may not be gauged for a few years, when the real Exxon salmon come back. Nonetheless, your last paragraph was pretty much on the mark -- as regards zero-risk environmentalists.

Malcolm Landis

Upland, Calif.

---

Your editorial will cause your "credibility among informed people," to borrow your phrase, to suffer a setback.

The effect of last year's spill on the salmon population will be most apparent in spring 1993, when the first of the young fish subjected to the toxic environment should be returning from their ocean sojourn. An uncertainty is introduced here by the differing times certain salmon remain in fresh water before transitioning to salt, so the effect may show up sooner because the four-year cycle is roughly common in all Pacific salmon. The question remains as to the subsequent years' populations, and the long-term effect the spill will have on young salmon making their way to the ocean. Your article serves only to confirm the long-held notion that maturing salmon, some of which are the four-year fish making up the record 1990 harvest, spend the greater part of their lives in the deep ocean, not in Prince William Sound.

The damage done by the spill may not be irreversible, but as an ardent salmon fisherman I have seen the results of devastating practices on salmon populations, and I know how long and difficult it can be to restore them. The environmentalists are right in being upset.

David R. Jones

Colonel, USAF, Ret.

Albuquerque, N.M.

Before you start crowing too loudly, you may wish to consider the following: When drought or other high-stress situations threaten the well-being of a pine tree, it marshals all of its remaining energy to set a massive crop of seed. This way, the endangered tree increases the odds that at least its progeny will survive.

Nature is rife with similar examples. They are not the acts of healthy species, but of endangered ones.

Ellie MacDougall

Writer/Broadcast

Producer Wells, Maine
