Wall Street Parched as Stock Issues Dry Up Under Glare of Gulf Crisis

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi and Randall Smith
  • Date: 09/27/90

NEW YORK -- The Mideast crisis has sabotaged one of Wall Street's biggest moneymakers. The market for new stock issues, which had been one of the hottest areas in the securities business before August, has slowed to a trickle since Iraq invaded Kuwait on Aug. 2. Previously, Wall Street firms had earned fat fees by buying new stock from corporate issuers and reselling them to the public.

The volume of new stock offerings has plunged 48%, to $3.2 billion thus far this quarter from $6.1 billion in the third quarter of 1989. Initial public stock offerings have taken it on the chin even harder, dropping 59% from the year earlier.

The reason is simple: Economic uncertainty triggered by skyrocketing oil prices has whacked the stock market, which has fallen 18% from its record level shortly before the invasion. Many corporations looking to sell stock therefore are hanging back, hoping for higher prices when the dust settles.

"The uncertainty emanating from the Iraqi Mideast crisis has caused gridlock in the equity marketplace," says Michael Madden, co-head of investment banking at Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc.

The pullback in stock issues has hit Wall Street where it hurts. Wall Street firms typically get fatter fees for underwriting stock issues than for debt. So far this year, according to Securities Data Co., investment banks have earned an average fee of $2.7 million for underwriting stock issues, and $3 million for each IPO -- far higher than the $1.1 million average for corporate debt offerings.

"This had been one of the few areas that produced the kind of returns on capital you need in this business," said T. Anthony Brooks, a managing director of First Boston Corp.

Since the Iraqi invasion, 22 stock issues have been postponed, while 34 have come to market, Securities Data says. On Aug. 24, for instance, Sun Microsystems Inc. pulled an offering of 7 million shares, or $217 million, after its stock price sank from $31 to $25.375 a share. Sun chairman Scott McNealy explained that market conditions "clearly have deteriorated."

A week after the invasion, May Department Stores Inc. yanked a $34 million stock issue for its Venture Stores Inc. unit, citing "market conditions." Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. shelved plans to sell shares in two $60 million funds investing, respectively, in Spain and New Zealand. And last week, King World Productions Inc. withdrew a $36 million stock offering tied to a program development unit.

There are other reasons that new stock offerings have slowed. Wall Street executives say investors want to avoid the shares of companies burdened by heavy debt loads, at a time when scores of leveraged companies have developed financial problems. Before the Mideast crisis, when the market was still riding high, some companies that had undergone leveraged buy-outs sold stock to the public in socalled reverse LBOs. "Investors are getting particularly concerned about credit quality," says Clayton Rhorbach, head of equity capital markets at Morgan Stanley & Co.

With oil prices soaring, investors still have an appetite for stock issues related to the energy business. Salomon Brothers Inc. last week led a $41 million stock offering for Grant Norpac, an energy exploration services company. And Orex Energy Co. plans to sell 7.3 million shares currently owned by charitable trusts, worth about $333 million.

Nearly one-third of all new stock issues this quarter have been by companies in oil and natural resources, according to Securities Data. Gold stocks, in particular, have gained from oil-related inflation fears.

Thomas Pratt, contributing editor of the IPO Reporter newsletter, says that September shapes up as the slowest month for new stock issues since November 1987, the month that followed the stock market crash.

And don't look for the picture to brighten any time soon. The volume of new stock issues probably won't pick up until the outlook for the economy and the outcome in the Mideast become clearer.

"The markets abhor uncertainty, and whatever you believe about the Mideast, it's certainly uncertain, and therefore the markets aren't receptive," says Denis Bovin, a managing director at Salomon Brothers Inc.

Wall Street is still holding out hope for the long term, however, banking on the expected need for new equity by many debt-burdened companies. But for now, Morgan Stanley's Mr. Rohrbach says business remains slow.

"Every morning my staff brings me a Street-wide offering calendar, a sheet with all the deals announced, deals priced, and their performance in the secondary market," he says. "These days the sheet is almost empty."
