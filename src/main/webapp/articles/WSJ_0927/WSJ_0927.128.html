Air Route Over Koreas Set in Multinational Pact

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/27/90

MONTREAL -- North Korea and South Korea agreed in principle to link their separate air-traffic control systems and allow airlines to fly over their heavily militarized border. The International Civil Aviation Organization said the two Koreas, China and Japan agreed to a new, shorter air route linking Tokyo and Beijing that will cross over both Koreas, cutting a four-hour flight to about three hours. The organization said about six airlines regularly fly the Tokyo-Beijing route.

Assad Kotaite, president of the aviation organization, based here, said he first approached the four nations in 1981, but, until this summer, political differences made an agreement impossible. "The political climate is much better now," he said.

Under the agreement, North Korean and South Korean air-traffic controllers will for the first time talk to each other regularly. The two countries will set up a permanent voice link so that, as planes cross their border, a controller in one Korea can "hand off" a flight to a counterpart in the other Korea.

All four countries involved will sign a memorandum of understanding later this fall. The aviation organization also will coordinate a meeting between North Korean and South Korean officials to settle technical details, after which the two nations will sign a separate operational agreement. The organization said the new route could open by year end.
