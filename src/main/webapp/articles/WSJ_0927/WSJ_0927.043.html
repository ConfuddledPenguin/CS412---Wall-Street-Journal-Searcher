International: Alan Bond Resigns From Bond Corp. Prior to Revamping

  • Author: S. Karene Witcher and Tony Patrick
  • Date: 09/27/90

SYDNEY, Australia -- Alan Bond resigned as chairman and director of the company he founded, Bond Corp. Holdings Ltd., in an apparent move to pave the way for the foundering concern to be restructured. Peter Lucas, a Bond Corp. director and the company's chief spokesman, was named chairman.

Mr. Bond in July foreshadowed his intention to step down if European holders of certain Bond Corp.-related securities would approve the proposed sale of the company's Australian breweries. The breweries are one of the last remaining major assets of a far-flung corporate empire that spanned property, media and telecommunications, natural resources and brewing.

Bond Corp. proposes to sell the breweries to its 58%-owned unit Bell Resources Ltd., which in turn plans to sell half a stake in the breweries to Lion Nathan Ltd., a New Zealand brewer.

The European investor group is expected to meet in London Thursday, and some of those investors have been discussing a plan to swap their debt for a stake in Bond Corp. If that takes place, further changes in Bond Corp.'s management are likely. As part of his commitment to the investor group, Mr. Bond has said he will reduce his voting rights in Bond Corp. to 25% from about 58% currently.

A spokesman in London for the investor group wasn't immediately available to comment.

Even if the European investor group approves the breweries' sale, it could still come unstuck unless a group of investors that hold $510 million in debentures issued by Bond Brewing Holdings Ltd. in the U.S. agree to sell their securities back to the company at a discount.

Bond Corp. said several of its long-time executives also resigned Wednesday. They include Tony Oates, who was in charge of the company's finances, and Peter Mitchell, who headed corporate development. The three men, though, plan to stay on as consultants until the proposed breweries' sale and shareholder restructuring are completed.

As the company has run into financial trouble and Australian securities regulators launched investigations into its affairs, a number of directors have resigned.

Some Bond Corp. watchers think the steps by Mr. Bond and other executives to distance themselves from the company may also be aimed at taking the steam out of a special investigation into the company's affairs by Australia's corporate watchdog. However, Charles Williams, deputy chairman of the National Companies and Securities Commission, said the resignations won't affect the probe.
