Meridian Bancorp To Post a Loss; Two Moves Cited

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 09/27/90

Meridian Bancorp expects to report a third-quarter loss of $35 million to $39 million, after boosting its loan-loss reserves by $75 million and taking one-time charges for the sale of several businesses. "Economic conditions have deteriorated and we felt it was time to beef up our reserves and strengthen our capital position," said Samuel A. McCullough, chairman and chief executive officer.

In recent years, the Reading, Pa., bank company was intent on diversifying its earnings base by moving into a variety of non-banking businesses. But now with a softening economy and troubled real estate markets, management has decided to sell some of these operations that aren't profitable. The bank will also leave businesses in which it doesn't have the critical mass needed to compete with big major-city banks.

Yesterday, Meridian put its title insurance business on the block. It's also looking to sell its $300 million credit-card portfolio and about $300 million of its indirect auto loan receivables.

For the time being, Meridian is holding to some of its other non-banking operations, such as mortgage lending, asset management, trust services, merchant banking and securities brokerage. These operations had been reorganized into a financial services group earlier this year.

Meridian's management "decided it was time to bite the bullet," said David Penn at Legg Mason Inc. "But it's not one of the disaster cases. {Meridian} has capital and liquidity, which is what the regulators are looking for."

Meridian said the additional $75 million in reserves was needed because certain loans in its commercial and real-estate portfolios continued to weaken during the summer. Nonperforming assets are expected to increase to about $135 million at the end of third quarter from $107.6 million at June 30. Less than one-third of the nonperforming loans are tied to real estate.

Mr. McCullough stressed that the reserve addition wasn't mandated by federal or state regulators, who concluded a review of the bank's books during the second quarter.

The bank said it would maintain its current 30-cent quarterly dividend. Unless the sale of the credit card portfolio and auto loans closes before year end, the bank wouldn't earn enough to cover the payout, Mr. McCullough said.

Meridian estimated a third-quarter per-share loss of 86 cents to 96 cents. In the 1989 third quarter, the bank earned $22.5 million, or 56 cents a share.

The company's shares closed yesterday at $11.75, off $1.50, in national over-the-counter trading.
