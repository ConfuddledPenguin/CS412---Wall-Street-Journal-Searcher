Credit Markets: Corporate Junk Bonds Take Another Beating On Falling Stocks, More Signs of Soft Economy

  • Author: Tom Herman and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 09/27/90

NEW YORK -- The badly battered corporate junk bond market took another pounding yesterday. Falling stock prices and further signs of economic weakness unnerved investors, helping to drive down junk-bond prices for the fifth consecutive day. Prices of many junk issues, those high-risk, high-yielding bonds with low credit ratings or no ratings at all, fell by more than 3/4 of a point, or over $7.50 for each $1,000 face amount, in moderate trading.

Junk bonds of some casino companies were hit even harder. Junk-bond prices now have dropped in 11 of the past 12 trading days, said Gregory Curran, research analyst at Salomon Brothers Inc.

Fears about the financial system's health once again depressed prices of several large banks' stocks and bonds yesterday. Prices of bonds issued by Chase Manhattan Corp., Citicorp and several other large banks have fallen sharply recently in the aftermath of Chase's decision to cut its dividend and reduce its employees by 5,000.

Worried investors increasingly are turning for safety to short-term issues, especially Treasury bills, driving up prices and pushing rates down. For example, the bond-equivalent yield on three-month Treasury bills fell yesterday to 7.53% from 7.59% Tuesday.

"Earlier this week, the main theme was fear about higher oil prices, but today the main theme was fear of financial fragility," said James Capra, executive vice president at Lehman Government Securities Inc.

Many investors yesterday also bought new four-year Treasury notes, auctioned at an average annual yield of 8.53%. "There was significant demand" from investors for the new notes, said Joseph Liro, a senior vice president at S.G. Warburg & Co. "For the small investor, a yield above 8 1/2% for four years on a Treasury note is probably a fairly decent investment. That's a fairly attractive return on a riskless asset."

Long-term Treasury bonds held steady, finishing at about the same levels as late Tuesday. For example, the Treasury's latest 30-year bonds were quoted late in the day at a yield of about 9 1/8%, unchanged from Tuesday. Traders said fears about higher oil prices were offset partly by speculation that U.S. government officials may be close to a budget deficit reduction agreement.

A budget accord probably would allow the Federal Reserve to cut short-term interest rates, many traders and investment advisers agree. "I would be very surprised if the Fed doesn't lower interest rates within the next few weeks," said Geoffrey Bell, president of Geoffrey Bell & Co., a financial consulting firm here.

Mr. Bell also expects further interestrate cuts later this year. He predicted yesterday the rate on federal funds, or overnight loans between banks, will fall to 7% by year end from its recent level of 8%.

But even if the Fed does cut short-term rates, don't expect long-term bond yields to follow suit, Mr. Bell says. "The long bond will dance to a different tune" than short-term rates, he said. By year end, he said, long-term Treasury bonds will yield between about 9% and 9 1/4%.

In the municipal bond market, prices ended mixed. Today, traders expect preliminary details to be announced on a huge new $1.3 billion bond issue by the state of Massachusetts. Some traders yesterday predicted the issue will include bonds due in about seven years and yielding around 8%. Merrill Lynch Capital Markets heads the group of underwriters.

Corporate & Junk Bonds

Corporate & Junk Bonds Junk bond prices slumped again yesterday.

"I think dealers are scared," said David Briggs, senior trader at Federated Investors Corp. "They are not committing any capital to the market at this point.

Many junk bonds finished with declines of between 3/4 point and one point. Junk bonds from casino companies were especially hard hit on fears that those companies' revenues would be hurt by a recession.

Trump Taj Mahal Funding Corp.'s 14% first mortgage bonds, due in 1998, fell two points to 35, while Caesars World Inc.'s 13.50% subordinated notes, due in 1997, dropped one point to 94.

Also hard hit were junk bonds of some companies -- such as retailers -- thought to be especially vulnerable to a recession.

In the investment-grade corporate bond market, issues of several large banks were hit hard again yesterday. For example, Citicorp's 9.75% subordinated capital notes, maturing in 1999, tumbled 5 1/2 points to a price of 83 5/8, bid, to yield 12.90%.

Chase Manhattan's 10% subordinated notes, due in 1999, fell two points to a bid price of 76 1/8. Manufacturers Hanover Corp.'s 8.50% subordinated notes, maturing in 1999, dropped 3 1/2 points to a bid price of 75.

The bank sector "was like a pinball machine," said Joseph Foley, a corporate bond trader at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "Nobody knew what was going to happen next."

Among the other banking sector issues that sustained sizable losses were Bankers Trust New York Corp. and Security Pacific Corp. bonds. Bankers Trust's 9.50% subordinated notes, maturing in 2000, finished down 1 1/8 points at 92 1/4, while Security Pacific's 9.75% subordinated notes, maturing in 1999, fell 1 1/4 points to 94.

Thomas Quigley, a corporate bond trader at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp., said a lack of liquidity in the bank sector accelerated the declines in some bonds. "The day was characterized by growing fears about bank bonds, and the lower levels got, the more sellers came in as fear dominated," he said.

Standard & Poor's Corp., the large credit-rating agency, said yesterday it has reduced its ratings on about $5.2 billion of Chase debt and preferred stock. Among its downgrades, S&P reduced its senior debt ratings on Chase Manhattan Corp. to triple-B-plus from single-A-minus. It also reduced its subordinated debt ratings on Chase Manhattan Corp. to triple-B from triple-B-plus. But it left unchanged its commercial paper rating at single A-2.

Chase issued a statement yesterday saying "we disagree" with the S&P downgrades, "particularly in light of S&P's own comments citing Chase's strong base of retail banking profits, the enhanced profits expected from our aggressive restructuring efforts, as well as our liquidity and financial flexibility."

Furthermore, the Chase statement said, "Chase will have $11 billion in total capital at Sept. 30 and capital ratios in excess of the 1992 regulatory guidelines."

Moody's Investors Service Inc. lowered its ratings Tuesday on Chase's long-term debt and commercial paper.

Treasury Securities

Treasury note and bond prices ended little changed.

The Treasury's bellwether 30-year bond was quoted late yesterday at 96 5/32, up very slightly from 96 3/32 late Tuesday. The latest 10-year notes were quoted at 98 1/2, to yield 8.98%. Late Tuesday, they were quoted at 98 11/32, to yield 9%.

The new two-year notes, sold Tuesday at an average annual yield of 8.18%, were quoted late yesterday at about 8.15%.

Rates fell on short-term bills. The discount rate on three-month bills fell to 7.29% from 7.36% late Tuesday.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds ended narrowly mixed as the rising price of oil again took its toll.

Earlier in trading, tax-exempt securities had reached session highs, gaining as much as 1/4 point, as traders cited a decline in oil prices as one impetus.But as oil reversed its losses, prices began slipping.

In new issue activity, a Chase Securities Inc. group reported an unsold balance of just over $20 million on an issue of $95 million Maryland general obligation bonds. The issue yields from 6.10% in 1993 to 7.15% in 2005.

Ahead on the calendar, a Merrill Lynch Capital Markets group is expected to set the preliminary pricing for $1.3 billion Massachusetts dedicated income tax bonds today.

Traders put yields at the long end of the issue at 8% for bonds maturing in 1997.

In secondary trading, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue due 2018 was unchanged at 95 3/4 to yield 7.55%.

Foreign Bonds

British government bond prices fell sharply.

Traders cited remarks by U.K.'s Chancellor of the Exchequer, John Major. Speaking in Washington, Mr. Major said Britain won't join the European exchange rate mechanism until inflation is falling, and that inflation isn't likely to fall until next year.

Britain's 11 3/4% bonds due 2003/2007 dropped 22/32 point to 99 2/32, to yield 11.87%. The 12% bonds due 1995 fell 14/32 to 99 10/32 to yield 12.17%.

In other major markets, Japanese bond prices also fell, and bond futures prices ended at their lowest levels since they were instituted five years ago.

Japan's benchmark government bonds, the No. 119, 4.8% issue due in 1999, fell 0.14 to 80.63, on the Tokyo Stock Exchange. The yield rose 8.71% from 8.675%.

Mortgage & Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities rose as much as 6/32.

Government National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% issue for October delivery rose 1/8 point to 97 25/32, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp.'s 9 1/2% issue rose 3/ 32 to 98 9/32, and Federal National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% securities rose 3/ 32 to 98 3/32.

In the asset-backed market, Salomon Brothers Inc. priced an $800 million Household Credit Card Trust 1990-1 offering. The issue is backed by Visa and MasterCard receivables from Household Finance Corp.
