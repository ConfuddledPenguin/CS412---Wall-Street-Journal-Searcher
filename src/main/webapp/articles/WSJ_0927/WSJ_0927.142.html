Politics & Policy: Bush Expected to Ask Congress to Clear $7.5 Billion Arms Sale to Saudi Arabia

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 09/27/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush is expected to ask Congress to approve an immediate $7.5 billion arms sale to Saudi Arabia, administration officials said. The sale, expected to include modern tanks, armored personnel carriers and anti-tank missiles, would be the first portion of a package of over $20 billion publicly debated here in recent days.

Originally, the administration hoped to submit the entire package -- including items with a longer delivery time, such as jet fighters. But supporters of Israel complained that the huge sale might endanger that nation, so the administration has decided to seek urgent items now and defer the rest until early next year.

Meanwhile, Turkey's President Turgut Ozal said his nation would support "armed intervention" against Iraq if it becomes necessary, provided the action is approved by the United Nations.

At a breakfast here with reporters, Mr. Ozal was asked whether Turkey would remain neutral in any war by the U.S. and its allies against Iraq. He said military force should be considered only if the economic embargo fails. But he added: "If the United Nations decides on the armed intervention, we will not be against it." He contrasted that stand with Turkey's neutrality in the Iran-Iraq war.

Mr. Ozal's comment was the second in two days by a major power hinting that a U.N.-sanctioned military strike against Iraq would be supported if the embargo fails. On Tuesday, Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze warned Baghdad that the U.N. has the power to "suppress acts of aggression" through force.

Separately, Secretary of Defense Dick Cheney said the Persian Gulf crisis may be entering a period of "danger" in which Saddam Hussein "may seek to use his military force to try to break the stranglehold that the embargo has imposed."

He said the administration is determined "to see to it that we send enough forces over there so that we don't leave them exposed and at the end of a long supply line and increase the risk to any higher level than is warranted." He asserted, "The president believes in what I describe as the `don't screw around' school of military strategy."

And Secretary of State James Baker disclosed that Iraq has demanded from the U.S. and other nations a list of nondiplomats being sheltered in their embassies in Baghdad. Mr. Baker said Iraq has threatened to execute Americans and others who hide such people, adding: "We've received a note to that effect. It falls under the heading of the principle of the three R's, I think -- we've read it, it's repugnant and we reject it."

The latest phase of the economic embargo against Iraq, which covers air traffic, got off to a confused start. Jordan continued to receive Iraqi passenger flights. The State Department said flights involving only passengers might not be covered by the embargo but should be barred by an earlier U.N. ban on financial transactions with Iraq. Jordan said it was reviewing the passenger flights, and U.S. officials said the U.N. might have to clarify the passenger-flight issue. Mr. Cheney said the U.S. military hasn't been asked to enforce the air embargo and he doesn't expect that it will do so.

Also, Kuwait's minister of oil and finance, Sheik Ali Khalifa al-Sabah, said he believes little more can be done to pressure Iraq short of using military force. "It's time either for Iraq to bend to the will of the world or be faced with the military option," he said.

But Mr. Ozal stressed that the use of force isn't imminent and that the world should wait at least three or four months to see if the embargo can force Iraq to withdraw from Kuwait. He said the U.S. hadn't asked Turkey to join in a military strike and added: "I don't want to speculate more about it."

A Turkish role in any military action would be crucial as Turkey borders Iraq and has a powerful army trained by the North Atlantic Treaty Organization. Mr. Ozal said "Turkey is on the side of the Western world in this problem."

The U.S. Export-Import Bank is expected today to clear a $1.37 billion loan guarantee to help Turkey purchase 200 military-type helicopters. The helicopters would come from the Sikorsky Aircraft Division of United Technologies Corp., Hartford, Conn., or other U.S. suppliers. The loan guarantee, which would be the largest single financial transaction in the history of the U.S. export credit agency, was tentatively cleared earlier, and Ex-Im Bank officials said the bank's board now could give it final approval because the House and Senate Banking committees haven't raised any objections during a mandatory 25-day review period.

Mr. Ozal, who met with President Bush Tuesday, said he doubts Iraqi President Saddam Hussein will be overthrown in a coup because of his control over Iraqi society. But he added that he thought the Iraqi leader would be ousted if he is forced to give up Kuwait. Because of that, he said, withdrawal will be "very difficult" for Saddam Hussein.

Nevertheless, Mr. Ozal said, Iraq can be forced to move, and the embargo might work. He said it is worth trying because it is unprecedented in history.

Mr. Ozal said the Kuwaiti rulers might lack popular support in their own nation if a referendum were held. But he said it would be a "grave mistake" to offer any concessions to Iraq in return for a pullout from Kuwait as that would encourage aggression by others and turn the "new world order" into a "jungle."

The Turkish leader said his nation is suffering economically from the crisis and hasn't yet received promised compensation. But he stressed that the financial plight of Turkey has been "exaggerated" in the press, causing a few banks to be unwilling to roll over short-term export financing. He said Turkey would survive the crisis economically and shouldn't be lumped together with much poorer states as Egypt and Jordan.

Meanwhile, the Treasury issued licensing regulations for the export of donated food to Iraq and Kuwait. The department said it will grant export licenses for food shipments "to relieve human suffering" on a case-by-case basis but only after the U.N. has approved each donation. It said such aid must be provided by humanitarian agencies under U.N. auspices.

So far, the Treasury has had only two inquiries about food licenses, said Richard Newcomb, director of the office of foreign assets control.
