U.S. Asks `Substantial' Term for Milken, Beyond Boesky's Three-Year Sentence

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 09/27/90

NEW YORK -- The government, as expected, urged a federal judge to sentence Michael Milken to a "substantial" prison term for six securities-related crimes to which he pleaded guilty in April. The government specifically urged that Mr. Milken get more than the three-year sentence received by Ivan F. Boesky, the convicted former stock speculator who cooperated with the government's investigation of insider trading and other securities crimes.

Meanwhile, lawyers for Mr. Milken acknowledged that he engaged in a dozen additional transactions -- to which he did not plead guilty -- that furthered an illegal conspiracy with Mr. Boesky.

The admission came in response to an order from U.S. District Judge Kimba Wood that the attorneys provide additional information about Mr. Milken's activities with Mr. Boesky. Mr. Milken, the former junk-bond chief at Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., is scheduled to be sentenced by Judge Wood on Monday.

In a sentencing memo made public on Tuesday, Mr. Milken's lawyers urged Judge Wood to sentence Mr. Milken to community service, saying he was a decent and charitable man who was remorseful about any wrongs he had committed.

In contrast, the government's memo depicted Mr. Milken as a man who systematically used his firm's junk-bond department, based in Beverly Hills, Calif., to pursue his illegal activity. According to the memo, the department became "the means through which Milken rewarded his friends, defeated his rivals and committed his crimes." Mr. Milken's objectives, the government said, "were simple: power and wealth."

Although Mr. Milken's lawyers have said that he intends to cooperate with the government after he is sentenced, the government indicated that it has doubts about Mr. Milken's willingness to do so.

"Our confidence in Milken's intention to be truthful and forthcoming has waned," the memo stated, because "nothing about Milken's conduct to date suggests the contrition or remorse that often predicts sincere cooperation by a defendant."

One of the crimes to which Mr. Milken pleaded guilty last April involved a conspiracy to commit illegal trades with Mr. Boesky that included transactions other than those to which Mr. Milken pleaded guilty. But Mr. Milken never said what the other trades were, and Judge Wood said on Monday that to issue an appropriate sentence she needed to know more about them.

In response, Arthur Liman, the lead attorney for Mr. Milken, wrote a letter to the judge listing additional transactions.

When he entered his plea last April, Mr. Milken said he forced an unwilling Fischbach Corp. into the hands of corporate raider Victor Posner by having Mr. Boesky take a large and unlawfully secret position in the company's stock. Mr. Milken also said he helped Mr. Boesky evade net-capital rules and used Mr. Boesky to artificially support the price of MCA Inc. common stock while Golden Nugget Inc., a Drexel client, was selling its MCA shares.

In the letter to Judge Wood, Mr. Liman said that Mr. Milken had further assisted Mr. Boesky in evading net-capital requirements by secretly agreeing to buy American Broadcasting Cos. and Enserch Corp. securities from him in early 1985, with the understanding that the securities would be bought back later at no loss to Drexel.

Mr. Liman also detailed securities trades in eight different companies that were intended to enable Boesky to recoup losses he had suffered on other illegal trades with Mr. Milken. These compensatory trades, which occurred in 1985, involved securities of Leasco, First Texas, Valero Energy Corp., MCI Communications Corp., Wickes Cos., Republic Airlines, MSA Realty Corp. and MSA Shopping Corp.

Mr. Liman also said that Mr. Milken had encouraged Mr. Boesky to invest in securities of Diamond Shamrock Corp. and Harris Graphics Corp., with the understanding that Drexel would share in any of the profits Mr. Boesky derived.
