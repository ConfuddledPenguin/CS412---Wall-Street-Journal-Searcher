Politics & Policy: Shift by Bush On Gains Tax Called Possible --- Alternatives to Cut in Levy Might Be Considered, Dole and Gingrich Say

  • Author: David Wessel and Jackie Calmes
  • Date: 09/27/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush is ready to back away from his insistence that the capital gains tax rate be cut, top congressional Republicans suggested after a White House meeting. "He might be willing to compromise on capital-gains taxes," which have been one of the main sticking-points in the budget talks, said Senate Republican Leader Robert Dole of Kansas.

Mr. Bush himself, questioned by reporters aboard Air Force One later in the day, wouldn't say if his stance has changed. "The Republicans know what I want when I talk about growth," he said.

Sen. Dole and Rep. Newt Gingrich of Georgia, the second-ranking House Republican, said the president is willing to consider alternatives to a capital-gains cut as a tax incentive for economic growth. The two GOP leaders mentioned such modest alternatives as tax breaks for research and development and tax-favored enterprise zones, ideas that many Democrats already support.

Budget negotiators have been casting about for a way to narrow the tax bite on the sale of assets other than a straight cut in rates. Negotiators have discussed such alternatives as sticking with the curent rate but applying it only to gains in excess of inflation; cutting the rate only for profits on new investments, and limiting the lower rate to investments in startup companies.

Democrats charge that the president's insistence on reducing capital-gains taxes is a major obstacle to reaching a deficit-reduction compromise. Republicans counter that other issues also remain unresolved, including ways to restrain government benefit programs.

On that score, lawmakers said that negotiators have discussed ways to trim Social Security spending in the latest round of closed-door talks. "It's achieved a new level of seriousness," said one Democratic aide.

Democrats have argued for taxing away more of the benefits paid to upper-income Americans while Republicans lean toward a delay in cost-of-living adjustments, the aides said.

But negotiators still haven't come to any final agreement on how to restrain benefit programs, one of the stickiest issues in talks. Any changes to Social Security are likely to be politically explosive -- particularly with all the recent attention devoted to the growing surplus in the Social Security trust fund.

As negotiators -- five top congressional leaders and three administration officials -- resumed their closed-door talks in the Capitol, Mr. Bush took the offensive.

"All through the talks, for 135 long days, time and again I've gone the extra mile. . . . And each time the other side has said, `It's still your move,'" the president charged during a campaign stop in Ohio. "If and when the ax falls, the Democratic Congress knows that it will be held accountable and I will take that message to every state in the union," he warned. "It is their fault. . . ."

The president's words, after weeks of conciliatory comments, seemed to suggest that negotiators still weren't near an accord just four days before fiscal 1991 begins Monday. On that date, the government faces a shutdown or $85 billion in crippling, automatic spending cuts.

Mr. Bush's broadside also reflected the feeling of congressional Republicans that Democrats have been winning the publicrelations battle. "One of the things the president has been doing wrong is he's been hammering the Congress -- not the Democrats in Congress," said Rep. Mickey Edwards (R., Okla.), who attended yesterday's White House meeting. "We decided it was time for him to really hit the Democrats hard."

But Senate Democratic Leader George Mitchell said, "The irony of the president's statement is we thought we were making progress." House Speaker Thomas Foley said Democrats won't let the partisan speech critically damage the talks.

Yesterday, Speaker Foley postponed action on an emergency spending resolution to keep the government funded for the first weeks of the new fiscal year. Democratic leaders wanted to avoid a divisive vote on a provision that would postpone the spending cuts required next week under the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law. The leaders are plainly ambivalent about trying to delay the cuts, given Mr. Bush's threat this week to veto any waiver, and may postpone action until as late as Sunday.

If congressional and administration negotiators reach a budget accord before Monday, then Congress and Mr. Bush are likely to quickly enact a stopgap spending bill to keep the government going and waive the Gramm-Rudman spending cuts.

If there's no agreement, however, it isn't clear what happens. If an emergency spending bill passes and is signed by the president, then the Gramm-Rudman ax falls. About $85 billion of the sums appropriated by that bill are withheld, and at least some agencies furlough employees.

But there are other alternatives. If Congress and the president can't even agree on the terms of an emergency spending bill, White House Chief of Staff John Sununu has said the administration is preparing to rely on 1980 and 1981 opinions by President Carter's attorney general, Benjamin Civiletti, to keep essential parts of the government going -- at least for a few days. Attorney General Dick Thornburgh said yesterday that the decade-old legal opinion "is held to and subscribed to by this department," but that he hadn't discussed its application with President Bush.

President Reagan relied on the Civiletti opinion when the federal government was without any spending authority for four days in 1981. Many government facilities closed, but air-traffic controllers stayed on the job, Social Security checks were mailed and meat was inspected.

Such a move would be controversial, however. "On the face of it, this appears to be nothing more than a thinly veiled attempt to selectively apply the sequester," said Sen. Robert Byrd (W., Va), referring to the spending cuts mandated by the Gramm-Rudman law.

"I don't think the president has that authority," added Rep. John Myers (R., Ind.). "Sununu is assuming a lot, but then he doesn't think much of the Congress. Never has."

As the talks have dragged on without success, rank-and-file Democrats in the House are pressing harder for a budget alternative that better reflects the party's priorities if the talks break up. "This is going nowhere," said Rep. Richard Durbin (D., Ill.) after House Democrats caucused yesterday. "There is no mutiny. There is nothing like it, but some of us feel we have to move on to the next step."

The Senate voted yesterday to apply the automatic cuts to the paychecks of members of Congress and top administration officials. The law currently exempts them -- though not their staffs -- from furlough or other direct hits.
