U.S. Moves to Start Talks With Mexico About Free Trade

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 09/27/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush formally notified Congress that the administration plans to begin negotiating a free-trade agreement with Mexico, administration officials said. Under U.S. trade laws, the Senate Finance Committee and the House Ways and Means Committee now have 60 legislative days to decide whether to allow the negotiations to occur under "fast-track" procedures. Fast-track authorization is considered crucial because it prohibits amendments and so helps prevent any proposed accord from getting bogged down in Congress.

Senate Finance Chairman Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas) said a U.S.-Mexico free-trade agreement "may offer both countries important economic opportunities." But he added that it is "critical any free-trade negotiations be analyzed and then managed carefully," particularly in light of the significant economic disparties between the U.S. and Mexico.

In Montreal, Mexican Minister of Industry and Commerce Jaime Serra said Mexico is seeking a pact that is "similar but not necessarily the same" as the existing U.S.-Canada free-trade agreement. Canada has indicated it wants to join the U.S.-Mexico talks, and Mr. Serra said it is "very likely" that talks among the three countries will result in a comprehensive North American free-trade agreement. But he conceded, "it will be a difficult exercise."

Mr. Serra and Chilean Finance Minister Alejandro Foxley agreed recently that their two countries would work to remove all tariff barriers between them within five years, Mr. Foxley said in an interview. President Bush is encouraging a western hemisphere-wide free-trade area.
