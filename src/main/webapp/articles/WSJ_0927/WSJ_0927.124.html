Giving a Hand: Betty Flood Finds Job With No Pay Holds Some Rewards All Its Own

  • Author: John D. Williams
  • Date: 09/27/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

AFTER A 7 A.M.-TO-3:30 P.M. DAY as a telephone-systems assistant at Public Service Electric & Gas Co., Betty Flood returns to her Newark, N.J., apartment and starts a second job. It doesn't pay, the hours are terrible and the noise can be deafening. The 56-year-old Ms. Flood wouldn't have it any other way.

Betty Flood runs her own game room/hang-out for the 254 children of the 540-unit apartment complex in which she lives. Concerned by the number of children who didn't seem to have a place to go after school, she started the project on her own in 1978, using space donated by the building's landlord. Despite open-heart surgery five years ago, she is still going strong. "As long as I can keep a child from roaming the streets, I'll stay right here," says Ms. Flood, who, with four children and 21 grandchildren of her own, has experience with growing children.

To a visitor, Ms. Flood's center (it has no official name) is a bedlam of TV blare, clicking billiard balls, clunking hand-hockey pucks and shouts and squeals. But Ms. Flood, whose own apartment is on the floor above, is in control from the moment she greets her charges with a "Hi, baby, how ya doin]" as they are signed in until she jots down their time out (the center opens at 4:30 p.m. and usually closes by 11 p.m.; those under 13 years of age must go home by 8 p.m.).

Should a youth become rowdy, Ms. Flood brings the offender back in line by brandishing a hollow plastic toy baseball bat. But that's as rough as it gets, and the posted rules -- such as "anyone cursing will be suspended for six weeks" -- are mostly obeyed. "When Betty Flood speaks, the kids listen," says John Jenkins, principal of a nearby elementary school. "She has the respect of all the kids in that complex and they're better children for the experience."

It's more than just a safe place to go after school. Ms. Flood leads rap sessions with the youths, whose ages range from a preschool four to a high school 18, and teaches them a range of skills from reading to neat dressing to chess playing. She organizes picnics, sponsored by her utility employer and held in the complex's outside area, and stages Easter egg hunts and Halloween parties.

Neither Ms. Flood nor Charlotte Young, a volunteer assistant, are paid for their efforts. Ms. Flood doesn't even have an estimate of the annual costs of running the center, which is supported by donations from local merchants and other businesses.

Fiori DePhillips, owner of the nearby Roseville Pharmacy which is contributing about $300 to Ms. Flood's Halloween party, has a high regard for Ms. Flood's work. "She helps keep the kids off the streets and out of trouble and is showing us how to get along and work together," he says.

Although some local politicians have urged her to seek public funding for her effort, she has declined. "If I took state, federal or city funds, I would have to take in children from other areas and I couldn't control them," she says.

Meanwhile, she's looking forward to January when her center will move into a twice-as-large space at the complex where there will be two bathrooms -- one for boys, one for girls -- and a full-sized pool table given by Ms. Flood's employer, PSE&G. She sees no problem filling the new quarters with active kids.
