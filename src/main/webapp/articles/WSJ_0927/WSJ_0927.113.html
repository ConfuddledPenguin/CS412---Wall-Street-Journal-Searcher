Salomon Fund Tied To Europe Currencies Raises $500 Million

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/27/90

NEW YORK -- Salomon Brothers Inc. said it raised $500 million for what it called a "low risk" fund that will seek to profit from currency-rate changes as Europe moves toward its 1992 unification. The securities firm's EMS Strategies Fund is Wall Street's latest attempt to sell products that are geared toward the unification of the 12-nation European Community in 1992.

Executives at the Salomon Inc. unit said they were somewhat surprised to be able to raise $500 million from institutional investors in the currently skittish markets. (The money was raised privately over the past three months.) But they explained that the EMS fund was marketed as a conservative alternative to high-yield, high-risk instruments, such as junk-bond funds, that have soured over the past year.

The EMS fund, unlike typical currency funds that promise yields of 25% to 30%, might not be able to return much more than 9.5% because it won't be highly leveraged, Salomon executives said. But clients are satisfied because it's a "risk-averse world" right now, said Peter Taggart, the fund's manager.

Rather than dealing in world-wide currencies, Salomon said, it will buy and sell only the currencies of EMS markets, as the European Community attempts to establish a single currency. The fund can make money by trading currencies that briefly move out of line with the intended EMS target, Salomon said.

"The risk is laid off mostly to the central banks because they are going to have to get the currencies in line as we move to 1992," said Michael Holland, head of Salomon's money-management unit, the fund's adviser, which manages more than $6 billion.

The EMS fund's base will be a portfolio of short-term U.S. government bonds.

Eventually, if all the European currencies are united, the Salomon fund must be dissolved because opportunities to profit from fluctuations won't exist. But no one expects the EC to unify all the currencies in 1992. Salomon said that its fund may not have to be wound down for four to six years.
