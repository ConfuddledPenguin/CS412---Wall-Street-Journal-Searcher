Technology & Medicine: Microsoft Net Increased 83% In 3rd Quarter

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 04/18/90

REDMOND, Wash. -- Microsoft Corp.'s net income soared 83% in the fiscal third quarter as the biggest maker of software for personal computers continued to outperform competitors in its industry. For the quarter ended March 31, Microsoft posted net of $75.2 million, or 62 cents a share, up from $41.1 million, or 36 cents a share, a year earlier. Year-earlier per-share earnings have been restated to reflect a 2-for-1 stock split effective April 13, 1990. Revenue rose 58% to $310.9 million from $197 million.

Net income was 24.2% of revenue and gross margins were 78.7%.

Microsoft's performance was slightly better than expectations, "but it wasn't a blowout quarter," said Rick Sherlund, an analyst at Goldman Sachs.

Microsoft's revenue was up only $10 million from the quarter ended in December. Mr. Sherlund noted that Microsoft introduced no new products in its March quarter but said he expects the company to release four new products in the current quarter ending in June, including a long-awaited new version of its Windows software package. Windows, which Mr. Sherlund said will be released on May 22, makes Microsoft's MS-DOS, the standard software used in most personal computers, easier to learn and more functional.

Microsoft cited as factors for its strong quarterly results continued high demand for its products overseas. Foreign sales accounted for 58% of total revenue.

The company also said it was helped by the growing popularity of its word-processing applications. WordPerfect Inc., a closely held firm in Orem, Utah, is the leader in this software category, but "we're gaining market share," said a Microsoft spokesman. He declined to endorse speculation that the company's gains come at WordPerfect's expense.

Despite its rosey financial picture, Microsoft continues to struggle with the dismal showing of a new operating system designed to replace MS-DOS. Sales of OS/2 are poor, so Microsoft "is now pushing Windows as the primary software for the desktop" and not OS/2, said Timothy McCollum, an analyst at Dean Witter Reynolds.
