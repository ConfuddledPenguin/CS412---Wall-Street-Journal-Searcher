Politics & Policy: Charles Keating Asks Court to Return Lincoln S&L to Him Under Supervision

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 04/18/90

WASHINGTON -- Charles Keating asked that Lincoln Savings & Loan Association, which the government seized last year, be returned to him under judicial supervision. Testifying in his court challenge to the government's takeover, Mr. Keating said "giving Lincoln back to the people who can run it properly will save the taxpayers from any losses."

Federal regulators say Mr. Keating and his managers ran Lincoln fraudulently and estimate that the cost of bailing out the Irvine, Calif., thrift will top $2 billion, making it one of the most expensive thrift failures ever. Mr. Keating is the chairman of Lincoln's former parent company, American Continental Corp. of Phoenix, Ariz.

Mr. Keating said that over several years he could manage Lincoln's assets so that taxpayers wouldn't be called in to cover the government-insured deposits at the thrift. But Judge Stanley Sporkin responded with some skepticism to Mr. Keating's request, saying regulators, not court-appointed managers, "have the congressional mandate" to manage thrifts under the massive S&L bailout enacted this past year.

Mr. Keating made his plea as he wound up several days of questioning by his own lawyers in the trial being held in federal district court here. Earlier yesterday, lawyers for Mr. Keating continued their assertions that regulators were incompetent and had a vendetta against Mr. Keating. Mr. Keating's lawyers disclosed a memo suggesting regulators knew they had made mistakes in calculating Lincoln's capital strength but refused to admit them. At the time, regulators were contending Lincoln was $114 million short of its capital requirements.

In the memo, dated Feb. 17, 1989, Federal Home Loan Bank Board official Alvin Smuzynski wrote to a superior that "it now appears that the examiners did make a significant -- $60 million -- error in their calculation." But Kevin O'Connell, another senior regulator, told reporters at the courthouse that regulators later determined they hadn't made a mistake.

Mr. Keating charged that other documents show regulators were intent on denying various proposals he made to sell Lincoln in the spring of 1989, before the government took it over in April. Regulators attached 21 conditions to a sale Mr. Keating proposed, but still refused to approve it even after Mr. Keating accepted the conditions, he asserted. In a Bank Board memo dated March 30, 1989, Mr. Smuzynski wrote, "We sometimes play a game with recommendations-you can deny a recommendation by conditioning it to death."

But as cross-examination by lawyers for the government began yesterday afternoon, other documents pointed to serious reservations the government had about some aspects of the sales Mr. Keating had arranged. The government was concerned that Mr. Keating was financing the sale himself and that American Continental executives were involved in the buy-out groups.

As part of one proposed transaction, government lawyers said, Mr. Keating's American Continental was to pay $40 million for a subsidiary of Trump Group Inc., a concern controlled by New York businessmen Jules and Eddie Trump. At the same time, the Trump brothers, who aren't related to Donald Trump, would provide about $35 million to finance the purchase of Lincoln.

In a letter introduced by lawyers for the government, Lincoln's own auditor, Touche Ross & Co., suggested that American Continental was paying $35 million more for the Trump Group subsidiary than it was worth. Mr. Keating disputed that Touche Ross had reached that conclusion.

James Murphy, a lawyer for the government, also said the proposed sales didn't meet the government's demand that American Continental agree to pay back to Lincoln $94 million in controversial tax-sharing payments that Lincoln had paid to American Continental.
