Executive Pay (A Special Report): Salary Scales --- What Am I Worth? A Simple Quiz to Gauge Whether You Earn Too Little, Just Enough or Too Much

  • Author: Gil Schwartz
  • Date: 04/18/90

NEWS OF THE $260 MILLION in bonuses paid out by Drexel Burnham as it was spiraling into insolvency has cast the question of executive compensation in a new and refreshing light. In a year when the firm lost $40 million, senior executives carted off envelopes engorged with seven- and eight-figure sums -- all for doing what even dispassionate observers might call a dramatically terrible job. That sounds like a pretty good deal to me.

Payment for performance is a noble concept. But to be grossly overpaid regardless of the result? That, my friends, is the American dream]

If you, too, are inspired by this tale, you're probably in the process of evaluating your compensation package and asking yourself: "How much am I worth? And in spite of that, how can I do better?"

To get a handle on your market value, please fill in the following information as truthfully as possible. Remember -- your career path is at stake]

1) It's 7:30 am. You're at your desk, eating a dry muffin. The phone rings. "Can you have the financial impact of the Kotzwinkle deal in shape by 11?" asks the controller. You:

a) Say, "Of course, sir" and proceed creatively to crunch numbers until they appear credible. You format them attractively, take them to the production shop, print and bind them by hand, and deliver them to your boss, who is kind enough to take them to the executive suite.

b) Call in three subordinates, complete the task quickly, and take the completed document over to the controller. You schmooze for a couple of hours, accepting his thanks and kudos with aplomb.

c) Say, "Sorry, Irv, I've got a full plate through the close of business." You then accept his apologies for having troubled you on such a busy day, and go to lunch.

2) How many people do you manage?

a) None. I listen to a lot of people and do whatever the greatest number seem to think is advisable. Why do you ask?

b) I can tell just about anybody around here to do something, with the exception of my secretary.

c) I don't know.

3) When discussing your pay, which term do you use?

a) Thousands of dollars.

b) Hundreds of thousands of dollars.

c) Lots and lots of millions of dollars.

4) Which of the graphs below represents the way you spend your average day?

a) Apple

Work 43%

Friends & Family 23

Sleep 22

Commuting 7

Recreation 4

b) Meringue

Work 85%

Sleep 15

c) Gourmet

Sleep 42%

Work 17%

Charming Associates 8

Being Conveyed 8

5) How do you feel about your pay?

a) I feel like Babe Ruth -- being paid to play a game I love].

b) Through my managerial excellence, this firm is producing significantly higher revenues and increased margins. I work hard, and there isn't a company in America that wouldn't be lucky to have me. As for this one, they better bump me or trade me.

c) I need a new boat.

6) Complete the following sentence: I'd like my next raise to:

a) Pay for the rent increase on my apartment and enough food to keep my wife and children happy, with perhaps a little left over for a magazine.

b) Give me enough to equip my office at home with all the latest technological miracles so I can keep in touch with my data base while I sleep.

c) Buy the new boat, and probably a crew for it, too, since I hate to sail. Oh, and one of those neat little yachtsman's caps. I love those.

7) I believe that as a business person I should be driving:

a) The same Volkswagen Beetle that I first drove to college back in the 1970s. The mileage those things get is unbelievable].

b) A company car with my name embossed on the transmission and radiator fluid so pristine you could drink it with an olive.

c) I'll sit in the back seat of anything with a stereo and a footrest.

8) Last year, how many vacations did you take, and where did you take them?

a) I took one very nice week at home, playing with my children in the parking lot of our condo.

b) I never get a vacation, thank you, although I was able to snatch a few long weekends at the company apartment six blocks from my office.

c) I take five weeks, although I'm entitled to eight. But it's so much fun sitting in my office with the door closed. I'm looking forward to seeing how that hand-made Persian rug will look on my newly burnished hardwood floors.

9) Which statement is true?

a) I could go on like this forever.

b) I'm mad as hell and I'm not going to take it anymore.

c) I could go on like this forever.

The Scoring

OK, now we're ready to score. Give yourself one point for every (a); two points for every (b); and three points for every (c).

If you scored between nine and 12, congratulations, you are earning exactly what you deserve. You can look forward to your next 3% increase and all the good things it will add to your quite satisfactory life. You possess the one thing that money can't buy.

If you scored between 12 and 23, you'd better get busy. No matter what you're hauling down, you're underpaid and you know it. You are donating your entire life to the enterprise, and given your justifiably high opinion of yourself and the value of your time and talents, any remuneration is barely worth it.

If your score tops 23, eat this column. A lot of people aren't worth what they earn, but you're a master at it]

We who are about to work salute you]
