Law -- Legal Beat: Freeman Is Sentenced for Insider Trading

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and Milo Geyelin
  • Date: 04/18/90

The legal saga that began with his controversial 1987 arrest on insider-trading charges concluded yesterday when a federal judge ordered Robert M. Freeman to serve four months in jail and pay $1 million in fines. U.S. District Judge Pierre N. Leval told the former head of arbitrage at Goldman, Sachs & Co. that while he believes Mr. Freeman is "a very much chastened person," jail time was necessary because of the "need for deterrence in the marketplace."

Looking tanned and fit in a navy blue suit, Mr. Freeman sat emotionless between his two attorneys as the judge sentenced him to one year in prison, with the last eight months suspended. Mr. Freeman was also sentenced to two years probation after his prison term and to a total of 300 hours of community service.

Once one of the country's most powerful arbitragers, Mr. Freeman agreed in August to plead guilty to a felony count and resigned from Goldman Sachs, the firm he had worked at for 19 years. Mr. Freeman admitted to using inside information received from Kidder, Peabody & Co. takeover strategist Martin Siegel in connection with the proposed takeover of Beatrice Cos. by Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co.

Mr. Freeman faced a possible sentence of as much as five years in prison, and the government put his maximum fine at $1,860,000. He and his lawyers declined to comment on the sentence issued.

Many of Mr. Freeman's friends and the lawyers who crowded the packed courtroom to watch the proceeding said they believed Mr. Freeman's sentence to be stiff; some said they believed he wouldn't have to serve any time in prison.

Indeed, Robert Fiske and Paul Curran, Mr. Freeman's lawyers, argued that his crime wasn't one that warrants jail time. They said in a sentencing memo last month, and again yesterday, that he should be treated with leniency because of the "suffering that Mr. Freeman has undergone for three years as a direct consequence of the government's precipitous and highly publicized arrest of him in February 1987."

But Assistant U.S. Attorney John McEnany, lead prosecutor in the case, recommended a prison sentence that "reflects the magnitude of the crime and the absence of any excuse" for committing it. Prison, he said, is the "only sanction with any meaning to wealthy and powerful individuals and which will provide a deterrent." Judge Leval seemed to agree.

"The trading of inside information has become a watchword or standard practice of arbitrage," the judge told Mr. Freeman. "Arbitragers think themselves authorized to disregard the law, and these practices seriously undermine the integrity of the marketplace. It is precisely because of those facts that the court cannot treat the matter as one of small importance."

The government's case against the 48-year-old Mr. Freeman began with his February 1987 arrest on insider-trading charges. The subsequent indictment of Mr. Freeman and two others was dropped in May 1987, and prosecutors promised another, broader one. But the new indictment never came and prosecutors last year said they wouldn't charge the two others charged along with Mr. Freeman.

Both Mr. Freeman and Goldman had vigorously asserted that Mr. Freeman was innocent of any wrongdoing until his decision to plead guilty in August. But Mr. Freeman is understood to have agreed to plead after prosecutors confronted his lawyers with the fact that another Goldman Sachs official was prepared to testify against him.

Mr. Freeman's sentencing was delayed for months because his lawyers sought to block prosecutors from presenting to the court allegations that he committed some 20 other crimes in addition to the one he pleaded to. Those other crimes, the government said, should be considered in meting out his sentence. But Judge Leval said he wouldn't consider any other allegations of wrongdoing in considering a sentence.

Mr. Freeman must still settle civil charges brought against him by the Securities and Exchange Commission. Now that Mr. Freeman has been sentenced, a date will soon be set for the sentencing of his accuser, Mr. Siegel.

Mr. Freeman's lawyers recommended that he serve his time in a minimum security prison on Pensacola, Fla. Mr. Freeman, whose primary residence is in Rye, N.Y., has a second home near Pensacola, according to lawyers in the case.

FIDELITY INVESTMENTS, in a reversal, supports Pennsylvania takeover bill.

With the Pennsylvania Legislature poised to pass the nation's toughest anti-takeover legislation this week, one of the measure's most vocal critics has switched sides -- and raised a lot of eyebrows.

Fidelity, a Boston mutual-fund group, had condemned the bill as harmful to shareholders because of provisions controlling rights of investors to mount proxy contests. But the bill is being strongly pushed by Armstrong World Industries Inc., which is the target of a proxy contest and a possible takeover attempt by the Belzberg family of Canada.

A possible reason for the switch, opponents of the bill say, is that Fidelity is on a short list of applicants being considered by Armstrong to manage its $180 million employee-thrift plan.

"Obviously, the timing of these two developments concerns me," John C. Bogle, chairman of Vanguard Group, an investment company, wrote Pennsylvania Gov. Robert P. Casey. Vanguard, which strongly opposes the anti-takeover measure, was recently dropped from consideration for Armstrong's account.

"I trust that your office . . . will carefully review this matter, to assure there were no improprieties in this situation," Mr. Bogle wrote.

Armstrong, of Lancaster, Pa., issued a vigorous denial. "In the strongest terms it is both preposterous and patently false for anyone to assert that either Armstrong or Fidelity Investments has offered or received a `deal,'" John N. Jordin, Armstrong's vice-president for governmental affairs, wrote Pennsylvania State Sen. Noah H. Wenger, the original sponsor of the bill whose district includes Armstrong's headquarters.

If the bill passes and is signed into law, as expected, it will become the first in the nation to garnish profits of short-term shareholders who sell within 18 months after a failed attempt to control the company. Among other controversial provisions, the law would also restrict voting rights of large shareholders in proxy contests. Fidelity loudly opposed the bill but changed its mind after amendments were added stipulating that the restrictions wouldn't apply to shareholders who mount proxy fights with no intention of taking over the company.

The bill has passed overwhelmingly in both chambers of Pennsylvania's General Assembly and Gov. Casey has indicated he plans to sign it into law once compromise measures are ironed out.

"We fought this very hard," said Robert C. Pozen, Fidelity's general counsel. "And we also knew how to count votes." Another provision in the bill would allow company directors to consider interests other than those of shareholders when assessing takeover offers. That provision remains the most objectionable to institutional investors.

"We believe that given the political reality, we did as well as we could," said Mr. Pozen. As for Vanguard's insinuations of a sellout? "We believe that the letter is slanderous and libelous," said Mr. Pozen, "and we are consulting our lawyers."

BANK ACCOUNTS ARE FROZEN in money-laundering investigation.

A federal judge in Atlanta froze 684 U.S. bank accounts that allegedly once contained hundreds of millions of dollars in illegal Colombian drug profits.

The unusual order by Judge William O'Kelley came at the request of the Justice Department, which said it would take legal actions to seize whatever money is left the accounts once it confirms they have been controlled by drug traffickers. Judge O'Kelley issued the order Monday, and the Justice Department announced the action yesterday in Washington.

The judge ordered banks holding the frozen accounts to give the government all relevant records so that investigators can continue to trace illicit drug profits. He ordered that records on about 70 additional accounts be turned over as well. The Justice Department said the total of 754 suspect accounts may have received illegal deposits of nearly $400 million as of early 1989. The accounts are held in 173 banks in 22 states and the District of Columbia, with the biggest concentrations in New York and Miami, the department added.

The department's actions are the latest phase in the government's largest-ever money-laundering inquiry, known as Operation Polar Cap. Prosecutors didn't charge any of the banks affected by the court order with wrongdoing. But when pressed on the issue, Peter Djinis, a lead prosecutor on the case, declined to rule out actions against U.S. banks and said the investigation would continue.

The suspect accounts allegedly received illegal proceeds from the operations of the world's biggest cocaine traffickers, including Colombians Pablo Escobar-Gaviria and Jorge Ochoa-Vasquez, leading members of the so-called Medellin cartel.

A "certain small percentage" of the targeted accounts may turn out to be controlled by legitimate businesses and individuals, and therefore won't be seized, said Wilmer Parker, another lead prosecutor on the case.

As a result of Operation Polar Cap, 127 people have been criminally charged, the Justice Department said. More arrests are expected.
