Law: High Court Clears Filing of Job Bias Suits in State Court

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 04/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court ruled unanimously that lawsuits alleging employment discrimination in violation of federal law may be filed in state, as well as federal, courts. The decision, by a 9-0 vote, isn't likely to cause a major change in the handling of lawsuits under Title VII of the 1964 Civil Rights Act, which prohibits job bias. But it may prompt plaintiffs to move some Title VII cases to state courts.

Last year, according to a recent federal courts study, more than 7,500 Title VII cases were filed in federal courts. Because the federal courts are devoting a growing percentage of their time to drug prosecutions, many Title VII cases face long delays and mounting legal fees. Some state court systems also experience significant backlogs, but many states are able to handle civil lawsuits faster than the federal courts. In those states, filing Title VII complaints in state court may be an attractive option.

The decision, written by Justice John Paul Stevens, resolves conflicting views among five federal appeals courts. Those in Philadelphia, San Francisco, Denver and Atlanta said that Title VII cases must be filed in federal court.

The fifth case was a ruling by the federal appeals court in Chicago upholding an award of back pay and benefits of about $27,000 to a Chicago woman who sued Yellow Freight System Inc. alleging sex discrimination. The lawsuit was filed in state court; Yellow Freight moved it to federal court and asked that it be dismissed. The company argued that while the case was pending improperly in state court, the legal deadline for filing in federal court had passed.

But a federal judge, and the appeals court, refused to dismiss the case, ruling that the filing in state court was proper and within the deadline. Yesterday, the Supreme Court affirmed that ruling in Yellow Freight System Inc. vs. Donnelly.

The high court ruled that a state may make the drug peyote illegal in all circumstances, including its use as a religious sacrament by members of the Native American Church.

In a 6-3 decision, the high court upheld an Oregon law that makes peyote, an hallucinogenic drug, illegal. The court, in an opinion written by Justice Antonin Scalia, said the First Amendment guarantee of religious freedom doesn't excuse an individual from complying with a state's valid criminal laws. The high court noted that some states -- 11 by one estimate -- have made an exception to their drug laws for religious use of peyote; but the court said the First Amendment doesn't require such an exception.

The Oregon Supreme Court ruled that the First Amendment rights of two workers at a private drug rehabilitation clinic were violated. They were fired for religious use of peyote, and were denied unemployment benefits due to their misconduct. But the high court yesterday reversed the state court ruling and said the workers' rights weren't violated in Employment Division vs. Smith. Justice Harry A. Blackmun dissented, joined by Justices Thurgood Marshall and William J. Brennan Jr.
