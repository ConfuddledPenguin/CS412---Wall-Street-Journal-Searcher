Great American Forced to Raise $350 Million --- Thrift Required by Auditor To Take Major Reserves That Decimate Capital

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 04/18/90

Great American Bank said it will have to raise $350 million in new capital by year's end after its auditor forced fourth-quarter loan-loss reserves that gutted much of the big thrift's remaining tangible capital. The capital infusion, required as part of a survival plan that Great American said yesterday had been endorsed by federal thrift regulators, raises substantial questions about the future of the nation's eighth largest thrift, based in San Diego, Calif.

At best, officials indicated they would have to sell or merge the company or undertake a dismantling of its prized Southern California branch network. But even those routes could be difficult or painful. Outside investors have been generally loath to buy into the sick industry, and Great American itself said yesterday that a sale would cause "substantial dilution" of current shareholder interests. The company conceded that if its capital-raising efforts don't bear fruit, it could face further operating restrictions and possibly even government conservatorship or receivership.

"Great American has a very slim chance of raising $350 million," said Joe Jolson, a thrift industry analyst with Montgomery Securities in San Francisco. "What they might be able to do is sell every asset in all their markets except San Diego, shrink and raise capital. They have about a 50-50 chance of pulling that off."

But Mr. Jolson said even such efforts wouldn't ensure the company's compliance with capital regulations in the long term, and that they would seriously impair the company's earnings.

Nonetheless, the fact that the Office of Thrift Supervision backed the idea seemed a breath of fresh air for Great American investors. Like many other capital-shy thrifts, Great American earlier this year was required to seek regulatory approval of a business plan to meet a new series of tough federal capital standards. Great American is among a distinct minority of undercapitalized thrifts that have been able to win approval of their plans from the government. Analysts said that development may have accounted for Great American stock getting a lift yesterday. Its shares closed at $4.625, up 87.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Great American has been hurt by real estate woes in the Southwest -- and by the insistence of its auditor, Deloitte & Touche, that it provide greater reserves against that real estate.

Yesterday, the company reported a revised loss of $198.9 million, or $8.30 a share, for the fourth quarter ended Dec. 31, reflecting provisions for losses on loans and foreclosed real estate totaling $271.2 million.

Previously, Great American reported a loss for the period of $59.3 million, or $2.48 a share, including an $87 million loan-loss provision, but subsequently said the figures would be revised at Deloitte's request.

Great American reported profit of $19.8 million, or 80 cents a share, in the year-earlier quarter.

For 1989, the company reported a loss of $263.4 million, or $11 a share, reflecting $405 million in loss provisions, compared with 1988 net income of $49.3 million, or $1.99 a share.

The company declined to immediately provide 1989 year-end data for loss reserves or troubled loans. At Sept. 30, it reported nonperforming loans of $584.7 million and reserves of about $208 million, and some analysts expect the year-end numbers to jump. It has about $15.9 billion in assets.

As a result of the losses, Great American said it met none of three federal capital standards pertinent to thrifts at year's end. It added that, in the auditors opinion for the 1989 financial statement, Deloitte will express concern over the possibility of Great American being able to survive as a going concern because of looming regulatory sanctions if the company falls short of its capital goals this year.

A spokesman said the company's investment banker, Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc., had been in contact with "a number of interested investors," but declined to name them. Analysts said possible suitors include Wells Fargo & Co., which has been scouting potential acquisitions in the lucrative Southern California retail market for years, and the Pritzker family of Chicago, which recently pulled out of a deal to buy control of New York-based CrossLand Savings FSB, the largest thrift outside Califronia.

Also apparently interested is Wedbush Corp., a Los Angeles financial services firm, which recently announced plans to seek permission from federal regulators to boost its 17% stake in Great American. Wedbush couldn't immediately be reached for comment.
