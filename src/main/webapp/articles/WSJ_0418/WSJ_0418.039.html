McDonald's to Buy Building Materials, Equipment Made From Recycled Plastic

  • Author: Bill Paul
  • Date: 04/18/90

McDonald's Corp. said it will give preference to goods made of recycled material when building, remodeling or re-equipping its U.S. fast-food restaurants, a move that could provide a big boost for plastics recycling. McDonald's already gives preference to a number of recycled paper products, including napkins and drink containers. Its new program -- dubbed "McRecycle USA" -- will also give preference primarily to items that can be made in whole or in part from recycled plastic. Among them: construction lumber, ceiling and floor tile, restaurant seats and table tops and playground equipment.

"McDonald's is acting where other people are only talking. They've put a lot of pressure on us for quick delivery," said Michael Dahl, general manager of Eaglebrook Plastics Inc., the Chicago-based manufacturer of Durawood, a wood substitute made of 100% recycled high-density polyethylene.

The potential demand for recycled plastics "ought to make communities more willing to include plastic in curbside recycling programs," said Dennis Saborin, vice president of plastics-recycler Wellman Inc., explaining that many municipalities shy away from plastic because it's bulky. Indeed, only about one million of the 10 million or so U.S. households covered by a curbside program presently have the option of recycling plastic, according to Resource Recycling, a trade publication.

Moreover, said Mr. Saborin, those towns that do include plastic generally limit collections to soda bottles and milk jugs, whereas the McDonald's program will encourage recycling of all sorts of plastic containers and articles.

McDonald's pledged to spend $100 million this year on recycled construction supplies and equipment, compared with $5 million to $6 million last year. But Shelby Yastrow, senior vice president and director of environmental affairs for McDonald's, said that to reach that goal, McDonald's may first have to encourage development of a manufacturing base for construction and remodeling goods that use recycled material. "McDonald's is talking about having things made that have not been made before," said Mr. Saborin.

To spur manufacturer interest, Mr. Yastrow said McDonald's will have a 24-hour 800-number that manufacturers can call for specifications and other information. In addition, Mr. Yastrow said McDonald's will fight to change local building codes wherever such codes effectively prohibit using construction materials that contain recycled material.

Mr. Yastrow said McDonald's plans to open about 370 restaurants in the U.S. this year, while remodeling an additional 1,000 outlets. Eventually, Mr. Yastrow added, the new recycling program will be expanded to include McDonald's units abroad.

In an interview, Mr. Yastrow also said that McDonald's has other environmental plans in the works. He denied that the company's concern about the environment in any way springs from efforts by several communities to ban McDonald's polystyrene packaging because it won't decompose in landfills.

Mr. Yastrow indicated that future plans might include having McDonald's spur the use of water-saving devices, energy-efficient light bulbs, and even backyard compost shredders. "If I can use the muscle of McDonald's, I will," he said.
