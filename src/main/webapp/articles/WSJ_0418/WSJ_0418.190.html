Timken Co. Reports 31% Decline in Profit For the First Quarter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/18/90

CANTON, Ohio -- Timken Co. reported a 31% drop in first-quarter profit, but company officials told the annual meeting that the outlook is brighter for the rest of the year. The producer of bearings and specialty steels earned $19.1 million, or 63 cents a share, for the quarter, down from $27.7 million, or 98 cents a share, a year earlier. Sales declined 6%, to $408.5 million, from $434.7 million.

Profit in the first quarter was reduced by the sluggish economy and lower steel prices, Chairman W.R. Timken Jr. told the annual meeting. He said profit was also lessened by lingering effects of a strike in the 1989 fourth quarter that caused some customers to temporarily seek alternate suppliers.

The company has "good momentum" going into the second quarter, however, and expects higher profits for the full year, he said, declining to make a specific projection.

In 1989, Timken earned $55.3 million, or $1.88 a share, all in the first half. The business slowdown, combined with the fourth-quarter strike, resulted in a loss in the final period that exceeded the modest third period profit.

Timken's bearings business is strong in most parts of the world, and prices are higher than a year ago, Joseph F. Toot Jr., president, said. "Steel demand is reasonable, but pricing is low, because there's a lot of excess capacity, particularly in bar steel," a major Timken product.

The company is on schedule with a $1 billion capital spending program extending through 1994, Mr. Toot said. Many of the major additions and plant improvements included in that plan will be described later this year, he said, after detailed plans are finished and negotiations with plant-site communities completed.
