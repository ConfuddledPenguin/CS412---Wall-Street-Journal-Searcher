Foreign Exchange: Dollar, Mark Strengthen Against Yen Ahead of Today's U.S. Trade Report

  • Author: Randolph Walerius Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/18/90

NEW YORK -- The U.S. currency surged above 160 yen briefly yesterday before settling to a more modest advance against the Japanese unit, with dealers going long dollars in expectation of today's release of a strong U.S. trade number for February. The mark, moving almost in lock step with the dollar, also marched higher against its Japanese counterpart, advancing to well above the important 95-yen level.

The dollar was little changed against continental European currencies in trading lightened by participants' anticipation of the U.S. merchandise trade data.

The mark and the dollar are running neck-and-neck and the winner will be determined by the February trade report, said Albert Soria, first vice president and foreign exchange manager at Kansallis Osake Pankki in New York.

If the trade deficit narrows to between $4 billion and $6 billion, the U.S. currency could rally to between 1.6850 and 1.6950 marks, said Mr. Soria, adding that there's little likelihood that the dollar will move far above 1.70 marks on strong data.

But if the trade gap comes in between $7.5 billion and $8 billion, the dollar will slip, possibly testing 1.6650 marks, he said. Mr. Soria also said that the yen will probably continue to weaken.

The U.S. merchandise trade deficit is seen narrowing to a seasonally adjusted $7.6 billion from January's $9.25 billion shortfall, according to the average estimates of 15 economists.

But there is talk in the currency markets, particularly in Japan, that the figure could be as low as $4.5 billion to $5 billion.

Susan Babcock, vice president of corporate foreign exchange at Hongkong & Shanghai Banking Corp., said such low estimates represent a Japanese tactic to build expectations of a very good trade figure and push the U.S. unit higher on dollar-buying ahead of the report. She noted that Japanese institutions provided strong interest in buying the dollar during morning trading in New York.

When the exaggerated optimism is disappointed by a more modest improvement in trade, the dollar will slide in a sell-off, she said.

The dollar hit an intraday high of 160.15 before drifting lower. The dollar had previously climbed over 160 yen April 2 in Tokyo, also settling lower that day.

Ms. Babcock also said the yen is suffering from an "increasing realization that there is no (Group of Seven industrial nations) defense for the yen." Before the G-7 meeting April 7, traders had worried about an agreement to cooperate with intervention to defend the Japanese currency.

Moreover, Kansallis Osake's Mr. Soria said, a strong U.S. trade performance in February would show that the dollar is already competitively valued and would weaken whatever reason the U.S. might have for joining intervention.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6768 marks, down from 1.6775 marks late Monday, and at 159.68 yen, up from 159.30 yen late Monday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6385, up from $1.6300 late Monday.

The mark ended at 95.23 yen in late trading in New York, up from 94.95 yen late Monday. Sterling rose to 2.7474 marks from 2.7355 marks late Monday in New York.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the U.S. currency opened for trading at 160.13 yen, up from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 159.83 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.6796 marks.

The West German currency performed well despite the announcement by the Soviet Union yesterday that it is starting to restrict the supply of natural gas to Lithuania as part of economic sanctions that Moscow had threatened to enact against the rebellious republic. Such political turmoil frequently prompts safe-haven dollar buying that buoys the U.S. unit.

But Kansallis Osake's Mr. Soria said there isn't any reluctance to buy the mark.

The dollar was neither harmed nor helped by a batch of data that showed conflicting measures of the strength of the U.S. economy.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $375.20 an ounce, unchanged. Estimated volume was a light 2.4 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Wednesday, gold opened for trading at $374.25 an ounce.
