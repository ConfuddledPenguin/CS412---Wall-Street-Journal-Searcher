Marketing & Media: Baton Broadcasting Sues Thomson's Globe & Mail

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/18/90

TORONTO -- Baton Broadcasting Inc. launched a lawsuit against Canada's national newspaper, the Globe & Mail, and one of its reviewers over a negative article about a television movie. The Globe & Mail is owned by Thomson Corp., a Toronto-based publishing, printing and travel conglomerate. Baton, which financed the movie, is seeking 8 million Canadian dollars (US$6.8 million) in damages, including C$1 million in special damages for loss of business, and C$1 million in aggravated and punitive damages.

Baton alleges in a statement of claim filed with the Supreme Court of Ontario that Globe & Mail reviewer John Haslett Cuff intended to leave readers with the impression that Baton financed the film "for dishonourable motives," and that the broadcasting concern "lacked talent, intelligence and skill in the production of television programs."

Baton said in the statement of claim that because of the article, it has "suffered injury to its character, credit and reputation and damage to its trade and business." It said it has lost the chance to sell the film for future distribution.

The television movie, concerning the life of a nineteenth-century Mohawk Indian leader, Joseph Brant, aired on Canadian television Feb. 13. The article by Mr. Haslett Cuff appeared Feb. 10, under the headline "Banal `epic' a costly disappointment."

Neither Mr. Haslett Cuff nor officials of the Globe & Mail could be reached for comment.
