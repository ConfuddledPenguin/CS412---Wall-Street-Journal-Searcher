AT&T, Unions Reach Accord On Retirement

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 04/18/90

BASKING RIDGE, N.J. -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co. and its unions tentatively agreed on a nonmanagement retirement plan that would nearly double the number of eligible workers to 34,000. The early retirement plan, agreed to by the Communications Workers of America and the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers, would add three years of age and three years of service to the records of all nonmanagement employees May 1. Unlike under the previous AT&T proposal, the added years count toward the calculation of the pension as well as toward eligibility.

As a rule of thumb, an AT&T employee's age and length of service must add up to 75 for pension eligibility. Currently, 18,000 nonmanagers are entitled to a pension. AT&T has 165,400 nonmanagers world-wide. The company now estimates it will trim 13,000 jobs this year, up from 12,000 before this agreement was reached.

The voluntary plan also allows individual AT&T units to offer special incentives to nonmanagement employees where there are more people than the workload requires -- including tacking on five years of age and five of service and offering a 15% "sweetener" that would boost monthly pension payments.

Last year, a special pension was offered to AT&T managers that added five years of age and service, plus 15% to pension checks for up to five years; it was almost identical to the offer that can be made to nonmanagers in overstaffed AT&T organizations. Out of 32,121 supervisors eligible last year, 12,535 took it. The program cost AT&T $163 million and yielded estimated annual savings of $450 million, the company said. Currently, AT&T has 116,000 managers.

The company employs 280,400 people world-wide -- about 29,000 fewer than in January 1989.

The move is part of AT&T's continuing effort to cut costs, largely through voluntary plans, to meet the competition. The company said it couldn't assess the financial impact, because it doesn't know how many workers will take advantage of the plan.

CWA President Morton Bahr called the tentative pact "a very mixed blessing." He said, "It is a sad statement that workers in their late 40s and early 50s can only look forward to pension enhancements, when they should have so many years of productive work ahead of them."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, AT&T closed at $42 a share, up 12.5 cents.
