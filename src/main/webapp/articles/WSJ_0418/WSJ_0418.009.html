Sales of Cars, Light Trucks Fell 11.2% In Early April, Easing March's Optimism

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 04/18/90

DETROIT -- Sales of domestically built vehicles in early April fell 11.2% from a year ago, tempering some optimism generated by a 2.9% rise in late March. The 10 companies that build vehicles in the U.S. sold 262,692 cars, vans and light trucks in the April 1-10 period, down from 295,735 vehicles a year ago, despite deep discounts and other incentives. Analysts attributed the lackluster performance to a still-sluggish economy.

"For a spring selling market still characterized by incentives, these numbers don't look so good," said Tom Webb, chief economist for the National Automobile Dealers Association. "Unless there is a major change in the economy, such as reduced interest rates, domestic new cars sales appear to remain in their current doldrums."

Following recent patterns, the drop in car sales was steeper than that for van and light trucks. Auto makers reported they sold 163,585 cars built in the U.S., Canada and Mexico during the period, down 13.3% from a year ago. Domestic truck and van sales fell 7.5% to 99,107 vehicles.

Car sales yielded a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 6.6 million cars, according to a Department of Commerce formula. That's down from late March's 7.2 million rate and the 7.6 million pace of a year ago.

Despite the overall downturn, the seven Japanese companies that build cars in the U.S. increased the number and percentage of car sales they made to 21,062, or 12.9%, from 13,894, or 7.4% a year ago.

Among the Big Three U.S. auto makers, General Motors Corp. reported a 13.6% drop in domestic car sales, but a 2.2% improvement in light truck sales, partly because GM has added a trio of plastic-body minivans to its truck lineup. GM was the only one of the Big Three that didn't lose ground year-to-year in the truck market.

Ford Motor Co. attributed its relatively weak sales to big incentives that boosted sales a year ago. In the most-recent 10-day period, Ford's U.S. car sales dropped 20.7%, while truck sales declined 9.3%.

Company officials have said the introduction of the 1991 Escort subcompact car later this month will help sales; Escort has been one of Ford's best-selling cars.

Despite the lower truck sales, a Ford spokesman said the company is pleased with customer reaction to its Explorer sport utility vehicle. Ford has sold almost 15,000 of the Bronco II replacements since they made their debut in mid-March, the spokesman said.

Sales at Chrysler Corp. continued to plummet. The auto maker's domestic car sales declined 29.2%, while sales of light trucks, which include Chrysler's minivans, dropped 24.9%.

Meanwhile, five of the Japanese auto makers that build cars in the U.S. reaped gains from plant capacity brought on line since last year. Toyota Motor Corp. nearly doubled its sales of domestically produced cars, while Mitsubishi Motors Corp. nearly tripled its sales.

Honda Motor Corp. registered a 17% gain. Fuji Heavy Industries, which markets Subaru vehicles, and Isuzu Motors Ltd. only recently began reporting sales of the cars and trucks produced at their joint venture plant in Lafayette, Ind.

The total for sales of all vehicles reported for early April, including imported vehicles sold by the U.S. Big Three, was 270,386, down 11.9% from a year ago.
