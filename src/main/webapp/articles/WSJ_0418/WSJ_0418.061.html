Inside Track: Tsai Sells 85% Of His Holding In Primerica Inc.

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 04/18/90

NEW YORK -- Gerald Tsai Jr., one of the first of the breed of "star" money managers, has sold 85% of his stake in Primerica Inc., parent of Smith Barney, Harris Upham Inc., filings with the Securities and Exchange Commission show. All together, he sold one million shares for $26.4 million in several sales on March 6 and 7, the filings show.

Mr. Tsai, 61-year-old chairman of Primerica's executive committee, rose to fame in the 1960s. He was one of the decades's new breed of "go-go" fund managers who specialized in picking speculative glamour stocks and trading in and out of them at breakneck speed.

But although Mr. Tsai built his reputation on stock market timing, he didn't have the knack at this recent sale. The stock has risen sharply from the average share price he received of $26.43, closing yesterday at $31, up 75 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

A portion of the stock's rise, $1.50 of it to be exact, has come since Monday, when Primerica announced its first-quarter results. Net rose to 73 cents a share from 53 cents a year earlier. Its Smith Barney unit earned $13.1 million, up about 15%. Smith Barney's results reflected the acquisition of 16 former offices of Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc.

Mr. Tsai was unavailable for comment, and Sanford Weill, the company's chairman, declined to comment.

Gloria Vogel, analyst at Bear, Stearns Cos., said "the earnings were very good, and the company has long-term merits," chief among them Mr. Weill's stewardship and the diverse businesses that Primerica has put together. Still, Ms. Vogel said, Primerica isn't on her "buy list." She might recommend it, she said, "if it got cheaper."

Mr. Tsai started his career as a mutual-fund manager at Fidelity Investments, moved on to launch his own Manhattan Fund and later became chairman of American Can, now Primerica. In December 1988, he stepped down as chairman in favor of Mr. Weill.

THE POWER OF SUGGESTION: When Chrysler Corp. Chairman Lee A. Iacocca talks, some people listen.

Mr. Iacocca has recently been encouraging the No. 3 U.S. auto maker's 100 top executives to own stock equal to a certain percentage of their salaries. Now filings with the SEC show that three executives and two directors bought Chrysler stock since Feb. 16.

While their purchases weren't large, each of these corporate insiders at least doubled his existing holdings, the SEC filings show. All together, the five bought 21,600 shares, more than Chrysler insiders bought on the open market in the past five years combined, according to the filings.

The largest purchase was by Robert A. Lutz, president of the Chrysler Motors division, who bought 10,000 shares, bringing his total holdings to 17,128 shares, according to the filings. Joseph Cappy, vice president of brand development, bought 4,900 shares, almost tripling his holdings. Mr. Lutz and Mr. Cappy declined to comment.

The other purchases, made by two directors and the vice president of vehicle development, ranged from 600 shares to 4,600 shares. Prices paid by the five insiders averaged $17.48 a share, slightly above the stock's close yesterday on the Big Board of $16.875, up 12.5 cents.

Thomas Houston, a spokesman for Chrysler, said additional purchases may have been made by Chrysler employees that the company isn't required to report to the SEC, based on that agency's definition of who is an "insider."

Jay Leopold, auto industry analyst at Legg Mason, Wood Walker in Baltimore, said he still has a "hold" on the stock, but has recently raised his earnings-per-share estimates for 1990 because a Chrysler cost-cutting program is running ahead of schedule.

FORTUNATE MEN: Three executives at King World Productions Inc. sold the majority of their company stock in March, reports filed with the SEC show. The Short Hills, N.J., company is the syndicator behind such television game shows as "Jeopardy and "Wheel of Fortune."

Sidney Cohen, president of the domestic TV sales division, sold all of his 40,778 shares for about $1.6 million, according to the SEC filings. Leonard Spilka, treasurer, sold 8,424 shares, 97% of his holdings, for $362,232, the filings show. Stephen Palley, general counsel, sold 20,000 shares, or 59% of his stake, for $800,390.

Mr. Cohen and Mr. Palley couldn't be reached for comment. Mr. Spilka said he had held his shares since January 1986, that they had appreciated substantially over the period, and that he sold them to pay for improvements on his home. He retains 300 shares, but said he intends to purchase more by exercising options.

The three sold at share prices ranging from $39.81 to $43, according to SEC files. The stock closed yesterday at $41.25, down 12.5 cents, in Big Board composite trading.
