NASD Proposes Limit on Fees Set by Funds

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 04/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The National Association of Securities Dealers, in a long-awaited move, proposed a rule to tighten the limits on the fees charged by mutual funds. The rule would set those stingier caps by classifying as sales charges the socalled 12b-1 fees, which allow mutual funds to pass on to customers sales-related expenses such as commissions to brokerage firms that maintain a customer account.

"It seems to us that to have a sales charge rule that applies only to one kind of sales charge doesn't make much sense," said A. John Taylor, vice president, investment companies, at the NASD. The NASD has the authority under congressional mandate to prohibit excessive sales charges on mutual funds sold through its members.

Most mutual funds are sold through firms that are members of the NASD, although some funds are marketed directly to investors. Mr. Taylor estimated that there are about 1,650 mutual funds that charge 12b-1 fees, although he couldn't estimate how many of the funds would be bumped into the excessive category.

The proposal, if passed, wouldn't drastically change the way most mutual funds charge investors, said Carl Frischling, a New York lawyer specializing in mutual funds. The rule "would leave most 12b-1 plans intact," he said. "It would only affect the excessive ones." Most of those would be equity funds, he added.

Some mutual funds, especially those that charge a fee when an investor cashes out, levy 12b-1 fees as high as 1.25% a year. That can eat into an investor's gains under current rules because there isn't a cap.

Under the proposal, most mutual funds would have a current maximum sales charge of 8.5%. But that cap would include the 12b-1 fees. For example, if a fund had a 6% front-end sales charge, or load, and annual sales expense of 0.5%, the fund could assess an investor for that expense amount for five years, until the total reached 8.5%. It would then no longer be able to charge an investor for 12b-1 expenses.

Mr. Frischling said he thought the proposal was "very positive for the industry" because it would cap sales charges at 8.5%, a rate higher than the level at which most funds are currently introduced. Most funds unveiled during the past few years charge fees of 4.5% or less, giving them plenty of leeway before they would have to stop assessing a typical annual 12b-1 charge of 0.25%.

But the new set-up could create accounting headaches for the funds, as they try to determine how much of a fee they can still charge each investor as years go by. Mr. Frischling suggested that could raise the funds' accounting expenses.

The NASD decided to rethink its salescharge rules in 1988, after the Securities and Exchange Commission suggested its own set of 12b-1 rules.

The SEC agreed to hold off on its rules until the NASD completed its review. The NASD proposal will be open for memberfirm comment until May 31, after which it will reviewed by the NASD board. Then it would be sent to the member firms for a vote. If they approve the proposal, it will then be sent to the SEC and for public comment.

Mr. Taylor said he doesn't think the proposal could be approved before the end of the year. "This is a very major change involving the mutual-fund industry and the NASD," he said. "These kind of things aren't settled overnight."

Corrections & Amplifications

MUTUAL FUNDS with sales-related charges called 12b-1 fees generally couldn't charge investors more than 7.25% of sales, under a proposal by the National Association of Securities Dealers. However, investors who hold their shares for a long time may be forced to pay more than the 7.25% level. Yesterday's edition misstated the proposed fee structure.
