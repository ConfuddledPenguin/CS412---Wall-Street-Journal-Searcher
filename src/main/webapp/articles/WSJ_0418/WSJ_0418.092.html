Coca-Cola Enterprises Net Fell 40% During the First Quarter to $8 Million

  • Author: Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 04/18/90

ATLANTA -- Coca-Cola Enterprises Inc. said first-quarter net income fell 40% to $8 million, or three cents a share, from the year-earlier quarter when a big extraordinary gain and other items skewed the bottling giant's results. The results matched estimates of many Wall Street analysts who see slow but steady improvement at the company. Coca-Cola Enterprises, which is 49%-owned by Coca-Cola Co., has had a dismal earnings record in the 3 1/2 years since it was created.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Coca-Cola Enterprises climbed 25 cents to $15.625.

Operating profit climbed 19% to $65 million as operating cash flow grew 11% to $123.6 million. The latest quarter's results included a gain of $3.7 million on the sale of the company's food service vending operations in Memphis, Tenn., while the year-ago period included a gain of $11 million from the sale of Canadian bottling operations. In the 1989 period, the company had net of $13.5 million, or seven cents a share.

Volume growth, measured in case sales, was particularly strong at 5%. Roy Burry, a senior vice president and beverage analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co., said the figure is probably twice the total market growth rate in the quarter. A Coca-Cola spokesman attributed the increase to strong promotions and a centralization of marketing functions at company headquarters here.

That growth, coupled with a moderate pricing increase of 2%, is a good sign that the bottler, which accounts for about 44% of U.S. sales of Coke products in bottles and cans, had both engines running in the first quarter, beverage analysts said.

"The ability to maintain volume increases and pricing increases at the same time is key," said George Thompson, an analyst with Prudential-Bache Research. "They are certainly on the right track. People will want to see the second and third quarters," which are historically the biggest for the soft-drink industry.

The company spokesman noted that "the seven cents a share last year was entirely from things other than operations, whereas this year at least two cents was from operations."

Mr. Burry at Kidder Peabody, who had expected per-share profit of three cents, without the one-cent gain from the sale of operations, believes the company can still achieve his earlier estimate of per-share profit of 45 cents in the full year.

The company said the 2% unit pricing increase was nearly enough to offset the 2.5% increase in related cost of sales for each unit -- a continuing sanguine sign for an industry that was pummeled by price wars in the 1980s.

"Things are gradually getting better," said Emanuel Goldman, a beverage analyst with PaineWebber Inc. in San Francisco. "It's just a slow process."

Revenue grew 5% to $906.4 million from $859.6 million. The company capped the increase in selling, administrative and general expenses at 2.2%.

Several analysts remarked that the latest quarter's tax rate of 51% was surprisingly high, a result largely of the tax attributes of selling the vending operations.
