Economy: Consumer Prices Climbed 0.5% in March --- First-Quarter Inflation Rate Jumped to 8.5%, Highest Quarterly Level Since '82

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 04/18/90

WASHINGTON -- Consumer prices jumped 0.5% last month, the same as in February, pushing inflation for the first quarter to an 8.5% annual rate, the Labor Department reported. Although that's the highest quarterly rate since the spring of 1982, when the country was emerging from prolonged double-digit inflation, few economists expect the pace to continue. Much of the steep run-up early this year -- consumer prices rose 1.1% in January -- stemmed from the bitterly cold December weather that ruined crops and taxed fuel supplies, leading to higher prices for both food and energy.

The inflation rate for the 12 months ended in March actually slipped a hair, to 5.2% from 5.3% for the 12 months ended in February.

Nevertheless, many analysts said the latest numbers suggest inflation will continue to be a problem for some time.

"When you run down the various categories, the fact of the matter is that in almost all of them, inflation is getting worse, not better," said Norman Robertson, chief economist of Mellon Bank in Pittsburgh. "That's disturbing in the overall context of slow economic growth."

Other government reports issued yesterday suggest that the persistence of inflation is occurring despite continued sluggishness in the economy.

The Federal Reserve reported that industrial production shot up 0.7% in March, but it said a weather-related increase in utility output and a surge in car manufacturing, following a campaign to trim inventories, accounted for much of the gain. "Elsewhere production was little changed, on balance, in March," the report said.

Reflecting the higher output levels, American factories, mines and utilities used 83.3% of their production capacity in March, up from 82.9% in February, the Fed said.

Analysts said utility output was actually no higher than normal for March, but because February was unseasonably warm, a return to regular energy usage with March temperatures registered as a large increase.

Also, economists said that while the pickup in auto production is good news for the industry, it indicates only a return to more normal levels of output after a severe campaign to reduce inventories earlier this winter. Many expect weak consumer demand for autos to continue leading to soft production.

"We know that auto sales have been treading water at best," said Alan Gayle, economist at Crestar Financial Corp. in Richmond, Va.

Also during March, housing construction slowed sharply, according to the Commerce Department. Work began on new homes and apartments at an annual rate of 1,321,000 during the month, a 9.3% drop from February, the department reported.

But again, the weather was partly to blame. Many builders started projects earlier than they had planned this winter because temperatures were unseasonably warm in January and February. As a result, March housing starts looked weak by comparison.

Permits for future construction were issued at an annual rate of 1,206,000 units, down 7.6% from February.

Mr. Gayle said the building industry should benefit from the good weather in the long run.

But for now, he said, the reports suggest "the economy is still limping along."

The economy has been sluggish for the past year, the result of the Fed's efforts to stave off inflation by pushing up interest rates. Aided in part by the unseasonably warm weather at the beginning of the year, the economy has shown signs of picking up a bit in the first quarter.

But Katsuyoshi Tamura, chief economist at the Bank of Tokyo New York Group, said after the latest round of economic reports yesterday: "I think the economy is in so-called stagflation -- slow economy, persistent inflation."

Mr. Robertson added: "I think it's now painfully obvious that a period of subpar economic growth isn't going to bring about a reduction in the inflation rate. Inflation looks very, very stubborn, very entrenched."

The latest inflation report was particularly disappointing because the government had reported only a few days ago that prices at the wholesale level declined in March. The drop stemmed largely from declines in energy and food prices.

But at the retail level, food prices rose 0.4% in March, on top of a 0.5% climb in February and a 1.8% surge in January. Energy prices, however, did fall for the second consecutive month. They declined 0.8% in March after dropping 0.7% in February and leaping 5.1% in January.

Clothing prices were another big factor in the latest rise in inflation. They jumped 1.6% after rocketing 3.3% in February. Most economists believe those increases are far too big to be sustained, and predict that clothing prices are sure to fall in the coming months.

Medical-care prices continued their relentless ascent. They grew 0.8% in March, matching February's rise. Housing prices increased 0.5% after rising 0.2% in February. Entertainment prices rose 0.4% for the second month in a row.

In another report, the Labor Department said the weekly earnings of American workers grew 0.1% in March after adjusting for inflation. In February, they increased 0.3%.

Figures in all the reports are adjusted for seasonal variations.
