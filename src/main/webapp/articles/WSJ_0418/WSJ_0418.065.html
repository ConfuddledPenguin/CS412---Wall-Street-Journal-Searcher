Law: Windshield Wiper Trial Begins Its Penalty Phase

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/18/90

DETROIT -- Maryland inventor Robert W. Kearns, who in January convinced a federal court jury here that Ford Motor Co. violated his patents for an electronic, intermittent windshield wiper system, now wants the same jury to shake Detroit's tradition of buying ideas on the cheap. In arguments that kicked off the penalty phase of the Kearns trial, Ford lawyer Malcolm Wheeler said a "reasonable" royalty for Mr. Kearns's wiper system should be no more than five cents or 10 cents a car, with a maximum of $1.5 million -- in line with standard auto industry practice. Ford paid no more to license such critical inventions as the automatic choke, Mr. Wheeler said.

But Paul Janicke, lead trial counsel for Mr. Kearns, told the jury not to follow normal auto industry practice in deciding his client's award. Auto companies "do things to inventors, not for inventors," he said. Instead, Mr. Janicke said Ford should pay Mr. Kearns a percentage of the revenue it received from sales of the wiper systems.

Mr. Janicke offered two methods for calculating a royalty rate. One came to 25%, the other 42%. Spread over the 20.5 million wiper systems Ford sold from 1971 to 1988, the 25% royalty would yield about $193 million, and the 42% royalty would produce $325 million.

The trial is expected to last two weeks.
