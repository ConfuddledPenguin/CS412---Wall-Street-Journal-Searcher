Wider Margins Help Three Big Thrifts Report Robust Earnings for 1st Quarter

  • Author: G. Christian Hill
  • Date: 04/18/90

Three California thrift giants reported robust first-quarter earnings driven by a healthy state housing market and widening net interest margins, at a time when the margins of most big commercial banks are declining. The margins of H.F. Ahmanson & Co., Los Angeles; Great Western Financial Corp., Beverly Hills; and Golden West Financial Corp., Oakland, rose partly because of an improved spread between deposit costs and the adjustable-rate mortgage index used in California. The big thrifts clearly benefited from less deposit and loan competition from desperate savings and loan associations shut down by regulators since passage of the thrift-rescue legislation in August 1989. This trend is helping boost earnings of many of the approximately 1,500 thrifts that are well-capitalized and profitable, an often-overlooked group.

Great Western, for instance, reported that its net interest margin -- the difference between its cost of funds and its yield on assets -- jumped to 3.01% from 2.43% a year ago, contributing to a $55.8 million rise in net interest income. Golden West's margin rose to 2.51% from 2.32%, and Ahmanson's rose to 2.68% from 2.41%.

In contrast, Wells Fargo & Co., a California competitor and one of the best-run commercial banks in the U.S., yesterday reported that its net interest margin fell to 5.09% from 5.23%, a typical performance for commercial banks, mainly because deposit costs a year ago lagged behind increases in the commercial banks' prime rate.

The much-feared collapse in the California real-estate market clearly has failed to materialize. Ahmanson's mortgage originations jumped 12% to a first-quarter record of $3.4 billion, up from $3 billion in the 1989 first quarter. Great Western generated $2.9 billion in new loans, down 19% from a year ago but still robust. Golden West reported the biggest decline in new loans, a 40% drop to $845 million.

Each of the big thrifts reported sharp gains in deposits, with Ahmanson showing a 12% gain from a year ago, Great Western 9%, and Golden West 14%. Golden West's deposit growth in the period was twice the rate recorded in the year-ago period.

"Our savings volume has benefited from the decreased competition for funds from troubled thrifts no longer allowed to grow by the regulators," said Herbert M. Sandler, Golden West's chairman and chief executive officer.

Golden West's provision for loan losses remained minuscule at $3 million, compared with $3.3 million a year ago. Great Western's $20 million provision was slightly below the year-ago level. But Ahmanson's provision doubled in size to $25.2 million, reflecting a $41 million increase in non-performing assets since the end of 1989.

Analysts expect the three thrifts to report full-year earnings about 25% to 35% above their 1989 net income, excluding asset write-downs. But their stocks continue to trade at six to eight times expected 1990 earnings, compared with an average multiple of nearly 15 for most stocks.

"It's an outrage," said Mr. Sandler. "We've recorded a 20% yearly compounded growth rate in earnings over the last 20 years. No other bank or S&L comes close to that."

"The stock prices are clearly discounting {expecting} an earnings decline this year," said Joseph Jolson, a thrift analyst for Montgomery Securities, San Francisco. "Investors are focused on the California real-estate market and expect home prices to decline. Sooner or later they'll wise up to the fact that there has been a soft landing for California home prices, and that there hasn't been the debacle everyone has been talking about."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Ahmanson closed at $17.125, unchanged. Great Western rose 12.5 cents to $16.50 while Golden West closed at $27, up 50 cents.
