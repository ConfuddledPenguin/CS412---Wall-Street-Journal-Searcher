Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Warner Bros. Plans an Offensive Against Ads in Movie Theaters

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 04/18/90

Warner Bros. said it will prohibit movie theaters from running commercials when they are showing Warner Bros. releases. The ban makes Warner the second major studio to go on the offensive against commercials in theaters, a growing but controversial form of advertising. In February, Walt Disney Co. introduced a similar ban, citing moviegoers' resentment of "commercial intrusions" in theaters.

Warner, a unit of Time Warner, said the ban will take effect with its film, "Gremlins 2: The New Batch," which is to open May 18 on 2,000 screens nationwide.

"We feel it's an imposition on the film patron," said Barry Reardon, president of domestic distribution at Warner. "In justice to our creative people and the people who make our movies, the movies should be shown" without ads.

Warner last weekend had its movies -- "Driving Miss Daisy," "Joe Versus the Volcano" and "Impulse," among others -- on about 2,500 screens.

Mr. Reardon said Warner will continue to run some public service advertisements before its films, but "once the lights go down, we don't want any commercial advertising."

Executives at firms that sell and distribute commercials for movie theaters reacted angrily. "I'm quite dismayed that Warner would follow this ill-conceived policy of Disney's," said Terry Laughren, chairman of Screenvision Cinema Network, which is based in New York.

"I don't understand why they're taking an action that will adversely affect" theater owners, Mr. Laughren said. He added that Screenvision, which distributes ads for Kodak and General Motors, among others, sends out checks totaling "millions of dollars" to theater owners every year for showing its ads.

About half of the nation's 23,000 first-run theaters show advertising.

Mr. Laughren also discounted a study released by Disney last week that showed that the vast majority of surveyed moviegoers don't want commercials to be shown in theaters. Mr. Laughren disputed that, citing his company's research that shows almost all moviegoers approving of Screenvision's ads.

With two of Hollywood's major studios now banning theater commercials, some observers believe a trend may be in the works. "The audience feedback is there -- audiences are booing and hissing," says A.D. Murphy, who track box-office results for Variety.

Officials at other major film studios, including MCA's Universal and Columbia Pictures, declined to comment.

Unlike Disney, most of the major studios also have interests in movie theaters. Warner is a major theater owner through a joint venture with Paramount, and MCA is the biggest shareholder in Cineplex Odeon, one of the largest movie chains, which shows commercials on almost all its 1,300 U.S. screens.

The largest U.S. theater owner, United Artists Entertainment Co., shows some onscreen commercials for Coca-Cola Co., but has increasingly been advertising on video monitors in the lobbies of its theaters. Of the Warner restrictions, a spokesman says, "to the extent that it's required, we'll comply."

Some movie theater chains already prohibit commercials, including General Cinema, Loews and National Amusements, a unit of Viacom. But those theaters are the exception. Other theater owners have been increasingly willing to accept ads, following the lead of European theaters, where commercials have been shown without controversy for years.

Saatchi Considers Shift

Saatchi & Saatchi is considering a management shakeup that would streamline the holding company's staff and move three executive directors to operating posts, industry executives said.

Within the next few months, Saatchi is likely to shift Jeremy Sinclair, Terry Bannister and Roy Warman into operating roles within the company's communications business, an individual familiar with the company said. Mr. Sinclair currently is Saatchi's deputy chairman, while Messrs. Bannister and Warman are co-chief executives of Saatchi's ad business.

The anticipated changes stem from Saatchi's effort to sell its troubled consulting businesses, and from the company's appointment last year of Robert LouisDreyfus as chief executive and Charles Scott as finance director. As a result, the need for other senior executives to work on corporate matters has diminished. Saatchi officials declined to comment.
