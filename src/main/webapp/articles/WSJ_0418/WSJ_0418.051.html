Medicare Payments to Rise 9.9% Under HHS Plan

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The Department of Health and Human Services recommended a 9.9% increase in payments to doctors under the Medicare program in fiscal 1991. Pointing out that Medicare physician payments have risen 12% annually in recent years, HHS Secretary Louis Sullivan called a target increase of 9.9% in the fiscal year starting Oct. 1, "a realistic goal for physicians, one consistent with both the prudent practice of medicine and the necessary restraint on cost increases."

Last year, Congress passed a law that will overhaul how doctors are paid under Medicare and, health policy makers hope, slow the rise in payments. The recommended 9.9% increase, called a "volume performance standard," is the administration's calculation of how much the payments to physicians under Part B of Medicare should rise after taking account of price increases, the growing number of Medicare beneficiaries, traditional service volume per beneficiary, and new technology.

The recommendation, which must be considered by a commission and by Congress, wouldn't necessarily hold the rise in payments to doctors to 9.9% next fiscal year. The restraint could work with a lag, either by slowing the rise in fees doctors charge or by reducing the volume of service they provide.

In fiscal 1991, the department estimates that 33.4 million beneficiaries will receive $44.75 billion in Medicare Part B payments.
