Members Approve Dual Trading Plan At the Chicago Merc

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/18/90

CHICAGO -- The Chicago Mercantile Exchange said its members approved a one-year-old proposal to broaden the number of contracts in which brokers can't trade for their own and customer accounts. The proposal to crack down on the practice known as dual trading was proposed in April 1989 by an exchange panel after an undercover investigation of futures trading at the Chicago Merc and the Chicago Board of Trade surfaced in January 1989.

The practice has been widely scorned for making abuse harder to detect. The Commodity Futures Trading Commission wants the exchanges to experiment with prohibiting dual trading in a few pits entirely.

The Chicago Merc, which opposes that measure, said yesterday that it will ask the regulatory agency to instead approve its member-adopted proposal.

The way the Chicago Merc proposal differs is that more pits would be involved, but not all the contracts in those pits.

In each pit, traders swap contracts that expire at different months throughout the year. In the hog pit, for example, members are currently trading contracts that expire in eight different months.

Under the Chicago Merc proposal, dual trading would be banned in contract months in which at least 10,000 contracts on average are traded daily over a six-month period.

Based on that volume, the dual trading prohibition would probably apply to the contracts that are nearing expiration in the pits that trade futures contracts for financial and currency instruments such as the yen and the Swiss franc.

But the rule probably would mean that dual trading could continue in some lower-volume futures contracts for commodities such as livestock.

The Chicago Merc said 76% of its members who voted approved the proposal, which was also approved by its Board of Governors in March.

In 1987, the Chicago Merc banned dual trading for its most-active stock-index futures brokers.
