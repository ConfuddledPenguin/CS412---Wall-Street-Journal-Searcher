Credit Markets: U.S. Government Bond Yields Surge as Reports On Economy Fan Investor Fears of Inflation

  • Author: Tom Herman and Kevin Donovan
  • Date: 04/18/90

NEW YORK -- Interest rates jumped on news of an unexpected burst of inflation last month. U.S. government bond yields surged to their highest levels in nearly a year. Long-term Treasury bonds now yield about 8 3/4%, up from 8% at the start of the year. Short-term interest rates also rose, and several analysts fear further increases soon.

"I think we're headed toward 9% within the next couple of months," said Roger A. Froehlich, president of Technical Dimensions Inc., a Mt. Kisco, N.Y.-based bond market forecasting company. "There's a bias in the market to shrug off any favorable news and accentuate all the unfavorable news. Witness what happened today."

Traders were especially startled by a government report yesterday showing a 0.5% surge in March consumer prices. That was far above the 0.2% increase that most economists had expected. Investors also were surprised by a 0.7% increase in industrial production. That was more than three times as big an increase as had been generally expected, thus fanning inflation fears even further. Housing starts fell sharply, but traders ignored it.

These reports greatly strengthen the case for the Federal Reserve to begin driving rates slightly higher, especially if further signs of economic strength emerge, investment managers said. Some Fed officials already are leaning toward tighter credit to fight inflation. Others fear the economy isn't strong enough to withstand significantly higher rates and thus they have argued for leaving rates where they are until more economic news is available. But yesterday's reports, at the very least, dashed what few hopes remained that the Fed might ease credit soon.

Reaction was swift. Bond prices, which move in the opposite direction from interest rates, began tumbling as soon as headlines of the inflation and economic news appeared on news wires early in the morning. Treasury bonds took the biggest hit, falling about a point, or $10 for each $1,000 face amount.

"This could be a summer of discontent in the bond market," said George R. Mateyo, president and chief executive officer of Carnegie Capital Management Co., Cleveland.

Many New Yorkers are so caught up in Wall Street's turmoil and massive layoffs that they are missing the U.S. economy's overall resilience, Mr. Mateyo said. "I don't know where the economy is weak," he said. "You go to Boeing in Seattle, or a grain company in Minneapolis, and they say they're going great guns."

Thus, he said, this isn't a good time to rush out and buy long-term bonds. "We've got a lot of cash," he said, "and are in a defensive posture."

Once again, the bond market's sinking spell caught many bond investors off guard. Just last week, a survey of 52 fixed-income managers by C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc. showed more than half were bullish on bonds.

That was the highest bullish reading since the beginning of this year-providing further evidence of how valuable such surveys are as contrarian indicators of where bond prices are heading.

But inflation fears often have proven to be greatly exaggerated in recent years, and some people think yesterday's kneejerk reaction was just another overreaction.

"I think it's a great time to buy bonds," said William H. Gross, managing director at Pacific Investment Management Co., Newport Beach, Calif. "I think the bad news is over. It's uphill from here on in for bond prices." He predicted that the inflation news "is bound to get better," while the economy "is bound to be slower. So we're basically at the end of the road for this minibear market that we've experienced" for the past few months.

"We think the pickup in inflation in the first quarter is temporary," said Nancy R. Lazar, a senior vice president at C.J. Lawrence. "There is plenty of anecdotal evidence" that companies are encountering great difficulties raising prices, she said. She added: "By year end inflation should be back down to about 3%."

Ms. Lazar and others argued that the economy isn't nearly as strong as the latest figures might indicate. Some also point to the sinking price of gold, which often is viewed as a barometer of inflation pressures.

The March consumer price increase "overstates the inflation threat," said Philip Braverman, a senior vice president at DKB Securities Corp. Nevertheless, he expects higher interest rates soon.

Besides inflation fears, investors also are worried about a big wave of Treasury borrowing coming next month. They are especially concerned because of speculation that Japanese investors, hurt by Japan's stock-market plunge, won't buy large amounts of new Treasury debt. Thus, Mr. Braverman predicted in a report yesterday that Treasury bond yields "seem likely to reach or slightly exceed 9% before yields top out and a sustainable rally begins."

Treasury Securities

Unexpectedly bad news on inflation drove Treasury bond yields to their highest levels since May of last year.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bonds were quoted yesterday afternoon at a yield of 8.72%, up from 8.63% Monday. The price fell one point to 97 18/32.

The previous high closing yield this year for a bellwether 30-year Treasury bond was 8.71%, reached March 13.

The government's 10-year notes yielded 8.73% yesterday afternoon, up from 8.66% Monday. Their price fell to 98 3/8 from 98 27/32 Monday.

Rates rose slightly on short-term Treasury bills. For example, the latest three-month T-bills were quoted late yesterday at a discount rate of 7.76% to yield 8%. That compared with an average discount rate of 7.71% set at Monday's auction, which translated into a bond-equivalent yield of 7.97%.

Corporate, Junk Issues

The Treasury bond market's woes spilled over into the corporate market, driving both junk bond and investment-grade issues lower.

Overall, high-yield, high-risk bonds -- securities with low credit ratings or no ratings at all -- fell by about 1/4 point. But the notable exceptions to the general market decline were junk bonds of Resorts International Inc. They climbed on news that the Atlantic City casino company has filed a new reorganization plan that is likely to get bond-holder approval.

Among the high-flyers were Resorts International's 11 3/8% subordinated sinking fund debentures, due in 2013, which closed up three points at 20 on light volume on the New York Stock Exchange. The company's 10% subordinated sinking fund debentures, due in 1998, also advanced, gaining 1 1/4 to end at 18, again on light trading.

Elsewhere in the junk bond market, Kroger Co. bonds fell 1/2 point after the company reported that its losses narrowed in the first quarter. Analysts said they had expected more improvement.

In the investment-grade corporate debt market, long-term corporate issues fell between 3/4 point and a point.

Foreign Bonds

For a change, Japanese government bond prices rose slightly even though the yen continued to slump against the dollar.

Tokyo traders said the bond market there was strengthened by speculation that Japan's Finance Ministry will buy Japanese government bonds to help strengthen the yen. Traders also pointed to a slowdown in the growth of Japan's money supply.

Japan's benchmark bonds due 1999 rose to 85.83 as of the close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange Tuesday, up from 85.79 Monday. The yield fell to 7.39% from 7.405%.

In other major markets, Canadian bond prices tumbled to their lowest levels of the year.

Canada's bellwether 20-year bond dropped 1 1/4 points to 85.75 to yield 11.27%. That is about 1.80 percentage points above its level at the beginning of the year.

Analysts said inflation fears as well as the high levels of Canadian short-term interest rates were contributing to bearish sentiment. Canadian 91-day Treasury bills yield about 13 3/8%, more than two percentage points higher than 20-year bonds. This is known in bond market jargon as an "inverted yield curve."

"There is no reason to expect the {Canadian} bond market to improve any time soon," said Ted Carmichael, senior economist at Burns Fry Ltd. He said Canadian March consumer price data, to be reported Friday, is likely to send another bearish signal to the market. He said Burns Fry is expecting a rise in the consumer price index of about 0.5% compared with a 0.6% rise in February.

Municipals

Prospective buyers of municipal securities found the pickings slim as for the most part investors held onto bonds, shrugging off the huge downward moves of the U.S. Treasury market.

Issues were flat to 3/8 point lower in thin dealings.

In one of the largest deals of the day, $274.9 million of Santa Clara County (Calif.) Traffic Authority sales tax revenue bonds were tentatively priced by a Morgan Stanley Inc. group to yield from 6.20% in 1992 to 6.50% in 1995.

Among seasoned dollar bonds, New Jersey Turnpike Authority 7.20% issue of 2018 was off 1/8 at 97 3/8 to 97 1/2 to yield 7.41%.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities ended 5/32 to 10/32 lower but fared better than did the Treasury market.

Traders said there was moderate buying of mortgage issues from underwriters seeking collateral for new issues of derivative mortgage securities, and purchases by institutional investors seeking the 10% yields on some pass-through issues.

Government National Mortgage Association 9% securities for May delivery finished at 95 3/8, down 12/32, after changing hands between 95 3/32 and 95 26/32.

In the asset-backed market, Chrysler Financial Corp. sold $609.28 million of securities backed by automobile loans at a yield of 9.501%, .80 percentage point above the Treasury two-year note, through First Boston Corp.

The issue was made through Asset-Backed Securities Corp. 7, which purchased the auto loans from Chrysler Financial and repackaged them as securities for sale to investors.
