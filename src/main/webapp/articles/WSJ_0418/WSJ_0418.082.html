SEC Charges Donaldson Lufkin Illegally Used Customers' Stock

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 04/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission charged Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. with illegally using customers' stock that was left in the care of the firm's back-office operations. The firm, one of Wall Street's most respected midsized houses, agreed to an SEC censure and will pay out $5.6 million, plus interest. "We think this is the largest disgorgement of any back-office case," said Kathryn Ashbaugh, an SEC branch chief in New York.

The agency said that Donaldson booked $1.9 million as profit from the transactions and has about $4 million in reserve for the disgorgement. The firm had already paid out $300,000 before the settlement.

Donaldson's Pershing division, its back-office operation, is the nation's largest "clearing" firm, handling execution and settlement for more than 500 other brokerage firms and a million customers.

Regulatory and Wall Street sources said the problems at Pershing aren't widespread. However, Charles Schwab & Co. two years ago repaid $3 million to investors after company officials discovered a similar situation.

Donaldson said it already has begun to dispense the funds to more than 5,000 customers of about 100 other Wall Street and regional firms that used Pershing's back-office operations. More than 90% of the recipients will be retail investors, said Joseph Donnelly, general counsel at Pershing. Donaldson is owned by Equitable Life Assurance Society of the U.S.

In addition, the head of Pershing's equities division, Michael Dreitlein, agreed to be suspended for 45 days on charges he "willfully aided and abetted" the firm in the alleged scheme, the SEC said. He settled the charges without admitting or denying guilt. Donaldson said he would return to the firm after the suspension to resume most of his supervisory duties.

The SEC said that between 1982 and 1988, Pershing tendered stock without authorization by customers in partial tender offers. In those offers, companies seek to buy a percentage of their shares, but not all the stock. Many such deals took place in the mid-1980s, when such companies as Unocal Corp., trying to fend off corporate raiders such as T. Boone Pickens, set up partial tender offers as defensive measures.

Pershing tendered the unauthorized shares, then waited for the stock price to drop, as it usually does after a partial tender is completed, the SEC said. Then the firm bought back the shares and replaced them in the customers' accounts, the agency said, adding that Pershing generated gains of about $4.3 million through this practice.

"This is a statement that firms cannot use customer securities for the firms' benefit," said Lawrence Iason, who heads the agency's New York regional office, which brought the case.

In other cases, Pershing would conduct similar activities in cases in which convertible securities were being redeemed by the issuing company but in which Pershing hadn't heard from customers, the SEC said. Other similar situations occurred with stock rights or warrants that were expiring and the firm hadn't received word on whether to exercise customers' rights or warrants, the agency said. Pershing gained about $1.5 million from the two practices, the SEC said.

Pershing and Donaldson lawyers said there wasn't any malice in the firm's actions. "This was not a profit motivated activity," Mr. Donnelly said. "It was a customer protection motivated activity."

Mr. Donnelly explained that Pershing ran a mostly manual operation in its reorganization department, which handles the types of transactions cited in the SEC's charges. Instructions on how to handle partial tenders or other transactions from Pershing customers at other brokerage firms "were not always properly directed to the reorganization department" even though they had been received by the firm, he said. "The case could be made that {Pershing} was in receipt of the instructions even though they weren't in a place they could be acted on." Pershing would then owe the customers money.

So, "what evolved was a practice of submitting for tender more shares than you had instructions for," Mr. Donnelly said. Extra funds were placed in a reserve fund, he said, and customers would be paid when they made "valid claims."

Still, the settlement with the SEC shows that Pershing paid out only $300,000 in those "valid claims," and kept the rest of the $5.9 million either in the reserve fund or to pay the firm's expenses.

Mr. Donnelly said "the practices discussed in the order were discontinued even before the SEC inquiry began. We now have the 100% accuracy of knowing where we stand and who's giving instructions."

Theodore Levine, a Washington securities lawyer who represented Donaldson, said the firm offered the distribution of proceeds in negotiations with the SEC. "It's a distribution of funds, not really disgorgement," he said. "The firm has been standing ready for a long time to distribute the funds."
