Technology: NCR Profit Fell 15% in Quarter On Flat Sales --- Price Rivalry, Product Shift To Thin Margins Cited; Surprise Batters Stock

  • Author: John R. Wilke
  • Date: 04/18/90

NCR Corp. surprised investors in posting a 15% decline in net income for the first quarter on flat sales, driving the stock down sharply in heavy trading. The big computer maker, based in Dayton, Ohio, said profit slumped to $52 million, or 73 cents a share, from the year-earlier $61 million, or 77 cents a share, on a much higher base of shares outstanding.

Revenue edged up just 1.2% to $1.27 billion from $1.25 billion.

Analysts attributed the poor results to stiff price competition and a broad shift toward smaller computers, with razor-thin profit margins. This shift "is a consistent theme across the industry," Patricia Laupheimer, an analyst at Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc., said. She noted that even International Business Machines Corp., which reported better-than-expected results Monday, has seen its margins shrink in the shift toward smaller, more powerful computers.

The NCR earnings news ignited strong selling of the company's shares, which had been one of the best-performing computer stocks during the first quarter. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, NCR shares plunged $5.375, to close at $65.375, slashing the company's market value by 7.6%. With volume of more than 2.1 million shares, NCR was the most active issue on the Big Board.

"People were looking for a lot more than they got. No one was ready for a down quarter," Jay P. Stevens, an analyst at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., said. "It was a real jolt."

Investors ignored the good news: NCR said it had record orders during the quarter. Wall Street focused instead on the falling margins and the company's cautious outlook for the second quarter.

NCR said first-quarter orders "showed a substantial gain" from a year earlier, including higher U.S. orders and sharply higher international orders, led by Pacific Rim nations.

NCR's chairman and chief executive officer, Charles E. Exley, said he was pleased with the strong order performance, but added, "We continue to be concerned about the competitive pressure on gross margins."

"With continuing good order performance, and emphasis on cost control, we still see the opportunity to achieve single-digit revenue and earnings growth for the full year," Mr. Exley said.

Also contributing to the lower earnings for the first quarter was NCR's aggressive stock buy-back program, which had a positive effect on per-share earnings but hurt net income because of lower interest income. NCR said it bought back 1.6 million shares, at an average price of $67.50, in the first quarter. The company had an average of 70.9 million shares outstanding in the latest period, compared with 79.7 million a year earlier.

The poor results prompted many analysts to cut their earnings estimates for the current quarter and the year. Mr. Stevens of Dean Witter reduced his 1990 estimate to $5.75 a share from $6.05, and forecast flat second-quarter net of $1.42 a share. Steve Milunovich of First Boston Corp. cut his second-quarter estimate to $1.35 a share from $1.55.

In the 1989 second quarter, NCR earned $110 million, or $1.42 a share, and had 1989 profit of $412 million, or $5.38 a share.

Some analysts suggested that investors were overreacting. "This is a well-run company with marvelous long-term prospects," Peter Labe of Labe, Simpson & Co. said. He called NCR's latest earnings results "a short-term ripple -- buy into it, and be patient for a few months."
