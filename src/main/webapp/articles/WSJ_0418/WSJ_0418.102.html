Abreast of the Market: Stocks Bounce Back From Selloff To Close Mixed in Light Volume

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 04/18/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks recovered from an early sell-off to finish mixed on the lightest volume in about a week. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 2.71 to 2765.77. Declining issues outnumbered advancers 844 to 662 on the New York Stock Exchange. Volume on the Big Board totaled 127,990,000 shares, the lightest since April 9 when 114.9 million shares traded.

Broader averages finished lower: Standard & Poor's 500-stock index fell 0.06 to 344.68, and the New York Stock Exchange composite index slid 0.03 to 188.91. The American Stock Exchange Market Value index dropped 2.26 to 354.72.

Prices initially tumbled with the bond market on the news that consumer prices increased more sharply than anticipated last month. The price index rose 0.5% overall and 0.7%, excluding food and energy prices, the most volatile components.

But late stock-index arbitrage buy programs helped boost blue-chip issues to higher levels in the final hour. (In stock-index arbitrage, traders buy or sell stock and offset the trade with futures or options to lock in fleeting price differences.) Also, traders said a few institutional accounts began shopping for stocks late in the session.

"There's not enough selling to kill the market, pure and simple," said Dudley A. Eppel, head of trading at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette, who noted that would-be sellers "have had every excuse" to dump stocks recently.

Today's report on the U.S. trade balance for February will be scrutinized for signs of stronger U.S. export growth, especially in capital goods. Many strategists believe the strong exports will help boost stocks whose fortunes are tied to economic growth this year.

NCR plummeted 5 3/8 to 65 3/8 and led the list of most active NYSE issues, as more than 2.1 million shares changed hands in composite trading, after the company reported a 15% decline in first-quarter earnings from a year ago. Its net income of 73 cents a share fell short of both analysts' expectations and the year-earlier level.

Paper and forest-products stocks moved lower after Louisiana-Pacific and Scott Paper reported first-quarter earnings that were down from a year earlier and below market forecasts. Louisiana-Pacific dropped 3/4 to 42 7/8, while Scott Paper slid 1/4 to 44; Boise Cascade slid 3/4 to 38 1/8, Champion International declined 7/8 to 27 7/8, Kimberly-Clark fell 5/8 to 67 3/4 and Weyerhaeuser dropped 3/4 to 25 1/8.

"People don't want to buy them {paper and forest-products shares} because they don't know how far the earnings are going to go down," said George B. Adler, an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., who added that most sectors of the industry are likely to come under continued earnings pressure through next year because of increases in production capacity.

General Electric gained 1/8 to 66 7/8 on 1.5 million shares. The company reported a 12% increase in first-quarter earnings, which was in line with expectations.

Citicorp, which reported that first-quarter earnings fell 56% from a year earlier, rose 1/4 to 24 1/2 as 1.5 million shares were traded. Separately, the company increased its quarterly dividend by 9.9%, to 44.5 cents a share.

United Telecommunications dipped 1/4 to 39 7/8 on 1.6 million shares after posting a 40.8% increase in first-quarter net. Also, the company said it anticipates completing the planned purchase of the 19.9% of US Sprint it doesn't already own from GTE by early July, at a price of about $500 million.

American Express climbed 5/8 to 29 1/4, extending its recent gains. This newspaper's "Heard on the Street" column Tuesday noted that some analysts are hoping the company has positioned itself for a strong performance next year by agreeing to bolster Shearson Lehman Hutton's capital.

L.A. Gear jumped 2 5/8 to 39 5/8. Kidder Peabody repeated its buy recommendation on the stock, citing evidence of strong demand for the company's products; its price target for the next nine to 12 months is $5 a share.

Columbia Gas System rose 2 7/8 to 48 7/8. Renewed speculation that Coastal may seek to acquire the company fueled the rally, traders said. Columbia Gas and Coastal both declined comment on the rumors.

Norton fell 1/2 to 76 as a bill mandating staggered terms for directors, which was drafted in response to the hostile takeover offer and proxy fight for the company by BTR of Britain, made its way through the Massachusetts Legislature. Lawmakers predicted the measure could become law by Thursday.

Pep Boys-Manny, Moe & Jack advanced 1 1/4 to 13 after outlining its operating plan for the 1991-1995 period at a conference in Atlanta for institutional investors. President Michael G. Leibowitz told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report the five-year plan calls for Pep Boys "to grow at a pretty aggressive clip with internally generated funds."

Vista Chemical gained 1 1/4 to 37. Bear Stearns, which had forecast earnings of $1.15 a share for the company's fiscal second quarter ended last month, said it now believes the results will exceed its forecast.

Interlake, which had soared 84% between April 5 and Monday, dropped 1 to 12 5/8. The earlier rise followed a favorable article on the company in the latest issue of Personal Finance, a biweekly market newsletter.

AM International slid 3/8 to 2 1/2. The company said it expects to post a loss for its fiscal third quarter, ending this month, and the year because of a charge to be taken as part of a planned restructuring.

American Stock Exchange volume totaled 13,510,000 shares. There were 238 issues advancing and 329 declining.

Turner Corp. gained 3/8 to 14 7/8. The company said it expects to report first-quarter earnings of 35 cents a share and that it had received a construction contract for a $95 million office building in Boston.
