Economy -- Tracking the Economy: This Week's Calendar of Economic Data Is Likely to Show Soft Output for August

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 09/10/90

NEW YORK -- Economists shave a bit more from already thin forecasts and brace for a big oil-inflation hit this week. Following Friday's weak jobs report, coming data on August industrial production and capacity utilization are expected to at best hover around July levels. And the much-awaited initial impact from the oil crisis hits the August producer price index -- the consensus view calls for a jump of 1.3%, up from July's dip of 0.1%. A soft August retail sales report is also in the cards, although the economic hand won't be dealt until Friday, when all the data are released. The week's only other major indicator is July consumer credit, due out today.

"The whole economy is just hanging on by its toenails," said Lawrence Kudlow, economist at Bear, Stearns & Co., New York. He said the weak employment data suggest real gross national product could drop into the negative column in the third quarter. One negative reading on GNP, or the total value of the nation's goods and services, doesn't make a recession. But it could be an official starting point. Still, Mr. Kudlow is among the group that says the recession may already be under way.

"Real business people have been telling me we're in a recession for six months. And that's good enough for me," said Mr. Kudlow. Still, he isn't a "doom and gloomer" and expects only a short duration for any downturn.

Robert McGee, chief economist at Tokai Bank Ltd., New York, lowered his expectations for August industrial output on the basis of the weak employment data. Mr. McGee now expects a decline of 0.2% in industrial production; previously he thought output would maintain July's flat reading. He also shaved his estimate for the related capacity utilization reading to 83.1% from 83.3%. In July, the nation's factories operated at 83.4% of capacity.

One forecast economists haven't changed much since the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait: sharply higher producer prices. "That's baked into the cake," said Mr. McGee, who expects a jump of one percentage point in wholesale prices.

Both producer and consumer price indexes are expected to soar for a while. The August consumer price index, due out next week, is expected to jump 0.8%, according to MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York. But economists will also be keeping tabs on the inflation gauges minus their volatile energy and food components. Excluding food and energy, August producer prices are expected to increase 0.3% and consumer prices 0.4%, according to MMS.

August retail sales are also expected to be soft, slipping 0.1%, according to the consensus view. In July, retail sales gained 0.1%. Ironically, the energy crisis could help support sales through higher gasoline-station receipts. Excluding autos, August retail sales are expected to increase 0.2%, about the same as in July.

Donald Fine, chief market analyst at Chase Manhattan Bank, New York, said he's ratcheting down expectations for the third quarter largely because of the weak employment data. Mr. Fine had been expecting GNP growth of 1% in the period, but now thinks a range of 0.5% to 1% is more realistic.

Still, he believes the quarter will manage to stay out of the red largely because of the giant service sector, which has stimulated job growth throughout this economic expansion. While service business employment has been slowing, "it will still carry us into the positive category" for the quarter, Mr. Fine said.
