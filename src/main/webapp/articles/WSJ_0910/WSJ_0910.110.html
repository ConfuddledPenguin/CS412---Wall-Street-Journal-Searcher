Credit Markets: Many See Fed Slicing Short Rates --- But Cut Isn't Viewed As Aiding Economy Or the Bond Market

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 09/10/90

NEW YORK -- In a rare show of unanimity, many economists, bond strategists and big investors are predicting that the Federal Reserve will reduce short-term interest rates within four weeks. But practically none of them expect the lower rates to help the economy or the bond market.

It is rare when large numbers of market pros agree on anything. Right now, there is a widespread consensus that the nation's central bank will soon lower the closely watched federal funds rate 1/4 to 1/2 a percentage point from its current target level of 8%. The federal funds rate, which banks charge one another on short-term loans, is extremely sensitive to changes in Fed policy.

Yet many economists say that a move by the Fed, if it comes at all, will be too late to stimulate economic growth and may not stave off a recession.

"It doesn't matter whether they ease now or in four weeks from now. There is not a lot they can do to influence the path of the economy during the balance of 1990," said Robert H. Chandross, vice president and senior economist at Lloyds Bank PLC.

Mr. Chandross, along with many of his colleagues, found in Friday's employment report the latest evidence that weakness in the economy is spreading. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that the unemployment rate rose to 5.6% of the work force, its highest level in two years. The report also showed that jobs growth now is weaker than during any time since the last recession.

Hardest hit last month were the construction and manufacturing industries, where the number of jobs declined by 40,000 and 45,000, respectively.

Nancy Kimelman, chief economist at Thomson Financial Networks, said the employment report was "legitimately bad news for the economy" that warrants an immediate Fed easing. She said that if the Fed doesn't lower rates by early this week, it will have missed its "window of opportunity" to do so without putting much strain on the financial markets.

Later this week, "the Fed will run into some obstacles," she said, specifically, the next inflation report -- Friday's producer price index.

The data are widely expected to show a sharp rise in inflation, with most economists estimating an August inflation rate of between 1% and 1.5%, which translates into an annual inflation rate of more than 12%.

An easing after the inflation reports might cause yields on long-term bonds to rise further, and widen the gap between short-term and long-term rates.

That would be a major blow to bondholders, who already have seen the value of their holdings depreciate sharply this year. The yields on 30-year Treasury bonds, for example, have risen from 7.97% at the start of the year to about 8.91% late last week. The nearly one-percentage point rise in the yield translates into a price decline of nearly 6% so far this year.

The possibility that long-term rates will continue to rise has prompted bond market strategists at several investment banking firms on Wall Street to recommend that investors continue to bail out of long-term bonds.

Merrill Lynch & Co. bond market strategist Thomas J. Sowanick recommended on Friday that fixed-income investors pursue a strategy of swapping 30-year bonds and other long-term securities for notes and bills with shorter maturities.

---

Friday's Market Activity

Bond prices ended higher, reflecting signs of a weakening economy, the chance of an interest rate easing and optimism about a deficit-reduction agreement.

The Treasury's 30-year bellwether bond ended at 98 7/32, up 3/8 point from Thursday. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, eased to 8.91% from 8.95%.

In addition to Friday's weak employment report, the bond market was buoyed by signs that a budget accord might not be far off. Analysts said the Persian Gulf crisis has spurred politicians to settle budget issues.

Falling oil prices also provided the fixed-income markets with some support, although analysts say that as long as oil remains in a $26-to-$30-a-barrel range, near-term price action in Treasury securities will be minimal. October oil futures settled $1.39 lower at $30.04.

In the tax-exempt municipal market, Standard & Poor's Corp. said it is considering downgrading its Single-A rating on $606.31 million of New York City Triborough Bridge & Tunnel Authority mortgage recording tax special-obligation bonds, Series 1988 and 1989. Interest payments on the bonds are paid from taxes collected by the city on new mortgages.

S&P said its action reflects a significant erosion in mortgage recording tax receipts over the past six months.

On the new-issue calendar, more than $2 billion in new securities are scheduled to be priced this week.

In one of the largest deals anticipated, New York City will sell approximately $750 million of general obligation bonds via negotiation. Bear, Stearns & Co. will serve as lead underwriter.

The issue's ratings are currently under review by Moody's Investors Service Inc. and S&P.

The city's existing general obligation bonds are rated single-A by Moody's and single-A-minus by S&P.

Philadelphia is expected to sell $395 million of temporary loan notes via a Merrill Lynch Capital Markets group.

Philadelphia and underwriting officials have yet to announce the backing on the issue. A letter of credit is expected, covering approximately $225 million of the notes and backed by a consortium of banks, including at least one foreign bank and Philadelphia area banks.

The city's existing general obligation bonds are rated Ba, which is below investment grade, by Moody's. S&P rates them triple-B-minus. Philadelphia is the only major city in the U.S. with a junk-bond credit rating.
