Letters to the Editor: Fuel for a Fight

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/10/90

Your patronizing editorial ("Energy Lesson 1," Aug. 8) "explaining" why it is "necessary" for retailers of petroleum products to raise immediately the price of inventory stocks in anticipation of an impending increase in wholesale prices is an insult to the intelligence of Journal readers. Extending your logic to other industries, it would be necessary for automobile dealers to raise the price of cars in inventory to the anticipated replacement cost upon notification of a forthcoming increase in the manufacturer's wholesale prices. Everyone knows it is not possible for automobile dealers, or retailers in any other industry, to do that. Moreover, even those who buy your convoluted logic will realize that the petroleum retailers in your example will profit excessively by charging customers the "anticipated" increased price for inventory stocks, and will then realize their normal profit when they actually sell new supplies purchased at a higher price. Americans are noted for their common sense and sense of fair play. That, rather than the economic ignorance implied by your editorial, explains why people of all backgrounds are enraged by the sharply higher prices they are being forced to pay for gasoline and fuel oil.

William L. Nighan

Manchester, Conn.

---

I read your Dick and Jane's "Energy Lesson 1" editorial with dismay. Why so quick to be condescending when it may be you who have missed the point? We all understand pricing with an eye toward "r-e-p-l-a-c-e-m-e-n-t costs." It's the switch to inventory cost pricing when the feed stock prices drop that highlights the contradiction. When the price of oil went down in recent years the gasoline prices stayed high for a while, we were told, because of high-priced oil inventories.

I fear your patronizing lesson concerning one method of pricing missed the issue.

Stephen D. Jarrard

Elgin, Texas

---

Rip-off. Again, RIP-OFF. That's R-I-P-O-F-F. This is the only way to summarize the fraudulent energy crisis resulting from Kuwait being Saddamized. Believe it or not, we consumers are not all ignorant about the oil market -- we know perfectly well that unrest in the Middle East (so what else is new]) does not lead to an instant oil shortage. We are also aware that:

-- Given time for shipment, refining, transportation through the system and storage, it is approximately two months' time from the extraction of oil to pumping into our cars;

-- A large surplus of oil was amassed by oil companies and governments during the period of low crude-oil prices in the mid-to-late 1980s;

-- Iraq and Kuwait together account for a whopping 4% of U.S. oil consumption;

-- None of the major American oil companies have a sizable stake in Iraq or Kuwait.

While you correctly state that it is unjust to blame service-station operators for raising prices if their costs go up, it is certainly appropriate to be suspicious of the oil companies' lust in chalking up their "replacement costs."

Jay H. Goldberg

Gaithersburg, Md.

---

What is making Congress and consumers upset is that the "replacement cost" theory does not work when prices are declining.

When crude-oil prices fell from $30 a barrel to $12 a barrel, retail gasoline prices lagged behind by six months, and even then did not move proportionally to the crude-oil price drop.

While I am certainly not advocating any form of price controls, and I agree that the free market eventually works, your editorial neglects to explain why the amazing sensitivity of the market on the way up is not mirrored on the way down.

Jeffrey A. Hertz

Santa Monica, Calif.
