What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/10/90

BUDGET NEGOTIATORS labored to find ways to cut the deficit, but aides said progress was very slow. Democrats proposed higher-income-tax rates for upper-income Americans, while the GOP proposed an even bigger capital-gains cut than Bush suggested. Negotiators, who hope to reach agreement by tomor row, are to meet with President Bush for two hours this morning. ---

Toyota plans to build a second U.S. auto-assembly plant, at a site to be announced within 60 days. The plant could open within two years. The move comes as Japanese car makers continue to win an ever-larger share of the U.S. market.

---

U.S. unemployment rose to 5.6% in August, up 0.1 percentage point to the highest level in two years. The Labor Department said private-sector job growth has virtually halted in the past two months, and an industry survey also indicates a hiring slowdown.

---

OPEC'S output is rebounding after the plunge caused by the Mideast crisis, as producers make good on promises to pump more oil. However, volume isn't expected to hit pre-crisis levels until late in the year.

---

Manville agreed to pay as much as $520 million over the next seven years to its asbestos personal-injury trust, which has run out of cash trying to pay the 130,000 claims against it.

---

Tandy charged Texas Instruments in a lawsuit with fraudulently claiming certain microprocessor technologies as its own and with demanding illegally steep royalty payments.

---

BankAmerica won the bidding for the defunct Benjamin Franklin S&L, offering $162.3 million, or 6.85% of the thrift's core deposits. Although the bid was relatively high, several analysts hailed the move as a sound one.

---

Stocks rose Friday, helped by lower oil prices. Bonds prices also rose, while the dollar ended mixed.

---

Airbus Industrie has little chance of ever becoming profitable, said a Commerce Department study, which warned that continued operations by the heavily subsidized European aerospace consortium could permanently harm U.S. competitors.

---

Bethlehem Steel wants to renegotiate a costly labor pact at its troubled rod and wire division, threatening to shut the operation entirely if the steelworkers' union balks.

---

A sale of U.S. fighter jets to South Korea was agreed to by the two nations. The $4.7 billion contract is for 120 McDonnell Douglas planes.

---

Oracle Systems will post a $27.4 million loss for its fiscal first quarter. The software concern had expanded in anticipation of a 50% rise in revenue, which failed to materialize.
