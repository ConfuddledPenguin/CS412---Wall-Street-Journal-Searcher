U.S. Says Airbus Has Little Chance To Be Profitable

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 09/10/90

WASHINGTON -- A Commerce Department report said that heavily subsidized Airbus Industrie has little chance of ever becoming profitable and that its continued operations could permanently injure the U.S. aerospace industry. The report was disclosed as U.S. trade negotiators stepped up their efforts to persuade four European governments to give up further financial support for the aerospace companies that make up the Airbus consortium. The U.S. has threatened to file a formal complaint with the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade if the issue isn't resolved by Sept. 30.

The report, prepared by Gellman Research Associates Inc. of Jenkintown, Pa., and adopted by the department, said there is "little likelihood" of Airbus ever paying back an estimated $13.5 billion in loans and other subsidies from the French, German, British and Spanish governments. By the year 2008, Airbus could have a cumulative negative cash flow of $30 billion in 1990 dollars, the report predicted.

"None of Airbus' projects, past, present and in development, appears to be commercially viable," the report concluded. The European governments claim that the consortium could become profitable as a result of the currently strong demand for passenger airliners.

The report criticized the Airbus partnership as anti-competitive. The report said the three largest civil aircraft makers in Europe have removed themselves as potential joint venture partners with companies from the U.S. and elsewhere unless the activity involves the entire consortium.

Continued Airbus subsidies could cut sharply into the profits of U.S. manufacturers, forcing them to either seek foreign capital or give up further chunks of the airliner market, the report said.
