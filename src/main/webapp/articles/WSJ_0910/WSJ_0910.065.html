Well, She's Not the First Scarlett To Make it Tough on the Big Boys

  • Author: Kevin Helliker
  • Date: 09/10/90

After years of invading small towns and squeezing out scores of mom-and-pop merchants, Wal-Mart Stores Inc. may have finally met its match in its own back yard: Scarlett Rabalais, who runs a bunch of hot dog stands in Texas. Last month, Sam's Wholesale Club, a Wal-Mart unit, ordered Ms. Rabalais's Hot Diggity Dogs company to remove its 21 hot-dog stands from outside Sam's stores, where for four years they had operated with the blessing of the Wal-Mart unit, which got a 10% cut of sales. In the eviction notice, Sam's officials said the company intended to sell its own hot dogs, as it already does at stores outside Texas. They did offer to relieve Ms. Rabalais of her stands, at a price "based on fair market value."

What the company didn't offer was any promise to hire Ms. Rabalais's 90 workers -- most of whom are elderly or disabled. Ms. Rabalais, who says she started Hot Diggity Dogs to provide employment for such people, argued for the preservation of their jobs. But she contends that her pleas went unheeded -- until, according to Ms. Rabalais, Sam's own customers started tipping off Texas newspapers to the story.

After newspapers in Dallas and Houston published articles about giant Wal-Mart evicting tiny Hot Diggity Dogs and its work force, company officials suddenly became eager to "work out the problem," Ms. Rabalais says. They even invited her to meet with them tomorrow at company headquarters in Bentonville, Ark.

Ms. Rabalais says she can't discuss the agenda for tomorrow's meeting. The reason: Company officials, embarrassed by the publicity, "asked me not to talk with the media anymore," she says. "And I promised."

In Bentonville, a Sam's spokeswoman says the discount marketer has gotten a bad rap. The company promised all along to interview Ms. Rabalais's workers, she says, and now it is prepared to guarantee jobs to them, assuming they pass drug tests and dress-code standards. "The elderly and handicapped fit into our corporate culture," she says, adding that Sam's could provide employee benefits not offered by Hot Diggity Dogs.

And with a unit of Wal-Mart, the so-called low-price king, running the stands, customers would benefit as well, she contends. "We could probably offer a lower-cost hot dog," she says.
