The Outlook: Troubling Signs Of Slower Growth

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 09/10/90

WASHINGTON -- The prospect for a recession is the economic topic grabbing headlines and financial-market attention these days. But recessions come and go. A more important question for American living standards is how the economy will perform over the next decade or two. And there are troubling new signs that the answer isn't good. For the first 25 years after World War II, the U.S. economy did a fine job of achieving every parent's dream: a better life for his or her children. Economic growth averaged better than 3% a year. There were ups and downs, recessions and recoveries, but the upward trend was always clear.

In the past two decades, however, the assumption of steadily rising living standards has been challenged. Growth slowed sharply in the 1970s. It picked up some in the 1980s, but generally remained short of the strong growth in the 25 years after the war.

Optimistic economists, including those who advise President Bush, have argued that the growth trend will rebound in the 1990s to the 3% average of the early postwar years. But other economists, including some who make monetary policy at the Federal Reserve, have been cautiously looking for growth of about 2.5% a year.

"We must accept the fact that the real economy can, over the foreseeable future, grow at a rate of no more than about 2.5%," New York Federal Reserve Bank President Gerald Corrigan said in December 1988. "Growth at a rate in excess of that amount would entail wholly unacceptable risks of accelerating inflation."

Now comes evidence that Mr. Corrigan may have been too optimistic. After sifting through the latest revisions of government data, some economists are beginning to wonder if the nation's growth trend has dropped to well below 2% a year.

That troublesome conclusion can be culled from revised Commerce Department figures released earlier this summer but widely ignored. They show that growth last year was substantially slower than previously reported. The nation's total output, or gross national product, expanded only 1.8% after adjusting for inflation, rather than the 2.6% previously reported. Yet unemployment remained at a 15-year low of 5.3%, suggesting that the economy, while sluggish, may be growing at its full potential.

"What it is telling us is that when we are fully employed, which is where we've been since 1988, we grow only 1% to 2%, not 2% to 3%," says economist Paul Samuelson, the Nobel laureate at Massachusetts Institute of Technology. "It also means that's all you should expect." If growth remains that slow, living standards seem sure to stagnate.

The economy can grow in two ways: by putting more people to work or by making each worker produce more. When nearly everyone is working, the second way -- greater productivity -- is the primary engine of growth. In the U.S., however, a decade or more of inadequate investment in education and equipment has made productivity gains disappointingly slim.

There's the possibility, to be sure, that the new data are misleading. They may be revised again someday. Moreover, productivity gains may increase if the slow-growth phenomenon stems from transitory circumstances. Commerce Undersecretary Michael Darby, who oversees the GNP figures, claims the numbers are temporarily distorted by the slump in the Northeast, with its highly skilled, productive workers, and the recovery in the Southwest, where each additional worker employed adds relatively little to the GNP total.

But there is no doubting the troubling implications if Mr. Samuelson's interpretation is correct. "It would be dreadfully profound," Mr. Darby acknowledges.

In such circumstances, the Fed, to keep the economy from overheating and reigniting inflation, would presumably try to hold growth to 2% a year or less. Americans would have to keep working harder -- longer hours, more moonlighting -- just to keep up, since wages wouldn't rise much. More would find that their living standards weren't much better than their parents' and the dream of steady economic progress would fade.

To avoid this, the U.S. needs to invest more. Then productivity can grow more rapidly. But a nation can invest for tomorrow only as much as it saves today -- or borrows from foreigners. The recent record suggests Americans don't save enough, with the federal deficit consuming much of what they do save.

This is why many economists argue that the best course is to cut the budget deficit now. Higher taxes and lower spending may pinch the economy over the next year or so and increase the odds of recession. But such measures would also help to solve the overriding problems that cloud the outlook for the longer run.
