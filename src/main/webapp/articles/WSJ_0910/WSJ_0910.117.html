Plans for Hiring By U.S. Employers Are Decreasing

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/10/90

MILWAUKEE -- Hiring plans by U.S. employers continue to weaken, although they haven't yet fallen to pre-recession levels of 1975 and 1980. The latest quarterly survey of 15,000 companies by Manpower Inc. found that 23% plan to enlarge their work forces in the fourth quarter, while 11% plan reductions.

The gap between the percentage of employers predicting staff increases and those planning to decrease hiring activity is the narrowest for any fourth quarter since 1981, the employment-services concern said. That difference of 12 is well below the 18-point gap for the third quarter, when 26% of the employers anticipated hiring and 8% foresaw reductions. The quarterly surveys aren't seasonally adjusted.

Prior to the 1982 recession, the gap -- which is used as a yardstick of hiring expectations -- fell to 11 in the 1980 and 1981 fourth quarters. Manpower officials said the yardstick was also lower than present in the 1975 pre-recession period.

The survey was conducted before the Middle East crisis erupted, and Mitchell Fromstein, Manpower's president, noted that activation of military reserve units could alter employer hiring plans if workers remain on active duty for an extended period. Activated reservists are guaranteed immediate return to their jobs when released from active duty.

The latest survey results were heavily influenced by the economically troubled Northeast, where the gap between those planning to hire and those planning reductions dropped to 5. The Midwest continues to be very stable, the survey suggests, with weakening trends emerging in the South and the West.

Among industries, wholesale and retail trades are the strongest as they staff up for the Christmas holiday selling season. Still, hiring plans in that sector don't quite reach the usual level of activity, Manpower officials noted. The education field continues to rebuild after years of reduced job opportunity, and service concerns also have active hiring plans.

In manufacturing, demand for workers is waning after two years of strong hiring. While 21% of manufacturers see their employment increasing, 10% expect to reduce job rolls. In construction, for the first time in eight years, more employers expect to decrease their work force than add to it. And in the finance, insurance and real estate sector, only 14% plan further hiring while 11% are trimming their staffs.
