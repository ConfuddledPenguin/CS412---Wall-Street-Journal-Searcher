International: EC Nations Split On Speed, Method Of Monetary Union

  • Author: Mark M. Nelson
  • Date: 09/10/90

ROME -- A serious split developed among European Community countries about the speed and method for achieving monetary union, raising the likelihood of increasingly difficult negotiations among EC members later this year. At an informal meeting of the 12 finance ministers here Saturday, British Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major won unexpected support for a more cautious approach to monetary union. The British plan emphasizes the need for convergence among the various economies of the EC -- reducing wide gaps in inflation rates, budget deficits, unemployment and other economic indicators -- before moving toward full economic and monetary union.

At the meeting, which was set up to lay the groundwork for a round of monetary-union talks in December, Mr. Major won backing from Spain, Greece, Ireland and Portugal, the community's smaller economies, which are worried that they could be severely damaged if monetary union is put in place before the yawning gap between rich and poor EC nations is narrowed.

EC President Jacques Delors said he was disappointed that Spain, in a surprise move, decided to break ranks with countries such as France and Italy, which are pressing for a speedy monetary union. Despite the setback, Mr. Delors said the overall movement toward a single currency "has been maintained."

Nevertheless, it is clear that the latest developments could slow the monetary-union momentum -- and rhetoric -- that had been building in recent months. France, Belgium, Italy and the EC Commission itself had spoken of moving toward the second stage of the Delors plan by Jan. 1, 1993, and toward creation of a single currency within as little as five years. Under the British plan, and even under a compromise proposal by Spain, the likelihood of a single currency's being established before the end of the century would be small.

"It's not going to be a mad race" toward monetary union, Albert Reynolds, the Irish finance minister, said. "Once you put a date on {monetary union}, people will begin to stand back for various reasons."

France, Belgium and Italy wanted to set Jan. 1, 1993, for the entry into force of the transitory stage of monetary union. But Mr. Major argued that those countries were concentrating too heavily on setting arbitrary dates and not enough on the economic practicalities that would be encountered by weaker economies in a monetary union. "Unless we get that convergence," he said, "we are going to get enormous tensions and enormous costs within different parts of the community."
