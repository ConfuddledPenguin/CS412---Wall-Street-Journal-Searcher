Letters to the Editor: America's Last Hurrah

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/10/90

Tom Marks ("Iraq's Not-So-Tough Army," editorial page, Aug. 21) is correct that Iraq's armored force of 5,500 tanks is equipped mainly with older designs inferior to the American M-1 or later versions of the M-60. Yet it still poses a formidable threat to the Marines and paratroopers rushed to Saudi Arabia, since they belong to light units that have no tanks at all. Due to a shortage of fast sealift, it will be weeks before the U.S. can deploy heavy units of armor and mechanized infantry, and even then it will be an open question how far American quality can offset the large Iraqi edge in numbers. Even if brought up to full strength before shipping out, the three heavy divisions slated for Saudi Arabia (the First Cavalry, Second Armored and 24th Mechanized) would field fewer than 900 tanks. Marine reinforcements could bring this up to 1,000. This would account for more than half the total allied armor defending Saudi Arabia. With control of the air, Saudi Arabia could be defended. But to attack into Kuwait against a larger force with the experience in defensive warfare that Iraq gained in the war with Iran would be very bloody and the outcome would be in doubt. The other allied forces would be of little help in such an offensive campaign. Despite the U.N. diplomatic cover, the U.S. would have to carry virtually the entire burden of the fight just as it did in Korea.

It is clear that the post-Cold War talk of maintaining only a small "contingency" army of light troops was motivated by political convenience rather than by strategic analysis. That President Bush can still speak of a 25% reduction in military manpower levels by 1995 indicates that the military budget cutters remain in control despite the severe shortages in troops, equipment and logistics revealed by the crisis.

Iraq is not the only regional power filling its arsenals with tanks, warplanes and missiles while pursuing chemical and nuclear programs. If the U.S. is to remain a global military power it needs larger, not smaller, conventional forces. That means more heavy divisions, more tactical fighter wings, more carrier battle groups and more airlift/sealift capacity. It also will have to modernize its forces to keep its edge in quality. Yet all of these programs are to suffer substantial cuts. Saddam Hussein simply should have waited a year or two. He would then only have had to dodge a few diplomatic notes instead of face what may be the last hurrah of the American military.

William R. Hawkins

President

Hamilton Center for National Strategy

Knoxville, Tenn.
