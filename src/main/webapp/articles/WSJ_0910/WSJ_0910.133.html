Medical-Insurance Ruling On Reservists Issued by IRS

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/10/90

WASHINGTON -- Employers cannot unilaterally cut off medical-insurance coverage for reservists called to Middle East duty and their dependents, the Internal Revenue Service ruled. The IRS said employers have the same obligation to employees and their families during a military call-up as they have when a worker is laid off or quits. Under the Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1985, a departing employee may obtain continued health coverage from the employer, usually for up to 18 months after he or she leaves the job, by paying the full insurance premium.

An employer may deny continued coverage to employees who become covered under another group health plan. But the IRS ruled that the medical coverage given military personnel, and the coverage for their dependents under the military's Champus health-benefits program, don't qualify as group health plans under the tax code. The ruling upheld the Defense Department's argument, although IRS officials previously had leaned against that position.

The call-up of reservists ordered by President Bush began two weeks ago. Employers typically have 14 days from the day a departed worker's regular medical coverage ends to notify the employee of his right to continued health coverage. Currently, most employers voluntarily are continuing medical benefits for the reservists and their dependents under existing terms of their plans, said Charles Kerby, a benefits consultant with William M. Mercer Inc. in New York.
