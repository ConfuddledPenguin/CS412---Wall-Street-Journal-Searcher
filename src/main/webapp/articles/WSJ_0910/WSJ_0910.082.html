Marketing & Media: Stations Often Overcharge Candidates For Campaign Ads, FCC Audit Shows

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 09/10/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal regulators found, in a spot audit of television and radio stations, that political candidates often are overcharged for campaign advertisements. The Federal Communications Commission staff said that 16 of 20 TV stations it checked charged political candidates more than commercial advertisers for air time in virtually every time period the agency analyzed. At one station, the agency said, candidates paid $5,500 for a 30-second advertisement, while commercial advertisers paid only as much as $3,000.

Under federal law, broadcasters aren't supposed to charge candidates more than they generally charge commercial advertisers. And during the 45 days before a primary election and the 60 days preceeding a general election, candidates are supposed to be billed the same low rates as a station's "most favored commercial advertiser."

Candidates fared better with radio stations, but Roy Stewart, head of the FCC's Mass Media Bureau, said that may reflect generally less use of radio for political advertising.

Mr. Stewart said the agency is continuing its investigation into compliance by the stations, and that it could eventually decide to fine some of them. Nevertheless, he said the main purpose of the audit and the report released Friday was educational: The FCC wants to help the broadcast industry comply with the law, especially since broadcasters should be offering even cheaper rates to political candidates now that there are less than 60 days before the general election.

This week, the FCC plans to send broadcasters information in question-and-answer form on the campaign advertising rules, and it will hold a seminar in Washington on compliance. In addition, the agency is planning to make a videotape that explains broadcasters' obligations.

The rules, however, are hardly clearcut. The problem, Mr. Stewart said, is that broadcasters "are not aware of the full range of their responsibilities in advising political candidates" on advertising rates.

Edward O. Fritts, president of the National Association of Broadcasters, said he was pleased with the educational effort. But he took issue with the FCC's approach, saying it was "unfortunate the commission issued a report which gives the impression that it has made final conclusions when it has not, and that presumes there are violations" before broadcasters even responded to the audits.

Sen. John Danforth (R., Mo.), a friend of FCC Chairman Alfred Sikes, said the audit "confirms what candidates have known for some time," and called the FCC's action a "constructive first step." He called for enactment of campaign finance legislation already approved by the Senate Commerce Committee, which is designed to clarify the existing law so as to ensure that candidates pay no more than commercial advertisers.

The FCC's audits, which began on July 16, covered four television stations and two radio stations in each of five metropolitan areas: Cincinnati, Dallas-Fort Worth, Philadelphia, Portland, Ore., and San Francisco. Mr. Stewart said the cities were selected because they are geographically diverse and some of the stations serve markets in two or three states, and thus were likely to be used by more candidates.
