Law -- Legal Beat: Manville to Pay Up to $520 Million Over Seven Years to Asbestos Trust

  • Author: Wade Lambert and Marj Charlier
  • Date: 09/10/90

Under pressure from a federal judge to come up with more money for asbestos victims, Manville Corp. agreed to pay as much as $520 million over the next seven years to the Manville asbestos personal-injury trust. The first installments of the new funding, however, fall far short of the amount the trust has tried to raise in recent months as it ran out of cash to pay the 130,000 claims against it. Moreover, the preliminary agreement, announced Friday, won't provide any new cash until the end of 1991. The plan is also contingent on many other factors.

The trust, the cornerstone of Manville's bankruptcy-court reorganization plan in 1988, was set up to pay all health claims stemming from exposure to asbestos products formerly made by the company. Judge Jack B. Weinstein of federal court in New York ordered the trust to come up with new financing this summer after the trust announced that it couldn't pay new claims for 25 years because it was out of cash.

Although victims' attorneys welcomed the new funding, some said they will challenge the change because they believe that Judge Weinstein does not have the right to alter Manville's bankruptcy reorganization plan. The attorneys fear that victims will lose some rights if the plan is revamped. They also are challenging a freeze on claim payments that Judge Weinstein has extended until Oct. 6.

"To the extent that Manville has come up with more money for the trust, I'm delighted," said Robert Hatten, a plaintiffs' attorney who is trying to collect a $60 million court judgment from the trust for 1,117 asbestos victims in Virginia. "The only caveat I have is, I want my money."

The preliminary funding agreement is contingent on the trust coming up with new procedures for paying claims, such as stretching payments over a period of time. Leon Silverman, appointed by Judge Weinstein and the bankruptcy court to negotiate the restructuring, said progress is being made in talks between the trust and plaintiffs' attorneys about the payment plan.

"While this is only the first step in the overall restructuring process, we are encouraged that a fair and comprehensive resolution will result from the continuing cooperation of all parties," said John C. Sawhill, chairman of the trust's board.

After paying about $638 million to asbestos victims, the trust still owes more than $300 million in settled but unpaid claims -- twice the amount of cash it has left. Before Judge Weinstein stepped in, the trust and the company had been unable to agree on new funding, although proposals to raise more than $500 million were discussed.

Under the agreement, Manville would convert the preferred stock held by the Trust into common, giving the trust an 80% stake in the company. The company would pay a special dividend to common shareholders of $125 million late in 1991, another $125 million a year later and $50 million in each of the next two years.

The agreement also provides for additional special dividends to be paid in the next four years of as much as $300 million, making a total potential dividend of $650 million, 80% of which -- or $520 million -- would go to the trust. Except for the first proposed payment, the dividends would be subject to Manville's ability to pay.

The new financing would not affect the company's obligation to pay $75 million a year on $1.8 billion in bonds held by the trust and to pay the trust 20% of Manville's profit beginning in 1992. The agreement also proposes exchanging the bonds for new ones that would be marketable. The trust would be able to sell the bonds at some point to raise money.

Manville's stock in recent months has suffered from the uncertainties surrounding the trust funding, and officials of the Denver-based building-products concern said they hope the investment community will "look on this agreement positively." In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange Friday, Manville closed at $6.75 a share, up 75 cents.

In a prepared announcement, Manville Chairman Tom Stephens said, "Our strong performance since the consummation of the plan of reorganization in 1988 has put us in the position to be able to accomplish this distribution program."

NEW YORK LAW FIRM bows out of fraudulent conveyance action.

Stroock & Stroock & Lavan, accused of a conflict of interest, agreed not to represent Southmark Corp. or its creditors in a multimillion-dollar suit against two former top officials of Southmark.

The alleged conflict arose because Stroock, which served as counsel to the Southmark unsecured creditors committee during Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings, once represented the former executives, Gene E. Phillips and William S. Friedman.

When Stroock was retained by the creditors last year, both men signed waivers saying they didn't object to the representation.

But then the creditors committee, acting on behalf of Southmark, filed suit seeking to recover more than $83 million in assets from Messrs. Phillips and Friedman and companies they control. The complaint alleged that the assets were fraudulently conveyed to the men as part of their resignation settlements last year.

The former executives retained the Dallas firm of Bickel & Brewer to sue to have Stroock dismissed from the case.

"I think it's one thing to consent to your former attorney handling a nonadversarial matter, and something else for them to turn around and sue you," said Mr. Friedman, former vice chairman.

After some legal maneuvering, Stroock agreed not to represent either the creditors committee or Southmark in the proceeding against the executives. But it is free to represent the company, which recently emerged from bankruptcy protection, in future legal matters.

"We determined that, rather than spending another couple of months litigating {the conflict issue}, reorganized Southmark should be freed to litigate on the merits" of its case against the executives, said Stroock partner Curtis Mechling.

COURT DECLINES TO EXPAND protection for Texas whistleblowers.

The case was brought by Richard Winters, a former Houston Chronicle employee who said he was fired for telling superiors about alleged circulation fraud and other illegal activities at the newspaper, owned by Hearst Corp. A lower court dismissed the suit, and the Texas Supreme Court affirmed the dismissal.

At issue was whether Texas should follow 23 other states that protect private-sector employees who report illegal activities in the workplace. Texas law does protect public workers, but private employers, with rare exceptions, are permitted to hire and fire employees at will.

In ruling against Mr. Winters, the Texas Supreme Court said his case didn't fall within the exceptions and said it wasn't inclined "at this time on these facts" to broaden the protection for private-sector whistleblowers. The decision offered little additional explanation, but a concurring opinion by Judge Lloyd Doggett suggested that more protection should be warranted in some cases.

"The judiciary should not ignore those unscrupulous employers who wield the powerful weapon of the pink slip to intimidate workers into silence in order to conceal and perpetuate activities in the workplace that endanger the public," Judge Doggett wrote.

James W. Holtz, a Houston attorney representing Mr. Winters, said he was disappointed the court didn't explain its decision more fully but hadn't decided whether to seek a rehearing.

G.E. McDavid, president of the Chronicle, said the paper had "expected" the court to rule in its favor, asserting that Mr. Winters had made a number of "wild allegations" that weren't true.

TAX ON LEGAL SERVICES challenged in Massachusetts.

The Massachusetts and Boston bar associations filed suit in the state's Supreme Judicial Court challenging the constitutionality of parts of a recently enacted 5% tax on services.

The associations are seeking to exempt legal services from the tax, arguing that it illegally restricts access to legal services, violates lawyer-client confidentiality and breaches state and federal provisions that place the regulation of lawyers solely under the judicial branch.

In a statement, John P. Driscoll Jr., president of the Boston association, said, "Although we have grave concerns for the commonwealth's fiscal health, we are compelled to take a stand against measures that play fast and free with our fundamental constitutional rights."

The suit also says that the tax is unfair and discriminatory because it targets only certain businesses.

The court has already determined that the tax might be permissible, but it didn't address key issues of applying the tax to legal services, the associations said.

The 5% levy on a host of business services is one of the broadest such taxes in the nation. It was signed into law in July.

SECOND OFFICER of New Jersey company pleads guilty to bank fraud.

Preston Davis, former assistant treasurer and assistant controller of Coated Sales Inc., pleaded guilty in federal court in Newark, N.J., to allegations he was involved in a fraud scheme. Mr. Davis admitted that he and other officers of the defunct textile converting concern lied about the amount of the company's assets to secure a $52 million restructuring loan from BancBoston Financial Co. He also admitted to conspiring to violate U.S. securities laws.

Peter V. Ryan, Mr. Davis's attorney, said his client "accepted culpability" but was "following directions of his superiors."

Shortly before Coated Sales filed for bankruptcy-court protection in June 1988, the company was considered one of the fastest-growing ones traded on the over-the-counter market, according to Michael Chertoff, U.S. attorney in Newark. By securing the loan with fictitious accounts receivable, the officers boosted the company's net sales, total assets and earnings per share, he said.

Last month, the company's former chief financial officer, Bruce Bloom, also pleaded guilty to participating in the scheme.
