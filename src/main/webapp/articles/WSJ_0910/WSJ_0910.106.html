Abreast of the Market: Stocks' Long-Term Trend Is Down, Technicians Say

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/10/90

NEW YORK -- If you listened to many technical analysts, you got out of stocks before Iraq invaded Kuwait and sidestepped an avalanche of falling stock prices. What are these technicians saying now? Stay out -- at least for a while. Then get back in, but only for a short-run rally. The market's long-term trend is down, they say.

A lot of people pooh-pooh technical stock market analysis as nothing more than statistical tea-leaf reading -- especially when the market is moving logically in line with economic trends. But as the economic landscape is obscured by the fog of other factors -- the threat of a Mideast war, for example -- and the market zigzags, investors increasingly seek out the technical gurus.

"The more people are confused, the more they look to the technical types for help," says Joseph Feshbach, technical analyst at Shearson Lehman Brothers.

Technicians basically peer under the hood at the market's inner workings to discern what will happen next; they place a lot of weight on stock price trends, with trading volume a close second. Fundamental analysts, by contrast, diagnose the health of the economy, an industry and individual companies, concentrating on such things as earnings growth, product lines and dividends.

"If you stuck to the technicals coming into August," says Timothy Clark, associate director of research at Avatar Associates, "you would have been in pretty good shape."

For the first half of the year, many bullish investors ignored the yawning gap between the briskly advancing Dow Jones Industrial Average and the lagging broader market indexes. They looked the other way as New York Stock Exchange trading volume dwindled while the industrial average rose.

"The market can't keep rising when you have those kinds of divergences, but you never know when it will end," Mr. Clark says. Though technical analysts were warning investors away from stocks even before the Dow set new records in July, "sometimes what's needed is a catalyst" to bring about the sometimes-abrupt adjustment. Before the 1987 crash it was rising interest rates. This summer, it was Saddam Hussein.

Nowadays, many technical analysts are looking at their repertoire of stock-market statistics from a new perspective: For the first time since 1982, they are interpreting the data in the context of a bear market rather than a bull market. For example:

PUT-CALL RATIO: Many analysts follow the ratio of volume in put options (a bet that stock prices are headed lower) to the volume in call options (a bet that prices will rise). In a bull market, heavy volume in puts during an intermediate decline in the market suggests stock prices will soon resume climbing, they say. That's because puts are popular with high-risk speculators who bet against the odds. But in a bear market, heavy put volume can be generated by conservative investors who are recognizing a trend.

Put-option volume soared in mid-August when the industrial average was at 2760, encouraging some investors with bull-market views to get back into stocks. But put volume continued to climb and the Dow sank to 2483 on Aug. 23. Some once-bullish investors were badly burned.

One reason for their misjudgment is that they, like the vast majority of portfolio managers, "weren't running money 10 years ago," says Kenneth Spence, technical analyst at Salomon Brothers. "Most of these people have looked at these sentiment indicators only in a bull trend." But they mean different things in a bear market, which is what Mr. Spence believes we are in now.

TRADING VOLUME: The Dow hit its recent low below 2500 on heavy volume, which is another troublesome signal for technical analysts.

"We need to see power" when the market rises to feel good about any interim rallies, says Richard Arms, a technical analyst with Eppler, Guerin & Turner in Albuquerque, N.M. "Right now we're still not seeing the money pouring in on the upside, but we are seeing it pour out on the down side."

ADVANCERS VS. DECLINERS: Another much-watched technical indicator is the 10-day average of the short-term trading index, which was developed by Mr. Arms. This is a ratio of volume in advancing and declining issues against the ratio of the number of rising stocks to falling ones. An index of 1.0 or higher indicates selling pressure is greater than buying interest. Under 1.0, the index is considered bullish.

Like the put-call percentage, the Arms trading index expresses a form of investor sentiment that varies depending on the market's overall trend. In a bull market, the index climbing to 1.25 during a decline in stock prices signals that bargain-hunters are about to swoop in and push the market higher. But in a bear market, a similar index reading can mean further declines are in prospect before a rebound materializes.

But how much deeper the market might fall, and then how much it might bounce back, is a matter of individual judgment. Mr. Arms says the index currently suggests the Dow might fall back to about 2500 in a matter of days, which could present a good buying opportunity. The subsequent rise, other analysts say, probably would be limited to about the 2700 level, 300 points shy of the July record.

Timing is another issue. Mr. Spence of Salomon Brothers agrees with Mr. Arms that the Dow will revisit the 2500 level, but he predicts it will take two to three weeks. He thinks this decline will occur on shrinking volume and that declining issues won't overwhelm advancers, as they did on Aug. 23. "That would indicate a dissipation of selling pressure in the market," he says.

Then would follow an advance lasting six to eight weeks, Mr. Spence says, with the Dow stopping at 2700. "What you get essentially is a rally in a downtrend," he says.

Mr. Feshbach of Shearson thinks the Dow will be confined in a range of 2475 to 2725. "The best thing to do now is what the charts are telling us to do: It's tough for the growth stocks. The interest rate-sensitive stocks are dead. So you should be long on some gold stocks, energy, and commodities stocks such as Freeport-McMoRan and Burlington Resources, and short the retail and technology stocks."

Not everyone is so pessimistic. Donald Fishback, research director of Investment Institute Research in Cincinnati, for example, is looking for a range of 2500 to 3000 for the rest of this year. While he concedes interpreting sentiment indicators is risky business, he is convinced that bearishness is overdone and the market has nowhere to go but up.

"You don't want to step in front of a freight train, which we did in August by turning bullish too early," he says, "but when the (short-term trading index) starts to head down, then it's a good time to step in and buy stocks."

But maybe the real lesson in all this isn't so, well, technical. "If investors are starting to respect the technicians," says William Raftery, technical analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham, "you know we're darn close to a bottom."

Friday's Market Activity

The stock market moved higher in quiet trading, supported by falling oil prices and optimism about President Bush's apparent resolve to reduce the federal deficit.

Shares of so-called "defensive growth" companies -- those expected to deliver strong earnings gains even if the economy slips into a recession or war breaks out in the Middle East -- set the pace for the advance.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average climbed 23.26 to 2619.55. In the energy markets, crude oil for October delivery tumbled $1.39 a barrel, to $30.04.

President Bush's expression of confidence that congressional budget negotiators would be able to complete a deficit-reduction agreement at their summit meeting over the weekend, along with his reaffirmation of a $50 billion target for fiscal 1991 deficit cuts, spurred buying interest, traders said.

Just 123,800,000 shares traded on the New York Stock Exchange. Advancing issues topped decliners by 922 to 560.

The Big Board's composite index added 1.45 to 177.51; Standard & Poor's 500-stock index gained 2.94 to 323.40, and the Dow Jones Equity Market index rose 2.71 to 299.63.

For the week, the Dow industrials rose 5.19 points, or 0.2%.

Among blue-chip issues falling into the "defensive growth" category, PepsiCo gained 3/4 to 25 1/4 in Big Board composite trading of 1.4 million shares, Philip Morris climbed 1/2 to 45 1/4 on 1.2 million shares, McDonald's rallied 1 1/8 to 27 7/8 on 1.2 million shares, Coca-Cola Co. rose 1 to 42 1/8, Procter & Gamble rose 1 1/2 to 78, Merck gained 1 1/4 to 81 1/8 and Pfizer added 1 3/4 to 71 1/8.

UAL, parent of United Airlines, climbed 8 to 100 1/4 as a report from Prudential-Bache Securities revived hopes that a union-led group will be able to complete its long-delayed buy-out of the company. Analyst Paul Nisbet wrote that the group's $201-a-share buy-out proposal or one "constructed more conservatively" -- possibly around $180 a share -- should be completed by October, barring an outbreak of a Middle East war, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Speculation that investor Marvin Davis may again be interested in UAL following the sale of Pebble Beach Co., which had been owned by a real estate partnership he controls, may have spurred the stock's sharp gains as well, Mr. Nisbet said. A spokeswoman for Mr. Davis, who offered to buy UAL last year, declined to comment.

Compaq Computer dropped 7/8 to 44 5/8 on 1.3 million shares. This newspaper's Heard on the Street column Friday noted, among other things, that some of the company's notebook-sized laptop computers have been developing hairline cracks after brief use.

International Business Machines advanced 3 1/8 to 105 1/2 on 1.6 million shares. Traders attributed the strength to optimism about the prospects for IBM's new line of mainframe computers.

Toys `R' Us, the most active Big Board issue, fell 5/8 to 23 1/8 on 2.4 million shares. Wertheim Schroder lowered its estimates of the toy retailer's 1990 and 1991 earnings.

Equimark rose 1/2 to 5 3/8 following the resignation of Alan S. Fellheimer as chairman and chief executive officer of the bank holding company, which omitted its common stock dividend earlier last week.

Saatchi & Saatchi skidded 1/2 to 3. The British company suspended dividends on its ordinary shares and some of its preference shares, citing a lack of available reserves to make the payouts.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 0.73 to 323.77, but rose 0.1% for the week.
