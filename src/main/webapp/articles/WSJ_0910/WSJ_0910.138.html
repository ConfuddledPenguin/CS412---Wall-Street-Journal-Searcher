Financial Inquiry Charges 2 British Miners' Leaders

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/10/90

LONDON (AP) -- Two top British mineworkers' leaders will be charged with improper accounting after an inquiry into union finances during the 1984-85 miners' strike, a court official said. John Richman, a Sheffield Magistrates' Court clerk, said he issued nine summonses against Arthur Scargill, president of the National Union of Mineworkers, and union general secretary Peter Heathfield, as well as against the union itself.

The charges are failure to keep proper accounting records, failure to maintain a satisfactory system of accounting control and failure to submit true and fair accounts. The two men face fines if convicted.

"We have done nothing wrong, and we shall contest the action all the way," Mr. Scargill said. "This is the latest in a long line of attacks on the national leadership of the miners' union from the establishment who have never forgiven us for what we did in the 1984-85 miners' strike."

Mr. Scargill led the bitter and futile showdown with the government over the closure of unproductive mines. The union, which had 235,000 members at the time, now has 50,000, and 98 mines have been closed.

In July the union sued Mr. Scargill to recover #1.4 million ($2.6 million) that had been donated by Soviet and Eastern European miners to help the British miners during the strike.

The court granted the union a delay while it sought to settle the issue by negotiation. Today, a union delegation is to go to the Paris headquarters of the International Miners' Organization to determine control of the funds.
