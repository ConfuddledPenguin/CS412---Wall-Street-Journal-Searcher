Johnson Controls Inc. Is Reviewing Options On Battery Business

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/10/90

MILWAUKEE -- Johnson Controls Inc., the U.S.'s largest maker of automotive batteries, said it may sell all or part of its slow-growth battery business. The company said it retained Salomon Brothers to explore options for the battery group, which in fiscal 1989 earned $34 million on sales of $659 million. Johnson Controls said that besides possibly selling all or part of the business, it could link up with a partner or opt to keep the business. The company didn't set a deadline for the review.

A spokeswoman said the decision to review its options on batteries reflects the dim outlook for the kind of profit growth Johnson Controls is seeking for its segments, which also include facility-management services, auto seats, and plastic containers. In the year ended Sept. 30, the company earned $97.5 million, or $2.55 a share, on sales of $3.68 billion.

"While we are looking for slightly improved earnings in fiscal 1990 and 1991, the battery business's participation in a slow-growth market will preclude the desired kind of profit growth," the spokeswoman said. The U.S. automobile industry has been in a 3 1/2-year-long sales slide.

Officials also believe that while long-term prospects for batteries are good, the business will require a "relatively high rate of capital expenditures over the next several years," she said. Johnson Controls spent $44 million in capital-spending funds in fiscal 1989 on the battery group, which operates 17 U.S. plants and has 5,500 employees. Overall capital spending last year was $192 million.

About 85% of Johnson Controls' battery sales are for the replacement market. The company makes all the batteries for Sears, Roebuck & Co., the largest replacement-battery retailer. But Johnson Controls also is an original-equipment supplier to every major domestic and foreign auto maker except General Motors Corp., which supplies its own batteries. Excluding GM, Johnson Controls has an estimated 50% share of the original-equipment battery market.
