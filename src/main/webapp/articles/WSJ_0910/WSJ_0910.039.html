South Korea to Aim For 7% Growth Rate In 1992-96 Period

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/10/90

SEOUL, South Korea -- South Korea's economic development plan for 1992 to 1996 sets as a goal average annual economic growth rate of 7%, the Economic Planning Board said Friday. The government agency said the goal was contained in a set of basic guidelines for working out the five-year plan to be completed by next August.

At that growth rate, officials said, South Korea's gross national product would rise from an estimated $231.7 billion in 1990 to $454 billion in 1996, with per capita GNP nearly doubling from $5,430 this year to $10,050 in 1996. South Korea's GNP grew 6.7% in 1989.

Planning board officials said the current account is expected to show a $5 billion surplus in 1996, compared with a deficit of $1 billion expected for this year.

South Korea is expected to become a net creditor nation over the five-year period, they said, with its external assets exceeding its external debts. South Korea has been a debtor nation for decades.

According to the guidelines, the five-year plan will stress technological innovation and call for increasing the ratio of investment in research and development from 2.2% at present to between 3% and 4% in 1996.

To prepare for a new global economic order as a result of the Uruguay Round of multilateral trade talks, the government plans to transform the current farm-based rural economy into a mixture of primary, secondary and tertiary industries, officials said.

They added that South Korea plans to play a greater economic role within the Asia-Pacific region and expand economic ties with the European Community and Eastern bloc countries.
