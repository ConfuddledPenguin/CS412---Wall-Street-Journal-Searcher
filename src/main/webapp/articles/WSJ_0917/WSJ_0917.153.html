International -- Washington Insight: Bush's Desire to Peacefully Restrain Iraq's Weapons Program May Be Elusive

  • Author: Roberts S. Greenberger
  • Date: 09/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration says stringent international controls could peacefully curb Iraq's biological, chemical and nuclear weapons programs, even if Saddam Hussein remains in power. But judging from recent experience, that may be a pipe dream.

Baghdad already has the facilities to continue producing massive quantities of its own biological and chemical weapons, and it has sophisticated missiles to deliver them, experts say. More worrisome, the nuclear nonproliferation treaty can't significantly restrict Saddam Hussein's quest for nuclear weapons: under the treaty, which Iraq has signed, he can continue Iraq's nuclear research effort.

These stark realities fuel the debate over whether the U.S. can achieve its goals in the Persian Gulf unless it topples Saddam Hussein.

So far, the Bush administration hasn't reached its own conclusion. In his address to a joint session of Congress last week, the president said only that one long-term U.S. objective in the Persian Gulf would be "to curb the proliferation of chemical, biological, ballistic missiles and, above all, nuclear technologies."

Lately, the administration seems to be hoping it can achieve its goal short of eliminating the Iraqi dictator. But many experts say containment won't work. They note that with international help, Iraq has built a nearly self-sufficient defense industry. West German companies built its chemical-weapons facilities. French companies helped it construct an electronics plant that makes computer chips and circuit boards used in air-defense equipment. A Chilean company designed an Iraqi factory that makes cluster bombs.

Similarly, Iraq has sophisticated missile technology. In December, it surprised U.S. and other intelligence services by launching a rocket capable of putting a satellite in orbit. And according to a report last week, Iraq successfully tested an intermediate-range ballistic missile loaded with chemical weapons.

"All of our experience over the last decade suggests you simply cannot effectively control the ability of a country to obtain technology if they really want to get it," says W. Seth Carus, research fellow at the Washington Institute for Near East Policy, a private group.

The U.S. itself lacks the means to block the flow of militarily sensitive technology to regional powers. Under current rules, the Pentagon only has the authority to monitor certain potentially troublesome exports to communist nations. But now that the communist bloc has dissolved, it is nations such as Iraq that pose the greatest threat. Last week, the Senate passed a measure giving the Pentagon more authority, but the measure must be reconciled with a House bill.

Concerning nuclear facilities, Leonard Spector, an expert on nuclear proliferation issues at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, says the current treaty isn't enough to safeguard the world against a maverick like Saddam Hussein.

Non-nuclear countries that have signed the treaty, such as Iraq, pledge not to develop nuclear weapons. But the treaty allows them to do nuclear work that ostensibly is for peaceful purposes. So, Saddam Hussein could continue his nuclear research program without violating the treaty until he is just weeks from being able to produce a bomb.

Mr. Spector says the U.S. prefers additional agreements, such as one it has with South Korea, that say certain kinds of nuclear facilities -- such as enrichment plants or large research reactors -- won't be built even though permitted by the treaty.

Last week, Secretary of State James Baker seemed to be suggesting similar arrangements could be applied in the Middle East. He told North Atlantic Treaty Organization allies that the major powers and Mideast nations "need to consider intrusive, internationally sanctioned measures and procedures to diminish the threat of Iraqi weapons of mass destruction."

But how could "intrusive" measures be peacefully used on Saddam Hussein? "It seems to me that if Saddam Hussein withdraws from Kuwait, we're going to be stuck with a situation where we would wish for more than the treaty, but won't be able to get it," says Mr. Spector.

As a result, some experts are concluding reluctantly that there is only one way to deal effectively with Iraq's nuclear capabilities. "Given their record on important treaties, we cannot be sure that their word means they will honor their obligations," says congressional aide Peter Galbraith, a Mideast expert. "The only way we can be sure is if their nuclear facilities are either dismantled or destroyed."
