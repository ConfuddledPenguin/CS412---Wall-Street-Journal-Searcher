Heard on the Street: Digital Equipment Stock Drop Isn't Over, Analysts Say, Despite Strong Fundamentals

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 09/17/90

BOSTON -- Digital Equipment stock plummeted 2 7/8 Friday to 56 3/8, its lowest point since 1985, but few analysts are willing to bet it has hit bottom. On the surface, there is much to recommend the world's second biggest computer maker at these levels. After all, many analysts were boosting the stock in May when it hit a 12-month high of 95. Digital is likely to survive any industry shakeout. It has about $17 a share in cash, a sterling balance sheet and a current price far below the $66-a-share book value long seen as a floor for the stock.

But with a deteriorating economy hurting sales world-wide and a huge work force being pared only slowly, Digital's outlook remains somber. Analysts slashed earnings estimates last week. For the fiscal first quarter, ending Sept. 30, many expect Digital to incur its second consecutive quarterly loss.

Moreover, there is growing doubt that Digital will initiate cash dividends at its November annual meeting. Digital's president and founder, Kenneth H. Olsen, indicated last year that he had dropped his 33-year opposition to paying dividends. A dividend would allow purchase of the shares by institutions that are prohibited from buying stocks without dividends. But now the chances of a dividend are "very slim," says Jay P. Stevens of Dean Witter Reynolds, who was bullish on the stock most of the year.

Analyst John Adams of Adams Harkness & Hill notes that Digital is pushing employees to leave under a voluntary severance plan; he thinks a dividend is unlikely when employees are being asked for sacrifices. Citing Digital's reluctance to breach its no-layoff tradition, he says, "you have to empathize with Ken Olsen. He is virtually the only industrial saint around."

Analysts expect Digital to start a new stock buy-back program soon to bolster the shares. In April it began a five-million-share buy-back for 4.1% of its stock. Institutional traders said Digital finished that buy-back last week, perhaps contributing to the stock's decline.

Patricia Laupheimer at Shearson Lehman Brothers cut her profit estimate last Monday after a Digital spokesman "described 75% to 80% of their markets as sluggish." She notes that some big, slow-growth computer stocks currently trade at multiples of 10 or less times earnings while Digital remains above that level, compared with Street estimates.

For fiscal 1991, ending June 30, many analysts have cut their estimates to $4 to $5 a share from a mean $6.22 estimate a few weeks ago. Last year's share earnings were only 59 cents after a big restructuring charge, down from $8.45 the year before. "The problem is you don't know what earnings are going to be," says Steven Milunovich of First Boston. "In the near term, there's not a lot of reason to be optimistic."

Many investors remain disenchanted by Digital's cost-cutting efforts. Last spring the Maynard, Mass., company began its first voluntary severance program with employment at a record 125,800 persons. By next June, officials have said, employment will be down to about 119,000.

Nevertheless, analysts say Digital remains overstaffed compared with competitors. Stephen Smith, a PaineWebber analyst who recommends the stock long term, calculates that if Digital's overhead and research and development levels were similar to International Business Machines Corp., the world's largest computer maker, and Hewlett-Packard Co., "that would add $8 a share to earnings. They hired too many people in the boom days."

John Levinson of Goldman Sachs, who has been bearish on Digital and other makers of midrange computers for years, says that its $103,000 revenue per employee, a measure of productivity, is less than half that of Sun Microsystems, the leader in the workstation market that Digital is trying to crack. "I believe DEC has many more restructuring costs ahead," he says.

Skittish as most analysts are about Digital, they sound like wild-eyed optimists compared with Marc Schulman of UBS Securities. A longtime Digital watcher, he was recommending the stock two months ago because he predicted accelerating earnings momentum. Now he thinks the company will have a net loss of $1 a share in the current quarter and only break even for the full year because of weak revenue.

Mr. Schulman thinks Digital may have a 1% revenue decline from last year's $12.94 billion, even though the new VAX 9000 mainframe computer may add $1 billion in sales. Last year's paltry 1.6% revenue gain came when the U.S. economy was "muddling along" and Europe was strong, he says. Today, "the U.S. economy is worse than before. The European economy is worse than before and the degree to which users are willing to buy minicomputers is worse than before."
