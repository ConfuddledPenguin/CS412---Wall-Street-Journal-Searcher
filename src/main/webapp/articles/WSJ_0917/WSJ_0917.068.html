Letters to the Editor: A Warning: Remember Cambodia

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/17/90

The Vietnamese lesson has borne much "bad" fruit: Iraq, after having invaded Kuwait, has purely and simply annexed it. In Kuwait, before its annexation, Iraq did exactly what Vietnam did in Cambodia on December 1978: massive attacks using hundreds of tanks, heavy artillery, airplanes and thousands of infantrymen; a setting up of a puppet government; the dispatching of settlers; then the so-called withdrawal of regular forces leaving only "volunteers." The difference between the Vietnamese case and that of Iraq is that Iraq has now annexed Kuwait while Vietnam continues to try to integrate Cambodia into its "Indochinese Federation." Vietnam has not yet succeeded, after having tried for more than 11 years, thanks on the one hand to the existence of a Cambodian National Resistance under the leadership of Samdech Norodom Sihanouk and on the other to the firm support of the member nations of ASEAN and other countries espousing the values of peace and justice.

The Vietnamese lesson, well learned by Iraq, gives one food for thought: The commander in chief of the Royal Thai Army, during his visit to inspect troops in the eastern region, has clearly warned them against a "possible intervention of foreign troops from Cambodia," referring to the recent Iraqi invasion. On this subject, the deputy prime minister of Singapore has used language even more firm and has launched an unequivocal warning. The other nations of the world, especially those of middle and small size, without mentioning them, should not be far from sharing the fear.

Unfortunately, the U.S. and Western Europe (the Common Market) are heading for a dialogue with Vietnam and a resumption of aid to this country, which would signify the acceptance by the Western nations of the Vietnamese fait accompli in Cambodia. A de facto recognition by the Western nations and the Western press of the Phnom Penh regime set up by Vietnamese tanks and carried out through the various forms of Vietnamese presence would prove to Iraq and others that acts of aggression will pay off as long as one has the audacity and patience.

Is it not a bitter lesson for us, the little and middle-size states, and equally for those who are friends of the West?

On Behalf of

Prince Norodom Ranariddh

Personal Representative,

HRH Samdech Norodom Sihanouk

in Cambodia and Asia,
