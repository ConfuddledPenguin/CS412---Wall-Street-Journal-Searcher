Marketing & Media: Weintraub Entertainment Group Files For Chapter 11 Bankruptcy Protection

  • Author: Richard Turner
  • Date: 09/17/90

LOS ANGELES -- Weintraub Entertainment Group, one of the last of the high-flying, hard-falling independent entertainment companies of the 1980s, said it filed for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code. The action was taken after two banks couldn't reach agreement with closely held WEG and bondholders about disposition of the company's assets. Some bondholders then threatened to file an involuntary bankruptcy petition.

Flamboyant company chairman Jerry Weintraub raised $461 million in 1986, including distribution deals and credit lines. He was helped by an alliance with Columbia Pictures Entertainment, a track record of producing such hits as "The Karate Kid," a gift for salesmanship and a friendship with then-Vice President George Bush. But Mr. Weintraub was later criticized for running the company with lavish overhead and showing poor judgment in picking motion pictures. Debt burdens created by purchasing a film library compounded his problems.

The company released eight films, the highest-profile of which was "My Stepmother Is an Alien." None were markedly successful. Since the spring of 1989, WEG has released only one movie -- its last in the pipeline -- and has been largely operating on revenue generated by its film library, which it bought from Cannon Group in 1987 for about $85 million and has been trying to sell. The last results made public were losses of $41.9 million in 1988. Meanwhile Mr. Weintraub and company president Kenneth Kleinberg have been trying to find new sources of money in Japan and elsewhere. Last year, they hired former junk-bond king Michael Milken to help with the rescue project.

Bondholders said they were concerned that the secured creditors, Credit Lyonnais Group's Dutch subsidiary and to a lesser extent Bank of America, would take possession of assets worth the approximately $85 million owed to them and there'd be nothing left over for bondholders. The company and bondholders hope the film library is an asset which will appreciate if the company can continue to operate. The library contains more than 1,700 films, many of them British, 28 television series and a number of classic newsreels.

Through Bear, Stearns & Co., WEG placed about $81 million in junk bonds, which paid 13% and were sold to institutions, notably failed Miami, Fla.-based CenTrust Savings Bank, whose chairman, David Paul, was a Weintraub director. The company has missed two $5 million interest payments in recent months.

A standstill agreement with the banks had helped allow WEG to operate as long as it did, according to individuals close to the negotiations. Employees have left the company in droves since its heyday, and overhead has been cut. Mr. Weintraub's initial contract called for a salary of $1.7 million a year. The banks were prepared to extend the agreement, but only under terms unacceptable to bondholders, and what one source described as "shuttle diplomacy" between the two parties by the company was unsuccessful.

According to individuals close to the situation, Credit Lyonnais has the first security interest in the library and Bank of America stands to take a loss on its loans.

Sources also indicated that although majority shareholder Mr. Weintraub would continue to be involved in the company, he may step down as chairman. Discussions have occurred with Time Warner Inc.'s Warner Brothers film unit, both on the part of the company and Mr. Weintraub personally, but executives wouldn't provide details.
