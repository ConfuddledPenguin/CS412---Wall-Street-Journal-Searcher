Mobil Group Awards Contracts For Oil Platform

  • Author: Rose Tamburri
  • Date: 09/17/90

OTTAWA -- A group of oil companies led by Mobil Oil Canada Ltd. awarded $1.4 billion of contracts to international engineering groups to build a giant production platform for the Hibernia oil field off Canada's east coast. The concrete platform will be located in an area known as Iceberg Alley and will be designed to withstand a direct iceberg hit.

The contracts were awarded Friday after Mobil and its partners signed an agreement with the governments of Canada and Newfoundland to undertake the $4.5 billion Hibernia development.

Doris Engineering of France, head of a group of companies, received a $1.1 billion contract to construct the production platform. A Canadian-European joint-venture led by SNC Group Inc. of Montreal was awarded a $309.8 million contract for engineering work.

Canadian Energy Minister Jake Epp predicted Hibernia's development would spur the development of other eastern offshore oil fields including the Terra Nova, White Rose and Ben Nevis fields.

Hibernia, located off Newfoundland, contains between an estimated 525 million and 650 million barrels of light, waxy crude oil, most of which is expected to be shipped to oil refineries in the U.S. Northeast. Production is expected to begin in 1996 and continue for about 20 years. During the peak production period in 1998, Hibernia is projected to produce 110,000 barrels of oil a day.

Mobil Oil Canada has a 28.1% stake in Hibernia. Its partners include Gulf Canada Resources Inc., which is controlled by Toronto's Reichmann family, with 25%; government-owned Petro-Canada with 25%; and Chevron Canada Resources Ltd., a unit of San Francisco-based Chevron Corp., with 21.9%. Mobil Oil Canada is a unit of New York-based Mobil Corp.

Some industry analysts claimed the Hibernia project wouldn't be economic without the heavy financial support of the Canadian and Newfoundland governments.

The Canadian government agreed to contribute $2.3 billion in cash and loan guarantees toward Hibernia's development costs. It also agreed to provide the consortium with interest-free loans if oil prices fall below $25 a barrel. In addition, the government said it would provide loans to cover part of any capital cost overruns. It also agreed to forgo any government revenues generated by Hibernia until the consortium repays all of its outstanding loans.

The Newfoundland government agreed to forgo some sales and income taxes on the project.

John O'Connor, president of Mobil Oil Canada, said Canada's investment into Hibernia "is sound." He predicted Canada "will get its loans back and more besides."

Newfoundland Premier Clyde Wells said that in addition to Hibernia, there is a "significant level of excitement" about Terra Nova's prospects.

Petro-Canada is expected to submit early next year its plans to develop the Terra Nova field. The field, which is located about 25 miles south of Hibernia, is estimated to contain 300 million barrels of crude.

Husky Oil Ltd. says it plans to drill a further three or four delineation wells in the White Rose field, which is expected to contain between 200 million and 300 million barrels. Currently there is one well being drilled in the eastern offshore region.
