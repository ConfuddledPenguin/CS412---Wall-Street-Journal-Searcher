Philippine's Economic Woes May Damp Demands for U.S. Military Withdrawal

  • Author: Lawrence MacDonald
  • Date: 09/17/90

MANILA -- The first round of formal talks for a new treaty covering U.S. military bases in the Philippines starts this week amid an economic downturn that is expected to undercut nationalist demands for a rapid U.S. withdrawal. Still, a gradual reduction of the forces remains likely. Talks on the bases in May, which were described as exploratory, came as the Cold War was collapsing, prompting predictions that the U.S. wouldn't be averse to relinquishing the bases. But more recently, the U.S. military buildup in the Persian Gulf and an accelerating slide in the Philippine economy have altered the context of the talks.

Analysts say these changing conditions have increased both sides' interest in reaching a new agreement that would provide for a gradual reduction of U.S. forces, with the possibility that the forces would continue to use Philippine facilities on a commercial basis.

The negotiations between panels headed by Philippine Foreign Secretary Raul Manglapus and U.S. special envoy Richard L. Armitage, a former assistant secretary of defense, are expected to begin tomorrow in Manila. The talks will focus on time and money: how long Manila will permit U.S. forces to remain and how much Washington will pay for the privilege.

The current agreement, signed in 1966, expires in September 1991, and Manila has already given formal notice that U.S. troops should withdraw by that date unless a new treaty is signed. There are about 16,000 U.S. troops in the Philippines, the third-largest U.S. military contingent in Asia after those in Japan and South Korea.

For the U.S., the Gulf crisis has highlighted the importance of maintaining a network of foreign bases despite the collapse of communism in Eastern Europe and sharply diminished perceptions of a Soviet threat. "It's pretty obvious that the Americans have tried to depreciate the value of the bases in the Philippines," says Filipe Miranda, a professor of political science at the University of the Philippines. Since Iraq's invasion of Kuwait on Aug. 2, however, there has been increased recognition in the U.S. "that the value of the bases is quite great," he adds.

Recent massive U.S. deployments to Saudi Arabia didn't pass through the Pacific, but moved from the East Coast. Nonetheless, U.S. bases in the Philippines are being used to supply the Gulf forces, a role that is likely to expand if the Mideast standoff continues, U.S. sources say. More generally, analysts say Iraq's invasion of Kuwait has increased awareness in both Manila and Washington of the greater potential for regional threats to peace since the end of the Cold War.

U.S. officials say they want to retain the bases as long as possible under an agreement acceptable to both sides, but they acknowledge that nationalist sentiment makes an indefinite U.S. military presence highly unlikely. "There are legitimate nationalist instincts," says a U.S. official. "The challenge of the talks is to merge our interests with Philippine nationalism."

Adds an Asian diplomat: "An indefinite stay is already out. The only thing to be decided is the {transition} period."

Rep. Stephen Solarz, chairman of the House Foreign Affairs Subcomittee on Asian and Pacific Affairs, said a week ago that he talked with U.S. negotiators before their departure for Manila and believed they would propose a 10-year extension that would include a gradual U.S. phaseout.

However, a source close to the U.S. Embassy in Manila says the U.S. negotiating team won't offer a withdrawal, even on a 10-year timetable.

For the Philippines, the Gulf crisis is only the latest in a string of natural and man-made disasters that have battered the economy in the past year, making it more dependent on benefits that derive from Clark Air Base, Subic Bay Naval Station and four smaller U.S. military facilities.

Reeling from a sixth coup attempt last December, drought, typhoons, an earthquake and floods, the government of President Corazon Aquino has repeatedly been forced to reduce its 1990 growth targets. Gross national product is now officially projected to expand 3.8% this year, well below the 6% growth in output of goods and services that government planners say is necessary to alleviate poverty and provide jobs for a swelling population. Even so, private analysts say the government's GNP figures are still overly optimistic.

The Mideast crisis has dealt the Philippines a double blow, bringing higher oil prices while cutting remittances from the estimated 65,000 Filipinos who were working in Iraq and Kuwait at the time of the invasion. Workers returning from the Gulf will only exacerbate unemployment. This economic squeeze has fortified the arguments that the Philippines can't afford to turn out the Americans.

Under a 1988 accord, President Ronald Reagan agreed to ask Congress for a total of $962 million as payment to Manila for use of the bases during the two years ending in September 1991. Congress appropriated only $385 million for 1990, $96 million short of one year's payment, and Manila and Washington are currently engaged in a dispute over the discrepancy. Still, the U.S. payment represents a substantial cash infusion for the strapped Aquino government.

More significant for the economy are business and employment spinoffs from the bases, which U.S. officials estimate at about $1 billion a year, or roughly 3% of Philippine GNP. The bases employ 78,000 Filipinos, a payroll second only to that of the Philippine government.

To counter concerns about economic dependence on the bases-and perhaps to strengthen the Philippines' negotiating position-the government's Bases Council has proposed that Clark be converted into an international airport and cargo facility and that Subic Bay become a commercial ship-repair facility, with the U.S. Navy as a major client.

The Bases Council has suggested that the conversion be financed by shifting Philippine military camps from Manila to Clark and selling the prime real estate they now occupy in the capital for an estimated 50 billion pesos ($2.07 billion). Even supporters of the conversion plan, however, acknowledge that it couldn't be implemented before the current agreement expires next year.

Nationalist sentiment against the bases remains strong, particularly among the political elite. Analysts question whether any treaty providing for an extended U.S. presence could win two-thirds endorsement from the 23-member Philippine Senate, as required by the country's constitution.

Philippine negotiators have refused to divulge their objectives in the negotiations, insisting that it is up to the U.S. to present proposals. Economics will clearly be on their minds, however.

Alfredo Bengzon, the panel's vice chairman, said recently that the Philippine negotiators would examine U.S. proposals from three perspectives: "First, the aspect of what is politically acceptable to us. Second, from the aspect of what is economically bearable. And third, from the aspect of what serves the needs of both the Philippines and the United States."
