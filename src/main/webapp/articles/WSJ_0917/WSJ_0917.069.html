Chase Bid to Raise Capital in Japan Runs Into Snags

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 09/17/90

TOKYO -- Investment bankers trying to raise $100 million in fresh capital for Chase Manhattan Corp. are colliding with a flood of fund-raising efforts by Japanese banks. The U.S. bank holding company is trying to privately place $100 million in subordinated debt with Japanese leasing companies, people familiar with the effort said.

Jayme Garcia Dos Santos, Chase Manhattan Bank's senior vice president in Tokyo, confirmed that an investment bank "has offered to raise the money for the {Chase Manhattan} corporation." A report on the capital-raising effort appeared in Friday's American Banker. Mr. Garcia said the investment firm, identified by others as CS First Boston Inc., had been "sounding the market to make a firm proposal."

Officials at CS First Boston could not be reached for comment.

At least one leasing company initially was interested in putting up the money and bankers said the company was hoping to sell the paper to other leasing companies.

But the other companies apparently showed less enthusiasm for the deal because it comes in the midst of a huge rush by Japanese banks to place subordinated debt of their own. "It's not a hot prospect," one U.S. banker said.

All international banks are facing pressure to bolster their capital bases by March 1993, when new international regulations come into effect requiring banks to maintain capital roughly equivalent to 8% of their assets. For Chase, new capital would help it expand beyond that level, since it currently has a 9.02% ratio.

Until this year, a booming stock market and a booming, cash-spinning economy made raising capital an easy task for Japanese banks. The stock market has since fallen sharply, though, not only removing a major source of funds from the banks but wiping out much of the capital they held in the form of stock.

As a result, big Japanese banks have been looking elsewhere for capital. Since July, nearly a dozen banks either have planned or completed large issues of subordinated loans.

Subordinated debt pays higher interest rates than straight loans and, because it gives its creditors last claim in a bankruptcy, it can be counted under international standards as capital.

Japan's life-insurance and leasing companies have been buying most of the paper. But their appetite for it is waning, and one banker said he would be "extremely surprised" if CS First Boston is able to place subordinated debt for an American bank such as Chase.

Chase is finding subordinated paper difficult to arrange. Another subordinated issue by the bank group in the U.S. recently was repriced to yield more than 13%, up sharply from its original yield of 9.66%.
