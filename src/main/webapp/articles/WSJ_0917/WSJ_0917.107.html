Ford Lowers Estimate Of Auto, Truck Sales By Industry in 1990

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/17/90

DETROIT -- Ford Motor Co. lowered its estimate for overall car and truck sales in 1990 amid growing gloom in the auto industry about the state of the economy. Ford, which had forecast the U.S. market at 14.5 million cars and trucks in 1990, dropped its estimate to 14.3 million vehicles, said Harold A. Poling, the company's chairman and chief executive officer.

Until recently, the industry had been planning for improved sales in the year's second half, but Mr. Poling said that now appears unlikely. "We'll have weakness in the fourth quarter, so we won't have the pickup we hoped for," he said. He predicted the sluggish sales climate would continue into 1991, but that sales would pick up in the 1991 second half. General Motors Corp., meanwhile, said it was sticking to its prediction of 14.5 million vehicles. Chrysler Corp., which put the market at 14 million to 14.5 million vehicles in June, has no new forecast, either. But a top Chrysler official echoed Mr. Poling's worries Friday.

"A possible downturn in the economy" is a "big concern to us all," said Robert S. Miller, Chrysler's vice chairman.

Detroit has reason to brace for a rough period. The jump in oil prices, resulting from Iraq's invasion of Kuwait last month, already seems to have had a chilling effect on the U.S. economy. And there's evidence that is hurting car sales. Overall sales of cars and trucks declined 17% in August from a year earlier, although sales rebounded somewhat in the first 10 days of September. The slow sales may translate into production cuts by auto makers in the fourth quarter, which, in turn, could drag down the national economy even further.

When consumers "get nervous, they don't buy cars," said John Casesa, an analyst with Wertheim Schroder & Co. "I think Detroit is facing at least a year of decelerating sales."
