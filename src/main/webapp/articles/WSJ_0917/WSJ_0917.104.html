Marketing & Media: Disney to Stop Making Dramas For Television

  • Author: Richard Turner
  • Date: 09/17/90

HOLLYWOOD -- Walt Disney Co. has quietly decided to cease development of hourlong dramatic television shows, giving up on a business which has been troubled since the mid-1980s. Disney will continue to produce half-hour comedies. Indeed, it has been spending lavishly to attract comedy writer/producers. It also will continue to produce "The Magical World of Disney" for NBC as long as the network wants it. Another new one-hour Disney show for NBC, "Hull High," premiered this year and will stay on the air if ratings permit.

But Disney's drama development department will be eliminated and its key executives reassigned, according to individuals close to the company. The contracts of the handful of writer/producers in the one-hour field who are committed to Disney won't be renewed.

At corporate meetings last week when six-month financial analyses were studied, executives decided that producing dramas was too expensive, given the paltry returns. The situation has existed since 1986, when the bottom fell out of the rerun syndication market for hourlong shows. Studios had been taking big losses in producing one-hour shows because they usually cost well more than $1 million per episode to produce while the networks would pay only $600,000-$900,000 for the right to air them. Producers would hope to make back these "deficits" by selling local stations the right to air reruns, and sometimes it paid off: MCA Inc.'s "Magnum P.I." reaped nearly $2 million per episode.

But stations lost interest. The most lucrative reruns for them are shows they can air weekdays between 4 p.m. and 8 p.m., and viewers seemed to gravitate to lighter fare, such as game shows and comedies, during those hours. An award-winning program like "Hill Street Blues" had little value for station managers, and though producers would try to cobble together a profit from foreign sales and cable deals, few succeeded. The only recent drama show with some success in syndication is Stephen J. Cannell Productions' "Hunter."

But since shows need to air on the networks for five years to have optimum syndication value, suppliers were faced with having to gamble that the market would turn around eventually, and they continued to produce dramas, slashing costs and filming in locations outside Hollywood to try to bring down deficits.

Last week, Disney said "enough already," an executive recounted. CBS had ordered a show called "N.Y.P.D. Mounted," which would have cost Disney about $1.3 million per episode to make; CBS was offering just over $900,000. Disney "refused" the order and the show will be taken over by Orion Pictures Corp. Disney then decided to bail out entirely.

Although some studios have been low-key about producing dramas, like Paramount Communications Inc., Disney is the first to formally abandon the practice. Disney hadn't even had a chance to inform the networks before a report leaked to the Los Angeles Times Saturday. And even though the networks will always need drama shows for their schedules, they aren't expected to complain too loudly because Disney, which didn't have much television presence until recently, is spending so much to become a force in comedy.
