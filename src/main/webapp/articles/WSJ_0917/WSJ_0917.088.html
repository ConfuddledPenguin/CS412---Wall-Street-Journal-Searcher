Prime Motors Inns, Rebuffed by Lenders, Says It May Seek Chapter 11 Protection

  • Author: Peter Pae
  • Date: 09/17/90

Prime Motor Inns Inc., unable to shake off its cash problems, said it may file for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code. The financially strapped operator of hotels said that it was having difficulty obtaining an additional loan from its bank lenders, and that unless the loan and other relief is granted, Prime will "seriously consider filing a petition for reorganization under Chapter 11."

The threat of a Prime bankruptcy-law filing for protection from creditors' lawsuits caught some securities analysts off guard. "The company doesn't even talk to investors anymore," Caroline Levy, an analyst with Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc., said. "It looks very bad."

Prime, based in Fairfield, N.J., said that although it asked for and received from the bank lenders an extension on the maturity date of loans to July 1991 from March 1991 and the authority to retain part of proceeds from asset sales, the banks were "reluctant" to extend any additional loans.

Prime had requested an additional $25 million line of credit, but Morgan Guaranty Trust Co. said it would consider providing the loan only if this could be secured by certain assets. The company, which had $328 million in bank debt outstanding last year, has been able reduce bank debt to $110 million under a debt restructuring program that calls for the selective sale of assets.

Prime said that it will suspend payment of dividends on its common shares and that it doesn't expect to make interest payments on its convertible debentures.

Two related limited partnerships said that if Prime were to seek bankruptcy court protection, they also would be hurt.

Prime Motor Inns Limited Partnership, which indirectly owns 16 Holiday Inn motels leased to a Prime Motor Inns unit, said a filing could result in smaller distributions to its unit holders. And Ramada Assured Income Associates, which is managed by two Prime Motor Inns units, said a filing could result in smaller payout to unit holders.

Prime's latest problems follow a tumultuous decline of what was once one of the nation's largest hotel companies. The company's motor inn business took a huge leap in 1986, when Prime acquired the Howard Johnson motel chain, followed by a further major acquisitions in the late 1980s.

Last Friday, Prime shares closed at $1.375, down 37.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. Volume exceeded 1.6 million shares, making it was the fifth most active issue on the Big Board. The stock, which traded as high as $38 last year, has swooned in recent days; the shares traded as high as $5 on Aug. 22 but fell to slightly more than $2 early last week.

Short-sellers also have put pressure on the stock. As of Aug. 15, bearish investors had sold short about 6.2 million Prime shares. The company has about 33 million shares outstanding. A short-seller sells shares in anticipation of a decline in price, and hopes to secure a profit by repurchasing the shares at a lower price.

Prime has been battered by a decline in its asset value and by lower earnings, which fell to $209,000, or one cent a share, in its third quarter ended March 31, from $18.2 million, or 55 cents a share, a year earlier.

The company last month said it would take a $150 million pretax write-off on certain assets, which would result in a loss for the fiscal fourth quarter and the fiscal year ended June 30.

Prime continued to shrink when it sold in July its Ramada and Howard Johnson franchise system to Blackstone Capital Partners, New York, for $170 million and its Rodeway franchise system to Manor Care Inc., Silver Spring, Md., for $14.9 million. That left Prime with about 136 hotels with 14,000 rooms, down from its heyday of more than 1,100 properties with 150,000 rooms.

The company said it needed the proceeds to reduce bank debt, which had increased to about $350 million as result of Prime's expansion strategy.

The expansion, however, came in a period of tight financing and softness in the hotel and real estate markets. Also, its financial woes were exacerbated by the inability of FCD Hospitality Inc. to pay back its debt to Prime. Last year, FCD borrowed $200 million from Prime to purchase Servico, a hotel operator, from Southmark Corp.

Following Friday's announcement, Moody's Investors Service Inc. said it lowered its ratings on Prime's convertible subordinated debentures to Caa from Ba-3. Another credit rating concern, Standard & Poor's Corp., said it downgraded its rating on Prime's subordinated debt to single-C from single-B-minus. A rating of C indicates that a default on the debt is imminent, S&P said.
