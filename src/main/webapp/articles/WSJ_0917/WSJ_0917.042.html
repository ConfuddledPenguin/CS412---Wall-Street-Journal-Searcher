Ex-Thrift Owner Gets Five Years On Fraud Charge

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 09/17/90

DALLAS -- Former thrift owner Laurence Vineyard Jr. was sentenced to five years in federal prison for defrauding the defunct Vernon Savings Association, Dallas, of $2.6 million, much of which was used to buy a mansion for Mr. Vineyard's family. The purchase of the home tangled thrift regulators' efforts to recoup the Vernon funds because Texas law protects homesteads, regardless of value, from creditors. The law was the subject of a page one article in this newspaper last December.

Mr. Vineyard recently agreed to sell his $1,180,000 home and use the proceeds to begin repaying $17 million he borrowed from Vernon Savings, according to Steven Hoard, an Amarillo, Texas, lawyer who represented the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. in its collection efforts.

Mr. Vineyard had borrowed the money from Vernon to repay earlier loans for his purchase of the now-defunct Key Savings & Loan Association of Englewood, Colo. Mr. Vineyard was convicted in 1987 of fraud charges related to the Key Savings purchase and sentenced to five years in federal prison. He has nearly completed that sentence.

The new five-year prison sentence was handed down last week by U.S. District Judge Joe Fish of Dallas. Mr. Vineyard had pleaded guilty in July to defrauding Vernon by using funds he borrowed from the thrift to purchase his home, pay his lawyers and establish trust funds for his children.

"That wasn't what it was supposed to be used for," Mr. Hoard said. "It was supposed to be used to pay off debt he had incurred in purchasing Key Savings."

Judge Fish ordered Mr. Vineyard to repay the $2.6 million as part of his sentence. Those funds will be added to the funds from the sale of his home and to the money to be returned from the Vineyard children's trust accounts. Mr. Hoard said negotiations are continuing on repayment of the fees to Mr. Vineyard's lawyers.

But Mr. Hoard noted that the chances of Mr. Vineyard's repaying the entire $17 million he borrowed from Vernon are slim. Mr. Vineyard, 41 years old, was stripped of his law license two years ago as a result of the Key Savings conviction and has said he intends to work as an electrician, a trade he learned in federal prison.
