Commodities: Revised Restrictions On Sugar Imports Go Into Effect Oct. 1

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The Agriculture Department said any sugar imported into the U.S. beyond a nationwide total of 1,725,000 metric tons will be subject to higher tariffs during the year starting Oct. 1. At the same time, U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills announced how much sugar each country could send here at the current, relatively low tariff rate.

The twin announcements put into effect a new system of sugar import restrictions. The U.S. is substituting higher tariffs for strict numerical quotas. The change was prompted by an international trade panel ruling last year that the old system of import quotas violated the rules of the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade.

U.S. trade-policy officials said the higher duties on imports exceeding 1,725,000 metric tons effectively would hold imports to that level. That would be up about 6.5% from the level of imports allowed under the old quota system for the year ending Sept. 30.

The tariff on imports exceeding 1,725,000 will be 16 cents per pound. Imports up to that annual limit will be subject to the current duty of 0.625 cents per pound. In addition, imports that come from a large group of developing nations eligible for special tariff preferences under U.S. trade laws will continue to be duty-free.

The country-by-country allocations of sugar import quotas by Mrs. Hills were based on the historical pattern of U.S. sugar imports and were computed on the same basis as previous quota allocations.

As in the past, countries with the largest allocations were the Dominican Republic (292,010 metric tons), the Philippines (262,147 tons), and Brazil (240,578 tons).

U.S. officials said the change to the so-called tariff-rate quota system for sugar imports probably will have little effect on domestic sugar prices, which are about 23 cents per pound, compared with world market prices of about 11 cents per pound.
