Economy: Economy Hurt by Oil Prices, Data Show --- Producer Prices in August Soared 1.3%; Retail Sales Industrial Output Fell

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 09/17/90

WASHINGTON -- Rising oil prices are clearly taking a toll on the economy, boosting inflation and crimping consumer spending, new government figures indicate. Releasing its first data on the nation's inflation, spending and production since Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait, the government said producer prices soared 1.3% in August, while retail sales dropped 0.6% and industrial output slipped 0.2%.

The figures bore out the fears of many analysts that the surge in oil prices brought on by the Persian Gulf turmoil would threaten the already weak economy by pinching Americans' purchasing power and piling a new burden on businesses.

An increasing number of economists now believe a recession in the near future is inescapable. "Look at what happened in August: employment declined, consumer spending declined, industrial production declined," said Donald Ratajczak, director of the economic forecasting project at Georgia State University. "At some point when it looks and smells and acts like a duck, then what is it?"

The jump in the Labor Department's producer price index, which measures the prices factories, farmers and other producers receive for goods, was fueled mostly by energy prices, which catapulted 9.6% during the month. But even excluding energy the index rose a substantial 0.5%, amounting to a 6% annual rate.

Gasoline prices leaped 16.9% while prices of home heating oil soared 38.8%. Crude petroleum prices skyrocketed 62.5%, the largest monthly increase ever recorded. That pushed up an index measuring prices of unfinished goods a startling 9.3% in August, the steepest rise since August 1973, when the government ended a price freeze.

Many economists believe the problems of inflation stemming from the oil crisis are only beginning.

"We know the inflation effect isn't over," said James Annable, chief economist at First National Bank of Chicago, predicting that it will take three or four months before the jump in crude oil prices works its way through to the gas pump, heating bill and other consumer energy costs. "So the rest of the year is going to be dominated by the oil price increase and feeding through to other products."

Already, Mr. Ratajczak noted, makers of petroleum-based chemical products have announced increases in the prices of their products. Nevertheless, he and other analysts predicted that many businesses will have a hard time passing higher energy costs on to their customers, given the weak economy.

Airlines are already finding that out. Many postponed plans to raise fares to cover huge rises in their fuel bills in the face of softening consumer demand.

The drop in retail sales came despite a huge 6.4% rise in the amount of money taken in by gasoline stations during the month. While higher gasoline prices pushed up sales totals at filling stations, business for most other types of merchants declined. Sales of car dealers plummeted 4.3%. Department-store sales slipped 0.2%. Hardware and building-supply stores dropped 2.4%. Restaurant sales fell 0.7%. Sales of furniture stores increased, however, climbing 0.7%.

"American consumers cut back sharply in August as their confidence about economic conditions plunged," said Donald Straszheim, chief economist at Merrill Lynch & Co. in New York.

The drop in industrial production last month reflected small declines in the output of a wide array of industries, including cars, computers and clothing. As a result, the operating rates of factories, mines and utilities fell to 83.1% of capacity in August from 83.5% in July.

The string of reports highlight the difficulty confronting the Federal Reserve. The central bank is under pressure to lower interest rates to jump-start the ailing economy. But officials are wary of allowing rates to fall while inflation remains a problem.

Few analysts expect inflation to spiral out of control as it did following the oil shock of the late 1970s. Global competition is likely to force companies to keep a firm grip on prices and wages, Mr. Annable predicted.

Still, the oil price jolt "probably but not certainly will push the economy over the edge into a mild recession," said Charles Schultze, an economist at the Brookings Institution in Washington who was chairman of the White House Council of Economic Advisers during the Carter administration.

Figures in all three reports were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
