Exemptions Allow Kuwait's Concerns In U.S. to Operate Despite Assets Freeze

  • Author: Bart Ziegler Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/17/90

NEW YORK -- Six weeks after the government froze Kuwait's U.S. assets to prevent Iraq from seizing them, Kuwaiti-owned companies in the U.S. say operations are back to normal thanks to exemptions granted by the Treasury Department. Customers and suppliers of Santa Fe International Corp., in Alhambra, Calif., initially expressed concern about dealing with the Kuwaiti-owned oil drilling company following Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait, said Vice President John Mika.

"They wanted assurance that they were not violating any freeze order by continuing their relationship with us," he said.

But the Treasury "cooperated quite well with us" in granting the exemption, which allows the company to continue writing checks, making payroll and otherwise operate normally, he said.

At Georgetown Industries Inc., a Kuwaiti-owned steel company in Charlotte, N.C., "our business is very much business as usual," said President Roger Regelbrugge. "We have no other restrictions than that we are not supposed to transfer assets to the government of Kuwait or to Iraq," he said.

Hilton Hotels Corp., whose Atlanta Hotel & Tower is owned by the Kuwaiti government, also has had no problems, said a spokeswoman.

Estimates of Kuwait's U.S. investments vary from under $10 billion to more than $50 billion because of the nation's policy of secrecy.

Kuwait's overall foreign assets last year totaled an estimated $124 billion, according to DRI-McGraw-Hill Inc. Much of that is in Europe, and includes such high-profile investments as a 14% stake in Daimler-Benz AG, the German auto, electronics and aerospace giant.

Santa Fe International, which state-owned Kuwait Petroleum Corp. bought for $2.5 billion in 1981, is believed to be the Mideast nation's largest single U.S. holding.

Other known Kuwaiti government assets in the U.S., according to the New York-based newsletter MidEast Report and other sources, include:

-- Occidental Geothermal Inc. of Los Angeles, which Kuwait Petroleum bought for $350 million in 1984.

-- About 12% of Corporate Property Investors, which owns real estate in New York as well as a number of shopping malls nationwide.

-- An undetermined stake in First American Bankshares Inc., a bank-holding company in Washington.

-- 45% of the Phoenecian resort in Scottsdale, Ariz., which was controlled by scandal-plagued Lincoln Savings & Loan Association until the federal government took over Lincoln's share.

-- 45% of the Crescent hotel in Phoenix.

In addition, private Kuwaiti investors -- not subject to the asset freeze -- hold an undetermined stake in Investcorp, which owns Saks Fifth Avenue department stores and other properties.

MidEast Report estimates Kuwait's U.S. assets at $50 billion.

Not even the Treasury Department, charged with enforcing the asset freeze, has a complete accounting of Kuwait's U.S. investments. The department began a list after Iraq's invasion, but had not finished it as of last Friday, a spokeswoman said.

The Treasury wouldn't disclose which companies have been granted exemptions. The spokeswoman said the exemptions were designed "to allow the Kuwati government to conduct its normal business in the smoothest way possible while assuring that no benefits accrue to Iraq."

The department also has issued general exemptions for categories of companies, such as telephone and foreign exchange companies that meet certain conditions.

According to one measure by the Commerce Department, Kuwait's direct investments in U.S. companies totaled $4.2 billion last year. That figure doesn't account for investments made through intermediaries such as Kuwaiti companies not based in Kuwait.

Nor does it include investments in companies that result in less than 10% of the voting stock, nor Kuwait's "portfolio" accounts of stocks, bonds and other investments being managed by U.S. investment houses.

David T. Mizrahi, editor of MidEast Report, estimates 25% of Kuwait's U.S. assets are held in portfolio accounts at firms including J.P. Morgan & Co., Morgan Stanley & Co. Inc. and Citicorp.

By a broader measure last taken in 1988, the value of all U.S. companies in which Kuwait held at least 10% control totaled $10 billion.

But since that Commerce Department figure includes the full value of those companies no matter what the size of Kuwait's stake, it most likely overstates Kuwait's investment.

Former Treasury Secretary G. William Miller helps manage Kuwait's U.S. assets, MidEast Report says. Among Kuwait's U.S. investment groups is the Wafra Investment Advisory Group of New York, which invests Kuwaiti pension funds.

Much of Kuwait's assets world-wide are managed by the Kuwaiti Investment Office in London.
