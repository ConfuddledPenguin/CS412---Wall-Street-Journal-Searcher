RTC, Ignoring Federal Judge's Ruling, Will Declare Kansas Thrift Insolvent

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 09/17/90

The Resolution Trust Corp., ignoring a federal judge's findings, said it will recognize new losses at Franklin Savings Association and declare the Kansas thrift insolvent with negative capital of $8 million. The new writedowns are contained in a financial statement to be filed today with the Office of Thrift Supervision in Topeka, Kan., and may spark another round of litigation between Franklin's owners and thrift regulators.

A new court fight, coming on the heels of a judge's ruling that Franklin properly posted positive capital, could also involve the accounting firm of Price Waterhouse, whose audit forms the basis of the financial statement to be filed by the RTC.

Franklin officials have been questioning the impartiality of Price Waterhouse since obtaining internal OTS memoranda about the retention of the accounting firm in the course of its lawsuit challenging the OTS's seizure of the thrift. "PW {Price Waterhouse} is best qualified to do the audit and shares our views on the hedge accounting issue," an OTS official said in one memorandum urging the RTC to retain the accounting firm. "It is in everyone's best interest to have PW do the audit."

Franklin was placed in conservatorship by the OTS and its management duties turned over to the RTC last Feb. 16 after the thrift refused to immediately recognize about $119 million in losses on futures contracts. Those contracts were held as a hedge against losses in the $10.26 billion of mortgage-backed securities that dominated Franklin's portfolio of assets.

Price Waterhouse couldn't be reached for comment after the RTC released its preliminary findings late Friday. An RTC spokesman said the agency conducted a review of Franklin's financial status independent of the accounting firm.

In its lawsuit against the OTS, Franklin's management successfully contended that the agency had applied different accounting methods to Franklin's unorthodox business practices than the ones that had previously been approved by thrift regulators. Franklin's methods, endorsed by its accounting firm, Deloitte Touche, allowed the thrift to delay recognition of certain losses on its hedging positions, among other things.

Earlier this month, U.S. District Judge Dale E. Saffels of Topeka ruled that the OTS had been "arbitrary and capricious" in its decision to place Franklin in conservatorship.

Judge Saffels's order was temporarily stayed by an appeals court in Denver the next day, putting the RTC back in control of Franklin. The OTS has appealed Judge Saffels's decision.

The RTC said the financial statement to be filed today reflects the RTC's assessment of Franklin's condition for the fiscal year ending June 30. While not nearly as devastating as the OTS's declaration that Franklin's capital stood at negative $44.3 million in mid-February, the RTC statement is nonetheless disputed by Franklin's chairman, Ernest M. Fleischer.

"For RTC to publish a statement that reflects a negative {capital} is either a misapplication of accounting methodology or gross mismanagement by the RTC or a combination of both," Mr. Fleischer said.

The RTC found, among other things, that Franklin should have recorded an additional $196 million in hedging losses for the year ended June 30, 1989.

The RTC also said Franklin should recognize $50 million in losses from six bond issues for which the RTC stopped paying interest on June 8. Some bondholders have challenged the RTC in court, and the agency said a successful challenge will result in additional losses of $142 million.
