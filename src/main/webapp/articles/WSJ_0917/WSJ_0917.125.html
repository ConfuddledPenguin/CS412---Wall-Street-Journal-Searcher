Law -- Legal Beat: Court Rules Company Lawyers Can Sue Over Retaliatory Firings

  • Author: Ellen Joan Pollock and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 09/17/90

An appeals court in Illinois has ruled that in-house lawyers can sue companies that have fired them in retaliation for giving unwanted advice. The ruling, by the Appellate Court of Illinois, is believed to be among the first to give staff lawyers the right to sue employers for retaliatory discharge. The case had been closely watched by in-house lawyers and legal ethics experts because courts in Illinois and Texas had ruled that in-house lawyers could not sue employers.

The case involves Roger J. Balla, an in-house lawyer fired in 1985 by Gambro Inc., a distributor of kidney dialysis equipment. Before he was fired, Mr. Balla had told the company that a shipment of dialyzers was "misbranded and/or adulterated," according to the court opinion.

A lower court ruled that Mr. Balla could not pursue his retaliatory discharge claim against Gambro, now based in Lakewood, Colo., its German parent and Swedish affiliate. But the appeals court said that Mr. Balla could pursue his claim, even though other courts had refused to allow in-house attorneys to sue on such grounds "fearing that the attorneys would breach their fiduciary duty by disclosing their client's confidential information."

"The sanctity of the attorney-client privilege is not an absolute bar to disclosure," the court ruled, when "public policies" or "serious bodily harm" are at stake.

Mr. Balla's suit against Gambro will be heard by a lower court.

Arthur Sternberg, the Chicago lawyer who represents Gambro, said his client will seek to appeal. He said the decision makes in-house lawyers "second-class citizens" because it will encourage companies to limit their "use of in-house attorneys to more mundane types of tasks rather than putting them in sensitive situations." He said the dialyzers were "not defective in any way."

Alan O. Amos, the Chicago lawyer for Mr. Balla, said the decision shows that in-house lawyers have "the same rights that any other employee would have under the same circumstances." He said the decision will discourage companies from hiding behind the attorney-client privilege.

The American Corporate Counsel Association (ACCA), a trade group, has taken the position that in-house lawyers should not be able to sue their employers, according to board member Lawrence A. Salibra. But ACCA will consider the issue again at its annual meeting in November, said Mr. Salibra, senior counsel at Alcan Aluminium Corp.

SEC DROPS BID for spot on Drexel creditors' committee.

Instead, the Securities and Exchange Commission has aligned itself with a court-sanctioned group of securities-litigation claimants suing Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc.

The SEC moved to join the creditors' committee soon after Drexel filed for bankruptcy-law protection on Feb. 13. The SEC is owed $150 million as part of Drexel's settlement of charges against the company last year. Although that claim makes the SEC one of Drexel's largest creditors, the motion sparked controversy because of a dispute over whether bankruptcy law allowed a regulatory agency to serve in that role.

Now, however, the SEC says its interests are better represented through the newly formed securities litigation claimants group. The group will negotiate with Drexel and the creditors' committee over how to provide for litigation claims in the company's bankruptcy reorganization plan. More than 400 suits are pending against Drexel.

The group includes the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., which is expected to file a huge claim against Drexel stemming from the failure of several savings and loans. The group also represents investors who have filed class-action suits against Drexel and Michael Milken, the company's former junk-bond chief. The group, which isn't an official bankruptcy committee, was approved in a court order by U.S. Bankruptcy Judge Howard Buschman III last month.

Richard Kirby, the SEC's senior litigation counsel, said the agency joined the litigation group because of the importance of addressing the eventual claims that Drexel will have to pay. The SEC's $150 million claim is meant to be distributed to investors who were hurt by alleged wrongdoing by Drexel. In a reorganization plan for Drexel, the money could be combined with other payments by Drexel to set up a fund for litigation claimants.

"This case can't progress toward a feasible reorganization plan" until an estimate is reached on the litigation claims, Mr. Kirby said.

In court filings, Drexel and the creditors' committee agreed to negotiate with the litigation group to develop a process for estimating the claims. They also said they will include the group in negotiating a reorganization proposal.

CRAVATH HAS LOST General Development Corp. as a client.

Cravath Swaine & Moore, a New York law firm, and the troubled Miami real-estate developer said the parting of ways was mutual and had been planned since the spring. In the 13 months ended April 30, the company paid Cravath $4.8 million, the law firm said.

General Development was indicted in March on federal felony charges of defrauding thousands of home buyers and was subsequently placed in Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law proceedings. Some critics, including Miami Beach attorney Paul Steinberg, who represents certain General Development shareholders, assert that Cravath ought to be held liable for advising General Development that its marketing practices were legal and sound.

Meanwhile, two General Development directors said they will call for an independent review of Cravath's legal representation to assess whether the law firm acted responsibly. The directors -- Chairman Charles J. Simons and Chesterfield Smith -- said they are not alleging impropriety, but rather feel an independent investigation is a sound fiduciary move in light of criticism of Cravath.

Ronald Rolfe, a Cravath partner, said "We think Cravath has no liability or potential liability at all." Paul Dodyk, who was Cravath's lead counsel for the company, declined to comment.

Mr. Smith, whose Miami law firm, Holland & Knight, is slated to succeed Cravath as lead counsel, said "it is in the interest of all concerned to ascertain if the allegations are substantial or insubstantial." The company's board hasn't yet formally considered an independent inquiry, which would review the roles of all involved parties -- not just Cravath, Mr. Simons said.

General Development had a close relationship with Cravath; its former chairman, David F. Brown, who is facing federal fraud charges, was a former associate at the firm and then became general counsel to City Investing, a Cravath client, before going to General Development.

Cravath will continue representing the company in three matters, it said.

STATE JUDGE REJECTS ethics charges against a chemical firm's lawyers.

Travelers Indemnity Co. of Hartford had alleged that lawyers for Stauffer Chemical Co. tried to prevent potential witnesses from testifying in a suit concerning who should pay for a cleanup of toxic waste.

The company named in the suit is now a unit of ICI Americas Inc., of Wilmington, Del., and is called Stauffer Management Co. A company currently called Stauffer Chemical Co. is not involved.

The Travelers Corp. unit, a plaintiff, alleged Stauffer lawyers violated ethics rules when they sent letters urging former employees to call Stauffer lawyers at the Wilmington law firm of Morris, Nichols, Arsht & Tunnell, the Washington law firm of Kirkpatrick & Lockhart, or the Philadelphia law firm of Morgan, Lewis & Bockius if they were contacted by the insurance companies.

In a ruling last week, Judge Vincent J. Poppiti of Wilmington wrote that Travelers failed to prove that the letters prevented the insurer from obtaining information that would otherwise be available. He said he would refer the matter to counsel for a lawyers' disciplinary board. Stauffer's lawyers declined to comment.

In a separate order, Judge Poppiti wrote that Jeffrey A. Januzzo and Pamela G. Ostrager, lawyers at the New York firm of Coudert Brothers, which is representing Travelers, had misled him about the practices of investigators working on behalf of the insurer. Stauffer had alleged that the investigators failed to properly identify themselves to former employees. The judge terminated the lawyers' right to appear before the court.

The Coudert lawyers declined to comment. In a prepared statement, Mark Lebow, head of Coudert's litigation department, said, "Two of the most respected ethics experts in this country, Professor Stephen Gillers of New York University and Professor Geoffrey Hazard of Yale University, filed affidavits with the Delaware court attesting that the conduct of the Coudert Brothers lawyers was ethical." A Travelers spokesperson said they would appeal.

Judge Poppiti also ordered Travelers to turn over to Stauffer any statements obtained from former Stauffer employees and said they would not be admissible at the trial, set for February 1991.

PENNY-STOCK INVESTIGATION NETS three more F.D. Roberts officers.

Three former owners of F.D. Roberts Securities Inc. last week pleaded guilty to federal felony charges in connection with their roles in a high-pressure penny-stock scam.

The U.S. Attorney's office in Newark obtained guilty pleas from Fred Galiardo, former F.D. Roberts board chairman; Alan Lieb, former vice president in charge of trading and a director; and John Perfetti, a former vice president in charge of trading and a director. The three are among the 25 F.D. Roberts brokers and officers who have either pleaded guilty or been convicted on charges stemming from the two-year federal investigation.

The three pleaded guilty to federal racketeering, securities, mail fraud and wire fraud charges. Mr. Galiardo and Mr. Lieb face maximums of 20 years in prison and fines equal to twice their gain or their victims' loss and restitution. Mr. Perfetti faces 10 years in prison and a maximum of $100 million in fines.

James Galdieri, Mr. Lieb's attorney, said that his client "accepts full responsibility for his wrong act." A lawyer for Mr. Perfetti declined to comment. Mr. Galiardo's lawyer could not be reached.

CRIME BILL: The U.S. Senate passed an amendment sponsored by U.S. Senator Arlen Specter (R., Pa.) and U.S. Senator Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.) that would require all post-secondary education institutions that receive federal aid to publish annual security reports that include the number of violent crimes committed against students. The report would be distributed to students and university employees. Prospective applicants could receive the information upon request. The drive to improve campus security has been spearheaded by Connie and Howard Clery, a Bryn Mawr, Pa., couple whose daughter was murdered by a fellow student at Lehigh University in Bethlehem, Pa.
