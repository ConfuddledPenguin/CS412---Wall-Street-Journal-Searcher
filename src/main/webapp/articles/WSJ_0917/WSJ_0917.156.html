Commodities: U.S. Pays Record Export Subsidies to Bolster Grain Sales to Counter European Competition

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 09/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The government has begun paying record export subsidies to boost U.S. grain sales overseas, but most analysts say it still won't be enough to shore up sagging wheat markets. The Agriculture Department is going to unprecedented lengths to counter the competitive challenge of the European Community at a time of bumper grain crops, swelling stockpiles and shrinking import demand. For the first time, so-called export bonuses under the government's Export Enhancement Program topped $50 a ton last week as Agriculture Department officials sought to match lavish export subsidies being offered by the French and other European exporters -- and to protect U.S. market share.

"You're looking at record levels," said one official. "I think we're well into new territory."

The department, partly in response to political pressure from the Wheat Belt to do whatever it can to prop up wheat prices, subsidized the sale of more than 500,000 tons of wheat last week. Export bonuses on a 100,000-ton sale of hard red winter wheat to Tunisia averaged a whopping $49.23 a ton, while bonuses on a 12,000-ton wheat sale to West African nations averaged a record $54.01 a ton.

Under the Export Enhancement Program, exporters sell U.S. wheat and other commodities at the world-market price and collect bonuses in the form of surplus, government-owned commodities. The bonuses make up the difference between higher U.S. prices and the world-market prices.

But several commodity analysts said such bursts of small- and medium-sized sales will give the depressed futures markets only a short-term psychological boost. Some faulted the Agriculture Department for not making aggressive use of export bonuses sooner.

"They were very slow in waking up to the fact that it's a buyer's market," said Katharina Zimmer, a Merrill Lynch analyst in New York. "They're now doing what they should have done long ago."

Until recently, added Anne Frick, a Prudential-Bache Securities analyst in New York, the bonuses "haven't been large enough to compete with the EC."

The gloomy consensus is that it will take a big sale to shore up the wheat market, and none appears to be imminent.

"If we did make a relatively large sale -- one million or two million tons -- that would trigger a rally of 20 cents a bushel," said Ms. Zimmer. "The price would fall back some, but it would put a floor under the market."

This fall, the Soviet Union is expected to resume buying grain in large quantities. The Soviets have expressed interest in obtaining $2 billion in U.S. credit, and the Bush administration has indicated a willingness to extend export-credit guarantees. Technically, the Soviet Union is eligible for credit, but the administration has taken the political stance that Moscow must liberalize Soviet immigration law to qualify for most-favored-nation status.

Most analysts believe credit won't be forthcoming for at least one month, and there isn't any guarantee the Soviets will use it for wheat purchases. Ms. Zimmer said, "They're already looking at a lovely wheat crop." Instead, they may be more interested in buying corn and soybean meal as well as certain processed foods that would help mollify disgruntled consumers.

Lately, the Soviets "haven't been buying anything except ready-to-eat items that they need to avoid revolution or anarchy," said John Schnittker, a Washington-based agricultural consultant. Two such items: chicken legs and cigarettes.

The prospect of bumper crops throughout the Northern Hemisphere and the loss of the Iraqi market due to the Persian Gulf crisis have battered wheat prices. But department officials take sharp issue with criticism that they have "used the EEP program very meekly."

"We have had {export} initiatives out there," said a senior official. "It's more a factor of good crops and lower buying." He said he expects the latest batch of subsidized sales to be "very beneficial to the export picture at a time when there hasn't been all that much export activity."

Meanwhile, a farm-policy showdown is shaping up between the Bush administration and lawmakers from grain-growing states. Rep. Dan Glickman, chairman of a House Agriculture subcommittee, intends to hold hearings, beginning today, on the possibility of using export bonuses to boost the sale of wheat flour and other processed foods, and on how much land wheat farmers should take out of production in 1991.

The Senate has adopted a bill requiring farmers to idle 15% of their wheat acreage. Mr. Glickman (D., Kan.) has proposed a similar measure, but it would give farmers the option of collecting payments for idling an additional 5%. The administration has consistently opposed acreage reductions of this size, arguing that they encourage farmers overseas to plant more.

Prudential-Bache's Ms. Frick, said that a 15% reduction, if enacted, "would give the market a slight short-term boost; it wouldn't fix the problem."

Looking ahead to other commodity markets this week:

Precious Metals

Gold prices firmed slightly Friday on heightened Persian Gulf tensions; the nervousness is expected to continue this week.

Traders bought contracts after it was reported that Iraqi soldiers had broken into a number of foreign embassies in Kuwait City and that a U.S. naval vessel fired a warning shot before boarding an Iraqi ship.

December gold rose only $2.20 an ounce to $391; while silver and platinum declined. The lack of significant response to the Middle Eastern developments was due to traders' ambivalence, one analyst said.

"There have been a number of weekends when traders have gone away expecting that fighting would break out in the Persian Gulf and it hasn't," he said. "This has made for Monday price declines."

On the other hand, he noted, the situation is explosive and any developments are likely to boost prices rather than weaken them. That's why a moderate demand for gold futures contracts developed, he said. A major factor hampering a strong rally, he added, is the lack of demand for gold bullion.

Energy

Headline-driven oil markets got a flurry of new headlines over the weekend as war in the Persian Gulf seemed more imminent than ever. The United Nations security council unanimously condemned Iraq's raids against French, Canadian, Dutch, and Belgian embassy compounds in Kuwait. President Bush appeared on Iraqi television to say that country stands "on the brink of war" because of its Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait.

In response, the Iraqis called the U.S. leader a liar and vowed to fight him and his allies, many of whom increased military and financial support of the U.S. stand against Iraq following the embassy assaults. Secretary of State James Baker returned to Washington after raising some $14 billion for use in the Middle East crisis.

The heightened Gulf tensions will doubtlessly continue to push crude oil futures skyward, as they have for the past six weeks. Trading early in the week should reveal whether a recent slackening in petroleum product futures prices is continuing. Some traders had attributed the weakness to technical factors, but others took note of recent reports from the U.S. Energy Department and the American Petroleum Institute that showed increases in petroleum product inventories. The weekly report from the API, due tomorrow, will be scrutinized for more evidence that product stocks are growing.

Livestock and Meats

A surge in hog and pork belly futures prices last week makes analysts optimistic about pork's prospects this week, with strong cash prices for pork likely to continue to support futures prices. Analysts said cash prices rose because of competitive bidding among meatpackers for supplies for slaughter. A larger-than-expected movement of pork bellies out of cold storage also exerted upward pressure on prices.

Also affecting the market last week were rumblings that the U.S. may be planning a food aid package to the Soviet Union, and analysts said such speculation may remain in the market this week. "Traders are trying to make something out of the meeting between President Bush and {Soviet President Mikhail} Gorbachev," one analyst said. "Personally, I think they had other things to talk about than food aid."
