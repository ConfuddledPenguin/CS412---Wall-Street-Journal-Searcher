Abreast of the Market: Stock Market Is Dancing to Oil's Tune --- Some Strategists Suggest Ways to Take Advantage Of Current Volatility

  • Author: Christopher Winans and Craig Torres
  • Date: 09/17/90

Stock investors only have eyes for oil. Ever since Saddam Hussein lit a fuse under the Middle East tinderbox on Aug. 2, the oil market has been shoving stocks around. Mainly down, as was the case again Friday: The Dow Jones Industrial Average sank 18.56 points to 2564.11, while the October crude oil futures contract rose 69 cents to $31.76 a barrel.

Since the invasion of Kuwait six weeks ago, the industrial average has moved counter to oil prices on 22 of 30 trading days. Of those moves, the industrial average fell -- and oil rose -- more than half the time, or 14 days. On the days when the predominant pattern didn't hold, changes in both the industrial average and oil prices were mostly small.

In a sense, oil simply has become the latest obsession of stock investors, who love to seize upon an economic indicator or some other windsock to point the direction they should run in. Over the past year, the stock market has on occasion been dominated for days and even weeks by bond yields, the monthly employment report and inflation indexes, among other things.

"Wall Street tries to make things very simple," says Al Goldman, director of market analysis at A.G. Edwards. "It's a one-note street. But if one makes the mistake of following that one note, you miss many critical factors. Admittedly, as with oil, the myopic view often works -- but only on a very short-term basis."

But to credit the market monomaniacs with certain good judgment, oil prices dovetail closely with the major issues on investors' minds: inflation, economic growth or recession, and corporate profits.

"Inflation is the single most negative variable for financial instruments," says A.C. Moore, research director at Argus Research, "and the price of oil is the single most important element. It affects pricing for everything. Secondly, oil-price increases translate into diminished demand throughout the economy. There's lots of justification for being fixated on oil. It's not a phantom worry."

Trouble is, when the obsession of the month is expressed in a price, such as oil, the stock market tends to be yanked around by those investors who are most sensitive to price -- namely, speculators. The impact of rising oil prices is felt first in that part of the stock market that is most like a commodity-stock-index futures.

With buy and sell programs, jumps in energy prices are telegraphed to the New York Stock Exchange through the Chicago futures markets. "Nobody is going to pull or make a bid based on the price of oil; but it is affecting the {futures} market, which causes programs" in stocks, says Michael LaBranche IV, managing partner at a Big Board specialist company, LaBranche & Co.

Some advisers suggest investors try to take advantage of the volatility. "When good companies get sold with the bad, an investor can use that as a chance to buy or add to a position," says Mr. Moore of Argus. He likes energy stocks and shares of growth companies with large interests overseas, such as the 48% of revenue and profit that Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing gets from foreign operations.

Joseph J. McAlinden, director of equity research at Dillon Read, also recommends playing the market's daily ups and downs in pursuit of a longer-term strategy. He thinks inflation is going to reaccelerate significantly, partly because of oil prices but also because he predicts a much looser monetary policy in the months ahead.

This scenario is bad for the bond market and a mixed bag for the stock market, he says. Companies that do well in inflationary periods will see their stocks rise, and vice versa. "Overall," he asserts, "the stock market will get pulled higher over the next three to six months," led by energy, basic industries such as metals and stocks of companies that benefit from increased capital spending.

Specifically, Mr. McAlinden likes Oryx Energy, a Dallas company with significant oil interests in the North Atlantic, and Burlington Resources, a natural-gas play. Capital-spending stocks he favors: CBI Industries, an Oak Brook, Ill., company that builds pressurized tanks for gas and oil, and Motorola, which is big in semiconductors and cellular telephones. Among natural-resource concerns, he recommends Amax, a metals producer.

If you believe in these plays, he says, buy them on days when inflation fears subside; and sit on your hands on days when oil prices -- and inflation fears -- rise.

Ellen Harris, chief domestic investment officer of PaineWebber mutual funds, suggests investors look at stocks whose ability to earn fatter profits aren't vulnerable to oil shocks. A favorite is Waste Management, and biotechnology stocks such as Chiron and Genetics Institute. "Their growth isn't affected by the events going on" in the Middle East, she says.

Fred Moran, president of Moran Asset Management, thinks investors should insulate themselves against recession in the U.S. as well as against the oil shock. He is avoiding cyclical groups such as airlines, autos and retailers and buying stocks of companies that generate sales no matter how slow the economy, such as cellular telephone and cable television concerns.

"When there was a recession in Dallas and Houston, cellular prospered as well there as anywhere in the country," he says. "Telecommunications grows even in a recession, or if oil prices are high." Among his favorites are McCaw Cellular, Associated Communications and Jones Intercable.

Friday's Market Activity

Evidence of growing tensions in the Persian Gulf and sluggishness in the U.S. economy weighed down the stock market and prices closed broadly lower.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped 18.56 to 2564.11, with 1,008 issues declining and 472 advancing.

Among broader market indexes, Standard & Poor's 500-stock index dropped 1.82 to 316.83, the New York Stock Exchange composite index slipped 1.01 to 174.15 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index fell 1.78 to 293.61.

Some 133,390,000 shares traded on the New York Stock Exchange -- the highest total since Aug. 27 -- but the pattern of lackluster activity since the end of last month remained intact as many institutions stayed out of the market.

For the week, the Dow Jones Industrial Average posted a decline of 55.44 points, or 2.1%. The S&P 500 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index each dropped 2%, while the Big Board's composite index fell 1.9%.

Prices headed lower from the opening bell as the market responded to the report on August producer prices, which rose sharply as expected, and other data on the economy's performance last month that proved mildly disappointing.

The producer price index jumped 1.3% last month, well within market forecasts, as a result of the steep increase in energy prices following Iraqi's invasion of Kuwait. But despite sharply higher sales at gasoline stations, retail sales fell 0.6% in August; additionally, industrial production fell 0.2% and capacity utilization dropped to 83.1% in August from 83.5% in July.

A sell-off in Digital Equipment gained momentum, with the stock falling to its lowest level in about five years. Digital, the most active Big Board issue, dropped 2 7/8 to 56 3/8 on trading of 2.2 million shares. Digital traded as low as 54 3/8.

The latest round of selling took place after Cowen & Co. slashed its estimate of the company's fiscal 1991 earnings by $2.85 a share, to $4.15. Analyst Richard S. Chu told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report the sharp revision reflects increasing concerns about the economic outlook and about Digital's current midrange computer line. Several analysts had lowered earnings estimates earlier last week as the company made downbeat presentations at two investment conferences.

LSI Logic tumbled 1 to 6 7/8 after Prudential-Bache Securities cut its earnings estimates for this year and next by 10 cents a share to 50 cents and $1.20, respectively. But a number of other technology issues bucked the market's slide; Compaq Computer gained 7/8 to 44 1/8, Cray Research went up 1/4 to 35 3/8, Texas Instruments climbed 1 to 28 1/4 and Motorola rose 1 5/8 to 63.

Deere fell 2 3/8 to 51 1/8 and Tenneco dropped 1 7/8 to 52 1/8. Oppenheimer & Co. cut 1991 earnings estimates on the two concerns and removed the stocks from its list of recommended issues. Additionally, Wertheim Schroder lowered its forecast of Deere's 1991 earnings from $6.35 a share to a range of $5 to $6.35.

Westinghouse Electric fell 1 5/8 to 29 7/8 on 1.1 million shares. PaineWebber lowered the stock's investment rating from "attractive" to "neutral" and cut its 1991 earnings estimate by 10 cents a share, to $3.60, to reflect the company's new forecast of a recession beginning later this year.

Energy stocks closed mixed as oil prices reacted to the Mideast developments. Exxon gained 5/8 to 51 3/8, Chevron fell 1/8 to 75 7/8, Texaco rose 1/8 to 65 1/2, Mobil eased 1/8 to 65 1/2 and Atlantic Richfield added 1 1/8 to 139 7/8.

The precious metals sector, benefiting from higher gold prices, fared even better.

Battle Mountain Gold added 3/8 to 10 5/8, Placer Dome gained 1/2 to 17 1/8, Homestake Mining climbed 3/8 to 19 7/8 and American Barrick Resources rose 1/2 to 21 3/8.

Chemical Banking fell 5/8 to 20 1/4 as 1.7 million shares changed hands. Speculation has surfaced recently that the company may be about to close its operations in Switzerland; Chemical has declined to comment on those rumors.

Prime Motor Inns declined 3/8 to 1 3/8 on 1.7 million shares. The company said it asked its bank lenders to restructure its debt and to provide an additional $25 million in loans, adding that it could file for Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection if an agreement can't be reached. Prime Motor Inns L.P., from which the company leases several hotels, fell 7/8 to 3 1/4.

Olin dropped 2 7/8 to 37 1/8. The company said it expects to post earnings of about $5 a share this year, below both analysts' estimates and the company's previous forecasts.

Manpower rose 5/8 to 11 1/8. The company agreed to sell five British-based units to Brook Opportunities, a new company, for about $200 million and will also receive a 15% stake in Brook.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index dropped 0.56 to 321.45, bringing its loss for the week to 0.7%.

Energy Service, the most active Amex issue, rose 1/8 to 3 5/8. The company, together with R.D. Smith & Co., agreed to sell 33 oil drilling rigs belonging to Penrod Corp. for $14.9 million to Diamond M Holding.

Ackerley Communications rose 1/2 to 3 1/4. Business Week magazine reported that one money manager, Starmont Capital's Michael Seyfert, is estimating the company's net asset value at $15 a share, while portfolio manager Harvey Sandler puts the figure at more than $20 a share.
