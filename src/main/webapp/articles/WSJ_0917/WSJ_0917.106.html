N.V. Philips, Seeking to Improve Profits, Is Expected to Quit AT&T Joint Venture

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 09/17/90

NEW YORK -- Dutch electronics giant N.V. Philips, under pressure to boost its sagging financial performance, plans to leave its joint venture with American Telephone & Telegraph Co., according to people close to the venture. An AT&T spokesman said reports last week of Philips's pullout in the Dutch press "are pure speculation at this point." But sources close to the Hilversum, Netherlands-based venture, AT&T Network Systems International, said Philips will announce that it is selling its 15% stake in the company within the next two weeks.

In Eindhoven, the Netherlands, a Philips spokesman declined to comment.

Meanwhile, Telefonica de Espana S.A. , Spain's national telecommunications company, has announced plans to take a 6% stake in the venture, which builds telephone exchanges and transmission gear for public telephone companies.

Telefonica will pay for its stake by swapping the 20% interest it now holds in another AT&T venture, AT&T Microelectronica Espana, which designs and builds electronic devices.

AT&T currently owns 59% of AT&T Network Systems International while Italy's Stet Group owns 20%. After the Philips pullout, AT&T will own 74% of the company, although it is expected to continue to seek European partners.

AT&T Network Systems International is AT&T's primary overseas equipment arm. It employs more than 5,000 people and develops, manufactures and markets the 5ESS digital switch and assorted transmission gear to foreign phone companies. Sales in 1989 rose 10% to $600 million while profit jumped 58% to $15 million.

"Philips's pullout wouldn't be surprising," said Charles Nichols II, telecommunications analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "AT&T had hoped that Philips's presence in electronics would give it an entre into new markets, but Philips didn't have a big telecommunications presence in Europe, so AT&T had to go it alone. Plus, Philips is a financial disaster right now," he added.

Mr. Nichols said AT&T will probably pay "$60 million to $90 million max," or no more than one times revenue, for Philips's remaining 15% stake. He said AT&T remains well-positioned against European competitors since Italy and Spain, its major partners, are also two of Europe's fastest-growing telecommunications markets, with combined plans to spend more than $30 billion over the next five years to modernize their phone networks.

Philips is narrowing its focus to improve its performance. Because of heavy restructuring costs, the Dutch company expects a loss of about $1.1 billion for 1990.

The sale of its stake in the joint venture wouldn't involve Philips's other telecommunications operations, which include making office switchboards, mobile phone equipment and modems. But some analysts believe Philips eventually may sell its entire telecommunications business, which employs about 15,000 people.

The partners, in any case, had drifted apart with AT&T having taken over most of the management of the venture more than two years ago. Philips cut its stake to 40% from 50% in 1987 and last year cut it again to 15%, saying it wanted to focus future investment on its core businesses.

The venture, originally called AT&T-Philips Telecommunications, was announced with great fanfare in 1983. But Western European politicians quickly warned that the linkup between one of Europe's biggest electronics companies and mighty AT&T would have a disastrous impact on competitors.

However, although the joint venture has proved moderately successful, competitors such as France's Alcatel N.V., Sweden's Telefon AB L.M. Ericsson and West Germany's Siemens AG have more than held their own.

On Friday, AT&T Chairman Robert E. Allen and Telefonica Chairman Candido Velazquez opened a new AT&T Network Systems plant to build 5ESS switches, in Tres Cantos, Spain, about 10 miles north of Madrid.

An AT&T spokesman said it "will take the plant about a year to get up to speed," by which time it is expected to employ about 900 people.
