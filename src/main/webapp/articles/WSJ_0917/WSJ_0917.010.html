The Real Capital-Gains Rate -- 75%

  • Author: David Goldman
  • Date: 09/17/90

The U.S. economy cannot long survive the present capital-gains tax environment. If you purchased a stock portfolio in 1975 and sold it today, the effective capital gains tax rate on the transaction would be about 75%. The 28% tax rate applies both to your real gain, measured in constant 1975 dollars, as well as to the inflation built into the present price of your portfolio. After paying taxes on both the real and inflationary gains, you are left with only a quarter of your capital gain, measured in the 1975 dollars with which you bought the portfolio. The 28% nominal capital-gains rate, in fact, is the highest in history, as the graph below indicates (see accompany illustration -- WSJ Sept. 17, 1990). But the real rate, taking into account past inflation, has risen much more, to about 75% now from only 23% in 1965. Of course, different holding periods for the same portfolio involve different inflation penalties.

Most of the nation's capital stock represents mature investments made during the 1970s or early 1980s. That is, risk investors who bought securities or formed businesses in such growth areas as computers or cable television now hold mature investments that have the bulk of their capital gains behind them. Normally, risk investors would sell such portfolios to income investors and seek new risk investments. The effective 75% capital-gains rate on an investment held for 15 years, though, immobilizes their capital, and reduces the relative price of risk investments. That is why the Dow Jones Industrial Average, representing the stocks of mature companies, has outperformed all other indices.

It is important to view the 1990 stock market in the context of recent economic history. Between 1954 and 1969, the rate of increase of stock prices was higher than inflation, i.e., investors had real capital gains. From 1969 to 1982, when the Reagan tax cuts began to take effect, the inflation rate exceeded the appreciation of stock prices, i.e., investors had real capital losses. Between 1982 and 1987, they enjoyed real capital gains once again. Investors who held out through the bad years now cannot afford to make their capital liquid. That is, the economy cannot turn the benefits of the good years into a new generation of investments.

These numbers make clear why both a lower basic rate and inflation indexing of capital-gains taxes are essential. Indexation is needed to break the logjam of mature investments, providing an incentive for risk investors to cash in their capital gains and take new risks.

High capital-gains tax rates make innovative companies less attractive to investors, with the result that technology stocks filled 35% of institutional portfolios in 1989 as against 47% in 1987. Similarly, venture-capital-backed stock offerings have disappeared since 1987.

A lower tax rate on capital gains is required to restore the attractiveness of new risk investments. The recession in the Northeast stems from the decline of real estate, high tech and the securities markets, i.e., the sectors most sensitive to the high capital gains rate. The deficits at state capitals and the bill for the S&L bailout would fall drastically if the capital-gains rate were cut to a tolerable level.

But the true significance of a capital-gains tax cut is not that it would avert recession in the short term -- although it would -- but that it would enable this country to unbottle the benefits of the Reagan years and mobilize capital for a new generation of investment.

Economic growth is the result not of investing "money," but effort and skill. At the margin, higher productivity comes from better skills, that is, technological innovation. The capital-gains tax as it now stands crushes innovation at both ends, by destroying the incentive to make new risk investments while imposing a prohibitive cost on cashing in mature investments.

Mr. Goldman is an economist at Polyconomics, in Morristown, N.J.
