Sears Launches New Steps in Bid To Reduce Costs

  • Author: Francine Schwadel
  • Date: 09/17/90

CHICAGO -- Sears, Roebuck & Co. launched a new round of cost cuts, eliminating the jobs of four key executives and offering cash incentives to 600 administrative staff employees to leave their jobs. A spokesman said Sears is reviewing another 1,400 administrative jobs at its headquarters here and may offer "reorganization incentives" to some of those employees.

The steps signal that Chairman Edward A. Brennan is trying to reduce costs this year as he had indicated when he took day-to-day control of the faltering Sears merchandise group last month. Just a year ago, Sears completed a massive reorganization of the unit, but sales have been sluggish and profits have fallen since then.

Sears officials wouldn't comment on how much they expect to save, but analysts said they don't expect the figure to be substantial. Instead, the analysts viewed the moves as largely symbolic. "I'm glad to see they understand their cost problem is as much a management problem as anything else," said Edward Weller of Montgomery Securities.

The posts of two merchandising senior executive vice presidents were eliminated and two other senior executives retired and weren't replaced. In addition, two top merchandising executives, Thomas E. Morris, 43, and Jerry A. Hand, 50, resigned unexpectedly.

The two executives whose jobs were cut, Matthew A. Howard and Eric D. Saunders, aren't leaving Sears. They assumed the responsibilities of Messrs. Morris and Hand, who had overseen marketing and home fashions, respectively. Neither Mr. Morris nor Mr. Hand could be reached for comment.

Sears said only that the two men resigned to "pursue other interests." But Mr. Brennan was understood to have carefully reviewed all of the executives' performances, suggesting that he was especially disappointed with the company's advertising programs. Mr. Howard's new title, senior vice president of marketing, also signals that Sears considers him more of a heavyweight than Mr. Morris.

Mr. Morris presided over last year's launch of Sears's "everyday low pricing policy," which failed to stimulate sales or reduce expenses, as expected. Moreover, the launch was accompanied by an advertising blitz that some insiders now acknowledge probably confused consumers. Sears touted the program as "a sale that never ends."

Mr. Morris's resignation comes at a time when three ad agencies are understood to be putting together pitches for the big Sears ad account. The Ogilvy & Mather unit of WPP Group PLC currently handles the account and has been assured by Sears that it isn't conducting a review. The Sears spokesman said Mr. Morris's departure doesn't signal a change in agencies.

The retiring executives are Con S. Massey, 53, senior vice president for human resources and administration, and Vern L. Page, 60, vice president for catalog merchandising.

Sears said it is combining four of its merchandise group and corporate headquarters administrative departments. They are personnel, planning, law and public affairs. Now under review are the company's finance departments and other administrative functions.

Managers who take the cash incentive will receive two week's salary for every year of service. Non-managers will get a week's salary for every year of service.
