RCM Technologies Expects to See profits As It Shifts Emphasis

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/17/90

LOS ANGELES -- RCM Technologies Inc. expects to become profitable as it changes from primarily being a provider of services to a provider of technology, Robert Sackett, chairman and chief executive officer, said. In the meantime, the company reported a loss of $88,598 for the fiscal third quarter ended July 31, compared with a loss of $149,913 a year earlier. Revenue rose 16% to $7.1 million from $6.1 million.

Mr. Sackett predicted that revenue for all of fiscal 1990 will exceed $28 million, compared with $22.7 million in fiscal 1989. He said the company expected a full-year loss of less than $400,000, compared with a loss of $774,166, or six cents a share, in fiscal 1989.

The executive said RCM expects to become profitable in fiscal 1991 because of sales of a new technology that has cost the company about $600,000 to $700,000 a year to develop, but which hasn't yet generated any revenue. RCM's revenue currently comes from providing engineering, technical and temporary services through its Intertec Design unit to such companies as Dow Chemical Co., Boeing Co. and United Technologies Corp. RCM is lessening its exposure to the aerospace industry component of this business, adding that United Technologies' Sikorsky unit has already been affected by defense-spending cuts.

But Mr. Sackett said he believes the company's new technology, its System 99, which is designed to remove pollutants from power plants and factories that burn high-sulphur coal, could create revenue of $600 million to $700 million a year within 10 years. The technology, which customers could use to comply with pending acid-rain legislation, currently is being considered by several coal-fired utilities in the Midwest, Mr. Sackett said.

The company recently reached agreement with a unit of CBI Industries Inc. under which CBI would do the construction work on all System 99s that are ordered.

"We're not that credible yet," Mr. Sackett acknowledged. "CBI gives us credibility in the marketplace." Each order for System 99 would be worth about $20 million to $30 million to the partners, he added, and CBI is big enough to handle the more than 20 jobs a year it would take for RCM to reach its eventual revenue projections. Just one order, Mr. Sackett said, would make RCM profitable.

RCM says its System 99 removes more than 90% of pollutants created by burning such coal, including sulfur oxides and nitrous oxides, without leaving mass quantities of sludge that can pollute groundwater. There also isn't any need to pump large quantities of water since it's a dry system.

RCM operates a commercial-size demonstration facility in Camden, N.J.
