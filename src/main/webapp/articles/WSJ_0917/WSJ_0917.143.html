Marketing & Media: Cleveland Newspaper Loses Suit Brought by Promoters

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/17/90

CLEVELAND -- Plaintiffs won a $13.5 million libel suit in Cuyahoga County Common Pleas Court against the Cleveland Plain Dealer. Former promoters of a car race called the Budweiser Cleveland Grand Prix alleged that they were defamed by stories in the newspaper in 1984, and followed up in 1985 and 1986. The plaintiffs sought $16 million in damages, including $10 million in punitive damages.

The $13.5 million verdict included $4.5 million in compensatory damages and $9 million in punitive damages.

A secretary for the paper's top news officials said the Plain Dealer hadn't yet prepared a statement about the verdict. Lawyers who represented the newspaper couldn't be reached for comment.

The articles said that Charles K. Newcomb and James H. Foster had paid themselves nearly $1 million from race proceeds for management fees, salaries, commissions, legal and accounting fees, and equipment rental fees in violation of the lease they had with the city, according to a story in Thursday's Plain Dealer.

Messrs. Newcomb and Foster and their company, C. K. Newcomb & Associates of Cleveland, sold the race to businessman and former race car driver Roger Penske in 1985. At that point the Plain Dealer became a co-sponsor.

Plain Dealer lawyers had argued the articles were correct, the newspaper reported. The Plain Dealer is part of the Newhouse chain, which is owned by Advance Publications Inc.
