Some People Will Try Anything Except Lowering the Asking Price

  • Author: Mitchell Pacelle
  • Date: 09/17/90

Some anguished home sellers are looking for a miracle, sort of. So don't be alarmed if one dark night you spot your neighbors digging a hole and burying a statue of St. Joseph. They're only seeking a little saintly assistance in a buyer's market.

Joanne Walker, a real-estate broker in South Bend, Ind., reports that one of her customers got two offers on a house the first Saturday after burying St. Joseph in the yard. The customer had been trying to sell for almost a year.

If the connection between St. Joseph and real estate leaves non-Catholics scratching their heads, they aren't alone. Even the church is a bit mystified.

The Rev. John McSpiritt, who presides over the St. Joseph Shrine in Stirling, N.J., says he learned of the practice from members of his former parish in rural Georgia. He hasn't the slightest idea how it got started. "I call it a `pious practice,'" he offers. "There's no theology behind it."

Real-estate agents who have seen St. Joseph go underground explain that as a carpenter and the husband of Mary, mother of Jesus, Joseph is the archetypal provider and guardian of the hearth.

With no guidelines from the Vatican on how to lay the saint to rest, conflicting methods have emerged:

Upside down, facing the house, "in a gracious area -- not next to the garbage cans," says Ms. Walker. Upside down is one preference, in the garden is another, says Father McSpiritt.

"In some kind of box, to keep something from happening to him," says Eva Kruse, who sells religious articles at LaSalle Bookstore in South Bend. "Oh, no, no, no. You can't leave it in the box," says Maplewood, N.J., broker Joan Dirken.

One point of agreement: When you sell the house, you dig him up, dust him off, and display him in a prominent place in your new home.

The practice has clearly expanded beyond the devout. Patrons of a gift shop adjoining the New Jersey shrine have been carrying off St. Josephs by the armloads, according to saleswoman Marie Murray, who is selling as many as 100 a week.

When Fort Lee, N.J., broker Michael Ignatieff heard about St. Joseph's powers, he went to a florist specializing in funerals and picked up a dozen cheap plaster versions to offer his customers.

"I'm not even Catholic," he concedes. "It's another gimmick we're trying. It's another marketing tool."
