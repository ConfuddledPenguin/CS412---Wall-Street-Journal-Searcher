White House Stiffens Its Opposition to Bill On Fuel Efficiency

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The administration stepped up its opposition to legislation that would tighten the fuel-economy rules on cars, releasing studies claiming that the measure would cause increases in deaths and injuries. At a news conference, Transportation Secretary Samuel Skinner called the bill, sponsored by Sen. Richard Bryan (D., Nev.), a "highway fatality bill" and said he would recommend President Bush veto the legislation.

Mr. Skinner's meeting with reporters came after proponents won a key procedural vote by a bigger margin than expected. The Senate voted 68-23 to end a filibuster that had kept the bill from being debated. The bill, which has gained momentum since the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait and subsequent embargo of Iraqi oil, could come up for a vote late this week or early next week.

The legislation would require auto makers to increase the fuel efficiency of their fleets by 20% in the model year 1995 and 40% by 2001.

Mr. Skinner said existing technology won't allow manufacturers to meet the requirements of the legislation without a significant decrease in the size of American cars. According to two studies done by the Transportation Department's National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, the Bryan legislation would require an 850-pound reduction in the average weight of passenger cars. This, the study asserted, would lead to an additional 600 to 1,140 deaths and 2,900 to 5,300 injuries a year.

Sen. Bryan and others continue to contend, however, that it is very possible for the goals of his legislation to be met using currently available technology.

A similar bill, sponsored by Rep. Barbara Boxer (D., Calif.), is pending before the House Energy and Commerce Committee.
