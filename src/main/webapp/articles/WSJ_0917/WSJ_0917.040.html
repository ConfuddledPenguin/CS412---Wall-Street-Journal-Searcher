Georgia Corn Crop May Be Tainted With Aflatoxin

  • Author: Scott Kilman and Peter Waldman
  • Date: 09/17/90

High levels of aflatoxin were discovered in the milk of a Georgia processor, according to state Agriculture Commissioner Tommy Irvin. The discovery is the first sign that the Georgia corn crop might have been contaminated by the potent carcinogen, which is excreted by a soil fungus under droughtlike conditions. After the nationwide drought in 1988, contaminated milk in several states was traced to dairy cows that had been unknowingly fed aflatoxin-laced corn.

The threat to consumers isn't clear. Mr. Irvin said that 1,395 gallons of milk from the unidentified processor were destroyed long before it reached stores. But Georgia milk processors face a difficult year. The early indication is that aflatoxin might be widespread in the state's corn crop, much of which is fed to livestock.

Of 45 Georgia corn fields recently surveyed for state and federal authorities, about 60% were found to have aflatoxin in excess of the amount considered safe for human consumption.

Because of the region's warm climate, corn grown in the South is the most vulnerable in the nation to aflatoxin outbreaks. Crop experts have been worried about the chances of an aflatoxin outbreak in Georgia since an intense hot and dry spell damaged much of the corn crop in June.

Aflatoxin is a carcinogen in laboratory rats and has been linked to human liver cancer in Asia and Africa. For more than a decade, the poison has been detected in Southerners and doctors have debated whether it is a serious health hazard.

Earlier this month, aflatoxin was detected by officials in Texas corn corps, the second such outbreak there in as many years.

The most serious aflatoxin outbreak occurred during the 1988 drought that seared the Midwest. Dairy farmers in six states dumped milk, and authorities detected the poison in milled corn and such products as popcorn and cereal.
