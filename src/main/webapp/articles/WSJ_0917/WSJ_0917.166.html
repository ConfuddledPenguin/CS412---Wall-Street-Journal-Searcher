Insurance Official Warns of Problems Over Insolvencies

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The head of the American Insurance Association told a House subcommittee that the state insurance regulatory system may be unable to handle the problem of insurance company insolvencies in the future. "The limitations of 50 separate entities attempting to regulate a business that often operates in a national or international arena may necessitate some federal involvement," Robert E. Vagley, president of the trade group for property and casualty insurance companies, told the House Energy and Commerce Committee panel.

The hearing was held in connection with the subcommittee's investigation of insurance company insolvencies.

Mr. Vagley said the AIA is considering several proposals to complement state insurance regulations, including federal minimum standards and the creation of a self-regulatory organization.

The trade group official said the most serious problem connected with state regulation of insurance companies "is the lack of will on the part of state regulators to take action against troubled insurers."

He added, however, that the insurance insolvencies detailed in a February report by the oversight subcommittee "were not representative of the thousands of well-managed companies that characterize the property-casualty insurance industry."

Mr. Vagley also objected to comparisons between the insurance industry's problems and the thrift crisis.

"Whereas regulation of the savings and loan industry was liberalized to permit operations to become more risky, regulators of the insurance industry have enacted stricter solvency standards over the past decade," Mr. Vagley said. "Having said that, it is important to note that insurance regulation for solvency can and should be improved," he said.

Meanwhile, Rep. John Dingell (D., Mich.), chairman of the subcommittee, repeated his call for more stringent regulation of the insurance industry, warning that it could be headed for "a solvency crisis rivaling the savings and loan debacle."

Mr. Dingell said the panel's investigation shows that the current system for preventing insurance insolvency "is failing to meet the needs of the public and the insurance industry in several crucial areas."

"Our goal is to combine the industry's expertise with the appropriate governmental authority to see that proper solvency regulation is implemented before a national crisis develops," Mr. Dingell said.
