Aetna Life Prepares to Play Major Role In Managed-Care Health Insurance

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 09/17/90

Aetna Life & Casualty Co. is positioning itself to become "a major player" in the emerging field of managed-care health insurance, Charles Bell, senior vice president, said. The Hartford, Conn., insurer has spent $400 million in the past five years to develop its managed-care operations. It now has 2.8 million members in its network of 29 health maintenance organizations and 125 preferred provider groups. Aetna ranks second behind Cigna Corp. among the publicly held insurers that have decided to establish themselves as significant providers of managed health-care services to corporations.

Since the beginning of the year, Aetna has been working to cement its position in the managed-care field. In January, Aetna bought Voluntary Hospitals of America's 50% stake in Partners National Health Plans, a network of HMOs the two companies began developing in 1985.

In July, Aetna won Citibank's group health insurance account. The New York-based bank chose Aetna as the single insurer for its 53,000 domestic employees and some 1,000 employees abroad. Citibank is one of Aetna's largest managed-care clients so far. Several major corporations, such as American Express Co. and Apple Computer Inc., already use the insurer's HMO network.

Late last month, Aetna consolidated all its employee-benefit programs. These include HMO and preferred provider group, or PPO, networks; traditional fee-for-service plans; utilization-review systems, and various mental and substance-abuse programs under the Aetna Health Plans name. The new name will make it easier to market these various benefit plans, Mr. Bell, who is in charge of Aetna's managed health-care operations, said in an interview.

With a new name and its managed-care network in place, Aetna is concentrating on refining and improving its operations, Mr. Bell said.

Work has started on a new computer system for its 29 HMOs to ensure that all the plans are accessing patient information and processing claims in the same manner. This new system was needed because Aetna acquired about two-thirds of the HMOs, each with its own way of carrying out these basic tasks. Aetna said the new system will be completed by next year.

During the coming year, Aetna will consolidate its three utilization-review programs -- one for HMOs, another for PPOs and a third for indemnity plans -- into a single system.

Also next year, the insurer will develop "point-of-service" benefit plans. These plans offer workers the flexibility of using a preferred provider network, while combining some of the utilization-control features of an HMO, Mr. Bell said. Each employee is assigned to a primary-care physician, who will coordinate all medical care the patient receives.

Employees who chose health-care providers within the Aetna network in certain cities will be reimbursed 100%, while those who go outside the network will pay deductibles and some percentage of their health-care costs. Mr. Bell said these "point-of-service" plans will be set up in at least the 60 largest metropolitan areas by the end of 1991.

Although he acknowledged that Aetna's HMO business lost money during the first six months of 1990, Mr. Bell declined to make a specific forecast for the remainder of the year. "Given our losses for the first half, it doesn't take a rocket scientist to figure out that we'll be in the red this year," he said. But he also said he expects a "major improvement" in the company's HMO business next year. "We'd like to get to a break-even position," he said.
