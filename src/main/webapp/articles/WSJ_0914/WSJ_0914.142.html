Law -- Legal Beat: U.S. Firm Sues Iraq in Attempt To Claim Portion of Frozen Assets

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica and Amy Dockser Marcus
  • Date: 09/14/90

A New Jersey engineering firm sued the Iraqi government in what is apparently the first attempt by a U.S. litigant to claim a portion of Iraq's frozen assets. In its complaint filed in federal district court in Washington, Consarc Corp. of Rancocas, N.J., and its British subsidiary seek $62 million in actual and punitive damages resulting from the collapse of an $11 million transaction to supply Iraq's Ministry of Industry and Minerals with several industrial furnaces. Consarc charged that the deal came apart because Iraq "misrepresented" the purpose for which the equipment was being bought.

The Iraqis certified early last year that the furnaces would be used to make artificial limbs for victims of the Iran-Iraq war. Last June, however, U.S. authorities blocked the shipment because of suspicions that the ovens could be used instead to fabricate parts for nuclear devices. The Iraqi invasion of Kuwait later prompted President Bush to freeze all U.S. transactions with Baghdad, including that for the Consarc furnaces.

The Treasury Department indicated that Consarc is the first U.S. business or individual to be given a license to seek recovery of Iraqi assets. All such litigants require the permission of the Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control.

The Consarc suit also named the state-owned Rafidain Bank Iraq; Pittsburgh National Bank, a unit of PNC Financial Corp.; and the Royal Bank of Scotland as defendants. Rafidain holds blocked accounts relating to the Consarc contract in New York and London, while the Pittsburgh and Scottish banks handled letters of credit related to the financial transactions, according to the suit. Officials of Pittsburgh National Bank said they hadn't seen the suit and declined to comment. Royal Bank of Scotland couldn't be reached for comment.

Consarc's British subsidiary, Consarc Engineering Ltd., was to have supplied part of the aborted Iraqi order.

It isn't known whether Iraq will contest the suit. The Iraqi Embassy didn't answer telephone calls yesterday. However, shortly after the U.S. froze the Consarc shipments, Ambassador Sadiq Al-Mashat said the U.S. suspicions were "completely without basis."

Consarc could win its case by default if the Iraqis choose not to respond to it, said lawyers familiar with the complaint.

COURT OVERTURNS BAN on selling book about Israeli intelligence service.

The appellate division of the New York Supreme Court yesterday lifted an order barring St. Martin's Press from selling "By Way of Deception: The Making and Unmaking of a Mossad Officer." The book was written by Victor Ostrovsky, who is now in hiding in Canada and claims he spent two years as a Mossad agent.

Earlier this week, State Supreme Court Justice Michael J. Dontzin signed an order temporarily halting the sale of the book and ordered the New York publishing house to recall approximately 18,000 copies that had already been shipped.

A four-judge appellate panel ruled that the Israeli government had failed to demonstrate that the lives of its agents would be endangered by the book's publication.

The Israeli government "has failed to overcome the heavy presumption against prior restraint on publication," the panel wrote. The panel noted that the distribution of the book to 1,500 wholesalers, as well as to book reviewers at major publications, meant that the order would be ineffective.

David N. Kaye, a lawyer for St. Martin's, said that the company immediately began shipping copies of the book to stores yesterday, and that there would shortly be 150,000 copies in print.

Jonathan Lerner, a lawyer for the Israeli government, said it is unlikely his client will seek a further review. But he said Israel may pursue an action seeking damages against St. Martin's.

"If St. Martin's knew, or should have known, that Mr. Ostrovsky was under contract not to publish any secrets, then the proceeds from the publication of the book should go to the Israeli treasury," said Mr. Lerner, a lawyer with the New York law firm of Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom.

SUIT FILED AGAINST ELI LILLY makes allegations about Prozac.

The son of a man who killed eight people sued Eli Lilly & Co., the maker of the anti-depressant drug Prozac, which his father was taking. In the suit, James Thomas Wesbecker alleged that Lilly failed to test properly Prozac and should have known that it was unsafe for use for the treatment of depression.

Joseph Wesbecker entered the Standard Gravure printing plant on Sept. 14, 1989, killed eight people and wounded 12 before killing himself. Mr. Wesbecker, on medical leave from the Louisville, Ky., company, was on Prozac for five weeks before the shootings.

The law suit, filed in Jefferson Circuit Court in Louisville, is among the first to blame criminal behavior on Prozac, widely acclaimed as an especially safe and effective anti-depressant. Other defendants in the case include Dista Products Co., a unit of Lilly, and Mr. Wesbecker's former psychiatrist, Dr. Lee Coleman. Five other parties involved in the Wesbecker shootings have filed suits against Mr. Wesbecker's estate, Lilly and others. A one-year statute of limitations will expire today.

A number of lawyers recently have started trying to use what they are calling a Prozac defense to prove that the drug made their clients commit murder and should not be held responsible, said Michael O'Brien, director of research for the Citizens Commission on Human Rights, a Los Angeles group established by the Church of Scientology that wants Prozac removed from the market.

Last week, for example, a Utah judge ordered Joseph Charles Gardner to undergo a psychiatric evaluation to determine whether he was influenced by Prozac when he fatally shot Janice Fondren last July, said Alan D. Boyack, the St. George, Utah, lawyer representing Mr. Gardner.

The suits have been sparked by a report in the February issue of the American Journal of Psychiatry by Martin Teicher, a Harvard University psychiatrist. The article identified six people who developed violent suicidal thoughts while taking Prozac. None had previously experienced such thoughts, the article reported.

Edward West, a spokesman for Indianapolis-based Lilly, said more than 11,000 people participated in clinical trials of Prozac, which was approved by the Food and Drug Administration in 1987, and more than two million patients world-wide have been treated with the drug.

"Unfortunately, assaultive behavior is common in our society. Mr. Wesbecker had a lengthy history of psychiatric disturbance, including suicide attempts and homicidal threats, prior to taking Prozac. Other drugs were found in Mr. Wesbecker's blood following his death," Mr. West said. Dr. Coleman couldn't be reached for comment.

Plaintiffs' lawyers acknowledge it is difficult to determine whether Prozac or their clients' underlying mental problems caused their behavior. But Leonard L. Finz, the lawyer for Mr. Wesbecker's son, said that despite Mr. Wesbecker's psychiatric history, he had never committed acts of violence prior to taking Prozac.

PUBLISHERS TEAM UP in case involving unauthorized anthologies.

The trial, which pits eight of the biggest U.S. book publishers against Kinko's Graphics Corp., a photocopying chain, began this week. A decision in the closely watched case before federal Judge Constance Baker Motley in Manhattan could clarify the legal use of copyrighted material for educational purposes.

At issue is the growing business of photocopying excerpts from college textbooks and then compiling them in customized versions to be sold to a professor's class. As a result, the professor can assign chapters from several sources without requiring students to buy each book.

Kinko's, a 520-store chain based in Ventura, Calif., defends its service as a practical way to meet students' educational needs. The publishers, however, seeking to protect their $2 billion-a-year college-textbook market, want Judge Motley to rule that such anthologies violate the copyright law and should be banned.

The plaintiffs are Harper & Row Publishers Inc., McGraw-Hill Inc., Penguin Books U.S.A. Inc., Prentice-Hall Inc., Richard D. Irwin Inc., William Morrow Co., Basic Books Inc. and John Wiley & Sons Inc.

Both the publishers and Kinko's "are in a marketplace competing for student dollars," said Ronald Rauchberg, an attorney for the publishers, in opening arguments earlier this week. "The plaintiffs do it with respect for the copyright laws, paying royalties to the authors who create these books. Kinko's doesn't do it that way."

Kinko's says it's merely following the practices that have been prevalent on college campuses for years and accused publishers of trying to gouge students.

"It would cost a fortune for a student" to buy all the textbooks used in one anthology," said Dean Olds, an attorney for Kinko's, in his opening statement. "If {the court's decision} deprives the students of this smorgasbord of sources . . . their educational process is going to suffer."

THE TEAMSTERS HAVE RETAINED a new lawyer.

Brendan Sullivan of the Washington law firm of Williams & Connolly has been tapped by the International Brotherhood of Teamsters, according to people familiar with the union.

For the past two years, the Teamsters union, the nation's largest union, has been represented by lawyers at the New York law firm of Mudge, Rose, Guthrie, Alexander & Ferdon. The firm has challenged the government's authority to oversee union affairs under the terms of a 1989 settlement of racketeering charges brought by the U.S. attorney in Manhattan.

But in recent weeks, say Teamsters insiders, Teamsters President William J. McCarthy has expressed dismay over the law firm's failure to win on any of its motions involving the settlement. Other members of the Teamsters executive board pressured Mr. McCarthy to replace Mudge Rose when it became public that the union paid the firm nearly $6 million, according to people familiar with the change.

Mr. Sullivan has represented Oliver North. Williams & Connolly represented former Teamsters President Jimmy Hoffa, as well as former United Mine Workers President Tony Boyle, who went to prison for the murder of a man who challenged his leadership.

FORMER MONEY MANAGER Peter Jeffer pleaded guilty to one count of wire fraud in connection with an alleged insider-trading ring. He was accused of trading on information he received from Steven Glauberman, a former associate at Skadden Arps. Mr. Jeffer, who is cooperating with prosecutors in the case, entered his plea before Judge Miriam Goldman Cedarbaum in federal court in Manhattan. Mr. Glauberman pleaded guilty to three insider-trading-related counts Wednesday.

A THIRD OFFICER of a New Jersey company pleads guilty to bank fraud.

Richard Bober, former vice president, secretary and director of Coated Sales Inc., pleaded guilty in federal court in Newark, N.J., to charges involving a fraud scheme that netted him $12 million.

Mr. Bober admitted that he and other officers of the defunct textile converting company lied about the amount of the company's assets to secure a $52 million restructuring loan from BancBoston Financial Co. He also admitted to violating U.S. securities laws.

Martin Greenberg, Mr. Bober's attorney, said, "Mr. Bober is anxious to have this matter put behind him and is cooperating with the government."

Shortly before Coated Sales filed for bankruptcy-law protection in June 1988, it was considered one of the fastest-growing companies traded on the over-the-counter market, according to U.S. attorney Michael Chertoff.
