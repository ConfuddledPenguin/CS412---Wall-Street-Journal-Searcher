Company Gambles On 5-Minute Ads, Disguised as Drama --- Canada's Royal Trustco Ltd. Hopes to Gain Awareness In a Weeklong TV Blitz

  • Author: Lynne Kilpatrick
  • Date: 09/14/90

TORONTO -- When Canadians tune in their favorite national newscasts Monday night, they'll watch a father and son play an emotion-charged game of pool. The teen-age son's plans for university are threatened by his father's financial problems, but the proud father is too stubborn to seek financial advice. Viewers won't be watching a report on the coming recession or a documentary on parent-teen-ager relationships. They'll be watching a commercial, cleverly disguised as drama.

Royal Trustco Ltd. has sewn up every second of ad time on Canada's two national nightly news programs, as well as on a host of independent programs, for a whole week. The company even managed to persuade the news shows to rearrange their usual commercial formats to accommodate its ads.

Each night next week, Royal Trustco will show a different five-minute ad. The spots will be broken into two segments. The first half is designed to hook viewers, so they'll stay tuned to see how the drama works out in the second half.

Along with the troubled father and son, there will be a daughter who doesn't feel comfortable talking about her mother's will, an engaged couple trying to sort out financial differences, two sisters away on vacation talking about money management and two brothers wondering if they can afford to buy the family house.

"Love, death, promises, memories and money. Watch the late news]" exclaim ads at Royal Trustco's branches. The company hopes the three million Canadian dollar (US$2.6 million) campaign will bring in well-heeled clients to seek its financial advice and banking services. The company's motto: "Peace of mind through sound financial advice."

The television ads, which Royal Trustco touts as "the world's first five minute-commercial dramas" are the brainchild of a small Alberta-based agency, Hayhurst Communications Alberta Ltd. Lois Lockwood, the company's senior account executive, said the ads are designed to "break through the clutter" of normal television fare.

"Our objective was to increase Royal Trustco's awareness significantly," Ms. Lockwood said. With the news shows, "the audience is already there, and they match our demographic target."

Hayhurst, which beat out large, established Toronto agencies for the job, its first national account, has a lot on the line. Its compensation is tied directly to how well the ads do. If consumer awareness of Royal Trustco goes up, so does the agency's fee. If the ads fail, Hayhurst is left with just enough to cover its costs.
