International: Bonn and Moscow Agree on Economic Aid to the Soviets

  • Author: Timothy Aeppel
  • Date: 09/14/90

BONN -- West Germany and the Soviet Union signed a 20-year friendship treaty that forms the framework for broad West German economic assistance for the shattered Soviet economy. Separately, West German Finance Minister Theo Waigel confirmed that his government is giving Moscow an interest-free loan of 3 billion German marks ($1.9 billion) to cover the skyrocketing costs of maintaining whatever Soviet troops remain in East Germany over the next four years. The Soviets have agreed to pull all their troops out of East Germany by 1994.

West German officials see the loan, which is to be paid out in installments, as a necessary part of the aid package. Since July, when East Germany adopted the West German mark as its own currency, Moscow has found it difficult to meet the costs of keeping forces there.

The new loan comes on top of 12 billion marks that Bonn has already agreed to pay directly to help cover the costs of removing the troops from East German territory. In addition, West Germany will pay one billion marks in interest costs on the 3 billion-mark loan -- making the nation's direct contribution to the pullout 13 billion marks.

"It's a considerable extra burden on the budget," Mr. Waigel told reporters. However, he added, it "should be viewed in the context of German unification."

Mr. Waigel also spelled out, for the first time, where the 12 billion-mark handout would go: 7.8 billion marks for the construction of new housing in the Soviet Union for returning forces; 3 billion marks for underwriting Soviet costs inside East Germany; 1 billion marks for transporting the troops home; and 200 million marks for retraining those Soviet soldiers who will be put out of work.

While Bonn's financial inducements may have helped to ease Soviet objections to German unity, the payments also illustrate where West Germany is focusing its international energies. "We view helping the Soviet economy as part of our contribution to maintaining European stability," says one West German Foreign Ministry official.

More important, though, is that the new links are guaranteeing a unified Germany a front-row seat in any economic revival of the Soviet Union. Indeed, a close look at the new agreements between Bonn and Moscow shows that the ultimate result is likely to be tight economic links of a sort not seen since the days of the czars, when German businessmen were active in most sectors of the Russian economy.
