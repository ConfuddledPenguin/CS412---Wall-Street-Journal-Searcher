Philadelphia Expects Drop in Bond Rating After Loan Bid Fails

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/14/90

PHILADELPHIA -- The cash-strapped city of Philadelphia said it expects credit-rating agencies to further lower its shaky bond ratings after it failed this week to secure $375 million in short-term loans. Elizabeth C. Reveal, Philadelphia's finance director, said the city would postpone the sale of short-term notes until state and local politicians acted to boost investor confidence in the city's long-term fiscal health. During Wednesday's attempt to price the tax-free notes, some investors were demanding interest rates of about 9.5% to compensate for the risk of default.

"Elected officials . . . have got to begin saying that they are prepared to do everything in their power so that Philadelphia does not become insolvent," Ms. Reveal said. She added that investors and foreign banks were unwilling to lend Philadelphia money because of continued political wrangling.

The city's poor credit rating didn't help. Moody's Investors Service Inc. rates the city's bonds at the below investment level. Standard and Poor's Corp. gives the city its lowest investment-grade rating. Both agencies declined to comment on pending downgrades.

Swiss Bank Corp.'s withdrawal Wednesday of a guarantee for $50 million of the notes also made the sale more difficult, the city said. Swiss Bank cited unspecified unmet conditions in pulling out.

But Ms. Reveal maintained that Philadelphia "fully complied" with all conditions of its contract. "The action taken by Swiss Bank in withdrawing its commitment is a breach of its contract with the city," Ms. Reveal said. She added that the city may take legal action.

Without an eventual note sale, Ms. Reveal said, the city will run out of cash in six to 16 weeks, depending on which accounts it can legally tap. Even with a loan, officials say the city's $206.4 million budget deficit will cause it to run out of cash before the fiscal year ends next June 30.

In an attempt to bring about needed consensus, the Greater Philadelphia Chamber of Commerce proposed a "financial summit" of state and local officials to be held next week.
