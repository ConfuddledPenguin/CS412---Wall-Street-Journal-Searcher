Credit Markets: Short-Term Treasury Securities Rise, Boosted By Investors Worried About Banking Industry

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 09/14/90

NEW YORK -- Perceptions that problems in the banking industry are deteriorating prompted investors to pour more cash into short-term Treasury bills and notes. The buying binge helped short-term Treasurys outperform the broader bond market. "Whenever you hear about problems in the banking system, that tends to help the bill market," said Sung Won Sohn, a senior vice president and chief economist at Norwest Corp., Minneapolis.

Bond dealers said investors were liquidating various bank deposits and bank-issued securities and investing the proceeds in three-month and six-month Treasury bills and, to a smaller extent, two-year Treasury notes. These securities are considered the safest in the world and tend to be in big demand when there are signs of economic or political trouble.

Traders said investors have been purchasing larger amounts of bills for several months, but the buying seemed to pick up modestly yesterday as a result of a number of recent reports about the poor health of the banking industry. And because the market was so thinly traded yesterday, some dealers said the Treasury bill purchases may have appeared more sizable than they really were.

On Wednesday the Congressional Budget Office told the Senate Banking Committee that it expects 600 to 700 banks failures to occur by 1993. On Tuesday, one of the nation's biggest banks, Chase Manhattan Corp., was forced to reset the rate on $200 million of auction-rate notes to 13.017% from 9.66%. The new high rate is considered a sign that investors are leery of owning securities issued by bank holding companies.

Many analysts have said the banking industry isn't as bad off as the CBO report or the Chase Manhattan auction results would seem to suggest. Moreover, Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan told Congress yesterday that banking isn't headed for a disaster on the scale of the savings-and-loan crisis. But investors apparently aren't taking any chances.

"We've been seeing investors who have moved out of {bank issued} commercial paper and {certificates of deposit} and into Treasury bills," for months, said William Brachfeld, manager of trading at Daiwa Securities America Inc. However, Mr. Brachfeld said the Chase Manhattan reset note auction cast a new spotlight on the problems of the industry.

He said that huge amounts of money aren't flowing out of the banks into the bond market. But, "even if you just have 10% of money shifting from one place to another place, it can have a profound impact on the market," he said.

The "safe-haven" buying of short-term securities widened the gap between the Treasury's two-year note and the benchmark 30-year bond to .96 percentage point from .92 percentage point on Wednesday. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended slightly lower while the yield rose to 8.96% from 8.94%. Prices of two-year notes ended slightly higher to yield 7.999%.

Junk Bonds

The junk bond market, weary after three days of heavy selling by the Resolution Trust Corp., fell yesterday as tumbling stock prices helped accelerate the market's decline.

According to Elizabeth Specter, director of the Resolution Trust Corp.'s division of finance and administration, the RTC had been selling about $150 million of junk bonds a week prior to the Persian Gulf crisis and now is getting back up to that level of sales.

In an appearance before the Senate Banking Committee yesterday, Ms. Specter also presented Congress with statistics showing that the thrift bailout agency sold $550 million of junk bonds to date. As of July 31, the RTC's junk bond holdings stood at $3.7 billion.

The high-yield market had been hit by selling pressure all this week after the RTC, the federal agency set up to liquidate the holdings of the nation's crippled thrifts, sold about $100 million in junk bonds.

In trading yesterday, junk bonds ended between 3/8 to 1/2 point lower in light trading.

Among the biggest losers of the day were pay-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc., which pay interest in the form of additional bonds instead of cash.

Since the bonds are widely held by equity investors, they tend to plunge whenever the equity market registers sharp losses.

RJR Nabisco's 17.375% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, finished down 1 3/8 points at 110 3/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely held RJR issue, the 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, ended 1 1/2 points lower at 86 7/8.

The recently depressed junk bonds of Burlington Holdings Inc. were hit again yesterday. Burlington's 14.25% subordinated debentures, due in 1999, shed four points to end at a bid price of 61 on continued concerns that the textile maker will be financially pinched in a recession.

Other high-yield bonds posting losses were securities issued by Darling-Delaware Co. Although Darling-Delaware's 14% senior subordinated notes, due in 1999, were quoted 3/4 point higher at a bid price of 22 3/4, high-yield traders said the bonds are now widely being traded "flat" or without seven points of accrued interest in this case.

Bondholders who bought the bonds before yesterday would have typically had to pay the seven points of accrued interest, bringing the actual price of the bonds to 29.

However, reports that the company lacks the cash to pay interest on its high-yield junk bonds prompted many traders to quote the bonds without accrued interest.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond ended lower after a rocky day. But short-term securities ended unchanged to firmer.

In early trading, long-term bond prices rose after Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan restated the Fed's position that an easing in short-term interest rates was probable if Congress approved a deficit reduction plan that was well-received by the financial markets.

But later in the session, Iraqi threats of possible terrorist attacks against American targets in retaliation for so-called "crimes" against the Arab and Islamic nations prompted a small rise in oil prices. Long-term bonds then headed lower.

Also pressuring bonds and intermediate maturity government securities was stronger-than-expected car sales for the early September selling period. North American-made autos sold at a 7.1 million unit seasonally adjusted annual rate in the 10-day period, unchanged from the previous period.

The steady sales pace forced many investors to reassess their outlook for the economy.

The Treasury's 30-year bond was quoted late at 97 23/32, down from 5/32 from Wednesday, to yield 8.96%. The two-year note ended at 100 6/32, up from 100 5/32, to yield 7.999%.

The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills fell to 7.36% for a bond equivalent yield of 7.58%, while the discount rate on six-month bills ended at 7.28% to yield 7.67%.

Municipal Issues

Municipal bonds were generally unchanged with some issues trading 1/4 point higher.

Secondary market activity was thin ahead of the release of August producer price index data today.

The focus continued to be on new issues, which were well-received, traders said.

In the new issue market, after a three-year hiatus, Louisiana received a creditable reception for an issue of $199 million general obligation bonds by a Lehman Brothers group.

The bonds were priced to yield from 6.10% in 1991 to 7.60% in 2009 and 2010. Underwriting officials said all of the bonds had been sold within three hours of the pricing.

Approximately 60% of the Louisiana bonds were placed directly with investors, with the remaining 40% going into dealer inventories, a Lehman official said.

Among active issues, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issues due 2018 was unchanged at 97 7/8 to yield 7.37%.

Mortgage & Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities prices edged slightly higher in listless trading.

Government National Mortgage Association 9 1/2% securities for September delivery rose 1/32 to 98 29/32 while Federal National Mortgage Association 9 1/2% securities rose 2/32 at 99 3/32.

Two new asset-backed offerings were announced yesterday-a $500 million credit-card issue by a unit of Signet Banking Corp. and a $300 million Federal National Mortgage Association Remic offering backed by Fannie Mae 9 1/2% securities. Both were priced by Goldman Sachs & Co.

The class A senior portion of the credit card securities were priced at 99 37/64 to yield 9.19% while the class B subordinated portion of the issue was priced at 99 5/8 to yield 9.588%.

Goldman didn't release details on the Remic issue.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese government auctioned 1.2 trillion yen ($8.69 billion) of new three-month bills at an average yield of 8.27%.

The yield, one of the highest at a Japanese bill auction in recent years, was 3/4 percentage point higher than yields on three-month U.S. Treasury bills, but still not high enough to generate strong demand.

"This was a very good auction for the government," said Nitoshi Ohara, a bond trader manager at Daiwa Securities Inc. But he said the bills were not popular with investors because the yields were too low. "We were looking for yields of more than 8.40%," he said.

Although the average yield on Japan's new three-month bills was up from 7.852% at the previous three-month bill auction, Mr. Ohara notes that new three-month certificates of deposit now yield about 8.46%.

Japan's Ministry of Finance said the auction garnered a total 2.213 trillion yen of bids. Thus, the bid-to-cover ratio, which measures the bids received for each bid accepted, was 1.84.

In trading yesterday, Japan's benchmark No. 119 10-year bond fell 0.23 point to 82.09 to yield 8.345%.
