Politics & Policy: Mulling of Rise in Medicare Premiums Spurs Attack by Special Interest Groups

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 09/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Reports that budget negotiators are considering increases in Medicare premiums have touched off a pre-emptive attack that illustrates the political perils any deficit-reduction plan will face in Congress. In the face of gathering opposition within Congress and from senior citizen's groups, there were reports from the closed-door budget negotiations late yesterday that negotiators had scaled back initial plans to boost Medicare premiums by as much as $3.9 billion next year and $40.8 billion over five years.

But the initial proposal, once nervously supported by both Democrats and Republicans at the budget summit, may have poisoned the well for other efforts to make 33 million elderly and disabled beneficiaries pay higher premiums for their federal health benefits.

"This kind of proposal, even it's withdrawn, probably makes it harder for the summiteers to encourage everyone to do their fair share" as part of an effort to achieve $500 billion in budget cuts over five years, said Martin Corry, director of federal relations at the American Association of Retired Persons, the nation's largest senior-citizens group.

Horace Deets, AARP's executive director, said, "We've been answering a steady stream of calls. People are incredulous. They just don't understand this."

The rapid response to the Medicare proposal, first described in press reports Wednesday, is only one of a widening tide of protests over the plans being worked out by budget negotiators.

For example, all 34 members of the New York House delegation wrote Majority Leader Thomas Foley and Minority Leader Robert Michel asking them to oppose a Republican effort to limit the deductibility of state and local taxes.

And George Gould, chief lobbyist for the National Association of Letter Carriers, said that the union is threatening to stop making campaign contributions to members of Congress who vote for a deficit-reduction measure that includes a proposal to slow the growth of federal pensions. Part of the budget plan, which is still subject to change, would cancel the next cost of living increase for federal employees and hold future increases to less than the rate of inflation.

The American Medical Association is worried about proposed Medicare cuts that could cost hospitals and doctors $3.4 billion in fiscal 1991, which starts Oct. 1, and a total of $31.5 billion over five years. But one of the provisions that irritates the AMA the most is a relatively low-cost change -- a proposal to charge doctors $1 for each Medicare form they file by mail, rather than electronically. The $300 million revenue generator would hurt rural doctors in particular, the AMA argues. "We definitely will oppose those kinds of things," an AMA official says.

Every deficit-reduction measure that's taken out of the package will have to be replaced with something else, unless the Congressional and White House negotiators trim their targets of $50 billion in cuts in fiscal 1991 and $500 billion over five years.

Many analysts believe that substantial adjustments are necessary to slow the rapidly growing cost of the $105 billion-a-year Medicare program. But the politics are treacherous.

"I don't know how they could even consider a capital-gains tax cut when they're sticking it to seniors like this" with a proposed premium increase, says William Ritz, public affairs director of the National Committee to Preserve Social Security and Medicare. The committee led the fight to repeal the law extending Medicare to cover catastrophic illnesses, along with the surtax the law imposed on some senior taxpayers.
