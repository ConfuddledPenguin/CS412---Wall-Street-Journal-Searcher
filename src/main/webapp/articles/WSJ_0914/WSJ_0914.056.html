Thursday's Markets: Stocks Dive On Oil Rise, War Rumor --- Industrials Fall 43.07 On Program Selling; Dollar Also Slumps

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/14/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices took a dive under pressure from program selling that was sparked by a midday jump in oil prices and a rumor of war. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 43.07 points in light trading to 2582.67, the lowest level since 2532.92 on Aug. 24. The dollar was sharply lower for the second consecutive day, this time because comments by Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan heightened traders' expectation that U.S. interest rates will drop soon. But bond traders weren't inspired; bond prices edged lower in very quiet trading.

Peter Grennan, head of stock-index futures trading at Shearson Lehman Brothers, said the September futures contract on Standard & Poor's 500-stock index fell to its lowest level since Labor Day as the October crude oil futures contract surged to $31.45 a barrel; the contract fell back to end at $31.07, up 10 cents.

The drop in stock-index futures dashed some investors' hopes that stock prices were poised to move up. It also attracted program traders, who swooped in to capture profits by buying the cheaper futures and selling stocks.

Mr. Grennan noted this was the fourth consecutive Thursday the market posted a substantial loss. Previously, the heavy selling had been on three successive Fridays. Mr. Grennan said investors may be trying to beat each other out the door so they aren't hurt by any Mideast developments or other surprises over the weekend.

Adding to the downward pressure on stocks, traders said, was an unfounded midafternoon rumor, apparently originating in commodities futures pits, that a television news program reported Mideast war would erupt within hours.

Trading in the dollar was relatively quiet until Mr. Greenspan told the House Banking Committee that the Fed would likely push interest rates lower if Congress and the Bush administration produced a credible pact to cut the U.S. budget deficit. That remark sparked a sell-off, which deepened when the Fed chairman went on to acknowledge that the U.S. banking system is facing serious problems.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell. Volume was 123.4 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues swamped advancers 1,009 to 430.

Bond prices eased nearly 1/8 point, or almost $1.25 for each $1,000 face amount. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose slightly to 8.96%.

The dollar fell. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5677 marks and 136.20 yen, compared with 1.5913 marks and 138.09 yen Wednesday.
