Letters to the Editor: Embarassing Reality Of Peculiar Institution

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/14/90

Judge Thomas Griesa's review of Robert Goldwin's book about the Constitution -- "Why Blacks, Women, and Jews Are Not Mentioned in the Constitution, and Other Unorthodox Views" -- contains the questionable assertion that ". . . when abolition (of slavery) finally came, it was the laws and practices of certain states that needed to be changed, not the Constitution." (Leisure & Arts, Aug. 22) He seems to have forgotten the 13th, 14th and 15th amendments to the Constitution, all of which specifically addressed slavery and its consequences. The 13th Amendment specifically uses the word "slavery," in order to abolish it.

Slavery was not abolished, as Mr. Griesa (or perhaps Mr. Goldwin) suggests, by changing the laws of a few states. It was partially abolished by the Abraham's Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation and the good offices of the Union Army; but several slaves states fought on the side of the Union and other states that had outlawed slavery long before had done so with grandfather clauses that perpetuated it in isolated instances. For example, there were still a few elderly slaves in New Jersey at the outbreak of the Civil War. It was the 13th Amendment that formally eliminated slavery.

The word "slavery" does not appear prior to the 13th Amendment because the authors of the Constitution preferred euphemism to defending the indefensible, a not uncommon political response to embarrassing realities. Even the slaveholders preferred euphemism, with "servant" being the favored description for the victims of the Peculiar Institution.

That embarrassment was particularly acute because the members of the Philadelphia convention were otherwise staunch defenders of human liberty and intended the Constitution to serve that noble end, and it has, for the most part, in the ensuing two centuries.

Edward B. Furey Jr.

Woodhaven, New York
