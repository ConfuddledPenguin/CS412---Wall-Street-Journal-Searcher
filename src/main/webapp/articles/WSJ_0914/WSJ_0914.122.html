Foreign Exchange: Dollar Tumbles on Greenspan's Hints Of Impending Interest-Rate Declines

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/14/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar was hurt by Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan's remarks, which hinted at a quick move to cut interest rates upon the completion of a federal budget-reduction accord. The greenback dropped an entire German pfennig in the half-hour following the remarks in the late New York morning, and was quoted late in the day at 1.5677 marks, well below 1.5913 marks late Wednesday. Against the Japanese currency, the dollar dropped to 136.20 yen, down from 138.09 yen in New York late Wednesday. That level was its worst since Aug. 1 of last year, when the dollar ended at 135.90 yen.

Sterling was trading at $1.8825, up from $1.8594 late Wednesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Friday, the dollar was quoted at 136.50 yen, down from 137.65 yen at the close there Thursday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5680 marks, down from Thursday's Tokyo close of 1.5911 marks.

The central banker's comments were hardly new, since they only repeated a promise made in June to administration officials and lawmakers eager for the cheaper money they believe is necessary to stave off a U.S. recession.

But the remarks caught many foreign-exchange traders off guard, slipped as they were into a question-and-answer session that followed House testimony on deposit insurance.

The dollar had been mixed throughout Asian and early European trading, and U.S. traders had entered their session adding to long, or overbought, dollar positions, especially against the mark.

"You get into these situations when everyone is trading technically," said David Gilmore, a currency analyst with McCarthy, Crisanti & Maffei. "Greenspan caught people right in the middle of building long positions, and they had to get out of them quickly. It's not what he said that was so startling, but it came at an inopportune time."

Keith Raphael, a Chase Manhattan vice president, agreed that long-dollar positions, especially against the mark, had set up the greenback for a fall yesterday morning.

In addition, Mr. Raphael said, the market was "unnerved" by Mr. Greenspan's earlier comments about U.S. banks.

"There remain all too many problems in the banking system, problems that have been growing of late as many banks, including many larger banks, have been experiencing a deterioration in the quality of their loan portfolios, particularly real estate loans," the Fed chairman said.

The General Accounting Office and the Congressional Budget Office had issued similar warnings this week. "It's nothing we didn't know," Mr. Raphael said, "but when Greenspan says it, he gives it weight."

The central banker went on to express the opinion that the market would lower long-term interest rates if it perceived a federal budget agreement to be credible, enforceable and long-lasting. Should that happen, Mr. Greenspan said he "would assume" that the Fed would be "moving toward ease" in its credit policy.

At his daily press briefing yesterday, White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said the administration is "still optimistic" for a budget pact in the next few days.

Also helping to push the dollar lower against the yen was selling of the mark against the Japanese currency after support broke at 87.20 yen. The German currency later rebounded and was quoted in late New York trading at 86.879 yen, up from 86.778 yen Wednesday.

The dollar is often used as an intermediary in such "cross-rate" transactions. For instance, a trader seeking to buy yen and sell marks might initially sell marks for dollars and then sell the dollars for yen. Such trades tend to boost the U.S. currency against its German counterpart while weakening the dollar against Japan's currency.

The greenback ended European dealings yesterday broadly weaker. The currency hadn't moved much through the bulk of European dealings, until Mr. Greenspan's comments caused it to skid in the final hour of trading.

The dollar finished volatile Asian dealings mixed. It dropped against the yen but finished well above its low for the session. The currency was last quoted in Tokyo Thursday at 137.65 yen, down from 138.30 yen late Wednesday.

The dollar began Tokyo trading down sharply against the Japanese currency, at 137.40 yen compared with 138.10 yen several hours earlier in New York. Yen bulls predicted the dollar would drop below 137 yen before the end of the day.

But rounds of profit-taking beginning at mid-morning revived the dollar, as participants locked in gains on long yen positions.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $382.90 an ounce, up 80 cents. Estimated volume was a moderate 2.8 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $383.95 an ounce.
