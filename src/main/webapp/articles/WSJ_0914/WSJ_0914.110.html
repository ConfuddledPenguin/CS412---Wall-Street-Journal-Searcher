International: Explosion at a Nuclear-Related Facility Stirs Concern on Soviet Industrial Safety

  • Author: Richard L. Hudson
  • Date: 09/14/90

LONDON -- An explosion at a Soviet factory that makes metals used in nuclear reactors injured several people and underlined mounting concerns about Soviet industrial safety. The accident, which occurred Wednesday in the Soviet Central Asian republic of Kazakhstan, didn't release radiation or kill anyone, according to Tass, the Soviet news agency. It reported yesterday that "several people" were injured in the fire, which began in the basement of the factory's beryllium metals workshop. Beryllium is a toxic but non-radioactive metal that the Soviets use in some of their scientific, space and military reactors.

Despite initial fears of a repeat of the 1986 Chernobyl nuclear accident, by late yesterday U.S. and West European nuclear experts were classifying the explosion as a serious but conventional industrial accident.

Normally, there wouldn't be much radioactive material at such a metals-forming factory; the only immediately apparent link to the nuclear industry was that the factory's products are used in some Soviet nuclear reactors. The plant is also far from the nearest nuclear-testing site.

"I wouldn't worry about it" posing any health threat outside the immediate area of the factory, said one U.S. Energy Department official, who declined to be named. Supporting that view, Soviet delegates to the International Atomic Energy Agency in Vienna indicated there wasn't any radiation danger.

But the accident, whose cause Moscow didn't disclose, stirred renewed concern in the Soviet capital and elsewhere about the country's worsening record of industrial safety. Underlining that concern, the Kremlin dispatched a deputy atomic-energy minister to visit the accident scene yesterday, according to one Soviet nuclear industry official reached by telephone in Moscow.

Tass said the fire was extinguished in about 90 minutes, but that beryllium contamination at the site was still twice the permitted Soviet safety level. Beryllium, when inhaled, can cause a serious lung ailment called berylliosis.

Since the 1986 Chernobyl accident, Soviet officials and journalists have shown more concern about safety throughout Soviet industry. Reliable statistics are hard to come by, but last year the official newspaper Izvestia lamented about the 200,000 Soviet industrial deaths and injuries that occurred in 1988, many due to careless practices, decrepit equipment, or even poor washroom facilities when dealing with toxic materials.

Part of the problem, Western analysts say, has been a paucity of comprehensive occupational safety laws or regulations; until recently, most safety standards were split up among the various ministries that supervised each industry. To tackle the problem, the Soviet Academy of Sciences recently launched a major new study of Soviet safety methodology. And Moscow this year created one, industrywide safety regulator. So far, there is little evidence of improved safety, and this week's accident is likely to put added pressure on Moscow to produce results.

According to Tass, the accident occurred at the Ulbinsky metallurgical plant near the city of Ust-Kamenogorsk, in the Soviet republic of Kazakhstan. The site is about 100 miles east of Semipalatinsk, where the Soviets conduct their nuclear weapons tests. There wasn't any evidence, however, of a link between the accident and weapons testing.

With few details emerging from Moscow, Western analysts speculated that a conventional chemical or gas explosion might have occurred at the factory, spewing smoke and beryllium into the air. The resulting smoke cloud was reported drifting towards the Chinese border, 200 miles away.

Some initial confusion was caused by Tass's description of the plant as "a major producer of fuel for nuclear power stations." But Soviet officials Thursday told the International Atomic Energy Agency that the description wasn't accurate, a spokesman for the Vienna-based United Nations affiliate said.

In fact, according to Soviet officials, the beryllium produced in the factory is used to make non-fuel components of reactors. Specifically, the material is often used as a "reflector" inside some reactors. A reflector is a layer of metal surrounding a reactor's radioactive core. As the uranium fuel inside the reactor fissions to produce energy, many high-energy particles race away from the core. A reflector bounces the subatomic particles back into the core, to help maintain the atomic reaction and reduce the amount of radiation escaping.

The big Soviet nuclear program, however, is apparently unique in using beryllium alloys in reactors; in the West, various zirconium-steel alloys are used. The U.S experimented with beryllium decades ago, but it dropped the material as too expensive and toxic. The Soviets have apparently continued producing it for some specialized, high-performance reactors in its space program and its military research.
