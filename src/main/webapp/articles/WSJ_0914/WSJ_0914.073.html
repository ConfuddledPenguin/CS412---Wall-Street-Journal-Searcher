CBOT Is Sued By Ferruzzi Firm In Soybean Case

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 09/14/90

CHICAGO -- Ferruzzi Finanziaria S.p.A. filed suit in U.S. District Court here, alleging the Chicago Board of Trade violated antitrust laws and the Commodity Exchange Act when it issued a 1989 emergency order requiring soybean traders to close out their positions. The CBOT's action last year was designed to avert a possible squeeze on traders holding short, or selling, positions in the contracts, where prices had skyrocketed in response to a drought-reduced soybean harvest. Ferruzzi, a grain merchant, had accumulated large quantities of soybeans and long, or purchase, contracts; liquidating that position under the order gave the Milan concern significant trading losses.

In one suit, Ferruzzi alleges that "certain members of the CBOT's board of directors, including its chairman, were affiliated with trading firms that held short soybean futures positions in proprietary or customer accounts." The board issued the order in an effort to avoid losses by "manipulating soybean prices to artificially low levels."

The CBOT chairman at the time the order was issued was Karsten Mahlmann, who was also chairman of Stotler Group Inc. Stotler held a short position, as did its customers, the suit alleges. And because of the firm's "dire financial straits" at the time, a loss on its position, or a customer default, "might have forced the company into default, which in fact happened a year later." As reported, Mr. Mahlmann resigned from the CBOT after Stotler collapsed earlier this summer; the company's financial woes weren't publicly known until July of this year.

In a separate suit, Ferruzzi asked the court to block a disciplinary procedure scheduled for next week, in which the CBOT's Business Conduct Committee will decide on charges that Ferruzzi's marketplace activities were designed to manipulate the price of soybean contracts. Ferruzzi's suit alleges that the CBOT launched the internal investigation "in response to the criticism of its apparent conflict of interest."

The Business Conduct Committee, which will hear the charges against Ferruzzi, "is the same body which recommended the emergency order, decided to initiate an investigation of plaintiffs, and approved the charges against them," the suit says. And if it wants to appeal the committee's ruling, Ferruzzi said, it has to appeal to the CBOT's board, which "approved and issued the emergency order."

The disciplinary procedures should be blocked because the CBOT "is incapable of providing (Ferruzzi) with a fair hearing."

In both suits, only the CBOT is named as a defendant.

Both the Commodities Futures Trading Commision and the General Accounting Office carried out investigations of the CBOT's emergency order, and concluded there hadn't been a conflict of interest.

In a statement yesterday, the CBOT called the suits "obviously frivolous," and added that it intends to resist them "to the fullest extent of the law. . . . We will not permit them to interfere with our responsibility to protect the integrity of the market through our disciplinary process."
