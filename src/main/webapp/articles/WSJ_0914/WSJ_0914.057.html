Greenspan Sees No S&L Crisis For U.S. Banks --- But Fed Chairman Backs Rise in Insurance Fees, Citing Industry's Woes

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 09/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Although the nation's banks face serious problems, the industry isn't headed for a disaster on the magnitude of the savings-and-loan crisis, Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan said. Testifying before the House Banking Committee, Mr. Greenspan endorsed the idea of raising banks' deposit-insurance premiums, a move that the administration and an increasing number of lawmakers support.

Mr. Greenspan also reiterated that the Fed would probably lower interest rates if Congress and the administration reach a "credible and enforceable" agreement to reduce the federal budget deficit. It was the first time the chairman has made such comments since the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait sent oil prices soaring and heightened fears of resurgent inflation.

Government officials have grown increasingly anxious over the health of the nation's banks in the wake of the multibillion-dollar thrift bailout. New alarms clanged this week when two congressional agencies warned that the federal insurance fund that protects bank depositors was dangerously low.

But Mr. Greenspan said the banking industry is much sounder than its sister thrift industry was several years ago, when problems that led to the failure of thousands of S&Ls and the huge taxpayer bailout were beginning to fester. Banks' levels of capital are higher, he said, and banking regulation is tougher. He also said bank assets are shorter-term than those of thrifts, allowing banks to more easily balance their borrowing costs with lending rates.

Consequently, the Fed chairman said, "The chance of having the type of problems with banks that we have with thrifts is very significantly lower."

But Mr. Greenspan agreed that the deposit-insurance fund "is under stress" with fund reserves, as a percentage of insured deposits, at a record low. He also said banks are threatened by an increasing number of sour real-estate loans brought on by a downturn in commercial real estate.

Mr. Greenspan said he would favor enhancing the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.'s authority to borrow from the Treasury to cope with any shortages in the insurance fund while Congress is working on changes in deposit insurance. The FDIC currently has a $5 billion line of credit with the Treasury, but has never used it.

If insurance premiums are raised, Mr. Greenspan said, officials must take great care to set a level that wouldn't push some institutions into insolvency. He suggested the FDIC, which manages both bank and thrift deposit-insurance funds, determine a new ceiling on insurance premiums. The premium on thrift deposits is 23 cents per $100 of deposits, and the premium for banks is set to rise 4.5 cents next year to 19.5 cents per $100 of deposits.

The chairmen of the Banking committees, Sen. Donald Riegle (D., Mich.) and Rep. Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas), yesterday introduced bills that would give regulators the authority to raise bank deposit-insurance premiums.

There is no question that the insurance fund has led banks to take risks, knowing that the government would protect their deposit funds, Mr. Greenspan said. It has also kept depositors from scrutinizing the soundness of the institutions they choose to house their money, he said.

Mr. Greenspan said raising the requirements for bank capital and strengthening supervision of banks would "go a long way to eliminate excessive risk-taking by insured institutions."

He expressed reservations about reducing the federal insurance limit of $100,000 per account, another idea gaining popularity. "If we were starting from scratch, the board believes it would be difficult to make the case that deposit-insurance coverage should be as high as its current $100,000 level," he said.

Nevertheless, banks, thrifts and millions of households have made decisions assuming the $100,000 coverage, he said. "Should Congress decide to lower deposit-insurance limits, a meaningful transition period would be needed," he suggested.

Mr. Greenspan also said that in the past "month or two" banks may have "gone over the line" in restricting their lending policies. Some banks have tightened lending standards this year, mostly in commercial real-estate loans, leading to widespread complaints of a credit squeeze. Mr. Greenspan had been welcoming the increased prudence, and yesterday maintained: "A substantial part of the pulling back is good, not bad."

Separately, Sen. Alan Dixon (D., Ill.) introduced legislation that would require the Fed to pay into the bank-insurance fund interest on the $33 billion the industry keeps in reserve at the Fed. The interest would provide roughly $2.5 billion annually to the insurance fund, which currently stands at $13.2 billion.
