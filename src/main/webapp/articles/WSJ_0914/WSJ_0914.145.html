Law: Judge Rules Oil Spill Victims Must Seek Relief From Fund

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/14/90

ANCHORAGE, Alaska -- A federal judge here tentatively held that victims of last year's Prince William Sound oil spill must first seek compensation from a special administrative oil-spill fund before trying to press their claims in court. The thought that the massive civil litigation stemming from the March 1989 wreck of the Exxon Valdez could be put on hold caught attorneys on both sides of the case off guard and prompted harsh words from plaintiff attorneys that the ruling could delay full resolution of the case for years more.

Judge H. Russel Holland said he considered the proposal a way of speeding up compensation to victims of the spill. The Trans-Alaska Pipeline Authorization Act fund provides $100 million to oil-spill victims without requiring them to prove any wrongdoing. The fund, which recently was amended by Congress in a partial attempt to speed compensation to victims, is financed through levies on owners of crude passing through the Alaskan pipeline.

"If those who have suffered monetary losses are allowed to proceed in state or federal court with classic common law litigation without first exhausting the fund's administrative payment procedures, the congressional purpose of expeditiously providing adequate compensation to all those injured . . . would be thwarted," the judge reasoned.

But plaintiff attorneys argued that actual damages dwarf the amount contained in the fund and that stalling inevitable court proceedings to recover the difference would only delay just compensation to victims. They said haggling over pro-rata shares to victims, as the fund requires if damages are found to exceed the $100 million fund limit, could take years.

"If there is going to be litigation, why stay it now and then rev it up again when the claims are becoming stale and memories are fading?" asked Lloyd B. Miller, an Anchorage attorney, representing a number of native Alaskan groups affected by the spill.
