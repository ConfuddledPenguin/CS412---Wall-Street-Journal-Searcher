Washington Wire: A Special Weekly Report From The Wall Street Journal's Capital Bureau

  • Author: Christopher Conte
  • Date: 09/14/90

IRAN EMERGES as the biggest potential embargo buster. U.S. officials anticipate Tehran will allow at least some food and medicine shipments to Iraq. It also could buy Iraqi oil and tolerate some smuggling across its border. The question is how much. American military officials are confident they can stop ships from delivering large Iraq-bound cargoes to Iranian ports, but even modest Iranian help for Iraq could encourage other cheaters.

U.S. officials believe Iran wants Western aid too badly to defy the embargo flagrantly. However, some analysts think it may try to bolster Iraq just enough to produce a stalemate. Worse, some fear Tehran wants the embargo to leak enough that Washington feels compelled to launch an attack that would destroy the Iraqi war machine Iran itself couldn't defeat.

Militarily, Iraq can survive the embargo indefinitely. "I don't think this embargo will hurt their military machine," warns Thomas McNaugher of the Brookings Institution.

QUIET DEVALUATION: The dollar falls as the Treasury looks the other way.

The greenback is near a 30-year low, but the administration emphasizes this will help the trade deficit. Meanwhile, Treasury Secretary Brady urges the Federal Reserve to ease interest rates, a move that would push the dollar lower still. Under pressure from the Fed and Congress, the Treasury at least has stopped selling dollars to hasten the currency's drop.

The lure of higher interest rates abroad, combined with fears that rising oil prices will hit the American economy especially hard, lie behind the dollar's fall. Although a budget agreement could bolster the American currency, the Paris-based OECD believes depreciation will aggravate inflation and eventually could help lead the U.S. to raise interest rates.

CONGRESSIONAL CRUNCH? Lawmakers face a crowded agenda.

Despite concerns about costs, prospects are good for the clean-air bill and for a compromise on child-care legislation. Lawmakers advance the civil-rights bill early to allow time for a possible veto-override attempt; former Urban League President Vernon Jordan warns that a veto would be "a terrible mistake," crippling Bush's relations with blacks.

Deficit pressures portend big battles over where to cut defense spending. Lawmakers talk about a 20% cut in the farm bill, the proposed Mars space mission is in trouble, and skirmishes loom over foreign aid. Sentiment also grows to override administration objections to textile trade protection.

The schedule is tight, but Senate Leader Mitchell and House Speaker Foley want to avoid a lame-duck session in their first Congress as top leaders.

CRIME BUSTER: The House probably will approve tougher gun-control provisions and softer death-penalty language than the Senate did in its crime bill. Bush has vowed to veto even the milder Senate version, but he may find that difficult since it includes politically popular measures aimed at cracking down on savings and loan fraud.

TAKING OFF? Proposals to let airports tax airline passengers get new wings. Senate Aviation Chairman Ford has blocked the idea, but he might back the "departure taxes" in return for limits on airport noise restrictions. Airports hungry for funds to finance expansion might go along; and airlines, eager to override stringent local noise rules, like the idea.

BACK BURNER: Despite closer links with moderate Arabs, the U.S. puts Israeli-Palestinian peace efforts on hold. Israeli Foreign Minister Levy warns Baker that U.S. pressure on the issue would bring down the government in Jerusalem. Baker also is wary of appearing to link the Palestinian and Persian Gulf issues.

TEAM PLAYER: The White House enlisted former Reagan Chief of Staff Kenneth Duberstein to help prepare it for Supreme Court nominee Souter's confirmation hearings. Presidential aides say Duberstein's low-key approach fits well with the "Bush style." Predicts one top aide: "He'll clearly be asked to do things in the future."

DEMOCRATS COUNTER Republican challenges in the South.

Democrats still are optimistic Lawton Chiles will wrest the key Florida governorship from Republican Martinez. They also see a possible upset of the incumbent GOP governor in Alabama; but hopes to win back the governorship in Texas fade. All Southern incumbent Democratic senators seem safe, and, at most, the GOP will score only minimal gains in House races despite several retiring incumbents.

Although the GOP hopes to end Democratic control of the Florida Senate, the party otherwise expects few gains in state legislative contests in Dixie. Republicans argue they could score big gains in 1992, when population growth probably will give them openings through reapportionment.

But analyst Charles Cook notes: "With Republicans in the South, every two years it's `maybe next time.'"

MINOR MEMOS: Democrats complain that while NBC declined to air their response to Bush immediately after his address, the network lobbies against proposed government fees by arguing that the airwaves are used for the public interest. . . . Tommy Downs, imprisoned for fraud involving student loans, tells senators: "The possibility of one of you gentlemen being in my position is probably greater than the other way around."
