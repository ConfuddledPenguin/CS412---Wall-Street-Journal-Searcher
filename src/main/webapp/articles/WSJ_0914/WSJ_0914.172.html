Accounting Group Switches Position On Blowing Whistle

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 09/14/90

NEW YORK -- The accounting profession, reversing its previous position, said it supports proposed legislation that would require auditors to notify regulators of possible illegal acts by companies. The American Institute of Certified Public Accountants, accounting's major professional body, reached the new position after sponsors in Congress proposed language that would put a greater responsibility on companies to report alleged illegal acts of corporate officers that have been identified by auditors.

Congress in recent months has explored whether accountants have failed to disclose evidence of possible illegal acts by management that is uncovered during audits, particularly in light of the savings and loan debacle.

In the past, auditors have argued that making them police corporate behavior would harm their relationships with clients and saddle them with an unfair burden of responsibility for the acts of others.

Rep. Ron Wyden (D., Ore.), who has sought to require accountants to report wrong behaviors for several years, asserts that the stiffer rules would "give us a chance to pick up more problems at the earliest possible stage."

Under existing requirements, when an auditor uncovers possible illegal acts on the part of a client, he is expected to inform the corporate audit committee and directors, and assess whether proper corrective steps have been taken; if not, the auditor resigns the account, which triggers a notification to the Securities and Exchange Commission.

Under the latest proposal, a company that received a letter from an auditor, expressing concern about failure to take proper steps, would have to notify the SEC of the dispute. If the company failed to do so, the auditor would have to furnish the SEC a copy of its report. The auditor would continue to have the option of resigning.

The accounting institute this week notified Rep. John Dingell (D., Mich.), chairman of the House Energy and Commerce committee, that it supports proposed changes to the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. The legislation could be attached to other pending legislation as early as next week.

In a letter to Rep. Dingell, accounting institute officials said the proposed changes would "improve investor confidence in the integrity and reliability of financial statements issued by those who access public capital markets." The proposal also has the support of the six major accounting firms.

Critics have asserted the current rules don't require prompt enough disclosure of potential problems uncovered during independent audits. In the latest proposal, if an auditor writes a letter questioning whether appropriate action has been taken, the company would have one day to report receipt of such a letter; if the company failed to do so, the auditor would have to notify the SEC on the following business day.

The changes include a new requirement that companies in their annual reports discuss whether any "material weaknesses" have been identified and remain uncorrected in their system of internal controls.

Though previous proposals required auditors to address compliance with all laws and regulations, the latest changes require them to report on potentially illegal acts that have a material effect on a company's financial statement, which accountants say they already do.

Separately, the proposal requires all publicly held banks and thrifts to report to the SEC; current rules permit banks and thrifts that aren't part of holding companies to report to financial regulators rather than the SEC.
