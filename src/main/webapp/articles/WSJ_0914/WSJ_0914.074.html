Ford Motor's Canadian Unit Is Facing Walkout at Midnight in Contract Talks --- By Larry M. Greenberg Staff Reporter of The Wall Street Journal

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/14/90

TORONTO -- Almost 13,000 employees at Ford Motor Co.'s Canadian subsidiary were slated to strike at midnight, as time appeared to be running out for a new contract settlement. Officials of the Canadian Auto Workers union were continuing to meet with Ford officials last night, but union insiders asserted that there wasn't any chance a new contract could be put together before the strike deadline. A Ford spokesman was more upbeat, but said reaching any agreement at this point would require "damn hard work."

A walkout by Ford's Canadian employees would idle nine of the company's plants in Canada. It also could hurt important Ford operations in the U.S., which depend on parts manufactured in Canada. Ford officials predict a shortage of engines and auto glass from Canada would result in several U.S. operations closing within two weeks of a strike.

The union began contract negotiations last week after selecting Ford as a strike target. Under the usual system of pattern bargaining, any contract reached at Ford will serve as the basis for subsequent negotiations with the Canadian subsidiaries of General Motors Corp. and Chrysler Corp. Current contracts at each of the Big Three auto makers, which cover a total of 61,000 employees in Canada, expire today.

Ford has made just one contract offer since the talks began. That offer, covering such economic issues as wages, vacations and cost-of-living allowances, was swiftly rejected by the union, which contended that the company wasn't offering nearly enough money. Even if Ford should return before the strike deadline with a mutually acceptable package of economic terms, there remain such time-consuming issues to be resolved as health and safety in the plants and medical benefits.

A Canadian strike would hit some of Ford's most profitable model lines. The company's St. Thomas, Ontario, plant is the sole source for the large Mercury Grand Marquis and Ford Crown Victoria models.

Ford's Windsor, Ontario, engine plant builds the six-cylinder unit for the Lincoln Continental luxury car. A lengthy shutdown could hobble operations at the highly profitable Wixom, Mich., plant that builds the Continental, as well as the Lincoln Town Car and Lincoln Mark VII. The Windsor plant also builds many of the engines for Ford's U.S.-built Taurus and Sable midsized cars.

Another Ford plant, in Niagara Falls, Ontario, is the sole provider of auto glass to four of the company's light-truck assembly operations in the U.S. A total of about 80% of Ford's Canadian output is exported to the U.S.
