New York Officials Urged to Broaden Nynex Unit Probe

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 09/14/90

The New York State Assembly's investigative unit rebuked state regulators for inadequately examining alleged wrongdoing in transactions between New York Telephone and other Nynex Corp. units. A report released yesterday by Richard Brodsky, chairman of the Assembly's Oversight, Analysis and Investigation Committee, recommended that state regulators broaden their investigation into New York Telephone. The report urged the state Public Service Commission to examine individual transactions between the phone company and other Nynex units as well as all management, construction and engineering contracts with affiliated companies.

It further suggested that the Public Service Commission begin civil penalty proceedings for alleged wrongdoing in New York Telephone transactions with affiliates. That includes, Mr. Brodsky said, any transactions arranged between participants at annual gatherings in Florida, nicknamed "pervert conventions," that were organized by an executive of Nynex's purchasing arm. The parties included several employees of Nynex, more than 30 suppliers, and prostitutes.

New York Telephone is in the middle of a major rate case in New York state. The company has asked for a rate increase of about $825 million beginning in January, a figure that some analysts say could rise beyond $1 billion after certain adjustments are made in subsequent months.

Assemblyman Brodsky, a Democrat from Westchester, said the committee inquiry into the Public Service Commission's performance started eight months ago. That was after the committee opposed a settlement agreement -- negotiated by the phone company and the PSC staff -- that would have ended any investigation into questionable transactions among Nynex affiliates.

A spokesman for New York Telephone said the report "fails to recognize the thousands of pages of testimony and hundreds of hours of direct questioning and cross examination" of outside experts who found no overpayments that boosted phone rates. Besides, he said, the phone company cut from the rate case any "questionable amounts arising from allegations of overpayments," and in March eliminated its purchasing unit's independent status, making it part of New York Telephone and New England Telephone.

Peter Bradford, chairman of the PSC, said that although he hadn't seen the committee report, New York Telephone customers "are not paying and will not pay for alleged improprieties." For the past five years, New York Telephone has been operating under a rate moratorium. "Since our investigation is far from over," he said, "it is hard for me to imagine any valid critique of it at this point."

But the report drew the endorsement ot the New York attorney general's office, which has been highly critical of the way the rate case has been handled. "We welcome the findings and hope the PSC will pay attention," said Charlie Donaldson, an assistant attorney general.
