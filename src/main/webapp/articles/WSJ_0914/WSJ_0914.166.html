Congress Votes Rise in Strategic Oil Reserve

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 09/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Congress approved legislation increasing the planned size of the Strategic Petroleum Reserve to one billion barrels from 750 million and setting up a pilot refined-product reserve. The measure was approved 391-0 in the House and by voice vote in the Senate. The Bush administration had threatened a veto over the billion-barrel size, but aides said yesterday that the president is expected to sign the measure.

Separately, Energy Secretary James Watkins told the Senate Energy Committee that, despite concerns about refinery capacity, the administration expects the nation to be able to get through the rest of the year without experiencing significant shortages of oil or refined products. But he added that the next meeting of the International Energy Agency on Sept. 26 will be "very key" in determining whether a multilateral drawdown of emergency oil stockpiles is needed.

In addition, Mr. Watkins unveiled a set of measures designed to boost domestic energy production and spur conservation. With this package, the department's efforts to date are aimed at offsetting the loss of more than 1.1 million barrels of oil daily by the end of 1991.

Separately, the Environmental Protection Agency released an analysis showing that clean-air legislation pending before a House-Senate conference committee would save 800,000 to one million barrels of oil a day by the year 2005, when the provisions are fully in place. Of this amount, 300,000 to 500,000 barrels would come from utilities' switching to natural gas from oil to meet requirements under sections of the bill aimed at curbing acid rain. The rest would result from provisions requiring cleaner-burning fuels and the use of nongasoline-powered fleet vehicles and cars in some cities.

The legislation authorizing the continued buildup of the reserve doesn't set a deadline for filling it, which currently contains about 590 million barrels of oil that the goverment began stockpiling in salt domes in Louisiana and Texas in response to the 1973-1974 Arab oil embargo. The 750 million mark was to have been reached by the mid-1990s.

The legislation also gives the administration additional flexibility to conduct a test of the SPR, something some lawmakers and oil-industry officials have been clamoring for to ensure that in the event of an emergency, any drawdown of oil would go smoothly. It also provides for a program in which the Energy Department would set up one or more reserves that would be separate from the SPR and contain a refined product, such as gasoline.

In increasing the reserve, the legislation also allows the U.S. to begin leasing some of the crude oil as a way to reduce the costs of filling it. In addition, it gives the president the authority to order a limited drawdown of the SPR to deal with a domestic disruption.

In his Energy Committee testimony, Secretary Watkins said, "I do believe we can manage" during the fourth quarter without serious shortages. He referred to the time between now and the end of the year as a "transitional period" when efforts to increase production and conservation are taking hold at the same time demand is increasing as winter approaches.

Still, he said the administration is "very concerned about the ability of refiners to continue operating at near-maximum capacity for an extended period of time." Moreover, he said there is a problem with the quality of oil coming on line to replace the lost Iraq and Kuwait crude and with the distribution of supplies. Currently, he said, gasoline inventories are tight but adequate.

In his testimony, Mr. Watkins said that "right now we don't see any across-the-board gross unfairness" by oil companies in responding to the Mideast crisis, though he added that "a few percent" are always going to try to take advantage of the situation. So far, he said, there has been just a "normal market response."

Mr. Watkins said the various measures the department has designed to increase domestic energy production and encourage industry to switch to natural gas or other fuels would offset 607,500 barrels of oil a day. Among major provisions, the department will work to resolve a problem over permits that is holding up production in the Beaufort Sea north of Alaska. In addition, the department urged Congress to open the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge for exploration.

Moreover, Mr. Watkins said the agency would work to expedite various natural-gas pipeline projects. He said the department wants to remove regulatory barriers that prevent the delivery of natural gas to California, where it could be used to recover hard-to-get oil.

The department also wants to speed the permit process of waste-to-energy plants and accelerate the conversion of the federal automobile fleet to non-gasoline fuels. As a first step, Mr. Watkins announced the award of a $910,740 contract to Ford Motor Co. and General Motors Corp. to provide 65 vehicles that can run on either gasoline or an alternative fuel.

The conservation measures are aimed at saving 530,000 barrels of oil a day. Mr. Watkins said the nation could save 30,000 to 80,000 barrels of oil each day by reducing the average octane level to 87 from 88.5. Octane measures an engine's anti-knock capabilities.
