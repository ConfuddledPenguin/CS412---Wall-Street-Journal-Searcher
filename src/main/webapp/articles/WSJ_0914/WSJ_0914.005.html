Japan's Economy Is Termed Robust, But Braking Is Seen

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/14/90

TOKYO -- The Bank of Japan's quarterly survey of Japanese business conditions shows a robust economy but analysts say the rise in interest rates and the drop in the stock market will eventually brake growth. Opinions are divided, however, about whether another increase in the official discount rate will be needed before that slowdown begins. The discount rate is the fee the central bank charges on bank loans.

The survey, known as the tankan, was conducted in August and took into account the rise in oil prices after Iraq's invasion of Kuwait in early August. The survey's results were released Tuesday.

Despite the jump in oil prices and August's drop in the stock market and rise in interest rates, "the more I look at the {tankan} the more I'm impressed with how robust the economy is," said Patricia Kuwayama, vice president of Morgan Guaranty Trust Co. of New York's economics department.

She said the outlook for most industries is the same or even improved since the previous survey as consumers and businesses continue to spend, fueling a domestic demand-led expansion that is spread evenly across the whole economy and regions of the country.

Other economic data this week support the bullish outlook of the survey. The Economic Planning Agency announced that machinery orders in July were up 24% from a year earlier and the Bank of Japan reported that the steady growth of personal consumer spending is expected to continue through this fiscal year, which ends March 31.

However, analysts believe rising interest rates and the slumping stock market will eventually slow the economy, especially capital spending.

"It will take some time for the effect of the higher interest rates to appear" but the effect will show up, said Kazuko Mizuno, economist at Baring Securities (Japan) Ltd.

Since May 1989, the BOJ has raised its discount rate five times from its historic low of 2.5%. It was last increased by 3/4 percentage point, to 6%, at the end of August.

With the supply of products tight, the labor shortage worsening and service costs rising, the timing of the rate boost was just right, said Kunio Miyamoto, director and chief economist of the Sumitomo Life Research Institute, an affiliate of Sumitomo Life Insurance Co.

Whether another rate increase will be needed depends on the situation in the Middle East and oil prices, he added.
