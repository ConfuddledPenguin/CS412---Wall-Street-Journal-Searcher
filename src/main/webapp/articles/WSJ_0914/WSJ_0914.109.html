Domestic Car, Truck Sales Rose 2.5% In Early September, Reversing Trend

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 09/14/90

DETROIT -- Sales of domestic cars and trucks gained momentum in early September, rising 2.5% on the heels of a weak August. It was the first 10-day period since late July in which the 10 major U.S. auto makers boosted sales from a year earlier. The modest increase surprised some analysts, who had expected car and truck sales would continue spiraling downward in the wake of rising gasoline prices, economic uncertainty and growing tensions in the Persian Gulf.

"It's as if the American consumer is oblivious" to these economic conditions, said David Healy, an analyst with Barclays de Zoete Wedd. "There doesn't seem to be much of an effect on people's willingness to buy cars."

U.S. auto makers sold 217,610 cars and trucks in the Sept. 1-10 period, compared with 212,334 in early September a year ago.

Sales of North American-made passenger cars rose 6.7% in early September, to 140,795 from 132,004 a year ago. General Motors Corp., Honda Motor Co. and Toyota Motor Corp. contributed the most to the overall gain.

Passenger car sales in the Sept. 1-10 period hit a seasonally adjusted annual sales rate of 7,130,000 cars, according to a Commerce Department formula. That rate is slightly lower than the 7,290,000 car-sales pace a year ago, and about even with the 7,110,000 cars in late August.

Chrysler Corp. fared the worst among the Big Three. The company's domestic car sales skidded 15.9%, while its domestic truck sales dropped 17%.

Ford Motor Co.'s domestic car sales were off 0.6%, despite a 13.9% increase in sales of the redesigned 1991 Ford Escort compact and the older version. Escort sales have been soft much of the year. But in early August, Ford started offering 4.8% financing on Escorts, as well as cash rebates. Ford's truck sales jumped 10.2% in the latest 10 days, largely on the strength of its Explorer sport-utility vehicle.

GM said sales of its domestic cars rose 6.4%. But the No. 1 auto maker's truck sales were down 13.8% from a year earlier.

Most of the Japanese auto makers that build and sell cars in the U.S. continued to show gains from a year ago.

Honda increased sales of its domestically made Accords and Civics by a whopping 89.1%, partly reflecting increased production. Toyota boosted sales of its domestically made cars by 70.9%.

Mitsubishi Motors Corp., however, saw sales of its U.S.-built cars drop 20.7%.
