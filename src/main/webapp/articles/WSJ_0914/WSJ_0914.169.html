What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/14/90

JUDGE SOUTER ACKNOWLEDGED an implicit constitutional right to privacy. However, the Supreme Court nominee, on the first day of his Senate confirmation hearings, declined requests to spell out what that right encompasses. Responding to questions that have been raised by Democratic senators and some liberal groups, Souter attempted to depict himself as a humane, compassionate judge. The hearing proceeded without rancor, even though Souter generally refused to give his views on Supreme Court decisions, including Roe vs. Wade, the 1973 ruling that recognized the right to abortion.

Nothing at the hearing, which will continue into next week, suggested that the New Hampshire federal judge faces any difficulty winning confirmation.

---

IRAN IS SEEKING to buy grain that the U.S. fears might be sent to Baghdad.

As Tehran apparently is planning big grain purchases from Argentina, there is concern that some of the shipments might be transferred to Iraq on humanitarian grounds, despite an international trade embargo. Meanwhile, Baker and Shevardnadze discussed whether to propose new U.N. actions that would tighten the embargo. In Baghdad, the Iraqis issued what some considered a warning of terrorism against American targets.

Japan pledged $2 billion in economic aid to Egypt, Turkey and Jordan, and said it would double a prior pledge of $1 billion to support Gulf forces.

---

Bonn and Moscow signed a 20-year friendship treaty forming the framework for broad West German economic assistance for the Soviet economy. Separately, West Germany's finance minister confirmed that his government is giving the Soviets an interest-free $1.9 billion loan.

---

Researchers said they "permanently reversed" diabetes in 10 of 13 laboratory rats by transplanting insulin-producing cells into the thymus gland of the animals. The novel approach of using the thymus as the transplant site could have promising implications for treating insulin-dependent diabetes in humans.

---

Gorbachev told Baker that another superpower summit meeting was possible before the end of the year. The secretary of state, winding up a Moscow visit, also met with Soviet Foreign Minister Shevardnadze to discuss remaining roadblocks to pending arms-control treaties.

---

South African youths went on a rampage in a commuter train in Johannesburg, hacking to death at least 26 people with spears and machetes and injuring about 100 as passengers leaped from the speeding coaches. Police gave no reason for the attack.

---

A Tennessee court granted joint custody of seven frozen embryos to a divorced couple. The Court of Appeals, an intermediate-level court, overturned a landmark ruling that had granted custody to the ex-wife. The embryos were created in vitro at a fertility clinic in December 1988, two months before the husband sued his wife for divorce.

---

A New York court overturned a ban on selling a book about the Israeli intelligence service. The appellate division of the New York supreme court lifted a state judge's order on Wednesday that had temporarily barred St. Martin's Press from continuing to sell the book.

---

West African troops evacuated 1,000 supporters of Liberia's slain President Doe from his executive mansion, while Monrovia was rocked by tribal reprisal killings. The U.N. appealed to the warring factions to allow safe passage of humanitarian aid.

---

The nation's space program was dealt a major blow by a Senate appropriations subcommittee, which wiped out nearly all new funding for the Bush administration's Moon and Mars initiative and cut $863.6 million from the space station's fiscal 1991 budget.

---

Nineteen people were formally charged with treason and murder in connection with the July coup attempt against Trinidad's government. The alleged coup plotters, arraigned in a court in Port of Spain, face the death penalty if convicted. Ninety-five others are still to be arraigned.

---

Sri Lankan troops broke Tamil rebels' three-month siege of a strategic northern fort in a surprise attack that left 100 guerrillas and 12 soldiers dead, military officials said. More than 350 soldiers took part in the pre-dawn assault on the fort in Jaffna.

---

Michigan scientists are developing a technique for inserting new genes into specific tissues in the body. In experiments on pigs, the researchers inserted a new gene into cells of an artery, and the genetically altered cells then began producing a new protein. The technique could be helpful in treating disease.
