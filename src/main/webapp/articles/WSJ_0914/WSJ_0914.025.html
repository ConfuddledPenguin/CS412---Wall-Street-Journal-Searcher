U.S. Agency Seeks Medicare Funds Improperly Paid

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 09/14/90

WASHINGTON -- The Department of Health and Human Services said it will contact more than one million employers in a program to collect as much as $600 million in Medicare payments that were improperly paid to people covered by private health insurance. Congress authorized the effort last year after this newspaper disclosed that Medicare had paid billions of dollars in health-insurance claims that should have been paid by private insurance companies.

The money was paid to working people over 65 and people over 65 who were covered by a working spouse's employer group health plan. In such cases, bills are supposed to be submitted to private insurance companies first, with Medicare picking up any qualified uncovered charges. Instead, the HHS inspector general found that many of these claims went directly to Medicare because corporations and their insurers didn't funnel them to private companies first.

Congress authorized the Health Care Financing Administration, which runs Medicare, to work with the Social Security Administration and the Internal Revenue Service to set up an extensive computer matching program to trace the employment of Medicare beneficiaries.

In addition, the HCFA has hired Group Health Inc. of New York to contact more than one million employers to determine if Medicare beneficiaries and their spouses were covered by private plans that should have paid their bills. This effort will start by the end of the year. Under the law passed last year, companies will face a $1,000 fine for each such request for information they fail to answer within 30 days.

"The 1989 legislation provides us with important new tools to identify cases in which Medicare can recover payments that should have been made by other parties," said HCFA Director Gail Wilensky.

In addition to the collection program, which could bring in as much as $600 million, the Justice Department has filed suits against several major insurance companies to recover funds. It is also in the process of launching a program to alert both employers and insurers to the risks they face by circumventing the Medicare payment rules.
