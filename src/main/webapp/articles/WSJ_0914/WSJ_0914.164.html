Accounting Panel Clears New Rules For Debt Issues

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 09/14/90

NEW YORK -- An accounting standards body voted to issue partial new guidelines on the way financial institutions account for debt securities held as investments. In a 15-0 vote at its meeting in Seattle, the Accounting Standards Executive Committee of the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants said it would require institutions to disclose information in the notes to financial statements about the amortized cost and market value of debt securities held as investments, as well as the gross unrealized gains and losses for various categories of debt securities it identified.

The committee's action represents a scaling back of a more stringent proposal the banking and accounting industries had opposed that would have required recognizing changes in the value of certain of the debt securities held as investments on the income statement. Currently, debt securities held as investments are marked at historical cost, while those held as trading assets are marked to market.

The accounting panel earlier this week softened its stand on the proposal after receiving overwhelmingly negative feedback from banks and accountants. Those groups criticized the original proposal because they say it would have created wide swings in earnings of financial institutions, rather than give investors a clearer picture of the value of debt securities held, as was intended.

The panel has yet to decide the issue of when to recognize a loss when an investment security declines in value. John L. Kreischer, committee chairman, said the panel would meet with the Financial Accounting Standards Board, the chief rule-making body, and staff of the Securities and Exchange Commission to try to reach a consensus on that issue.

The new guidelines will apply to all financial statements for periods ending after Dec. 15. Mr. Kreischer said the panel would probably issue the statement within 60 days.

The categories of debt securities addressed by the new guidelines include those issued by the U.S. Treasury and other U.S. corporations and agencies, government and government-agency securities, municipal debt and mortgage-backed securities.

Institutions also will be required to disclose the amount of debt securities due in one year or less, after one year through five years, five to 10 years and after 10 years. In each of those categories, the amortized cost and market value must be disclosed. Proceeds from the sale of debt securities held as investments and gross realized gains and losses on such sales also must be disclosed.

The changes were made as part of an effort to standardize accounting for debt securities by financial institutions. They also were prompted by concern about abuses of current guidelines. Critics say that under existing practice, financial institutions can enhance their financial profile by selling securities in which they have a profit while continuing to carry at cost a different batch of the same securities in which they have a paper loss.
