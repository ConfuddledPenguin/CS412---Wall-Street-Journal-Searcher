Meeting of G-7 Is Unlikely to Act To Bolster Dollar

  • Author: Tim Carrington
  • Date: 09/14/90

LONDON -- When deputies from the Group of Seven gathered in Paris this week, they reportedly expressed little dismay about the falling U.S. dollar. Economists predict that the mood of acceptance will extend to the official G-7 meeting in Washington a week from tomorrow. Specifically, forecasters expect financial leaders to stand by rather than intervene in the currency markets, even though the U.S. currency recently slumped to 1.53 German marks, a postwar low. "We're not looking for the G-7 to do anything at this stage," said Julian Callow, international economist with Chase Investment Bank Ltd.

The G-7 countries are the U.S., Japan, West Germany, Britain, France, Italy and Canada.

Mr. Callow and other Chase economists take the position that the G-7, officially the world's most powerful economic club, is in "demise," with each of the big economies going its own way for a time. The Chase team also predicts that the dollar will test new lows of 1.50 marks and 125 yen in the next three to six months.

"My feeling is that the economic fundamentals would indicate that the dollar should fall," said Bill Martin, chief economist at UBS Phillips & Drew in London. "It looks like the U.S. economy is moving into recession and that the Fed will ease" up by dropping interest rates.

European economists aren't very worried about the dollar's slide. "There's no reason for action now," said Adolf Ahnefeld, corporate economist at Bosch G.m.b.H. "The present value of the dollar is about right."

With the American economy edging toward recession, Europeans are cheered to see a glimmer of good news in the export picture. "European firms will mostly benefit from stronger U.S. economic activity," said Sudhir Junankar, economist with the Confederation of British Industries in London. "Higher exports make the U.S. less prone to full recession, and that's good news."

But if the dollar stays low, Europe could find itself inundated with comparatively cheap American products, warned David Mars, an economist with S.G. Warburg Securities in London. "The U.S. exports everything from vegetables to jumbo jets," he argued. "There's a tendency (in Europe) to underestimate the competitive pressure." He said he foresees a day when U.S. exports could force price cuts in Europe, and lead to calls for the G-7 to prop up the U.S. currency.

Another possibility is that the U.S. will be forced to keep rates higher than its sluggish economy would otherwise dictate, in order to compete for international savings it needs to finance its budget deficit. That could make it harder for the U.S. to jump-start its economy out of the current slump. However, the Chase report warned, "other G-7 members aren't likely to be prepared to `bail out' the U.S. by inflating."
