Hunt, Creditors Set A Tentative Accord Over Mineral Rights

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/14/90

DALLAS -- Nelson Bunker Hunt and his major creditors have tentatively settled a mineral-rights dispute that had been holding up the sale of more than $70 million of real estate from the oilman's estate. Hit by heavy losses from their investments in silver and oil, Mr. Hunt and his brother, William Herbert Hunt, filed for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code in 1988. Under a reorganization plan negotiated with creditors last year, Nelson Bunker Hunt was permitted to keep a 50% interest in the mineral rights to certain non-producing properties. Mr. Hunt and his creditors subsequently disagreed over which properties were included under that clause.

Under an agreement filed in federal bankruptcy court here this week, Mr. Hunt will receive $315,000 from the sale of two family-owned Canadian oil companies, a 50% interest in the coal and lignite leases on one Texas ranch and a 60% interest in oil and gas leases on one Oklahoma property. Although mineral rights to certain other properties will be shared, control of most will revert to the trustee liquidating Mr. Hunt's estate. U.S. Bankruptcy Judge Harold Abramson is expected to rule on the settlement next month.

"We're moving full-speed ahead," said trustee Carter Pate, who said settlement would allow him to finalize sales on Mr. Hunt's Circle T Ranch near Dallas and several other properties in Texas and Kentucky.

The Hunt brothers and their creditors have yet to settle a dispute involving how much the Texas oilmen should be paid for their efforts to help the liquidating trustees in both cases sell their collections of rare coins and antiques.
