Budget Talks Offer Few Signs Of an Accord --- Optimism Remains as Aides To Bush, Congress Make Some Similar Proposals

  • Author: Jackie Calmes and David Wessel
  • Date: 09/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Budget talks continued for the seventh consecutive day at Andrews Air Force Base near here with few hints of a breakthrough on crucial remaining issues, such as President Bush's proposal to cut capital-gains taxes. White House officials indicated they expect the talks to continue into the weekend. Congressional and administration negotiators, who met until about 1 a.m. Thursday, divided into two groups when the talks resumed yesterday to discuss taxes and ways to enforce a five-year deficit-reduction pact.

"We still have got a big gap," Rep. Silvio Conte (R., Mass.), one of the negotiators, said yesterday afternoon. Still, most of those close to the talks remained confident an accord eventually will be reached, if only because the economic and political stakes are so high.

The outlines of a possible compromise on taxes are becoming apparent as similar proposals to tax luxury goods, increase taxes on alcoholic beverages and raise energy taxes appear in offers made by both Democrats and Republicans in the closed-door talks. Analyses of each side's plans showed that the Republicans' proposal would go easier on rich people than would the Democrats' proposal.

People close to the talks indicated yesterday that an increase in the cigarette tax -- currently 16 cents a pack -- is also likely to be supported by both sides.

On spending, both sides have proposed making Medicare beneficiaries pay more for their health insurance, but negotiators have scaled back a plan to increase premiums for upper-income Americans in the face of strong criticism, people close to the talks said.

On cutting capital-gains taxes, about the only agreement is that the tax cut would cost the Treasury billions. GOP negotiators Wednesday acquiesced to figures of the bipartisan Joint Committee on Taxation showing that the Republican capital-gains proposal would mean $24.5 billion less in revenue over five years, according to Rep. Bill Archer (R., Texas). Previously, the Republicans said the tax cut would essentially break even over five years.

To offset that loss, the administration proposed increasing levies on luxury goods, ozone-depleting chemicals and some petroleum-based products, and indexing certain excise taxes for inflation -- all ideas that have at least some acceptance among the Democrats.

The administration proposal would cut the capital-gains tax, now as high as 33%, to a maximum rate of 15%. The cut would only apply to individuals. The administration's previous proposal called for a top rate of 19.6% for assets sold after three years. Republicans continue to resist the Democrats' demand that any cut in capital-gains taxes be accompanied by an increase in income-tax rates for upper-income Americans.

Analyses of the competing tax packages by the Joint Tax Committee show that the Republican plan would reduce the burden on taxpayers with incomes above $50,000 and increase it on those with incomes below that. The dividing line in the Democratic tax package is $20,000.

The contrasts are particularly sharp for upper-income Americans because Republicans are pressing the cut in capital-gains taxes while Democrats would impose a new surcharge on the richest Americans. For taxpayers earning more than $200,000 a year, the Joint Tax Committee analysis shows, the GOP plan would cut taxes by 5.5% while the Democratic plan would increase them by 6.7%.

Both sides are now supporting a new 10% tax on luxury goods, although they define luxuries slightly differently. Both parties would tax electronic goods above $1,000, jewelry above $5,000 and furs above $500. For autos, the Democrats' tax would kick in above $25,000, the Republicans' above $30,000. Republicans have a $30,000 threshold for yachts while the Democrats proposed $50,000. Democrats would also tax the entire cost of private planes, while Republicans would exempt them. Aircraft makers Cessna and Beech are based in Senate Republican Leader Robert Dole's home state of Kansas.

The Democrats' version would raise $1.3 billion the first year and $10.2 billion over five years while the Republican plan would raise about $900 million in 1991 and $7.8 billion through 1995.

On "sin" taxes, Democrats want to double the levies on beer and wine, and index the taxes for inflation as of 1992. That would raise $1.5 billion in 1991 and $8.3 billion through 1995. An increase of $2 a proof gallon in the tax on liquor, currently $12.50 a proof gallon, would raise another $400 million the first year and $2 billion over five years. A proof gallon is the equivalent of 100-proof liquor, or 50% alcohol.

Republicans would more than double beer and wine taxes, which haven't been increased since 1951. But they wouldn't change the taxes on hard liquor, last raised in 1984.

Despite initial speculation that the Mideast crisis would doom any new energy taxes, a broad-based tax remains a possibility, both to raise revenue and to encourage conservation.

Democrats propose to tax all sources of energy roughly based on their costs at a rate of 4%, raising an estimated $7.8 billion the first year and $58.4 billion over five years. While Republicans are said to be considering some form of energy tax, their formal proposal has been limited to a one-cent-a-gallon tax on gasoline and other refined petroleum products, excluding feedstocks.

The Democrats want a seven-cent increase in the nine-cent-a-gallon federal gasoline tax, which would raise $5.4 billion the first year and $33.2 billion over five years. The Democrats' broad-based energy tax would add about four cents a gallon more to the cost of gasoline at the pump.

Both sides would increase the airline ticket tax, and they appear to be moving toward raising taxes on life insurance companies by about $1 billion in the first year, and about $7 billion over five years.

Two ideas common to both parties' plans would each raise about $100 million a year. One would extend the 0.1-cent-a-gallon gasoline tax that flows into a trust fund to clean up leaking underground storage tanks at filling stations. Another would raise levies on ozone-depleting chemicals.
