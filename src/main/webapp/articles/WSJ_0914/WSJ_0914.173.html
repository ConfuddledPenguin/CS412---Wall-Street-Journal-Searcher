Ford Rolls Back Price Increases On Some Models

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 09/14/90

DEARBORN, Mich. -- Ford Motor Co. has scaled back some of its 1991 model prices increases, slashing more than $600 from the previously announced prices of certain Taurus models. The move indicates that Ford believed its original price increases were too steep for today's highly competitive car market.

"I think Ford is listening to what's going on in the market place," said Jim Hoffer, manager of Jordan Ford in Mishawaka, Ind. Mr. Hoffer, who was critical of Ford's original price strategy, said he's "very happy" with the new prices.

A Ford spokesman said that the new price list "reflects changes in the market and what competitors have done."

Some of the biggest rollbacks came on the high-volume Taurus midsized models. The Taurus, a mainstay of Ford's product line, has come under increasing competitive pressure in the past year. Sales of the five-year-old model are down 11.4% through the end of August, nearly twice the overall market decline.

Ford said the cheapest 1991 Taurus L sedan now will have a suggested list price of $13,352, up 5.6%, or $712, from a year ago. But that is $365 less than the original 1991 price of $13,717 that Ford announced to dealers in August. The 1991 Taurus GL wagon had an even sharper price rollback, dropping to $14,990 from an initial 1991 price of $15,599. A year ago, the GL wagon started at $14,722.

Even after the rollbacks, the Taurus faces stiff price competition. Last week, Honda Motor Co. announced a mere $200 increase for its Accord model, which was beefed up in size and power a year ago to compete head on with the Taurus in the midpriced family car market. The cheapest 1991 Accord with an automatic transmission will start at $13,295. That is $57 less than a comparable Taurus. Before the price rollback, the Honda cost $422 less.

Yet another Taurus competitor, the 1991 Chevrolet Lumina, will start at $12,870.

Ford had earlier tried to undercut these rivals by announcing a $750 rebate on the 1991 Taurus even before it hit the showrooms. General Motors Corp. also has announced discounts of $500 to $1,000 on 1991 models.

Ford chopped prices on some other key models, too. The base price of the cheapest 1991 Thunderbird will jump 2.3% to $15,318. Earlier, it had been slated for a 5.4% price increase.

The price of the cheapest four-door, four-wheel-drive Ford Explorer will be $18,905, down from the previously scheduled price of $19,066. The latest price still represents a 1.6% increase from the Explorer model's $18,605 introductory price in March.

The new list isn't all good news for car buyers. On a few models, Ford actually boosted prices from the earlier 1991 list. Consider the 1991 Mustang LX convertible, which now has a base price of $16,222, compared with $15,852 on the earlier, August price list. The latest increase means the Mustang convertible will jump 7.1%, or $1,081, from the year-ago base price of $15,141.
