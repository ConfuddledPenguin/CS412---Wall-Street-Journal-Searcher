Law -- Legal Beat: Tough Tests for Property Owners In Forfeitures Tied to Drug Cases

  • Author: Wade Lambert and James S. Hirsch
  • Date: 08/20/90

A federal appeals court ruled that even innocent property owners must meet stiff standards in challenging drug-law forfeitures. The U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals in Manhattan said the government can seize property on which drug transactions occurred unless the owner "did all that reasonably could be expected to prevent further illicit use of the premises."

Although the law protects innocent property owners from having their buildings seized, the appeals court said lack of knowledge isn't enough to avoid forfeiture -- property owners must also prove that they didn't consent to the drug activity. To prove lack of consent, the owner must show that strong steps were taken to deter the drug violations, the court ruled.

"Consent in this situation must be something more than a state of mind," the appeals-court panel said. Affirming a lower-court ruling, the appellate judges said, "Once an owner has knowledge, he gives his consent to the narcotics activity unless he then does all that reasonably could be expected to prevent further illicit use of the premises."

Federal appeals courts and district judges have been divided on the innocent property-owner issue. In a similar case earlier this month, a U.S. district judge in Manhattan ordered the government to return the property after finding that the law "doesn't require claimant to prove that he has done all he could reasonably be expected to do to prevent" the illegal activity.

The federal appeals court in Manhattan, however, said the seriousness of the drug problem must be considered in deciding how the forfeiture law applies.

"Congress intended forfeiture to be a powerful weapon in the war on drugs. The illicit sale and use of drugs is taking an ever increasing toll on our nation," the appeals court said. "Given the present circumstances, defining `consent' . . . as the failure to take all reasonable steps to prevent illicit use of premises once one acquires knowledge of that use is entirely appropriate."

In the case before the appeals court, the owner of the property, Manhattan-based West 141st St. Realty Corp., argued that the company took adequate measures to stop drug activity at the six-story Manhattan building by refusing to accept rent from tenants who were arrested in a drug raid. The company claimed that the trial judge erred by telling the jury that the innocent-owner defense could prevail only if Realty proved that it had taken every reasonable measure to prevent the drug trafficking.

Based on the trial judge's instructions, a federal jury decided in favor of the government and approved the forfeiture.

Attorneys for the company couldn't be reached for comment.

JUDGE RAISES THE STAKES in suit against USX and steelworkers' union.

U.S. District Judge Edwin L. Nelson in Alabama said an employee suit over a concessionary labor contract can be certified as a class action with regard to liability and compensation. Thirty-eight current or former workers of a USX Corp. plant near Birmingham allege that they lost income from an allegedly illegal pact between USX and the union, the United Steelworkers.

If the Pittsburgh-based steel and energy concern and the union lose the case, they would have to compensate about 4,000 workers.

Prior to the judge's decision this week, J. Vernon Patrick Jr., who is representing the plaintiffs, submitted documents to the court alleging $144 million in damages. Mr. Patrick said he will seek treble damages under the federal Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act.

"It's been a hard-fought battle, but it's only the battle and not the war," he said.

USX and union officials declined to comment.

The suit, filed two years ago, alleges that a 1983 labor contract covering steelworkers at USX's Fairfield, Ala., plant violated federal labor laws because two union representatives, E.B. Rich and Thermon Phillips, received special pension benefits from the company.

Last month, a federal jury in Birmingham found USX and the union officials guilty of violating federal labor laws in a pension bribery scheme. USX and the union said they would appeal the decision.

The civil case, which had been suspended while the criminal case was in progress, is due to go to trial in September 1991.

SECURITIES INDUSTRY SUES to halt Florida broker-arbitration law.

The Securities Industry Association and eight member brokerage houses sued the Florida comptroller last Friday, challenging the constitutionality of a recently enacted law that allows investors the option of bringing their disputes with brokers to arbitrators not affiliated with the securities industry. Florida is the first state to enact a law that requires brokers to allow their customers the freedom to take their disputes to the American Arbitration Association, an independent forum, or to arbitration panels subsidized by the industry.

AAA president Robert Coulson has hailed the Securities and Investor Protection Act as a safeguard for small investors' "right to select." But last Friday, William Fitzpatrick, the SIA's general counsel, said a provision of the Florida law unfairly singles out arbitration clauses in brokerage firm contracts. Mr. Fitzpatrick cited a recent U.S. Supreme Court decision that the Federal Arbitration Act prohibits states from singling out arbitration agreements for tighter controls than other business contracts. In that ruling, the high court knocked down a Massachusetts regulation similar to Florida's law.

Mr. Fitzpatrick contends that if the Florida law stands, each of the other 49 states might also impose their own special conditions on arbitration agreements. "Soon we will have 50 different versions of what an arbitration agreement should look like and nobody would use arbitration," Mr. Fitzpatrick said.

A spokesman for the Florida comptroller said the office had not received the suit and declined to comment. The bill goes into effect Oct. 1.

HOUSTON LAWYER gets jail term for anti-Semitic remarks about judge.

State District Judge Michael McSpadden sentenced attorney Jack Love to six months in jail along with a $1,500 fine for the derogatory comments, which were aimed at County Court-at-Law Judge Sherman Ross.

According to testimony, Mr. Love had become angry last June 21 after Judge Ross adjourned a court session without setting a trial date for one of Mr. Love's clients. Witnesses testified Mr. Love denounced Judge Ross to several court workers, referring to him as a "little Jew" and threatening to burn a cross on his lawn.

After learning of the remarks, Judge Ross scheduled a hearing last month and held Mr. Love in contempt, sentencing him to 90 days in jail and imposing a $500 fine. The action was appealed to Judge McSpadden who last week upheld the contempt finding and increased the penalties. Calling Mr. Love's behavior "obscene" and "reprehensible," Judge McSpadden said he would ask the State Bar of Texas to look into the matter.

Edward Wheeler, a Pasadena, Texas, attorney representing Mr. Love, had argued that a contempt finding wasn't warranted because the court wasn't in session when Mr. Love made his remarks, and some were made outside of the courtroom. He also contended that the remarks were protected by the First Amendment.

Mr. Wheeler called the punishment "harsh" and said he would attempt to get the penalties overturned.

ODOR ORDINANCE in Jacksonville is struck down by judge for vagueness.

The city of Jacksonville, Fla., lost a round in its three-year battle against foul-smelling industries when Duval Circuit Judge John Southwood declared that criminal provisions of the city's ordinance aimed at stemming odors are unconstitutional.

The judge said the criminal portions of the ordinance, which has been on the books for three years, failed to provide an objective standard as to what constitutes a criminal violation. Criminal penalties included a fine of up to $500 a day and 90 days in prison for each violation.

"What we're saying is there is not an ascertainable standard of conduct to measure what is criminal behavior," said Edward Booth Sr., a Jacksonville attorney for SCM Glidco Organics Corp., a chemical company that makes various products from wood-pulp residue and pine tars, one of two companies that challenged the criminal provisions of the odor ordinance. "It was vague and ambiguous," he said. Mr. Booth said SCM Glidco, a unit of London-based Hanson PLC, has made every effort to eliminate offensive odors by installing the best technology to cope with smell problems.

Daniel D. Richardson, chief of the environmental law division for the city, said Jacksonville, which has a large wood-pulp and related chemical manufacturing industry, will continue to battle foul smells with its civil penalties.

It will also assess whether to appeal the ruling or to fashion a new criminal ordinance.
