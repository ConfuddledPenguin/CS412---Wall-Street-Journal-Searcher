Foreign Exchange: Dollar Likely to Fall More Against Mark But Central Bank Intervention Is Unclear

  • Author: Alison Woods Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/20/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar's slide to new lows against the mark is expected to continue and traders say it is unclear whether central banks will move to bolster the U.S. currency. Rumors of impending central bank intervention to prop up the dollar ran through the currency markets last week as it moved to a new postwar low of 1.5437 marks Thursday before recovering slightly to 1.5495 marks late Friday, down more than four pfennigs on the week.

With the dollar's decline orderly, major central banks haven't been seen intervening in the market.

Currency market participants generally expect to see still weaker dollar levels against the mark in coming weeks as evidence of the U.S. economy's sluggishness continues to mount.

But since the dollar is in new territory, traders are hesitant to predict just how low it might drop.

"From a charting perspective, we're at previously unknown levels," says Trevor Woodland, chief corporate trader at Harris Trust & Savings Bank in Chicago. "I tend to feel that 1.50 deutsche marks is an obvious psychological support level, and I think we could go another two or three pfennigs lower."

The dollar's recent slide has reflected a weakening U.S. economy -- apparent in recent reports on declining housing starts and falling auto sales.

The forecast for the U.S. economy is so gloomy that even the Iraq-Kuwait crisis -- which under other circumstances would have drawn strong "flight-to-quality" support for the dollar -- isn't giving much of a boost to the dollar.

Andrew Rojek, international market analyst at Merrill Lynch & Co., agrees that the 1.50-mark level is a critical one for the dollar, and that the dollar could push even below that. "If we break 1.50 convincingly, I look for 1.4890 as the next target," he says. "But it's hard to say because we haven't been there, ever."

In spite of the central bank intervention rumors that have persisted in the foreign exchange markets, analysts say the dollar's weakness may not prompt central banks to support the currency.

They note that the weak dollar is one of the few things the U.S. economy has going for it as it helps boost U.S. exports. "I imagine the Fed is quite happy with the weak dollar," says Graham Beale, chief trader at Hongkong & Shanghai Banking Corp. in New York.

But traders say that if the dollar's drop gets out of control, the central banks could step in.

"What the central banks don't want to see is disorderly markets," says Mr. Woodland of Harris Trust. "Whether the authorities intervene depends less on how low the dollar goes than on how it gets there."

"Sliding half a pfennig to pfennig at a time, as the dollar has been" doesn't represent a disorderly situation, Mr. Woodland says.

Mr. Beale agrees: "I think it would take a breaking of 1.50 marks, very aggressively, to prompt Federal Reserve or other central bank intervention."

While the dollar has hit new lows against the mark, it is faring better against the yen, which is still dogged by market concerns about the Japanese inflation rate and the effect of rising oil prices on the Japanese economy.

But analysts believe the yen is due to climb higher. Merrill Lynch's Mr. Rojek forecasts that the dollar could fall to as low as 146.80 yen soon, down from 147.58 yen in New York late Friday.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the dollar was quoted at 147.57 yen, up from Friday's close there of 147.35 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was at 1.5540 marks.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery settled at $409.60 an ounce, up 40 cents. Estimated volume was a very heavy seven million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Monday, gold was quoted at $412.50 an ounce.
