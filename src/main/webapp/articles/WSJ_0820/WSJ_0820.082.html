International: Brazilian State-Run Firms To Be Sold to Cut Deficit

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/20/90

BRASILIA, Brazil -- The government said it would shortly sell 10 state-run companies and its minority holdings in one petrochemical plant and two major petroleum refineries in an effort to streamline Brazil's economy and reduce a national deficit of $31 billion. The sale of state companies is a key to the sweeping economic reforms announced March 16 by President Fernando Collor de Mello.

The package, which froze $115 billion in savings accounts and money market deposits for 18 months and made deep cuts in spending, curbed record inflation of almost 5,000% a year.

The companies, including some steel concerns were expected to bring between $9 billion and $10 billion in revenue, said Eduardo Modiano, head of the government's commission on privatizing state firms.

Mr. Collor, who took office March 15, also promised to sell 200 state-run enterprises with a combined debt of $62 billion by 1992.

Brazilian law permits foreign investors to purchase up to 40% of investment shares with voting rights. Foreign investors can buy 100% of a company's shares without voting privileges.

Foreign companies with subsidiaries in Brazil can use the frozen new cruzados blocked in the Central Bank under the plan to purchase shares with no limits.
