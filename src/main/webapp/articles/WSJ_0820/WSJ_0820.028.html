Program Trading's Share of Big Board Volume Declines

  • Author: Peter Pae
  • Date: 08/20/90

NEW YORK -- Program trading, reflecting for the first time the effects of a newly approved "circuit breaker," declined to 8.4% of New York Stock Exchange volume, or 17.5 million shares a day, in the week ended Aug. 3. In the previous week, program trading accounted for 9.9%, or 17 million shares a day, the Big Board said in its weekly report on computer-aided trading strategies.

At 9:53 a.m. on Aug. 3, a "circuit breaker" designed to restrict program trading went into effect after the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 50 points in reaction to the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait. The restriction requires index-arbitrage traders to wait for stock prices to rise from previous trades before executing the stock side of their transactions.

In the first thirty minutes before the restriction on program trading, the Big Board said CS First Boston Inc.'s First Boston Corp. unit sold about 1.4 million shares to facilitate customer orders while Kidder, Peabody & Co. sold 633,000 shares in program trading for its own account and 387,000 as an agent for customers.

Another circuit breaker called the sidecar went into effect at 1:19 p.m. when September futures on the S&P 500 index showed a loss of 12 points, the Big Board said. The sidecar, a special Big Board computer file, handled program trading orders for a five-minute period. The Big Board said Bear Stearns & Co. sold 683,000 shares in program trading for its own account while Susquehanna Investment Group sold 535,000 shares between 1 p.m. and 2 p.m.

The Dow Jones Industrial then reversed its fall, rising 52 points between 2 p.m. and 3 p.m. During that time Kidder Peabody sold 506,00 shares for its own account and bought 147,000 shares as an agent. Goldman, Sachs & Co. bought 465,000 shares for its own account or as an agent, and Merrill Lynch & Co. bought 408,000 shares as an agent.

For the week, the five most active program traders, which accounted for 60% of all program trading on the exchange were: Kidder Peabody (18.1 million shares), Morgan Stanley & Co. (12.5 million), Susquehanna (9.2 million), Bear Stearns (6.4 million) and PaineWebber Inc. (6.4 million).

The Big Board said of the five most active program traders, Kidder Peabody, Susquehanna and Bear Stearns were primarily involved in trading for their own accounts, while the other two firms executed most of their program trading for customers.

Program trading is the purchase or sale of a group of at least 15 and often hundreds of different stocks. Stock-index arbitrage involves stock purchases and sales with offsetting trades in futures and options. For the week, stock-index arbitrage was led by Kidder Peabody and Susquehanna, which accounted for 55% of the total.
