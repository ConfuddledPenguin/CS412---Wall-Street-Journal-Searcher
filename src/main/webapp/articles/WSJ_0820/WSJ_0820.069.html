Plastics Industry, Seeking to Head Off Packaging Bans, Plans Recycling Plant

  • Author: Randolph B. Smith
  • Date: 08/20/90

The plastics industry, seeking to prevent further bans on plastic packaging, plans to open the nation's largest plant to recycle the polystyrene widely used for fast-food containers. National Polystyrene Recycling Co., a joint venture of major plastics makers that owns the plant, says the facility will start operating in October in Corona, Calif., east of Los Angeles. It will have a capacity to recycle as much as 13 million pounds of polystyrene a year, including foam plastic food containers, hard plastic and packing materials.

James Schneiders, president of the Lincolnshire, Ill., joint venture, says he expects the plant to operate at a profit by 1992, despite doubts among environmentalists that polystyrene recycling can be economical. The company's success will depend on whether it can persuade schools, institutions and fast-food chains to separate polystyrene and ship it to the plant. Mr. Schneiders says the plant will pay the market price of four cents a pound, but won't subsidize transportation costs. So far, about 20 elementary schools and a university have signed up. "We have just started to organize our collection programs," Mr. Schneiders says.

A pilot recycling program has already persuaded the Los Angeles Unified School District to drop plans to ban foam plastic food containers.

Julie Korenstein, a school board member, says the school district decided 18 months ago to phase out polystyrene because of environmental concerns. But the cash-strapped district had hoped for another solution because paper containers would cost double the $1.9 million a year spent for polystyrene, she says.

"The pressure we applied forced the industry to find better ways of producing and recycling plastic," Ms. Korenstein says. She says a ban won't be necessary if the new plant is as successful as a four-month pilot program in which 1.5 million plastic lunch containers from 17 schools were recycled. The district saved $400 in landfill costs and earned $1,200 for the plastic, but didn't incur extra transportation costs. The district hopes to increase its savings by expanding the program to 200 elementary schools next year, Ms. Korenstein says.

The plastics industry joint venture is planning a total of five recycling plants nationally to prevent the spread of bans and restrictions adopted in Minneapolis, Newark, N.J., and other communities because of the absence of recycling facilities. A pilot plant in Leominster, Mass., serves more than 450 McDonald's restaurants and 200 school systems. Three other commercial plants are scheduled to open in Philadelphia, Chicago and San Francisco by the end of this year. The industry has set a goal of recycling 25% of fast-food polystyrene by 1995, up from about 1% currently.

In the Los Angeles area, National Polystyrene Recycling is contacting fast-food chains, hospitals, Disneyland and other large producers of plastic waste. The company hopes that customers will save money through reduced landfill costs and payments they receive for plastic. But, Mr. Schneiders concedes, "a lot will depend" on whether customers can get discounts from their regular trash haulers to offset the added cost of collecting and transporting plastic to the recycling plant.

The plant, which will cost less than $5 million and be operated by closely held Talco Recycling Inc., will wash the plastic, grind it up and convert it into "high quality" pellets. These will be sold for 40 cents or more a pound to makers of plastic products, such as trash containers, trays, foam insulation and office products.
