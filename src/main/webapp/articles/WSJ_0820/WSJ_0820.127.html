International: Perez Hints Venezuela, Others Will Lift Oil Output

  • Author: Jose de Cordoba
  • Date: 08/20/90

CARACAS, Venezuela -- President Carlos Andres Perez strongly suggested that Venezuela and other OPEC countries will soon increase production to make up for embargoed Iraqi and Kuwaiti oil. "OPEC has the obligation to maintain production at the 22.5 million barrels a day level agreed to at the last OPEC conference," said Mr. Perez during an interview at his ornate office in the presidential palace here.

But Mr. Perez said Venezuela, like Saudi Arabia, wanted if possible to reach agreement with the other members of OPEC, or the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, to "provisionally" increase their production quotas. The higher production would make up a large part of the roughly four million barrels a day of Kuwaiti and Iraqi oil removed from the world market by United Nations sanctions following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait.

Under such an OPEC agreement, Venezuela could increase its production by up to 500,000 barrels a day from its current OPEC quota of 1.95 million barrels a day. "OPEC must maintain its cohesion," said Mr. Perez.

Mr. Perez said a steep price rise resulting from a failure to replace the lost production would be "disastrous" for the world economy, and especially for the economies of developing countries.

Venezuela, along with Saudi Arabia, has issued a call for an OPEC meeting to consider a production increase. But as energy minister Celestino Armas flew to Geneva Saturday to meet with his Saudi counterpart, only Ecuador and the United Arab Emirates had agreed to attend the meeting.

Venezuelan officials said the proposed meeting would be unlikely to produce an agreement on quotas, since several member countries are afraid of crossing Iraq. If the meeting fails, then OPEC members who are in a position to increase their production -- Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, Nigeria and the United Arab Emirates -- would probably agree among themselves to temporarily apportion the four million barrels a day oil-production shortfall until the crisis is over.

The officials, who are anxious to preserve OPEC, said it would be possible to reassign the shortfall under an OPEC regulation that permits re-allotment of quotas that are not being used by member nations. At any rate, the issue should be settled this week, the officials said.

Mr. Perez, who has been accused at home and in the U.S. of waffling on the issue of raising Venezuelan production, denied that he has been pressured by the U.S. to do so. "The U.S. attitude towards Venezuela's position has been one of frank gratitude," Mr. Perez said.

During the interview, Mr. Perez repeatedly emphasized what he called OPEC's "compromise" to produce 22.5 million barrels a day of oil for the world market, and the calamitous consequences for developing nations in particular of another oil shock. Mr. Perez said that Venezuela had "unbreakable obligations" to supply the U.S. and Latin America. "Brazil used to get 200,000 barrels a day from Iraq," he said. "They are living from day to day. Venezuela will have to help."

Mr. Armas, the energy minister, said that while high oil inventories had so far helped cushion the crisis for industrial countries like Japan, countries like Argentina and Brazil, with small or nonexisting inventories, were fast approaching a "critical situation."

Mr. Armas said that most of the projected increase in Venezuelan production would be heavier crudes. That could cause some marketing and refining headaches: In most instances, the refining of heavy crudes provides a higher percentage of fuel oil, for which Venezuela would have to find new markets, said a former senior official of Petroleos de Venezuela, the state oil company.

Mr. Armas said that at the beginning of the Gulf crisis, Venezuela had briefly raised production over its OPEC quota "out of nervousness." But he said the increase sent the wrong signal to the market and the country was now keeping within its quota.
