The Soviets' Big Aspirin Headache

  • Author: P.J. Wingate
  • Date: 08/20/90

I met Boris Roshnikoff a few months ago browsing in a drugstore in Wilmington, Del., where I had gone to buy a bottle of aspirin. He was standing in front of a shelf filled with various brands of headache tablets and was writing in a small notebook. "Excuse me, sir," he said after I had made my choice, "but why did you choose one of the most expensive brands of aspirin on display here?"

His question startled me, but his manner was so courteous and his accent such an intriguing mixture of Oxford English and Slavic overtones that I answered, explaining that I always bought the more expensive brand because it contained an agent to neutralize the aspirin, which can irritate the stomachs of some people.

"Are you a chemist?" he asked. When I admitted that I was, he closed his notebook and launched into a series of questions about the chemical composition of aspirin tablets. I finally broke in with a question of my own.

"Why are you so interested in aspirin?"

"I am a Russian," he replied, "and I came over here with one of the advance groups which preceded President Gorbachev's summit visit. I wanted to look at the aspirin situation in the U.S. because we seem to have a perpetual shortage of it in the small town where I live. I found that there is a plentiful supply of aspirin in Washington, and it comes in all sorts of packages and under a dozen or two name brands. So when I got the chance to visit an old friend here in Wilmington I decided to see how the supply was in a small town."

It occurred to me that Delaware's largest city is not exactly a country village, but it also occurred to me that you probably could find a dozen or so brands of aspirin in just about every American country village; even a crossroads filling station is likely to carry three or four brands. So I said nothing about community size. Instead, I complimented him on his excellent English and continued our conversation about aspirin.

"It is always available in Moscow," he said of the elusive pain killer, "and my neighbors asked me so often during the past year to bring them some that I finally asked a deputy minister of supply if there was a national shortage. He said he had not heard of any and that the same amount had been produced during each of the past three years, and all of it had been distributed. But his records did not show where it had been shipped."

He asked me how many brands of aspirin we had in the U.S. I told him it might run as high as a hundred or two hundred, since I knew that most chains and some individual stores sold their own special brands. He carefully recorded this information in his notebook, which contained a list of the brands of aspirin he had discovered.

"Do all these stores and chains manufacture aspirin?" he asked.

"No," I told him. "There probably are only four or five producers of aspirin in the country and they sell to the many others who buy in bulk for compounding and conversion to tablets or capsules. Sometimes the basic manufacturers do everything, including putting the tablets into bottles, while the company which sells to the public provides nothing except the labels."

"Does the government run controls on all these hundred or so different brands and see that the supply is adequate for each brand all over the country?"

I said I was sure the government did nothing to prevent shortages and that the quality and supply of aspirin were backed only by the efforts of the many companies out to make a profit.

A few minutes later, Mr. Roshnikoff and I parted company, and I presume he is back in Russia now. But his questions made me think, and on reflection I'm sure I was too conservative in my estimate of how many brands of aspirin are sold here. There are probably several hundred brands in any big city alone, since a great many stores carry "generic aspirin" under their own labels. All told, there may be more than a thousand brands of aspirin available one place or another in the U.S. I wish I had told him that.

I wish even more that I had told him that if the production and distribution of aspirin were turned over to just one government agency in Washington, D.C., the American bureaucracy would soon find a way to have every one of the thousand brands of aspirin in short supply. This is the economic lesson I hope Mr. Roshnikoff took home with him.

---

Mr. Wingate nurses his occasional headaches in Wilmington, Del.
