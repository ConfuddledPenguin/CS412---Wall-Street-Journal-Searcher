More Franchises Get A Mexican Accent

MORE U.S. franchisers are expanding south of the border.

Partly in response to a Mexican effort to encourage franchising, 20 American franchisers so far this year have registered to open units in Mexico -- matching the entire 1989 total, says Michael Brennan, a franchising attorney in Chicago. Mr. Brennan and others say the figures suggest Mexico eventually could rival Europe as a foreign destination for U.S. firms.

Kwik Kopy Corp., a Houston franchiser of photocopying centers, recently signed an agreement to open 100 units in Mexico over the next few years. The first is expected to start up next month. Philadelphia's West Coast Video Inc., a franchiser of video-rental stores, is seeking a Mexican partner to open five units in Mexico City, with one additional unit to open every six weeks thereafter.

Until this year, Mexican law severely restricted the control a foreign franchiser could exert over its franchisees. But in January the government exempted such businesses from many of the old rules and granted franchisers an array of new powers.

Mexico's growing middle class and favorable economic trends and tax structure bode well for franchising in Mexico, as does a free-trade agreement that the country is expected to reach with the U.S. by the end of 1991.

However, Mr. Brennan warns, "there are still some problems." For example, Mexican law prohibits non-compete clauses in contracts and forbids franchise agreements of any kind that run for more than 10 years.

Thriving Scams Aim

At Small Companies

INFLATABLE RAFTS have floated into the sea of small business scams.

The U.S. attorney in Los Angeles recently obtained fraud convictions against two men who allegedly used telephone boiler rooms to sell overpriced advertising trinkets to small-business owners after telling each entrepreneur that he was the winner of a new powerboat. The only "boat" ever sent was a small, inflatable raft-like vessel that arrived in a cardboard box, say the prosecutors.

Telephone-marketing tricksters, mostly based in Southern California, are still thriving despite increased efforts by authorities, and many of their victims are entrepreneurs, says Beverly Hills lawyer David A. Katz. He's the former head of a multiagency government task force aimed at boiler-room operators. Many scams involve selling nonexistent or overpriced office supplies, he says, and small businesses are particularly vulnerable because they often order more informally.

In Capitol Hill testimony last month, Mr. Katz told how scamsters often develop a "mooch" -- a company insider bribed to order thousands of dollars of pens, pads and other items.

In another popular scam against small businesses, the crook orders merchandise on credit, never pays and disappears with the goods. Small-business owners should be particularly wary of new customers who make a few small orders and then suddenly start making giant ones, experts warn.

Carving Out a Niche

In Employee Training

SMALL BUSINESSES thrive helping corporations with training and education.

"As technology proliferates into all aspects of the workplace, corporations are busy retraining workers," says venture capitalist Yuval Almog of AIA Ventures, Minneapolis. "Much of that work will be farmed out to small, specialist firms." Mr. Almog says he has invested in several such startups.

Authorware Inc., also of Minneapolis, recently received a multimillion-dollar contract from American Airlines to develop training tools for a broad spectrum of its employees. Using Authorware's system, for instance, pilots can evaluate the impact on takeoff of such variables as runway length, wind velocity and aircraft weight. Northern Telecom Ltd. is working with Authorware to develop ways to help employees understand new technology.

Multimedia technology -- combining text, graphics, video, sound and animation -- "provides a level of realism and simulation that traditional training means can't," says John "Bud" Colligan, chief executive officer of Authorware and a former Apple Computer executive.

Meanwhile, salesmen and clients of database developer Oracle Corp. are using software of Boston's Relational Courseware Inc. for training. Big corporations have the internal capability to provide training and education, but "smaller companies often can do the same things faster and cheaper," says John Carson, a former McKinsey & Co. consultant who launched his own start-up this year to help corporations provide employees with parenting information. Educational Publishing, Mr. Carson's start-up, publishes a newsletter that Polaroid Corp. and Pacer Systems Inc. are distributing to staff.

Odds and Ends

ENTREPRENEURS start young, says a survey of 1,000 small businesses by United Group Information Services. The average entrepreneur surveyed was about 29 years old when he or she started a business, and 82% took the plunge before turning 40. . . . Boredom and burnout are the biggest reasons business owners cite for selling their firms, says David R. Hoods, division president of Geneva Cos., an Irvine, Calif., merger and acquisition services firm. Lack of capital and absence of a child who wants to take over are the next biggest reasons, he adds.
