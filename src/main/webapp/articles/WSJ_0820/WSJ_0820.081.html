REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Whose Wetlands Mistake?

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/20/90

"To the Journal, Pozsgai was a `flabbergasted truck mechanic' who discovered to his sorrow `how little regard the Environmental Protection Agency has for property rights' . . . Balderdash] The Journal's indignation should be focused on a better cause. The only flabbergasting aspect of this case can be found in the defendant's flabbergasting contempt for the law. -- James J. Kilpatrick, "Don't Cry for Mr. Pozsgai," in the Washington Post, January 30, 1990.

"The government's brief in the Court of Appeals asserted that the aerial photographs introduced at trial showed the stream flowing into the canal. That representation, we have now determined, was inaccurate. We have examined the photographs and determined that they do not show the stream flowing into the canal . . . In sum, the record is admittedly quite thin with regard to the two elements needed to establish federal jurisdiction {over Mr. Pozsgai's property}.

-- Solicitor General Kenneth Starr, in a recent Supreme Court brief.

---

The story of John Pozsgai will stand as a cautionary tale in the current age of environmental zealotry. Solicitor General Starr recently acknowledged that federal prosecutors misrepresented facts to the U.S. Court of Appeals in their "wetlands" case against John Pozsgai (the subject of a Journal editorial, "Property Busters," January 11).

Mr. Pozsgai was sentenced to three years in prison, five years probation and a fine of $202,000 for placing topsoil and clean fill on a part of his property that was considered "wetland" by the EPA. Apparently, Mr. Pozsgai needed a permit to develop the site, which before he bought it was a landfill and dumping ground for tires and rusted auto parts. The government considered most of the dumpsite "wetland," even though it is not listed in the National Wetland Inventory Map, and it is not a "marsh, swamp or bog" under the regulatory definition of "wetland."

The Justice Department in court claimed that the EPA had jurisdiction over Mr. Pozsgai's private property because of a tiny stream adjacent to the property. Justice and the EPA claimed that the stream flowed into the Pennsylvania Canal, which is used for "interstate commerce." Justice also claimed that aerial photographs of Mr. Pozsgai's property showed that the stream flowed into the canal, creating federal jurisdiction.

Well, it turns out that the photographs showed no such thing. Mr. Starr also says that the fine given to Mr. Pozsgai by the district court might have been a mistake. Mr. Pozsgai's lawyers at the Washington Legal Foundation hope this admission by Justice will move the Supreme Court to hear the case.

The blunt use of the "wetland" statute in the Pozsgai case reflects a troublesome tendency of the environmental movement to use the full weight of the law and its own heavy arrogance to bludgeon those who stand in its path.
