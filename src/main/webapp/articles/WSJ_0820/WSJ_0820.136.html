Korea Rejects Bid By Samsung to Make Commercial Vehicles

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/20/90

SEOUL -- The South Korean government rejected the Samsung Group's application to make commercial vehicles here, fearing a possible oversupply that would weaken the country's auto industry. The Ministry of Trade and Industry said that Samsung's plan to import technology to make large-sized special vehicles from Nissan Diesel Motor Co. of Japan would undermine South Korea's auto industry, which has suffered from sharp wage increases and shrinking exports in recent years.

The ministry, however, promised to re-evaluate the application by the South Korean business group after October 1991.

The case has been a hot issue locally because the ministry's decision amounts to a test of a new government policy to allow more competition among industries. In 1989, the government abolished an industry protection law, but retained the power to restrict imports of technology, such as that proposed by Samsung.

Ministry officials added that the current shortage of large-sized commercial vehicles, such as dump trucks, will be resolved by next year when the present construction boom subsides.

Samsung had sought government permission to make commercial vehicles in Korea, citing an excessive demand of those vehicles relative to supply.

Currently, four companies in Korea make similar vehicles, but their output meets only about 70% of demand, estimated at 20,000 trucks and 10,000 buses annually.

Existing local auto makers have strongly opposed Samsung's plan to assemble vehicles, fearing oversupply. The local auto makers also presume that Samsung's eventual plan is to enter the passenger car business, despite the group's repeated denial of that intention.
