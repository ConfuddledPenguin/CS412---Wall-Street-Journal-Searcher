Trump Is Stalled On Bailout Ruling; Griffin Plan Passes

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 08/20/90

ATLANTIC CITY, N.J. -- The New Jersey Casino Control Commission delayed a decision on Donald Trump's $65 million bank bailout until tomorrow and approved Merv Griffin's plan to have Resorts International Inc. emerge from bankruptcy-law protection. Following the commission's meeting Friday, Mr. Trump's lawyers said they doubted the delay would cause any of about 90 banks participating in Mr. Trump's restructuring to withdraw approval.

Should the commission reject the bailout tomorrow, it will come as a major surprise and a setback to Mr. Trump who, documents prepared by his accounting firm show, needs the $65 million to continue day-to-day operations.

If the bank deal is completed, Mr. Trump is expected next to tackle a restructuring of $1.3 billion in casino bonds he issued to finance his Atlantic City empire. "I expect war with the bondholders," Mr. Trump said Friday. "But I think things will work out well for both sides."

For Mr. Griffin, the commission's action marks a possible turning point in his ill-fated foray into the casino business. Mr. Griffin purchased Resorts in 1988 after a takeover battle with Mr. Trump. Within less than a year, the company failed to make interest payments on about $925 million in junk bond debt and was forced to seek bankruptcy-law protection. Adding to Mr. Griffin's troubles, Mr. Trump was quoted frequently saying he had bested Mr. Griffin in their negotiations.

Included in the Resorts plan is a $5 million litigation trust fund to pursue possible lawsuits against Mr. Trump stemming from the Resorts sale.

Mr. Griffin said he harbored no animosity toward Mr. Trump and wished him well through his current troubles. "The irony of the whole thing is that it boiled down to today, in the same room," Mr. Griffin said. "How did he end up in such trouble in the morning and me in such great shape in the afternoon?"

Mr. Griffin will pay a significant price for getting out of bankruptcy-law protection. Under the restructuring plan put together by Salomon Brothers Inc., Mr. Griffin will cede about 78% of the equity in the company to bondholders and put up an additional $28.5 million. As part of its plan, the company intends to sell its Paradise Island property in the Bahamas.

In addition to the new equity, some bondholders will receive deferred interest notes that will alleviate the company's debt burden and allow it to accumulate cash.

"They're not out of the woods yet, but the deal will help stabilize the company," said Kaye Handley, an analyst with Sass Lamle & Rubin who specializes in distressed securities.
