Heard on the Street: Weaker Dollar Is Helping Some U.S. Companies

  • Author: John R. Dorfman and Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 08/20/90

NEW YORK -- The slumping dollar is boosting the case for stocks of U.S. companies that do a lot of business overseas. When Iraq invaded Kuwait, investors fled to the dollar as a safe haven, causing the U.S. currency to soar briefly. But now the dollar is declining again, bolstering the foreign-exposure theory that began gaining favor on Wall Street earlier this year.

That theory has two pillars. Foreign economies are growing more quickly than that of the U.S., which limps along at a 1.2% annual pace. And the dollar's weakness is beneficial because it gives U.S. exporters a price advantage against foreign competition, while also making foreign profits fatter when translated into dollars. All of which is good for the stocks of companies with major foreign involvement, be they U.S.-based exporters or multinationals with subsidiaries abroad.

The dollar has retreated so fast that last week it tumbled to three successive postwar lows against the German mark. Since the invasion-inspired peak, the U.S. currency is down about 4.5% against the mark and British pound and nearly 3% against the Japanese yen.

Sure, analysts say, higher oil prices could slow the economies of Japan, West Germany and various European countries. But those countries are starting off with much faster growth than the U.S. And the damage from an oil price rise to Japan and Europe, which are more dependent on foreign oil than the U.S., may not be as great as some fear. "Japan has become extremely oil-efficient," says William P. Sterling, international economist for Merrill Lynch.

He thinks rising oil prices may force Germany and Japan to raise interest rates to keep inflation from getting out of hand. As the U.S. economy is in a fragile state, the Federal Reserve Board is unlikely to foster similar interest-rate increases here. As a result, Mr. Sterling figures, the dollar could easily fall in coming months, for higher interest rates abroad tend to reduce the dollar's allure to international investors and speculators.

And so money managers may well continue to snap up shares of companies such as General Electric, Corning Glass and International Business Machines, which get a large percentage of their sales and earnings abroad.

For American companies, "the growth is perceived to be overseas," says Jeff Landle, a portfolio manager at Stovall/Twenty-First Advisers in New York. "The U.S. economy is certainly not in the near future expected to exhibit any great growth spurt. So the idea of companies with large foreign exposure makes a lot of sense."

Three that Mr. Landle likes are GE, IBM and Dow Chemical. Besides being big exporters, he says, all three have production and distribution facilities in place in many countries. That advantage is important, he says, because trade barriers are falling fast within regions (such as Europe or North America) but not between regions.

Jack Orben, chairman of Associated Family Services, a New York money management firm, praises International Flavors & Fragrances and Gillette for management savvy in understanding foreign markets. "They are tuned in to the changing political situation in various countries," he says.

"The U.S. is a shrinking portion of demand in many industries," says Remy Fisher, a vice president of J.& W. Seligman & Co. in New York. Foreign exposure is one attribute she typically looks for in a stock. Among her favorites are GE and Corning Glass, each of which derives at least 40% of sales and earnings abroad. GE, she says, historically has been good at channeling capital spending to where the growth is going to be, a couple of years in advance. Recently, she says, GE has been pushing capital spending in Europe.

Fluor, an engineering and construction company, has heavy orders from Germany, France and Hungary, Ms. Fisher says. About 40% of the firm's backlog now comes from abroad, compared with 20% only a year ago.

Gerald Perritt, a Chicago money manager who usually emphasizes smaller companies, says that having operations abroad is often a sign of foresight and sophistication on the part of a small, growing business. Three stocks of small companies active in Europe that he owns are Porta Systems, which makes electrical surge-protection equipment for telecommunications systems; Vishay Intertechnology, an electronics concern that recently expanded into England through an acquisition, and Air Express International, a freight forwarding firm.

The dollar is giving such companies a hand in their foreign operations. Since year end, the dollar has fallen 16% against the pound and more than 8% against the mark. The U.S. currency could decline by an additional 5% to 10%, says Nicholas P. Sargen, an international economist at Salomon Brothers. But he warns that investors shouldn't expect it to fall out of bed.

Of course, some people are skeptical of the thesis that foreign exposure is a basis on which to pick stocks.

"It sounds like a lot of baloney to me," says Don Mitchell, chairman of Mitchell & Co., a Weston, Mass., consulting firm. Foreign economies have been growing faster than the U.S.'s "for a long time now," he says, so this particular theme may be played out. "To the extent there's anything new, it's the dying-dollar story," Mr. Mitchell says. But, "There are two things that nobody predicts worth a darn. One is interest rates and the other is currency."

Besides, Mr. Mitchell asks, "If it's so wonderful to be abroad, why buy U.S. stocks? Why not just buy foreign stocks?" Proponents of U.S. exporters and multinationals reply that these stocks are relatively easy to research, and would benefit further if the dollar continues to fall. Also, rising interest rates in countries such as Germany or Japan could pull those countries' entire stock markets lower.

James H. Morgan, president of Morgan Investments in Greenville, S.C., says foreign exposure shouldn't be the main factor in stock selection. "If the dollar weakens enough to help on {the trade} front, it would create other problems" such as higher U.S. interest rates, he says. More important, he says, is a company's ability to withstand an economic slowdown.

Randall Eley, president of Edgar Lomax Co. in Alexandria, Va., expects a "world-wide slowdown" in economic activity. So he's looking for companies with "low P/Es {ratios of stock prices to annual per-share earnings}, great financial strength, very little debt." Still, he is hoping a falling dollar will give a boost to his firm's holding in Dow Chemical, which has been a laggard.

There's one very big risk in the foreign exposure thesis. If Mideast saber-rattling evolves into a full-fledged war and major oil facilities are blown up, all bets are off. Europe and Japan can cope with higher oil prices but their economies could grind to a halt if acute shortages develop. And in a hot war the dollar could skyrocket.
