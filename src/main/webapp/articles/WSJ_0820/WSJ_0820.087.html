Japan's Aid and Investment In Asia Outstrip America's

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/20/90

As Japan steps up its interest in Southeast Asia, the U.S. economic presence in the region is in eclipse. U.S. government aid has been reduced to several Southeast Asian countries over the past few years. Aid to the Philippines continues high, but that to Indonesia has steadily dropped, and U.S. aid for Thailand fell almost 50% last year (to $10 million). Malaysia gets no aid.

Officials at the Agency for International Development say the shrinking numbers are due to broader U.S. budget cutbacks, the region's booming economies, and needs in newly free Eastern Europe.

The squeeze comes while Japanese aid is flooding Southeast Asia. In 1988, Japan sent $984 million to Indonesia, $360 million to Thailand and $534 million to the Philippines. That represented more than 20 times U.S. aid to Indonesia, 15 times U.S. aid to Thailand, and almost double the U.S. aid to the Philippines. Japan says it is the largest aid donor in 17 Asian nations.

In private investment, a similar Japan-U.S. gap shows up. U.S. firms invested about $3.9 billion in Southeast Asia last year, roughly half what Japanese concerns are expected to spend there this year, the Conference Board estimates. The business research group says that in the first half of 1990, U.S. companies launched 110 foreign manufacturing ventures, but only five were in Southeast Asia.

"U.S. companies have a very strong European orientation and very limited orientation to Southeast Asia," says Gail Fosler, the Board's chief economist. Yet she expects Asia's economic growth to far outstrip Europe's in coming years.

To be sure, several U.S. companies are already established in Southeast Asia through subsidiaries and joint ventures. Among them are Du Pont Co., Coca-Cola, Dow Chemical, Goodyear and Hewlett-Packard. Still, Japan's greater rate of investment in Asia is likely to give it a larger share of some of the fastest-growing markets in the world, as well as greater access to inexpensive labor.
