Economy -- Tracking the Economy: U.S. Growth May Have Fallen to Zero, But This Week's Data Won't Show It

  • Author: Lindley H. Clark Jr.
  • Date: 08/20/90

NEW YORK -- Most analysts continue to think, or hope, that the economy is only flirting with recession and soon may start moving upward slightly faster. At the moment, however, the economy is so close to zero growth that one or more unfavorable developments in the Mideast oil crisis, domestic interest rates or somewhere else could push it into slump territory. Once again the current week's statistics, mostly covering developments in July and even earlier, aren't going to tell us much about the outlook. One of the few August numbers will be sales of domestically produced cars for the second 10 days of the month, and that figure isn't likely to reveal much.

Sales in August's first 10 days were down 18.8% from a year earlier, and the result for the latest period is likely to be about the same. Sales were unusually strong a year ago as manufacturers increased rebates to move large inventories. Those inventories are much smaller this year and so, naturally, are the rebates.

The increased uncertainty touched off when Iraq invaded neighboring Kuwait early this month has led many economists to reduce their forecasts for the inflation-adjusted gross national product. Edward S. Hyman of C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc. looks for the preliminary GNP report for the second quarter, to be announced Friday, to be revised downward to zero growth. The advance number, announced a month ago, was an increase of 1.2% annually.

But Stephen S. Roach of Morgan Stanley & Co., though he has cut his forecasts, still looks for preliminary GNP growth of 2% a year. "The economy," he says, "is not likely to fall into an outright recession at any point in the next year and a half. We continue to maintain that the economy has enough forward momentum to avoid recession -- even in the face of an energy shock."

The 49 economists interviewed monthly by Blue Chip Economic Indicators, a Sedona, Ariz., newsletter, cut their average GNP forecasts for both 1990 and 199l, predicting growth at a rate of 1.3% this year and 1.7% next year. A month earlier, they predicted gains of 1.9% for 1990 and 2.2% for 1991. But the averages obscure deeper concerns on the part of some. R.J. Eggert of Blue Chip notes that in August there was a sharp rise in the number of analysts who predicted a quarter or two of negative growth between now and the end of next year.

Donald H. Straszheim, chief economist of Merrill Lynch, Pierce Fenner & Smith, was predicting a recession last year but later changed his mind. At the moment, he doesn't seem too comfortable with that change. "The U.S. economy is on the edge of recession and may fall in," he says. "Though we think that the economy can avoid the plunge, the risk of a downturn is high and rising."

Among the other numbers due out this week, durable goods orders for July are expected to show a reasonable gain of more than 2%. Mr. Hyman of C.J. Lawrence says, "Our model indicates that July durable goods orders rose 2.5%, largely due to an estimated 3% increase in auto production," which fell 2.8% in June.
