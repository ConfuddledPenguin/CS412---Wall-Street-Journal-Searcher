Technology: Businessland Posts Fourth-Quarter Loss Of $21.5 Million; Stock Plummets 58%

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 08/07/90

Businessland Inc. reported a net loss of $21.5 million for its fiscal fourth quarter ended June 30 and restated results for its fiscal second and third quarters, increasing the net losses for those periods. Investors drilled the San Jose, Calif., computer dealer's stock, which tumbled 58% to close at $2.875, down $4, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. Among other computer dealers, MicroAge closed at $13.25, down $4, and Intelligent Electronics at $15.875, down 37.5 cents, both in national over-the-counter trading.

Businessland said its fourth-quarter loss was equal to 73 cents a share. Most analysts expected the company to post a small profit or at worst a loss of five cents a share. In the year-earlier period, Businessland had a profit of $7.4 million, or 23 cents a share, on sales of $322.6 million.

The collapse of Businessland's stock prompted speculation that the company now may be a takeover target. The company's market value, based on about 30 million shares outstanding, was a mere $86.3 million at the close of yesterday's trading. At that price, some observers think Businessland, which reported revenues of $339 million for the fourth quarter and $1.35 billion for the fiscal year, would make an attractive acquisition, especially for a healthier computer dealer.

Even if Businessland isn't in play, the company's weakening condition appears to threaten its independence. "The question is: Can this company survive as an independent entity?" asked Charles Wolf, an analyst at First Boston Corp.

David Norman, Businessland's chief executive, is confident the company can recover. "We believe we have the staying power to be a major player," he said.

Observers are less sure, saying Businessland is paying dearly today for some disastrous moves made in recent years. These include an enervating price war Businessland sparked last year and a 1988 acquisition of ComputerCraft, a store-front retailer that's posting losses.

Businessland also is suffering from the after-effects of a rift with Compaq Computer Corp. In February 1989, Compaq dropped Businessland as a distributor and although the two companies made up this March Mr. Norman said he is only now "making progress in winning back customers that we lost."

"Basically, the company has never recovered from the Compaq thing," said Benny Lorenzo, an analyst at Volpe Welty & Co.

Observers are disturbed as well by Businessland's need to restate results for its second and third quarters. Mr. Norman said the restatements were the result of "accounting errors," but these mistakes doubled the company's third-quarter loss and their disclosure has weakened confidence in Mr. Norman's management.

"The company's poor financial results heighten the fact that Businessland's management and entire culture must change fairly soon," said Seymour Merrin, a computer retailing analyst in Palo Alto, Calif.

Mr. Norman insists changes are in the works. For much of this year he has been streamlining operations while at the same time expanding overseas. In January, he closed about 90 outlets and cut the work force by about 7%. Then in June, he dismissed his two highest-ranking sales and marketing executives, citing poor performance, and has yet to name successors.

Mr. Norman admits, however, that Businessland, which has tried to position itself as a sophisticated supplier and networker of computers, still doesn't receive enough revenue from services, maintenance and training. The company gets about 7% of its revenue from such high-margin services, well below the double-digit percentage Mr. Norman believes will insure the firm's profitability.

He also concedes he erred in investing so heavily in selling higher-priced machines such as servers and workstations, which aren't typically sold by personal computer dealers. "We got ahead of ourselves," he said.

Mr. Norman blamed the poor fourth quarter on losses from the firm's expanding international operations and its 20-store ComputerCraft retail group. Also contributing to the loss was a $6 million charge involved in closing 15 facilities, the deferral to future quarters of credits from some vendors, and increases in inventories of spare parts for computers serviced by the company.

For the year, Businessland had a net loss of $23.1 million, or 76 cents a share, in contrast to net income of $32.9 million, $1.04 a share, in its fiscal 1989. Revenue rose to $1.35 billion from $1.18 billion.

The losses placed Businessland in violation of certain covenants with lenders, and Mr. Norman said he hadn't yet learned what the lenders' position is on revising loan agreements.
