Economy: U.S. Trade Gap Shrank in June To $5.07 Billion --- Deficit Hits Lowest Level In 7 Years, but Oil Prices Likely to End Narrowing

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 08/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The huge June reduction in the U.S.'s trade deficit, to the lowest level in seven years, is likely to be as short-lived as the oil-price drop that helped bring it about. Still, record exports were a major factor in the improved trade outlook, and one that has more potential for lasting.

The trade deficit shrank to $5.07 billion in June, $2.7 billion less than May's $7.77 billion gap, the Commerce Department reported. Exports grew 4.6% to a record $34.30 billion, while imports fell 2.9% to $39.38 billion.

But the U.S. is unlikely to see another deficit that small anytime soon. One of the main ingredients in the improvement was a drop in oil prices, which helped reduce the nation's petroleum import bill by $631 million. Given the subsequent spike in oil prices following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait earlier this month, analysts said the trade deficit is certain to widen again.

"I think it has nowhere to go but up," said John Endean, an economist for the American Business Conference, an organization of chief executives of fast-growing midsized companies. "The June oil prices were low even by pre-Kuwait-invasion standards."

According to the report, oil prices were averaging $14.64 a barrel in June. On Friday, the day the trade report was disclosed, oil was trading at around $28 a barrel on the spot market. Spot prices aren't directly comparable with import prices, which are usually set by contract, but analysts agree that oil prices of all sorts are heading up.

But economists were encouraged by progress in other areas of trade, particularly the continued growth in exports. "The ongoing rise in exports suggests there will be continuing support for the U.S. economy from export demand," said Richard Rippe, chief economist at Dean Witter Reynolds in New York.

U.S. exports have grown enormously over the past 2 1/2 years, although the pace of increase has slowed in recent months. But Mr. Rippe and many other analysts said they expect continued gains in exports, as the economies of many of the U.S.'s major trading partners are growing at a fairly healthy pace. That means demand for goods both foreign and domestic is likely to continue to be strong.

In addition, the dollar has been generally declining in value against foreign currencies, a favorable development for exports because it will translate into lower prices for foreigners buying American goods.

"Outside of oil you should see continued sizable improvement in the net export position," predicted Lincoln Anderson, an economist at Bear Stearns & Co. in New York.

American companies' success in selling goods overseas has been one of the U.S. economy's few sources of strength in recent months. "Trade is going to be the key as to whether the economy merely slows down and we have a couple of quarters slump in GNP (gross national product) or whether things will get a lot worse," said Mr. Anderson.

While rising oil prices are likely to fatten the nation's import bill, the weak economy will probably curb imports in other areas, many analysts believe.

June's rise in exports reflected increased shipments of U.S. aircraft, other capital goods and food. The drop in imports included decreases in purchases of foreign-made cars and industrial supplies.

The numbers were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
