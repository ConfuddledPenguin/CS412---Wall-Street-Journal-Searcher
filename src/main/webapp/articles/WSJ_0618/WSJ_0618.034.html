World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Decline on Index-Linked Selling Amid Light Trading; London Shares Also Fall

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/18/90

Tokyo stock prices finished lower in light trading Friday on index-linked selling in the last half hour of trading. The Nikkei index of 225 issues, which advanced 296.34 points Thursday, was down 129.71 points at 32538.40.

London shares were moderately lower.

Tokyo stock prices moved in a narrow range most of the day, as investors remained on the sidelines ahead of releases of U.S. economic indicators later Friday, traders said.

Investors picked up laggards among pharmaceutical, machinery and food sectors as well as small-capitalization second-section stocks. Shipbuilding, construction and electrical shares, meanwhile, ended mostly lower.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the Nikkei index rose 88.85 points to 32627.25.

Friday's volume on the first section was estimated at 450 million shares, in line with 456.2 million Thursday.

Declining issues outnumbered advancing issues 477-443, while 198 remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which gained 21.56 points Thursday, was down 3.65 points, or 0.15%, at 2396.32.

The second section index gained for the fourth consecutive day. The index, which advanced 19.87 points Thursday, was up 47.61 points, or 1.16%, to close at 4153.60.

Volume in the second section was estimated at 33 million shares, up from 26.7 million Thursday.

"The first section trading was lackluster," said Shigeru Akima, head of equity trading at UBS Phillips & Drew International. "Little money is coming into the section, and investors are passing off the time by trading in small-cap stocks on the second section with bright earnings outlooks," he said.

In addition to the second-section stocks, traders said, high-priced shares on the Osaka and Nagoya stock exchanges advanced strongly, drawing buy orders from investors going after these smaller stocks that often move up significantly on relatively small amounts of buying.

Trading in large-capitalization shares on the first section requires encouraging factors such as a sign of lower interest rates, traders said.

Even a favorable market "mood" such as expecting the yen's rise or interest rates to fall would be helpful, said Atsushi Koishihara, general manager of stock trading at Taiheiyo Securities. "Everybody is waiting to see such a cue," he said.

Expectations of lower U.S. interest rates grew last week, triggered by Wednesday's report of unexpectedly low U.S. retail sales for May and fueled by Thursday's remarks by U.S. Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan that U.S. interest rates will fall "very significantly" if a credible budget-deficit reduction accord is reached.

"But after all, investors are unable to tell which way rates are heading," said a senior trader at Dai-Ichi Securities.

Traders predicted that stock trading for this week will depend largely on how the foreign exchange market reacts to the latest U.S. economic data.

Pharmaceutical shares, which had been lagging in recent weeks, bounced back Friday as investors shifted their attention from shipbuilding and electrical shares, both of which had been traded actively earlier in the week, traders said.

Dainippon Pharmaceutical went up 400 yen ($2.59) a share to 3,210 yen, Mochida gained 80 to 3,950, and Daiichi Pharmaceutical rose 100 to 2,500.

Also higher were retail issues such as Isetan, which advanced 120 to 4,480, textile shares such as Kurabo, which increased 52 to 1,000, and Kuraray, which was up 60 at 1,550.

Shipbuilding and steel shares advanced through midday but ended lower on late profit-taking, traders said.

Electrical stocks also softened. Sony dropped 60 to 8,510, while Sharp declined 20 to 1,880.

London share prices finished a sluggish session moderately lower amid disappointment over the latest U.S. and U.K. economic figures and a soggy early performance on Wall Street.

Traders termed the session frustrating, as many had awaited the outcome of the U.K. data for most of the week as well as a slew of U.S. figures.

But the indicators caused little stir in the market, with many traders closing their books early for the weekend. "There was such a great buildup to Friday that we thought we'd see some action," said one trader. "But it just didn't happen."

U.K. inflation figures for May showed that the retail price index rose 0.9% from April and was up 9.7% on a year-on-year basis, compared with expectations of a 9.5% increase.

At the close, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index was down 10.7 points at 2392.3, up from its midafternoon low of 2387.4 but well below the early morning peak of 2414.4.

The final calculation of the narrower FT-30 share index showed a 2.7-point drop to 1925.9. Volume amounted to a modest 400.2 million shares, compared with 554.2 million Thursday.

Reuters shares chalked up the largest advance on the day, surging 44 pence (75 cents) to #12.94 ($22.10). Dealers suggested a stock shortage forced the price higher, while talk also circulated that the company will win the contract to provide the electronic dealing system for the American Stock Exchange in New York.

Polly Peck surged 16 to 435 after a series of bullish presentations to analysts in the U.S., dealers said.

Securities house Smith New Court reported pretax profit for the year to April 27 rose 115% from year-earlier levels. Its shares finished 5 higher at 106.

Employment services concern Manpower, formerly known as Blue Arrow, slipped 1 to 86 after reporting six-month interim pretax profit down 14%. The results were worse than expected, analysts said.

Elsewhere, share prices closed lower in Paris, Brussels, Amsterdam, Milan and Madrid. Prices were mixed in Frankfurt, Zurich and Stockholm. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Share prices closed lower in Sydney, Taipei and Seoul and higher in Manila, Wellington, Singapore and Hong Kong.
