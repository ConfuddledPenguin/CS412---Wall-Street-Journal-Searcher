Caterpillar's Schaefer Set to Pass Reins

  • Author: Robert L. Rose
  • Date: 06/18/90

PEORIA, Ill. -- When George A. Schaefer assumed the reins at Caterpillar Inc. in 1985, Japan's Komatsu Ltd. was on the move in the construction-equipment industry. It opened a plant in Tennessee and vowed to wrest market share from the industry leader. Caterpillar pounced back with a plan to virtually remake its factories: PWAF, for Plant With a Future. The $2 billion project, coupled with the restraining of price increases, helped Caterpillar retain its No. 1 position among global construction equipment companies.

But that didn't help profits. In Mr. Schaefer's five years as chairman and chief executive officer, profit went on a roller-coaster ride of up, down, up, up and down. Although Caterpillar says the PWAF payoff will start this year, Mr. Schaefer won't be around to see it.

After 39 years at the company, his long-planned retirement starts July 1, at age 62. Before leaving, he shared his views about everything from the competition to his wife's critique of his office decor.

Q: Have you learned anything from Komatsu?

A: We used to be not overly concerned about Komatsu. That all changed radically as we moved through the early part of the '80s and we started to see erosion of our historic market shares, particularly in North America.

So I can tell you today that we know all we can legally know about our friends from Japan. We read everything that's published. We digest it. We look over their shoulders from our vantage point in Tokyo, as I'm sure they do from their vantage point in Peoria. They have a plant right here in Peoria.

Q: Are you saying you spy on them?

A: No, I wouldn't say that. We know everything we can legally know, but we sure don't spy on them. We know they've had excellent quality. We know they've had a determination to prevail and grow in this industry. And we know that they are long-term players. They absolutely are not concerned about next quarter's profit. They're concerned about market share and long term.

For us to maintain the lead in the industry we have to excel in those areas. That's the reason we aren't the favorite around Wall Street, although recently the stock has been moving pretty well considering the earnings pressure we're under.

Q: Have you felt it was unfair to demand of Caterpillar or George Schaefer better short-term results?

A: You can look at that from two different vantage points. When we sit down with our pension fund managers, we say, "Well come on, where were you {in performance} last quarter?" There, we're kind of talking short-term.

If you look at our situation, we're kind of saying, we have to be long-term players, or we're not going to be around.

I don't think it's unfair. We are making the right long-term judgments, and if people want to take us to task for doing that, that's their prerogative.

Q: Why is market share so important?

A: For us to take a less aggressive stance vis-a-vis market share means that we lose that long-term stream of parts and service. Parts are more profitable than prime product or machines. It's really like the razor blade business, when you get right down to it.

Q: Why did the cost of your plant modernization program increase so much over your original projection?

A: We had an overall blueprint within our own company, but there was no case study or textbook. We are doing something very unique in a major manufacturing company. We are simultaneously restructuring world-wide our manufacturing processes from A to Z, and it's a gigantic undertaking. We are 60% to 65% along, but it has cost more to get there, and it has taken longer than we thought.

Q: Is there a peace dividend for Caterpillar?

A: We're already seeing some results, faster than we thought. We recently took our West German dealer and gave him the East German dealership. And we've sold over 100 machines in East Germany this year. And I think that's just a start.

Granted, all these Eastern Bloc countries need the wherewithal to pay for all this stuff. But you'll see increasing evidence of the Western countries, Japan and others willing to make some of the funds available.

Q: Are we losing ground to foreign competitors in manufacturing?

A: There isn't any question we were losing ground in the early '80s, but we've got it stabilized. The advent of the world-wide competitive situation is making management everywhere -- not only the U.S.smarter. It's caused us to do a better job.

Q: Will your domestic work force change in the 1990s?

A: There's no question. And it will even accelerate because a lot of these jobs in the plants are being reoriented to higher-skilled jobs. Running major complexes with computer assistance means we've got to have higher-skilled people on those things. We're doing a lot of training, and a lot of the menial jobs of the past are gone.

Q: Why aren't there more women executives at Caterpillar today?

A: It's probably just a function of the industry we're in. There just aren't that many women engineers. We are seeing upward movement in some of the other areas -- financial, specifically. We do recently have a lady running our service tool company, which is a pretty substantial ancillary. But the base that we've drawn from has not been there historically.

Q: Your successor, Donald Fites, has reorganized the company into profit units. Does this mean the company will be focusing more on short-term profit than it has in the past?

A: The tendency will be there to focus more on the short term because the heads of these profit centers will be challenged to meet their business plan. As they find they're having difficulty, they may want to take short-term actions as the year {goes on}. Therein is the challenge of what we call the executive office, to make sure that the long-term interests are being tended to.

Q: Will this mean Caterpillar managers will be worrying about their jobs more?

A: In a word, yes.

The guy that runs that business unit is totally responsible for product, for design, for engineering, for manufacturing, for pricing, for everything. And he's got his target, which is the bottom line of his business unit. And a fair amount of his compensation will eventually be determined by how well he meets or exceeds or falls short of that target. That's never been in our approach.

Q: Can a top executive at an American corporation also be a good parent?

A: I think so, but he or she better be very, very specific in allocating their time and prioritizing their time. Fortunately, our children were in the formative stages, through the later grade school and high school, when I was much less active -- when I was plant manager in Decatur {Ill.}, for example. I spent a lot of time with my children. If I had teen-age children and I was a CEO, I would have a problem trying to find the time they really need -- and should have.

Q: Will you miss this job?

A: I'll miss all the fancy ability to pick up the phone and ask for something. But I tell you, the demands that are there are such that I'm going to relish some time to reflect and do some things at a slower pace.

Q: Who gets this big corner office?

A: Well, the best office in the place is the one that Don {Fites} already is in. I don't care where I'm at. I really don't. My wife thinks this is a terribly poorly decorated office, and it is.

Q: What doesn't your wife like about it?

A: She doesn't think things go together very well. I don't care about any of that kind of stuff. I'm different, I guess.
