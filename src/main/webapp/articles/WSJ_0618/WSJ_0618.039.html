Marketing & Media: Seven Journalists File $330 Million Suit Against Atex

  • Author: Wade Lambert
  • Date: 06/18/90

NEW YORK -- Journalists from five newspapers and a news service sued Eastman Kodak Co.'s Atex electronic-publishing unit for $330 million, claiming that using the Atex computer system at work caused them permanent injuries. The lawsuit, filed in federal court in Manhattan, alleged that Atex knew the computer system could pose health risks, such as injuries to the wrists and arms, from repetitive use. The lawsuit accused the company of negligence in not notifying users of the risks or changing the computer system, which is used for reporting and editing.

The lawsuit said Atex "disregarded medical and scientific information, studies, tests, data and literature concerning the causal relationship between the repetitive use of computer systems and cumulative trauma disorders," as the ailments are known.

A spokesman for Atex said the company hadn't seen the suit. He said the company doesn't comment on issues that are in litigation. Eastman Kodak, the parent company, isn't named as a defendant.

The plaintiffs are seven journalists working for both the New York and Long Island editions of Newsday, Discount Store News, the Village Voice, American Banker and the Associated Press, and one former American Banker reporter. Three of the plaintiffs' spouses also are plaintiffs, seeking additional damages for the loss of the journalists' support.

The journalists claim their injuries prevent them from pursuing normal activities and employment. The injuries include carpal tunnel syndrome, tendinitis and nerve disorders, the lawsuit said.

In the lawsuit, the journalists also alleged that the computer system -- including the keyboard and video display terminal -- was defectively designed. A similar suit was filed against Atex in state court in Manhattan earlier this month by three other Newsday reporters.
