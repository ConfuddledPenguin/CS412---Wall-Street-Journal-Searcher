Boat Builder, Facing Slump, Broadens Lines --- Outboard Marine Purchases Grumman Unit, Boosts Overseas Sales Efforts

  • Author: Andrew Patner
  • Date: 06/18/90

WAUKEGAN, Ill. -- Outboard Marine Corp. isn't positioning itself for "tomorrow or the next quarter, but for the long term" during the current slump in the recreational boating industry, James C. Chapman, president and chief executive officer, said. Outboard Marine is sticking with its program of vertical integration of engine and boat manufacturing and stepping up its global sales and marketing efforts, Mr. Chapman said in an interview.

Initially a leading manufacturer of boat engines, Outboard Marine will step up its presence in all aspects of boat and engine sales and manufacturing to "compete in every product line," according to Mr. Chapman. He said Outboard Marine also will work to rebuild its relationship with its 5,000 North American dealers, many of whom are wary of expanding inventory as consumers shun recreational boats.

In its second quarter, ended March 31, Outboard Marine had a loss of $5.6 million, or 29 cents a share, from continuing operations. It had profit of $9.2 million, or 47 cents a share, in the year-earlier period. Sales in the quarter fell to $309.2 million from $402.6 million.

While expanding its interests from engine manufacturing to boating packaging and sales, Outboard Marine has shed its unrelated units. After selling its Cushman industrial vehicle and turf-care business to Ransomes America Corp. for $150 million and its Lawn-Boy lawn mower division to Toro Co. for $85 million, Outboard Marine is in a strong cash position with a debt-to-equity ratio of about 26%. A flurry of acquisitions has added the Donzi and Chris-Craft boat and cruiser lines to Outboard Marine's industry-leading Johnson and Evinrude outboard brands.

Mr. Chapman said Outboard Marine's recent acquisition of Grumman Corp.'s boat division brought it 11 boat makers with 13 brand names. He said the purchase has allowed Outboard Marine to push hard to boost its position in the world market.

In the 3 1/2 years that it has been selling packaged boats, Outboard Marine brands accounted for 25% of all U.S. boats sold overseas, Mr. Chapman said. International sales accounted for 23% of the company's $1.46 billion revenue in 1989, and its goal is to boost overseas sales to as much as 35% of the total.

Mr. Chapman said Outboard Marine will continue to hold down manufacturing costs in the capital-intensive engine manufacturing business. From 1980 to 1985, the firm spent $100 million to move 70% of its manufacturing capacity from Northern plants to technically improved sites in the South, Mr. Chapman said. Those moves resulted in a 50% reduction in labor costs, he said.

Outboard Marine has responded to the 15% industrywide sales downturn over the past 18 months by closing one plant and idling workers at others. The closing last month of an engine assembly plant in Rutherfordton, N.C., will result in a $3 million charge for the current quarter.

"It may seem contradictory to close an assembly plant and buy a boat company," Mr. Chapman said. "But the closings help us consolidate our outboard manufacturing in the here and now. The acquisitions are for our long-term strength. The next 100 years, that's what we're looking at."
