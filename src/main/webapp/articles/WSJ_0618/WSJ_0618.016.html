Telecredit, Check-Guarantee Provider, Holds Merger Talks With Several Firms

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 06/18/90

LOS ANGELES -- Telecredit Inc., a provider of check-guarantee and credit-card processing services, said that it is holding talks with several companies about a possible business combination. "Telecredit believes it is in the best interests of its shareholders to explore the possibility of such a transaction," said Lee A. Ault III, chairman and chief executive officer.

Telecredit told shareholders last year that it was considering various ways to use its excess cash, including a stock buy-back, recapitalization or leveraged buy-out. After "we were approached by some companies of substance, we thought we might as well include {a merger} as one of the alternatives, " Mr. Ault said Friday.

The announcement propelled Telecredit shares up $9.375 to $52.50 in national over-the-counter trading. Nearly 1.2 million shares changed hands, more than 25 times the stock's average daily volume. The closing price gives Telecredit a market value of about $578 million.

Telecredit is best known for its check service used by retailers and other merchants to verify customers' personal checks. If a check bounces after Telecredit approves it, Telecredit "purchases" the check from the retailer. Dominating the market, Telecredit guarantees more than $7 billion of checks annually.

Credit-card and debit card processing services, mainly for MasterCard and Visa transactions, make up the other main part of Telecredit's business. The company's customers are 1,700 credit unions and small and medium-sized financial institutions that don't process their own credit-card payments.

Analysts suggested that Telecredit's suitors probably are large financial institutions seeking to expand existing services or add new ones. Most banks wouldn't be interested because they wouldn't want to reduce their regulatory capital by putting substantial goodwill on their books, the analysts said.

Mr. Ault wouldn't describe the suitors, but he said that "the only {companies} that make sense would be those where there is a strategic fit and they have some size to do it."

The board hasn't set a deadline to review the options, but the process "won't drag on for a long time," he said. Telecredit has retained Dillon, Read & Co. and Alex. Brown & Sons Inc. as financial advisers.

Telecredit isn't widely followed by securities analysts, possibly because its niche business doesn't neatly fall into any large industry group. Joseph A. Stieven, an analyst at Stifel, Nicolaus & Co. in St. Louis, counseled clients Friday to "take profits" because the stock "could drop as quickly as it came up" if a transaction doesn't materialize.

Mr. Stieven, who has followed Telecredit for four years, praises management for achieving pretax profit margins of 27% for the nine months ended Jan. 31. After more than 20 years at the helm, Telecredit's 53-year-old chief executive, Mr. Ault, "is highly energetic and has sharp people working for him."

One of Telecredit's attractions is that it throws off a lot of cash. The company currently has about $82 million, or $7 a share, in cash and no debt. While earnings growth has slowed a bit in recent years, analysts are forecasting future growth of about 13% to 15% a year.

Telecredit was hurt in recent years by the disappointing performance of a subsidiary that launched a computerized system to prevent counterfeiting of stocks and bonds. The unit, most of whose operations have been phased out, accounted for $38 million in operating losses in fiscal years 1984 through 1988, one analyst calculated.

In the year ended April 30, 1989, Telecredit posted net income of $24.9 million, or $2.26 a share, on revenue of $158.7 million. For the nine months ended Jan. 31, the company had net income of $21.9 million, or $1.98 a share, on revenue of $129.2 million.
