Investors in Britain Raise an Eyebrow To Big Board's Nighttime Trading Plan

  • Author: Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 06/18/90

LONDON -- Among British investors being wooed by the New York Stock Exchange with its plan to trade stocks in the wee hours, reaction runs the gamut from lukewarm acceptance to icy opposition. Some big investors doubt there will be enough heavy hitters to provide smooth trading during the graveyard shift.

Under the plan, continuous trading in Big Board issues would be conducted by computer after the exchange closes at 4 p.m. Eastern time, which is 9 p.m. London time. The Big Board said it would begin such trading this year, but hasn't specified dates. Starting in 1991, it also would let brokers enter potential trades all night, with computers matching and executing orders at 8 p.m. (1 a.m. in London), midnight (5 a.m.) and 5 a.m. (10 a.m.).

The plan is designed partly to woo business from London's International Stock Exchange, where the Seaq International system trades 49 Big Board issues. The London system recently has amassed a healthy, but far from enormous, chunk of volume in Big Board stocks, mostly when the New York market was closed. Between last February, when London began keeping such statistics, and the end of May, Seaq International volume in those stocks totaled 816 million shares -- about one good week of volume in New York.

The London exchange said Friday that it hadn't any plans to expand trading hours in response to the Big Board's move. The London market is open from 8:30 a.m. to 4:30 p.m., but Seaq International trades can take place at any time. Few dealers linger after the close, however.

Investors are concerned about inadequate service in an after-hours market. "I'm skeptical about how much liquidity would be provided," Graeme Johnson, a fund manager for Glasgow-based Scottish Amicable Investment Managers, said. "Trading should be fairly thin. That would mean wide spreads and greater volatility. We would reserve judgment as to whether we'd trade."

Moreover, the Big Board plan seems to overlook the long-view approach many British investors take. Baillie Gifford & Co., an Edinburgh-based fund manager, said this is why it isn't interested in the Big Board's plan. Gavin J.N. Gemmell, chief pension investments manager, said: "We try to buy stocks in successful companies and hold them for a long period. We don't care if we pay an extra eighth {of a point, or 12.5 cents, for a share}. If we've made the basic decision right, we're going to make a lot of money, anyway. If you've got that basic attitude, it doesn't make much sense to come into the office in the middle of the night."

Even some who cautiously endorsed the idea predicated this on the after-hours market's attracting enough trading volume to minimize wild price swings. But they acknowledged that their reticence could lessen the new market's chances of success. "We're cautiously positive. The move is a good one," Ian Young, U.S. investment manager for Norwich Union Insurance Group, said. "If the liquidity is there, we're prepared to use it."

Some investors, while generally opposed to the plan, said after-hours trading could be a useful tool in emergencies, such as the October market plunges of 1987 and 1989. "We certainly wouldn't trade after hours as a matter of course, but we probably would do it in exceptional circumstances when quite a lot of dealing has to be done," said Richard A. Barfield, chief investment manager for Edinburgh-based Standard Life Assurance Co.

Some London units of Big Board member firms, eager for more business in a market long bereft of heavy volume, welcomed the plan. "It must help us. These are serious moves" by the Big Board, Bernie Leaver, head of equity trading at Shearson Lehman Hutton International Inc., said. "If that's the way of the world, we're ready for it."
