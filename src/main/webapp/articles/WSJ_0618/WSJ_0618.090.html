California to Use Satellite To Help Track Water Use

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/18/90

LOS ANGELES -- Drought-conscious bureaucrats in Southern California are employing the latest in electronic surveillance to identify businesses that use too much water. The Metropolitan Water District of Southern California is using pictures from a Landsat satellite circling 450 miles above the Earth to determine areas of excessive water use. Computer analysis of the photographs will measure infrared light waves from plants, with the wettest areas appearing in bright red. The analysis is being provided under an agreement between the water district and Terra-Mar Resource Information Services Inc., Mountain View, Calif.

Homeowners needn't worry just yet about overwatering their lawns, however. Measurements will only be taken for large landscaped areas such as golf courses, cemeteries, parks and big residential projects, as the satellite can only pick up areas about as big as a football field, a spokesman for the water district said.

While the district won't be nabbing water gluttons, "this data is essential for determining potential water savings in urban landscaping and will be used in developing future water conservation programs," says Carl Boronkay, general manager of the water district, which provides more than half the water used by 15 million people living in Los Angeles, Ventura, Orange, San Diego, Riverside and San Bernardino counties. The information will also be used in state water allocation hearings.
