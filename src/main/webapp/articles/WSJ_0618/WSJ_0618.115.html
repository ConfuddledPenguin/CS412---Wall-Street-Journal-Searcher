What's News -- Business and Finance

DONALD TRUMP'S debt includes more than $500 million in bank loans that he personally guaranteed, bankers say. Lenders are increasingly seeking the liquidation of most of his properties, and demanding that he relinquish day-to-day control of his operations. The market value of his assets is believed to fall well short of the amount the developer has borrowed. Trump missed $73 million in debt payments that were due Friday. ---

UAL's nonunion workers may oppose the proposed union buy-out of the company. Such a stance could pose a public-relations problem for the takeover group as it seeks financing.

---

The domination of big airports by one or two airlines is the subject of a broad Justice Department investigation into possible antitrust violations. Prosecutors have asked USAir for information about the allocation of gates at a new terminal in Pittsburgh.

---

An after-hours system for trading stocks and stock options is to be announced today by the American and Cincinnati exchanges, the Chicago Board Options Exchange and Reuters. The alliance is a response to the Big Board's nighttime trading plans.

---

Norway's oil minister said the North Sea producer will probably stop limiting its output, a practice begun four years ago in a gesture of good will to OPEC. The country's posture could rattle jittery petroleum markets.

---

Factory output rose 0.6% in May as auto production rebounded. Consumer prices increased a modest 0.2% during the same month. Economists say the figures depict continued slow growth with steady inflation.

The U.S. merchandise trade gap narrowed to $6.94 billion in April as a 6.2% plunge in imports more than offset a 3.5% decrease in exports.

---

Bond prices fell sharply Friday on a stronger-than-expected industrial-production report, while stocks were mixed. Broad stock-market measures ended little changed, but the Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 7.67 points to 2935.89, a new high.

---

Ford raised base prices on many cars and trucks, its first midyear increases in three years. The move comes despite lagging auto sales. The boosts range from $58 to $134.

---

Thrift-bailout officials transferred $2.1 billion in deposits from two insolvent S&Ls to Sunbelt Savings of Dallas. The action, intended to make government-owned Sunbelt more attractive to potential buyers, will cost taxpayers an estimated $1.68 billion.

---

Martin A. Siegel was sentenced to two months in prison for securities fraud. The judge cited the former Kidder Peabody takeover strategist's cooperation in the federal government's insider-trading probe.

---

Businessland dismissed the two executives in charge of marketing and sales, reflecting management's concern about the computer retailer's performance in those areas.

---

Hewlett-Packard today is to introduce its first workstations incorporating the product line of Apollo Computer, which the company acquired last year for $476.4 million.
