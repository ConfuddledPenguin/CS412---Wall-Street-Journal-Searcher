Politics & Policy: Bush Vetoes Bill That Would Relax Hatch Act's Curbs

  • Author: Michel McQueen
  • Date: 06/18/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush vetoed a bill that would ease restrictions on political activity by federal civil servants, saying the change would "inevitably lead to repoliticizing the federal work force." Mr. Bush's veto, which was expected, was his 12th since he took office. All his previous vetoes have been sustained; most recently, the Senate last week sustained his veto of an Amtrak authorization bill by two votes. Mr. Bush is also expected to veto a measure passed by Congress late Thursday that would guarantee workers as much as 12 weeks' unpaid leave for childbirth, adoption or serious family illnesses.

In rejecting the measure to lift restrictions on federal workers' campaign activities, Mr. Bush said the current system protects workers from harassment and ensures the integrity and neutrality of the federal work force. Since 1939, the so-called Hatch Act has barred federal workers from most partisan political activity aside from voting, including holding partisan political positions or participating in campaigns. Federal workers can, however, participate in non-partisan activities such as school board races.

Labor leaders and representatives from jurisdictions with a substantial federal work force pressed for the changes, saying three million federal and postal workers should have the same rights as other citizens. The changes approved by Congress last week, by a vote of 334-87 in the House and 67-30 in the Senate, would allow federal and postal workers to hold positions in the political parties, to attend party conventions as delegates and to raise money from fellow members of the same union.

Although the original votes in both houses were large enough to override Mr. Bush's veto, GOP leaders have expressed confidence they can muster the votes in the Senate to sustain the veto.

Meanwhile, some Republicans are urging Mr. Bush not to follow through on his threat to veto the parental-leave bill. "I would hope the First Father wouldn't make this decision without first hearing from Republicans who support the bill," said Rep. Marge Roukema (R., N.J.). "There are reasons why business should be concerned about intrusive new mandates, but this isn't one of them."
