Rate of Inflation In Britain Rose To 9.7% in May

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/18/90

LONDON -- Britain's annual rate of inflation rose more than expected to 9.7% in May from 9.4% in April. The so-called underlying rate, which by excluding mortgage-interest payments and the recently introduced "community charge" is more comparable with European inflation measures, rose to 7% from 6.5%.

A Treasury spokeswoman said the inflation rise, which was caused by increased food and clothing prices and the residual impact of higher excise duties on tobacco and alcohol prices, is "not very welcome."

Economists warned that the rise in the underlying level indicates that inflationary pressures are even more entrenched than expected, and that will make it even more difficult for the government to achieve the economic conditions it has set for taking the British pound into the exchange rate mechanism of the European Monetary System.

The government has repeatedly said that it won't take that step until British inflation is down closer to European levels. Although it remains unclear exactly what inflation measure the government will use in making its judgment, Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major has said that an underlying level will be used.

Stephen Hannah, head of research at Natwest Capital Markets, predicted that the underlying inflation rate won't fall toward European inflation levels until early next year. "If we take the government at its word," sterling probably won't be able to join the exchange rate mechanism until the spring, he said.

Sterling rose sharply last week on speculation that the government is considering taking sterling into the mechanism as early as this autumn, but it slipped back after the inflation report was released.

The rise in the annual rate of inflation, as measured by the retail price index, was higher than the 9.5% average of economists' forecasts and took the rate back up to its highest level since March 1982, when it was at 10.4%.

The 0.5-point increase in the underlying rate took it to its highest level since October 1982, when it was also at 7%.

Steven Bell, chief economist at Morgan Grenfell & Co., said price rises in clothing and food confirm that the country is facing continued demand-led inflation. Retail sales figures last week indicated that consumer spending remains buoyant despite the government's tight-money policy.
