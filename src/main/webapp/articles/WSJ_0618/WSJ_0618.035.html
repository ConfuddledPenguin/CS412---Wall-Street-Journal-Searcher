International: Sisters, Los Albertos Settle on Running Big Spanish Concern

  • Author: Nicholas Bray
  • Date: 06/18/90

MADRID -- The Koplowitz sisters get the construction companies. Los Albertos get the finance firms. That, roughly, is the result of a settlement affecting one of Spain's biggest private business empires, Construcciones y Contratas S.A.

In a pact ending months of wrangling, principal owners Esther and Alicia Koplowitz agreed with their estranged husbands, Alberto Alcocer and Alberto Cortina, known in Spain as los Albertos, to divide the empire into the two zones of influence. To maintain the group as an entity and avoid possible fiscal charges, however, no shares are expected to change hands. One result is intended to be the concern's future passing to the two couples' six children, three on either side.

The nucleus of the group, Construcciones y Contratas, was founded by Ernesto Koplowitz, the father of Esther and Alicia, as a public works contractor in the 1940s. In the 1970s and 1980s, under the management of their husbands, who are cousins, it grew to be one of Spain's most powerful private business empires. But it failed in its most ambitious project, launched in 1987, to gain control of Banco Central, then Spain's biggest bank. The business conflict that ensued led to tensions within the group, contributing ultimately to the breakdown of the two couples' marriages.

Under the agreement, the Koplowitz sisters will retain control of Construcciones y Contratas, the construction company Fomento de Obras y Construcciones S.A., the cement company Portland Valderrivas S.A. and about 50 other companies. Los Albertos will take charge of the group's stakes in Banco Zaragozano, in Carlo De Benedetti's Spanish holding company Cofir and in the planned Spanish pay-television station Canal Plus, and will retain their plush offices on the 43rd floor of Madrid's Torre Picasso, owned by Portland Valderrivas, and two country estates. The holdings involved have an estimated value of 10.6 billion pesetas ($101.3 million).

The pact brings to a close one of the most passion-filled chapters of Spain's recent business history. Los Albertos made repeated headlines in the past 2 1/2 years, first with their abortive bid for Banco Central and later because of their amorous affairs. They were the subject of a page-one article in The Wall Street Journal in December.
