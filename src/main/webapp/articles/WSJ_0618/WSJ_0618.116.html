Control of Major Airports by Carriers Is Focus of Justice Department Inquiry

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani and Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 06/18/90

The Justice Department is investigating one of the most controversial offshoots of airline deregulation -- the domination of major airports by one or two large airlines. A department spokesman confirmed that the agency is in the preliminary stages of a broad inquiry into possible antitrust violations in these markets. As an initial step in the probe, federal prosecutors asked USAir Inc. to supply information about how gates will be allocated at a new terminal at Greater Pittsburgh International Airport.

A Justice Department spokesman said the agency wants, among other things, to find out if arrangements between the airport and USAir could make it harder for other airlines to start service in Pittsburgh. The unit of Arlington, Va.-based USAir Group Inc. already controls 81% of the market in Pittsburgh, measured by the number of passengers carried. Including its commuter airline affiliate, USAir has an 87% market share. USAir denies any wrongdoing.

The USAir investigation, first reported last Friday by the Pittsburgh Post Gazette, is part of a broader federal probe, said Joseph Krovisky, a Justice Department spokesman. He declined to identify the other airlines or airports under scrutiny. A number of major carriers, including NWA Inc.'s Northwest Airlines, Delta Air Lines Inc. and UAL Corp.'s United Airlines, said they hadn't been contacted by the Justice Department in connection with this investigation.

The trend toward the building of "fortress hubs" by major carriers has been a controversial one in recent years. The wave of airline mergers in the 1980s left some airports with little open competition. In cities like Pittsburgh, Minneapolis, Detroit and St. Louis, a single carrier now controls nearly all the business. At others, only two airlines share most of the business.

A study by the General Accounting Office, Congress's investigative arm, found that ticket prices at these airports have risen faster than elsewhere. The average one-way fare between Pittsburgh and New York, for instance, has risen 70% to $90.28 in 1988 from $52.98 in 1984.

The USAir investigation, which is "in the preliminary stages," is separate from a continuing Justice Department probe of possible ticket price-fixing by major airlines, the spokesman said. The price-fixing investigation targets AMR Corp.'s American Airlines, among other carriers, although the Justice Department has never formally confirmed which companies are under scrutiny.

"Justice Department has turned up the heat," said William E. Kovacic, a former antitrust attorney at the Federal Trade Commission. "We've seen the {amount of} activity pick up."

The agency's concern about market domination at certain airports dates back to the mid-1980s when it opposed big mergers such as Trans World Airlines -- Northwest-Republic. But the Transportation Department, which had final authority over the mergers, approved the transactions. At the time, airlines and officials of the Transportation Department reasoned that competition between airports was a more important consideration than what would happen in individual markets. They also decided that the threat of new competition would force carriers to hold down fares in their home markets.

But now there is concern about whether or not carriers are taking steps to keep new competitors off their turf. For instance, the agreements under investigation in Pittsburgh would allow USAir to control 50 of the 75 gates at the new terminal. This "may limit the entry of new competitors into the Pittsburgh market," in violation of federal prohibitions on restraint of trade and attempted monopolization, the Justice Department spokesman said.

The issue of market domination also was raised last year, when the Justice Department threatened to file a civil lawsuit to block the sale of Philadelphia gates and other assets owned by Eastern Air Lines Inc., a unit of Continental Airlines Holding Inc., to USAir, which is the largest carrier in Philadelphia. Midway Airlines Inc., a new entrant in the market, got the okay instead. Two years ago, the Justice Department asked United Airlines for information about the control of takeoff and landing slots at Chicago's O'Hare International Airport. In that case, which is still under review, the agency is concerned that the way slots are acquired could restrict competition.

The latest investigation came to light when USAir disclosed it in documents related to the issuance of about $85 million in revenue bonds for the Pittsburgh airport expansion.

The Justice Department has sent USAir formal requests for documents on its activities at the Pittsburgh airport. The requests, known as civil investigatory demands, are the civil equivalent of criminal subpoenas. They ask for a broad range of documents and data related to possible antitrust violations.

"Nobody has accused USAir of anything," says a spokesman for the carrier. The airline says it believes its agreements with airport authorities won't prevent increased competition at Pittsburgh and aren't designed to do so.

Herb Hovenkamp, an antitrust attorney at the University of Iowa, considers the Justice Department's series of investigations into the airline industry "a silent recognition that one important aspect of the Reagan era's antitrust policy didn't work very well."

"I think they believe that the error of indulgence toward mergers was most serious toward the airline industry," says Mr. Hovenkamp.

The Justice Department's investigation of major airport dominance by one or two big carriers reflects the Bush administration's stepped-up concern about antitrust enforcement, especially in the airline industry. In contrast, the Reagan administration took a generally lenient approach toward mergers among rivals and allowed three major airline combinations, which significantly increased the market power of the surviving carriers.

Although he hasn't blocked any notable mergers, President Bush's chief antitrust enforcer, James Rill, has talked tough about increased federal scrutiny. He has launched investigations in a number of industries, including airlines, health-care and higher education, reminding corporate America that the antitrust laws aren't dead.

Mr. Rill, an assistant attorney general, has indicated personal concern about single-carrier domination at key airports resulting in higher ticket prices. During a recent interview, he pointed out that an internal Justice Department "discussion paper" had concluded that two recent airline mergers had resulted in higher prices at major airports.

Specifically, the document concluded that the merger of TWA and Ozark "appears to have caused a slight increase in fares and a far greater reduction in service" out of the St. Louis airport. The paper also concluded that the merger of Northwest and Republic "appears to have caused a significant increase in fares and a significant reduction in overall service" out of the Minneapolis-St. Paul airport.
