Drexel Creditors' Committee Is Taking Depositions of Employees Over Bonuses

  • Author: Wade Lambert
  • Date: 06/18/90

NEW YORK -- The creditors' committee of Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc. has begun taking depositions of company employees to determine whether any improprieties occurred in the company's controversial bonus payments. The committee also is conducting fact-finding interviews of current and former Drexel officials about transactions involving gold loaned to Drexel by foreign central banks. The central banks are estimated to have lost more than $500 million in the deals, in which Drexel paid them interest for use of the gold as capital.

Robert T. Ladd, an executive vice president of First City, Texas-Houston who represents the bank on the committee, said about 50 former and current officials will be questioned in the next two months. Also under investigation are partnerships in which Drexel employees benefited from selling securities underwritten by the company. First City, Texas-Houston, is the main banking unit of First City Bancorp. The officials to be questioned include Drexel President Frederick Joseph and Richard Wright, chief financial officer.

Under an order signed Friday by U.S. bankruptcy court Judge Howard Buschman III, the committee's findings will be part of a disclosure statement to be released about Dec. 10 that will also include proposals for Drexel's reorganization.

Mr. Ladd said the investigation could turn up hundreds of millions of dollars that may be recoverable under bankruptcy law. He said the committee is focusing on whether Drexel acted imprudently in granting the bonuses and entering into the other transactions just months before filing for bankruptcy-law protection. The creditors have said they won't seek to recover the bonuses if the liquidation of Drexel's assets covers its liabilities. Drexel filed under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code on Feb. 13.

Drexel has said there was nothing improper about the bonus payments, the gold transactions or the partnerships.

If the committee finds any improprieties, the report could give weight to the creditors' demand that current Drexel management be ousted in the reorganization. Drexel and the creditors have until mid-November to come up with a consensual reorganization plan, but the two sides are far apart in their negotiations.

In the order issued by Judge Buschman in federal bankruptcy court in Manhattan, the committee has until Oct. 31 to analyze potential steps that could be taken to go after the bonuses or other payments. The depositions must be completed by Sept. 15.

The judge also ordered that the value of Drexel's portfolio of securities be analyzed by Sept. 15. The order sets Feb. 28, 1991, as the goal for getting a reorganization plan approved.

A spokesman for Drexel said the order sets forth "a reasonable time frame to accomplish what we have to accomplish, which is to get a consensual plan that is fair to creditors and to shareholders." He added that the judge's order doesn't give the creditors any investigative authority they didn't already have.
