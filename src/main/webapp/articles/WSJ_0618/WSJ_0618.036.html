Reebok Itself May Be Most LIkely Buyer For Pentland's 31.5% Stake in Company

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley and Joann S. Lublin
  • Date: 06/18/90

Reebok International Ltd. itself is the most likely buyer for the 31.5% stake in the company put on the block by Pentland Group PLC, its largest shareholder, analysts say. Several analysts speculated that London-based Pentland will shop the block around for several months, but find few bidders because of the limitations on what a buyer could do with the shares. However, the effort may help determine a fair price. "I think it's a dance to determine price," said Gary Jacobson, footwear analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co. "Pentland feels it's worth a premium. Reebok probably doesn't," he said.

Reebok disclosed Pentland's plan late Thursday, and simultaneously announced new takeover defenses. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Reebok rose 50 cents to $18.50. At that price, Pentland's 36.2 million shares of Reebok would be worth about $670 million.

U.S. analysts struggled to come up with names of companies that might be interested in acquiring a minority stake in a maker of trendy footwear. They noted that standstill agreements would prohibit a buyer from expanding its stake. In addition, Reebok's second biggest holder, Chairman Paul Fireman, who owns 14.5% of the firm's stock, has a right of first refusal on the Pentland shares.

In a telephone interview Friday, Frank Farrant, finance director of Pentland, a footwear trading firm, said "we have a list of people that we intend to contact" about possibly buying the stake. He declined to identify then or provide the timing of any sale. He said Pentland plans to sell the stock as a block in order "to get strategic value for it."

Mr. Farrant, who also sits on Reebok's board, emphasized that Pentland was ending its relationship amicably. He said Pentland originally invested $77,000 in Reebok in 1981 and has realized #37 million ($63.2 million) through previous sales of Reebok shares.

The executive said Pentland is disposing of its remaining Reebok shares because it doesn't reap cash flow from a minority position, only dividends. Reebok pays a quarterly dividend of 7.5 cents a share. Mr. Farrant added: "We don't want to take the money and run. We want to take the money and invest it in a business that will give us cash flow." But he conceded that Pentland won't easily find another growth stock like Reebok: "The reality of life is you only are blessed once."

Pentland has hired S.G. Warburg & Co., London, to help it sell its Reebok stake. Reebok has hired First Boston Corp., a New York investment banking firm, to advise it, according to Wall Street executives. First Boston declined comment.

A Warburg official indicated that Pentland expects to receive offers for its Reebok shares from companies outside the U.S. which could help Reebok grow abroad. "We think it is important to help Reebok to grow in international markets and I think that's where a lot of interest will come from," the Warburg official said.

The Warburg official declined to comment on whether Pentland had asked Reebok to buy its holdings, or how Reebok might have responded.

Pentland decided to sell its Reebok shares now because a series of contracts under which Pentland supplies Reebok with merchandise expire this year, the Warburg official said.

If Reebok does end up buying back the shares it would be good for Reebok holders because it would "shrink the market capitalization and accelerate the earnings gains," said Alice Ruth, an analyst at Montgomery Securities in San Francisco. She said Reebok has ample cash flow to support borrowing money to buy back the Pentland block. She calculated that if Reebok bought the Pentland stake at $20 a share it would cost about $82 million in annual interest costs, well below the company's estimated $250 million in available annual cash flow, she said.

Ms. Ruth said Reebok's value in a takeover might be as high as $24 to $26 a share, but she said she considers a takeover unlikely.

Heidi Steinberg, who follows specialty retailing for Salomon Bros., said that in a hostile takeover, "Reebok would be more than a $2 billion purchase. The companies that could afford it wouldn't be interested. Those who would be interested couldn't afford it. I don't think Reebok is likely to be put into play."

However, Dennis Rosenberg, a special situations analyst with Oppenheimer & Co., said the Pentland plan "definitely" puts Reebok in play. He said that between its substantial cash flow and its well-known brand name, the company would be attractive to acquirers who might bid for the Pentland block. He speculated that such a bidder would want to make an offer for the entire company, despite Mr. Fireman's wish that it remain independent.

"Fireman has to act in the interests of all the shareholders," Mr. Rosenberg said.
