Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Third Reading on First-Period Strength In Economy Is Expected to Be Unchanged

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 06/18/90

NEW YORK -- The third and "final" reading on first-quarter economic strength is likely to be unchanged, but that's about where agreement ends. Consensus holds that the government's third version of first-quarter gross national product, due Thursday, will be unchanged from the 1.3% annual growth rate estimated in May. Still, many economists expect wrenching revisions in July when the government tidies up past reports with annual benchmark and seasonal revisions. What's more, some analysts disagree on just what GNP numbers mean these days, revised or not.

The week's other indicators include May housing starts and building permits, durable goods and personal income and consumption.

Real GNP, or the value of the nation's total output of goods and services, is only one of the indicators whose reliability has been questioned lately. But it is an important one because GNP is a critical part of both policy-making and economic forecasting.

David B. Bostian Jr., chief economist at Jesup & Lamont Inc., believes that the "dramatic deterioration" in the quality of GNP data obscures the fact that "we're in a recession now." On the other hand, Michael R. Englund, economist with MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., defends a brighter GNP outlook. He notes that he raised his second-quarter GNP forecast a bit to 2% from 1.8% Friday after favorable reports on both May industrial production and the April trade deficit.

Mr. Bostian says that recent downward revisions to 1989 data indicate that this past year was weaker than generally thought. What's more, he adds that revisions to industrial production dragged the second half of 1989 into negative territory, and that revised estimates of 1989 wage and salary income suggest a broad overstatement.

He believes there's a "50-50 chance" that GNP reports for both the fourth quarter of 1989 and the first quarter of this year will be revised to show negative growth. Mr. Bostian adds that the economy may be close to a "real-time" negative GNP in the third quarter. Based on recent weak indicators, including this past week's report on a drop in May retail sales, he says: "The economy is going to slow down further. So, we are going to see one or more negative {GNP} numbers." Mr. Bostian acknowledges that his is one of the more pessimistic economic views these days.

A survey of GNP forecasts taken by MMS after Friday's data were disclosed showed positive, albeit small, growth forecasts. The consensus view called for GNP growth of 1.5% in the second quarter, 2.1% in the third quarter, and 1.7% in the fourth quarter -- all down 0.5 percentage point or less from the mid-March consensus.

MMS's Mr. Englund has one of the rosier views. He defends his outlook by noting that recent, unusually bumpy data only mask some fundamental, long-term changes in the nature of the economy. In short, Mr. Englund holds that U.S. economic strength has been moving geographically to West from East, spatially to small from big, and sectorally to aerospace from autos. So, classic indicators like industrial production are skewed toward large auto makers and tend to undercount the contribution of smaller companies.

"The composition of the economy at the end of this recovery is quite different than at the beginning," says Mr. Englund.

Economists are keeping a close eye on consumer spending, which so far continues to be one of the economy's main engines. The consensus view on May personal consumption, due Friday, calls for a small increase of 0.2%, following April's 0.6% gain. Gary Ciminero, economist at Fleet/Norstar Financial Group, Providence, R.I., says spending on services probably grew 0.7%, only to be offset by a 0.7% decline in goods consumption. The consensus view on May personal income calls for a gain of 0.4%, down slightly from April's gain.

Economists say durable goods orders in May probably rebounded with the help of better aircraft orders. The goods report, due Friday, is expected to show an increase of 2% following April's 4.3% drop.

May housing starts, due tomorrow are expected to have another lackluster month, slowing to an annual pace of 1.22 million units from 1.25 million units the month before.
