Marketing & Media: Nielsen Ratings Will Face Competition As Britain's AGB Plans to Re-Enter U.S.

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 06/18/90

NEW YORK -- The A.C. Nielsen Co., whose audience ratings methodology is harshly criticized by the three major broadcast networks, is about to get new competition: AGB Television Research, a British-based ratings company owned by Robert Maxwell, the British media conglomerate owner. The AGB plan is only one of several expected from research companies as the networks seek alternatives to the Nielsen measurement system. For example, the Arbitron Co. is testing a sophisticated system in Denver, titled ScanAmerica, that connects product purchasing with the viewing of television commercials.

AGB challenged Nielsen, a division of Dun & Bradstreet Corp., in the 1980s, but lost $67 million after only CBS Inc. paid for the service. Capital Cities/ABC Inc. and National Broadcasting Co., a unit of General Electric Co., declined to participate in AGB's attempt to take on Nielsen's monopoly in measuring the American television audience. AGB shut down its American subsidiary in August 1988.

AGB's move, which is expected to be announced today, is the latest salvo in the increasingly bitter dispute between the three networks and Nielsen, which earns a combined $15 million annually from the networks for suppling ratings data on which advertising rates and the fates of prime-time series are decided.

Each network alleges that Nielsen's ratings based on the so-called people meter, an electronic device with push-buttons, are inaccurate. For ratings to register, an individual must press a button on the people meter each time a program is viewed. The networks say the audience easily tires of such an exercise.

The networks have openly encouraged other companies, domestic or foreign, to take on Nielsen, and they said they will pay for the service.

"We welcome the competition," said David F. Poltrack, a CBS senior vice president.

Mr. Poltrack said he didn't expect AGB, a subsidiary of Audits of Great Britain, will be able to have a service operational until September 1992. "We anticipate the 1991-92 television season as a year of experimentation for AGB," he said.

A Nielsen spokesman said: "We welcome the challenge. We've competed before, we'll compete again. We'll compete with anybody. Competition is nothing new to us."

AGB pioneered the people meter system in Britain. It was AGB's arrival in the American market that forced Nielsen to quickly switch to people meters from handwritten diaries. AGB supplied ratings during the 1987-88 television season.

Although he declined specific comment, another network executive said Mr. Poltrack was briefed by AGB officials Friday about how AGB's system would differ from Nielsen's. "Clearly the industry does not need a copy cat approach to Nielsen," he said.

For example, AGB will remove an individual or family from its survey if it discovers that the household has stopped using the people meter. Nielsen doesn't turn over its sample of 4,000 people meter households for two years.

Nielsen recently challenged AGB for the British market. But AGB prevailed in intense bidding. "We came a lot closer than anyone expected," said a Nielsen official. Nielsen is used in other overseas markets, including Australia.

The controversy with Nielsen deepened recently when Nielsen data showed television network viewing plunging by 7% in the first quarter. Neither the networks nor Nielsen could explain the sharp drop, which resulted in the networks giving back millions of dollars in free ad time to advertisers. Sponsors receive free commercial time, or "make goods," when audience viewing levels fall below those guaranteed by the networks.

All three networks said last week that they will no longer give advertisers traditional audience guarantees. Instead, the networks said, they will adopt a plan presented by NBC last week. That plan uses an eight-year audience average to project ratings, instead of relying exclusively on Nielsen ratings.

The moves come as the television and advertising industries prepare for the projected $4.4 billion up-front market, when advertising is sold in advance of the 1990-91 season, which begins in September.
