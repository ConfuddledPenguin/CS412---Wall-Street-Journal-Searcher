Law: Siegel's Cooperation With U.S. Results In Lenient Sentence for Securities Fraud

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 06/18/90

NEW YORK -- In a move that reflects the extraordinary value that courts and prosecutors place on cooperation by defendants in securities fraud cases, former Wall Street investment banker Martin A. Siegel was sentenced to two months in prison for his crimes. In imposing the lenient sentence, U.S. District Judge Robert J. Ward said that the two securities fraud-related felony counts to which Mr. Siegel pleaded guilty in February 1987 were "serious." But Judge Ward added that Mr. Siegel's "cooperation, his contrition and his candor under difficult circumstances" prompted him to order a much lighter sentence than many had expected. Mr. Siegel could have been sentenced to as long as 10 years in prison.

Judge Ward also noted that the government's position, put forth in a sentencing memo released on Friday, was "not typical." The government, in its memo and in court, praised Mr. Siegel as a "credible and reliable" witness who cooperated despite "an intense campaign of villification." The government said the assistance of the 42-year-old Mr. Siegel enabled prosecutors to learn about wrongdoing they would not otherwise have uncovered.

The sentencing of Mr. Siegel, Kidder, Peabody & Co.'s former chief takeover strategist, ended a saga that began on Nov. 14, 1986, when a federal marshal thrust a subpoena into Mr. Siegel's hands relating to his dealings with Ivan F. Boesky, the convicted former stock speculator. Mr. Boesky's cooperation, which began the insider trading investigation, led the government to Mr. Siegel.

Mr. Siegel began cooperating with the government and eventually pleaded guilty to exchanging insider information with Mr. Boesky and with Robert M. Freeman, the former head of arbitrage at Goldman, Sachs & Co. Mr. Siegel also paid more than $9 million in fines.

Mr. Siegel's cooperation proved a bonanza for the government. The Kidder unit agreed in June 1987 to pay $25.3 million to settle Securities and Exchange Commission charges in connection with Mr. Siegel's actions. The government also disclosed for the first time in its sentencing memo that leads provided by Mr. Siegel resulted in the conviction last year of five officials of Princeton/Newport Partners and a former Drexel trader on racketeering and other charges.

Mr. Freeman pleaded guilty last August to one felony count. Mr. Freeman, who had long denied that he had done anything wrong, admitted to using inside information received from Mr. Siegel in connection with the proposed takeover of Beatrice Cos. by Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. In April, Mr. Freeman was sentenced to four months in jail.

The government has said the crime to which Mr. Freeman pleaded guilty was one of many he committed. But until Friday, the government hadn't elaborated on Mr. Freeman's other alleged misdeeds. In its memo, the government said that beginning in 1984, Messrs. Freeman and Siegel passed inside information to each other about companies involved in "at least twelve takeover situations."

The government also said that Mr. Siegel provided credible information about a 1985 telephone call in which Mr. Siegel received a tip from Mr. Freeman about Unocal Corp. Mr. Siegel then ordered Kidder traders to buy securities based on that tip. The government said in the memo that it also seized a tape from Princeton/Newport on which Mr. Freeman is heard giving that defunct firm similar information about Unocal.

On Friday, Mr. Freeman's attorneys continued to deny that he had engaged in further wrongdoing. "Mr. Freeman has taken and passed a lie detector test on his statements that no illicit arrangement with Siegel ever existed and that he never deliberately gave Siegel confidential information to help Siegel make a trading decision," the attorneys, Paul Curran and Robert Fiske Jr., said in a statement.

According to individuals familiar with the drafting of the government's memo, Messrs. Curran and Fiske successfully sought to block the government from including more details about Mr. Freeman's actions. Those details were included in a probation report that was not made public. Mr. Curran declined to comment.

Mr. Siegel had accepted $700,000 in cash payments from Mr. Boesky in 1983 and 1984 in exchange for information he provided to Mr. Boesky. Judge Ward noted that the profits of Mr. Boesky, who recently completed a three-year prison term, totaled $30 million.

"After Mr. Boesky received a three-year sentence {in 1987}, I began to think about Mr. Siegel," Judge Ward said. "At that time, I was of the view that" a prison term of "18 months to two years would be reasonable" for Mr. Siegel.

Judge Ward said that in the "intervening period, I was advised that the government wished to defer Mr. Siegel's sentence because of his cooperation." Largely because of that cooperation, Judge Ward decided that Mr. Siegel should serve "a sentence less than imposed on Mr. Freeman," who has refused to cooperate with the government.

Legal experts say that Judge Ward is likely to be attacked for the lenient sentence. Jed Rakoff, an attorney for Mr. Siegel, said: "The sentence was fair and just and reflected very well on the degree to which Judge Ward immersed himself in the particulars of the case."

Mr. Siegel was also sentenced to five years probation and ordered to perform 1,500 hours of community service in Jacksonville, Fla., near his home. He is to begin serving the sentence on July 16.
