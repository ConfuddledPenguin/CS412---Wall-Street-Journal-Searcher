Southeast Banking Slashes Its Dividend, Expects to Post Losses for Quarter, Year

  • Author: Peter Waldman
  • Date: 06/18/90

Southeast Banking Corp. slashed its dividend, announced asset sales, boosted loan-loss reserves and said it expects to post losses for the second quarter and year, all reflecting the worsening condition of the South Florida real estate market. As recently as March 23, Charles J. Zwick, chairman and chief executive officer of Miami-based Southeast, said the bank holding company would be profitable this year, and many banking analysts had expected Southeast to turn a modest profit in the second quarter.

But Friday, the company disclosed that the credit quality of its large South Florida loan portfolio is much lower than previously believed.

The bleak outlook sent Southeast stock tumbling Friday to $11, down $2.375, in heavy New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Southeast said it would take several steps to shore up its balance sheet. First, the company slashed its quarterly dividend by 64%, to 10 cents a share from 28 cents a share. The dividend is payable July 17 to shares of record June 25. The last time Southeast cut its dividend was 1976.

Second, the company said it reached a preliminary agreement to sell certain assets of its mortgage subsidiary to Sears Mortgage Corp., a unit of Sears Roebuck & Co. The transaction, if completed, would include Southeast's residental mortgage servicing portfolio, a number of branch offices of the mortgage unit and other assets and would result in a payment to Southeast of as much as $100 million, the firm said.

Southeast said it is looking for other "significant cost savings," including the possibility of deferring some capital projects.

Third, Southeast said it is adding between $2 million and $22 million to its loan-loss reserve, raising its total provision for bad loans to a projected $260 million to $280 million by June 30 from $258 million March 31. The increase reflects a surge in nonperforming assets. The company said it expects its total nonperforming loans to range between $600 million and $620 million by June 30, up at least 26% since March 31.

Loan-loss reserves are a fund that banks set aside to offset expected losses from bad loans. Nonperforming assets are loans that have been foreclosed upon or are more than 90 days past due.

Southeast didn't elaborate on its projected results for the second quarter or year except to say that neither period will be profitable. In the 1989 second quarter, Southeast had net income of $32.3 million, or $1 a share. For all of 1989, it had net of $80.5 million, or $2.42 a share.

"The condition of Florida's economy has not improved and continues to adversely affect the loan porfolio," Mr. Zwick said. But, he said, "The situation now is better than it was in the mid-'70s," the time of the last severe slump in Florida real estate.

Cheryl Swaim, a banking analyst with Oppenheimer & Co., said Southeast's loan portfolio is deteriorating faster than she expected. In addition to the Florida real estate problems, she said, Southeast added a $55 million leveraged buy-out loan to its nonperforming category in the second quarter. Before Friday, Ms. Swaim projected Southeast would earn between 25 cents and 30 cents a share in the second quarter and at least break even in 1990.

Mr. Zwick confirmed that Southeast added an LBO loan to its nonperforming list in the period, but he wouldn't elaborate on the size of the loan or provide any other details. He said 70% to 75% of Southeast's nonperforming assets are from real estate loans, with the rest from commercial credit.

John J. Mason, a banking analyst with Interstate/Johnson Lane, said he expects Southeast to record a roughly $50 million capital gain from the sale of its residential mortgage unit, compared with the gross price of as much as $100 million that Southeast said it should receive for the assets. The difference reflects Southeast's hefty investment in acquiring the mortgage subsidiary, said Mr. Mason. "They paid real money for that company," he said.

Separately, Moody's Investors Service Inc. said it lowered its senior debt ratings for Southeast as well as the company's commercial paper rating. The rating service also said it downgraded the ratings of long-term and short-term deposits of the company's Southeast Bank N.A. unit.

The changes affect about $375 million of debt.
