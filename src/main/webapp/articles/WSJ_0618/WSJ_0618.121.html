SEC, Congressional Panel Investigate `Roll-Ups' to Check Fairness to Investors

  • Author: Jill Bettner
  • Date: 06/18/90

The Securities and Exchange Commission and a congressional subcommittee are looking into reorganizations of public limited-partnerships called "roll-ups" to see if they are fair to investors. The SEC said it initiated a staff inquiry after receiving complaints from some limited partners involved in the reorganizations earlier this year. An SEC spokeswoman wouldn't discuss details of the inquiry and said the agency won't make the results public.

Rep. Edward J. Markey (D. Mass.), chairman of the House Subcommittee on Telecommunications and Finance, told SEC Chairman Richard C. Breeden this past week, however, that the subcommittee will conduct hearings on roll-ups next month. The subcommittee, which deals with securities issues, has asked the SEC for answers by July 13 to an extensive list of questions the agency has about the reorganizations.

Roll-ups already in progress or planned by syndicators will affect an estimated 146,000 investors who put about $1.2 billion into limited partnerships, Rep. Markey said in a letter to Mr. Breeden. "The subcommittee is generally concerned that individual investors haven't been well-served by such reorganizations in the past and that regulatory or legislative changes may be necessary to assure that limited partners are treated fairly in future roll-ups."

In a roll-up, several non-traded limited partnerships -- typically including some healthy ones and some that aren't -- are consolidated into new, publicly traded investments. Limited partners usually approve the reorganization to gain liquidity.

But often, the supposed exchange value of their interests is far below the price the market puts on the new units when they begin trading. Most roll-ups so far have involved real estate partnerships or those that invested in oil and gas.

Many analysts expect a wave of the reorganizations because of the large number of syndicators that are financially troubled. A roll-up effectively transforms limited partnerships, which have a finite life of about 10 years, into an open-ended investment that continues to generate management fees for the general partner.

The subcommittee has asked the SEC for ways to mitigate such conflicts of interest, suggesting that establishing an independent board of trustees might be one remedy. The panel has also requested that the agency evaluate whether the SEC's current disclosure rules on roll-ups and the legal remedies available to investors are adequate.
