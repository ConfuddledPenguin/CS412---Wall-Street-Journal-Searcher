REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Crackdown in Romania

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/18/90

A woman named Florica Ichim is hiding for her life in an apartment on Petre Cretu Street in Bucharest. Florica Ichim is the executive editor of Romania Libera, until last week Romania's biggest opposition daily. Wednesday, Romania Libera was still printing 1.5 million copies. Early Thursday, its own printers -- doubtless pushed by the government -- arrived at the paper's door and beat up the only person around to greet them -- the receptionist. Then they took clubs to Romania Libera's printing presses and ransacked editors' offices. Next arrived vandals who called themselves miners from the countryside. Their leader told a frightened staffer, "Romania Libera will not be printed anymore." President-elect Ion Iliescu made this sudden surge of government-initiated brutality official when, in a public speech, he deemed the Romanian press responsible for the anti-government uprising that has shaken the capital in past days. Strangers have already appeared at the doors of Mrs. Ichim's colleagues with trumped-up excuses for house searches.

The repression, of course, goes beyond Romania Libera, or the press in general. Crowds looted offices of opposition parties and systematically beat up pedestrians. A photocopying machine at the office of the opposition Liberal Party was presented on television as evidence that the party was running a counterfeit operation. Leaders of another party, the Peasant Party -- a group of oldsters mostly well into their 70s -- were charged with dealing narcotics.

It's rather clear that much of this mob is Ceausescu's old Securitate police. Many of the "miners" who traveled into Bucharest to aid Mr. Iliescu this past week wore suits -- hardly the garments of workers. Witnesses also report that the beatings these forces delivered -- they wielded truncheons and metal cord -- betrayed professional skill.

That such behavior comes from a government that is the product of the freest election Romania has experienced in 40 years is disheartening. What is clear is that whatever mandate Mr. Iliescu garnered in last month's elections is now cracking. Petre Roman, Romania's prime minister, and Silviu Brucan, another leading figure, spent this weekend taking care to distance themselves from the bloody Iliescu crackdown. There will be a simple way to recognize when real change has returned to Romania. It will be the moment when Mrs. Ichim is able to start printing a free paper again.
