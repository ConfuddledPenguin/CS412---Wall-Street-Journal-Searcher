Technology: Texas Instruments To Introduce New Laptop Computer

  • Author: Andy Zipser
  • Date: 06/18/90

DALLAS -- Texas Instruments Inc., continuing its effort to ease back into the personal computer market after an absence of nearly a decade, will today introduce its entry into the crowded field of so-called "notebook" personal computers. The TravelMate 2000, to be introduced at the annual PC Expo trade show in New York, is a 4.4-pound notebook-sized laptop computer that measures 8.5 inches by 11 inches and is just 1.4 inches thick. The machine runs on an Intel 80C286 microprocessor, has a 20 megabyte hard disk drive, has a rechargeable, nickel cadmium battery that will run for two hours, an optional battery pack that will extend operating time to five hours, and will retail at $3,999.

Having survived a disastrous plunge into the personal computer market in the early 1980s, Texas Instruments returned to the business last fall with three laptop computers. But those machines were market followers. With the TravelMate 2000, which the company claims is the thinnest and lightest of the notebooks that run on the 286 chips, Texas Instruments hopes to get a leadership position.

But it won't be easy to gain or maintain a leading position in the competitive new market for notebooks. The field has gotten crowded with formidable competitors such as Compaq Computer Corp. and NEC Corp. And the technology is advancing rapidly.

Also to be unveiled at PC Expo, for example, is a notebook designed by Airis Computer Corp. that weighs 6.5 pounds, runs an Intel 80C286 microprocessor and has a 20-megabyte hard disk drive. But the Airis machine runs on C batteries, rather than the heavy and expensive rechargeable batteries now in general use, and will retail for only $1,899 -- roughly half of what Texas Instruments, Compaq Computer Corp. and virtually every other manufacturer is charging for a comparable machine. Some analysts, however, question whether the Chicago-based start-up company will have the resources to mass produce the machine in a reasonable amount of time.

Faced with such formidable competition and chastened by its earlier failure, Texas Instruments is trying to share the risks and costs with other computer resellers. The company manufactures a virtually identical machine that was recently introduced by Sharp Corp.

The machine being introduced today will be marketed under the Texas Instruments label. But unlike many of the competing notebooks, the TravelMate is being targeted primarily for business users and won't generally be available at consumer-oriented retail stores.
