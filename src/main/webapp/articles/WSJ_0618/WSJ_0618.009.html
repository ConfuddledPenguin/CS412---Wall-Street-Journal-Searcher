Economy: Factory Output Climbed 0.6% During May --- Consumer Prices Rose 0.2%; Economy Is Said to Be In a Slow-Growth Rut

  • Author: Alan Murray
  • Date: 06/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. economy continues to show little luster, with slow growth and steady inflation. Friday's economic data gave some cause for optimism. The Federal Reserve reported that a rebound in auto production pushed factory output up 0.6% in May. And the Labor Department said consumer prices rose a modest 0.2% in the same month.

But in the wake of Wednesday's dismal retail-sales report, economists say the latest data only confirm that the economy is stuck in a slow-growth rut.

"Friday's numbers offer some hope that activity in the manufacturing sector is at the very least stabilizing, and perhaps improving," said Norman Robertson, chief economist for Mellon Bank in Pittsburgh. "But that comes at a time when activity in almost every other sector is showing signs of weakness. Putting it all together, it's very hard to see anything much better than an economic growth rate in the 1% to 2% range."

The slow growth in consumer prices during May provided little reason to fear that inflation is accelerating. But the report also offered little evidence that the rate of inflation is coming down.

"Year over year, excluding food and energy, you are still dealing with 4.8% core inflation," said Lyle Gramley, chief economist for the Mortgage Bankers' Association and a former Federal Reserve Board governor. "Inflation has been contained, but there hasn't been an outright reduction."

Michael Boskin, chairman of the president's Council of Economic Advisers, said the latest figures are in line with the administration's expectations. "As we said last year, we expected the economy to grow more slowly in late '89 and early '90, and that is indeed what is happening," he said. The administration believes "the economy will do better in the second half of this year," Mr. Boskin said.

Privately, administration officials have been hoping the Federal Reserve would give a boost to the economy by easing credit and bringing down short-term interest rates. But with few signs of a recession, and with inflation unchanged, the Fed is unlikely to change its policy soon. Fed officials say that before easing, they would like to see convincing evidence that the inflation rate has started to drop.

"The only way to keep inflation under control is to keep a tight rein on the economy," said Robert Ortner, former Commerce Department undersecretary and author of the book "Voodoo Deficits." Referring to the Fed chairman, he added: "Alan Greenspan's job is to promote a healthy economy; it is not to get George Bush re-elected."

May's rise in industrial production followed a month in which factory output, according to revised estimates, was unchanged. The Fed initially reported a 0.4% drop in output in April. Most of the rise in May was in motor vehicles and parts. Excluding those, production rose only 0.2% from the previous month.

The rise in production brought the operating rate at the nation's factories, mines and utilities to 83.6% in May, up from 83.3% in April.

May's 0.2% rise in consumer prices was the same as April's increase, but it followed a string of much bigger jumps in the first three months of this year. Prices in May were 4.4% above their level during the same 1989 month.

Medical care costs showed the biggest increase in the month, while prices for gasoline and apparel dropped. Excluding food and energy prices, which are volatile, prices rose 0.3% in May.

The Labor Department also reported that the average weekly earnings of nonsupervisory workers rose 0.3% in May, after accounting for inflation. That followed a 0.2% rise in April. During the past year, earnings have fallen a steep 1.8% after inflation.

All the figures are adjusted for predictable seasonable variations.
