Appeals Court Says Merrill Must Pay Punitive Damages --- Award in `Churning' Case Is Upheld Even Though Account's Value Rose 50%

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 06/18/90

NEW YORK -- Small investors can sometimes punish their brokers in court even when they make big bucks. At least that's the message from the Eighth U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals in St. Paul, Minn. In a 3-0 decision, the court Friday upheld a $2 million punitive damage award against Merrill Lynch & Co. in an unusual excessive-trading case involving the account of a deceased widow.

Merrill, the nation's largest securities firm, says it is "greatly disappointed" with the decision, and may appeal. One reason: The investor's account actually rose 50% during the time of the complaint, Merrill Lynch says.

It's rare that investors are awarded punitive damages when they haven't suffered monetary losses, securities lawyers say. "It's extraordinary," says Sam Scott Miller, a former general counsel at PaineWebber Group Inc. and currently a partner at Orrick, Herrington & Sutcliffe in New York.

Punitive damages are generally awarded to punish and deter a defendant found to have acted recklessly or maliciously. (Most cases involving punitive damages occur when investors' nest eggs have been depleted.) Punitive damages are applied in addition to awards intended to compensate for actual injury or loss.

The award for the estate of Ethlyn Davis, the investor, also included $100,000 in compensatory damages. Merrill Lynch said the $2.1 million total has been "previously reserved for." A federal jury awarded the amount in Sioux Falls, S.D., in August 1988.

In court papers, Mrs. Davis alleged that from February 1982 to August 1984 former Merrill Lynch broker Steven Ulrich "churned" her account, which had an average balance of $144,000, by making 141 trades totaling $2 million. Churning is excessive trading in a customer's account by a broker solely to generate commissions.

Despite the heavy trading, Merrill says in court papers that the account actually made a net profit of more than $53,000. But the appellate panel, in a 42-page decision, said: "The gravity of the wrong committed by Merrill Lynch is not mitigated by the fact that the account was nevertheless profitable."

Mr. Ulrich was terminated in August 1984 after other customers complained of unauthorized trading in their accounts, a Merrill Lynch spokesman says. Mrs. Davis died in November 1986 at age 91.

Arlo Sommervold, a lawyer for Mrs. Davis's estate, says Mr. Ulrich should have made much more for his client, contending that stocks were rising for much of the time the alleged excessive trading took place. He says punitive damages were justified.

"You can't rob a bank and then, if they catch you, just give the money back and everyone's even," Mr. Sommervold says. "She's entitled to all the money she should have earned."
