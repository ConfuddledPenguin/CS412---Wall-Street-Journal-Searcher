Pickens Poised Again to Battle Koito But Sees Little Chance of Early Gains

  • Author: Kathryn Graven
  • Date: 06/18/90

TOKYO -- Proclaiming himself wiser in the ways of Japanese business, Texas oilman and corporate raider T. Boone Pickens Jr. comes to Japan this week for another round of battle with Koito Manufacturing Co. But analysts, and even Mr. Pickens himself, don't expect this visit to produce any breakthroughs in his campaign to gain a voice in the auto-parts company's operations.

As the largest shareholder, Mr. Pickens has been fighting for more than a year for seats on Koito's board as well as access to the company's financial records and pricing policies. Through Amarillo, Texas-based Boone Co., a merchant bank he controls, Mr. Pickens owns a 26% stake in Koito.

"Sure, I've learned a lot in a year's time," Mr. Pickens said in a telephone interview. "But I don't have any hope that Koito will change its mind."

Nevertheless, Mr. Pickens is determined to make his presence felt at Koito's annual shareholders' meeting June 28. He is paying the airfare for 50 other Americans who own Koito shares so they can attend the session in Tokyo with him. And in a sugary appeal for support, he mailed videotapes to around 4,000 Koito holders. In the video, which cost about $20,000 to produce, Mr. Pickens outlines his proposals -- which include representation on the board, increased dividend payments and an anti-greenmail resolution -- as he rides horseback across the Texas range.

"I've done everything I can to make it clear I am a long-term investor," insists Mr. Pickens.

But Koito is hardly preparing a red-carpet welcome. The company argues that Mr. Pickens is really a greenmailer -- someone who tries to force a company to buy back his shares at a premium. Mr. Pickens is welcome to attend the annual meeting -- the company will even provide simultaneous interpretation for the visiting Americans -- but that's as far as the hospitality may go. "All of his proposals will be denied," a Koito spokesman says.

Koito's top brass aren't the only ones skeptical of Mr. Pickens's intentions. Last week, Koito's labor union adopted a resolution urging management to reject Mr. Pickens's proposals. Sources say Japanese banks also are under pressure to refrain from lending money to Mr. Pickens or any companies associated with him.

Mr. Pickens confirmed reports that a U.S. subsidiary of a major Japanese bank had withdrawn its intent to join a group of banks that backs Mesa Limited Partners Oil & Gas Co., a publicly held company that Mr. Pickens heads.

Other major shareholders such as Toyota Motor Corp., Nissan Motor Co. and Matsushita Electric Industrial Co. appear to have no time or sympathy for Mr. Pickens. "We contacted all the large shareholders and said we'd like to call on them. They have all said they will be out of town," says Mr. Pickens.

While Koito and its supporters may be turning a deaf ear to Mr. Pickens's complaints, Mr. Pickens has found more eager listeners back in the U.S. Tapping into growing U.S. resentment of Japanese business practices, Mr. Pickens has emerged as a spokesman for those frustrated by Japan's closely connected corporate relationships and has taken his case to Washington to testify before congressional committees.

Analysts in Tokyo agree that Koito and Mr. Pickens seem headed for a long, drawn-out fight. Mr. Pickens is suing Koito in Tokyo District Court for access to its accounting books and other records. He says Koito's pricing policies favor Toyota -- its second largest holder, with a 19% stake -- to the detriment of other holders. The next hearing is set for July 12.
