Economy: Norway May End Output Restrictions It Imposed on Itself in Support of OPEC

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 06/18/90

OSLO -- Norway, an independent North Sea oil producer, may eliminate output restrictions imposed on itself four years ago in support of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries. "It is likely we will not continue these measures" for the second half of the year, Eivind Reiten, the Norwegian oil minister, said in an interview. Since 1986, Norway has voluntarily held its expanding production below capacity in sympathy with OPEC's similar efforts to bolster prices by limiting output. Currently, Norway pumps 5% less oil than it could.

Rescinding the so-called holdback would mean little to the volume of oil reaching world markets, adding only about 80,000 barrels a day to the nearly 1.7 million a day Norway pumps. But the country's posture plays a psychological role in jittery oil markets. Norway was among the first -- and now is the last -- non-OPEC producer to curb output in a goodwill gesture to OPEC.

"We are very pragmatic about it," said Mr. Reiten. He said current oil market conditions suggest Norway no longer needs to hold down production. While petroleum markets recently have softened because of high OPEC production, prices still are nearly double the lows reached during 1986 when Norway volunteered to cooperate in production-cutting.

In fact, Mr. Reiten, named oil minister only a few months ago, has already been changing Norway's policies; in January, he reduced the holdback to the current level from 7.5%.

Mr. Reiten cited other reasons for putting the entire policy under review. He noted that most OPEC members except Saudi Arabia are currently exceeding that organization's production quotas, and that "we seem to be the only country outside OPEC doing anything at all." He said Norway might consider production coordination again "if the situation changes."

Meantime, the country is actively expanding capacity -- largely by developing smaller fields. Mr. Reiten disclosed that he will present applications in two months to Norway's parliament to begin development of two such new fields.

Also, Norway will take bids for exploration licenses on 52 offshore tracts in September, the country's 13th such licensing round and one of its largest ever. The tracts are divided about equally between the Norwegian sectors of the North Sea and the Barents Sea. Although Mr. Reiten said oil companies are "queuing up" for the bidding, the Barents Sea has proved disappointing for Norway so far. "The North Sea is much more promising than expected," he said.

He estimated current spending for exploration and development of North Sea fields at a record 30 billion to 35 billion Norwegian kroner ($4.62 billion to $5.4 billion) annually.

Although North Sea oil workers have threatened to strike July 1 and close production temporarily, Norway's total output is expected to continue rising, at least for a few more years. "We will see a peaking of production at two million barrels a day around 1995," Mr. Reiten said. "We expect it to continue that level for another five or six years" and then start to drop, he said.
