Columbia S&L Sights Reasons For Insolvency --- Thrift Says Declines of 50% And Junk Bond Holdings Pose Liquidity Concerns

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt and Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 04/03/90

Columbia Savings & Loan Association disclosed that declines of 50% or more in the value of certain of its junk bond holdings contributed to its previously reported insolvency and that it faces possible liquidity problems because of newly imposed restrictions on its ability to obtain funds. Over the weekend, Columbia, based in Beverly Hills, Calif., reported that fourth-quarter losses as well as losses for the first two months of 1990, stemming from the deteriorating junk bond market, left it insolvent as of Feb. 28 and susceptible to an imminent federal seizure. The company disclosed further details in a filing yesterday with the federal Office of Thrift Supervision. The firm also revealed that its troubles had left its independent auditor, Deloitte & Touche, unable to render an opinion about its year-end financial statements.

The weekend announcements drew a harsh reaction on Wall Street yesterday. In New York Stock Exchange trading, Columbia closed at 71.9 cents, down 40.6 cents.

Among other things, Columbia disclosed that 32 of its largest junk bond holdings were valued at $308 million less than it paid for them, with a market value of about $1.8 billion compared with a cost of $2.1 billion, a decline of 15%. Some individual securities owned by the company had even greater percentage reductions. The company disclosed, for instance, that its holdings of such troubled issues as Memorex Telex Corp., E-II Holdings Inc. and Harcourt Brace Jovanovich Inc. -- for which it paid a total of $280.9 million -- were now valued at only $140.7 million, or about half as much. It disclosed that Hillsborough Holdings Corp. bonds for which it paid $65.3 million were currently valued at $13.2 million -- a decline of 80%, the worst drubbing of any single issue that the thrift disclosed.

By regulation, the thrift is required to detail its junk bond holdings only once a year, and the latest filing gives investors the first detailed look in a year at Columbia's junk bond portfolio, by far the largest of any thrift in the country. Its largest holding was RJR Nabisco Holdings Corp., which, with a market value of $117.2 million, had actually increased in value by about $2.3 million since Columbia purchased it.

Columbia also disclosed that because of its insolvency and other troubles that it was facing new restrictions on gathering funds from federal sources as well as through the Wall Street money brokers through which it has historically garnered roughly two-thirds of its deposits. The company added, however, that despite the restrictions, it believes "it will have sufficient liquid assets or financing sources to meet its reasonably foreseeable liquidity needs in 1990."

Columbia officials declined to comment on the filing.

The filing said the company had roughly $1.5 billion in cash or cash equivalents as of Dec. 31 to soften any run. Moreover, its brokered deposits, totaling about $5.0 billion as of Dec. 31, or about 65% of total deposits, have maturities ranging from two to 12 years and can't be withdrawn. The company added in the filing, however, that it didn't think it would be able to obtain "significant volumes" of brokered funds in the future because of institutional concern over its financial condition as well as federal limits on such activities.

Columbia also disclosed that retail deposits obtained through its branches declined to about $2.8 billion as of Dec. 31, compared with $3.1 billion a year earlier, and that it had sustained an additional $78 million reduction this past January and February. It was unclear how individual depositors were reacting to news of the insolvency.

Columbia also said its recent sale of $3.3 billion in mortgage-backed securities, which it had used as collateral for obtaining funds, would complicate its deposit-gathering activities. It said it was counting on making up any deposit shortfalls through other sources, including advances from Federal Home Loan Bank of San Francisco. But it said federal rules covering insolvent institutions make it more costly to obtain such advances, which stood at about $600 million as of Feb. 28.
