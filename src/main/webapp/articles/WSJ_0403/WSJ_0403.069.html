Technology & Health: Stratus Unveils Fault-Tolerant Computer Line

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 04/03/90

Stratus Computer Inc. introduced a new line of fault-tolerant computers, replacing its three-year-old products and expanding into the high-powered mainframe arena for the first time. Analysts said the new products should help Stratus, which is based in Marlboro, Mass., resume its rapid growth next year. The company has faced a slowdown in recent quarters as customers waited for new products.

"This announcement will help it regain its momentum in the market," said Neil Weintraut, an analyst with Hambrecht & Quist Inc. Last year, Stratus's net income rose 21% to $35.4 million, or $1.71 a share, on revenue of $341.3 million. Mr. Weintraut estimated that per-share profit only will rise to $1.80 this year, but will jump to $2.25 in 1991.

Stratus's fault-tolerant computers, which are designed to keep working even if parts fail, are used for on-line transaction processing. Such applications include bank-teller networks, stock-trading systems and airline reservations.

Stratus has been a distant No. 2 in the fault-tolerant market to Tandem Computers Inc. But lately the business has gotten more crowded with Digital Equipment Corp. and Hewlett-Packard Co. entering.

International Business Machines Corp., the leader in transaction processing, doesn't make its own fault-tolerant computer. IBM resells those made by Stratus and accounted for about 30% of Stratus's sales in past years. Yesterday IBM said it will resell Stratus's new systems as an added part of its System 88 family.

Stratus unveiled 13 new systems in its announcement at prices ranging from $94,000 to $9 million. It also will continue to sell a $37,000 system introduced last fall. Stratus's new midrange systems, the 200 series, about double the performance of their 100 series predecessors and cost about the same amount, from $225,000 to $1.1 million. They will be available in May. The new mainframe computers, also available in May, cost as much as $9.4 million.

William Foster, president of Stratus, said Stratus doesn't expect to sell any of the mainframe systems right away. Carol Muratore, an analyst with Morgan Stanley & Co. said the mainframe systems mean that Stratus's "high-end customers don't go elsewhere" if they need bigger fault tolerant computers in the future.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Stratus closed at $21.625, down 50 cents.
