Letters to the Editor: Family Planning: Right vs. Rights

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/03/90

In your March 8 page-one article "Fertility Rights," I sympathize with Eloisa Silva. Here she is, a single mother of three, trying to work, maintain and survive. Barely above the poverty level, she stretches every cent of her earnings in order for her children to eat a fairly well-balanced meal and wear decent clothes. Birth control is a prime choice for her. Why shouldn't there be facilities for low-income families to receive qualitative and affordable health care? And yes, any type of birth control or family planning available?

The theory that family-planning centers promote promiscuity is ludicrous.

Iris V. Kennon

Aurora, Colo.

---

Ms. Silva is portrayed as being a "pawn" in the "war over abortion rights" because her birth-control pills are no longer provided free by a state-subsidized planning center. She now has to buy the pills, and if the situation continues, "I won't be able to make soup for my kids."

This attitude has me livid. What is more important, her hormones or her children? What about her sexual partner(s) and his responsibility in the situation? Our society is so hung up on "rights" that we all too often neglect "responsibilities."

I believe in helping others, but I do not believe in carrying them, swaddling them in cotton to insulate them from reality and responsibility. This merely cripples them, causing any latent sense of responsibility to atrophy rather than develop and become stronger. I feel sorry for Ms. Silva's children, but I have no patience with their mother.

Alice G. Hooker

Lexington, Ky.

---

It's Oh] so easy and Oh] so cowardly to force others to follow your beliefs when you don't have to pay the price. Make the anti-abortionist anti-family-planning-center people pay in hard cash for the costs of raising these unwanted children, including the costs of any crime-related activities they get into because they were unloved and grew up in deprived circumstances. Make them donate time to raising these unwanted children they are trying to force other women to have. Do their religious convictions go that deep? If it's wrong to abort a fetus, it's even more wrong to make an innocent child grow up unwanted and in squalid poverty.

Brenda ReVelle

Orange, Calif.

---

Whenever I hear the words "rights" and "refunding" in the same sentence, I check to see if my wallet is still in my pocket. Why do Ms. Silva and her boyfriend have a right to taxpayer-underwritten fun time? In the real free market, the users bear the costs of a good or service.

You throw in a couple of smoke screens: These people are poor; the government-funded clinics do other things, too. And one threat: If you don't cough up for the condoms, we'll make you pay for the welfare babies.

David Shoup

Seattle

---

Hallelujah for California and Gov. George Deukmejian for cutting the family-planning budget greatly. None of us can demand financial or supportive help in anything if we refuse to obey certain standards, laws, ethics or at the very least self-control.

I for one do not want to encourage or give to the Silvas of this country as they seek to drain funds and show a poor example to the next generation by demanding we pay for their play.

Cynthia Page

Santa Ana, Calif.

---

Thanks for describing how anti-abortion organizations forced the closing of family-planning clinics in California and are close to doing so in several other states. For many years, pro-choice and family-planning advocates have tried to make the public aware that a significant faction of the anti-choice community seeks to eliminate not only the right to choose abortion but the right to use contraception as well. The response has been that pro-choice groups were engaging in hyperbole to further their own cause, or that they were foolish to believe that opponents of contraception could be influential in modern America. Now perhaps those who have dismissed the anti-contraception forces as unimportant fringe extremists will wake up to the damage these people can do.

Lynn Hecht Schafran

New York

---

Wouldn't it be wonderful if each anti-abortionist adopted an unwanted child -- not the perfect, healthy little ones, but the drug and AIDS victims, the abused and neglected and unloved? There seem to be plenty of them these days.

Then those folks could give these threatened children the advantages of a loving family life they might never have otherwise.

Evelyn B. Smith

Demarest, N.J.
