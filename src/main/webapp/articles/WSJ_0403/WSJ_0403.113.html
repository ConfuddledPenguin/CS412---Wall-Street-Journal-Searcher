Sonnenblick-Goldman Forms Venture, As Japanese Continue Foray Into U.S.

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 04/03/90

NEW YORK -- Mortgage broker Sonnenblick-Goldman Corp. formed a partnership with Daishinpan Co., in the latest Japanese foray into the U.S. real estate market. Daishinpan will own less than 40% of the new firm, with options to increase its holding to about 55%, people familiar with the transaction said. The Japanese company also will contribute an unspecified amount of capital to the firm.

Real estate executives say deals such as this partnership are a way for Japanese institutions to invest in U.S. real estate, while escaping the limelight that attended Japanese purchases of such properties as Rockefeller Center in Manhattan.

"The Rockefeller deal scared some people and made them want to see Japanese deals done in a certain way," said Gary Barth, managing director of Jones Lang Wootton USA, a British-owned real estate firm.

The Sonnenblick-Daishinpan partnership follows a pattern set by other Japanese-U.S. ventures where the American entity provides the expertise and the Japanese company supplies the money and overseas distribution network.

Daishinpan, a credit company with about $13 billion in assets, will give Sonnenblick entry into Japan's vast consumer market, allowing Sonnenblick to sell small chunks of U.S. properties to individuals. More significantly, Sonnenblick executives said, Daishinpan's capital base will allow Sonnenblick to compete in growing Western European and Asian markets.

"We have never had to be a capital-rich company before," said Jack Shaffer, Sonnenblick managing director. "With the changing market, we felt we had to have a capital base to do things we couldn't do before."

Sonnenblick Goldman is an old-line mortgage broker whose main lines of business involve raising money for real estate developers and acting as adviser on property sales. The company is structured as a partnership; before the Daishinpan transaction, it was 25%-owned by Robert M. Bass Interests and 25% by Interstate Properties Co. In the recent past, Sonnenblick has raised money through such Japanese institutions as Sumitomo Trust, Industrial Bank of Japan and Kokusai Motors.

Daishinpan is Japan's second-largest credit company. It is controlled by a long list of Japanese institutions, including Sanwa Bank, Daiwa Bank, Industrial Bank of Japan and Nippon Life, which own from 2% to 5% each of Daishinpan.

Daishinpan's entry into the U.S. real estate market mirrors other Japanese efforts to hook up with existing companies. Early this year, Mitsui Trust & Banking Co. acquired a non-controlling interest in Lepercq Capital Partners. Also, Nomura Securities owns 50% of Eastdil Realty, and Dai-Ichi Mutual Life Insurance Co. of Japan's owns 25% of LaSalle Partners Ltd., a Chicago pension adviser.

Goldman, Sachs & Co. is also negotiating an agreement with Daiwa Securities Co. to form a joint venture that would sell U.S. real estate securities in Japan.
