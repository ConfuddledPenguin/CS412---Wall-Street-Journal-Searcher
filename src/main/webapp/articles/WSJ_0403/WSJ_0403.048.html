Who's News: Xerox's Allaire Will Become Chief Executive --- Kearns Stays as Chairman; Financial Services Head Plans to Retire on May 1

  • Author: Laurence Hooper
  • Date: 04/03/90

Xerox Corp. said its president, Paul Allaire, will succeed David Kearns as chief executive officer Aug. 1 with Mr. Kearns remaining as chairman. At the same time, Xerox said Vice Chairman Melvin Howard, who heads Xerox Financial Services, will retire May 1.

Although Mr. Allaire's succession had been expected since 1986 when he was named president and heir apparent, the retirement of the 55-year-old Mr. Howard came as a surprise. Analysts interpreted both moves as signaling the company's satisfaction with its recent emphasis on its core business equipment operations and growing disillusionment with financial services. Several analysts suggested Xerox Financial Services is now ripe for piecemeal selling, a move long favored by Wall Street.

After years of missteps and disappointments, Xerox, based in Stamford, Conn., has been performing relatively well lately, especially in copiers, printers and other document-processing equipment. It posted a $212 million profit on revenue of $4.7 billion in the fourth quarter of 1989, compared with a $77 million loss on revenue of $4.4 billion in the year-earlier period. The 1988 fourth quarter results included a one-time restructuring charge of $244 million.

But dismal results in the financial-services sector have masked this progress, and the firm has been troubled by its investments in VMS Realty Partners, a Chicago real estate concern being rocked by lawsuits and liquidity problems.

Though he was considered a long-shot candidate for the chief executive's post, Mr. Howard didn't decide to retire because he was passed over, a company spokesman said. Nor did his retirement have anything to do with the VMS Realty imbroglio, the spokesman added.

Mr. Howard, who was unavailable for comment, will be succeeded as head of Xerox Financial Services by Stewart Ross, the company's senior vice president and chief financial officer.

Mr. Allaire, 51, has been strongly associated with Xerox's recent successes, and has distanced himself from its failures. A 24-year Xerox veteran, he made his name during two stints at Rank Xerox, the company's London-based marketing and services subsidiary. Faced with strong Japanese rivals, he used a hands-on approach to cut costs by $200 million and re-establish the company's competitiveness. By 1980, he had risen to Rank Xerox's top position, managing director.

In 1983, Mr. Allaire returned to the U.S. as senior vice president and chief staff officer. He was elected president, traditionally a waiting room for CEOs-to-be, three years later, and since then has overseen the company's business equipment operation.

Over the past year or so, this operation has re-emerged as Xerox's core strategic business, providing a boost for Mr. Allaire. Meanwhile, the company's move to financial services, promoted by Mr. Kearns in the early 1980s as an antidote to Xerox's then-declining market share in copiers, has seemed destined for an ever-decreasing role.

"They got into that business when their backs were against the wall," said Alex Henderson, an analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "There's no strategic value in it any more."

By stepping aside as chief executive but staying as chairman, Mr. Kearns follows a tradition established by Xerox founder Joseph Wilson: At age 60, top officers relinquish day-to-day responsibilities and make way for their successors. Mr. Kearns will be 60 on Aug. 11.

The Xerox spokesman said Mr. Kearns intends to stay involved in the company, albeit much more on an advisory basis than previously. Many Xerox watchers speculated that Mr. Kearns will eventually find his way to a high government post, perhaps secretary of education. He currently serves on the President's Education Policy Advisory Committee, and has written a book on reforming the U.S. public school system.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Xerox closed at $55.375, down 25 cents.
