Letters to the Editor: Nicaragua's Voters Smart, Not Scared

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/03/90

Alexander Cockburn's March 1 Viewpoint, "U.S.-Backed Terrorism Won in Nicaragua, Not Democracy," pushes the "Blame America First" syndrome to a new depth. Stating that Violeta Chamorro's landslide victory is a result of the campesinos' fear of the U.S. is not only absurd, it defies logic as well as fact. In the past 10 years 400,000 Nicaraguans have voted with their feet by leaving Daniel Ortega's workers' paradise. Since it is highly doubtful that those emigres would have voted en masse for the Sandinistas, Mrs. Chamorro's victory is even more amazing. Mr. Cockburn also seems to have forgotten that the Sandinistas outspent UNO by 3 to 1. I am certain Mr. Cockburn was initially stunned (along with the party cadre, Sandinistas and the liberal establishment of this country) at the size of the Chamorro victory in light of pre-election polls. The Sandinistas maintained a widespread informant network that operated on a neighborhood level. With that in mind, why would the electorate tell a Sandinista-sponsored pollster its true feelings? Obviously fearful of honesty (with potential negative repercussions), the people took the only sensible course in answering a presidential-preference question. They lied.

Mr. Cockburn too easily dismisses the wisdom of the majority of Nicaraguan voters. They have joined Eastern Europe and Lithuania in their open expression of contempt for oppression, mindless propaganda and economic destruction of their land by Marxist regimes.

Scott B. West

Houston

---

Mr. Cockburn tries to ridicule the already historic speech of Czech President Vaclav Havel to Congress "as a way of coaxing money from Congress for his country" -- completely disregarding the fact that Czechoslovakia is the only country recently liberated from communism that asked for no money at all from the U.S. In an attempt to debase Mr. Havel further, he chooses "my side suffered more than yours" tactics by counting the people killed in El Salvador, Guatemala and Nicaragua. On his morbid abacus he proved that "the Eastern Europe under Soviet domination was benign indeed." Probably he realized the need for more sophisticated computers to handle the murdered millions by Stalin in U.S.S.R. alone.

In contrast, Mr. Havel states that wherever an innocent person is killed it is a tragedy for the whole of mankind. In his words: ". . . the salvation of this human world lies nowhere else than in the human heart, in the human power to reflect, in human meekness and in human responsibility."

I would sincerely recommend to Mr. Cockburn that he study the writings of this Czechoslovakian president, philosopher and playwright. They were written during the dark ages of Eastern Europe, sometimes while the author was in jail, but they never contained anger or vengeance against the oppressors as human beings. What Mr. Havel was fighting against was the dehumanizing philosophy of our time; the results of the Czechoslovakian "velvet revolution" is a proof that this way of fighting is successful.

Karel Kriz

Mt. Prospect, Ill.

---

In his cynical critique of Mr. Havel's address to Congress, Mr. Cockburn piously mentions "the terrible death toll in Hungary in 1956," but the only East European figure he gives to contrast with the large figures for Central America is 92 dead from the Soviet invasion of Czechoslovakia in 1968. Estimates of "the terrible death toll in Hungary in 1956" range from 6,500 up to 32,000; about 190,000 Hungarians fled their country. The Hungarian revolt lasted less than a month; those Soviets, they don't fool around.

John Maher

Michigan City, Ind.

---

Only Alexander Cockburn could be so outrageous as to contend that the stunning defeat of his beloved Sandinistas in Nicaragua, in an election so closely monitored it was impossible to fix, was a capitulation to U.S.-sponsored terrorism rather than a thrilling example of democracy working precisely as intended.

Charles H. McCrea Sr.

Las Vegas, Nev.
