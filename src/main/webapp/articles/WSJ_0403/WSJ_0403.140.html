Technology & Health: U.S. Plans Talks With the Soviets On Joint Missions to Moon, Mars

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. plans to open talks with Moscow that could lead to Soviet participation in a costly and complex program to send astronauts to the moon and on to Mars. Similar talks also are planned with Western allies.

A joint Soviet-U.S. mission to Mars has long been a goal of many U.S. space officials, who say it would lead to greater cooperation between the two superpowers. But critics of space cooperation say the U.S. risks giving up advanced technology. Moreover, regardless of who participates in such ventures, a mission to Mars would face congressional opposition because of its huge costs.

Since July 1975, when the U.S. and Soviet Union docked manned rockets in space, cooperation has been limited mainly to sharing information on weather and the medical effects of weightlessness on crews.

Any new U.S.-Soviet venture is still far off. A White House statement said the talks will focus "on conceptual possibilities" for cooperation. Apart from the Soviets, the U.S. will try to set up talks with Western Europe, Canada and Japan.

The statement said international cooperation "could offer significant benefits" to the U.S., but didn't say what they are. In the past, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration has sought international partners to build political support for a project and to share expenses. Europe, Canada and Japan are expected to spend about $8 billion to build parts of a manned space station; the U.S. will spend about $30 billion on the project.

Manned missions to the moon and Mars could be the most expensive project in history. NASA has estimated the cost, in 1991 dollars, at between $471 billion and $541 billion over 35 years. For the current fiscal year, the Bush administration is asking for $188 million to develop technology for manned expeditions to the moon and Mars.

NASA Administrator Richard Truly recently testified in Congress that those costs could grow to between $445 million and $1.7 billion the following year, depending on how fast the project is pursued. Preliminary plans call for a return to the moon near the turn of the century, and a trip to Mars 15 to 20 years later.

It's doubtful that Congress would go along with big budget requests for exploration. Rep. Bob Traxler, (D., Mich.), who chairs the House appropriations panel overseeing NASA, hinted that Congress might cut about $1 billion from NASA's budget request of $15.2 billion for the year beginning next Oct. 1. Funding for the Bush exploration initiative would probably be slashed as part of those cuts.
