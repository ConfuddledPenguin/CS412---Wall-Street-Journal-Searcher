Politics & Policy: Erskine Henderson Appears to Lead Candidates for Appointment to SEC

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The White House is close to nominating Erskine Henderson, a New York securities lawyer, to the Securities and Exchange Commission, administration sources said. Mr. Henderson, who would be the second black commissioner in the SEC's history, is a special counsel at Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom, a New York law firm known for its mergers and acquisitions work. Mr. Henderson specializes in securities and takeover litigation, and colleagues at the firm said he has experience in accounting law although he isn't an accountant.

White House sources insisted that no decision has been made, and that the background check on Mr. Henderson hasn't been completed. One administration insider said that it could be two months before the White House offers a nomination, but that Mr. Henderson heads the short list of candidates. Others mentioned include Washington lawyers Bruce Mendelsohn, Albert Beveridge and Mahlon Frankhauser.

If nominated, Mr. Henderson would fill the Democratic commission slot left open by the January resignation of Joseph Grundfest, a staunch free-marketeer who is now a Stanford University Business School professor.

The recent swearing-in of former Time Warner Inc. lawyer Philip Lochner Jr. gives Chairman Richard Breeden a virtual hammerlock on the five-member commission. It isn't clear how the nomination of Mr. Henderson might affect agency policy.

While Mr. Lochner hasn't yet voted at an open meeting, his statements at his Senate confirmation hearing showed a similar political bent to that of the chairman. Mr. Breeden has pushed hard to expand the SEC's enforcement program and to broaden the agency's jurisdiction over disclosure by brokerage firms. Another commissioner, Mary Schapiro, often sides with Mr. Breeden in commission votes.

Edward Fleischman, who predates Mr. Breeden at the commission by several years, has begun to emerge as the agency's most dissident voice. He disagrees with the chairman on the SEC's most controversial issue -- the chairman's desire to regulate stock-index futures, which are now overseen by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission.

Mr. Henderson, 40 years old, couldn't be reached for comment. But colleagues describe him as a team player without much ideological fervor. He has been a lawyer at Skadden Arps for about 11 years following his graduation from Columbia University Law School.

He has represented a number of companies in takeover situations, including Hammermill Paper Co. in the company's effort to fend off a foray early in Carl Icahn's career.
