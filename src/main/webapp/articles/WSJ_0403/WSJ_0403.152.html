SEC Chief Criticizes Pennsylvania Proposal In Anti-Takeover Law

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- Securities and Exchange Commission Chairman Richard Breeden criticized a Pennsylvania anti-takeover proposal that would restrict shareholder proxy fights against management. Mr. Breeden, in his first public comment on the state proposal, said the measure would "virtually eliminate shareholders' well-established right to replace a board of directors." Entrenched directors, he said, "could run a company into the ground" with shareholders powerless to react.

The Pennsylvania proposal, which is awaiting final action by the legislature, would toughen the state's anti-takeover law to require holders who acquire voting rights for over 20% of a company's shares to offer to buy all the company's shares outstanding.

Supporters say the change would enhance what would become the nation's stiffest state regulation against hostile takeovers. Opponents say that under the measure the sponsor of a proxy resolution trying to line up votes could be forced to buy the company.

Mr. Breeden indicated that the SEC would intervene if the final legislation approved by the state interfered with federal proxy regulations. "We will not hesitate to seek to enforce federal interests," he said.

Mr. Breeden spoke at a meeting of the Council of Institutional Investors, a group made up mostly of public-employee pension funds with assets totaling more than $300 billion.

Council members, including the statewide pension funds from California, Florida, Connecticut and Massachusetts, approved a statement saying they would review their holdings in Pennsylvania companies if the proposed measure was adopted.
