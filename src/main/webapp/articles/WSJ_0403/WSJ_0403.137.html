Movies: `Ninja Turtle' Juggernaut Demolishes Box Office Record

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 04/03/90

NEW YORK -- Cowabunga] "Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles," the movie based on the comic book and wildly successful animated TV series, grossed approximately $25.4 million in its first weekend at the box office, making it the largest non-summer, non-Thanksgiving weekend opening of a movie ever, according to the trade newspaper Daily Variety. That shattered the previous record set by "The Hunt for Red October," which grossed $17.2 million its first weekend.

The movie is only the latest success for the four turtles who, as the story goes, were deposited into a manhole, doused with radioactive goo and grew to human-sized superheroes with such expressions as "Surf's up]" "Cowabunga]" and "Let's party, dude."

Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles are already an industry unto themselves. Last year, for instance, the Turtles earned more than $350 million in license fees and merchandising, double the 1988 earnings. This year, that figure will be "at least, easily doubled," says Jim Prindle, director of licensing and merchandising at Mirage Studios, a Northampton, Mass.-based partnership of the comic's creators, Peter Laird, 36 years old, and Kevin Eastman, 27. Messrs. Laird and Eastman couldn't be reached yesterday.

More than 100 manufacturers hold licensing agreements to put the creatures on everything from Burger King posters to McCain Ellio's Foods Inc. frozen pizzas. They also appear as action-figure toys and on T-shirts.

The success of the movie is also buoying the fortunes of New Line Cinema Corp., the film's domestic distributor, whose other projects include another mutant of sorts, Freddy Kreuger of the "Nightmare on Elm Street" series. "We have to have the nostrils for smelling the good stuff," says Robert Shaye, New Line's president. New Line is also benefiting from the recently released film, "House Party," which the company produced for $2.5 million and which so far has grossed $16.4 million. New Line Cinema's shares rose 37.5 cents to $11.25 yesterday in composite trading on the American Stock Exchange.

"Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles," which cost about $12 million to make, was financed and produced by Golden Harvest Films, a unit of Golden Communications Co. Ltd., based in Hong Kong. Dennis McAlpine, an analyst at Oppenheimer & Co., figures Golden Harvest stands to clear about $18 million from its share of domestic revenue of the film if the movie grosses $100 million, and the company also has foreign distribution rights. New York-based New Line bought the domestic theatrical rights for about $3 million.

The movie -- which received mixed reviews -- is only the latest vehicle for the Turtles, who are named after four Renaissance artists: Leonardo, Raphael, Michelangelo and Donatello. There will, of course, be a sequel. In September, a live stage show will make its debut at New York's Radio City Music Hall. And recently, Burger King began selling videotapes of episodes of the TV show "Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles."

These tapes, distributed by Family Home Entertainment, a subsidiary of Live Entertainment Inc., contain a 2 1/2-minute commercial promoting various Turtle merchandise, including the movie. Family Home distributes other tapes of the TV show in the home-video marketplace and said it has sold over three million of the videotapes. Live Entertainment also bought the domestic home-video rights to the movie for about $5 million. Live Entertainment's stock jumped $2.50 to $19.25 in national over-the-counter trading yesterday.

Just what is it that makes four talking turtles so appealing?

"They have a sense of humor," says Mark Freedman, licensing agent for the creatures and president of Surge Licensing Inc., a company based on Long Island in New York. "They're like the Bruce Willis character in `Moonlighting.' They talk directly to the audience, and they don't take themselves too seriously."

Four years ago, Mr. Freedman was a "marketing consultant" when he says he happened upon the Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles -- then simply comic book characters. He approached Messrs. Laird and Eastman about a marketing deal. "The toy industry was looking for a breakthrough item and I had a feeling this would be it," Mr. Freedman says.

Another draw, says Mr. Freedman, 35, is that "parents don't get it."

The Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles were created in 1983 for a black-and-white comic book. According to Cheryl Prindle, executive director at Mirage Studios, there are 28 volumes and over four million copies of the comic books in print.

Some of Mr. Laird's other illustrations were published in a small comic book titled "Scat" when Mr. Eastman, an amateur cartoonist then working as a short-order cook, noticed a copy of the comic book left on a Northampton bus.

Mr. Eastman took samples of his work to "Scat," whose editor suggested he team up with Mr. Laird. "It was instant friendship," Ms. Prindle says. It was Mr. Eastman whose brainchild was the large turtle donning a ninja mask and weapon, which he called "Ninja Turtles." Mr. Laird came up with the full name.

The TV series is responsible for the playful jargon as well as a song that calls the turtles "heroes on the half-shell."

The movie, complete with the turtles tossing into the air their favorite food, pizza, and slicing it with their katana sword blades, features the work of Jim Henson's Creature Shop, based in London.

Says Mr. Freedman, "I knew this would either bomb or be the biggest hit."
