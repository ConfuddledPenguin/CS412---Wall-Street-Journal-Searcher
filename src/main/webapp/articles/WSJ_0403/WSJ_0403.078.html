Abreast of the Market: Stocks Fall on Investors' Concern About Tokyo, 1st-Quarter Reports

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 04/03/90

NEW YORK -- Investors sold stocks on concerns about bad first-quarter earnings, the plunging Tokyo stock market, and the belief that interest rates won't fall anytime soon. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 6.76 to 2700.45. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index fell 1.24 to 338.70. For the quarter, the S&P 500 finished down 3% on a total return basis. Volume totaled 124,360,000 shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues swamped advancers 1,122 to 434.

Byron Wien, U.S. investment strategist for Morgan Stanley, boosted the cash position in his model all-stock portfolio to 10% yesterday from 3%. "I felt that sentiment had driven the market," he explained. "To move up the next leg, the market needs help from interest rates. While I am optimistic, I wanted hope and reason to be in better balance."

Mr. Wien's colleague, Barton Biggs, chairman of Morgan Stanley Asset Management, said he recently raised cash levels to 15% in his global portfolio, reducing the equity portion to 45% from 55%. The bond portion remains at 40%.

Mr. Biggs said he is concerned about the decline in Tokyo shares. "I think a 32% decline in the biggest market in the world is an event," he said. "I think our market is de-linked from ordinary performance in Japan, but not from a financial panic." He added that margin calls and credit scares in Japan could be two factors that create selling in other markets.

"I think there are going to be aftershocks; it will ripple through all markets," said Mr. Biggs, who added that he wants more cash on hand to hunt for values if stock markets tumble.

Disappointing earnings news from Network Equipment Technologies sent the stock plummeting 6 3/8 to 23 1/2 on 1.8 million shares. The company said it expects revenue and net income for the March quarter to be significantly lower than it had anticipated, with per-share earnings possibly falling short of 33 cents a year earlier.

The first quarter's dividend activity confirms the dismal state of corporate profits, according to Arnold Kaufman, editor of Standard & Poor's "Outlook" newsletter.

"There has been a downtrend in corporate profits since 1988 and dividends are starting to reflect that very clearly," said Mr. Kaufman.

S&P, surveying 7,557 companies, found that dividend increases shrank to 414 in the first quarter, down 20.5% from the same period a year earlier. The number of dividend decreases and omissions rose 64.5% to 79 in the first quarter from the same period in 1989.

"We are not going to see an improvement through the rest of this year," Mr. Kaufman predicted.

Several of the put warrants on Tokyo Nikkei stock index topped the most active list on the American Stock Exchange yesterday. Nikkei put warrants allow investors to bet on a decline in the Tokyo stock market. The Denmark issue closed up 3/4 to 11 3/4; the January 1993 Salomon Brothers issue rose 5/8 to 10 7/8; a February 1993 issue from Salomon gained 7/8 to 11 3/4, and the Bankers Trust issue rose 1 to 25 3/4.

American General dropped 1 7/8 to 36 3/4 on 1.2 million shares. Torchmark withdrew its bid to acquire the company for $50 a share in cash and stock and said it would wage a proxy fight for five board seats. American General turned down Torchmark's slate, however, contending it wasn't submitted by a deadline for nominations set in its bylaws. Torchmark rose 1/4 to 45 1/2.

Pfizer slipped 1 5/8 to 59 on 1.1 million shares. Barron's magazine reported that the company faces a potentially "huge liability" if it loses a lawsuit, filed by a recipient of one of the heart valves once made by its Shiley unit. The suit seeks damages for anxiety resulting from the news of problems with the valve. The report included several documents indicating that the company was aware of potential hazards with the valve but urged doctors not to publicize them.

Fleet/Norstar Financial Group fell 5/8 to 21 1/4 on 1.8 million shares after disclosing that it is being examined by federal and state banking regulators. The company said it might have to boost its loan-loss reserve, based on their findings.

Unocal rose 1 3/4 to 33 5/8 on 1.3 million shares amid continued speculation that British Petroleum may be interested in taking over the company. BP declined comment; the stock fell 1 3/4 to 63 as a sell-off on the London market hurt shares of British companies traded in the U.S.

Motorola rose 1 to 66 after a federal court stayed an injunction against sales of the company's flagship microprocessor. The ban had been imposed as part of the court's decision in a patent-infringement case between Motorola and Hitachi.

Digital Equipment gained 3/8 to 77 1/8 in advance of today's expected introduction of new engineering workstations. Compaq Computer, which unveiled a high-speed controller for use in computer networks, climbed 2 1/8 to 97 5/8.

UAL fell 7/8 to 161 1/8 amid continuing discussions about a buy-out bid from the three major unions at its United Airlines unit. The group is offering cash and securities valued at $185 a share.

Wheelabrator Technologies rose 1 1/2 to 35. Waste Management agreed to increase its stake in the company to 55% from 22% through a stock swap. Waste Management rose 1/8 to 34 1/4.

Mortgage & Realty Trust rose 5/8 to 8 1/8 after reaching a preliminary agreement with a group of lenders for $425 million in financing. Shares of the trust dropped sharply last month after it didn't make a payment on a note issue; Mortgage & Realty said the funding would permit it to make that payment, among other things.

General Development dropped 1 7/8 to 4. The company said it hadn't been able to reach agreement on a $60 million credit facility over the weekend and it is considering a Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing, along with alternatives.

West Point-Pepperell fell 1 1/4 to 37. The company that is holding investor William Farley's stake in Pepperell defaulted on $796 million in bank debt and on interest payments due on $705 million in subordinated debt.

Hotel Investors Corp. fell 1/2 to 4 1/2 after reporting a $60.3 million loss for 1989, reflecting a $55.8 million provision for losses on its hotel and gaming properties and goodwill. The company also cut back an agreed-upon sale of some of its hotel interests.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 2.38 to 359.37. Volume on the American Stock Exchange was 14,490,000. There were 405 issues declining and 165 advancing.

New Line Cinema rose 1/2 to 11 3/8. The company said its new movie, "Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles," grossed approximately $25.4 million in its first weekend at the box office.
