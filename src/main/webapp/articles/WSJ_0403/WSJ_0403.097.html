Law: Punitive Damages May Be Facing Constitutionality Test by Justices

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court, in a case that may be a vehicle for a ruling on the constitutionality of punitive damages, agreed to review a $1.1 million verdict against Pacific Mutual Life Insurance Co. An Alabama jury in 1987 awarded $1,077,978 in compensatory and punitive damages to four municipal employees of Roosevelt City, Ala., for fraud by a Pacific Mutual agent.

The four paid premiums to the city to provide them with life and health insurance. Beginning in 1981, the city bought policies from an agent who worked for Pacific Mutual, a Newport Beach, Calif., company. The life insurance was issued through Pacific Mutual, but the health policy was from another, unrelated company which the Pacific Mutual agent also represented -- Union Fidelity Life Insurance Co. of Trevose, Pa., an AON Corp. unit.

The agent arranged to have all correspondence with Union Fidelity sent to his Pacific Mutual office in Birmingham, Ala., rather than to the city. The four Roosevelt City workers alleged in their lawsuit that the agent didn't forward their premium payments to Union Fidelity. Union Fidelity canceled the policy a few months after it began, but the notices weren't sent to the city or the employees.

Only when one of the four employees incurred $2,500 in hospital bills in January 1982 did they discover that they were no longer insured. They sued the agent and Pacific Mutual, as his employer. The jury, holding the company liable for its agent's actions, awarded a $1.1 million verdict against Pacific Mutual. The verdict was upheld by the Alabama Supreme Court last September.

Appealing to the high court, the insurance company challenged the constitutionality of punitive damage awards. The high court ruled last year that large punitive awards don't violate the Eighth Amendment prohibition of "excessive fines." But the justices left open the question of whether such awards, issued with few limits on a jury, violate the 14th Amendment guarantee of "due process."

But while the high court agreed to hear the appeal, which raises the "due process" issue, the case appears to be a dubious vehicle for such a ruling. In its verdict, the Alabama jury lumped the damages together, making it unclear how much of the award is compensatory and how much is punitive.

It is possible the court will address only the narrower question of whether a company may be held liable for punitive damages when the actions were taken by a subordinate employee, rather than by the company itself. (Pacific Mutual Life Insurance Co. vs. Haslip)

Airbag Lawsuits

Without comment, the justices let stand four rulings by state and federal appeals courts that people injured in car accidents can't sue the vehicle manufacturers for failing to install airbags. The high court declined to hear appeals in three lawsuits against General Motors Corp., one of which also named Honda Motor Co.'s U.S. subsidiary, and in one lawsuit against Nissan Motor Co.'s U.S. subsidiary.

All of the cases involved the issue of whether the 1966 federal motor safety law or safety standards issued by the Transportation Department were intended to bar manufacturers' liability under state law for lawsuits alleging that vehicles were unsafe because they lacked airbags. The latest safety standard, issued in 1984, required manufacturers to phase in passive restraints, either airbags or automatic seatbelts, in new cars by the 1990-model year.

Denial of the four appeals may not settle the issue of whether such lawsuits under state personal injury laws are pre-empted, however. Thus far, all appeals courts that have addressed the issue have ruled that federal law pre-empts state personal injury claims. If a state or federal appeals court reaches the opposite result in the future, that will make new high court appeals likely.

All four cases were filed by injured people or the families of people killed in car accidents between 1981 and 1985. (Wood vs. General Motors Corp.; Kitts vs. General Motors Corp.; Taylor vs. General Motors Corp.; Meier vs. Nissan Motor Corp.)

California Death Penalty

The high court, by a 6-3 vote, refused to clear the way for the first execution in California in 23 years. A federal appeals court judge in San Francisco last Friday stayed the execution of Robert Harris, which was scheduled for today. California Attorney General John Van de Kamp appealed to the Supreme Court, asking that the state be allowed to send Mr. Harris to the gas chamber. But the high court refused, over the dissenting votes of Chief Justice William Rehnquist and Justices Antonin Scalia and Anthony Kennedy. Mr. Harris's appeal now will be heard by a three-judge appeals court panel.
