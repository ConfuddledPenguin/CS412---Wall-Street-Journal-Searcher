Two Retail Units Of Campeau Post Losses for 4 Weeks

  • Author: Jeffrey A. Trachtenberg
  • Date: 04/03/90

NEW YORK -- Federated Stores Inc. said its Federated and Allied retailing units incurred losses totaling $45.8 million for the four weeks ended March 3. The figure excludes the costs of filing for Chapter 11 status. The Campeau Corp. unit said the performance "exceeded its expectations" for the first complete month of operation since filing Jan. 15 for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code.

But Federated didn't provide financial results for February 1989, so a direct comparison couldn't be made independently.

Federated Stores Inc. was formerly known as Campeau (U.S.). Late last week, Federated filed for Chapter 11 status, calling the filing a "procedural step" as part of its reorganization plan. Federated Stores remains fully owned by Campeau, based in Toronto.

The Federated Department Stores Inc. division incurred losses of $29.9 million on revenue of $277 million. Its outlets include Bloomingdale's, Abraham & Strauss, Burdines, Lazarus and Rich's/Goldsmith's.

The Allied Stores Corp. division had a loss of $15.9 million on revenue of $156.7 million. Its stores include Bon Marche, Jordan Marsh, Maas Brothers/Jordan Marsh, and Stern's.

Although February wasn't profitable for Federated Stores, it was a marked improvement compared with the losses incurred for the 20 days ended Feb. 3, 1990. In that period, Federated had a loss of $37 million on revenue of $158.6 million; the Allied unit posted losses of $19 million on $77.3 million of revenue.

Altogether, the Allied and Federated units have incurred losses of $101.8 million since filing for Chapter 11 status. "Half of that loss is interest expense being accrued by the companies at the same rate as was paid in the pre-petition period," said a Federated Stores representative.

"It's a lot of money to lose in such a short period," said Howard Bloom, president of Chetta B. Inc., a women's apparel maker. "We are shipping to them, however, and I would like to continue doing business with them."

Other manufacturers, as well as one important factoring company, also expressed support for the Allied and Federated units.

"February is generally a weak retailing period," said Frank DeRita, senior vice president, Congress Talcott Corp., a major factoring company. "There is a relative safety net because they have enough cash on hand to meet trade obligations in April and beyond. Considering the filing, and this period in the retail cycle, this is to be expected." Factors buy retail payables from manufacturers at a discount, thus providing them with cash.

"It's fairly normal to show losses like this in a restructuring," added Bud Konheim, president of Nicole Miller Ltd., a women's apparel supplier. "What we want to know is whether the bleeding has stopped. If this performance means they've cleaned up their inventory, that's fine. The history of retailers coming out of Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings isn't great."

Federated Stores said that it ended the February selling period with "stocks in better shape" than anticipated.
