Corporate Focus: Thaw in Cold War Stymies Daimler-Benz

  • Author: Its Recent Investments In Defense Face Tough Going
  • Date: 04/03/90

BONN -- It took Daimler-Benz AG five years and billions of Deutsche marks of investments to mold itself into one of the world's leading defense companies. Then history intervened.

Early last November, just as the company was wrapping up its tortuous negotiations to acquire the state-owned Messerschmitt-Boelkow-Blohm G.m.b.H. aerospace and defense group, the Berlin Wall began breaking apart. Communist regimes in Eastern Europe were swept away by pro-democracy movements, and the threat of war in Central Europe evaporated. By year's end, West Germans were clamoring for a "peace dividend" of their own.

The Bonn government, after playing midwife to the Daimler-MBB merger, is now considering deep cuts in its defense budget to help finance the staggering costs of rebuilding East Germany. Entire arms projects -- including the controversial 60 billion mark ($35 billion) Eurofighter project, in which MBB plays a key role -- are now on thin ice.

The rush of events caught Daimler off balance. A maker of everything from missiles to toasters, the company was counting on lucrative defense contracts to bankroll its ambitious growth strategy. The expansion, launched in the mid-1980s, was designed to create a global technology conglomerate and to reduce Daimler's dependence on the automotive business.

As Daimler hurriedly refocuses its strategy away from defense, many of the basic objectives remain the same. In one new area of emphasis, the group is seeking powerful alliances in commercial aerospace and technology with established players in the U.S. and Japan. Meanwhile, its prestigious Mercedes cars and trucks will play a larger role than ever in maintaining sales and profits.

"Daimler was thrown a curve ball by developments in Eastern Europe and their impact on MBB's main sales source, defense," says Stephen Reitman, an analyst for UBS-Phillips & Drew in London. "Now they are scrambling for other sources of income and turnover. You can't fault the strategy, but they need partners to do it."

Daimler stirred up a small storm last month when it announced that it was in the initial stages of forging a loose "technological alliance" with Japan's Mitsubishi industrial group. It set tongues wagging again last week by announcing a much more specific move to merge its jet-engine line with that of Pratt & Whitney, a subsidiary of United Technologies Corp.

Daimler appears to be set on going wherever necessary to get the partners -- and technology -- it needs for market access. "These linkups suggest Daimler-Benz is getting a step up the ladder in these new growth areas by joining forces with other people's technology," says Simon Miller, an analyst at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Ltd. in London. In return, he says, the partners get access to Europe, with the Daimler name behind them.

With MBB, Daimler already controls more than 60% of West Germany's aeronautics technology. But the West German conglomerate's units are relatively small compared with either British Aerospace PLC or France's Aerospatiale, making the need for global partners more urgent.

Daimler's link with Pratt & Whitney, for instance, is likely to give it a quick boost into the front rank of global jet-engine producers. The two companies also are hinting that the relationship could expand to other United Technologies divisions, perhaps involving the joint manufacture of turbochargers.

The Mitsubishi and United Technologies ventures also illustrate an aspect of Daimler's strategy that distinguishes it from many of its European counterparts. While many Europeans, particularly the French, tend to focus on forging links with other companies inside the European Community, Daimler takes a world view.

"We have now reached a stage where, instead of a project here and a project there, we wanted a partnership with more permanency," says Juergen Schrempp, chairman of Deutsche Aerospace AG, Daimler's aerospace unit, and a member of Daimler's management board.

Mr. Schrempp says Daimler is talking about "a number of concrete projects" with Mitsubishi, including a proposal for joint development of a 75-seat airliner.

Besides the draw away from defense, analysts say "risk-sharing partnerships" have become the order of the day in civil aviation. Even by pooling Daimler's vast technological resources in the aerospace field -- which include not only MBB and Motoren & Turbinen Union G.m.b.H. but also the Dornier G.m.b.H. aerospace concern -- the company would be hard pressed to launch a fully independent venture. And management is against trying to go it alone.

Against this background, Daimler is still struggling to reshape itself into an effective conglomerate. It is often accused of trailing its smaller and more creative competitors. Critics grumble that the company may be adapting too quickly to its special position as the nation's favored defense contractor -- and not making the strides necessary to break away from the trough of government subsidies.

MBB was added to a rapid succession of technology and aerospace acquisitions in 1985, when AEG AG, MTU and Dornier were taken aboard. The process catapulted Daimler into the role of West Germany's No. 1 company by sales. But management has conceded that, since then, the hoped-for synergies between its automotive business and the technology additions have been slow in coming. Analysts estimate that in 1989, for instance, Daimler's nonautomotive business accounted for more than a third of sales but only a tenth of profits.

Daimler Chairman Edzard Reuter unveiled his solution in 1988. He broke up the group into three largely autonomous operations with their own management boards: Mercedes-Benz AG for autos and trucks; Deutsche Aerospace for aerospace and defense; and AEG for electronics.

Philippe Haspeslagh, a business professor at Insead, the European management school in Paris, says Daimler has successfully structured its many acquisitions. "But the next step -- the strategic step of reorienting these new businesses to survive in the global market -- is very different, and much more difficult," he says.

The possible loss of defense contracts won't make the transition any easier. After the inclusion of MBB, defense technology accounts for just under half of Deutsche Aerospace's sales. Among other projects, MBB accounts for 33% of the four-nation Eurofighter project, designed to phase out the Tornado as Europe's next generation of jet fighter. AEG and MTU, for their part, are major contractors in the Tornado project, as well as in other defense programs. In recent weeks, Bonn Defense Minister Gerhard Stoltenberg has been facing growing political pressure to shelve the Eurofighter after its development stage.

For all of Daimler's worries, Bonn couldn't be happier. The government braved antitrust protests when pushing through the sale of MBB to Daimler-Benz late last year, laying the cornerstone for the badly needed restructuring of West Germany's scattered aerospace industry. Without the sale, Bonn officials say, the proposed ventures with Mitsubishi and United Technologies might never have come about, and an important growth market would have been lost.

"Daimler's coordination with America and Japan continues to improve the value and dimension of the German aerospace industry," says a defense ministry official. He adds, "It proves that the Daimler-MBB merger was the right decision."

Still, antitrust officials in both West Germany and the EC say they are watching Daimler's activities closely. "Bigness alone is not a problem," says an EC spokesman, adding that the important thing is "how a company behaves once it acquires this sort of position in a national -- and ultimately the international -- market."
