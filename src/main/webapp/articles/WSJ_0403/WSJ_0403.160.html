Some House Members Agree On Proposal on Emissions

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- Several important House lawmakers agreed on an approach to regulating cancer-causing industrial emissions that would be more stringent than President Bush's clean-air proposal. Under the approach, backed by Chairman John Dingell of the House Energy and Commerce Committee; Rep. Henry Waxman (D., Calif.), chairman of the panel's health subcommittee; and New York Rep. Norman Lent, the ranking GOP member of the panel, factories that emit such pollutants would have to install the best available technology for reducing emissions. All sources of cancer-causing pollution would have to be regulated within 10 years, except for those that pose a cancer risk to nearby residents of less than one in one million.

The Environmental Protection Agency would study whether any cancer risk remains after this technology is applied. It would then establish standards that provide an "ample margin of safety."

The plan could be voted on in committee as early as today.

Under the president's proposal, 50% of all sources of toxic pollution would be regulated within 10 years. Additional, deeper cutbacks could be required on a case-by-case basis if EPA, taking cost and health into account, finds a plant's fumes still pose "unreasonable" risks.

The Senate plan would aim to protect an individual with slightly above-average exposure and would set a cancer-risk standard of one in 10,000. This provision is part of the clean-air bill that the Senate is expected to approve tonight.
