Politics & Policy: GAO Is Unable To See Extent Of Corn Toxin

  • Author: Scott Kilman
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The General Accounting Office said it can't determine the extent of aflatoxin contamination in the U.S. corn crop. Its conclusion that "information is inadequate" helps clear the way for a push in Congress to strengthen screening for the carcinogen. The conclusion by Congress's investigative arm wasn't surprising, since industry officials and academics have said that the spotty surveys conducted by the Food and Drug Administration and by some states aren't large enough to be statistically reliable. Moreover, the tests for aflatoxin often give conflicting results.

The House Agriculture Committee is widely expected to debate whether the next five-year farm bill includes a provision to screen all U.S. corn exports for aflatoxin and to improve testing procedures.

In February, the White House signaled that it favored mandatory screening of corn exports as a way to soothe importing countries. The voluntary testing of current exports came under criticism last year from Congress and abroad when some importing nations detected aflatoxin in U.S. corn and corn products.

The House subcommittee on wheat, soybeans and feed grains asked the GAO last year to study how federal regulators handled a widespread outbreak of aflatoxin in corn during the 1988 drought. Aflatoxin is excreted by a fungus during hot and dry conditions and is suspected of causing human liver cancer in regions of Africa and Asia.

The FDA restricts corn contaminated with tiny amounts of aflatoxin from moving across state lines. But a February 1989 article in The Wall Street Journal reported that surveillance is spotty and enforcement rare.

Between October 1988 and June 1989, according to the GAO, the FDA found unsafe levels of aflatoxin in 5.5% of the whole corn it tested as well as in 2.2% of the milled corn it tested. Food containing more than 20 parts of aflatoxin per billion is unsafe for human consumption under FDA rules.

In testimony presented yesterday, John W. Harman, director of the GAO's food and agriculture issues division, briefed the subcommittee on the preliminary results of the study, which is expected to be completed this summer.

The fact that the GAO can't determine the extent of aflatoxin contamination weakens the chance that Congress might try to compensate growers for contaminated corn.

Corn growers favor compensation as a way to stop farmers from trying to evade detection by food companies. But it's impossible to calculate the expense of such a program.
