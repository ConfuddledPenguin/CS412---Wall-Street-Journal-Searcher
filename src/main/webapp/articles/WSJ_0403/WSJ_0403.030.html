Technology and Health: Export License Rule Eased For U.S. Computer Firms

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The Commerce Department said U.S. firms will no longer have to apply for export licenses to ship low-capacity computer hard-disk drives to most non-communist countries. Dennis Kloske, undersecretary of commerce for export administration, said that freeing these products from unnecessary paperwork would "enable U.S. manufacturers to compete on an equal footing with foreign competitors who don't face any exporting constraints." The order will take effect tomorrow.

The department's latest move to liberalize technology trade within the western bloc covers hard-disk drives with a capacity of up to 45 megabytes of memory. Nearly all personal computers are equipped with such built-in data-storage devices. The department estimates that U.S. shipments of these products were valued at close to $500 million last year.

The department decided to ease licensing requirements for these products after determining that drives of comparable quality are being widely traded by countries that don't participate in export controls adopted by the U.S. and its allies.

The department said it will continue to require export licenses for shipment of these products to the Soviet Union and Eastern European countries, pending changes in export rules by the U.S. and its allies.
