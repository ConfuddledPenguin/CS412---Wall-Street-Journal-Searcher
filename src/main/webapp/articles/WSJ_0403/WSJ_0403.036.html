Well, Lots of Off-the-Wall Moves End Up Less Than Picture Perfect

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 04/03/90

PITTSBURGH -- Things of beauty aren't always joys forever. Ask USX Corp. Apparently forgetting that public relations is also an art form, the steel and energy giant packed up 64 valuable paintings that had adorned its headquarters and sent them off to Christie's for auction.

The move has shocked local art buffs, but the company's reasoning was simple: A sale, which could reap more than $4 million in today's world of escalating art values, was in the best interest of shareholders. (One holder in particular, investor Carl Icahn, is breathing down the company's neck, demanding that it restructure to get greater stock value.)

"Shareholders are not well served -- nor am I comfortable -- having paintings worth hundreds of thousands of dollars on the walls of executive offices," explains USX Chairman Charles A. Corry.

Then hang the paintings in local museums, shout art aficionados. Get a tax write-off and be a hero instead of a heel.

"It's a loss to the city," laments Leon A. Arkus, who put the collection together when he was director of the Carnegie Museum of Art. The collection is believed to be the third-largest in the city, behind only the Carnegie and Frick museums, and includes noted works of Mary Cassatt, Robert Motherwell and Childe Hassam.

Can't the museum go and bid on the pieces themselves? "Sure we can go to Christie's -- and sit and watch because we don't have the money," says DeCourcy E. McIntosh, executive director of the Helen Clay Frick Foundation, which operates the Frick Art Museum.

What really galls the local art community is that USX will make millions while the Carnegie -- which helped put the collection together for free -- doesn't get a thing. "It's not fair," pouts a local art expert.

The Pittsburgh Post-Gazette ran an editorial: "We can't imagine that the corporation is so pressed for cash that its executives couldn't donate" at least part of the collection.

USX counters that two years ago it donated $1.5 million to the Carnegie, which includes the natural-history museum as well as the library. Moreover, many employees have participated in local fund-raising efforts to assist cultural institutions.

And not everyone even liked the paintings. "I didn't see any executives throwing themselves across the doorway saying `Please, don't take the paintings away,'" says a USX spokesman.
