Credit Markets: Mortgage-Backed Securities Market Gets Jolt; Moody's May Downgrade S&L-Issued Paper

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 04/03/90

NEW YORK -- The mortgage-backed securities market received another jab yesterday when Moody's Investors Service Inc. said it may downgrade the credit rating on $26 billion of securities issued by the nation's savings and loan associations. Moody's said the possible action would involve the mortgage-backed bonds and medium-term notes issued by all of the nation's savings and loan associations -- those under government conservatorship along with those that are independent.

Moody's announcement follows similar action taken late last month by Standard & Poor's Corp., which said it may lower the rating on $20 billion of debt issued by S&Ls.

Meanwhile, Treasury bonds ended little changed after a day of quiet trading.

The reviews by the nation's two largest credit rating agencies were prompted by recent statements by Resolution Trust Corp., the federal agency created to manage the savings and loan bailout.

Last month, following the seizure of Franklin Savings & Loan, the agency said it believes that under the thrift industry restructuring law, it has the authority to stop paying interest on fixed-income securities that were issued by thrifts placed under RTC conservatorship -- even when the securities are backed by collateral.

If RTC decides to exercise such authority, "this may be considered a default on the interest payments," said Raymond McDaniel, an associate director at Moody's. "It is potentially changing the original promise that investors believed they were getting in the securities."

Mr. McDaniel said that in a meeting with credit rating agencies yesterday, RTC outlined five options concerning the resolution of the interest-payment issue. Under the RTC option most unfavorable to investors, Mr. McDaniel said they would at the most lose one or more interest payments but not the principal.

Moody's said RTC's five options concerning interest payments on thrift bonds were: No post-receivership interest; government rate of interest from the date of receivership until the date of repudiation with no interest until collateral liquidation and payment to investors; government rate of interest from the date of receivership until collateral liquidation and payment to investors; payment at the security's stated rate of interest until repudiation, with a government rate of interest until liquidation; and payment at the security's stated rate of interest until liquidation (the regulatory practice before the thrift bailout law).

Mr. McDaniel said he expects RTC to make a decision within two weeks.

Treasury Securities

Treasury bonds drifted lower yesterday, along with the U.S. dollar, after a day of dull trading activity.

At the start of yesterday's session, dealers expected bonds to post modest gains in a follow-through from a rally overseas that was triggered by falling Japanese stock prices. The Nikkei index of the Tokyo stock market fell 1978.38 points, the second largest decline since the October 1987 stock market crash.

As the Nikkei plunged, some investors sold stocks and bought U.S. Treasury bonds. That lifted prices of the bonds in Tokyo. But trading in the U.S. was quiet.

The benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at 98 17/32 to yield 8.62% compared with 98 19/32 to yield 8.62% on Friday. The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 99 to yield 8.63% compared with 99 1/32 to yield 8.63% on Friday.

Short-term rates were lower when compared to last week at the U.S. government's regular weekly auction.

The Treasury said that the average discount rate on three-month Treasury bills was 7.83% yesterday and that the average discount rate on six-month bills was 7.81%. The rates were down from last week's auction when they were 7.85% and 7.83% respectively.

The average rate on three-month bills was the lowest since the average of 7.72% at the auction Feb. 26. The rate on six-month bills was the lowest since the average of 7.79% at the auction on March 5.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Prices of mortgage-backed securities were unchanged to up slightly, with issues falling from highs earlier in the day in thin trading.

Among new issues, Citibank offered $210 million of mortgage pass-through securities yesterday that are backed by residential mortgage loans.

The issue, which is expected to be rated triple-A by the credit rating agencies, is guaranteed by Financial Security Assurance.

Junk Bonds

The junk bonds of Southland Corp. were little changed despite late news that the firm posted a loss of $1.01 billion in the fourth quarter after a $947 million write-off of good will.

The Dallas-based convenience store operator said its most recent internal forecasts show that it won't meet certain financial covenants with its banks, which hold more than $1 billion of senior debt.

The company also said it may be forced to seek bankruptcy-law protection if it can't complete a restructuring, which is contingent on Southland swapping about $1.8 billion in public debt for stock and new zero coupon bonds. So far, many bondholders have balked at terms of the swap. Southland's 16 3/4% subordinated debentures, due in 2002, were offered unchanged at 14 1/2. But there were no bids for the securities. The company's 15 3/4% senior subordinated notes, maturing in 1997, were also little changed at 23 1/2.

Municipals

Outperforming U.S. Treasury issues, municipal bonds were flat to up 1/8 point in late dealings, with bonds showing little price movement after gains posted earlier in the day.

Among actively traded bonds, New Jersey Turnpike Authority issue of 2018 was up 1/8 point at 97 3/8 to yield 7.42%, which was 0.01 percentage point lower than that of late Friday.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese government bond market followed the yen yesterday, opening weaker as the dollar traded as high as 160.30 yen but recovering as the yen rose.

At the close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the yield on the benchmark No. 119 4.8% bond due in 1999 was at 7.355%. The price was up 0.04 at 85.99.

Dealers were mixed on whether the drop in the Nikkei stock index of the Tokyo stock market was a bullish or bearish factor.

"There was talk of selling stock and buying bonds," said one dealer. Other dealers, however, believe the weakness of the stock market is bearish for the bond market.

In other markets:

-- West German bonds ended about 0.10 point firmer after an early morning rally. Bonn's 7 3/4% benchmark issue due 2000 ended at 95.95 to yield 8.36%.

-- British government gilts ended firmer in quiet trading after a late-day rally.

Bonds and sterling had been lower during the morning as overseas investors reacted bearishly to reports of a riot over the weekend in London. Violence erupted during a demonstration Saturday over Britain's new system of local taxation, the so-called community charge. But reaction was more muted among local participants. Meanwhile, sterling advanced against the mark, which bolstered sentiment in government bonds. Britain's 11 3/4% Treasury bond due 2003-2007 rose 6/32 to 98 15/32 to yield 11.95%.
