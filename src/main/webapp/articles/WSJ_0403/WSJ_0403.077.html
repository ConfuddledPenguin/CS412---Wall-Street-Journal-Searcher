Technology and Health: Psyllium's Ability To Cut Cholesterol Backed by Studies

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- Three new studies financed by Kellogg Co., Battle Creek, Mich., found that cereal laced with the grain psyllium reduces cholesterol. But the findings may touch off a fresh round of health claims and counterclaims similar to the dispute over the benefits of oat bran. All three of the new studies tested people with high cholesterol levels who had already been put on low-cholesterol diets. The addition of psyllium, which is high in soluble fiber, further reduced cholesterol levels and lowered cholesterol more than other fibers, including oat bran, they found.

As a result, the Kellogg-financed researchers said, cereal containing psyllium can reduce the risk of heart disease by fighting cholesterol-clogging of the arteries. Kellogg, which orchestrated a big public-relations effort to promote the new studies, sells Heartwise, the only cereal with psyllium sold nationwide by a major cereal company. But psyllium isn't without controversy. Last year, the Food and Drug Administration looked into the safety of its use in large quantities in cereal. In response, General Mills Inc. took a psyllium-based cereal off the market.

Psyllium, also known as flea wort, is grown primarily in India. It has been used for years by Procter & Gamble Co. in its laxative Metamucil. The new studies, which were released at the annual meeting of the Federation of American Societies for Experimental Biology here, all concluded that psyllium is safe in cereals, although some of those in the tests reported such effects as gas, bloating and cramping -- symptoms common to a sudden increase in fiber consumption.

All three studies measured the impact of psyllium after the subjects had been put on a low-fat diet and fed cereal supplied by Kellogg. The studies were conducted at the University of Kentucky, George Washington University, and the University of Massachusetts Medical School in Worcester.

A Kellogg spokeswoman said, "We're encouraged by the studies," but she wouldn't say how they might influence the company's marketing plans for Heartwise, which isn't a big seller, or other new cereals.
