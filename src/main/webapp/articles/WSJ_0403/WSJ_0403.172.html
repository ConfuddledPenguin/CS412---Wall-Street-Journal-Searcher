GM To Name Today Stempel as Chairman And Chief Executive

  • Author: Paul Ingrassia
  • Date: 04/03/90

DETROIT -- General Motors Corp. will announce today that Robert C. Stempel, its 56-year-old president, will be named chairman and chief executive officer, succeeding Roger B. Smith, who is to retire Aug. 1. Mr. Stempel, it was learned, won't be succeeded as president of GM. Instead, the auto maker will promote two executive vice presidents to vice chairmen: John F. Smith Jr. and Robert J. Schultz. Both will retain their current duties, Mr. Smith as head of GM's international operations and Mr. Schultz as head of technical staffs and GM's two big subsidiaries, Electronic Data Systems Corp. and GM Hughes Electronics.

Lloyd E. Reuss, another executive vice president who's responsible for GM's huge North American auto operations, will become president of those operations, essentially a change in title. However, he will also gain additional responsibility by overseeing GM's huge components operations. Those operations will continue to report to William E. Hoglund, who continues as executive vice president but now will report to Mr. Reuss.

Two other executive vice presidents, Robert T. O'Connell and F. Alan Smith, will remain in their current posts, Mr. O'Connell as head of financial operations and Alan Smith as head of corporate staff functions. None of the Smiths -- Roger, John or Alan -- is related.

Mr. Stempel has long been regarded as the favorite to succeed Roger Smith as chairman of the world's largest industrial company, but his appointment marks a significant departure from tradition at GM. Mr. Stempel is an engineer. GM usually draws its chairmen from its financial ranks.

The company's theory has been that only financial men can enforce cost discipline at a company where spending is measured in the tens of billions of dollars. If GM's engineers were left to their own devices, the reasoning goes, they would overspend like unfettered Pentagon generals, bringing mediocre earnings to a company where the boast has long been: "General Motors doesn't make cars. It makes money."

But that theory has been blown apart during this decade. GM spent more than $70 billion in capital outlays during the 1980s, but the company's U.S. market share has dropped more than 10% and for three years in a row earnings fell behind those of its smaller rival, Ford Motor Co. Many at GM are convinced that the company needs a "product man" to revive its fortunes in its largest market.

Mr. Stempel has that reputation, even though his track record is somewhat hard to guage. He has had six different jobs at GM in the past 11 years.

The jobs that brought Mr. Stempel to the fore were his leadership of GM's Buick-Oldsmobile-Cadillac group, which makes most of the company's large cars, between 1984 and 1986, and head of truck and international operations in 1986 and 1987.

The BOC group adopted a decentralized operating structure that has made it far more effective than its counterpart, the Chevrolet-Pontiac-Canada group. BOC leads in product quality and in profitability by a wide margin. GM's international operations, meanwhile, have turned the corner to handsome profitability in recent years, especially in Europe.

Because of his relatively young age, Mr. Stempel, like Chairman Smith, can be expected to have a long tenure at the helm of GM. His most significant challenge will be turning around the company's troubled North American auto operations, which have suffered from a decade of decline.
