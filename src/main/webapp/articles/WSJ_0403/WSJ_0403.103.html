Your Money Matters -- Investment Dartboard: Stock-Picker Wins Big by Thinking Small

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 04/03/90

Who says small stocks don't do well these days? Certainly not Jonathan Steinberg, publisher and editor in chief of Individual Investor magazine.

He won this column's stock-picking contest for March with a recommendation for American Film Technologies Inc., a leading colorizer of movies that recently turned profitable. The price of the company's shares surged 33% during the month.

Thanks to that big gain, the contests' investment pros, as a group, scored a 7.7% advance for the month -- handily beating both the Dow Jones Industrial Average (up 3%) and a randomly selected "dartboard portfolio" (down 1%). In 18 monthly outings, the dart throwers have prevailed five times, and the pros have won 13 times.

Now Mr. Steinberg is back with another small-company pick: Image Entertainment Inc., which makes laser video disks. Never heard of them? You will, Mr. Steinberg declares.

A laser video disk is to a videotape as a compact disk is to a phonograph record. With a laser video disk player, film buffs can enjoy sharper, more vivid pictures. Though there are only some 400,000 laser disk players in the U.S., Mr. Steinberg thinks the number will increase rapidly, now that some sell for less than $500.

Image Entertainment, based in Chatsworth, Calif., has a lot to gain, Mr. Steinberg figures. The company makes the disks, not the players. It has about 60% of the market, he says, with exclusive rights to distribute more than 1,500 movies.

Image Entertainment reported sales of just $7.8 million for the third quarter ended Dec. 31. Its earnings were smaller yet -- less than a penny a share. But its $109,882 profit for the quarter represents a break into black ink, after losses during its start-up period. The shares (of which there are 133 million) sell for about 69 cents apiece, and Mr. Steinberg recommends buying at any price up to 85 cents per share.

Stocks that sell for less than $1 a share -- so-called penny stocks -- can be highly volatile. And they're often more expensive to trade than higher-priced stocks. But Mr. Steinberg doesn't think Image Entertainment is a stock to trade in and out of: He thinks it's a stock to hold for two years or more.

Also back for another try is money manager Remy Fisher, a vice president with the New York money-management firm J. & W. Seligman & Co., who is making her third appearance in the contest. Her recommendation at the end of January to buy Airborne Freight Corp. is the best pick of the past six months. Borne aloft by strong earnings, it was up 38% as of last Friday.

Ms. Fisher's latest pick is Santa Fe Pacific Corp. She likes Santa Fe because it is re-emphasizing its railroad business, selling off portions of its oil and gas, mining and real estate interests.

Ms. Fisher says railroads will benefit from a "better labor-bargaining climate" in the next couple of years. She especially likes Santa Fe because about a third of the freight it carries is wheat, and because it emphasizes "intermodal" (combination road and rail) transportation. She expects a bumper crop of wheat, and heavy demand for it from foreign buyers. As for intermodal transport, she says it is finally becoming cost-competitive with straight trucking.

Santa Fe should earn $1 a share this year (60 cents of it from the railroad), Ms. Fisher thinks. For next year she predicts earnings of $1.75 a share, with the railroad contributing a full dollar of that.

Ms. Fisher thinks the operations Santa Fe is shedding have a value of about $16 a share. The railroad currently has a value of $15 a share, she reckons, but will grow in value as results improve. She would buy the stock, which closed yesterday at $19.25 a share, at prices up to $24.

Joining the contest this month are two experienced money managers, Thomas S. Mench of Leshner Financial Services Inc. in Cincinnati and William Kapner of Justin Asset Management Inc. in Washington, D.C.

Mr. Mench, who is a portfolio manager for three mutual funds, endorses QMS Inc., a Mobile, Ala., manufacturer of computer printers and imaging systems. "The earnings trend for the company is extremely strong," he says. He expects it to earn $1.25 a share this fiscal year (ending in September), up from 96 cents last year. In the year ending September 1991 he expects $1.45.

A stock whose earnings are growing that fast deserves to sell for 15 to 16 times earnings, Mr. Mench figures. So he concludes that QMS will deserve a $19-to-$20 share price by the end of this year. That's considerably above yesterday's closing price of $13.50. Mr. Mench considers the stock a buy at as much as $16.

Mr. Kapner suggests MCI Communications Corp., the No. 2 long-distance telephone company. At yesterday's closing price of $36.50, MCI sells for only about 12 times the earnings Mr. Kapner expects for 1990 -- an average multiple. But it deserves better, Mr. Kapner says.

"We think rapid earnings growth at a 20-25% {annual} pace is sustainable," he says. "MCI spent the bulk of the 1980s pouring money into building a long-distance network. The network is largely built. So the incremental cost of bringing on new customers or having new volume go through those lines is very small."

In addition, Mr. Kapner says, MCI (with 12% of the market) is continuing to nibble market share away from AT&T (which has 65%). He would buy MCI shares at prices up to $45 or so.

For April, the pros are up against a dartboard portfolio consisting of Caesars World Inc., Floating Point Systems Inc., Longview Fibre Co. and Putnam Diversified Premium Income Trust.
