Canadian Bank Sells 50% Stake In Australia Bank

  • Author: Robert Melnbardis
  • Date: 04/03/90

TORONTO -- Royal Bank of Canada sold its 50% stake in its Australian bank for 200 million Australian dollars (US$150 million) in the first part of a A$3.4 billion merger (US$2.6 billion) between a big bank and an insurance concern in Australia. Royal Bank retreated from full-service retail banking in Australia by selling its half-interest in National Mutual Royal Bank Ltd. to Australia & New Zealand Banking Group, or ANZ. National Mutual Life Association of Australasia Ltd., Royal Bank's joint-venture partner in the 130-branch bank, also sold its stake to ANZ. Total proceeds for the transactions were A$400 million.

The sale of the bank was the first leg of a proposed merger in which ANZ will pay A$3.4 billion for a 51% stake in National Mutual Life. The merger, if accepted by shareholders and policyholders and approved by the Australian government, would create Australia's largest financial-services concern, said David Trude, analyst at First Boston Corp. It also would protect ANZ from a raid by its competitors, who are mainly Westpac Banking Corp. and National Australia Bank Ltd., of Melbourne, he said. ANZ said the merged entity will have A$91 billion of group assets and A$39 billion under management.

National Mutual Life policyholders will be asked to approve the merger with ANZ on May 29, with combined operations set to begin on Oct. 1. Following the merger, ANZ will ask its shareholders to change its name to ANZ-NM Banking and Insurance Group Ltd. ANZ is a large banking and financial services group and National Mutual Life is Australia's second-largest life insurance concern. Both are based in Melbourne.

In a statement, Australian government Treasurer Paul Keating said he approved in principal ANZ's acquisition of National Mutual Royal Bank, but said he would consider various policy issues regarding the union of ANZ and National Mutual Life. It is likely that Mr. Keating will approve the ANZ-National Mutual Life merger because the two concerns don't operate to a significant degree in the same businesses, Mr. Trude said.

Mr. Keating said that in the event the merger doesn't go ahead, ownership of National Mutual Royal Bank will revert to National Mutual Life.

Although National Mutual Royal Bank "exceeded expectations" on performance, Toronto-based Royal Bank said it sold its interest in the bank to accommodate its joint-venture partner.

"I would think that they are happy to get out of there," said First Boston's Mr. Trude, adding that foreign banks created when the Australian government granted 16 new banking licenses in 1985 have faced tough competition from Australian domestic banks. He noted that National Mutual Royal Bank was one of the more successful new entrants in the deregulated Australian banking sector.

A spokesman for Royal Bank said the bank had invested about A$140 million of capital in National Mutual Royal Bank. He said Royal Bank's focus in recent years has been to withdraw from retail full-service banking outside of Canada, except in parts of the Caribbean, and to focus instead on corporate banking.
