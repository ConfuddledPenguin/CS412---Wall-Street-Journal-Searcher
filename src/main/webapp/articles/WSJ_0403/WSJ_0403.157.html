Education System Gets Poor Grades In Student Survey --- National Assessment Tests Find Lack of Knowledge Of U.S. History, Civics

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 04/03/90

WASHINGTON -- High school seniors approaching voting age lack basic knowledge about U.S. government and history, according to a federal survey. Tests administered by the federally funded National Assessment of Educational Progress in 1988 -- a presidential election year -- showed that only 57% of the nation's 12th graders knew that presidential candidates are nominated by national conventions. Only 51% knew that the Constitution guarantees religious freedom.

The National Assessment administered history and civics tests to national samples of students in the fourth, eighth and 12th grades. Its tests on these subjects and others will be used to measure progress toward the ambitious education goals set for the year 2000 that President Bush and the nation's governors announced earlier this year.

The tests reveal an uneven grasp of history and civics. For instance, although 84% of the eighth graders knew how Abraham Lincoln died, only a quarter knew that his goal in the Civil War was to save the Union. In civics, only 38% of the eighth graders knew that Congress makes laws, and only 41% knew that the Supreme Court can declare a law constitutional.

The history test showed that only 5% of the high-school seniors scored high enough to demonstrate an ability to interpret historical information and ideas. "Far too many of our students know far too little about where we have come from and about how we as a nation became what we are today," said Richard Riley, former governor of South Carolina and a member of the National Assessment governing board.

Like earlier assessments of reading, writing, science and math, the latest surveys concluded that students watch too much television and do too little homework. The surveys showed that only 28.9% of the high-school seniors tested spend two or more hours on homework each night, while 47.2% watch more than two hours of TV. Less than half the students read more than 10 pages daily in school and for homework.
