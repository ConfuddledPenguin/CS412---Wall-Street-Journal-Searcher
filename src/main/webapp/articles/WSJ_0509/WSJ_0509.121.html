Tax Report: A Special Summary and Forecast Of Federal and State Tax Developments

  • Author: Scott R. Schmedel
  • Date: 05/09/90

A LEGISLATIVE PROPOSAL on small S corporations has tax lawyers in a stew. In recent years, hundreds of thousands of closely held corporations have converted to S status for the benefits of being taxed at the owners' level, much as partnerships are. Conversion isn't taxable. But a staffer of Congress's joint tax committee on Friday disclosed an unexpected proposal to change that. The plan would create a "toll charge" that "would practically eliminate conversions," says Richard Shaw of Shenas, Shaw & Spievak, a San Diego law firm.

The proposal is in recent joint-committee-staff recommendations to the House Ways and Means panel for simplifying taxes; it came out at a meeting of American Bar Association tax lawyers in Washington. It would treat a conversion as a liquidation of the corporation and a distribution of assets. Thus, corporate tax would be imposed on the concern's accumulated gain in value; then the owners would owe personal tax on the remaining balance. The effective total tax rate could be 52.5%.

This treatment, rejected by legislators in 1982 and 1986, eliminates many other S-corporation tax complexities, Shaw says, "but at too high a cost."

PEACE AND COMFORT may return to the IRS's private-ruling policy.

The IRS provoked protests a year ago by saying it would stop issuing requested "comfort" rulings to confirm obvious effects of proposed transactions; it saw no need to rule on issues "clearly and adequately addressed by published authority" and hoped to ease strains on its resources. But responding to the outcries, it sought comments and delayed the sweeping policy change to this week. Now Announcement 90-65 says the IRS has dropped the plan and will adopt some suggested alternatives.

The measures will include providing a list of specific no-ruling topics, model language for certain documents, checklists of "safe-harbor" tests for some transactions, and procedures for automatic rulings on certain issues. The IRS says it will discuss each measure with tax-professional groups before issuing specific guidelines.

BANKRUPTCIES are demanding 23% of Justice Department tax lawyers' time.

Uncle Sam is a major creditor in many cases. The job of protecting IRS tax claims is surging along with bankruptcy-court filings, Assistant Attorney General Peterson says. Justice's tax division got almost 12,000 new cases in fiscal 1989 and expects almost 16,000 in 1990-up from 5,000 in 1986. The worst may be yet to come, she says, perhaps arising from leveraged buy-outs of the 1980s. "LBO has in some minds come to stand for `large bankruptcy opportunity,'" she told the ABA tax lawyers last week.

Peterson says the government intends to play "an aggressive role" in bankruptcy cases and in large cases will seek a place on committees that speak for creditors. So far, that has happened only in the cases of Texans William Herbert Hunt and Nelson Bunker Hunt, where it was very productive. The government expects to receive about $170 million in those cases and could get an additional $165 million, depending on the Hunts' future income.

Bankruptcy-court statisticians expect 735,000 filings in fiscal 1990, up from 658,000 in 1989, and are projecting roughly 10% rises in 1991 and 1992.

SOCIAL SECURITY numbers for dependents as young as two must be listed on 1989 returns so that the IRS can check dependency claims. It is sending notices to filers who didn't supply the numbers or insert the words "applied for" on returns. Filers who omitted a number for the second year will get a stronger notice and may be assessed a new $50 penalty.

A $25,000 PENALTY, the new maximum, has been levied on Charles W. Singer Jr. of Philadelphia for persisting in frivolous and delaying tactics in Tax Court. Judge Cohen also upheld civil fraud penalties. Singer previously pleaded guilty in a district court of criminal tax evasion.

ALLEGED IRS MISCONDUCT and the agency's plans to deal with it are to be discussed today at a hearing before a House Government Operations panel headed by Rep. Barnard (D., Ga.). IRS Commissioner Goldberg and Treasury Inspector General Kirkendall are scheduled to testify.

MINIMUM-TAX RULES affect June payments of corporate estimated taxes.

Starting this year, corporations must calculate an amount called adjusted current earnings in determining whether they owe the 20% alternative minimum tax instead of regular tax at a lower effective rate. Last week, the IRS proposed rules for computing the amount. Calendar-year corporations should use the rules in calculating estimated taxes due June 15, Samuel Starr of Coopers & Lybrand, CPAs, recommends. On the whole, the rules seem reasonable, he says.

Banks, life-insurance concerns, and corporations that are partners in partnerships in particular are affected by several provisions. And a section related to employee stock-ownership plans will hit many companies, Starr says: In effect, it denies for minimum-tax purposes a full deduction for dividends paid to an ESOP. Such deductions are allowed in calculating regular taxes, on certain conditions.

The IRS has scheduled a hearing on the proposed rules on Oct. 16 and 17.
