EPA Issues a Rule Covering Treatment Of Hazardous Waste

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency issued a rule for treating hazardous wastes that will cost industry $350 million to $440 million annually, but environmentalists claim the regulation is too lax. The rule, the last in a set of five regulations issued since 1984, will demand the biggest expenditures from the electric, gas and sanitary service industries, chemical plants and petroleum refineries.

The latest regulation, covering more than half of the hazardous waste generated today, requires that contamination by more than 300 chemicals, toxicity and other hazardous characteristics be reduced before sludge or waste water can be disposed of in landfills, deep wells or other facilities by at least May 1992.

The EPA said the rules will keep "significant quantities" of hazardous wastes off the land, help prevent groundwater contamination and encourage companies to reduce the amount of wastes they generate. But environmental groups charged that the agency buckled under to chemical and other interests by setting standards that don't always require industry to use the best available technology, such as incineration or solidification techniques, to treat hazardous wastes before disposal.

Karen Florini, an attorney with the Environmental Defense Fund, called the rule "extremely lax" and said the EPA is "abandoning a golden opportunity for pollution prevention." She said environmental groups are considering filing a lawsuit.

In writing the rules, the EPA was under pressure from both industries that generate wastes and hazardous-waste treatment companies that would have profited from tougher regulation.
