Greyhound Head Holds Secret Talks With Union Group

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 05/09/90

DALLAS -- Just hours after Greyhound Lines Inc. declared a strike by the Amalgamated Transit Union irrelevant, company Chairman Fred Currey met secretly with union leaders in his Dallas office. The hour-and-a-half bargaining session, held at the request of ATU officials, marked Mr. Currey's first direct involvement in negotiations since the start of the often-violent strike on March 2, by 9,400 employees of Greyhound, a unit of GLI Holding Co. But the meeting apparently drove the two sides farther apart and may have marked an end to bargaining for the forseeable future.

"We left it that we are not going to get back together," said P. Anthony Lannie, Greyhound executive vice president and chief labor negotiator. "There are no future meetings and there are no plans for any future meetings. Our position is that until they are willing to talk about what the company can afford, bargaining is fruitless."

In a news conference Monday, Mr. Currey said he had effectively defeated the ATU strike because as a result of strike defections and newly hired replacement drivers, he had nearly enough employees to operate the Greyhound system.

In addition to Mr. Currey, Mr. Lannie and a federal mediator, also present were ATU vice president James Hayes and Edward Strait, president of the Amalgamated Council of Greyhound Local Unions, the ATU's bargaining unit.

Mr. Strait, who flew to Dallas from Washington, D.C., said he requested the meeting with Mr. Currey because he didn't want to "leave any stone unturned."

According to participants in Monday's session, neither side made any new economic proposals or offered substantial modifications from previously announced positions.

Last weekend, after announcing $55.8 million in strike-related losses in the first quarter, the company sharply reduced its original contract offer and proposed a six-year pact with a four-year pay freeze.

Union officials have called the company's offer an "insult" and say they are standing by their previous demand for a contract that would raise drivers' base pay 20% over three years. Company officials complain that the union proposal would cost the company more than $140 million and is unrealistic in light of the bus company's deteriorated financial picture.

"They don't care what we can afford," said Mr. Lannie. "They want what they want."

Mr. Strait said that in Monday's meeting, company officials refused to discuss the idea of laying off the replacement drivers or reducing their seniority so that strikers could go back to work.
