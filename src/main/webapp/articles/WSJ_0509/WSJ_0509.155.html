Foreign Exchange: Dollar Steadies in Sluggish Dealings; Treasury Auction Makes Market Wary

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/09/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar steadied after two days of broad declines and as the Treasury's long-awaited debt auction got under way. The auction, which stretches over three days, instilled caution in market participants. Also inhibiting major position-taking was the scheduled release Friday of data on U.S. retail sales and producer prices for April, traders said.

Gillie Augeri, vice president and chief dealer at Connecticut National Bank in Hartford, said traders generally expect the $30.5 billion Treasury refunding to go well but are reluctant to move prior to learning the outcome.

Amid sluggish dollar dealings, activity in non-dollar exchange rates, particularly the mark-yen cross rate, drew attention, traders said.

The dollar slipped against the yen in response to buying of the Japanese unit against the mark, dealers said. The West German currency was seen correcting a bit against the yen after a rise Monday in New York to 95.52 yen, participants said. The mark ended yesterday's New York trading at 95.12 yen.

The $10.5 billion of three-year notes auctioned yesterday brought an average 8.74% yield, the Treasury announced. The result, which was in line with expectations, came too late to affect foreign exchange trading. Preliminary indications showed good participation by Japanese investors.

The Treasury will sell $10 billion of 10-year bonds today, followed by the sale of $10 billion of 30-year notes tomorrow.

The dollar posted a modest gain against the mark on a slight squeeze of short positions that developed in the wake of the U.S. unit's two days of declines against the West German currency, said Valerie Walker, vice president and corporate foreign exchange manager at Harris Trust & Savings Bank in New York.

The market is still bearish on the U.S. unit, Ms. Walker said. Many of those who were squeezed out of their short positions yesterday are merely waiting for the dollar to fall to 1.6470 marks before selling it once again, she said.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6573 marks, up from 1.6558 marks late Monday, and at 157.62 yen, down from 158.19 yen late Monday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6705, up from $1.6685 Monday.

In early trading in Tokyo Wednesday, the U.S. currency was quoted at 157.30 yen, down from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 158.05 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.6575 marks.

The dollar still is suffering from a more pessimistic view of the U.S. economy after last Friday's disappointing employment report, said Morris Armstrong, vice president and manager of foreign exchange at Girozentrale Vienna.

"We have a situation now where you have inflation and no growth," Mr. Armstrong said, referring to the combination of Friday's disappointing employment numbers and the rising inflation shown in the report on first-quarter gross national product.

In contrast to Ms. Walker, though, Mr. Armstrong said the dollar seems to have found a comfortable trading area at current levels against the mark and yen. It must be remembered that the dollar's lower level against both currencies is still within trading bands seen this year and "the more the dollar stays in these (bands), the more you have to believe it's in equilibrium," he said.

Despite the gains made by the yen against the dollar and the mark, Ms. Walker, Mr. Armstrong and others said the Japanese currency won't strengthen appreciably in the absence of a rise in Japanese interest rates.

Comments by French Economics Minister Pierre Beregovoy that Group of Seven monetary officials believe the yen shouldn't decline any further were brushed off by the market, said Girozentrale's Mr. Armstrong.

With a net outflow of capital from Japan and no rise in Japanese interest rates, "there's no reason to send the yen higher right now," he said.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $370 an ounce, down $1.90. Estimated volume was a moderate 2.5 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $370.70 an ounce.
