Germans May Market Stock-Information System in London

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/09/90

FRANKFURT, West Germany -- A West German stock-exchange official said the nation is considering marketing its Ibis price-information system in Britain if the London Stock Exchange follows through with plans to install its Seaq International system in German trading rooms. But Ruediger von Rosen, executive vice chairman of the Federation of German Stock Exchanges, said he would prefer if the major European stock markets would cooperate in the Price Information Project Europe, a pan-European screen-based securities-trading system.

Such a cooperation would cut the cost of information and facilitate the creation of a unified European market place for the trading of blue-chip shares, the official said in remarks prepared for the Financial Markets Group Conference at the London School of Economics. A text of the speech was made available in Frankfurt.

Seaq, which handles around 12% of the trading volume in the key German blue-chip stocks, launched a marketing campaign to install its monitors in Frankfurt bank offices, which would permit traders to conduct their business directly on the London exchange.

In return, the German exchanges could offer their new Ibis system to traders outside West Germany once it has been expanded into a full-fledged trading system able to handle stocks and bonds from all major European markets, Mr. von Rosen said. Ibis's main advantage over Seaq and other systems is its direct line to the German Central Securities Depository, which permits automatic clearing and settlement within a two-day period, he said.

But such a strategy would result in a situation where traders are surrounded by monitors from Ibis, Seaq, the French CAC system, and even the U.S. Nasdaq network, Mr. von Rosen said.
