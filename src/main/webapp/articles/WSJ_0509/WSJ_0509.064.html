Canadian Union Sets Contract Talks At Stone Container

  • Author: G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 05/09/90

MONTREAL -- The Canadian Paperworkers Union picked three Quebec mills owned by Stone Container Corp. for contract negotiations that will set the pattern for most of the forest-products industry in Eastern Canada, which is a major supplier of newsprint to U.S. publishers. The choice of Stone Container wasn't expected by many industry observers, including Roger Stone, chairman, president and chief executive officer of the Chicago paper products concern.

"We have somewhat of a reputation as a hard bargainer. I'm surprised," Mr. Stone said.

The union represents about 30,000 workers at mills in Eastern Canada, which is a producer of newsprint, pulp, and other paper products. As a rule, the union negotiates a contract with one company or group of mills and that settlement is then adopted by the rest of the industry.

Analysts said the union may have picked Stone Container because the union believes the company isn't prepared to endure a strike. "If that's true they've misjudged the situation," Mr. Stone said.

Don Holder, union president, said that "we believe we can get a settlement without a strike." The union is seeking an 8% wage increase in each year of a two-year contract and an automatic cost-of-living increase. But Mr. Holder conceded that "it would be a little naive to expect to come out with 8%." The base wage rate at the Stone Container mills is currently 15.37 Canadian dollars (US$13.22) an hour. Contracts at most Eastern Canadian mills expired April 30.

The contract negotiations will be watched closely by newsprint buyers. Most major newsprint producers have announced a June 1 price increase of US$35 a metric ton to US$685 a ton on the East Coast, and a similar increase in the West, where the price scale is slightly different. Transaction prices, the actual prices paid by buyers, are much lower with 20% discounts widespread. Industry executives said they expect that an increase in the list price would lift transaction prices from about US$520 a ton to about US$550.

Initially, many publishers didn't expect the price increase to go through, but now opinion is mixed. An extended strike at the three Stone mills, one of which produces standard newsprint, could help producers push through the price increase. The newsprint market is highly sensitive to minor shifts in supply and demand.

"A strike is probably the only chance" for a price boost, said J. William Cox, senior vice president at the Dallas Morning News.

The increase is "a contrived non-market driven farce," said Homer Taylor, vice president of supply at Knight-Ridder Inc. in Miami. While a strike could temporarily lift newsprint prices, once production returned to normal, prices would fall back to current levels, Mr. Taylor said.
