Sotheby's Auction Ends With 37% Of Artwork Unsold

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 05/09/90

NEW YORK -- A cold flap of reality hit the art market at Sotheby's Holdings Inc. At the auction house's sale here of contemporary art last night, art collectors went bargain-hunting with avid bidding for "mid-price" six-figure artworks. But there was little interest in the low-end and high-end paintings and sculptures.

In a packed sales room that grew increasingly uneasy, again and again bidders rejected even household-name contemporary artists like Jackson Pollack and Roy Lichtenstein as too expensive.

Of the 87 artworks up for bid, 37% didn't sell. Overall, the total for the sale reached $55.9 million, very much below the $88.1 million to $114.8 million range predicted by the auction house. In contrast, Sotheby's last major contemporary art sale in November raised $98.3 million for 68 paintings and sculptures.

Morever, many pieces last night sold at prices below the lowest published Sotheby's estimate. That indicates that the secret minimum price the sellers would accept might have been slashed since the pieces were originally scheduled for auction a few months ago -- in general, the sign of a softening market.

Lucy Mitchell-Innes, head of Sotheby's contemporary art department, said the market was still "strong" and "confident," but "expectations among collectors have gotten a bit ahead of themselves."

Sotheby's sale was less successful, however, than the contemporary art sale Monday night held by its rival Christie's International PLC in New York. At that sale, one-third of the artworks didn't sell, but several of the most expensive ones found buyers.

At Sotheby's, Willem de Kooning's "Woman as Landscape," a huge riot of red and black once exhibited in the Museum of Modern Art, failed to sell. It was estimated to sell for $9 million to $12 million. Bidding ended at $4.8 million.

However, Mr. de Kooning's "Palisade" fetched the highest price of the evening at $7.2 million. Richard Gray, a Chicago art dealer, purchased the piece, which was estimated to sell for $8 million to $10 million.

Francis Bacon's dark, contorted oil painting of a pope, "Study for Portrait," was sold for $5.5 million to an unnamed European art dealer. The pre-sale estimate range was $5 million to $7 million.

One of the surprises of the sale was Andy Warhol's "12 Jackies," a haunting black and white silkscreen featuring repeated images of the former first lady, shown in mourning for President Kennedy. Estimated to bring $2 million to $2.5 million, it also didn't sell.

The major auctions of the spring season, the evening sales of impresssionist and modern paintings, will be held at Christie's and at Sotheby's Tuesday and Thursday of next week, respectively.
