Law -- Legal Beat: Arbitration Award Is Made Under RICO

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and Milo Geyelin
  • Date: 04/05/90

An arbitration panel awarded damages under the federal racketeering law to Kerr-McGee Refining Corp. in what was apparently the first use of the law by arbitrators. The Supreme Court affirmed the right of arbitrators to make such awards in 1987. Because damages under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations law automatically are tripled, the Kerr-McGee award amounted to $512,520.

The case involved an incident in which Kerr-McGee Refining, a unit of Kerr-McGee Corp. of Oklahoma City, Okla., had chartered a tanker from Triumph Tankers Ltd. to transport oil. The three-member arbitration panel found that in March 1984, Triumph Tankers illegally took a substantial quantity of oil from Kerr-McGee by diverting the oil into a secret tank in the tanker. The panel found that because Triumph had converted cargo for its own use on previous voyages, there was a "pattern" of unlawful activity. To win a case under the civil portion of the RICO statute, a plaintiff must prove a pattern of serious wrongdoing conducted through a business or other enterprise.

Kerr-McGee's contract with Triumph had a clause mandating that disputes be arbitrated rather than tried by a court.

Joseph C. Smith, a New York attorney for Triumph, declined to comment on the case. The tanker Kerr-McGee chartered has since been sold for scrap. John P. Vayda, a New York attorney for Kerr-McGee, said Kerr-McGee doesn't know where Triumph is based or whether the company is still in business.

Mr. Vayda said he believes the arbitrators' decision will serve as a "very large deterrent" and will show "that arbitration shouldn't be treated lightly." But he said it's unclear whether Kerr-McGee will be able to collect damages from the company.

The arbitration award that Triumph would be required to pay is in addition to a previous partial award of $332,138 the arbitrators granted to Kerr-McGee in September 1988. The latest award includes $368,496 in damages and $111,544 in legal fees and out-of-pocket expenses, as well as other fees.

In a dissenting opinion, Alexis Nichols, one of the arbitrators, called Kerr-McGee "greedy," and warned that "although one arbitration does not establish a precedent, aggressive maritime attorneys, who ordinarily handle run-of-the-mill commercial maritime disputes, will henceforth be tempted to also plead treble damages under the civil RICO statute, having nothing to lose and everything to gain."

HYATT LEGAL SERVICES must pay damages in AIDS discrimination case.

Hyatt was ordered to pay $157,000 to a former regional partner in Philadelphia for removing him from his job because he has acquired immune deficiency syndrome. The amount, which includes $50,000 in punitive damages, is believed to be the highest award in the country in an AIDS-related employment discrimination case, according to the Lambda Legal Defense and Education Fund in New York, a national group that tracks AIDS-related issues.

In a 41-page opinion, federal Judge Raymond J. Broderick in Philadelphia found Hyatt's conduct "not merely inexcusably insensitive and illegal, but so outrageous that the sanction of punitive damages is warranted."

Hyatt is the nation's biggest provider of prepaid legal service plans. In a prepared statement, the firm conceded that "in retrospect" it may have mishandled the matter and said it would not appeal. "Hyatt Legal Services believed that its conduct . . . was a fair and honest balancing of its responsibilities," the firm added.

The case, involving Clarence B. Cain, 38, is not the first to go against an employer for discriminating against an employee with AIDS; federal courts in California and Louisiana have made similar rulings. But it is the first to test Pennsylvania's recently enacted Human Resources Act, which requires employers not only to accommodate AIDS victims in the workplace but to allow them to work at their accustomed jobs.

Mr. Cain, who was stricken with AIDS 1 1/2 years after becoming Hyatt's regional manager in Philadelphia, was offered three alternatives several days after his superiors learned the news: a $12,000 severance package, a demotion with a 50% pay cut to a staff position in Virginia or help getting treatment at an experimental AIDS treatment program.

Judge Broderick, who described Mr. Cain as a "brilliant attorney with a promising future," wrote that he found the choices "a corrupt assault" on Mr. Cain's dignity.

"The court sent the message loud and clear," said Richard J. Silverberg, Mr. Cain's attorney. "You can't {remove} someone with AIDS who can still do his job simply because he has contracted the disease."

Mr. Cain currently is hospitalized in Newport News, Va., in serious condition.

BEATRICE FOODS CO. prevails against appeal by families of leukemia victims in toxic-waste suit.

The refusal by the First U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals to grant the families a new trial appears to end what the Boston-based court called "this long safari of a case."

The families had sued Beatrice and W.R. Grace & Co. in 1982, alleging that toxic chemicals dumped by the companies polluted two suburban wells in Woburn, Mass., and caused eight cases of leukemia, resulting in five deaths.

A jury in 1986 found that Grace had been negligent in dumping the chemicals but that Chicago-based Beatrice had not contributed to the contamination. Before a planned second phase of the trial, Grace settled out of court without admitting liability.

The families appealed the jury verdict for Beatrice, and the case grew tangled when they later learned that Beatrice had withheld two environmental studies conducted at the site. They asked the judge for a new trial, arguing their case had been damaged by Beatrice's conduct. The judge denied their request.

In 1988 the appeals court upheld the jury verdict in Beatrice's favor. But the court asked the trial judge to hold hearings on whether the new evidence regarding the environmental studies required a new trial. The trial judge ruled it did not, and last week the appeals court affirmed that decision.

The appeals court upheld the trial judge's finding that even if the families had received the reports they still would have been unable to prove that Beatrice disposed of the chemicals at the site.

"The jury has been right all along. There is no basis for Beatrice's involvement in the case," says Jerome P. Facher, an attorney for Beatrice.

Jan Schlichtmann, an attorney for the families, said he will ask the appeals court to rehear the case, arguing the court did not address all the evidence concerning Beatrice's misconduct.

MARCUS SCHLOSS & CO. ex-official settles insider-trading case with SEC.

D. Ronald Yagoda, former head of arbitrage at Marcus Schloss, was sued by the Securities and Exchange Commission for allegedly receiving inside information on pending stock transactions from Andrew Solomon, a former analyst at Marcus Schloss. Mr. Solomon was part of the so-called Yuppie Five ring, broken up after an SEC inquiry.

In July, a jury acquitted Mr. Yagoda of similar charges in a criminal case brought by federal prosecutors.

Under the SEC settlement, Mr. Yagoda, without admitting liability, received a one-year suspension from the securities industry, according to Stanley S. Arkin and Robert J. Hausen, his attorneys. The settlement involves no payments by Mr. Yagoda, they said.

An SEC official said she couldn't comment on the matter as no settlement has been filed with the court.

FEDERATED LEGAL BILL shows lawyers will reap big fees in bankruptcy case.

The law firm for Campeau Corp.'s troubled U.S. retail units, Federated Department Stores Inc. and Allied Stores Corp., submitted a bill of $1.6 million for the first six weeks of work in the matter. The bill from Jones, Day, Reavis & Pogue included $290,156 in out-of-pocket expenses.

The total legal bill represented only 85% of the firm's hourly billings, in addition to 100% of the out-of-pocket expenses. The Cleveland-based law firm will get the remainder after the bankruptcy judge in the case approves the fees. Federated and Allied filed for bankruptcy-court protection on Jan. 15.

According to filings in bankruptcy court in Cincinnati, Jones Day professionals logged 9,538 hours at an average rate of $169 an hour. The average includes billings by law partners, associates and paralegals. About 40 partners, 55 associates and 25 paralegals worked on the matter.

David Heiman, the partner in charge of the matter, topped the list, logging 371 hours at $285 an hour.

The firm's disbursements included $85,523 in travel expenses, $61,294 for food and lodging and $31,385 for photocopying.

SLEEPING PILL'S side effects are targeted in lawsuit.

Attorneys for two people who are suing Upjohn Co. of Kalamazoo, Mich., over the widely marketed sedative Halcion are seeking class-action status for the lawsuit.

Stuart Wechsler, an attorney for the plaintiffs, says he personally suffered depression, amnesia and other side effects after taking the drug, and was forced to take a leave from his law firm of Wechsler Skirnick Harwood Halebian & Feffer in New York City. Mr. Wechsler isn't a plaintiff.

The lawsuit, filed in federal court in Cincinnati, seeks $50,000 in damages for each plaintiff. It alleges that Upjohn failed to disclose known harmful side effects to the Food and Drug Administration before Halcion received U.S. approval in 1982.

Upjohn voluntarily withdrew its large-dose version of the drug in 1987. It continues to market a smaller dose. Dr. Ted Cooper, chairman and president of Upjohn, declined to comment on the suit.

STORY OF GOLD-LADEN SHIP kept Khashoggi in contact with Marcoses, attorney says.

James Linn, attorney for Saudi businessman Adnan Khashoggi, said his client kept in close touch with the Marcoses after they fled the Philippines in 1986 not because he was part of an alleged racketeering conspiracy but because he was promised a portion of the gold. Federal prosecutors in the case say Mr. Khashoggi's numerous phone calls to Imelda and the late Ferdinand Marcos in 1986 were part of an effort to cover up the alleged racketeering.

In his opening statement to the jury in federal court in Manhattan, Mr. Linn said, "I'm almost hesitant to mention this because it sounds crazy, but it's not. Mr. Marcos told (Mr. Khashoggi) that he had a ship of gold. And President Marcos told Mr. Khashoggi, `I'll give you 10% of this gold if you will find a safe port for it.'" Mr. Linn said Mr. Marcos, who was ousted in February 1986, claimed he would use the gold to raise an army to restore him to power some day.

"He told Mr. Khashoggi he would give him 10%, 10% of this whole ship of gold," Mr. Linn said, "and this guy fell for it." He said Mr. Khashoggi, who is being tried on charges of obstruction of justice and mail fraud, regularly communicated with the Marcoses because he wanted the gold -- which never materialized.

Mr. Khashoggi is being tried alongside Mrs. Marcos, who is charged with taking more than $160 million from the Philippines and illegally hiding the assets in investments in the U.S.

CANADA'S TOP JUDGE TO RETIRE: Chief Justice Brian Dickson of the Canadian Supreme Court has decided to retire June 30. A government spokesman in Ottawa said a successor will be named later. Chief Justice Dickson, 73 years old, was appointed to the Supreme Court in 1973 and named chief justice in 1984.
