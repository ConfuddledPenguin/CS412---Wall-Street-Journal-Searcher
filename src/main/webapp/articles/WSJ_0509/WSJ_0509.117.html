Childbirth Isn't Seen As Career Ending For Women Today --- Census Study Cites Growth In Employer-Provided Maternity-Leave Benefits

  • Author: Cathy Trost
  • Date: 05/09/90

WASHINGTON -- A Census Bureau study shows that childbirth in many cases has become a brief work interruption instead of the "career-halting event" it often was for women a generation ago, a trend facilitated by the growth in employer-provided maternity-leave benefits. "In the early 1960s, the vast majority of women either in the months before or after the birth took at least a year off from work. Now, only about one-third take 12 or more months," said Martin O'Connell, chief of the Census Bureau's fertility-statistics branch.

Mr. O'Connell, who wrote the section of the report dealing with maternity benefits, points out that women today have more job skills, greater commitment to jobs and are more able to pay for the child-care assistance they need in order to go back to work after the birth of a child.

But his research also indicates "a very, very strong factor" in women's rapid return to work is the provision of maternity benefits by employers, including leaves with and without pay and guarantees they can retain their jobs and seniority after such leaves. Such benefits often spare women the burden of looking for work anew after childbirth, and strengthen their ties to employers.

In the early 1960s, only 16% of pregnant workers received maternity leave with an assurance their jobs would be held for them. Twenty years later, 47% of pregnant workers received maternity benefits. More than 60% of women quit their jobs at some point during pregnancy or shortly after birth in the early 1960s, a percentage that fell to 28% in the early 1980s.

Besides compiling information on changing maternity-leave benefits and their impact on women's participation in the work force, the wide-ranging Census report reaffirms that women are marrying and beginning childbearing later.

Mr. O'Connell surveyed data on women whose first children were born between January 1961 and December 1985. He found that 65% of women worked during pregnancy in the early 1980s, up from 44% in the early 1960s. Of those pregnant workers, 78% stayed on the job into their last trimester, compared with 52% during the earlier period.

"Even more remarkable in the last 25 years has been the change in the role women play as family providers within the first year of their child's life," Mr. O'Connell said. In the early 1960s, one of every six women went back to work before her child's first birthday; now one-half of women with newborns return to work within a year of childbirth.

The women most likely to work during their first pregnancy are relatively older, white and have at least a high-school education. Teen-agers, blacks and high-school dropouts are the least likely to be employed during pregnancy or at other times. And because of financial reasons, those teenagers, blacks and high-school dropouts who did work during pregnancy were the most likely to return to work within six months of childbirth.

But the report adds that overall, "regardless of economic need, returning to work rapidly after childbirth is becoming the norm among all social groups."

The report says maternity-leave policies can benefit employers by reducing potential costs associated with finding replacement workers. It suggests that employers who offer maternity benefits with the promise of job retention may encourage more women to work longer into their pregnancy and return to work more swiftly. But it warns that such inducements might not work for employees in clerical and other jobs that foster little long-term job commitment.

The report comes as the U.S. House of Representatives is preparing to vote on legislation that would require employers of 50 or more workers to give them unpaid time off to care for newborn or adopted babies; or for the serious illness of a child, spouse or parent, or for personal-health problems. The bill is opposed by the White House and business groups because it mandates the provision of benefits.
