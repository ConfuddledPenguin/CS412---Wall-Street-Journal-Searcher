Ford Motor Wins Partial Victory In Wiper Case

  • Author: Joseph B. White
  • Date: 05/09/90

DETROIT -- Ford Motor Co. won a partial victory in its battle with inventor Robert W. Kearns when a federal jury ruled that the auto maker didn't willfully infringe his patents for an intermittent windshield wiper system. The jury is scheduled to continue deliberations today to decide how much money, if any, Mr. Kearns should collect from Ford.

Attorneys for Mr. Kearns argued during the two-week penalty phase of the trial that Ford should pay the Maryland inventor as much as $325 million. The attorneys also contended that Ford engineers and executives didn't "care one way or the other" about Mr. Kearns's patent claims.

Had the jury agreed that Ford's infringement was willful, U.S. District Court Judge Avern Cohn could have doubled or tripled any financial award.

Any payments awarded to Mr. Kearns would be compensation for Ford's use over 17 years of three windshield wiper systems that infringed on at least three of Mr. Kearns's patents, according to his attorneys.

A Ford spokesman declined comment yesterday, citing a gag order imposed by the judge in the case. Lawyers for Mr. Kearns also declined to comment.

Ford argued in an earlier phase of the trial that while its wiper systems were similar to those patented by Mr. Kearns, those patents were invalid and thus there was no infringement. But the jury ruled in January that Mr. Kearns's patents were valid, and in effect said Ford had infringed the Kearns patents.

But the jurors apparently agreed with Ford lawyers who argued that company officials acted in good faith and "didn't believe they had stolen" Mr. Kearns's ideas. Moreover, Ford lawyers argued that company officials acted responsibly during negotiations with Mr. Kearns and his lawyers that dragged on for nearly seven years.

Mr. Kearns sued Ford in 1978, charging the auto maker with infringing his patents for an electronically controlled, intermittent windshield wiper system. He subsequently filed similar suits against nearly every other major auto maker in the world. The Ford case is the first to go to trial. Intermittent windshield wipers are offered as a standard or optional feature on millions of cars.
