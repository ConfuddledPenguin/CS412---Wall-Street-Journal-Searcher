Drexel Burnham Trading Unit Faces Creditor Pressure

  • Author: Jeff Bailey and Scott McMurray
  • Date: 05/09/90

Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc.'s commodities trading unit could be forced by creditors into bankruptcy court alongside its parent as early as today. Some of the lawyers representing foreign central banks and others owed a total of more than $500 million said they expect a Chapter 7 involuntary bankruptcy filing to be made against the unit, Drexel Burnham Lambert Trading Corp. The unit "upstreamed" more than $600 million to its parent company before the parent faced a liquidity crisis and filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy law protection on Feb. 13.

The trading unit's creditors include foreign central banks as well as several international trading firms that made precious metals, petroleum or foreign-exchange transactions with the Drexel unit.

The biggest creditor appears to be Nissho Iwai Corp., a Japanese concern, owed about $160 million. Portugal's central bank is owed $95 million to $120 million, depending on the market price calculation of gold it lent the unit. Yugoslavia's central bank is owed about $70 million, Malaysia's about $43 million, and Poland's $10 million, say lawyers involved in the matter.

A Drexel spokesman said the trading unit, based in Fort Lee, N.J., is no longer doing business and has been "winding down" its positions since Feb. 13. He declined to comment on relations with creditors. The unit's chief asset, he said, is a $600 million or more claim against the parent company.

Richard F. Levy, a lawyer at the Chicago law firm of Kirkland & Ellis who is representing the National Bank of Yugoslavia, said the bankruptcy petition is signed by three creditors and will be filed today. "We don't have confidence that the current management of Drexel will prosecute this {$600 million} claim" on the trading unit's behalf, he said. Mr. Levy said his client asked the other creditors to sign the bankruptcy petition. He refused to name the others who signed, and wouldn't say why the Yugoslavian central bank is apparently angrier than other creditors.

Drexel has said that it expects all creditors to be repaid, though the spokesman wouldn't specifically say whether the trading unit's creditors will be paid.

Creditors of the unit suspect that, as the Drexel parent's access to commercial paper funding and other sources dried up, it turned to the unit for cash. "Our general impression is that they were helping to finance the parent," said Peter Reed, a vice president of CRT Energy L.P. in Chicago, which is owed about $1.9 million.

Drexel denies that. The spokesman said the trading unit regularly provided cash to the parent, as needed, and that such advances went up and down -- and that the $600 million level reached before Feb. 13 was by no means the highest the advances had ever been. "This was not operated as a vehicle to raise money for the parent company," he said.
