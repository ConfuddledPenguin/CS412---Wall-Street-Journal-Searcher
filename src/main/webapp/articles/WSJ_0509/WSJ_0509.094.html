Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Greyhound Ads Take a Detour And Avoid Mentioning the Strike

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 05/09/90

Ignoring the strike by bus drivers, Greyhound is rolling out a new radio campaign that it hopes will win back riders with feel-good messages about riding across the U.S. on a bus. "Oh beautiful for spacious skies . . . the road leads ever onward and it's calling out to me," a blues singer belts in one ad for the campaign, which was created by Bozell's Dallas office. The ads, which begin this week, feature a new Greyhound slogan: "Take a window seat on America, take a Greyhound cruise."

Oddly -- to some people at least -- the ads make nary a mention of the 10-week-old strike that has already resulted in one death and several injuries. "This could very well backfire," says Jeffrey B. Hirsch, president of Hirsch Marketing, a Venice, Calif., consulting concern. "People might think it's a joke to hear some cheerful song about riding along merrily on a Greyhound bus. They know in reality these buses are getting shot at."

Greyhound executives say they decided to ignore the strike in the campaign in large part because they didn't want to get stuck with an expensive campaign that they couldn't use if a settlement suddenly was reached. "We wanted something that we can run over a long period of time," says Ralph Borland, Greyhound's vice president of marketing.

Still, the campaign is a major departure for Greyhound, whose most recent ad efforts featured the tag line "Catch a Money Saver on Greyhound" and pitched almost exclusively its low fares. The new effort, Mr. Borland says, seeks to leverage some "long-term good will" that consumers have for Greyhound.

"We need to rebuild a positive image" by reminding folks that we're the ones that drove Americans to war, took the country to the World's Fair and brought Tom Cruise to New York in the movie "Cocktail," he says. "Although Greyhound awareness is very high, I'd be the first to admit that it isn't all positive," Mr. Borland says.

Mr. Hirsch says, however, that Greyhound can only rebuild its image by acting to reassure its potential riders that the problem has been dealt with, and that the company can be counted on to deliver a safe ride. "A business-as-usual message is a mistake, because it's not business as usual," he says.

But Jane Fitzgibbon, the senior vice president who heads up Ogilvy & Mather's TrendSights division, says Greyhound is taking the right road. "This thing has been going on for a long time. . . . I'm wondering if the strike is still top-of-mind for people" whom Greyhound is trying to reach, she says. Ms. Fitzgibbon says to bring it up again in ads would be stirring up bad images.

In fact, at a news conference earlier this week, Greyhound effectively declared the strike over. The company said it has hired enough of its own drivers to keep the highways full and contends calls from prospective passengers are pouring in.

Greyhound's Mr. Borland says it did run out a quick campaign at Easter that offered riders free tickets if their bus didn't arrive on schedule. But it opted to launch the major campaign, on which it will spend about $11 million, without bringing up the strike.

The new radio campaign includes ads that have been done in a number of musical styles, such as blues, gospel and soft rock. The blues and gospel versions are largely intended to reach the black market, which Mr. Borland says makes up a large share of Greyhound's riders.

Greyhound, a unit of GLI Holding Co., is trying to minimize the damage done to its ridership ranks by recruiting new riders. Mr. Borland says that with the new campaign, Greyhound is broadening its target audience to include people aged 18 to 55. Its previous target was 18 to 34. Toward that end, Greyhound says it plans to advertise on a number of CBS News programs, including some news shorts hosted by CBS personality Charles Kuralt, a favorite of older listeners.

AT&T: New ads for American Telephone & Telegraph warn consumers of its competitors' telemarketers that pitch long-distance services. The ads, which begin airing this week, depict competitors' phone representatives as hard-sell bullies that make wild promises and exaggerated price claims. The campaign, which has the theme "Put it in writing," was developed by Ayer, New York.
