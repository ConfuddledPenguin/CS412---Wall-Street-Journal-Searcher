What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/09/90

BUSH HAS DECIDED to seek a major deficit-cutting accord with Congress, even if it means some higher taxes, senior aides say. The president is said to remain opposed to any rise in income-tax rates. But aides hint Bush might accept increases in other taxes if he wins more control over congressional spending. Democrats agreed they have little choice but accept Bush's invitation to a so-called budget summit, though both Democrats and Republicans are already wrangling over taxes.

---

The Treasury's auction of three-year notes drew a record participation by individual investors, who were lured by the highest yields in a year. The success of the $10.5 billion sale sparked a late rally by stocks, boosting the Dow Jones industrials 11.94, to 2733.56. The dollar finished mixed.

Oil futures prices rose but then retreated from daily highs as threats of supply shortages subsided.

---

The thrift-bailout agency hopes to speed the sale of real estate amassed from ailing S&Ls by cutting prices on hard-to-sell properties by up to 20%. The agency also plans to sell about $300 million in properties through a televised auction this summer.

---

Financier Charles Knapp is said to be under federal investigation for his activities in trying to build his Trafalgar Holdings into a personal financial empire. Also, insurance regulators are said to be looking into Knapp's recent activities in that industry.

---

MasterCard and Visa abandoned plans for a joint nationwide "debit card" as part of an antitrust settlement. But the move is expected to prompt both firms to introduce their own versions of the card, which would function almost like cash.

---

The Treasury told Congress it will offer a plan this week to shift regulation of stock-index futures to the SEC, ending months of uncertainty. But the move is likely to reignite the debate over regulation of the products.

---

LIN Broadcasting posted a $180.8 million loss for the first quarter. Also, LIN definitively agreed to buy Metromedia's cellular assets in New York for $1.9 billion in cash and stock.

---

Lockheed decided to pull all its aircraft operations out of Burbank, Calif., reflecting the slowdown in Pentagon work and the need to cut costs.

---

TWA's Machinists union is working on a plan to wrest control of the carrier from Chairman Carl Icahn through an employee takeover. The effort is the latest twist in the conflict between Icahn and TWA's unions.

---

Ford won a partial victory in its battle with inventor Robert Kearns. A jury ruled the auto maker didn't willfully infringe on Kearns's patents for a windshield wiper system.

---

Chemical Banking's credit rating on long-term debt and preferred stock was lowered by Moody's, which cited the bank's profit outlook. Moody's also cut its rating on Fleet/Norstar Financial Group's senior debt.

---

The computer workstation is about to become cheaper and portable, continuing its penetration of the PC market. Two models will be unveiled soon by Sun Microsystems and Toshiba.

---

Time Warner's J. Richard Munro stepped down as co-chairman and cochief executive, as expected. The announcement came at a sometimes contentious annual meeting.
