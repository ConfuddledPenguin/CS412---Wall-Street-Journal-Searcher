OTC Focus: Stocks Hold Win Streak on Bullish News From Bond Market; Dress Barn Up 1/2

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 05/09/90

Buoyed by bullish news from the bond market, over-the-counter stocks managed to hold their own after dipping in intraday trading. The Nasdaq Composite Index edged up 0.61, a 0.14% gain, to close at 431.84, after slipping slightly most of the day. Volume increased to 133.8 million shares from 122.6 million. However, the margin between advancing and declining shares narrowed, 965 to 922, and more stocks hit new lows than new highs, 109 to 97.

Yesterday's successful quarterly auction of three-year Treasury notes supported OTC stocks, said Robert Padala, vice president of OTC trading at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette, New York. "We'll be going right on the heels of the bond market," Mr. Padala added, anticipating a stronger OTC market if the bond market rallies today.

Lance Zipper, head of OTC trading at Kidder Peabody, agreed that institutions seemed to gain confidence from the bond market and bought some traditional OTC leaders: MCI Communications gained 3/4 to 39; Apple Computer rose 1/4 to 41 3/4, and Sun Microsystems advanced 1/4 to 25 7/8.

Mr. Zipper added that he also saw interest in some regional airlines such as Comair Holdings, which rose 1 1/4 to 17 5/8, and Atlantic Southeast Air, up 1/8 to 17 5/8.

However, Walter Carucci, vice president of Carr Securities, which makes markets in OTC stocks for other dealers, said he continues to see a "tremendous" divergence between better-known issues and "the rest of the universe." Mr. Carucci said that split continues to keep him bearish about the market.

California automobile insurers, among them Mercury General of Los Angeles and 20th Century Industries of Woodland Hills, got a boost after this newspaper reported that a hearing officer's interpretation of California's insurance-overhaul initiative, Proposition 103, might deal a potentially devastating blow to proponents of automobile insurance reform.

Mercury General climbed 1/2 to 17 and 20th Century rose 2 to 22 5/8. The Nasdaq insurance index was among the day's leading indexes, gaining 0.63% to 484.35.

A potential takeover move triggered a 1 5/8 rise to 7 1/8 in shares of Horizons Industries, a Calhoun, Ga., carpet manufacturer. A group including Beaulieu of America Inc., a Dalton, Ga., yarn and carpet manufacturer, announced it holds a 13.3% stake in Horizon and might seek control.

Sweden's L.M. Ericsson Telephone Co. rose 4 7/8 to 165 7/8 after the international telecommunications company reported that pretax earnings for the first quarter ended March 31 nearly doubled. The company said it expects pretax earnings for the year to be up 25% over 1989.

Dress Barn was among the day's mostactive issues, closing up 1/2 at 10 on 1.1 million shares. The Stamford, Conn., apparel concern reported that sales for the third quarter ended April 28 rose 18% and estimated earnings in the quarter rose to 18 to 19 cents a share from 17 cents a year earlier. The rosy sales news prompted Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst Stacy Ruchlamer to repeat her "buy" rating on the stock, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Concord Camera jumped 1 1/8 to 3 7/8 on more than 10 times average volume. Michael Rea, chief financial officer of the Avenel, N.J., maker and distributor of cameras and related products, told DJPIR he had no explanation for the gain and declined to comment on the company's earnings outlook. Concord Camera is due to report earnings for the third quarter ended in March by May 15.

Calgene rose 1 5/8 to 9 after Piper Jaffray Hopwood analyst George Dahlman repeated his "buy" opinion on the stock, according to DJPIR. Calgene is a Davis, Calif., developer of genetically engineered agricultural products. Mr. Dahlman said he thought the shares had come under pressure because of some negative publicity by environmentalists and a lack of major announcements. The company is expected to report earnings for the second fiscal quarter by May 15, according to DJPIR.

Washington Bancorp rebounded 1 1/4 to close at 4 3/4 after Monday's 5 1/2-point dive. The holding company of National Bank of Washington, said Monday it's discontinuing its commercial paper program and isn't able to repay about $36.7 million of unsecured commercial paper obligations.

Jetborne International, which soared 7/8 Monday to close at 2 13/16, closed unchanged after the Miami overhauler and reseller of aircraft components said it's in preliminary talks with three organizations to buy part or all of the company. Jetborne said it also is considering spinning off one of its subsidiaries to the public.

Bancorp Hawaii tumbled 2 to 44 3/4 after the Honolulu bank filed a secondary offering of 1.5 million shares of common stock.

American Integrity dropped 1 3/8 to 6 3/8 after the Philadelphia insurance company reported operating income for the first quarter ended March 31 of six cents a share, down from 20 cents a year earlier. The company also hired an investment adviser to review strategic alternatives.

Pinkerton slipped 1/2 to 17 1/4 after the Van Nuys, Calif., security company said it expects to take a $2.6 million charge in the second quarter for early debt repayment. Pinkerton, which had an initial public offering April 4 at $14 a share, reported net income for the first quarter ended March 23 rose to 14 cents a share from 11 cents a year earlier.

Engineered Support Systems slipped 1/8 to 2 5/8 after the St. Louis maker of military ground support systems said delays in beginning production of several key programs will hurt revenue in fiscal 1990, ending Oct. 31. Some production and related revenue previously expected in 1990 won't appear until fiscal 1991, said the company's chairman and chief executive officer, Michael F. Shanahan Sr.

General Parametrics lost 5/8 to close at 2 3/8. On Monday, the Berkeley, Calif., maker of computer graphics products said it expects to report a net loss for the second quarter, ended April 30, the company's first quarterly loss since it became public in 1986.

Siliconix fell 1/2 to 1 13/16. On Monday, the Santa Clara, Calif., maker of semiconductors and circuits said it's in negotiations with International Rectifier to resolve four-year-old patent litigation. Last month, Siliconix filed for Chapter 11 protection under the federal Bankruptcy Code, one day after a federal District Court in Los Angeles ruled Siliconix had infringed five of International Rectifier's patents.
