CD Yields Show Mixed Changes For Latest Week

  • Author: Ellen E. Schultz
  • Date: 05/09/90

NEW YORK -- Yields on most small-denomination certificates of deposit at major banks rose slightly in the week ended yesterday, although average yields fell at major brokerage firms and on "jumbo" CDs. CDs in denominations of $90,000 and more and those sold by broker-dealers both tend to closely follow interest-rate trends. Rates generally turned down sharply Friday.

The average yield on a one-year consumer CD, which typically requires a minimum deposit of between $500 and $25,000, rose to 8.10% from 8.08%, according to a survey of 18 major banks by Banxquote Money Markets, an information service based here. Five-year consumer CDs went to 8.32% from 8.26%.

In contrast, Banxquote reported that the average yield on one-year CDs sold by major brokerage firms fell to 8.42% from 8.49% in the latest week, while the average on one-year jumbos fell to 8.25% from 8.33%. Yields on longer-term CDs fell the most. Average yields for five-year, broker-sold CDs fell to 8.78% from 8.93%. Average yields on five-year, large-denomination CDs slipped to 8.50% from 8.59%.

"Savings CDs went up because they were still lagging the market by about a week or two," said Norberto Mehl, chairman of Banxquote, noting that some banks raised savings CD rates while lowering yields on other types of CDs. New York's Citibank, for example, raised the yield on its two-year savings CD to 8.60% from 8.06%, but lowered the yield on one-year jumbo CDs to 8.11% from 8.42%. "This illustrates the sharp contrast in the two segments of the market," said Mr. Mehl.

Despite the recent decline in interest rates, it's still possible to find CDs that pay more than 9%. Continental Bank in Illinois offers a five-year savings CD with a yield of 9.06%, and a five-year jumbo CD at 9.16%, while Bank of America in California offers a five-year jumbo CD with a yield of 9.03%.
