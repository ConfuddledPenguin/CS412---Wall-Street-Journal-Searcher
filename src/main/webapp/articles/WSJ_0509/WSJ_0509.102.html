Who's News: Walton Leaves Morgan Grenfell U.S. Unit's Post

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 05/09/90

NEW YORK -- J. Richard Walton, president of Morgan Grenfell Capital Management Inc., the U.S. money management unit of Morgan Grenfell Group PLC, resigned yesterday along with four other people. Morgan Grenfell, a wholly owned unit of Deutsche Bank AG, named James E. Minnick, executive vice president in charge of marketing and sales, to replace Mr. Walton as president.

Mr. Walton said he was leaving because of his desire along with others to form "my own firm or something that's part of another firm where we own the majority of the equity in our unit." He said that within the last two months he had been approached "by five to 10 people" who either asked him to join them or who were willing to back a new venture.

Mr. Walton said that the other four people who resigned were all part of the unit he initially set up in December 1985. He said that some of them could join him in his next job.

Both Mr. Walton and Morgan Grenfell said that the departure was friendly. Nonetheless, Morgan Grenfell's London-based asset management operation has been wracked with difficulties, including the departure of some key portfolio managers, employee dissatisfaction with management and deteriorating performance.

Three weeks ago, Keith Percy, who resigned as chairman of Phillips & Drew Fund Management, was named the chief executive officer of Morgan Grenfell Asset Management PLC, which manages about $25 billion. Phillips & Drew is a unit of Union Bank of Switzerland.

Last November, Deutsche Bank paid about #900 million ($1.41 billion at the time) for the 95.1% of Morgan Grenfell Group PLC it didn't already own. The friendly takeover was the world's largest acquisition of an investment bank.

Mr. Walton said his resignation was unrelated to the Deutsche Bank acquisition. Still, investment bankers noted the big risks in purchasing a merchant bank like Morgan Grenfell where the assets are mostly people who can literally walk out the door. To avoid that, the West German bank reportedly offered a few key portfolio managers three-year contracts, according to money managers in London.

Under Mr. Walton, the U.S. Morgan Grenfell unit grew to where it currently manages $2.3 billion of clients' funds, investing in U.S. securities mostly for American institutional investors and a few international clients.

Mr. Walton "is an exceptional individual and has all the talents any organization would want," said Mario Gabelli, chairman and chief investment officer of the Gabelli Group Inc., a New York money management firm.
