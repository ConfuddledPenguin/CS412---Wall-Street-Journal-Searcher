Who's News: Time Warner's Munro Resigns Posts; Holder Discontent Expressed at Meeting

  • Author: Dennis Kneale
  • Date: 05/09/90

NEW YORK -- J. Richard Munro, presiding over the first and sometimes contentious annual shareholders meeting of the merged Time Warner Inc., stepped down as co-chairman and co-chief executive officer, as expected. Mr. Munro announced his move at the start of the long meeting in which many shareholders expressed support for Time Inc.'s debt-laden $14 billion acquisition of Warner Communications Inc., while a few others sternly criticized it.

The level of discontent -- stemming from Time Warner's $10.8 billion debt load, its more than $400 million in losses for the past two quarters, and a stock price that has plunged to half of its 52-week peak -- showed up in surprisingly large shareholder votes against measures involving executive stock plans.

Some 25% of the shares that were voted weighed in against the proposed 1989 stock incentive plan, which passed nonetheless with a 70% vote in its favor. In voting on changes to a restricted stock plan for outside directors, just over 21% of the ballots cast were against the plan, which won approval with 75% in favor.

"That's surprising. That's great," said Nell Minow, general counsel of Institutional Shareholder Services Inc., which advises institutional holders in proxy matters. "I haven't seen that level of opposition to a stock plan before. It shows shareholders are wising up."

In addition, 43% of the shares that were voted for the annual meeting were registered in favor of a proposal that Time Warner had urged holders to vote against. The proposal would have rescinded a "poison pill" plan that makes Time Warner a tougher takeover target or would have required shareholder approval of the plan.

"I think it's because of the bad break" in the stock price, said Steven J. Ross in explaining the 43% vote in favor of the poison-pill measure that Time Warner had opposed. Mr. Ross, the longtime chairman of Warner Communications, now reigns as the merged company's sole chairman and its co-chief executive officer.

But Ms. Minow of Institutional Shareholder Services said many institutional investors vote in favor of outside proposals for putting "poison pills" up for shareholder approval, regardless of the company involved.

Time Warner executives had come under fire for reaping millions in personal profits on the merger even as the price of Time Inc. stock, which was driven to as high as $182.75 by what Time contended was a bogus takeover offer by Paramount Communications Inc., fell to the $90-plus range.

But Mr. Ross blames the loss of more than $2.5 billion in the company's market value on the specter of re-regulation of local cable systems now gaining sway in Washington. Cable systems are Time Warner's "single biggest asset today," he said after the meeting. Once the cable industry makes its case against regulation, "I think that cloud will pass," Mr. Ross said.

Mr. Munro, 59 years old, becomes chairman of the board's executive committee and remains on the company payroll. He had joined Time Inc. in 1957, rising to publisher of Sports Illustrated and then moving through corporate posts to become chief executive in 1980 and chairman in 1986.

His co-chief executive title will be assumed today by Nicholas J. Nicholas, Mr. Munro's longtime No. 2 at Time Inc. and the president of the merged concern. Mr. Nicholas, age 50, remains president and will share chief executive duties with Mr. Ross until the latter steps aside in mid-1994.
