Read This Story and Pass It On To Five of Your Friends -- or Else

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 05/09/90

Most folks who receive chain letters toss them out, ignoring threats of bad luck for those who break the "chain." Not in Hollywood. In recent months, hundreds of hot-shot executives in Los Angeles have received copies of a chain letter, complete with stapled notes from scads of others who have already passed it along. "Do not keep this letter," it says, warning of bad luck for the recipient who doesn't send the letter on to five others.

The gimmick has worked. The chain "packet" has now jumped coasts and is making its way through media circles in New York. It has reached such big names as film producer Steve Tisch; Christopher Whittle, chairman of Whittle Communications; and Arthur O. Sulzberger Jr., deputy publisher of the New York Times.

"The timing was terrible," says Harris Katleman, president and chief executive officer of Fox Inc.'s TV-production unit, who received the letter late last year. "I would've chucked it, but the letter came just as we were getting our pilots ready for the fall {TV season of 1990}. Let's just say I'm superstitious."

Mr. Katleman sent the packet to a few pals, including Kim LeMasters, who shortly thereafter lost his job as entertainment chief at CBS. Another recipient, Edward Gradinger, who was chief of New World Entertainment, also lost his job. "Maybe they didn't respond," muses Mr. Katleman. (Mr. LeMasters declined to comment; Mr. Gradinger did send the letter on.)

In attached notes, executives have apologized for passing on the curse. "Sorry] But I've got my family to think of," wrote Eric Carlson, an agent at Creative Artists Agency in Beverly Hills, Calif. "I'm not superstitious] (But I feel a lot better playing it safe)," apologized Rob Kenneally, senior vice president at Fox Broadcasting. Amy Introcaso Davis, casting director at CBS Entertainment, wrote, "I hope my unborn child forgives me for this]"

Puzzled participants say they have no idea who started the chain. In any event, it has kept secretaries on both coasts busy; the packet -- which now runs about 50 pages -- has to be photocopied four times.

Ms. Davis, however, says she's getting a little tired of the chain, having received the packet three different times. "I sent the thing out twice, but I thought the third one itself was bad luck," she says. Still, "you sort of feel like you have to send it on."
