Technology & Health: Arkansas Campus To Receive Patent On Superconductor

  • Author: David Stipp
  • Date: 05/09/90

The University of Arkansas said it was notified by the U.S. Patent Office that it will be issued a patent for thallium-based superconductors discovered two years ago by scientists at the school. A spokesman for the school in Fayetteville said the patent is expected to broadly cover the thallium compounds, which show superconductivity at the highest temperature known for the phenomenon. Last year, International Business Machines Corp. received the first patent covering certain thallium superconductors.

The IBM patent surprised experts in the field, for the Arkansas scientists, Zhengzhi Sheng and Allen Hermann, were widely regarded as the discoverers of thallium-based superconductors. IBM's patent, however, covers only a narrow range of thallium superconductor compositions.

The expected broader Arkansas patent may set the stage for a patent dispute between the school and IBM. The school declined to comment on that possibility, and IBM executives weren't immediately available for comment. Mr. Hermann, who is now at the University of Colorado but stands to receive part of the proceeds from commercialization of the patent, said, "Now we have something to sell."

A different high-temperature superconductor compound based on the element yttrium earlier spawned a patent office "interference" proceeding between IBM and three other organizations claiming rights to the compound. Such disputes involve sizable stakes -- Du Pont Co. in 1988 bought rights to the University of Houston's patent applications on the yttrium compound for $4.5 million.

Controlling patent rights to the new compounds could someday be worth far more than that. Superconductors conduct electricity without resistance when cooled and promise breakthroughs in electronics, power generation and other technologies. Several of the new "high-temperature" superconductors, which work when cooled with easily obtained liquid nitrogen, have been discovered during the past four years. A version of the thallium superconductor works at minus 234 degrees Fahrenheit, which is the record for the phenomenon.
