REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Freer Markets or More Aid

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/09/90

The G-7 nations have agreed to boost IMF resources by 50%, to $180 billion, but Third World nations are grousing nonetheless. If the new credit is siphoned off to Eastern Europe, Zimbabwe's finance minister warns us to expect a "very explosive and dangerous situation . . . conditions likely to undermine international peace and security." Don't lose sleep over that threat. The peoples of the Third World seldom see the IMF money funneled to their governments and are not likely to take up arms because their oligarchies have less to salt away in Swiss bank accounts. But it is true that the IMF quota increase got a big boost from the changes in Eastern Europe. Western governments seem to feel that they should be doing something. In the absence of any concrete strategy, building up multilateral institutions is a natural response.

To be sure, the multilaterals talk a lot these days about their new focus on financing private entrepreneurship and promoting market economies. But it is virtually impossible for them to funnel credit into a country other than through government bureaucracies. Public finance and private initiative seldom make a good economic brew. The Housing and Urban Development scandal, among others, testifies to that. A U.N.-sponsored study by East European economists concluded last month that building market economies will depend not so much on credit as on creating new legal structures and market skills.

Some of the people who know state banking best are already pursuing this course. In January, Czechoslovakia moved to rejoin the IMF specifically asking for technical aid, not cash. In April, a blue-ribbon commission of leading Hungarians published the results of their future economic course. It stressed private investment, not loans from bodies such as the IMF.

The Hungarians in particular should know that the IMF can unwittingly set back the cause of reform. Hungary's high per-capita indebtedness is attributable in part to the generosity of the IMF and World Bank. Its Communists loved the Western largesse because it helped keep them in power during those many years that preceded their ultimate downfall. But today, a free Hungary wants its smartest people in the private sector, not in state bureaucracies.

We suspect that the fears of Third World oligarchs are unwarranted. Their money will continue to come through. The IMF was created at Bretton Woods to finance balance-of-payments adjustments under fixed exchange rates, but long ago turned to financing efforts to restructure the debts and economies of debtor nations. Today, at least some national leaders, such as Jamaica's Michael Manley, are promising to abandon socialism in return for new aid. But there remains the contradiction inherent in trying to stimulate private enterprise with public money.

If the industrial nations continue creating the credit, there will always be countries wanting to use it. Zimbabwe's threat of violence if the credit tap is shut off made that point quite clear this week. However, credit resources world-wide would be more effectively employed if more nations followed the lead of Czechoslovakia and Hungary and focused more on free markets and less on Western aid.
