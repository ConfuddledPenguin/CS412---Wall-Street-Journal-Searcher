Canadian Occidental Plans 35% Increase In Capital Outlays

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/09/90

TORONTO -- Canadian Occidental Petroleum Ltd. plans to increase its capital spending this year by about 35%, in part to expand its production of sodium chlorate, a compound that is increasingly being used by the pulp and paper industry as a more environmentally friendly substitute for chlorine in the bleaching process. Jerry Wright, Canadian Occidental's chief operating officer, said capital spending this year will rise to about 220 million Canadian dollars (US$189.2 million) from C$163.4 million in 1989. He said the company will need to increase its long-term debt by between C$20 million and C$30 million by the end of 1990 from C$258 million currently in order to finance the increased capital expenditures.

Calgary, Alberta-based Canadian Occidental, which is mainly involved in oil and gas, is 49%-owned by Occidental Petroleum Corp. of Los Angeles.

Mr. Wright, speaking after the company's annual meeting here, said capital spending this year will include about C$70 million to expand two existing sodium chlorate plants in western Canada and to add another near Edmonton, Alberta. He said he expects the company's production of sodium chlorate to rise to 210,000 metric tons by mid-1991 from 90,000 metric tons currently.

Sodium chlorate is used as an ingredient to make chlorine dioxide, an alternative to elemental chlorine for bleaching pulp and paper products.

Ronald Slinn, vice president, pulp materials, at the American Paper Institute in New York, said the industry has been under pressure from both the U.S. and Canadian governments to reduce its use of elemental chlorine, which has been linked with the formation of dioxin, a suspected carcinogen. He said pulp companies have been converting to chlorine dioxide at such a rapid rate that he doesn't have available firm statistics about its use.

Mr. Wright said Canadian Occidental's 1990 spending plans also include C$50 million for oil and gas exploration, up from C$30.7 million last year, as the company seeks to expand its foreign energy interests.
