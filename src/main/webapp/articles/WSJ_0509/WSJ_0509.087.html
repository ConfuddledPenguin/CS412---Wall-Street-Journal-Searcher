Marketing & Media: Grove Weidenfeld Taken Off Block; Bids Were Too Low

  • Author: Meg Cox
  • Date: 05/09/90

NEW YORK -- Unable to attract the price she wanted, Ann Getty said she has decided to take her publishing company, Grove Weidenfeld, off the block and continue running it as an independent company. Mrs. Getty, wife of oil millionaire Gordon Getty, is president and owner of the highbrow publishing company, which has been for sale since February. Patrick Peyton, a financial adviser to the Getty family, said that insufficient money wasn't the only reason for ending the sale.

Mr. Peyton, who will serve as interim president of the publisher until a new chief executive officer can be hired, said that Mrs. Getty also couldn't get potential buyers to promise to keep the Grove Weidenfeld staff intact and maintain the Grove "imprint" on books in the future.

Most of the major publishing houses in the U.S. and a number of foreign-owned companies took a look at Grove Weidenfeld, and at first, it was expected to fetch as much as $15 million. As the number of bidders narrowed to three in recent weeks, reports circulated that potential bidders were dismayed at the company's expensive contracts with authors working on future books, and the potential problems of transferring some old contracts to a new owner.

A spokesman for Penguin USA, a unit of London-based Pearson PLC, confirmed that Penguin was the final bidder until talks broke down this week. Penguin is believed to have bid somewhere between $7 million and $9 million. The chief executive of another publishing house that examined Grove Weidenfeld's books said he would have only been willing to bid "about $5 million or $6 million."

Grove Weidenfeld is a literary publisher that includes the 40-year-old Grove Press, publisher of Henry Miller, Samuel Beckett and others, and a newer house formed in 1985 by Mrs. Getty and British publisher Lord Weidenfeld. Although it has had losses in the past, Mr. Peyton said Grove Weidenfeld is making money so far this year and will publish about 70 titles in 1990, down from 140 in 1989.

"We believe we have a certain niche in the marketplace to publish small, literary books," said Mr. Peyton. "We won't go after the mega-deals and compete with the big boys like we did before."
