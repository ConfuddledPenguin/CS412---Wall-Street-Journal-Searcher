Abreast of the Market: Stocks Advance on Late Buying; Success of Treasury Sale Is Cited

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 05/09/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market preserved its winning streak, as a late burst of buying following a successful start of the Treasury's quarterly sale of notes and bonds pushed prices higher in quiet trading. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ran its string of gains to seven consecutive sessions, its longest uninterrupted rally since last July, by climbing 11.94 to 2733.56.

Among broader market measures, Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index rose 1.48 to 342.01, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 0.66 to 187.11 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 1.41 to 318.22.

For nearly the entire session, the industrial average remained stalled in a trading range of about 10 points as market players awaited the results of the initial phase of the Treasury's quarterly refunding, a $10.57 billion sale of three-year notes.

When the results were released about 10 minutes before the close, the market promptly broke out of that range with the help of some futures-related program buying and rallied to session highs.

The average yield of 8.74% on the Treasury's three-year issue was in line with market forecasts, and the note sale attracted a record $2.45 billion in noncompetitive offers -- a good sign for the remainder of the three-part refinancing.

The Treasury will sell $10 billion in 10-year notes today and wrap up the refunding tomorrow with the sale of $10 billion in 30-year bonds.

Advancing and declining issues were about even on the Big Board for most of the session, but gainers closed with a lead of 813 to 650. Volume totaled 144,230,000 shares, up from 132.8 million Monday.

Despite the market's late surge, traders said many players were taking a "wait-and-see" attitude toward stocks amid fears that interest rates might have to go higher for the Treasury to complete its refunding, especially the 30-year bond sale.

"It's just unbelievably dull," said Thomas Gallagher, managing director in charge of capital commitment at Oppenheimer & Co., and the next couple of sessions "are probably going to be just like this" as market players focus on the Treasury auctions.

With the market's latest advance, the industrial average surpassed a streak of six consecutive gains set last Oct. 2-9. The average rose for nine consecutive sessions last July 3-14, with broader measures matching that streak.

American General gained 1 1/2 to 50 1/2 on Big Board composite volume of 2.3 million shares amid rumors that Nippon Life Insurance and Chubb might make offers for the company, which has put itself up for sale. Nippon Life was said to be considering a $60-a-share bid, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report, while Chubb was said to have an offer of $62 a share under consideration.

An American General spokesman told the investor report he wasn't aware of any talks between the company and either rumored suitor. He declined further comment. A Chubb spokesman called the rumors of its interest "totally unfounded."

Federal National Mortgage Association rose 3/8 to 35 3/4 on 1.7 million shares. Chairman David O. Maxwell said the company's capital is "fully adequate to meet extraordinary adverse conditions, should they arise," and added that Fannie Mae plans to increase its capital by $2 billion to $2.5 billion by the end of 1991.

CalFed, which rose 1/8 to 17 3/8, topped the list of most active Big Board issues as a result of dividend-related trading that pushed its composite volume to 2.5 million shares. Shares of the thrift carry an 8% dividend yield and go ex-dividend today.

The savings-and-loan sector was among the market's best performers; Golden West Financial climbed 3/4 to 28, Great Western Financial rose 5/8 to 17 1/2 and HomeFed went up 1 to 29 5/8. H.F. Ahmanson, parent of the nation's largest thrift, rose 1/4 to 18; the company said its core operating earnings are continuing to increase.

Baxter International added 3/4 to 22 7/8 after projecting annual increases of 15% to 18% in operating income through 1993 on sales gains of 8% to 10%. Separately, its board approved the repurchase of as many as 13.5 million common shares, along with buy-backs involving two preferred issues.

Pan Am rose 1/8 to 2 3/4 as 1.3 million shares were traded. Chairman Thomas Plaskett told the annual meeting that the financially troubled company, which has been seeking a merger partner, has "talked to every other major airline."

Republic New York rose 1 3/4 to 45 1/4. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York cleared Edmond J. Safra to acquire as many as 1.5 million additional voting shares of the company. He already owns about 9.8 million shares, or a 29% stake.

Ethyl rose 2 1/8 to 27 7/8. The company said it would hold a news conference tomorrow to announce its plans to ask the Environmental Protection Agency for approval of a gasoline additive that it claims will cut automotive emissions, among other things.

Rowan rose 5/8 to 12 5/8. PaineWebber analyst James Carroll said that the company's meeting with analysts in New York was "very positive," and he reiterated an "attractive" rating on the stock.

Sotheby's Holdings fell 7/8 to 19 5/8 amid pessimism about the outcome of its auction of contemporary art. Christie's International, another major auction house, reported somewhat disappointing results from a contemporary-art sale Monday night in New York.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.25 to 347.58. Volume totaled 13,090,000 shares.

California Energy fell 2 to 9 3/4. Its partners in a geothermal-energy project in Southern California have filed a complaint with the American Arbitration Association, in which they allege the company overcharged them by $17 million between 1986 and 1989, the San Francisco Chronicle said.

Landmark Land retreated 1 7/8 to 10 3/8 after the Office of Thrift Supervision, which regulates the company's Oak Tree Savings Bank unit, objected to the proposed sale of much of its real estate and resort holdings for $967 million to developer Barry G. Hon.

ISS International Service System climbed 2 3/4 to 10 1/2. ISS A-S, the company's major shareholder, offered to buy the stock it doesn't own for $10 a share.

Catalina Lighting went up 1/2 to 9. The company reached an agreement with David Moss, its former chairman and chief executive, under which he won't compete with Catalina for two years and will serve as a consultant.

Hampton Industries dropped 3/8 to 7 1/2 after reporting a first-quarter loss of 13 cents a share, compared with net income of 41 cents a share a year ago.

Cenvill Development slipped 3/8 to 5 3/8. The company declared a 5% stock dividend in lieu of a cash payout.
