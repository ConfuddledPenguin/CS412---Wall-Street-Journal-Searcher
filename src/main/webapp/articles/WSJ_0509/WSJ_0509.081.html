TWA Machinists Union Works on Plan To Wrest Control of Carrier From Icahn

  • Author: Judith Valente
  • Date: 05/09/90

The Machinists union at Trans World Airlines Inc. is at work on a proposal to wrest control of the carrier from chairman Carl C. Icahn through an employee takeover, union officials said. Timothy Connolly, an international representative of the International Association of Machinists, said the union plans to meet this weekend with its financial adviser to discuss how such a transaction might be structured.

Brian M. Freeman, Machinists financial adviser, confirmed he is drafting an acquisition proposal, but wouldn't comment on its specifics. Mr. Freeman played a major role in putting together the proposed employee buy-out of UAL Corp., United Airlines' parent. Four years ago, he helped engineer a 10% ownership stake for TWA's pilots and machinists.

The machinists' efforts are the latest twist in the conflict between Mr. Icahn and TWA's unions. Mr. Icahn has been selling off aircraft and recently threatened to transfer TWA's domestic route network to America West Airlines unless the pilots union makes wage concessions. The unions, meanwhile, have approached a number of outside investors about taking over the airline.

Efforts toward an employee buy-out reflect the failure of either union to interest any buyers in TWA, which had a $300 million loss in 1989 and is carrying $2.8 billion in debt. The carrier had been profitable until Mr. Icahn took it private in 1988, saddling TWA with debt.

Mr. Icahn said TWA isn't for sale, although he has been selling assets. He added, however, that "if the Machinists want to talk, we're willing to listen."

This isn't the first time TWA's unions have floated the idea of an employee takeover. However, stepped-up threats from Mr. Icahn to sell assets and TWA's deteriorating finances have thrown the carrier into crisis, Mr. Connolly said. "We're going to have to do something in the next couple of weeks," he added.

Any effort at an employee acquisition would face several obstacles. So far, no major equity investor has come forward. It is also unlikely TWA employees would want to make the kind of major wage concessions that United employees agreed to in order to finance the UAL buy-out. That accord has been approved by UAL's board and the employees now have four months to obtain financing for the sale. TWA's mechanics haven't had a pay increase since 1983 and the pilots are already among the lowest paid in the industry.

Mr. Freeman conceded that any acquisition proposal would have to have the support of the pilots union, if not all of the TWA employee groups, to succeed. Pilots union leaders said they have held preliminary discussions with the Machinists union and plan to continue talks on employee ownership.

Mr. Icahn reportedly has sought as much as $1 billion for TWA, although pilot leaders said he offered the airline to its former president, Joseph Coor, two years ago for $450 million. TWA's most valuable assets are its vast European route network and Kansas City maintenance base.
