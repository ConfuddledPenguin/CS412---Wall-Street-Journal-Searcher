UPS to Acquire `Quiet' Engines Of Rolls-Royce

  • Author: Charles W. Stevens
  • Date: 05/09/90

United Parcel Service said it is replacing Pratt & Whitney engines on its older cargo jets with engines built by Rolls Royce PLC to bring the planes within U.S. noise limits. UPS, based in Greenwich, Conn., said the decision will cost it at least $400 million. It comes as a setback for Pratt & Whitney, a United Technologies Corp. division, which is trying to sell "hushkits" to owners of hundreds of planes powered by the company's older engines that exceed federal and more restrictive local noise standards.

By persuading fleet operators to install quieting equipment rather than new engines to meet noise rules, Pratt & Whitney hopes to preserve customers for its highly profitable spare parts business. But when air carriers opt to re-engine the planes instead of installing the quieting gear, Pratt & Whitney risks losing the business if the customer buys replacement engines from a competitor.

That's what happened in UPS's decision to install Rolls-Royce Tay 650 engines on the 35 Boeing Co. 727-100 planes in its fleet and some others it plans to acquire.

The sale represents a further disappointment for Pratt & Whitney because the re-engining will result in London-based Rolls-Royce replacing Pratt & Whitney as a primary engine-supplier to UPS. "It's our territory and we don't like to see that violated," a Pratt & Whitney executive in East Hartford, Conn., said.

UPS said the $400 million re-engine agreement includes payments to a third party to modify the three-engine planes for the Tay 650 and to get the reconfigured aircraft certified by the Federal Aviation Administration. The first of the modified planes will enter service in 1992, UPS said.

UPS and Rolls-Royce said their agreement also includes options for the re-engining of as many as 80 planes, should the carrier acquire more 727s. If all of the options are exercised, Rolls-Royce would receive about $600 million for 280 engines, including spare engines, the companies said.

UPS said it rejected buying quieting equipment for its 727-100s because the gear -- developed by Pratt & Whitney and Memphis-based Federal Express Corp., and marketed by Federal Express -- would only have brought the JT8D-7-engined planes just within the latest U.S. noise standard, called stage three. The Rolls-Royce engines, on the other hand, are just half as noisy as the altered Pratt & Whitney engines, making the planes compliant with the more restrictive rules set by some local airports. The new engines also burn less fuel, spew fewer pollutants, and extend the range and payload of the planes, UPS said. The new engines will also be cheaper to maintain, the company said. "Looking at all the alternatives, the Rolls-Royce choice was best for us," a UPS spokesman said.

Installing quieting gear would have cost UPS about $1.6 million per plane, a Federal Express spokesman said. The Tay 650 engines will cost the carrier about $6.6 million per plane, industry executives said.
