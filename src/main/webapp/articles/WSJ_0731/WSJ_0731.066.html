Abreast of the Market: Stocks End Mixed After Late Rally; Interest-Rate Outlook Brightens

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 07/31/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices closed mixed as the market's focus shifted from concerns about the economy to a brighter outlook for interest rates. The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which dropped more than 23 points in early trading in a continuation of a sell-off Friday, bounced back from the decline to finish with a gain of 18.82 at 2917.33.

Several waves of futures-related program buying, which traders attributed to a strong showing by the bond market, helped push stock prices higher in the last hour of trading.

"Everyone's looking at rates as a reason to be bullish," said Christopher Pedersen, head of trading at Twenty-First Securities, following Friday's report that gross national product rose at a slower-than-expected annual rate of 1.2% in the second quarter.

Utility stocks, among the most sensitive to rate expectations, were among the issues to set the pace for the market's turnaround. The Dow Jones Utilities Average equaled its sharpest rise since mid-May by gaining 3.70 to 207.05.

Investors also continued to show buying interest in oil stocks in the wake of OPEC's agreement last week to raise the target price for crude oil by $3 a barrel, to $21, and adopt a new production ceiling for the second half.

But the reversal didn't translate into a pickup in the pace of trading activity. Volume amounted to 146,470,000 shares on the New York Stock Exchange; on Friday, 149,700,000 changed hands as the industrial average fell 22.28 points.

Declining issues outnumbered advancers by 792 to 717 on the Big Board. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index rose 2.11 to 355.55, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index went up 0.86 to 194.18 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 1.57 to 329.49.

In late trading, Treasury notes and bonds showed gains of as much as 7/8 point, or $8.75 per $1,000 face amount. The increase in prices translates into lower yields on Treasury issues; for example, the yield on the benchmark 30-year bond fell to 8.40% from 8.48% late Friday.

Bond prices closed near session highs, and traders said that performance fueled buying in stock-index futures. As the futures contracts rallied to sharp premiums to the cash indexes, traders started selling the futures and buying baskets of stocks to lock in profits; at least three waves of index-arbitrage buying hit the market in the last hour of the session.

Solid gains in three oil stocks -- Exxon, Chevron and Texaco -- accounted for about half of the advance in the industrial average. Exxon added 1 1/2 to 50 3/4 in Big Board composite trading of 1.6 million shares, Chevron climbed 1 3/4 to 79 and Texaco rose 1 3/8 to 62 7/8.

Among other major oil issues, Mobil rose 5/8 to 65 1/8 on 1.6 million shares and Amoco added 1/2 to 54 3/4. In the oil field-services sector, Halliburton rose 7/8 to 53 1/4, Baker Hughes added 3/4 to 31 and Schlumberger went up 3/8 to 63 1/8.

But Ashland Oil, which dropped 1 1/8 to 33 5/8, was left out of the sector's advance. The decline came amid a block trade of 500,000 shares, equivalent to 0.9% of the company's common stock outstanding, which traded at 33.

Issues that paced the advance in the Dow utility average included Consolidated Edison of New York, which went up 1 to 24; Peoples Energy, up 1 to 22 3/4; Commonwealth Edison, up 1 to 31 3/4, and Pacific Gas & Electric, up 5/8 to 22 5/8.

Meanwhile, the Dow Jones Transportation Average fell 9.67 to 1125.00 as airline stocks were hit with selling, reflecting the impact of higher oil prices on jet-fuel costs and worries about the economy.

AMR, the parent company of American Airlines, dropped 7/8 to 55 3/4; Delta Air Lines fell 5/8 to 64 5/8, and USAir Group slipped 1/4 to 22. UAL tumbled 6 1/8 to 156 7/8; Chemical Banking withdrew as one of the five lead banks for the proposed $4.38 billion employee buy-out of the parent of United Airlines.

Entergy dropped 1/4 to 19 1/2 on 1.4 million shares in the wake of a board meeting Friday, at which the utility's directors voted to boost the size of a stock buy-back program. Some investors were disappointed that the board didn't approve a dividend increase, Salomon Brothers analyst Gregory B. Enholm said; however, he added that he is recommending the stock at $19 a share or less.

Shawmut National fell 5/8 to 9 3/8 in trading that included a block of 1.1 million shares, the equivalent of a 1.5% stake in the bank holding company. The block, which changed hands at 9, accounted for the bulk of the 1.4 million shares traded in composite dealings.

Limited Inc. rose 7/8 to 20 3/4 following news of its plans to develop a $300 million to $325 million shopping mall, to be anchored by a number of the company's own stores, on a site it owns in Columbus, Ohio.

Genentech added 3/8 to 28 1/8. A Food and Drug Adminstration advisory committee voted to recommend approval of the company's gamma interferon drug, used to treat a rare disorder in which children suffer recurring infections.

Broad Inc. went down 5/8 to 9 3/8. The company said fiscal 1990 net income may fall short of its target of $1.20 a share, based on "a modestly higher level" of investment losses than expected earlier in the year.

Equitable Real Estate Shopping Centers gained 5/8 to 6 7/8. The partnership, which owns shopping malls in the suburbs of Detroit and Minneapolis, said it is evaluating the possible sale of its assets.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.84 to 353.65. Volume totaled 10,590,000 shares.

California Energy added 3/8 to 9 3/4. The company said it has begun settlement discussions with Mission Power Engineering in a dispute over a contract for engineering and construction work on three geothermal power plants.

New Line Cinema dropped 3/4 to 12 following its second-quarter earnings report, which showed that revenue was more than triple the year-earlier level and net income soared.

Air Express International rose 3/4 to 18. The company reported that net for the quarter climbed 36% from a year ago.
