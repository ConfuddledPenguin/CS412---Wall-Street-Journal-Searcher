Economy: Bush, Congressional Leaders to Meet Again on Scheduling of Budget Talks

  • Author: David Wessel and Michel McQueen
  • Date: 07/31/90

WASHINGTON -- Still maneuvering over schedules rather than substance, President Bush and top congressional leaders are to meet again this morning to attempt to fashion a timetable for the deficit-reduction talks, the White House said. As Republican budget negotiators left a meeting with Mr. Bush yesterday, they complained that their Democratic counterparts were dragging their feet. "We've walked the extra mile on revenues," said Senate Republican Leader Robert Dole of Kansas. "We've laid out a specific plan and we're still waiting to hear from the Democrats."

Rep. Bill Frenzel (R., Minn) said he and some other Republican budget negotiators urged Mr. Bush to demand that Congress delay the start of its scheduled recess. But other GOP congressional leaders -- and some administration officials, as well -- counseled a less confrontational approach and are resigned to waiting until the fall to get down to hard bargaining. White House Chief of Staff John Sununu is already meeting with Republican strategists to develop a consistent public explanation for the plodding progress of the talks.

Democrats, though not refusing to put forward a plan, continue to question the wisdom of doing so before Congress begins its August recess. "It is not particularly useful to have plans publicly aired, and then not the subject of negotiations in an active, continuous basis for days or a couple of weeks," Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.) said.

Democrats are divided internally on both tactics and substance, but privately say they are prepared to agree to present a proposal on a specific date in late August or early September. If Republicans threaten to delay the congressional recess, then Democratic staff members expect Democratic leaders to endorse a plan -- crafted largely as a political statement of the party's priorities -- that relies on deep cuts in defense and on tax increases aimed at the rich.

As previously reported, the draft Republican deficit-reduction plan for fiscal 1991, which starts Oct. 1, would raise taxes on beer, wine and liquor; cap the deduction for state and local income taxes at $10,000; cut capital gains tax rates; trim spending on defense by $7 billion and trim spending on government benefit programs -- but not Social Security -- by about $16 billion.

Meanwhile, Sen. Daniel Moynihan is preparing to unveil a revision of his proposal to reduce Social Security payroll taxes. He wants to make it more attractive to his deficit-fearing colleagues when he offers it as an amendment to a must-pass bill to raise the federal debt ceiling this week or next.

Instead of slashing the payroll tax over two years, aides say the New York Democrat today will propose phasing in the tax cut by dropping the Social Security tax by 0.2 percentage point a year for five years beginning in fiscal 1991. Workers currently pay 6.2% of their earnings under $51,300 for Social Security; employers match that.

The new plan would cost the Treasury $6.6 billion in fiscal 1991 and $147.6 billion over five years, Sen. Moynihan figures. His original proposal to cut the tax rate to 6.06% in the current year and 5.1% in 1991 would have cost the Treasury $4.4 billion this fiscal year and $38 billion in fiscal 1991. Sen. Moynihan wouldn't alter the additional 1.45% payroll tax that goes to finance Medicare.

The House is scheduled today to consider raising the debt ceiling to $3.444 trillion from $3.122 trillion, sufficient to allow government borrowing through September 1991. Mr. Foley warned that the measure may fail the first time, but he has said that the House won't begin its recess until it lifts the debt ceiling.

The House Ways and Means Committee has approved an alternative, which isn't slated for consideration today, that would authorize the Treasury to keep borrowing only through Oct. 15.

The Treasury says it will default unless Congress lifts the debt ceiling by Aug. 15.

The congressional recess is scheduled to begin Friday, although Senate leaders say they may delay their departure. Mr. Bush is currently scheduled to begin a vacation in Maine on Aug. 10.
