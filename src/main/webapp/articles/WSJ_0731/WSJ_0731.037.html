Foreign Exchange: Dollar Plunges to Lowest Level Against the Mark in 31 Months

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 07/31/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar slumped yesterday to its lowest level against the mark in nearly 31 months, weighed down by rising interest rates overseas and signs of a slowing economy at home. Late in New York trading, the dollar stood at 1.6041 marks and 147.83 yen, down from 1.6120 marks and 149.15 yen late Friday. Meanwhile, the British pound rose to $1.8470 from $1.8375 on Friday.

At its nadir yesterday, the dollar traded at 1.6023 marks, its lowest level since Jan. 4, 1988, when the dollar briefly touched 1.5615 marks, its lowest level since the modern exchange rate-system was established after World War II.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the dollar was quoted at 147.65 yen; it closed in Tokyo Monday at 148.60 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6058 marks, down from Tokyo's Monday close of 1.6128 marks.

"We feel {the dollar} has got further to go down," said Joe Scott-Plummer, joint managing director at Martin Currie Ltd., an Edinburgh, Scotland, money manager.

Analysts said the U.S. currency is caught in a vise. Interest rates in Europe and Japan have been rising as major foreign economies -- especially those of West Germany and Japan -- are booming. At the same time, a softening U.S. economy is raising the prospect of lower U.S. interest rates.

"It seems we're heading into a recession or a moderate slowdown, which would imply lower U.S. interest rates," said Ian Spence, chief dealer at Manufacturers Hanover Trust Co. "In conjunction with that, it appears that Europe -- particularly Germany -- is on a roll with a booming economy and opportunity for investment."

Lower U.S. rates, combined with higher rates overseas, generally hurt the dollar by reducing its allure to international investors and speculators. The dollar is also burdened by big U.S. budget and trade deficits.

Although U.S. stocks and bonds rose yesterday, analysts and money managers warned that the weakening U.S. currency is damping foreign investors' appetites for dollar-denominated securities. Foreign investors have never held the same sway over the U.S. stock market as they have over the bond market, but Japanese and British investors at times do influence U.S. share prices.

Traders said there was scant buying of U.S. bonds by Japanese investors yesterday. This comes as other foreign investors are generally shunning U.S. markets in favor of either high-yielding fixed-income securities abroad or foreign stock markets.

In the first quarter, foreign investors dumped a net $3.3 billion of U.S. stocks and $900 million of American bonds, according to the Security Industry Association. The Japanese alone unloaded $700 million of U.S. shares and $3.9 billion of U.S. Treasury securities. Analysts say that evidence points to the trend continuing in the second quarter.

"All the Japanese money that had been heading into the U.S. is either heading for Europe or remaining at home in Japan," said Mr. Spence.

Yesterday, the dollar in intraday trading also tumbled to a 30-month low against the Swiss franc and nearly a 20-month low against the British pound.

For Americans, a weakening currency is a double-edged sword. It can help to lower the U.S. trade deficit by reducing the prices of U.S. exports while boosting the prices of foreign imports purchased by American consumers. At the same time, however, higher import prices contribute to U.S. inflation by allowing domestic companies that compete with foreigners to raise prices.

If the dollar fell far enough, it could also make it more expensive for the U.S. to finance its budget deficit. That's because foreign investors would demand higher yields on Treasury securities to compensate for the weakened dollar. Those higher rates, in turn, might damage U.S. economic growth.

While the dollar has been weakening for several weeks, traders and analysts say that the U.S. currency was hurt by last Friday's Commerce Department report that the U.S. economy grew at an anemic annual inflation-adjusted rate of 1.2% in the second quarter -- down from 1.7% in the first quarter.

Traders saw the report as evidence that U.S. interest rates will fall further and the dollar with them. By contrast, the West German and Japanese economies each zoomed at annual rates of 10.4% in the first quarter.

But it is the rising gap between U.S. and foreign interest rates that is the biggest factor driving down the dollar, analysts said. In the past year, yields on three-month Eurodollar deposits -- dollar deposits held offshore -- have fallen to 7.88% from 8.55%. Meanwhile, yields on equivalent mark deposits have jumped to 8.24% from 6.84%, and those on equivalent yen deposits have climbed to 7.69% from 5.32%.

"It's the expectation that U.S. rates will continue to go lower in the second half of the year, and we expect rates in Germany and Japan to rise," said Nigel Randell, an international economist at James Capel & Co. in London. "With that as a background, there is little reason to be buying the dollar aggressively."

There is an even greater gap between U.S. and British interest rates; threemonth Eurosterling rates currently stand at 15.08%, up from 13.75% a year ago.

"With something like a 15% yield, you can't go wrong" with sterling, said George Kuster, a portfolio manager at Gulf & Occidental Investments S.A. in Geneva, who is also buying French bonds, Swiss franc bonds issued by foreign corporations and German time deposits. Mr. Kuster said that only 3% to 5% of the portfolios he manages are in dollar-denominated investments.

Japan's money supply is expanding at a rapid annual rate of 12.6%. And many analysts predict that West German inflation will grow along with the increased expenditures associated with the monetary union between East and West Germany.

Mr. Randell predicts that Japan will raise its official discount rate -- which the central bank charges on loans to financial institutions -- by 1/2 or 3/4 percentage point from its current 5.25% level "over the next couple of months." He also forecasts that after German rates remain stable for a few months, West Germany will push them higher in October.

Higher rates abroad would mean more pressure on the dollar and could even prompt the Federal Reserve to prop up the dollar by raising U.S. rates.

The weakening dollar has made Martin Currie, the Edinburgh money-management company, pessimistic about buying U.S. stocks. For its non-dollar based investors, the company's U.S. stock holdings currently represent about 80% to 85% of what they would be if the firm's portfolios were weighted to reflect the value of the U.S. market relative to the rest of the world. That's down from roughly a 135% weighting six months ago. The capitalization of the U.S. stock market is about 34% of the world's market, according to Morgan Stanley Capital International.

Said Mr. Scott-Plummer of Martin Currie: "While we aren't bearish on the U.S. stock market, we feel any returns {there} could be eliminated by the weakness of the U.S. dollar."

Roberto Bianchi, a first vice president at Union Bank of Switzerland in Zurich, noted that investors who were selling dollars yesterday were buying mostly high-yielding currencies such as the pound, Spanish peseta and Italian lira. He predicted that in the next week or two, the dollar could tumble another 2% to 3% against the mark, bring it to its record lows against the German currency.

And the Martin Currie company sees the dollar falling an additional 7% against the yen to 135 yen over the next five to six months.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for August delivery settled at $367.80 an ounce, down 20 cents. Estimated volume was a heavy five million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $368.55 an ounce.
