International: Swiss Ban on Laundering Money to Go Into Effect

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/31/90

ZURICH, Switzerland -- Legislation outlawing money laundering will go into effect in Switzerland tomorrow, and offenders can face penalties of as much as five years in prison and one million Swiss francs ($734,200). Switzerland, one of the world's major financial centers, has been vulnerable to misuse by organized crime attracted by the country's bank secrecy laws. But after a billion-dollar money-laundering scandal involving drug money in 1988 -- the so-called Lebanon Connection -- the government pushed for the legislation aimed at stemming the flow of such money into the nation.

The new law covers all professionals, from bankers and portfolio managers to accountants and lawyers, who handle money for others.

An addition to the penal code also takes effect tomorrow, obliging those who accept, look after, invest or transfer funds for others to know the identity of the funds' owner. Offenders can be punished by prison terms as long as one year. Until now, the need to identify customers fully has been part of a gentleman's agreement among banks to assure that funds they accepted weren't derived from criminal activities. But this had no firm basis in law.

The Swiss Banking Commission said the legislation casts doubt on the practices of lawyers representing clients whose names aren't disclosed to banks. Until now, lawyers had only to sign a statement declaring that they were acting in their professional capacity and not as investment fronts, and that the funds weren't obtained illegally.
