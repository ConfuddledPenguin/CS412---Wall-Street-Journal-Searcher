Confidence of Consumers Remained Steady in July

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/31/90

NEW YORK -- Consumer confidence held fairly steady in July after a significant loss in June, the Conference Board said. The board's index, which measures consumer attitudes about the economy, based on a survey of 5,000 households, was down about half a point in July to 101.8, from a revised 102.4 in June. The index was 120.4 a year ago. The survey uses 1985 as a base of 100.

The monthly survey, conducted for the Conference Board by National Family Opinion Inc. of Toledo, Ohio, found consumers a bit more positive about current conditions but less positive in their expectations. Slightly more respondents than last month say jobs are "plentiful," 25.6%, up from 23.4%. But more people expect "fewer" jobs will be available in the next six months, nearly 18%, up from 16.5%.

Fabian Linden, executive director of the Conference Board's Consumer Research Center, said consumer confidence declines since the beginning of the year are "markedly different in various regions of the country." In the past three months, compared with a year earlier, New England's index is lowest, followed by the Middle Atlantic states. The Pacific states, while falling below the year-ago index, remain at a level above the rest of the nation.

Car buying plans in July, slightly below June's, are stronger than last year by one percentage point. Home buying plans perked up compared with June and with a year ago. Major appliance buying plans softened moderately.

The Conference Board is a nonprofit, business-research organization.
