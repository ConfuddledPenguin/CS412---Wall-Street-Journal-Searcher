Wang Reports Widened Loss For 4th Period --- Charges and Write-Downs Totaled $496.7 Million; Revenue Declined 13%

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 07/31/90

Wang Laboratories Inc. took $417.4 million in one-time write-downs and restructuring charges, resulting in a widened net loss of $496.7 million, or $3.01 a share, for the fourth quarter ended June 30. The Lowell, Mass.-based maker of minicomputers and office automation equipment said revenue continued to decline, dropping 13% to $652.2 million from $750.9 million.

Despite the loss, Richard W. Miller, chairman and chief executive officer, was upbeat. He said that without the one-time charges Wang would have had a loss on continuing operations of $5.6 million, which he characterized as "near break-even." He added, "We achieved our fourth-quarter goal of bringing our operating revenues and operating expenses into balance."

Mr. Miller, who was brought in as president last summer in the midst of a financial crisis, predicted that in fiscal 1991, Wang will achieve its first profitable year since fiscal 1988. He said he doesn't expect any further write-downs or restructuring charges.

Stock analysts, while praising Wang's cost-cutting moves, remained skeptical about Wang's future. "The underlying issue remains whether and when they can get revenue to expand rather than contract," said Marc Schulman, an analyst with UBS Securities Inc.

In American Stock Exchange composite trading, Wang Class B stock rose 25 cents to $4.125.

In the year-earlier fourth quarter, Wang had a net loss of $374.7 million, or $2.29 a share. That loss included one-time charges of $234 million and an operating loss of $61.1 million.

Mr. Miller said Wang took the latest round of write-offs to cover declines in value "in the whole infrastructure rather than having this plague us as we go forward."

Some of the write-offs involve inventories of unneeded parts, inventories of older Wang computers, service depot inventories that aren't needed because new computers are more reliable then older ones and sales and service facilities that have been consolidated or have declined in value. He said Wang has closed an operation that previously rebuilt and resold Wang computers that were traded in by customers.

Mr. Miller said in the current quarter Wang will reduce its staff by more than 1,000 people to about 19,500. Wang currently has 20,900 employees, down 27% from a year earlier. In Lowell, it has cut employment 34% to 6,085 from 9,174 a year ago.

Mr. Miller said that after the write-downs "we've done everything we wanted to do. Revenue and expense are in line." He noted that Wang has paid off all but $30 million of the $575 million in bank debt that caused the cash crisis last year. Despite the charges, Wang has $165 million in cash and is generating cash from operations, he said.

Mr. Miller declined to predict results for the current quarter. Jay P. Stevens, an analyst with Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., said "the tone for the whole industry is to be careful about (predicting) the summer quarter." He said he thinks Wang may earn 25 cents a share for the full fiscal year.

For fiscal 1990, Wang reported a net loss of $715.9 million, or $4.35 a share, compared with a net loss of $424.3 million, or $2.59 a share. The latest year includes restructuring and other special charges of $388.3 million and losses from discontinued operations of $87.3 million. The year-earlier period included restructuring and special charges of $142 million and losses from discontinued operations of $100.1 million.

Revenue for the year fell 14% to $2.5 billion from $2.91 billion.
