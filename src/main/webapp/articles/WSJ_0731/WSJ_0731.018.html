Letters to the Editor: Soccer Snobbery Could Hurt Business

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/31/90

The Journal certainly jumped on the soccer-bashing bandwagon in David Brooks's "World Cup: Hooligans and Mad Cows" (Leisure & Arts, June 19). No, I'm not an English hooligan or a West German partial to funny hats. Born in the U.S., I began playing soccer at age 12 and haven't stopped. I still play on an over-30 team.

I think the Journal, and the American media in general, misses an important point when it comes to soccer. Just as golf has evolved as a prized means of doing business in corporate America, a knowledge and understanding of and affinity for soccer among American business people could be critical to our economic well-being in the emerging global economy.

If that sounds like balderdash, consider some of the asinine reasons the media give for soccer's flagging success in America: low-scoring, no hand-eye coordination required, hooligans, mad cows. Enough already.

The whole world plays the game. America is part of the world, even though some closet jingoists continue to deny the fact.

It's time to get off our myopic high horse. We may not know much about soccer, but then again, ask some Italian, German or Cameroonian who won last year's Super Bowl.

Jack Bell

Patterson, N.J.
