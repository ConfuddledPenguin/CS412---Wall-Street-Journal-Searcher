Economy: U.S. Consumer Spending Rose 1% Last Month --- June Gain, Following Weak May, April Results, Was Strongest Since January

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 07/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The vast consumer sector of the economy mustered some energy in June after languishing for the previous few months, the latest Commerce Department figures indicate. Americans' personal spending rose a strong 1% in June after remaining flat in May and edging up 0.2% in April, the department reported. After adjusting for inflation, consumer spending grew 0.5% after declining 0.3% in May and inching up 0.1% in April.

The hearty June gain, the biggest since January, was welcome news. Just last Friday, the Commerce Department had reported that in the second quarter, consumer spending -- which accounts for two-thirds of the nation's economic activity -- actually declined, along with business investment, construction and exports.

"Consumer spending is sluggish, but what these numbers confirm is it's not sluggish to the extent of being recessionary," said Anthony Vignola, an economist and managing director at Kidder Peabody in New York, after the new spending figures were released. "The second quarter ended on a much stronger note than it began."

But Americans' personal income continued to grow only modestly, the report said, edging up 0.4% in June after rising 0.3% in both May and April. Wages and salaries, the bulk of most people's income, grew 0.5% following a 0.4% increase in May.

Slow growth in job markets throughout much of the nation suggests that gains in income could continue to be mild for a while. And that means spending isn't likely to gather much more vigor.

Last month, however, a key sector of consumer spending -- purchases of big-ticket items known as durable goods -- grew for the first time since January. Analysts attributed much of the improvement to a jump in car sales in late June, but few expect substantial increases in auto purchases in the near future.

Purchases of nondurable goods -- items including food and clothes, whose sales are less likely to be affected by economic trends -- also increased.

With spending outstripping income growth last month, Americans' savings rate fell to 4.9% of disposable income from 5.4% in May.

Low savings has been a nagging problem for the nation for years, leading to a scarcity of funds to finance private business investment and the federal budget deficit. That has forced the government and industry to seek capital overseas.

Overall, personal income equaled a $4.64 trillion annual rate in June, while spending came to a $3.64 trillion annual rate.

All the numbers were adjusted for regular seasonal fluctuations.
