Circle K Posts $747.4 Million 4th-Period Loss

  • Author: Jim Herron Zamora
  • Date: 07/31/90

Troubled Circle K Corp., the nation's second-largest convenience store chain, which is operating under Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection, reported a net loss of $747.4 million, or $17.19 a share, for the fiscal fourth quarter ended April 30. The loss included a reorganization and restructuring charge of $639.3 million, the Phoenix, Ariz.-based company said. In the year-ago fourth quarter, Circle K had a net loss of $23.1 million, or 57 cents a share. Revenue declined 2.6% to $907.9 million from $932.1 million.

For the year, Circle K reported a net loss of $772.9 million, or $17.91 a share, compared with earnings of $15.4 million, or 21 cents a share, for the preceding fiscal year. Revenue rose 6.9% to $3.74 billion from $3.49 billion.

Circle K took the fourth-quarter reorganization and restructuring charge as a result of its financial condition, reorganization efforts and Chapter 11 filing, the company said. Stockholders equity as of April 30 was a negative $451.6 million.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Circle K shares closed unchanged at $1.125.

Circle K got into its present situation by expanding too fast -- from 1,300 stores in 1983 to about 4,650 at the end of last year -- and taking on $1.1 billion in below-investment grade junk debt to buy and open new stores. The company's new acquisitions weren't producing nearly enough operating profit to offset the debt burden they imposed. During this expansion and acquisition phase, Circle K also drove customers away by raising prices above the competition and replacing standard inventory with high-profit margin products customers didn't want.

The company also found itself unable to compete with companies such as Los Angeles-based Atlantic Richfield Co., which could market its gasoline less expensively than Circle K, thus attracting customers to its AM-PM minimarkets. Nor could it expand to beyond its traditional customer base of blue-collar men between the ages of 18 to 35 years old to include more white-collar workers and women.

Circle K filed for reorganization May 15 under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code after failing in a last ditch effort to win lender approval for a plan to reschedule debt and reorganize the company. The filing followed the May resignation of Circle K's widely criticized chairman, Karl Eller, who oversaw the rapid expansion.

Circle K and its affiliated subsidiaries in the bankruptcy-law proceedings plan to file a list of all liabilities and assets on or about Aug. 28, and the company said it expects to file a reorganization plan "as soon as practicable."

As of April 30, Circle K operated 4,631 convenience stores in 32 states and had about 1,298 stores under license or joint venture agreements in 14 foreign countries.
