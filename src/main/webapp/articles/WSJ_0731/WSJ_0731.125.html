Economy: Thrift Bailout to Require $100 Billion In Next 12 Months, Bush Officials Say

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 07/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration's savings-and-loan cleanup, which is running as low on political support as it is on cash, will require about $100 billion in the next 12 months, officials said. Even Republicans balked over the short-term costs disclosed by administration officials before the House Banking Committee. The new spending plan, which includes $60 billion in "temporary" outlays that the administration hopes to recoup through sales of S&L assets, won't increase overall cost projections, but it requires more cash sooner than the lawmakers had been told to expect.

"The taxpayers of my state are paying too much money for this cleanup already," said Rep. Chalmers Wylie of Ohio, the committee's ranking Republican. Added Rep. Thomas Ridge (R., Pa.,), "Is this the last moving target we are going to try to hit?"

Administration officials, who have been looking at ways to change the S&L bailout law to lower costs, held out other alarming barometers of the S&L cleanup. The real estate slump has spread across the nation, adding to losses, they said. Junk-bond losses from S&Ls seized by the government will total more than $1 billion. "That would certainly be a minimum," said William Seidman, chairman of the Resolution Trust Corp., the bailout agency.

The administration has so far depended on its budget summit with Congress to consider the S&L funding along with other fiscal issues. That hardly sat well with Banking Committee members. "We just don't want to get blind-sided," said Chairman Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas).

But if the budget summit doesn't produce an agreement, the costs of the S&L bailout will be pushed higher, said Robert Glauber, undersecretary of the Treasury. He predicted that interest rates will rise if the budget summit fails. "These institutions are extremely vulnerable to interest rates," he added.

Most recently, the Bush administration has estimated that it will cost about $130 billion to protect depositors at sick S&Ls; it has spent about one-third of that so far.

But that doesn't include two complicating factors that regularly anger Congress -- interest on the spending and the temporary cash required to hold S&L investments until they are sold. Those items, barely discussed when the law was passed last year, will probably quadruple the costs to exceed $500 billion in 10 years.

"Now we are looking at reality," said Rep. Bruce Vento, (D. Minn.), "and see an S&L bailout of Grand Canyon-sized proportions."

The cleanup could squeak through to the end of January 1991 if Congress committed as little as $5 billion now and the administration took advantage of a "literal reading" of an error in the bailout law's language, which would free up $18 billion more, Mr. Glauber said. Thus, Congress could avoid committing a more explosive chunk of cash before the fall election. While Mr. Glauber raised the idea, he didn't endorse it.

Without new funding, the S&L cleanup will grind to a halt, probably in the October-December quarter. But the Treasury already has reached some cleanup limits. It eliminated plans to replace high-cost brokered deposits at the sick S&Ls with low-cost Treasury funds. That pushes up interest rates that all banks and thrifts pay. "We believe that this is a very high and unwise cost to pay," warned Mr. Seidman.

The White House, meanwhile, is steeling itself for more election-year criticism for the thrift cleanup, and refused a request by the National Governors Association to create a bipartisan commission to investigate the causes of the S&L mess. "We feel that it's not time to point fingers now, but it's time to get the money that's owed to the depositors back in their hands," a White House spokesman said.

But both the administration and Congress are taking a hard look at how to overhaul -- for the second time in two years -- thrift and bank regulation. Among the proposals currently in Congress are those that would expose personal income tax records of directors of failed S&Ls, freeze lucrative compensation packages for directors at failed thrifts, and allow banks and thrifts to extend their branch networks nationwide.

The administration is examining ways to reduce costs for the cleanup, too. "We have asked people to start all over again and see if there are changes in direction that could change costs," said Mr. Seidman.

That includes a pilot program to sell nine sick thrifts, not yet seized by the government, to private investors in coming weeks. Officials believe the thrifts would attract higher bids, because their value wouldn't have been tarnished by government seizure.
