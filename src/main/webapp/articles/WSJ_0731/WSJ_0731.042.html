Roger Smith Reflects on Role at GM

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/31/90

Roger B. Smith retires today after leading General Motors Corp. for nearly a decade -- certainly the period of greatest change in the company's modern history. Mr. Smith completely reorganized GM in 1984, shelled out billions of dollars to modernize factories and spent billions more on high-tech and defense acquisitions. He started a joint venture to build cars with Toyota Motor Corp. and created Saturn Corp., a separate subsidiary, to develop new ways of making and selling cars. To his backers, all this indicates Mr. Smith's vision, and it has made him the most visible GM chairman since the legendary Alfred P. Sloan Jr., who ran the company between 1937 and 1956.

But Mr. Smith, who became chairman on Jan. 1, 1981, has also been GM's most controversial chairman ever. He clashed with dissident director H. Ross Perot and became the subject of three critical books and a scathing movie.

Mr. Smith's detractors say his vision was no substitute for focusing on the basics of the car business: building exciting, high-quality vehicles and making customers happy. GM lost nearly 10 points of U.S. market share during Mr. Smith's tenure, mainly because of look-alike cars and poor quality in the mid-1980s. The share loss saddled GM with so much extra plant capacity that its huge North American auto business barely breaks even.

Mr. Smith spent 41 years at GM. His official duties took him around the world. But he never found the time, he says, to visit the Tower of London or stroll through the Louvre. Mr. Smith recently discussed his record with Paul Ingrassia and Joseph B. White of the Journal's Detroit staff. Here are excerpts from the interview:

Q: If you gave yourself a letter grade for your performance as chairman, what would it be?

A: I don't know that there's any single grade. We went from a $763 million loss {in 1980} to making more money than anyone in the world except Royal Dutch Shell. That probably isn't too bad. A lot of the grades have to be incomplete. What kind of grade do you give Saturn? On mergers and acquisitions, you've almost got to have an A-plus there, when you look at $2.5 billion for EDS {Electronic Data Systems Corp.} that's now worth $10 billion. Preparedness for the 21st century should get a pretty good grade. . . . Overseas {performance} should get a good grade.

Market share, obviously, we're not happy with that. I had a guy {who} wrote me a letter the other day. He said, "If you ask Jack Nicklaus the best round of golf he ever had on the easiest golf course, and he told you it was 60, but yesterday he shot an 82 on St. Andrews, would you say he lost 22 strokes off his game?" The market share we had in the late '70s and 1980s was on a course that was a lot easier to shoot than the one we're on today.

Q: What's your proudest accomplishment?

A: Getting the corporation ready for the 21st century is what I feel the best about. I'm convinced the automobile industry has yet to catch up with Hughes technology. {GM bought Hughes Aircraft Co. for about $5 billion in 1985.} And when the auto industry catches up, you'll see us kick in the afterburners.

Q: What's your biggest disappointment?

A: The thing we didn't do right was the communication on the restructuring. {The move in 1984 eliminated two huge GM operations -- Fisher Body and the Assembly Division -- and reorganized GM's car divisions under two umbrella groups.} We got a little misled, overconfident, that this thing was going to go down better than it did.

Q: What, in hindsight, could you have done to avoid the drop in market share? Why didn't GM act sooner to fix the look-alike car problem and the quality problems that hurt market share?

A: To get the look-alike thing {fixed}, you can't come with paper and paste. You have to come with new models. We did get locked into basic body designs. If you and I, with all the resources in the world, said, "Hey, we want a new body so they don't look alike and have higher quality," it takes three to five years. And in those days it took even longer. We had to re-equip our plants. We took the long-term approach not to go in there with Band-Aids.

Q: Did you suspect from the start that the relationship with Mr. Perot wouldn't work out? {GM bought EDS in 1984. Mr. Perot left the company he founded and GM's board in 1986 as part of a $700 million buy-out.}

A: The board discussed that. One director said in his own company, with something like 20 acquisitions, he'd only saved one CEO. We thought we could do it. A lot of people did a lot of surface reporting {on the Perot controversy}. That stock was $33; now it's $140 {adjusted for splits}. That's not too bad.

Q: Early in your tenure, the company focused on technology. Recently, there's been more emphasis on people. Does that reflect an evolution in your own thinking?

A: People said you guys made a horrible mistake, you overshot on technology. That's not a mistake, that's a deliberate thing. If you keep pulling string out of the box and stop, you never know how much string is in the box, right? We've taken technology out of some places that we put it in. But I would tell you if we hadn't put it in, we'd never know we should take it out.

Q: What's the strategic importance of Saturn?

A: We wanted to find out if we could build a car of that size and that quality in this country at a competitive cost and sell it at a profit. No U.S. manufacturing company has done that yet. Using our regular designs, our regular production techniques, you can't do that. So we said: "Have a go at it on a clean sheet." You've got to see Saturn when it's running. That plant is so different from our normal plants.

Q: You've been the subject of books, of a movie, and a lot of critical news coverage. How did that affect you personally, and how did you deal with it?

A: Fair criticism I never minded. It's difficult when you read in a book that you foam at the mouth and throw paper on the floor. The only time I foam at the mouth is when I brush my teeth. I've never thrown a paper on the floor in my life.

Q: Do you put the criticism out of your mind?

A: You like to say that you do that. I don't know that that's true. {What} almost bothers me as much is what I call surface reporting. If someone had looked just one more little bit into GM's actions to try and understand the long-range consequences of what we were doing, then people would have gotten a better story.

Q: Why did you decide to make higher pensions retroactive to executives like yourself, who are retiring now, if the stated goal was to attract and retain management talent?

A: They weren't retroactive. It was over a year ago, last June or July, that the incentive compensation committee {of GM's board}, who'd been tracking compensation programs, told management our pensions were pretty bad. By October 1989, the board acted to make the change {subject to shareholder approval}. They made it effective the end of that month. Seventy-nine percent of companies had better pensions than we did. Even today, 61% of companies have better pensions.

Q: How do you think you'll be regarded by history?

A: I'm more concerned about what I've been able to do for the corporation and the stockholders. I was never out to make my mark in history. I wanted to do it for the company.

Q: What does U.S. industry overall, not just the auto industry, need to do to become more competitive?

A: Manufacturing technology is really undergoing tremendous change. . . . The whole approach to design for manufacturability is undergoing a big revolution. . . . You've got to take out levels of management. You've got to get decision-making pushed down in the organization to where the action is.

Q: The Japanese got 28% of the car market during the first half of the year, which was a record. Is it going to go higher?

A: One thing that's happening is U.S. quality is coming up. I'm certainly never going to tell you we're going to drive the Japanese from the shores. Customers get more from the competition, and in being competitive everybody has to do better. I like it.

Q: Japanese companies have built eight plants here. Why don't you go over and build one in Japan?

A: You can't do that. We have a piece of Isuzu and a piece of Suzuki. But that really is a closed market over there. . . . When they came out of World War II, they closed that market up tighter than a drum. It's only in recent days that you see any opening.

Q: You think the Japanese don't play fair?

A: They've got an enviable position. They're able to get along in world trade without giving people access to their home markets.

Q: Their answer is that BMW is doing fine in Japan. {BMW is the leading import luxury make and sold 33,076 cars in 1989, giving it a market share of 0.8%.}

A: What's doing fine? In a market of that size, I call 38% doing fine, or 28%.

Q: What are your retirement plans?

A: I don't believe I'm the type of person who can slam on the brakes and run for the rocking chair. I want to do a lot of things I owe my wife and family, and then I want to do some real estate development. I want to do something, be active, then complete it and then not have to start another one for two to three months if I don't want to.

Q: What will you do for play?

A: Hunting, fishing, traveling. You'd be surprised, with all the places I've been, how few things I've really seen that people go to see. {There are} lots of things in this country I haven't seen. Lots of great rivers I haven't fished. . . . I wish I were 20 years younger, in some respects. There are so many terrific things happening. {But} it wouldn't be to spend 20 more years as chairman, I'll tell you that. If I were 20 years younger, I'd want to start down the ladder.
