OTC Focus: Market Ends Lower on Profit-Taking; Nasdaq 100 Index Falls 3.43 to 447.84

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 07/31/90

Over-the-counter stocks continued their slide as profit-taking in blue chip and technology issues joined forces with ennui to push the OTC market to its lowest level in three months. The Nasdaq Composite Index fell 3.21 to 439.38, or 0.7%, after trading as low as 473.05 before it began to recover as exchange-listed shares rose. Declining stocks swamped advancers, 1,216 to 732, and volume eased to 120.4 million shares from 121.8 million Friday.

"There's a total lackadaisical attitude toward the secondary stocks," said William Sulya, director of OTC trading at A.G. Edwards & Sons, St. Louis. "There just isn't any support for them at this point."

Mr. Sulya was among those who attributed sharp losses in several Nasdaq indexes earlier in the day to continued profittaking among institutions, unwillingness among dealers to commit capital to smaller stocks and aggressive selling by some so-called "hedge funds" and other short-term investors.

Selling continued to hammer the market's larger issues and some of this year's best performers. Amgen, a biotechnology concern which opened the year at 49 and rose to an all-time high last week of 92, fell 1/2 to 87 5/8. The stock traded as low as 84 1/2 earlier in the day. Among other OTC market leaders, Intel eased 1/8 to 43 3/8, Liz Claiborne sagged 1 1/8 to 28 7/8; Microsoft fell 1 to 68; Sun Microsystems dropped 5/8 to 32 1/8; Conner Peripherals declined 1 to 26 1/2, and Nordstrom fell 5/8 to 29 3/8.

Analysts said profit-taking in those larger stocks continues to pull down the broader market. The market value of the Nasdaq 100 Index, which tracks the performance of the 100 largest OTC stocks, comprises 42% of the total market value of the Nasdaq Composite Index, which consists of 4,056 stocks. The Nasdaq 100 fell 3.43 to 447.84 and has lost 3.55% this month.

Morgan Stanley analyst L. Keith Mullins said he finds the sell-off in the market's technology and larger issues a "healthy correction" as portfolio managers continue to take their gains from the first half of the year. "It's natural," he said in an interview. "I'm glad to see it shake out some of the speculators."

Among other large issues, MCI Communications closed unchanged at 37 5/8 on 1.8 million shares. The Washington, D.C., long-distance carrier said it signed contracts with the American Hospital Association and Continental Corp.

Traders and analysts said they don't expect smooth sailing soon among over-the-counter stocks, particularly with investors wary of earnings disappointments. Mr. Mullins said he expects to see more selling, particularly of technology shares that have performed well.

"Volatility now is higher than normal," said Dick Holden, senior OTC trader at Alex. Brown & Sons, Baltimore. Mr. Holden explained that dealers are hesitant to put up capital for stocks during periods with earnings surprises. "They don't want to have any commitment one way or the other," he said.

The thin trading in smaller stocks adds to their volatility, explained Stephen Lieber, manager of the Evergreen Fund of Purchase, N.Y. For example, he pointed to New England Critical Care, which fell 2 1/4 to 28 1/4 after the Marlborough, Mass., provider of home-infusion therapy reported net income for the second quarter ended June 30 rose to 21 cents a share from 15 cents a year earlier.

Despite that profit increase, the earnings were a couple of cents shy of analysts' estimates, Mr. Lieber said. "It's been promptly destroyed," he added, suggesting that investors who were concerned they couldn't easily sell the stock, which has 12.2 million shares outstanding, quickly took their profits.

"A substantial number of holders are not investors," Mr. Lieber said, attributing some of the volatility in smaller stocks to institutions, traders and so-called hedge funds, which invest for short-term profits.

Cetus, the day's most-active stock, traded in a wide range from 17 1/4 to 13 7/8 amid speculation about whether a Food and Drug Administration advisory panel will recommend approval of use of the Emeryville, Calif., biotechnology company's drug, Interleukin-2, for use in the treatment of kidney cancer.

Dow Jones Professional Investor Report said after the market closed that the panel delayed recommending approval of the drug until it reviews further details of recently available data about the drug. Cetus fell 1 3/4 to 14 1/8 on more than three million shares.

Among other biotechnology shares, Immunex fell 2 1/4 to 31. DJPIR said analysts were concerned that an FDA advisory panel, scheduled to meet today, might not recommend that the Seattle biotechnology concern receive full approval to market a white blood cell stimulant known as GMCSF, to treat graft failure in bone marrow transplants.

A question given the panel raised the possibility Immunex may be given approval pending ongoing testing of the drug, which would mean the company couldn't immediately earn a profit from the sale of the drug, analysts told DJPIR. However, analysts disagreed on whether the panel's concerns are negative or neutral for the company.

Shorewood Packaging skidded 1 1/4 to 21 1/4 after Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst Susan Hirsch lowered her near-term rating on the Farmingdale, N.Y., maker of paperboard packing to "neutral" from "attractive" and reduced her earnings estimate for the company's first quarter ending July 31 to 23 cents a share from 21 cents, DJPIR reported.

NEW RULE ADOPTED: The Securities and Exchange Commission gave the National Association of Securities Dealers the go-ahead to adopt a "one-share, one-vote" rule for 90 days while the regulators review it. NASD rushed to adopt the provision after a federal appeals court ruled last month that the SEC overstepped its authority in adopting a similar measure. The rule prohibits any company listed on Nasdaq from issuing stock that "nullifies, restricts or disparately reduces" the voting rights of stockholders. The New York Stock Exchange has a similar rule, but the American Stock Exchange doesn't.
