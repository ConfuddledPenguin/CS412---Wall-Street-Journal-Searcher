SEC Is Investigating Stotler Group Inc. For Possible Disclosure Rule Violations

  • Author: Scott McMurray
  • Date: 07/31/90

CHICAGO -- The Securities and Exchange Commission is investigating financially troubled Stotler Group Inc. for possible violations of disclosure rules governing publicly held companies, according to individuals familiar with the investigation. Futures industry regulators, meantime, have questioned the propriety of intercompany loans totaling $5.5 million made to Stotler Group late last year by two pools of customer funds raised by Stotler Funds Inc., a unit of Stotler Group. The loans appear to raise serious conflict of interest issues for Stotler Group and may represent a breach of the company's fiduciary duty to investors in the pools, futures industry officials said.

The heightened regulatory scrutiny follows the collapse over the past few weeks of Stotler Group's major subsidiary, Stotler & Co., which has been forced to withdraw from the futures business for lack of capital. The unit's collapse has pushed the publicly held parent company to the brink of insolvency.

SEC officials as a matter of policy don't comment on whether they are investigating companies for such possible violations, said Mary Weiss, an investigator in the SEC's Chicago office.

The first hint of trouble at Stotler surfaced publicly July 12, when the company asked the American Stock Exchange not to open trading in its stock because it had "news pending." Since that time, futures industry officials privately have criticized Stotler officials for not being more forthcoming about the company's problems. The company's only public announcements about its cash crunch have been a handful of brief statements released to the press, the first dated July 15.

The company's three outside directors resigned last week, saying they hadn't been kept informed of the decisions Stotler was making to cope with their cash crisis, according to one of the directors.

Stotler officials also have consistently refused to return reporters' calls for comment on events affecting the company.

Stotler Group's stock last traded on July 24 at $2.50, down from $7.875 before it was halted.

Futures regulators have been questioning Stotler Group's loans from funds for over two months. The National Futures Association said in a May 7 letter to Stotler Funds President Thomas Kolter that two intercompany loans, in the form of a $1 million demand note from Advanced Portfolio Management and $4.55 million of Stotler Group commercial paper purchased by Compass Futures Fund Inc., "may be in violation of the pools' respective disclosure documents." The Compass fund's disclosure document "specifically disallows such lending," the NFA added.

Mr. Kolter responded in a letter a few days later that pool investors had been informed of the loans, and that they were made to boost the funds' yield on their investments.

Individuals close to Stotler said Mr. Kolter has written Stotler Group in recent days demanding that the $5.5 million be returned to the two pools.

Neither Mr. Kolter nor attorneys acting on behalf of Stotler Funds Inc. returned calls seeking comment.

Stotler & Co.'s troubles began earlier this month when futures industry regulators, disallowing questionable intercompany funds transfers between Stotler & Co. and the parent, ruled that the unit didn't meet minimum capital requirements.

After failing to secure an emergency $10 million cash infusion, Stotler Group last week said it defaulted on $750,000 in commercial paper payments. The company said it may have to restructure or sell assets to meet $7.6 million in debt payments due within 30 days.
