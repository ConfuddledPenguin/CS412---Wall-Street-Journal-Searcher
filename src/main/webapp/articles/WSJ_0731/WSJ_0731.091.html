Politics & Policy: House Approves Food-Labeling Bill That Regulates Use of Health Claims

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 07/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The House approved a food-labeling measure that regulates the use of health claims and requires the food industry to put a uniform nutrition label on almost all packaged foods. The bill was adopted by voice vote after seven major food-processor groups agreed to a compromise on the volatile issue of federal pre-emption of state labeling requirements.

"The purpose of this legislation is to make sense of the confusing array of nutrition labels that confront all consumers everytime they enter the supermarket," Rep. Henry Waxman (D., Calif.) told his House colleagues.

The bill would require food manufacturers to relabel thousands of supermarket products within two years of its enactment. Under the proposed requirements, the new labels would list the amount of calories, fat, salt and other nutrients. The measure would also empower the Food and Drug Administration to regulate health messages and disease-prevention claims, dispelling lingering doubts about the agency's statutory authority in this labeling area.

Some consumer advocates had mixed feelings about the compromise. "Congress has voted to protect the consumer's health, but in the process, has made it easier for food companies to pick the consumer's pocketbook," asserted Bruce Silverglade, attorney for the Center for Science in the Public Interest, a Washington-based consumer group. Under the bill, federal labeling regulations would pre-empt generally stronger state consumer-protection laws in the realm of weights and measures, food-quality standards and ingredient-labeling, Mr. Silverglade complained.

The industry has insisted on uniformity in labeling, arguing that a proliferation of state labeling laws will only create consumer confusion and cause costly disruptions in the food-distribution system. But industry leaders dropped -- at least for now -- their demand that the legislation pre-empt state laws similar to California's Proposition 65. Passed in 1986, Proposition 65 requires manufacturers to put health warnings on any food containing an ingredient believed to pose a risk of cancer or birth defects.

The compromise legislation, co-sponsored by Rep. Edward Madigan (R., Ill.). "strikes a better balance" between consumers and manufacturers, said Manly Molpus, president of the Grocery Manufacturers of America.

The Senate may take up a similar food-labeling measure later this week. John Cady, president of the National Food Processors Association, urged the senators to embrace the "basic tenets" of the House bill.

The bill, among other things, would require that nutrition claims such as "low" and "light" be consistent with food-content definitions to be set by the FDA. "On some products, `light' means low in fat; on others, such as some brands of olive oil, it refers to the color of the product," Mr. Waxman said on the House floor. "The bill would correct this deceptive and misleading state of affairs."

As for disease-prevention claims, he said, none could be made until the FDA has reviewed the scientific evidence and ruled on the validity of the claim.

The bill would require disclosure of serving size, the number of servings per container, the number of calories per serving and the number of calories derived from the total fat and saturated fat in a serving. It would also require manufacturers to list on the label the total amount of fat, saturated fat, unsaturated fat, cholesterol, sodium, sugar, fiber, protein and carbohydrates.

Restaurant and delicatessen food would be exempt from the labeling requirements, as would meat and poultry, which are regulated by the Agriculture Department. Supermarkets would be expected -- but not required -- to post nutritional information or pass out brochures regarding fruits, vegetables and seafood.

The House action is likely to give impetus to the FDA's efforts to develop a comprehensive nutrition-labeling package. "From our perspective, the bill and the FDA's {labeling} proposal are fairly much in agreement," said Frank Scarbrough, a senior FDA official. "What we've proposed may give us a leg-up in implementing whatever legislation is passed."
