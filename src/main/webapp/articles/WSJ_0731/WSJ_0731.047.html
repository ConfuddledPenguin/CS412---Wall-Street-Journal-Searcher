Letters to the Editor: Japan's Masterpieces Never Leave Japan

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/31/90

In discussing the Japanese preference for van Gogh (page one, June 11), you showed just how little you know about culture in Japan. For most of this century, Japan scrupulously has maintained laws forbidding the export of important artworks. It is ludicrous to compare van Gogh's "Portrait of Dr. Gachet," one of his finest works, with the 17th-century screens, produced in one of Kyoto's anonymous commercial art studios for tourists, that recently sold at Christie's for $1.76 million. A Japanese painting even half the stature of van Gogh's would not be permitted to leave the country. Works of high quality in Japan can be sold only to Japanese or to foreigners willing to store their collections in Japan and fly over to visit them on occasion. In practice, few Western buyers would recognize a Japanese masterpiece even if it did come on the auction block in New York. Since the great works don't leave Japan, the international auction market for them is virtually nonexistent. At the van Gogh level, Japanese art is sold in extremely discreet, entirely private transactions. In 1984, at the same time that Mantegna's "Adoration of the Magi" set a world auction record at $10 million, a masterpiece hand scroll quietly changed hands in Japan for $13 million. Buyers consider it a privilege to be given an opportunity to buy a great work of traditional art at any price. They do not want their photographs splashed across the world media. Ryoei Saito, despite his spectacular gesture in buying "Dr. Gachet," is not one of Japan's leading collectors, just as Alan Bond (who briefly owned "Irises") does not represent the great collectors of the West.

The truth is that screens like the pair at Christie's would have sold three years ago for about $200,000.

Andrew Pekarik

Executive Director

Museum of Asian Art

New York
