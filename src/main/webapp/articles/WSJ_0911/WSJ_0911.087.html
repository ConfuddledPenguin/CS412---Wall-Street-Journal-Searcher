Foreign Exchange: Yen Surges as Japan's Institutions Adorn Balance Sheets Before Fiscal Half Closes

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/11/90

NEW YORK -- The yen overpowered all other currencies yesterday as Japanese investors who had forsaken their home market all year exchanged their foreign-currency profits into yen. "Window dressing" was the talk of trading rooms as insurance companies and other big institutions moved to decorate their balance sheets before the close of a particularly tough fiscal first half on Sept. 30.

The biggest losers for the day were those currencies that had recently been seen as attractive investments -- continental European currencies and "high-yielders," such as the pound and the Canadian and Australian dollars, that are backed by high domestic interest rates.

Coming in second was the dollar, which rose against the sold-off currencies because of its role as a medium for cross transactions. For example, investors trading marks for yen will often pass through dollars to take advantage of the greater breadth of the dollar market.

But the greenback also exhibited some independent strength and ended global trading off only half a yen. The dollar was pushed briefly below 139 yen, but bounced back after finding support at 138.75 yen.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 139.55 yen, down from 140.05 yen late Friday in New York. But the U.S. currency rose to 1.5820 marks and 1.3165 Swiss francs from 1.5679 marks and 1.3055 francs in New York late Friday.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the dollar was quoted at 140.23 yen, up from 139.43 yen at the close there Monday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5784 marks, up from Monday's Tokyo close of 1.5710 marks.

Sterling was trading at $1.8575, down from $1.8930 late Friday. The Canadian dollar fell to 85.71 U.S. cents from 85.98 U.S. cents, while the Australian dollar slipped to 81.22 U.S. cents from 82.23 cents.

At their recent peaks a little over two weeks ago, the pound was only four cents shy of $2, while the Canadian and Australian dollars were at 88.5 cents and 83.5 cents respectively.

The yen's performance yesterday was much stronger against non-dollar currencies.

For example, among continental European currencies, the mark fell to 88.211 yen from 89.323 yen Friday. Among high-yield currencies, the pound dropped to 259.21 yen from 265.11 yen late Friday.

Kiyohiko Yoshizawa, a pound and yen trader for the U.K.'s Midland Montagu in New York, said the market is seeing the yen version of what happened to the dollar late last year.

Following the New York Stock Exchange's minicrash last October, Mr. Yoshizawa said, U.S. investors brought home profits from foreign investments to cover their losses. Accordingly, the dollar gained last fall.

Japanese investors, he said, are following the same path now, making up for devastating reversals on the Tokyo Stock Exchange last spring and last month. "Major Japanese investors must buy yen," Mr. Yoshizawa said. "They need yen right now."

Mr. Yoshizawa, agreeing with many traders in Asia, said this will be the week when most cash will be brought home to Japan. Companies face internal reporting deadlines that precede the month-end close of the fiscal half, he said. "This is the week of the real buying."

Come Oct. 1, the yen may drop. Institutions have been known to put their money right back where it was after the close of the fiscal half.

But the dollar's chances against the yen are threatened not only by the prospect of a U.S. recession but by credit-easing to ward off such a recession. Returning from a whirlwind tour around the world to find financial support for the U.S. military deployment in Saudia Arabia, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady repeated his oftstated opinion that U.S. interest rates should be lower.

Also working in the yen's favor is the general easing of Persian Gulf tensions following the show of solidarity in Helsinki by President Bush and Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev.

Meanwhile, sentiment is split on the mark. Some German technicians are pointing to a ceiling for the dollar at 1.5875 marks. "The dollar is near sell levels now" against the mark, warned a Frankfurt dealer.

But others aren't so sure. "It's looking bad for" the mark, said Claus Wagner, chief trader at Citibank in Frankfurt. "I really believe we've seen the worst for the dollar. We've seen all the bad news. If a currency is going to weaken, it's the mark."

Ian Foakes, foreign-exchange manager at PaineWebber, noted that the market is always uncomfortable with a weak dollar. Because so many people have a finger in the U.S. pie, a weak dollar and a weak U.S. economy are painful to everyone. Foreign-exchange volume "really started to peter out" as the dollar fell to its recent record low against the mark, he said.

The dollar ended European trading mostly higher. Dealers said dollar activity was sparse, as the market's attention had turned squarely on the yen.

In Asian trading, the dollar ended mixed, as funds continued to flow into the yen from European currencies. The greenback was last quoted in Tokyo Monday at 139.43 yen, down from 140.25 yen late Friday.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery plunged $10.90 to $378.60 an ounce. The close was the lowest since Aug. 3. Estimated volume was a heavy five million ounces.

In Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $378.25 an ounce.
