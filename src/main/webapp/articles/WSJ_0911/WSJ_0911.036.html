International: Bank Duta Is Bailed Out By In-House Foundations

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/11/90

JAKARTA, Indonesia -- P.T. Bank Duta, the private Indonesian bank whose huge, unexplained foreign-exchange losses came to light last week, has had its financial position put right in surprisingly short order. Industry sources close to the bank said the losses were made good with capital from Bank Duta's majority shareholders, three foundations chaired by President Suharto. And, as authorities had promised, Bank Duta is functioning normally.

The central bank, Bank Indonesia, won't say how big the foreign-exchange losses were. But the sources, who insisted on anonymity, said losses totaled about $300 million, or nearly double the capital of Bank Duta, one of the country's largest private institutions.

The in-house bailout is good news for Bank Duta's public shareholders, who own 20% of the shares, but adds to the many unanswered questions about the bank's troubles and the soundness of the country's financial system. Indonesia has hundreds of charitable foundations similar to the three that, together, own 72% of Bank Duta. They aren't required to publish accounts or financial statements, and little is known of their activities.

Bank Duta has long been considered politically well-connected. Some bankers said the three foundations were able on their own to raise the funds to prop up the bank, while others said they could have received some discreet aid from Bank Indonesia. Such a move would have allowed the central bank to help without having to publicly violate its oft-repeated pledge not to bail out private banks that get into difficulties because of mismanagement.
