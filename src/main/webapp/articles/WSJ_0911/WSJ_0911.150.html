Marketing & Media: Coleman to Market An `Organic' Beef Without Clearance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/11/90

DENVER -- Tired of waiting for the U.S. Department of Agriculture and Congress to give approval, the nation's largest marketer of so-called natural beef said it will begin marketing an organic beef to the public. The new beef, however, won't be called "organic" on the label, a move designed to skirt trouble with the USDA.

Mel Coleman, chairman and founder of Coleman Natural Meats Inc., said his new brand of meat will carry a label that defines what he thinks "organic" means. The small print on the label will include: "Our animals never receive antibiotics or growth hormones from the time they are born. . . . They receive only feeds produced without herbicides and pesticides."

The Coleman Organic Beef name will be used only on advertising materials -- not on labels -- because the USDA has yet to approve the use of the word "organic" on labels. Congress, however, may force the USDA's hand by approving the use of "organic" and a definition of the word along with the 1990 Farm Bill, which is currently in conference committee. Both the House and the Senate have passed similar versions of an amendment that would allow the USDA to approve organic labeling on consumer foods.

Mr. Coleman, who lobbied for the bill, said he has received certification for the product from the Organic Crop Improvement Association, an international group. He is the only meat producer to receive the OCIA's certification.

Coleman Natural Beef will continue to outsell Coleman Organic Beef, because it is less expensive, Mr. Coleman predicted. But he currently has more organic beef orders from retailers than he can fill, he said. The difference between the organic and natural beef is that the natural beef cattle may eat feed that isn't certified as organic, Mr. Coleman said.
