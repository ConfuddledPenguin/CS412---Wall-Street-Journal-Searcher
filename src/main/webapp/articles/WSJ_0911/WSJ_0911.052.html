Japan's Plan to Sell More NTT Shares In Privatization Put Off for Second Year

  • Author: Clay Chandler
  • Date: 09/11/90

TOKYO -- Government officials have again punched the hold button on investors who want to buy a stake in the world's biggest telecommunications company, Nippon Telegraph & Telephone Corp. With Tokyo stock prices down nearly 40% since January and NTT shareholders raising a ruckus, officials at Japan's powerful Ministry of Finance confirmed what market analysts here have been predicting for weeks: For the second year in a row, the government will postpone plans to sell nearly two million state-owned shares of NTT to private investors as part of a continuing privatization program.

Ministry officials weren't available to comment, but issued this statement late Monday afternoon: "Further {government} sales of NTT shares could trigger more declines in NTT stock prices and could have a bad influence on the stock market."

The ministry still owns about two-thirds of the 15.6 million NTT shares outstanding; it is required by law to hold at least one-third, but it would like to sell as many shares as legally possible to use the proceeds to reduce the government's debt.

Rumors of the announcement had cheered some investors. NTT shares, the most expensive on the Tokyo Stock Exchange, closed Monday at 881,000 yen ($6,285.94), up 21,000 yen. But most analysts said the move indicates the telecommunications conglomerate's distress.

"It's absolutely predictable," Michael Jeremy, an analyst at Baring Securities (Japan) Ltd., said of the ministry's move. "This company is in just as much difficulty as it was last year at this time." And John Baldwin, an analyst at Jardine Fleming Securities Ltd., said, "We took it as a foregone conclusion."

Foreigners may buy and sell NTT shares but can't receive their dividends.

Perhaps no other company listed on the Tokyo exchange has proved as disappointing to Japanese investors as NTT, the world's most heavily capitalized company. The public clamored to own the stock after plans to privatize NTT were announced in 1985. The press of eager investors was so great at the first public auction of NTT shares in 1986 that the government instituted a lottery to thin the ranks of bidders. Individual investors snapped up the shares, assured by enthusiastic Japanese brokers that it was impossible to lose on NTT stock, because the biggest holder, the Finance Ministry, would never allow its price to drop.

And at first, the predictions came true: By April 1988, the price of NTT shares -- issued at a par value of 50,000 yen -- soared to a high of 3.18 million yen. But the price declined relentlessly in the months that followed, as the government continued to sell additional shares, even as investors started to discover that NTT's financial prospects weren't as rosy as anticipated. When the Finance Ministry sold an additional 1.95 million shares in 1989, the price had dropped to 1.19 million yen.

Jeffrey Zavattero, an analyst at Jardine Fleming, said he expects NTT's operating profit in the year ending next March to contract to 550 million yen from 665 million yen in fiscal 1990.

NTT faces competitive pressures in its most profitable business, long distance. As a regulated monopoly, NTT must maintain unprofitable local telephone businesses. It also is caught in a cross fire between three regulatory agencies, each of which has a different agenda for the company.

Corrections & Amplifications

JAPAN'S MINISTRY OF FINANCE first auctioned stock in state-owned Nippon Telegraph & Telephone Corp. in 1987. It last offered shares to the public in 1988. At the time, NTT's stock traded at just below two million yen a share, down from a high of 3.12 million yen in April 1987. In Tuesday's edition, the history of the stock price was reported incorrectly.
