Heard on the Street: Short Sellers Betting Heavily That Sara Lee, Down 20% This Year, Will Keep on Falling

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 09/11/90

Somebody doesn't like Sara Lee. Even though the Deerfield, Ill., marketer of food products and clothing has posted 13 years of record earnings, its stock has declined 20% this year, closing at 26 3/4 yesterday in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. In the past two months, short sellers have been placing big bets that the stock will continue to decline.

Short sellers borrow stock and immediately sell it, hoping the price will drop. Eventually they must buy shares to replace the ones they borrowed. But if the price drops enough in the meantime, they profit. Short interest -- the number of shares borrowed and not yet replaced -- in Sara Lee stock ballooned to 3.9 million shares as of mid-August, the latest tally available from the New York Stock Exchange.

Sara Lee is hardly the sort of stock short sellers normally swarm around. Shorts generally look for troubled companies with painfully heavy debt loads. But Sara Lee has been consistently profitable, with earnings growing about 14% a year. Its debt load is higher than some people prefer, with debt at about 36% of capital, but isn't far out of line with that of competitors.

Nevertheless, three highly regarded money management firms -- Steinhardt Partners, Tiger Management, and Soros Fund Management -- are shorting the stock heavily, according to some money managers and analysts. The three firms won't confirm or deny the reports.

Sara Lee's chief financial officer, Michael Murphy, says he has heard that the short sellers' main concern is Sara Lee's T-shirt business, part of its Hanes clothing unit. T-shirt sales are growing, but capacity to produce T-shirts has grown faster. An inventory glut looms and will take at least two more quarters to work off, Mr. Murphy says.

But Mr. Murphy says he is "puzzled" by the large-scale short selling because T-shirts are only a small part of Sara Lee's sales. How small? One analyst says 2%. Mr. Murphy won't disclose a figure, but says it's "a little bit more than that."

People familiar with the shorts' thinking say their concerns go well beyond T-shirts. Sara Lee "has been one of the faster growing food companies, but in the past 12 months it has disappointed, slowed down," says Kurt Feuerman, a Morgan Stanley analyst. "It's not going to be that easy for them to ramp up the growth rate again." Mr. Feuerman himself rates the stock a "hold."

Operating earnings in bakery goods and packaged meats fell 16% in the fiscal year that ended in June. Bakery business was hurt by the trend to lower-calorie products, which Sara Lee is belatedly joining. Meats were hurt by rising hog prices, a factor that has since reversed. The company says both businesses will do better this year.

Sara Lee's earnings now come mainly from clothing, rather than food. Wall Street usually values food stocks more highly, on the theory that food earnings are more dependable.

That's a "gross mistake," claims Sara Lee's chief executive, John H. Bryan Jr. At a meeting Friday with analysts and money managers in New York, he said that Sara Lee's biggest apparel business, hosiery (L'Eggs and Hanes brands), is steady and has outstanding profit margins, making it in many ways a better business than food, where competition is fierce.

Short sellers also point to what they say is a weak pricing environment for underwear in general, not just T-shirts. Sara Lee's Mr. Murphy says pricing lately is "nothing out of the ordinary." He acknowledges that Hanes has cut underwear prices three times, but says that the most recent cut was some time ago.

One short seller claims that hosiery shipments lately have been disappointing. The company, though it readily admits to problems in T-shirts, won't stand for that kind of talk. Sara Lee's hosiery business "is wonderful," Mr. Murphy says.

Mr. Bryan concedes that the U.S. hosiery industry "has been in decline for several years," partly because of a trend to more casual clothes. But he says Sara Lee can thrive in the business nevertheless, by continuing to gain market share (now about 43% in the U.S.). His target: a 60% U.S. share, and similar dominance in Europe, where the hosiery business is extremely fragmented.

Finally, some pessimists cite a report by Kellogg Associates, a Los Angeles research firm, pointing to what Kellogg sees as classic warning signs in Sara Lee's financial statements -- a buildup of inventories and accounts receivable, or money owed to the company. Sara Lee officials say those figures are explainable by such things as a changing business mix and the fact that "retailers are in tough shape."
