Technology: Pact to Sell Lasers In Japan Reached By U.S. Company

  • Author: Amal Kumar Naj
  • Date: 09/11/90

HARTFORD, Conn. -- United Technologies Corp. entered into a marketing agreement with a Japanese company to sell high-power laser systems in Japan, opening a major market for this emerging technology. In recent years low-power lasers have been replacing conventional tools for cutting sheet metal and welding. As with many other technologies invented in the U.S., the Japanese have been in the forefront in integrating lasers into their manufacturing processes. The pact with a leading developer of high-power lasers will "give them . . . significant advantage" in their manufacturing costs, said Edward Peters, who follows lasers at the consulting firm Arthur D. Little Inc.

High-power lasers can cut thicker materials and can weld more precisely and at higher speeds than low-end lasers, allowing significant manufacturing flexibility. The recently commercialized technology hasn't attracted many takers in the U.S., however. "The demand isn't as intense as in Japan and Europe," said Jeffrey Carstens, general manager of United Technologies Industrial Lasers. "That's one reason we want a presence in Japan."

Under the agreement, Hanwa Co. in Tokyo will market and distribute United Technologies' carbon dioxide lasers, whose powers will range between 10 and 25 kilowatts. Most lasers in use in Japan produce less than one kilowatt, and the highest-power laser available from Japanese companies is in the 10-kilowatt range.

In Japan, the market for industrial lasers was $200 million last year. "We expect the Japanese market for high-power lasers to expand by over 400% in five years," said Shigeru Kita, president of Hanwa.

Mr. Carstens said that the lasers will be supplied from the U.S. and that there are no plans for joint production because of the size of the market. A five-kilowatt laser costs $460,000. A 25-kilowatt version is priced at $1 million.

Arthur D. Little's Mr. Peters said that laser applications are also likely to get a major boost from European manufacturers. Under a plant modernization program, European Community members have singled out high-power lasers to increase manufacturing efficiency and are spending tens of millions of dollars on laser technology, he said.

Mr. Peters estimated that the world market for industrial lasers totaled $830 million in 1988, with Japan accounting for 40% of all installed industrial lasers, the U.S. 33% and Europe 27%. He said he expects the world market to grow 8% to 10% annually through 1994.
