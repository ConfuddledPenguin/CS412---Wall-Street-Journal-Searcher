International: French Utility, Socialists Split on Outside Capital

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/11/90

PARIS -- The heavily indebted French utility Electricite de France, or EDF, said it needs capital from private investors to pursue its development, but the Socialist government is saying "no." While France is loosening investment restrictions on some of its state-owned companies, EDF hasn't convinced the government that its needs justify outside investment in the sensitive electricity sector. "Opening its capital would require a new law, and there are no plans to change EDF's statutes," an Industry Ministry official said.

In an internal memo to department heads this month, management said EDF's financial situation was a serious handicap to its development. "The disastrous structure of EDF's balance sheet and especially our very heavy indebtedness means that the company . . . loses credibility . . . with the international financial community and foreign clients," the memo said.

EDF, which has financed most of its ambitious nuclear program through international bond issues, had total debt outstanding at the end of 1989 of 230 billion francs ($43.8 billion), about 1 1/2 times its annual revenue. It had a 1989 loss of 4.25 billion francs, which EDF officials blamed on the government's refusal to allow the utility to raise rates.
