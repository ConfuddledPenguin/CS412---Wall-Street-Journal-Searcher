Workers Strike At Most Paper Mills Of Abitibi-Price

  • Author: G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 09/11/90

TORONTO -- Workers at most Abitibi-Price Inc. paper mills in eastern Canada went on strike, shutting down about 7% of North American newsprint capacity. Analysts and industry executives said a short strike won't have much impact on the newsprint market. Other producers can make up some of the production lost at Abitibi's mills. As well, U.S. publishers and other users had an average 39-day supply of newsprint on hand at the end of July, the latest figures available.

But a long strike could cause problems for publishers and push up newsprint prices. "The newsprint market was pretty tight already," said Raymond Kilroy, an analyst at Nesbitt Thomson Deacon Inc.

Publishers are worried about possible strikes at other big Canadian newsprint concerns. On Friday Canadian Pacific Forest Products Ltd. presented a final offer to workers at three newsprint mills and one pulp mill in eastern Canada. The company expects an answer by tomorrow, a spokesman said. The three Canadian Pacific Forest newsprint mills account for about 7% of North American capacity.

Talks with Abitibi broke down late yesterday, a spokesman for the Canadian Paperworkers Union said. The company presented its final offer to the union early yesterday morning. The union presented a counteroffer yesterday afternoon but the company wouldn't alter its final proposal, the union spokesman said.

Industry and union officials said workers at Abitibi and other pulp and paper mills in eastern Canada aren't happy with the contract accepted earlier this summer by workers at Stone Container Corp.'s Canadian unit. The Stone Container contract was supposed to set the pattern for workers at other mills in the region.

The strike has shut down seven Abitibi mills in Ontario, Quebec and Newfoundland, six of which make newsprint, Abitibi said. A union official said two other mills in Quebec are expected to join the strike. Abitibi mills at Alma, Quebec, and Pine Falls, Manitoba, which are represented by other unions, will continue to operate, Abitibi said. As well, the company's two U.S. mills aren't affected by the strike. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Abitibi closed at $13.375, down 12.5 cents.

Separately, the Canadian Pulp and Paper Association said Canadian production of newsprint rose 1.4% in July from a year earlier as the industry operated at 94% of capacity.

The industry group said Canadian mills produced 826,000 metric tons of newsprint in July compared with 815,000 metric tons a year earlier. For the year to date, output fell 1.4% to 5.56 million metric tons from 5.64 million metric tons a year earlier. A metric ton equals 2,204.6 pounds.

The July 94% operating rate was down from 95% in May and from 96% a year earlier.

July shipments fell 3.0% to 751,000 metric tons from 775,000 metric tons a year earlier. Shipments to Canada rose while shipments to the U.S. and other foreign markets fell. For the year to date, shipments rose 0.2% to 5.49 million metric tons from 5.48 million metric tons in the year-earlier period.
