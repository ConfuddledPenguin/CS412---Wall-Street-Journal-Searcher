Roses Were Reds, Violets Blues, Till They Redid Crayola's Hues

  • Author: Virginia Daut
  • Date: 09/11/90

It was certainly one of the most colorful protests in business history. A dozen marchers paraded outside a Pennsylvania factory, dragging along a human skeleton in a wooden coffin. They carried placards that said: "When Does 8 x 8 Equal Less Than 64? When We Hate the New 8]" and "Indians Call It Maize, We Call It Gone." Then they solemnly took a handful of crayons, broke them in half, and dropped them into the coffin.

The target of this demonstration was Binney & Smith Inc., the maker of Crayola crayons. This summer the company, a unit of Hallmark Cards Inc., retired eight colors from its well-known, 64-crayon flip-top box: raw umber, maize, lemon yellow, blue gray, violet blue, green blue, orange red and orange yellow. In their place came eight sexy new hues for the 1990s: wild strawberry, vivid tangerine, fuchsia, teal blue, cerulean, royal purple, jungle green and dandelion.

To the company's surprise, the move has drawn an outpouring of protest from a veritable spectrum of crayon loyalists. Robert Pagani, a Phillipsburg, N.J., theme-park worker, created CRAYON, Committee to Re-establish All Your Old Norms. When Binney & Smith held a special ceremony to enshrine the eight old colors in the Crayola Hall of Fame, Mr. Pagani staged the protest march with the coffin and the skeleton. "They call it a retirement. I call it a burial," he says.

Karen Latinik, a sophomore at James Madison University in Harrisonburg, Va., founded the National Campaign to Save Lemon Yellow and sent a petition with about 100 signatures. Binney & Smith's polite reply was signed in lemon yellow crayon. "That seemed like a slap in the face to me," Ms. Latinik says.

Kenneth Lang, a Locust Valley, N.Y., gifts and housewares manager, sent an aggrieved letter from RUMPS, the Raw Umber and Maize Preservation Society. Loretta Lampkin, the associate minister of the First United Methodist Church in Bossier City, La., wrote Binney & Smith to sadly recall restoring the polish on her scuffed shoes with the old colors. Another letter writer, artist Jane Hankins of Gardner, Mass., warned: "Have you forgotten what happened to Coca-Cola when they also decided to abandon tradition in favor of a new `recipe.'"

And first-grader Ebony Faison from Philadelphia asked: "Whenever I draw me, I use raw umber. What color should I color now?"
