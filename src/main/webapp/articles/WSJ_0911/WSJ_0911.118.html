Consideration of Luxury Tax Is Touching Off an Old Debate

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 09/11/90

WASHINGTON -- As if Donald Trump didn't already have enough to worry about, the luxury tax may be making a comeback. Democrats' insistence on skewing any tax increase so that it hits the rich and Republicans' resistance to raising income-tax rates have revived consideration of the luxury levy, which was imposed by the federal government during World War II and not abandoned until 1965.

Industries that make and sell goods the budget negotiators consider luxuries are dusting off old arguments and coming up with new ones in an effort to head off any new tax on their wares.

"The arbitrary selection of certain commodities as luxuries is going to distort the economy," complains Matthew Runci, executive director of the Manufacturing Jewelers and Silversmiths of America.

The pleasure boat industry says any new taxes would weaken an already depressed industry and reduce demand for yachts so much the government wouldn't make any money taxing them. And the Electronics Industries Association says consumer electronics aren't a luxury. "Our products are ubiquitous," says Gary Shapiro, vice president for consumer electronics.

As previously reported, Democrats have proposed a 10% tax that would apply to a retail purchase price in excess of $500 for furs, $1,000 for electronics, $5,000 for jewelry, $25,000 for autos, $50,000 for boats and yachts, and the entire purchase price of private aircraft, according to a written summary of their opening proposal at the budget talks under way at Andrews Air Force Base.

Under this proposal, for instance, the purchaser of a $50,000 Porsche would have to pay $2,500 in federal luxury taxes -- 10% of the price over $25,000 -- on top of any local sales taxes.

Republican aides say their side has contemplated a 5% levy that would apply to the part of a car's price that exceeded $30,000, as well as to other items, but they apparently haven't put their proposal on the table.

Thresholds at these levels would, in most cases, limit the tax to a small fraction of all purchases. The jewelry makers, for instance, were relieved; they had anticipated any luxury tax would apply to items that cost far less than $5,000, and there are indications that Republicans have considered just such a plan.

The vast majority of cars purchased by Americans would be spared the tax. Only between 5% and 7% of all cars cost more than $25,000, says Bernard Campbell of DRI/McGraw Hill, a Lexington, Mass., consulting firm. The average car costs less than $16,000, he says.

But that isn't much consolation to auto dealers. "When you start talking about that price range -- $25,000 -- it would definitely have an impact in a market that's already in turmoil," says Bill Fitzpatrick, general sales manager of Metropolitan Motorcars of Marlow Heights, Md., which sells Mercedes-Benz, Porsche, Audi, Saab and Subaru autos. Dealers, he says, would be forced to absorb at least some of the tax with discounts or bigger trade-in allowances.

Plans to revive the luxury tax were examined by the Treasury during the Reagan years but never were proposed. Defining just what is a luxury and what is not is always tricky and controversial.

And enforcing the tax can be a problem. The Democrats' plan to tax consumer electronic products that cost more than $1,000 could, for instance, encourage dealers to break products into components that cost less than $1,000. It wasn't immediately clear if the Democrats' plan covers personal computers. Most of them sell for more than $1,000 but could easily be separated into parts that might sell for less.

The Joint Tax Committee of Congress estimates that the Democrats' version of a luxury tax, if effective Jan. 1, 1991, would raise $600 million in fiscal 1991, which begins Oct. 1., and $8.1 billion over five years.

The luxury tax is part of a package of Democratic-backed tax increases aimed at upper-income Americans that would raise $14.4 billion in fiscal 1991.
