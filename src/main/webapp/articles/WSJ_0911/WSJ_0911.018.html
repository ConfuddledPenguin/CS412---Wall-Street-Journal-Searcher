Aerospace Firm To Restructure Retiree Plan --- McDonnell Douglas Corp. Will Realize Big Gain In the Third Quarter

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 09/11/90

McDonnell Douglas Corp. said it would restructure its retiree benefit plan, allowing it to recognize a $234 million gain in the third quarter. News of the windfall, equal to $6.11 a share, helped raise the struggling aerospace concern's price on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday; it finished in composite trading at $48.75, up $1.125

An accounting adjustment will be made as the St. Louis concern transfers its benefit obligations for 14,200 salaried retirees, currently covered by an overfunded company-administered pension plan, to a group annuity contract with Metropolitan Life Insurance Co. The action will decrease the number of liabilities in the company-run fund, which will still have 26,400 participants. In turn, the plan will become even more overfunded than before, allowing for the third-quarter gain.

At the end of 1989, the plan had $5.9 billion in assets and was overfunded about $2 billion.

McDonnell Douglas stressed that the annuity arrangement "in no way alters the benefits due affected retirees or the manner in which the payments are made." In fact, it said the new system will "strengthen the benefit security" for salaried employees. It noted that other corporations, including Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co., Manufacturers Hanover Corp. and Edison Brothers Stores Inc., have taken similar steps.

In the past, however, the Pentagon has stepped in when defense contractors have tinkered with their overfunded pension plans, saying the government is entitled to at least some of the assets because money poured into these funds was derived from military contracts.

Just last month, for example, the Defense Department's inspector general's office disclosed that it was investigating whether Ford Motor Co. should be able to remove $100 million from the pension fund of its aerospace unit before selling the subsidiary to Loral Corp. In addition, according to a 1989 Bear Stearns study, FMC Corp. and Fairchild Industries attempted to recapture their excess pension funds, but had to share or give back the surplus to the Pentagon.

But the McDonnell Douglas situation appears different.

Although the company's equity position will improve as a result of its deal with Metropolitan Life, it is not terminating its pension plan, as has happened in other cases, and it will receive no cash as a result of the transaction. "There appears to be a real distinction here," said Lawrence Harris, a securities analyst who follows McDonnell Douglas for Bateman Eichler, Hill Richards Inc., a Los Angeles brokerage firm.

"Not a penny will change hands," added Jerry Cantwell, of Wertheim Schroder & Co. in New York. A Pentagon spokesman said he couldn't comment on the particulars of the McDonnell Douglas move.

Actually, the company has been recording asset gains to recognize the overfunded portion of its pension plan since changing its accounting methods in 1987. The pact with Metropolitan Life -- the precise terms of which weren't disclosed -- will merely allow McDonnell Douglas to accelerate the recognition of that situation and adjust its books all at once; smaller gains will continue to be amortized in the future.

In the year-ago third quarter, McDonnell Douglas posted net income of $38 million, or 98 cents a share, on sales of $3.72 billion. The results included an extraordinary gain of $6 million, or 14 cents a share, for the divestiture of the bulk of the company's information systems group.

McDonnell Douglas, the nation's No. 1 defense contractor, also got a lift last week when the U.S. said it completed negotiations with South Korea for the sale of 120 of the company's F/A-18 jet fighters to Seoul. Moreover, analysts noted, the Persian Gulf crisis has been a boon by providing an opportunity for more F/A-18 and F-15 jet sales to the Middle East.

Before the pension-plan announcement, Mr. Harris said he expected McDonnell Douglas to earn about 50 cents a share in the current period, reflecting possible cost overruns on some military programs and continued development expenses on the new MD-11 commercial jetliner. Still, Herbert Lanese, McDonnell Douglas's chief financial officer, said in a recent interview that a massive cost-cutting drive initiated in July would begin to bear fruit soon. "You should see some effect in the third quarter, and it will really kick in in the fourth," he said.
