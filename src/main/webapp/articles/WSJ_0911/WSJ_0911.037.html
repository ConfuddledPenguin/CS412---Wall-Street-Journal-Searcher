Letters to the Editor: Worse Even Than You Think

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/11/90

I could not agree more with your Aug. 20 editorial on "Oil Disarmament." Actually, things are worse than you think. I am a chemical-engineering consultant specializing in developing new hydrocarbon manufacturing processes. Last spring I cooperated with Lehigh University to respond to the Department of Energy's solicitation of research proposals on the kind of fuel technology that would make the U.S. less dependent on oil. We are expecting to learn soon whether we will get any award to carry out the research.

This morning I called DOE to ask whether the Iraq crisis has provided the department with new opportunities for further research. My contact laughed. He said that, to the contrary, he is concerned about the budget process leading to a massive across-the-board cut. If the Gramm-Rudman ax falls, many of the important research awards will not be announced this fall.

I had hoped that the current crisis would finally teach us a lesson. I was wrong.

M. Orhan Tarhan

Rockville, Md.

---

Your editorial significantly overstated the alleged potential of the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge.

According to Ronald Reagan's pro-development Interior Department, which likely erred on the high side but has at least some credibility:

-- There is only a 19% chance of finding a commercially viable oil field below the refuge's spectacular coastal plain.

-- If such a field were found, the most likely output (3.2 billion barrels) would satisfy just 2% of U.S. oil demand over its productive life.

-- There is only one chance in 100 that a field as large as the one at Prudhoe Bay exists there.

How can this generation justify tearing up "America's Serengeti" to create a sprawling industrial-pollution factory when a mere two-mile-per-gallon increase in the efficiency of new U.S. cars would save more crude than the oil industry hopes to find in that unique wildlife refuge?

Gaylord Nelson

Counselor

The Wilderness Society

Washington

---

You tell of the U.S. oil reserves available in Alaska and off the California coast, but you fail to mention the Kettleman Hills Naval Petroleum Reserve in Kern County here in California.

You will recall the pieces the Journal wrote back in the '70s urging that the reserve be released. You pointed out that Congress, as it usually does, would not listen to reason and refused to act when release was definitely called for.

The situation is even more urgent today. You would do the nation a favor by publicizing the reserve's potential.

Reynold Blight

Burbank, Calif.
