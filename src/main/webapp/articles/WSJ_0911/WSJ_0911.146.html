House Passes Bill to Limit Cable-TV Fees --- Bipartisan Support Shown; Senate and White House May Prove to Be Hurdles

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 09/11/90

WASHINGTON -- The House, in a strong show of bipartisan support, passed on a voice vote a bill that would reregulate cable television rates for basic service and assist emerging competitors. The bill's swift passage, however, doesn't guarantee enactment of the legislation this year. The bill faces an uncertain future in the Senate as well as a veto threat from the White House.

Under the House-passed bill, the Federal Communications Commission would establish a maximum fee that cable operators could charge for retransmission of broadcast signals, as well as public education and government channels.

To discipline so-called bad actors, the FCC would develop guidelines to identify exorbitant rates, and local or state authorities would be allowed to step in on a case-by-case basis when they suspect price gouging. Local cable authorities would also monitor customer service standards developed by the FCC.

While the bill permits cable operators to buy exclusive programming, it also requires operators to provide their signals to competitors, such as satellite and microwave services, in a "reasonable" and "nondiscriminatory" fashion.

The measure received a swift rebuke from the White House, which threatened a veto. The White House drew up a laundry list of complaints, saying that the bill would impose additional federal regulation over cable rates and hamper cable programmers' ability to distribute their products.

In addition, the Bush administration criticized restrictions on foreign ownership of cable systems, expressing concern that this would invite retaliation overseas. And it said the "must carry" requirements, which spell out cable companies' obligations to carry signals of broadcast stations in the area, raise First Amendment questions.

On similar grounds, it raised concerns about the bill's requirement that cable operators offer a basic tier of service that doesn't include special cable programming. As for competition, the administration reiterated its recommendation that Congress lift restrictions that bar telephone companies from entering the cable business, a suggestion that cable companies adamantly oppose.

The National Cable Television Association, the industry's main trade association, expressed dismay with the legislation but didn't say it would oppose the bill. NCTA President James Mooney said his group "continues to be deeply troubled by the legislation" and it remains "an open question whether a reasonable cable bill" can be enacted this year. "What began as a consumer issue is now turning into an effort by other industries to pile on, and hobble cable as a competitor," he said.

The cable bill was spurred by sizable increases in monthly cable fees since rates were largely deregulated in 1987. In some cases, rates have doubled or tripled, although increases have moderated in recent months.

House passage puts pressure on the Senate to take up its version of cable revision soon, but whether Congress can pass cable legislation this year remains uncertain. The Senate measure, which has cleared the Commerce Committee, includes tougher language on providing programming to competitors but gives cable operators more leeway to raise rates. Even if the Senate acts soon, the measures would have to go through a conference committee to iron out details.

In the House, Rep. Edward Markey (D., Mass.), chairman of the telecommunications and finance subcommittee and co-author of the bill along with Rep. Matthew Rinaldo (R., N.J.), said the bill "addresses real problems in a surgical and balanced manner." He promised it would provide strong consumer protection, ignite competition and expand diversity in video programming.

Gene Kimmelman, legislative director of the Consumer Federation of America, applauded the House bill. He called it "one of the bigger victories for consumers this year because it combines immediate pocketbook protections with the promotion of competition to cable down the road."
