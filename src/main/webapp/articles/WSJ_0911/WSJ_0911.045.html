BRITISH TRIAL

The British shipping company P&O European Ferries and seven former employees denied manslaughter charges at the start of their trial stemming from a ferry disaster that killed 193 people.

The trial follows a police investigation into the capsizing of the ferry Herald of Free Enterprise as it left the Belgian port of Zeebrugge March 6, 1987. The company, three officials and four crew members are accused of unlawfully killing just one passenger, Alison Joy Gaillard, 27, selected to represent those who died.

The seven men and the company pleaded not guilty to the charges.

Manslaughter carries a maximum sentence of life imprisonment; the company faces an unlimited fine if found guilty. The trial could last up to five months.

PORTUGUESE AIR FARES UP

The Portuguese government increased domestic air fares by some 10% yesterday, citing rising oil prices. A one-way economy fare between Lisbon and the southern resort of Faro rose 9.5%, to 10,400 escudos (about $75). Economy rates between the capital and the northern city of Oporto rose by the same margin to 11,500 escudos. Air fares between mainland Portugal and the Atlantic islands of Madeira and the Azores were unchanged.

INDONESIAN PETROCHEMICALS

A new Indonesian company, PT Chandra Asri, signed an agreement to invest $2.4 billion in a complex to produce olefins, which are petrochemical byproducts used in making plastics. The signing increases doubts that a previously proposed $1.7 billion olefins project involving Royal Dutch/Shell Group and Indonesia's Bimantara Citra Group will go ahead. Shell and its Indonesian partners haven't been able to agree on shareholding arrangements. Analysts said that if the Shell-Bimantara deal falls through, either Bimantara or its chairman, Bambang Trihatmodjo, a son of President Suharto, could play an equity or other role in the Chandra Asri development.

PIZZA HUTS FOR MOSCOW, BEIJING

With the Soviet Union's first two Pizza Huts scheduled to open in Moscow today, China's first Pizza Hut opened yesterday in Beijing and served 200 pizzas to an invitation-only crowd of Chinese and foreigners before running out of dough. In the future, the two-story, 180-seat restaurant expects to serve about 285 pizzas daily, mainly to foreign tourists and foreign residents of Beijing. But the company also hopes to attract Chinese customers, not yet used to pizza.

SPECULATION IN BRAZIL

Brazilian President Fernando Collor de Mello blamed speculators for high prices and called for a "national dialogue" to reduce inflation. "High prices still exist in some sectors, due to a perverse combination of inflationary memory and speculation," Mr. Collor said in a nationally broadcast speech. "Save. Buy only essentials. Haggle," he urged. When Mr. Collor took office in March, prices were rising more than 80% a month; in August, monthly inflation was 10.5%.

SANYO-SIEMENS LINK

Sanyo Electric Co. said it would start marketing next April in Japan selected consumer electric products made by Siemens AG of West Germany. Officials of the Japanese company said Sanyo would sell four Siemens products -- refrigerators, dishwashers, washing machines and stoves -- as part of its system kitchens. The marketing tie-up will help Siemens establish a toehold in Japan while at the same time help Sanyo bolster sales of its system kitchens by incorporating the German-made equipment, which Sanyo officials say is in strong demand in Japan.

TECHNOLOGY FOR SOUTH KOREA

South Korean companies are beginning to use Soviet technology for commercial applications.

Samil Pharmaceutical Co., a midsized South Korean medicine company, plans to introduce by the end of this year in South Korea a patented Soviet technology for measuring cholesterol. An official at Samsung Co., the Korean agent for Licensintorg, a Soviet government agency handling technology trade with foreign companies, said the Samil deal is the first of about 15 that combine Soviet technology with South Korean manufacturing.

Licensintorg took hundreds of patented Soviet technologies to South Korea for commercial applications during a Soviet trade show in Seoul earlier this year.

The Soviet Union and South Korea are expected to establish formal diplomatic relations within two months, Seoul's state radio said yesterday.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

Chinese Vice President Wang Zhen marked National Teachers' Day yesterday with a call to resist foreign and domestic plots to implant democracy in China. The People's Daily newspaper carried a letter written by Mr. Wang calling on primary and middle-school teachers to improve the "quality of political thinking" by teaching Marxism and Maoist thought. . . . South Korea's Constitution Court, which rules on the constitutionality of laws, determined yesterday that the country's prohibition of adultery is constitutional. "It is imperative that adultery remain outlawed to protect the existing marital system and harmonious family life and prevent social harms that might be caused by it," the ruling said.
