Seidman Urges Reassessment Of Policy Of Closing Sick but Functioning Thrifts

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 09/11/90

WASHINGTON -- The government strategy guiding the savings-and-loan cleanup may have outlived its usefulness, according to the chairman of the federal bailout agency. Testifying before the Senate Banking Committee, William Seidman, chairman of the Resolution Trust Corp., expressed some doubt about whether it is prudent to maintain the policy of automatically seizing sick S&Ls. "Closing an institution rapidly does not necessarily mean that we'll end up without loss," Mr. Seidman said. "If the institution is not being badly managed, we simply transfer bad assets from the institution to the government."

The government already has seized most of the biggest and most insolvent thrifts, leaving the RTC bloated with billions of dollars in assets. The assets remain a taxpayer burden until the agency finds buyers -- a process that is expected to take years. The RTC now must evaluate whether this tourniquet approach makes sense for thrifts not bleeding red ink so profusely.

But by allowing undercapitalized S&Ls to remain open, the regulators risk repeating mistakes of the 1980s, when regulators allowed ailing thrifts to continue operating in the hope that the economy would improve. The strategy, called "forbearance," simply gave the thrifts more time to pile up losses.

"With the history during the 1980s, any notion of forbearance tends to be automatically seen as unattractive and not facing up to problems," Sen. Donald Riegle, chairman of the committee warned. But the Michigan Democrat urged the regulators to reverse course if new tactics can save money in the long run.

Many industry experts have complained that the RTC has taken an unyielding approach toward undercapitalized thrifts, whose management and portfolios may differ radically. "The RTC is already choking on everything it has," said Randy German, a thrift accounting expert for Grant Thornton, a New York-based accounting firm. "They are going to have to come up with other ideas." He said the regulators should delay seizing sick thrifts, while monitoring their operations.

With the bailout fund expected to run out of cash by the end of the year, a strategy of easing up on undercapitalized thrifts would allow cleanup officials to conserve cash in the short term. And even after Congress sets aside new funds for the cleanup, soft real-estate markets are going to push the S&L cleanup costs still higher, Mr. Seidman said. "Every time we estimate losses now, we're looking at lower real-estate markets, which means the losses are higher," he added.

Also at the hearing, the banking regulators agreed to resolve within 30 days a dispute over the amount of capital that banks must hold in reserve. New regulations require banks and thrifts to reserve capital based upon the amount of risk in their lending. Mr. Seidman, also chairman of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., said that the FDIC agrees with rules proposed by the Federal Reserve governing minimum capital standards. The two agencies are discussing differences in the capital rule language with the comptroller of the currency.
