GM, UAW Bargainers Are Still Far Apart Over the Talks' Top Issue of Job Security

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 09/11/90

DETROIT -- With four days to go until a strike deadline, bargainers for General Motors Corp. and the United Auto Workers union are still far apart on the top issue in the talks -- job security for union members. But both sides say they're optimistic they can reach an agreement by the Friday night deadline, despite the differences over job guarantees.

UAW officials want GM to promise that the company's 310,000 UAW workers will be employed for all but a specific number of weeks per year. The requirement, which would allow the company to close plants temporarily based on market demand, would discourage the auto maker from moving production work outside the country or to non-union plants. The requirement is similar to the agreement that the UAW reached with Tenneco Inc.'s J.I. Case division, a Wisconsin farm-equipment maker.

GM officials, however, say they can't match the terms at J.I. Case because the auto maker needs to reduce its North American work force to cut costs. J.I. Case agreed to its job security provisions when its work force was at a historic low, GM officials say.

GM bargainers have indicated they might offer greater job security to the 280,000 UAW members currently working for the company. But that doesn't include some 30,000 now laid off indefinitely. Instead, GM has indicated it might be willing to boost compensation for those now laid off, some of whom receive payments from GM in addition to regular government unemployment benefits.

A key element in the discussions will be the current requirement that GM replace one of every two workers who retire or quit. The UAW wants to keep this provision in the contract. GM wants it eliminated to speed downsizing efforts. The company also is offering to sweeten pensions to encourage older workers to retire.

Officials on both sides say the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait has cast a shadow over the talks. The situation has intensified concerns about a broad economic downturn, and a worsening of the Big Three auto makers sales slump. "It's had a very sobering effect on negotiations," said a GM official close to the talks.

Still, UAW and GM officials agree the talks until now have gone better than expected. The two sides quickly agreed to language regarding basic health and safety issues. Bargainers aren't expecting much trouble over basic wage and benefit issues, either.

However, these matters remain secondary to job security and pensions. Said one GM official: "If we have a strike, everyone will know what it's about."
