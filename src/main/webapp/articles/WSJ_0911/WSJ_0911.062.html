Mercedes to Introduce 3 Diesel Models To Help It Meet U.S. Fuel Standards

  • Author: Krystal Miller
  • Date: 09/11/90

DETROIT -- Mercedes-Benz of North America said it will introduce three diesel models later this month, after nearly eliminating diesels from its U.S. product line during the last three years. Mercedes, a unit of Daimler-Benz AG of West Germany, said it is counting on the midsize 300D, and the larger 350SD and 350SDL turbos, to help it meet government fuel economy standards. The 1991 diesels range in price from $41,000 for the 300D model to $57,800 for the 350SDL.

Mercedes hasn't had a diesel among its medium or large-sized cars in the U.S. since 1987, and didn't sell many diesel-equipped versions of its compact 190 model. Company officials concede that some customers complained that the diesels performed poorly, and that diesels simply weren't particularly attractive in an era of low gasoline prices.

Now that era may be ending. While Mercedes was planning to reintroduce diesels before the Persian Gulf crisis sent gasoline prices soaring, it is clear the company was concerned that federal fuel-economy standards would increase in the long term.

The U.S. Senate is preparing to debate legislation that could raise the average standard to 40 miles a gallon or more by the year 2001. Currently, only a few subcompact cars meet that requirement. Diesel engines generally get better fuel economy than gasoline engines, though they also tend to have more tailpipe emissions. In fact, the new diesel models won't be sold in California, because they don't comply with that state's strict emissions standards.

Mercedes, however, says its new diesels will perform better, run quieter and have cleaner exhausts than their predecessors. Mercedes said the 1991 models also get more miles a gallon: The 300D, for example, gets 27 miles a gallon compared with the smaller 1974-model 240D, which got 24 miles a gallon.

At 27 miles to the gallon, the new 300D will come close to meeting the current federal corporate average fuel economy standard of 27.5 miles a gallon.

So far, Mercedes hasn't fared well in its attempts to meet existing fuel laws, because it builds big cars. Last year, Mercedes had to pay $20.4 million in federal fuel-economy penalties. The auto maker's estimated penalty for 1990 is $18.6 million, based on an average fuel economy of 21.2 miles a gallon for all cars it sold in the U.S. In 1989, the auto maker's average fuel economy was 21.4 miles a gallon on cars sold in the U.S.

"If you build large automobiles, it isn't easy to comply with fuel-economy regulations," Albert Weiss, general marketing manager for Mercedes-Benz of North America, said at a preview of the auto maker's 1991 model lineup. "If building big cars brings penalties, I guess we'll have to live with that."

The company also will have to live with federal "gas-guzzler" taxes of between $500 to $1,300 a car on about half of its 1991 model line.

Mercedes officials said they don't expect a big sales surge from the new diesels despite the Mideast crisis, because the rise in gasoline prices hasn't been strong or sustained enough to influence its customers. The company wouldn't project U.S. sales for next year. For the first eight months of this year, Mercedes sold just over 48,000 cars in the U.S., about equal with last year. For all of last year, though, the company's U.S. sales were 24% below the peak year of 1986.
