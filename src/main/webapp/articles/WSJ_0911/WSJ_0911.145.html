Your Money Matters: Bank Customers Gain Faster Access To Funds, but It's a Two-Way Street

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 09/11/90

Bank customers are getting faster access to funds deposited in their accounts under new federal banking rules that took effect Sept. 1. But there's a catch. Now customers have got to put money in their accounts more quickly to cover the checks they write. And in some cases, banks are imposing new limits on customers who make deposits in automated teller machines.

These changes are due to the final phase of a 1987 federal law that directs banks, thrifts and credit unions to give customers quicker access to funds deposited in their accounts. That also means that accounts are being debited more quickly.

For checks written on another local bank or other financial institution, depositors are getting use of the money within two business days after the day of deposit. That's one day sooner than they had been getting it since the last prior speed-up in check-clearing in 1988.

For checks written on a nonlocal institution, the funds must now be available within five business days after the day of deposit, instead of seven. As before, funds deposited in the form of cashier's checks, certified checks and government checks must be available the next business day.

The new schedule for funds availability is part of an effort to wring "float" out of the nation's financial system. Float is the time that elapses between the moment a check is deposited in a customer's account, the bank verifies that the check writer has an ample balance to cover the check, and the money is actually credited to the customer's account.

Many consumers long have used that time-lag to pay bills by check when their accounts lacked sufficient funds. Usually, they were counting on adequate funds being in their account by the time the check was presented for payment. With the new clearing schedule, that free ride is shorter.

But there are others who say the tightening of the check-clearing process is long overdue. Many consumers have long believed that bankers have dragged out the check-clearing process to use the float for their own benefit.

A 1986 survey by the U.S. Public Interest Research Group found that 53% of the 458 banks and thrifts that it surveyed held local checks anywhere from three to five days before crediting an account, and that 76% held out-of-state checks for more than one week.

But in the zeal to reduce float, bankers say, lawmakers have heightened the risk of fraud.

Bankers have argued that time is needed to catch checks backed by insufficient funds before money is credited to an account. While the shorter availability period is little problem for checks written on accounts with adequate balances, it poses real problems for returned checks. On average, it takes more than six business days for a bank to learn that a check is uncollectible.

Officials at two large East and West Coast banks say that they have been able to trace higher incidence in fraud, such as counterfeit checks, to the tighter clearing schedules. However, they didn't want their institutions identified and wouldn't quantify the sums lost to fraud.

Robert Hutchinson, senior vice president of Manufacturers Hanover Corp., says, "The trend in terms of fraud involving checks is definitely up, but it's impossible to determine how much of the problem is due to faster availability. The professional crooks are very knowledgeable about the regulations."

To counter the fraud risk, banks are taking various steps. The New York Clearing House Association, a group of large New York banks, has plans for clearing checks on other clearinghouse banks the same day that they are deposited. That will allow the clearinghouse banks to spot insufficient-funds checks before they are credited to an account.

The tighter clearing schedules could prompt some regional networks of automated teller machines to say they may no longer accept deposits.

In August, National Bank of Detroit, a unit of NBD Corp., announced that it would no longer allow its customers to make deposits at other institutions' ATMs. A bank spokeswoman said risks posed by the shortened availability period prompted the move.

And several regional ATM networks, such as the 2,900-ATM Money Stations network in Ohio, may consider refusing deposits. Edmond Gough, president of Money Station Inc., says, "We're hoping Congress amends the law before it gets to that."

Still, it's relatively rare for a bank customer to make a deposit using an ATM of a bank where he or she doesn't already have an account. For instance, the Money Station says that only 5.5% of the 4.5 million transactions that it handles each month involve such deposits.

Many had assumed that advances in ATM technology would allow banks to know within two days whether an account at another bank had adequate funds.

But that hasn't happened, although CoreStates Financial Corp., Philadelphia, is experimenting with an ATM that can read checks.

Marcia Z. Sullivan of the Consumer Bankers Association says, "The problem is that there isn't any way to read both the amount of the check and the routing code" electronically. "That has to be done manually, and it's too expensive."

The Fed has backed the banks up on this and is pushing to get the law amended so that a bank will have five business days to credit a deposit made at another bank's ATM for another four years.

Edmond Mierzwinski, a consumer advocate with the U.S. Public Interest Research Group, says, "We agreed not to oppose the amendment in the Senate, but that's not to say that we're supporting it. We're unsatisfied with how the banks have spent the last three years getting ready for this. They say the only solution is an ATM that reads the code and the amount."
