Foreign Exchange: Yen Surges Against Most Currencies, Pushing Dollar to a Four-Month Low

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/11/90

NEW YORK -- An increasingly popular yen pushed the dollar broadly lower yesterday. The Japanese currency led active and volatile trading, surging against most of its counterparts. The dollar declined almost three yen, or by about 2%, to end New York trading at a four-month low against its Japanese counterpart. The greenback also fell about a German pfennig to a two-month low against the mark and allowed the British pound to extend its gain nearly 1 1/2 U.S. cents, closing the day at an 18-month dollar high.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.6425 marks, down from 1.6530 marks late Monday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 148.30 yen, well below 151.15 yen in New York late Monday.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the dollar was trading at 148.50 yen soon after the opening, down from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 149.60 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at 1.6445 marks, up from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 1.6394 marks.

On Tuesday, the yen registered gains on the mark and, to a lesser extent, the pound. Briefly in Asian trading, cross-selling of the mark for yen allowed the pound cross rate to rise past three marks -- the level thought to be favored by the U.K. government for sterling's entry into the price bands of the European Monetary System.

Yesterday's global trading session, like many recently, was driven by interest rates. But rather than single-mindedly buying currencies, such as Australian and Canadian dollars and sterling, with a huge yield edge, investors moved their money around based on the smaller interest rate differentials, both real and potential, among the world's three leading currencies.

"The whole play is interest-rate based," said Peter Connelly, foreign exchange manager for Wells Fargo Bank in San Francisco

The yen's main source of strength was the renewed prospect of a tighter credit policy in Japan. Also buttressing the yen was speculation that leaders of the Group of Seven leading democracies meeting in Houston will issue a statement supporting the Japanese currency at the conclusion of their talks today.

Most currency dealers saw the spark for the yen's rally in a widely circulated report from Smick Medley International, a Washington-based investment advisory firm. The company said Sunday, "there is now an 80%-90% chance the Bank of Japan will raise interest rates over the next several months."

Tokyo traders said they sold the dollar on hearing talk about the report during the Asian morning Tuesday. When the Japanese translation appeared on domestic wire services soon after trading began for Tokyo's afternoon session, the dollar was sold off again.

Rumors about Bank of Japan tightening have been bandied about the market ever since Japanese negotiators acceded to a U.S. demand that the government of Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu spend more money on public works. Yen money market rates have been rising recently, and Japanese monetary officials have expressed concern at the quick growth of the nation's money supply.

In late New York trading the pound was trading at $1.8230 and 2.9943 marks, above $1.8080 and 2.9886 marks late Monday. The British currency, however, closed at 270.35 yen, down from 273.28 yen late Monday.

The dollar closed at its lowest level against the yen since Feb. 23, when it stood at 147.07 yen. Against the mark, the greenback finished at its lowest level since May 11, when it traded at 1.6380 marks. The last time sterling finished at such a high dollar level was Jan. 3, 1989, when a pound bought $1.8225.

"The yen is catching up with the Deutsche mark," said Mr. Connelly of Wells Fargo, noting that the Japanese currency lagged other non-dollar currencies when the greenback began it current downward trend in June.

Added Tomi Kilgore of Toyo Trust & Banking Co.'s New York office, "the yen is getting a lot of support on the crosses." The market "isn't only selling dollars, it's buying yen."

Mr. Connelly commented that no interest rate changes need occur for the dollar's troubles to continue. "Even if the (U.S. Federal Reserve Board) doesn't do anything, the dollar will lose ground," he said.

Mr. Kilgore said that the U.S. is currently the only nation with a major currency whose monetary bias is toward easing. As long as that situation continues, the market will be eager to sell dollars, he said.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $355.50 an ounce, down $2.70. Estimated volume was a moderate 4.2 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $354.75 an ounce.
