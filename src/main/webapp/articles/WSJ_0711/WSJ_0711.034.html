Letters to the Editor -- Voice of America: `I Was a Contender'

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/11/90

Dorothy Rabinowitz's well-researched article on the continuing popularity of the World War II television documentaries (Leisure & Arts, June 18) confirms the impression one gets on scanning daily TV listings. And her analysis of what lies behind the phenomenon is certainly correct. We wonder, however, if one couldn't take it a step further. World War II was undoubtedly America's finest hour, certainly in this century. The courage we displayed in response to treacherous and unprovoked attack, the industrial genius that permitted the war to be pursued on the vast scale necessitated by global fronts, the relative cheerfulness of the civilian public in accepting the sacrifices imposed on them, the myriad acts of heroism, individual and collective, by our land, sea and air forces as they pushed the war to victory and, perhaps most important, the generosity America displayed toward its defeated enemies -- all this is something to look back upon with appropriate pride, with swelling heart and filling eye, America at its very best.

But alas, it all happened nearly half a century ago, and there has been little in our history since then to excite our pride to anything like that degree, and much to be ashamed of. No wonder we want to see the pictures and hear the words over and over again.

Does an analogy suggest itself? To the stock figure of the broken-down prizefighter or the has-been entertainer getting out his scrapbooks and poring over the old pictures and press notices by the hour and endlessly telling anybody who will listen the story of his triumphs long ago?

It's a chilling thought.

Bruce and Susanmary Young

Allegan, Mich.
