Technology & Health: Doctors Connect Forms of Niacin To Liver Damage

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 07/11/90

Doctors reported four cases of liver damage in patients who took sustained-release forms of the vitamin niacin that they had bought without a prescription. Niacin, one of the B vitamins, is known to be capable of causing liver damage in some patients in high doses. But in their new reports in the Journal of the American Medical Association, doctors suggest that the "sustained-release" or "slow-release" forms sold by a large number of vitamin packagers may be capable of damaging the liver in lower doses and in a much shorter time.

Niacin, also known as nicotinic acid, is widely available in pharmacies and health food stores without a prescription. Recently, doctors have been increasingly prescribing it for heart patients with high blood cholesterol levels because of niacin's ability to lower LDL-cholesterol, the "bad" type of blood cholesterol, while raising HDL-cholesterol, the "good" type. Niacin also is one of the cheapest available drugs capable of lowering cholesterol levels.

Most often, doctors prescribe the regular crystalline form of niacin. But the new reports indicate that patients often switch, on their own, to one of the many sustained-release forms of the vitamin. The sustained-release forms need be taken less frequently during the day, and are less likely to cause unpleasant flushing of the face, which the vitamin sometimes induces.

In one of the new reports, two doctors at the University of Alabama in Birmingham and their colleague, a doctor at Johns Hopkins University in Baltimore, describe three patients who developed drug-induced hepatitis after taking sustained-release forms of niacin.

One, a 62-year-old man with a high cholesterol level, had been taking 4,000 milligrams of crystalline niacin a day as prescribed by his doctor. Five days after he switched on his own to a sustained-release niacin, he fainted and was taken to the hospital, where laboratory tests indicated liver damage. He recovered and subsequently resumed using the crystalline niacin without any problems, Jere P. Segrest of the University of Alabama and his colleagues reported.

Similar episodes of liver damage were experienced by a 50-year-old woman whose doctor prescribed the sustained-release niacin, and by a 47-year-old man who switched to a sustained-release niacin at the suggestion of his pharmacist, the physicians reported.

In a separate letter to the medical journal, Howard N. Hodis at the University of Southern California medical school in Los Angeles reported the case of a 32-year-old man who developed acute liver failure while taking one 500-milligram "super-timed-release" niacin tablet a day for two months. The man recovered after 10 days in the hospital.

"Awareness of the potentially greater {liver toxicity} of sustained-release niacin and the fact that low doses of such preparations of niacin can lead to the same complications as high doses is important for both the physician and the public," Dr. Hodis wrote.
