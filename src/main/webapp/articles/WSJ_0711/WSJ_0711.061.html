Credit Markets: Government Meets With Huge Success in Sale Of Resolution Funding's Thrift `Bailout' Bonds

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 07/11/90

NEW YORK -- The Resolution Funding Corp.'s sale of 30-year "bailout bonds" was a huge success, a sign that investors are far more comfortable with 30-year securities than the oddball 40-year variety the agency sold previously. Resolution Funding, or Refcorp, is the federal agency created to raise cash to finance the government's bailout of the troubled thrift industry. Yesterday's auction was Refcorp's fourth, and marked the agency's return to issuing 30-year bonds after two disastrous auctions of 40-year bonds in January and April.

But unlike its previous bombs, yesterday's auction "was tremendous," said Alan McClymonds, co-head of government trading at First Boston Corp. He said that bidding for the bonds was stronger than at some recent Treasury auctions.

The Treasury, which auctioned the bonds yesterday on behalf of Refcorp, said it sold $5.03 billion of Refcorp bonds at an average yield of 8.93%, nearly 0.40 percentage point higher than yields on 30-year Treasury bonds. At the first Refcorp auction of 30-year bonds in October, the average yield was 8.15%.

The Treasury said it received a total of $15.48 billion in bids for the $5.03 billion bonds it sold, a bid-to-cover ratio of 3.1-to-one. That was the best bid-to-cover ratio at any Refcorp auction and even better than on the Treasury's own 30-year bonds in recent auctions.

The Refcorp sale "saw unusually strong demand by any standards, and compared to the Refcorp 40-year auctions, it was like night and day," said F. Ward McCarthy, a managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates Inc.

Buyers ran the whole gamut of investors. Individuals, life insurance companies and pension funds were attracted by the relatively high yield.

Other buyers, such as corporations and municipalities, apparently bought bonds as part of a complex plan to defease existing debt. Recently, one credit rating agency and the accounting firm of Deloitte & Touche issued reports suggesting the credit quality of Refcorp debt is high enough to use for defeasing purposes. The Treasury Department encouraged the firms to issue the reports.

"We had been working on a number of ways to increase demand for Refcorp bonds and this was one of them," said Michael E. Basham, deputy assistant secretary of the Treasury.

Defeasance is a technique whereby a bond issuer may discharge old, low-rate debt by purchasing a smaller amount of higher-yielding U.S. Treasury debt. The Treasurys are put in a trust to service the old debt being retired.

While the Refcorp bonds don't carry the full faith and credit backing of the U.S., the Treasury guarantees interest payments, and principal payments are secured by zero-coupon Treasurys, purchased by Refcorp from the government.

In a recent report, J.P. Morgan Securities Inc. cited the Deloitte & Touche report as a factor that would stimulate demand for Refcorp bonds.

Traders also said many bond-market arbitragers were attracted to the wide "spread," or gap, between yields on Refcorp bonds and 30-year Treasury bonds, which yielded about 8.56% yesterday. Some of these arbs expect the gap to narrow in the next few weeks, which should cause Refcorp prices to rise.

Traders at First Boston estimate that arbs purchased about $2.5 billion of the Refcorp bonds while selling U.S. Treasury 30-year securities. The selling was one factor that caused Treasury prices to move lower. The benchmark 30-year bond fell 1/4 point, or $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The bonds briefly rose after the Refcorp auction results were released, but didn't hold those levels very long.

Treasury Securities

Although Treasury bond prices ended lower, analysts said the bond market showed surprising firmness considering that the value of the U.S. dollar sank yesterday.

Typically, bonds prices fall in tandem with a decline in the dollar. But that relationship may be changing, at least when the Japanese yen is involved. According to James Capra, senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc., Treasury prices have recently held firm or risen on days when the dollar is off against the yen. Mr. Capra said he first noticed the pattern in early May.

The bond market doesn't react negatively to a rising yen anymore, he said, largely due to speculation that a stronger yen encourages Japanese investors to jump into the U.S. market and buy Treasury bonds, which then of course are viewed as cheap.

In trading, the benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 101 27/32 to yield 8.56% compared with 102 4/32 to yield 8.54% on Monday. The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 101 28/32 to yield 8.57% compared with 102 to yield 8.55% on Monday.

Short-term rates were unchanged.

Corporate, Junk Bonds

Long Island Lighting Co. bonds outperformed the broader investment-grade bond market after Monday's credit rating upgrade from Standard & Poor's Corp. The utility recently settled two suits that posed threats to its health.

Lilco's 8 5/8 general and refunding bonds due in 2007 were 5/8 point higher since S&P's announcement and about three points higher since a federal appeals court ruling on July 2 that effectively settled a dispute with New York State concerning cost overruns at a nuclear power plant. The bonds now trade at 90 to yield 9.90%.

In the junk-bond market, bonds of Revlon Inc. jumped after company owner Ronald Perelman said he would sell a 15% stake in a subsidiary company to help repay bank debt. Revlon's 11.75% senior subordinated notes jumped 1 7/8 point to 90 7/8. Junk bonds are high-yield, speculative-grade securities that have low credit ratings or no ratings at all.

Municipal Bonds

Tax-exempt issues ended unchanged to 1/8 point higher.

In one of the largest deals of the session, a PaineWebber Inc. group tentatively priced $870 million of New York State Dormitory Authority revenue and special obligation revenue bonds for the State University Educational Facility.

The bonds were priced to yield from 5.80% for special obligation bonds due in 1991 to 7.68% for revenue bonds due in 2014.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% of 2018 were 1/8 higher at 98 3/4 to yield 7.30%.

Ahead on the calendar, Phoenix, Ariz., is scheduled to sell today $137.6 million of bonds, including $65 million street and highway user revenue bonds, via competitive bid at noon EDT.

No large negotiated issues are expected to be priced today.

Foreign Bonds

Although the Japanese yen soared yesterday, Japanese government bonds ended weaker due to renewed speculation that the Bank of Japan will soon raise its discount rate.

Feeding the latest speculation about rates was a statement by Washington D.C.-based investment advisory firm Smick Medley International that Japan's central bank is highly likely to boost its discount rate "in the next several months."

Smick Medley's report, which didn't cite any sources, serves to reinforce recent expectations that the Bank of Japan's grip on credit is going to remain tight and may get tighter in the coming months. For several weeks analysts in Tokyo have been saying that the chances are increasing that the Bank of Japan will boost its discount rate again to combat the money supply's explosive growth and other potential inflationary pressures as well as defend the yen's recent recovery.

At the 3 p.m. JST close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the benchmark 10-year yen bond was quoted at a price of 86.74, down 0.22, to yield 7.255%. (Yields quoted here are in local terms and differ from yields calculated for this market by U.S. investment firms, which tend to use the semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets.)

Mortgage-backed securities

Mortgage-securities prices ended unchanged to off less than 1/8 point after an uninspired session.

Three real-estate mortgage investment conduit offerings for which pricing details were announced Monday appear to be off to a good sales start, traders said.

The offerings are a Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. $500 million remic from Prudential-Bache Securities Inc., backed by 9.5% Freddie Mac securities; a $400 million Government National Mortgage Association issue from Fund America Investors Corp. by Kidder, Peabody & Co., backed by Ginnie Mae 10% securities; and a $400 million Federal National Mortgage Association issue by Morgan Stanley & Co., backed by Fannie Mae 9.5% securities.

Among actively traded secondary issues, Ginnie Mae 9.5% were off 1/32 at 99 1/32 to yield 9.77%.
