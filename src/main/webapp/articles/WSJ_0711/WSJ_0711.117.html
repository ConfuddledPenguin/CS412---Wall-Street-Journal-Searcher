Summit Leaders Agree to Study Soviet Economy --- U.S. Holds Talks to Resolve Old Claims So Moscow Can Use Capital Markets

  • Author: Peter Truell and Michel McQueen
  • Date: 07/11/90

HOUSTON -- The leaders of the industrial world agreed to make a major study of the Soviet economy by year end to define that country's needs, as the U.S. disclosed it is negotiating with the Soviets to resolve longstanding financial claims as a prelude to lifting restrictions on Soviet borrowing in U.S. capital markets. But at the annual economic summit here, leaders of the U.S., Japan, West Germany, Italy, Britain, France, Canada and the European Community weren't able to break the deadlock on agricultural subsidies in current international trade talks. President Bush and Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney rejected a compromise proposed by British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher yesterday morning, U.S. and British officials said.

Later in the day, Mr. Bush presented a U.S. compromise proposal on agriculture based on a recent paper by Aart de Zeeuw, chairman of the agricultural negotiating committee at the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, according to U.S. Agriculture Secretary Clayton Yeutter.

The paper was intended to serve as a catalyst for the GATT talks in Geneva. The negotiations pit major food-exporting countries such as the U.S., which favor the eventual elimination of farm subsidies, against the EC, which argues that such subsidies are needed to maintain Europe's social fabric.

Mr. de Zeeuw proposed dividing agricultural subsidies into four categories: domestic supports such as direct payments to farmers; measures applied at borders, such as import quotas and tariffs; export subsidies; and health and sanitary requirements imposed on importers.

Mr. de Zeeuw recommended that trading nations agree to put a ceiling on internal supports, according to officials familiar with the text of his plan. He would convert all border measures to tariffs. Export subsidies would be reduced quickly and eventually eliminated. And nations would adopt a code to assure that health regulations aren't used to restrict trade.

"In my opinion, we'll either have some appropriate reference to the {GATT proposal} or we'll agree to disagree," Mr. Yeutter said of the summit. "It's too late to paper over our differences," the agriculture secretary said. "The president understands that, and he's communicated that to his negotiating colleagues."

Staff members were preparing to work through the night in hopes of reaching an agreement by the time the summit concludes today.

The summit's political communique, published yesterday, masked the tensions on trade and focused instead on the increasing acceptance of market-oriented democracy throughout the world. The 1990s should be "a decade of democracy," the communique said, welcoming recent moves toward democracy in Eastern Europe and Latin America. President Bush and the other summit leaders also gave a qualified endorsement to the more tentative political and economic changes in the Soviet Union and South Africa.

In that context, the summit nations agreed yesterday to proceed with a study of the Soviet economy and its requirements. The study would be conducted by the International Monetary Fund, the World Bank, the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development and the new European Bank for Reconstruction and Development, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady told reporters.

The study is a compromise between the U.S. and the EC, which recently commissioned a separate study. That would have excluded the U.S., Canada and Japan. The new study will draw on the much greater resources of the IMF and World Bank, which are dominated by the U.S., and American officials believe these organizations will do a more rigorous analysis of Soviet needs.

The Soviets also could soon have better access to U.S. financial markets, which has been blocked under U.S. laws that prohibit debtors in default from returning to American financial markets. The Soviet Union, struggling to modernize its economy, is willing to negotiate its debts outstanding, while the U.S. is prepared to help the Soviets gain access to financial markets.

The Soviet Union has substantial debts to the U.S. unsettled from earlier eras. These include $300 million in borrowings by the Czarist government and the provisional Kerensky government prior to the Bolshevik revolution of 1917. The sum owed would amount to many billions of dollars if accrued interest is taken into account. The Soviets also owe the U.S. about $700 million for military hardware and other war-related aid provided during World War II.

"We'll have to find a way to resolve it somewhere short of their claim and our claim," said Secretary of State James Baker. "It's a negotiation under way." Besides being ineligible for commercial bank credit from the U.S., the Soviets can't receive loans backed by U.S. government guarantees because of the disputed loans.

The Soviet Union and its floundering economy have attracted much of the attention at this summit. President Mikhail Gorbachev wrote to President Bush before the gathering asking for economic aid and technical assistance. The U.S. so far has extended only limited technical assistance to the Soviets.

Asked whether the U.S. would advance economic aid to the Soviets, Mr. Baker said, "I don't think you should ever say never." But he added, "There are, as I think we articulated, particular problems that face the United States today in using taxpayer dollars to provide economic assistance to the Soviet Union." The U.S. approach could change if the Soviet Union makes a fundamental effort "toward free-market reform," reduces the scope of military spending and cuts aid to governments such as Cuba, which he said, "support instability."

In their political communique, the summit leaders affirmed they would help countries that choose freedom, and they said political and economic freedoms are "closely linked and mutually reinforcing." They said Europe is "at the dawn of a new era" because of changes in Eastern Europe and the unification of Germany, but added that the industrial countries shouldn't neglect reforms elsewhere, including such countries as Nepal, Namibia and Chile.

The communique said sanctions should remain on China because of the massacre of pro-democracy demonstrators in Beijing. But it acknowledged some "recent developments" there. The summit also offered the prospect of some expansion in World Bank lending to China, particularly to address environmental concerns.

The language on China was designed to finesse differences between Japan and the other summit nations. Unlike its partners at the summit, Japan favors resuming lending to China and soon will restart a $5 billion loan held up since the Beijing killings in June 1989.

The summit group congratulated Namibia and Chile on their democracies, and urged Cuba and Romania to do more to foster democracy. It also sounded a cautionary note on the surge of ethnic and religious violence, saying "such intolerance can lead to conflicts, which can threaten fundamental human rights."

On South Africa, the summit leaders said they hope for "a peaceful transition to a non-racial democracy and the complete dismantlement of the apartheid system." The group called on all parties to "refrain from violence or its advocacy."
