Turf War: Markets Fight for Private Placements

  • Author: Craig Torres and William Power
  • Date: 07/11/90

NEW YORK -- The two biggest U.S. stock exchanges will launch systems for trading privately placed stocks and bonds, in separate efforts to unseat the National Association of Securities Dealers' new electronic Portal system. The American Stock Exchange, in a joint venture with Reuters Holdings PLC, and the New York Stock Exchange are each seeking to share in the expected boom in the $170 billion private-placement market.

The two stock exchanges are trying to catch up with the NASD's Portal system. Launched last month, Portal was the first out of the gate for trading privately placed stocks and bonds that fall under the government's so-called Rule 144a passed in April.

The stakes are high. Becoming the primary market for privately placed securities, which are traded by large institutions and exempted from normal disclosure requirements, means more income in years to come, and an avenue to more listings by foreign companies that are expected to raise money in the U.S. in coming years.

Even more important, dominance in trading privately placed securities would give the winning market an important foothold in electronic stock trading for institutional investors, clearly a growing trend in the 1990s.

Eventually, only one electronic system for trading private placements will dominate, exchange officials agree.

"It will be difficult to have three formal controlled markets," says Joseph Hardiman, president of the NASD. Indeed, Mr. Hardiman sees Portal as a vindication of the electronic trading system his Nasdaq over-the-counter market system has used for years for publicly traded stocks.

"Increasingly, you are seeing the institutional market move to a screen-based market -- whether it's trading options, futures or ordinary shares," says Mr. Hardiman. "I don't think that exchanges are going to fold up their tents. But they will adapt to what the market is telling them."

Enacted by the Securities and Exchange Commission in April, Rule 144a is designed to encourage capital raising and trading in the private placement market. That market is likely to be limited to several hundred large institutional investors and several dozen investment banks. Small investors don't qualify as participants under SEC rules.

But securities executives say that the separate challenges from the Amex and the Big Board underscore some of the drawbacks they see in the NASD's Portal, which stands for Private Offerings, Resales and Trading through Automated Linkages. Portal is an electronic billboard which allows traders and institutions to advertise buying and selling prices of privately placed stocks and bonds.

Since Portal went live June 15, the NASD has signed on nearly 30 institutional investors and about 20 major investment banks. So far, about 10,000 shares of stock have traded on Portal, according to the NASD. Firms such as First Boston Corp. and Goldman, Sachs & Co. are already making live markets on Portal.

Monte E. Wetzler, a lawyer at Breed, Abbott & Morgan in New York who has made a speciality of Rule 144a and its implications, says: "Portal has been slower in getting out of the box than people thought."

The Amex and Reuters hope to offer a more flexible system that builds on Reuters' technology and huge share of financial market data. By the first quarter of 1991, they hope to have their "Situs" (System for Institutional Trading of Unregistered Securities) trading system up and running.

Individuals familiar with Situs say Reuters's world-wide securities data base, software capabilities, and links to big investors through products such as Instinet, an electronic trading system, could mount a powerful challenge to Portal.

Situs will be able to show exchange rates, home-market prices for a stock, and live U.S. quotes for the unregistered security all on the same screen, according to people familiar with the system. Like Portal, Situs will have an automatic execution system.

Individuals familiar with the talks said that Instinet and Reuters's U.S. subsidiary have approved of the Situs project, but the matter is awaiting final approval by senior Reuters executives in England.

American Stock Exchange President James Jones says: "We think our system will be superior to Portal. It's going to be head-on competition. We will have better display, better systems and better hardware."

What the Amex doesn't have, however, is Portal's head start, which the NASD is trying to solidify by not charging any fees to use the system now. And NASD officials say they can upgrade Portal to match efforts by competitors.

Big Board President Richard Grasso won't discuss details of the exchange's own private placement trading system. He says the exchange is studying Portal and is waiting for the Amex system, with the hopes of improving on both.

"I don't think there is an insurmountable competitive advantage {the NASD} gains by being up," Mr. Grasso says. "Being late may be advantageous."

The Big Board says that if there is enough institutional demand, it could have a system on trading desks around the country by the end of this year.

Trading executives say the Big Board's name will be a powerful marketing tool with foreign issuers. They note, too, that an unregistered securities trading system would be the third major recent effort by the Big Board to cater to large institutions. The others are the exchange's stock basket product and planned after-hours trading.
