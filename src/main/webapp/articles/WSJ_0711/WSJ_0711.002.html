DRT Says Affiliate In U.K. Is Planning To Merge on Aug. 1

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/11/90

NEW YORK -- DRT International, an accounting firm whose affiliates include Deloitte & Touche, said its British member firm, Touche Ross & Co., will merge with the U.K. practice of Spicer & Oppenheim. The combination, planned for Aug. 1, would create Britain's fifth-largest accounting firm, moving Touche from sixth place and Spicer from ninth place. The resulting firm, which will retain the Touche Ross & Co. name, will have annual fees of more than #262 million ($473.7 million.)

A spokesman for DRT in New York said the merger of the firms "significantly strengthens" its operations in Britain. DRT says it is the world's third-largest accounting group, with annual fee income of $3.68 billion.

DRT was formed after the merger last year of the U.S. accounting firms Deloitte, Haskins & Sells and Touche Ross & Co. Deloitte & Touche is the third-largest U.S. accounting firm. Some international affiliates of the accounting firms, including the British firm of Deloitte Haskins & Sells, declined to participate in the world-wide merger. Although Britain's Touche Ross kept its DRT affiliation, the British Deloitte Haskins affiliate merged instead with the U.K. affiliate of Coopers & Lybrand, resulting in the largest U.K. accounting firm.

"Deloitte & Touche had a big hole there {Britain} after Deloitte Haskins jumped to Coopers," said Arthur W. Bowman, editor of Bowman's Accounting Report. Mr. Bowman speculated that some European partners may have "rebelled" against what they could have viewed as the domination of the practice by U.S. partners.

Touche Ross officials, while conceding the firm would have ranked in first or second place in Britain if Deloitte Haskins had participated in the world-wide merger, maintained Touche has been courting Spicer & Oppenheim in Britain for the past 10 years. "They are one of the city of London's best-known names," said George Westropp, a Touche Ross partner. He said Spicer & Oppenheim, whose clients include many investment firms, has been hurt by the slump in the financial district in the past year. "They felt they had a need to belong to a bigger firm," he said.
