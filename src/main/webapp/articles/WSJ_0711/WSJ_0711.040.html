Letters to the Editor: You're Right -- It's Unmentionable

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/11/90

Regarding your June 12 editorial "Unmentionable Prescription": In your unending crusade for a reduction in the current capital-gains tax rate, you are now claiming that as the result of such a move "Real-estate values would improve overnight . . . since the unmentionable prescription would lower a tax on potential gains." Well, maybe, but probably not. Even less well conceived is the idea that "The tax was . . . lower when the properties . . . were initially purchased; their current low values . . . reflect a higher tax." During the early 1980s, the real-estate industry convinced the Reagan administration and Congress that a quick tax fix for high interest rates was both fair and necessary. The exact rules changed several times over the next few years, but by the time of the 1986 Tax Reform Act, real property investors were depreciating their appreciating holdings over 15 years at accelerated rates. The resulting annual "tax savings" were at income-tax rates that, I'm sure you will remember, were 50% at that time, not 28%. The most egregious cases of legal tax abuse came with limited partnerships, through which limited partners did no work but saved taxes, and general partners earned high fees and supposedly managed property.

I doubt that the capital-gains tax was even considered by many real-estate investors, whose gaze was fixed on all those immediate deductions. If they had considered the long-run consequences they would have realized that the capital-gains tax on depreciable assets is applied not to the gain in price but to the difference between the net sales price and the depreciated book value. Whether the rate is 20% or 28% matters less than the fact that the amount taxed is the entire value of the property after 15 years. But after all, what the tax man has given, the tax man can take away. You only get to "save taxes" once.

Naturally, the short-term quick tax fix led to overbuilding, poor management decisions, outrageous fee schedules and poorly conceived projects. The industry is paying for the previous "prescription" now with high vacancy rates, useless projects, and a severely constrained lending industry that lost sight of its own need to evaluate projects for realistic income potential, as well as tax saving.

A return to a 20% capital-gains rate has not been necessary to increasing prices in the stock market and will do nothing for the real-estate market. High vacancy rates, abandoned projects, changing demographics and a still-reeling, blind-sided lending industry are real economic problems that will not respond to another quick-tax-fix prescription.

Deborah Ann Ford

Associate Professor of Finance

University of Baltimore

Baltimore

---

You are right on the mark when you say that in order to pump some life into the nation's sluggish economy "we need . . . a policy that would increase the values of real estate. . . ." After all, the real-estate and construction industries are the largest sector of our economy.

However, your prescription -- cut the capital-gains tax -- would be only a good left jab. In order to knock the problem out, it would have to be followed by an overhand right -- namely, slash interest rates. This would allow more buyers into the market and reduce the carrying costs for investors and developers.

At their current levels, interest rates are simply too high to make real-estate projects feasible; thus, construction is grinding to a halt. It is no coincidence that Japan, which until recently has had very low interest rates, also has the highest real-estate values in the world.

Roderick T. Russo

President

Coventry Homes Inc.

Kinnelon, N.J.
