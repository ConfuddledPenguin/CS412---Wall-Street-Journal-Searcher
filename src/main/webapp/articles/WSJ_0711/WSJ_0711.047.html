Report Links Mouth Sores With Smokeless Tobacco

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/11/90

CHICAGO -- Boys and young men who emulate their baseball heroes by chewing tobacco risk developing mouth sores, dental problems and perhaps even cancer, a new report says. In a study of 1,109 major and minor league baseball players, health researchers found that nearly half of frequent users of smokeless tobacco developed white, callus-type sores on their gums. Such players were also more likely than those who didn't use the smokeless tobacco to have other gum problems that could ultimately lead to loss of teeth later in life.

The study, published in today's Journal of the American Medical Association, adds to growing concern about the health effects of smokeless tobacco -- including chewing tobacco and snuff -- and increasing use of the products by children.

"The players know in general that it has bad health effects," says Deborah G. Grady, assistant professor of epidemiology and medicine at the University of California at San Francisco, and a co-author of the study. "A lot of young boys emulate them and they're concerned about that."

About 75% of the players in the study used snuff; other players used a coarser wad of chewing tobacco.

The researchers didn't uncover any evidence of cancer among the players, most of whom were between 20 and 29 years old. But other studies have linked long-term use of snuff in particular with oral cancer, Dr. Grady says.
