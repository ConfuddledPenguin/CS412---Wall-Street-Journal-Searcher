Tuesday's Markets: Dollar Falls On Prospect Of Yen Rise --- Currency Weakness Hurts Bond Prices; Stocks Drop 23.27

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 07/11/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar fell broadly against all major foreign currencies amid expectations of higher Japanese interest rates and a stronger yen. The dollar's weakness helped kill an early-morning rise in bond prices. Stocks fell under the weight of last-minute sell programs. The Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped 23.27 points to close at 2890.84 in moderate trading.

The drop in the dollar stemmed directly from rumors out of Washington and Houston, site of the economic summit, that the Group of Seven major industrial nations favors a strong yen and a stable mark. The dollar fell to 148.30 yen from 151.15 yen in late New York trading Monday, breaking through the psychologically important level of 150 yen. The dollar dropped to 1.6425 marks from 1.6530 marks.

Jeffrey Mondschein, managing director of foreign exchange at Continental Bank, said the prospect of higher Japanese rates made it hard for the dollar to gain any ground against other currencies, especially in the absence of any hope for easing interest rates elsewhere overseas.

"Yields from holding foreign investments right now are far superior" to those on dollar investments, Mr. Mondschein said, and the expectation that the underlying foreign currencies will appreciate only adds to the appeal.

The rumor of an imminent rise in Japanese rates was "the one the currency market latched onto," said Nicholas Sargen, an analyst at Salomon Brothers. "The trigger mechanism is that there is an economic summit going on, which lends credence to any rumor, no matter how erroneous."

Not that Mr. Sargen doubts the possibility of higher interest rates in Japan. A more likely reason to suspect such a move, he said, is that the overnight money rate in Japan crept up at the end of June. Japan's central bank now may be contemplating a rate boost because of a continuing stream of evidence that Japan's domestic economy isn't slowing down, he said.

The latest number, released early in the day, showed that machinery orders in Japan, an indicator of economic growth, were up 13.4% in May from a year ago.

The retreat in the dollar was bad news for U.S. bonds, said Philip Braverman, chief economist at DKB Securities Corp., "because it creates a potential loss for foreign buyers." But he said a successful auction of Resolution Funding Corp. bonds late in the day bolstered hopes that the bond market is poised to improve.

Stock prices fell on volume of 147.6 million shares in New York Stock Exchange trading. Declining issues far outnumbered advancers, 969 to 529.

Bond prices fell about a quarter of a point, or about $2.50 for each $1,000 face amount. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 8.56%.
