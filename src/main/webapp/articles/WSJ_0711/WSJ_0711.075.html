U.S. Jury Finds USX, 2 Union Officials Guilty

  • Author: Gregory Stricharchuk
  • Date: 07/11/90

A federal jury in Birmingham, Ala., found USX Corp. guilty of 14 criminal counts in connection with a conspiracy to unfairly influence a labor contract in 1983. The Pittsburgh-based energy and steel concern, which faces maximum fines of $4.1 million, said it was "extremely disappointed" and plans to appeal the verdict to the U.S. Court of Appeals.

Two United Steelworkers union officials, E.B. Rich, 60 years old, and Thermon Phillips, 62, were also convicted. Messrs. Rich and Phillips each were found guilty of two counts, including conspiracy and violation of a provision of the Taft-Hartley Act barring union representatives from taking something of value from an employer. They each face up to 10 years in prison and $500,000 in fines.

The verdicts in the 14-week trial came after two weeks of jury deliberations.

The only USX official indicted, former top labor negotiator J. Bruce Johnston, has yet to face trial on a four-count indictment, according to a spokesman at the U.S. attorney's office.

USX said it believes there was "clear error in the conduct of the trial which will lead to a reversal of the verdicts." The company added that it believes the "indictments and verdicts were the products of strained and erroneous interpretations of complex statutes."

The company said it continues to believe that its officers and employees acted properly throughout the events prior to and following the Fairfield Works labor contract of 1983.

Sentencing is scheduled for Aug. 20 before U.S. Judge E.B. Haltom.

The case stemmed from 1983 labor negotiations when USX, then U.S. Steel Corp., sought substantial concessions from steelworkers to reopen its idled Fairfield works near Birmingham.

The U.S. attorney's office said evidence showed that USX and Mr. Phillips, district director of the United Steelworkers, and Mr. Rich, sub-director of the union, conspired to violate the Taft-Hartley Act. One of the purposes of the act is to eliminate practices that might impair the impartiality of union representatives or corrupt collective bargaining.

Specifically, the government said USX influenced the two men by arranging for their pensions to be enriched beyond what they would have received as regular company employees. For instance, Mr. Rich received a pension based on 30.9 years of service while he had actually worked only 13 years for the company. Mr. Phillips received a pension based on 37.75 years of employment while he had only worked at the company for five years, between 1946 and 1951.

The case against Mr. Johnston, who faces charges of violating federal law by concealing information about the pension change and failing to inform employees about it, is expected to receive a boost from the USX verdict.

In addition, 38 Fairfield workers filed suit in 1988 to recover income they lost as a result of what they consider an illegal pact. Their case may also receive a boost from yesterday's verdict.

The union said it expects the convictions to be overturned on appeal. The union said the trial was marked by error and that testimony that was barred "would have shown that the pension arrangements at issue were widespread in American industry, served proper labor-relations purposes and never had been regarded as unlawful by the Department of Labor."

The case may influence events far beyond the courtroom. The Steelworkers union suffered a sharp decline in membership during the 1980s as steel companies closed plants, streamlined and laid off thousands of members. The union began trying to organize smaller plants and businesses in the service industry to make up for those losses.

Yesterday's verdict, however, could damage the union's reputation and its credibility as a representative of workers. Even before the verdict, anti-union groups were pinning articles about the case on company bulletin boards to thwart union organizing attempts.

USX, for its part, has traditionally been a tough negotiator with the steelworkers. The two are to begin negotiations by the end of this year on a new national contract. Most other major steelmakers negotiated pacts last year, and USX officials have called them too generous. The combination of a union seeking to regain credibility damaged by the verdict, and a company pledged to maintain a hard line could lead to rancor in those talks.
