Canadian Court Says Tribunal Can't Cite Firms for Contempt

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/11/90

OTTAWA -- The Canadian Federal Appeal Court ruled that Canada's Competition Tribunal doesn't have the power to punish for contempt those who fail to comply with its orders. The tribunal, which was established in 1986, hears competition cases and issues orders against anti-competitive practices. The agency's jurisdiction was challenged by Chrysler Canada Ltd., a unit of Chrysler Corp.

Chrysler Canada was summoned before the tribunal for alleged contempt of a tribunal order requiring the company to supply auto parts to Richard Brunet Co., a Montreal concern. It appealed the contempt case to the Federal Appeal Court, claiming that the tribunal didn't have the jurisdiction to punish for contempt.

The court, in granting Chrysler Canada's appeal, said it might be desirable for the tribunal to have the power to punish for contempt. But the court said "it seems that Parliament thought otherwise" when it passed legislation establishing the tribunal in June 1986. The tribunal's members include Federal Court judges and experts in such fields as business and economics.

The federal government's Bureau of Competition Policy, which brought the case against Chrysler Canada before the Competition Tribunal, said it was studying the court ruling and would issue a statement later.

The appeal court's decision is the Competition Tribunal's second jurisdictional setback this year. In April, the Quebec Superior Court ruled unconstitutional various operations of the tribunal and certain merger provisions of the federal Competition Act. The Quebec Superior Court ruling has been appealed to the Quebec Court of Appeal by the Bureau of Competition Policy.
