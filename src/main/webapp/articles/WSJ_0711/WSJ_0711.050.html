And They Don't Want Anybody Walking the Dog Near It, Either

  • Author: Jim Herron Zamora
  • Date: 07/11/90

Perhaps only God can make a tree, but Marvin Davis is claiming that one is his own "intellectual property." His Pebble Beach Co., owner of the scenic golf and hotel resort on the central California shore front, has informed photographers that the Lone Cypress standing on a rocky point of its land is a company-trademarked image.

In November, company attorneys told Westlight, a Los Angeles photo agency that had sold a Lone Cypress photo, to stop marketing images of the tree. The same month, says free-lance photographer Ed Young, Pebble Beach denied him permission to take pictures if he planned to sell them "in violation of their trademark."

That has pro photographers scurrying to their lawyers for rebuttals to Pebble Beach's view of the trademark laws.

"Is Mount Rushmore next?" asks Robert M. Cavallo, whose New York-based Cavallo & Wolff law firm represents Westlight and the Picture Agency Council of America. He contends that the Pebble Beach trademark protects only the company's distinct logo rendering of the tree with waves in the background, and that after 100 years of being freely snapped, the tree is now in the public domain.

Pebble Beach says its attorney knowledgeable on the issue is unreachable. But in a letter to Cavallo & Wolff, the company said it has applied to "register the tree itself as a trademark no matter how depicted."

Even if the company is successful in this application, it's questionable how any restrictions would affect the thousands of commercial photographs already in circulation. "They're trying to lock the barn door after the horse is out," says Pat Hathaway, a Carmel photographer whose firm, California Views, has more than 100 versions of the Lone Cypress, including one picture taken in 1880.
