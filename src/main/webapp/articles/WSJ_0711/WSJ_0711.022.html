Trump Is Likely to Face Questioning By House Panel on Debt Restructuring

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 07/11/90

WASHINGTON -- Donald Trump is bracing for his latest battle. Call it Trump: The Hearings. Following from the wrenching $65 million restructuring of his bank debt, the New York developer is about to be asked to defend the plan before House Banking Committee Chairman Henry Gonzalez. The maverick Texas populist is planning to hold hearings next month to investigate how Mr. Trump's debt was restructured, and to assess whether the bailout has been scrutinized by banking examiners.

But Mr. Trump, in an interview yesterday, bristled over the committee's inquiry, and said he didn't know if he would voluntarily testify. He hasn't yet been called, but committee staffers say he will be.

"I haven't missed a single interest payment. My assets are great," he said. "If I'm asked to testify, then everyone who is restructuring a loan should be asked to testify. That's when I'll testify."

In recent hearings, Mr. Gonzalez hasn't been shy about issuing subpoenas to anyone who has piqued his curiosity or sense of outrage. Junk bond king Michael Milken and Lincoln Savings chief Charles Keating were both subpoenaed.

In addition, Neil Bush, President Bush's son, appeared voluntarily before Mr. Gonzalez's committee recently to discuss his business relationships with two major borrowers of Silverado Banking Savings & Loan Association, a failed thrift in Denver of which he was a director.

Throughout the hearings, Mr. Gonzalez has pointedly asked why federal examiners weren't more vigilant in regulating the banks whose loans went bad. Mr. Gonzalez indicated, in a letter dated yesterday to Currency Comptroller Robert Clarke, that he would pursue that course again.

"The highly publicized loans and debt-restructuring arranged for Donald J. Trump and the Trump Organization in New York and New Jersey illustrate current problems in the banking industry and real estate markets," Mr. Gonzalez wrote in the letter, in which he asked Mr. Clarke to testify. Mr. Gonzalez said he would pursue "the manner in which the various regulatory agencies have reacted to the developments."

Mr. Clarke oversees many of the big banks that loaned millions to Mr. Trump, including Citibank, Chase Manhattan and Manufacturers Hanover, all based in New York. Federal Reserve officials and New York state regulators will also be asked to testify, House staffers said.

As for Mr. Trump, he says what the banking examiners do isn't his worry. "The banks and I worked out an intelligent deal for both parties," he said. "I haven't dealt with the regulators."
