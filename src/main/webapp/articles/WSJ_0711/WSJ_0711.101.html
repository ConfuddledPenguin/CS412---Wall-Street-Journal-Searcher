Heard on the Street: Investors Have One Word for Berlitz's Stock, And at 36 Times Earnings It's Spelled: `Pricey'

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 07/11/90

In any language, shares of Berlitz International are getting expensive. Since the end of April, Berlitz stock has risen 48%. Yesterday, it closed at 22, meaning that a share now sells for 36 times the past 12 months' per-share earnings for the language-instruction company. That puts it in the priciest 10% of all New York Stock Exchange stocks, according to FactSet Data Systems. Not bad for a company that went public only last December.

In 1988, Britain's Maxwell Communication acquired Macmillan, which had owned Berlitz since 1965. In December, Maxwell offered 44% of Berlitz shares to the public at 16 1/2 each. The price lingered in that neighborhood for several months, then shot upward.

A spokeswoman for Berlitz yesterday cited three reasons for the stock's strength: favorable reports by brokerage analysts, increased interest in global businesses, and the opening up of Eastern Europe.

Gail Raiman, analyst with C.J. Lawrence, sees another reason. Investors were intrigued when Robert Maxwell, chairman of Maxwell Communication (and of Berlitz), said recently that he plans to sell $800 million of "non-strategic business assets." Some investors figured that this might mean Berlitz and that Mr. Maxwell might wangle an above-market price, at least for his own shares and possibly for the whole company.

In light of the recent run-up, however, even fans of the company are beginning to concede that the stock is pricey.

"We still own some; obviously we still like it," says Robert Buhrmann, analyst with Sun Bank Capital Management in Orlando, Fla. "Nobody else has the international reach that they do, and nobody else can charge the premium price that they do." He says Berlitz has "a defendable niche," and "we like niche stocks."

Even so, Mr. Buhrmann says, "I would not add {to holdings} at these prices." He suggests that long-term holders "accumulate" the stock on dips below 20 and "be aggressive" in buying on any declines below 18.

Sun Bank Capital owned 365,000 shares as of March 30, according to CDA Investment Technologies, which tracks money managers' government filings. The firm expands or trims its core holdings based on short-term price movements. Lately it has been trimming its Berlitz holdings. "But I certainly wouldn't sell all my position," he says. "You know, really good businesses are hard to find."

Berlitz should benefit from the increasing unification of Europe and the growing ties between the old West and East blocs. It caters to business people who want to learn (or brush up on) a language before a trip or assignment abroad. About half of its business comes from Fortune 500 companies. For its intense, short-term courses, it generally charges higher prices than competing language schools.

More than 75% of its business comes from outside North America: Berlitz is much more likely to teach a Japanese businessman English than to teach an American businessman Japanese. Its revenue comes 40% from Europe, 27% from Asia, 23% from North America and 9% from Latin America. For people apprehensive about the sluggish U.S. economy, that's a plus.

Analysts expect Berlitz to earn about 67 cents to 70 cents a share this year, and close to $1 a share next year. The company will say only that it expects this year's earnings to top last year's 63 cents a share, but it doesn't quarrel with analysts' estimates.

Reported earnings understate the cash flow, because there's about 50 cents a share in amortization of goodwill, mostly from the Maxwell acquisition of Macmillan. And debt -- a $50 million bank loan from Societe Generale in France -- is moderate.

Nevertheless, the stock is still priced at 22 times next year's earnings. The average Big Board stock sells for about 16 times last year's earnings -- and even that multiple is a bit above historic norms.

At Goldman Sachs, which handled the initial public offering of Berlitz shares, analyst Robert Adler rates Berlitz "attractive." But because the stock already has hit his target price of 22, he says: "I guess I rate the stock an excellent long-term hold, but I'd be less aggressive at current levels."

The company projects that its sales will grow about 15% a year for the next five years (10% volume increases and 5% price increases), with earnings growth of perhaps 17% a year (based on modest improvements in profit margins). That's nice growth. But it isn't the sort of scorching growth for which investors usually pay fat earnings multiples.
