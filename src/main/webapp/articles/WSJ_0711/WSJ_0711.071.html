Economy: Greenspan Meets With GOP Senators To Hear Concerns About Credit Crunch

  • Author: Alan Murray
  • Date: 07/11/90

WASHINGTON -- A group of Republican senators summoned Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan to Capitol Hill to hear their concerns about a credit crunch and a stagnating economy. Mr. Greenspan met in a closed session over lunch with members of the Senate Republican Conference. Several of the senators used the opportunity to complain about tightening credit standards and urge the Fed to ease credit.

"The senators were on his back about interest rates, obviously," said Sen. Richard Lugar (R., Ind.) after the meeting. According to Sen. Christopher Bond (R., Mo.), "There were a lot of concerns expressed about the credit crunch."

In his responses, the Fed chairman gave the senators little reason to expect the central bank to cut interest rates soon. Instead, he argued that the Fed is walking a very narrow road right now, with the risk of recession close on one side, but the risk of accelerating inflation close on the other.

The unusual Senate meeting was yet another sign that the political pressure on the Fed to cut interest rates is growing increasingly intense. The meeting comes just a few days after Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady made a rare public plea for lower interest rates. So far, however, the Fed chairman shows no sign of budging.

Mr. Greenspan spent part of the session discussing the unusual employment figures released last week, according to several people who attended. He said unemployment remains very low, and labor-force growth is very sluggish. In particular, he said, women and young people seem to be entering the labor force at a surprisingly slow rate. As a result, there aren't enough new workers to allow the economy to grow at a much faster rate.

In addition, the Fed chairman argued that if he eased credit now, that might boost the economy for the next six months or so but would ultimately result in more inflation and force the Fed to crack down even harder in the future.

Mr. Greenspan declined to say what decisions the Fed's policy-making committee had made at its meeting last week. But his comments appeared to reinforce previous reports from government officials that the Fed has no immediate plans to ease credit.

But by acknowledging repeatedly that the Fed is operating "at the edge" and walking a very narrow line, Mr. Greenspan also indicated that any sharp change in the economic data could lead the central bank to change its course.

Chairman Greenspan also used the opportunity to make a plea for cutting the budget deficit this year, according to those who attended. The Fed chairman said that with slow growth in the labor force, the only way to get the economy moving is to increase the output, or "productivity," of the existing labor force. And the best way to improve productivity, he argued, is to increase investment by boosting national savings and cutting the budget deficit.

"He was very strong on that," said Sen. Bond. "He said the best way to increase savings is to run" a budget surplus.

The one clear voice of support for the Fed chairman came from Florida GOP Sen. Connie Mack. According to several people attending the lunch, Sen. Mack, a former banker, questioned whether there was really a dangerous credit crunch under way. He said that when bankers tell borrowers the regulators won't let them make a loan, what they really mean is the loan is too risky.
