McDonnell Makes Further Changes At Helicopter Unit

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 07/11/90

McDonnell Douglas Corp.'s helicopter unit, in the latest of a series of restructuring attempts, said it was changing its management framework and announced the departure of five executives. The company said other actions being taken include "significant cost and budget-cutting measures," but details won't be disclosed until later.

"The company's financial performance in the past year makes it clear that a change in the way we do business is necessary," Tom Gunn, president of the Mesa, Ariz.-based subsidiary, said in a statement. About a year ago, the helicopter concern said it would streamline and restructure, and in February it initiated another cost-reduction plan that included about 630 layoffs.

So far, though, little seems to have been turned around. In 1989, significant writedowns were recorded at the helicopter unit, especially on the AH-64 Apache program. Moreover, the General Accounting Office in April branded that aircraft as breakdown-prone. "The Apache's problems are serious and go beyond normal growing pains," a representative of the congressional investigative arm testified at a House hearing. Then in May, Mr. Gunn's predecessor stepped down amid the company's continuing poor financial performance.

The helicopter unit, which employs about 7,000 people, has four product lines: the AH-64; the LH, on which it has teamed up with Textron Inc.'s Bell unit in a bid to develop a light armed reconnaissance helicopter for the Army; the MD-500, which features a no-tail rotor system; and the MDX, a twin-engine commercial helicopter that can seat up to eight.

"The need to restructure is particularly acute in light of the dramatic changes in the world political picture and the resultant reduced spending for defense," Mr. Gunn said.

McDonnell Douglas, the nation's No. 1 military contractor, disclosed last month that it would try to pare $700 million a year from its cost structure, corporationwide. The St. Louis aerospace firm, stung by losses principally at its transport aircraft division in California, has in the past year piled up debt and watched its stock price sink.

But a spokesman in Mesa said the helicopter unit's actions were largely unrelated to its parent's recent plea for more efficiency. "I don't think I would connect the two," he said.

As part of the changes, McDonnell Douglas Helicopter Co. said it would break into four autonomous divisions covering its distinct product lines, supported by six other divisions. Previously, there were only seven divisions in all.

In addition, two unit vice presidents and two unit directors elected to take early retirement or to resign. Norm Hirsh, an executive vice president and a 27-year McDonnell Douglas veteran, also retired.
