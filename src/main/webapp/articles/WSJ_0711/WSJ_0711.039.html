Senate Committee to Start Work Today On Defense-Spending Bill for Fiscal '91

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 07/11/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Armed Services Committee today begins drafting a $289 billion defense-spending bill that would slash at least 100,000 active-duty troops in 1991 while rejecting Pentagon proposals for comparable cuts in reserve forces over the next few years. The committee also is expected to avoid wholesale termination of new multibillion-dollar weapons, keeping alive such controversial programs as the Air Force's B-2 Stealth bomber, the Navy's expensive Seawolf submarine, and a group of sophisticated aircraft still under development.

Final details, however, won't be worked out until the budget summit between Congress and the White House winds down and the House Armed Services Committee has had its chance to chop away at uniformed strength and cherished Pentagon weapons plans in the next few weeks.

Meeting behind closed doors, members of the Senate panel are expected to approve about $289 billion in military-spending authority for the fiscal year starting Oct. 1. That level, $18 billion less than President Bush's January budget request, is the one advocated by Chairman Sam Nunn (D., Ga.), who is one of the leading lawmakers seeking gradual cuts.

But in an unusual procedure, the committee is expected to map out at least $4 billion more in contingency cuts -- including the elimination of an additional 30,000 or so troops next year -- reflecting the likelihood that greater Pentagon sacrifices will be part of any summit agreement with the White House.

Defense Secretary Dick Cheney cut short an official trip to Europe to attend another meeting of the budget summiteers here today, although spokesmen said Mr. Cheneny didn't have any plans to submit revised spending figures to the group.

The committee's action will be the opening salvo of what is certain to be an emotional and drawn-out congressional debate pitting Sen. Nunn and other Pentagon supporters against an increasingly powerful bipartisan coalition determined to gut some of the military's most expensive research and procurement programs. Depending on how the budget summit ends, fights over many of the cuts may have to be repeated on the Senate floor.

By voting out a bill before the budget summit concludes, Sen. Nunn and his allies apparently hope to take the initiative away from lawmakers who advocate deeper cuts. The strategy, according to Senate aides and defense officials, is to draw a line in the sand and demonstrate how much pain even the committee's cuts would cause the Pentagon.

Still, the Senate bill is bound to be more favorable to the Pentagon than the measure taking shape on the other side of Capitol Hill. House Democratic leaders, for example, advocate substantially deeper cuts that Secretary Cheney complains could force more than 700,000 active-duty troops and some 500,000 reserves off the Pentagon's payroll by the late 1990s. Currently, there are about 2.1 million active-duty troops and 1.1 million reserves.

In a series of speeches earlier this year, Sen. Nunn emphasized that he would strongly oppose such hefty personnel cuts, especially in reserve units that are less expensive to maintain than active-duty forces and often are viewed as important parts of the local community. "The presumption," Sen. Nunn argued in one of the speeches, should be to transfer "as much capability into reserve {forces} as possible in this new era."

Sen. John Glenn (D., Ohio), chairman of the manpower subcommittee, also has come out strongly against cutbacks in the National Guard and Army reserves, according to Pentagon officials and committee aides. As a result, these officials said, the Senate panel is expected to approve only marginal reductions in 1991 reserve strength. Pentagon officials are struggling to finish a comprehensive study on the responsibilities reserve units should assume, but the congressionally mandated report won't be available until well after both committees finish work on Pentagon spending bills.

In weapons-buying accounts, the Senate panel is expected to reject administration proposals to aggressively develop both the multiple-warhead MX missile and the smaller Midgetman missile. The committee also is expected to trim requests to build a new generation of attack helicopters and combat vehicles for the Army, scale back purchases of guided-missile destroyers by the Navy, and cap research funding for the Strategic Defense Initiative, a proposed space-based anti-missile system, at less than $3.5 billion.

Mr. Cheney still hasn't announced the results of his own reassessment of SDI, the Army's LH helicopter program and the Navy's overall submarine and ship-buying plans.
