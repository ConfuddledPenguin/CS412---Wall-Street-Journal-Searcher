Law: Fearful of Future, Plaintiffs Are Suing Firms for What Hasn't Happened Yet

  • Author: Amy Dockser Marcus
  • Date: 07/11/90

Lawsuits claiming fears about the future are giving companies anxiety attacks. More and more suits are being filed that seek compensation for plaintiffs' worries about events that may happen at a later time, and defense lawyers say the trend poses serious problems for corporations. The specter of liability for fear and distress, they argue, could deter manufacturers from introducing beneficial products. They also say it's unfair to make companies pay for worries about incidents that are statistically unlikely to occur.

But modern society increasingly views " `mental tranquillity' as a protectable interest," says Paul Rothstein, a professor at Georgetown University Law Center. "Part of the good life today, as opposed to medieval times, is that we're not only entitled to be free from physical injuries but we're also entitled to a degree of mental happiness."

Consequently, individuals and companies have been sued by people who believe they may develop cancer because of their proximity to manufacturing plants, worry that life-saving equipment may eventually kill them and fear that they will contract sexually transmitted diseases.

Decisions on compensating the victims of fear have varied from court to court, but the U.S. Supreme Court is expected to address the issue in the fall. The court will hear a case filed by passengers of an Eastern Airlines flight who say they suffered emotional injuries when they were told by a pilot that their plane might crash.

Courts have long recognized the right to seek damages for emotional distress, but until recently compensation was only granted when the distress was the result of a physical injury. Many judges viewed fear and distress claims skeptically because they thought that emotional injuries were easy to feign. Many courts also believed that such injuries were temporary and thus difficult to compensate.

But a growing understanding about the devastating impact of emotional injuries has made judges more willing to compensate plaintiffs, even when their fears don't have physical manifestations.

"From a financial standpoint, the suits have the potential to be devastating," says Arvin Maskin of the New York law firm of Weil, Gotshal & Manges, who defends corporations in fear litigation.

Last year a Los Angeles jury awarded $14.5 million to Marc Christian, Rock Hudson's former lover, who claimed he suffered emotional distress after learning in a 1985 news report that Mr. Hudson was dying from AIDS. It is believed to be the largest amount awarded to someone who tested negative for a sexually transmitted disease. The trial judge reduced the award to $5 million, but lawyers for Mr. Hudson's estate have asked the appeals court to grant a new trial, arguing that there was insufficient evidence that Mr. Christian suffered severe emotional distress.

"When someone says I had a relationship and I'm fearful I'll get AIDS, how can you prove or disprove that? It's your word against his," says Robert Parker Mills of the Pasadena law firm of Mills & Schroeder, which represents the estate.

But Kent L. Richland, who represents Mr. Christian, says that medical science can't say with complete certainty that his client will never contract AIDS. "The threat that is hanging over his head is a horrible disease and a miserable, painful death," says Mr. Richland, a lawyer with the Beverly Hills law firm of Greines, Martin, Stein & Richland.

Lawyers say the Hudson verdict has wide implications and has been used to support claims involving alleged exposure to toxic substances, where diseases often have a long latency period and proving an injury is difficult. In those cases, the courts have grappled with determining how reasonable the fear is, especially in incidents where the odds of a disease materializing are remote. Many judges are beginning to accept the notion that probability isn't a good measure of a person's fears.

That was the reasoning applied by U.S. District Judge Robert J. Bryan in a suit brought by nine past and present residents of an Air Force base in Tacoma, Wash., against the federal government for damages resulting from the alleged contamination of their water wells. In a 1987 decision that included more than $80,000 for the plaintiffs' emotional distress, Judge Bryan wrote that the plaintiffs hadn't been exposed to a risk "greater than one in one million. The actual risk was probably much less, and may be zero." But he noted that the Environmental Protection Agency had advised the plaintiffs not to drink or use the water, which contained possible carcinogens, and so he found the residents' anxieties reasonable.

Other courts have determined that a person's fears are reasonable based on a wide range of evidence, including letters from a doctor prescribing regular checkups, transcripts of congressional hearings in which the health risks associated with certain chemicals are discussed, scientific articles, and even TV broadcasts.

Mr. Maskin of Weil Gotshal, who lectures regularly about phobia suits, says he worries that "the courts have allowed perceived risks to transcend actual risks. If you jettison the reality of risk, then the sky is the limit."

But plaintiffs' lawyers say that medical science still can't definitively state how exposure to toxic substances affects individuals, so courts must find other methods to assess fear.

Employees of Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. have filed a suit claiming the company fraudulently concealed information about hazardous substances used at the company's Salinas, Calif., facility and seeking funds to pay for regular checkups to monitor their health. Firestone has denied the allegations.

In a 1987 opinion refusing to dismiss the case, U.S. District Judge Robert P. Aguilar of San Jose acknowledged medical science hadn't developed to the point it could detect the employees' injuries and that the legal system "must evolve to meet the needs of society." The case is pending.

John W. Fowler of the San Francisco firm of McCutchen, Doyle, Brown & Enersen, a lawyer for Firestone, says that providing lifetime medical monitoring could be costly for employers and open up manufacturers to liability for diseases that never materialize. But the employees' lawyer, Gordon A. Stemple of the Century City firm of Stemple & Boyajian, says companies that knowingly send employees into a dangerous workplace should bear the cost of monitoring for health problems.

Fear suits raise particularly troubling questions in the area of medical technology, where advances in science have led to breakthroughs that are often accompanied by health risks.

The issue was highlighted by a suit filed by Judy Khan, an artificial heart valve patient. Mrs. Khan claims she has lived in constant fear of death since she learned that Shiley Inc., the valve's maker, was recalling the product because a defect could cause the valves to fracture. The California Supreme Court ruled in May that Mrs. Khan can seek damages for emotional distress if she can prove Shiley concealed material information.

"Whether you're talking about an AIDS vaccine, an organ implant or artificial heart valves, few medical products are risk-free. If you allow these lawsuits {to proceed}, in effect you create universal liability," says David Klingsberg of the New York law firm of Kaye, Scholer, Fierman, Hays & Handler, which represents Pfizer Inc., Shiley's owner.

But Mrs. Khan's lawyer, Bruce A. Finzen of Robins, Kaplan, Miller & Ciresi of Minneapolis, says Pfizer should be held liable for concealing the risks associated with its product. "Their breach of duty has caused people anguish," he says.

Not all such litigation involves fears of future events. Lawyers say a growing number of cases are being filed by passengers who survive airplane accidents and seek damages for the emotional distress when they thought they might die.

In the case currently before the U.S. Supreme Court, 25 passengers and their spouses are seeking damages for mental distress they say they suffered aboard Eastern Airlines flight 855 on May 5, 1983. Bound for the Bahamas, the plane ran into trouble after leaving the Miami airport. One engine malfunctioned and had to be shut down by the crew. The other two failed soon after.

As the plane rapidly lost altitude, the captain announced it might crash in the Atlantic Ocean. But the crew managed to restart one of the engines, and the plane returned safely to Miami.

John M. Murray, a lawyer for Eastern from the Miami law firm of Thornton, David, Murray, Richard & Davis, said the passengers shouldn't be able to collect damages because they suffered no physical injuries. He predicts that if the case goes forward, "the number of lawsuits that are filed against air carriers will vastly increase."

But the plaintiffs' lawyer, Joel D. Eaton of the Miami firm of Podhurst, Orseck, Josefsberg, Eaton, Meadow, Olin & Perwin, sees it differently. "The passengers were told they were going to crash," he says. "They were nearly frightened to death."
