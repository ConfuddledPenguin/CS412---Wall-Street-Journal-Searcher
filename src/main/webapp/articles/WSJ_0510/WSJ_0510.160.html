Fundamentalist Templeton Puts `Quant' Into Portfolio

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 05/10/90

NEW YORK -- Is money manager John Templeton, one of the patriarchs of fundamental stock picking, turning in his sharp pencil for a computer? Mr. Templeton's money management firm has quietly acquired Dais Group Inc., a high-tech stock trading and research operation orphaned by the collapse of Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc. Templeton, Galbraith & Hansberger Ltd. paid about $1 million for Dais, a number-crunching outfit that in its Drexel days traded big baskets of stock using computer models. That might seem to be a far cry from Mr. Templeton's own roam-the-globe strategy of digging up neglected companies trading below their real worth.

Mr. Templeton insists he isn't changing his stripes. "Buying bargains hasn't become obsolete," he said from London, where he is administering his $680,000 Templeton Prize for Progress in Religion.

He is betting that other money managers, in particular giant pension funds, are going to rely more and more on computer-aided "quantitative" research, especially when it comes to global investing.

Mr. Templeton, of course, is in a prime position to watch for trends in the money management business. His firm manages 13 mutual funds with combined assets exceeding $9 billion, including nine broker-sold funds and four of the publicly traded "closed end" type funds in the U.S.

He is going to keep Dais Group at arm's length. "It will operate under its own name, in its own office with its own people," he said, and such independent status is a first for a Templeton subsidiary. Mr. Templeton may not want the "quants" to get too close to his stock pickers, though he concedes that his staff already uses some of the Dais Group's research.

Martin Flanagan, Templeton's chief financial officer, said the firm views Dais "like an undervalued security that would be good to have in our portfolio. That isn't to say that all of a sudden we are going to take on quantitative research in the management of the Templeton funds."

Mr. Templeton manages more than $15 billion in stock for thousands of investors. A pioneer international investor, he is famed for finding hidden values in stock markets around the world.

But among the holdings, there are other signs that Mr. Templeton is quietly betting on the business of high-tech stock trading and research. Among the holdings of the Templeton funds is 695,000 shares, or about 10%, of Jefferies Group Inc., the aggressive third-market trading firm that is making a major investment in high-tech trading systems.

Templeton funds are also large holders of other financial-services concerns. They own 1.3 million shares of Dreyfus Corp., the mutual fund company; about 500,000 shares of Salomon Inc., parent of Salomon Brothers, and his funds are among the largest stockholders of money manager American Capital Management & Research Inc.

As the money-management business consolidates into bigger and bigger pools of capital, computers have given portfolio managers a variety of tools -- from the ability to rapidly buy and sell big baskets of stocks to sophisticated systems that compare the day-by-day performance of thousands of stocks in dozens of markets.

But there is another reason why Templeton thinks quantitative investing will become more popular: it's cheaper. Money managers are trying to cut costs and beat market averages, and quantitative techniques have in recent years accomplished both tasks.

"Many of the traditional, fundamental investors are coming up for retirement, and the new guard taking over is being trained" with quantitative techniques, said Nicholas Lopardo, chief investment officer at State Street Bank & Trust Co., which manages about $50 billion solely with quantitative strategies.

Last February, when Drexel's parent filed for bankruptcy-law protection, Dais Group was quickly bought by its two top executives, George Douglas and Robert Butman.

Messrs. Douglas and Butman held daily huddles with the staff to keep up morale, and have sought to fend off competitors who tried to lure away some of their 125 institutional clients. The Templeton purchase, they say, will give Dais access to foreign stock-market data and the capital the firm needs to expand.

Mr. Templeton launched his first mutual fund, Templeton Growth, in 1954. But his really spectacular performance didn't come until the 1970s, when he managed to sidestep the 1973-74 U.S. bear market while making a large and hugely successful bet on Japanese stocks.

According to Lipper Analytical Services, Templeton Growth soared 18.8% a year during the 1970s, versus 5.9% for the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index.

By the start of the 1980s, Mr. Templeton was managing two funds, Templeton Growth and Templeton World, with combined assets of just over $300 million. As money flowed in, Mr. Templeton has continued to produce handsome, though not spectacular, results. Through the 1980s, Templeton Growth returned 16.4% a year, and Templeton World 17.6%, versus 17.5% for the S&P 500 and 18.1% for the average global stock fund.

Born in Tennessee, Mr. Templeton became a British national and was knighted in 1987. He now lives in the Bahamas, at the exclusive Lyford Cay Club. He sponsors a big cash prize for significant contributions to religious thought and life. Past recipients include Mother Teresa of Calcutta and Alexander Solzhenitsyn.
