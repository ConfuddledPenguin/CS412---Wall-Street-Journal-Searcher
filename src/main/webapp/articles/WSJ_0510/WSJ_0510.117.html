World Markets: Tokyo's Share Prices Slip a Bit on Profit-Taking As Nikkei's Five-Day, 1500-Point Rally Ends

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/10/90

Tokyo's Nikkei Index finished slightly lower on profit-taking, ending the five-day, 1500-point rally. In London, shares ended broadly lower after extremely lackluster dealings, action that largely signaled a consolidation following modest day-earlier gains.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues fell 24.97 points to 30945.61. The index gained 14.31 points Tuesday.

But the Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which gained 7.11 points Tuesday, sustained the rally and added 5.19 points, or 0.23%, to 2308.79. The Topix has posted consecutive gains since April 26, adding 108.69 points, or 4.9%, since that time.

In Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index rose 296.34 points to close the morning session at 31241.95.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 700 million shares, off from 746 million shares Tuesday.

Winning issues outnumbered losers 572 to 409, while 139 issues were unchanged. Traders said the tally reflects gains on the broad-based Topix, noting that declines on the Nikkei Index were likely on more expensive issues.

The second section index surged 46.02 points, or 1.25%, to 3717.90. The gain marks the seventh consecutive advance for the minor index. Volume was estimated at 18 million shares, up from 14 million shares.

Early morning bargain-hunting couldn't outpace professional selling, which began midmorning and continued throughout the day, and, after a softer morning close, prices drifted in the afternoon, observers said.

A vice president of equity research at a U.S. investment bank compared the market's major movements with acts in a theater play, with rumors and various corporate news fillers being equivalent to small, short stories told to keep the audience entertained between acts.

In short, he said the market will look for rumors and corporate news while it's between acts, as the market seeks to recover from its retracement since the beginning of the year. However, not being prepared to make a full-scale rebound, the market is trending upward, helped by favorable technical price patterns and improved fundamentals.

The key for a stock rally remains in brokers' abilities to attract institutional investors, said Christopher Leighton, manager of international equity sales at Schroder Securities (Japan) Ltd. He added that institutional and individual investors have been reluctant to participate mainly because they're unsure where to put their money for sustained strength.

Buying trends are indicating a shortterm marketplace, observers said, with interest focused mostly on domestics. "But domestic issues by no means provide a clear indication of where to invest," Mr. Leighton said, as they can quite easily and quickly reverse gains.

In addition, observers noted that arbitrage activity may be hindering a fullscale recovery.

"Elements of arbitrage-related selling" were evident yesterday, Mr. Leighton said, with arbitragers buying stock-index futures and selling the cash indexes.

Although the market was in a bullish mood, he noted the disparity between arbitragers' ability to sway the indexes and brokers' ability to convince customers to participate.

Market talk yesterday included a story that Nippon Zeon has developed the world's smallest pacemaker, according to Kevin Haag, a dealer in overseas operations at Nomura Securities Co. The stock ended at its stop-high.

Industrial machinery stocks were helped by a story in a leading Japanese economic daily regarding fiber cables projects, while some pharmaceuticals and chemicals were lifted on news that a Waseda University team has synthesized red blood cells.

Among industrial machinery stocks, Daikin Industries added 80 yen to Y1870 (51 cents to $11.86), Ebara rose 70 yen to 1880 yen and Kubota rose 70 yen to 1100 yen.

Dainippon Pharmaceutical soared 270 yen to 2880 yen, Mochida rose 130 yen to 3910 yen and Ono rose 150 yen to 6270 yen.

Nippon Oil & Fat rose 109 yen to 1100 yen.

London's Financial Times Stock Exchange 100 share index fell 19.3 points to 2162.7, a touch above the intraday low of 2160.6 and substantially lower than the day's high of 2179.7, just after the start of official trading.

The Financial Times 30-share index was down 14.5 points at 1695.6. Volume was a 425.8 million shares, modestly better than 309.0 million a day earlier.

The market's "just getting a touch tired," said a trader with a large U.K. brokerage firm, particularly after posting moderate gains since late last week.

Wariness on the part of investors ahead of tomorrow's scheduled disclosure of U.K. retail price index figures for April helped pressure the market a bit, dealers said.

Traders added that investors were also waiting to see what effect the U.S. Treasury's $30.5 billion quarterly refunding was having on Wall Street.

A slew of corporate results had diverse effects on individual share prices.

Construction and property concern Trafalgar House fell 10 pence to 286 pence (17 cents to $4.78) after interim results that showed a 2.7% pretax profit rise.

Pharmaceutical concern SmithKline Beecham dropped 7 pence to 472 pence after first-quarter pretax profit sagged 6.8% to #219 million. The results, described by one trader as "pretty grim," brought the rest of the sector lower.

Glaxo shed 5 1/2 pence to 781 pence; Reckitt & Colman lost 15 pence to #11.44; and Wellcome slid 7 pence to 643 pence.

The banking sector was hit hard following a profit downgrading of Barclays and Midland by Barclays de Zoete Wedd, the securities arm of Barclays. Barclays plunged 16 pence to 524 pence; Lloyds sank 10 pence to 265 pence; Midland fell 5 pence to 290 pence; and National Westminster lost 7 pence to 329 pence.

Elsewhere, retailer Great Universal Stores dropped 23 pence to 944 pence in a correction to strong gains Tuesday; Reuters lost 15 pence to #11.04 on profit-taking; and industrial gases and health care concern BOC fell 13 pence to 507 pence ahead of half-year results today.

Meanwhile, prices closed higher in Zurich and Stockholm; lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Amsterdam, Milan; mixed in Brussels. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Also, stocks closed lower in Hong Kong, Seoul and Taipei, were mixed in Manila, higher in Wellington and little changed in Sydney. Singapore was closed.
