Aetna Acts to Stop Insuring Drivers In Pennsylvania

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 05/10/90

Aetna Life & Casualty Co. is trying to withdraw from the personal auto-insurance market in Pennsylvania. Although the insurer filed a lawsuit last week challenging the constitutionality of two laws that bar its withdrawal from Massachusetts, Pennsylvania is the first state where it has started the process to leave the auto-insurance business. It has begun sending out nonrenewal notices to its 200,000 policyholders in the state. Aetna has also begun cutting back on its auto-insurance business in other states.

Aetna, the fifth largest auto insurer working in Pennsylvania, expects to have pulled out completely in one year.

Yesterday, the Hartford, Conn., company also amended a complaint filed in February in U.S. district court in Philadelphia, asking the court to enjoin the Pennsylvania insurance commissioner from taking any action to block Aetna's withdrawal from the auto-insurance market in this state.

Insurance regulators view Aetna's action as a violation of a 1968 law that governs the circumstances allowing an insurer to stop renewing policies in Pennsylvania. The insurance department will issue a temporary order to block Aetna's action today.

Earlier this week, the insurance department asked a state court to prevent Harleysville Mutual Insurance Co. and a Safeco Corp. unit from not renewing their auto-insurance policies in Pennsylvania.

New auto-insurance legislation passed last February required insurers to offer drivers rate reductions of 10% to 22% in premiums. Yet, more than 100 companies have failed to comply with the new law last week, when rate filings incorporating the rollbacks were due. Some asked for rate boosts, and two other companies tried to pull out entirely from the market.

Aetna filed for a rate increase, averaging 24.7% for all classes of drivers. The company said the filing was returned without comment by the insurance department.

"The inability to receive adequate rate relief, coupled with the burdensome rollback provisions of the new state auto insurance law, makes this decision necessary," said John J. Martin, president of Aetna's personal financial security division.
