International: Some Germans Fear U.S. Military Pullout --- Primary Concern for Most Is One of Finances

  • Author: Terence Roth
  • Date: 05/10/90

ZWEIBRUECKEN, West Germany -- A half-dozen U.S. airmen are gathered around a tree stump anchored to the floor of Heiney's bar, absorbed in the task of pounding nails into it. Whoever drives home his nail first wins. Between turns with the hammer, the young servicemen draw absently on their beers and nod to the thundering rock music. It is only a Tuesday night, but Philip Bochicchio knows he is turning a profit. The saloonkeeper jabs a thumb at the busy dartboard, the packed bar and the group around the stump.

"Americans like to have a good time," says Mr. Bochicchio, an American who has lived here for most of his 38 years. But he doesn't expect his booming business to last forever. "When they leave, I leave," he says.

Across town, the office of Gerd Fallboehmer's furnace and drainage firm is cluttered with contracts and service awards from U.S. military bases and housing compounds outside town, which account for 60% of his business. Mr. Fallboehmer notes a change in how the locals view the American presence.

"A lot of people used to grumble into their beers that we don't need them here," he says. "But they're talking a little different now that {withdrawal plans are} cutting to the core of their existence. We have to rethink everything, and there are no alternatives in sight."

The Cold War was money in the bank for the scores of West German communities where U.S. troops have been based since World War II. But as German reunification nears and the likelihood grows that foreign troops will leave for good, many of these garrison towns are realizing too late that their communities relied too heavily on military spending.

In late January, U.S. Defense Secretary Richard Cheney dropped a bombshell on Zweibruecken's business community by confirming a dreaded rumor: The U.S. air base here, home to the 26th Tactical Reconnaissance Wing, is to be closed in 1993, and the 7,000 military personnel and dependents are to be withdrawn.

Their departure threatens 2,500 jobs in this city of 33,400, and about $83 million in annual business. Officials fear this is only the first step in a regionwide U.S. troop cut that casts doubt on every fifth job in the area.

Older Zweibruecken residents aren't sure the U.S. should rush out of West Germany with the fate of Soviet President Mikhail S. Gorbachev's reform programs in question. But the primary concern for most is one of finances. In Mainz, for example, Mainz Industries Panzerwerk G.m.b.H. employs 5,000 workers solely to repair and maintain American tracked military vehicles. A U.S. Army decision to cut its repair programs threatens half of the company's jobs, Mainz officials say.

Three years ago, the U.S. European Command calculated that the U.S. military pumps about 15 billion marks ($9.05 billion) into the West German economy annually, the bulk of it from 29 major U.S. installations in seven of West Germany's 11 states. That total, which includes local spending by 252,000 U.S. servicemen and their 100,000 dependents, as well as base contracts extended to German firms, is supplemented by the expenditures of the more than 100,000 British, French, Canadian, Belgian and Dutch troops in West Germany.

Of course, many West Germans see benefits in an allied troops reduction. Big urban areas are eager to get their hands on valuable property denied their developers for years. The government of the wealthy central German state of Hesse is eyeing the huge American air base that abuts Frankfurt's international airport, as well as the U.S. air base in nearby Wiesbaden. Developers are lining up for the U.S. army's priceless real-estate holdings in Frankfurt itself.

But the picture is a little different in Rhineland-Palatinate, the state between France and the Rhine River where Zweibruecken is located. It has eight North Atlantic Treaty Organization air bases and the greatest concentration of troops in West Germany, a situation born of the logic that the river is a natural defense against a Soviet-bloc attack.

The U.S. fighters that regularly roar overhead on training missions generally cause little comment, except when they crash and kill civilians on the ground.

"It will be a big loss when they go," says Cornelia Winkelmann, day manager at a local hotel that gets half its business from military transients. "On the other hand, there is always that fear" of military airplane crashes.

"For Hesse, an American withdrawal is like hitting the lottery," says Rainer Bruederle, deputy premier and economics minister of the Rhineland-Palatinate state government. "It has a financial-services center with unbelievable growth that needs the housing and the real estate." But in his state, he adds, the possibility of a troop withdrawal has generated only muted enthusiasm.

The U.S. military is the biggest employer in this part of the country. Mr. Bruederle estimates that as much as two-thirds of his state's 70,000 U.S. troops, plus their dependents, will be gone by the end of the decade. About 60,000 regional jobs are in jeopardy. "There is a lot of handwringing going on here," he says.

But while regional promoters are touting a program called "Computer Chips Instead of Jet Planes," they acknowledge that prospects of attracting growth industries are grim. "Nobody ever thought about what we could do without the military," says chamber of commerce Director Karl Herzberger.

"We made the mistake of assuming they would always be here," says Heinz Moosmann, a local journalist. "It's going to set off a chain reaction through this state's entire economic structure. It's a mistake by the Americans to make this decision thousands of kilometers away without first talking to us. It's alienating -- even arrogant."

A full-scale pullout also bodes ill for the housing markets in rural garrison towns that have depended on U.S. troops for demand. In Zweibruecken, base personnel occupy about 600 houses and apartments.

Much of the housing currently used by Americans could stand empty unless the town can attract new industries. Even the huge air base might be of little use. Town officials talk vaguely of converting the facility into an industrial park, but they wonder if decades of oil and aviation-fuel spillage has contaminated the soil. Worse, locals fear that NATO will retain the vacated air base for future emergency use, denying it to the community.

Mr. Bruederle, the state's deputy premier, is torn between his state's economic problems and Germany's new role in history. Reflecting the prevailing opinion in Bonn, he describes the U.S. as an indispensable ally, but he adds that the time has come for it to begin leaving German soil for the two countries to define a more equal partnership.

And, he says, "we also have to recognize that the American taxpayer can't be persuaded to continue paying for the security of those rich and sassy Europeans now that the threat is nearly zero."
