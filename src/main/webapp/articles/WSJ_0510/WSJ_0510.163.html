Junk Bonds May Get Lift From Proposal Of Insurance Officials

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/10/90

NEW YORK -- The high-yield junk bond market is likely to get a lift today as state insurance commissioners present a stretched-out timetable for new investment regulations that affect junk bonds. At issue are the statutory reserves that insurance companies must build up against the possibility of defaults in their bond portfolios. State insurance commissioners this spring have been drafting tougher new reserve rules that could take effect as soon as Jan. 1.

The new rules' biggest impact is expected to be in the junk bond market, where insurers own about 30% of the $200 billion of publicly traded junk bonds outstanding. In some cases, regulators plan to require that reserves be built up five to 20 times as fast as is currently being done. Because those greater reserve demands would make junk bonds less appealing to insurers, fears of an insurance-company sell-off of junk bonds have built up.

But insurance companies appear to have persuaded regulators to move cautiously. At a Washington meeting today, regulators are expected to present the insurance industry with a plan to phase in the new rules gradually over five years. "People are saying they want a transition period so this doesn't affect their balance sheets," said Terence Lennon, chief examiner of the New York State Insurance Department. "We're taking a very slow and measured approach."

Mr. Lennon heads a working group of the 50-state National Association of Insurance Commissioners that is drafting the new reserve rules. The latest version of the rules is expected to be adopted by the entire association next month.
