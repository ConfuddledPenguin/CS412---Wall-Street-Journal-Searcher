Marketing & Media: Sun Publications' New Newspaper Seeks to Defy Odds

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 05/10/90

When the Evening News hits the streets Monday in Kansas City, Mo., owner Stephen F. Rose will be trying to do the seemingly impossible: successfully launch a metropolitan daily newspaper and publish it in the afternoon. Mr. Rose, president of Sun Publications Inc., Overland Park, Kan., will publish the Evening News Monday through Friday, hitting newsstands about 2 p.m. The 50-cent tabloid newspaper will be heavy on international and national news, and sports.

The Evening News will operate as a unit of Sun Publications, a family-owned publisher of 18 suburban weekly newspapers in the affluent suburbs of Kansas City's Johnson County, as well as publisher of the Kansas City Jewish Chronicle.

Mr. Rose is attempting to buck a nationwide trend: the steady decline of metropolitan evening newspapers. The number of evening dailies has shrunk from 1,450 in 1950 to 1,125 in 1989. Some evening papers have simply died, though many -- at least 140 since 1976 -- have converted from p.m. to a.m. newspapers, according to the American Newspaper Publishers Association.

News of startup city newspapers is so rare that recent attempts have drawn wide publicity, but no success. On April 25, Ingersoll Publications Co. closed down the St. Louis Sun, a colorful tabloid newspaper started with great fanfare only seven months before, citing slow growth of circulation and costly home delivery.

Mr. Rose's plan for the Evening News has different twists. In St. Louis, Mr. Ingersoll spent millions on home delivery; The Evening News will have no home delivery. It will be sold instead from street vending machines, at convenience stores, and by newsboys on street corners. Initial circulation goals are 25,000 daily.

The St. Louis Sun also went head-to-head with Pulitzer Publishing Co.'s Post-Dispatch, also a morning paper. The Evening News, however, will fill the afternoon gap left by the demise March 1 of the Kansas City Star's afternoon edition.

The odds on the Evening News's survival are long, despite Mr. Rose's strategy. "You like to see any one try it, but the cost of daily newspaper production is pretty heavy to handle with only circulation revenue," said Paul Kessinger, consulting director of the Jordan Group, a New York consulting and media brokerage firm. Daily newspapers typically get 80% of their revenue from ad linage and 20% from single-copy and home subscription sales.
