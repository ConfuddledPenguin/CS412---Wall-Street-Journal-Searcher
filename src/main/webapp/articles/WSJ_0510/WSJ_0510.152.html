Legg Mason Joins Regional Brokers With Record Profit

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 05/10/90

NEW YORK -- Here are two words not often heard on Wall Street these days: record earnings. But several smaller regional securities firms -- including Legg Mason Inc., A.G. Edwards Inc. and Raymond James Financial Inc. -- posted record revenue and earnings for past 12 months. Regionals typically are based far from New York and focus more on clients around the corner than around the globe.

Yesterday, Legg Mason reported a 75% jump in net income for its fiscal fourth quarter ended March 31, and a 97% rise for the fiscal year, capping relatively robust returns for the nation's regional securities firms -- and a far cry from the woes engulfing many big national brokerage firms.

The regionals "continue to add to their sales forces and bring in more customers," says Perrin Long, senior securities analyst for Lipper Analytical Securities Corp. "At most regional firms, revenues are rising more rapidly than expenses."

Legg Mason's results underscore the good news at many small regional concerns. Their brokerage business serving individual investors and their investment advisory business, particularly mutual funds, are prospering, compared with a lackluster year-earlier period. The improvement in Legg Mason's retail, or individual-investor, segment reflected a "modest increase" in the number of the firm's brokers and a jump in average revenue per broker. And revenue from Legg Mason's investment advisory business, primarily mutual funds, rose 22% from a year earlier.

Fourth-quarter net at the Baltimore-based securities firm was $1.7 million, or 20 cents a share, up from $951,000, or 11 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 10% to $58.7 million from $53.2 million.

Not all regional securities firms have prospered recently. Net in the most recent quarter fell 19% at Piper Jaffray Inc., for example. And some big securities firms, particularly Morgan Stanley Group Inc., are thriving even amid the current Wall Street recession.

But it generally has been tougher going for most full-service national brokerage firms, many of whom have been sharply cutting back staff. The big securities firms have been hit hard by a slowdown in investment banking revenue-down more than 20% at many big firms. And the investment-banking slump was offset at some firms only by fat trading gains, which can't be counted on to continue.

Regional firms, however, are much more dependent on the activity of individual investors; indeed, their performance is often viewed as a gauge of small-investor interest in the stock market. And despite their recent rebound, regional firms' net income still stands at just half its peak in 1986. In 1989, regionals' net doubled to $215 million but was still well below 1986's record $546 million, according to the Securities Industry Association.

Regionals face stiffer competition, moreover, from some full-service national firms such as Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. that are redirecting their energies back to serving individual investors. Still, regionals have a head start in courting the little guy, cutting costs and focusing on a few profitable businesses.

Regionals "already are well on their way -- if they're not already niche firms," says Alison Deans, an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. "They're sticking to their strengths and keeping costs low."

Legg Mason stock fell 12.5 cents to close at $13.125 a share in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday.
