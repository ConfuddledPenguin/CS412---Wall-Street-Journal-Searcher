National Realty Says It Resolved Lawsuit Filed by Unitholders

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/10/90

DALLAS -- National Realty L.P. said it settled a class-action lawsuit brought by unitholders who claimed they were bilked when the real estate master limited partnership was formed in 1987 out of 35 pre-existing limited partnerships. National Realty was created by a controversial rollup of partnerships operated by troubled Southmark Corp., some of which were devastated by the real estate bust in the Southwest.

While National Realty didn't admit any liability in making the settlement, terms of the agreement call for the company to issue to plaintiffs five-year warrants to purchase 2.75 million partnership units at $11 a unit for the first year and $12 thereafter. Also, certain co-defendants agreed to contribute to the partnership cash and notes totaling $5.5 million.

National Realty's general partners agreed to contribute $2.5 million over four years, with the remainder coming from the former E.F. Hutton & Co. (now Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc.), the broker-dealer in the rollup, and Robert A. McNeil, the former general partner of the partnerships involved in the litigation.

A spokeswoman for National Realty said that other defendants, primarily Southmark-related entities, haven't reached a settlement with plaintiffs yet.

The settlement also raises the possibility that National Realty's general partners could be removed if the value of the partnership's units doesn't reach agreed-upon targets for the next five years. After one year, the agreement stipulates, National Realty units must be worth at least $11; at the end of five years, the target is $30. Achievement of the targets can be made both by increases in the share price of units and by distributions to limited partners.

In composite trading on the American Stock Exchange, National Realty closed yesterday at $4.875, up 25 cents.

If the target in any year isn't met, an oversight committee formed by limited partners can require National Realty to sell a percentage of its assets.

The settlement still must be ratified by a state court in San Mateo, Calif.
