Heard on the Street: Ethyl Plan to Seek Approval for Gas Additives Fuels Interest by Analysts in Market Potential

  • Author: Randolph B. Smith
  • Date: 05/10/90

PHILADELPHIA -- This week's uptick in one chemical company's shares caught analysts by surprise and prompted some to wonder: "What's up, Ethyl?" The Street is intrigued by Ethyl's Tuesday announcement that it will seek federal approval for a gasoline additive designed to cut air pollution.

With all the environmental hoopla and government initiatives to reduce automobile emissions, the Street is finding "an opportunity to take a new liking to Ethyl," says one analyst. Ethyl jumped 2 1/8 Tuesday, before subsiding 3/8 yesterday to 27 1/2.

Several analysts say that if the additive is approved -- and that's a big if at this point -- Ethyl's annual earnings could grow as much as $1 a share above projections and the stock could rise to the high 30s within two to three years -- with $5 of that coming from the additive.

The manganese-based additive, known as MMT, could have a potential market of $350 million a year if used in all gasoline sold in the U.S. Ethyl estimates potential sales initially at $100 million to $150 million a year.

"This could be the right product at the right time" to help oil refiners and auto manufacturers meet stricter Clean Air Act standards, says Leonard Bogner of Prudential-Bache Securities.

Even the most bullish analysts, however, warn that chances for approval are probably only 50% or slightly better. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, which must issue a decision within six months, is concerned that the additive could actually increase certain types of emissions -- hydrocarbons -- that contribute to smog. And the auto industry has previously opposed MMT on grounds that it could damage pollution-control equipment, especially catalytic converters.

Charles LoCastro of Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette says he isn't comfortable recommending the stock because chances of approval are only "50-50." He notes that the EPA twice rejected the additive, in 1978 and 1981, and there isn't any sign that car makers will be more supportive this time. Ethyl's stock is fully valued at 27 1/2 and stands to decline from there if MMT fails again, Mr. LoCastro says.

Ethyl says results of exhaustive testing will convince even the most hardened skeptics. The company drove a fleet of 48 new cars more than 75,000 miles each to compare tailpipe emissions and reliability using unleaded fuel with and without MMT. Ray Wilkins Jr., a senior vice president of Ethyl, says the additive produced a striking 20% reduction in nitrogen oxide emissions, but a 6% increase in hydrocarbons, causing an overall 7.5% reduction in emissions, including nitrogen oxides, hydrocarbons and carbon monoxide.

Many cars using MMT in the test met strict nitrogen oxide emission standards being proposed in Congress, and none violated any existing standards (including those for hydrocarbons), Mr. Wilkins says. Even with increased tailpipe hydrocarbons, Ethyl argues, there could be a drop in hydrocarbons overall because the additive would allow gasoline refiners to make fuel that is less susceptible to evaporation. Yet the projected overall 7.5% reduction in tailpipe emissions still wouldn't meet standards that Congress is considering mandating for the smoggiest cities.

"I'm very optimistic we'll have support from the auto and oil industries and environmentalists," Mr. Wilkins says. The additive, which would be marketed under the name HiTEC 3000, has been used widely in Canada for more than a decade with no apparent ill effects on cars or public health. Athough Ethyl invented the additive in 1954, so far the U.S. has allowed its use only in leaded fuel, which accounts for just 6% of gasoline sales.

Mary Smith, an EPA official in charge of reviewing Ethyl's test results, said she couldn't comment on the additive's chances of approval. But, she said, EPA will scrutinize closely whether it would reduce the ability of cars to meet hydrocarbon standards. Spokesmen for Exxon and General Motors said they couldn't comment until they review Ethyl's test results.

Though the additive could become Ethyl's fastest-growing product, some analysts like its other businesses. Mr. Bogner calls Ethyl an "aggressive buy" that could earn $2.10 a share this year and $2.40 next year. If the additive is approved, he might raise his 1991 estimate five cents to 10 cents a share. Ethyl, based in Richmond, Va., "could trade in the low 30s over the next six to nine months" without the additive, and in the high 30s within two years if MMT is approved, he says.

John Henry of Merrill Lynch Capital Markets has recommended Ethyl for some time, in part because of the additive's potential. His estimates are $2.05 this year and $2.35 in 1991, and he says MMT could be worth an extra 20 cents to 30 cents a share in 1991 and a $1 a share in several years: "All this proposed legislation is creating markets for products that help gas burn cleaner."

Other companies are benefiting from the drive for cleaner fuels. Arco Chemical reports fast sales growth for MTBE, an ether-based product that increases octane and reduces emissions when blended with gasoline. Ethyl says its additive can be used with MTBE or other agents to provide greater reductions in nitrogen oxides.
