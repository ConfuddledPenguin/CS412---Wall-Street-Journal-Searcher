War Against Federal Waste Not Won

  • Author: Alan Keyes
  • Date: 05/10/90

President Bush wants budget negotiations "unfettered with conclusions about positions taken in the past." In plain English, Mr. Bush is distancing himself from his "read-my-lips" no-new-taxes campaign pledge. By this action, the president may be taking the country a step closer to economic disaster. Mr. Bush may believe that soaring deficits will do more harm to his political fortunes than will his capitulation on the issue of new taxes. But if this is his view, it is short-sighted, because new taxes will further depress an already sluggish economy, ushering in a new recession for which the president -- not Congress -- will be held responsible.

Further, the notion that deficits are caused by low taxes is a complete falsehood. In fiscal 1990, federal revenues will increase by $70 billion as a result of economic growth. We have chronic deficits because, while income tax revenues have grown by 8% a year during the past decade, Congress has increased spending by 11% a year.

In January 1990, on national television, Charles A. Bowsher, head of the General Accounting Office, Congress's investigative agency, estimated that the federal government squanders $180 billion of the taxpayers' money each year through waste, fraud and mismanagement.

This is an amount equal to more than one-third of estimated personal income tax receipts in the current fiscal year, and $50 billion more than the estimated $129.7 billion in corporate income taxes. And it's nearly three times current fiscal year's Gramm-Rudman deficit target of $64 billion.

Just where is the waste? Perhaps a more appropriate question is: Where isn't it? In November 1989, the General Accounting Office reported that 17 major federal agencies had "material weaknesses" in financial management and internal control systems that "cost taxpayers billions of dollars" every year. Specific losses cited by the GAO include: $6 billion at the Export Import Bank; $12 billion at the Commodity Credit Corporation; $10-$20 billion in foreign military sales; and $4 billion at the Department of Veterans Affairs.

The worst single example of government waste in our nation's history is, without question, the savings and loan fiasco. This was a pure product of the perverse incentives built into the deposit insurance system, combined with lax oversight by Congress and the federal regulators.

Official estimates of the cost of the S&L bailout now run between $325 billion and $500 billion. This will more than wipe out any savings that our nation may gain through the so-called "peace dividend."

In an April poll by the Wirthlin Group, taxpayers were given a choice as to how they would reduce the budget deficit. Among four options, the results were: eliminate government waste, 64%; cut defense spending, 27%; cut domestic programs, 4%; and raise taxes, 2%. Where should the president and Congress begin? Citizens Against Government Waste has compiled a list of waste-cutting initiatives that could save taxpayers $280 billion over the next 5 years. Nearly all of these initiatives come from reputable sources readily accessible to the president and Congress, such as the General Accounting Office, the Congressional Budget Office, the Grace Commission, the Heritage Foundation, and the Inspectors General of the various federal departments. A few examples:

-- Requiring employers to make prompt payment of accumulated payroll tax deposits of $3,000 or more would generate approximately $14.5 billion in savings to the Treasury, Social Security and Medicare Trust funds.

-- Establishing user fees for federal services enjoyed chiefly or exclusively by specific individuals or businesses would save taxpayers $12 billion.

-- Closing down strategically insignificant military bases and properties would save taxpayers $5 billion.

Alexander Hamilton wrote in the "Federalist Papers" that the real test of a democratic form of government, the necessary but not sufficient condition of its survival, would be whether or not it could sustain a good administration, the frugal and proper and wise use of the resources of the government and the people. Wasteful spending by the federal government has put the financial integrity of the U.S. at risk, and is undermining the accountability of our political system.

---

Mr. Keyes is president of Citizens Against Government Waste, in Washington, D.C.
