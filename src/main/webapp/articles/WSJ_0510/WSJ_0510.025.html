Politics & Policy: ABB Unit Proposes High-Speed Rails In Eastern Canada

  • Author: Larry M. Greenberg
  • Date: 05/10/90

TORONTO -- Asea Brown Boveri Inc. unveiled a 2.9 billion Canadian dollar (US$2.5 billion) plan to build a high-speed train link between Quebec City and Windsor, Ontario, that rivals a more expensive plan previously proposed by Bombardier Inc. Peter Janson, president and chief executive officer of ABB, said an initial 540-kilometer (338-mile) section of the line could be built between Montreal and Toronto for C$1.8 billion in about three years. The total line, comprising 1,160 kilometers, would take about seven years to construct, he said. Montreal-based ABB is a wholly owned unit of Swiss engineering concern Asea Brown Boveri Group.

Mr. Janson spoke at a news conference here to announce the results of a preliminary study of the high-speed train plan. He expects a full feasibility study to be completed by the end of the year. The company today will present its findings to an Ontario-Quebec task force that has been examining options for passenger transportation in the Quebec City-Windsor corridor.

In February, Bombardier, an aerospace and transporation concern based in Montreal, offered its plan for a high-speed rail link in the Quebec City-Windsor corridor at an estimated cost of C$5.3 billion. The plan, which would use the French TGV (very fast train) technology, would require about C$1.6 billion in government backing.

But Mr. Janson said the ABB plan could be funded mostly with private money. "We believe . . . the project can be done with minimal or no government financing," he said.

The ABB train, dubbed the Sprintor, would employ the company's X2 rail technology currently in use in Sweden. This enables electrically powered high-speed trains, traveling at up to 250 kilometers (156 miles) per hour, to use existing tracks without any alterations the banking of track curves. By contrast, the Bombardier plan would require the laying of new tracks along existing rights-of-way.

Both ABB and Bombardier estimate their high-speed trains would cut travel time between Montreal and Toronto to about two hours and 45 minutes from about five hours currently.

A Bombardier spokesman declined to comment on the ABB plan.
