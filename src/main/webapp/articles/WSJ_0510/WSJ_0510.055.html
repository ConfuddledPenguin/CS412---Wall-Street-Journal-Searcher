Marketing & Media: Lindner-Run Firm Is Seeking Buyers For Radio Stations

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 05/10/90

Great American Communications Co. is quietly shopping around some of its radio properties. The move, by the Cincinnati company run by financier Carl H. Lindner, underscores the pressure for cash facing the highly leveraged company. Great American, which purchased Taft Broadcasting Co. in October 1987 for about $1.5 billion, had a $115.6 million loss in 1989 on revenue of $300.6 million.

Great American's long-term debt as of Dec. 31 totaled $1.19 billion. In October, the company will have to pay $165 million as its first principal payments on that debt. According to several individuals in the radio industry, Great American would like to raise about $150 million in the sale of the radio stations.

The company owns 11 FM stations, six AM stations and five network-affiliated TV stations. None of the television stations are for sale. It also has a controlling interest in Spelling Entertainment Inc., which produced such TV shows as "The Love Boat" and "Dynasty," and Hanna-Barbera Productions Inc., the producers of such animated shows as "The Flintstones" and "The Jetsons."

Carl J. Wagner, president of Great American Broadcasting Co., a unit of Great American, couldn't be reached to comment. However, several people close to the process said Great American is interested in selling stations in Atlanta, Kansas City, Mo., Columbus, Ohio, and Indianapolis. One person with knowledge of the situation said that New York radio broker Gary Stevens is handling the bidding for the radio stations.

Mr. Stevens declined to comment.

"Great American will sell some media properties," said one person. "Ultimately, it may not be the radio stations, but something will go."

Great American is asking from $50 million to $60 million for its Atlanta FM station, $50 million for the Indianapolis AM-FM properties, from $25 million to $30 million for Columbus AM-FM stations, and about $30 million for the Kansas City AM-FM properties.

One person close to the process said Great American "is pretty firm on these prices at this point. They're independent thinkers and if they don't get the price they want, they won't sell."

There are other options to raise cash.

In October, Spelling Entertainment put itself on the block six months after control of the company went from founder Aaron Spelling to Mr. Lindner. Spelling Entertainment retained Merrill Lynch & Co.'s Capital Markets unit and Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. to handle the possible sale.

To date, Spelling Entertainment hasn't been sold. And it isn't clear what price Spelling Entertainment could fetch.

Spelling Entertainment has a library of one-hour prime-time series, such as "Charlie's Angels" and "Hotel." Such shows have recently not sold well in syndication, the traditionally lucrative marketplace where shows are sold to individual stations. Half-hour situation comedies now are the hot properties in syndication sales to local stations.

In over-the-counter trading yesterday, Great American rose 37.5 cents to close at $6.625.
