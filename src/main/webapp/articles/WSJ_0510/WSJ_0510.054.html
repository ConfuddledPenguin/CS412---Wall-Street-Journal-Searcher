Bank-Failure Rate Has Slowed Sharply This Year, Chairman of the FDIC Says

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr. and Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 05/10/90

WASHINGTON -- Bank failures have slowed considerably this year, said William Seidman, chairman of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. As of Tuesday, 52 banks with assets of $4.7 billion had failed in 1990. By contrast, at this time last year, 73 banks with $19.7 billion in assets had failed or received FDIC assistance in order to continue operating, Mr. Seidman said. No banks have received such FDIC aid this year.

Last year's figures included 20 bank subsidiaries of Dallas-based MCorp, with assets of $15.6 billion. For all of last year, 207 banks with $29 billion in assets failed or were assisted by the FDIC.

Mr. Seidman said in an interview that initial figures show the FDIC's bank deposit-insurance fund had a loss of about $800 million last year, larger than the $250 million-to-$500 million loss he predicted late last year. He said the larger loss stemmed from greater-than-expected costs from the MCorp failures and from the failure this year of Seamen's Bank for Savings, based in New York. (Under its accounting rules, the FDIC must account for some of those costs in its 1989 results.)

That loss will leave the deposit-insurance fund with about $13.2 billion, or about 0.7% of insured deposits. Government policy is to try to maintain the fund at about 1.25% of insured deposits. Final figures on the fund's performance last year are to be released May 18.

Mr. Seidman said the fund could increase somewhat during this year, but the failure of a large bank would probably cause a loss. He wouldn't say whether he was expecting a large bank failure.

Mr. Seidman, who is also chairman of the Resolution Trust Corp., said the RTC may abandon its policy that permits buyers of insolvent thrifts to repudiate rates paid on certificates of deposit after two weeks. The RTC is the agency charged with selling or liquidating hundreds of insolvent thrifts taken over by the government.

In a meeting scheduled for May 18, the agency will consider requiring buyers to honor CD rates for three months, he said. The policy has recently been recommended to the RTC board by its staff, he said.

The move would mean that buyers of insolvent thrifts would know exactly the cost of funds from the market, Mr. Seidman said, and that depositors -- often big-deposit brokers -- would have the assurance that their rates would be locked in for at least 90 days after the thrift is sold. Depositors, for example, would be able to compare rates at insolvent thrifts with those of 90-day Treasury instruments, he said.

Eliminating the uncertainty ultimately means lower costs for obtaining funds and greater liquidity, Mr. Seidman said. "If someone has uncertainty, they are going to require a higher rate," he said.

Mr. Seidman said the RTC's ambitious project to sell about 140 thrifts by the end of June to get the troubled thrift-industry bailout off the ground is "on schedule."

He said sales of assets separately from thrift institutions were also moving forward, with about $600 million in sales last month. But he said rising interest rates may make such asset sales more difficult in coming weeks.
