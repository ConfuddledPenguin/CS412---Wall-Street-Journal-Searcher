Abreast of the Market: Stocks End Mixed Amid Weakness In Bonds and Decline in the Dollar

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 05/10/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market's advance over the past week and a half finally faltered, as prices finished mixed amid weakness in bonds and a sell-off in the dollar. The Dow Jones Industrial Average broke a string of seven consecutive advances by easing 0.68 to 2732.88. Earnings-related selling in Woolworth and weakness in a few other blue-chip issues put pressure on the average.

Other market measures, however, managed to extend their winning streaks. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index went up 0.85 to 342.86, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 0.38 to 187.49 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index rose 0.46 to 318.68.

Market speculation that the Treasury's auction of $10 billion in 10-year notes, the second stage of its quarterly refinancing, had been disappointing sent the market to session lows in the last hour of trading.

But after the Treasury released the results of the note sale, which were in line with market expectations, prices quickly recovered as bonds bounced off their lows and stock-index futures reached sharp premiums to the cash indexes, sparking index-arbitrage buying in stocks.

Other arbitrage-related buy programs, involving the sale of index futures and the purchase of baskets of stocks to profit from price differences between them, had surfaced early in the session to lend support to the market.

Despite the late improvement, Treasury notes and bonds showed declines of as much as 1/2 point, or $5 per $1,000 face amount, in late dealings.

Analysts also noted the dollar's downturn, attributed to a growing belief that U.S. interest rates may have reached a peak, as a drag on the market. In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.6360 marks and 156.50 yen, down from 1.6573 marks and 157.62 yen late Tuesday.

Edward P. Nicoski, managing director of technical analysis at Piper, Jaffray & Hopwood, Minneapolis, said the market appears headed for some slippage during the next one to two weeks after approaching the upper end of its trading range this year. "I'd be real concerned about tomorrow just because of the dollar situation," he said.

Others said today's final stage of the Treasury refinancing -- a sale of $10 billion in 30-year bonds -- may pose problems for stocks if it doesn't go well and interest rates rise.

Woolworth, which reported that its fiscal first-quarter earnings went up 5.5% from the year-ago level, dropped 1 to 60 5/8. The results for the latest period were on the low end of analysts' forecasts.

Other blue chips to weigh on the industrial average included Westinghouse Electric, which went down 1 1/2 to 74; Aluminum Co. of America, off 1/2 to 63 1/2, and USX, down 1/2 to 32 7/8.

SmithKline Beecham, whose first-quarter earnings were in line with analysts' forecasts, gained 1 to 37 3/4 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading of 1.9 million shares. Pretax profit fell 6.8% from the year-earlier period on a pro-forma basis, which takes into account the merger of SmithKline Beckman and Beecham Group last year, while sales rose 15%.

Another drug stock, Bristol-Myers Squibb, gained 1/2 to 58 1/2 and topped the Big Board's list of most active issues as 2.2 million shares were traded. The brisk activity apparently was spurred by talk that the New England Journal of Medicine would carry two positive reports and a favorable editorial on the company's AIDS drug DDI in today's issue, market contacts told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Also, Sanford C. Bernstein reiterated its buy recommendation on the issue, partly because of the expected articles.

Warner-Lambert gained 7/8 to 108 1/2. The company told investors attending a health care conference in Baltimore that it is "on target" to achieve its goal of at least a 16% rise in earnings per share this year. Eli Lilly, which was also scheduled to appear at the conference, rose 3/4 to 69 on 1.1 million shares.

Sotheby's Holdings plunged 3 1/8 to 16 1/2 on 1.3 million shares amid disappointment with its sale of contemporary art Tuesday night. About 37% of the art works that the auction house put up for bids weren't sold, and many other pieces were sold at prices below Sotheby's lowest published estimate.

Eastman Kodak added 1/2 to 38 1/8. The company said it expects to post "higher sales, better earnings and improved cash flows" later this year because of its restructuring efforts; it also forecast annual sales gains of 20% in Eastern Europe for its photography operations.

Federal Express dropped 1 1/2 to 52 1/8.

Deutsche Lufthansa confirmed that it is holding talks along with "other parties" to acquire a minority stake in DHL Worldwide Express, seen as a possible defensive move against an aggressive overseas expansion by Federal Express. Others in the talks reportedly include two Japanese firms, Nissho Iwai and Japan Air Lines.

Shaw Industries skidded 2 7/8 to 30 1/4. Merrill Lynch and Wheat First Butcher & Singer lowered estimates of the company's earnings for its fiscal fourth quarter, which ends in June. The company acknowledged results are slightly behind internal projections, but wouldn't elaborate.

Sherwin-Williams jumped 2 to 38 1/8. The company said it is unaware of any reasons for the move.

Stone Container fell 7/8 to 16 1/2. The Canadian Paperworkers Union has selected three paper mills owned by the company as the target of contract negotiations that will set the pattern for most of the forest-products industry in eastern Canada.

PNC Financial dropped 1 1/4 to 36 7/8 amid speculation that the company might be facing problems in its financial dealings with NVRyan, a Virginia-based home builder. But PNC said that its loan exposure to NVR is "modest" and the loan is current in principal and interest.

Nalco Chemical rose 1 1/4 to 53. Salomon Brothers reiterated a buy recommendation on the stock and said the company can be considered an environmental play because it sells chemicals for water and waste treatment.

Mattel rose 1/2 to 24 after declaring a common dividend of three cents a share, its first payout since June 1983. Separately, its board authorized the repurchase of as many as 500,000 shares over the next 12 months to fund a stock-option plan.

American Capital Management & Research gained 3/4 to 9 1/8. Primerica, which already has an 82.6% stake in the company, agreed to swap 0.3 share of its common for each of the shares it doesn't own. Primerica finished unchanged at 29 5/8.

Xtra dropped 3/8 to 22 1/8. The company posted a loss from continuing operations of $1.66 a share for the March quarter and cut its dividend by 33%, to 12 cents a share; it called the reduction temporary.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.95 to 348.53. Volume totaled 14,560,000 shares.

California Energy, which plummeted 1 7/8 Tuesday, rebounded 5/8 to 10 3/8. The company said it had arranged financing for its proposed acquisition of some of Chevron's geothermal operations.

Greiner Engineering went up 3/4 to 15 3/4. The company signed a contract with the Hong Kong government to serve as planning and engineering consultant for the development of a new airport.

Forest Laboratories gained 3/8 to 37 7/8 after reporting that net income for the fiscal quarter ended in March rose about 26% from a year earlier.
