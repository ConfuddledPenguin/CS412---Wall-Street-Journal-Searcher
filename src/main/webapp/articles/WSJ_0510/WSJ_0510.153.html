Credit Markets: Treasury's Sale of 10-Year Notes Is Big Success As Investors Flock to Get 8.88% Average Yield

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 05/10/90

NEW YORK -- The Treasury's 10-year note auction was a big success, drawing unexpectedly strong demand from institutional and individual investors who were attracted by the best yields in a year. "It was a very strong auction," said Thomas Pura, a partner at Goldman Sachs & Co. "Investors find securities attractive at these levels."

The Treasury sold $10 billion of the new 10-year notes at an average yield of 8.88%. That was much higher than the average yield of 8.59% at the previous 10-year note auction in February and the highest yield at an auction since 9.18% last May.

Yesterday's sale was the second part of the Treasury's three-part $30.5 billion quarterly refunding of the federal debt. The Treasury sold $10.5 billion of three-year notes on Tuesday and will conclude the auction with the sale of $10 billion of 30-year bonds today.

Mr. Pura said yesterday's auction, coming on top of a strong three-year note auction on Tuesday, increases the odds that today's 30-year bond sale will go smoothly.

Bond dealers said that all types of investors -- large, small, domestic and foreign -- were eager to buy the Treasury's new 10-year notes.

F. Ward McCarthy, managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates, said that investors were especially happy because yields at the auction were just high enough for the Treasury to set a 8 7/8% coupon rate on the notes.

"If the average yield had been one basis point (one one-hundredth of a percentage point) lower, the coupon would have been set at 8 3/4%. That one basis point was important and made the notes more attractive to the investment community," said Mr. McCarthy.

Michael Moore, manager of government trading at Continental Bank, said investors eager to buy the 10-year notes spent much of the morning session selling 30-year, seven-year and five-year Treasury securities "to make room for the new 10-year." He said demand for the 10-year notes was strong because the yield was relatively high compared to yields on some other Treasury securities.

The success of the auction was clearly unexpected. Traders said that some dealers, expecting only moderate or even weak demand for the 10-year notes, placed bids that were too low and subsequently did not get enough securities to meet customer orders. Mr. McCarthy estimates that only 33% of the major New York dealers that tendered bids were actually awarded bonds.

As a result, some firms were forced to scramble to buy the new 10-year Treasury notes in the secondary market. Evidence of that was apparent in the unusually heavy trading activity in the new 10-year note by interdealer brokers, intermediaries that buy and sell securities for the 44 primary dealers of government bonds. Interdealer brokers do not trade with the general public.

Traders said about $1 billion of the notes were exchanged in when-issued trading activity at yields of 8.85% to 8.86%, far below the auction rates. Of that amount, $630 million was said to have been traded by RMJ Securities Corp., one of the five major interdealer brokers. RMJ declined to comment.

Individual investors, who purchased a record amount of three-year notes at Tuesday's sale, also showed a hearty appetite for the new 10-year notes.

Individual investors typically buy securities at Treasury auctions through noncompetitive tenders, which totaled $659 million at the 10-year note sale. That was well above the average noncompetitive bids of $427 million at the previous 12 auctions, according to Maria Ramirez Capital Consultants Inc.

The last time noncompetitive bids at a 10-year auction were higher than yesterday was in August 1985, according to Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., when noncompetitive tenders totaled $686 million.

Japanese investors, as expected, bought large amounts of the 10-year notes. A trader at one of Japan's largest securities firm estimates that the Japanese purchased about 30% of the new 10-year notes.

Treasury Securities

Bond prices moved sharply lower ahead of the government's 10-year note auction, as dealers cut prices in an effort to drum up demand for the government's new securities.

Traders said the market's weakness ahead of auction also reflected higher commodity prices, a weaker dollar on foreign exchange markets and a large sale of mortgage-backed securities by the Resolution Trust Corp. RTC, the government agency created to bailout the nation's troubled savings and loan associations, could not be reached for comment.

Analysts said the RTC sale depressed the market because buyers of the securities had to sell Treasurys to hedge their purchase of mortgage-backed securities.

Treasury bond prices received a modest boost late yesterday as dealers scrambled to buy new 10-year notes in when-issued trading activity. But the lift wasn't large enough to offset the large declines that occurred earlier in the day.

The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond, which was quoted as much as 3/4 point lower in early trading, ended about 1/2 point lower. The bond was quoted late at a price of 96 2/32 to yield 8.86% compared with 96 16/32 to yield 8.82%.

Short-term rates were slightly lower. The rates on three-month Treasury bills was 7.75% for a bond equivalent yield of 7.99% while the rate on six-month bills eased slightly to 7.77% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.18%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended 7/32 to 9/32 lower in moderate trading.

Government National Mortgage Association's 9% securities for May delivery finished at 94 1/4, down 9/32. The agency's 9 1/2% issue was at 96 25/32, down 8/32, and its 10% issue was quoted at 99 7/32, down 8/32. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp.'s 9 1/2% issue ended at 96 18/32, down 7/32.

The Ginnie Mae 9% issue was yielding 9.99% to a 12-year average life assumption.

In the derivative markets, Freddie Mac issued a $500 million real estate mortgage investment conduit, or Remic, backed by its 10% securities through Salomon Brothers Inc. UBS Securities Inc. offered $300 million of Remic securities backed by Ginnie Mae's 9 1/2% issue.

In the asset-backed market, Rochester Community Savings Bank offered about $125 million of certificates backed by automobile and light-truck loans at a yield of 9.51% through First Boston Corp.

The issue by the Rochester, N.Y.-based savings bank was rated triple-A by Moody's Investors Service Inc.

Junk Bonds

Trump Plaza junk bonds climbed yesterday after The Wall Street Journal's "Heard on the Street" column reported that some bargain hunters are buying the bonds.

Trump Plaza is one of several Atlantic City casinos operated by real estate developer Donald Trump. The Plaza's 12 7/8% bonds maturing in 1998 gained 1 1/2 to end at 83. Other Trump junk bonds, including the Trump Taj Mahal Funding and Trump Castle Funding, showed little change.

Most junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, ended unchanged to about 1/4 point higher.

Investment-grade corporate bonds were 1/8 to 1/4 point lower.

Municipal Issues

Municipal issues fell 3/8 point in quiet dealings but the market still feels positive, according to traders.

"Retail is still buying bonds," one trader said. He attributed the lack of activity to investors waiting for the outcome of the final leg of the Treasury refunding today.

An unsold balance of $17.8 million remained on an $65 million issue of Delaware general obligation bonds won by a Manufacturers Securities Inc. group. The bonds were priced for reoffering to yield from 6% in 1991 to 7.20% in 2009 and 2010.

Among dollar bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue due in 2018 were 3/8 lower at 96 1/4 to yield 7.50%.

Foreign Bonds

A stronger yen and falling money market rates pushed up Japanese government bond prices.

The dollar ended at 157.03 yen in Tokyo, down from 157.63 yen in late New York trading and 158.05 yen at the Tokyo close on Tuesday.

But dealers don't expect the yen's firmness to last. They say that after this week's $30.5 billion U.S. Treasury refunding that the demand for dollars will increase among Japanese investors who have bought the new Treasury issues.

Elsewhere, West Germany government bonds rose when international investors, long absent from the market, rushed to buy mark bonds.

"There's a bit of a positive sentiment in this market and no selling except for some domestic investors taking profits," said a West German bond trader. "And that seems to be drying up."

Bonn's 7 3/4% issue due in 2000 rose 0.35 point to 95.20 to yield 8.48%, while its 8% notes due in 1995 jumped 0.25 point to 96.60 to yield 8.89%.

Bond prices fell in Britain.
