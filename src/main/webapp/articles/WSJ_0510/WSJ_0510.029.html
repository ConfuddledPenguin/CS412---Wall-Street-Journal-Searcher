LEISURE & ARTS: TV Preview: Cellist, Chuckles and Plenty of Sweepers

  • Author: Martha Bayles
  • Date: 05/10/90

Thursday, May 10, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on A&E (repeated May 13): "Yo-Yo Ma: A Month at Tanglewood." Also appearing in this mellow film about the cellist at work are Isaac Stern, Bobby McFerrin, Emanuel Ax and Nali Gruber, among others.

Saturday, May 12, 8 p.m.-1 a.m. EDT, on HBO: "Comic Relief." This fund-raiser for the homeless is now an annual event, preceded by local comedy shows. Billy Crystal, Whoopi Goldberg and Robin Williams co-host.

Sunday, May 13, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on CBS: "Shattered Dreams." The abusive marriage and eventual recovery of Charlotte Fedders (wife of prominent SEC official John Fedders) are dramatized in this May Sweeper, starring Lindsay Wagner.

Sunday-Monday, May 13-14, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on NBC: "People Like Us." Murder and intrigue among hoity-toity New Yorkers are featured in this rival Sweeper starring Connie Sellecca and Ben Gazzara.

Tuesdays, May 15-29, 8-9 p.m. EDT, on TDC (repeated on subsequent Thursdays and Fridays): "Arabia: Sand, Sea & Sky." Mobil and two Saudi corporations (Sabic and Saudi Aramco) underwrote this nature walk from the lush mountains of the southwest to the Red Sea and the great desert.

Tuesday, May 15, 9-10 p.m. EDT, on PBS (PBS air dates and times vary, so check local listings): "Seven Days in Bensonhurst." You may not want to relive the racial paroxysm that followed last August's murder of a young New Yorker named Yusuf Hawkins. But you may want to wrestle with its meaning, as writer Shelby Steele tries to do in this edition of "Frontline." More in Monday's column.

Friday, May 18, 5-5:30 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "Adam Smith's Money World." A rare TV interview with the legendary billionaire investor Warren Buffett. Also featured: Katharine Graham and Daniel Burke.

Saturday, May 19, 8-9:40 p.m. EDT, on HBO (repeated May 21, 24, 27, 29, June 1, 6 and 10): "By Dawn's Early Light." What if they remade "Dr. Strangelove" without the anti-military satire? The result would resemble this HBO film about the early stages of a nuclear conflict begun by mistake during an era of good superpower relations. With Powers Boothe, Rebecca De Mornay and James Earl Jones as a senior officer -- a nice touch, since he played a junior officer in the 1964 "Dr. Strangelove."

Sunday-Monday, May 20-21, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on ABC: "Voices Within: The Lives of Truddi Chase." This Sweeper miniseries stars Shelley Long (formerly of "Cheers") as a woman with multiple personalities caused by a traumatic childhood. The obvious comparison is with "Sybil," an outstanding 1976 TV movie starring Sally Field and Joanne Woodward (now available on video). Unfortunately, not even Ms. Long's intelligent acting can rescue the present film, which takes the attitude that multiple personalities can be fun.

Sunday, May 20, starting at 6 p.m. EDT, on Bravo (repeated on May 29): "The South Bank Show" and "The Modern World: Ten Great Writers." Both regular programs will focus on the fiction of Franz Kafka. Included is an interview with Steven Berkoff, who adapted Kafka's "Metamorphosis" to the stage; interviews with three actors who have played Gregor Samsa, the character who turns into a giant cockroach; a TV version of "Metamorphosis"; and a presentation of another Kafka work, "The Trial."

Tuesday, May 22, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on CBS: "Killing in a Small Town." Meanwhile, back among the Sweepers . . . This film about a nice Texas lady who also happens to be an ax murderer has a fine cast -- Barbara Hershey, Brian Dennehy and Hal Holbrook.

Tuesday, May 22, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on NBC: "Last Flight Out." Don't blame me -- Sweepers are always broadcast opposite one another] This entry, fully sponsored by AT&T, stars Richard Crenna and James Earl Jones as an American airline pilot and airport official who presided over the departure of the last overloaded Pan Am plane from Saigon on April 24, 1975. It's refreshing, after a few seasons of Oliver Stone, to see a Vietnam movie in which the Communists are actually perceived as a threat. But unfortunately, historical accuracy doesn't automatically lead to good drama -- the film is earnest but plodding.
