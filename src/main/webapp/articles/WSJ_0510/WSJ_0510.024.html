Alaska's House Rejects Senate-Backed Ethics Bill

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/10/90

The Alaska House of Representatives rejected a Senate-sponsored code of ethics bill that would have made the state's ethics law perhaps the toughest in the nation. The measure died 26-14 in the final minutes of the legislature's regular annual session.

Proponents vowed to introduce a similar bill when the legislature reconvenes next fall. As written, the measure would have severely curbed legislators' dealings with lobbyists, banned the acceptance of travel expenses of more than $100 from private sources, outlawed post-election fund-raising to retire campaign debt and kept legislators from serving on most corporate boards.

The measure earlier sailed through the Alaska Senate, which has been rocked in recent years by prolonged ethics investigations involving two of its members. The bill was modeled upon recommendations of an outside ethics expert brought in by Senate leaders seeking to rehabilitate that body's battered public image.

The bill received the strong endorsement of state government watchdog groups and most of the state's newspapers. Opponents, including most of the state's registered lobbyists, argued that the bill was overly broad.
