Senate Ethics Panel Expands Durenberger Case

  • Author: Jill Abramson
  • Date: 05/10/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Ethics Committee has expanded its case against Sen. David Durenberger (R., Minn.) and will examine a new charge that he violated government ethics laws regarding blind trusts. The committee also announced yesterday that it had found "substantial credible evidence" that Sen. Durenberger may have "abused" his Senate office by improperly receiving reimbursement for staying in a condominium he partly owned; submitting improper reimbursement vouchers for travel; misrepresenting the ownership of the condominium, and back-dating real estate transactions.

Although the allegation that Sen. Durenberger may have violated the Ethics in Government Act's provisions relating to communication about his blind trust is new, the committee's expansion of the investigation to include the condominium arrangement was expected.

"This is the logical next step," said Sen. Durenberger's administrative assistant, Bert McKasy. According to Sen. Durenberger's press secretary, Lois West, the blind-trust issue involves the senator's communication with his attorney, who was also administrator of the trust, concerning the condominium.

Last January, Sen. Durenberger repaid the Senate $11,005 after the Rules Committee decided he shouldn't have been reimbursed for use of the condominium during congressional recesses.

The ethics panel had announced earlier this month that it would hold trial-like hearings beginning June 12 to determine whether Sen. Durenberger had broken Senate rules and federal law in a $100,000 book deal he had with Piranha Press. The expanded charges announced yesterday will be combined with the charges relating to the book deal.

Ethics committee members will act as a jury to hear evidence and determine whether Sen. Durenberger, who has served in the Senate for 12 years, should be punished. If any of the allegations are proven, possible punishment ranges from reprimand to expulsion.
