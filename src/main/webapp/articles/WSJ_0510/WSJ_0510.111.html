Letters to the Editor: What Does `Homeless' Really Mean?

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/10/90

Randall K. Filer gave a good analysis of why policy makers are bombarded with conflicting assessments of the homeless problem ("What We Really Know About the Homeless," editorial page, April 10). The confusion may lie more in how "homeless" is defined in the various studies, rather than the differing estimates and explanations derived. Federal statutes define homeless to mean "lower-income families and individuals, elderly families and individuals, and handicapped families and individuals that lack permanent housing, reside in grossly inadequate or grossly substandard accommodations, or whose primary residence is in institutions of any type without a fixed and permanent place to live upon discharge."

Other definitions include those who sleep in shelters, parks, abandoned buildings, and rented hotel and motel rooms paid for by public or private agencies; individuals doubled-up with friends and relatives, or living in short-term rental housing; those in institutional care with no permanent address; or those considered "at risk" of becoming homeless.

This diversity leads to contradictory estimates. For example, the U.S. Conference of Mayors estimates that as much as 50% of the homeless population is made up of families. The Campaign to End Hunger and Homelessness indicates that roughly two-thirds of the homeless are single men. The Friends Committee on National Legislation claims that nearly 30% of all homeless people are employed. The National Academy of Sciences' Institute of Medicine estimates that there are at least 100,000 homeless children on any given night. And the National Coalition for the Homeless claims that 60% of the homeless population consists of women and children.

We may never be able to deal with the homeless problem until there is a consensus on what it means to be "homeless."

Rep. David Dreier (R., Calif.)

Washington

Mr. Filer talks about "mental health," drug and alcohol problems, and criminal records being more common among low-income people and the homeless. He is, of course, really talking about mental illness. The substitution for mental health when we mean mental illness comes from our strong tendency to accentuate the positive. But such desire is misplaced when it lets us ignore the fact of serious mental illness.

Once we acknowledge that mental illness does not go away by focusing on mental health, but that it does respond to treatment, we will seek asylum for those who need it, and cut down the homeless and the prison populations by that much.

Elsie Roberts

Rhodesdale, Md.

The housing hurdle for the homeless has been raised another notch, this time by Mr. Filer, who takes the not unusual academician's approach to a problem -- study it to death before taking any action. No matter that Mr. Filer admits, "Issues of causality are complicated by the fact that many factors are interrelated." According to Mr. Filer's logic, we should not attempt to save a drowning man, but rather reflect on how he came to be in his predicament.

There are loads of available statistics on the homeless, and perhaps Mr. Filer's desire is a $1 million government grant to unearth them. Let me save Uncle Sam some money.

General Assistance in the Minneapolis-St. Paul area provides $203 a month for single people who have little or no income and meet eligibility standards. Food stamps, county health-care assistance and clinics are also available. But affordable housing is not. The Metropolitan Apartment Guide figures state that a studio apartment in North St. Paul rents for $292 a month, a one-bedroom unit for $377. In the suburbs, the rents are $505 and $571 respectively.

One doesn't need a Ph.D. to get the message -- a homeless person can't afford housing here or in most places in the country. The answer is money or -- more precisely -- lower-cost housing for the poor.

It doesn't matter whether the homeless number 350,000 or three million. Even 350,000 is an intolerable number and a national disgrace in a country so blessed with abundance.

Michael P. Ducar

IDS Financial Services Inc.

Minneapolis

Mr. Filer attributed great significance and mystery to just another buzzword of these weird times in America. The words "homeless" and "homelessness" occurred 46 times. Left out entirely were the terms Foodless, Shoeless, Clothesless, Transportationless and even Dollarless. Assetlessness would have a nice buzz. But how about using the term "Poverty"? Buzzwords are harmful; they are used, with enormous help from the media, to empower advocacies and government bureaus. As almost everyone knows, problems are thereby created, and seldom solved. An afterthought: Someone should address the topic, "Poverty Problems Much Worse Elsewhere Than in the U.S. -- Why?" There should be thoughtful discussion on what we're doing right. That might lead to some useful non-programs that would surely help the impoverished.

Richard K. Wilson

Palos Verdes, Calif.
