Who's News: Unisys' Caswell Regigns as Head Of Mainframes

  • Author: Paul B. Carroll
  • Date: 05/10/90

Hollis Caswell, the widely respected head of Unisys Corp.'s mainframe business, resigned to run a start-up business that makes electronics using superconductors. Mr. Caswell, 58 years old, said in an interview that his decision didn't relate to the heavy losses that the computer and defense-electronics company has posted over the past year and a half. Still, executives who have worked with him said his departure is a loss for Unisys.

"He was one of my bosses at IBM, and one of the best bosses I've worked for," said Paul Stern, chief executive officer of Northern Telecom Ltd. "He's a terrific guy, and I don't say that lightly."

Mr. Caswell wins points inside Unisys for his work in bringing to market the much-delayed replacements to the 1100 series of mainframes made by Sperry Corp., which merged with Burroughs Corp. in 1986 to form Unisys. Although there was a last-minute hiccup that delayed the midsized models by three months, Unisys executives said the process went much more smoothly after Mr. Caswell took over. Customers generally compliment the machines -- even as some consider moving to hardware for which there is more software available.

"There are some real nice things about the hardware, things that IBM doesn't have," said James H. Johnson, director of financial reporting and budgeting at Giant Eagle Inc., a supermarket chain based in Pittsburgh. He added, though, that "There aren't many {software} systems that run on the Unisys hardware. That's one of the problems."

Mr. Caswell will become chief executive of Hypres Inc., Elmsford, N.Y., a closely held concern with about 40 employees and $5 million a year in revenue. He said the move gives him a fresh challenge and returns him to an early interest of his: He worked on superconductor research when he joined International Business Machines Corp. in the late 1950s and later did extensive research on superconductor-based devices known as Josephson junctions. Mr. Caswell said he thinks Hypres has a good chance to get its devices used in such equipment as telephone switches, which may require the huge increases in speed that superconductors can provide.

Unisys, which is based in Blue Bell, Pa., said that responsibility for its mainframe operations will be shifted to Cyril Yansouni, 47, who will also retain control of its personal-computer and workstation businesses. A spokesman said the company had been considering merging the two operations, in any case. Unisys, like many computer companies, is focusing primarily these days on making it easier for all types of machines to communicate with one another, and the company hopes the combination will facilitate that.

Mr. Yansouni, who had been a senior vice president, becomes one of two executive vice presidents.

Unisys also said that it consolidated some of its marketing operations to improve the coordination among them. Reto Braun, president of the Pacific Asia Americas division, was also given responsibility for the general U.S. marketing operation and one targeted at corporate customers. Mr. Braun, 48, also becomes an executive vice president; he, too, had been a senior vice president.
