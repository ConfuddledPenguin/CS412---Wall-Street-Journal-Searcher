Interco Won't Pay Interest Due June 15 On Bonds; Firm Sets Debt Restructuring

  • Author: James P. Miller and George Anders
  • Date: 05/10/90

Interco Inc. said it won't pay interest due June 15 on its bonds, proposing instead that bondholders accept a debt restructuring of nearly $900 million. St. Louis-based Interco's finances have deteriorated steadily since the furniture and shoe company carried out a leveraged recapitalization in late 1988. At that time, Interco borrowed about $2 billion to fight off a corporate raider. Since then, Interco has posted a series of operating losses and has failed to meet its price targets for selling assets.

Interco's restructuring plan isn't likely to appeal to bondholders. It offers little in the way of new interest-paying bonds, and relies heavily on issuing more Interco common stock, which closed yesterday in composite New York Stock Exchange trading at 62.5 cents a share, unchanged.

In fact, analysts regard the Interco plan as just the first step in what may be a long-running series of negotiations with bondholders. "The first restructuring plan never goes through, and the companies know that," said Andrew J. Herenstein, an analyst at Delaware Bay Co., a securities concern specializing in distressed bonds. In recent months, militant bondholders have frequently won concessions from companies in financial straits.

In announcing yesterday that it couldn't pay interest on its bonds, Interco effectively conceded that its 1988 leveraged recapitalization hadn't worked. When the recapitalization was devised by Interco's Wall Street advisers, Wasserstein Perella & Co., it was valued at $76 for each Interco share. But most of the new securities issued in the Interco recapitalization have sunk drastically in value. Traders estimated yesterday that the Wasserstein Perella recapitalization package ultimately proved to be worth only about $42 a share.

For the past year, Interco management has avoided criticizing Wasserstein Perella by name. But in yesterday's announcement of the restructuring plan, Interco hinted at reservations about the high-debt strategy that it chose in 1988. The restructuring plan, Interco said, is designed to relieve "the problems resulting from our over-leveraged capital structure."

The restructuring plan begins with about $400 million of 13 3/4% senior subordinated debentures due 2000. These bonds have recently been trading at about 27 cents on the dollar. Holders of those bonds would receive, in exchange for each $1,000 principal amount of such debt, 70 shares of Interco common stock and $900 principal amount of a new issue of 6% accrual convertible debentures, which pay accrued interest at maturity. The new 6% debentures are convertible into 504 shares of Interco common; additional terms weren't disclosed.

The Interco restructuring plan doesn't offer any new debt for holders of more than $450 million of subordinated bonds. These bonds have recently been trading at just five cents on the dollar, reflecting the bond market's pessimism about Interco's condition. In the restructuring plan, holders of these subordinated discount debentures due 2003, and 14 1/2% junior pay-in-kind subordinated debentures, also due 2003, would exchange each $1,000 principal amount of such debentures for 150 and 130 shares of Interco common, respectively.

Interco also said it will ask shareholders to approve the reclassification of each share of the company's existing Series D $7.75 cumulative convertible preferred stock into 100 shares of Interco common, and its Series E preferred stock into 8 shares of Interco common.

Interco makes Converse sneakers and operates Florsheim shoe stores; it also owns the Lane and Broyhill furniture companies.

The restructuring plan is conditioned, among other things on acceptance of the offers by at least 90% of each of the debenture groups. Interco said it will file a registration statement covering its exchange offers with the Securities and Exchange Commission in the next "one to two weeks."
