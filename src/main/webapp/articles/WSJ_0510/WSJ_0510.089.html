Mattel to Pay First Dividend In Seven Years --- Senior Notes to Be Repaid; Licensing Pact Reached On `Simpsons' Products

  • Author: Pauline Yoshihashi
  • Date: 05/10/90

LOS ANGELES -- Mattel Inc. announced its first quarterly dividend in seven years and said it plans to repay $75 million in senior subordinated extendible notes. The move reflects improved earnings during the past several quarters. The toy maker also announced at its annual meeting a licensing agreement with Twentieth Century Fox Licensing & Merchandising to produce toys and related products based on the hit television cartoon series "The Simpsons." Twentieth Century Fox is a News Corp. unit.

The dividend of three cents for each share of common stock is payable July 2 to stock of record June 15. Mattel's last common stock dividend was paid in June 1983, the year the company's plunge into electronic toys and other strategies resulted in a $394 million loss. That prompted a major restructuring and eventually a change in senior management.

John W. Amerman, Mattel's chairman and chief executive officer, said the board will review its dividend policy each quarter, but he said it hadn't determined a set amount to be paid.

Mattel won't extend $75 million of 10 7/8% senior subordinated extendible notes, and it plans to repay the notes on the July 15 due date. The board also authorized the purchase of as much as 500,000 of the company's 50.5 million common shares during the next year on the open market to fund Mattel's stock-option plan.

Mattel's stock climbed 50 cents on the news, closing at $24 a share in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

In an interview, Mr. Amerman said he is comfortable with analysts' per-share earnings estimates of $2 to $2.10 for 1990.

In 1989, Mattel earned $68.7 million, or $1.38 a share, on sales of $1.2 billion.

The world-wide licensing pact with Twentieth Century Fox will feature the offbeat members of the Simpson family, especially the spiky-haired son, Bart. The line will include action figures, vehicles and play sets, as well as dolls, preschool products and child-sized accessories.

The products will be rolled out this fall with the new episodes of "The Simpsons," and new products will be introduced for the spring 1991 season. Al Ovadia, Twentieth Century Fox's vice president, licensing, said the company has signed more than 100 licensing agreements on "The Simpsons," ranging from apparel, school supplies and games, to skateboards and computer games.

Mr. Amerman estimated that the licensing agreement could produce more than $20 million in sales by year end, but he said he couldn't predict the value of the agreement over the long run.

Entertainment-related character tie-ins can be fraught with problems, as it is extremely difficult to gauge the public appetite for movies, TV shows and cartoons.

Mattel passed up the licensing for "Star Wars" characters in the mid-1970s, and that line proved to be one of the most lucrative ever. Mattel also passed on licensing pacts for a current hit movie "Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles." Conversely, others in the toy industry have bombed when characters suddenly fell out of favor.

More recently, though, Mattel has launched some successful licensed product lines. Its Disney preschool and infant toys racked up $135 million in sales last year. For 1990, Mattel has introduced an infant toy using Muppet's characters.

Despite the unpredictability of such a venture, most toy-industry analysts say the Simpsons tie-in could be a profitable sideline for Mattel.

"If you do it as an adjunct part of your business and not the mainstream, and if you manage your products carefully, you'll be in good shape," said Thomas Kully, an analyst with William Blair & Co. in Chicago. But Mr. Kully and other observers did voice some uncertainty over how much appeal "The Simpsons" has among younger viewers. "Is it really a kids' property?" Mr. Kully asked.

Twentieth Century Fox said Simpson-licensed clothing and other merchandise has been selling briskly through special departments in 1,200 J.C. Penney Co. department stores, and consumer demand should remain steady as licensees such as Mattel introduce fresh products during the fall television season.
