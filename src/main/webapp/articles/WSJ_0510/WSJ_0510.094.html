Letters to the Editor: To Know Badminton Is to Stop Reviling It

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/10/90

I read James P. Sterba's April 6 Leisure & Arts article, "Down With Shuttlecocks," with intense amusement. Obviously, nobody likes to have his recreational fun spoiled by a change in his gym's schedule. And to have only "three adults and four children" take over the gym for two hours to play badminton, replacing the "dozens of basketball-loving men and boys from New York's mean streets," must have been very frustrating. Basketball is a great game, but Mr. Sterba jumped to several conclusions in his article that warrant comment.

First, unless he plays with basketball players very different from those I have played with through the years, he makes a rather generous "leap of faith" to term them "God-fearing males gathered into the warm Christian bonds of basketball fellowship." The hoopsters' contempt and disrespect for other sports are notorious, as is obviously the case with Mr. Sterba.

His desire to "smear badminton fair and square" by linking the late Bhagwan Shree Rajneesh and badminton (by way of Poona) is a non sequitur. Real shuttlecocks are made from real geese (and often ducks). But, Mr. Sterba would have lost his bet, because they are not pulled from "unsedated" geese. Rather, the feathers are leftovers from the down industry, i.e. sleeping bags and down jackets, etc.

Requiring nearly two weeks elapsed time of manufacturing, huge baskets of feathers are sorted, trimmed, bleached, dried, re-sorted, inserted, glued, sewn and precisely tested for weight, flight distance and vibration and graded accordingly. Thousands of families, primarily in China and Taiwan, make their livelihood as shuttlecock makers. I personally have seen the process.

Admittedly, badminton in the U.S. has a long way to go. Only a very few badminton players can afford to train full time. But progress is being made. U.S. National Badminton teams have shown marked improvement in world rankings recently, the promotional nightmare of badminton's backward stigma is slowly dissolving, and more facilities are providing some badminton court time.

Guy Chadwick

Scottsdale, Ariz.

---

With the exception of soccer, badminton is played by more people in the world than any other sport. One of the main reasons China, Indonesia and other badminton powers are powers is because almost all of their children start playing at a very early age and are properly coached. This gives those countries an enormous pool to draw from. We all realize that is exactly what is needed in the U.S. Naturally, the more individuals available for competitions, the better showing that can be made.

I believe it's important that Mr. Sterba and the 44% of the sportscasters who wanted badminton removed from the Olympics become familiar with the game. If he and they do, they all will be supportive -- I'll guarantee it.

Raymond G. Scott

Director of Development

U.S. Badminton Association

Papillion, Neb.
