Jury Deadlocks On Damages In Ford Case

  • Author: Joseph B. White
  • Date: 05/10/90

DETROIT -- A federal judge declared a mistrial in the final, damage-award phase of a patent suit brought by Robert W. Kearns against Ford Motor Co. after seven jurors couldn't convince a lone holdout that the inventor should get no more than $3 million. For Mr. Kearns, who had earlier won a judgment from the same jury that Ford infringed his valid patents for an electronically-controlled, intermittent windshield wiper system, the mistrial means another delay in a 12-year battle that involves not just Ford, but nearly every other major auto maker in the world.

But interviews with four of the eight jurors suggest that Ford, which switched its lead counsel after losing the first phase of the trial, came close to emerging with just a scratch from a case some patent law experts -- including presiding U.S. District Court Judge Avern Cohn -- said had the potential for a huge jury award. Ford had stipulated that it installed wiper systems that infringed on the Kearns patents on more than 20.5 million vehicles between 1971 and 1988.

The jury foreperson, Sharon Harrison-Hamburg, said last night that after six days of debate, she and six other jurors felt Mr. Kearns should receive between $2.5 million and $3 million -- not much more than the $1.5 million maximum Ford lawyers argued was reasonable. She called Mr. Kearns's lawyer's plea for an award of $325 million an "insult (to) our intelligence."

All eight jurors agreed on Tuesday that Ford's infringement of Mr. Kearns's patents wasn't willful. A finding of intentional infringement would have allowed the judge to double or triple any damage award.

But on the question of money, "there was one who would not negotiate and held out for $20 million," Mrs. Harrison-Hamburg said. Three other jurors reached yesterday described the deadlock similarly. One said that the majority at one point considered awarding Mr. Kearns as much as $7 million. The woman identified as the dissenter by two of the jurors couldn't be reached for comment.

"It was a horrible feeling to leave there" without reaching a verdict, said juror Anne Grasser.

Now another jury could have to wrestle with the damages issue in a new trial, unless the two parties settle. The two sides didn't know yesterday evening what had gone on in the jury room, because Judge Cohn didn't allow them to question the jurors in court.

Mr. Kearns declared yesterday that he would fight on. "It was a disappointment, of course," Mr. Kearns said of Judge Cohn's ruling. "If the journey has to be longer, we're ready to make that journey," he said.

Ford, in a statement yesterday, said it, too, was disappointed that the jury couldn't reach a decision on damages. "However, we are pleased that the jury recognized that Ford didn't intentionally infringe the Kearns patents," the company said.

Ford added that it has asked Judge Cohn to overrule the jury's earlier verdict upholding the validity of the Kearns windshield wiper patents. That's the first step in what is likely to be a lengthy appeals process.

"We continue to believe the Kearns patents were invalid because the ideas covered by them were disclosed by patents previously issued to other inventors," Ford said.

Ford has admitted that three of its intermittent windshield wiper systems are similar to Mr. Kearns's designs, but has argued that doesn't matter because Mr. Kearns's patents are invalid.

The impasse in the jury room provoked a lengthy battle, during which the majority of the jurors sought to convince the single dissenter that Mr. Kearns's inventions simply weren't worth tens of millions of dollars.

"We were trying to get reasonable numbers," said juror Stanley Shackman, who, like Mr. Kearns, is an electrical engineer.

Judge Cohn declared the mistrial shortly after 4 p.m. EDT yesterday after jurors sent a note describing the deadlock as hopeless. He ordered the parties to stay in the courtroom for several minutes while the jurors left the federal courthouse in downtown Detroit.

The Kearns-Ford litigation is being watched with intense interest by the world's major auto makers, nearly all of whom are defendants in a series of patent infringement suits filed by Mr. Kearns since 1978. The Ford case is the first to come to trial.

After Ford lost the first phase of the trial in January, it brought in a new team of lawyers headed by Malcolm Wheeler of the Los Angeles office of Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom.

During the second phase of the trial, Mr. Wheeler rained blows on Mr. Kearns's argument that he was the victim of willful wrongdoing by Ford. Among other things, Mr. Wheeler presented letters sent by Mr. Kearns to his lawyers and a business partner during the early 1970s in which the inventor appears to urge that his patent claims be expanded to cover specific details of Ford's existing wiper systems.

Mr. Kearns's lead trial lawyer, Paul Janicke of Houston-based Arnold, White & Durkee, argued to the jurors that they should focus instead on Ford's admission that its wiper systems mirrored the claims of Mr. Kearns's patents. Ford, he said, had reaped $557 million in profits from the infringing systems. Ford's argument that Mr. Kearns was owed just $1.5 million "isn't reasonable, it isn't fair, and it isn't just," he told the jury.

Mr. Janicke offered jurors two different methods for calculating a "reasonable royalty." One yielded about $190 million, while the other produced a royalty of $325 million.

"That fell by the wayside quickly," said Mrs. Grasser. "There was no basis to it."
