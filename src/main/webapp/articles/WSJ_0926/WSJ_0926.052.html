Technology & Health: Coffee and Sodas Are Said to Defeat Bid to Quit Smoking --- Acidic Food, Drinks Render Nicotine Gum Ineffective By Preventing Absorption

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 09/26/90

Smokers trying to quit shouldn't try to chew nicotine gum and drink coffee or soda at the same time. Addiction researchers said a laboratory experiment with eight longtime smokers showed that coffee and cola-type soft drinks will prevent the absorption of nicotine from the nicotine chewing gum, thus defeating the use of the gum as an aid to breaking the cigarette habit. The gum is sold in the U.S. under the brand name Nicorette by Marion-Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals Inc., a subsidiary of Dow Chemical Co.

The researchers said many smokers may fail to quit smoking because they inadvertently render the nicotine gum ineffective by consuming slightly acidic food and drinks at the same time they chew the gum. The drinks increase the acidity of the saliva and change the gum's nicotine into a non-absorbable form, researchers said. They suggested that other foods and drinks, including beer, tomato juice and various fruit juices, are also acidic enough to defeat the purpose of the gum.

It is difficult enough for smokers to get an effective dose of nicotine from the chewing gum even under the best of conditions, a research team from the federal National Institute of Drug Abuse and the University of Kentucky said in this week's Journal of the American Medical Association.

The Nicorette gum sold in the U.S. contains two milligrams of nicotine per stick, or cube, of gum. But even if the patient chews the gum precisely according to instructions, he or she will absorb only 0.8 to 0.9 milligrams of nicotine per stick, which is slightly less than that which is absorbed from a cigarette with a moderate amount of nicotine.

Interviews with nicotine gum users found that many of those who failed to get an adequate dose of nicotine from the gum drank coffee, fruit juices or soft drinks as they chewed gum, Jack E. Henningfield of the NIDA's Addiction Research Center in Baltimore and his colleagues said.

To see if these acidic beverages were affecting absorption of nicotine, they enlisted eight adult smokers who agreed to sit in the lab for three hours, submitting to blood tests, chewing nicotine gum and rinsing their mouths with coffee, a cola or distilled water at precise intervals. The nicotine gum used was a version sold in the U.K. and Canada by the Swedish drug maker, Pharmacia AB; this version contains four milligrams of nicotine, twice the dosage of the U.S. brand of Nicorette.

Whenever the volunteers rinsed with distilled water, or did a simulated rinse with their own saliva while chewing the high-strength gum, nicotine levels in their blood rose sharply within five minutes and stayed up for at least 15 minutes. But whenever they rinsed with coffee or the cola, there was only an insignificant rise in blood nicotine levels.
