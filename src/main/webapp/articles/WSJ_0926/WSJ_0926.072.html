Tuesday's Markets: Oil's Decline Spurs Stock, Bond Prices --- Industrials Gain 32.67 After Monday's Skid; Dollar Ends Mixed

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/26/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices rebounded from Monday's sell-off as oil prices retreated and speculators who had bet that stocks would keep falling rushed to cut their losses. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 32.67 points in moderate trading to 2485.64, but advancing issues on the New York Stock Exchange barely outnumbered decliners, 770-745.

The slump in oil-crude oil futures for November delivery fell 72 cents a barrel to $37.53 -- also boosted bond prices, though trading was light. The dollar rose against the mark and fell against the yen on a West German official's comment that the yen could strengthen against European currencies.

Peter G. Grennan, head of stock index futures trading at Shearson Lehman Brothers, said yesterday's rally was fueled mainly by short-sellers -- investors who had bet on a further decline from Monday's level. When a drop failed to materialize, these investors sought to trim their losses by buying back shares.

Oil's drop attracted some bargain hunters to stocks, and they pushed prices high enough to spark the short-covering rally.

Kenneth Spence, director of the technical analysis group at Salomon Brothers, said he wasn't surprised to see the rebound. He explained that Monday's drop -- in which the Dow Jones industrials lost 59.41 points to close at their lowest level of the year -- came on less volume and with fewer stocks reaching new lows than on Aug. 23, the day of the industrials' previous 1990 low. Trading "will remain very choppy," he said, "but the downside risk is starting to diminish."

But others weren't convinced yesterday's rally has staying power. "The broad list of stocks continues to receive a considerable amount of selling pressure even during sporadic short-term rally attempts," said Mr. Grennan at Shearson.

Jack Solomon, market technician at Bear Stearns, said he has noticed increased investor interest in shorting stocks -- or borrowing shares and selling them, in hope of profiting from a price decline. "Everybody wants to go short who never went short before," he said.

But Mr. Solomon also said that oil won't rise further for the time being; he noted that oil-company stocks haven't followed oil to higher prices.

In fact, Sal Gilbertie, an oil futures trader at Merrill Lynch, said Monday's surge in oil was a "purely emotional high based on war fears" in the Middle East. Yesterday, common sense -- and profit-taking -- prevailed. "There are ample levels of heating oil in the world," Mr. Gilbertie said. "New York harbor is filled to the brim with tankers loaded with heating oil."

In major market action:

Stock prices rose. Big Board volume was 155.9 million shares.

Bond prices rose half a point, or $5 for each $1,000 face amount. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 9.13%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5725 marks, up from 1.5590 late Monday, and at 136.72 yen, down from 137.35.
