U.S. Rules Imports Of Argentine Leather Are Subject to Duties

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/26/90

WASHINGTON -- The Commerce Department, reversing an earlier determination, ruled that imports of Argentine leather are unfairly subsidized and thus subject to countervailing duties. The department said the Argentine government's prohibition of exports of cattle hide is tantamount to a subsidy for Argentine leather producers because the ban assures those producers of lower hide prices than what their U.S. competitors have to pay. This ruling supersedes an earlier finding that Argentina didn't unfairly support its leather producers in other ways.

The ruling authorizes the U.S. Customs Service to collect countervailing duties of 8.02% and 24.16% on the value of imports from Antonio Esposito S.A. and Ultrahide S.A., respectively. Duties of 14.97% are to be collected from other Argentina suppliers. These duties are intended to offset the allegedly unfair price advantage that these imports have over comparable U.S. products. The department said the subsidy rates represent the estimated differences between U.S. and Argentine hide prices.

The ruling covers many kinds of leather, including that made out of sheep, swine, reptile and chamois skins. The value of all these Argentine imports last year was $143.6 million, according to the Commerce Department.

The department acted on a petition filed by eight U.S. tanning companies.
