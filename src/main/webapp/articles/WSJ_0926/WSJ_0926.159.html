Senate Clears Bill Broadening SEC Regulatory Power

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 09/26/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate passed a compromise bill that broadens the Securities and Exchange Commission's ability to gather Wall Street information and for the first time sets guidelines for how the agency can curb forms of program trading. The measure, passed by voice vote, is the first Senate-approved legislation to include provisions about program trading, in which computers help trade baskets of stocks and futures on those stocks. The bill, which is expected to breeze through the House in the next few days and be signed by President Bush, also gives the SEC the clout to close down the stock market in periods of extreme stress, provided the president doesn't oppose such a move. Currently only the president can order such a closure.

Much of the SEC-sponsored bill is direct fallout from the 1987 crash. The bill was first proposed by the SEC under Chairman David Ruder in 1988 but progressed slowly after being introduced in March 1989. It then was jump-started by the market drop in October 1989 and Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc.'s collapse earlier this year, and reflects reactions to those drops and the Drexel bankrupcty filing.

One section of the bill, for example, would allow the SEC to set up record-keeping procedures for large securities transactions. After the 1987 crash, the SEC complained that it didn't have sufficient information to determine risks to the overall financial system.

The bill also would allow the federal regulators to strengthen the clearance and settlement system for stocks, options and futures, again in an effort to reduce risks to the financial markets.

A third section would give the SEC the power to ask brokerage firm holding companies for financial information. The SEC has said that before the Drexel collapse, it didn't have enough to determine data that the brokerage firm's holding company was having liquidity problems.

"This measure helps regain the {public's} trust by providing regulators the tools they need to ensure the continued integrity of our capital markets," said sponsor Sen. Christopher Dodd (D., Conn.), who heads the securities subcommittee.

But the most intriguing section of the legislation focuses on program trading. The measure originally was introduced in the House by Rep. Edward Markey (D., Mass.), who pushed the provision through that body largely by narrowing the language to meet concerns of other members. There was no program-trading provision in the version of the bill the Senate approved last month.

Although the bill approved yesterday never mentions the words program trading, it allows the SEC to prohibit "during periods of extraordinary market volatility" any trading practice that the commission has found "previously contributed significantly to extraordinary levels of volatility." The SEC must determine that the practice could contribute to that type of volatility in the future. Another section lets the agency "prescribe means reasonably designed to prevent manipulation" of stocks or the market.

But the measure doesn't define any of its terms about volatility, providing the commission leeway to determine how to enforce the provision.

Senate staffers played down the significance of the program-trading measure. "I think 99.99% of all program trading will never be affected by this bill," said a Senate securities subcommittee aide.

But the House report on the bill said that it would allow the agency to adopt rules similar to the one recently inaugurated by the New York Stock Exchange on a trial basis. That rule, which sharply limits a common form of program trading when the Dow Jones Industrial Average has moved 50 points or more, has kicked in several times over the past month.
