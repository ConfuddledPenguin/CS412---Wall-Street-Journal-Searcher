Navy Plans to Buy Northrop Jet Parts Despite Unit's Falsification of Test Data

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 09/26/90

The Navy plans to buy $300,000 of crucial AV-8B Harrier jet parts from Northrop Corp., even though the division that makes the parts has been suspended from Pentagon contracting for falsifying test data on the program. "A procurement system in which the Navy is forced to obtain parts from a corporate criminal is intolerable," said Rep. John Conyers (D., Mich.), chairman of the House Government Operations subcommittee on legislation and national security, which held a hearing on the matter yesterday.

The Navy maintained that suspending the waiver is required to keep the Harrier fleet flying. Already, four of the jets have been taken out of service because of a shortage of replacement parts, and 18 could be down within a year if the parts aren't available, the subcommittee was told. Some of the Pentagon's 200 Harriers are being flown by the Marines in the Persian Gulf.

The Defense Department is not out "to cheat the taxpayers," Col. James Hart, program manager on the AV-8B, assured lawmakers. But without a qualified second source, Northrop is the only short-term supplier of the spares, and going elsewhere could take as long as two years and cost more than $1 million, he said.

The officer alleged that "Northrop has cheated all of us, but most of all the young Marines" who require their aircraft to perform flawlessly. There is no doubt, he said, that "the reliability of the system would have been improved" had Northrop conducted tests properly.

A Northrop spokesman in Los Angeles reiterated that the company has fired the employees who falsified testing, and that the parts now meet all specifications. "People are trying to smear the whole corporation for the acts of a handful of people," the spokesman added.

The devices in question, known as rate sensor assemblies, are used in systems that help stabilize the vertical-takeoff Harriers, especially while hovering. The parts -- "vital to safety of flight," according to an internal Navy memo -- have lasted an average 999 flight hours instead of the 1,525 hours intended.

Last February, Northrop pleaded guilty to falsifying test results on the Harrier program. One test completely omitted, according to subcommittee investigators, was for life-expectancy of the devices.

An angry Rep. Conyers complained that going back to Northrop for more parts "looks like business as usual" for the company, and he vowed to press Defense Secretary Dick Cheney to "personally ensure that Northrop does not make a dime off this forced procurement."

Asked about this, the Northrop spokesman countered: "If you get a contract, you ought to make a profit on it."

Subcommittee members also were exasperated that Northrop declined to testify yesterday. "It goes without saying that until Northrop is prepared to be forthcoming. . . . I will be reluctant to support any payment of U.S. taxpayer dollars to this contractor," fumed Rep. Frank Horton (R., N.Y.).

Lawmakers also said that McDonnell Douglas Corp., the AV-8B prime contractor, and Honeywell Inc., maker of the system containing the sensor assemblies, didn't cooperate.

The Air Force also was supposed to address the hearing on the reliability of the nuclear-tipped cruise missile, which contains a Northrop-built guidance unit identified as having problems operating in subzero weather. But Jack Jones, an Air Force engineering supervisor, appeared with only a short statement saying the guidance unit hadn't ever caused an operational test-flight failure. This, however, is contradicted by documentation previously disclosed by this newspaper.

Rep. Conyers ordered Mr. Jones to appear at another hearing to be held soon, and to be better prepared. "He thought he'd come out here and stonewall us," Rep. Conyers asserted, adding: "There was no point in even taking his testimony because it amounted to nothing."
