FDIC Aide Favors Cutting Insurance On Pension Funds

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 09/26/90

WASHINGTON -- William Seidman, chairman of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., said that he favors scaling back the FDIC's insurance coverage for billions of dollars of pension funds held in banks. Congress and the Bush administration are looking for ways to reduce taxpayer risk in deposit insurance, following the massive S&L bailout law passed last year. Such a proposal also would inevitably restructure the banking industry. The changes are likely to break down the decades-long separation between commercial banking and investment and to allow new freedom for banks to open branches across state lines. Mr. Seidman, however, didn't offer a specific legislative package.

At the same time that a consensus is forming to limit federal insurance coverage for wealthy depositors, multibillion dollar pension funds continue to benefit from full deposit insurance coverage. Under current FDIC policy, each beneficiary of the pension fund is protected up to the $100,000 maximum coverage.

"The FDIC might be moving toward becoming the insurer for the nation's pension programs," Mr. Seidman told the House Banking Committee yesterday, calling the trend "not attractive."

Most of the pension funds held in banks are in instruments called investment contracts, which have grown from a few hundred million dollars in 1987 to nearly $10 billion currently, said a spokesman for Morley Capital Management Inc. of Lake Oswego, Ore., which places the investment contracts in banks. "Whether or not this could lead to an exodus {of the funds} from the banks, I can't say," he said.

The Bush administration is expected to unveil its own study on deposit insurance before year end and is likely to include similar pension fund limitations, an administration official said. "Our notion is to try to return deposit insurance to its historical purpose, to protect small savers," he said.

Before the October recess, it's likely that Congress will pass a law permitting the FDIC to raise bank insurance premiums above current caps in order to bolster the FDIC bank fund, which is at a record low. The Senate Banking Committee will hear testimony today on the Senate legislation to raise the insurance caps. The House already has passed its version.

Mr. Seidman said, however, that regulators haven't yet decided whether to raise premiums, and he warned against using the FDIC fund as a tool to help balance the federal budget. One recent budget proposal by Democrats hinged on $12 billion in higher bank insurance premiums as an offset for other spending. "I wouldn't count on us as part of the budget process to increase {the premiums} to that rate," he said.

Aside from raising premiums, consensus is forming around other limitations on deposit insurance. House Banking Committee Chairman Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas) lauded a bill introduced yesterday by Sen. Donald Riegle, (D., Mich.), chairman of the Senate Banking Committee. Both chairmen want to abandon the "too-big-to-fail" policy, in which all depositors in large banks are protected during a failure -- even those with deposits of more than $100,000. Mr. Seidman said the idea had "certain appeal" in reducing risk to taxpayers but could cause instability in banking markets.

He also joined Messrs. Gonzalez and Riegle in leaning toward preserving the current $100,000 limit on deposit insurance, rather than scaling it back to a lower amount as some in the industry have urged. Such a move would hurt small banks disproportionately, he said, and limit their ability to attract funds.

Mr. Seidman also said that banks should be restructured sooner rather than later, urging stronger supervision, higher capital requirements, and reduced bank investment risk. He has proposed that banks be permitted to engage in riskier activities only in separately financed subsidiaries. "We have to decide what we are going to do and move ahead on it," he said.

Separately, the Resolution Trust Corp., the S&L cleanup agency, said the 247 thrifts it controls posted net losses of $2.6 billion in the second quarter, compared with $2.2 billion in the first quarter.
