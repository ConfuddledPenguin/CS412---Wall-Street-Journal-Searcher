Moscow's Novice Traders Tour Pits To See the `Crazy House' in Action

  • Author: Stanley W. Angrist
  • Date: 09/26/90

NEW YORK -- A delegation of Soviet Union businessmen, who this month helped start the first commodity exchange in Moscow since the mid-1920s, came to see Wall Street with their own eyes. "It's a crazy house]" exclaims Victor Zaplatkin, a member of the U.S.S.R. Chamber of Commerce, after gawking around the New York Stock Exchange's trading floor for a few minutes Monday afternoon.

The Big Board is a far cry from the embryonic Moscow market. Last Wednesday, 200 Soviets gathered in a Moscow office building to trade whatever any of them wanted to buy and sell, which turned out to be mostly building materials, such as cement and lumber. It resembled an auction more than a Western-style securities or futures exchange.

The Soviets knew they had a long way to go even before the seven visitors ventured to Wall Street. One highlight of their two-week tour: mock trading of platinum futures last night on the New York Mercantile Exchange.

"It's better to see it done once than hear it a thousand times," says Mr. Zaplatkin, quoting a Russian proverb.

There are a few little problems to iron out back home before the Moscow market goes big time, however. "Until we resolve the question of property ownership, it will be hard to resolve any market questions," says Gennadi Poleshuk, co-chairman of the Moscow Commodity Exchange. He argues a commodity exchange could help improve the Soviet Union's poor system for distributing goods.

Another difficulty is that to function properly, futures markets need risk-takers -- also known as speculators, which is an onerous term in the Soviet Union. Such activity is illegal and people convicted of it are subject to stiff prison sentences.

A proposal to change the law on speculation has been introduced, says Alexander Pochinok, a deputy of the Supreme Soviet of the Russian Republic and secretary of the commission on budget, taxes, securities and banking legislation for the republic. "However," he adds, "it is much more difficult to change the consciousness of the people."

Konstantin Zatulin, a member of the board of the exchange and head of an association of entrepreneurs, says the Soviet people "are moving from a complete denial of the market -- an attitude they held for 70 years -- to viewing it as their salvation."

To help them learn how markets work, the Soviets have hired two Americans. One of them is Danielle Downing, a recent Russian-speaking graduate from the MBA program at the Wharton Business School, who is executive assistant to the chairman of the new exchange and is based in Moscow.

"I see the Moscow exchange developing in a pattern similar to the way American exchanges developed," first trading warehouse receipts for real bushels of grain or tons of metal, then evolving into futures markets, she said. Her boss, Mr. Poleshuk, predicts the new auction-type marketplace will evolve into a futures market when goods become freely priced, in five to seven years.

John Damgard, president of the Futures Industry Association, says he believes there would be world-wide commercial interest in a Soviet futures market that traded energy products, precious metals and the ruble.

The government of the Russian Republic has passed a law that, beginning late this year, will permit all Soviet enterprises, but not individuals, to trade foreign currencies. According to Ms. Downing, the Moscow exchange is in good position to become one of the places where such trading is permitted. It would start as a cash market for the ruble against all the major Western currencies.
