Technology & Health: Child Deaths From AIDS Seen Soaring

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 09/26/90

Acquired immune deficiency syndrome will become a major killer of children by the year 2000, offsetting some of the recent global reduction in child mortality rates, the World Health Organization reported. By the end of the century, 10 million or more children will have contracted the AIDS virus, and most will have died, the WHO estimated in a new report. In some parts of sub-Sahara Africa, where AIDS is particularly prevalent, deaths for pediatric AIDS may boost the child mortality rate by as much as 50%, the Geneva-based agency warned.

Over the last decade, some 700,000 AIDS-infected babies have been born around the world, but the number is expected to expand rapidly. In most countries, but not the U.S., heterosexual intercourse is becoming the primary method of transmitting the human immunodeficiency virus, which causes AIDS. The WHO also estimates that 15 million to 20 million adults will have been infected by HIV by the year 2000.

The WHO issued the report, its first on pediatric AIDS, as world leaders are preparing to meet at the United Nations this weekend for the world summit on children. Michael Merson, director of the WHO's AIDS program, said the report is designed to call attention to a growing health and welfare problem that must be addressed by world leaders.

Not only will many children die of AIDS, but millions who aren't HIV-infected will be orphaned as their parents die of AIDS. "We have to plan for the implications of this," Dr. Merson said. "This is going to be a burden on society. There are going to be big economic and welfare implications." Because there is no cure for AIDS, the only way to combat the epidemic is stronger programs to reduce the sexual transmission of AIDS.

Over the last decade, expanded childhood immunization programs have significantly reduced mortality from such diseases as diphtheria, measles, polio, tetanus and tuberculosis. "Now because of AIDS, we fear this progress will be reversed," said Hiroshi Nakajima, director-general of the WHO.
