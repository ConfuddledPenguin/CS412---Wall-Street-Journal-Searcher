Chemical Waste Gets $3.75 Million Fine In Pollution Case

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/26/90

CHICAGO -- Chemical Waste Management Inc. agreed to pay a $3.75 million fine for pollution violations at a toxic-waste incinerator. Under the agreement with the Environmental Protection Agency, Chemical Waste admitted no wrongdoing, but dropped its earlier opposition to the fine. The company is 77%-owned by Waste Management Inc. of Oak Brook, Ill.

The EPA charged that Chemical Waste's incinerator on Chicago's South Side didn't properly monitor emissions when burning toxic polychlorinated biphenyls, or PCB's, and didn't stop burning the compound when the pollution-monitoring system failed. The agency additionally cited the company for other, related violations of the Toxic Substances Control Act, including improper record keeping.

The problems became known after a former company employee, who had been terminated for unrelated reasons, informed Chemical Waste officials in 1987 that he and others had deliberately disconnected the monitoring devices. Such a move would permit concealment of overloading at the incinerator. Chemical Waste subsequently fired two managers at the incinerator and notified the EPA of the problem.

In the agreement, the EPA reduced the penalty from an proposed $4.3 million, partly in consideration of Chemical Waste's reporting to the agency. The lowered fine also reflects $353,000 in penalties Chemical Waste paid to state pollution authorities for the same violations, the EPA said, as well as the company's "current compliance with Federal regulations and substantial improvements in its monitoring operations."

A Chemical Waste spokesman stressed that the "alleged violations" occurred in 1986 and 1987, and "resulted in no threat to {public} health or the environment."
