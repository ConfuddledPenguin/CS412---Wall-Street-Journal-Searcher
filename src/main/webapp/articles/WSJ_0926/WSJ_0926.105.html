Commodities: Oil Prices Ease After Previous Session's Surge; Gasoline, Heating Fuel Contracts Also End Lower

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 09/26/90

Oil prices generally pulled back after Monday's sizzling gains. Traders and analysts attributed the pause to a correction and to profit-taking in what has been a frenzied market. Whatever the reason, futures prices for so-called prompt-month crudes fell in both New York and London.

On the New York Mercantile Exchange, the November crude contract, which surged $2.82 a barrel Monday, fell 72 cents yesterday to settle at $37.53. December crude was up $1.25 a barrel to $36.07, but that was a catch-up move after exchange limits held its Monday gain to $1.50.

On the International Petroleum Exchange in London, crude oil futures for November and December delivery were lower after Monday's stunning run-ups. The November contract dropped 58 cents a barrel to settle at $37.10, while December crude fell 62 cents to $35.50.

There were continuing reports of some North Sea crudes being priced at more than $40 a barrel, but trades at these "notional" levels weren't confirmed. Meanwhile, some fuel prices weakened.

In U.S. futures trading, heating oil for October delivery fell 2.72 cents to settle at $1.012 a gallon. Unleaded gasoline for October delivery fell 2.18 cents to $1.0205 a gallon, and traders said the gasoline market actually was much weaker than that for heating oil.

One reason for the slackening of interest in gasoline was concern in the market that the latest weekly report by the American Petroleum Institute would show another big buildup in inventories. That could mean demand for motor fuel would fall more quickly than seasonal factors would suggest, mainly because of the slowing U.S. economy and motorist reaction to the past few weeks' surge in prices at the gasoline pump.

The report, released as usual after the futures market had closed, appears to have confirmed those market fears. Although the API reported a decline of 5.7 million barrels in crude oil inventories last week, it also showed that gasoline inventories rose by nearly 5.3 million barrels, a much bigger increase than had been expected.

"This definitely removes the bullish scenario for gasoline," said Andrew Lebow, an energy analyst with E.D.& F. Man International Futures. He had been expecting an increase of only 200,000 to 1.2 million barrels in gasoline inventories.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

PRECIOUS METALS: Futures prices eased. December delivery gold was off 70 cents an ounce to $407.60. December silver slipped eight cents an ounce to $4.9150. October platinum dropped $8.90 an ounce to $451.80. Oil prices took a breather, backing away from their recent strong advances in the absence of any new threatening rhetoric from Iraq. As a result, precious metals also retreated from Monday's gains. The small size of the decline in gold indicated that the metal still has a strong underpinning, according to analysts. Also a supporting factor was that producer selling hasn't yet appeared. It did appear to some degree in silver and platinum, however, one analyst said. Because those markets have less investor interest and are thinly traded, they showed significant losses. Another saving grace for gold, the analyst said, was that industrial demand for it is remaining strong, while demand for platinum is dropping with sagging automobile sales. Yesterday, U.S. auto makers reported a drop in sales for mid-September.

GRAINS AND SOYBEANS: Futures prices closed mixed in moderate trading. Soybean futures for November delivery gained four cents a bushel to $6.175 on the Chicago Board of Trade. Prices were supported partly by the outlook for continued dry weather in the Mississippi River valley and some other Southern growing areas, analysts said. Because of the dry weather, "there's a feeling that soybean yields are less than they were two to three weeks ago," said Conrad Leslie, a futures analyst in Chicago. December corn futures fell one cent a bushel to close at $2.2775, and December wheat futures edged one-half cent a bushel lower to $2.7475.

COPPER: Most futures prices eased. The September contract, which will cease trading today, was up 0.80 cent a pound at $1.2920, mostly because of a relatively large number of contracts remaining to be fulfilled by delivery of the commodity or nullified by offsetting trades. Warehouse stocks of copper for delivery against the September contract are low. December, however, was off 0.55 cent a pound to $1.1750 after trading in a wide range. The market was influenced by the liquidation that has continued since prices fell in London last week, one analyst said. However, he said, demand for copper remains strong in West Germany, Japan and even the U.S., where buying interest has held up despite the slowdown in the economy. According to another analyst, copper prices have a good prospect of rebounding because North American supplies are relatively tight and futures have tended to be oversold recently.
