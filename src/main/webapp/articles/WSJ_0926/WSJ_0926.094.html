Short Interest Rises in OTC's Two Categories --- Rouse Has Biggest Boost; Larger Issues Gain 4.5%, Smaller Shares Up 6.5%

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 09/26/90

NEW YORK -- Short interest among over-the-counter stocks, both large and small, rebounded in the month ended in mid-September after easing in August. Short interest among larger OTC stocks gained 4.5%, while short interest among smaller shares rose 6.5%.

Rouse Co., a Columbia, Md., real estate developer, led the issues with the largest increase in short interest. On Aug. 24, President Michael Spear died in an airplane crash. Mathias J. DeVito, chairman and chief executive officer, assumed Mr. Spear's duties as president. Short interest in Rouse rose more than 1.1 million shares to 3.3 million.

Short interest in troubled Oracle Systems jumped 93.7% to more than two million shares. Shares of the Redwood City, Calif., software maker plummeted this summer amid signs that its fast-paced growth had slowed. Oracle, which laid off 10% of its work force last week, yesterday reported a loss for the first quarter, ended Aug. 31, of 27 cents a share, compared with net income of nine cents a share a year earlier.

Short interest jumped in three health care-related stocks: SciMed Life Systems, St. Jude Medical and Healthcare Services. Among technology companies, Software Toolworks and Adobe Systems both turned up large increases in short interest. Among financial institutions, Sunwest Financial Services and BayBanks posted large boosts in short interest.

First Executive, the Los Angeles life insurance company that once held a large portfolio of junk bonds, continued to hang on to its lead as the stock with the largest short position with 8.5 million shares sold short.

Traders who sell short borrow stock and sell it, betting that the stock's price will decline and that they can buy the shares back later at a lower price for return to the lender.

Short interest is the number of shares that haven't yet been purchased for return to lenders. Although a substantial short position reflects heavy speculation that a stock's price will decline, some investors consider an increase in short interest bullish because the borrowed shares eventually must be bought back.

And some short interest doesn't reflect any opinion on the stock at all but shows up as part of another strategy, such as arbitrage or dividend capture.

The National Association of Securities Dealers Inc., the self-regulatory agency that operates the Nasdaq computerized system on which more than 4,300 OTC issues trade, compiles short interest data in two categories: the approximately two-thirds, and generally biggest, Nasdaq stocks that trade on the National Market System; and the other, generally smaller stocks that don't.

The NASD reported that short interest in 2,320 National Market System securities as of mid-September totaled 266,883,329 shares, up from 255,404,934 shares among 2,319 NMS issues for August.

Short interest in 1,217 other smaller issues totaled 39,176,536 shares for September, compared with 36,799,476 shares in 1,215 issues in August.

The National Market System's short-interest ratio at mid-September rose to 2.93 from 2.19 a month earlier, while the short-interest ratio for other OTC issues climbed to 1.42 from 1.04 a month earlier.

The ratio -- the number of trading days at the market's average daily trading volume that would be required to convert the total short interest position -- is a measure of negative trader sentiment. Some analysts suggest, however, that the ratio has weakened in value as an indicator because options and other products can be used to hedge short positions.
