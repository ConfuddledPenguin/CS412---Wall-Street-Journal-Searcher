United Services Says It Isn't Linked To Inquiry Target

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 09/26/90

United Services Advisors Inc., which manages $650 million in mutual fund assets, is again trying to distance itself from a possible business embarrassment. Last year, the San Antonio, Texas, concern changed hands after its founder, Clark Aylsworth, was forced by the Securities and Exchange Commission to give up control. The SEC action followed a discovery in the mid-1980s that one of the fund group's portfolio managers was conspiring with two Vancouver stock promoters to stack the mutual fund he ran with worthless Canadian mining stocks.

Now, United Services, which specializes in gold and natural resource stocks, is eager to deny any connection with Mr. Aylsworth's son, C. Allyn Aylsworth.

The firm sent a letter last week to 500 United Services fund shareholders who had invested through the younger Mr. Aylsworth's coin and gold bullion business, Auric United Corp. The letter advised the shareholders that Auric was being investigated by the Texas attorney general's office and said that there isn't any connection between Auric and United Services.

Yesterday, a district court in Bexar County, Texas, granted a temporary restraining order stopping Auric from doing business or disposing of any assets, according to the Texas attorney general's office, which requested the court order. In addition, the attorney general's office filed a suit alleging that Auric had violated the state's Deceptive Trade Practices Act.

According to the state's assistant attorney general, Raul Noriega, there is a gap between the value of the coins and bullion held by Auric and the amount it should be holding on behalf of customers. C. Allyn Aylsworth's lawyer declined to comment on the case; a recorded telephone message at Auric said its office was closed yesterday.

Paul Robertson, general counsel for United Services, said the firm owned 24.3% of Auric until June 1987, when it sold its stake to Allyn Aylsworth. In that same month, Mr. Aylsworth, who was then a vice president for marketing at the fund group, left United Services, Mr. Robertson said.

Mr. Aylsworth's father, Clark Aylsworth, agreed to sell his controlling stake in the firm late last year to settle SEC charges of failing to properly supervise a fund manager. The charges, which he didn't admit or deny, grew out of the manager's purchase of the worthless mining stocks.

The elder Mr. Aylsworth had continued to work as a consultant with United Services, but yesterday the firm said the arrangement had been terminated. Mr. Aylsworth couldn't be reached for comment.

United Services' largest fund is a gold fund, the $250 million U.S. Gold Shares fund.
