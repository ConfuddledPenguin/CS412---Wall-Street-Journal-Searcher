Fed Panel Member Opposes Lowering Interest Rates

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/26/90

Declaring that the Federal Reserve's "anti-inflationary resolve is being tested," Fed board member Wayne Angell said the central bank shouldn't lower interest rates now. Acknowledging the mounting pressure on the Fed to ease its grip on credit amid signs of a weakening economy, Mr. Angell warned that "hard-fought gains against inflationary psychology achieved in the 1980s could be lost" if the Fed moves prematurely.

Mr. Angell's views aren't shared by all of his colleagues at the Fed. The Fed hasn't moved interest rates since July, and Mr. Angell was among a minority who argued against even leaning toward lower interest rates when the Fed's policy-making committee met in August. The committee meets again Tuesday.

The Fed governor stressed, as Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan did last week, that the Fed can't prevent the pain that higher oil prices will cause. "Attempting, through an easing of policy, to somehow avoid or postpone the . . . adjustments of the oil shock would only serve to undermine" the credibility of the Fed's pledge to bring down the inflation rate and risk a deeper recession in the future, Mr. Angell said.

Mr. Angell indicated he would advocate that the Fed nudge down the short-term interest rates it influences if the price of gold and commodities fell, long-term interest rates dropped and the dollar's value rose -- all signs that markets foresee a weaker economy and less inflation. Those indicators currently are going the other direction, suggesting that the economy may not be as weak as some fear, Mr. Angell said.
