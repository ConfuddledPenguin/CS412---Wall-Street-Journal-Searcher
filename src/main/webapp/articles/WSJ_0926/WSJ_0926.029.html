Marriott Cuts Back Capital Spending, Construction Starts

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 09/26/90

Marriott Corp. said it will delay construction starts of most new hotels and senior living centers as well as reduce next year's capital expenditures because of the weakness in the real-estate, lodging and financial markets. The lodging and food-services company, based in Washington, D.C., also said asset sales would be lower than expected earlier this year. In the spring, the company told analysts it expected to sell about $2 billion of assets. Yesterday, William J. Shaw, Marriott's chief financial officer, said the company has sold about $900 million in assets this year and expects to sell a further $700 million in the next few months. The company didn't detail which assets it was looking to sell.

James Meyer at Janney Montgomery Scott in Philadelphia said the Japanese would probably be the most likely buyers for some of Marriott's properties as U.S. demand has died away.

Capital expenditures will be reduced to about $650 million next year, compared with $1.3 billion this year, Mr. Shaw said. Construction projects already under way, such as a new senior living center in Boca Raton, Fla., will be completed.

Marriott is also putting a different spin on its business strategy. Traditionally, the company has developed hotel properties and then sold them to investors, while retaining the management contracts. Going forward, Marriott will place greater emphasis on prefinancing development projects as well as working with third parties and developing joint ventures, Mr. Shaw said.

While most analysts said Marriott's scaled-back construction and capital-spending plans were a step in the right direction, few are bullish on the company. Caroline Levy at Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc. is worried that some of the company's assets might be overstated on its balance sheet.

Ms. Levy suspended her earnings estimate on Marriott Monday because she believes the company might have to "take write-downs against up to $3 billion of its assets." Sizable write-downs "could feasibly wipe out its net worth," she said. The company had $461 million in equity at the end of the second quarter, with $3.4 billion of debt.

Marriott's announcement yesterday came just one day after its stock hit an eight-year low at $10.875. The stock has lost about half its market value in recent months as worries about the slowdown in the lodging industry and sinking real-estate markets have increased.

Yesterday, Marriott shares rose 75 cents to $11.625 in active New York Stock Exchange composite trading.
