Foreign Exchange: Dollar Rises Against Most Currencies On Fed Comments and Mideast Warnings

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/26/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar rose against all major currencies except the yen as dealers focused on reassurances by a Fed governor about credit policy and new warnings from a Soviet official about the Persian Gulf powder keg. Competitive interest rates attract foreign funds to the U.S. And the dollar is still the first currency that investors buy when seeking a haven from global instability, though its place as a long-term safe harbor has been tarnished by U.S. economic problems.

The dollar fell against the yen in the New York afternoon, but gained strength against the mark, when a senior German finance ministry official said in Washington that he believes the Japanese currency could appreciate further against its German counterpart.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.5725 marks, up from 1.5590 marks late Monday in New York. The U.S. currency dropped to 136.72 yen, however, from 137.35 yen in New York late Monday. Sterling was quoted late in the day at $1.8680, down from $1.8810 late Monday.

In Tokyo early Wednesday, the dollar was trading at 136.70 yen, unchanged from the close there Tuesday.

Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5735 marks, up from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 1.5553 marks.

On Tuesday, the dollar had started lower in a belated reaction to the Saturday meeting in Washington of the Group of Seven industrialized countries. The finance ministers and central bankers registered their approval of the recent depreciation of the dollar with direct reference to the yen.

The mark, which had closed in New York the previous day at 88.17 yen, was slowly losing ground to the Japanese unit and stood at 87.75 yen before the comment by the German official was reported. Within 10 minutes, it shed an entire yen before rising slightly to finish at 86.94 yen.

As a conduit for cross, or non-dollar, transactions, the greenback falls against the purchased currency and rises against the sold counterpart.

Of more direct benefit to the dollar was a speech before the United Nations by Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze. He denounced Iraq's invasion of Kuwait and warned that "war may break out in the Gulf region any day, any moment."

The U.N. Security Council scheduled a full meeting for late yesterday, possibly to discuss expanding its land and sea embargo of Iraq to include air transport.

The U.S. reported a sharp increase in the number of Iraqi troops and tanks in southern Kuwait, as Baghdad set Oct. 15 for a "trial" of President Bush for "crimes against the people of the world."

Federal Reserve Board Governor Wayne Angell reaffirmed the central bank's commitment to price stability in the face of rising oil prices. "Attempting, through an easing of policy, to somehow avoid or postpone the implied real adjustments of the oil shock would only serve to undermine the credibility of policy and lead to potentially more severe adjustments over the longer run," he said.

Echoing comments by Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan, Mr. Angell emphasized the importance of long-term interest rates over short-term rates. High long-term rates threaten the recovery of the U.S.'s weak real estate industry.

Only a "credible commitment to price stability," meaning holding Fed-influenced short-term rates high, will "bring about a moderation in long-term interest rates," Mr. Angell said in the Washington speech.

Mr. Angell suggested the Fed would wait for the market's reaction to a federal budget accord before easing credit. Bond traders are expected to see a diminished flow of Treasury issues resulting from a reduced U.S. budget deficit and to bid them higher, lowering their yield.

"Should a budget agreement lead to lower market rates, the Federal Reserve would likely not want to curtail such declines. In the face of falling interest rates, it wouldn't be desirable for the Federal Reserve to hold up the short end of the market," Mr. Angell said.

Alfonso Alejo, a corporate foreign exchange dealer in New York with Mitsui Taiyo Kobe Bank, called Mr. Angell's speech a "big comment" that "put a bid under the dollar."

The prospect of interest rate cuts was further diminished by disarray in Washington. With budget talks still at an impasse over President Bush's proposal to cut capital gains taxes, a bill to delay the Gramm-Rudman spending cuts due Monday was cleared for a vote on the floor of the House. Mr. Bush repeated his intention to veto the bill.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $402.40 an ounce, down 60 cents. Estimated volume was a heavy 6.5 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $401.50 an ounce.
