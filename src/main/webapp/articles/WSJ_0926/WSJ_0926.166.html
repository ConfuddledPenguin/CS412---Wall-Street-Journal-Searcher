Technology: Motorola Cuts Prices On RISC Chips in Bid To Lift Market Share

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/26/90

AUSTIN, Texas -- Motorola Inc.'s microprocessor products group said it slashed prices on its reduction instruction set computer (RISC) chips by as much as two-thirds, in a bid to improve its share in that fast-growing market. RISC chips are speedy, streamlined microprocessors currently used in workstations, but they have become so inexpensive that personal-computer makers are about to adopt them. They pose a threat to conventional PC microprocessors built by Intel Corp., Santa Clara, Calif., and Motorola. Those two companies' own RISC chips lag significantly in sales behind those of Sun Microsystems Inc., Mountain View, Calif., and MIPS Computer Systems Inc., Sunnyvale, Calif., according to analysts. The market, however, is still young. Motorola estimates that about 200,000 RISC chips were sold last year, a small fraction of PC chip sales.

Motorola said it cut the price on its 16 megahertz 88100 chip to $49 from $148, and made cuts of similar magnitude on chips that operated at speeds of 20 to 33 megahertz. The prices apply to orders of 1,000 chips or more.

"The 88000 family now sets a RISC price/performance standard for the industry," said Jeff Nutt, marketing manager for the 88000 family. "We believe these are the lowest-price RISC chips for the computer market."

But Andrew Allison, a consultant and editor of RISC Management newsletter, Los Altos, Calif., said Sun and MIPS are likely to keep their early leads.

"This is in response to Motorola's negligible market position," Mr. Allison said. "They haven't succeeded in acquiring any volume customers for the product and time is flashing by." He added that Motorola's new prices are "now in the ballpark, but given their market position that isn't enough."
