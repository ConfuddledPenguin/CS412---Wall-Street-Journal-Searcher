Shearson Lehman Will Pay CFTC Fine

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/26/90

WASHINGTON -- Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc. agreed to pay a $220,000 fine to settle an administrative complaint by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission for its role in helping a Brazilian investor violate position limits in orange-juice futures. The agency said that from October 1987 to June of the next year, Horst Happel of Sao Paolo, Brazil, an individual trader and general manager of a Brazilian frozen concentrated orange-juice processor allegedly traded through accounts at Shearson. Shearson and account executive Charles Barley, Windermere, Fla., allegedly aided and abetted Mr. Happel's actions.

Shearson also was charged with failing to disclose information in reports, failing to update those reports, and filing innaccurate reports to the CFTC. Shearson agreed to settle the charges without admitting or denying them.

Shearson called the CFTC allegations "highly technical in nature," involving "no customer losses and related to activity at only one of our branches several years ago." The brokerage firm is a subsidiary of American Express Co., New York.

Mr. Barley also agreed to settle the charges without admission or denial, will be suspended for 30 days and must pay a $35,000 fine. Mr. Barley's manager, Gerald Helmich, Longwood, Fla., without admitting or denying wrongdoing, agreed to CFTC charges of failing to supervise and won't be allowed to supervise any brokers for 14 days.

Mr. Happel hasn't settled the agency's charges. His lawyer, Howard Schneider, said Mr. Happel "doesn't think that the CFTC is correct and he intends to vigorously defend himself."

The CFTC said that Mr. Happel, through his account and others he controlled, held far more than the maximum number of 800 juice contracts on the Citrus Associates of the New York Cotton Exchange for 166 days during the October to June period. Each contract is equal to 15,000 pounds of solid frozen juice. On some of those days, Mr. Happel's positions exceeded the limit by 1,142 contracts, the CFTC said.

"Shearson knew they were all his accounts and as a member firm should have known what the limits were," said Dennis Klejna, the agency's enforcement director. "The positions should have been aggregated."
