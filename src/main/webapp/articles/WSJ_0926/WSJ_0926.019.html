Trump Bankers Expected to Seek Shuttle Pay Cuts

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 09/26/90

Donald Trump's bankers are expected to demand today that he slash salaries and institute layoffs at the Trump Shuttle in exchange for keeping control of the airline. The demands come on the heels of disclosures that Mr. Trump won't be able to make interest payments on the $245 million in unsecured shuttle debt he is required to make under last month's $65 million bank bailout. Mr. Trump borrowed $380 million to purchase the airline from Eastern Airlines in 1989 and has already missed a $1.1 million interest payment.

"You've heard about what happened at the New York Post?" asked one banker, referring to the 20% salary cut employees of that paper agreed to recently. "Well Trump can do the same thing."

Mr. Trump couldn't be reached to comment.

About 20 of Mr. Trump's creditors who hold pieces of the $245 million loan plan to meet with Mr. Trump at the offices of the law firm of Weil Gotshal & Manges, which has represented Citibank throughout the Trump restructuring talks. The Citicorp unit was the lead lender for the $245 million unsecured loan, as well as an additional $135 million Trump Shuttle equity loan. The bank subsequently sold off most of its shuttle debt, and now is believed to hold only about 15% of the unsecured debt.

Citibank has emerged as a conciliatory force in negotiations with other Trump lenders, largely because it has the most to lose in the event Mr. Trump cedes control of his assets. As part of the bailout, Citibank received second and third mortgages on many Trump assets; should he lose them to other creditors, Citibank's chances of recovering its losses would be greatly diminished. Including the shuttle, Citibank lent Mr. Trump a total of $1.1 billion.

Some of the shuttle lending group, which includes Fuji Bank, Bank of Tokyo, Natwest, and Bank of America, were startled to hear about Mr. Trump's restructuring plans last week, only weeks after Mr. Trump personally assured them the debts would continue to be paid.

Meanwhile, America West Airline's plans to purchase both new planes and the Pan Am Shuttle, which competes with the Trump Shuttle, are believed by bankers to be another setback for Mr. Trump. If it closes on its purchase of the Pan Am Shuttle, America West will most likely be able to use its Boeing 737s for the shuttle, which are more modern than the 727s used by both Pan Am and the Trump Shuttle.
