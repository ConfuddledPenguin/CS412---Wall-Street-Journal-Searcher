REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Beltway Armageddon

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/26/90

The end is nigh. October 1 is the day. We know this because all around Washington politicians have begun to wear sandwich boards announcing that if the budget summit doesn't yield up an "agreement" by next Monday, the hellfire of "sequester" will rain down upon us all. "We are 10 days from Armageddon," said Minnesota Republican William Frenzel last Friday. Picking up Darman's Book of Revelation, Wisconsin Republican Steve Gunderson said on Monday, "Seven days to Armageddon. Seven days and the government stops."

The government stops. The Education Department is furloughed. There are riots in the streets. It's enough to make you think about life's meaning, or at least the government's.

For example, there's the fact that the dreaded Gramm-Rudman sequester would cut spending by only $85.4 billion. Not exactly peanuts, but out of a federal budget of $1.3 trillion, not mass starvation either. It's a cut of merely 6.5% -- from the previously scheduled levels. In fact, the cut is less than the rate that spending was already scheduled to increase. In other words, even after the dreaded sequester, overall federal spending would increase.

Armageddon. Beltway style. Out in the real world, Chase Manhattan just said it plans to lay off 5,000 employees, Pan Am 2,500.

Rather than actually cut anything in government, though, Washington threatens the country with the worst layoffs possible. Rather than force choices, Congress and Budget Director Darman somehow manage to agree that air-traffic controllers are the first to go. Threaten military defeat in Iraq. Shut down O'Hare Airport before sending home congressional staff.

What will expiate our sins? Of course, a budget agreement. Some $50 billion in "savings" next year -- out of an economy of $5 trillion. Higher income-tax rates. More for cigarettes, beer and gas. We don't know about our readers, but the end we're praying for isn't October 1, but November 6. That's Election Day.
