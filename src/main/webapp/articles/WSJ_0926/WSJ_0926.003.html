You Can Withdraw Money Here, But Don't Say You Prefer Singles

  • Author: Kathleen A. Hughes
  • Date: 09/26/90

SANTA MONICA, Calif. -- In the 1960s, there were protest rallies over the Vietnam War, civil rights, farm workers and other important issues. Social activism has taken a different turn in the 1990s: A dating service organized a protest rally here against a bank's advertising campaign.

It all started when Jeffrey Ullman, president of Great Expectations, a large video-dating service, was driving on the freeway behind a bus. On the back of the bus, an ad for Santa Monica Bank said: "WE HANDLE MORE ZEROS THAN A DATING SERVICE."

"I was so shocked I almost ran off the road," says Mr. Ullman. "I got on my car phone and I called the bank. I was offended." He was transferred to Pat Robertson, a vice president of Team One, the advertising division of Saatchi & Saatchi that dreamed up the line. "She laughed it off," recalls Mr. Ullman. "She said it was a whimsical campaign."

The ad agency isn't laughing now. At Mr. Ullman's protest yesterday in front of the bank, only 21 protesters showed up, but so did reporters and television cameras. Picketers carried signs that said: "Santa Monica Bank thinks singles are zeros. Reduce your balance to zero."

"I don't consider myself a zero," says Corinne Balzac, a crisis counselor, who attended the rally. "I take offense at the ads." Brian Baker, a sales engineer at the rally, said: "The implication is that anyone who belongs to a dating service is a zero. If I had an account at the bank, I would close it."

Bank officials didn't return calls. Ms. Robertson brands the rally a publicity stunt: "Mr. Ullman told me he was going to seize the moment and turn this to his advantage. We certainly didn't mean to demean any organization or person."

So what did the agency mean by zeros? "We all have dates, myself included, that aren't love connections," she says. "We were playing off the zero as a double entendre -- cash zeros and a zero that doesn't connect in a dating sense."

Ms. Robertson says the bank won't buckle to protesters. She says the monthly ad campaign will change as scheduled, on Oct. 1, to: "MONEY DOESN'T GROW ON TREES. JUST AT OUR BRANCHES." Past slogans included, "MONEY ISN'T EVERYTHING. (YEAH, RIGHT.)" and "WE SELL CDS DOWN BY THE SEASHORE."
