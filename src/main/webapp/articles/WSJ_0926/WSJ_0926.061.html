Citibank Plans Staff Cutbacks In LBO Unit --- Concern Expects to Lay Off 100 Workers, Transfer Hundreds in Division

  • Author: Fred R. Bleakley
  • Date: 09/26/90

In another sign that the boom in leveraged buy-outs has come and gone, Citibank will soon lay off 100 workers in its leveraged-lending unit and transfer hundreds of others, according to a senior official at the bank. The move, expected to take place over the next two weeks, is the first in a series of reductions aimed at slashing $250 million to $300 million in annual expenses in the sector serving corporations in the U.S., Europe and Japan. Other Citibank business units in the U.S. are expected to announce cutbacks internally over the next six to eight weeks, with other units outside the U.S. announcing similar moves and restructurings in the first quarter of next year.

Citibank, a unit of Citicorp, has about 450 persons in its Leveraged Capital division. The majority of those remaining will be transferred along with their clients to existing units that promote the full range of the bank's service, said Todd Slotkin, a Citibank senior corporate finance executive. Citibank has leveraged lending offices in five other U.S. cities besides New York.

"The leveraged buy-out market is down about 50% this year and the outlook is flat for another year or so," said Mr. Slotkin, who confirmed rumors about the cutbacks. He added that the conflict in the Mideast and further uncertainty over the economy dashed a rebound in leveraged buy-outs this summer. "August was almost a non-month" for leveraged buy-outs, he said.

Citibank's retrenchment in leveraged lending and expected cutbacks in other areas of corporate finance is in line with staff reductions in those areas at other major banks and on Wall Street. Bankers Trust cut 75 professional employees from its 750 person professional corporate finance staff in June. And Chemical Bank reduced staffing in those areas as part of a 1,100 staff rollback in February.

On Wall Street, about 200 investment bankers specializing in leveraged buy-outs have lost their jobs at major firms since the first of the year, estimates Perrin Long of Lipper Analytical Services.

Ironically, leveraged lending is not one of the areas slated for cutbacks at Chase Manhattan Bank, which announced Friday plans to reduce staff by 5,000. Chase did not build up as large a full-time leveraged-lending staff as Citibank, and it handles a broader mix of financing alternatives, including infusions of equity, said Maria Beechey, head of merchant banking at Chase. In addition, she said, "There still are a significant number of middle market leveraged buy-outs that are keeping us busy."

The cutbacks are part of a business-by-business scrutiny at Citibank that has been underway for the past two months. It is expected by analysts to reduce the 15,000 person staff of the wholesale banking division for the developed world by at least 1,000 persons.
