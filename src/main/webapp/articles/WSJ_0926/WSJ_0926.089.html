Defense Spending By Soviets Fell 5%, Pentagon Reports

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 09/26/90

WASHINGTON -- The Pentagon said the Soviet Union cut overall defense spending last year by 4% to 5% and slashed production of tanks, ammunition, helicopters and other weapons. Nevertheless, Defense Secretary Dick Cheney, in releasing the department's annual review of the Soviet military, warned that the Soviet Union remains a military threat that must be countered.

The ninth edition of Soviet Military Power reported a cut of between 6% and 7% in weapons procurement spending, a halving of tank production to 1,700 from 3,500, and lesser cuts in the output of fighter planes, bombers, artillery and other important weapons. It also disclosed a "dramatic decrease" in Soviet space launches and predicted that Moscow will scrap as many as 450 navy ships by 1996 and cut 80,000 sailors from its navy.

The slick book still features photos of menacing Red Army weapons, just as it has since it was first issued by the Reagan administration to gather public support for the military buildup of the 1980s.

But this year, the book also included photos of Soviet troops leaving Eastern Europe, Soviet missiles being destroyed to comply with arms-control treaties, and Soviet bombers whose tails had been cut off to make them unusable.

The report, however, repeatedly noted that in some weapons categories, especially strategic nuclear weapons, Moscow is continuing to build strength and that even reduced output levels for items such as tanks still top Western production rates. It also stressed that the Soviets still field the world's largest military force.

"What it does not reveal, no matter how much we might wish it," said Mr. Cheney, "is an eviscerated Soviet force structure and an evaporating threat . . . The Soviet Union remains an enormous military superpower."
