HONG KONG HIRING CONTROVERSY

A decision by the Hong Kong government to open the British colony's labor market to Chinese nationals living overseas has been welcomed by the local business community but has prompted questions about the government's motives.

The government said it hoped that allowing Chinese nationals to seek employment with Hong Kong companies would help offset the steady departure of professionals from the territory in the run-up to 1997, when Hong Kong reverts to Chinese rule. Political observers and others outside the government, however, said local authorities have bowed to Beijing's demands that they allow an increased flow of Chinese nationals into the territory.

Under current regulations, mainland Chinese enter the Hong Kong labor market by only two routes: a one-way immigrant visa, granted to 75 Chinese nationals each day; or sponsorship by a mainland employer operating in the territory. Under the new policy, Chinese nationals who have lived overseas for at least the past two years may apply for jobs with private Hong Kong companies.

Separately, Hong Kong Tourist Association Chairman Martin Barrow disclaimed an earlier zero-growth forecast for 1990 tourism and predicted 8% growth instead. He cited gains in arrivals from Southeast Asian countries and from Taiwan, Japan and South Korea.

SPANISH THEME-PARK ROW

In a last-minute effort to salvage plans by Anheuser-Busch Cos. to build a multimillion-dollar theme park and resort in northeastern Spain, a top official with the Catalonian regional government is traveling to St. Louis for direct talks with the U.S. brewing company. The city of Vilaseca-Salou, in which the project was to be built, recently split into two warring municipalities, one of which now refuses to allow the park to be built within city limits. Anheuser-Busch last month threatened to relocate the project.

EAST BLOC REQUEST FOR HONDA

Japan's Honda Motor Co. said that state-owned motorcycle companies in the Soviet Union and Poland have asked it to provide technical support for their operations. Honda is studying the feasibility of offering such support, a spokesman said, adding that the companies are interested in Honda's small-engine technology.

HELPING THE MIDEAST'S MIGRANTS

The International Labor Organization said it plans to send teams to several countries with large migrant communities in Iraq and Kuwait to assess financial losses of workers returning home. The Geneva-based United Nations agency urged the world to heed the plight of migrant workers in the Persian Gulf region. Among the countries the ILO teams will visit are Egypt and the Philippines.

WARNINGS FROM ARGENTINA

The director of Argentina's state telephone company said Manufacturers Hanover Corp. wouldn't be given more time to line up cash and debt papers it needs to take over a section of Entel Oct. 8. Manufacturers Hanover Vice President Cesar Deimonas told Radio Mitre the consortium led by the bank hadn't formally requested an extension, and "we think we can put together" the funds in time. Separately, Argentina's public-works minister said that the Spanish airline Iberia and its Argentine partner have three days to come up with a $130 million down payment for 85% of the national carrier Aerolineas Argentinas or lose the bid.

VIETNAMESE-SOUTH KOREAN LINKS

Vietnamese officials have proposed the opening of Vietnamese and South Korean trade offices in Seoul and Hanoi to help expand trade relations between the two nations. The officials, in Seoul for a trade and investment seminar, said foreign businesses participating in Vietnam's reconstruction would be given tax and other benefits.

BRITAIN'S ANTI-POLLUTION PACKAGE

The British government unveiled a package designed to curb pollution of the air, land and waters.

Draft legislation, promised by the ruling Conservatives for more than two years, pledged to spend $53 billion on environmental protection measures by the year 2000.

The 300-page document outlined 350 proposals for tackling increasing pollution but contained no surprises and repeated many prior commitments. Among those are a drive to improve water quality and a pledge to phase out ozone-depleting gases by 1997.

Environmental groups attacked the plans as "too little, too late." But Environment Minister Chris Patten described the paper as the "first really comprehensive statement of government policy on the environment, ranging from the street corner to the stratosphere."

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

A banknote denominated at one million British pounds, printed just after World War II but no longer legal tender, will be auctioned in London next month. The note, used by the British Treasury as a record of the movement of money from the Marshall Aid Fund, is expected to fetch up to $56,500. . . . Tanzania announced the arrest of 2,500 poachers and the seizure of 11,000 weapons and 3,000 elephant tusks in a two-year anti-poaching campaign. Wildlife Director Costa Mlay said most of the poachers arrested had either been fined or sent to jail for terms ranging from five to 22 years.
