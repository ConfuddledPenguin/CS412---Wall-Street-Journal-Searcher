Senate Leaders Work to Salvage Anti-Crime Bill

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 06/08/90

WASHINGTON -- Democratic and Republican leaders in the Senate said they will continue efforts to salvage an omnibus anti-crime bill, after a second attempt to limit debate on the wide-ranging legislation failed. After private negotiations last night, proponents of the bill said they will try to preserve for a future vote provisions already fiercely debated on the Senate floor, including a three-year ban on nine types of semi-automatic "assault" weapons, expansion of the federal death penalty, and sharp curbs on constitutional appeals by death-row inmates.

But George Mitchell, the majority leader, and Robert Dole, the minority leader, emerged from the closed-door session to report that they didn't have an agreement. Democrats, Mr. Mitchell said, "will review and take a look at" a Republican proposal to avoid a filibuster that has threatened to kill the entire bill.

Delaware Sen. Joseph Biden, who is managing the legislation for the Democrats, sounded more optimistic in a separate press conference. Calling the Republican proposal "a serious effort," he said, "my gut tells me something will break and we will get a bill."

Among other things, the GOP proposed to strip a measure that would allow criminal juries in certain instances to consider unconstitutionally gathered evidence. Mr. Biden predicted there would be several days of negotiations before any further votes.

Earlier yesterday, Democratic proponents of the bill had pronounced it dead after the Senate failed for a second time to limit debate on the legislation. The second vote in favor of a 30-hour debate limit was 57-37, but 60 votes are needed under Senate rules.

With some 300 amendments pending at that point, debate could have dragged on indefinitely. Mr. Mitchell had said that without a time limit, he would be forced to end consideration of the bill. That outcome is exactly what opponents of the gun-control provision, led by the National Rifle Association, had sought in helping orchestrate the avalanche of amendments.

But South Carolina Sen. Strom Thurmond led a Republican effort to save some portion of the crime legislation. In a private caucus, GOP leaders agreed to propose to Democrats that each party be allowed to propose a maximum of 12 amendments to the current version of the bill, with a predetermined time limit on debate, Republican aides said.

Another set of noncontroversial amendments might also be added under the Republican plan, aides said. GOP leaders proposed that the Senate drop consideration of a Biden proposal to allow certain exceptions to the so-called exclusionary rule, which bars the use of unconstitutionally gathered evidence in criminal prosecutions. Republicans have advocated even broader exceptions to the rule, and the issue was sure to provoke sharp conflict.

In his news conference, Mr. Biden indicated a willingness to consider eliminating the exclusionary-rule provision, as well as his proposal to create a new division for drugs and organized crime within the Justice Department.
