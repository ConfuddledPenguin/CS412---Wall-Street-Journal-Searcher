Chicago Merc Pays $100,000 Penalty To Settle Charges

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 06/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The Chicago Mercantile Exchange, in the second-largest settlement ever accepted by a commodity exchange, agreed to pay a $100,000 civil penalty for allegedly failing to report violations by four futures firms. The settlement reached with the Commodity Futures Trading Commission concludes a 13-count administrative complaint filed by the agency in January 1987.

The penalty comes at a time when the agency would benefit from appearing to be a strong regulator. The Bush administration is proposing to wrest oversight of stock-index futures away from the CFTC and grant the power to the Securities and Exchange Commission. That shift is vehemently opposed by the commodity regulator, which is backed in the fight by the commodity exchanges.

The CFTC said the Merc violated rules requiring exchanges to tell the agency if a member firm fails to provide CFTC-required reports. When the four firms didn't comply, the Merc didn't disclose that, the agency said.

A spokesman for the exchange said the complaint resulted from a disagreement over interpretation of CFTC regulations.

"I think there was honest disagreement over what should or shouldn't have been reported," Merc spokesman Andrew Yemma said. "You can't always know exactly what somebody wants, and our interpretation of what to provide was different than theirs."

"The whole purpose of the case," said CFTC enforcement director Dennis Klejna, "is to drive home the point that the exchanges have an obligation to inform the commission when they learn of the inadequacies and we haven't been informed."

It isn't unusual for the agency to charge exchanges with failing to follow their own rules, as in this case, he said. But, "This is the first time we've brought an action against an exchange for failing to provide notice to the commission" of firms' deficiencies, Mr. Klejna said.

The four firms -- CRT Services Inc., Chicago Grain & Financial Futures Co., Rouse Woodstock Inc. and ContiCommodity Services Inc. -- failed to make reports relating to early warning net capital levels, which tell the agency that the firm might be headed for trouble; major inadequacies in internal controls; and non-current books and records, the CFTC said. Financial audits of the firms from September 1982 to March 1984 showed the problems, the agency said.

A CRT Services official said the problem resulted from an error in a computer program used by the firm that was immediately corrected. The three other firms have gone out of business or have been acquired by other commodity-trading firms since the alleged violations occurred.

Although no charges have been filed against the firms for similar reporting violations, both Rouse and ContiCommodity in recent years have settled CFTC charges of fraud and other violations, the agency said.

The Merc, which settled the complaint without admitting or denying guilt, also agreed to let the CFTC more closely monitor the exchange's enforcement of its financial compliance program for two years.

The Merc's agreement was second in size to the $200,000 settlement by the New York Mercantile Exchange in the early 1980s for problems stemming from the 400-ounce gold contract. The case resulted in the delisting of the contract.
