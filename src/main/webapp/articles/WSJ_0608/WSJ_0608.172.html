Your Money Matters: Bank-Lobby Shopping for Mutual Funds May Be Convenient, but at What Price?

  • Author: Ellen E. Shultz
  • Date: 06/08/90

Probably more people could tell you which six states border Arkansas than could tell you whether the mutual funds they buy in banks are different from other funds. But this ignorance can be costly. While it is possible to find top-performing mutual funds at banks, many do little better than passbook savings accounts. There can also be other pitfalls.

Banks have been offering stock and bond mutual funds for only a few years. But more and more of them are getting into the act these days, lured by lucrative fees and a captive consumer base.

"It's a natural addition to their product line," says Patrick Kennedy, a marketing vice president at Northern Trust Co. in Chicago. "They have the distribution channels in place, and they have the image as a more secure organization, whether right or wrong."

Lipper Analytical Services Inc. tracks the performance of 435 bank-advised funds, representing $71 billion. That compares with a mutual fund universe of 2,549 funds, at $1 trillion. Only about 88 of the largest banks actually manage investments themselves. Other banks without the in-house resources to manage money often contract with mutual-fund companies to sell outside funds in bank offices, sometimes under a bank name, in exchange for a percentage of fees.

The banks' biggest selling point is convenience. Customers can purchase mutual funds with the same ease that they buy a certificate of deposit -- and have the ability to redeem shares into their checking accounts with a phone call. Some, including Chase Manhattan Bank, also count the money in mutual funds toward the minimum balance necessary for free checking.

But investors who limit themselves to mutual funds they find for sale in bank lobbies may be curtailing their choices unnecessarily. Unless a bank has a brokerage affiliate that enables depositors to buy "outside" funds, they may have to settle for a family with six or fewer funds.

"Investors who want a growth fund, a money market and a fixed-income fund would be lucky to find that the three offered by their bank all have better than average returns," says Matthew Fink of the Investment Company Institute, a trade group for the mutual funds industry.

Further, investors in most cases are limited to funds that charge sales fees, usually 4.5% to 5%, though a few major institutions such as Chase and Citibank offer their own no-load funds. Anyone with the energy to pick up the phone has access to many top-performing mutual funds that are available from mutual fund companies without sales fees.

As for performance, bank officials challenge the prevailing belief that mutual fund companies are inherently better at managing money. "One of the myths on Wall Street is that banks aren't good at managing money," says Gregory Sanford, a managing director at Security Pacific Corp. in Los Angeles.

Since most bank-advised funds are relatively new, they don't have performance figures available for longer than three years, which makes long-term performance impossible to evaluate. But the numbers that are available show that returns vary widely. Moreover, some of the major bank fund families include both stellar and mediocre performers.

"The bottom line is that the performance is generated by the investment managers, and you have good and bad in banking, and good and bad in outside fund management," says Stephen Mintos, director of sales and marketing with the Winsbury Co., a Columbus, Ohio firm that provides mutual fund services to banks.

Investors evaluating the performance of a bank-advised fund should compare it with other funds of its type, such as growth funds or balanced funds, "regardless of whether it's a bank fund or a traditional mutual fund," Mr. Mintos says.

Says Northern Trust's Mr. Kennedy: "Only go with a bank if you get the type of fund you're looking for, and the performance."

Some bank funds have done quite well. Over the past 12 months, for instance, Chase Manhattan's Vista Growth and Income was up 36.87%, compared with an average of 9.8% for all growth-and-income mutual funds, according to Lipper Analytical; Vista Capital Growth was up 10.35%, compared with 11.24% for all growth funds.

Other bank funds with healthy returns over the past 12 months include Security Pacific's Pacific Horizon Convertible (up 16.81% vs. 4.48% for all convertible funds) and Banc One Co.'s Helmsman Income Equity (up 12.22%, compared with 6.85% for all equity-income funds).

On the other hand, Citibank's Landmark Growth and Income was up just 2.08%, far below the 9.8% group average, Lipper's numbers show.

Investors who stick with their banks should also know that the funds available there tend to be smaller than other mutual funds. "They haven't been around as long, and aren't marketed nationally," says James McLaughlin, director of trust and securities for the American Bankers Association.

Their smaller size may give some bank funds the same disadvantages all smaller funds typically face, such as being more difficult to track, and having higher expense ratios, which can have a negative effect on performance. However, many banks have been willing to waive some investment advisory fees until the fund reaches a target size.

"Read the prospectus to find out what fees they're waiving, and for how long," says Michael Ryba, product manager for Columbus, Ohio-based Banc One's Helmsman funds, which charge no advisory fees on most funds.

(Finally, for those who've been racking their brains about geography, the six states bordering Arkansas are -- starting to the north and moving clockwise -- Missouri, Tennessee, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas and Oklahoma.)

How Some Bank-Advised Mutual Funds Compare

Bank mutual-fund families typically include money-market, growth-stock and fixed-income funds; some also include such other offerings as aggressive-growth, growth-and-income, balanced and international funds. Most bank fund families offer telephone switching and redemptions, and fund investments are sometimes counted toward balances required for no-fee checking.
