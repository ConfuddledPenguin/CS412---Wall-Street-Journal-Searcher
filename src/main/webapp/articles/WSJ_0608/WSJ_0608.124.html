Foreign Exchange: Dollar Has Mixed Day as Currencies Of Switzerland, Australia Get Attention

  • Author: John Bader and Brandon Mitchner Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/08/90

NEW YORK -- Reports on monetary policy in Switzerland and Australia sparked traders' interest against a background of mixed U.S. dollar dealings. The Swiss franc fell against the dollar and other currencies on a news-service dispatch, later termed misleading, about comments made Wednesday by Swiss National Bank Chairman Markus Lusser in San Francisco. The market took the report as a sign that the central bank might soon ease its tight monetary policy.

Meanwhile, a comment by Australian Treasurer Paul Keating that the nation's monetary policy would stay tight drove the Australian dollar higher.

In other activity, the U.S. dollar extended its gradual rise against the mark, but slipped slightly against the yen. U.S. dollar dealings were characterized by participants as slow, amid a general dearth of news on which to trade.

But the U.S. currency leapt against its Swiss counterpart after traders seized on the news-service report. The report appeared to signal a change in tone by Mr. Lusser, who in the past has vowed to do whatever is necessary to maintain the strength of the franc, meaning in practice a policy of relatively high interest rates.

But a central bank official later termed the report misleading, and added that the bank hasn't any intention of easing policy soon. The bank raised its Lombard rate to 10.875% from 10.75%.

In Australia, Mr. Keating said the continued tight grip on money is needed to prevent a rebound in spending. He said it will be some time before policy can be relaxed, because the country's inflation and current account deficit are too high.

In late afternoon New York trading, the dollar was at 1.6935 marks, up from 1.6885 marks late Wednesday, and at 152.55 yen, down from 152.65 yen. Sterling was at $1.6890, up from $1.6875.

The dollar was at 1.4445 Swiss francs, up from 1.4360 francs late Wednesday. The Australian dollar was at 77.75 U.S. cents, up from 77.23 U.S. cents.

In Tokyo Friday, the dollar was trading at 152.55 yen soon after opening at 152.58 yen; it closed in Tokyo Thursday at 152.55 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6925 marks, up from Thursday's Tokyo close of 1.6884 marks.

In other dealings, the U.S. dollar continued to strengthen against its West German counterpart, which has been weakening in past days on nervousness about labor unrest in East Germany.

A nationwide strike by retail, restaurant and other workers at small, independent businesses has been called for today. They are protesting terms of the planned integration of the two Germanys July 2, which employers' groups have said would result in the near-term loss of as many as 350,000 jobs.

In Europe, the U.S. dollar was mostly firmer, although the currency continues trading in well-established bands. In Tokyo, the U.S. dollar was narrowly mixed in thin trading.

Separately, U.S. monetary authorities sold $1.78 billion for yen and marks in the three months ended April 30, according to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York.

While the level of intervention was greater than the $750 million sold for yen in the November-January period, it was small relative to other recent periods, and mainly was intended to moderate the dollar's rise against the yen, the Fed said. In the May-July quarter of 1989, the U.S. sold a record $11.92 billion.

For the year ended Jan. 31, the Fed spent approximately $21 billion in intervention operations, according to Sam Y. Cross, executive vice president of the New York Fed in charge of its foreign group, who presented the Fed's quarterly foreign-exchange report to Congress.

The Fed sold $1.58 billion to buy yen during the most recent period, and $200 million to buy marks, often in concert with the central banks of other Group of Seven industrial nations, Mr. Cross said. G-7 nations include the U.S., Japan, Britain, West Germany, France, Italy and Canada.

Mr. Cross said the Fed didn't intervene against any other currencies in the period. The Fed does, however, occasionally act as agent for other central banks.

The dollar rose 10% against the yen and fell less than 0.5% against the mark on balance during the period, Mr. Cross said.

The Fed didn't comment on any current activities in the currency market.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery fell $2.80, to $354.60 an ounce. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.5 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $354.25 an ounce.
