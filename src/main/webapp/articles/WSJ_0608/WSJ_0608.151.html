April Consumer Debt Grew At Annual Rate of Just 1%

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/08/90

WASHINGTON -- Consumer debt barely grew in April, weighed down mainly by a plunge in car loans, the Federal Reserve said. Consumer installment credit outstanding -- generally all forms of personal debt but mortgages -- grew a scant $584 million in April, amounting to an annual growth rate of 1%, the Fed said. That followed annual growth rates of 4% in March and 0.1% in February, both far smaller than most gains last year and weaker than many economists had predicted.

Consumer credit has expanded at a faint pace all year. Many economists say the slowdown mainly reflects a new caution among consumers in response to uncertain economic conditions.

"Consumer spending had held up pretty well through the first quarter, but it has begun to weaken a bit, as has the job market and income gains," said Sandra Shaber, an economist for the Futures Group. "So we can't take consumer spending as much for granted as we had." She noted that the expansion of consumer debt has slipped behind personal-income gains during the past six months.

Automobile loans, which fell in two of the past three months, tumbled at a 10.3% annual rate during the month, a drop of $2.5 billion. But revolving credit, which includes mostly short-term debt such as credit-card and bank-card borrowing, shot up $2.4 billion, or at a 14% annual rate.

The numbers were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations. Without adjusting, consumer installment credit outstanding stood at $715.82 billion at the end of April, up 4.3% from a year earlier.
