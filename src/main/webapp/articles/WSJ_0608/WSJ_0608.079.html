Thursday's Markets: Stocks Slide; Bond Prices Inch Higher --- Profit-Taking Erases An Opening Surge In Industrial Average

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/08/90

NEW YORK -- A third day of selective profit-taking wiped out an opening surge in stock prices, pushing that market lower while bond prices inched higher on more evidence of slowing economic growth. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 14.32 points to close at 2897.33 in moderately active trading. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices rose an eighth of a point -- or $1.25 for each $1,000 of face amount -- in light trading. The dollar was mixed against the mark and the yen, with many traders awaiting new readings next week on the inflation rate.

Money manager Rajiv Sharma, president of Serco Investments, Sausalito, Calif., was among the stock-market profit-takers. "We have been net sellers since it went over 2900," he said. At 2850, he started selling technology, oil-drilling and environmental-services stocks. At 2900, he started shedding Dow industrial stocks.

The rally that took the industrial average above 2900 Friday was sparked by a run-up in bond prices. Rather than risk the possibility of bonds heading into a severe downturn, "It's better to take profits now and let the market play itself out," Mr. Sharma said.

But without a bond-market retreat, Gene Seagle, technical-research director at Gruntal & Co., says the industrial average still could make a run for 3000. If the extreme bears throw in the towel and the New York Stock Exchange has a few 200 million share days, "we'll go through 3000 to 3150 before we have an intermediate correction," he predicted.

Concern about too much bullishness may be tempering buying interest, some analysts say. This week's Investors Intelligence survey of investment advisers shows the bulls rising to 47.8% of those surveyed from 45.5% last week; bears fell to 35.4% from 41.1%. The worry is that advisers who call themselves bulls are in the market, which implies there are fewer investors on the sidelines waiting to get in.

The bullish percentage is "still below a level that would suggest the market is overbought," said Michael Burke, editor of the survey for Chartcraft Inc., New Rochelle, N.Y. "When it gets into the 53% to 56% range, that normally signals a correction."

Bond prices moved narrowly as investors tried to figure out how -- or if -- Manuel Johnson's resignation as a Federal Reserve Board governor might affect Fed policy on interest rates. Prices then rose on the news that consumer-installment credit in April rose much less than expected.

Some analysts say consumers are leery of taking on debt because they lack confidence in the economy's health. This additional indication of sluggish economic growth in turn raises investors' expectations that the Fed will lower interest rates to stimulate business activity and keep the economy from tipping into recession.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell on volume of 160.4 million shares, down from 164 million Wednesday, in Big Board trading. Declining issues outnumbered advancers, 852 to 638.

Bond prices rose. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 8.42%.

The dollar gained slightly. In late New York trading, the currency was at 1.6935 marks and 152.55 yen, compared with 1.6885 marks and 152.65 yen Wednesday.
