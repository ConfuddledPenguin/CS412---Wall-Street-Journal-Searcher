Rockwell Realigns Its Big Defense Electronics Sector

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 06/08/90

EL SEGUNDO, Calif. -- Rockwell International Corp., trying to squeeze what it can from one of the only potential growth areas left in the military budget, has realigned its defense-electronics organization. The action won't affect the 16,000 employees in the sector. But it streamlines the number of divisions that fall under Rockwell's defense-electronics rubric to five from six, and the number of individual operating segments to 14 from 17.

"We're always looking for ways to bring like businesses together, to build on each other," Kent Black, Rockwell's executive vice president and chief operating officer, said in an interview. He said the move was made not to save money per se, but to allow the company to bid more effectively on Pentagon contracts and overseas work.

For example, the segment that makes missile-guidance equipment will be combined with the shop that designs navigational gear for submarines, allowing the two to share similar ideas and technologies. Likewise, units that deal heavily with computer software will unite.

The push to regroup defense-electronics apparently was heightened by several recent contract awards. "It's created pressure to rationalize some of these things," Mr. Black said. Among the wins: exclusive production of the Hellfire missile, which had been shared with Martin Marietta Corp.; the manufacture of parts for the MX and Midgetman missiles, which had been handled primarily by Northrop Corp.; and the integration of a combat system for the Australian navy's new submarine program.

Mr. Black also said the company has been notified that it beat out General Dynamics Corp. for a $150 million pact to upgrade the avionics on F-111 aircraft for the Australian government, though the contract award hasn't been officially announced.

Rockwell's efforts in the defense-electronics realm mirror those under way throughout the industry. Electronics is one of the few parts of the Defense Department budget that might grow through the mid-1990s, because even if new aircraft, armored vehicles and ships aren't built, the insides of the older models will have to be retrofitted.

As a result, competition is heating up. "A lot of companies are trying to eliminate their redundancies," said Chuck Quinn, a marketing executive with United Research, a consulting firm that works closely with aerospace concerns. Abeyance, he said, will spell doom.

Mr. Black said he thinks that Rockwell's defense-electronics group, which now accounts for about 15% of the diversified parent corporation's $12.52 billion in total revenue, could expand slightly in the next few years.
