Canada Talks Stall After Fifth Day Over Quebec's Bid for Special Status

  • Author: John Urquhart and G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 06/08/90

OTTAWA -- Negotiations to resolve Canada's constitutional impasse stalled last night after five days of talks, and several provincial premiers warned that the situation is critical. Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney and the nation's 10 provincial premiers have been meeting here since Sunday night to try to resolve the constitutional dispute. They will meet again this morning. The premiers are stalemated over recognition of French-speaking Quebec as a "distinct society" within Canada.

"We say enough is enough," said Quebec Premier Robert Bourassa. Quebec "can't and won't accept amendments" to the Meech Lake accord, a package of proposed constitutional amendments. One Meech Lake clause recognizes Quebec as a "distinct society."

The provision is highly prized by Quebec as recognition of its cultural uniqueness even though constitutional lawyers say it won't add to the province's powers. The distinct society clause "simply confirms the obvious," said Mr. Mulroney. Quebec, he said, is different, and "that difference is very much worth conserving in the interest of Quebeckers and of all Canadians."

Newfoundland Premier Clyde Wells said "it seems highly unlikely" the premiers will be able to reach an agreement if Quebec refuses to budge on the "distinct society" issue. Manitoba Premier Gary Filmon said "we are in a very critical situation."

Several premiers were optimistic they would be able to reach an agreement yesterday as they entered the government conference here where they are meeting. Over the course of the day provincial goverment officials said the premiers were making progress. By late afternoon the officials were saying the talks were at a decisive stage. They said all issues had been discussed, proposed solutions were on the table and it was now up to the premiers to accept or reject the proposals.

Talks also involve reform of the federal Senate. Ontario and Quebec each has 24 seats in the 104-seat chamber. The smaller provinces are demanding a more equitable distribution of seats.

The smaller provinces have also been urging that the Canadian Senate become more like the U.S. Senate in order to get more power over the federal government. The Canadian Senate is supposed to be a forum for regional concerns, but it currently has only limited authority because its members are appointed instead of elected. The appointments are made by the prime minister.

Some Canadians have reservations about a more powerful Senate. "An elected Senate will bring substantive change to Canada's parliamentary system, weaken central government, diminish the powers of the House of Commons and make the political process more derivative of American practice and less influenced by the British experiences," said Dalton Camp, a former senior aide to Mr. Mulroney.

Quebec has refused to participate in negotiations on Senate reform or any other constitutional issue until its demands on the accord are met. The Quebec government says the Meech Lake Accord will satisfy its "minimum" demands.

The other provinces agreed several years ago to give priority to Quebec's concerns because major constitutional changes were made in 1982 without the consent of the Quebec government. Government leaders had also pledged to address the province's constitutional demands after Quebeckers rejected separation from Canada in a 1980 provincial referendum.

Several provincial leaders said they recognized that Quebec Premier Robert Bourassa had little room for maneuver in the negotiations because he is faced with an opposition party, the Parti Quebecois, which advocates Quebec independence.

Parti Quebecois leader Jacques Parizeau attacked Mr. Bourassa earlier this week for allegedly making concessions to the other provinces, and he demanded that Mr. Bourassa abandon the negotiations.
