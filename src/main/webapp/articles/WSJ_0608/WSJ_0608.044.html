International: U.S., Allies Ease Technology Curbs On Eastern Sales

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/08/90

PARIS -- In a further sign of warming East-West relations, the U.S. and its 16 main Western allies agreed to ease their 41-year-old embargo on sales of high-technology products to the East bloc. The agreement, reached at a two-day meeting of allied officials here, authorizes a major liberalization of technology-trade restrictions to the East. All export curbs will end for most Western personal computers and other commonly available technology products. More important, the allies agreed to greatly reduce restrictions on sales of mainframe computers, telephone exchanges, machine tools and other more-sophisticated products.

The accord, based on a Bush administration proposal announced May 2, opens the door to East-West trade in a wide range of technology products. As one indication of the accord's importance, U.S. officials said the products involved currently make up about $45 billion, or 30%, of annual U.S. technology exports around the world. The East hasn't enough hard currency to place orders anywhere near that volume. But Western businesses expect the accord to allow a gradual rise in direct sales to the East, and an immediate surge in East-West technology joint ventures.

Many Western businesses cheered the accord, even though some had urged even greater liberalization. "We are very pleased," said a spokesman in Armonk, N.Y., for International Business Machines Corp. "This represents substantial progress." An official of the West German Industry Federation called the accord "a step forward" in East-West trade.

The pact also was attacked by some U.S. conservatives. This "ill-advised" accord will "enable the Soviets for far less {effort} to field far more capable military forces in the future," said Frank Gaffney Jr., a former Pentagon official who heads the Center for Security Policy, a conservative Washington think tank.

Before sales can begin, the agreement has to be implemented by laws or rules in each of the 17 countries participating in the trade organization, called the Coordinating Committee on Multilateral Export Controls, or Cocom. The 17 countries are the U.S., Japan, Australia, and all of the West European countries except Iceland that belong to the North Atlantic Treaty Organization.

Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher, who helped craft the U.S. proposal to Cocom, said the U.S. strategy of "differentiation" prevailed over West European plans to treat the Soviet Union and the East European countries essentially the same way. Cocom's decision to reward the more-democratic Eastern European countries with higher technology could induce the Soviets to "move towards the same direction," he said.

Mr. Mosbacher said he expects both the former East bloc nations and U.S. companies to take advantage of the opportunities being opened. "American companies are going to leap into Eastern Europe," he predicted. And their presence could help to "jump-start" those nations' economies.

The success of the Paris meeting proves that Cocom is "very much alive," he said. Cocom will have to closely watch how the special provisions for East Germany work, but after the two Germanys unite they will be "treated like any other Cocom member," he said.
