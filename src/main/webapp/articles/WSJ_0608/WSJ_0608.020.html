Letters to the Editor: New Civil-Rights Bill Won't Mandate Quotas

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/08/90

In "The 1990 Civil Rights Bill Is Wrong" (editorial page, May 4) Abigail Thernstrom misrepresents the intent and the effect of the Civil Rights Act of 1990. This bill corrects a series of Supreme Court decisions made in June 1989 that narrowly interpret equal-employment opportunity and civil-rights laws. It does not mandate or "invite" quotas, as Ms. Thernstrom asserts. For 18 years, employers had the burden of showing that employment practices with a "disparate impact" (that operated to exclude minorities and women disproportionately) are required by business necessity. The Supreme Court's June 1989 decision in Wards Cove Packing Co. v. Atonio razed this settled law and placed an extreme and unprecedented burden on discrimination victims by making them prove specifically the practice that ultimately led to discrimination. The Civil Rights Act of 1990 sends the message that employment discrimination is never acceptable and returns the burden of justifying any business practice that results in disparate impact to the employer.

Furthermore, on May 2 the bill was amended so that employers can prove business necessity by showing that a challenged practice "bears a substantial and demonstrable relationship to effective job performance." In other words, employment decisions made to create an effective work force are justifiable under the Civil Rights Act of 1990. Thus, the law allows employers to hire the best-qualified people and does not place us on the crest of the slippery slope of hiring and promotion quotas as envisioned by Ms. Thernstrom.

Ms. Thernstrom says the Civil Rights Act of 1990 may "deliver a message that minority groups amount to separate nations and separate nations require distinct treatment." To the contrary, the act seeks to prevent dangerous separate treatment, which is in part what triggered Wards Cove. In this case, Frank Atonio and other salmon-cannery workers, mostly Filipinos and Alaska Natives, sued their employer for hiring practices that excluded them from the better-paying jobs and favored whites. For instance, the Wards Cove Packing Co. maintained racially separate hiring pools and job categories. The company's dormitories and cafeterias generally separated white employees from nonwhite employees. "Separate" and "distinct" treatment is precisely what the act seeks to eliminate by providing discrimination victims a reasonable chance of proving discrimination.

The Civil Rights Act of 1990 also addresses other intolerable inconsistencies created by the Supreme Court's June 1989 decisions. The law ensures that job bias based on gender and race is always illegal, that statutes of limitation are applied fairly and consistently, and that victims of intentional discrimination can recover damages.

If passed, the Civil Rights Act of 1990 will enable this country to continue to eradicate workplace discrimination by restoring the well-tested legal standards used to prove employment discrimination for the past 25 years.

Antonia Hernandez

President and General Counsel

Mexican American Legal Defense and Educational Fund

Los Angeles
