Letters to the Editor: Oil Tankers Need Double Hulls

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/08/90

As the author of language in the House oil-spill bill to require double hulls on all tankers operating in U.S. waters within 15 years, I take great exception to your May 7 editorial "Double Whammy." Your use of phrases such as "dubious" technology, "popular quick fix" and "an expensive way for Congress to show it is doing something" is misleading and incorrect.

You ignore the large and ever-growing body of evidence that a double hull is the most effective available technology to prevent many spills and reduce the damage from others. The Coast Guard says that of five recent major spills, four, including the California spill caused by the vessel's own anchor, would have been prevented if the vessels had been constructed with double hulls; it also said the spill from the Exxon Valdez accident would have been reduced 60% with a double hull.

Calculated over the lifetime of a tanker, the added cost of double-hull construction is one-tenth of one cent per gallon.

If, in fact, the value of double hulls is still in doubt, why do we currently require them on ships carrying liquified natural gas and other hazardous materials?

The House supported my double-hull requirement 376-37; a similar requirement in the Senate fell two votes short.

We have a window of opportunity now that we will not have again for more than two decades. By 1992, more than 40% of the current world fleet will be more than 20 years old, and another 25% will be in the 15-to-19-year-old range. In the Alaskan fleet, the average age is 18 years.

We will lose a valuable opportunity to increase safety at a reasonable cost if we wait for additional studies that are likely to confirm what we already know -- that double hulls prevent accidents.

Rep. Dean A. Gallo (R., N.J.)

Washington

---

You stated that tanker double bottoms may not prevent every oil spill, yet for many years hazardous cargoes have been required to be carried in double-bottom or double-hull ships. In fact, with few exceptions, all ships carrying products other than petroleum have double bottoms. Also, there is little evidence to suggest that ship-stability problems are aggravated by double hulls or double bottoms.

The Exxon Valdez disaster was an extraordinary occurrence and, as you point out, oil would still have been spilled even if the ship had been fitted with a double bottom/double hull. However, if the ship had been designed and built with a double hull or bottom, the oil spilled would have been reduced in quantity and flow, thereby aiding in the cleanup. The reality is that normal collisions and groundings of single-hull tankers will almost always result in the release of oil, and the reverse is true for double-hull tankers.

Most of us in the marine industry are just as concerned with maintaining the quality of the oceans as the environmentalists your editorial discredits. Within our industry, there is little argument about the superiority of double-hull construction as a deterrent to oil pollution.

The combination of increasing prices, tightening of some world-wide capacity, and an international agreement eradicating subsidy practices leaves U.S. shipyards in an enviable position since American yards have available capacity, a stable labor situation, and long-term competitive pricing to pursue commercial market opportunities. This has led U.S. yards and naval architects to design double-hull tankers that will cost less to build and be cheaper and safer to operate.

John J. Stocker

President

Shipbuilders Council of America

Washington
