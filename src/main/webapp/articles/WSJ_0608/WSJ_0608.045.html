U.S. Forest Service Plans a Cutback In Logging Allowed

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 06/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. Forest Service plans a significant cutback in logging in national forests over the next five years while putting greater emphasis on recreation and wildlife protection. The annual harvest of timber in national forests averaged 12.2 billion board feet from 1983 through 1988, but it will fall to 10.8 billion board feet by 1995 under the Forest Service's new master plan for managing its vast domain of natural resources. The annual harvest rate won't be restored to the 12 billion board feet range until the year 2040, despite a projected 79% increase in demand for hardwoods and a 35% increase for softwoods by then.

The agency also plans to permit less livestock grazing on national forest land, an important source of pasture for the nation's cattle and sheep industries.

"We are looking at multiple-use management from a new perspective," said Agriculture Secretary Clayton Yeutter. "Where timber and mineral production and livestock grazing cannot be accomplished in an environmentally acceptable manner, production levels will be reduced."

Representatives of the timber industry warned that the plan will only aggravate tight timber supplies, particularly in the Pacific Northwest. Luke Popovich, spokesman for the American Forest Council, dismissed the Forest Service's projections as "essentially useless" because they ignore such critical factors as the anticipated listing of the rare northern spotted owl as a threatened species. The industry fears the listing will trigger logging bans across large sections of northern California, Oregon and Washington. The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service is due to rule on the owl's status by June 23.

The Forest Service's five-year plan calls for greater environmental sensitivity and a better balance among multiple uses of public lands. Among other things, the agency intends to permit 49% less clear-cutting of national forests by 1995.

Clear-cutting, unlike selective cutting of trees, is regarded as environmentally harmful because it causes soil erosion and destroys wildlife habitat. The plan also strengthens the Forest Service's commitment to protecting "special places" and expanding wilderness areas.

Under the new plan, the Forest Service projects its annual costs will hit $3 billion by 1995. Last year, its programs cost $2.3 billion. Jobs created by Forest Service activities, including logging, are expected to total 623,000 by 1995, up 49,000 from 1989.
