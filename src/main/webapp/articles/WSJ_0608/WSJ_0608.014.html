U.S. Banks Slip Further Behind Rivals --- Curbs on Activities Hamper Efforts to Stay Competitive

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 06/08/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- The last time the International Monetary Conference held its annual meeting here, in 1976, 56 U.S. banks were members. Now, 35 are. In his opening remarks this week to top executives from the industrial world's leading banks, Rainer Gut, chairman of Credit Suisse, made a second telling -- and, to Americans, disturbing -- observation: In 1976, seven of those banks hailed from California; but now, "three of those are owned by the Japanese and one has withdrawn from international business."

Douglas Kidd, a senior vice president at Bankers Trust Co. in New York, said that "a major part of our growth over the past five years has been overseas" because of constraints on what the bank can do in the U.S. "You have our taxes moving offshore and our employees moving offshore," he said.

Nobody in the room needed to be reminded that U.S. banks aren't as competitive as they used to be. In the past few years, many have retreated from international business, or sold operations to meet new international capital requirements, and watched foreign banks gobble up an ever-increasing chunk of the U.S. market.

This trend threatens to damage the competitiveness of U.S. industry -- especially the export sector -- and erodes revenue in industries that serve banks, such as computer companies, according to analysts and bankers. It also means fewer jobs.

Moreover, the growing foreign presence in the U.S. raises concerns about the concentration of economic and financial power, especially in terms of doling out credit to U.S. industries and companies that compete with foreign ones.

U.S. offices of foreign banks now control roughly a quarter of U.S. banking assets -- Japanese owners alone account for 14% -- compared with 14% foreign control in 1982 and 3.8% in 1973. The consolidated U.S. assets of six Japanese banks would rank them among the 30 largest U.S. bank holding companies, and "a couple would be within striking distance of the 10th largest banking organization in the U.S.," according to a study by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York.

At the same time, U.S. banks are shedding businesses either to help stay afloat or to meet international capital standards planned for 1992. In the past few years, Chase Manhattan Bank sold a Dutch bank with $5 billion of assets to France's Credit Lyonnais, and BankAmerica Corp. sold its Italian bank to Germany's Deutsche Bank AG. Just last week, Bank of Tokyo Ltd. closed its $500 million purchase of Bank of New England Corp.'s leasing unit.

Noting that U.S. banks are unloading operations in Asia as well as in Europe, Alexis Wolkenstein, head of the international division of Credit Lyonnais said, "I don't understand the American banks; they are giving up markets and relationships that took years to build." He added: "This is a loss to the U.S.; it gives it less influence in the world."

At home, U.S. banks suffer from excess capacity -- there are simply too many banks -- as well as from geographic limits on where they can take deposits and restrictions on what products they can sell. U.S. banks also have shot themselves in the foot, lending billions of dollars to developing countries that may never be able to repay them and lending too much to the real estate and oil industries in the U.S.

John Reed, chairman of Citicorp, said the company wanted the right to take deposits anywhere in the U.S., in addition to securities powers and possibly the right to underwrite and sell insurance. "Clearly, the geography thing killed us, and the degree to which industrial companies are in our business," Mr. Reed said. "It takes a lot of banking business away from us."

Charles S. Sanford Jr., chairman of Bankers Trust, said, "Not having securities powers makes U.S. banks less competitive with European banks, and this will be exacerbated when Japanese banks get more powers."

Meanwhile, 15 years of unsteady economic growth and volatility in the inflation rate in the U.S., plus the nation's deteriorating trade position and its low savings rate "have all contributed to a financial environment that breeds difficulties at home and contributes to slippage abroad," E. Gerald Corrigan, president of the New York Fed, said in recent congressional testimony.

In the Far East, U.S. banks face rich, aggressive Japanese banks; in Europe, powerful banking institutions are allowed to enter businesses in their home markets -- such as investment banking and insurance -- that are denied to U.S. banks in their own markets. Europe's big banks also stand to gain from the planned 1992 abolition of trade and financial barriers among the 12 countries of the European Community.

The turmoil in the Japanese stock and bond markets this year resulted in lower earnings for Japanese banks and chewed up some capital represented by stock they hold in Japanese companies. But Kaneo Nakamura, president of Industrial Bank of Japan Ltd., and Teruyoshi Yasufuku, deputy president of Sanwa Bank Ltd., both said that talk of a pullback by Japan's big banks was exaggerated. "The markets {in Japan} have improved, and the {Japanese} economy is strong," Mr. Yasufuku said.

"It will still be several years until Japanese banks' advantages in cost of equity and borrowed funds dissipates," said Perry Quick, a principal at Quick, Finan & Associates, an economic consulting firm in Washington.

And while U.S. banks still are regarded as leaders in financial innovation and sophistication, "our lead in technology is narrowing," he said. "Japan is developing its own financial technology or buying it."

U.S. banks are also considered to be more profitable than many of their foreign competitors. Nonetheless, Mr. Reed of Citicorp said that neither the West German nor the Japanese stock markets penalize their banks for their low profitability. By contrast, the shares of many U.S. banks have been severely depressed.

The West German banking system, said Hans-Georg Engel, resident counsel at J.P. Morgan & Co.'s Frankfurt branch, is one of the world's strongest. Because its big universal banks can bank anywhere in the country, and can trade stocks and bonds and sell insurance, "they are less exposed to cyclical shocks," he said. "The universal banks are well-capitalized, have good asset quality, weren't hurt by the stock market crash in 1987 and have absorbed their LDC {less developed country, or Third World lending} problems."

In his testimony, Mr. Corrigan of the New York Fed said that the U.S. is "the only major country that does not have a true national banking system." Even after the planned dismantling of some barriers to interstate banking, he said, the U.S. will still be left with a "crazy-quilt pattern" of state and federal laws and regulations.

Structurally, the U.S. banking system is increasingly viewed as obsolete, especially when rapid technological change is creating instruments that blur traditional product lines. Moreover, many corporations whose credit ratings are higher than those of banks now can raise money through bond and stock markets, bypassing U.S. banks altogether.

"Financial services have become commodities available from a number of differing bank and non-bank sources, and distinctions between products have faded," said Cynthia Glassman, director of research for Furash & Co., a consulting firm in Washington.

Noting that a European corporation can have all its financial needs met by a single institution, she said: "A U.S. company has to go different places for a loan, underwriting and insurance. The difference is like buying a car and buying the parts separately to build the car."

Mr. Engel of Morgan said: "If a Volkswagen, a Lufthansa or a BASF asks you to do for it in America what you do for it in Europe, you're forced to say you can't, even though it's your home market, your currency and the world's major currency. That has a tremendous impact on the competitiveness of U.S. banks."

As Mr. Corrigan himself testified last month: "The U.S. banking system is simply out of step with the rest of the world, and more importantly, it is out of step with the realities of the marketplace."
