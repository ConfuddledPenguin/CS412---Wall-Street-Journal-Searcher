Piper Jaffray Sees Slightly Higher Profit For Year, Citing Small-Investor Activity

  • Author: Robert Johnson
  • Date: 06/08/90

Despite declining revenue from bond funds, Piper Jaffray Inc. expects small investors' increasing enthusiasm for stocks to slightly boost its earnings for the fiscal year ending Sept. 30, Charles Hayssen, chief financial officer, said. "The days are over for {bond} funds. People have had enough of them," Mr. Hayssen said in an interview. Yields have fallen on many such funds since the October 1987 stock market crash. Revenue from a new bond fund helped boost net income of Piper's regional brokerage concern, Piper, Jaffray & Hopwood Inc., last year to $11.7 million, or $2.92 a share, on revenue of $195.2 million.

Piper, based in Minneapolis, is typical of regional brokers who specialize in analyzing industries in their area for individual investors. Piper's expertise is largely in food, agricultural and medical technology concerns.

The financial performance of such regional firms is viewed by some analysts as a gauge of small investors' interest in the market. Regional brokers generally haven't been hit as hard by cutbacks on Wall Street as have their national full-service counterparts, some of whose revenue from investment banking is off by 20% or more.

At Piper, brokers' commissions on sales of mutual funds over the past seven months are running 50% ahead of the like period in the previous fiscal year, according to Mr. Hayssen. Commissions on listed stocks rose 20% in the same period, he said, which indicates to him that "the small investor is showing some courage."

The turnover rate in the ranks of Piper brokers is about 10% this year, compared with 18% a year ago. The industrywide broker turnover rate a year ago was about 20%. Piper currently has about 750 retail brokers, compared with 675 just before the 1987 market crash.

Piper has helped its earnings in the past two fiscal years by starting bond funds in its fourth quarter that sold well. Last year, 35% of Piper's earnings came on sales of such a fund. "That isn't likely this year," Mr. Hayssen said.

But, he insisted: "Small investors are listening to brokers' stories about stocks again. They're starting to get interested. The fear from the crash of October 1987 is disappearing."

He declined to forecast net for the fiscal third quarter, ending June 30. But he said revenue in the first two months of the quarter was 15% higher than a year earlier. In last year's third quarter, Piper had net of $3.3 million, or 83 cents a share, on revenue of $53 million.

Mr. Hayssen said it's too early to make a specific forecast for full-year earnings, although analysts expect them to be flat. He disclosed that planned initial offerings and additional offerings of stock by certain of Piper's Midwestern clients could cause earnings in the fourth quarter to surge from last year's $4.1 million, or $1.03 a share, on revenue of $57.4 million. But he said such offerings might also be delayed until the next fiscal year.

For that reason, a specific forecast isn't possible, and earnings for the year "may be about the same, plus or minus a little," he said.
