TRW `Space Tug' Canceled by NASA To Preserve Funds

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 06/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The National Aeronautics and Space Administration said it canceled TRW Inc.'s $736 million project to build a "space tug" that could boost satellites into higher orbits. The cancellation reflects the severe budget pressures facing NASA, which recently scaled back the project in hopes of saving it. The Bush administration has asked Congress to approve a 24% increase in the NASA budget, to $15.13 billion, in the coming fiscal year, but lawmakers are expected to vote a substantially smaller increase. A NASA spokesman said the agency wanted to preserve funds to renovate the space shuttle and its main engines.

The space tug -- officially known as the Orbital Maneuvering Vehicle -- looked more like a flying saucer than a tugboat. It was an unmanned craft 15 feet in diameter that would have been carried into orbit on the space shuttle for the first time in January 1995. The tug's main purpose was to boost the Hubble Space Telescope and a proposed X-ray telescope into higher orbits to increase their orbital life. The space shuttle also can boost the telescopes, but not as high as the tug could have.

NASA had grander designs for the space tug when it started work on the project in 1986. The craft was supposed to help build NASA's proposed space station, pull useless satellites out of orbit and go into orbit around the Earth itself to await new orders. But the project quickly ran into trouble.

Harold Coldwater, a space-tug manager at NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center in Huntsville, Ala., said the project had a cost overrun of about $110 million. The project fell 18 months behind schedule and, overall, was running at about twice its original cost estimate, NASA said.

Last fall, the space agency tried to save the project by cutting back the tug's capabilities. By choosing cheaper rockets and less sophisticated navigation, the agency reduced the tug's ability to maneuver in space and to boost satellites. It also gave up the ability to base the tug at the space station -- which had fallen well behind schedule too -- or have it orbit independently.

But the reprogramming left the tug with too little to do, and NASA decided it was better off spending its money elsewhere. William Lenoir, NASA's associate administrator for space flight, said he figures the space agency will eventually need a tug-like capability but that NASA has at least two or three years to decide exactly what kind of craft will be necessary.

NASA estimated it has spent $200 million so far on the tug, which was still in the design stage. However, canceling the project won't help NASA immediately with its budget problems. The space agency had asked Congress for $85 million for the program in the coming fiscal year -- just about the amount it said it needs to close down the project.
