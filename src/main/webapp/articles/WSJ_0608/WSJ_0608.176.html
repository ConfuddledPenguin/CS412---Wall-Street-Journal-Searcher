Poland Gets Approval For $250 Million Loan From the World Bank

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The World Bank approved a $250 million loan to Poland to increase natural-gas production and reduce the country's dependence on coal. The bank said its loan will help finance rehabilitation of existing gas fields and expansion of gas-transmission and storage facilities. The European Investment Bank also will provide Poland with a $60 million credit for the same projects, World Bank officials said.

"As Poland moves toward a market economy, demand for gas and oil is expected to increase, while demand for coal, which produces large quantities of air pollution, is expected to decrease," the World Bank said.

The Energy Sector Management Assistance Program, jointly financed by the World Bank and the United Nations Development Program, will provide Poland with technical assistance on pricing gas and setting up a contractual framework to expand the country's petroleum industry through foreign and domestic private-sector enterprises.

In other actions, the World Bank cleared a $190 million loan to Indonesia to improve public-water-supply facilities in the Jakarta region, while the International Development Association, a bank affiliate, approved a $175 million credit to Bangladesh to strengthen the country's banking and financial system.

The bank also approved a $50 million loan to Papua New Guinea to support economic reforms and help maintain financial stability in the wake of the closing of the country's main gold and copper mine.

In addition, the bank cleared a $32 million loan to Algeria and a $17 million loan to Tunisia to help finance agricultural research.

The World Bank loans will be at variable interest rates, currently 7.75% a year; the IDA credit to Bangladesh will be interest-free but will involve administrative fees.
