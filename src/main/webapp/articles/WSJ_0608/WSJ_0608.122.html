World Markets: Tokyo Stock Index Rises 239 Points to 33192.50 As a Broad Range of Issues Post Moderate Gains

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/08/90

Tokyo stock prices finished higher with a broad range of issues adding moderate gains on active buying by corporations, individuals and dealers who traded for their own accounts, analysts said. Meanwhile, stocks in London steadily lost ground in late afternoon trading, with the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index ending at the midway point of its intraday range.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues gained 239 points to 33192.50, finishing above 33000 points for the first time in five trading days. The index gained 31.88 points Wednesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Friday, the Nikkei index rose 54.09 points to 33246.59.

With help from some arbitrage buying, the Nikkei Index moved at higher levels Thursday afternoon, before closing just below its intraday high, traders said.

Because Thursday was the last trading day for the June stock futures and options, fears of a possible heavy sell-off linked to the unwinding of the futures contract had discouraged buying until yesterday.

But growing confidence that the market can absorb such futures-related selling prompted investors to buy stocks without waiting for the special quotation today, when settlement prices for the June futures and options are determined, said Hiroyuki Murai, general manager of stock trading at Nikko Securities.

"That's because investors are expecting gains when uncertainty {about the impact of unwinding of the futures} is cleared," the analyst said.

Volume on the first section was estimated at 650 million shares, up from 599.6 million shares Wednesday. Advancers outnumbered decliners 567 to 372, while 185 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which shed 1.86 points Wednesday, was up 5.37 points, or 0.22%, at 2428.44.

The second section index advanced for the eighth consecutive trading day, gaining 17.75 points, or 0.44%, to 4057.12. Volume was estimated at 24 million shares, down from 30.6 million shares Wednesday.

"Fears of a vast sell-off were wiped out," said Masashi Okuma, a senior trader at UBS Phillips & Drew International, as arbitragers opted to roll over their positions held against the June futures contract to the September contract.

Analysts said the prevailing view on the market is that stock prices will advance strongly today and in the first half of next week if domestic interest rates and exchange rates remain at current levels.

One analyst said not many investors are willing to buy any issue steadily, pushing its price higher. But investors place buy orders whenever stock prices fall, gradually pushing up the bottom of the Nikkei Index's range.

Buying interest was spread over a wide range of sectors. Volume failed to grow in the afternoon session, as large-capital shares, which were the focus of morning trade were replaced by small-capital shares, traders said.

On the one hand, large-capital shares led by shipbuilding stocks gained, partly supported by major brokerage houses' efforts, traders said. Brokers were trying to make up profit declines caused by lowered commission fees this month by increasing trade volume, observers said.

Buying interest, including that from institutions, continued to stream toward small-capital shares with bright earnings outlooks. Such interest wasn't limited to the Tokyo Stock Exchange first and second sections, but was consistent on over-the-counter trading, as well as on the Osaka and Nagoya stock exchanges.

Other issues that gained were laggards in sectors such as mining, cement, machinery and automobiles.

Winners included construction companies such as Kajima, which gained 20 yen to 1980 yen (13 cents to $12.98), and Kumagai Gumi, which added 50 yen to 1250 yen.

Some car makers ended firmer, with Isuzu gaining 20 yen to 1110 yen, and Honda up 20 yen to 1810 yen.

In London, traders said that the market was being pulled in two directions, with London continuing to look undervalued relative to other leading exchanges but being dictated to by Wall Street's fortunes.

"There's a lot of nervousness about Wall Street because it's all over the place," said one market maker, referring to Wall Street's recent erratic trading pattern.

If Wall Street holds steady, London could easily breach upside resistance levels, traders noted. With the recent phenomenon of trade houses' running short positions largely ended, the market is in a healthier position to scale new heights, the traders added.

A gain would be aided by an actual takeover bid. Current takeover rumors are helping to shore up prices, but an actual bid would swiftly add 30 points to the FTSE 100 index, the traders reckon.

The FT-SE 100 Share Index ended up 19.9 points yesterday to 2378.4. The index initially fell, but the day's reversal was largely attributed to bargain hunting by institutions with considerable cash.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index rose 23 points to 1904. Volume was 592.5 million shares, compared with 429 million issues Wednesday.

In the futures markets, the June FT-SE 100 index contract ended at 2425, up from 2399 Wednesday.

Banks issues suffered yesterday, running out of steam after a recent run-up on speculation of a takeover. Lloyds fell 6 pence to 291 pence (10 cents to $4.91), while Midland shed 42 pence to 354 pence.

Unilever's 7 pence loss to 698 pence was ascribed to U.S.-led selling. Burton dropped 10 pence to 164 pence, as the retail sector generally fell.

Boots, which reported a rise in pretax profit for the year to March 31 to #358 million from #306.7 million, eased after an initial rally. The stock shed 2 pence to 296 pence. Traders said the results were discounted in the share price, which rose earlier in the week. In addition, the company's much-vaunted new heart drug won't likely yield profits for several more years, traders said.

Meanwhile, stocks closed higher in Milan and Brussels, lower in Stockholm, Frankfurt, Zurich and Madrid, and mixed in Paris and Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed sharply lower.

Elsewhere, shares ended higher in Manila and Singapore, and lower in Sydney, Taipei, Hong Kong, Seoul and Wellington.
