Washington Wire: A Special Weekly Report From The Wall Street Journal's Capital Bureau

  • Author: Rich Jaroslovsky
  • Date: 06/08/90

WHITE HOUSE WORRY: Will Greenspan cut rates to support a budget deal? The Fed chairman's tight-fisted policies have administration officials on edge. They believe last month's weak employment growth should have prodded the Fed to ease; they argue that with the economy already growing at less than 2% a year, a stingy Fed could mistakenly tip that into negative numbers.

But the big issue, Bush aides say, is whether the Fed will provide a major cut in short-term rates if there is a budget accord with Congress. The administration thinks a deal should be worth at least 1 1/2 percentage points. But Greenspan indicates the Fed would pursue a more cautious path.

"The odds are high they will move when the time comes," says an administration aide. "The question is whether it will be far enough or fast enough."

MANDELA AIMS to use his U.S. trip to keep the heat on South Africa.

Despite President de Klerk's partial lifting of the state of emergency, the African National Congress leader will use his June 26 speech to a joint session of Congress to rebut arguments from U.S. conservatives that sanctions should be eased. He will stress the importance of isolating South Africa until apartheid truly is dead.

The rest of his trip will be used to generate U.S. political support and raise money. In addition to a New York ticker-tape parade, he plans appearances in Boston, Atlanta, Detroit and Oakland, as well as a Miami address to AFSCME to thank organized labor for its anti-apartheid stance. A major event in Los Angeles will seek financial support from the entertainment industry.

SUMMIT DEALS raise political hackles in both the Soviet Union and U.S.

Soviet press commentaries increasingly complain that while the Soviets are giving up their military edge on land, Bush refuses to negotiate cuts in navies, a traditional area of American strength. Shevardnadze warns Baker that the newly independent Supreme Soviet might balk at ratifying pacts it sees as unequal.

In the U.S., meanwhile, conservatives criticize the trade treaty and other economic moves as sops to Gorbachev just when he is losing power to Yeltsin and others. "Bush is about six months behind in understanding how far along the disintegration of the Soviet Union is," says Heritage Foundation official Kim Holmes.

Conservatives complain the chemical weapons treaty isn't verifiable.

DEMOCRATS CHEER the weak showing of Montana GOP Senate candidate Kolstad in Tuesday's primary; they now think Sen. Baucus could have an easier time than anticipated. The Republicans, meanwhile, say North Carolina Sen. Helms will have no problem painting Democratic nominee Gantt, who is black, as ultraliberal. But Democratic strategists think Gantt will be able to raise a lot of money for his uphill battle.

SPRING SLUMP? The high-powered Democratic political consulting firm of Doak, Shrum & Associates isn't off to a good start this year. Their candidates have already lost high-stakes gubernatorial primaries in Texas, California and Iowa, and trail in Georgia and Florida. Says Bob Shrum: "Unlike some people in this business, we do difficult campaigns."

NERVOUS REGULATORS await Tuesday's first-quarter banking report. Delinquencies on consumer loans have been creeping up in recent quarters; the number typically drops off in the first quarter, but that might not happen this time, which would indicate consumers are living beyond their means and that banks ought to worry.

INFRA REDS: Gorbachev had hardly left town when salesmen for the Soviet space agency arrived to pitch their Almaz radar satellite, which can take pictures through cloud cover and at night. The Soviets believe the technology -- originally developed to track U.S. aircraft carriers -- will draw customers ranging from oil companies to urban planners.

PRESSURE GROWS to allow more flights into already congested airports.

Prodded by upstart America West Airlines, the Senate Commerce Committee is working on a bill to loosen or eliminate limits on the number of flights into LaGuardia, JFK, O'Hare and Washington National. Landing rights are currently dominated by more-established carriers, leading newer competitors to complain they are shut out of lucrative markets.

Transportation Department officials worry that changing the current system would increase noise and overcrowding problems, and could raise safety questions as well. To defuse congressional pressure, though, they might fashion a compromise requiring airlines that don't use their slots enough to cede them to others.

Secretary Skinner says the issue is "something we wish we didn't have to decide."
