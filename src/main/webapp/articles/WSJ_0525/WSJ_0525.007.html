Pan-European Finance Services Market Years Away, Says U.K. Aide

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/25/90

LONDON -- Stanislas Yassukovich, chairman of the U.K. Securities Association, said yesterday that a Pan-European financial services market will likely remain years away, even though Europe is moving toward a single market in 1993. Mr. Yassukovich, a senior advisor to the U.S. brokerage and investment banking house Merrill Lynch, and the former chairman of its European and Middle East operations, told a European Monetary Union (EMU) seminar that "it will be quite some time before we have a truly Pan-European retail financial services market."

He noted that even with the ongoing deregulation of Europe's financial barriers, retail services will probably remain checked by fundamental factors, most significantly the extensive diversity of client demands throughout the continent.

Such diversity, he said, will likely continue to limit the ability of financial services concerns to introduce products that create economies of scale and profitability.

"I don't think we're looking at anything as optimistic as a quantum jump in Pan-European services (after single-market regulations are fully implemented)," he added.

The Merrill Lynch executive compared his company's 450 branch offices in the U.S. to the five it operates in West Germany. He noted that Merrill Lynch is unable to offer clients in Hamburg the same products it offers in Munich, even though clients in both German cities "have the same economic profile."

"In the U.S. we are able to apply economies of scale (to the range of products offered)," he said.

Mr. Yassukovich stressed that this condition applies in Europe strictly to retail markets, while he said that wholesale financial services have operated on a Pan-European basis for decades. He doesn't anticipate any significant changes in the wholesale market after 1992.

Regarding Britain's entry into the exchange rate mechanism of the European Monetary System, Mr. Yassukovich said that he didn't see the timing of the entry, or the government's commitment to the system, as of significance to London as a financial center.
