Senate Rejects Death Penalty Defense Built Around Racial Discrimination

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 05/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate rejected a provision that would have enabled defendants to fight the death penalty by presenting evidence that executions are imposed in a racially discriminatory manner in the jurisdiction where they are being sentenced. The defeat of the measure on a 58-38 vote removed a major obstacle to passage of the omnibus anti-crime bill, which includes provisions that would extend the death penalty to six additional federal crimes and would bring 23 long-dormant death statutes into conformity with Supreme Court procedural requirements.

Bush administration officials have said the president would veto legislation allowing a legal defense based on evidence of racially disproportionate imposition of capital punishment.

The bill's future still remains uncertain because it contains other provisions vehemently opposed by conservative senators and the president -- in particular, a three-year ban on nine types of semi-automatic "assault weapons."

Mr. Bush urged lawmakers to approve "a good, strong anti-crime bill" without the assault-weapon ban. The Senate didn't complete action on the crime bill yesterday but set it aside until early next month, when lawmakers return from their Memorial Day recess. The House is far behind in considering comparable legislation.

The Senate bill also includes a provision that would reverse a 1989 Supreme Court decision and prohibit executions of mentally retarded defendants in federal cases. By a 59-38 vote, senators rejected a Republican amendment that would have allowed execution of mentally retarded persons if they are shown "to understand the difference between right and wrong." In addition, negotiators from both parties compromised and said no one under the age of 17 could be executed for a federal crime.

The Senate also reversed a decision it made Wednesday and adopted more-stringent curbs on appeals by death-row inmates. Senators voted 52-46 to reconsider a previously approved Democratic version of the death-row appeals measure, and then they approved a Republican alternative on a voice vote.

Both versions were designed to limit so-called habeas corpus petitions, by which death row residents may challenge the constitutionality of their convictions or sentences -- typically in multiple, piecemeal federal appeals. Among other differences, the Republican version would establish a 60-day time limit for filing the federal petitions, while Democrats proposed a one-year time limit.

To the surprise of Bush administration lobbyists, the Republican version was altered in private negotiations to include language that would allow death-row inmates to file appeals based on certain new constitutional rights announced by the Supreme Court after their convictions. President Bush strongly opposes such language, which appears to overturn Supreme Court precedent restricting such appeals, and Chief Justice William Rehnquist also has made vehement attacks on it.

Proponents of the racial-disparity defense to execution, which was sponsored by Sen. Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.) reacted angrily in defeat, citing numerous studies purporting to show racism in application of capital punishment.

"In this country, if you're white and you kill a black person, it's almost certain that prosecutors won't seek the death penalty," said Richard Burr, a lawyer with the NAACP Legal Defense Fund. "But if you're black, and you commit precisely the same crime against a white person, you're far more likely to be condemned to die." Conservatives had warned that the Kennedy provision would eliminate capital punishment on both the state and federal levels because it would place a huge burden on prosecutors to disprove race was a factor in death sentences.
