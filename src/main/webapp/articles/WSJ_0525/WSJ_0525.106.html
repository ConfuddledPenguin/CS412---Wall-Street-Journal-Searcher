Thursday's Markets: Bonds Sag And Stock Lose Steam --- Lukewarm Demand For U.S. Notes Cited; Dollar Ends Mixed

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 05/25/90

NEW YORK -- U.S. Treasury bond prices fell following lukewarm demand for an $8.5 billion issue of government notes, which helped break a string of records in the Dow Jones Industrial Average. The industrial average closed 0.71 point lower at 2855.55 in moderately active trading. Broader market indexes also fell. Long-term Treasury bond prices fell three-eighths of a point, or $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount. The dollar was mixed against major foreign currencies.

Bond prices headed lower in the morning after the government said the economy grew only 1.3% in the first quarter, instead of 1.8% as expected.

News of a slower economy usually is bullish for bonds, because it may portend lower interest rates; bond prices move in the opposite direction of rates. But much of the slower-than-expected growth was attributed to a drop in inventories, which suggests businesses may step up production to replenish supplies. That action would strengthen the economy, and possibly boost interest rates.

However, bond prices recovered to move slightly higher until shortly after the 1 p.m. EDT auction of five-year, two-month notes. Rumors of weak demand for the notes drove prices lower for the rest of the day, and stocks followed.

One reason the Treasury note auction didn't go well was little participation by Japanese investors, who are finding attractive yields in their home market. The average yield on the notes auctioned yesterday was 8.54%. The yield rose immediately afterward, indicating that dealers had to cut prices to sell them.

Opposing influences are keeping the bond market from establishing a clear direction, said Hugh Johnson, chief investment officer at First Albany Corp., in Albany, N.Y. Because of mounting evidence during the past few weeks that the economy is weakening and inflation seems in check, bond prices have rallied as interest rates eased. But, he observed, there is a limit on how low rates can go and still be competitive with West German rates, which have remained high.

This ambivalence also is starting to affect the stock market, where "it's a real struggle between the bears and the bulls," Mr. Johnson said.

He expects both stocks and bonds to continue drifting aimlessly until a week from today, when the government report on employment growth and unemployment in May will give investors their next significant reading on economic trends.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose in active trading, with 155.1 million shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange. Declining issues outnumbered advancers, 791 to 725.

Bond prices fell. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 8.60%.

The dollar ended mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was at 1.6830 marks and 151.05 yen, compared with 1.6765 marks and 151.45 yen Wednesday.
