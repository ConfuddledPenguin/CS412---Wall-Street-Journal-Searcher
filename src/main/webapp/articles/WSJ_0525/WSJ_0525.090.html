Bill to Strengthen SEC Is Approved By a Senate Panel

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 05/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Banking Committee unanimously approved a bill that would give the Securities and Exchange Commission substantially broader fining power and allow the agency to shut down alleged securities-industry wrongdoers. The legislation also will give the SEC easier access to grand-jury investigations in securities-law cases, a power the SEC has sought since it found resources strapped by the insider-trading cases of the mid- to late-1980s.

The Senate version of the bill was amended prior to committee approval to set stricter guidelines for the civil fines, which would greatly expand the agency's enforcement powers. The House hasn't moved far on its version of the bill, which remains at the subcommittee level.

Under the new system, a first tier sets a maximum of $5,000 for each violation for individuals and $50,000 per violation for brokerage firms and other entities for a range of mostly inadvertent securities violations, such as late or incorrect filings. The next tier sets maximums of $50,000 for individuals and $250,000 for others if the violation involves "fraud, deceipt, manipulation" or disregard for law. The third level sets limits of $100,000 for individuals and $500,000 for others if the second-tier criterion holds and the violation "directly or indirectly resulted in substantial losses."

Currently, the SEC has the authority to impose fines for insider-trading violations.

The legislation approved by the panel allows the SEC to order a brokerage firm or other entity regulated by the agency to "cease and desist" from wrongdoing. That rule is narrower than the earlier proposed legislation, which would have allowed the SEC to take such unilateral action against a corporation as well. Currently, the SEC must ask the courts for a temporary injunction to stop such abuses.

Under the bill's new authority, the SEC would still be able to seek cease-and-desist authority over a corporate violator if the company is provided with notice and an opportunity for a hearing before the order is issued.

The bill was tightened to relieve concerns that the SEC would become too heavy-handed in its enforcement efforts. "We have tried to address those concerns by clarifying the scope of the bill in virtually every section," said Sen. Christopher Dodd (D., Conn.), who heads the securities subcommittee.

The bill also would allow courts to bar officers or directors from serving in those capacities if they are found to have violated anti-fraud laws. Earlier versions of the bill granted that power to the SEC, but corporate groups contended that the authority was too broad.
