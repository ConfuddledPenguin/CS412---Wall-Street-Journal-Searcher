House Panel Votes To Freeze Target Prices of Grains

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 05/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Agriculture Committee, bowing to budget pressure, discarded a proposal to give grain farmers an additional $6.5 billion in subsidies over the next five years. The lawmakers, led by Committee Chairman Kika de la Garza (D., Texas), continued to hold the line on farm spending, voting unanimously to freeze so-called target prices for wheat and feed grains at current levels from fiscal 1991 through 1995. The committee signaled a new era of frugalness last week as it voted to freeze target prices for cotton and rice and tentatively agreed to freeze grain target prices.

The committee also voted to establish a marketing-loan program for soybeans, sunflowers and other oilseed crops, but with the proviso that it would operate at no cost to the government.

Many farm-state lawmakers had wanted to tie target prices for commodities to farm production costs, but that would have added $6.5 billion above the baseline spending level. Instead, the committee adopted a plan that would freeze target prices for wheat at $4 a bushel, corn at $2.75, barley at $2.36 and oats at $1.45.

However, in years when the U.S. is expected to import more oats than it exports, the agriculture secretary would be authorized to allow farmers to harvest oats on land that otherwise would be taken out of production to prevent surpluses of other commodities. Lawmakers hope this provision will provide an incentive to reverse the prolonged decline in oat production.

Under the farm program, the government dispenses deficiency payments if market prices fall short of target prices.

After lengthy debate, the committee also adopted a plan to give farmers a free hand to plant a variety of crops, including experimental crops with industrial uses, on 25% of the land they customarily devote to cotton, rice, wheat, feed grains and oilseeds.

But to mollify soybean processors and the American Soybean Association, the lawmakers decided against allowing farmers to plant such subsidized crops as cotton and corn on oilseed acreage. The processors and the trade group fears a drop in soybean production.

But farmers who take advantage of this planting flexibility provision wouldn't be eligible for subsidy payments on that land.

Last week, the lawmakers tentatively agreed to a 20% flexibility provision.

Committee staffers said freezing target prices would have negligible impact on food prices.
