Chrysler Corp. Treasurer Is Said To Plan to Quit --- Zuckerman Tells Associates He Expects to Leave, But Timing Is Unclear

  • Author: Melinda Grenier Guiles
  • Date: 05/25/90

HIGHLAND PARK, Mich. -- Frederick W. Zuckerman, Chrysler Corp.'s treasurer and day-to-day link with the investment community, is preparing to quit the No. 3 U.S. auto maker. Persons familiar with his plans say that Mr. Zuckerman, 56 years old, has told them he expects to leave. But these persons add that Mr. Zuckerman hasn't specified where he's going or the timing of his move. One co-worker said his departure is probably more than a month away, however.

Mr. Zuckerman had "no comment" about any move when reached by telephone in his office yesterday. "We don't confirm or deny rumors like that," he said.

Last week, Michael N. Hammes, also a vice president, resigned as head of Chrysler's international operations to take the top position at Black & Decker Corp.'s core power-tools division. The departures of the two men aren't believed to be related, but it couldn't be determined why Mr. Zuckerman decided to leave.

Mr. Zuckerman joined Chrysler in 1979 during the darkest of the auto maker's dark days. He acted as Chrysler's liaison with the federal board that monitored Chryler's loan guarantees. He also served as a financial adviser during labor negotiations.

He is currently responsible for Chrysler's pension funds and for its relations with commercial banks. He recently oversaw the $1 billion bond offering related to Chrysler's sale-leaseback deal for its Auburn Hills, Mich., technology center.

The voluble native New Yorker is popular among investment bankers and the press because of his financial acumen and candor. That candor has, however, sometimes caused consternation among his superiors at Chrysler.

Just days after the stock market crash in 1987, he told the Dow Jones News Service he was cutting the Chrysler pension fund's stock and bond portfolio because "the dollar will go to hell. Therefore, interest rates will go to hell."

"You ain't seen nothing yet," he added. "I do not believe it is politically possible to do what the country has to do to reassure the world financial community that we will . . . run our fiscal affairs in a responsible manner."

Chrysler quickly put out a "clarification," which quoted Mr. Zuckerman as saying he "was giving my personal opinion of the overall economic situation, and trying to wake up the people in Washington. Obviously there are good buys in the market. I'm buying Chrysler {stock} today."

Another example came early last year, when Mr. Zuckerman told securities analysts in New York the company believed "earnings in each quarter of 1989 will be greater than those in each corresponding quarter of 1988," despite new incentives. The new-car market soured soon after, however, and Chrysler ended the year with its largest quarterly loss ever after a $577 million one-time charge for shrinking company operations.

Prior to joining Chrysler, Mr. Zuckerman spent 12 years in financial management at International Business Machines Corp. Prior to that, he spent almost eight years at Ford Motor Co.

While at Ford, he worked with a number of his current fellow Chrysler executives. Among them: Vice Chairman Gerald Greenwald, who once reported to Mr. Zuckerman as a financial planner when Mr. Zuckerman was controller of the Ford Division's parts and service operations.
