CMS Wins Round In Continuing Bid To Transfer Assets

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 05/25/90

A Michigan appellate court has ruled that state regulators don't have authority to prohibit CMS Energy Corp. from transferring $1.5 billion in assets out of its utility subsidiary to the parent corporation. The ruling is unlikely to be the last word in a running dispute between CMS and Michigan officials, both parties said yesterday. CMS wants to use the assets to expand its unregulated energy businesses and to buy back stock. State regulators say the assets should be used only to improve the financial footing of its regulated utility subsidiary, Consumers Power Co.

Conflicting rulings have sent CMS's stock on a roller coaster ride. It plunged $2.375, to $28.625 a share, after Wednesday's order by the Michigan Public Service Commission prohibiting the transfer. The stock eased yesterday morning, but after word of the appeals court decision reached the markets, CMS shares recovered to close at $29.125 each, up 50 cents, in heavy New York Stock Exchange trading.

CMS Energy officials said that because of the likelihood of further litigation, they have put on hold their plans to spend the $1.5 billion.

Consumers Power received $1.2 billion in interest-bearing notes and a $341 million equity stake last month from its partners in the Midland Cogeneration Venture. The cogeneration venture is a once-failed nuclear-power plant now converted to a gas-fired cogeneration facility.

The public service commission's order said the notes couldn't be transferred from Consumers Power to CMS Energy, but instead Consumers Power must use the funds, "in ways that will secure {Consumers Power's} financial health."

But a three-judge panel of the Michigan Court of Appeals in Lansing said the utility regulators didn't have the authority to set such limitations. State officials said they haven't decided on their next move, but still believe they have authority to restrict allocation of the funds.

Although the appeals-court ruling clearly is in CMS's favor, company officials said the prospects of further litigation mean shareholders won't soon gain the benefits of the $1.2 billion in notes.

"There's still some other shoes to fall," said John W. Clark, a CMS senior vice president. "There's still the issue of who's running this company. Lansing {where state regulators are} or Dearborn," where CMS's headquarters is located.

State regulators contend Consumers Power conceded oversight authority for the $1.5 billion in 1985 when the company was on the verge of bankruptcy after sinking $4.1 billion into the failed Midland nuclear-power venture. In return for a special rate increase to ease its financial problems, CMS agreed to allow the public service commission to control the use of any proceeds derived from the sale of the failed power plant's assets.

CMS officials contend they have fulfilled the state's requirements by getting Consumers Power back on sound financial footing and by retiring $1.9 billion in debt. Any further extension of state authority is meddling, CMS executives say.
