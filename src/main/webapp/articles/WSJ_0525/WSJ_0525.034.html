Thorn May Sell Light-Bulb Line To GTE Corp.

  • Author: Richard L. Hudson and John J. Keller
  • Date: 05/25/90

LONDON -- In a sudden change of corporate strategy, Britain's Thorn EMI PLC said it is discussing selling its big European light bulb and light fixture business to GTE Corp. of the U.S. Such a transaction, valued by London analysts at roughly 350 million pounds ($592.7 million), would expand Stamford, Conn.-based GTE's European lighting business and strengthen its standing in the increasingly fierce world lighting market. The Thorn business is the world's fifth biggest light-bulb maker, and claims to tie N.V. Philips of the Netherlands as the largest manufacture of fixtures. GTE ranks third in making light bulbs, but is a smaller player in fixtures.

For Thorn, the move to sell what the company had previously called a "core" business puzzled many London financial analysts. Since 1985, when plunging profits and a boardroom coup brought Chairman Colin Southgate to power, the company has been in a near-constant state of reorganization. It has shed a movie-making division and a semiconductor unit, and has been trying to sell its defense-electronics division. If lighting goes -- Thorn's original business at its 1928 founding -- two main divisions will remain: renting household appliances, and publishing and recording music.

A Thorn spokeswoman said the lighting-business talks were necessitated by harsher market conditions. In the past few years the world lighting industry has been consolidating, with bigger companies buying smaller ones or forming alliances. The Thorn business "wasn't of a sufficient size to compete on an independent basis," she said. Also, according to London-based analyst Ron Littleboy of Nomura Research Institute, the Thorn lighting business's operating profit slipped to about 36 million pounds in the year ended March 31, from 40 million pounds a year earlier. A Thorn spokesman declined to comment on Mr. Littleboy's estimate.

Neither Thorn nor GTE was willing to discuss the possible sales terms. "Where'd they get that number?" responded Dean Langford, president of GTE's Electrical Products Group, when asked about London analysts' estimates of a possible 350 million-pound price tag. "It's a bit premature to say what the price is going to be when we're just beginning negotiations," he added.

GTE and Thorn have already been partners for some time. The two companies have a joint Italian light-bulb venture. Mr. Langford said that combining with Thorn's business would make GTE a "strong number two" in the world-wide lighting business after Philips -- though it wasn't possible to confirm that assessment independently. Mr. Langford said the transaction would strengthen GTE in Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, Scandinavia and Australia.
