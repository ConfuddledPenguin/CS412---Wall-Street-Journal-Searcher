Politics & Policy: House Approves $4 Billion Spending Bill That Cuts Military Funds by $2 Billion

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 05/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The House approved a $4 billion fiscal-1990 spending bill that would cut more than $2 billion from military accounts to help finance increased domestic appropriations and President Bush's long-sought aid package for Panama and Nicaragua. Provisions to expedite distribution of the $720 million for the fledgling Central American governments were upheld 277-126, and the underlying measure was adopted by a 308-108 margin despite conservative objections to a litany of parochial projects added by influential lawmakers.

The bill was sent to the Senate, which struggled last night to overcome the final snarls delaying passage. But a fragile marriage of convenience appeared to survive between the Bush administration and the Democratic leadership eager to complete action before the Memorial Day recess.

With the Pentagon's backing, the House easily stripped out $238 million for a costly ammunition plant in Louisiana, but among domestic projects, the White House appeared to turn a deaf ear to repeated GOP assaults on Congress's appropriators. "We want the supplemental to go through," an administration official last night, "and we want it to go through today."

The result was a lopsided series of votes upholding a laundry list of provisions ranging from a $6 million Iowa wildlife refuge to a directive that the Department of Housing and Urban Development fund an assortment of previously appropriated community projects costing over $28 million.

The most closely fought battle came on the internal question of how the House would finance lawmakers' own election-year mail. The bill had proposed to permit the transfer of as much as $25 million in unobligated appropriations to meet an expected shortfall for the free-mail, or "franking," privilege. But after sharp debate, Republicans scored an upset over the Democratic leadership on a 208-161 roll call that allowed them to strip the provision.

About one-quarter of the Central American aid is to pay off back debts to international financial institutions. In Nicaragua -- the more desperate of the two countries -- a focus is retooling private enterprises. Of the $300 million slated for the new Chamorro regime, $128 million is to finance imports including oil and agriculture-related products, while medical and education funds total less than $30 million.

Among domestic accounts, more than $1.7 billion is provided to meet increased costs for food stamps and veterans programs. Southern and Western states hit by recent flooding would share in an estimated $216 million in new disaster assistance. Child-education and vaccine programs would receive increases, along with the Census, and as the bill has grown steadily, so have the consequent cuts from the Pentagon.

Final bargaining failed to resolve the question of how to pay for another $1.45 billion shortfall in the military's own personnel and hospital costs. Defense Secretary Dick Cheney balked at proposed adjustments taking $230 million from major weapons programs such as the B-2 bomber and the Strategic Defense Initiative.

The list of new transfers had been proposed by House Armed Services Committee Chairman Les Aspin (D., Wis.), but the Pentagon apparently was reluctant to make any further concessions on strategic programs before next week's summit talks with the Soviets. A new deadline for resolving the personnel problem was set for mid-June, but as the date is pushed backward, the potential disruptions only grow, making the Senate Armed Services Committee reluctant to release the overall appropriations bill without first addressing the problem.

In the atmosphere of cutbacks, the Pentagon portion of the supplemental-spending bill for the fiscal 1990, which ends Sept. 30, became a battleground for lawmakers. The fight over the Louisiana ammunition plant pitted Sen. Bennett Johnston (D., La.) against the ranking Republican on the House Rules Committee, Rep. James Quillen, whose Tennessee district is the home of a competing facility.

Report language inserted in House-Senate negotiations lifts prior restrictions on the expenditure of $20 million by the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency for high-definition television. Magnascreen Corp. of Pittsburgh, and Planar Systems Inc. of Beaverton, Ore., currently hold contracts with the agency, and two senior appropriators from those states, Rep. John Murtha (D., Pa.) and Sen. Mark Hatfield (R., Ore.), were described as influential in winning release of the funds.

For policy reasons -- and the support of Senate Appropriations Committee Chairman Robert Byrd (D., W.Va.) -- a written report accompanying the final bill seeks to block the Army from terminating work on 105mm tank ammunition being developed by Hercules Inc.
