Foreign Exchange: Yen Rises on Strong Japanese Economy; Mark Sags on Unification Nervousness

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/25/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar came full circle from its recent rally against the yen by finishing just above 151 yen yesterday on a renewed market appreciation of Japan's robust economy. The mark weakened against the dollar, though, its strength sapped by continued market nervousness about the costs of German unification.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6830 marks, up from 1.6765 marks late Wednesday, and at 151.05 yen, down from 151.45 yen late Wednesday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6895, down from $1.6940 late Wednesday.

At midmorning Friday in Tokyo, the dollar was trading at 150.75 yen, down from Thursday's Tokyo close of 151.43 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6825 marks.

The apparent recovery of Japanese stocks from their plunge early this year has removed a temporary factor that had turned participants' focus away from the underlying strength of the Japanese economy, said Sung Won Sohn, chief economist at Norwest Corp. in Minneapolis.

Release earlier in the day of a sharp downward revision of U.S. first-quarter gross national product to an annual growth rate of 1.3% didn't have much effect on trading.

Traders said the fall from the originally announced 2.1% annual rate was due to an unusually large fall in business inventories that in itself doesn't change the outlook for continued modest growth in the U.S. economy. Expectations were for a downward revision to 1.8% annual growth.

The recent rally by the dollar against the yen, which began May 16, was fueled mainly by a belief that inflationary pressures were rising in Japan at a time when monetary authorities are reluctant to raise interest rates for fear of once again plunging the stock market into disarray, participants said. The dollar last ended New York trading below 151 yen on May 15, when it closed at 150.83 yen.

But sustained trading of the Nikkei stock index at levels around 32,000 points in the past week, along with the prospect of an easing of inflationary pressures due to stabilizing prices, have lent support to the Bank of Japan's policy of steady rates, Mr. Sohn said. The Nikkei average rose 135.24 points Thursday to close at 32,311.75.

The unexpectedly sharp downward revision in U.S. first-quarter GNP was accompanied by an unchanged estimation of the implicit price deflator at a 5.7% annual rate.

The GNP growth rate announcement was neutral for the dollar when taken with the inflation figure because figures showing a sluggish economy and continuing inflationary pressure had been expected, participants said. Taken together, the numbers mean the Federal Reserve still doesn't have any reason to change its policy of maintaining steady interest rates, they said.

The fall in inventories, which swung to a revised decline of $5.9 billion in the quarter from an originally announced gain of $2.6 billion, was discounted partly because of inventories' volatility and partly due to their natural tendency to rebound from a fall to a gain the next quarter, participants said.

Meanwhile, the mark weakened against the dollar and other currencies on continued nervousness about how much unification will end up costing West Germany, said Peter Iversen, a trader at Shawmut Bank of Boston N.A.

Late in the New York day, the Treasury said that a comment made earlier by Assistant Treasury Secretary Sidney Jones that the dollar is undervalued against all major currencies except the yen wasn't meant to suggest a change in the Treasury's views on the dollar.

Mr. Jones's remark ruffled the market briefly, spurring selling of the mark against the yen, traders said.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $366.50 an ounce, up $2.40. Estimated volume was a heavy five million ounces.

In Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $368.45 an ounce.
