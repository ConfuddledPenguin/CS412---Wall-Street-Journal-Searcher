Politics & Policy: Amtrak Spending Bill of $1.4 Billion For Next Two Years Is Vetoed by Bush

  • Author: Laurie McGinley
  • Date: 05/25/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush vetoed a bill authorizing $1.4 billion in Amtrak spending for the next two fiscal years, saying that a provision requiring increased government review of freight-railroad takeovers represented a "step backward for the entire rail industry." The disputed provision would have given the Interstate Commerce Commission jurisdiction to review proposals by non-railroad companies to acquire any of the nation's 16 largest freight railroads.

The ICC already has the authority to review the acquisition of an entire railroad or a segment of it by another railroad. It also has authority to review the purchase of a piece of a railroad by a non-railroad company. But it may not review the proposed takeover of an entire railroad by a non-rail company -- a loophole that needed closing, according to the proponents of the provision.

President Bush disagreed, saying in his veto message to Congress that the provision was "an unwarranted regulatory roadblock to financial restructuring of the industry."

Amtrak spokesman John Jacobsen said that, as a result of the veto, "no trains are going to grind to a halt." But he complained that it clouds Amtrak's financial future, making it more difficult for the government-owned passenger railroad to raise private capital.

Prospects for a congressional override of the veto, which would require two-thirds votes by both the House and the Senate, are hard to gauge. The House passed the bill by an overwhelming 322-93, but the Senate approved it on a voice vote, so the depth of support isn't clear. Since President Bush has taken office, Congress hasn't overridden any of his 10 previous vetoes.

The Amtrak bill included authorization of $630 million for fiscal 1989, which ended last Sept. 30, $656 million for fiscal 1990, $684 million for fiscal 1991 and $712 million for fiscal 1992. Congress has already appropriated funds for the railroad for 1989 and 1990; the vetoed bill includes authorizations for those years simply for bookkeeping reasons.

The administration, in its January budget proposal, hadn't proposed any funding for Amtrak. But President Bush didn't cite funding levels as a reason to veto the authorization measure.

Authorizing legislation defines which programs the government may carry out and sets spending ceilings for them, while appropriating legislation funds the programs.
