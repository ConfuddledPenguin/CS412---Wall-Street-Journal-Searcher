Sounds as If It Will Take More Than Soft Soap to Clean This Up

  • Author: Charles McCoy
  • Date: 05/25/90

When a business folds, people lose their shirts. But when a business folds and bundles, people can lose their pants as well. It happened recently in the Seattle area, when Dollar Saver Cleaner Club Inc., a chain of dry cleaning and laundromat clubs that offered discount cleaning and delivery for anybody paying a $20 annual membership fee, washed out. Owners Jules and Marion Dabbous filed for Chapter 7 bankruptcy-law protection and apparently split town -- without bothering to inform customers or return their clothes.

Hundreds of former clients have been hung out to dry. "There is outrage," says Paula Selis, an assistant attorney general in Seattle. "It's rough on a person when they can't get their clothes back from the cleaners."

Rough indeed. Darnell Coles, an outfielder for the Seattle Mariners baseball team, heard that the operators of his Dollar Saver outlet had padlocked the doors and bolted, leaving only a scribbly note on yellow legal paper proclaiming the club permanently closed. This troubled Mr. Coles, who had nine shirts locked inside. But he wasn't entirely surprised.

"They'd been taking longer and longer to return my clothes," he says. "They'd always tell me it'd be a couple of days, a couple more days. But I never thought they'd stiff me and skip town."

Rumors about the affair are spreading like a red-wine stain. Did the Dabbouses mean to take their clients to the cleaners all along? They do have a spotty past: Records at the King County prosecutor's office show that the Dabbouses pleaded guilty in 1987 to permitting prostitution at their Relax-eena Massage Parlor. The state, sniffing for dirty laundry, has filed suit against the Dabbouses, alleging violations of consumer-protection laws.

Another element of the mystery: If Dollar Saver was legitimate, what, then, took the starch out of the operation? Ms. Selis says the clubs had operated for about three years and apparently had a lot of customers. "On the surface, it looked like they were doing pretty well," she says. Ms. Selis says her office continues to press its investigation into the matter.

The Dabbouses haven't come clean as to the motives for their actions. Greg Cavagnaro, an attorney representing them in their bankruptcy case, says they're unavailable for comment. Meanwhile, Ms. Selis says, authorities are taking steps to redress the balance and return clothes to their rightful owners.
