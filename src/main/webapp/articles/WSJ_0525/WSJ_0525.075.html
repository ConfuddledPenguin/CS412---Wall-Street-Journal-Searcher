Disney Plans Zero-Coupon $1.5 Billion Sale

  • Author: Earl C. Gottschalk Jr.
  • Date: 05/25/90

BURBANK, Calif. -- Walt Disney Co. is tapping its financial imagination to help pay for the multibillion-dollar expansion of its U.S. theme parks and resorts over the next decade. The entertainment concern filed yesterday for the proposed offering of $1.5 billion of zero-coupon bonds due 2005. The bonds are convertible into cash equal to the market price of Euro Disneyland S.C.A., a publicly held French company established to build and operate Euro Disneyland.

"It's a unique twist," said William Gross, managing director at Pacific Investment Management Co., a Newport Beach, Calif., fixed-income manager.

Disney is the largest company to raise cash by selling convertible zeros. The offering is the largest convertible bond issue ever and the first time that a zero-coupon bond purchased by U.S. investors is convertible into the cash equivalent of the price of a foreign stock, said Lee Cole, managing director at Merrill Lynch Capital Markets, the Merrill Lynch & Co. unit that is sole underwriter of the issue.

"There isn't anything else like this," said Michael Carr, a Manasquan, N.J., money manager who specializes in convertible bonds. "It's an intriguing investment." Obviously, investors won't buy it for the 6% return but for the possible appreciation of the Euro Disneyland stock, Mr. Carr said.

Other companies, such as Motorola Inc., MCI Communications Corp. and Turner Broadcasting System Inc., have sold convertible zeros, but those bonds were converted into the U.S. shares of these concerns.

The investment will be a complicated one for U.S. investors to analyze since its return will depend not only on U.S interest rates and how well Euro Disneyland does, but also on the relationship of the French franc to the dollar at time of conversion. Investors won't actually get stock in Euro Disneyland but U.S. dollars equivalent to the price of Euro Disneyland stock in francs. "The calculations will make the computer jockeys delirious with joy," says Mr. Carr.

The zero-coupon bonds, which are called Liquid Yield Option Notes, will pay a yield of 6% to maturity. They will be priced at a deep discount of about $412 per $1,000 bond, and they will be convertible at any time at a premium of 15% to the price of Euro Disneyland stock on the day the bonds are issued.

Euro Disneyland is listed on stock exchanges in Paris, London and Brussels. It closed yesterday at the equivalent of $18.625 on the Paris exchange. Euro Disneyland, a theme park and resort complex near Paris, is scheduled to open in 1992. It is 49% owned by Walt Disney Co.

Merrill Lynch has been the dominant underwriter for these zero-coupon convertible securities since they were introduced five years ago.

Richard Nanula, Disney's treasurer, said the proposed June offering is expected to bring in about $600 million that will be used for capital spending and to repay more expensive commercial paper outstanding. "Normally, it would cost us much more to issue debt, but since we are sharing a portion of the Euro Disneyland stock appreciation with investors, we're able to issue the debt at an attractive rate for us," he said.

Mr. Nanula said Disney doesn't plan to hedge its franc holdings in case Euro Disneyland's stock soars and the franc weakens, providing a big payout for investors who opt to convert the bonds. The reason: "We own 83 million shares of Euro Disneyland ourselves," he said.

Zeros have an added attraction over traditional convertibles in that the issuer doesn't make periodic interest payments to bondholders yet is allowed to deduct the implied interest from taxes. But bondholders do have to pay taxes on the implied interest they receive -- one of the drawbacks in investing in zero-coupon bonds.

In January, Walt Disney announced a multibillion-dollar expansion of its theme parks and resorts in Florida and California over the next decade, including new separate-admission parks in both states.
