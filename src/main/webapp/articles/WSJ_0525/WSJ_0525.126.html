Credit Markets: Treasurys Fall on News of Weak Buying Interest At Latest Government Auction of Five-Year Notes

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Stuart Drown
  • Date: 05/25/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices skidded yesterday after it became apparent that investors were showing only mild interest in the Treasury's new issue of five-year, two-month notes. The $8.5 billion of notes were auctioned by the Treasury yesterday at an average yield of 8.54%. That was the highest yield set at a five-year, two-month auction since May 1989 and was even slightly higher than the previous five-year, two-month note auction Feb. 22 when the average yield was 8.53%.

But some traders said the yield may not have been high enough to attract investors. Bond dealers said the poor results reflected weaker-than-expected demand by domestic and foreign buyers, although they weren't sure why investors shied away. Other dealers said that Japanese investors, who tend to buy moderate amounts of the notes, were nearly nonexistent at yesterday's auction.

"It seems like the psychology in the market just suddenly turned from positive to negative," said John Costas, co-manager of government trading at First Boston Corp. He said that investors expressed interest in buying the notes, "but when it came time to bid, we didn't see," much interest. He suspects that some investors may have already "burned a lot of cash" at Wednesday's two-year note auction and several weeks ago at the Treasury's huge $30.5 billion refunding of the government debt.

In early trading yesterday, bond prices were unchanged to slightly higher. But when rumors circulated that the auction was poorly received, prices of Treasury securities moved lower as dealers reduced prices in an effort to drum up demand.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended 3/8 point lower, a loss of about $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount, to yield 8.60%. In when-issued trading activity, the yield on the new five-year, two-month notes rose to 8.56%.

In the junk bond market, prices of R.H. Macy & Co. junk bonds plunged on news that a leading factoring firm tightened its credit standards toward the retailer.

Some Macy bonds fell five points, or $50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount, and yields spurted as high as 25%.

Henry Kassebaum, a senior vice president at Heller Financial Corp., confirmed rumors that Heller Financial shifted from a "revolving credit" policy toward Macy suppliers to an "order-by-order" policy. Instead of extending a revolving credit line to Macy suppliers, Heller will now decide whether to extend credit to suppliers on an order-by-order basis.

In response, Macy's 14.5% subordinated debentures, maturing in 2001, fell more than five points to 57. The retailer's 14.5% senior subordinated debentures, due in 1998, also shed five points to end at 67.

Factoring companies like Heller buy receivables at a slight discount from suppliers that sell goods to retailers. A supplier is paid in cash for its receivables and the factoring agent assumes the risk that the retailer will pay its bills.

The problems of Macy's pulled the entire junk bond market lower, with most issues ending between 1/4 and 1/2 point lower. Market participants expect bids for the bonds to weaken further when the market opens today.

Treasury Securities

U.S. Treasury notes and bonds traded lower after the government's $8.5 billion auction of new five-year, two-month Treasury notes failed to stir enthusiasm in investors.

The auction results were unexpected, especially coming after a successful twoyear note auction on Wednesday.

In trading, the benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 101 13/32 compared with 101 27/32 on Wednesday. The latest 10-year notes ended at 101 16/32 to yield 8.63% compared with 101 27/32 to yield 8.58%.

Short-term rates were higher. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.68% for a bond equivalent yield of 7.92% while the rate on six-month bills fell to 7.71% to yield 8.11%.

Junk Bonds

The problems of R.H. Macy's spread to the broader junk bond market, pushing prices moderately lower.

Besides Macy's, other big decliners included RJR Nabisco Inc., whose bonds fell on talk that a major investor was dumping large amounts of the bonds.

RJR Nabisco's 14.07% convertible payin-kind debentures, due in 2009, finished down 1 1/2 points to close at 84 on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely held RJR issue, the 14.07% exchangable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, ended 1 3/8 points lower to close at 70 1/4.

Duracell Holdings' 13.5% subordinated debentures, maturing in 2000, dropped 1/4 point to end at 103, while Kroger's 12 7/8% senior subordinated debentures, due in 1999, shed 1/2 point to 102 1/4.

In the investment-grade market, actively traded corporate issues fell 1/4 to 3/8 point.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities posted small price advances, outpacing Treasurys and causing yield spreads to tighten slightly.

Government National Mortgage Association 9% bonds for June delivery firmed 1/32 to 96 for a yield of 9.72%, 1.10 percentage points above the yield on 10-year Treasury notes. That spread is 0.02 percentage point tighter than late Wednesday.

Among other actively traded issues, Ginnie Mae 9.5% bonds for June delivery were up 1/32 at 98 14/32; Ginnie Mae 10% bonds were at 100 7/8, up 3/32; Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% bonds for June delivery were up 2/32 at 97 31/32 and June Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% bonds were trading 2/32 higher at 98 15/32.

Meanwhile, activity remained at a standstill in the market for high-coupon mortgage securities in the aftermath of the Public Securities Association statement Tuesday that it will impose trading curbs on certain high-coupon mortgage issues that are experiencing rapid prepayment rates.

The PSA's move is aimed at stemming the recent issuance of above-market mortgages, used as collateral mainly for GNMA mortgage-backed securities, that are often prepaid quickly to refinance more quickly than usual. For investors who have been paying premiums for those issues, expecting prepayment to come much later, the value of their purchases has been sharply reduced.

Municipals

In a repeat performance of Wednesday's lifeless trading, most actively traded municipal bonds finished rock steady after barely budging all session.

"It's basically a sideways market in municipals," said Lewis Nassikas, a trader at First Albany Corp. Trading, he added, was particularly light with market participants sluggish before the long Memorial Day weekend.

There was little response to the revised U.S. gross national product data for the first quarter.

GNP was revised downward to a 1.3% annual rate from the 2.1% pace originally estimated last month. Inventory reduction was the chief reason for the revision.

Economists surveyed by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report had forecast, on average, an updated first-quarter clip of 1.8%.

In secondary activity, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bonds of 2018 were unchanged at 97 7/8 in late trading, yielding 7.37%.

Foreign Bonds

A stronger yen and speculation that some banks will soon cut the prime rate triggered a modest rally in the Japanese government bond market.

Japan's prime rate, a benchmark long-term loan rate, is currently 7.9%. The rate is set monthly by three large credit banks, which are expected to cut the prime rate to 7.6%, effective June 1. The announcement could come as early as today.

Japan's bond market rally was muted, however, because investors believe the Bank of Japan will continue to put upward pressure on short-term interest rates, over which the central bank has the most immediate influence. Economists said the central bank wants short-term rates to remain high in order to support the value of the yen, among other things.

Japan's benchmark 10-year bond rose 0.14 point to 87.71 yesterday to yield 7.02%. (The yield is calculated in local terms, but would be much lower if calculated by the semi-annual compound measure that is used in the U.S.)

Financial markets were closed in West Germany, France, Switzerland and Holland in observance of Ascension Day.

In Britain, where trading activity was quiet due to the holiday in other countries, government bonds ended moderately lower.
