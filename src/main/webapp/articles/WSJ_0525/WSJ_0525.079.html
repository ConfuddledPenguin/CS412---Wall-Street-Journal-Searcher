REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Democracy in Armenia

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/25/90

Mikhail Gorbachev continues to struggle with the question of political legitimacy. He has announced plans to expose the government's new economic program to a referendum, though it's hard for us to see precisely what might be learned from the exercise. He would be asking voters to pick between the doleful status quo and what he's offering -- a watered-down reform called the "regulated market economy." Will a vote against the reform mean people want a more radical program or less change? Democracy is normally about real choices, and while the Kremlin continues to toy with the idea, it's well along elsewhere in the Soviet empire. Elections Sunday in Soviet Armenia for its Supreme Soviet provided important gains for the Pan-National Party, an anti-communist grouping on the ballot for the first time. Turnout was low -- about 50% -- and results were so unclear that the republic faces a runoff. But the anti-communists won the Armenian capital of Yerevan and the city of Kirovakan.

Voters in Parakar, just outside Yerevan, sent a particularly forceful message. They gave 91% of their vote to Paruyr Hayrikyan, who backs secession from Moscow. Mr. Hayrikyan rolled to this margin of victory without shaking a single voter's hand. The Soviet government dumped him in Ethiopia back in 1988, and he conducted this campaign from exile in Glendale, California.

That Soviet Armenia could produce such a result shows the continuing attraction of the democratic impulse. It must be remembered that Armenia today is a state in considerable distress. It has recently endured the devastating 1988 earthquake, a lengthy railroad blockade by Azerbaijan and a January massacre of Armenians in Baku. Not surprisingly, these events have sent much of the republic into new lawlessness and despair. The neighboring republic of Georgia, which will have elections this fall, has similar instability, with anti-communist forces forming to produce democracy or simply to destabilize the republic.

It would be wrong, though, to write off the Caucasus as little more than a budding Lebanon, as is often the tendency in the West when dealing with this part of the world. To dismiss the region as a caldron of inter-ethnic animosity is to give the Kremlin a rationale for suppressing any disturbances in the region, as it did last January in Nagorno-Karabakh.

We're not advocating bloodshed in the Soviet Union, but it is important to recognize that there are now people struggling to gain the advantages of democratic self-determination in places such as Armenia. If the democratic impulse is to take hold in an Armenia, Georgia or Lithuania, it is going to be necessary for the West to notice their advances and support their legitimate needs.

Moscow took Mr. Hayrikyan's citizenship when it jettisoned him from the country, barring his return to what is now his own constituency. If Mr. Gorbachev wants to offer voters choices about their politics or their economy, he could start telling the exiled but newly elected Mr. Hayrikyan that he can have his passport back.
