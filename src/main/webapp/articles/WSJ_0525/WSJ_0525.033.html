U.K. Economic Data Show Rise In Output, Pay

  • Author: Paulette Roberge Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/25/90

LONDON -- New U.K. economic data showed a continued rise in industrial production against a background of steady wage increases in manufacturing, reinforcing economists' views that the Thatcher government is having mixed success in cooling the economy. The Central Statistical Office reported industrial production rose 1.8% in March from month-earlier levels, while rising 1.7% from March 1989. In addition, unit wage costs in the manufacturing sector, while easing 0.3% in March from February, were up 7.5% from the year-earlier month.

The data come on top of a wider-than-expected April merchandise trade deficit of 1.78 billion pounds ($3.01 billion), reported Wednesday.

"One month ago, we had tentative signs" of a slowdown in economic activity that could lead to lower inflation, says David Owen, economist at Kleinwort Benson Securities. Now, he says, that optimism appears to have been misplaced.

A Treasury official noted that the year-on-year rise in industrial production showed that the "rate of growth has clearly slowed over the past year," as expected in the current fiscal climate.

But the market was anticipating a rise in industrial production of only about 0.45%. The higher-than-expected rise led many economists to revise next week's first-quarter output-based Gross Domestic Product (GDP) figures upward to stand even with quarter-earlier levels or to show a modest gain.

GDP in the fourth quarter was up 2% -- from a year earlier. Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major has forecast that GDP growth would be 1% for 1990.

Although unit wage costs in the manufacturing sector showed a modest fall in March, UBS-Phillips & Drew Ltd. economist Joe Roseman notes that wage settlements are still running at an "uncomfortably high level" on an annualized basis, putting considerable pressure on profit margins.

The squeeze on profits has led to some employment contraction in the manufacturing sector, which economists say may shortly lead to lower wage demands. But they add that this has yet to be felt in the service sector, where the wage problem is exacerbated by an acute shortage of skilled labor.

Mr. Roseman and other economists take comfort from the historical pattern of high wage settlements of the early 1980s and early 1986, which were followed by sharp falls in pay settlements. But these also suggest that any sudden shakeouts in the labor market can't be expected in 1990.
