Budget Negotiators Disagree on Target For Deficit Cuts Over Next Five Years

  • Author: David Wessel and Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 05/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The budget summit ran into rougher waters as congressional and administration negotiators disagreed on how ambitious a goal to set for reducing the deficit over the next five years. Senate Budget Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.) said Democrats want to aim for a package of spending cuts and tax increases that would total $550 billion to $600 billion in the fiscal years 1991 through 1995.

But White House and Republican congressional negotiators resisted, questioning the wisdom of rushing to set a five-year goal without any discussion of the policies such a goal would entail, participants said. Some Republicans lean toward a five-year goal closer to the $420 billion, five-year figure set in President Bush's budget.

The dispute illustrates the political obstacles the summit faces. Democrats say they want to wipe out the deficit over five years without relying on the Social Security surplus, a target that would require deep spending cuts and large tax increases. They fear the White House simply wants a pact to get past the 1992 election.

Republicans are concentrating on achieving a solid first-year deficit-reduction package of $50 billion or so, and are in less of a hurry to pin down a five-year target. They fear the Democrats are trying to lock them into a big tax increase or are maneuvering to avoid blame should the talks break down.

"People had different views on what should be done," said House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.).

Others stressed that the talks remain in what White House Budget Director Richard Darman has called "the seminar stage." Sen. Wyche Fowler (D., Ga.), said flatly: "We are not in negotiation."

An administration official, who asked not to be named, said, "We all agree: `Let's get something responsible.' We still haven't decided what `responsible' is."

Participants said the two sides are talking about aiming for a first-year package of spending cuts and tax increases of between $45 billion and $60 billion, a significant sum, but one that would still require altering the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law.

Negotiators set two full days of meetings for June 6 and 7, after Congress returns from its Memorial Day recess, to examine federal spending and revenues in greater detail. Negotiators haven't agreed how to proceed after that, but negotiators on both sides say they hope to begin hard bargaining promptly.

"The political parties agree that it's far better to see if we can try to accelerate this," House Budget Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) said. Uncertainty over the summit is already complicating congressional campaigns because candidates are taking positions on taxes and spending.

Before yesterday's meeting, President Bush charged that inaction by Congress has created the current impasse over the budget deficit, and again refused to offer any detailed solution of his own.

In a news conference, Mr. Bush deflected suggestions that he make a televised statement to the American people explaining why the summit is necessary. Lawmakers would be disappointed if he gave the issue such a high profile now, he suggested, because he would note "the fact that the Congress appropriates all the money and raises all the revenues. That's their obligation." Moreover, he asserted that recent polls indicate "that people understand that the Congress bears a greater responsibility for this."

Democrats were quick to respond. "It's the first time in this century we've seen a president want to give power to the Congress," Mr. Gephardt said. "The president is wrong if he thinks the budget is more our responsibility than his."

Meanwhile, Mr. Bush continued to hear from Republicans who want him to avoid agreeing to raise taxes. Outspoken tax foes Rep. Newt Gingrich of Georgia, the No. 2 Republican in the House, and Rep. Vin Weber of Minnesota, joined by House Republican Leader Robert Michel of Illinois, met at the White House with Mr. Bush and administration budget negotiators.

At the session at the Capitol, Budget Director Darman gave negotiators updated deficit forecasts incorporating the latest Treasury estimates of the cost of cleaning up the thrift industry. For fiscal 1991, the budget office sees a deficit of between $179 billion and $205.8 billion, well above the $64 billion Gramm-Rudman target. This includes thrift spending of all sorts of between $41.1 and $67.9 billion. For the current year, the budget office now sees a deficit of $181 billion to $202.7 billion, including $26 billion to $47.7 billion in thrift costs.

The forecasts rely on revised economic projections that are subject to change again before the July 15 forecast required by the Gramm-Rudman law. The budget office now estimates inflation-adjusted economic growth of 2.9% between the fourth quarters of 1990 and 1991, down from its January forecast of 3.3%. Compared with its earlier forecast, it now sees slightly higher inflation and significantly higher interest rates for this year and next. It also is forecasting substantially lower corporate profits for each of the next five years.

The budget office now forecasts rates on three-month Treasury bills will average 7.7% in 1990 and 6.8% in 1991, compared with its earlier forecast of 6.7% and 5.4%. It forecasts rates on 10-year Treasury notes at 8.6% for 1990 and 8% for 1991, compared with 7.7% and 6.8% in its earlier forecast.
