R.H. Macy Bond Prices Fall as Concern About Its Financial Condition Increase

  • Author: Jeffrey A. Trachtenberg and George Anders
  • Date: 05/25/90

NEW YORK -- Prices of R.H. Macy & Co. bonds dropped as much as 8% as the credit markets and the retailing industry were swept by rising concerns about the giant retailer's financial condition. Heller Financial Corp., a major lender to Macy suppliers, said it will review its standards for financing shipments to the chain. An executive at another major credit company, who declined to be identified, said his firm has tightened its standards on Macy shipments. Several bank lenders also voiced concern about Macy, which has over $5 billion in debt following its 1986 buy-out by management.

A Macy spokesman insisted the company's financial condition is sound, and several key suppliers said they are continuing to ship new merchandise. "We're watching developments, and yes, everybody is nervous, but we are shipping as usual," said Jerome Chazen, chairman of Liz Claiborne Inc., Macy's largest supplier. "We've checked with both our factoring companies, and they have given us a green light."

Factoring companies like Heller play a critical role in the retailing business by extending credit to suppliers. Without approval from factors, most vendors won't ship new goods. Should the factors lose confidence in Macy, it would put the store in a serious situation. The recent bankruptcy-law filings of L.J. Hooker Corp. and Campeau Corp.'s retailing operations reflected in part a loss of faith by factors.

The head of one major factoring company, who asked not to be identified, said he hasn't seen any material change in Macy's business to indicate Macy's prospects have changed in the last few months. The head of a second major factoring firm said that his firm is approving Macy shipments through the end of May but will want to review all June and July shipments before they are sent. "We have to be prudent," said this executive, who asked not to be identified.

"The idea is to stay calm," said Jon Levy, president of St. Gilliam Inc., a women's apparel vendor which does business with Macy. "If the factors panic now, everybody will panic."

Henry Kassebaum, a Heller executive vice president, said Heller isn't cutting its credit lines to Macy suppliers but will review those lines when they expire at the end of this month. Mr. Kassebaum said Heller has approved all of Macy's orders thus far. Separately, he told the Dow Jones News Service that Heller has shifted from a "revolving credit" policy toward Macy suppliers to an "order-by-order" policy.

The bankruptcy-law filings in the last year by Hooker, Campeau's retailing operations and Ames Department Stores Inc. have made investors skittish about the least sign of difficulty at another retailer -- particularly one with a big debt load and high-yield "junk" bonds. The Ames filing last month, which caught some manufacturers by surprise, has left the apparel industry especially jittery.

Yesterday, Macy's 14 1/2% senior subordinated debentures due 1998 finished the day at 69 cents on the dollar, down 7% from the day before. Its 14 1/2% subordinated debentures due 2001 were quoted at 59 cents on the dollar, down 5%. The biggest percentage drop was shown by Macy's zero-coupon bonds, which finished the day at 24 cents on the dollar, off 8%.

Macy executives declined to be interviewed, but a spokesman said the company is meeting its financial projections and has arranged to meet next week with eight to 10 factoring companies to update them on its financial status.

Earlier this week, Macy provided some factors with updated forecasts reflecting results for the close of the third quarter ended April 28. "That information was even more favorable than had been forecast in February," said the spokesman.

Although Macy hasn't yet reported its third-quarter results, the company has indicated its losses were less than the $64 million it reported during the same period in 1989. For the second quarter, which included Christmas results, Macy reported a $39 million loss.

Macy may face some friction with its bank lenders, who provided $735 million of loans for the company's 1986 buy-out. When the banks lent the money, they set financial targets for Macy, one of which the company recently has failed to meet.

Macy in July will likely seek a further six-month waiver of a condition that its cash flow -- net income plus interest expense, taxes and depreciation -- exceed its annual interest expense by 1.55:1. Bankers have already voted one such waiver for Macy in March, allowing it to meet a less-stringent 1.2:1 ratio.

But some bankers don't appear in the mood to grant Macy further waivers, unless they get concessions in return. One major European lender said he would like to see Macy sell assets, such as its credit-card company, so that it can pay off its bank debt more quickly.

"We're concerned about the retail trade," said George Morgan, a top lender at U.S. National Bank of Oregon, which helped finance the buy-out. "If a credit isn't performing to expectations, you're probably entitled to some compensation."

Macy's lead bank, Manufacturers Hanover Corp., has been telling lenders that Macy has ample borrowing capacity to cover its needs this year.

Arnold Cohen, president of Mahoney Cohen Paul & Co., the apparel industry's largest accounting firm, said he is suggesting clients be cautious in dealing with Macy. "I'm asking the healthy businesses to find a number that they are willing to invest in this situation in the hopes that it will work out. Companies which are in weak condition can't afford another writeoff."

Richard Posner, executive vice president of Credit Exchange Inc., a source of credit information for apparel manufacturers, said he is telling clients to sit tight until he meets with Macy executives. "We want to talk to the company," said Mr. Posner. Both Solo Credit Service Corp. and Bernard Sands Credit Consultants Inc. continue to recommend Macy shipments.

Some apparel executives suggested the recent concern about Macy may be related to the shipping and payment practices in the retailing business. Typically stores pay vendors on the tenth of the month for goods shipped 20 to 50 days earlier. On June 10, Macy is expected to pay its bills on merchandise shipped between April 20 and May 20. But it won't have to pay for goods shipped between May 21 and June 20 before July 10.

"The question is whether you want to extend them credit for what amounts to 50 days," said Bud Konheim, president of Nicole Miller Ltd., an apparel maker. "Recent experiences tell me that where there is smoke, there's fire."
