World Markets: Taiwan Shares Resume Plunge Amid Pessimism; Prices Strengthen in Tokyo, Decline in London

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/25/90

Taiwan shares resumed their plunge Thursday, as a lack of confidence persisted. In Tokyo, stock prices strengthened on moderate volume, as arbitrage buying and a surge in large-capitalization domestic issues sparked a round of buying in the late afternoon. In London, equities finished a lackluster session lower, as futures selling and a widespread lack of interest led the market down.

Taiwan's weighted index, which rebounded 294.08 points Wednesday, plunged 426.63, or 6.08%, closing at 6587.77, a 15-month low. The index has tumbled nearly 2,000 points, or 23%, since May 16.

All eight sectors registered major losses, falling between 4.86% and 6.48%. Volume, including stocks and mutual funds, totaled about 969.8 million shares valued at 61.08 billion New Taiwan dollars (US$2.23 billion), compared with 1.19 billion shares valued at NT$76.45 billion a day earlier. Among 193 losers, 156 stocks dropped to their 7% limit; only three stocks gained, and two were unchanged.

The market opened lower, as investors took quick profits on Wednesday's gains. The declines then widened, as sellers poured onto the trading floor.

"Weak confidence destroys the game," Elvis Yang, a broker with Tycoon Securities Co., said. "We see little hope for the market to reverse its downward trend over the short term at least." He sees a recovery only if the government takes actions, such as to reduce the 0.6% stock transaction tax and to instruct banks to support the market. "Investors were so bearish, they need someone to lead them," he said. "The government is the best choice."

Dealers blamed continued drops mainly on concerns about political uncertainty and capital outflow.

"A calm-down is all investors need right now," said Miao Tai Sheng, assistant manager of Taiwan International Securities Corp. He said the market shouldn't be far away from its bottom, as long as no panic selling surfaces. "The market is oversold," he said. "It's time to buy low-priced blue chips."

In Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index of 225 issues, which added 238.21 points Wednesday, was up 135.24 at 32311.75. First section volume was estimated at 600 million shares, off from 640 million shares a day earlier.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index, or Topix, of all first section issues, which gained 4.36 points Wednesday, added 0.95 to 2395.90 Thursday.

Friday in Tokyo, the Nikkei index advanced 286.67 points to close the morning session at 32598.42.

Traders said Thursday that the biggest news affecting the market was Japan Airlines' order for 20 Boeing Co. 747-400s, with options for an additional 34. A trader said heavy-industrial machinery makers and other manufacturers that could supply parts for the project got investors' attention.

Interest in the domestic manufacturers boosted prices in other large-capitalization shares, sparking a rally in steel issues and trading companies, traders said. Moreover, arbitragers in the late afternoon worked to keep the futures index at high levels, to sell profitably and then buy through program trading on the cash index. Consequently, higher cash prices, coupled with the surge in less expensive, domestic issues, pushed the Nikkei index into a firmer position within the 32000 level.

In expectation of strong results from Sony, buying interest was heavy in the company's shares, as well as in those of other blue-chip electricals. Sony rose 50 yen to 8,750 yen ($57.76). After trading closed, Sony posted a 37% rise in consolidated pretax profit to 227.43 billion yen ($1.5 billion) for its year ended March 31, exceeding expectations. Other high-technology companies posted similar, favorable results, a trader said.

Mitsubishi Heavy Industries gained 60 to 1,060, mostly on speculation that the company will be linked to the JAL-Boeing project, but also that shipbuilding companies' earnings will be favorable. Ishikawajima-Harima Heavy Industries received aerospace attention, rising 70 to 1,210, but a trader said the rise also was linked to rumors the company may take over Sasebo Heavy Industries, which is said to be having financial problems.

In London, market watchers said much of the confidence in equities built up over the past several weeks was knocked out by Wednesday's worse-than-expected trade data for April. "No matter how you look at the figures, they aren't good news," a market maker said. "It has rocked U.K. investors back on their heels."

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index finished down 10.3 points at 2277.1, off its early-afternoon high of 2295.4 and well above its midmorning minimum of 2269.3. Turnover amounted to a reasonable 420.3 million shares, compared with 542.1 million a day earlier. The FT 30-share index ended off 4.8 points at 1805.2.

Traders said the market lacked direction amid an absence of striking corporate or economic news. They also said there weren't any European buyers because of the religious holiday, which closed most financial centers Thursday in Europe.

British Telecom reported a 5.5% decline in full-year pretax profit and a #390 million ($660.5 million) restructuring charge. The figures were in line with expectations, a dealer said, and analysts were optimistic for the company in the coming year. The stock rose 2 pence to 276 pence.

MEPC, a property company, posted a 20% rise in interim pretax profit but its shares were hit as a reaction to the warning that investment values were likely to be in decline through the year. The company's shares skidded 20 to 489.

One of the day's biggest gainers was Thorn EMI, which added 21 to 733. The entertainment and technology company said it is holding talks that could lead to the disposal of its lighting subsidiary to GTE Corp., a U.S. telecommunications, lighting and precision materials company.

Oil issues were weaker across the board on a sagging crude price, dealers said.

British Petroleum fell 7 to 316, Shell Transport & Trading lost 7 to 457, Lasmo dropped 8 to 385, Enterprise slipped 6 to 611, and Ultramar lost 2 to 339.

Equity prices closed higher in Milan. The Ascension Day observance closed markets in France, West Germany, the Netherlands, Belgium, Sweden, Switzerland, South Africa and elsewhere.

Share prices rose in Hong Kong, fell in Sydney, Singapore and Manila, and were little changed in Wellington and Seoul. Hong Kong stocks extended their rally to a fifth consecutive session in active trading.
