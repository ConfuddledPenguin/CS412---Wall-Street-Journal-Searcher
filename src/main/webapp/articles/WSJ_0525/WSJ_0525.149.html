Bombay Palace, Two Accused in SEC Suit, Settle Civil Action

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/25/90

NEW YORK -- The Securities and Exchange Commission filed a civil suit against Bombay Palace Restaurants Inc.; Sant Chatwal, the company's chairman; and Kewal Chopra, its former vice president and chief financial officer. The lawsuit, filed in federal court in Manhattan, alleged securities-law violations in connection with Bombay's takeover of Lifestyle Restaurants Inc. in 1987. The company operates Bombay Palace and Beefsteak Charlie's restaurants.

At the same time as the suit was filed, the defendants agreed to a permanent injunction barring them from future violations of the securities law. The defendants neither admitted nor denied the allegations in agreeing to the injunction, which settles the suit. The SEC didn't seek any penalty other than the injunction.

Bombay also agreed to engage independent auditors to review its recordkeeping and agreed to adopt policies and procedures to ensure that its recordkeeping complies with securities law.

In September, Bombay filed for Chapter 11 status under the U.S. Bankruptcy Code. Chapter 11 protects a company from creditor lawsuits while it works out a plan to pay its debt.

On behalf of the defendants, Mr. Chopra, who remains an employee of the company, said: "We have settled with the SEC in an effort to put behind us events that occurred several years ago. By settling with the SEC, we hope to be able to devote full attention to the future of the Bombay Palace companies and to strive for their successful emergence from bankruptcy reorganization proceedings."

The SEC's lawsuit alleged that in acquiring Lifestyle, the defendants falsely represented that they wouldn't use Lifestyle to generate cash for Bombay, except in certain specified circumstances. The SEC's suit said the defendants failed to disclose that $2 million was taken from Lifestyle and turned over to Bombay, and that they overstated Bombay's financial results.

Bombay announced in April 1989 that the SEC was investigating it. Jeffrey Zuckerman, an SEC associate regional administrator in New York, said Bombay's bankruptcy-law filing delayed the agency's civil action.
