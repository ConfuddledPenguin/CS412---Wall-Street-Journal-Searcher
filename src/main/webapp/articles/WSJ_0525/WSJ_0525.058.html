Technology: State Department Opposes AT&T Plan To Use Intersputnik for Phone Traffic

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 05/25/90

NEW YORK -- The U.S. State Department wants to disconnect American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s plan to use the Soviet Intersputnik satellite system to carry phone traffic between the U.S. and the U.S.S.R. Lawrence Eagleburger, deputy secretary of state, in a May 14 letter to Federal Communications Commission Chairman Alfred Sikes, said AT&T's proposal would violate international policies among the U.S. and the 118 countries -- including the North Atlantic Treaty Organization allies and Japan -- that own the International Telecommunications Satellite Organization (Intelsat).

U.S. policy bars U.S. communications carriers from sending public voice and data traffic over satellite networks other than Intelsat's system.

Mr. Eagleburger added that "insufficient foreign policy reasons exist to deviate from applying the existing U.S. separate systems policy to the AT&T application."

The FCC has yet to rule on AT&T's request, but it is doubtful the commission will buck the State Department's finding.

The use of Intersputnik is critical to AT&T for several reasons. The plan, which has been waiting for FCC and State Department approval since last December, was a cornerstone of a wider-ranging agreement signed earlier this month between AT&T and the Soviet Ministry of Communications that called for the two to explore joint ventures in telecommunications services, equipment manufacturing and research and development.

In addition, world-wide satellite capacity has grown tight in recent years, and AT&T and the Soviets wanted to use Intersputnik to handle some of the voice and data communications traffic that has increased 2 1/2 times between the U.S. and the Soviet Union since 1987. What's more, Moscow could pay for Intersputnik transmission service in rubles much easier than paying for Intelsat service in Western currency, which is scarce in the U.S.S.R.

An AT&T spokesman said the company had received favorable reaction for its proposal to use Intersputnik from the State Department's telecommunications policy experts. But a spokesman at the State Department's Bureau of International Communications and Information Policy declined to comment, saying Mr. Eagleburger's letter speaks for itself and the department's position. He added that "the department would support an amended AT&T application for use of Intersputnik" for private communications traffic.

Intersputnik is run by a consortium of 15 Socialist countries, which it services exclusively. Those members use Intelsat to communicate with nonmembers.

AT&T currently derives less than 1% of its $3.5 billion in international communications revenue from the Soviet Union. It planned to link its network with Intersputnik to provide 150 circuits to augment the 32 now being used between the two countries and to build a satellite Earth station in Roaring Creek, Pa., to link with the service. Intelsat provides 20 of the 32 circuits; the other 12 are handled by underground and undersea cables owned by AT&T and numerous foreign telecommunications authorities.

Asked whether the State Department's decision on the Intersputnik plan would hamper AT&T's other projects with the Soviets, Charles Miller, AT&T vice president and liaison with the State Department, said, "down the road it could. At this point, we just don't know."
