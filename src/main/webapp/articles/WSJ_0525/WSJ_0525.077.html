Law -- Legal Beat: Proxy-Expert Carter Faces Suit by VPI

  • Author: Wade Lambert and Edward Felsenthal
  • Date: 05/25/90

VPI Group PLC, which acquired Donald Carter's proxy solicitation firm in 1987, sued Mr. Carter in federal court in Manhattan, N.Y., accusing the former proxy king of racketeering, fraud and breach of contract. The British company, formerly known as Valin Pollen International, claimed Mr. Carter's alleged misconduct inflated the price VPI paid when it acquired Carter Organization. The suit charges that because of the alleged improprieties, Carter Organization's credibility and business prospects were hurt. The suit seeks unspecified damages.

Mr. Carter, credited with transforming the stock-proxy business into a tool of corporate raiders, pleaded guilty in March to charges of grand larceny and personal income-tax evasion. Prosecutors said the charges stemmed from a scheme in which clients of Mr. Carter's firm were overcharged and billed for false expenses -- the same allegations made in VPI's suit.

Mr. Carter, who continued as Carter Organization chairman and chief executive officer when it was acquired by VPI, was fired after he entered his guilty plea in state court in Manhattan.

Carter Organization specializes in collecting shareholder votes or proxies for stockholder meetings, as well as identifying large stockholders that can be solicited in takeover battles.

John Tigue, Mr. Carter's attorney, said he hadn't seen the suit and couldn't comment.

In the suit, VPI said that prior to July 29, 1987, the closing date of its purchase of Carter Organization, Mr. Carter repeatedly directed his company to overbill clients for expenses. The suit alleged that Mr. Carter then created false documentation to cover up the overcharges.

VPI also alleged that Mr. Carter charged large amounts of personal expenses to Carter Organization, disguising them as business expenses. The suit said the personal expenses caused Carter Organization's tax liability to be understated and resulted in the firm's filing false tax returns.

It also accused Mr. Carter of making false statements in the sale of his company. VPI paid about $76 million in cash and stock to acquire the company, and it was to make additional payments based on Carter Organization's earnings. By not disclosing the overcharges, VPI says, Mr. Carter understated the company's liabilities and overstated its income.

In a statement released in London, VPI Chairman Angus Maitland said: "We have been more than willing to reach an out of court settlement with Mr. Carter, but in that effort we have been frustrated at every turn."

COPYRIGHTED WRITINGS can be used in critical biography, court rules.

A federal appeals court in New York reversed a lower court ruling that appeared to signify tighter restrictions on the use of copyrighted materials by authors and journalists.

The appeals court ruled that Carol Publishing Group's critical biography of L. Ron Hubbard, founder of the Church of Scientology, doesn't violate federal law by including copyrighted quotations from Mr. Hubbard's published writings.

The plaintiff was New Era Publications International, a Copenhagen publisher of Scientology books which holds the license on all of Mr. Hubbard's works. Mr. Hubbard died in 1986.

Coming after a series of decisions in New York federal courts that placed copyright concerns ahead of free-press interests, the opinion seems to reflect a broader interpretation of what constitutes fair use of copyrighted materials. The decision "reaffirms the vitality of the fair-use doctrine where material is being used for journalistic scholarly purposes," said Melvin Wulf, a laywer with the New York law firm Beldock Levine & Hoffman, which represented Carol Publishing.

The court's opinion relies in part on the fact that Mr. Hubbard's works had already been published. The U.S. Supreme Court has granted copyright protection to unpublished works. And in a much publicized 1986 case, the New York appeals court created a strong presumption against the use of unpublished works when it forbade a biographer of J.D. Salinger to quote from unpublished letters.

New Era Publications hasn't decided whether to appeal to the U.S. Supreme Court, says its lawyer, Michael Hertzberg of Rabinowitz, Boudin, Standard, Krinsky & Lieberman in New York.

DU PONT WINS ROUND in unusual dispute over rights to corn seeds.

Du Pont Co. and two co-plaintiffs won an early round in a complicated legal battle over who owns the rights to seeds developed by the University of Illinois.

Du Pont and Pfister Hybrid Corn Co. signed an exclusive licensing agreement with the university to develop seeds used for the production of corn that is high in oil. They sued Pioneer Hi-Bred International Inc., after the company obtained some seeds from a professor.

In recent years more companies have defended their legal claims to specific plant varieties. But this case is one of the first to address the issue of what rights seed breeders retain to novel varieties when they don't have a patent or a certificate issued by the Department of Agriculture, according to William T. Cahill of Chicago-based Phelan Pope & John, which represents the plaintiffs.

In 1989 Du Pont of Wilmington and Pfister, of El Paso, Ill., signed the agreement with the university. The companies agreed to pay the school royalties in exchange for the right to use the seeds it developed commercially. The companies have spent about $4 million on the project.

The seeds had been developed over a 30-year period by a professor, Denton Alexander. Using sophisticated genetic selection and breeding techniques, he developed a corn with an oil content at least two times greater than the amount in ordinary corn. Livestock and poultry fed the corn grow faster and produce more meat.

Six months after the agreement was signed, the university learned Mr. Alexander had given five special lines of the corn seed covered under the licensing agreement to Pioneer.

Mr. Alexander said he had been seriously ill at the time and regretted his mistake. Pioneer refused to return the seeds, which the company says is property in the public domain that it obtained properly. In their suit, the university, Du Pont and Pfister alleged that Pioneer misappropriated the seeds in violation of the Illinois Trade Secrets Act, among other claims.

In a ruling made public this week, federal Judge Harold A. Baker of Danville prohibited Pioneer from using any of the disputed seeds pending the outcome of the trial, scheduled for November.

Jerold Jacover of Chicago-based Willian Brinks Olds Hofer Gilson & Lione, which represents Pioneer, said the company will appeal.

The Pioneer lawyer said he is concerned that the company might have to dig up the seeds it had planted.

ACLU PRESIDENT will step down.

American Civil Liberties Union President Norman Dorsen said he won't seek another term as president of the 70-year-old public-interest legal organization.

Mr. Dorsen is a constitutional scholar and a professor at New York University Law School. In his 14 years as president of the ACLU, he headed its efforts to oppose the confirmation of Supreme Court nominee Robert Bork and successfully argued U.S. Supreme Court cases that established constitutional rights of illegitimate children and the right of juveniles to fair hearings in delinquency proceedings.

His successor will be chosen this fall.
