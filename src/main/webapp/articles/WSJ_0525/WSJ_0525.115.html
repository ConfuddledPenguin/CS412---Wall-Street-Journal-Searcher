B&C Intends to Sell Exco Brokerage Unit To Improve Finances

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/25/90

LONDON -- British & Commonwealth Holdings PLC, the troubled U.K. financial services group, said yesterday it plans to sell its money, government-securities and stock-brokerage subsidiary, Exco International PLC. Exco operates in 17 major financial centers and forms one of the core businesses of B&C. The unit is one of the larger players in the London money-broking business, a professional interbank market in which several big firms function as intermediaries among trading banks and financial institutions.

The decision follows last month's announcement that B&C intends to sell various assets in an attempt to strengthen its finances in the wake of massive losses at its Atlantic Computers subsidiary.

B&C is writing off the whole of its #550 million ($931.4 million) investment in the subsidiary and is moving to sell as much as #750 million of its own assets to reduce group debt to more manageable levels of #250 million.

The latest set of results -- for the year ended Dec. 31, 1988 -- show that Exco's pretax profit declined 39% to #48 million from #79 million the year before and that its sales rose just 3% to #205 million from #199 million. Its results for 1989 have been delayed due to its current financial woes. B&C paid #650 million for Exco in 1986.

In 1988, B&C's total pretax profit slipped 6% to #122.8 million from #130.9 million while sales rose 5.3% to #968.8 million from #919.7 million.

Exco may attract a price of between #230 million and #240 million, or roughly eight times recent pretax earnings, according to analysts at London securities firm Smith New Court. The estimate comes despite previous B&C views that the business could fetch #300 million or more. Said one analyst: "The world and his wife may have a look, but once you get to the end of the day, only a few people may want to bid."
