Dividend News: Bank of Boston Keeps Quarterly Dividend, Suprising Analysts

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/25/90

BOSTON -- Bank of Boston Corp. declared a quarterly dividend of 31 cents, surprising some analysts who had been anticipating a cut because of New England's embattled economy. But the bank said that beginning with the next quarter, its board would meet in the third month of the quarter, instead of the second -- which has been its custom -- to consider future dividends. The company explained that the action would bring the "practice into line with other major banks."

The move, however, was seen by some as a cautionary action, given the area's uncertain economic climate. "By pushing it to the third month, it will clearly give the board a better handle on the quarter's earnings and economic outlook," said Gerard Cassidy, bank analyst for Tucker, Anthony & R.L. Day Inc.

Mr. Cassidy, along with a few others, had thought the bank might cut its dividend to preserve capital. "If the economy continues on a decline we feel their numbers will reflect that weakness," he added.

For the first quarter, Bank of Boston reported net income of $43.6 million, or 56 cents a share, less than half the $89.1 million, or $1.23 a share, reported in the year-ago period.

Separately, Moody's Investors Service Inc. lowered its rating on approximately $450 million of the bank's debt to Ba-1 from Baa-3 and the long-term deposits of its bank subsidiaries to Baa-2 from Baa-1. The banks' ratings for short-term deposits were confirmed at P-2.

The rating actions reflect the continuing uncertainty associated with the corporation's exposures to the New England economy and the national real estate markets, Moody's said.

"Clearly, we are disappointed with the action taken today by Moody's," Peter J. Manning, the bank's chief financial officer said. "We remain steadfast in our focus on credit quality . . . we have the financial strength and are well positioned to deal with uncertainties in the current environment."
