Technology: Europe Defeats Japan's Proposal On TV Standard --- Move Indicates Each Side Will Separately Develop High-Definition Sets

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 05/25/90

WASHINGTON -- European countries, with some help from the U.S., defeated a Japanese proposal to set uniform standards for high-definition television, making it more likely that the new technology will develop differently on three continents. The decision to defer adopting an HDTV production standard, made at an international telecommunications conference in Duesseldorf, West Germany, was largely a symbolic blow to the Japanese. Japan Broadcasting Corp. plans to start high-definition TV broadcasts in Japan next summer, after the launch of two video satellites. So far, the Japanese are about one year behind schedule. European television makers, grouped together within the Eureka EU 95 project, are pushing their own standards. So are Zenith Electronics Corp. and others in the U.S.

High-definition TV refers to a host of new technologies aimed at producing video monitors with super-sharp images. The technology has become an important trade issue because of predictions that the new sets will come to dominate the consumer electronics market and produce huge business for makers of semiconductor chips. The first sets are expected to go on sale in the U.S. in the mid-1990s.

In 1986, Japan, backed by the U.S., proposed to set a standard for the production of high-definition videotapes. These standards would be important for producing such studio equipment as cameras and professional video-tape recorders, a relatively small market.

The Japanese also apparently hoped that getting their production standard approved would help win approval of a separate standard for transmitting high-definition shows to homes world-wide. Winning approval of that standard could mean tens of millions of dollars in royalty payments and give a leg-up to producers of the new television sets.

European countries, which wanted to develop their own standard to protect their electronics industries, defeated the 1986 proposal. Since that time, the U.S. backed away from the Japanese proposal, and the Europeans continued their opposition.

In the U.S., a production standard isn't likely to be adopted until after 1993, when the Federal Communications Commission is scheduled to chose a standard for HDTV broadcasts. It now seems likely that the U.S., Europe and Japan will use different production and transmission systems for the new technology.
