Grand Met Decides Against the Sale Of Its Alpo Unit

  • Author: Carlee R. Scott
  • Date: 05/25/90

Grand Metropolitan PLC said it decided against selling its Alpo Pet-foods unit because it couldn't find a buyer willing to pay the price it sought. The London-based food, beverage and retailing company said it received several bids for the Allentown, Pa., pet-food maker, which has been on the block for three months. But it was "unable to reach agreement as to the value of Alpo's recently launched cat-food business."

Industry analysts suggested that Grand Met may have had inflated expectations about the value of the business, which ranks sixth in the domestic pet-food industry, especially the cat-food line. They speculated that Grand Met hoped to fetch between $600 million and $700 million for the unit, which had sales in excess of $400 million in the year ended Sept. 30.

"Obviously, the bone of contention with potential buyers was cat food," said Roger Spencer, an analyst with PaineWebber Inc. "It hasn't been around long enough to build up a sustained value."

Alpo officials said Alpo's share of the cat-food market has steadily increased since its introduction last November. For the 12 weeks ended April 20, Alpo claims to have snared more than 9% of the market for premium canned cat food and more than 5% of the dry cat-food market.

Grand Met put the pet-food business up for sale at a time when competition in the mature industry has been especially fierce. Sales growth has been sluggish, reflecting pet owners' growing preference for cats over dogs.

Since 1983, the number of dogs in the U.S. has been steady at around 50 million, while the number of cats has climbed 25% to 57.9 million, according to industry estimates.

With its entry into the cat-food business, Grand Met is betting on this trend continuing. It is also marketing its cat food by using the popular cartoon character, Garfield, as its spokescat.

A spokeswoman for Grand Met said, "It makes more sense to keep the company {and expand it} and make it increasingly more profitable than to sell it at a price that doesn't reflect the value of the company." Previously, the company had said it wanted to focus on its other food businesses, including Haagen-Daaz, Pillsbury and Green Giant brands.

Alpo, acquired by Grand Met in 1980, was one of the last major pet-food makers to enter the cat-food market. Its canned dog food leads that segment of the market, although it has shrunk in recent years, according to estimates of analysts.

Several companies were rumored to be among the bidders for Alpo, including Quaker Oats Co., H.J. Heinz Co., and Nestle SA's Carnation Co. unit.

"Some awfully smart companies looked at it and said no," said PaineWebber's Mr. Spencer, who said he finds Grand Met's reversal interesting. "Maybe we are going back to real values in the evaluation process."
