Technology: Adobe Systems To Post Lower Net For Second Period

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 05/25/90

MOUNTAIN VIEW, Calif. -- Adobe Systems Inc. said it expects to report lower-than-anticipated fiscal second-quarter earnings, reflecting significantly lower revenue from its biggest customer, Apple Computer Inc. For the quarter ending June 1, the software company said it expects to report earnings ranging from 27 cents to 35 cents a share, on revenue of $32 million to $35 million. It added that it expected results for the remaining two quarters of the year to improve from second-quarter level, but that it expected fiscal 1990 results to be below analysts' current expectations.

In the 1989 fiscal-second quarter, Adobe had net income of $7.6 million, or 35 cents a share, on revenue of $28.1 million.

"It is startling," said Bruce Lupatkin, a software analyst at Hambrecht & Quist in San Francisco. Adobe stock will "take a major beating tomorrow," he added. The company estimates were dislosed after markets closed yesterday. Adobe, finished at $50.50 a share, up $1.50, in national over-the-counter trading.

Mr. Lupatkin said he had expected second-quarter per-share earnings of 51 cents and revenue of $42 million.

Adobe cited lower-than-expected revenue from Apple, reflecting a 23% drop in shipment of Apple products incorporating Adobe technology compared with the first quarter.

The company also said two other "significant customers" -- NEC Corp. and International Business Machines Corp. -- were experiencing reduced shipments because of new product development and delays.

Adobe develops and sells programs that create images and type fonts on desktop computer screens and printers. Last fall, Apple, along with software giant Microsoft Corp., said they would offer a product that would rival Adobe's technology, but Apple has continued to be Adobe's most important client. In March, IBM gave Adobe a boost when it said it would provide Adobe's printer software with its OS/2 operating system for IBM PCs.

"Adobe appears to be caught in a jetwash of a transition here," said Mr. Lupatkin, noting that companies such as IBM are "ramping up volumes, while Apple is fading into the sunset. It is a transitional issue."
