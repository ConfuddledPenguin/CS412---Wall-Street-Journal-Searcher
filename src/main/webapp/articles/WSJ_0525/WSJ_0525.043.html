Lufthansa Plans To Let Interflug Stay Separate

  • Author: Susan Carey
  • Date: 05/25/90

BRUSSELS -- Lufthansa says it doesn't plan to gobble up Interflug completely. But the big West German airline won't be a passive investor in its East German counterpart. If Lufthansa's bid for 26% of East German airline Interflug is approved by regulators, the West German carrier will treat Interflug as a "subsidiary profit center," Lufthansa's chairman said. But he added that "we want Interflug to remain a separate company."

In an interview here, Heinz Ruhnau also said that the letter of intent Lufthansa signed in March gives it the right of first refusal if additional shares of Interflug are offered to the public. And it's expected that another 23% of the shares will be put on the market, he said, leaving East Germany with a 51% stake in Interflug.

"If there is more to buy, I would have a right to buy more" shares, he said, adding that the letter of intent doesn't spell out how much more Lufthansa could purchase. But Mr. Ruhnau didn't say whether Lufthansa would boost its stake above 26%.

Any link with the East German carrier can only increase Lufthansa's might in a unified Germany. The large airline, which has 55,000 employees and carried 20.4 million passengers last year, already dominates not only its home market but also is a major player in Europe. Interflug, with its aged, mostly Soviet-built fleet, its 7,000 employees and 1.7 million passengers a year, hardly will be in a position to resist the wishes of its new benefactor.

As a result of this and other factors, Lufthansa's plans for Interflug now are being being examined by the West German Cartel Office, the European Community Commission and the newly formed Office for Competition in East Berlin.

An official of the West German competition agency said this week that he couldn't predict when those investigations will be completed. But he said East Germany already has agreed that Interflug shares won't include access to East German airports or to landing slots. Separating these rights from airline ownership probably will mute regulators' concerns that Lufthansa's market position would be unduly strengthened by the acquisition, the official said.

Mr. Ruhnau said there is much work ahead before Interflug can sell its shares. The airline currently is being valued by Western auditors so that a price can be set for the assets. Interflug probably will need to be restructured as well. Currently, the airline also runs five East German airports, does aerial photography and air ambulance work and its agricultural division sprays crops with a fleet of small aircraft.
