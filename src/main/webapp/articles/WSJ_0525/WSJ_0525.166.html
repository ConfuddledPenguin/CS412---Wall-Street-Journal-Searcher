Heard on the Street: GM Wins Wall Street's Applause On Strength of Plant Closings

  • Author: Joseph B. White
  • Date: 05/25/90

DETROIT -- At General Motors' annual meeting here today, executives probably will tell shareholders that things are looking up at the No. 1 auto maker. And for once, Wall Street will agree. GM stock "looks like a really terrific opportunity," says money manager Mario J. Gabelli in New York.

What is so terrific about an auto stock at a time of skidding car sales and a sluggish economy? The answer probably won't be spoken out loud at the meeting, because it involves two nasty words: plant closings.

Slowly and quietly, GM is moving to close enough plants to be able to operate its North American factories at full capacity, boosting its lagging domestic profits. The Street applauds this Ultra Slim-Fast routine and its potential long-term rewards, both for GM and for other auto makers.

"If I was pressed on what is the sleeper group for the second half, I would say autos," says Byron R. Wien, portfolio strategist for Morgan Stanley. "That is one group that has been beaten down."

John Neff, head of the $8 billion Windsor Fund, calls auto stocks "attractive, particularly for those who believe in the soft-landing" scenario for the economy.

GM isn't just waiting for the economy to bail it out, and that's what has analysts upbeat about the No. 1 auto maker. In a series of announcements dribbled out since early 1988, GM has "indefinitely idled" or laid plans to close four car-assembly plants and three truck plants over the next three years.

What's more, GM is converting three car plants to truck production. With those conversions, GM is eliminating a total of seven passenger-car plants -- just one less than Ford Motor has in the U.S.

The idled plants aren't officially closed and written off the books because that isn't allowed under the current labor contract. But the shutdowns are real, nonetheless, and they represent some 1.4 million vehicles that GM won't have to sell to get its plants running at full speed.

Philip Fricke of Prudential-Bache Securities argued in a recent report that GM needs just one more plant closing by 1993 to boost its factory operating rate to a healthy 90% from the current 80% level. "I think they are virtually there" in terms of solving the excess-capacity problem, says Mr. Fricke. That's why his report reaffirms a buy recommendation on GM, despite the industrywide slump in auto sales this spring.

At Morgan Stanley, analyst Scott Merlis says GM's capacity-cutting plans have led him to boost his earnings estimates for the company. GM's annual earnings for the 1990-1995 period should average $8 a share, Mr. Merlis now says, instead of the $7 a share he previously estimated. He, too, says now is the time to buy GM.

What's more, GM's retreat could provide something of a peace dividend for all the auto makers that compete in North America, says analyst John Casesa of Wertheim Schroeder.

"GM has been the spoiler for pricing in the industry," he says. The giant auto maker, through its frantic efforts to move merchandise by offering big rebates and other discounts, has forced domestic and foreign companies to discount their wares, too, hurting profits. "If they {GM} fix their capacity, it will help everybody out," Mr. Casesa says.

Indeed, by 1992, GM will have cut its North American car-building capacity to about 4.3 million cars from more than 6 million cars in 1986, according to figures compiled by the WEFA Group, an economic-forecasting concern in Bala Cynwyd, Pa. (Mr. Fricke puts his 1992-93 number at 4.4 million cars.) Last year, GM sold about four million North American-built cars in the U.S., Canada and overseas.

If GM can sustain this pace, its factories can run at 90% speed, spreading the company's fixed costs over a greater number of vehicles and boosting profitability. That isn't 100%, but bullish analysts say GM could run close to full speed in a strong sales year.

GM's stock has been swept up in the recent stock-market rally. Yesterday, the shares closed at 48 3/8, off 5/8, after Wednesday's reports of lower than expected mid-May car sales for the Big 3 auto makers.

But the shares still are up 9% from the end of April. Since Dec. 31, GM has outperformed both the Dow Jones Industrial Average and the Standard & Poor's 500 index.

Are there skeptics left? Absolutely.

Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst Joseph Phillippi says he's waiting for evidence of a "significant improvement in market share that I could view as sustainable" before changing his opinion that GM stock will lag the broader market.

The man in charge of GM's North American vehicle business, president-elect Lloyd E. Reuss, says the company's car market share could eventually rise back to 40% from the current 35.5% level. But GM's market-share projections have proved too optimistic for years, and some analysts say GM isn't through losing ground to Ford or the Japanese. If new products slated for the early 1990s don't capture the public's fancy better than those on which GM bet billions in the 1980s, the company's North American comeback could stall out.

Also, much of GM's remaining underused capacity is in relatively new plants that are building unpopular products, such as the GM-10 line of midsized cars. GM can ill afford to abandon these facilities. So this fall, analysts expect, GM's divisions will redouble efforts to sell the GM-10 models, which are the Chevrolet Lumina sedan, Pontiac Grand Prix, Oldsmobile Cutlass Supreme and Buick Regal. New ads, fresh exterior and interior designs and possibly lower prices could all be part of the strategy. All will cost big money.

But even skeptics say GM is on the right track -- and is finally moving to match production to market share rather than the other way around.

GM chairman-elect Robert C. Stempel's goal is to have GM's North American factories running as profitably as the European operations he helped turn around in the mid-1980s. Three of GM's European car plants now run on three-shift production schedules that allow them to boost output by nearly half. (U.S. plants generally run two shifts.)

Such aggressive moves helped GM to earn more than $1,100 average profit for every vehicle it sold in Europe last year. By contrast, the company earned just $277 for each vehicle sold in the U.S. and Canada. (Both figures include some profit from GM's finance and technology units, but a much larger share of GM's domestic profit comes from non-auto operations.)

GM bulls caution that Europe-like profits won't emerge from GM's North American operations overnight. But in the meantime, there's always GM's $3 annual dividend rate, which gives the stock a respectable 6.2% yield to tide over investors who have the patience to wait a while.
