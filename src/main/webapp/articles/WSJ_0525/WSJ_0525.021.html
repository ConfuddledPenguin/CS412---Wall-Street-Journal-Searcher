THE PLIGHT OF AFRICA

Africans lead shorter lives and have the worst infant-mortality and literacy rates of all developing regions, according to a new study by the United Nations Development Program.

The study rated personal well-being in 130 countries based on a formula combining life expectancy, adult literacy and per-capita income. Of the 44 countries the study listed as having low "human development," 33 are in Africa. The African nation with the highest rating was Mauritius, ranked No. 49.

Africa's per-capita income fell by a quarter in the 1980s, the report said. It predicted that nearly 400 million Africans will be living in extreme poverty by 1995, up from 250 million in 1985.

The study also found that some of the world's poorer countries such as Sri Lanka and Costa Rica have a higher quality of life than wealthier ones such as Brazil because they put the poor ahead of the middle class or the military.

JAPAN-OHIO AGREEMENT

Japan agreed to send trade experts to Ohio to promote imports from that state. Ohio Gov. Richard F. Celeste signed the agreement with an official of the Japan External Trade Organization, which has decided that the state-based approach is Japan's best bet for reducing its $49 billion trade surplus with the U.S. Under the agreement, the Japanese representatives will be sent to Ohio for one-year stays to advise government trade officials and companies that want to export to Japan. The trade organization has already signed a similar agreement with Indiana.

ROME AIR-TRAFFIC DISRUPTION

Air-traffic controllers at Rome's Leonardo da Vinci airport announced strikes for tomorrow and Sunday and from June 4 through June 6. The strikes are expected to seriously hamper flight schedules in other Italian airports. Tens of thousands of fans will be arriving in Italy over the next two weeks to attend matches of the month-long World Cup soccer competition, which begins June 8. The controllers are protesting what they call unfair working conditions.

INVESTING IN THE PHILIPPINES

Foreign investment in the Philippines reached $47.9 million in the first quarter of the year, up 62.5% from $29.5 million in the like period a year earlier, the Central Bank of the Philippines reported. Britain topped the list of foreign investors during the quarter with $14.8 million, or 30.8% of the total, followed by the U.S., with $12.7 million, and Japan, with $7.8 million.

A MADRID PLAN FAILS

Motorists in traffic-congested Madrid ignored calls for a "Day Without Cars" yesterday, snarling the Spanish capital with traffic jams as usual. Police said traffic decreased by only 3%. Extra commuter trains remained idle, and the number of passengers on subway services did not increase. Madrid's commuters have been urged by Mayor Agustin Rodriguez Sahagun to use public transport on weekdays and save cars for weekends.

`GODDESS' PROJECT ENDS

Organizers of the radio ship Goddess of Democracy, which planned to broadcast pro-democracy messages to China, are abandoning their mission. Pascal Dupont, an organizer of the mission, was scheduled to announce the termination of the project at a Tokyo news conference today, the Foreign Correspondents' Club of Japan said in a press release. "The ship, still in Taiwan, has been turned away from Hong Kong, made unwelcome in Taiwan and told by the Japanese it would not be welcomed. . . . In the face of this opposition, the organizers have decided to give it up," the release said.

U.N. REFUGEE AGENCY IS BROKE

The Office of the High Commissioner for Refugees, a United Nations agency that depends on voluntary contributions from governments, said its financial situation is so desperate that it could "result in unacceptable suffering and could even take its toll in terms of human lives."

The agency said it needed $80 million to make ends meet this year. Staffers said the agency cannot afford to buy food or blankets for 800,000 Mozambicans who have fled civil strife into neighboring Malawi. Officials point out that the agency, which was set up in 1951 to deal with one million displaced people in Europe, now cares for 15 million on five continents.

The agency's 43-nation Executive Committee has recommended that the agency limit the scope of its operations to the survival of refugees -- food, water and housing -- and leave the education and self-help programs that have proliferated over the years to other specialized bodies.

In Japan, the Foreign Ministry said 427 Chinese boat people who disguised themselves as Vietnamese refugees to seek jobs will be sent home today.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

Most West Germans favor slowing down German unification, according to a poll published in Stern magazine. Though most support eventual union, many fear haste will jeopardize their affluence, since East Germany will be heavily reliant on West German financial aid. . . . Commenting on the plans of a Hungarian businessman to turn Budapest into the Bangkok of Eastern Europe, replete with brothels and "sex boats," Interior Minister Balazs Horvath said prohibiting the scheme would be useless, "but we hope very much to create a society in which there is no demand for such services."
