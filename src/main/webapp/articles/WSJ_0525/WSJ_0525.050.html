Who's News: Equitable Life Financial Chief Quits in Management Shakeup, Costcutting

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 05/25/90

NEW YORK -- Equitable Life Assurance Society of the U.S. is being shaken up as management moves to revamp the executive suite and slash expenses. Yesterday, Equitable said chief financial officer, Glenn T. Gettier, resigned to pursue other business interests. The resignation of Mr. Gettier, 47 years old, follows John B. Carter's abrupt departure from the company.

Mr. Gettier was unavailable for comment yesterday.

Last week, Equitable announced that Mr. Carter, 55, was retiring as president and chief executive officer, effective immediately. The company said Mr. Carter had no immediate future plans. Mr. Carter's duties were assumed by Richard H. Jenrette, the company's chairman and chief investment officer.

People within the company who didn't want to be identified say Equitable's board had been extremely displeased with Mr. Carter's inability to improve the company's financial condition consistently in recent years.

Now that Mr. Jenrette has been installed as Equitable's top executive, he wants to move quickly to streamline other management positions and reduce expenses, a problem that has plagued the insurer in recent years.

Equitable said Thomas M. Kirwan will assume the chief financial officer duties for the entire company as part of a program to consolidate three separate financial oversight groups into one unit. Mr. Kirwan had previously served as an executive vice president and chief financial officer of Equitable Insurance Cos., Equitable's main operating unit.

"The merging of the financial reporting functions will streamline operations, reduce costs and will provide improved financial controls and information," said Mr. Jenrette.

A companywide program to cut costs, which began late last year and is known around Equitable as "Project Focus," appears to have intensified in the last week. Company employees quoted Mr. Jenrette as saying in the last week that "over the next 100 days, the company has to cut $100 million in expenses."

In an interview with The Wall Street Journal last week, he said that Equitable, the nation's third-largest life insurance company, had cut expenses by about $50 million in the last two years and had plans to reduce costs by an equal amount this year.

As part of Project Focus, agents' commissions on incentive life insurance policies and certain health insurance plans have been reduced. The company said a new incentive life product was being designed that would carry higher commissions. Travel has been severely curtailed and operating departments' purchasing practices and expenses are being closely monitored.

There are also rumors at the company that incentive bonuses for 1990 will be reduced or eliminated. Middle managers could get bonuses ranging between 12% and 14% of their salaries, while executives at the vice-president level and higher might receive year-end bonuses of 20% to 50% of their annual pay. A company spokesperson said decisions on bonuses aren't made until year's end.

In 1989, Equitable reported a much smaller increase in surplus, which is equivalent to a decrease in earnings. Surplus increased $14.9 million to $1.33 billion. A year earlier, Equitable's surplus increased $63 million to $1.31 billion. Mutual companies don't report earnings as such.

The company attributed the results to a downturn in its guaranteed income contract business, which produced a loss that it didn't specify. Equitable also cited a one-time, after-tax charge of about $20 million, related to the decline in market value of its investment in Lomas Financial Corp. A still high expense level contributed to the lower 1989 results.

Raymond L. Colotti, chief financial officer for the Equitable Investment Corp., which oversees the company's five investment units, will be transferred to Mr. Kirwan's group as part of the consolidation.
