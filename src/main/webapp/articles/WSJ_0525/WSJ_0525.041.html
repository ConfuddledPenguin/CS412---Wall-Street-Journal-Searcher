South Korea Growth In 1st-Period GNP Topped Expectations

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/25/90

SEOUL, South Korea -- South Korea recorded a larger-than-expected 10.3% growth in gross national product during the first three months of this year, according to the Bank of Korea. The central bank's preliminary report said the nation's GNP in the first quarter of 1990 amounted to 28.4 trillion won ($40.46 billion), up 10.3% from the previous year's 25.7 trillion won, at 1985 constant prices. In the first quarter of last year, GNP rose 6.2%. The two-digit number is far higher than the government's original projection of 6.5% to 7%.

In all of 1989, GNP rose by 6.7%, far down from an annual average of 12% during the 1986-1988 period.

Bank officials attributed the sharp increase in GNP growth so far this year to brisk performance in the manufacturing and construction sectors. The comparatively low growth rate during the first quarter of last year was a contributing factor to the sharp increase, they add.

But officials say the economy isn't firmly in the track of recovery. The rise wasn't led by exports, which have traditionally been the engine of growth in South Korea, but by strong private consumption and a temporary construction boom, spurred by a housing construction rush.

The construction sector grew 39.1% during the first quarter from a year ago, in contrast to a 10.2% rise in the first quarters of 1988 and 1989. The manufacturing sector posted 7.1% growth, up from the year-ago first quarter level of 1.8%.

The utility sector rose 18.1%, compared with 14.2% a year ago, largely because of higher electricity and gas consumption.

The agriculture, forest and fishery sectors, however, declined 3.6%, compared with a 9.9% increase a year earlier.

Private consumption rose by 11.9% largely because of strength in sales of automobiles and other durable consumer goods as well as an increase in spending on services. Fixed investment rose by 32.1% largely because of heavy investment in construction and facilities.

Merchandise exports rose a mere 0.1% because of slow exports of automobiles, toys and other strategic export items, while imports soared by 15.2%.

The service sector rose by 9%, compared with 7.2% a year ago.
