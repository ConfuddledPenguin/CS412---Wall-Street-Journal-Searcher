Politics & Policy: Navy Reopens Inquiry of USS Iowa Blast After Gun Powder Ignites During Test

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 05/25/90

WASHINGTON -- Bags of powder from 16-inch Navy guns ignited in a test yesterday, blowing a hole in the service's explanation for last year's fatal gun-turret explosion aboard the battleship Iowa. Navy Secretary Lawrence Garrett ordered an immediate halt to firing of the large-diameter guns on all four of the Navy's World War II-vintage battleships and reopened the search for the cause of the April 1989 Iowa explosion, which killed 47 sailors.

The Navy originally concluded that the cause of the Iowa explosion "most probably" was an intentional act by one of the ship's enlisted men, Clayton Hartwig, who died in the explosion. Lawmakers weren't satisfied with that explanation, however, and insisted on further tests.

At those tests yesterday, Secretary Garrett said in a brief statement, there was an "unexplained ignition" of bags of powder used to fire such guns.

Pentagon officials said yesterday's "follow-up" tests were intended to evaluate a theory, developed by scientists and engineers at the Energy Department's Sandia Laboratory, that friction inside the gun could have sparked the Iowa explosion and the resulting intense fire. According to these officials, the tests simulated the force of ramming five fully loaded bags of powder into the firing mechanism of the battleship's gun with more force than Navy procedures call for.

Officials said the first 17 simulated loadings didn't create any explosion, but all of the powder bags exploded during the 18th test.

The Navy's embarrassing reversal came just one day before officials from Sandia and the General Accounting Office are scheduled to challenge some of the service's basic conclusions about the accident in testimony before the Senate Armed Services Committee.

Mr. Garrett and Navy brass apparently hope to deflect some of that criticism by quickly ordering a further investigation of their own. But Sen. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio), one of the strongest critics of the Navy's Iowa probe, said in an interview that the Navy waited too long to reopen the probe.

"It's long overdue for them to admit their mistake . . . and acknowledge that they had absolutely no credible evidence" to support the theory that Mr. Hartwig purposely caused the explosion, the Ohio lawmaker said. "It's obvious that they know what's coming in the GAO report."

Rear Admiral Richard Milligan, who was in charge of the initial investigation, has maintained that months of laboratory tests uncovered "unique foreign material" on the projectile that was in the Iowa's gun barrel at the time of the accident, indicating that the explosion was caused by some kind of detonator rather than by unstable powder or improper handling of the charge by the ship's crew.

Scientists at Sandia, however, recently told Pentagon officials and lawmakers that the laboratory's series of test firings showed that friction from improper handling could have been enough to ignite the powder used in the battleships' guns.
