Taiwan's Plan to Open Banking Industry To Foreigners May Increase Competition

  • Author: Cynthia Owens
  • Date: 05/25/90

HONG KONG -- Taiwan's plans to give foreign banks greater access to the country's long-protected banking industry during the next year are likely to heat up competition in a sector already simmering from recent liberalization measures. "The whole industry is going to get blown wide open," says John Engle, chief country officer for Hoare Govett Securities Taiwan Ltd. "It's going to be a very big year."

That isn't to say Taipei will soon be edging out Hong Kong or Tokyo as regional financial centers. The wheels of change for the country's insulated financial sector were set in motion only last year, with laws designed to liberalize financial services, and the current proposals will take time to bear fruit. Moreover, while the industry is relatively underdeveloped and the amount of business expected to expand, the onslaught of new banks at a time when the economy is being hit by setbacks means some of them may have to swallow red ink for a while.

"It's a very competitive market already," says Edward Chien, vice president of Hua Nan Commercial Bank, one of the country's largest commercial banks.

It's a safe bet that the country's large, government-owned commercial banks will have a dominant role. Their main domestic rivals will be the regional banks and the five to 10 new domestic institutions opening sometime next year. And as foreign banks take advantage of the increased access, they will assume small but significant roles.

Among those outside entries, Japanese banks are piquing interest by coming back to Taiwan after a 30-year absence motivated by economic and political considerations.

The huge government-owned commercial banks have an edge with their existing branch networks countrywide. They also are benefiting from the government's efforts to eliminate the country's underground investment houses. The collapse of many of these operations has sent depositors scurrying for the safety of the commercial banks.

The government also is now encouraging its commercial banks to develop an international profile and help channel Taiwan's huge cash reserves and corporate earnings into investments around the world.

"We are very, very strong in many areas," says Mr. Chien, who thinks the regulatory changes will enhance the commercial banks' advantages.

But government ownership also sometimes undercuts their competitiveness. A government plan to sell part of its stakes in three of the biggest commercial banks, including Hua Nan, flopped early this month.

Only a few of the investors lucky enough to win the right to some of the shares actually showed up to buy them because the market price had sunk far below the sale price announced by the government weeks before.

The failure startled analysts at the time because the banks' shares had been so popular that the government was forced to hold a lottery to determine who could buy the shares.

But the tepid response now is thought to reflect poor planning and a weak stock market rather than investor sentiment on the future health of the banking industry. It also reflected the common perception that bank shares in general are overpriced and underscored the uncertainty those old behemoths face in trying to evolve from a protected market to a competitive one.

Even if the government goes ahead with the sale as expected in the next few months, the state will remain majority shareholder, and it may take two years to complete the privatization plan for those and other state-owned banks.

Meanwhile, the commercial banks are saddled with government-imposed budgets and hiring policies, which bank officials fear will limit their ability to compete with new, unfettered banks and some of the aggressive regional banks.

"The pace of privatization has slowed, and perhaps the government will go ahead and approve new banks while we are still hand-tied," says Mr. Chien. "We are looking for a free hand to do our own business."
