Black & Decker Is Said to Pick Hammes Of Chrysler to Lead Power-Tool Division

  • Author: Melinda Grenier Guiles and Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 05/15/90

Black & Decker Corp. is expected to name Michael N. Hammes, Chrysler Corp.'s vice president of international operations, to head its largest unit, the $2 billion power-tools division. Joseph E. Cappy, Chrysler vice president of brand development, will succeed Mr. Hammes, according to Chrysler officials who asked not to be identified. This could lead to dismantling a new marketing organization Chrysler set up just six months ago.

A Chrysler spokesman refused to confirm the moves. "It's all rumor and speculation and as you know we don't comment on these things," he said."

A Black & Decker spokeswoman said she couldn't comment on "who had been selected" to head the power tool unit, "or when any announcement would be made." But, she added, it has been no secret that Black & Decker has been searching for a "global manager type" for its core power-tools business since February. That's when George M. Sherman, who turned the division around with innovative cordless and professional products backed by savvy marketing campaigns, became president of Danaher Corp., the Washington, D.C., industrial products company controlled by Steven and Mitchell Rales.

The Chrysler officials say Black & Decker has actively courted Mr. Hammes, a Chicago native with an undergraduate degree from Georgetown University's School of Foreign Service and almost 30 years experience with Ford Motor Co. and Chrysler, which he joined in 1986.

The 48-year-old Mr. Hammes's last jobs at Ford were vice president of truck operations for Ford of Europe and president/managing director of Ford's Mexican subsidiary.

During his four years with Chrysler, however, Mr. Hammes's work has consisted mainly of overseeing increased sales and joint venture activities. Chrysler hasn't any assembly plants outside North America but is establishing several overseas production ventures including one with Renault S.A. for Jeep vehicles in Europe and another with Steyr-Daimler-Puch of Austria for minivans.

At Black & Decker, based in Towson, Md., Mr. Hammes is expected to pick up Mr. Sherman's title of executive vice president and president of the Power Tools and Home Improvement Group. This is considered the company's second-highest post behind Chairman and Chief Executive Officer Nolan Archibald.

Replacing Mr. Sherman will free Mr. Archibald to focus on divesting some operations following last year's acquisition of Emhart Corp., a move designed to broaden Black & Decker's product lines and expand overseas. But the roughly $2 billion acquisition saddled the company with high debt and several unrelated businesses that have been put up for sale -- an effort that, so far, has been slow and frustrating.

Mr. Cappy, who is expected to succeed Mr. Hammes, was named Chrysler's vice president for brand development only last November. At that time, the Highland Park, Mich., auto maker streamlined its marketing operations, eliminating one vice presidency and several middle management positions.

Mr. Cappy's role in the new structure was to assure that Chrysler created distinct products for each of its six brands, a role that seemed to duplicate responsibilities in other operations. John B. Damoose was given responsibility for developing distinctive marketing strategies and the title of vice president, brand marketing.

It couldn't immediately be determined whether Chrysler will replace Mr. Cappy, who joined Chrysler in 1987 when the No. 3 auto maker acquired American Motors Corp. Some Chrysler executives are said to favor even more streamlining to cut costs and improve efficiency. This could mean development activities currently reporting to Mr. Cappy could be merged with existing design, sales, marketing and engineering operations.
