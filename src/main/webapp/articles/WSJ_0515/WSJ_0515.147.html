Habsburg Feldman Auction of Paintings Brings Dismal Results

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/15/90

NEW YORK -- The first auction of impressionist and modern paintings of the spring art auction season met with dismal results. Habsburg, Feldman Inc., a small, upstart Geneva-based auction house saw only about 15% of its paintings sell here last night.

And while the auction was relativly small in dollar terms, it set a somber stage for the larger multimillion dollar art auctions to be held here at Christie's International PLC and Sotheby's Holdings Inc. later this week.

Art dealers stress that this first sale wasn't a true test of the strength of the art market. They contended that unlike the evening sales later this week, last night's auction was filled with art works by such weaker painters as Maurice Utrillo and Maurice de Vlaminck and by some lesser works by such renowned painters as Claude Monet. Nonetheless, the percentage of unsold works was almost double that of Habsburg Feldman's similar sale in November.

The mood of the room grew increasingly leaden as painting after painting was passed up by bidders. At one point, to lighten the mood, auctioneer Archduke Geza von Habsburg reminded the crowd that bidding was allowed.

In several cases, in a break with auction tradition, sellers appeared to have set the minimum price they would accept at the high end of the presale estimate. That indicates that the auction house expected much stronger bidding.

The top lot of the sale was an oil on canvas of two women painted by Pierre-Auguste Renoir. It fetched $2.6 million, at the high end of the presale estimate of $2.3 million to $2.6 million.

The next major sale will be tonight at Christie's. Up for bid is Vincent van Gogh's evocative and melancholic masterpiece "The Portrait of Doctor Gachet." It is estimated to sell for $40 million or more.
