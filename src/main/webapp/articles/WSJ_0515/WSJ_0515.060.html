Technology: Cray Research Rolls Out New Generation Of Supercomputers Aimed at Entry-Level

  • Author: Richard Gibson
  • Date: 05/15/90

Cray Research Inc., seeking to protect its dominance in the supercomputer market, is introducing a new generation of relatively inexpensive, entry-level machines. The machines will start at $2.2 million, a price that puts them in competition with entry-level technology from Japanese rival Fujitsu Ltd. as well as intensive-computing devices from such domestic competitors as International Business Machines Corp., based in Armonk, N.Y., and Convex Computer Corp., based in Richardson, Texas.

Called the Y-MP2E, the latest Cray supercomputers are a downward extension of the company's primary line of Y-MPs (for multiprocessor). Until now, the cheapest Y cost $5 million while the eight-processor version -- the world's largest -- cost nearly $23 million.

The new machines contain the same processing and memory technology, and run at the same six nanosecond (billionths of a second) cycle speed, as other members of the Y family. But the small versions won't need the cumbersome cooling "plumbing" its larger siblings require. Instead, they can be either air-cooled or water-cooled, allowing them to fit into current computer-room environments.

Four years ago, Minneapolis-based Cray rolled out a $2.5 million machine called the X-MP/14se, which the company then described as a "starter set." Now discontinued, it was intended to offer supercomputing to potential users who either couldn't afford or didn't need full-sized machines. But the one-processor 14se was limited in its capabilities, and couldn't connect to large data-storage devices.

Those limitations as well as the development of a new generation of technology led to that machine's demise.

The 2E also replaces earlier one-processor and two-processor Y machines, which didn't offer air cooling and which cost twice as much. Moreover, the new machines offer larger memories than the previous models.

Cray said it is positioning the new small machines for two types of customers -- those looking for an entry into supercomputing and those current users with computing problems too small for their current supercomputers but too complex for easy solution elsewhere. Thus, the latter will be able to "download" those discrete problems to the small Y.

Supercomputers typically are used to tackle problems too complex for regular mainframe machines; often the problems are sophisticated mathematical models used to design such products as automobiles, airplanes and drugs, or simulations of such events as a nuclear explosion.

Although the company says it made two-thirds of the world's current supercomputer systems, it has been facing increasing competition at the low end of the market, particularly as it fights for more commercial customers. The new line extension seeks to "offer customers real supercomputer power at affordable prices," said Marcelo Gumucio, Cray's president.

Acknowledging what he called supercomputing's changing landscape, Cray's chairman and chief executive officer, John A. Rollwagen, said the company was responding to the increasing popularity of computer networks while continuing "aggressive development at the high end of the performance spectrum."

Mr. Gumucio said shipments of the 2E would begin within three or four months but declined to estimate how many the company was projecting to sell or lease in the next year.

Sanford C. Bernstein & Co. analyst Barry Willman estimates that the new machine will contribute only marginally to revenue this year but forecasts about 15 systems being shipped next year. Gary Smaby, a Minneapolis-based analyst who heads his own company, said he expects margins on the new products to be "modestly lower" than on larger Cray machines.

Cray stock closed at $48.75, up 75 cents, in composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange.
