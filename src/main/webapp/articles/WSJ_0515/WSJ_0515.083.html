Pennsylvania Bill Would Bar Insurers Who Quit Auto Market From Other Lines

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 05/15/90

HARRISBURG, Pa. -- Two state senators proposed legislation that would bar insurers from selling new policies in all other insurance lines if they decided to pull out of the auto insurance business in Pennsylvania. There are also plans to amend an antitrust statute, being considered by the state senate judiciary committee, removing a current partial exemption for the insurance industry. Pennsylvania is the only state without an antitrust law.

However, Pennsylvania's fight to keep auto insurers from bailing out of the state was dealt a blow when a state court judge ruled last Friday that current Pennsylvania law doesn't prevent an insurer from pulling out of the auto-insurance market if staying costs the company more money than it would make.

The measure unveiled yesterday is designed to block three insurers who sent nonrenewal notices to their auto insurance policyholders in Pennsylvania. The proposed bill is similar to a Massachusetts law that's being challenged in federal court by Aetna Life & Casualty Co., which claims it's unconstitutional.

A new auto insurance bill, signed into law in February, requires premium rate rollbacks of 10% and as much as 22%, if drivers choose a no-fault provision limiting their right to sue. The measure proposed yesterday would be retroactive to Jan. 1. The bill will be introduced in the senate later this week.

Auto insurers "who have pulled the rug out from under Pennsylvania motorists should not be doing other new business in the state," said State Senator Stewart J. Greenleaf, one of the two sponsors of the new bill. If losing new business in all their other insurance lines isn't enough of a deterrent to keep auto insurers in Pennsylvania, then Sen. Greenleaf said he would ask the legislature to bar companies from all other business in this state.

Aetna is one of the three insurers who want to pull out of the auto insurance market. It said that the rate rollbacks ordered by the new auto insurance law would force it to lose about $20 million over the next 12 months. The Hartford, Conn.-based company is the fifth-largest insurer working in Pennsylvania, with 200,000 policyholders.

Harleysville Mutual Insurance Co. and a unit of Safeco Corp. have also sent nonrenewal notices to their policyholders in Pennsylvania. They also claim the rollback would hurt them.

Officials at Harleysville and Safeco couldn't comment until they'd reviewed a copy of the proposed legislation. Aetna couldn't be reached.

In ruling on a suit brought by Harleysville against the state insurance commissioner, a Commonwealth Court said if an insurer sustains "a signficant loss if they continue to issue private passenger automobile insurance policies, that's "a good reason" for nonrenewals.

The state insurance department immediately appealed the ruling to the state supreme court. Some state officials fear a mass exodus by auto insurers if the lower court decision is upheld.
