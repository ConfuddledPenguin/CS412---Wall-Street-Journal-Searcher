Letters to the Editor: `English Rule' Could Cripple Victims' Rights

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/15/90

In his April 11 Rule of Law column, "The Good News Is There Are No More `Frivolous Lawsuits,'" L. Gordon Crovitz argues for adoption of "English rule" on legal costs, whereby plaintiffs who lose in court are forced to pay defense costs and attorneys' fees. This country has never accepted such a doctrine because of the chilling effect it would have on victims' rights to obtain fair compensation in court. Even those with strong cases would fear pursuing a legitimate claim, on the chance they would lose and be economically devastated by having to pay legal costs on top of medical bills. Mr. Crovitz should review the article published in this newspaper on May 15, 1986, by James L. Gattuso of the conservative Heritage Foundation. Mr. Gattuso demonstrates that far from encouraging frivolous filings, as Mr. Crovitz argues, the contingency-fee system actually screens out baseless suits. Since plaintiff attorneys get paid only upon winning, they have no incentive to file or drag out insignificant lawsuits.

The contingency-fee system and the absence of draconian measures such as the "English rule" have served to make our civil-justice system the global model for emerging democracies throughout the world. At least the sick and injured have a chance for justice here. The same can't be said for citizens of most other nations.

Joanne Doroshow

Bhopal Justice Campaign

Santa Monica, Calif.
