Economy: Flat Inventories Of $794 Billion Posted for March --- Stockpile Buildup Avoided; Month's Sales Rose 0.6% To $539.13 Billion Total

  • Author: Katherine Walsh
  • Date: 05/15/90

WASHINGTON -- Business inventories were virtually unchanged in March, the Commerce Department reported, signaling that managers are avoiding any buildup in stockpiles of unsold goods that could lead to a recession. Overall inventories totaled $794 billion during the month, compared with $794.02 billion in February. Manufacturers' inventories declined 0.3%, while those of retailers and wholesalers increased 0.3% and 0.2% respectively.

Sales, meanwhile, rose 0.6% during the month, to $539.13 billion, following a 1.4% increase to $535.85 during February. According to the report, it would take 1.47 months to sell all the inventories if sales continued at the March rate. The last time the inventories-to-sales ratio was so low was January 1989 when it also was 1.47, the lowest recorded ratio since 1982.

Sara Johnson, managing economist at Data Resources Inc. in Lexington, Mass., said the figures suggest there are no unwanted inventories. Excessive inventories indicate that production is outpacing sales, an imbalance that can result in production cutbacks by manufacturers.

Retail sales fell 0.2% in March, to $149.55 billion, following an increase of less than 0.1% in February to $149.90 billion. Ms. Johnson attributed the March drop to the fluctuating automobile market. However, she said retailers need to be aware that consumers are spending cautiously. Manufacturers' and wholesalers' sales increased 1.1% and 0.8% respectively.

"The report generally confirms that the economy is continuing to expand, but at a gradual pace," said Ms. Johnson, later adding that the report "wouldn't give the Federal Reserve any impetus to ease its monetary policy."

Overall, the report depicts a "dull picture," said Neil Soss, chief economist at First Boston Corp. Mr. Soss predicted modest growth in production for a short period and says he believes that inventories are under control.

All the numbers are adjusted for normal seasonal fluctuations. But Mr. Soss said inventory figures for the past few months have been distorted because of unusual weather fluctuations, including both unseasonably cold and unseasonably warm weather.
