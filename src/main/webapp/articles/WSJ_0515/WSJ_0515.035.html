Here's the Latest Dope to Make Thrifts' Troubles Go Up in Smoke

  • Author: Carrie Dolan
  • Date: 05/15/90

As the cost of bailing out the battered savings and loan industry continues to escalate, one group has suggested letting the whole thing go to pot. A California group called Business Alliance for Commerce in Hemp (BACH) says the plant, the leaves of which make marijuana, could "save" troubled thrifts.

In a release titled "Hemp and the Economy," the organization says "the savings and loan industry could recoup its losses . . . by allowing debtors to invest in hemp."

"Marijuana income for 1988 was $41.6 billion," the group claims, adding that "money generated by sales of relaxational hemp alone could pay off" a sizable portion of the bailout. "Estimates of the total legitimate revenues that would be generated by legal commerce in nonsmoking hemp range from 10 to 100 times that amount." The low end of that range would just about do the trick: The government now estimates the S&L bailout will cost between $400 billion and $500 billion.

Nonrelaxational uses for hemp are numerous, the group says. It can be made into fuel or cooking oil ("So long OPEC and oil shortages]") or into clothing (fabrics made from hemp hold "shape as well as polyester" and can be woven as "smooth as silk or intricate as lace"). Dale Gieringer, coordinator for California's branch of the National Organization for the Reform of Marijuana Laws, says hemp shorts and shirts imported from China are "one of our best sellers."

BACH appeals to nationalist feelings, asserting that the Declaration of Independence and the U.S. Constitution were probably both originally drafted on paper made from hemp. And "hemp could save the family farm in America. But special interest groups have kept it out of the reach of our patriotic and hard working farmers."

Not out of the hands of all farmers, of course. "Although illegal, cannabis is already the largest cash crop in several states, including California," the group says. A spokeswoman for the state's multi-agency Campaign Against Marijuana Planting effort concurs.

Still, the prospect of turning to hemp to help thrifts remains hazy, according to federal regulators. "I see political problems" with using marijuana to support failed S&Ls, says a spokesman for the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. in Washington. "My alternative is to make it available to taxpayers to ease the trauma of paying for the S&L bailout."
