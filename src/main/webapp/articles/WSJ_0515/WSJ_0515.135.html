Wilfred American Seeks Protection Under Chapter 11

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/15/90

NEW YORK -- Wilfred American Educational Corp., the beauty-school operator, sought protection against lawsuits from creditors by filing under Chapter 11 of the federal bankruptcy law. "The company was compelled to take this action" for a number of reasons, said Philip E. Jakeway Jr., chairman and chief executive officer. These include denials of the company's claims for reimbursement by the federal Education Department. Wilfred derives much of its revenue from government student-loan programs. Also forcing the filing were legal costs, operating losses and changes in government policies, Mr. Jakeway said in a statement. Wilfred officials were unavailable for comment.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Wilfred's shares closed unchanged at 56.25 cents. The Big Board said yesterday that in light of the bankruptcy-law filing, it is reviewing whether to continue listing Wilfred's shares.

The company's problems stem from an October 1988 indictment of Mr. Jakeway and a number of other employees. A Tampa, Fla., federal grand jury charged them with misusing financial-aid funds. In March 1989, nine of the other employees entered so-called Alford pleas, agreeing to plead guilty to certain charges but denying any wrongdoing. The trial for the remaining defendants is scheduled for January 1991.

In October 1989, a federal grand jury in Massachusetts indicted the company on mail fraud charges. The company denied the charges, and the case is scheduled for trial in June.
