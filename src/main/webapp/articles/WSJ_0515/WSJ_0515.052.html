Technology: Symantec to Buy Peter Norton, Software Concern

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 05/15/90

Symantec Corp., a maker of software for personal computers, said it agreed to buy Peter Norton Computing Inc., a closely held software concern, for about $70 million in stock. The accord is evidence that smaller software firms, concerned over the possibility of two behemoths -- Lotus Development Corp. and Microsoft Corp. -- dominating the industry, are eagerly looking for acquisitions. Historically, the business of supplying software for personal computers has been a haven for smaller companies. Acquisitions generally have been avoided out of a fear that they might disrupt the sensitive process of software development.

Symantec, of Cupertino, Calif., said shareholders of Peter Norton Computing will receive 2.9 million shares of Symantec common. The combined company will be the leading supplier of a category of software called utilities, which users of personal computers frequently buy to improve the basic operation of their machines.

Analysts said the accord was a surprise because Peter Norton, of Santa Monica, Calif., until recently had been preparing to file for an initial public stock offering of its own. But they said that the rapid growth of Microsoft, the biggest supplier of PC software, and last month's move by Lotus Development to purchase Novell Inc. for $1.5 billion, have many software companies worried that they may not possess the size to compete effectively.

"There's a drive towards consolidation for that very reason," said David Readerman, an analyst at Shearson Lehman Hutton.

Regarding Peter Norton's preparations for an initial public stock offering, Ron Posner, chief executive officer, said the company even had told Salomon Brothers it would serve as one of the underwriters.

But Mr. Posner thinks it unlikely the offering would have reaped more than Symantec's offer, which he describes as "the upper end of the scale." And had the company remained independent, he said, it needed to build a national sales force, a costly process.

Mr. Posner said the tie with a bigger company -- Symantec had $50 million in sales in its fiscal year ended March 31, twice the amount of Peter Norton -- is an important benefit of the merger, though he said it wasn't the prime motivation for the deal. Citing recent consolidation among distributors of software, Mr. Posner said "getting attention at those places takes more size than it used to."

Symantec shares closed yesterday at $22.50, up $1, in national over-the-counter trading.
