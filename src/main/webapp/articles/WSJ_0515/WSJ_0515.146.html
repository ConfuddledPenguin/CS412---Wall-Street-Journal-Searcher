Blinder Wins Reversal Of Court Limitation On Asset Transfers

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/15/90

ENGLEWOOD, Colo. -- Blinder, Robinson & Co. said that the Third U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals vacated a preliminary injunction entered against it almost a year ago that limited the brokerage's ability to transfer assets outside its ordinary business. Blinder is a unit of Intercontinental Enterprises Inc. and once was the nation's largest penny-stock broker.

The preliminary injunction was granted in May of last year by a Philadelphia federal judge, who enjoined Blinder from transferring assets outside of its ordinary business and prohibited it from transferring funds overseas without advance notice or court approval. The judge also ordered Blinder to return funds transferred to overseas bank accounts to the U.S.

However, in late May of 1989, the appeals court agreed to stay the provisions limiting transfers overseas and the requirement to return assets from overseas.

The injunction was issued as part of class-action lawsuits filed by three groups of former Blinder Robinson customers who are charging the brokerage with securities fraud, excessive markups on stocks and racketeering. A trial date for the lawsuit hasn't been set, a Blinder lawyer said.

Separately, Intercontinental Enterprises said it had a net loss of $4.5 million, or six cents a share, for its year ended Jan. 26, compared with a net loss of $9.6 million, or 13 cents a share, in the previous year. Revenue fell about 50%, to $42.1 million in fiscal 1990 from $84.6 million in fiscal 1989. The company attributed the revenue decline to a downturn in the securities industry and said the latest-year loss includes the cost of closing several Blinder Robinson offices.
