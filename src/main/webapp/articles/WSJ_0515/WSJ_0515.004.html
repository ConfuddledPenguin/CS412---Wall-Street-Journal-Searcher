Varian to Cut 600 Jobs, Sell Some Units And Take Pretax Charge of $74 Million

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 05/15/90

Varian Associates Inc. moved to improve flagging profit and distance itself from the weakening defense business, announcing plans to eliminate 600 jobs and sell operations with annual sales of $200 million by the end of September. The moves will sharply reduce Varian's costs and its dependence on military contracts, but require it to take a one-time pretax charge of $74 million for its fiscal third quarter ending June 29.

The Palo Alto, Calif., concern currently has about 12,000 employees.

The size of the charge was a surprise -- almost twice as big as what some analysts had expected. But the market welcomed the news. Varian's stock rose 87.5 cents, to $26, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday.

"This will boost profits quite dramatically," said Michael Lauer, an analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co.

Net income at Varian, a diversified high-technology concern, has fallen for four consecutive quarters, and in March the company named J. Tracy O'Rourke its new chief executive officer and chairman. In a prepared statement, Mr. O'Rourke said the job cuts were dictated by the need to reduce costs and that the operations offered for sale aren't part of the company's core businesses, which include medical therapy and semiconductor process equipment.

Mr. O'Rourke came to Varian from Rockwell International Corp., where he had a reputation for turning industry trends to his company's advantage. While president of Allen-Bradley Co., which Rockwell acquired in 1985, Mr. O'Rourke was credited with helping transform the private electromechanical concern with $450 million in annual sales into a $1.4 billion global high-tech supplier.

At Varian, Mr. O'Rourke wants to lead "a pared-down" organization that can better manage rapid changes in markets and technologies. "A number of these activities we plan to exit should repesent interesting opportunities for others," he said. "But in our particular situation, they proved to be a diversion that was no longer acceptable or affordable."

Mr. Lauer, the analyst, said he expects Varian to receive as much as $70 million to $85 million from these operations, which employ about 1,600 people and largely make products purchased by the military. The operations include Continental Electronics in Dallas, TVT Ltd. in Cambridge, England, and two units in Northern California that make night-vision goggles and radiation-resistant semiconductors for the military.

Varian hopes to complete the sale of these units, which account for about one-seventh of its annual revenue, by the end of September, when the company ends its fiscal year. But a spokesman said it could take longer than that to complete the sales and that it was prepared to operate the units until buyers were found.

The sale of these operations should help earnings because they were expected to have losses of as much as $10 million this year, Mr. Lauer said. Varian's profit also will benefit from the job cuts, which Mr. Lauer said should save the company $20 million in annual costs.

While these savings will ease the pressue on Varian, they won't take the company out of the woods. The company also must improve its pivotal semiconductor equipment operation, which has suffered from weakened demand and troubles in finishing new products.

Mr. Lauer said that because of unsolved technical difficulties the company has a backlog of two dozen orders for one important product, which is priced at more than $1 million. In the area of chip equipment, "what's killing the company are technical issues," he said.

A spokesman for Varian said the company had sold about 30 of the machines, but called it a "tough new product." He said "we believe those problems are getting sorted out . . . those are largely behind us now."
