Foreign Exchange: Kohl's Political Setback Triggers Sell-Off Of West German Unit Against the Dollar

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/15/90

NEW YORK -- Market nervousness over a weekend political setback for West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl's Christian Democrats caused a sell-off of the mark against the dollar yesterday. Participants described the day's market activity as driven by activity in mark rates, with the dollar and Swiss franc emerging as the main beneficiaries of the West German currency's weakness.

The mark weakened on news that Mr. Kohl's governing coalition lost its majority in the upper house of the West German parliament as a result of losses in state elections Sunday, traders said. Mr. Kohl's Christian Democrats govern in a coalition with the Bavarian Christian Social Union, a sister party, and with the centrist Free Democrats.

It was feared the loss of the upper house to the left-of-center Social Democrats could complicate the reunification process as the government tries to rush legislation through parliament in order to meet a strict deadline for monetary and economic unification. Mr. Kohl's government has set July 2 as the effective date of a state treaty covering the monetary, economic and social aspects of the Germanys' reunification.

Traders emphasized, however, that the market remains bullish on the mark in the long term.

"The election throws a wrench into the works to some degree, but both {the Christian Democrats and Social Democrats} still want unification" and neither will let political bickering get in the way of the bottom line, said Bill English, senior trader at Banca Nazionale Dell' Agricoltura in New York.

Despite some gains against the mark and other currencies yesterday, the dollar exhibited the fundamental weakness that caused its losses Friday, said John McCarthy, chief foreign exchange dealer at Amsterdam-Rotterdam Bank N.V. in New York.

The dollar's gains yesterday came from special factors weakening the mark, and the currency was steadied against other units by underlying support from surging U.S. stock prices, Mr. McCarthy said. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ended the day at a new all-time high of 2,821.53, up 19.95 points. The previous record high had been 2,810.15 points, reached last Jan. 2.

The Swiss franc also benefited from the mark's weakness, traders said. The Swiss unit attracted buyers because of Switzerland's high interest rates and comparative political stability, they said.

The switch to the franc from the mark following news of the West German election results helped the Swiss unit extend its gains against the dollar for the second consecutive day, they said.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6425 marks, up from its close of 1.6380 marks in very late trading Friday. At about 4 p.m. Friday, the dollar was at 1.6478 marks, but then subsequently continued falling to the 1.6380-mark level.

The dollar was quoted at 152.47 yen in late dealings yesterday, down slightly from 152.65 yen late Friday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6825, up from $1.6790 Friday.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the U.S. currency was quoted at 151.70 yen, down sharply from Monday's Tokyo close of 153.35 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.6455 marks.

Yesterday's focus on the mark relegated dollar-yen dealings to the back burner following Friday's sharp yen jump. The U.S. currency spent the New York day little changed from Friday's closing level against the yen.

Market activity generally was muted yesterday by participants' preparation for important economic indicators due later in the week, dealers said.

Participants said the most important economic figure expected during the week is the Consumer Price Index for April, due tomorrow.

The CPI is being awaited as a further signal of inflationary pressure on the U.S. economy, and by extension, an indication of which direction interest rates will take, said Dell' Agricoltura's Mr. English and Peter Iversen, a trader at Shawmut Bank of Boston N.A.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York yesterday, gold for current delivery settled at $368.40 an ounce, down 40 cents. Estimated volume was a moderate two million ounces.

In Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $368.45 an ounce.
