Banks See Golden Opportunity in Junk

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 05/15/90

NEW YORK -- Amid the rubble of the battered junk-bond market, some powerful new players are stirring: the major banks. Using banks' newly won powers to underwrite corporate bonds, Citicorp, J.P. Morgan & Co. and Bankers Trust New York Corp. have all established groups specializing in sales and trading of high-risk, high-yield junk bonds. And First Chicago Corp., has just begun staffing up for a junk-bond effort.

It's another example of how big banks are trying to muscle in on the securities business dominated by Wall Street.

Executives at all three New York banks say they plan a long-term effort to develop relationships with investors who want to buy junk bonds, and with companies that want to issue junk.

Even after the near-collapse of the $200 billion junk market last fall, the bankers think they are smart enough to navigate the risks of a market where Wall Street securities firms have stumbled.

"Credit is something we feel we know a little bit about," says Ramon de Oliveira, head of the high-yield group at J.P. Morgan. He says commercial banks can thrive in the minefield of junk by using "the skills we have developed in our banking practice."

Despite the junk market's current woes, which began with credit crises last summer at Integrated Resources Inc. and Campeau Corp., bankers believe the market will survive and, eventually, prosper.

But some Wall Street executives find it ironic that banks are invading the junk market at a time when they are retreating, under pressure from banking regulators, from making bank loans for leveraged buy-outs and other takeovers involving heavy debt loads. These so-called highly leveraged transactions often include large amounts of junk bonds.

But bankers insist they won't get in over their heads in the junk market, the way banks did with loans to less developed countries or real estate developers. Banks will avoid going overboard, they say, by keeping their junk-bond holdings modest and trading in and out of the market quickly.

"It's deliberately not our strategy to acquire assets as an asset holder," says Robert Lawrence, manager of the high-yield department of Citicorp's securities unit, Citicorp Securities Markets Inc. "We intend to sell and trade these securities much as we trade in any other instruments."

As yet, none of the banks has underwritten a single new issue of junk bonds, an important goal if banks are to establish themselves as serious players in the junk market. That's because the new-issue market has been moribund since late last year; only two new junk-bond issues have come to market this year.

Mr. Lawrence concedes that the junk market "has certainly deteriorated since we made our original plans" to jump in. Citicorp executives have even asked themselves whether they are getting in too late, he says.

But bankers insist the junk market is too important for them to stand aside. "You can't ignore something that represents more than 20% of the entire corporate bond market," says J.P. Morgan's Mr. Oliveira. "Whatever shape the market is in now, it still represents a legitimate market and asset class."

Mr. Oliveira notes that despite its blue-chip image, Morgan has many clients who invest in and issue junk bonds. "We have to cover the high-yield market to service these clients," he says.

Because only a handful of new issues have been sold this year, the banks have concentrated on research, sales and trading to build up their customer base. Bankers Trust has been busy doing trades averaging $2 million apiece in size; it recently ranked second among all junk-bond dealers in one trading category in a poll of institutional investors by Greenwich Associates, a consulting firm.

Tim Yates, managing director in charge of high-yield and structured sales at Bankers Trust, says his company aims to join Wall Street securities firms as one of the top five firms in junk. "We entered trading and sales in order to build a strong new-issue distribution capability," he says.

Citicorp last fall lured Mariel Clemensen, head of junk research, from Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. to run Citicorp's research operation. Recently, First Chicago hired Robert Lupo, formerly head of junk research at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co.

Bankers believe that the research will result in a new-issue capability that can be exploited once the junk new-issue market comes back. Mr. Lawrence of Citicorp believes that new junk issues, which reached a peak of more than $45 billion in 1986 in public and private offerings, can rebound to $30 billion a year.

What will make that happen?

Although the market value of all junk bonds outstanding may have shrunk from $200 billion to as little as $150 billion by some estimates, those bonds are throwing off interest income of roughly $25 billion annually.

Bankers believe that eventually this income, together with funds from bargain-hunting investors, will generate enough demand for junk to overcome the selling pressure from thrifts, insurance companies and mutual funds. That would cause the junk market to resume expanding, they say.

The big banks generally insist they are in the junk market for the long haul. They won't cut and run when times get tough, as many big banks have in the London securities market.

"We don't look at businesses here with a short-term orientation," says Citicorp's Mr. Lawrence. "It may take three to five years to build a business franchise. This is a long-term business, and we decided we should take our time and do it well." At any rate, he says his group has made money this year and last.
