Media: MCA Views Top Creator Of Cartoons

  • Author: Richard Turner
  • Date: 05/15/90

UNIVERSAL CITY, Calif. -- MCA Inc. wants to build a new part of its business on Bedrock, not to mention Jellystone Park. According to individuals close to the company, MCA is in discussions to acquire Hanna-Barbera Productions, the cartoon company that created the Flintstones and their Bedrock friends, along with Yogi Bear, Huckleberry Hound, the Jetsons, Scooby Doo and other characters.

Great American Communications Co., which owns Hanna-Barbera, declined to comment, as did MCA executives. Executives close to the situation cautioned that talks were very preliminary and that a deal was far from concluded. However, Great American is known to be cash strapped and Great American executives are scheduled to be in Los Angeles this week and are to meet with MCA officials.

MCA's reported interest reflects a mad rush by entertainment giants to tie up with well-known fantasy characters for use in making and marketing movies, TV shows, theme park attractions and consumer merchandise.

MCA already has a close relationship with the animation studio: It has a Hanna-Barbera attraction at its new Universal Studios Florida theme park and is co-producing and distributing "Jetsons: The Movie," a feature-length cartoon coming out July 6. Similarly, a Flintstones movie is in development at MCA's Universal Pictures. In addition, many Hanna-Barbera cartoons air on USA Network, 50%-owned by MCA. Finally, Hanna-Barbera's newly appointed president is David Kirschner, the executive producer of MCA's animated movie "An American Tail."

Owning Hanna-Barbera would help MCA compete with Walt Disney Co., which last year added the Muppets to its stable of characters, and Time Warner Inc., which has been promoting Bugs Bunny's 50th Anniversary and licensing Looney Tunes characters to endorse products. Meanwhile, Time Warner bought a 19.5% stake in the Six Flags theme park chain, which uses the Warner characters.

An industry executive likened the interest in Hanna-Barbera to MCA's acquisition of Geffen Records for $550 million in stock earlier this year. "You get to be a serious player overnight, and you're not going to get there by making `An American Tail' and `The Land Before Time' every two years," referring to MCA's last two animated movies. The executive added that like Geffen, which was the last major independent record company, Hanna-Barbera is one of the few well-known cartoon franchises in existence that kids and their parents both know.

Cincinnati-based Great American, controlled by investor Carl Lindner since he acquired Taft Broadcasting in 1987, is laboring under a junk-bond debt load. Its long-term debt was $1.19 billion at the end of last year, and a $165 million payment is due in October. Last week, it was disclosed the company was looking for a buyer for its radio stations. Great American is 61%-owned by Mr. Lindner's American Financial Corp.

A report yesterday in the trade newspaper Variety said Merrill Lynch had prepared a prospectus offering the company's entertainment units for $700 million to $1 billion: Aside from Hanna-Barbera, these include a 48.6% stake in Spelling Entertainment, which has a library including "Dynasty," "Charlie's Angels" and other programs, Worldvision Enterprises Inc., a distribution company owned by Spelling, and Ruby Spears Enterprises, a smaller animation company. Spelling has been on the block since last fall.

Hanna-Barbera, founded in 1957 by William Hanna and Joseph Barbera, is "the jewel of the enterprise," says Paul C. Marsh, an analyst with Bateman Eichler Hill Richards. He places the value of the cartoon studio at about $300 million and the rest of Great American's entertainment assets at $500 million. Mr. Marsh said he believes Great American had hoped to hold on to Hanna-Barbera and let it appreciate, but that the debt pressure became too intense. Lisbeth R. Barron, research vice president at S.G. Warburg & Co., values Hanna-Barbera at about $200 million.

MCA also could use Hanna-Barbera's current production facilities to create TV cartoons. The company recently hired a former Hanna-Barbera senior vice president, Jeff Segal, to start a children's entertainment division. News Corp.'s Fox Inc., Disney and Warner have been actively accelerating their production of television cartoons.

One possible complication to the acquisition is that most of the Hanna-Barbera library is encumbered by existing distribution agreements. MCA's world-wide distribution machinery would have to wait for these agreements to expire or buy them out, a possibility since Worldvision has most of these contracts.

A Hanna-Barbera spokeswoman confirmed that the Great American executives would be in Los Angeles but said the trip was for routine operational meetings.
