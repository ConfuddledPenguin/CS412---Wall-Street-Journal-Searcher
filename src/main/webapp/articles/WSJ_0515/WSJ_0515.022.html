Economy: Treasury Asks Congress to Require Firms Backed by U.S. to Have Triple-A Rating

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 05/15/90

WASHINGTON -- The Treasury Department urged Congress to require the Federal National Mortgage Association, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. and five other government-sponsored enterprises to qualify for and obtain triple-A credit ratings -- without factoring in the government's implicit guarantee. In testimony before the House Ways and Means Committee's oversight panel, Robert R. Glauber, Treasury undersecretary for finance, said the Treasury believes a triple-A rating from at least two major credit-rating concerns "represents the most safe and sound level of credit quality, and thereby the best protection from potential risk."

Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac buy home loans from lenders and package some into securities for sale to investors and hold the rest in portfolio. The two entities together purchase about one-fourth of the nation's home loans. Stricter standards raise concerns that homeowners' costs could increase or that the two corporations might be forced to curb their purchases of riskier loans, often for low-income or moderate-income housing.

The Treasury, however, argued that taking steps to meet high credit-rating standards "should not increase the borrowing costs of any government-sponsored enterprise, nor would it significantly impair" their ability to fulfill their missions. A triple-A rating, Mr. Glauber testified, "would only reduce the potential need for another federal rescue."

In addition to Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the group includes the Student Loan Marketing Association, called Sallie Mae; College Construction Loan Insurance Association, or Connie Lee; Federal Agricultural Mortgage Corp., or Farmer Mac; Farm Credit Administration; and Federal Home Loan Banks.

The corporations were set up by Congress to improve the flow of money to private borrowers for agricultural loans, housing and student loans. Under last year's legislation rescuing the thrift industry, the Treasury was ordered to examine taxpayers' risk posed by the seven entities.

Credit-rating concerns rate the debt of companies, providing a clue that investors use in trying to determine the level of risk associated with owning a company's securities.

Neither Fannie Mae nor Freddie Mac meets the triple-A standard, and both companies said the Treasury proposal needs further study to see how it would affect their ability to carry out their mission of providing money for housing. "We could live with it, but it raises a lot of questions," a Freddie Mac spokesman said.

Thomas Gillis, a managing director at Standard & Poor's Corp., one of the major credit rating concerns, said that meeting requirements for a triple-A rating "would not be impossible" for Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. "However, they would need to make some fairly significant changes to their balance sheets and their operations." The changes, he said, would probably diminish the services they offer.

The Treasury maintained that data generated in the credit-rating process would serve to disclose the magnitude of the government's implicit federal subsidy.

In addition to suggesting Congress adopt a high credit-rating standard for the enterprises, Mr. Glauber said the Treasury also is recommending that Congress sever ties with any of the seven enterprises that aren't adequately capitalized and don't meet high credit and operations standards. The Treasury Department also concluded that the enterprises must have "effective federal supervision."

Mr. Glauber said some of the government-sponsored organizations "are among the most thinly capitalized of major U.S. financial entities," and, unlike other corporations, "are not subject to the usual market-imposed disciplines of increased cost or reduced access to capital as their balance-sheet leverage increases beyond normally prudent levels." While it may translate into better returns for shareholders for a successful corporation, the balance-sheet leverage "can result in tremendous pressure" for federal aid for an unsuccessful one, he warned.

The Treasury Department also recommended that Congress separate regulatory authority for the corporations. Under the proposal, the agency that monitored financial safety and soundness standards wouldn't evaluate whether a government-sponsored enterprise is fulfilling its congressional mandate.
