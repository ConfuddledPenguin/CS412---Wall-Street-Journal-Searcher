Marketing & Media: Hachette S.A. Unit Is Planning Sale Of Woman's Day

  • Author: Meg Cox
  • Date: 05/15/90

NEW YORK -- Diamandis Communications Inc., a unit of French publisher Hachette S.A., said it plans to sell Woman's Day, one of the largest circulation women's magazines in the U.S. Hachette declined to say what price it is seeking for Woman's Day, which it said had operating profit in 1989 of more than $25 million. Investment bankers and publishing industry consultants said the magazine could fetch between $175 million and $200 million, based on a multiple of between seven and eight times its operating profit.

But Hachette is selling Woman's Day at a difficult time. The magazine's ad pages are down about 15% below last year's level, reflecting a general slump in magazine advertising. And many big publishers are backing away from their buying sprees of recent years.

Didier Guerin, the president of Hachette's U.S. subsidiary, Hachette Publications Inc., said "When we acquired Diamandis two years ago, we took on a lot of debt. It's good to reduce that debt. And we want to concentrate on special interest magazines in the U.S. and magazines with an international appeal, such as Elle."

Woman's Day, with an average circulation of just under five million, is one of the so-called Seven Sisters women's magazines. It isn't the classiest, selling mostly single issue copies in supermarkets, but it is solidly profitable, said publisher Bob Mate, and reaches roughly one in four American women. As a very large, mass-oriented publication, Woman's Day is quite different from the other tightly targeted magazines published by Diamandis, which include Road & Track, Car and Driver and Popular Photography.

Investment bankers speculate that possible buyers for Woman's Day include the owners of the other six "sisters," including Hearst Corp., which publishes Good Housekeeping and Redbook. New York Times Co., which publishes Family Circle, just bought McCall's last year and isn't expected to be in the market again so soon. Meredith Corp. owns Ladies' Home Journal and Better Homes & Gardens. Some say Cahners Publishing Co., a unit of Britain's giant Reed International PLC, is a potential buyer because it owns similar publications in England and is expanding in the U.S.

Dan McNamee, a media consultant, said Diamandis has been "quietly discussing the prospect" of selling Woman's Day for at least six months. Mr. McNamee agrees that "it's a bad fit for Diamandis, which has a slew of special-interest titles." Despite the fact that magazines are selling for lower multiples than they were a year ago and the weakness of the advertising market, Mr. McNamee said he expects Woman's Day to find a buyer. "I think the franchise is still strong, though whoever buys it may need to invest some money," said Mr. McNamee.

Included in the sale, which is being handled by investment bankers Veronis, Suhler & Associates Inc., will be 21 Woman's Day Specials, single-subject special magazines on such topics as home decorating and health. A spokesman said the company sells about six million copies of the special issues a year, for annual revenue of about $15 million.

Hachette bought Diamandis in 1988 for $712 million, which included 13 publications.
