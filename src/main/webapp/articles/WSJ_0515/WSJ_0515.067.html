Patrons Are Defecting To Foreign Carriers

IF U.S. AIRLINES don't watch out, they may end up with the same problem U.S. auto makers have: the reputation that foreign is better.

Increasingly, consumers are rating foreign airlines ahead of U.S. airlines in service. A reader poll last fall by Business Traveler International magazine is typical. Only three U.S. airlines cracked the readers' list of top 10 international airlines. The most popular U.S. carrier on the list, AMR Corp.'s American Airlines, ranked only fifth overall, behind Singapore Airlines, Swissair, British Airways and Lufthansa.

The trend is showing up in market-share figures, too. In 1989, only 40.9% of all trips between the U.S. and Europe were on U.S.-owned carriers, down from 42.7% in 1988, according to Airline Economics Inc.

In reputation, "U.S. airlines have undoubtedly fallen behind," says Ed Perkins, editor of Consumer Reports Travel Letter. "They've frittered away their leadership position and are just fighting to stay even now."

The biggest difference in service, consumer groups say, is crew attitude. "Foreign airlines just seem to be attentive to people's needs," says Richard Livingston, spokesman for the International Airline Passenger Association. Consumers also complain that domestic airlines are often just plain cheapskates, charging for movies and drinks, while many foreign carriers don't. "Little things like that really annoy people," Mr. Perkins says.

For their part, U.S. airlines insist that foreign service often isn't really better, just different. "It seems better because it's not what they normally get," says a spokesman for the Air Transport Association, an airline trade group based in Washington. Airlines also say some travelers still avoid U.S. carriers because they think terrorists target them.

It Would Be Big of You

To Take Another Seat

TRAVELERS are starting to lose their patience with chubby seat companions.

At least one consumer newsletter, Ashington-Pickett Airlines & Travel Reports, says it is getting more complaints about overweight passengers squishing their seatmates. One Delta Air Lines passenger wrote he even had to spend most of a recent trip to Orlando, Fla., on the flight attendant's jump seat because the obese passenger assigned to sit next to him made his own seat unbearable.

"People really get upset about this and they're not the least bit shy about complaining," says Michael Ashington-Pickett, publisher of the newsletter, which is based in Orlando.

Consumer groups blame the planes. They insist airlines have been cutting seat size on some wide-bodied jets to pack in more seats, although seat manufacturers deny it. Seat manufacturers do concede that leg room in coach has been cut to 31 or 32 inches from about 34 inches.

Technically, airlines can bar obese travelers if they can't fit in the seat or if they block aisles, but airline officials concede they almost never do that.

In Hotels, Newer Means

Fuller -- and Costlier

WOULD YOU PAY an extra $10 a night to stay at a hotel just because it was newer than another one?

Apparently so. The average occupancy rate of hotels less than three years old is two percentage points higher than at older properties, according to a survey of more than 1,000 new hotels by Smith Travel Research. The average nightly price for the newer properties is $69, compared with $59 for older establishments.

The trend is even more pronounced in the budget sector. The younger accommodations, which on average cost $4 more than the older hotels, have an average occupancy rate of 61.9%, compared with 57.4%.

Given that even one percentage point can be the difference between profit and loss, the newer properties obviously "have a tremendous advantage," says Randy Smith, president of Smith Travel, Hendersonville, Tenn.

The appeal has as much to do with the design of new properties as it does with age. New hotels usually have interior hallways, which many travelers prefer to the outdoor ones, Mr. Smith says. The newer hotels also tend to have smaller dining rooms and lobbies, which guests say they prefer because they hold down overall costs.

New hotels also tend to be marketed better, he says, because the pressure to earn profits is higher. Years ago, there were so many tax advantages to building hotels that a lot of owners didn't need to make much money from the operations. But with the tax laws changed and the industry in a slow period, hotels have become much more aggressive -- and new hotels tout their advantages.

Odds and Ends

AFTER YEARS of spending freely on their tourism departments, many states are sharply cutting back. Massachusetts has budgeted $6.9 million for its tourism department this year, down from $9.5 million in 1989. New York plans to cut its tourism budget about 15%. The cutbacks reflect the downturn in the state economies. There are, of course, exceptions. California and Hawaii are among those that have increased their tourism budgets, according to the U.S. Travel Data Center. . . .
