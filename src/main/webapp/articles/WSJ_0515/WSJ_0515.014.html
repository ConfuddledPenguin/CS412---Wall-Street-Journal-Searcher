Platinum Output By South Africa Is Expected to Grow

  • Author: Neil Behrmann Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/15/90

LONDON -- South African platinum mines are planning to raise production by one million troy ounces, or 39%, during the next five years, Johnson Matthey PLC said in its latest annual review of the metal. This year, the world's largest platinum producing nation will produce as much as 150,000 ounces more than 1989's record output of nearly 2.6 million ounces, the precious-metals-refinery firm said.

However, in futures trading, platinum prices rose to $485.75 an ounce, up $2, on expectations that strikes and strife in South Africa will pare the mines' estimates during the next few years.

Moreover, dealers responded favorably to Johnson Matthey's findings that exports by the Soviet Union, the second-largest producer, were sharply lower in this year's first quarter.

Last year, Soviet platinum supplies soared to 550,000 ounces, the highest levels since 1977, Johnson Matthey said. Yet production fell this year, possibly because of an explosion in November at a platinum-producing complex in Norilsk, Siberia, said Jeremy Coombes, author of the report.

Although the Soviet Union could well raise sales later in the year, he said, total exports this year could decline to 1988 levels of 440,000 ounces.

Meanwhile, world-wide fabrication demand rose to approximately 3.2 million ounces in 1989 from approximately three million in 1988, mainly because of buoyant jewelry demand in Japan. Platinum used in automobiles also grew.

But investment in platinum bars and coins tumbled 75% to 160,000 ounces last year, from 660,000 ounces in 1988, because of high real returns on other investments, Mr. Coombes said.

Growth in industrial demand could also slacken, Johnson Matthey said. Faced with higher borrowing costs, industrial consumers may prefer to reduce inventories rather than buy new metal. Platinum sales could also be hindered by weaker U.S. auto production, and after five successive years of growth, platinum consumption in Western Europe may also stagnate.

Excluding investment demand, supplies exceeded fabrication consumption by 110,000 ounces, the third year of surplus, the report said. But with such a narrow differential between demand and supply, prices could range between $470 and $520 an ounce this year.
