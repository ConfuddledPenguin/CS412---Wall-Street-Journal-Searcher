Credit Markets: Portfolio Managers' Buying Spree Pushes Prices Of Bonds Up; 30-Year Treasury Gains 5/8 Point

  • Author: Sam Alcorn and Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 05/15/90

NEW YORK -- Fixed-income portfolio managers, worried about missing the strongest bond market rally this year, went on a buying spree. The buying pushed bond prices moderately higher. The Treasury's new benchmark 30-year bond, which climbed more than two points on Friday, rose 5/8 point yesterday for a gain of $6.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

Analysts said many of the buyers were institutional investors who had been waiting until the Treasury's huge $30.5 billion refunding auction was complete before venturing into the market.

These investors believed that a poorly received auction would force bond dealers to cut prices, so they decided to wait until after the auction to buy bonds. However, last week's note and bond auction was considered a success. Moreover, bond prices rose sharply on Friday after two economic reports reinforced the view that interest rates are heading lower.

"There has been an extraordinary change in psychology," said F. Ward McCarthy Jr., managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates Inc. "Investors who were reluctant to buy bonds ahead of the auction {now} feel uneasy about having missed something."

Meanwhile, the focus of the bond market has shifted to Washington, where Congress and the White House begin negotiations today on a compromise to shrink the swelling budget deficit. In March, the latest month for which Treasury data are available, the deficit widened to a monthly record of $53.3 billion.

Traders said deficit-reduction talks have contributed to the market's positive tone. Although many market players are somewhat skeptical that the talks will lead to a concrete deficit-cutting accord, they are more hopeful than they have been in the past.

"I think this is another ritual dance," said Victor Chang, president of V.C. Management Co., an investment advisory firm. "However, I do think conditions have deteriorated enough that something positive will come out of it."

He said the bond market, having lived with prior year deficits and little significant progress on reducing the deficits, has "become a little jaded about any really substantial headway in these matters. But this time, because of the thrift crisis and because we're so far behind on this process, everyone has just concluded that something will come out of it, that something will be done," said Mr. Chang.

Ian MacKinnon, bond manager at Vanguard Group of Investment Cos. in Valley Forge, Pa., said he would look "favorably upon initiatives to raise taxes" as a way to cut the deficit. He said the move could pave the way for lower interest rates.

"It would have a slowing effect on the economy as revenues are diverted toward cutting the deficit, with beneficial effects on inflation, as well as reducing" the supply of Treasury securities used to finance the deficit, said Mr. MacKinnon.

Marshall Front, a partner at the investment advisory firm Stein Roe & Farnham, Chicago, also believes that a deficit-reduction package could help result in lower interest rates.

"Easing inflation, slowing business activity, the budget summitry all create preconditions for lower long-term and short-term rates," said Mr. Front. He said long-term U.S. interest rates may work themselves down to about 8 3/8% to 8 1/2% in the near future.

The one possible big negative, he said, is if interest rates "come down too quickly, it could hurt the dollar and upset the financial markets."

Treasury Securities

The new benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 101 24/32 to yield 8.58%, up from a price of 101 4/32 to yield 8.63% on Friday. The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 101 22/32 to yield 8.60%, up from 101 14/32 to yield 8.64% on Friday.

Short-term interest rates were lower at the Treasury's weekly bill auction, compared with rates at last week's auction.

The government sold $16.86 billion of three-month and six-month bills. The average rate on the three-month bills was 7.67%, while the average rate on the six-month bills was 7.68%.

Those rates were down from last week's auction, when they were 7.79% and 7.84%, respectively.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

The Treasury's recommendation that the Federal National Mortgage Association, the Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. and other government-sponsored enterprises raise their credit standings to meet triple-A status, had little impact on the mortgage-backed securities market.

These government-sponsored enterprises are the largest issuers of mortgage securities. Although the recommendation caused the stock prices of many government corporations to tumble yesterday, most mortgage securities were unaffected by the news.

Standard & Poor's Corp. and Moody's Investors Service Inc. currently rate the debt of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac triple-A. But S&P said that without the support of the federal government, the agencies would be rated triple-B or single-A.

But the Student Loan Marketing Association would have a top-notch credit rating without its ties to the federal government, according to S&P. Sallie Mae common stock came under pressure, ending at 48 3/4, down 1 3/8.

Corporate Issues

Cheered by gains in the equity market, junk bond investors bid up high-yield issues, driving prices of most actively traded bonds more than 1/4 point higher.

The advance was broad-based and included such well-known high-yield names as RJR Nabisco Inc., R.H. Macy & Co. and Harcourt Brace Jovanovich Inc.

Although trading volume wasn't exceptionally heavy, junk bond traders said there was evidence of active buying interest among a variety of players, including investors who traditionally buy stocks.

"A lot of people are trying to buy paper that they think is dramatically undervalued," said David Feinman, a high-yield trader at Los Angeles-based Jefferies & Co.

Among the bonds that climbed were RJR's 13.71% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2009, which chalked up 1 3/8 point to end at 83. The other widely held RJR issue, the 13.71% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2007, added 3/4 point to finish at 69 1/2.

Macy's 14 1/2% senior subordinated debentures, maturing in 1998, moved up a point to end at 71 3/4. Trump Taj Mahal's 14% bonds rose 3/4 point to 69 1/2 on the expectation that the company will make coupon payments due today.

There were glaring exceptions to yesterday's bullish market. The junk bonds of Charter Medical Corp. and Southland Corp. fell on poor earnings reports.

Charter Medical's 14% senior subordinated debentures lost another five points to end at 49 in the wake of sharply lower second-quarter earnings. The bonds, which were trading at 70 in early March, dropped five points Friday after the earnings report was first released.

Charter Medical bonds have lost ground ever since the company reported in mid-March that accounting errors would result in an increase in its first-quarter loss.

The junk bonds of Southland, the Dallas-based convenience store operator, weakened by more than a point on news that the cash-strapped firm reported a first-quarter operating loss of $121.5 million, compared with an operating loss of $113.4 million in the year earlier.

Among the biggest losers were Southland's 16 3/4% subordinated debentures that shed more than a point to end at 7.

In the market for investment-grade corporate bonds, prices ended between 1/4 to 1/2 point higher.

Municipals

Narrowly underperforming U.S. Treasurys, municipal bonds were trading as much as 1/2 point higher in late dealings.

Meanwhile, short-term municipal issues were unchanged, traders said.

Despite the lack of supply and thin trading activity, traders characterized the market's tone as firm ahead of the next round of economic data today.

In a light new-issue market, a Goldman, Sachs & Co. group set the preliminary pricing for $70 million of Rhode Island Housing Mortgage Finance Corp. homeownership opportunity revenue bonds. This week, more than $1.7 billion in new debt is scheduled to hit the market.

In secondary action, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was 3/8 higher at 98 1/4 bid to yield 7.33%.

Foreign Bonds

West German government bonds fell more than 1/2 point in reaction to Sunday's defeat of the conservative Christian Democrats in local elections.

Chancellor Helmut Kohl's Christian Democrats were heavily beaten by rival Social Democrats in the states of Lower Saxony and North Rhine-Westfalia, which together contain more than 40% of the nation's voters.

West Germany's 7 3/4% bond due 2000 fell 0.53 point to 94.80 to yield 8.55%.

In Japan, bond prices ended stronger, reflecting a rise in the value of the yen and a decline in short-term interest rates. Rates on three-month certificates of deposit fell to about 7.36%, down from 7.4% on Friday.

Dealers say that while most of the activity involved dealers trading on the foreign-exchange action, there was also investor buying.

"There was talk that investors have begun to reconstruct their portfolios," said one dealer.

At the close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, Japan's benchmark 10-year bond was quoted at 87.69, up 0.34 point to yield 7.02%. The price rose further in over-the-counter trading.
