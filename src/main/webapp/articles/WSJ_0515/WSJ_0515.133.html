THE WORLD'S POPULATION

The world population will increase by one billion in the 1990s, an increase that may prove catastrophic to the environment, a United Nations report predicted.

The estimate of growth in the world population -- currently 5.3 billion -- outstrips all previous forecasts, the State of the World Population report from the U.N. Population Fund said. The environment and poorer countries, where 90% of the rise will occur, will suffer most as the population increases by three people every second -- about a quarter of a million each day -- the report said.

The report said that while the industrialized countries were mainly responsible for damage to the ozone layer and for roughly two-thirds of global warming, the combination of poverty and population growth was causing deforestation and land degradation in the developing world.

The 1990s will see greater growth than any decade in human history, with 90 million to 100 million more people each year, the report said. During the next century, it predicted, global population will probably double and could triple.

INDIA CLEANSES FILM INDUSTRY

India, whose huge film industry turns out more than 700 films a year, has ordered censors to purge the nation's movies of sex and violence, said Information and Broadcasting Minister Parvathaneni Upendra. Although Indian films are conservative by Western standards -- both nudity and kissing are banned -- rape scenes are common, and "Scenes glorifying sex and violence have crossed the limits of decency," Mr. Upendra said. Under new rules, censors would also refuse to approve movies promoted by posters showing vulgar scenes, he said.

SOUTH KOREA FIGHTS CORRUPTION

As part of a new anti-corruption investigation of government officials in South Korea, the prosecutor general's office announced the arrest of four present or former Seoul city officers. The four were charged with taking bribes ranging from $14,000 to $47,000 from a businessman in 1988 for giving him favors in issuing permits to build a hotel in downtown Seoul, prosecutors said. President Roh Tae Woo appointed a 54-member presidential task force Saturday to root out corruption by year's end.

CITIZEN'S EAST BLOC PLANS

Japan's Citizen Watch Co. will begin marketing its watches in Eastern Europe through local agents, a company spokeswoman said. Citizen's West German subsidiary, Citizen Watch Europe G.m.b.H, will find companies and shops to sell watches priced between 3,000 yen (about $20) to 5,000 yen, according to the spokesman. The move is the first major attempt by a Japanese watchmaker to enter the rapidly changing region, she said. The company plans to sell 100,000 watches annually in the region.

HONG KONG'S JOBLESS

Hong Kong's jobless rate rose to 1.7% in the first quarter from 1.3% in the previous quarter, marking its highest level since mid-1988. While the colony's labor shortage has been a prime factor behind spiraling local prices, Jason Kwok, senior economist at Bank of East Asia, warned that it's premature to say inflation may be easing. "I'd like to wait to see data for a few more months," he said. "We also have to take into account what the property market will be doing before making any pronouncements about inflation."

SOVIETS' LATE BILL PAYMENTS

Japan reportedly doubled the rate on export insurance to the Soviet Union after hearing from major Japanese trading houses that Soviet trading organizations were late in paying their bills. A spokesman for trading house Nissho Iwa Corp. said the government raised the export-insurance rate for several products, such as steel, chemical products and textiles. The insurance rate varies according to product, and specific rates aren't released, but trading-house officials say that the rate has been raised to an average of about 0.02% from about 0.01%.

CZECHOSLOVAK TELEVISION

Czechoslovak television started an experimental broadcast on a third channel yesterday that incorporates U.S, Soviet and West European programs.

The broadcast, which replaced Soviet programming formerly available on the same channel, will reach approximately 30% of Czechoslovakia's territory, the Socialist daily Svobodne Slovo said. Of the other two Czechoslovak channels, one broadcasts nationwide and the other provides regional programming.

The programming of the third channel was made available by foreign TV stations such as La Sept and TV 5 of France, CNN of the U.S. and the U.S.-government run World Net and Soviet television.

The third channel will include language courses, sports, news, and music, Svobodne Slovo said.
