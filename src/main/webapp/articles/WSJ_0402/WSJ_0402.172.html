World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Continue to Plunge Monday As Dollar Tops 160 Yen, Big Investors Sell Off

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 04/02/90

Tokyo's stock prices continued to plunge Monday, as the dollar soared above 160 yen and institutional investors apparently began an aggressive sell-off. At the close of the morning session, the Nikkei index, an average of 225 issues, was off 1720.13 points, or 5.7%, to 28260.32. That was sharply below the previous intraday low for the year of 28830 on March 22.

The stock market was undercut by the surging dollar. The U.S. currency met little resistance as it jumped to 160.25 yen late Monday morning, up from 158.35 yen late Friday in New York.

That sent the stock market tumbling. Its slide also was greased by fears that Japanese insurance companies would begin selling stock at the start of their new fiscal year this morning. A report in the Nihon Keizai Shimbun, Japan's leading economic daily, suggesting that life and casualty insurers -- among the country's biggest institutional investors -- would reduce their Japanese stock holdings appeared to be coming true, one stockbroker said Monday morning. "They are selling already," she added.

The report magnified the market's troubles on Friday, when fast-paced sell-offs by institutions trying to raise their cash positions by the end of the fiscal year compounded uncertainty about the market's direction.

On Friday, the indicator plunged 1045.71 points, or 3.4%, to 29980.45, closing below the 30000-point level for the second time in March. On March 22, the index dropped to 29843.34. The index lost 237.41 points Thursday.

Tokyo stock prices plunged in moderate trading Friday because of fast-paced selloffs by institutions that raised cash positions on the last trading day of fiscal 1990. Uncertainty about the market's future deepened.

The Nikkei Index of 225 issues fell 1045.71 points to 29980.45, closing below the 30000-point level for the second time in March.

In London, meanwhile, stocks fell as most investors closed existing positions on the last trading day of the first quarter and found little inspiration to trade anew.

Tokyo traders said volatile moves in the foreign-exchange market and higher yields on the bond market dried up buying interest. Meanwhile, the pace of decline in stock prices was accelerated by index-linked program selling by foreign brokerate houses, traders said.

The Nikkei index, which ended the Friday morning session with an 850.15-point loss, rebounded moderately in midafternoon with the help of buying by some investment trust funds. But the index tumbled again, to the intraday low of 29827, in final rounds of sell-offs, before the average collected 150 points in the last 15 minutes of trading.

Volume on the first section was estimated at about 500 million shares, down from 685 million shares Thursday.

Losers outnumbered gainers 866 to 133, while 93 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section was down 59.37 points, or 2.59%, to 2227.48, after falling 20 points Thursday.

The second section index fell 37.45 points, or 0.97%, to 3817.90. The index gained 11.10 points Thursday. Volume was estimated at nine million shares, down from 10 million shares Thursday.

The market had seen small-capital stock prices pushed up by "window dressing" operations by some institutions, which evaluate year-end values of their holdings according to Friday's close. These stock prices had been expected to add further gains Friday.

But such expectations were shattered when the market began the day with active selling by foreign investors. The strong selling pressure made institutions give up strategic buying and begin reducing their positions instead, analysts said.

Dealers also refrained from buying Friday.

"Every Japanese brokerage house couldn't do anything beyond remain on the sidelines because they knew that if they bought, they'd only be battered by heavy selling," said Yoshihiro Ito, general manager of stock trading at Okasan Securities.

As the Nikkei index extended losses during Friday afternoon, individual investors finally joined the selling activity, as prices fell to levels that require investors to place additional margin, the official said.

Shin Tokoi, deputy general manager of stock trading at County NatWest Securities, said market expectations that the yen will weaken further and top the 160-yen level against the dollar was strong, despite the yen's temporal recovery in the middle of the day.

The heavy selling by foreign investors and the continually weakening yen led some investors to blame Japanese politics, which are often poorly appraised by foreign nations.

Traders predict stock prices will drift at low levels this week.

"The market has no strength for a strong rebound at the moment," one analyst said, adding that the Nikkei index will fluctuate around the 30000-point level this week.

High-technology shares, which advanced strongly this past week on expectations that these exporters will benefit from the yen's weakness, were targets of active profit-taking Friday.

Fanuc plummeted 320 yen to 7,450 yen ($2.05 to $47.62). Kyocera fell 210 yen to 7,560 yen. NEC dropped 50 yen to 2,120 yen and Hitachi Ltd. declined 50 yen to 1,590 yen.

NTT rose 30,000 yen to 1,160,000 yen following the government's announcement to freeze discussion over a breakup of the giant telephone company for five years, until 1995. But market sentiment was too sluggish Friday to respond to the news widely, traders said.

In London, shares languished within a very narrow band much of the day, as traders said participants were waiting for the opening of a new quarter this week.

But an early weak start on Wall Street gave London shares a fresh nudge lower, although traders said there wasn't much behind the drift downward.

Some traders said trading probably would remain sparse until fresh earnings and indicators were disclosed later this week.

"We're not inclined to do anything just at the moment," said one dealer. "I'm afraid there's a lot of market maker price movement but not a lot behind it."

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 share index was off 15.1 points to 2247.9, and just above its late Friday afternoon intraday minimum of 2245.5, but lower than its midmorning peak of 2271.

The Financial Times 30 share index fell 12.5 points to 1768.7. Volume amounted to a sketchy 453.7 million shares, little changed from Thursday's 435.2 million issues.

In the extremely quiet market, dealers said there were few overall features to discuss. Many said a large part of any price movement among the major stocks came as a result of market makers adjusting prices to see if they could lure some fresh buyers to the scene.

Among individual shares, British Steel gained 4 pence to 144 pence (6.5 cents to $2.36). Analysts said the shares gained after the company said that because of a pension fund surplus, results for the next few years will be better than expected.

Grand Metropolitan, the food and drinks giant, shed 11 pence to 585 pence in mild profit-taking after a very strong surge in the week, thanks to bullish news emerging from analysts' visits to the company's Burger King, Pearle Vision and Pillsbury operations in the U.S.

Pearson, the publishing conglomerate, climbed 4 pence to 695 pence after saying that 1989 pretax profit gained 26%, beating market expectations. Polly Peck jumped 7 pence to 400 pence on the back of strong earnings disclosed Thursday.

Elsewhere in Europe, prices closed higher in Brussels, Paris, Frankfurt, Milan, and Zurich, and mixed in Amsterdam and Stockholm. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Meanwhile, stocks ended down in Sydney, Singapore and Wellington; and higher in Taipei and Seoul. Prices ended little changed in Manila and Hong Kong.
