Valeo Sees a Period Of Consolidation In Wake of Expansion

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/02/90

PARIS -- French auto parts maker Valeo S.A. is heading into a period of consolidation after adding production facilities and purchasing several companies in the U.S. and Europe during 1989, Noel Goutard, chairman, said. "We invested the equivalent of 9.3% of revenue in 1989 -- about 1.8 billion francs ($315 million) -- on modernization, new products and increasing capacity," he said, adding that the company now has to work on improving productivity and profitability of new operations.

Mr. Goutard was presenting the company's full results for 1989 to French financial analysts, following the release of revenue and profit figures to the press Tuesday.

Valeo, the No. 2 auto parts manufacturer in Europe after West Germany's Robert Bosch G.m.b.H., recorded a 15% net earnings rise last year to 936 million francs on revenue of 19.5 billion francs, up about 18% from 1988.

Analysts said they were disappointed with the results, concluding that net profit below one billion francs during a relatively good year for the auto industry portended hard times for Valeo in 1990, during which the global car market is expected to shrink.

Mr. Goutard said acquisitions and inflationary pressures weighed on the balance sheet in 1989. New purchases included engine cooling systems maker Delanair Ltd. of the U.K. and Blackstone Corp. of the U.S., as well as auto electronics concern G. Cartier Systemes S.A. of France.

Mr. Goutard said the companies acquired haven't yet contributed positively to Valeo's balance sheet. The companies generally had poor results on their own, and it's Valeo's task this year, through rationalization, to turn these new units into strong performers.

Regarding the inflation effect, which cut into sales, the Valeo executive said, "We weren't able to raise prices at the same pace as inflation" in most markets, reflecting sharp stiff competition from foreign auto parts makers.

He added that as part of the company's rationalization plan, 15 factories will be closed in 1990, leading to a decline of about 10% in Valeo's work force. The number of employees reached 34,200 last year, with 19,200 located in France and 15,000 abroad.

Mr. Goutard said the plant closings primarily will involve factories that don't fit in with the company's product focus. As an example, he cited a plastic injection plant run by Cartier Systemes.
