Dillon Read Loses Money Manager Timothy G. Dalton

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 04/02/90

NEW YORK -- Dillon, Read & Co. lost its top money manager, as Timothy G. Dalton Jr. left along with the securities firm's entire U.S. money-management unit to start their own company. Mr. Dalton, 51 years old, formed Dalton, Greiner, Hartman, Maher & Co. along with what he said would be about 20 co-workers from Dillon Read. The new company hopes to assume the $3 billion in assets that Dillon, Read Capital Inc., the unit that he headed, currently manages for 70 corporate pension funds and other institutions.

Mr. Dalton resigned as Dillon Read Capital's president and as a Dillon Read director.

Mr. Dalton's departure is one of a string of personnel changes at Dillon Read recently. The heads of risk arbitrage and private placements and the co-head of the corporate bond group have left, in what Dillon Read said are unrelated moves.

Dillon Read, a unit of Travelers Corp., said it will help Mr. Dalton transfer all its U.S. accounts to his new company, but that Dillon Read's international money-management units will remain part of Dillon Read. The company said it would have a "significant financial interest" in the Dalton Greiner company, but no direct equity interest or control over it.

"Dillon Read has been a great place to have been," said Mr. Dalton, who joined the company in 1982 when it set up Dillon Read Capital. The unit's clients include the Illinois Municipal Retirement Fund and the pension funds of Inland Steel Industries Inc. and Hughes Aircraft. Mr. Dalton said, "We're in the process of talking with everybody" about transferring their business to the new company.

Mr. Dalton has done well compared with other money managers. His stock accounts rose 10% in 1987, the year of the stock-market crash, and significantly outperformed the market with a 20% rise in 1988. But Mr. Dalton probably stayed bearish too long in 1989, when he managed only a 24% gain, compared with the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index's 31.7% gain with dividends reinvested.

Smaller stocks are the place to be these days, says Mr. Dalton, who says the market is in a "significant correction," rather than a bear market. But he is keeping 30% of his accounts' assets in cash to prepare for the stock-market declines ahead. Why small stocks? "The big ones haven't really corrected, and the small ones {already} have," he said.
