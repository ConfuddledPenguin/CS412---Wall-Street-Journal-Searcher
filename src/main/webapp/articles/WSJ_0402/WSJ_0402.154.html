Law -- Washington Docket: Legal and Political Skirmishes Preceded High Court Decision to Rule on Flag Law

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 04/02/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court's announcement that it will rule this spring on the new federal flag-burning law followed some unusual legal and political maneuvering. The justices announced on Friday that they will hold a special, hour-long argument on May 14, two weeks after the scheduled end of oral arguments for the term, to consider the constitutionality of the flag law passed last year by Congress. A decision in the case is expected in late June or early July.

A recent vote in the House demonstrates the strong passions the case has stirred. By a 309-101 vote, the House withdrew a brief filed in the Supreme Court by House counsel Steven Ross. The brief urged the court to hear the case and uphold the law. But the hot point of contention was the timing of Supreme Court review.

When it passed the law, Congress included a provision calling for expedited review by the high court. Based on that provision, after federal judges in Seattle and the District of Columbia declared the law unconstitutional, the Bush administration on March 14 urged the high court to put the appeal on a fast track for argument this month -- even though the usual deadline for April hearings had passed.

In contrast, the House brief had said Congress simply meant that the court should "give the case priority when it devises its argument calendar for October."

House Republican leaders provoked the vote to withdraw the brief, complaining that they weren't consulted on it, and focusing on the brief's suggestion that there was no need for the high court to rush to hear the case this spring.

The vote came to be seen as an election-year test of lawmakers' commitment to protecting the flag. The issue, said Rep. James Sensenbrenner (R., Wis.), was whether the House would "leave that flag unprotected for a longer period of time, maybe all summer, maybe till after the election."

After the House vote, Mr. Ross and Democratic leaders met with the Republicans; the next day, the brief was refiled, but the discussion of how quickly the case should be decided was deleted.

Democratic leaders insisted they hadn't been trying to delay Supreme Court action until after the November election. The suggestion in the brief for a routine schedule, rather than a fast track, was to give the lawyers ample time to research the case, they said.

Rep. Craig Washington (D., Texas), said that the November election had nothing to do with the issue, "except someone wants to use it for political purposes."

Ever since the high court struck down a Texas flag-desecration law last June, the question of how to protect the flag has been a tough one for Democrats. Most Democratic lawmakers pushed for the new federal law in the hope that it would be upheld by the Supreme Court and there would be no need for a constitutional amendment favored by many Republicans.

If the Supreme Court now strikes down the federal law, Democrats will find it difficult to oppose a constitutional amendment, especially a few months before the November congressional elections.
