Survey of Underwriting: Japan's Lead In Euromarket Appears to Ebb

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 04/02/90

NEW YORK -- Japanese dominance of the Euromarket, the hub of international capital raising, is showing signs of weakening. In the 1960s and 1970s, British and European banks dominated the Euromarket -- the offshore market where corporations and governments borrow outside their home countries. U.S. concerns led the pack during the first half of the 1980s. Then came the Japanese.

Now, the stranglehold of Japan's so-called Big Four -- Nomura Securities Co., Yamaichi Securities Co., Daiwa Securities Co. and Nikko Securities Co. -- is loosening. The Europeans are making a comeback.

But the volume of new issues of Eurobonds and international equities slumped 28% in the first quarter to $46 billion.

Nomura -- while still ranked No. 1 with a 13.1% market share -- had a 45% drop in its volume of Euromarket underwritings to $6 billion for the first quarter, according to Securities Data Co. Nikko saw its market share erode by 75%; Daiwa's share fell 55%. Yamaichi, ranked fourth a year ago, didn't even crack the top 10.

A major reason: The plunging yen and Tokyo stock market have dried up demand for once-hot Euromarket warrant securities issued by Japanese corporations. During the quarter, Euromarket underwriting deals by the Japanese with equity kickers have plummeted 72% to $5.5 billion amid a recent moratorium declared by Japanese securities firms on warrant deals.

"The yen has had an uncertain patch, which in turn has caused a blip in the warrant market," says a spokesman for Nomura in London. "It's not surprising to see a slight loosening of the grip of Japanese {investment} banks."

West Germany's Deutsche Bank was in second place, raising $4.1 billion, for a first-quarter market share of 9%. Union Bank of Switzerland jumped to $3.3 billion, or a 7.1% share. CS First Boston had a 5.7% share. Depending on the progress of German reunification in the coming months, specialists say it is conceivable that Deutsche Bank could eclipse Nomura this year, particularly if the Japanese market continues to stumble. "This might be the end of the Japanese dominance," says a U.S. securities executive.

Most U.S. investment banks haven't yet been able to take much advantage of the Japanese slip. J.P. Morgan & Co. is ranked seventh among all Euromarket underwriters, arranging for $1.7 billion in securities for a 3.8% market share. Salomon Inc.'s Salomon Brothers Inc. unit's share of the Euromarket jumped to 5% from 1.2% a year ago; Salomon ranked sixth, arranging for $2.3 billion in securities.
