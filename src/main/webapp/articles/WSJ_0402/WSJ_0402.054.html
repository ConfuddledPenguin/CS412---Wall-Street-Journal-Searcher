Calcium Supplement in Some P&G Juices Strengthens Children's Bones, Study Says

  • Author: Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 04/02/90

A patented calcium supplement found in some Procter & Gamble Co. juices builds bone density in children, according to a study funded by P&G and the National Institutes of Health. Researchers at Indiana University's School of Medicine said the first two years of a three-year study on the calcium supplement show that adding it to children's diets could help them avoid bone fractures and diseases later in life.

The results, to be released today, confirm what many scientists and consumers already knew: calcium builds bones. But this research shows a link between childhood diets and bone mass.

Marketers are skeptical, however, that the results will help the consumer products giant's Citrus Hill brand juices, which trail in third place in market share behind Minute Maid and Tropicana.

"This won't set the world on fire," said Michael Bellas, president of Beverage Marketing Corp. Added Tom Pirko, president of Bevmark Inc., a management consulting firm: "They may be able to make more claims about their juice, but it's not like they're rediscovering oat bran."

Besides, P&G launched its calcium-enriched juices three years ago and it hasn't made a big difference in P&G's share of the juice market. "People aren't enthralled," Mr. Pirko said.

The study, funded by P&G and the National Institutes of Health, was conducted on 50 sets of identical twins, from five to 14 years old. Testing twins eliminates genetic differences and helps control other factors, like environment and diet.

Each twin ate a daily diet containing about 900 milligrams of calcium. One twin also received a 1,000 milligram tablet of the calcium supplement each day. The preliminary results show substantial improvements in bone density in the forearm of the twin receiving the supplement. Precise measures of the improvement weren't available because the tests will continue for another year, researchers said.

The only place this supplement is found is in P&G's Citrus Hill Plus Calcium juices. To get the same dose, consumers would have to drink three, six-ounce servings a day.

Previous research had already indicated that the supplement is more fully absorbed than calcium carbonate, a common calcium supplement, said Judy Z. Miller, associate professor of medicine at Indiana University.

"Calcium is calcium," she said. "But this supplement makes more calcium available to the body."

Others were skeptical of the supplement's impact on disease, such as osteoporosis, a brittle bone disease that affects about 20 million people in the U.S. "Children that get supplemental calcium or a high-calcium diet, regardless of the source, will end up with denser bones," said James Simon, a reproductive endocrinologist at Georgetown University. "Any extrapolation on the risks of osteoporosis {later in life} is unwarranted."

P&G officials said they don't have immediate plans to launch new promotions of the Citrus Hill products or to introduce new products containing the supplement.
