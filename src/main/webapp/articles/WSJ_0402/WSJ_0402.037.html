Japan's Steel Exports In February Declined 20%, Sustaining Trend

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/02/90

TOKYO -- Continuing a sustained trend, Japan's steel exports in February declined 20% from a year earlier to 1.359 million metric tons, the Japan Iron and Steel Federation said. In November 1988, steel exports showed a year-to-year gain for the first time since August 1986, but they have fallen steadily again since then. A metric ton is equal to about 2,204.62 pounds.

The latest data followed drops of 22% in January and 24% in December.

Federation officials reiterated that Japanese steelmakers have been preoccupied with efforts to meet strong domestic demand that has been fueling sustained economic expansion. Recent surveys have shown that demand for steel from major domestic users such as auto makers, shipbuilders and construction companies remains strong.

"Demand in overseas markets . . . appears to be diminishing," an official said. "Other countries are also trying to diversify their sources of supply."

Reversing a long string of declines, exports to the U.S. rose 16% from a year earlier to 260,000 metric tons. Officials said shipments of seamless pipe, often used for oil drilling because of its strong resistance against pressure and corrosion, were especially strong. These more than doubled from the year-earlier levels. This may be an indication, the officials said, that oil drilling has been accelerating in the U.S.

The officials also pointed out, though, that exports in the year-earlier month, at about 225,000 metric tons, were particularly small as a result of brisk demand in the domestic market. This comparison made the pace of growth in February 1990 look good, they said. In January 1989, for example, steel exports to the U.S. were more than twice as large as in February 1989, or about 560,000 tons.

Steel exports to communist countries continued a persistent, sharp fall. Shipments to the Soviet Union plunged 52% to 47,000 metric tons, while those to China tumbled 62% to 134,000 tons. Officials said that China and the Soviet Union have been hurt by foreign currency shortages, and that this apparently is responsible for the relentless downturn. Economic conditions in the two countries haven't generated strong demand for steel, either, they said.

Exports to South Korea and Taiwain also slumped, off 44% to 89,000 metric tons and 34% to 114,000 tons, respectively.
