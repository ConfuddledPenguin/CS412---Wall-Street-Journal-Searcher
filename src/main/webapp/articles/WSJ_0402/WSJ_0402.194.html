Marketing & Media: Matsushita Subsidiary Unveils Erasable Disk For Video Recording

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/02/90

Matsushita Electric Industrial Co.'s Panasonic Communications & Systems Co. unveiled what it called the first erasable video disk recorder for a commercial market, aiming the pricey product at broadcasters but predicting wider uses. The new recorder, which uses lasers to encode up to 60 minutes of television pictures on a 12-inch, two-sided optical disk, offers a significant advantage over current videotape recorders: Its pictures can be recalled instantaneously, without having to rewind through unwanted footage. That could be a godsend for television sports producers, for example, allowing them to replay any part of a game on demand.

The device can also erase and re-record its images at least one million times, making its laser technology more economical because disks can be reused. And it has a computer hook-up, making it useful for big image databases.

Several Japanese companies already make erasable optical-disk drives for computers. Canon Inc. supplies such a drive for Next Inc.'s much-publicized workstation. But these drives store information digitally -- as ones and zeros -- making them impractical for information-intensive video images. Panasonic's device stores the images in analog form -- similar to traditional video tape -- rather than in digital form.

Ed Rothchild, a San Francisco consultant who specializes in optical storage, said erasable video disk recorders such as Panasonic's will eventually replace videotape recorders in the consumer marketplace -- but only when someone figures out a cost-effective way to fit a feature-length movie on one side of a disk.

Panasonic didn't announce a price for the recorder, which will be available in the fourth quarter, but indicated it will likely cost between $40,000 and $50,000.
