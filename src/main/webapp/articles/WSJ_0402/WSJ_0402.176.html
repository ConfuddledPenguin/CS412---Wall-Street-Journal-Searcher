COLUMBIA S&L DISCLOSED it is insolvent, likely resulting in a costly federal takeover. The big California thrift posted a $379.1 million loss for the 1989 fourth quarter, reflecting huge junk bond losses. Columbia was once the nation's most profitable thrift due to its big stake in junk bonds, many of them bought from Drexel and Michael Milken. ---

Wall Street's profits from underwriting stocks and bonds teetered near six-year lows in the first quarter. And the recent collapse of junk bonds promises even leaner times ahead.

Merrill Lynch continued to be the world's biggest underwriter of stocks and bonds in the quarter, but its lead shrank as financing volume eased.

---

Tokyo stocks plunged 5.7% by Monday morning's close as the dollar soared above 160 yen and institutions apparently began a sell-off.

---

Japanese trade negotiators will unveil a package of proposed market-opening measures today, but the steps are likely to disappoint the U.S.

---

Southmark filed a new reorganization plan with the help of unsecured creditors. The company hopes the plan may let it emerge from Chapter 11 proceedings by July.

---

A Farley company set up to buy West Point-Pepperell defaulted on a $733 million bank loan after talks to restructure the loan bogged down.

---

Chrysler is asking its top 100 executives to own stock equal to a certain percentage of their salaries. The unusual program is aimed at having the officials bet more of their pay on Chrysler's long-term financial health.

---

McDonnell Douglas confirmed it won a $2 billion jet order from Japan Air Lines that includes options for a stretched version of the MD-11 jet.

---

Factory orders rebounded 1.8% in February, helped by gains in the auto sector. The increase followed a revised 5.5% plunge the month before, the biggest in 15 years. Also, the February rise in durable-goods orders was revised to 2.6% from 3.3%.

Fed policy makers voted in February to leave credit unchanged. But for the first time in months, some officials talked of raising rates.

---

The Dallas investment firm Hicks Muse acquired seven I.C.H. insurance units for $530 million, one of the first major leveraged buy-outs since the UAL deal fell apart in October.

---

The American Heart Association is ending its controversial seal-of-approval program for low-fat foods, sources said. The FDA strongly objected to the two-month-old program.

---

Thrift nominee Timothy Ryan may face a tough confirmation fight on the Senate floor following the disclosure of his drug use in the early 1970s. Ryan was narrowly approved Friday by the Senate Banking Committee.

---

Motorola was allowed by a U.S. court to continue selling its key microchip, easing worries among computer makers about a shortage. The decision came in a patent infringement case involving Hitachi.

---

Digital Equipment plans to unveil new engineering workstations tomorrow, but they aren't as fast as the models IBM introduced in February.

---

The U.S. parent of Campeau's department store units filed for Chapter 11, saying it was a "procedural step" in the units' own reorganization.

---

BTR filed suit to prevent Norton from canceling its April 26 annual meeting. Norton said it needed more time to weigh alternatives to BTR's hostile $1.64 billion takeover bid.
