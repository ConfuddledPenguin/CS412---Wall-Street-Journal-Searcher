Big Steelmakers To Report Drop In 1st-Period Net --- Some Producers May Post Losses, Analysts Warn; Price Erosion Continues

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 04/02/90

Major steelmakers stumbled in the first quarter as continued price erosion nibbled away at their profits. Some producers may have even been unprofitable, analysts warn. John Jacobson, who monitors steel for AUS Consultants, believes the industry, including many mills, will actually lose about $400 million.

Analysts said profits for each ton of steel shipped will range from $15 to $25 in the first quarter. That result compares with $26 in the fourth quarter of 1989 and $39 a ton in last year's robust first quarter.

Among major steelmakers, USX Corp. appears to have fared the best in the latest period, partly reflecting the company's labor cost advantages. Analysts predict that the Pittsburgh steel and energy concern should earn between 55 and 60 cents a share compared with a $1.09 a year ago.

Most major steelmakers, except for USX, had higher first-quarter labor costs stemming from new labor pacts negotiated last year. Bethlehem Steel Corp., the first major steelmaker to sign a contract last year, is expected to earn about 15 cents a share in the latest quarter compared with 78 cents last year.

The automotive sector, which consumes about 23% of U.S. mills' output, was weak through much of the quarter, picking up toward the end. As a result, steelmakers, such as National Steel Corp., that are largely dependent on auto-related customers are expected to fare badly. Analysts expect National Steel, a Pittsburgh joint venture owned by National Intergroup Inc. and NKK Corp. of Japan, to earn about $12 a ton.

The outlook for the rest of the year is mixed. Mr. Jacobson said he initially thought the steel industry would suffer at least two quarterly losses in 1990. But now he expects a quicker upturn as prices begin to climb. "I think we're pretty much out of the woods," he said.

Other analysts, though, expect the lackluster first quarter to persist through 1990. "Look for anemic profits in 1990" said Peter Marcus, an analyst with PaineWebber Inc. He cited flat shipments, higher labor costs and continuing price weakness as reasons for the slump.

Some analysts, such as John Tumazos of Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette, believe softness in automotive and construction markets will extend to the previously strong capital goods market. "Historically, after the consumer segment is weak for two to four quarters, the capital goods market begins to ease," he said.

Steelmakers suffered from lower prices in the first quarter, a trend that began in the second half of 1989. Mr. Tumazos said average first-quarter prices fell to $491 a ton from $503 a ton in the fourth quarter. "I don't think the public will be aware of how deep the discounts were until they see the first-quarter results," he said.

Price declines have been most severe for commodity-type products such as hot rolled sheet. Mr. Marcus noted that the base spot price for that product fell to about $310 a ton from $325 in the fourth quarter and $400 a ton last spring. Moreover, world spot prices for even high quality products like cold rolled sheet recently fell an additional $40 a ton, partly as a result of overproduction, Mr. Marcus said.

Those steelmakers with more long-term contracts and less exposure to the spot market, such as Armco Inc., Parsippany, N.J., and Chicago-based Inland Steel Industries Inc., are less vulnerable to spot price discounting.

Also hurting first-quarter results, noted analyst Christopher Plummer of WEFA Group, were lower operating rates. Those rates were about 82% in the first quarter compared with the year-earlier quarter's 90%. As a result, fixed interest and labor costs were spread over fewer tons of steel shipped. "It's enough of a drop to have a significant impact on earnings," he said.

Still, shipments held up relatively well in the quarter despite the lackluster economy. One reason was the low level of imports. First-quarter shipments should total about 20 million tons, slightly higher than the fourth quarter but lower than the 1989 first quarter's 21.9 million tons. The strength in shipments is expected to continue in the second quarter as buyers rebuild inventories, a move that should also relieve some pressure on pricing.
