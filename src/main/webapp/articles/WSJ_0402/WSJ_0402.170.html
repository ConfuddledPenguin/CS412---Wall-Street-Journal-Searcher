Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Vigorous Data May Reflect Detour's End And Not a New Trend, Economists Warn

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 04/02/90

NEW YORK -- As one quarter slides seamlessly into the next, economists offer this advice: Don't confuse recovery with vigor. The March purchasing managers' index, to be released this morning, is believed to have gained ground -- particularly after a strong regional report last Friday from Chicago-area purchasers.

But economists caution that a better tone to the index -- as in some other indicators lately -- may simply reflect the end of a short detour, rather than the start of a new economic trend. Similarly, analysts plan to take with a grain of salt Friday's important employment report for March, because of some odd factors that help boost job totals. The week's other offerings include March auto sales and February data on consumer credit, the index of leading indicators and construction spending.

An improved purchasers' index "is more auto-related than anything else," Donald Fine, chief economist at Chase Manhattan Bank, New York, said. "The auto industry virtually shut down in January. In combination, February and March brought back production levels to near where they were in the fourth quarter."

Michael P. Niemira, economist at Mitsubishi Bank, New York, said the rebound in Chicago purchasers' data reaffirm his view that a "mini-inventory correction" that began at the end of last year may have run its course. Last Friday, the Purchasing Management Association of Chicago reported its index of area business activity rose to 55.1% on an unadjusted basis in March from 51% in February.

The broader report, which is to be released today by the National Association of Purchasing Management, is expected to inch ahead to 48.5% from 48.3% in February, according to a survey by MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc. The purchasing data have become increasingly important to economists and traders who are eager for data on economic activity and the possible direction of interest rates. In addition, the report usually provides the month's -- and the new quarter's -- first reading on the nation's manufacturing pulse.

A reading above 50% indicates an expanding economy, while a reading below 50% suggests contraction. The National Association of Purchasing Management index has been under 50% since May 1989.

The MMS survey on the national purchasers' index, conducted after Chicago's data were reported, showed a strengthening of 1.2 percentage points from a week earlier, when the median expectation was for a 47.3% reading on the national gauge.

Mitsubishi Bank's Mr. Niemira said he too raised his estimate of the association's index to 49% from 48.5% after the Chicago report came out. Still, he said: "The Chicago report should be kept in perspective. It's right where it was in the fourth quarter. Things aren't exactly booming."

Samuel Kahan, chief economist at Fuji Securities Inc. in Chicago, agreed that his city is "still enjoying a rebound" that probably plumped the local purchasers' report. But he also noted that numbers without context can be misleading. Pointing to the recent hearty employment data, he said: "People are taking the employment numbers at face value and extrapolating them to what the economy is doing. The truth is, I'm not sure you can hypothesize."

The unemployment rate for March is expected to remain unchanged at 5.3%. And new job growth, while a bit less significant than that in January and February, is likely to be fairly strong, with 175,000 nonfarm jobs created, according to MMS. But economists noted that early-year data were influenced by an unusual increase in construction jobs owing to mild weather. Recent jobs data have been affected by the ebb and flow of auto jobs. In addition, this week's report is likely to reflect the first major addition of specially hired census workers.

All in all, Mr. Kahan said, "It looks to me like the economy isn't gathering steam, but that we are continuing to flounder at this level."

While the unemployment rate is expected to be unchanged, estimates on new-job creation range broadly, from 100,000 to 303,000, according to MMS. In February, 275,000 new jobs were generated. In addition to job totals, economists are keeping a close eye on wage levels and their possible effect on inflation. Hourly earnings in March are believed to have increased about 0.2%.

February construction outlays, in a report to be released today, are expected to have risen about 0.5%, following a 1.8% increase in January, when the weather was unseasonably warm.

Economists don't expect much forecasting help from tomorrow's leading indicators index. The February index is believed to have dropped a sharp 0.7%, largely because of a steep drop in the number of housing permits issued. Housing permits skyrocketed in the previous month, as developers rushed to escape new handicapped-access rules.

Consumer credit, another indicator tied firmly to auto sales, is expected to have fallen in February. The credit tally, to be issued Friday, is expected to have slowed to about $3 billion, following January's $3.5 billion gain.
