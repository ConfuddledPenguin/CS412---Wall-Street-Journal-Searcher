Technology: Medicare Pays Billions for Health Claims Covered by Private Plans, Report Finds

  • Author: Walt Bogdanich
  • Date: 04/02/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal health officials need to plug holes more aggressively in the Medicare program or risk losing several billion dollars through unnecessary payments on medical claims, a new report concludes. The Medicare program for the elderly has been losing as much as $1 billion a year because it paid claims for which private health plans were liable, according to the report issued by Richard P. Kusserow, inspector general for the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services.

HHS's Health Care Financing Administration had proposed new legislation that would have provided data it needed to identify and stop the unnecessary payments, but the Office of Management and Budget objected, and HHS consequently backed off. That decision, said Mr. Kusserow, could cost the Medicare program $2.7 billion in unnecessary payments over the next five years. The dollar figure "is too large to ignore," he said. While HCFA has taken some administrative steps to identify the overpayments, he added, "none has proven completely effective."

HHS has known of these unnecessary payments for years, but until recently did little to stop them, investigators found. Nine insurance companies, including some of the nation's largest, are now being investigated by federal authorities for failing to pay claims.

Mr. Kusserow, in a separate report, asked the HCFA to pressure private health plans into reimbursing Medicare for billions of dollars that should have been paid previously. Under the inspector general's plan, insurers would have one year to voluntarily make restitution, or be subject to treble damages, plus costs, for each claim uncovered by the government.

The federal financing agency called Mr. Kusserow's suggestion appealing, but thus far has failed to back it fully. "We believe that the magnitude of the program losses . . . compels us to take creative approaches, such as the voluntary disclosure proposal," Mr. Kusserow said.

Gail R. Wilensky, recently appointed director of HCFA, said she wants to study the issues before deciding what to do.

In 1982, Congress tried to contain rising Medicare costs by making Medicare a "secondary insurer" for the so-called working aged. That made the employer-sponsored health plan the "primary insurer" for any claimant whose employer provided medical benefits.

Federal health officials, however, made little effort to identify those individuals who should have had their claims paid by private health plans. One reason was because the government turned over administration of the program to some of the very insurance companies that stood to benefit from erroneous payments. Some insurance companies looked the other way when they discovered Medicare overpayments that benefited their own companies, according to government investigators.
