American Continental Suit Citing Regulators Is Dismissed by Court

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/02/90

PHOENIX, Ariz. -- A federal judge dismissed the lawsuit of American Continental Corp. against federal regulators it had accused of damaging its Lincoln Savings & Loan Association subsidiary by leaking confidential information to the press. Federal Judge Richard M. Bilby acted on a motion by American Continental to dismiss the case without prejudice, meaning that it may be refiled or reopened. But Judge Bilby added that unless American Continental takes action by July 1, the case would be dismissed with prejudice.

American Continental filed for reorganization under Chapter 11 of the federal bankruptcy code last April 13. The government seized Lincoln Savings, of Irvine, Calif., the next day. More than 23,000 investors claim they weren't warned of the risks in buying uninsured junk bonds of Lincoln Savings. They lost $200 million when the thrift failed.

Judge Bilby precipitated the company's March 7 dismissal motion when he barred American Continental from continuing to pay attorneys to pursue the case. He said he feared that attorneys' fees would use up the equity in American Continental.

James Feder, an attorney for American Continental, said it wasn't clear whether the firm will attempt to refile or reopen the suit. "We don't have the time and resources to pursue this suit with the other litigation we have going," he said.

Government regulators have estimated that the Lincoln failure may cost taxpayers $2 billion.

American Continental had argued that top thrift regulators regularly leaked information to the press that hurt the survival possibilities of Lincoln Savings. Named as defendants were M. Danny Wall, former director of the Office of Thrift Supervision; Darrel W. Dochow, acting principal supervisory agent for Lincoln, and William K. Black, general counsel of the Federal Home Loan Bank Board in San Francisco.

The defendants couldn't be reached for comment.
