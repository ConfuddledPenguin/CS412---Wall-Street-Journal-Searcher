Advertising: AHA to Drop Seal Endorsing Low-Fat Foods

  • Author: Joanne Lipman
  • Date: 04/02/90

The American Heart Association is about to close down its controversial seal-of-approval program for low-fat foods, just two months after the program was launched, marketing executives say. The AHA is expected to announce today or tomorrow that it is discontinuing the seal because of objections from the Food and Drug Administration. AHA officials wouldn't confirm the move, but Scott Ballin, vice president of public affairs, conceded that "the FDA has problems with the seal, and with third-party endorsements of food products by us or anyone. The next question is, based on those facts, where do we go from here?"

AHA officials met throughout the weekend to discuss their options. Industry executives believe the group, while doing away with the seal itself, will refocus on other parts of the seal program, known as HeartGuide. Among the alternatives they are considering are education efforts and working with the FDA either on a different labeling program or to refine the regulatory agency's own proposed food labeling regulations.

The seal-of-approval concept has been troubled from the start. The AHA developed the program to identify foods it deemed low in cholesterol, sodium and fats. Brands that passed muster -- and paid a fee -- could display the AHA HeartGuide logo on their labels and in advertising. The AHA had grand visions of making the seal the equivalent of the Good Housekeeping seal, and encouraged products that bore it to trumpet the fact in ads. When the seal was introduced in early February, several brands, including Mazola and Promise margarines and Pam non-stick spray, were planning major marketing campaigns around it.

But the program immediately raised the ire of the FDA, which promptly threatened regulatory action. In a warning letter sent in February, James S. Benson, acting commissioner of the FDA, told the AHA that its program would "increase consumer confusion and hamper any comprehensive solution to the food-labeling problem." For example, the FDA was concerned that the HeartGuide seal on margarine and oils would lead consumers to believe that these were health foods. Friday, the FDA repeated its objections to third-party endorsements in another letter to the AHA.

The FDA's complaints prompted most of the 103 brands in the initial launch of HeartGuide to back out before the program even started; among the defectors were Mazola, Pam, Promise and C&W Frozen Foods. But several dozen remained, with some going through the expense of redesigning their packaging to show off the seal. They will now have to remove the seal, if they haven't already.

The HeartGuide seal's demise comes as the FDA is considering its own health-claim labeling regulations, perhaps with input from the AHA. The FDA earlier this year proposed tough new labeling restrictions that would crack down on many claims, and probably would discourage many of the labeling claims made today. But consumer activist groups have criticized the FDA for dragging its feet on health claims.

"I'm concerned that the FDA won't go far enough or act quickly enough," says Bonnie Liebman, director of nutrition for the Center for Science in the Public Interest, a consumer activist group. "The demise of this program means we need to put more pressure on the FDA. It provides one more reason to push for legislation that would force the FDA to overhaul food labels." Two bills pending on Capitol Hill would require the FDA to define terms like "light" and "low fat."

The FDA declined to comment. It may have been concerned because other health groups were considering their own stamps of approval for foods, which could have led to consumer confusion.

Still, says Ms. Liebman, the demise of the HeartGuide seal "is a shame for consumers who want simple thumbs up or thumbs down advice on whether a food is good for their heart. This kind of labeling is something the FDA should have been doing a long time ago."

Campbell Soup Accounts

Campbell Soup announced a long-expected consolidation of its ad agency lineup, cutting out three agencies while spreading the bulk of its $150 million account more equally among four others.

In the reorganization, Campbell cuts out DDB Needham, Hal Riney & Partners and Saatchi & Saatchi/London, each of which had relatively small pieces of Campbell business. BBDO and FCB/Leber Katz Partners are the biggest beneficiaries of the consolidation, picking up most of that business and putting them closer in league to Backer Spielvogel Bates, which had been Campbell's largest agency.

Backer Spielvogel, which had an estimated $35 million in Campbell business, wasn't affected by the agency shake-up, but now faces formidable competition from its fellow Campbell agencies. BBDO's billings now rival Backer Spielvogel's. Industry executives say FCB/Leber Katz's billings are slightly less than that, as are those of Ogilvy & Mather, another major shop that retained all of its business except for a small Swanson account.

Campbell, based in Camden, N.J., said it made the shifts because of its planned agency incentive program, in which compensation will be tied to sales performance. Campbell said it plans to tell its agencies details of the plan "in a few weeks." The program is to go into effect August 1.

BBDO, like DDB Needham an Omnicom Group agency, picks up Swanson frozen foods, which had been handled by both Riney and Ogilvy & Mather. BBDO also won Mrs. Paul's Kitchens frozen food, an assignment previously handled at DDB Needham. BBDO will continue to handle Campbell's Chunky soups and Campbell's Home Cookin' soups.

FCB/Leber Katz, a unit of Foote, Cone & Belding that currently handles the Prego spaghetti sauce account, picks up the Souper Combo account, which had been handled at BBDO. The agency was also named to handle the V8 juice business, which had been at Saatchi & Saatchi, London.

Ogilvy & Mather, a unit of WPP Group, will continue to handle a number of brands, including Le Menu dinners and Pepperidge Farm products. Backer Spielvogel Bates, a Saatchi & Saatchi agency, will retain Campbell's condensed soups and Campbell's Special Request line of soups.

Ad Notes. . . .

WELLS RICH: Jim Lawrence, the top executive on the Ford Motor account at Wells, Rich, Greene's Detroit office, is returning to New York to direct the shop's newly won Continental Airlines account, industry executives said. Mr. Lawrence, an executive vice president at Wells Rich, is expected to continue to work on the Ford business from New York. Mr. Lawrence, who couldn't be reached, is the stepson of agency founder Mary Wells Lawrence.

AMMIRATI: Ammirati & Puris said it received financing commitments from a New York bank to complete the buy-back of its stock from Omnicom Group. The buy-back, estimated at about $30 million, is expected to be completed by the end of May.

BBDO: Pacific Telesis Group's Pacific Bell unit, San Francisco, named BBDO, San Francisco, agency of record for its Information Services Group account. Billings are $5 million to $10 million.

DMB&B: Allied-Lyons PLC's Hiram Walker unit awarded its Harveys Bristol Cream ad account to D'Arcy Masius Benton & Bowles, Bloomfield Hills, Mich. Billings are $4 million to $6 million.

FOOTE CONE: Foote, Cone & Belding, Chicago, said it merged its Melbourne, Australia, office with Thomson White & Partners, a Melbourne agency. The agency will be called Thomson White/FCB. Foote Cone also said it acquired an equity stake in Fancourt Holdings, the third-largest agency group in New Zealand. Terms of the transactions weren't disclosed.

FALLON: General Mills, Minneapolis, named Fallon McElligott, Minneapolis, ad agency for its Bringer's food delivery venture. Billings weren't disclosed.
