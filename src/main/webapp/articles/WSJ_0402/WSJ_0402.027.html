Rubbermaid Says First Quarter Net Rose About 13%

  • Author: Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 04/02/90

Rubbermaid Inc. will post first-quarter net income of about $33 million, or 45 cents a share, up 13% from $29.3 million, or 40 cents a share, a year earlier, Stanley C. Gault, chairman and chief executive officer, said. The expected earnings increase reflects higher sales of Rubbermaid's housewares and lower raw-materials costs, the executive said in an interview.

Mr. Gault said revenue in the quarter rose slightly from $349 million in the 1989 first quarter. The revenue gains would have been stronger, officials said, had Rubbermaid not held back on its retail inventory-building due to concerns about the economy. "We want merchandise to move through the stores, not just get the numbers up," Mr. Gault said.

In its core housewares division, orders from retailers were "on the light side" during the early weeks of the quarter but have improved, said Walter W. Williams, president and chief operating officer.

Despite some worries about the economy, Rubbermaid continues its fast-paced rate of new product rollouts. The company unveiled 187 new housewares and specialty products in January, including a 50-item line of brooms and brushes.

Rubbermaid is optimistic of fast growth in that category, despite the mature state of the market. The reason: larger retailers could choose to stock the Rubbermaid line instead of buying a variety of products from numerous suppliers.

Besides, Rubbermaid hopes to create more demand by boosting its advertising and promotion spending on that product line and others. "We want to further enhance our image with the consumer," Mr. Gault said. Last year's advertising and promotion budget was about 3.6% of sales, or about $45 million.

For 1990, that will increase slightly, although the company officials didn't offer details. One factor is how much Rubbermaid saves on its raw-material costs. The company expects polyethylene prices to drop during the year as suppliers add capacity to plastic plants.

Rubbermaid's Little Tikes toy division is expected to post sales gains of between 10% and 12% over the year-ago quarter, Mr. Williams said. The division's new management is considering how to expand that business.

The company's commercial products business should show "average growth" in the quarter after lagging behind in 1989, Mr. Williams said. One factor is the cautious spending among food-service companies and others.

For all of 1990, Rubbermaid agrees with analysts' earnings estimates of between $1.80 and $1.85 a share, Mr. Gault said, with revenue for the year topping last year's $1.34 billion. However, the revenue figure won't include about $70 million in sales from the housewares business in Europe. Those sales are now part of a joint venture that isn't consolidated in Rubbermaid's income statement.

Capital spending is expected to climb 17% to about $100 million in 1990. The increase will cover new product introductions, equipment replacements and other improvements, Mr. Williams said.

During the year, Rubbermaid will focus on management succession in anticipation of Mr. Gault's retirement in May 1991. Mr. Williams is expected to become chief executive. "I won't be running the company, but I'll be around to talk about things," said Mr. Gault, who will remain a board member.
