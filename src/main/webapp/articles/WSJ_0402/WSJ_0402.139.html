Highway Agency Keeps Fuel Standard at 20.2 MPG

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/02/90

WASHINGTON -- The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration said it will keep the fuel economy standard for 1992-model light trucks and vans at 20.2 miles per gallon. In January, the agency had said it would set the standard no lower than 20.2 mpg but might increase it to as high as 21. NHTSA administrator Jerry Ralph Curry said the agency decided to leave the standard unchanged from 1991 models after considering auto makers' "fuel economy capabilities, current projections, underlying product plans and possible technological improvements."

Both Ford Motor Co. and Chrysler Corp. praised the decision to keep the standard at 20.2 mpg. "We had strongly urged that they set the standard at the low end of the proposed range and that's what they did," said David L. Kulp, Ford's manager of fuel economy planning and compliance.

Mr. Kulp said the "the market has been moving in the direction of full-sized trucks," making it difficult for Ford to raise its average fuel economy, currently at 20.2 mpg.

Under federal law, the agency must establish fuel economy standards for light trucks each year. Manufacturers failing to meet the standards are subject to civil penalties. The standard for 1990 models is 20 mpg.

The new standard applies to pickup trucks, vans and utility vehicles with a gross vehicle weight of as much as 8,500 pounds.
