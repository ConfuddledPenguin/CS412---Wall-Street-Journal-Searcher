U.S. Tops Exporters in '89 With $364 Billion in Sales

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/02/90

WASHINGTON (AP) -- The U.S. regained its spot as the world's leading exporter last year after losing it to West Germany, according to the International Monetary Fund. The IMF said U.S. foreign sales were valued at $364 billion for 1989, compared with $341.4 billion for West Germany. Japan was third with $273.9 billion.

West Germany, which toppled the U.S. from the No. 1 trade spot in 1986, and Japan both had trade surpluses, while the U.S. still had a deficit of $128.9 billion, down from $137.1 billion, according to the IMF's trade calculations.

The deficit is because of the fact that the U.S. is also the world's greatest importer. Its imports also grew rapidly, to $492.9 billion from $459.5 billion.

The West German surplus, currently the world's largest, amounted to $71.4 billion, and Japan's was $63.9 billion, the IMF reported.

The Commerce Department, using different means of calculating the trade imbalance, has put the 1989 merchandise trade deficit at $109 billion.

Of six major countries, the U.S. had the largest increase in foreign sales, 12.5%, the IMF said. West German exports also rose, by 5.6% to $341 billion. Since East Germany also is an important trader, a reunified Germany may take over the world's first place as exporter once again.
