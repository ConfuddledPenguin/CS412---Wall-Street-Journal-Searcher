Credit Markets: Bonds Got Whipped in Quarter by Rising Rates But Junk Issues Start Staggering to Their Feet

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 04/02/90

NEW YORK -- The bond market was an awful place to be in the first quarter, although junk bonds finally began showing a glimmer of strength. Across the board, bond investors found their fixed-income portfolios clobbered by rising interest rates.

In the four major bond-market categories -- long-term Treasurys, mortgages, municipals and corporates -- not one produced a positive return, according to data from Merrill Lynch Capital Markets.

Investors who bought long-term Treasury bonds at the start of the year had posted a miserable total return of negative 4.02% by the end of the first quarter. Total return measures price changes and interest income. That dead performance reflects a sharp rise in interest rates. The yield on 30-year Treasury bonds, for example, rose to 8.62% last week from 7.99% at the end of December.

Last year, when interest rates were falling, long-term Treasury bonds were the best performing sector of the bond market, which goes to show how volatile the bond market has become lately.

The first-quarter bond market report held some encouraging news for the long-suffering junk market. Although junk bonds produced a negative 2.11% total return in the quarter, they put in a respectable performance in March.

First Boston Corp., which tracks the performance of 343 junk bonds, said that total return of junk bonds rose nearly 3% in March following two sharp declines in January and February.

The junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. fueled the advance amid intensifying rumors that Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. would buy back as much as $2 billion of the company's reset pay-in-kind debentures. On Friday, some RJR bonds advanced as much as 2 1/8 points -- about $21.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount -- as a Business Week article said RJR was leaning toward a buy-back. People close to KKR disputed the report, saying no such decision has been made.

RJR's junk bonds aren't the only gainers these days. First Boston said that three junk bonds advanced last month for each junk bond that declined. The advance-decline ratio "shows that the rebound is broad based," said Robert Long, a First Boston managing director.

Mr. Long said junk bonds are being supported in part by corporate buy-backs. "Some companies have been buying $10 million to $20 million of their bonds back. That's been putting more cash in the hands of (junk bond) portfolio managers, and we're starting to see that cash go to work," said Mr. Long. "Our trading desk is running 2% to 3% higher volume."

Still, most investors would have been better off by avoiding bonds altogether and putting their money into cash and cash equivalents such as short-term Treasury bills. Merrill Lynch said that three-month Treasury bills produced a total first-quarter return of 1.96% while six-month bills produced a 1.89% return. Cash levels help to protect portfolios during down markets.

Despite the first quarter's dismal performance, some money managers remain optimistic about the remainder of the year.

"Our view is that this is a correction in a bull market" for bonds, said Roger Early, a portfolio manager at Federated Investors Corp. Mr. Early says that the economy is growing very slowly, corporate profits have been poor and corporate borrowing is dwindling. Such indicators, he said, typically indicate that interest rates will be heading lower, a trend that boosts prices for existing bonds.

Friday's Market Activity

Treasury bonds were pummeled after the release of a stronger-than-expected March survey of purchasing agents in the Chicago area.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond end 3/8 point lower, or about $3.75 for each $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, rose to 8.62% from 8.58%.

The bond market started the session on solid ground after prices got a modest boost from buying in Europe. But the market quickly switched directions, with 30-year bond prices falling a full point immediately after news that the Purchasing Management Association of Chicago had seen its index of area business activity rise to 55.1% on an unadjusted basis in March from an unrevised 51.0% in February.

The increase in the Chicago index was greater than had been expected. It raised concerns that the March survey of purchasing agents nationwide, due out today, would show a similar acceleration in business activity.

In the corporate market, the high-yield junk bonds of RJR Nabisco continued to surge. The bonds were up all week on rumors that KKR would buy back a portion of the reset notes. Officials at RJR have consistently declined to comment on the rumors.

RJR's 13.71% convertible pay-in-kind debentures maturing in 2009 gained 2 1/8 to finish at 74 3/4 in moderate volume on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely held RJR issue, the 13.71% pay-in-kind exchangeable debentures due in 2007, advanced 1 3/8 points to end at 67 1/8. Pay-in-kind debt pays interest in the form of additional securities.

Most other junk bonds, which are speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or none at all, fell between 1/8 point to 1/4 point.

Municipal bonds ended mostly unchanged, with some issues down 1/8 to 1/4 point.
