What's News -- World-Wide

  • Date: 04/02/90

THE SOVIET UNION SENT military reinforcements to Lithuania's capital. As the breakaway Baltic republic's legislature met to consider its next moves, dozens of Soviet military vehicles lumbered through the streets of Vilnius, and the last Western journalists were ousted from the republic on orders from the Soviet government. The activity prompted some Lithuanians to express concern about an impending Kremlin military crackdown. On Saturday, Gorbachev had urged the republic to renounce its March 11 proclamation of independence and to enter talks with Moscow.

Lithuania's president rejected Gorbachev's appeal, but said the republic would send negotiators to Moscow today to arrange talks with Soviet officials.

THATCHER BLAMED extremists for turning a tax protest into a riot.

The British prime minister, as well as opposition politicians, criticized "extreme groups who used violence" for the "mayhem" that erupted Saturday at an anti-tax protest in London. More than 130 people were injured and about 340 arrested in the rioting, considered the worst in decades. Sporadic unrest flared yesterday as the unpopular tax, which generally replaces a property tax with a levy on each adult, took effect in England and Wales. Thatcher made clear she won't back down over the issue.

About 300 inmates rioted at an overcrowded prison in Manchester, England, occupying rooftops and setting fires. About 50 people were injured.

---

East Berlin officials warned that a Bundesbank proposal for a 2-to-1 conversion rate for East German marks into West German marks could hinder the unification of the two Germanys. The recommendation marked a retreat from pledges made by Bonn's leader during recent East German elections.

---

Mandela offered to meet with South Africa's President de Klerk to discuss how to end violence, but said police conduct would determine when the African National Congress holds talks with the government. The black nationalist leader indicated that there were differences within the ANC, which canceled April 11 negotiations with Pretoria.

---

Police in China barred the general public from entering Beijing's Tiananmen Square, where dissidents had urged a silent protest against last year's government crackdown on student-led demonstrations. Paramilitary forces ringed most of the huge square in the latest sign of tightened security in the capital ahead of anniversaries marking unrest.

---

Idaho Gov. Andrus vetoed what would have been the nation's most restrictive abortion law. The legislation would have prevented about 95% of the state's abortions. Friday's veto by Andrus, considered an opponent of abortion, left anti-abortion leaders searching for new opportunities to press their efforts before the Supreme Court.

---

Hundreds of civilians in Lebanon fled the country's Christian enclave as a cease-fire ended two days of fighting between rival Christian forces. More than 60 people were killed and about 130 injured in the battles. The truce, the 15th in two months of fighting, was negotiated by a three-member mediation committee backed by the Vatican.

---

Cases of venereal disease are rebounding at gay clinics in Los Angeles, Seattle and Denver, and the rate of new AIDS-virus infections in Chicago has risen for the first time in five years. The increases were attributed by some to a return to unsafe-sex practices within the homosexual community and elsewhere.

---

Medical researchers reported that damage from spinal-cord injuries, frequently the cause of paralysis, can be reduced by a common generic drug. Such injuries afflict about 10,000 people a year. Researchers said that large doses of methylprednisolone, an anti-inflammatory drug, given within eight hours of injury can limit damage.

---

Zimbabwe's President Mugabe vowed to press ahead with plans to turn the nation's democracy into a one-party state after results from last week's elections gave the country's ruling party all but three of the 150 seats in Harare's legislature. But the 66-year-old Mugabe indicated that opposition parties wouldn't be immediately outlawed.

---

Honduras's military pledged to search for gunmen who attacked a bus carrying 28 U.S. airmen about six miles north of Tegucigalpa, leaving eight wounded, at least two seriously. A leftist rebel group, the Morazano National Liberation Front, claimed responsibility for the assault Saturday.
