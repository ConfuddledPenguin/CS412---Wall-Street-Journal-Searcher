U.S. Finds McKinzie, Thrift Business Aide, Guilty on 22 Counts

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/02/90

SANTA ANA, Calif. -- Janet Faye McKinzie, business manager and executive consultant for the late president of the now-insolvent North America Savings & Loan Association, was found guilty by a federal jury of stealing $15.5 million from the thrift. The jury found Ms. McKinzie guilty of 22 counts of racketeering, conspiracy, wire and bank fraud, and interstate transportation of stolen property. Under the various counts, she could be sentenced to a total of 175 years in prison. A sentencing date hasn't yet been set.

Thrift regulators said the collapse of Santa Ana-based North America Savings is one of the worst examples of insider corruption ever found at an S&L. The thrift's demise eventually cost taxpayers $120 million, regulators said.

The jury of six men and six women took eight days to return its verdict, after a trial that lasted eight weeks.

Ms. McKinzie had pleaded innocent by reason of insanity. Her attorneys had argued that her judgment was impaired by prescription drugs she was taking. She couldn't be reached for comment.

Duayne D. Christensen, a Westminster, Calif., dentist, invested $6.4 million to open North American Savings in 1983. Ms. McKinzie was a consultant to Dr. Christensen in operating the thrift. In 1986, the thrift lost $8.9 million in the first 11 months. Government prosecutors charged that the two lived a lavish life style, using thrift funds to buy Rolls Royce autos, a $125,000 gold eagle statue and an $18,500 gold letter opener.

In 1987, a week before federal regulators declared the thrift insolvent, Dr. Christensen, 57 years old, died when his Jaguar crashed into a freeway overpass. The Orange county coroner classified the cause of his death as undetermined, failing to rule whether it was an accident or suicide.

The jury will decide in a second trial in April whether Ms. McKinzie will forfeit benefits from a $10 million life insurance policy left her by Dr. Christensen. Government prosecutors contend that Dr. Christensen purchased the policy with proceeds from the theft of North America's assets. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. also is trying to seize the insurance proceeds in a case pending in Los Angeles federal court.
