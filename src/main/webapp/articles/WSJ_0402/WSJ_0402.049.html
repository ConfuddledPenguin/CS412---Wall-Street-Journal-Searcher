Lomas Financial To Sell Lease Unit To GE Subsidiary

  • Author: Michael Allen
  • Date: 04/02/90

DALLAS -- Lomas Financial Corp. said it agreed to sell its commercial leasing subsidiary to General Electric Co.'s General Electric Capital Corp. for about $161 million. The price, which was far below the book value Lomas assigned to the subsidiary as of June 30, 1989, or $324.2 million, underscored the erosion of Lomas's net worth since it filed for federal bankruptcy-court protection last September.

But Lomas officials hailed the development, saying that it would allow the financial-services company to shed the leasing unit's $1 billion of debt and $30.6 million of preferred stock. "Although in terms of the recoupment of equity it was not as high a price as we might have hoped, our focus has remained on the fundamental objective" of slashing debt, Chairman Jess Hay said in an interview.

Mr. Hay said the sale will fulfill the company's goal, formulated last April, of cutting its then $5.8 billion debt load by 60% to 65% and focusing its operations on its core mortgage operations. Debt will be reduced to about $2.3 billion if the sale is completed, he added.

The low sale price wasn't unexpected. In its second quarter ended Dec. 31, Lomas took a $125 million charge against earnings to cover net losses it expected to incur by selling its leasing unit, known as Ellco Leasing Corp., and its life-insurance segment. Lomas said Ellco's book value is now $164 million, reflecting a write-down of the unit's intangible assets.

Frank Anderson, an analyst with Stephens Inc., Little Rock, Ark., had previously estimated that the unit would bring between $150 million and $200 million. "Once you enter bankruptcy, especially if you're a financial-services company, individual units as a general rule tend to deteriorate," he said.

Lomas paid about $263.3 million in cash and stock to acquire Ellco from Equitable Life Assurance Society of the U.S. in 1987. The unit, based in San Diego, employs about 300 people and leases computer equipment and other high-technology office gear to large corporations throughout the U.S.

General Electric Capital Corp., a financial services unit of Fairfield, Conn.-based GE, couldn't be reached for comment.

Sale of the Lomas unit would require approval from a federal bankruptcy court in New York. Under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code, a company receives court protection from creditors while it works out a plan to repay its debts.
