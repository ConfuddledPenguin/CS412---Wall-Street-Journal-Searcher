Survey of Underwriting: Merrill Hangs On to Top Underwriting Spot

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 04/02/90

NEW YORK -- Merrill Lynch & Co. is still tops among global underwriters of all stocks and bonds, but its grip slipped in the first quarter as world-wide financing volume eased. Merrill, the nation's largest securities firm, saw its underwriting lead in the U.S. cut in half from a year earlier. Goldman Sachs & Co. is hot on Merrill's heels in both the U.S. and global underwriting races. And Morgan Stanley & Co. and Bear Stearns Cos. made headway at the expense of First Boston Corp.

"Merrill does well when the run-of-the-mill company is seeking additional funds," says Perrin Long Jr., senior securities analyst for Lipper Analytical Securities Corp. "When it becomes quality-type companies, the top-tier investment bankers -- Morgan Stanley and Goldman -- do well."

Merrill was hard hit by the pullback of financial institutions from issuing new securities. These firms, including commercial banks and savings and loans, raised just $13.5 billion in the first quarter, down nearly 50% from year-earlier levels. This helped pull down the volume of conventional debt offerings underwritten by Merrill to $6.5 billion from $9.8 billion a year ago.

Merrill officials say many corporate issuers are sitting tight, waiting for clues to the direction of interest rates. And Merrill contends that it's difficult to draw conclusions from just one quarter.

"It's a little bit like talking to the coach at the end of the first quarter in a basketball game," says Edson Mitchell, a Merrill managing director.

Despite its slip, Merrill continues to lead in most sectors of the debt market. It's still the leading manager of domestic new issues, raising $12.4 billion, and tops in global new issues, raising $12.8 billion in the first quarter, according to Securities Data Co.

And Mr. Mitchell expects Merrill to continue its dominance because of what he says are a strong capital markets operation and trading and distribution outlets.

Overall, a burst of new issues in the past two weeks propped first-quarter stock and bond offerings in the U.S. to $77.7 billion, up from $73.3 billion a year earlier. But political and economic uncertainty hammered overseas activity, particularly the Japanese market; the volume of global stock and bond issues slipped 10% from 1989's first period.

The collapse of the junk-bond market, meanwhile, helped trigger a pickup in mortgage and asset-backed debt. Investors, skittish about junk, turned to high-quality mortgage paper backed by the U.S. government, analysts said. For the first time, mortgage and asset-backed securities accounted for more than 50% of total new-issue volume of stock and debt securities.

But the traditional leaders of this sector -- Salomon Brothers Inc., First Boston and Goldman -- were toppled. Bear Stearns took the crown for the period, underwriting $5.9 billion of mortgage-backed bonds for a market share of 13.7%.

Goldman's overall strong performance stemmed from common stock offerings; it raised $1.4 billion for a market share of 19.1%. (Overall, issuers of common stock raised more than $7 billion in the first quarter, an 82% increase from a year earlier.) Goldman also topped the list of initial public offerings, underwriting $926.6 million in the period.

Morgan Stanley led the pack in underwriting conventional debt securities, raising $7.6 billion, a market share of nearly 30%. The bulk of Morgan Stanley's success in the quarter stems from $1.7 billion raised for employee stock ownership trusts and the firm's strong relationship with federal credit agencies, especially the Student Loan Marketing Association, or Sallie Mae.

Sallie Mae, 1990's largest issuer of corporate debt, called on Morgan Stanley to underwrite 49% of the $3.9 billion it issued so far this year.
