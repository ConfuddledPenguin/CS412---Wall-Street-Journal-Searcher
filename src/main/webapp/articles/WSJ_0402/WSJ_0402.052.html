International: West German Plan On Mark Exchange Stirs Ire in East

  • Author: Terence Roth and Mark M. Nelson
  • Date: 04/02/90

The Bundesbank's proposal for a 2-to-1 conversion rate for East German marks into West German marks triggered an uproar in East Germany. The recommendation by Bundesbank President Karl-Otto Poehl at a meeting of European Community finance ministers and central bank governors in Galway, Ireland, marked a retreat from pledges made by West German leaders during recent East German elections. Mr. Poehl said a 1-to-1 conversion rate should be limited to a maximum 2,000 marks per East German citizen. The rest of East German savings, debt and wages would be converted at the rate of two East marks for one West German mark. A West German mark has a value of 59 U.S. cents at current exchange rates.

Furious East Berlin officials warned that anything less than a broadly implemented 1-for-1 rate would impoverish most East German wage earners and even hinder the unification of the two Germanys. Leaders of the major East German parties joined together over the weekend to reject the plan, and East German Premier Hans Modrow said in a newspaper interview the proposal has already stirred widespread unrest in the East German population. He insisted that West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl and other Bonn officials had promised East German savers a 1-for-1 rate in the run-up to East Germany's March 18 election, which resulted in a landside victory for Bonn-supported conservatives.

"The election result was the result of these promises, and nothing else," Mr. Modrow declared. The head of the caretaker government insisted that East Germans had interpreted the promises as having a broad application, including wages.

Mr. Modrow warned that Bonn would be held responsible for any strikes or demonstrations that might result from the apparent policy shift. Officials for the East German Christian Democrats reported that their East Berlin headquarters was flooded with calls from angry citizens.

Rainer Eppelmann, head of the Democratic Awakening party, one of the three East German parties making up the CDU-led conservative alliance, predicted a fresh wave of emigration to West Germany.

The proposal unsettled Mr. Kohl's allies back in Bonn. Otto Lambsdorff, head of the Free Democratic Party, the junior partner in the Bonn government coalition, told a newspaper the proposal could be seen as "a breach of faith."

East Germans can now buy West German marks at a subsidized rate of one for each three East marks. Monetary officials calculate the East mark's true purchasing power suggests a rate of 4.4 to one. Even a 2-to-1 rate would be a vast improvement for East German workers, they say.

While the West German government hasn't accepted the 2-for-1 proposal, Finance Minister Theo Waigel said it was "economically and financially well founded" and that it couldn't be ignored, while Economics Minister Helmut Haussmann called the recommendation an "important decision" that wouldn't inflict economic harm on East Germans.

While unpopular among the populace, a 2-for-1 rate would be a boon to highly indebted East German state companies, which wouldn't have to use as much hard currency to pay off debts, and would make East German companies more competitive with Western ones. Such a rate would also be less inflationary.
