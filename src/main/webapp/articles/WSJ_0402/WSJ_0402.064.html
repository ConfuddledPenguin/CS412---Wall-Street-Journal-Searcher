Machine Tools Slump Expected To Continue --- Weak Orders, Thin Profits Forecast for 1st Quarter Amid Price Competition

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 04/02/90

The nation's machine tool builders have earned sparse profits during the current economic expansion, and so far 1990 doesn't signal any improvement. Earnings in the March 31 quarter will reflect weaker orders in recent months, continued tough price competition and restructuring costs at two large producers. Builders hope for a stronger second half, but that's contingent on improved orders.

Machine tools are complex, parts-making machines such as metal-cutting lathes and metal-forming presses. They shape metal parts as diverse as refrigerator doors and industrial bearings.

Since mid-1989, uncertainty about the economic outlook has caused many durable-goods makers to hold back on buying machine tools. Auto and truck companies, auto-parts producers and defense contractors, all major machine tool users, have slashed orders, as have some small machine shops. There are exceptions, such as producers of commercial aircraft, who are ordering briskly.

However, in the eight months ended Feb. 28, orders placed with U.S. machine-tool plants dropped 25% from the year-earlier period, according to NMTBA-Association for Manufacturing Technology, a McLean, Va., trade group.

Shipments held up well through the end of last year, but the backlog of unshipped orders is dwindling, forcing production cutbacks.

Based on current production schedules, Cincinnati Milacron Inc., the nation's largest machine-tool producer, expects much lower profits for the first quarter. The second quarter will be better than the first, the company says, but probably will be below a year earlier. Besides the lower orders, Milacron's first quarter will reflect some continued costs of a restructuring of production.

Milacron had profit from operations of 23 cents a share in the 1989 first quarter and 16 cents a share in the second. Tax credits brought net income for the first quarter to $10 million, or 41 cents a share, and for the second quarter to $5.2 million, or 21 cents a share.

Ronald Petrie, machinery analyst with McDonald & Co., Cleveland, expects relatively weak machine-tool profits for the first and second quarters. However, he's forecasting an upturn in manufacturing by midyear that will spark machine-tool sales and result in higher profits for the second half.

Mr. Petrie figures Milacron will earn $1 a share for the year, in spite of a slow start. A Milacron spokesman indicates that may be a bit optimistic, but the company also is expecting an improved second half. For 1989, profit from continuing operations was $18.4 million, or 75 cents a share, and net income, after tax credits and losses on a discontinued business, was $17.4 million, or 70 cents a share.

Mr. Petrie expects Cross & Trecker Corp., Bloomfield Hills, Mich., to break even for the year ending Sept. 30, compared with a fiscal 1989 loss from operations of $15.3 million and a net loss of $46.5 million, or $3.76 a share, including restructuring charges. Company officials have forecast a return to profitability during the year, but declined to say whether profits late in the year will balance off losses in early months.

However, intense import competition also is curbing profits for U.S. machine-tool builders. Machine-tool imports last year rose 17% to a record $2.4 billion, NMTBA said. Imports supplied about half the new machine tools purchased by U.S. factories last year. And transplant factories owned by Japanese concerns are a growing part of U.S. production.

Exports are a bright spot for U.S. machine-tool builders. U.S. machine-tool exports in 1989 totaled $949.8 million, up 24% from a year earlier, NMTBA said. However, even those markets are threatened. The declining value of the Japanese yen against the dollar makes Japanese machine tools even tougher competitors in Canada, Mexico, Europe and the Pacific Rim, all areas where U.S. machine-tool exports have been growing.

U.S. machine-tool builders say Eastern Europe and the Soviet Union are promising long-term markets, as those countries attempt to modernize inefficient factories. However, 1990 sales in that region aren't likely to be large enough to affect the bottom line.

Nonetheless, some of the specialized machine-tool companies, especially those that do a large business in Western Europe, are likely to report higher profits for the first quarter and full year. European companies have been investing heavily in new equipment in recent months.

Hurco Cos., Indianapolis, reported net for the first quarter ended Jan. 31 of $2.2 million, or 38 cents a share, on revenue of $22.7 million, up from year-earlier net of $1.3 million, or 28 cents a share, on revenue of $18.4 million.

Hurco specializes in small milling machines and machining centers equipped with electronic controls. About half of this year's sales will be overseas, says Michael K. Campbell, vice president, finance and chief financial officer.

Mr. Campbell says Hurco's earnings for the fiscal second quarter will exceed the year-earlier's 40 cents a share, and he has no quarrel with an estimate by McDonald & Co.'s Mr. Petrie that net for the year ending Oct. 31 will rise to $2.10 a share from $1.70 a share in fiscal 1989.

Some analysts also forecast higher profits for Brown & Sharpe Manufacturing Co., North Kingstown, R.I., which makes measuring machines used with automated-manufacturing equipment.

Giddings & Lewis Inc., Fond du Lac, Wis., specialist in computer-directed manufacturing cells and systems, is likely to report higher profits for the first quarter and year, says F. John Mirek, machinery analyst for Blunt Ellis & Loewi, Milwaukee. "They started the year with a strong backlog of orders that are priced higher than the backlog of a year ago," says Mr. Mirek. He's estimating first quarter net at 40 cents a share, up from 31 cents a year earlier, and profit for the year of $1.80 a share, compared with 1989 net of $17.1 million, or $1.65 a share. Giddings & Lewis declines to comment on the earnings estimates.
