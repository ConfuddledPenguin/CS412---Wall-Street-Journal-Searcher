Molson Cos. Sets Spending Plans, Sale of Operations

  • Author: Suzanne McGee
  • Date: 04/02/90

TORONTO -- Molson Cos. said it plans to spend 90 million Canadian dollars (US$76.8 million) over three years on its chemicals unit and intends to sell some retailing operations as part of a strategy to focus on industries with high-growth potential. The company, Canada's largest brewing concern, said it expects a one-time, after-tax charge of C$45 million in the year ended Saturday as a result.

Molson said C$30 million of the charge is attributable to moves to streamline the product line and upgrade the manufacturing processes of its Diversey Corp. unit, a specialty chemical maker.

Molson said the remaining C$15 million is linked to the sale of part of its retailing operations, which had a loss of about C$3 million in the fiscal year.

Company officials declined to comment on the prices they expect to receive for the retailing operations. The stores to be sold include chains of kitchenware, lighting, hardware and kitchen appliance stores. Molson said it plans to retain its lumber and do-it-yourself chain, Beaver Lumber, as well as a chain of home decorating stores, B.B. Bargoon's. A spokesman for the company said those two chains account for about 90% of Molson's revenue from its retailing division.

The spokesman said the company is "very close" to concluding the sales of some of the retailing chains.

The company didn't estimate fiscal 1990 income. In fiscal 1989, Molson had net income of C$87.1 million, or C$2.65 a share, on revenue of C$2.6 billion. And in the latest nine months, the company had net of C$157.6 million, or C$4.79 a share, including a one-time gain of C$62 million, on revenue of C$2 billion.

Molson said the C$90 million in capital spending planned for Diversey will go toward converting manufacturing facilities to state-of-the art processes. The company said that about C$65 million will be internally funded, by reducing working capital and redirecting spending priorities. Molson didn't give further details.

Diversey, which makes cleaning and sanitation products, has 51 operating facilities in 37 countries. It accounted for about 28% of Molson's revenue in fiscal 1989, and about 31% of operating profit.

Some analysts weren't surprised by Molson's move to divest itself of most of the retailing chains, which are vulnerable to an expected downturn in the business cycle. The analysts said they have been expecting such a move since Molson's president and chief executive officer, Marshall Cohen, who has a reputation as a deal maker, joined the company in November 1988.

In addition to Diversey and the retailing operations, Molson has holdings in a malting company and an investment concern. Molson also owns the Montreal Canadiens hockey team and other sports and entertainment assets.
