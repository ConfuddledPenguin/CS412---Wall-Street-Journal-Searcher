BTR, Pursuing Its Takover Bid, Sues Norton for Canceling Annual Meeting

  • Author: Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 04/02/90

LONDON -- BTR PLC said it filed suit in federal court in Boston to have Norton Co.'s canceled annual meeting reinstated for April 26. Norton, target of an unsolicited $1.64 billion bid by BTR, a London-based international conglomerate, on Thursday canceled its annual meeting and scheduled a special shareholders' meeting for June 26. Norton, a Worcester, Mass.-based chemical and abrasives maker, said it needed more time to weigh alternatives to BTR's bid, which it also rejected as inadequate.

"If we get the date reinstated, we'll start our proxy fight quite soon," said John C. Cahill, BTR chief executive officer, in a telephone interview. BTR had given Norton until Friday to accept its offer or face a possible proxy fight. But if the June 26 special meeting date were allowed to stand, Mr. Cahill indicated, any proxy fight wouldn't begin for some weeks.

In arguing that the annual meeting should proceed April 26 as scheduled, BTR noted that Norton's corporate bylaws state that the annual meeting be held on the fourth Thursday in April and that "no change in the date of the annual meeting of stockholders may be made within 60 days before the date fixed in these bylaws."

BTR contended that Norton's board has tried to get around the bylaws "by `cancelling' the annual meeting and resurrecting it in the form of a `special meeting,' " but that such a change is "impermissible."

In response to BTR's request for an expedited hearing on the annual-meeting issue, the court scheduled a hearing for April 6.

BTR's suit also asks the court to declare null Norton's shareholder-rights plan and to declare that certain anti-takeover laws of the state of Massachusetts are inapplicable in the Norton takeover fight.

Norton said it would "vigorously oppose" the BTR suit, and filed its own suit in federal court in Boston seeking to enjoin BTR's tender offer and to stop BTR from soliciting proxies. Norton charged that BTR has violated federal securities laws by, among other things, failing to disclose "its practice and pattern of closing or moving plants and laying off workers after various acquisitions it has made."

The BTR chief executive said Norton's rejection of the bid "was a very weak response." He added: "Norton made no reference to shareholder value, and there is no expectation that Norton can do anything short-term or medium-term" to improve the company's operating prospects.

Mr. Cahill urged Norton's board to provide BTR and other Norton holders with whatever information it used to conclude that BTR's bid was inadequate. BTR owns a fractional stake in Norton. Mr. Cahill noted that before BTR made its $75-a-share bid last month, Norton's share price never had exceeded $64 in the company's 105-year history.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange Friday, Norton closed at $73.50, down 37.5 cents.

Mr. Cahill said he expected to meet Massachusetts Sens. Edward M. Kennedy and John Kerry in Washington, D.C., this week to try to defuse political opposition to BTR's bid. "We want to sit down in a spirit of good will and explain the benefits of the bid to Norton and to the city of Worcester," Mr. Cahill said.

He also said BTR would run Norton "as a free-standing, independent company" using Norton's current management.

He held out the possibility that BTR might make capital investments in Norton. Norton's capital investments "went flat in the last four years, and I don't know why," he said. "Perhaps it was because of poor cash flow."
