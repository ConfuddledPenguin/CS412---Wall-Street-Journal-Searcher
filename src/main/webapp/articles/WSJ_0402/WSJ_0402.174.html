Farley's West Point Unit Is in Default On Some Bank Debt

  • Author: Robert Johnson
  • Date: 04/02/90

William Farley's troubles with the acquisition of West Point-Pepperell Inc. worsened when the unit he set up to buy the company fell into default on about $733 million in bank debt after negotiations to restructure the loan bogged down. According to a Wall Street person close to the negotiations, Mr. Farley plans as soon as today to offer the holders of roughly $905 million in West Point-Pepperell subordinated debt a significant equity position in the company in exchange for delaying interest payments for as long as three years.

If Mr. Farley is successful in the exchange offer to bond holders, his bankers, led by Wells Fargo & Co. and Bankers Trust Co., are more likely to commit a restructuring of his bank loan rather than forcing him into federal bankruptcy-law proceedings.

The bankers are known to be worried that the holders of subordinated notes for which Mr. Farley might miss interest payments in the next few months may force him into federal bankruptcy-law proceedings.

The bankers, concerned about the possible costs of paying bankruptcy-law attorneys to oversee lengthy legal proceedings, would take it as a good sign to see approval of the forthcoming exchange offer on the subordinated notes.

Farley officials couldn't be reached for comment over the weekend, but they are known to be continuing negotiations with bankers.

Last month Mr. Farley successfully negotiated a $1.2 billion bridge-loan extension through the end of 1994, easing some of the pressure on the West Point acquisition. But the bank loan that was due Saturday is an additional debt that was used to help make the tender offer in the $1.56 billion West Point acquisition in March 1989.

Of the several entities under the umbrella of Mr. Farley's investment and industrial empire, the bank-loan default affects only West Point-Pepperell Acquisition, the holding company that was set up last year to acquire the West Point, Ga.-based textile concern.

Mr. Farley has completed his tender offer on 95% of West Point-Pepperell. But he is about $83 million short of the cash needed to complete the transaction. Unless he completes that deal, he can't use West Point's cash to pay interest on the debt it produced. Thus Mr. Farley's only chance to finish the transaction and keep control of the company is to restructure certain of his bank and subordinated note debt.

Mr. Farley, based in Chicago, built a collection of manufacturing companies, largely on junk bond financing. But his investment banker, Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., has collapsed. Farley Inc., his closely held manufacturing concern, had a pretax loss of about $40 million in 1989, analysts estimated.
