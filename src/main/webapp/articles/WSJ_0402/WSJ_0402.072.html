Lexis Seeks to Have the Last Word, Even If It Will Take an Infinity

  • Author: Bradley A. Stertz
  • Date: 04/02/90

Anyone who thinks that a court decision is the final word in American justice should check with one of the nation's largest legal-information networks. Last year, Mead Data Central's legal-research service, named Lexis, sued Toyota Motor Corp. for coming a little too close with the Lexus name that the Japanese auto maker chose for its new brand of luxury cars. The case went all the way to a federal court of appeals before Toyota won the right to use the Lexus name.

Problem is, Toyota didn't win the war. The Mead Corp. unit is coming back with a contest for its 400,000 regular customers. The contest's name: "Win an Infiniti from Lexis." Infiniti, of course, is the new luxury car line just introduced by Toyota's archrival, Nissan Motor Co.

The sweepstakes contest will run from this month until June. The lucky person who logs on a Lexis computer-research terminal at just the right moment will win a shiny new $38,000 Infiniti Q45 sedan. The winner also gets a Lexis workstation for the office and can designate a professional school, most likely a college of law, to receive another Lexis system.

It's the first time that Mead Data Central, which is based in Dayton, Ohio, has run such a contest. To make sure enough people know about it, the company is running advertisements coast to coast in major legal publications such as the National Law Journal, the ABA Journal, Texas Lawyer and California Lawyer. The ads feature a picture of the Infiniti. That's something that Infiniti itself didn't do in its own ads until recently.

Is Lexis trying to get revenge on Lexus? No way, contends spokesman Michael A. Winter. "This isn't an overt attempt to take a shot at the Lexus cars."

So why give away an Infiniti car instead of a BMW or Cadillac -- or even a Lexus? "We thought the Infiniti was one of the most talked about cars in the market right now, and we wanted to offer a car that is uppermost in people's minds," Mr. Winter says. "Besides, we think the cars have a lot of intrigue." He acknowledges Lexis doesn't exactly have warm feelings toward Lexus. "We had our day in court and we have to abide by that decision. That doesn't mean we're happy about it."

Toyota isn't taking the bait. Last year it vigorously defended the Lexus name in court, but now mum's the word. Says spokesman Arthur Garner: "We really don't have anything to say on this."
