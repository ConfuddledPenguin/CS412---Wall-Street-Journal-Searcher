Chrysler Urges Its 100 Top Executives To Bet More of Their Pay on Firm's Fate

  • Author: Bradley A. Stertz
  • Date: 04/02/90

HIGHLAND PARK, Mich. -- Chrysler Corp. is asking its top 100 executives to own stock equal to a certain percentage of their salaries in an unusual program to focus their attention on the auto maker's long-term future. The program, promoted by Chairman Lee A. Iacocca, is the latest such measure aimed at giving Chrysler officials a direct financial interest in the company's fate above and beyond the usual executive stock options.

Already, most of the auto maker's top 2,000 officials have gambled as much as 10% of their base pay on the company's ability to cut $1 billion from its costs by Dec. 31. If the cost-cutting drive succeeds, the officials will profit handsomely. But they lose their money if the goal isn't met.

"We are trying a number of unique methods to get top management to share the risks or rewards of the next few years," said company spokesman Steven Harris of the new stock-ownership program.

In composite trading Friday on the New York Stock Exchange, Chrysler common closed at $16.625, off 25 cents, on volume of 642,200 shares. In the past 52 weeks, the stock traded at a low of $15.875 and a high of $27.125.

Separately, Ford Motor Co. reinstated merit-pay raises for its 55,000 salaried employees effective yesterday. Ford had suspended merit-pay increases in January because of sagging financial results, but said then that it would consider reinstating such raises at the end of the first quarter.

In a letter to employees in the U.S. and Canada, Ford executives said the company believes the sales downturn has bottomed out and that the worst is behind Ford. The No.2-ranked auto maker cautioned employees that many uncertainties remain this year.

Still, Ford's guarded optimism contrasts with the austerity mode at Chrysler, which alone among the Big Three auto makers didn't pay executive bonuses this year.

The Chrysler stock-ownership goal for executives, which began recently, isn't mandatory. But it does provide each executive with guidelines on the amount of Chrysler stock that he or she should own to have a "fair share" of the company.

The guidelines are based on a formula tied to the executive's pay and seniority with the No. 3-ranked U.S. auto maker. Shares the executives already control through stock options, recent purchases and so-called 401K savings plans count in the program. Chrysler declined to provide further details.

It's unclear how many U.S. companies have similar stock-ownership targets, but such goals don't exist at Ford or at No. 1-ranked General Motors Corp. Three years ago, GM did change its management bonus payments to stock from cash. But because the stock can be sold, that doesn't amount to a specific stock-ownership goal for executives.
