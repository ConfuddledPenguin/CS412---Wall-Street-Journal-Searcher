Marketing & Media: Chrysler Provides Yet Another Twist In Buyer Incentives --- Purchasers of Minivans Get Offer of Possible Rebate, But There's a Big Catch

  • Author: Melinda Grenier Guiles
  • Date: 04/04/90

HIGHLAND PARK, Mich. -- Chrysler Corp., the auto maker that pioneered the rebate, has invented the non-incentive incentive. The No. 3 auto maker is guaranteeing consumers who buy a 1990 minivan between April 1 and Sept. 30 that they will get the amount of any rebate that may be offered on the vehicles during the six-month period. The only catch is, Chrysler isn't offering any rebates on minivans, at least not now.

Specifically, Chrysler said it isn't resuming the $1,000 cash-back incentive it adopted during January on Caravan, Grand Caravan, Voyager, Grand Voyager and Town and Country. And company spokesmen say there aren't any plans to reinstate the program, which guaranteed consumers that they would get the difference if Chrysler announced a higher rebate on the vans before the Sept. 30 end of the 1990 model year.

But Chrysler's minivan sales plummeted from almost 64,000 in January -- more than double the normal sales rate -- to fewer than 20,000 in February. March is expected to be only slightly better; final totals are due today. Also, General Motors Corp. recently began offering $1,000 off on its new plastic-body Chevrolet Lumina APV, Pontiac Trans Sport and Oldsmobile Silhouette minivans. And Ford Motor Co. raised the rebate on its Aerostar minivans to $750 from $500.

So Chrysler dealers -- feeling pressure from consumers -- have pushed the auto maker to do something to boost sales of what has been Chrysler's most popular product. The program announced yesterday is the result. A Chrysler spokesman said the auto maker doesn't plan to advertise it, but will send dealers a "wall-poster-size dealer-gram" with a message from Chairman Lee A. Iacocca urging customers to "buy now and be assured that Chrysler wants you to have the best deal possible."

Wouldn't resuming the rebates be simpler? Perhaps, but Chrysler doesn't want to lose any income from one of its most profitable vehicles. In addition, the auto maker has said it fears it could run out of minivans because the two plants that build the models will be closed for four weeks this summer. Chrysler is substantially changing the vehicles for the 1991 model year.
