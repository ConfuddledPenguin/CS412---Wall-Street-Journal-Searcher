Technology: Agency to Propose A Security Standard For Computers by Fall

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/16/90

Bowing to political pressure, a Commerce Department agency said that by September it will propose a standard for computer security that experts say will be a boon for the high-tech industry. John Lyons, director of the National Institute of Standards and Technology, the agency in charge of producing a standard, said Friday that work on a proposed standard should be completed "in a matter of a few weeks" and will be published "by the end of summer."

The standard should help the spread of electronic-data communications by encouraging computer-network operators to install a single method of ensuring that documents sent via computer networks are authentic and haven't been altered. But because such an approach to security, called public-key cryptography, also provides a defense against computer snoops, it has prompted opposition from the National Security Agency, the nation's chief electronic spy.

Members of Congress and officials in the computer industry have complained about opposition from the NSA.

The NSA, one of the government's most secretive agencies, declines to say publicly why it fears such improvements in electronic communications. Mr. Lyons says the NSA's objections turn on national security concerns, but he wouldn't be specific.

RSA Data Security Inc., a tiny concern in Redwood City, Calif., controls the most widely used approach to public-key cryptography. Experts say the federal government probably should adopt RSA Data's approach, since it is popular in Europe and catching on in the U.S.

But there are other alternatives. "Technically, its an open question," said Willis Ware, a computer scientist at Rand Corp. who chairs a Commerce Department advisory committee on computer security.

Mr. Ware said that while RSA Data "has got a corner on the marketplace," there may be weaknesses in the company's approach to security that are only known by the NSA.
