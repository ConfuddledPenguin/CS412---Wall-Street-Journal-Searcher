Samsung's Plans to Open Auto Plant Elicit Vigorous Opposition in Korea

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/16/90

SEOUL, South Korea -- Samsung Group's recent announcement that it plans to open an auto assembly plant in Korea next year has sparked strong opposition from the nation's five existing auto makers and posed a difficult choice for the government. Samsung Heavy Industries, a unit of Samsung Group, South Korea's major business conglomerate, has announced plans to build 1,200 large-sized commercial vehicles in Korea next year and to boost output to 3,800 units annually by 1995. Parts would be supplied by Nissan Diesel of Japan.

The Korea Automobile Manufacturers Association, however, has asked the government to reject Samsung's application for permission to import technology to make the vehicles.

The government's power to stop new entries into the auto business was abolished recently as part of a new industrial liberalization policy. The government can, however, restrict imports of technology if they are deemed to be harmful to an industry or the economy in general.

KAMA, which represents Hyundai Motor Co., Daewoo Motor Co., Kia Motors Co., Ssangyong Motor Co. and Asia Motors Co., said Samsung's entry into the commercial vehicle business would create a serious oversupply problem that would damage the domestic auto industry.

Analysts note, however, that although Korea is well stocked with auto makers, the four Korean concerns that make large commercial vehicles are unable to supply more than about 70% of the 20,000 trucks and 10,000 buses that Koreans buy each year.

In Japan, by comparison, four companies meet a domestic demand of 100,000 vehicles, analysts say.

Because of the high sensitivity of the issue, the government is now faced with a very difficult choice, says Huh Kyung, assistant director at the ministry's transportation machinery division.

By opposing Samsung's application, Mr. Huh says, KAMA is asking the government to reverse its free competition policy.

"Basically, it is against the ongoing liberalization policy of the government to reject private projects," Mr. Huh says. He adds that the government might have to delay the decision.

While the ministry considers Samsung's application, the five auto makers are mounting major lobbying and public opinion campaigns opposing it.

In a proposal to the government, KAMA predicted that Korea's commercial vehicle market, which currently enjoys growing demand due to an extraordinary construction boom, will face a sharp decline in demand beginning next year and will be oversupplied by 1992.

KAMA also asserted that some of the Nissan technology Samsung plans to use has already been imported by Ssangyong Motor Co. Duplicating the technology would be detrimental to the local economy, the proposal warned.

Analysts note, however, that behind the auto makers' strong opposition is their concern about a possible entry by Samsung into passenger car business.

Despite Samsung's denials, analysts here believe that Samsung's commercial vehicle project is a first step toward a venture in the passenger car business, which has been the group's major goal for many years.

In the mid-1980s, Samsung vigorously sought a business partnership with Chrysler Corp. for the manufacture of passenger cars. The deal was aborted at the last moment by a government law prohibiting repeated or excessive investment in certain areas. Recently, the group unsuccessfully contacted Honda Motor Co. and other foreign automakers for technical and other assistance.
