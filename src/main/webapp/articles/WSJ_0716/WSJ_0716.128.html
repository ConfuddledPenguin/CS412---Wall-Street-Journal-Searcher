Credit Markets: Refcorp's Bonds Gain New Luster

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 07/16/90

NEW YORK -- The government's thrift-bailout bonds, scorned as the dogs of the government bond market just a few weeks ago, are suddenly being viewed as prime investments. Bailout bonds are the debt securities sold by the Resolution Funding Corp., a federal agency created by Congress to help finance the government's rescue of savings institutions.

So far Refcorp has issued $18 billion of the bonds, including $5 billion of 30-year bonds sold Tuesday. The latest auction went smoothly, in contrast to two previous disastrous auctions of 40-year Refcorp bonds. In the earlier mishaps, the government sold most of the bonds to Wall Street dealers, which got stung when they couldn't drum up enough demand from investors.

But now investors are warming to the Refcorp bonds. Demand for the securities has picked up considerably since last week's auction.

"The yield pickup is very attractive," said Allen David Ellison, an analyst and portfolio manager at Fidelity Management & Research in Boston. He said that with Refcorp offering essentially the same ironclad government guarantee as a plain vanilla Treasury bond, some investors are wondering: Why invest in a Treasury at 8.5% when Refcorp yields are 8.9%?

Even some money managers who were down on the bonds a few months ago like Refcorp a little better now.

Take Ian McKinnon, head of fixed-income investments at Vanguard Group, the mutual fund company. After Refcorp's 40-year bond auction in April, he riled Treasury officials by telling USA Today that the Refcorp bond sales were producing "dogs" for the market and saying: "Maybe the government is trying to start a kennel."

Well, Mr. McKinnon isn't so harsh these days. Although he didn't buy Refcorp bonds at last week's auction, "we will take a good look at them in the secondary market and at the next auction," he said. "I guess this proves that every dog has its day."

The new fondness for Refcorp stems from several factors: the higher relative yields on the securities, analysts' reports saying Refcorp bonds are nearly indistinguishable in credit quality from Treasury securities, and a general perception that interest rates eventually will fall, which would boost the value of all fixed-income investments.

And as investor acceptance grows, analysts expect the spread, or gap, between Treasury securities and Refcorps will narrow, which in effect will make Refcorp bonds more valuable. At last week's auction, the 30-year Refcorps were issued with an average yield of 8.93%, or 0.39 percentage point higher than yields on Treasury securities.

Although marginal yield differences don't always excite small investors, in the intensely competitive institutional world of mutual funds, insurance companies and pension funds, even a quarter of a percentage point is considered a tremendous difference.

But there's more allure to Refcorp than just a higher yield. Last week, traders said, Metropolitan Life Insurance Co. purchased a large amount of Refcorp bonds -- possibly as much as $1 billion -- while selling corporate and mortgage-backed bonds. On the surface, this might seem like an odd trade, considering mortgages and corporate bonds have higher yields than government bonds.

Although MetLife doesn't like to talk about its investment decisions, other money managers say that big institutional investors -- especially insurance companies -- are playing what they call "a relative performance game" with the Refcorp bonds. Currently, prices of high-grade utility and telephone company bonds and certain mortgage-backed bonds have become what the market considers "expensive" relative to Treasury bonds, meaning only that yields on these securities have narrowed relative to yields on Treasury bonds. Meanwhile, Refcorp was sold at a relatively wide spread to Treasurys.

But, says Barbara Kenworthy, a portfolio manager at Dreyfus Corp., "if you buy the economic downturn theory, Refcorp spreads will narrow because they are government-backed bonds, while corporate spreads will widen." Ms. Kenworthy essentially is suggesting that corporate bonds will become cheaper and Refcorp bonds will rise in value.

If that happens, "what you give up in yield you more than make up for in relative performance," she said.

"We view this as a win-win situation," said Mike Martino, executive vice president and portfolio manager at Back Bay Advisors, a subsidiary of the New England Life Insurance Co. "You can win with rates coming down, and you can win with yields on Refcorp coming closer to yields on Treasurys."

Mr. Martino said that many investors shied away from Refcorp bonds in the beginning because they didn't fully understand what they were. "No one likes to invest in the unknown," he said.

Part of the unknown had to do with the future size of Refcorp offerings. Congress limited Refcorp's borrowing ability to $30 billion. Yet as estimates of the S&L bailout's cost grew from $100 billion to $500 billion, some investors feared the government would raise the limit and swamp the market with Refcorp bonds, which would overwhelm demand and put pressure on prices. Now, however, it appears that more and more Treasury borrowings will be used to finance the thrift rescue, which will add to the size of the budget deficit but not the size of future Refcorp offerings.

Once the full $30 billion have been issued, the size of Refcorp bonds on the market will be large enough to make it a fairly liquid market, but small enough for the bonds to maintain a certain "scarcity" value.

Refcorp bonds were also boosted by analysts' reports. J.P. Morgan & Co., which is trying to convince reluctant investors that Refcorp bonds are suitable alternatives to Treasurys, said in a recent report that Refcorp bonds are "essentially indistinguishable from U.S. Treasurys."

Corporations and municipalities also are being targeted by investment firms as potential Refcorp buyers and have been told that Refcorp bonds could be used as part of a complex plan to "defease" existing debt. Recently, the accounting firm of Deloitte & Touche said the credit quality of Refcorp bonds is high enough to use for defeasing purposes.

Defeasance is a technique whereby a bond issuer may discharge old, low-rate debt by purchasing a smaller amount of higher-yielding U.S. Treasury debt. The Treasurys are put in a trust to service the old debt being retired.

Though the Refcorp bonds aren't direct obligations of the Treasury, the Treasury guarantees interest payments. And the principal is secured by zero-coupon Treasurys, purchased by Refcorp from the Treasury.

Friday's Market Activity

Bond prices rose as the Federal Reserve sent what traders interpreted as a clear signal of a credit-easing move.

Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan prepared investors for the action by saying Thursday that the Fed was poised to ease credit because of mounting evidence of a credit crunch. He didn't say when the move would come, but the answer came Friday, shortly before noon EDT, when the Fed took action in the money markets to inject funds into the banking system.

"It's clear now that the Fed has eased," said Donald J. Fine, chief market analyst at Chase Manhattan Bank. Henry Engler, economist at Chemical Bank, agreed: "I think it's about as clear as it gets."

The Fed's maneuver led to a further widening of the gap between short-term and long-term interest rates, known in bond market jargon as a widening of the yield curve. Several investment managers fear the Fed pulled its trigger too soon, and thus that the bond market's rally may be nearing an end.

Easing credit now could lead to higher inflation later this year and possibly also drive down the American dollar in the foreign-exchange markets, investment strategists say. Some also argue that the Fed should have waited until Congress and the White House reach a definitive agreement on slashing the government's huge budget deficits. Now, they say, Washington lawmakers face less pressure to take bold action on the deficit.

The Fed's move "probably won't have much of a stimulative effect on the economy because it's so small, but it also does little to improve the inflation outlook," says Bob DiClemente, senior economist at Salomon Brothers Inc. "If you're looking to the Fed as a bulwark against inflation, then this doesn't support that case."

Thus, Mr. DiClemente adds, "if you've missed the bond rally, now is not the time to get in, because we're not likely to get a big rally from here."

Despite the Fed's move to lower rates, banks once again showed they are in no rush to cut their own rates. The prime, or base, rates at major banks remained unchanged at 10%. Banks use the prime as a peg to set rates on loans to many small and medium-sized companies, as well as on loans to many individual investors.

Banks long have demonstrated an uneven way of reacting to Fed policy changes. When the Fed tightens credit, banks typically react quickly by raising their prime rate. But when the Fed cuts rates, banks discover reasons to delay lowering their rates.

In the long-term bond market, Treasury bond prices rose Friday by about half a point, or $5 for each $1,000 face amount. For example, the government's 30-year bonds were quoted late in the day at 103 7/32, up from 102 21/32 Thursday evening. The yield fell slightly to 8.45% from 8.49%.

Yields fell more on shorter-term Treasury issues. For example, the yield on the government's two-year notes fell to 8.12% from 8.21%. Thus, the gap between the two-year and 30-year issues widened to 0.33 percentage point from 0.28 percentage point Thursday and only 0.14 percentage point in late June.

Rates also fell on short-term Treasury bills. The latest three-month Treasury bill was quoted late Friday at a discount rate of 7.58%, for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.83%. That compared with a rate of 7.68% to yield 7.92% Thursday. The rate on six-month bills wound up at 7.49% to yield 7.89%, down from 7.57% to yield 7.96% Thursday.

The Fed's move Friday came as the interest rate on federal funds, or reserves that banks lend each other overnight, was trading at 8.25%. Now, traders say, the funds rate is likely to hover close to 8%, down from 8.25% previously.

Mr. Fine of Chase Manhattan described the Fed action as "a very modest" move and predicted it won't be the last this year. "I think further ease will come but not for awhile, not until they {Fed officials} see definitive evidence of a sustainable moderation in inflation," he said.

In other markets:

-- Corporate bond prices rose. Investment-grade bonds rose about 1/4 to 3/8 point. In the high-yield market, junk bond prices rose, too.

-- Junk bonds issued by department and chain stores posted strong gains, following reports showing stronger retail sales.

-- Municipal bond prices also rose. The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bonds of 2018 climbed about 1/2 point to 99 1/2 to yield 7.23%.
