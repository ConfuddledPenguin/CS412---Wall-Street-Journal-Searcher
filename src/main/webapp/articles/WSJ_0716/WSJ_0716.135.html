What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/16/90

THE FED EASED interest rates modestly, but signaled that further reductions shouldn't be expected soon. The central bank pushed down the federal funds rate, which banks charge each other for loans. That could cause other short-term interest rates to decline as well.

Stock prices surged, with the Dow Industrials closing at a record 2980.20, up 10.40 points, after trading above the 3000 level briefly. The dollar rose, but analysts said the credit easing could hurt the weak U.S. currency.

---

RJR Nabisco said it is beginning a $6.9 billion refinancing to retire many of its junk bonds by open-market purchases and an exchange offer.

---

Eastern Airlines' lawyers are expected to confer with federal officials today on how the carrier will respond to criminal charges that it falsified maintenance and safety records.

---

Cummins Engine agreed to sell a 27% stake to Ford Motor, Tenneco and Kubota Ltd. for $250 million. The complex deal could mean hundreds of millions of dollars in added revenue for the diesel-engine maker.

---

The SEC charged an "unknown" group of investors with insider trading in Contel securities in connection with the company's $6.2 billion plan to merge with GTE Corp.

---

Producer prices rose 0.2% in June and retail sales increased 0.5%, suggesting that inflation is somewhat under control and that consumer spending may be in a modest upturn.

---

U.S. auto makers plan to boost third-quarter domestic output 11.6%, despite recent cutbacks by Ford and weak sales. However, sales of U.S.-built cars rose a surprisingly strong 2.5% in the first 10 days of July.

---

Persian Gulf oil exporters will hold down current production in return for larger shares of OPEC output later.

---

Federal regulators asked a court to block R.R. Donnelley's planned $487.5 million purchase of a printing venture owned by Meredith Corp. and members of a West German family.

---

A New York state panel recommended that Nynex's New York Telephone unit cut current rates by $81 million, and rejected the firm's proposed $919.7 million increase.

---

Thornburgh said he won't appoint an independent counsel to investigate Neil Bush, the president's son, in the $1 billion failure of a Denver thrift unless he is accused of a crime.

---

General Development said a judge rejected the company's plea-bargain agreement with the government involving an alleged conspiracy to defraud thousands of home buyers.

---

The Commodity Exchange's new president will be David Halperin, executive vice president of the New York Mercantile Exchange, according to a person close to the exchanges.
