Politics & Policy: Secret Service Would Get Green Light For S&L Probes in Bill Passed by House

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 07/16/90

WASHINGTON -- A House-passed appropriations bill would authorize the Secret Service to initiate investigations of savings and loan fraud and would direct the Internal Revenue Service to step up its tax enforcement against foreign-owned U.S. companies. The $20.7 billion measure won easy approval on a 300-72 roll call, but the expanded power for the Secret Service is provoking a behind-the-scenes turf war with the Federal Bureau of Investigation over control of savings and loan cases.

While Secret Service officials have quietly welcomed the initiative, the White House has lined up behind the Justice Department and the Federal Bureau of Investigation, whose representatives scrambled unsuccessfully to put any such Secret Service operations under the authority of Attorney General Richard Thornburgh.

"There are more FBI guys up here than investigating the S&Ls," joked Rep. Silvio Conte of Massachusetts, the ranking Republican on the House Appropriations Committee. And while all sides promised to coordinate efforts, Mr. Conte and House Democratic Caucus Chairman Steny Hoyer of Maryland used their positions to establish a legislative record allowing the Secret Service to initiate investigations where the FBI isn't directly involved.

The underlying measure, which goes next to the Senate, funds the Treasury Department, Postal Service and much of the White House's own operations for the new fiscal year beginning Oct. 1. IRS funding would grow almost $633.7 million, to $6.13 billion, and the bill requires the agency to use at least $10 million of the new money to crack down on foreign-owned businesses.

The administration has protested this provision, but politically the issue has drawn support from a striking coalition of Republicans, opposed to raising new taxes, and industrial-state Democrats looking for a vehicle to strike back at competing Japanese companies.

By a 201-178 margin, the House rejected a proposal to cut 2% from all accounts other than the IRS. But the committee leadership agreed separately to freeze allowances for former presidents at the current fiscal 1990 level of $1.8 million. Reflecting concern over former President Reagan's trip to Japan last year, where he reportedly received $2 million in speaking fees, the bill directs the Treasury to report in six months on how to reduce expenditures in such cases. The measure gives the Secret Service authority to accept private donations to offset security costs in cases where a former president travels for the purpose of earning a speaking fee.

Among White House accounts, the Office of Management and Budget would grow to $49.3 million. After late-night negotiations between the administration and the House Government Operations Committee, Director Richard Darman was able to sidestep a move to strike an estimated $4.5 million for the Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs.

Government Operations Committee Chairman John Conyers, who met Friday with Mr. Darman and White House counsel C. Boyden Gray, said he was satisfied that an "agreement in principle" had been reached on changes sought by Congress in a long-running fight over the role of the office in determining regulatory decisions. The Michigan Democrat is among critics who have demanded greater access to internal documents and justification on any cost-benefit analysis used in future cases. Mr. Conyers indicated concessions made by the White House could expedite new authorization legislation pending in his committee.

The House action came as Senate Appropriations Committee Chairman Robert Byrd (D., W.Va.) signaled he would seek to cut military spending to the level called for in the House budget resolution. But he said he would trim back as well on increases in House-passed appropriations bills for domestic and foreign aid accounts. Discretionary appropriations for non-defense programs would be $4.8 billion less than the House, but to preserve some flexibility under Gramm-Rudman rules, outlays would be pared back by only $3 billion.

Mr. Byrd's defense numbers are more favorable to the Pentagon in some respects than the Senate Budget Committee's initial recommendations. But by accepting the House ceiling, he is also demanding an estimated $1.5 billion more in direct-outlay cuts than the Senate Armed Services Committee bill reported last week.
