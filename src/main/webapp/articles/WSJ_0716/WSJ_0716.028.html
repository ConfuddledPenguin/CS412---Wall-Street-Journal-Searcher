Merc's Halperin Is Said to Become President of Comex

  • Author: Stanley W. Angrist
  • Date: 07/16/90

NEW YORK -- David A. Halperin, executive vice president of the New York Mercantile Exchange, has accepted an offer to become president of the Commodity Exchange, a person familiar with the two exchanges said. Mr. Halperin declined to comment on his new job, pending a meeting of the Comex board Wednesday. The board is expected to vote on the appointment at that time.

The Comex is the nation's largest gold, silver and copper futures exchange. But it is a distant fourth in size behind the Chicago Board of Trade, the Chicago Mercantile Exchange and the New York Merc.

Mr. Halperin's stay at the Merc was short-lived, having moved there last December 1989 from the position of executive vice president at the Comex. Mr. Halperin took the job at the Merc as part of the Merc board's reorganization plan for the exchange.

A senior official at the Merc said that the 47-year-old Mr. Halperin will be missed, calling him "a top-flight administrator."

One Merc administrator thinks Mr. Halperin's departure might be a roadblock in the current attempt to merge the Comex and the Merc, New York's largest commodity exchange. He noted that Mr. Halperin was a part of the core group at the Merc who were privy to its negotiating positions in the merger talks.

Exchange officials have indicated that within a month or so the discussions were expected to produce one or more merger plans that would be presented to the members of both exchanges for a vote.

The last president of the Comex, Arnold Staloff, a former senior official at the Philadelphia Stock Exchange, was brought in with considerable fanfare in August 1989. He resigned May 1. Some say Mr. Staloff's resignation was due to the highly charged political atmosphere of the exchange. Mr. Halperin, having worked at the Comex for eight years, is likely to be familiar with the environment.

Before Mr. Staloff took the Comex presidency it was widely believed that Mr. Halperin was a candidate for the job. Four months after Mr. Staloff took office, Mr. Halperin left the Comex to take the Merc position.

Before becoming executive vice president at the Comex, Mr. Halperin was senior vice president in the areas of finance, administration and development. He is a certified public accountant and, prior to joining Comex, worked as controller of Columbia University and as an audit supervisor for Coopers & Lybrand.
