Gulf Canada Ends Plan for Test Well In Arctic Ocean

  • Author: Rose Tamburri
  • Date: 07/16/90

OTTAWA -- Gulf Canada Resources Ltd. said it canceled plans to drill a 60 million Canadian dollar (US$51.9 million) exploratory oil well in the western Arctic Ocean because of a lack of industry support for the project. The decision means that no new wells will be drilled this year in Canada's Beaufort Sea region, which leads into the Arctic Ocean. Gulf Canada, an energy concern, is a unit of Toronto-based Olympia & York Developments Ltd., which is controlled by the Reichmann family. It had proposed to drill as many as four wells in the region over the next three years.

Frank Mitton, manager of Gulf Canada's northern operations, said the company will decide in the next few months whether to carry out its plans to drill the remaining wells. Mr. Mitton said Gulf's decision to cancel the well was unrelated to an earlier ruling by a Canadian environmental panel. The panel had recommended that the Canadian government reject Gulf's plans because the company was unprepared to deal with a major oil spill. "We cannot drill the well because we don't have sufficient industry support for it," Mr. Mitton said.

The well was to have been located about 18 miles east of Gulf Canada's big Amauligak oil field. Several oil and gas finds have been made in the Beaufort Sea but none of the discoveries has been brought into production.

Gulf is the second company that has canceled an Arctic exploration program this year. Amoco Canada Petroleum Co., a unit of Chicago-based Amoco Corp., recently canceled plans to drill two wells in the Beaufort Sea, because of insufficient interest from its partners. Amoco plans to carry out some seismic work this summer.

Ed Zederayko, an oil industry analyst, said low energy prices and reduced federal government exploration incentives are causing more companies to defer their offshore drilling plans.

But a spokesman for Chevron Canada Resources Ltd., which was to have held about 22% interest in Gulf's proposed well, said Chevron was committed to going ahead with the plan. Chevron Canada, a unit of San Francisco-based Chevron Corp., "has an ongoing interest in the North," he said.

The Canadian Environmental Impact Review Board had recommended earlier this month that government regulators reject Gulf Canada's Arctic drilling plan because of the potentially devastating effects of an oil spill on the area's environmentally sensitive coastline. The board, which is made up of representatives from the federal government, the governments of the Yukon and Northwest Territories and the Western Arctic Inuit natives, criticized Gulf Canada and the government for their "startling lack of preparedness" for dealing with a major oil well blowout.

Canada Oil and Gas Lands Administration, the government regulators, had yet to issue its decision on whether it would accept the environmental board's ruling.

Gulf Canada officials estimated that a hypothetical worst-case well blowout could release 40,000 barrels of oil a day for 66 days, or 10 times more oil than was released when Exxon Corp.'s Valdez tanker ran aground in Alaska last year. They said the cleanup of such a spill could be beyond Gulf Canada's financial capabilities.
