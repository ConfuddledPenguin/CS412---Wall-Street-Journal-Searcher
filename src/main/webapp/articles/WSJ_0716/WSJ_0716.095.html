Cummins to Sell A 27% Holding To Three Firms

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 07/16/90

Cummins Engine Co. agreed to sell a 27% aggregate stake at a sizable premium to a trio of friendly corporate investors for $250 million. The complex series of agreements associated with the buy-in holds the promise of "hundreds of millions of dollars" in additional revenue annually from Ford Motor Co. and "significant new business opportunities" with Japan's Kubota Ltd. and Cummins's joint-venture partner Tenneco Inc., said Henry B. Schacht, Cummins' chairman and chief executive officer.

Mr. Schacht said in an interview that the move "provides major new strength to our balance sheet" and will reduce interest costs. The proposed pact will also provide additional anti-takeover protection for the diesel-engine maker, which has blocked overtures from unwanted suitors twice in little more than a year.

Under the agreements, Ford, already a significant Cummins customer, will pay $62.50 a share for 1.6 million newly issued shares of Cummins common, representing an indicated 10.8% primary holding. With the $100 million investment, Dearborn, Mich.-based Ford also will receive the right to boost its stake to as much as 20%.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Cummins shares closed at $51.25, up 50 cents.

As part of the agreement, Ford signed a long-term agreement under which Cummins will supply midrange diesel engines for Ford medium trucks. The auto maker has exclusively manufactured its own midrange diesel engines, although it currently buys heavy-truck engines from Cummins. Mr. Schacht said, "We will transition" from the current status to supplying Ford with all its midrange engines "over a period of years."

The executive said Ford has annual sales of about 48,000 such medium-size trucks. The agreement "will be several hundred million dollars of new business for us" annually when the transition is complete. The pact will also enable Cummins to "take a major stake" in the U.S. midrange diesel market. The company's principal focus has traditionally been on heavy-duty truck engines.

Houston-based Tenneco, Cummins's partner in a joint-venture that makes engines for Tenneco's J I Case farm-equipment unit, will buy a similar stake, also for $100 million, but it won't receive an option to boost its stake. Cummins said it sees "further opportunities to build on the existing 50-50 joint venture," possibly in fields other than agricultural gear. But the companies are still discussing what form that possibly broadened relationship might take, Mr. Schacht said.

Japanese tractor manufacturer Kubota will buy 800,000 Cummins shares, also at $62.50 apiece, to acquire an about 5.4% primary stake for $50 million. A Cummins unit currently manufactures Kubota-designed generators in the U.S. under a licensing agreement. Cummins called the latest pact, formally disclosed early this morning, "only the beginning of a broad strategic alliance." As part of the pact, Cummins said the two companies are discussing manufacturing Kubota engines through a joint venture in Europe.

Ford and Tenneco will each be permitted to name a representative to the Columbus, Ind., company's board, which currently has 13 members. All three companies "have agreed to certain voting, standstill and other provisions" for at least six years, Cummins said.

Cummins's largest holder is Industrial Equity (Pacific) Ltd., with a 14.9% stake. Stock purchases by the unit of New Zealand-based Brierley Investments Ltd. last year sparked speculation that Industrial Equity would bid for Cummins. But after a protracted legal struggle, Industrial Equity reached a standstill accord that formally eliminated any possibility that it would further increase its stake. Prior to that, Britain's Hanson PLC had acquired an 8.8% Cummins stake, but members of the Cummins founding family bought the Hanson stake, eliminating that potential threat.

Cummins has been spending heavily on new products designed to meet overseas competition and satisfy new pollution guidelines. Its stock has been hurt in recent quarters by the significant fall in heavy-truck production, although that cyclical downturn is thought to have bottomed out.

Mr. Schacht called the buy-in "a vote of confidence in the company's investment in technical activities." He said he thinks companies' search for "global competitiveness" means "you'll find networking of transactions like this" more frequently.

"We believe that, taken together, the agreements we are announcing today represent giant steps forward for Cummins as the world leader in our industry," the executive said.

Part of the proceeds from the stock sale will be used to pay down debt. But $67 million will be used to buy back the Hanson stake from the founders' descendants, the Miller family. The family last year converted that stake into Series A preferred stock. The remaining $37.4 million of the family's Series A holdings will be exchanged for 580,247 common shares, representing a price of about $64.50 a share.

The result of all the agreements will be to increase Cummins common shares outstanding 45% to 14.8 million from 10.2 million.

In 1989, Cummins had a net loss of $6.1 million, or $1.52 a share, on sales of $3.51 billion.
