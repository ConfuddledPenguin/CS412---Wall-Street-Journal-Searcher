Politics & Policy: Thornburgh Has Ruled Out Bush Inquiry

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 07/16/90

WASHINGTON -- Attorney General Dick Thornburgh said he wouldn't appoint an independent counsel to investigate Neil Bush, the son of the president, in the $1 billion failure of a Denver thrift, unless Mr. Bush is accused of a crime. Led by Rep. Patricia Schroeder (D. Colo.), 12 Democrats on the House Judiciary Committee requested Friday that Mr. Thornburgh appoint an impartial investigator to look into Silverado Banking, Savings & Loan Association. Rep. Edward Feighan of Ohio later withdrew his support, calling it an "error," leaving the Democrats short of the support needed to request an independent prosecutor. Such a request requires a majority of the Democrats on the committee.

Neil Bush is already facing an administrative hearing in September for his role as a director of Silverado. While a director, he failed to disclose his business associations with two major Silverado borrowers who later defaulted on loans. One of those men, Denver developer Kenneth Good, also loaned Neil Bush $100,000, which he never was asked to repay.

Mr. Thornburgh said that even if Democrats secure the necessary support, it's unlikely he would name an independent counsel because the Justice Department doesn't investigate civil matters. "I am not aware of any evidence or any allegation from a credible source that Neil Bush has participated in any criminal activity," he said on ABC News's "This Week" yesterday.

But Democrats on the Judiciary Committee have alleged the potential for criminal conduct at Silverado. Moreover, they said at least the appearance of a political conflict of interest exists. One Silverado borrower and bondholder, Larry Mizel, raised $1 million for Republican candidates in 1984.

"The case has criminal implications that should be aggressively investigated, but the potential for political, financial, and personal conflicts of interest . . . raise serious questions about the Department's ability to conduct a fair and vigorous investigation," said a letter to Mr. Thornburgh from the committee Democrats.

S&L losses have been piling up for a decade, but heading into this fall's congressional elections, both parties are trying to capitalize on the scandal. Democrats have zeroed in on Neil Bush's woes with federal regulators, while Republicans have pointed to large campaign contributions to Democrats from Charles Keating, who sought to have senators intervene on behalf of Lincoln Savings & Loan Association. The president is feeling the heat, particularly from his own party, resulting from his backtracking on his no-new-taxes pledge, a reversal necessitated partly by the S&L losses.

Edward Rollins, co-chairman of the National Republican Congressional Committee, said that Neil Bush earlier should have agreed to sign a consent order with federal regulators, ending the matter. "I think obviously he should have done it, but Neil Bush think's he's innocent," he said on NBC News's "Meet the Press" yesterday.

Under the administrative hearing proceedings for Neil Bush, he faces punishment no worse than an admonishment not to engage in similar actions again. Earlier this year, five other Silverado directors signed consent agreements under which they agreed not to work in the banking or S&L industries again. Federal regulators also are considering a $200 million civil suit against Silverado directors, including Mr. Bush.
