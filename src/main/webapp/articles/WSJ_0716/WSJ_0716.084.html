Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Consumer Prices Probably Weren't Held Down Much by June's Lower Fuel Costs

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 07/16/90

NEW YORK -- Lower energy costs probably won't work quite as much magic on the service-heavy consumer price index as they did on June producer prices. The June CPI, due out Wednesday, is expected to maintain its recent habit of outpacing the more heavily energy-dependent producer price index, which rose only slightly in Friday's report. This week's other major indicators include: the May trade deficit, which is expected to widen after April's sharp shrinkage; June industrial production and capacity utilization, which are likely to slow, and June housing starts, which are expected to remain soft.

"The CPI has shown a much more stubborn pattern through the first half," said Michael R. Englund, economist for MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York. For one thing, there's been no abatement in the fastest-rising component: health care services. For another, the PPI benefits much more directly from a drop in oil prices.

Mr. Englund said he expects June's sharply lower energy costs to continue holding down the PPI during July. But he added that tamer energy costs are "unlikely to have the same effect in the CPI," noting that energy makes up about 8.8% of the PPI and 7.6% of the CPI. MMS expects the disparity between the indexes to hold for all of 1990; its forecast calls for a 4.8% year-over-year jump in the CPI and a 4.2% increase in the PPI.

Roger Brinner, chief economist at DRI/McGraw-Hill, Boston, noted another reason that the PPI can be expected to lag behind CPI rises: the aging expansion. "Producer prices are much more sensitive to the business cycle than services," he said. As the industrial sector slows, "you'll see much more softness in the PPI than the CPI," he said. Only recently has the service sector begun to show signs of softness.

The consensus view expects a 0.3% increase in the June CPI, up slightly from May's rise of 0.2%. Economists said that food prices continued to moderate in June but that lower crude-oil prices hadn't yet worked their way down to retail gasoline prices. Service costs in general, and medical care in particular, are thought to have continued a steady march upward. Apparel prices were expected to slide again in June after May's giveback to earlier sharp gains.

Tomorrow's report on the May trade deficit is expected to disclose backsliding after April's contraction. The consensus view calls for a widening to $7.7 billion from April's $6.9 billion. "We haven't lost our appetite for foreign goods," said DRI/McGraw-Hill's Mr. Brinner. He expects the trade gap expanded to "at least $8 billion," noting the likely jump in imports presaged by already reported April customs-duty receipts. Exports are also thought to have increased, although not as much.

Some economists said oil imports jumped in May as prices slipped, suggesting some summer stockpiling against cold-weather needs.

The recent lackluster employment data for June pretty much set the tone for industrial output and capacity utilization data due out tomorrow. Industrial output is expected to eke out a gain of 0.2%, following May's 0.6% increase. Capacity utilization is expected to be unchanged at 83.6%.

Housing starts, due out Wednesday, are expected to continue the sluggish level of recent months with an annual rate of 1,150,000 units during June.
