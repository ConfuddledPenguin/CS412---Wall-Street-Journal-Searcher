Car Makers Plan 11.6% Rise in Output From Depressed Third Quarter of '89

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 07/16/90

DETROIT -- Despite recent cutbacks by Ford Motor Co. and continuing weak sales, U.S. auto makers still plan to boost domestic car output by 11.6% in the third quarter from anemic year-earlier levels. Slow sales are forcing Ford to cut 11,300 cars from its original third-quarter production plans. However, the No. 2 auto maker still plans to build 11.1% more cars in the current quarter than a year earlier and is adding to its third-quarter truck output schedules.

Overall, the 10 major U.S. auto makers plan to build 1,560,704 cars in the third quarter, about 162,000 more than a year earlier. Truck production is forecast to rise 8.3% to 907,334 vehicles.

Their latest plans are an indication that auto makers still expect a modest second-half rebound in consumer demand despite some analysts' concerns about the sluggish U.S. economy. The plans also reflect how restrained output earlier in the year is paying off in the form of lean summer inventories.

But a major force behind the overall increase in U.S. car production for the quarter is the growing strength of the U.S. operations of Japanese auto makers. Plants owned by Japanese auto makers or operated as joint ventures with U.S. companies are expected to build 334,704 cars in the July-September period, a jump of nearly 59,000 from the third quarter of 1989. These operations will build 21.4% of all domestic cars in the period, compared with 19.7% a year earlier.

Most auto makers should be able to hold to their third-quarter production schedules because stocks of unsold cars are low, analysts say. On June 30, the car industry had an estimated 59-day supply of cars, which is at the low end of historical norms, said analyst John Qualls of Hill & Knowlton Inc. That compares with an uncomfortably high 75-day supply of cars a year earlier.

"Unless the bottom falls out of the market, I don't see any problems for the industry," Mr. Qualls said.

Thus, auto makers may be able to spend less on sales incentives to move out 1990 models by the start of the new model year Oct. 1, analysts said. Reduced incentives and increased production would help third-quarter profit at Ford and General Motors Corp. At Chrysler Corp., however, company officials have said reduced output of minivans and cars will sharply depress third-quarter profit.

Auto makers are still forecasting that sales in the third and fourth quarters will improve from weak 1989 levels despite signs of sluggish U.S. economic growth. Most company officials say they expect U.S. car and light truck sales to total about 14.5 million vehicles, roughly flat with last year.

Car makers are drawing comfort from surveys such as one by the Consumer Research Center that shows 8.1% of Americans plan to buy a car in the next six months, up from 6.7% in June 1989. "We've been getting fairly good readings in both May and June," says Fabian Linden, the center's executive director.

Other analysts are not so sanguine. They say car sales could be dragged down by a slowing national economy and by prospects of a tax increase.

Among the Big Three U.S. auto makers, GM plans the biggest jump in third-quarter car output -- a 12.7% increase from year-earlier levels. But that largely reflects production levels at this time last year that were the lowest since 1970, when a strike shut many of the company's plants. GM plans to increase its light truck production for the quarter by 4.6%.

Ford has cut its quarterly car production forecast since last month, partly because of its plan to shut down its Mustang assembly plant in Dearborn, Mich., for a week in July.

On the truck side, Ford has boosted its third-quarter production schedule since June and is running some assembly plants on overtime. Ford now plans a 25.3% increase from year-earlier levels. Earlier, the company planned an 18.8% increase.

Chrysler, which closed a subcompact car plant in Detroit this year, projects that quarterly car production will drop 5.7% from a year earlier. Chrysler's truck production will be off a whopping 25.7%, mainly because the company plans to shut its two minivan plants for a month each to retool for the redesigned 1991 Chrysler minivans.

All Japanese auto makers with U.S. operations are planning increased car production except Nissan Motor Corp., which projects an 18.2% drop because of retooling for the redesigned 1991 Sentra subcompact model at its Smyrna, Tenn., assembly plant.
