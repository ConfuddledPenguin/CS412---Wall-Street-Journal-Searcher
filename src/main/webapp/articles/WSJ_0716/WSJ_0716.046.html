U.S. Judge Orders Teamsters to Disclose Full Details of Labor Contract With UPS

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 07/16/90

WASHINGTON -- A federal court ordered the Teamsters union to provide full details of a proposed labor contract with United Parcel Service of America Inc. to all union members eligible to vote on the pact. The order by U.S. District Judge Stanley Harris granted a petition filed by five members of Teamsters for a Democratic Union, a dissident Teamsters group. The dissidents want to see all contract documents, including all 30 supplements that apply to particular segments of the work force, before taking a stand on whether to ratify the pact. The Teamsters international has provided each member with just the basic contract and the supplement that covers that member.

The court order was issued as UPS and its customers began bracing for a possible strike when the current contract expires at the end of this month. The union and local leaders have recommended that the 140,000 members who work for UPS reject the company's offer. The union could call the walkout if more than 50% of those voting opt to reject it.

Sheila Pekar, president of Internal Audit Bureau Inc., a consulting firm based in Hamlin, Pa., sent a letter to its clients urging them to "consult with retail accounts concerning the possible need to change" from UPS to other carriers.

Ms. Pekar's clients, who include apparel makers now entering a peak shipping period, use UPS to ship consumer goods. She noted that UPS spokesmen are optimistic about a settlement, but don't rule out the "possibility" of a strike.

Alan Caminiti, a UPS spokesman, said that Teamsters members who work for UPS are paid at least 78 cents an hour more than other Teamsters and have been offered "a very good package." He added, "Our people understand the competitive pressure we're under."

Judge Harris said his injunction is part of "a saga of litigation" between the dissident group and the union. The dissidents won similar court orders against the international's leaders during contract-ratification balloting in 1987 and 1988. They say they want to see all the UPS supplements partly to judge whether certain types of workers are being shortchanged.

Teamsters officials weren't available for comment. Judge Harris noted that the union contends that the dissidents don't need all the information they seek and that they already have decided how to vote anyway. But, he said, it's not up to the international to determine how much of the information is enough and when members should stop thinking about how to vote. The only possible harm to the union leaders would be "the loss of another political battle with the TDU," Judge Harris said.

The latest UPS "final" offer, somewhat sweetened from an offer made late last month, includes a wage increase of 50 cents an hour, or about 3%, each year for three years, and a cost-of-living increase, capped at 20 cents an hour, in the second and third years of the contract. It also includes new, lower-wage tiers for newer employees. And, it calls for an increase of 35 cents an hour in the employer contribution to the union health and welfare fund and to its pension fund.
