International: Poland Privatization Bills Advance in Parliament

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/16/90

WARSAW -- Poland's Solidarity-led government took its radical economic overhaul plan a step further, as the Sejm, or lower house of parliament, passed two separate bills to privatize the more than 80% of industry owned by the state and to create an agency to oversee the changes. The legislation requires Senate approval, but the measure isn't expected to face opposition.

The bills allow for the sale of shares of state enterprises under an Agency for Ownership Transformations, which will evaluate companies and put those in the best shape on the auction block first.

Reacting to public concerns that foreign investors or the former Communist elite "nomenklatura" would be the only ones with the needed capital to buy state property, the government promised that "privatization coupons" would be issued to all citizens for buying part of the state-owned property.

Finance Minister Leszek Balcerowicz also promised that citizens will be allowed to buy shares on an installment basis and, in some cases, on favorable credit terms.

Employees will be allowed to purchase as much as 20% of their companies' shares on preferential terms. Foreign investment will be at the discretion of the ownership agency's head.
