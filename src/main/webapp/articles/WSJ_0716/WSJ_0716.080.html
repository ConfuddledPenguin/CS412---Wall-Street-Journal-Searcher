Chrysler Delays Plans to Market Hyundai's Sonatas

  • Author: Bradley A. Stertz and Larry M. Greenberg
  • Date: 07/16/90

Chrysler Corp. is delaying plans to market by next year a midsized car built by South Korean auto maker Hyundai Motor Co. Last year, Hyundai agreed to provide Chrysler with a version of its Sonata sedan to sell as 1992 models through Chrysler's Jeep-Eagle dealerships. But the No. 3 U.S. auto maker said Friday that the two companies "have now agreed to focus on a more highly differentiated version of the successor to the Hyundai Sonata," which analysts don't expect to arrive until 1994.

Chrysler, of Highland Park, Mich., and Hyundai had already planned to make their two versions of the car look different. But they apparently decided further changes were needed that couldn't be completed by the fall 1991 launch of the 1992 models.

"The only way to be successful selling those cars through Chrysler will be if Chrysler gives them their own distinctive styling cues," said Christopher Cedergran, an analyst with J.D. Power & Associates, an Agoura Hills, Calif., research and consulting firm.

A Hyundai official in Canada, where some Sonatas are built, said his company expects delays of several months in the Chrysler agreement, which calls for Chrysler to buy up to 30,000 cars a year.

Still, Willy Lee, assistant to the president for corporate planning at Hyundai Auto Canada Inc., said his company will be able to deliver about 10,000 cars to Chrysler during the arrangement's first year in 1991.

Mr. Lee said the pricing of the cars is the primary sticking point between the companies. He said Chrysler "is requesting our parent company to share certain portions of the incentives" that Chrysler thinks will be necessary to sell the car in the U.S. Chrysler didn't comment on the pricing issue.

Hyundai hasn't been very successful selling the Sonata in the U.S. to date. Last year in the U.S., Hyundai sold just 35,000 of the cars, which compete head-on with the Toyota Camry and Honda Accord. Sales through June this year in the U.S. and Canada are running even lower. "That may be an element of the deal's being held back," Mr. Lee said.

The slow sales have caused Hyundai's Bromont, Quebec, plant to run far below its annual capacity of about 100,000 vehicles -- a problem the Chrysler deal was supposed to help alleviate. Mr. Lee said the plant will assemble only 40,000 units this year. Hyundai is thinking about shifting some Excel subcompact car production from Korea and possibly adding a new minivan-type vehicle to make the Bromont facility more efficient.

Chrysler's talks on possible product marketing and development ventures with Italian auto maker Fiat S.p.A are further complicating the relationship with Hyundai.

Chrysler officials and analysts said the Fiat talks may result in Chrysler getting a version of a Fiat car that would make the low-volume version of the Sonata less important in Chrysler's line-up.

The Hyundai venture was deemed important to Chrysler when it was signed last year because Chrysler was the last U.S. auto maker without ties to a car maker in South Korea. At the time, South Korea was an increasingly important source of low-cost cars. But that adavantage has slipped away from South Korean companies recently because of rising labor and other costs.
