The Fate of the Earth Plays Out on PCs

HOW would you like to change the Earth's past and shape its future?

That's the concept behind SimEarth, a personal computer game created by Will Wright, the Berkeley, Calif., author of SimCity, last year's hit computer game with highbrows that let players rebuild actual cities, such as San Francisco after the 1906 earthquake and Hamburg after it was bombed in 1944, or design imaginary cities from scratch.

SimEarth extends this idea to the planet itself, giving players a chance to change the course of the Earth's history by altering environmental forces. A few inspired choices, and "dinosaurs won't die off but will rule, and humans won't ever appear," Mr. Wright says.

Mr. Wright hopes that SimEarth, which will be available on Apple Macintosh computers next month and on International Business Machines compatibles soon afterward, will raise awareness about the intricate connections among natural forces. In the game, for example, it's a challenge merely to lay the conditions for multicellular life, much less to spark the Renaissance. Indeed, promoting civilization is hard: Mr. Wright says a player bent on improving humanity's lot may have to unleash a meteor shower that puts an entire prehistoric people at risk. "Without periodic extinctions," he says, "evolution may not occur."

In designing a program to simulate the possible lives of the Earth, Mr. Wright joins a growing tradition. Computer models came into fashion in the 1970s, when the Club of Rome issued a gloomy warning on environmental trends based on a simulation. Today, such models lay the basis for everything from forecasting the depletion of the ozone layer to predicting next week's weather.

Mr. Wright doesn't take too gloomy a view. A skillful player can keep the Earth alive until the Sun explodes five billion years from now. And even then, Mr. Wright affords the player the tools to colonize outer space.

Laptop Makers in U.S.

Look to Japan for Color

NEW color screens will whet people's appetites for laptop computers, but they may upset the balance of power between U.S. and Japanese computer makers.

The Japanese are the dominant suppliers of these critical parts, and the specter of a shortage has U.S. companies hustling to nail down long-term supplies of color displays for laptops, especially screens that use an advanced active-matrix technique just now coming on the market.

U.S. companies fear a repeat of the the 1980s, when Japan's behemoths gained control of the market for memory chips by pricing these parts at below cost to drive U.S. suppliers out of the business. As a result of that coup, U.S. computer makers have paid more for memory chips, a major materials cost for their products.

Among U.S. computer makers, IBM seems least likely to be caught in a bind. It struck a deal last year to produce these screens jointly in Japan with Toshiba. Hitachi and Sharp also are offering these displays to U.S. companies. Once volume production begins next year, they will sell their now-pricey color screens at below cost in order to prime demand, predicts Stanford Resources Inc., a San Jose, Calif., market researcher.

Making Networks as Fast

As Machines They Link

DESIGNERS are starting to pay attention to a thorny problem involving increasingly powerful desktop computers used by engineers and scientists.

Almost all of these computers are linked with other machines, and the networks aren't getting any faster. As a result, the raw speed of the new workstations is being wasted when users share or swap data, which occurs often.

Designers are tackling the problem by building devices that transfer data among workstations and among different networks of machines at a hundred times the current speed of 10 million bits of data a second.

The National Science Foundation, the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory and many private companies are funding efforts to build speedier networks, which will change the nature of computing by making it possible for a scientist to receive live video or realistic color models on a workstation.

But this change won't be painless. Current standards for networks, such as Ethernet and Token Ring, may need to be overhauled or even scrapped. And so will current desktop computers used by researchers. "This is probably the single most interesting problem that I'm dealing with," says Andreas Bechtolsheim, a computer designer at Sun Microsystems Inc.

Recording Music on CDs

Isn't Around the Corner

CONSUMERS won't be able to record on blank compact disks for a few years.

Equipment that both records on and plays CDs is considered a sure-fire hit with consumers, but a combination of factors have kept such machines off the market so far.

Officials at Sony Corp. and N.V. Philips, which control patents on most CD technology, say that a few technical problems remain before such products are finished, but that the biggest hurdle is persuading the music industry to withdraw its opposition to recordable CDs, which the industry thinks will reduce sales of prerecorded CDs.

After seeing the music industry delay the introduction of digital-audio tape, there's little optimism that fears about recordable CDs can be dispelled quickly. "We expect the same (opposition) . . . so we won't force-feed this," says a Sony executive.
