Who's News: Robert Levine Joins Nomura's U.S. Unit To Head New Credit Research Group

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 07/16/90

NEW YORK -- The U.S. subsidiary of Japan's Nomura Securities Co. named Robert Levine, a top Kidder, Peabody & Co. official, to head a new department to conduct credit research and design new fixed-income products for corporations. Mr. Levine's hiring is a key step in Max C. Chapman Jr.'s effort to convert Nomura's American operations from a mediocre broker-dealer to a competitive U.S.-style investment bank. Since he became co-chairman of Nomura's U.S. unit in October 1989, Mr. Chapman has been pulling Nomura away from being a firm that mainly acted as a middle-man buying and selling stocks and bonds for customers to one emphasizing aggressive asset and liability management.

At its core, credit research involves determining how much debt a company can carry and still operate successfully. But Mr. Levine said that he and the team he will build would go beyond that, "assessing corporate strategies with an eye to evaluating different options that a corporation has." Credit research, he added, "is really the engine that drives the assessment of corporate securities."

At Kidder, which he joined in 1977, Mr. Levine was a managing director and president of Kidder, Peabody High Yield Asset Management Inc., which Wall Street sources said managed about $750 million. In 1989, he created the first publicly underwritten asset-backed bond involving high-yield debt.

Mr. Levine, 45, will be a senior vice president at Nomura Securities International Inc., the U.S. subsidiary, beginning today. He is the third senior Kidder executive to be lured away by Mr. Chapman since he came to Nomura. Mr. Chapman is a former president of Kidder.

Two of Mr. Chapman's goals have been to develop Nomura's capability to apply sophisticated, quantitative methods that use computers to do securities trading and to assist U.S. companies in converting their heavy debt loads to stock. He also intends to aggressively manage and commit Nomura's own capital, something that traditionally risk-averse Japanese firms aren't known for.

Mr. Chapman's efforts are especially important to Nomura's parent company in Tokyo as well as its U.S. operations. American securities firms such as Salomon Brothers Inc., Morgan Stanley & Co., and Goldman, Sachs & Co. -- all more technologically advanced -- have invaded Nomura's own domestic market using mathematically oriented techniques and derivative instruments, such as futures and options, to trade Japanese stocks in Tokyo.

To address these issues, Mr. Chapman has already hired an expert in derivative products, two of Wall Street's best-known program traders and a leading trader of takeover stocks, or risk-arbitrager.

Mr. Levine, with a background in designing new bond products for corporations, will fit into this group, said Mr. Chapman. "High quality credit research is essential to our efforts in committing capital and merchant banking," said Mr. Chapman.

Apart from some previous investments in U.S. leasing, real estate and merger companies -- all of which are related to its investment banking business -- Nomura hasn't actively engaged in merchant banking, which involves taking a stake in a company for investment reasons.
