Early-July Sales Of U.S.-Built Cars, Trucks Rose 2.5%

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 07/16/90

DETROIT -- Sales of domestically built vehicles were surprisingly strong in early July, causing guarded optimism that the industry's lingering sales slump is easing. Auto makers reported that sales of U.S.built cars, trucks and vans rose 2.5% in the first 10 days of July to 255,493 from 249,298 vehicles a year earlier. Following the trend of recent months, Japanese auto makers reported a 36% gain in sales of their U.S.-built cars in the period. Chrysler Corp. posted a double-digit percentage decline, leading a 2.5% drop in domestic car sales by the Big Three.

The overall early-July sales gain came on the heels of robust sales in late June. Early-July sales ran at a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 7.3 million cars -- the first time since February that the annual rate of car sales in the first 10 days of a month topped seven million. Sales in the period the previous year ran at a 7.4 million clip, according to a Commerce Department formula.

Car dealers were pleasantly surprised by the improved results, especially because the Big Three auto makers recently raised base prices on 1990 models after trimming some buyer incentives.

"You've got a buyer out there today who's really not afraid of anything, unless he loses his job," says Hoot McInerney, owner of several new-car dealerships.

The stronger sales results, combined with lower inventory levels, make it less likely that auto makers will be forced to offer steep incentives in order to clear lots for 1991 models coming out this fall. The results also make it easier for auto makers to boost vehicle production in the third quarter as they had planned.

Sales of trucks and vans improved in the first 10 days of July more than car sales. Domestic auto makers said lighttruck and van sales rose 5.6% to 99,273 vehicles, while sales of U.S.-built cars rose less than 1% to 156,220 vehicles.

The market "was stronger than I figured," said David Healy, an auto industry analyst with Barclays de Zoete Wedd in New York. "My temptation is to raise my {industry sales} forecast for the year." But Mr. Healy said he wants to see what happens in the mid-July sales period before making such a move.

General Motors Corp.'s car sales stayed about even with sales a year earlier but saw an 18% gain in truck sales. The company attributed that increase to across-the-board gains in its Chevrolet and GMC Truck units. However, sales of the auto maker's new plastic-body minivans have been weak. So far this year General Motors has built 90,089 of the vehicles but has sold only 62,326.

Chrysler lost ground with its cars and trucks. The No. 3 U.S. auto maker's domestic-car sales fell 19% despite advertisements in which company chairman Lee Iaccoca seeks to compare Chrysler cars favorably with Honda Motor Co. models. Chrysler truck sales dropped 14% partly because its Jeep Cherokee models are facing stronger competition.

The biggest challenge in that segment comes from Ford Motor Co.'s Explorer, which helped raise Ford's truck sales 6.1%. Ford's car sales remained flat.

Nissan Motor Co. said its sales appeared weaker because sales in the previous year were greatly boosted by incentives. Nissan also blamed the decline on lower availability of its Sentra subcompact, which will be replaced with a redesigned version in September.

Total sales of U.S.-built vehicles plus imported vehicles sold by the Big Three in early July increased 1.8% from 262,625 vehicles a year earlier.
