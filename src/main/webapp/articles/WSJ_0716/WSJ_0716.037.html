Economy: Producer Prices Climbed 0.2% During June --- Retail Sales Were Up 0.5%, Hinting That Spending May Be Turning Around

  • Author: Tuesday Georges
  • Date: 07/16/90

WASHINGTON -- The government's latest economic reports suggest that inflation is somewhat under control and that consumer spending may be experiencing a modest upturn. Producer prices for finished goods rose a moderate 0.2% in June, following a 0.3% increase in May and a 0.3% decline in April, the Labor Department said. In a separate report, the Commerce Department said retail sales rose 0.5% in June, the first increase in three months.

"These two reports give us a win-win situation at least in the short term," said Ron Schreibman, vice president of the National Association of Wholesaler-Distributors in Washington.

The new figures may have contributed to the Federal Reserve's decision to ease its grip on credit Friday for the first time since December.

For the most part, analysts said they were encouraged by the June producer price figures even though, excluding foods and energy, prices of finished goods rose 0.6%. About half the increase was in tobacco and automobiles.

However, on the down side, economists said a lower rate of inflation often reflects a weakening in production stemming from a slowdown in demand.

In the 12 months ended in June, overall producer price inflation was 3.1%, the department said.

Prices of intermediate goods, which have had some processing, declined for the second month in a row, falling 0.2% in June after a 0.1% drop in May.

Prices of crude goods also decreased, plunging 2.4% last month, after significant drops in the three previous months.

The producer price index measures the prices factories and other producers receive for their goods. It doesn't track services.

Also sparking a bit of hope for the strength of the economy, retail sales totaled $147.82 billion in June, after falling 0.6%, to $147.04 billion in May, the Commerce Department said.

"The retail trade report gives us more confidence that the consumer has not totally withdrawn from the retail market," said Stacy Kottman, an economist for the Economic Forecasting Center at Georgia State University.

"Consumers are still being conservative; they are still being cautious, but if we had had a fourth month of decline, we would have to start talking recession," Mr. Kottman said.

Sales of durable goods -- items expected to last more than three years -- declined 0.1% in June, following a 0.3% decline in May. Sales of nondurable goods increased 0.9% in June, after falling 0.7% the previous month.

Automobile sales edged up 0.1% in June -- for the first rise in four months -- following a 0.2% drop in May.

Furniture sales dropped 1.9%. Building materials sales increased 1.3%. Purchases at apparel and accessory stores moved up 0.7% and department-store sales jumped 2.9%.

Excluding automobile sales, which fluctuate widely from month to month, overall retail sales were up 0.7%.

All the numbers are adjusted for seasonal variations.
