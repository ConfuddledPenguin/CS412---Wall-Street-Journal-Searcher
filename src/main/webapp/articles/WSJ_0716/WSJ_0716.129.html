Marketing & Media: FTC Seeks to Block Bid by Donnelley For Printer Partly Owned by Meredith

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 07/16/90

Federal antitrust regulators asked a U.S. court to block R.R. Donnelley & Sons Co.'s planned $487.5 million acquisition of a commercial-printing venture owned by Meredith Corp. and members of West Germany's Burda family. The Federal Trade Commission said it "has reason to believe" the acquisition "could substantially reduce competition in high-volume publication rotogravure printing" in the U.S.

If the agreement is frustrated by the FTC lawsuit, its collapse would seem unlikely to seriously damage either Chicago-based Donnelley, the nation's largest commercial printer, or Meredith, a Des Moines, Iowa, publisher of such magazines as Ladies' Home Journal and Better Homes and Gardens. The deal has already been significantly delayed by an unusually lengthy FTC review.

But the FTC's move underscores the increased rigor with which the commission has been scrutinizing proposed mergers in recent months, compared with the relatively passive stance it held when dominated by appointees of former President Reagan. FTC Chairwoman Janet Steiger ordered the more aggressive review policy when she took office in late 1989.

Friday's move thus seems likely to be keenly noted by other companies planning to merge within an industry. ConAgra Inc., which is seeking to buy another food company, Beatrice Co., from Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., recently disclosed that completion of its proposed $1.34 billion deal is being delayed by an FTC request for additional information. A $190 million tender offer for JP Industries Inc. by British auto-parts maker T&N PLC has similarly been twice extended because of an FTC review.

Throughout the FTC review of their plan, both Donnelley and Meredith had continued to express confidence the deal would proceed. Both indicated on Friday that they will carry the fight into the U.S. District Court in Washington D.C., where the FTC filed its lawsuit.

And both sounded miffed: In Des Moines, Meredith President and Chief Executive Officer Jack D. Rehm issued a statement saying the company is "extremely disappointed that this action will further delay closing on a transaction which we feel is both proper and legal." Donnelley's chairman, president and chief executive John R. Walter said in a news release that the company's "case is strong and we believe the planned acquisition is legally proper."

The acquisition of the Meredith/Burda venture would be the largest ever for growth-minded Donnelley, which is considered among the best-managed and most technologically advanced companies in the fragmented printing industry.

The announcement in late December of the pact for the Meredith/Burda venture was seen by some industry experts as a signal that Donnelley was ready to undertake a more aggressive acquisition strategy. Indeed, Mr. Walter said in Friday's release: "Acquisitions are one important element of our growth strategy and we are continuing to pursue other acquisition opportunities both here and abroad."

If the transaction doesn't proceed, says Rita Spitz, an analyst with William Blair & Co. in Chicago, "it's a lost opportunity, {for Donnelley} but they'll find other things to do."

Ms. Spitz said that if the acquisition doesn't go through, she would trim her projection of Donnelley's annual rate of growth to about 10% from 13%, "but that assumes they don't buy anything else. . . . They would love to buy in Europe, because they have only 7% of their sales overseas, and most of their major clients are global companies."

Meredith's broadcast operations, and to a lesser extent its magazines, have recently felt the impact of a nationwide dip in advertising. Part of the reason for the company's agreement to sell its 50% stake in the Meredith/Burda venture was the heavy capital outlays the printing operation requires. Meredith indicated in December that it would weigh a share repurchase plan with proceeds from the planned sale, as well as looking at possible acquisitions and "continuing to launch new properties" in its magazine line.

J. Kendrick Noble, an analyst at PaineWebber Inc., said a possible collapse of the deal wouldn't have much impact on Meredith. "They're in pretty good financial shape," he said, adding that the company hasn't committed itself to any significant expenditures of the expected funds.
