Politics & Policy: Army Plans to Cut ROTC Programs At 50 U.S. Colleges

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 07/16/90

WASHINGTON -- The Army, as part of a budget-cutting drive, said it will phase out reserve officer training programs at 50 colleges and universities across the U.S. The announcement is a symbolic turning point for the Army as it struggles to bring personnel and spending plans in line with post-Cold War budget realities. While the schools involved have produced relatively few officers in the past few years, the service's civilian and uniformed leaders nevertheless debated for months whether to cut politically popular officer-training programs. Such programs give the military high visibility on campuses while providing large amounts of scholarship aid to students.

The move means that nearly one out of every eight Army Reserve Officers' Training Corps units nationwide will be disbanded by the fall of 1991. The affected schools are in 27 states and range from Dartmouth College and other large institutions to such small schools as Bemidji State University in rural Minnesota.

Army ROTC programs also will be consolidated at dozens of other campuses, but officials said details of those shifts haven't been worked out. So far, Air Force and Navy brass have avoided closing officer-training units because they face proportionately smaller long-term cuts in the number of officers and enlisted troops.

Over the next five years, the Army will need at least 20,000 fewer officers than envisioned under earlier budget scenarios. Well over half of the reduction will be realized by cutting the projected number of newly commissioned ROTC officers.

Kim Wincup, the Army's top personnel official, said in a recent interview the Army is determined to reduce by 20% the number of new officers produced by ROTC programs. Currently, ROTC programs turn out about 7,400 second lieutenants a year.

But before the announcement, Maj. Gen. Wallace Arnold, who runs the Army's ROTC programs, said that "every student with an {ROTC} scholarship will be allowed to get a commission." To achieve the cutbacks, the Army intends to recruit fewer new cadets, reduce the number of nonscholarship students taking ROTC courses, and increase competition among graduates seeking active-duty assignments.

If Congress demands deeper personnel cuts than the Army currently plans, ROTC is certain to go through another shake-up, and thousands of additional would-be officers will be encouraged -- or forced -- to leave the program.
