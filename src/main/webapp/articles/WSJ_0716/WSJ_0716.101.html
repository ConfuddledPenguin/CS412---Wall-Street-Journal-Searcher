Japan, EC Officials To Begin a Round Of Trade Talks

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 07/16/90

TOKYO -- With the ink barely dry on a broad economic agreement signed with the U.S. last month, Japanese officials will sit down today with European Community officials to begin talks on some finer points. The so-called working group on trade issues, agreed on during a meeting last May between Japanese and EC ministers, isn't expected to achieve any major breakthroughs this week. An official of Japan's Ministry of International Trade and Industry describes it largely as a get-acquainted session that will set the agenda for discussions in coming months.

But the European side is bringing to the table several issues on which it hopes to see at least some signs of movement. The EC will argue for an easing of tariffs on sweets, cheese, sherry and port wine, among other processed foods. Frozen fish quotas are also likely to be discussed.

European officials say they appear to be making progress in an effort to ease a Japanese ban on imports of at least one niche product: Italian prosciutto. The prosciutto now draped over melon at fancy Tokyo restaurants is imported from the U.S. and other countries believed to be free of certain swine diseases. (The U.S., itself, lifted a ban on prosciutto imports just last year after tests indicated that the ham-curing process eliminated any such diseases in the meat.)

European officials say the Japanese government is preparing to send a team to Parma, Italy, to inspect curing plants in that area; but an official dealing with the issue in Japan's agriculture ministry declined to comment, explaining that it wouldn't be helpful to encourage "speculation."

However, European hopes for further liberalization of a strict tariff system for leather and leather shoes are likely to meet with strong resistance. Japan has long argued that its leather industry needs special protection.

Imports of European shoes to Japan have increased rapidly since Japan converted its quota system to a two-tiered tariff system several years ago. Imports from the EC are estimated to have exceeded 1.5 million pairs in the fiscal year that ended last March, up from 692,000 pairs three years earlier. But EC officials contend that the current tariff structure effectively caps annual growth in imports.
