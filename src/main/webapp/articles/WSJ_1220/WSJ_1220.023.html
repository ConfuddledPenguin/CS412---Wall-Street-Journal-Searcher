CHANCELLOR LAMONT STANDS FIRM

Britain's Chancellor of the Exchequer Norman Lamont said the nation's deepening recession wouldn't move him to adjust current exchange-rate and monetary policies.

"We will do whatever is necessary to maintain sterling's position in the exchange-rate mechanism," he declared in his first press conference since assuming his post last month. In holding firm to the arrangement, which sets a median exchange-rate of 2.95 marks for the pound, Mr. Lamont dashed hopes for a Yuletide cut in interest rates or a realignment within the European Monetary System.

Meanwhile, government figures released separately showed that the British economy contracted by 1.2% between the second and third quarters this year. Amid such signs that the U.K. recession is proving to be harsher than anticipated, industry officials have stepped up a campaign for a cut in base rates, which stand at 14%.

The Confederation of British Industries' latest survey reported that the manufacturing sector expects the biggest falloff in production in 10 years.

THE EC LOOKS EAST

The European Community will begin today to negotiate accords with Poland, Czechoslovakia and Hungary that could eventually lead to membership in the 12-nation bloc. The EC said the accords would set up free trade in manufactured goods and strengthen economic and cultural cooperation. Provisions for financial assistance from the community may be included in the accords, the EC said.

SAS PLANE FOR VIETNAM AIRLINES?

Scandinavian Airline System plans to lease one of its Boeing 767 jets to Vietnam Airlines. However, Kjell Fredheim, head of SAS Airline, said, "A prerequisite for the signing is the approval of the U.S. government." He added: "We will not enter an agreement that violates American regulations." If the deal goes through, the aircraft will operate between Thailand and Vietnam.

REFORESTING THE AMAZON

Brazil has approved the first major plan to reforest part of the Amazon, at a cost of $3 billion, officials said. The plan, to be funded by private industry and foreign contributions, envisages the reforestation of 2.5 million acres over 10 years. The state mining concern Companhia Vale do Rio Doce, which helped draft the project, said that the project should begin within a year.

SOUTH KOREA'S TRADE POSITION

South Korea told the U.S. it would review its position on global trade in an effort to contribute to the success of stalled trade talks. Seoul officials said the U.S. complained that South Korea had sided with the European Community and Japan on the thorny farm-subsidy issue in the Uruguay Round negotiations that broke down in Brussels earlier this month. South Korea also promised efforts to prevent the country's controversial frugality campaign from discriminating against imported goods.

`FORGOTTEN EMERGENCY'

A 10-month civil war has made Liberia the world's biggest human tragedy at present, but there has been no response to a United Nations appeal for aid, a U.N. relief official said.

"It's a forgotten emergency," said Frank Verhaegen, senior relief coordinator with the U.N. Disaster Relief Organization. "Government structures are non-existent, hospitals are not functioning, no one has been vaccinated since the beginning of the year and agriculture is at a standstill."

Mr. Verhaegen said an aid appeal launched by U.N. Secretary-General Javier Perez de Cuellar on Dec. 5 had gone unheeded. "The response from the international community has been zero," he said. "There has been an embarrassing silence . . . at a time when everyone is rushing to the rescue of the Soviet Union."

Mr. Verhaegen said some countries, including the U.S., were probably waiting for a political settlement before offering aid. "In the meantime," he said, "the lives of thousands of Liberians, some threatened by famine-related diseases, are at risk."

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

In Australia, the top of the Sydney Opera House sported a huge pair of sunglasses yesterday, put there by the environmental group Greenpeace. The inflatable sunglasses -- 70 feet wide by 20 feet high -- were accompanied by a banner that read: "Ozone Depletion: stop it or we'll go blind." A portable generator inflated the glasses at about 6:30 a.m., and they stayed in place for about an hour until police pulled the plug. . . . Four out of 10 Britons are bored with eating turkey at Christmas, according to a new poll. The survey, by Market & Opinion Research International, showed salmon and duck as top choices for alternative Yuletide fare.
