U.S Interest Rates Linked To German Reunification

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. has a big stake in whether the German government finances reunification through raising taxes or borrowing, the U.S. Congressional Budget Office said. If Germany does what former President Ronald Reagan did a decade ago and borrows heavily in world markets to finance government spending, the CBO projects that U.S. interest rates in the 1990s will be about one percentage point higher than they otherwise would be.

But if Germany raises the money by taxing its citizens, U.S. interest rates will be only about three-tenths of a percentage point higher, the CBO study projects.

For years, Germany has been supplying savings to the rest of the world, but the huge cost of rebuilding the former East Germany will change this signficantly.

"In recent years, the U.S. budget deficit has kept real {inflation-adjusted} interest rates high," said the congressional Joint Economic Committee, which requested the study on how changes in Europe will affect the U.S. economy. "The budget agreement just enacted is designed to alleviate the deficit's upward pressure on interest rates, but this could be countered by the interest-rate effects of German fiscal policy."

The CBO projections rely on a computer model of the economy developed by Warwick McKibbin of the Reserve Bank of Australia and Jeffrey Sachs of Harvard University.
