Foreign Exchange: Dollar Climbs, Defying Bearish Drag Of Lower Rates as War Jitters Continue

  • Author: Audrey Choi Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 12/20/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar rose, defying the bearish drag of lower U.S. interest rates. Helping the U.S. currency hold firm was the reluctance to let go of dollars in a war-threatened environment. Meanwhile, louder speculation about sinking rates in Japan battered the yen across the board, even as expectations of strict monetary policy in Germany kept the mark robust against its European counterparts and the yen.

Late in New York, the dollar was quoted at 1.4785 marks, just above 1.4762 marks late Tuesday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 134.05 yen, up from 132.62 yen in New York late Tuesday. Sterling was trading at $1.9305, down from $1.9430 late Tuesday.

In midmorning trading in Tokyo Thursday, the dollar was quoted at 134.20 yen, up from 133.27 yen late Wednesday.

Potentially setting the stage for a sharply lower dollar was aggressive action by the U.S. Federal Reserve, which had announced a cut in the discount rate to 6 1/2% from 7% late Tuesday afternoon. The Fed followed this move with action in the money markets yesterday, signaling a new federal funds target rate of 7%, down from 7 1/4% previously. The discount rate is the interest rate the Fed charges member banks for loans; the federal funds rate is the rate banks charge one another for overnight loans.

Federal funds had been trading at 7 1/16% when the Fed entered the market, arranging $1.5 billion of customer repurchase agreements. Analysts said this signaled a 1/4 percentage point reduction in the target rate; a round of system repurchase agreements would have been interpreted as a 1/2 point relaxation.

While the quick loosening of credit seems surprisingly aggressive for the Fed, which until now has taken the slow and steady approach, traders noted, the dollar was able to take the moves in stride.

After the initial decline late Tuesday, there was little follow-through. Participants said that a 1/2 percentage point cut in the discount rate had already been factored into exchange rates. The lowering of the federal funds target rate didn't budge currencies at all.

"People expected the discount rate cut. The only surprise was {the timing}," said Sung Won Sohn, chief economist at Norwest Corp. in Minneapolis.

The timing of the discount cut may have been aggressive, said Marc Cohen, vice president at Fuji Bank in New York. "But when you look at the {U.S.} economy . . . then you think, maybe drastic times call for drastic measures," so that softening of U.S. rates doesn't have to trigger a broad dollar sell-off.

Participants said that under different market conditions, the dollar might have suffered more. But, in thin markets, "with many banks already square for the year end, it's a recipe for doing little or nothing," said David Jaques, senior corporate trader at Barclays Bank in New York.

Until the market has a better sense of which way the Mideast situation will swing, it's imprudent to try establishing any strategic long-term positions, Mr. Jaques added.

Among the developments keeping the markets on edge about the Persian Gulf crisis were disparate reports about the U.S. capability in the Mideast. Sen. Daniel Inouye, (D., Hawaii) indicated that a war with Iraq would last no more than five days, while one of the U.S.'s top military deputies said U.S. troops wouldn't be ready to mount an offensive by the Jan. 15 United Nations' deadline for Iraq's withdrawal from Kuwait.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery jumped $9.50 to $386 an ounce. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.5 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $382.20 an ounce.
