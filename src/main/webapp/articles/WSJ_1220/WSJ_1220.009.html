Politics & Policy: Quayle-Led Panel Helped Kill Plan On Recycling Rules

  • Author: Barbara Rosewicz
  • Date: 12/20/90

WASHINGTON -- A White House council headed by Vice President Quayle used its muscle to kill a government proposal for trash recycling just days before it was to go into effect. Opposition from the vice president's Competitiveness Council led the Environmental Protection Agency to drop a proposed regulation that would have required municipal waste incinerators to recycle at least 25% of the trash destined for burning.

The regulation would have imposed the first federal rules mandating recycling of the nation's trash. But the requirement also stirred complaints from the business community that reregulation is on the rise under President Bush.

EPA Administrator William Reilly said the action doesn't mean the Bush administration is reneging on the president's campaign commitment to reduce waste through recycling. "Everyone in the administration remains committed to recycling," Mr. Reilly said in an interview.

Mr. Reilly said the panel concluded that it was inappropriate to enact a major recycling requirement as part of a broader rule designed to curb air pollution from the scores of incinerators that now burn 14% of municipal waste in the U.S. After stripping out the recycling provisions, the remainder of the air-pollution regulation will be issued and put into effect later this month, Mr. Reilly said. And recycling ideas will be considered next year when Congress reauthorizes the nation's principal waste-disposal law, the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act.

Opposition to the recycling measure, however, wasn't merely on technical grounds. Michael Deland, chairman of the president's Council on Environmental Quality, said many panel members felt that voluntary recycling works best and "we don't want to force it down people's throats."

The recycling rule also was strongly opposed by the National League of Cities. The group objected to it being mandatory and said that it would be harder to get sites approved for new incinerators to handle the nation's mounting trash problem.

Also opposing the rule was the waste-handling industry, which includes companies like Wheelabrator Technologies Inc. and a subsidiary of Ogden Corp. that make incinerators that burn trash to make energy.

This was the first time that Mr. Quayle's obscure panel, which was created to stop rules that stifle industry, has intervened to block a regulation. The panel is the successor to the deregulation task force Mr. Bush had headed as vice president during the Reagan years.

The aggressive recycling initiative was the highlight of the broader air-pollution rule that the EPA proposed for incinerators last December after getting clearance from the Office of Management and Budget.

While the Quayle commission is only an advisory panel, officials said there was so much opposition to the recycling proposal within the administration that the EPA had little choice but to agree to strip it out.

A White House official said Mr. Reilly was the only official to speak in favor of the proposal at the cabinet-level meeting. The official said Mr. Quayle also stated at the meeting that the administration continues to support recycling.

The panel's actions suggest, however, that the administration will find it hard to deliver on the president's campaign commitment to recycling and the EPA's goal of reducing the nation's waste 25% by 1992.

"Dropping this element from the rule sort of cuts the legs out from under that campaign promise, and it leaves everyone wondering how the administration will meet its commitment to make recycling more than a slogan," said Eric Goldstein, a lawyer with the Natural Resources Defense Council. A lawsuit by the council, an environmental group, forced the EPA to draw up the incinerator air-pollution regulations. Environmentalists widely supported the recycling requirement.
