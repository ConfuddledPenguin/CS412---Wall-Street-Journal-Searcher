The Homeless Lose Sympathy, Says U.S. Mayors' Study

  • Author: Timothy Noah
  • Date: 12/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. Conference of Mayors reported that public sympathy toward the homeless is dwindling. "People don't seem to have the tolerance for homeless people that they used to have," said Sue Myrick, mayor of Charlotte, N.C., at a news conference held to announce the mayors' latest study on homelessness.

The change is reflected in the trend toward limiting homeless people's access to public property. Nine of the 30 cities surveyed in the mayors' study reported such actions, ranging from a panhandling ban on New York City subways to enforcement of a no-sleeping ordinance in San Francisco's Civic Center Plaza to removal of homeless camps under bridges in Portland, Ore.

City officials not identified in the study reported that the public seemed increasingly testy about the homeless population. "People seem to be tiring of the problem," said an official from Providence, R.I. "There is an increasing tendency to blame the homeless for being homeless."

Advocates of the homeless also noted the public's growing hostility. "Certainly, as panhandlers have gotten more aggressive, I think there have been instances where people are intimidated," said Joan Alker, assistant director of the National Coalition for the Homeless. "After that's been happening for a few years, people's attitudes start to harden."

Even the opinion-making class, whose members just a few years ago were trumpeting the plight of the homeless, appears to be fed up with the issue. In a recent New York magazine cover story titled "How to Save New York," commentators ranging from novelist Michael M. Thomas to real estate developer and publisher Mortimer Zuckerman called for a less sympathetic attitude toward "vagrants," as Mr. Zuckerman called them.

One reason for the new hostility may be the growing size of the homeless population. According to the mayors' study, requests for emergency shelter increased during the past year by an average of 24%. Nineteen percent of the homeless people requesting emergency shelter were turned away, usually because the cities lacked proper facilities to house them.

Another factor in the public's attitude may be the lack of adequate facilities for the mentally ill homeless, who are estimated in the mayors' report to constitute 28% of the homeless population. "I'm seeing more and more mentally ill people on the street," said Boston Mayor Raymond Flynn, who every night ferries homeless people to Boston shelters. "I don't even know where to take them."

The housing bill recently signed by President Bush sets aside $123.2 million for housing for the homeless, tied to the provision of services such as mental health care and the rehabilitation of drug users. But Congress hasn't yet funded the bill.
