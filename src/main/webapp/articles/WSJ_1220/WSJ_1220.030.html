International: Gorbachev Says He'll Use Direct Rule In Areas Posing `Serious Threat' to State

  • Author: Peter Gumbel
  • Date: 12/20/90

MOSCOW -- President Mikhail Gorbachev, in the clearest sign yet of his growing authoritarian mood, declared that he will bypass local authorities and introduce direct presidential rule in areas where events "pose a serious threat to the state." The Soviet leader's remarks to the Congress of People's Deputies, the supreme national legislature, seem sure to aggravate an already bitter battle between the Kremlin and the nation's 15 republics over the future structure of the Soviet Union.

Only a few hours before Mr. Gorbachev spoke, Boris Yeltsin, the president of the Russian republic, attacked the Kremlin for ignoring the republics' sovereign rights and accused it of "seeking to keep its absolute power at any cost."

"Russia does not agree with the creation of a dictatorship of the Kremlin," Mr. Yeltsin said, adding that Soviet leaders seemed to be motivated by "a mad rage" against the republics.

Brushing off such criticism, which has been echoed by other republican leaders at the congress, Mr. Gorbachev said he was worried about growing nationalism and ethnic conflicts in the republics of Moldavia and Georgia, and was "extremely concerned" about events in the Baltic states of Lithuania, Latvia and Estonia.

"I would like to stress that where the situation becomes especially tense and there is a serious threat to the state and to people's well-being, I will have to introduce a state of emergency or presidential rule," he said to enthusiastic applause from the largely conservative Congress.

"In such a situation, I think the president will have to take full responsibility on himself, because my attempts to reach an agreement on working together end up by us not being able to agree," he added.

Over the past few days, Mr. Gorbachev has frequently called for discipline and the restoration of law and order. At the congress, he took issue with critics of his new stance, describing them as being motivated by "a populism with rotten roots."

He spoke shortly after 53 deputies, including several top-ranking military leaders, issued an appeal for the president to clamp down on "separatism" and "subversive anti-state activity."

"Perestroika is bogged down in ruinous darkness," the appeal said, adding that the West would understand any tough measures that were taken. "You will be resolutely supported by the leaders of world politics who no longer dread the U.S.S.R. as a totalitarian superpower, but fear that fragments of the collapsing colossus could destabilize the world order," it added.

Behind such rhetoric lies a growing fear in the Soviet establishment that their powers are being usurped by leaders of the republics. Local authorities throughout the nation have recently ignored or directly contradicted numerous decrees issued by Mr. Gorbachev on restoring order and stabilizing the economy.

The Soviet president says disarray is a result of his lack of executive powers, and he is seeking to persuade the congress to give him more direct authority. At the same time, he has made some token concessions to the republics in a new union treaty that the congress is discussing.

But the restless republics are not impressed. Four of them, Georgia and the three Baltic states, are flatly refusing to sign any new treaty because they want to regain their independence. Delegates from Moldavia walked out of the congress on Tuesday, and Armenia, Russia, the Ukraine, Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan have all said they won't sign Mr. Gorbachev's treaty unless it is redrafted.

The issue of giving the Soviet president greater powers has now overlapped with wrangling over the union treaty, casting doubt on Mr. Gorbachev's ability to create any functioning power base.

Mr. Yeltsin, who only four months ago appeared ready to cooperate with Mr. Gorbachev, denounced the planned new executive powers, saying they would give the president more unchecked legal rights than such discredited former leaders as Joseph Stalin and Leonid Brezhnev.

An opponent of Mr. Yeltsin, Prime Minister Nikolai Ryzhkov, in what could be a farewell speech, lashed out at unidentified "political forces" he claimed were trying to destroy the Soviet economy. He said many groups were using the cover of market economics to wage "a real political struggle."

At the same time, he acknowledged the failure of his government to halt the Soviet economy's rapid decline. "We have not managed to implement perestroika in the form that it was originally conceived," he said, referring to Mr. Gorbachev's economic restructuring program. Excusing the Soviet president's "tactical errors," he added that he himself was partly to blame because he allowed ideology to take priority over economics.
