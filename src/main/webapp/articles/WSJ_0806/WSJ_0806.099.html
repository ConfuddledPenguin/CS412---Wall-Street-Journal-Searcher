Court Blocks Bid To Make S&L Chief Set Aside Money

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 08/06/90

A Los Angeles federal judge invalidated a novel government order that would have forced former Columbia Savings & Loan Association Chief Executive Officer Thomas Spiegel to place $21 million in escrow before a hearing on charges that he wasted assets of the insolvent thrift for personal gain. Judge Stephen Wilson held that the Office of Thrift Supervision exceeded its authority when it made the escrow demand last month as part of an administrative complaint against Mr. Spiegel. Mr. Spiegel is charged with misapplying $21 million in thrift funds for a variety of personal uses. The OTS also is seeking a $5 million civil penalty. The case is considered the largest single civil matter that thrift regulators have brought against an individual.

Judge Wilson held that, under relevant federal statutes passed by Congress, the OTS was limited to taking emergency action only in cases where there was evidence of ongoing or future harm to an institution, as opposed to cases in which it was acting primarily to correct past alleged abuses. Mr. Spiegel hasn't been an official of the Beverly Hills, Calif., thrift since last December, and thus hasn't been in a position to affect the affairs of the institution, his attorneys argued. The government had argued that there was a threat that Mr. Spiegel might move his assets offshore or otherwise dissipate them.

Judge Wilson also held that the order would have resulted in an unconstitutional taking of property without a hearing.

Previously, the judge had temporarily barred the government from enforcing the order, and late Friday he made his decision permanent. The government could appeal the ruling. OTS officials couldn't be reached.

Rather than a major turning point in the case, the decision is more of a public relations blow to government regulators' recent efforts to assume a tough-cop legal posture against operators of troubled or failed S&Ls.

Dennis Perluss, Mr. Spiegel's lawyer in Los Angeles, said he was "delighted" at the ruling. In the past, he said, thrift regulators have "only been interested in taking actions before an administrative hearing that were designed to prevent future injury, such as to remove an officer. What we had is a much more aggressive and far-reaching action. In effect, they wanted the judgment to be paid in advance."

Mr. Perluss added, however, that he understood that the OTS was considering adding charges to its complaint against Mr. Spiegel. A hearing on all charges is currently scheduled for Sept. 4 in Los Angeles.

Columbia's insolvency reflects nearly $1 billion in losses from declining values and losses in junk bonds that Mr. Spiegel and Columbia purchased, largely from Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. and junk-bond chief Michael Milken. Despite the losses, Columbia hasn't been taken over by regulators, although a takeover is considered likely. The thrift's new managers recently agreed to sell the junk-bond portfolio to a Canadian investor partnership for $3 billion.
