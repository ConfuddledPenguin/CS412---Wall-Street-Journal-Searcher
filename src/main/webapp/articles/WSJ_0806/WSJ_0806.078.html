The Mideast Conflict: Bush Allows Iraqi Oil to Unload

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 08/06/90

After aggressive oil-industry lobbying, the Bush administration reversed course to allow hundreds of millions of dollars of Iraqi and Kuwaiti crude already on tankers headed to the U.S. to unload. Initially the president embargoed all Iraqi imports, including crude already ordered and paid for. That sent oil companies scrambling for substitute supplies and partially contributed to last week's big run-up in crude and gasoline prices. Customs officials for a time even seized at least one cargo belonging to Coastal Corp. in Corpus Christi, Texas.

Late Friday the Treasury department issued rules stating that any oil loaded before last Thursday morning with a bill of lading issued before then was exempt if it arrived in the U.S. before Oct. 1. Any remaining payments due on such oil would be made into a "blocked account" rather than to Iraq.

The precise amount of oil involved couldn't be determined. But the U.S. had been importing about 633,000 barrels of crude from those two countries a day; at current prices a month's worth would exceed $430 million. Some industry reports have put the amount of oil at sea 50% higher. A Coast Guard spokesman in Washington said about 15 tankers carrying Iraqi crude were due to dock at five ports along the Gulf Coast just through next week.

A Treasury spokeswoman said the rules were issued "to minimize the negative effects on the U.S. economy" while making sure "that no benefits accrued to Iraq."

"I talked directly to Henson Moore," the deputy energy secretary, said Ron Haddock, chief executive officer of American Petrofina Inc. in Dallas. The company was awaiting five million barrels of Iraqi and Kuwaiti crude loaded before the embargo was issued. "Once people understood the situation we had a sympathetic ear," Mr. Haddock said.

The company's Washington law firm also pressed the issue, as did a group of companies through their main trade group, the American Petroleum Institute, Mr. Haddock said. "Embargoing that oil wasn't hurting Iraq, it was hurting U.S. companies and consumers," he said.

In San Francisco, a Chevron Corp. spokesman said: "We had people in our Washington office working with the government on it." Chevron has nine million barrels of Iraqi crude on order, including 1.9 million barrels that was waiting to be unloaded at its Pascagoula, Miss., refinery. That process began yesterday morning, he said.

A spokesman for Ashland Oil Inc., a large refiner and retailer in Ashland, Ky., said: "We had a tanker on the water with one million barrels." But he said Ashland hasn't decided whether to try to take delivery because it hasn't paid for the crude yet. About 500,000 barrels of Iraqi crude is nearing an Amoco Corp. refinery, a spokesman said, with several more cargoes also en route.

A Coastal spokesman said it was unloading the 500,000 barrels of crude that had been embargoed. "We have several more tankers that apparently will be coming and will be exempted," he added.
