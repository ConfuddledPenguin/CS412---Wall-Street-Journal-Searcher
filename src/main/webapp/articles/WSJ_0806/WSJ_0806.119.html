Credit Markets: Long-Term Bond Rates Push Higher --- Anxieties Over Oil, Inflation Send Buyers To Short-Term Issues

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 08/06/90

NEW YORK -- In the wake of the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, short-term and long-term interest rates are parting company. Investors, worried about the inflationary impact of rising oil prices, are shying away from buying long-term Treasury bonds, driving long-term interest rates higher.

But convinced that the Federal Reserve may soon cut short-term interest rates to revive a weakening economy, they are snapping up short-term notes.

"I think short-term rates will continue to go lower" on expectations that the Fed will ease credit, said Maria Fiorini Ramirez, president of Maria Ramirez Capital Consultants Inc., a unit of John Hancock Freedom Securities.

But even if the Fed reduces short-term interest rates, she said, long-term rates may spurt still higher on fears that a weak economy won't reduce stubborn inflation.

The bond market's schizophrenic behavior is showing up clearly in the gap between yields on two-year Treasury notes and 30-year bonds, which is now the widest it has been in two years.

Traders refer to this as a "steepening yield curve," and economists generally regard it as a sign that investors have become jittery about the prospects of rising inflation.

Yields on two-year Treasury notes have fallen sharply in recent weeks and now are about 7.8%. Meanwhile, yields on 30-year Treasury bonds have risen slightly to about 8.56%. The difference between the short-term and the long-term yields, which is called the yield spread, is about 3/4 percentage point. Ms. Ramirez said the spread is likely to widen to one full percentage point.

The yield curve had been slowly steepening for several weeks as short-term interest rates fell faster than long-term rates. But last week, when Iraqi troops invaded Kuwait, investors made a mad dash for short-term Treasury securities, buying up three-month and six-month bills as well as two-year, three-year and five-year Treasury notes.

"Nobody was willing to sell" short-term securities, said James Capra, a senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc.

Some of the buying was so-called "flight-to-quality" purchases made by investors rushing to get cash out of the Middle East and into the U.S., which is considered a safe haven. But the bulk of buyers last week were domestic traders and arbitragers who are betting that the Fed will soon cut the benchmark federal funds rate from its current level of 8%, which in turn will boost the value of securities purchased when rates were higher and allow those investors to book profits.

A cut in the federal funds rate, which banks charge each other on overnight loans, could also nudge other short-term and intermediate-term rates lower. Commercial bankers could react to such a move by cutting the prime, or base, lending rate for corporations as well as short-term consumer lending rates like those on home-equity loans.

Speculation that the Fed will soon ease interest rates has been swirling for weeks, but the prospects that such an easing will occur sooner, rather than later, were heightened on Friday when the government released a bombshell July employment report.

The biggest surprise was that employment growth actually declined, the first time that has happened since June 1986. The Department of Labor said the number of non-farm jobs declined 219,000 last month. Even when layoffs of temporary census workers were excluded, the decline was 57,000.

With so many workers handed pink sheets, the unemployment rate jumped 0.3 percentage point to 5.5%.

"The employment numbers confirmed that the economy is weak; I think the Fed will ease," said Samuel D. Kahan, chief financial economist at Fuji Securities Inc. He said that if the Fed decides to make its move, it will likely do so early this week -- before the Treasury's huge quarterly auction of government securities. Otherwise, he said, the Fed may wait until late this month to ease rates, after its next policymaking Federal Open Market Committee meeting.

Mr. Kahan and many other economists on Wall Street think the Fed will probably ease despite oil-inspired inflation fears.

"Central bankers always say that their job is to fight inflation, no matter what," Mr. Kahan said. "But the truth of the matter is, this central bank will only fight inflation when it is sure that economic activity is growing. If it looks like the economy is sliding toward recession, the fight against inflation will be postponed -- not aborted -- but postponed."

But the long-term end of the bond market is worlds apart from the short end. In fact, long-term investors are worried about how events unfolding in the Persian Gulf will affect inflation.

Fears of spiraling prices came from two sources. One is a big spike in oil prices triggered by the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, which raises the specter of less-plentiful and more-expensive oil supplies. The other is the six-cent rise in hourly earnings in the July employment report, a figure that analysts say makes the threat of inflation even greater.

With the threat of inflation looming large, investors are cooling toward long-term Treasury bonds. They are not dumping, but simply aren't buying any new ones. And that's depressing prices.

Still, at a time when the stock market appears shaky, the economy is on the verge of recession and numerous financial uncertainties exist, some investors are sticking with long-term Treasurys.

"You can get 8.5% yield on 30-year Treasury bonds and that looks pretty good to us," said Ian MacKinnon, head of fixed-income investments at Vanguard Group. "If it turns out that what Iraq has done is push inflation up, then I will have to modify my outlook," he said.

All of this is a mixed blessing for the Treasury, which is scheduled to auction $32.25 billion of securities this week in its quarterly refunding of the federal debt. The Treasury will sell $11.5 billion of three-year notes tomorrow, $10.5 billion of ten-year notes Wednesday and $10.25 billion of 30-year bonds Thursday.

"Considering the appetite for short-term securities, the first auction should be the smoothest," said Ms. Ramirez. She said, however, that the ten-year and 30-year bonds may encounter more investor resistance.

Friday's Market Activity

Prices of long-term bonds continued to tumble in the aftermath of Iraq's conquest of Kuwait.

The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond fell nearly 1 1/4 points, or $12.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount, on Thursday as news of turmoil in the Middle East sparked speculation that oil supplies would become less plentiful and more expensive, putting upward pressure in inflation.

The bond rout continued Friday with prices of the benchmark 30-year bond plunging an additional 1 1/8 point.

Prices of short-term issues, however, rose as employment data hinting of a recession seemed to indicate the Federal Reserve might have to ease policy quickly to keep the economic expansion alive.

In the corporate-bond market, the payment-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. settled sharply lower after investment firm Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. -- which controls RJR -- bought back a hefty chunk of its exchangeable bonds Thursday. Payment-in-kind bonds pay interest in the form of additional securities.

The bonds fell because investors assume that the repurchase of RJR Nabisco's pay-in-kind bonds is nearly complete.

Officials at RJR Nabisco declined to comment on the buy-backs, but traders estimate that RJR in the past three weeks has bought back $1.5 billion of its 17% bonds of 2007. Much of that buying was done last Thursday, traders said, when Iraq's invasion of Kuwait spooked many participants in the financial markets. People familiar with the open-market repurchase program estimate that RJR did most of its purchases at 90 cents to 94 cents on the dollar.

On Thursday alone, KKR, working through Goldman, Sachs & Co., bought between $800 million and $1 billion of the RJR bonds, according to people familiar with the repurchase. At least some of those purchases were below the bonds' New York Stock Exchange closing price of 91 3/4 cents on the dollar, traders said. KKR, Goldman and RJR all declined to discuss their roles.

Market sources speculated that some of the bonds may have come from the accounts of takeover-stock speculators as well as from Nebraska investor Warren Buffet. Mr. Buffet declined to comment.

RJR's 17% exchangeable payment-in-kind debentures ended 1 3/4 points lower to close at 90 on robust volume of nearly $20 million on the New York Stock Exchange. RJR's 17 3/8% convertible payment-in-kind debentures fell 2 1/8 points to close at 111 1/2 on relatively heavy volume.

In the broader high-yield market, junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, finished 1/2 to 3/4 point lower.

Hard hit were the junk bonds of casinos operated by real estate developer Donald Trump. Trump Plaza Funding Corp.'s 12 7/8% first-mortgage bonds ended down three points at 76, while the Trump Taj Mahal Funding Corp.'s 14% bonds closed down 1 1/2 points at 44 in over-the-counter trading.

Some Trump bonds have been been falling due to news that gaming revenues at the Trump casinos fell sharply during July.
