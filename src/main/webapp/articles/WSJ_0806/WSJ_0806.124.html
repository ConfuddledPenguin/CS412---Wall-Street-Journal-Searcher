Government May Need Several Weeks To Resolve HEAF's Financial Problems

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 08/06/90

WASHINGTON -- Efforts to find a solution to the financial problems facing Higher Education Assistance Foundation, a major student loan-guarantee agency, may take several more weeks as the Bush administration looks for a wider range of options. When HEAF's problems surfaced last month, administration officials said they hoped to work out an orderly transfer of HEAF's estimated $9 billion student-loan portfolio to another guarantee agency within a week.

But officials haven't embraced either of the major proposals before them -- one from the Student Loan Marketing Association, the other from United Student Aid Funds. Both agencies have proposed taking over HEAF's best loans, while asking the government to pay the full amount of loans on which borrowers default.

Under the federal loan program, students borrow from banks, and one of the nation's 55 nonprofit guarantee agencies, of which HEAF and USAF are the largest, insures the loan. The Education Department then reinsures the loan.

In most cases, the Education Department pays the guarantee agency 100% of the balance on defaulted loans. But that rate can drop as low as 80% if the guarantee agency's payments on defaulted loans exceed 9% of the agency's insured loans. HEAF's problem is that it guaranteed a large volume of loans to students in proprietary schools. More than one-third of the borrowers who attend proprietary schools, which train beauticians, mechanics and other trades, default on their loans. As a result, HEAF is receiving a government reimbursement of 80% on defaulted loans on which it's paying banks and other lenders 100%.

At issue is how much the federal government is going to reimburse guarantee agencies -- and perhaps the lenders as well -- for the loans in default. At a hearing before the education panel of the Senate Labor Committee Friday, Education Secretary Lauro Cavazos noted that the federal government has no legal obligation to banks and other lenders to make sure they are reimbursed 100% on defaulted loans. He said, however, that he was "optimistic that we will have in place a way to keep the guarantees sound" within the next few weeks. He gave no details of the current negotiations.

As an alternative to the proposals before the government, some U.S. officials suggest that the Education Department might take over the HEAF portfolio, as well as its modern computer operations in St. Paul, Minn., in some sort of undefined conservator capacity. It isn't clear, however, whether this is a real possibility or just a negotiating ploy to encourage USAF and Sallie Mae to consider making changes in their proposals.

So far, government and industry officials have assumed that after an orderly transition, HEAF would cease to exist. But at a news conference Friday, HEAF Chairman Richard Hawk, who complained that he had been excluded from recent negotiations, said he may make his own proposal for keeping HEAF alive. He declined to give any details.
