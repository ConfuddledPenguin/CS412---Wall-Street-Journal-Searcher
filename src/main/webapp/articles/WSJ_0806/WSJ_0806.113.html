Who's News: Central Capital's Chairman, Chief Unexpectedly Quits

  • Author: Suzanne McGee
  • Date: 08/06/90

TORONTO -- The chairman and chief executive officer of Central Capital Corp., Peter Cole, unexpectedly resigned from the financial services and real estate concern to "seek other opportunities." Thomas Hodgson, vice president of Central Capital, said the board hadn't had an opportunity to appoint a successor to Mr. Cole, 53 years old, who had been chief executive for five years.

Mr. Hodgson said he wasn't aware of Mr. Cole's plans or the specific reasons for his resignation. "There certainly weren't any corporate developments that triggered this," he said. "If there had been any problems at the company, he wouldn't have left."

Mr. Cole said in a statement that he oversaw the building of Central Capital into a major player in the Canadian financial services industry and that he thought it was "an appropriate time to move on." He also said he will remain a director of Central Capital and be a consultant to the company. Mr. Cole couldn't be reached to elaborate.

During Mr. Cole's tenure as chief executive, Mr. Hodgson said, the company's own assets grew from 3.5 billion Canadian dollars (US$3.04 billion) to C$16 billion, with another C$20 billion in fiduciary assets under management. This aggressive growth has made Central Capital one of the major non-bank participants in Canada's financial services industry.

Among other acquisitions, Mr. Cole oversaw the C$420 million purchase of Trader's Group Ltd. in 1987, which gave Central Capital control of what now is Central Guaranty Trust Co. and Canadian General Insurance Co.

But the aggressive expansion left the company with a debt of about C$1.4 million by the end of 1989, which resulted in one of the company's two major shareholders, the financier Reuben Cohen, taking the unusual step of publicly challenging Mr. Cole at the annual meeting in April to improve Central Capital's performance. Mr. Cohen voiced concern about the company's debt and said he wanted to see its net income increase from C$92.2 million in 1989 to $200 million by 1993.

In the first quarter, Central Capital earned C$11.1 million, or 10 Canadian cents a share, down from C$28.2 million, or 34 Canadian cents a share, a year earlier.

Mr. Hodgson said Leonard Ellen, Central Capital vice chairman and, with Mr. Cohen, one of the major shareholders, will step in to run the company on an interim basis as the board's executive committee chairman. Messrs. Cohen and Ellen together hold a 60.5% voting stake in Central Capital.

In trading on the Toronto Stock Exchange Friday, Central Capital common shares closed at C$8.50, down 25 Canadian cents, and Class A shares closed at C$6.50, down 38 Canadian cents.
