Aeritalia Seeks To Expand, Find New Markets --- Italian Concern Is Working On Buying de Havilland And Building Bigger Jet

  • Author: Guy Collins
  • Date: 08/06/90

The Italian aerospace group Aeritalia S.p.A., faced with an uncertain outlook for defense-related sales, is driving to expand its commuter-aircraft business and develop markets in other sectors of civilian aviation. Following the signing of its first contract with the Airbus Industrie consortium earlier this year, Rome-based Aeritalia is negotiating jointly with Aerospatiale S.A. of France to buy the troubled commuter-plane maker de Havilland Aircraft of Canada Ltd., a unit of Boeing Co.

The Italian state-owned group also is working with Aerospatiale on studies for a 100-seat regional jetliner to complement the smaller ATR-42 and ATR-72 turboprop commuter planes that the two companies already build in a 50-50 joint venture, Avions de Transport Regional.

For the moment it is the de Havilland talks that top the agenda. Seattle-based Boeing bought the company from the Canadian government for $112 million in 1986 and has since invested hundreds of millions of dollars in an unsuccessful attempt to turn it around. Now, Aeritalia and Aerospatiale have a chance to snap up a major competitor and gain access to a wider market.

"Today it is certainly important to have a large share of the market . . . The ATR consortium already has 23% of the world market, and if we add de Havilland we'll have 45%," Fausto Cereti, Aeritalia's managing director, said in an interview in Rome.

The ATR consortium was set up in 1982, initially to build the 48-seat, $9.2 million turboprop ATR-42 plane. The consortium later added a larger version, the 66-seat, $12 million ATR-72. Between them, the two aircraft have won a total of 435 orders and options from 45 customers, with 185 planes delivered so far.

De Havilland is competing in a similar market with its three versions of the Dash 8. The two types currently flying seat as many as 39 and 54 passengers, respectively, while de Havilland is planning to build a new series seating 65 to 70 people.

"We have an outline accord on the eventual purchase of de Havilland by Aerospatiale and Aeritalia. But we have to see whether the conditions are acceptable for the shareholders of Boeing, Aeritalia and Aerospatiale and the Canadian government," Mr. Cereti said. News of the talks has provoked a stormy debate in the Canadian press, with critics contending the European companies are more interested in eliminating a competitor than developing the company.

"If we buy de Havilland, we'll see what products to develop," Mr. Cereti said, refusing to be drawn on specific plans for the company.

While pursuing the de Havilland acquisition to broaden its commuter plane sales, the 100-seat regional jet project is another angle Aeritalia is pursuing to deepen its penetration of the civilian market.

"The demand is there in the market. There are many airlines asking us if we can supply a 100-seater plane," Mr. Cereti said. But he added that the specifics of any eventual production model would depend on customer requirements.

Aeritalia's interest in acquiring joint control of de Havilland is part of the group's wider strategy to become a world market leader, not only in commuter planes but also in other sectors of civilian aviation, particularly the refurbishing and maintenance of aircraft and avionics.

Defense-sector sales accounted for nearly 60% of Aeritalia's 1989 consolidated revenue of 2.4 trillion lire ($2.06 billion). With defense budgets under pressure in Italy and the West following the disintegration of the communist regimes, the group is anxious to reduce its reliance on defense contracts to about 50% of sales within the next couple of years.

As part of this aim it is about to embark on a major corporate restructuring, merging with Selenia S.p.A., another state-owned group in the electronics and aerospace sector. Both companies are owned by Finmeccanica S.p.A., the engineering holding company for the giant state industrial conglomerate Istituto per la Ricostruzione Industriale. But despite past cooperation, the concerns have until now operated as separate corporate entities.

"It's the sum of two forces who already work together . . . It's a question of putting the right label on a bottle of wine that has already matured," Mr. Cereti said.

But analysts, while recognizing the company's success in winning contracts in a competitive market, have been somewhat critical of its overall business performance.

"It's true that Aeritalia, according to what they say, has had a very good performance in terms of orders, but that hasn't translated into any reasonable profit," said Gabriele Muzio, analyst for SBC Stockbroking in London.

Consolidated net profit attributable to the company fell to L60.4 billion last year from L77 billion in 1988, despite a rise in orders to L5.89 trillion from L4.89 trillion.
