Economy: Jobless Rate Rises to 5.5%; Hiring Slumps --- July Unemployment Figure Hit a Two-Year High; Payrolls Fell by 219,000

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 08/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The sharp rise in the unemployment rate and the slump in hiring in July confirm that the U.S. economy is slowing substantially. The civilian jobless rate, which has barely moved one way or the other for nearly two years, jumped 0.3 percentage point to 5.5% in July, the highest rate in two years, the Labor Department said. It hadn't risen that much in one month since February 1986.

Payrolls, dragged down by the dismissal of 160,000 temporary census workers, fell 219,000 in July. The number of jobs in private industry dropped 45,000 as construction employment declined sharply, factory payrolls continued to shrink and the growth in private service industries came to an abrupt halt.

In the past six months, private industry has added workers at a rate of only 66,000 a month, the slowest rate since the recession of the early 1980s. "The services industry has been the major engine of job growth during the current economic expansion," said Janet Norwood, commissioner of labor statistics. "But that engine seems to have sputtered in July."

Although the U.S. economy has been exhibiting signs of sluggishness for months, the latest employment figures were bleaker than most economists and investors had anticipated. "The economy is deteriorating faster than expected," said economist Elliott Platt of Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp.

The new data dispel any lingering hope that the U.S. economy is bouncing back from the lethargic growth of the first few months of the year -- and indicates it is now growing even more slowly than the Federal Reserve had intended when it braked the economy in an effort to restrain inflation.

The Fed, which last month pushed interest rates down a bit for the first time in more than six months, is now likely to ease more aggressively to try to head off a recession -- or curtail one, if it has already begun.

A disproportionate share of the increase in unemployment in July was among teen-agers, the Labor Department said. The jobless rate among teen-agers rose 2.2 percentage points to 16.3%, about a percentage point higher than it has averaged in the past two years.

"While we cannot say for certain why teen-agers had more difficulty in the labor market this summer," Mrs. Norwood said, "we do know that job growth in the retail trade and services industries, which employ many teen-agers, has slowed considerably."

The Labor Department figures highlighted the divergent economic conditions among regions of the country. In Massachusetts, the most populous state in recessionary New England, the jobless rate hit 6.1% in July, up from 4.3% a year earlier. In New York, it rose to 5.3%, up from 4.8% a year earlier. But in California, the unemployment rate was 5.1%, up only slightly from June and down from 5.2% in July 1989.

The jobless rate rose in July even though the labor force -- all those working or looking for work -- shrank a bit. The surprisingly slow growth of the labor force in the past several months has helped keep the unemployment rate steady at the lowest levels in 15 years as the economy's growth rate slowed.

In the past 12 months, the labor force has grown by only 660,000, the Labor Department said. In the past five years, the labor force has grown by an average of two million from one July to the next. Although the shrinking number of young people entering the labor force is a major factor, economists say it doesn't fully explain the slow labor force growth.

A measure of the national employment rate that includes the armed forces rose to 5.4% in July from 5.1% in June.

The Labor Department's survey of employers, closely watched by economists as among the most reliable guides to the economy, showed hardly any bright spots.

Construction employment dropped 50,000 in July and has declined more than 100,000 since last fall. Manufacturing employment was down another 7,000, a smaller drop than in recent months. Factories employed 325,000 fewer workers in July than at the recent peak in March 1989. The health-services industry, which has added an average of 50,000 jobs a month in the first half of the year, added only half that many in July.

All the figures are adjusted for usual seasonal fluctuations.
