Hubbard of AFG Industries Buys Stakes In Owners of Thoroughbred Race Track

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 08/06/90

Randall D. Hubbard, a horse-racing enthusiast and chairman of AFG Industries Inc., acquired 9.9% stakes in two companies that own and operate the Hollywood Park race track in Inglewood, Calif., and said he may seek their control. Hollywood Park Realty Enterprises Inc. owns the thoroughbred race track and 347 acres of surrounding land roughly five miles east of the Los Angeles International Airport. Hollywood Park Operating Co. operates the track.

In national over-the-counter trading Friday, the companies' shares, which are paired for trading, rose 75 cents to $30.

Mr. Hubbard said that "if you took a survey of patrons and employees I think you'd find a lot of dissatisfaction with the park. . . . I think the park can be brought back to being one of the premier tracks in the country."

Mr. Hubbard said he wants board representation initially, but that he hasn't ruled out a proxy contest. "I haven't seen their business plan. We're willing to listen. If we're satisfied, then representation on the board would be fine."

Hollywood Park Realty's board has seven members and Hollywood Park Operating's board has 11 members.

In a statement, Hollywood Park said "it makes no sense to turn control of the company over to someone with no experience managing a major thoroughbred race track." Mr. Hubbard, however, owns Ruidoso Downs in Ruidoso, N.M., Woodlands Race Track in Kansas City, and is a partner in the Los Alamitos race track, which Hollywood Park sold last year.

Hollywood Park has reported three consecutive years of losses, stemming in part from its disastrous attempt to expand into real estate development with the purchase of the Los Alamitos track and adjacent land in Cypress, Calif., several years ago. Last year, the Hollywood Park companies had a combined loss of about $11 million, compared with losses of $10 million in 1988 and $6.3 million in 1987.

Hollywood Park management "drained all the cash out of the company" to pay the interest on the loan used to acquire Los Alamitos, said Kenneth Campbell, editor of Realty Stock Review, a Montale, N.J., investment newsletter. Development was blocked by a zoning dispute and Hollywood Park finally sold Los Alamitos last year to restore Hollywood Park's financial stability.

Hollywood Park said second-quarter profit rose 87% and that recently implemented initiatives promise future profits. In June, the companies paid their first dividend on common stock in nearly three years.

The initiatives include experimental night races and an expanded program to simulcast races at other race tracks and legal betting parlors, said a spokesman for the companies. The track has undergone a major renovation in recent years and was the site of the national Breeder's Cup race in 1984 and 1987, he said.

In a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Mr. Hubbard said he acquired 391,882 paired shares of Hollywood Park Realty and Hollywood Park Operating, including 271,983 shares bought in the open market from June 19 to July 31 for $26.50 to $30.52 each.

Mr. Hubbard may have an ally in Denver investor Thomas W. Gamel, who owns a 5.6% stake. Last year, Mr. Gamel threatened a proxy fight and called for the ouster of Marjorie L. Everett, chairman and chief executive officer of Hollywood Park Operating. After he was offered a board seat, Mr. Gamel withdrew his rival slate and agreed not to acquire more stock as long as he is a director.

Mr. Gamel declined to comment on Mr. Hubbard's actions. Mr. Gamel, Mr. Hubbard and a third investor offered to buy Hollywood Park in 1986 for about $133 million, or $35 a share. The offer was rejected by the board.

Last month, a Beverly Hills, Calif., partnership headed by Harry Ornest increased its stake to 9.9%. Mr. Ornest is an executive with Hollywood Park Realty.

At Dec. 31, according to an SEC filing, Mrs. Everett held an 11.1% stake in the realty company and a 6.2% stake in the operating company; other directors who own paired shares include investor and former talk-show host Merv Griffin, 1.3%, and investor Marvin Davis, 4.8%. Mr. Davis resigned as a director in May.

Two years ago, Mr. Hubbard led an investor group that took AFG Industries private and moved the glassmaking concern to Fort Worth, Texas, from Irvine, Calif. He also was involved in an unsuccessful bid by AFG and other partners in 1986 for Lear Siegler Inc. The aerospace and industrial concern later was taken private by Forstmann Little & Co. in a $2.1 million leveraged buy-out.
