Abreast of the Market: Market's Sell-Off Hammers the Bullish Case for Stocks

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 08/06/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks took a hard fall Friday. But the Dow Jones Industrial Average remains above 2800, and that's still too high, many strategists say. Far more than a mere correction, Friday's spasmodic sell-off in stocks was the first significant re-evaluation of stock prices since October 1989. The ebullient assumptions of a "soft landing" -- a slowly growing economy with moderating inflation -- seemed dashed.

Now the interrogation begins: Investors will review every stock in their portfolios and ask if it's worth the risk of owning.

"I am going to be rigorous," says James Craig, manager of the $1.2 billion Janus Fund. "Whether it's Barbie Dolls with Mattel or TV revenue at Capital Cities/ABC, we will re-evaluate everything."

Because of last week's events, he adds, "my complacency with a rocking-along slow growth has been called into question. And my complacency with low inflation has been called into question."

For three weeks, the caution flag has been waving over the stock market. Dozens of companies delivered surprisingly soft second-quarter earnings, and gave analysts no cheer about the second half of the year. Recent economic reports, including the one Friday showing unemployment rose in July, point to weakening growth.

But this message went unheeded by many stock investors. After last year's 31.7% gain in Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, most money managers were more inclined to take the risk of racing with the market than to drop out and preserve their capital. Investors gave into what Henry Van der Eb, manager of the Mathers Fund, calls "seductive rationalizations."

But today the naysayers are standing on the high ground, and they foresee even lower stock prices.

"We've gone from, `Don't worry, be happy,' to a more realistic interpretation" of stocks' worth, says Abby Joseph Cohen, the chief strategist at Barclays de Zoete Wedd. The first hammer blow of this shift in perception could hit the market this week as investors re-evaluate both earnings estimates and the multiple of per-share profits that stock prices should properly command.

The S&P index closed Friday at a price 15.91 times the 500 companies' aggregate earnings over the past year. That price/earnings ratio is still too high, says Carmine Grigoli, chief strategist at First Boston. "Pricing the market at 16 times earnings is a ridiculous valuation" given the more immediate risks of a recession now, he says. A more reasonable valuation would be 13, in his opinion, and to get there stocks would have to fall about 13% from current levels.

Ms. Cohen doesn't think investors are prepared for a declining stock market. "People have got it into their heads that bear markets are over very quickly," she says, like the 1987 crash. But back then the economy was strong, while now it is limping at a near-recessionary pace and an inflation shock is threatened by higher oil prices. "We are likely to see a gradual erosion" in stock prices over several months, she says.

Most large institutional investors have to be at least partially invested in stocks, in good times and bad. The marginal buyers, who can have a big impact on stock prices, tend to be the "asset allocators," who have the flexibility to dart among stocks, bonds, and cash, and the millions of small investors. One sure sign that a lengthy correction is under way would be if either group bailed out of stocks to any significant degree.

So far there's no evidence of such moves, although as the stock market fell on Friday mutual fund companies were swamped with telephone calls. Early Friday afternoon, Vanguard Group called up its "Swiss Army," a band of its money market fund managers, company lawyers and accountants who are drafted to help handle customers' calls during busy periods.

"Anybody who can answer the phone is doing so," reported a spokesman for Twentieth Century Investors, where the call volume on Friday was close to double its normal level. "Whenever you have this sort of day, all kinds of {investors} come out of the woodwork."

At Baltimore's T. Rowe Price Associates, the call volume was running over 25% above normal. Many investors switched out of stock funds and into money market funds.

But some investors took the Kuwait crisis as a buying opportunity. The T. Rowe Price New Era Fund, a natural resource stock fund heavy in precious metals and energy shares, enjoyed strong sales on Friday.

At Financial Programs in Denver, investors switched out of the fund group's two money market funds and into Financial Strategic Energy Portfolio. "That tells me that since 1987 people have become a lot shrewder and aren't so quick to panic," says a spokesman for Financial Programs.

As for asset allocators, these investors have generally increased their stock investments through the summer, says James Carmack, a consultant who tracks them. He says he talked to one such investor Friday who decided to remain fully invested in stocks.

Such investors don't suddenly change their minds about everything, which is why bear markets don't strike like lightning. Prolonged declines in stock prices typically consist of slow capitulation by one investor after another.

Or it could be that small investors and asset allocators are looking way beyond today's bad news. Jack McCarthy, manager of the $3.6 billion Affiliated Fund says there is a case to be made for higher stock prices. Certainly a correction is taking place, he acknowledges, and he even expects a six-month recession in this year's second half.

But just as investors are giving up on stocks, it might be time to buy. Mr. McCarthy says weak economic growth will prompt the Federal Reserve to pump money into the economy, "and the stock market will go up." Meanwhile, rising unemployment should moderate inflation concerns.

Therefore, he concludes, "We are not in a bear market."

Friday Market Activity

Stock prices plunged in a torrent of selling sparked by nervousness over Iraq's military actions and evidence that the U.S. economy may be heading for a recession.

But the market took comfort in a Soviet statement that Iraq would soon withdraw from Kuwait, and prices finished well above their session lows.

After being down more than 120 points, the Dow Jones Industrial Average finished with a loss of 54.95 at 2809.65. That was the industrials' lowest close since May 11.

Market players dumped stocks across the board, traders said. Oil issues, which shot up on news of the Iraqi invasion, ran into selling even though oil prices continued climbing.

Declining issues routed advancers on the New York Stock Exchange by about 1,457 to 264. Big Board trading swelled to 292,360,000 million shares, the busiest since October's "mini-crash."

For the week, the Dow Jones industrials fell 88.86 points, or 3.1%, the second largest weekly point loss this year. Standard & Poor's 500-stock index lost 2.4%, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index dropped 2.3% and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index fell 2.6%.

Concerns about recession surfaced in the market after the July employment report proved considerably weaker than anticipated. And reports that Iraq was amassing troops near the Saudi Arabian border also sent shock waves through the market, traders said.

The Big Board's new restrictions on program trading, which require index-arbitrage traders to wait for stock prices to rise from previous trades before executing the stock side of their transactions, went into effect for the first time at 9:53 a.m. EDT, after the industrials showed a loss of 50 points. This "circuit breaker" mechanism was approved last month by the Securities and Exchange Commission.

Although traders said index-arbitrage selling was a factor in the market's downturn, the Big Board curbs "indicated that the index arbs couldn't be selling aggressively," said Christopher Pedersen, head of trading at Twenty-First Securities.

Another "circuit breaker" took effect in the afternoon when September futures on the S&P 500 index showed a loss of 12 points. Starting at 1:19 p.m. EDT, a special Big Board computer file known as the "sidecar" handled program trading orders in the underlying stocks for a five-minute period.

The rally in oil stocks faltered. Exxon added 1/8 to 53 3/8 in volume of 3.3 million shares, while USX, which owns Marathon Oil, closed up 1/4 at 34 7/8 and was the Big Board's most active issue with 5.1 million shares changing hands.

But Chevron skidded 1 5/8 to 78 3/4; Texaco fell 1 1/4 to 64 5/8; Mobil eased 3/8 to 66 7/8; Amoco dropped 3/4 to 57 1/2; and Unocal went down 7/8 to 32 3/8. Among oil services issues, Baker Hughes slid 1 to 32 3/8; Schlumberger dropped 1 1/4 to 67 1/2; Halliburton eased 1 5/8 to 55 3/4; and McDermott International lost 2 1/2 to 32.

Raytheon soared 3 1/8 to 63 7/8. Analysts told The Wall Street Journal that the company is either planning or proceeding with sales of its Patriot defense missile system to Saudi Arabia, Israel and Italy.

General Dynamics, which is selling tanks to Saudi Arabia, rose 3/4 to 29. Other defense stocks also bucked the market's fall: Lockheed rose 3/8 to 28 3/8; McDonnell Douglas went up 1/2 to 44 1/2; Northrop climbed 1/2 to 15 1/8, and E-Systems rose 5/8 to 27 1/2.

Boise Cascade jumped 4 7/8 to 36 3/8 amid rumors that the company may receive a takeover offer. Prices ranging from $50 to $70 a share were cited in the speculation, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. The company and International Paper, a rumored suitor, both declined comment; International Paper advanced 1 5/8 to 58 3/8.

Selected regional bank stocks also showed strength. Banc One gained 1 to 27 3/4; Signet Banking rose 1 5/8 to 16 1/8 and Bank of Boston added 5/8 to 10 3/4.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 5.52 to 346.63, bringing its loss for the week to 2.5%. Volume Friday totaled 26,650,000.

Put warrants on the Nikkei average of 225 Japanese stocks rose sharply, indicating that market players expect the Tokyo stock market's plunge in the wake of the Iraq-Kuwait conflict to continue.

PaineWebber's warrants added 1/2 to 4 1/4 as more than 1.2 million were traded. Salomon's January 1993 warrants rose 3/8 to 10 7/8; Eksportfinans' warrants gained 3/8 to 4 1/8, and Denmark's went up 3/8 to 11 7/8.
