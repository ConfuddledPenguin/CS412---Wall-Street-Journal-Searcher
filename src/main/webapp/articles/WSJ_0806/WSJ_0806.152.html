Marketing & Media: Turner Plans to Back '94 Goodwill Games, Despite Current Loss

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/06/90

SEATTLE -- Despite big losses and lower-than-anticipated TV ratings at the Goodwill Games, Ted Turner said he is committed to sponsoring the games in 1994 in Leningrad. But Mr. Turner, chairman and chief executive officer of Turner Broadcasting System Inc., acknowledged that the company's board might veto the idea.

Speaking on the final weekend of the self-styled Olympiad, Mr. Turner said the 17-day event had scored a rating of about 3.2, meaning the games were watched by 3.2% of the 40-million-or-so households that could receive the broadcasts.

That's roughly 60% of the viewership that Atlanta-based Turner Broadcasting had promised to advertisers. This lack of viewers means the company will have to provide free air time -- perhaps as much as $18 million worth, according to executives close to the company -- to make up the difference.

Such "make goods," as the free time is called, could push Turner Broadcasting's loss on the Seattle games to as much as $40 million. But Mr. Turner tried to sound upbeat in assessing the event. "In my view, these games were more successful" than the 1986 Goodwill Games in Moscow, he said, citing improved TV viewership and a fivefold increase in advertising sales.

Looking to 1994, Mr. Turner said he would like to support a third Goodwill Games in Leningrad. But on the question of whether Turner Broadcasting's board might have other thoughts, Mr. Turner said: "It's a possibility. Anything is a possibility."

Indeed, the games might be able to survive the company's departure. Nikolai Rusak of the Soviet Union's Goskomsport athletic authority insisted the games would go on in Leningrad in 1994, with or without Turner Broadcasting.
