Quarterly Review of Profits: Second Quarter, 1990 --- Companies' Net Income Fell 11%, And More of the Same Is Expected

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 08/06/90

The continued weakness of the economy was reflected in another poor profits showing in the second quarter. On a year-to-year basis, it was the fourth consecutive decline, and analysts see little hope for improvement in the current quarter. In the second quarter, net income of 639 major corporations fell 11% from a year earlier, a Wall Street Journal survey finds. This compared with an 18% drop in this year's first quarter. After-tax earnings on continuing operations dropped 7% in the second quarter.

Lynn Reaser, vice president and senior economist of First Interstate Bank of Los Angeles, thinks the only chance for improved profits is a stronger move toward easier money by the Federal Reserve. "If that happens we might even get a modest year-to-year gain in after-tax profits by the fourth quarter. At the moment, however, earnings are still being squeezed by rising wage-and-benefit costs."

Analysts at Goldman Sachs, a brokerage firm, see indications that companies are already bolstering profit margins, mainly by holding down labor costs through slower hiring.

In an advance report late last month, the Commerce Department estimated that the inflation-adjusted gross national product rose at an annual rate of only 1.2% in the second quarter. That's below the level most economists had been predicting for GNP, which is the total value of the nation's output of goods and services. At last report the 52 economists interviewed monthly by Blue Chip Economic Indicators, a Sedona, Ariz., newsletter, had been looking for a 1.8% growth rate in the second quarter.

"Below-average economic growth will hold profits essentially flat over much of the 1990-92 period," say economists at the Lexington, Mass., consulting firm of DRI/McGraw-Hill. "Although no recession is expected soon, neither is a boom."

Most analysts, however, expect economic growth to begin to show some improvement late this year and in 1991. The Blue Chip group at last report was expecting GNP to rise 2.2% next year, not exactly a roaring upturn but enough to be of some help to profits.

Among major groups, earnings fell for autos, banks, thrifts and steel in the second quarter, while oil companies and the computer industry posted mixed performances.

A weaker U.S. economy, strong Japanese rivals and troubles overseas combined to batter profits for the Big Three U.S. auto makers. General Motors, Ford and Chrysler all blamed weak demand and discounts that averaged about $1,000 a vehicle for slumping U.S. auto profits. Despite massive cost-cutting, the Big Three can't simultaneously maintain earnings growth and charge prices that a soft market will bear. Meanwhile, Ford and GM had big losses in Brazil, where economic upheaval is hobbling many manufacturers' operations.

Detroit won't get much relief during the rest of the year. The Big Three are bargaining with the United Auto Workers union to replace national contracts that expire Sept. 14. If talks break down, a strike could cut output and hurt suppliers, too. Meanwhile, Ford and Chrysler have signaled plans to widen profit margins by boosting prices. But analysts say big increases may not stick in a recession-wary market flooded with competitively priced Japanese cars.

Chrysler faces a special problem. Output of its profitable minivans will slump this summer as the plants that build the vehicles close for major retooling.

Bank earnings were weak because of continued high levels of problem loans, especially for commercial real estate. Manufacturers Hanover took a $100 million onetime charge for the elimination of about 1,400 jobs. At several banks, including J.P. Morgan, strong trading results helped offset otherwise flat corporate finance activities. A few banks, including Norwest and Banc One, managed to post healthy gains in earnings.

Analysts expect banks' nonperforming loans to continue to rise in the third quarter, keeping up the pressure on earnings. However, the rate of increase in problem loans is expected to slow somewhat.

Thrifts were pounded by real estate problems, which increasingly reflected weakness in California. That's troubling to many big thrifts, because California has been the last major real estate market to withstand the localized real estate slumps plaguing the Southwest, Northeast and other regions.

CalFed said its loss in large part reflected deterioration in California markets. HomeFed, after special credit reviews, added $234 million to its reserve for loan losses in the second quarter.

Other thrifts avoided the trouble. Great Western boosted its return on assets to 0.82% from 0.58% a year earlier. Golden West, bouyed by some of the lowest costs in the business, saw its return on assets jump to 0.95% from 0.86%.

Steelmakers, faced with higher costs and lower prices and shipments, saw profits plunge from last year's robust levels.

Nucor, one of the few steelmakers with improved earnings, had higher shipments on all key products and wasn't squeezed by new union contracts signed by most major steelmakers last year. Along with those higher labor costs, Bethlehem Steel was hampered by scheduled maintenance programs at its prize Sparrows Point plant, which significantly reduced capacity and forced it to buy steel from other producers to meet customer orders. Armco, the only major steelmaker to report a loss, took about $101 million in unusual charges, including $56 million relating to a litigation settlement, while Inland Steel Industries was penalized by start-up costs from a high-tech steel finishing mill.

Still, steelmakers -- which had expected a soft 1990 -- have been buoyed by recent strength in automotive orders and the partial realization of price increases.

Oil profits were mixed depending on the strength of companies' gasoline refining and marketing operations, which in some cases posted record results. Sharply lower crude prices reduced exploration and production profits almost across the board. But those lower prices for oil -- the feedstock for gasoline -- combined with stable pump prices to propel big increases in refining earnings. Petrochemical results declined sharply as a glut continues. Exxon's year-earlier quarter included an $850 million provision to clean up the Alaskan oil spill. Mobil had one-time gains and losses in the 1990 and 1989 quarters; without them earnings would have declined 18%.

With the surge in oil prices already this quarter, major oil companies expect to report much stronger results, mostly reflecting the run-up since the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait.

The computer industry's results in the latest quarter were again mixed, as competition remained cutthroat. Some companies also reported slowing demand in parts of Europe, which would cause big problems, because the European market has been making up for weakness in U.S. sales for the past few years.

IBM did better than expected, but, even there, there were worrying signs. Mainframe sales slowed, and IBM may face problems starting in the second half as it tries to manage the transition to a new generation of the big computers. Digital Equipment reported its first loss because of a $400 million charge related to a plan to cut 5,000 to 6,000 jobs. It was also one of those companies seeing weakness in Europe. NCR and Amdahl had strong quarters, but Unisys and Hewlett-Packard posted lackluster results, and Tandem had problems in Europe.

Cray's results showed their normal sharp fluctuation, which reflects the timing of supercomputer sales.

The spotty results are likely to continue for the next quarter or two, especially if IBM does run into problems on its mainframes or if the problems in Europe spread to more computer makers.

Earnings for most aluminum producers were hurt by slumps in the automotive and construction markets. Maxxam's consolidated earnings include forest products and real estate, and also reflect above-market prices for aluminum under long-term contracts and increased production from a Jamaican refinery.

Copper company earnings were generally lower because of a 3%-4% decline in the average spot price of copper. Cyprus Minerals earnings were also affected by a large write-down for its coal operations. Magma Copper, however, boosted profit by increasing output.

Gold prices changed moderately compared with the year-earlier quarter, but the cost of recovering gold increased. Newmont Mining slashed costs and increased its gold output. Net income at Newmont Gold, the company's primary business unit, rose 35%. Homestake Mining's earnings increased because of an asset sale.

Lingering overcapacity continued to hurt forest-product companies. Newsprint and uncoated white paper producers managed price increases in the quarter, but markets and prices for linerboard and containerboard weakened. Pulp prices also declined from a year earlier.

Several companies blamed operational difficulties for poor earnings. Some analysts believe the cost of raw materials has peaked. But there are growing worries in the Pacific Northwest over restrictions brought by the designation of the spotted owl as a threatened species. Moreover, analysts said timber sales will begin to reflect the drop in housing starts as the year proceeds.

Dow Chemical, Quantum, Vista and Carbide were hurt by the continuing slump in prices for basic plastics and other commodity chemicals. Dow Chemical's results also reflected higher manufacturing costs because of prolonged plant maintenance, a strike in Brazil and power outages. More diversified companies, such as Du Pont and Monsanto, fared better, though their sales of agricultural chemicals were hampered by adverse weather. Du Pont also noted lower demand in the U.S. for apparel and carpet fibers. Lubrizol benefited from higher prices for its specialty chemicals. Hercules had higher interest expenses, and its effective tax rate rose to 49% from 38%. Analysts expect continuing low commodity-chemical prices this year, given additions in production capacity.

In the oil field sector, a pickup of more than 20% in U.S. drilling activity along with steady overseas activity boosted re sults. The giant equipment and service companies reaped the benefits of several years of cost-cutting and industry consolidation. A number of companies also said they were able to make price increases that stuck. And drilling companies narrowed their losses as restructuring is paying off. This sector could show steady improvement if expected increases in drilling activity materialize.

With prices and volume low for the quarter, natural-gas pipeline companies felt the pinch. Customers in the Northeast switched to other fuels, while markets in the Midwest continue to be overrun with competitors. Those and other factors combined to hammer Panhandle Eastern, which slashed its dividend 60% and expects more belt-tightening because of environmental cleanup costs and adverse regulatory rulings. That company has tabled a number of expansion projects and could see a further drop in volume when certain rates to the Northeast go up in December. Meanwhile, Burlington Resource's pipeline unit saw a 4% increase in operating profit.

Coal companies posted higher earnings for the quarter largely from stronger prices and volume. Another reason for the improvement was that last year's results were hurt by wildcat strikes staged in support of the United Mine Workers union against Pittston.

Slow industrial freight shipments pared rail earnings at Consolidated Rail, Norfolk Southern and Santa Fe Pacific. But Burlington Northern benefited from strong coal shipments, and CSX reported improved results at its rail, barge and container shipping units. Excluding gains of $62 million from sale of subsidiaries in the 1989 quarter, CSX's earnings from continuing operations rose 20%.

Trucking-company earnings were hurt by overcapacity and higher fuel and labor costs. Consolidated Freightways said losses at its Emery air-freight unit more than offset earnings improvement at its truck units. A 3% rate increase implemented in June is expected to boost third-quarter results for carriers that handle less than full-truckload shipments.

The slump in second-quarter net at American President, an ocean, rail and truck transporter, reflected a decline in international shipment volumes, rate pressures and increased equipment costs.

The air-freight industry performed about as expected, with Airborne Freight continuing its torrid growth and Federal Express still having problems overseas. Airborne, known as the industry's lowest-cost producer, is still benefiting from problems at Consolidated Freightways' Emory unit, and may also be eating into Federal Express's U.S. business. Federal Express continues to post international losses of millions of dollars a month, and is facing lower Asian demand and slower U.S. volume growth as well. Airborne is expected to continue its heady growth, while there's no end in sight for Federal's woes.

In transportation equipment, the downturn in the market for big trucks continued to squeeze profits, and there's no consensus on when a rebound might start. Navistar sees an 18% decline in industry shipments of heavy trucks and a 4% decline in medium trucks for its fiscal year, which ends Oct. 31. Cummins Engine says third-quarter production of heavy trucks is expected to fall 10% from a year earlier; it expects a loss for the period. But Eaton, which makes truck transmissions and axles, sees "indications" the heavy truck market will improve by year end.

Profit gains continued for heavy machinery, but a steep decline at giant Caterpillar dragged down overall results. Brazil's stiff anti-inflation and economic-reform plan was partly behind the tumbling profit at Caterpillar. Troubles in Brazil also curbed the profit gain at Clark Equipment. Sales of Caterpillar's construction equipment slowed in the U.S. and other industrialized nations. For the full year, the company expects "substantially lower" profit than in 1989. While farm-equipment sales for companies such as Deere are likely to continue robust, there are signs of a slowdown for other heavy machinery. Clark says "emerging softness in world markets" could reduce its second-half earnings. Deere is "cau tious" on the outlook for sales of its construction equipment in North America.

CONSUMER, CYCLICAL

2ND QTR. 1990 2ND QTR. 1989 % CHG.

5,937,840 8,619,109 - 31

Housing starts fell again in the second quarter, particularly in the northeastern U.S., reflecting weakening demand and a liquidity crunch affecting small builders brought on by the S&L debacle. In California, where homebuilding had remained relatively healthy, concern over anticipated defense cuts has stalled consumer confidence and started to weigh heavily on new construction. Standard Pacific and Kaufman & Broad said the weakening California market contributed to earnings declines. Analysts predict a continued drop in homebuilding through year end.

Retailers are likely to report mixed results for the July quarter. But some companies posting higher profits are expected to show smaller increases than in recent quarters.

Sears Roebuck, which operates on a calendar year, already reported a 39% decline in net income; but sluggish sales and higher expenses resulted in a 78% profit plunge at the company's core U.S. retail business. Analysts expect J.C. Pen ney to report sharply lower earnings, and Nordstrom and Dayton Hudson are likely to post slightly lower net. U.S. Shoe projects a profit decline because of falling sales at its Lenscrafters stores. Earnings growth is likely to remain robust at Wal-Mart, Dillard, Limited and Gap. K mart is expected to show a small increase in profit.

Hotel and casino companies faced continued overcapacity in the lodging market, as well as new casino competition. Some hotel operators, including Marriott, Motel 6 and La Quinta Motor Inns, reported slight to moderate improvement in operating results. Hilton Hotels experienced mixed results from its hotels and a sharp drop in operating revenue at one of its Las Vegas, Nev., casinos. Golden Nugget's large new Las Vegas casino-resort produced a major improvement in earnings, while Aztar's operating improvement was overshadowed by a provision for a legal judgment against the company. Circus Circus Enterprises' fiscal quarter ended April 30 didn't include results from its big new casino in Las Vegas.

Some analysts worry that sluggishness in consumer spending may rub off on travel and tourism in this quarter.

The restaurant business generally remained soft. Price discounting at fast-food sites was rampant, intensifying competition and squeezing profit margins. McDonald's 10% earnings gain trailed analysts' expectations. The quarter was brighter for casual sit-down places, such as Shoney's.

Casual sit-down restaurants have benefited from the narrowing gap in non-promotional menu prices between midpriced diners and fast-food outlets. For the third quarter, with the same trends intact, Montgomery Securities forecasts a 12% increase in McDonald's per-share earnings and a 44% gain for Shoney's.

Entertainment-industry earnings "are becoming weaker," said Harold Vogel, a first vice president at Merrill Lynch & Co. "The domestic box office is down from a year ago and the cost of making and releasing films is up." Theme-park attendance, he said, "has flattened out for the current quarter." Lisbeth Barron, a vice president of research at S.G. Warburg Securities, said Time Warner was hurt by weakness in magazine earnings and flat performance in its Home Box Office unit. "The record business has slowed dramatically," she added. Paramount's year-earlier results include $70.9 million in after-tax income from discontinued operations.

Demand for outboard engines continued to decline. "Sluggish demand for boats continues. It's more of the same for Outboard Marine and Brunswick," said Mr. Vogel.

In the media sector, television networks stood out with moderate to strong results. CBS was boosted largely by its telecast of the National Basketball Association playoffs and championship game, and by a drop in programming cost from a year earlier. At Capital Cities/ABC, lukewarm results were due to sluggish advertising sales at its television and radio stations and to losses on the new baseball contract at its 80%-owned cable channel, ESPN.

Software makers continued to benefit from strong sales of desktop computers and the growing appetite for more sophisticated business software. Lotus Development posted the biggest profit gain on the strength of broad acceptance of its newest spreadsheet products. Microsoft's net income was boosted by rapid growth in Europe and the initial reaction to its new version of its Windows product. Novell and Oracle Systems were aided by the continued shift to networked computing, which puts a premium on the data base and networking software sold by them.

Communications Satellite inched up after software problems in its Video Entertainment unit offset gains in its International and Systems businesses.
