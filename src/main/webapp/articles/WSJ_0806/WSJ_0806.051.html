Loss of National Intergroup Is $281.7 Million

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 08/06/90

PITTSBURGH -- National Intergroup Inc. posted a $281.7 million loss for the fiscal first quarter ended June 30 as gains in its wholesale drug distribution business failed to offset expected losses from the sale of non-core operations. In the year-earlier quarter, National Intergroup earned $13 million, or 53 cents a share. Revenue rose 16% to $666.8 million from $577.2 million.

The dismal results are the latest bad news to hit the company. Last month, National Intergroup lost three board seats, including one held by Chairman Howard M. Love, in a proxy battle to dissident shareholder Centaur Partners. While still representing a minority on a 10-member board, Centaur Partners wants to liquidate National Intergroup, which has had seven consecutive years of operating losses.

With the stock market already skittish about the Iraq-Kuwait situation, the reported loss of $13.01 a share sent National Intergroup stock tumbling even further. In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange Friday, National Intergroup stock closed at $15.875 a share, down $1 a share.

National Intergroup said earlier that it expected charges from the sales of its Ben Franklin general merchandise chain and Permian Partners L.P. oil operations, but it didn't signal the extent of the write-off. The company created a $239.5 million reserve to cover anticipated losses on the sales of those operations, including $30.3 million in good will relating to the Ben Franklin chain. The company also recorded charges of $26.7 million to close its Pittsburgh headquarters and $14.8 million relating to the sale of most of its interest in National Steel to NKK Corp.

Gregory Drahuschak, an analyst with Butcher & Singer, said no one expected such large write-offs but added that most reflect the discontinuation of losing operations and not an actual cash loss.

The company tried to put its best face forward, noting that FoxMeyer Drug Co. posted a 40% rise in operating profit and that National Intergroup should be profitable in the second quarter. FoxMeyer, which is the only going operation, earned $10 million on sales of $666.8 million, compared with last year's $7.2 million on sales of $577.2 million.

"Though the loss is significant, it clears the decks and allows us to move forward with our plan to focus our attention and resources on the drug distribution business," said Mr. Love. He contended that the sales will "enhance our ability to reduce debt and pay a special dividend or buy back stock."

Centaur, which has questioned the company's commitment to paying shareholder dividends, said it was disappointed in the size of the write-offs. "We remain committed to a timely sale of all of the assets of National Intergroup and distribution of the proceeds to shareholders, as the best means to maximize shareholder values," Centaur said.
