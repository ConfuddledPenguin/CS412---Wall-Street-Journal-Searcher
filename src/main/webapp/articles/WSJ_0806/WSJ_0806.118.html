Technology: Lotus, in Profit Slump, Moves to Reduce Costs With Hiring Freeze, Some Layoffs

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 08/06/90

CAMBRIDGE, Mass. -- Lotus Development Corp., struggling with slumping earnings, has begun a cost-cutting program that includes a hiring freeze and layoffs of as many as 40 people. The layoffs, while small relative to Lotus's 3,100-member work force, indicate management's concern about the company's outlook. In the past, Lotus has occasionally closed small divisions, but employees were generally absorbed elsewhere by the fast-growing software producer.

Revenue is growing more slowly than it used to, so "They're doing everything they can to keep the lid on" costs, said Richard Sherlund, an analyst with Goldman, Sachs & Co. But he warned that with growing competition, Lotus will have to be careful about reducing advertising and marketing spending. "Market share may be more difficult to hold," he said.

In national over-the-counter trading Friday, Lotus shares fell 50 cents to close at $22, amid the market collapse. The stock, which traded at $34 as recently as July 17, has fallen steadily since officials warned that earnings are likely to decline in the current quarter.

By most measures, Lotus isn't overstaffed, analysts said. Jeffrey Tarter, publisher of SoftLetter, an industry newsletter based in Watertown, Mass., said Lotus's revenue per employee last year of $188,000 was above the industry average of $175,000.

But William Bluestein, an analyst with Forrester Research, a market research firm in Cambridge, said, "Their cost structure is out of line, considering they are a mature, single-product company." Lotus continues to get about two-thirds of its revenue from its 1-2-3 spreadsheet, despite repeated efforts to diversify its product line. In the second quarter, earnings more than doubled to $23.5 million, or 54 cents a share, on revenue of $175.2 million, from a year earlier.

Robert Schechter, chief financial officer, played down the significance of the job cuts. He said that through consolidation and re-evaluation of some positions in the marketing area, 17 jobs were eliminated. As many as 20 additional people will be fired, but they will be replaced later by people with different skills, Mr. Schechter said.

In a memo sent to all Lotus managers last month, Mr. Schechter said that through year end, hiring is frozen, with a few exceptions, and that any new hires must be approved at the senior vice presidential level. Even filling vacancies created by attrition will require approvals, he wrote. Only senior vice presidents can authorize hiring search firms. About 10% to 14% of Lotus employees leave annually.

Lotus is trimming in other areas, too. Even purchases of new personal computers now require special approvals. The small army of contract programmers that had been working in the company's spreadsheet division isn't being retained; their work will be done by current employees. And Lotus aims to cut travel and entertainment spending 50% in the second half.

Nevertheless, Lotus will add employees in some areas, including paid customer support, consulting, and sales and support of its Notes software product, Mr. Schechter said. "We continue to see lots of ways to grow this business," he said.
