Unlike Most People, These Guys Seek Out Fair-Weather Friends

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 08/06/90

BOSTON -- In Massachusetts, where everybody talks about the weather, restaurant owners want to do something about it -- the talk that is. They want Boston TV weather forecasters to stop predicting on Monday and Tuesday bad weather for the weekend ahead.

"I understand their side. There is a demand for it," says Thomas Larson, owner of the Pillar House Restaurant in Newton and chairman of the Massachusetts Restaurant Association Weather Task Force. "But if the {long-range} forecast doesn't look so good, hey, keep it a secret because it's so harmful to business."

To that end, the association has solicited complaints and forwarded packets of its members' letters to the weathermen.

Mr. Larson says the problem is that long-range forecasts aren't very accurate. He doesn't object to three-day forecasts, which he concedes are usually right.

Restaurateurs are bitter about predictions that keep tourists away from Cape Cod or the Berkshires. "Before Memorial Day weekend, weathermen forecast terrible weather on the Cape," recalls Cindy Eid, public-affairs coordinator for the Massachusetts Restaurant Association. "They told people if they wanted a peek of sun to head north. But it turned out to be one of the best weekends of the summer."

Frank Cardullo, owner of the Wursthaus Restaurant, says his Hyannis branch suffered $75,000 in lost revenue last summer on good weekends after rain had been predicted.

Other restaurant owners say winter-storm warnings are even worse.

M.S.M. Management Co., Danvers, says that two years ago inaccurate predictions of a big storm for Feb. 24 cost its restaurants $100,000 in lost revenue. That same weekend, the Harvard Club of Boston says, "the irresponsibility of the weather forecasters" led to the cancellation of a dinner party for 250.

Some weathermen are sympathetic. Dick Albert, of WCVB-TV in Boston, says the letters have sensitized him to the problem, but he adds that he has avoided five-day forecasts for many years.

But most stations say it's more important to give the public what it wants. "Whenever we don't have time to do it, the public complains," says Barry Burbank, of WBZ-TV. He says he makes it clear that the long-range view is less reliable than the short-range picture: "We don't call it a forecast. We call it an outlook," he says.
