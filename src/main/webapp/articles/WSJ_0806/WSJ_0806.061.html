South Korean Bid To Ease Rules Stirs Takeover Worry

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/06/90

SEOUL -- South Korea's latest moves to ease regulations on share ownership -- which are designed to stimulate the sagging stock market -- are expected to prompt takeover attempts by corporate raiders, according to industry officials. The Korean Securities and Exchange Commission decided last week to lift a rule prohibiting minor shareholders from increasing stakes in listed companies to more than 10%.

The securities regulation agency also chose to lift a restriction on major shareholders' stock purchases, allowing them to hold as much as 51% stakes, to protect their management rights.

The government aims to expand new demand and boost a sluggish market, which has undergone 17 months of bearishness. The new rules require commission approval for purchases beyond former limits, but that approval will come easily, commission officials said.

The new measure makes it easier for corporate raiders to take over listed firms, says Hong Jin-Ki, an analyst at Daishin Securities Co. By accumulating stock, or allying with other shareholders, minor shareholders can increase their stakes and outweigh major shareholders, he says. On the other hand, major shareholders will try to enhance their ownership to protect their management rights, he notes.

The rule was announced Wednesday and went into effect immediately. Since then, investors have placed heavy emphasis on small-capital and medium-capital shares, which are easy targets for takeover attempts.

On Wednesday, small-capital shares with capital of less than 5 billion won (US$6.9 million) rose 2.4% in their index value, compared with a 1.8% gain for large-capital shares with more than 15 billion won of capital.

On Thursday, that difference widened. Small-capital shares rose a further 1.7% and middle-capital shares 1.2%, while large-capital shares fell 0.4%.

Major shareholders of smaller firms are buying back shares to protect management rights, says Kim Chong-Dae, general manager at Tongyang Securities Co. Mr. Kim doesn't detect any takeover attempts for small firms currently, but he expects a lot of such attempts in the near future.

Analysts say the new measure will greatly stimulate lackluster merger and acquisition activity. While there have been friendly takeovers in South Korea before, there has never been a hostile takeover before, partly because of regulations on shareholdings and partly for cultural reasons, says a foreign broker.

The average ownership of major shareholders in local listed firms is estimated at about 25%, down from about 30% at the time of original listing, Tongyang's Mr. Kim says. If all major holders were to bring their stakes back to 30% through stock buy-backs, about 4 trillion won would be funneled into the stock market, he adds.
