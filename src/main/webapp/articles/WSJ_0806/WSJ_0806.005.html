Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Mideast Tensions Will Affect Producer And Consumer Prices, Analysts Say

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 08/06/90

NEW YORK -- Statistical casualties of the oil war: producer prices will suffer first, but consumer prices will suffer longer. The July producer price index narrowly escaped impact from the roiling of energy prices that occurred when Iraq seized Kuwait as August began. Economists note the July PPI report, due out Friday, is expected to gain only 0.2% in what's expected to be the last mild inflation reading for some time. The July consumer price index, due out next week, is also likely to be relatively tame.

But starting in August, the PPI could jump as much as one percentage point and the CPI could log a gain of 0.5% to 0.8%, said Michael Englund, economist with MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc.

"It's unclear what to expect because oil prices are changing by the hour," Mr. Englund said on Friday. But he and others noted that one thing seems certain: the heavier weighting of energy in the PPI forces that index to respond faster and stronger to change while the CPI takes a while longer to reflect price moves. But once ingrained at the consumer level, energy price increases tend to linger, largely because they become embedded in labor contracts and long-term pricing strategies. Energy accounts for about 9.2% of the PPI and 7.6% of the CPI, according to the government.

The CPI is an especially pervasive indicator. Among other things, it indicates the effectiveness of government economic policy; affects wages of three million union workers covered by collective bargaining pacts, and influences the income of 60 million Americans who receive benefits as diverse as Social Security payments and school lunches.

Mr. Englund noted that when oil prices jumped around year's end, "the January PPI showed it hard while we saw gains filter out several months in the CPI."

In June, the PPI rose a moderate 0.2% and the CPI rose a sharper 0.5%. Oil wasn't the only thing heating up inflation forecasts last week. The July employment report was anemic in most respects except wages: average hourly earnings rose 0.6% to $10.09. That sort of higher inflation coupled with an economy near standstill underscored economists' concerns about stagflation.

"That reflects the severity of the cost-push problem" right at the time the economy is shrinking, said Donald H. Straszheim, chief economist at Merrill Lynch, New York. "That's very reminiscent of stagflation," he added, "even without the oil crisis."

Analysts were reluctant to predict just how severely oil prices could affect the economy down the road because there is now so much uncertainty in the Middle East. Based on movements through last Friday, Mr. Straszheim expected oil prices to add 0.3 to 0.4 percentage point to the PPI and 0.2 percentage point to the CPI in August and September. Such increases brought his overall PPI forecast to a gain of 0.7% in August and a gain of one percentage point in September. Mr. Straszheim's overall CPI forecast increased to a gain of 0.6% in August and 0.8% in September.

"Your headlights aren't very much good after that, since you don't know what's going to happen" in the Mideast, he said.

Economists added that the weak July employment data virtually assured lower readings on July industrial output and capacity utilization.

Other economic indicators could also feel the brunt of higher energy prices hitting just as the economy flattens. Samuel Kahan, chief economist at Fuji Securities Inc., Chicago, noted that higher oil prices could pinch consumer spending. He added that consumers could be forced to pay substantially more for petroleum products just as the important services sector of the economy begins to cool. Normally, a slowdown in the service sector -- which accounts for about half of the CPI -- could set the stage for cooler prices in that sector. "But energy could offset that," Mr. Kahan said.

For the moment, Mr. Kahan isn't making radical changes to his full-year inflation forecast. "The problem is more than the price increases we've seen to date. The problem is uncertainty on how high prices can go," Mr. Kahan said Friday. "We have to see if Mr. Hussein gets away with it."

The week's only other major indicator is June consumer credit due out tomorrow. The consensus view calls for an increase of $2.5 billion, down from May's $3.9 billion.
