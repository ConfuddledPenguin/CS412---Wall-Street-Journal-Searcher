Transit Union and Blackstone Group Negotiate on Greyhound Buy-Out Plan

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 08/06/90

The Amalgamated Transit Union has begun negotiating with Blackstone Group, a New York investment banking firm, on a plan to buy Greyhound Lines Inc. "We are trying to put a deal together that would lead to the purchase and reorganization of Greyhound Lines and get striking drivers back to their jobs," said Earle Putnam, ATU general counsel. The ATU's strike against Greyhound began March 2. On June 4, the company filed for protection from its creditors under Chapter 11 of the federal bankruptcy code, blaming strike-related losses for its action.

The involvement of Blackstone Group, with its $800 million buy-out and investment fund, adds muscle to the union's recent efforts to wrest control of the ailing unit of Dallas-based GLI Holding Co. away from Chairman Fred Currey.

Blackstone Group's efforts are being led by one of its general partners, Richard Ravitch, a turnaround specialist and former public official who devised a successful bailout plan for the New York State Urban Development Corporation during the mid-1970s. Mr. Ravitch helped rebuild the New York State Metropolitan Transit Authority following a bitter 1980 strike. In 1989, he ran unsuccessfully for mayor of New York City.

"We are exploring the situation, but before we make a decision, we have to collect more information," Mr. Ravitch said through a spokesperson.

Greyhound spokesman George Gravley said the company hadn't yet heard from Blackstone Group. "They haven't contacted us; we don't have anything to say," he said. Greyhound officials have previously said the company is not for sale.

Persons close to the situation said Blackstone Group plans to ask closely held Greyhound for detailed financial information in the near future. If the company refuses such data, the union or Blackstone Group will try to get it through federal bankrupcty court, union leaders said, adding that an ATU-Blackstone Group buy-out bid might also be made through the court in the form of a competing plan of reorganization to Greyhound creditors.

Under federal bankruptcy court rules, Greyhound maintains until Oct. 4 the exclusive right to propose a reorganization plan, but Mr. Putnam said the union might petition the court to shorten that period.

"We'll do what we have to do to proceed," said Brian M. Freeman, who has been retained as investment advisor to the ATU.

In recent weeks, Mr. Freeman and ATU officials have met with other Greyhound creditors in an effort to gain support for a union buy-out bid. None have yet agreed to support a possible ATU-Blackstone Group bid, but those talks continue, said Mr. Freeman, who recently engineered a proposed union buy-out of UAL Corp.

Union leaders said any proposal would probably include an employee stock ownership plan, reinstatement of striking ATU members that Greyhound has replaced with new hires, and a settlement of the union's unfair labor practices case pending against Greyhound.

That case, the result of a complaint filed by the Milwaukee, Wis., regional office of the National Labor Relations Board, is set for trial before a federal administrative law judge in next month. A ruling in the union's favor could force Greyhound to reinstate striking ATU members and give them back pay totaling an estimated $3 million per month, or more.

With the prospect of such a huge backpay claim looming, some Greyhound creditors have said that peace with the ATU is crucial to Greyhound's successful emergence from Chapter 11.
