Stock Manipulation Victims Are Covered By RICO, Says Court

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/06/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- Victims of stock manipulation may use civil racketeering suits to recover losses, even if the victims weren't the direct buyers or sellers of the manipulated securities, a federal appeals court held. The 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals ruled the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act contains no limitation that a person damaged by stock manipulation must be a purchaser or seller of the security.

The ruling expands the power of the Securities Investor Protection Corp. to recover damages from brokers and dealers accused of defrauding customers. Attorneys said the decision could expand the ability of private investors to file such suits, too.

The decision reinstates a 1983 lawsuit against 75 people accused of conspiring to defraud investors of $14 million through the manipulation of stock prices in six companies traded over-the-counter between 1964 and 1981.

The ruling will allow SIPC to pursue its efforts to recover the $14 million it had to pay investors when it liquidated two securities brokerage firms, First State Securities Corp. and Joseph Sebag Inc.

Although SIPC wasn't an investor in the allegedly manipulated securities, it sued for triple damages under RICO, claiming it had losses as a result of racketeering. A Los Angeles federal court previously dismissed the claim because SIPC had neither bought nor sold the manipulated securities, a usual requirement in stock-fraud suits. But the appeals court held that RICO didn't contain any such limitation.

SIPC was created by Congress in 1970 in the wake of the collapse of numerous brokerage houses. It was established to provide protection to customers of brokers and dealers and members of the national securities exchanges.

The six firms allegedly involved in the stock manipulation were: Aero Systems Inc., Aerosonic Corp., Bunnington Corp., Creditbank Inc., Dash Industries Inc. and Osrow Products Corp.
