GATT Says Reform In New Zealand to Aid Its Economic Growth

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/06/90

GENEVA -- New Zealand's economic reform program is likely to promote its structural adjustment and economic growth, according to a new study by the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, or GATT, the international body that governs most world trade. The study, a mix of praise and criticism formulated by the GATT secretariat, said the reforms will "strengthen the country's links with the world economy and provide for trade policies and practices which are more firmly based on GATT rules and principles."

The report also said, "The potential gains from international trade are particularly large for New Zealand."

At the same time, however, the study hinged its judgment on the proviso that the reforms are "consistently and fully continued and implemented."

The report said New Zealand's trade policies "have generally become less distortive and more transparent over the last 10 years."

And it continued, "With the recent orientation of trade-related policies, the country is already a more attractive market for many trading partners through improved market access and the prospects of accelerated medium-term growth in a more efficient New Zealand economy."

The report praised New Zealand's liberalization drive. "The coverage of import licensing has been reduced, tariffs have been cut through a general tariff reduction program, most export incentive schemes and subsidies have been eliminated, the role of the state in trade has been reduced, foreign-exchange restrictions have been removed, and financial controls and rules on foreign investments have been relaxed," the report said.

But it added that the country is "still a considerable distance from being an open economy." GATT said that New Zealand's tariff rates "don't yet appear particularly stable or predictable," and that marketing boards still "enjoy considerable monopoly powers in domestic and foreign trade in agriculture."
