Heard on the Street: Which Petroleum Stocks Will Gain The Most From Mideast Turmoil?

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 08/06/90

Saddam Hussein's military moves have suddenly made investors look at oil stocks in a new light. The prospect of shrinking supplies from the Middle East propelled oil shares higher Thursday and Friday morning even though stock prices generally were collapsing. Although the oils weakened Friday afternoon on profit-taking, many market strategists believe more gains lie ahead as oil prices stabilize at higher levels.

Now that the first indiscriminate surge in oil stocks is over, analysts are left to ponder which companies will be helped most by the expected higher prices for crude.

"My oil-price forecast is that oil prices will be whatever Mr. Hussein says that he wants them to be," says a Friday market bulletin from Robert A. Brusca, chief economist for Nikko Securities Co. International.

Iraq's invasion of Kuwait "could lead down 50 different paths, and 40 or 45 of them mean higher oil prices," says Stephen A. Smith of Bear Stearns.

"The whole stock market, unfortunately, is going down, and oil basically is the only game in town," said Kidder Peabody analyst William Brown in a Wall Street Journal television interview Friday.

Charles Maxwell of C.J. Lawrence Morgan Grenfell, often considered the dean of oil analysts, thinks oil prices will average about $24 a barrel next year, up from about $20 this year and $19.50 last year.

To Bernard J. Picchi, Salomon Brothers' oil specialist, companies involved mainly in exploration and production have the advantage over those that also do a lot of refining and marketing. For explorers, higher oil prices are a blessing. For refiners, they're an added cost.

Accordingly, Mr. Picchi's favorites are Oryx Energy and Louisiana Land & Exploration. Both are exploration-and-production companies, both are mainly domestic, and both produce a lot of natural gas.

At the moment, Mr. Picchi notes, natural gas can produce a given quantity of energy more cheaply than oil. And natural gas is less polluting. Yet natural gas stocks haven't moved up as much as oil stocks, partly because much gas use is seasonal -- for home heating.

After the pure explorers and producers, his next choices lie in the realm of the big integrated companies -- those that do refining and marketing of petroleum products, as well as exploration and production. Mr. Picchi prefers those that are "more production oriented," such as Atlantic Richfield and Pennzoil.

In the next tier, he places the large international oils like Royal Dutch/Shell, Mobil, Exxon, Texaco and British Petroleum. He says they're too large to grow very fast, and they "tend to be fairly large refiners." They "will benefit, but less than the first category," he says.

Mr. Picchi says he'd be least likely to buy smaller integrated oil companies "that have been mentioned repeatedly as acquisition candidates" such as Murphy Oil, Amerada Hess, and Kerr-McGee. In many cases they're "very fine companies," he says, but takeover speculation has bid up their prices too much.

Mr. Maxwell, the industry veteran, sees things differently. He says people look down their noses at refining because the business was typically unprofitable in the past. But that reputation stems from the days when exploration enjoyed big tax advantages. Back then, it was in the oil companies' interest to run their business so that the profits were attributed to the exploration side.

Now the tax code has changed, he says, so this incentive has vanished. Meanwhile, by his calculations, U.S. refineries are running at about 95% of their true capacity, which is often less than the officially stated capacity. Hence, he doesn't view big refining operations as a drawback.

Mr. Maxwell likes the international oils such as Chevron, Mobil, Royal Dutch, British Petroleum and Texaco, with Exxon "slightly behind." He reasons that it's too expensive to find significant amounts of new oil in the U.S. Oil consumption is growing fastest in the Far East and Europe, he says, which should benefit the internationals.

Mr. Maxwell also is fond of oil-service companies such as Baker Hughes, Schlumberger and Halliburton. However, he cautions that "these companies have done so well, they are now a little on the expensive side."

Mr. Smith of Bear Stearns gives a little edge to domestic producers. "Back in 1979, when oil was getting very expensive, there was frequently a domestic premium," he recalls. "I'll give a couple of extra points for having it {oil} in a secure location."

His three favorites are Atlantic Richfield, Amoco and Unocal. He, too, favors producers with extensive natural-gas operations and expects natural gas prices to catch up with oil. Freak weather last year is one reason gas prices currently lag, he said. The winter started with the coldest December in decades, then turned unusually mild, leaving many users stuck with oversupplies of gas.

Walter Kass, a New York money manager, says he's getting ready to take a flier on Devon Energy (traded on the American Stock Exchange). He likes it because it's "all U.S., all exploration." He plans to buy convertible preferred stock rather than the common stock.

Mr. Kass already owns one unusual energy play -- Maritrans Partners, a master limited partnership traded on the Big Board. Maritrans transports oil with tugboats and barges (its stock symbol is TUG). Though it recently had an oil spill in the New York area, Mr. Kass says it responded quickly to minimize the damage.

Marine transportation of oil is likely to be a growth area, he says, because environmental concerns mean "you're not going to see too many more pipelines built."

Of course, not everyone is on the bullish side of energy stocks. In a report dated July 27, just before the Iraqi invasion, Sanford C. Bernstein strategist David Eisenberg wrote that oil service stocks are "20% overvalued" and that investors should "gradually cash in your winnings here." The energy sector as a whole should be slightly underweighted, Mr. Eisenberg wrote. He couldn't be reached for comment Friday.

Also, some analysts are fretting about the outlook for companies that specialize in refining, since they will have to pay more for oil to refine. For that reason, Dean Witter Reynolds analyst Gene Nowak advised clients Thursday to avoid Ashland Oil, Sun Co. and Mapco.

---

Kuwait's rulers weren't the only royalty deposed by Iraq's attack last week. By driving up oil stocks and punishing most others, the Mideast war also helped dethrone General Electric from the top of the stock market.

GE reigned for just a week as the U.S. company with the largest market capitalization -- defined as stock price times the number of shares. It lost $2 billion of market value last week as the shares slid to 69 3/4 from 72. Its market capitalization fell to $61.98 billion from $63.98 billion.

International Business Machines, long the U.S. leader in market capitalization, also fell back to a market capitalization of $61.98 billion at Friday's closing price of 108 1/8, down from $63.85 billion the week before.

The new leader? Exxon, whose shares rose 3 1/8 last week to close at 53 1/8, adding $3.9 billion in market value for a total of $66.3 billion.
