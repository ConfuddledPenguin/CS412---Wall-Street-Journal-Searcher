Morgan Stanley Said To Be Considering Leaving New York --- Securities Firm May Move To Connecticut, People Close to Company Say

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 08/06/90

NEW YORK -- The Big Apple, long the heart of the securities world, could lose a plum Wall Street tenant. Morgan Stanley Group Inc. is seriously weighing a move from New York to Connecticut when its lease expires in 1997, people familiar with the firm said.

In an effort to cut costs, the big securities firm recently has been looking at office space in Stamford and Norwalk, among other cites, the people said.

But some analysts say Morgan Stanley may be posturing. They point to Dreyfus Corp., the mutual-fund group that last year rattled the city's cage by threatening to leave New York for New Jersey. Dreyfus finally won some last-minute tax concessions and remained in New York.

Morgan Stanley may end up moving some of its operations outside the city, analysts say. But it would be impractical for the firm to leave New York entirely.

"Maybe they'll set up a nominal office . . . as a place to stop on the way to the golf course," says John Keefe, a securities-industry analyst for Barclays de Zoete Wedd in New York. But leaving the city altogether would "be taking themselves out of the flow" of client contacts and market information, Mr. Keefe says.

Morgan Stanley declined to comment on its plans.

The firm currently has about 4,100 of its 6,800 employees world-wide in six locations in New York, including an operations office in Brooklyn. Some Morgan Stanley employees privately say they aren't thrilled with the prospect of leaving New York.

But any move would be a blow to New York, which already is reeling from sharp cutbacks in the securities and retailing industries. Since the October 1987 stock-market crash, the number of securities-industry employees in New York has fallen 14% to 139,700, according to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics.

Connecticut isn't Morgan Stanley's only target. The firm will scour other cites outside New York, industry executives said. But the firm's next step, they said, is to take an option on some land in Connecticut to get a sense of the fixed costs involved.
