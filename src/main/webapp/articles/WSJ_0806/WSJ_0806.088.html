RasterOps Corp. To Post Big Rise In Quarterly Net

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 08/06/90

RasterOps Corp., riding strong demand for high-end computer graphics products, estimated that earnings in its fourth quarter ended June 30 exceeded analysts' forecasts about 7%. Keith Sorenson, chief executive officer, said RasterOps should post net income of 16 cents a share, or $809,600, on revenue of $13 million. RasterOps, based in Santa Clara, Calif., makes computer-peripherals, including high-end color monitors and graphics devices for desktop computers.

Most analysts are expecting RasterOps to report net of 15 cents a share on revenue of $12 million. RasterOps is expected to release its financial results today.

In the year-ago period, the company earned $301,000, or three cents a share, on $7.2 million in revenue.

This is the first time RasterOps has disclosed quarterly results since its initial public stock offering in May. The stock was offered at $12 and closed Friday at $16.75 in over-the-counter trading.

Founded three years ago, RasterOps has ridden burgeoning demand for computers used in publishing and graphic arts. In recent years, Apple Computer Inc. has been the major supplier to the arts and publishing world because its Macintosh computers are designed to process and display graphics. Apple offers large-screen monitors and extra circuit boards that improve its standard machines, but it did so only after RasterOps and several other companies carved out the market.

About 85% of RasterOps's sales come from Macintosh-related products, but the company is moving quickly to piggyback its business onto that of International Business Machines Corp., the largest supplier of personal computers, and Sun Microsystems Inc., the biggest supplier of workstations.

This week, RasterOps expects to begin shipping its first products for IBM's PS/2 line of personal computers. The company already sells products for Sun's Sparcstation models, desktop machines popular with engineers and scientists.

RasterOps also intends to build peripheral devices for Digital Equipment Corp.'s computers. Today, the company will announce plans to develop devices for Digital's machines that provide improved color and live video feeds or television in a window on the monitor's screen.
