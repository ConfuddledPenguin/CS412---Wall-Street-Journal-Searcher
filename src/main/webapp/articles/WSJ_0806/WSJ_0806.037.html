HHS Proposes Lab Inspection Fee Schedule

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 08/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The Department of Health and Human Services proposed a schedule of basic fees ranging as high as $2,870 to be paid by medical laboratories to cover the cost of new federal inspection. Even labs that are exempt from regulation would have to pay $156 every two years.

Under a 1988 law designed to improve the accuracy of clinical tests, all laboratories, including those in doctors' offices, must be certified as meeting new federal standards in order to receive payments from Medicare and Medicaid. These federal health programs for the elderly and the poor pay about 40% of the nation's clinical lab costs.

The law requires that the government charge fees high enough to pay for the cost of the inspection and certification program. It will cover more than 300,000 labs, a huge expansion of current Medicare regulations that cover some 12,000 labs.

The American Medical Association and other groups have complained that regulations that HHS proposed in May to carry out the law are too costly and complex. The large volume of comments the department has received on the May proposals could delay the start of the program.

Many labs will be inspected every two years, although labs that perform only simple, low-risk tests, such as urine pregnancy tests, will be exempt from regulation. Under the fee proposal, however, they would still have to pay $156 every two years to maintain their certificate of waiver. All other labs would have to pay a one-time fee of $261 for a provisional certificate of compliance.

When regulated labs are inspected they would then pay a fee ranging from $840 to $2,870, depending on their size. The inspection fees would be paid every two years. The department's Health Care Financing Administration also proposed fees for follow-up investigations, if necessary, and for hearings if sanctions are imposed.

The federal government would pass most of the fees on to the state agencies that will perform inspections. A separate set of fees would cover inspections performed by professional accreditation groups rather than state agencies.

"Patients demand and deserve accurate, high-quality medical laboratory work," HHS Secretary Louis Sullivan said. "The fees will support our system to assure that these requirements are met." The department is allowing 60 days for comment on the proposed fee schedule before deciding on its final form.
