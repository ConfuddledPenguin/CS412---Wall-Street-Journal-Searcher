Senate Approves Broader Powers for SEC To Gather Data, Shut Down Stock Market

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 08/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate passed a bill that would broadly expand the Securities and Exchange Commission's ability to gather Wall Street information and for the first time allow the agency to close the stock market in times of extreme stress. The bill, passed by voice vote shortly before the Senate recessed for the summer, marks the Senate's first broad-based reaction to the sharp stock-market drops of October 1987 and October 1989 and to the collapse of Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc.

But the Senate bill lacks a measure contained in the House version allowing the SEC to ban some forms of program trading, a provision opposed by the Bush administration. House-Senate conference discussions are likely to focus on how to recast the program-trading provision into something the Senate and administration can support. The House bill grants the SEC the power "to constrain during volatile markets legitimate trading strategies which nonetheless result in extraordinary volatility."

Moreover, both the House and Senate versions of the bill exclude a Bush administration-sponsored proposal to grant the SEC regulatory oversight of stock-index futures, which allow investors to buy the equivalent of a basket of stocks but are traded on a commodity exchange. Those futures currently are regulated by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission.

The Senate legislation would allow the SEC to gather information on holding-company parents of Wall Street brokerage firms. Those parents often hold many of the risky, short-term loans taken on by firms as interim financing for takeovers.

But the SEC had expressed concern that it didn't have jurisdiction over the parent -- only the brokerage unit -- and couldn't properly assess the risk to both entities. When Drexel was forced to file for bankruptcy earlier this year, the SEC noted, it moved huge sums to its financially strapped holding company from the brokerage unit, eventually forcing the subsidiary to fall under SEC capital rules. The SEC argued that it was hampered in monitoring the situation by the fact that it couldn't tell how badly the parent was doing.

The bill gives the SEC other major data-gathering abilities as well. It allows the agency to set up record-keeping procedures for large securities transactions, a measure designed to help the agency monitor troublesome trades.

"It was clear from the events of last October, just as it had been in October 1987, that the regulators did not have sufficient information to fully assess the risks to the system," said Sen. Christopher Dodd (D., Conn.), a sponsor of the bill. The measure also will help the agency "detect manipulative or other illegal trading activity."

The bill also would aim to strengthen the clearance and settlement system for stocks, options and futures, reducing the threats of systemic risk to financial markets.

Both the House and Senate versions also would grant the SEC the power to close the stock market in times of severe market stress. Currently, only the president can halt trading on the overall market.

The provision was weakened in negotiations over the past year after SEC Chairman Richard Breeden expressed concern that investor uncertainty over whether the SEC would take such a step would have a chilling effect on the market. So the measure now would permit the SEC to close the market if the president doesn't oppose the move.
