Letters to the Editor: Nine High Priests Interpret U.S. Holy Writ

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/06/90

Regarding your June 27 editorial "The Court's Angels": the U.S. Constitution can hardly be ". . . transformed into Holy Writ. . . ." It always has been Holy Writ. The English refugees who first settled these shores came here in large part because the medieval mind-set of Tudor England was being replaced by James I's modernizing ideas, and those who couldn't adapt emigrated. The essence of medieval polity was that all authority was ultimately vested in "natural law," i.e. God's law, which was interpreted for the people's use by the church. While the drafters of our Constitution were far removed from medieval times ecclesiastically, their political instincts were still rooted in Tudor England, not Stuart, Orange or Hanoverian.

Compare Britain and the U.S. in terms of the locus of "sovereignty." In the former, the law is whatever the House of Commons says it is, and that House is by and large a fair reflection of the "vox populi". "The people" are, through the representational mechanism of the House of Commons, indeed sovereign.

In the U.S., by contrast, sovereignty is vested in a piece of paper, the meaning of which, while reasonably clear when written, is now available to the people only through the interpretation of the Supreme Court. Our elected representatives, both executive and legislative, are helpless in the face of contrary "interpretations" by a majority of our nine high priests.

You and I may understand, for example, that so long as the U.S. does not have an "official" church, the Constitution's prohibition of the "establishment" of religion is being complied with, and no further action or decision is necessary. However, the simple words in the Constitution no longer mean what they say; they mean whatever our unelected lifetime-appointed neoclergy decides they mean.

Therefore, unless there is a fundamental restructuring of the American system of government, the Constitution will remain, rather than be transformed into, "Holy Writ." I leave it to you and your readers to figure out what can be done about it.

David B. Seidler

Fair Lawn, N.J.
