Judge Approves a County Redistricting In Los Angeles to Bolster Hispanic Vote

  • Author: Jim Herron Zamora
  • Date: 08/06/90

LOS ANGELES -- Federal Judge David V. Kenyon approved a new map of county supervisorial boundaries here with a voting-rights order that could shift the balance of power in one of the nation's most populous counties. Judge Kenyon's order creates a new district designed to assure that a Hispanic is elected to the county's five-member governing board, but also allows candidates who don't live in the district to seek to represent the district on the board.

Judge Kenyon adopted the redistricting plan proposed by the American Civil Liberties Union and the Mexican American Legal Defense and Education Fund. An estimated 35% of the county's 8.7 million residents are Hispanic, according to a demographer who drew up the plan.

The Los Angeles County plan affects more people than any other local voting-rights case and may serve as a precedent for other municipalities, said Chandler Davidson, a Rice University sociology professor and author of a book about the underrepresentation of minorities in state and local elective offices.

Noting that most of the voting-rights litigation has occurred in the deep South and Texas, Mr. Davidson said "California is ripe for this type of litigation," with large Hispanic, black and Asian minorities. "There are a lot of potential plaintiffs out there."

Conservative county supervisors Michael Antonovich and Deane Dana said they filed a motion Friday with the 9th Circuit Court of Appeals in San Francisco to block the judge's order. With the new district lines the pair face losing their sway over the board.

"It's a judge running wild -- making law instead of interpreting law," Mr. Antonovich said. "It's disgusting to see Judge Kenyon work with two special interest groups . . . in pursuing their liberal ideological agenda."

ACLU attorney Mark Rosenbaum disagreed saying, "The board of supervisors is the most powerful local government in the United States and some want it to remain an exclusive all-white club . . . but {the judge's order} means for the first time in 115 years the club is open to Hispanics."

Under the plan adopted by Judge Kenyon, a supervisorial district represented by a retiring conservative supervisor was redrawn to give it a Hispanic majority. The other four districts would undergo minor adjustments that will generally preserve the voter bases of two liberal and two conservative supervisors.

Judge Kenyon's order could change 10 years of a conservative majority on the board, possibly leading to sweeping reversals on health care and social services spending policies. The order was the judge's solution to correct supervisorial boundaries that he earlier found discriminatory.

The decision also sets a special election for Nov. 6 for the new Hispanic majority district and voids results of a June primary election. The district had been represented by conservative Supervisor Pete Schabarum, who is retiring.

Judge Kenyon ruled June 4 that the board discriminated against Hispanics when redrawing district boundaries after the 1980 census. He found the supervisors violated the federal Voting Rights Act and ordered the board to submit a plan that would make it easier for a Hispanic win a seat. Last week, he rejected the board's proposal as "nonsensical" and "insensitive" to Hispanic and black residents.

In setting the new election date, the judge waived a residency requirement to permit the two top June election finishers under the old boundaries to run as candidates in the redrawn district. Ironically, the top vote getter was Sarah Flores, an Hispanic woman who is also a conservative Republican. If she were elected in November, she would become the first Hispanic supervisor since 1875, but the board would continue to be dominated by conservatives.

Ms. Flores, a former assistant to Mr. Schabarum, blasted the judge's order because it voided what she called the only election in the last 30 years in which a Hispanic has a chance of winning.
