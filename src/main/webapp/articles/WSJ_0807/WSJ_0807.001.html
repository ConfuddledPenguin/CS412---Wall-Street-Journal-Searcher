International: Japanese Employees May Not Be Quite So Loyal After All --- Survey by Dai-Ichi Discovers That Many New Recruits Expect to Change Jobs

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 08/07/90

TOKYO -- It may be time to scrap the image of the loyal, hard-working Japanese employee. Lifetime employment? At one of Japan's largest insurance companies, Dai-Ichi Mutual Life Insurance Co., only two-thirds of new male recruits expect to finish their careers there. Indeed, after only two months on the job, nearly a third already have contemplated quitting.

Truthful? Four in 10 new male workers at the insurance company say they would lie to cover up the fact that they overslept on a weekday. For most, the biggest change in their lives resulting from their new jobs was having to get up early.

These tradition-shattering findings are contained in a survey the company recently conducted of more than 300 new recruits. The results were published in the employee newsletter at Dai-Ichi, one of Japan's major employers with a sales force of more than 60,000.

Dai-Ichi has surveyed new employees before, but always about themselves, a spokesman says. This was the first time they were asked their views about working and the company.

To some extent, the employees say, their answers reflect changes in Japan. When young recruits start work, says Koji Noda, a 22-year-old who joined Dai-Ichi in April, they discovered "they have to do things they didn't or couldn't see in the company's PR brochure."

To be sure, many of the survey's findings confirm stereotypes about office workers in Japan. For example, new male employees at the insurance company are most interested in automobiles, while new women recruits mostly want to travel. The men would prefer to get married at age 30, the women at 25.

More surprising, perhaps, was the finding that workaholic Japanese men aren't always as hard-working as their so-called office lady counterparts. Only a third of the men said they would stay at work if a boss asked them to put in overtime on an evening when they had a date scheduled. Forty-one percent of the women said they would labor for the company in those circumstances.
