Waste Management Stock Is Slammed After Barron's Item

  • Author: Jeff Bailey
  • Date: 08/07/90

OAK BROOK, Ill. -- Waste Management Inc.'s stock came under heavy selling pressure after a Barron's article questioned some of the waste hauler's accounting treatments. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading of 2.8 million shares, the stock slumped $3.125, to $36.375, slashing the company's market value more than $1.2 billion. Despite the stock market's overall decline yesterday, Waste Management said it believes the Barron's article was the primary reason its stock opened yesterday morning down five points, but James Koenig, chief financial officer, said that by trading's close, the stock's drop was in line with other growth stocks.

Waste Management issued a statement standing by its accounting and asserting that the article "flies in the face of reality." The company also said its outside auditors, Arthur Andersen & Co. in Chicago, had reiterated their support for Waste Management's financial statements.

The article in Barron's, which like The Wall Street Journal is published by Dow Jones & Co., questioned a $70.8 million gain Waste Management realized last year when a company it owns a majority stake in, Chemical Waste Management, sold some stock. The article also questioned Waste Management's practice of amortizing goodwill from acquisitions over 40 years, the maximum allowable time to write off such intangible assets.

The article was written by Abraham J. Briloff, an accounting professor.

Both treatments are allowable under generally accepted accounting principles. Waste Management said it had booked an earlier gain related to the sale of some Chemical Waste Management stock, and the 1989 sale had to be treated consistently.

As for the 40-year goodwill schedule, a spokesman said that over the 20 years the company has made acquisitions of local trash-collection businesses, many of those operations' values have increased "significantly." On landfills, which have a limited life, Waste Management writes off the good will over a shorter period.

"We want to reassure all of our investors that our financial statements are comprehensive, complete, meet the highest accounting standards and exhaustively represent their interests," the company said.

The combined impact of not realizing the Chemical Waste gain and of writing off good will much faster could have reduced Waste Management's reported profit for 1989 more than $170 million, according to the Barron's article. Net income for the year was $562.1 million, or $1.22 a share, on revenue of $4.46 billion.

Marc Sulam, an analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co. who has recommended Waste Management stock for several years, noted, however, that the company has billions of dollars of unrealized gains on its Chemical Waste shares and on its landfill holdings. He said the company's lofty stock price of about 29 times earnings is based on its leading market share in a growing business that is difficult for others to enter.
