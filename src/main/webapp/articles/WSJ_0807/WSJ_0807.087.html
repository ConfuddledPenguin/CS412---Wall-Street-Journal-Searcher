Politics & Policy: Saddam Hussein Is Now Possible Broker In U.S.-Iranian Dispute Over Hostages

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 08/07/90

WASHINGTON -- By seizing Kuwait, Saddam Hussein has made himself a possible broker in the U.S.-Iranian dispute over the six U.S. hostages held in Lebanon by pro-Iranian kidnappers. That's because Iraq now gains control over 15 pro-Iranian Shiite terrorists who have been imprisoned in Kuwait and whose freedom has been demanded repeatedly by both the kidnappers and Iran's government as a condition for releasing the U.S. hostages.

The Shiite group, members of the pro-Iranian Da'wa party, includes a brother-in-law of one of the Lebanese hostage-holders, Imad Mugniyah. Mr. Mugniyah's faction, Islamic Jihad, or Islamic Holy War, has demanded the release of the Da'wa prisoners in return for freeing the U.S. hostages it says it holds, including Terry Anderson, chief Middle East correspondent of the Associated Press.

Kuwait's charge d'affaires in Jordan, Faisal al-Mukhaizem, told the Reuters news agency Sunday that the 15 had been taken to Baghdad Friday. "They are going to use them as bargaining counters," Reuters quoted the diplomat as saying. Earlier, ABC News had reported that the men had been freed during Iraq's invasion of Kuwait.

But U.S. intelligence hasn't been able to confirm either report or other claims that the prisoners have been executed. "We just don't know where they are right now," says one State Department official.

The 15 men are serving sentences for the December 1983 bombings of the U.S. and French embassies and other targets in Kuwait City that killed six people and injured about 60.

The Kuwaiti ruling family had refused to release the prisoners, despite recent appeals from Iranian officials eager to end the hostage crisis in order to rebuild ties to the West and gain financing for reconstruction in the wake of their country's war with Iraq. One reason, U.S. officials say, is that the conservative Kuwaiti rulers agreed with the U.S. policy of refusing concessions to the hostage-holders. Another was that the terrorists had tried to overthrow the Kuwait government and rouse the country's large Shiite population to rebellion.

U.S. intelligence analysts believe that any Iraqi-controlled regime could be just as likely as Kuwait to keep the Shiite prisoners in jail, or even kill them. The Da'wa party has actively opposed Saddam Hussein's regime and carried out killings and terrorism within Iraq as well as Kuwait, with the aim of establishing an Iran-style Islamic Republic in Baghdad. Saddam Hussein's regime is made up of minority Sunni Moslems ruling a majority Shiite population.

In addition, Mr. Hussein would be eager to prevent Iran from ending the hostage crisis and possibly improving relations with the West.

But Mr. Hussein might also use the prisoners as a bargaining chip to exercise influence over Iran or the U.S. He could release them, as Iran wishes, in return for concessions from Tehran, such as support for his new puppet regime in Kuwait, whose ruling family was hated in Tehran. Or he could threaten to execute or harm them, placing the U.S. hostages in danger of retaliation, in an effort to blackmail the Bush administration.

For its part, Iran might be prompted to step up its pressure on the kidnappers to free the six Americans, to seize an opportunity to gain Western sympathy.
