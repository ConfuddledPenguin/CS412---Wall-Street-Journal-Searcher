Letters to the Editor: Geography Realigned In a Matter of Minutes

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/07/90

I knew Eire, or the Republic of Ireland, or simply Ireland as it is more commonly known, prided itself on its neutrality but I wasn't aware that it had seceded or been expelled from "Europe." Writing on the July 3 Leisure & Arts page, Nicholas King proclaims that Spain's Cape Finisterre is "the westernmost point of Europe" ("Mobile Guide: Celtic Touch on the Coast of Spain"). A quick check of some handy maps in my appointment calendar (and not, I must confess, bona fide nautical charts) confirmed my gut reaction that Cape Blasket Island off Ireland's Dingle peninsula lies about 10 degrees, 34 minutes West vs. Cape Finisterre's nine degrees, 20 minutes West longitude. As long as we're talking "islands," what about the Canary Islands (17 degrees, 20 minutes West)? Although technically off the African coast, they are politically part of European Spain.

OK, if you want to stay in Europe and don't want to count "little" islands, then the tip of the Dingle peninsula lies only a few minutes of longitude east of Blasket. I would almost agree if Mr. King had modified his description to "the westernmost point of mainland Europe," although the Cabo de Roca, southwest of Lisbon probably would challenge Finisterre to make that claim -- that is if Portugal hasn't seceded from Europe.

Assuming that we want to stay with inhabited places, or at the very least places that one could or would ever want to visit, I suppose that eliminates that spit of rock -- ever the subject of Anglo/Irish debate -- Rockall. Alas, however, Icelanders might take exception, as their homeland has -- from time to time -- been considered a European country.

Perhaps by now some of Mr. King's colleagues at the Foreign Press Center have pointed out the abyss one falls into when one makes "ultimate" geography claims.

John C. Tuffy

San Pedro, Calif.
