Economy: Productivity Made Recovery In 2nd Quarter --- U.S. Data Show an Increase At 1.9% Annual Clip, Led By Manufacturing Sector

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 08/07/90

WASHINGTON -- The productivity of U.S. businesses, paced by an increase in the manufacturing sector, rebounded in the second quarter from a disappointing first quarter, the Labor Department reported. The productivity of all U.S. businesses -- output per hour worked -- increased at an annual rate of 1.9% after falling at a 1.5% rate in the first quarter. Nonfarm productivity rose at an annual rate of 1.6%, its first gain in six quarters. Manufacturing productivity rose at a rate of 3.7%, somewhat slower than the 5.1% rise in the previous quarter.

Gordon Richards, staff economist for the National Association of Manufacturers, said he was "a little bit surprised how strong {the productivity figures} WERE, considering how slowly GNP grew." The Commerce Department has estimated that real gross national product, or the nation's total output of goods and services, adjusted for inflation, rose at a 1.2% annual rate during the period. "I think if the productivity figures are correct," said Mr. Richards, "they suggest that GNP may be revised upwards."

But Priscilla Trumbull, senior industrial economist at WEFA Group, was less optimistic. "It's a fairly bad report for the non-manufacturing side of things," she said. "It shows a weakness in a number of sectors, and yet labor costs are increasing."

The measure of the productivity of American business is an important indication of the economy's strength and competitiveness. If companies can improve their productivity, they can raise employees' wages without raising prices or cutting profits. Productivity increases can be attributed to advances in technology that allow workers to work more efficiently, a more highly skilled work force and increases in capital investment.

In an indication that inflation pressures remain, wages in the second quarter rose at an annual rate of 5.9%, the most since the second quarter of 1987. Real wages, which take into account the effect of inflation, rose at an annual rate of 2.2% during the second quarter, the first time in seven quarters that wages have risen faster than inflation. In the first quarter, wages rose at an annual rate of 3.8%, while real wages fell at a rate of 4%.

The labor costs of a unit of production grew at a 4% annual rate, following a rise at a 5.3% rate during the first quarter.

Donald Straszheim, an economist with Merrill Lynch & Co., says that inflationary pressures from the increasing labor costs should be limited: "As slow growth continues, some relief from labor cost pressures is expected, but not immediatelyhefty wage increases were reported for June and July. Still, better inflation figures are possible in 1991, after the recent run-up in oil prices has worked its way through."

While the manufacturing sector's productivity rose, the overall increase was tempered by a sluggish service sector. The overall annual growth rate of 1.6% in the nonfarm sector was far short of the 3.7% growth in the manufacturing sector, an indication that services continue to be a drag on productivity. The Labor Department doesn't publish separate statistics for the service sector.

The figures are adjusted for seasonal variations.
