Politics & Policy: Former Bush Aide Agrees to Testify In Thrift Inquiry

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 08/07/90

WASHINGTON -- Robert Thompson, a lobbyist and former aide to President Bush, dropped his defiance of a Senate subcommittee subpoena and appeared yesterday to defend his role in helping an Arizona insurance executive buy 15 insolvent savings and loans from the government in 1988. While still maintaining that Sen. Howard Metzenbaum's antitrust subcommittee has no jurisdiction in the matter, Mr. Thompson said, "I do not want to give anyone any excuse to say that my absence from these proceedings indicates I have something to hide. Far from it." Mr. Thompson had previously said he planned to battle the subcommittee's subpoena in court.

The Arizona executive, James Fail, also appeared and said that he invested in the thrifts because "I thought I could contribute to the resolution of the greatest financial disaster to strike this country since the Great Depression."

Mr. Metzenbaum, an Ohio Democrat, has been conducting an inquiry into the December 1988 sale of the thrifts to Mr. Fail, and has raised questions about whether Mr. Fail properly disclosed to regulators legal problems an insurance company he controlled faced in 1976 in Alabama. On certain forms filed with the regulators, Mr. Fail noted that securities fraud charges brought against him individually were dismissed, but he didn't indicate that one of his companies pleaded guilty to fraud and paid a $5,000 fine.

A top savings and loan regulator said at a hearing last week that if he had known about the guilty plea he would have asked for more information about Mr. Fail's background, possibly blocking transaction.

Mr. Metzenbaum has also charged that the government gave Mr. Fail a sweetheart deal, in which Mr. Fail put up only $1,000 of his own money and the government pledged $1.8 billion in assistance. Mr. Fail said those assertions were "ridiculous" because he committed to invest $120 million in the thrift and has pledged "all of my business holdings" as collateral for loans to raise part of the $120 million.

In addition, Mr. Fail said "substantially all" of the $1.8 billion "will go to the depositors and creditors of the 15 failed thrifts, paying for mistakes made long before" he bought them. Mr. Fail merged the thrifts and now runs them as Bluebonnet Savings Bank, based in Dallas.

Nevertheless, Mr. Fail was pressed assiduously by two Republican senators, Orrin Hatch of Utah and Arlen Specter of Pennsylvania, about the disclosure of his legal problems in Alabama. "I admit these forms are at best incomplete," Mr. Fail said in reference to the documents in which he neglected to mention his company's guilty plea. But Mr. Fail maintained that the guilty plea was disclosed in other documents and in conversation with regulators. Concealing the plea, he protested, would have been like "trying to slip an elephant into a girls' dormitory."

But Sen. Specter, summing up his questioning of Mr. Fail, concluded that "there was not an adequate disclosure."

Though Mr. Fail testified for nearly three hours, the hearing left many questions dangling about the sale of the thrifts. For instance, Mr. Thompson, the lobbyist, said that as part of his compensation for making calls to regulators and helping Mr. Fail make his bid, Mr. Fail agreed to pay him 2% of any proceeds Mr. Fail made from the thrifts.

But Mr. Fail said that no such agreement existed. He said he had asked regulators whether that kind of agreement was allowed and "they said emphatically no." The subcommittee didn't question Mr. Thompson after he made a prepared statement because it plans to call him back in September after sifting through documents Mr. Thompson provided yesterday.
