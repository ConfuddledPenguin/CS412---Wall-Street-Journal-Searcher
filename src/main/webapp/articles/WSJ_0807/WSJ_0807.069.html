THE COURSE OF MONETARY UNION

The monetary and economic unification of the two Germanys has been progressing smoothly since its start on July 1, West German Finance Minister Theo Waigel was reported as saying in an interview with the newspaper Die Welt.

Monetary union "on balance just a few weeks after it began is without doubt positive," Mr. Waigel was quoted as saying.

In the interview, Mr. Waigel noted that the consumer-buying explosion that had been feared prior to the monetary union hasn't occurred; rather, East Germans have shown a tendency toward saving.

Mr. Waigel reportedly noted that East Germans are taking advantage of a "building savings account," which enables them to save toward building or buying a home under favorable conditions.

NO BIG MACS NEAR SEOUL PARK

A plan to open a McDonald's franchise next to Olympic Park in southern Seoul was rejected because of public concern about commercialization of the historic facility, municipal officials said. The outlet was built last month, but its opening had been delayed pending city approval. The fast-food restaurant is about 100 feet from the six-story Gate of World Peace at the main entrance to the park, which was the centerpiece of the 1988 Seoul Olympics.

JAPANESE SPEED LIMITS

Motorists will be able to drive at speeds of up to 85 miles an hour by the next century on two planned expressways in central and western Japan, a Japanese news agency reported. The current top speed limit on Japanese highways is 65 miles an hour. In addition to the new speed limit on the sparsely populated mountainous parts of the new No. 2 Tomei Expressway linking Tokyo with Nagoya and the Nagoya-Kobe Expressway, the limit will also be increased to 65 from 50 miles per hour in urban areas, the Construction Ministry reportedly said. Construction of the expressways will begin in 1992, for completion in the early 21st century, the news agency said.

PEACE CORPS IN THE PHILIPPINES

The U.S. plans to bring back its Peace Corps volunteers to the Philippines and would consult the Philippine military on safe places for postings, U.S. Ambassador Nicholas Platt said. Mr. Platt accompanied Peace Corps worker Timothy Swanson to a meeting with President Corazon Aquino yesterday, four days after communist guerrillas freed Mr. Swanson from 50 days captivity. The U.S. ordered the withdrawal of all Peace Corps volunteers from the country in June.

SUZUKI TRAINS SOUTH KOREANS

South Korea's Daewoo Group will send employees to Japan this year for training with Suzuki Motor Co., the Japanese company said. Daewoo initiated the training program to expand its experience in production of minicars, a Suzuki spokesman said. Daewoo will begin building the compact vehicles at a new factory later this year. The employees will spend one to two months with Suzuki.

PORTUGAL'S MONETARY POLICY

The Bank of Portugal may impose new curbs on foreign-capital inflows, Vice President Antonio Borges reportedly said. "We are forced to pursue a tight monetary policy with consequences for Portuguese investment, while the main reason behind monetary growth is the inflow of foreign capital," the central-bank official was quoted by the Diario de Noticias newspaper as saying. Officials of the bank are worried that capital inflows, which totaled $4 billion in the first six months of 1990, are fueling an inflation rate that now stands at over 13%.

VIETNAMESE RICE CRISIS

Vietnam has been exporting so much rice that in the last month domestic prices for the staple have doubled in Ho Chi Minh City and some provinces, Radio Hanoi reported.

Exporters "have rushed to the city to buy up all the rice available," Vietnam's official radio said. "This has given the people the impression that there is a scarcity of rice, thus prompting many families to buy rice to keep in reserve." The report gave no price figures.

Officials are trying to control rice prices by banning sales to unauthorized exporters and placing one agency in charge of buying and selling grain within Ho Chi Minh City, formerly Saigon, the radio report said. Vietnam exported 1.15 million tons of rice in the first six months of 1990, double its exports for the same period last year.

Vietnam went from food shortages in 1988 to become the world's third-largest rice exporter last year. Economic reforms have given farmers incentives by allowing them to keep more profits.
