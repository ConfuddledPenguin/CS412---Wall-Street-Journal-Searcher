Technology: IBM Plans to Unveil Several New Models Of Popular AS/400 Minicomputer Line

  • Author: Laurence Hooper
  • Date: 08/07/90

International Business Machines Corp. plans to unveil on Aug. 21 several new models of its popular AS/400 minicomputer, a product that continues to defy gloomy predictions, according to industry consultants. The new models will be clustered at the low end of the AS/400 family, the consultants said. They will be easier to use than previous AS/400s, because IBM also plans to introduce a new version of the minicomputer's operating system.

Since its introduction in 1988, the AS/400 has become a runaway success that contributes about $13 billion a year to IBM's revenue, producing more than $1 billion in profit, consultants said. Revenue from the product line increased 19% in 1989. In the first half of 1990, it grew at a double-digit rate again, IBM said last week, despite some analysts' fears that it had started to hit roadblocks.

Nonetheless, the low end of the family has consistently underperformed IBM's higher-priced AS/400s. Thus, this month's announcement, which will include an entry-level minicomputer priced in the $10,000 range, should cheer skeptics.

Consultants said the easy-to-use machines may help attract users of a previous IBM minicomputer, the System/36. So far, System/36 users have resisted moving to the AS/400 because its operation is more complex and transferring software can be difficult.

For these prospective customers, identified by consultants as a potential $20 billion market, the new operating system will ease users through the more complex aspects of the AS/400. It also will make connecting to other computers, including non-IBM machines, easier.

For entry-level minicomputer users, IBM plans to offer a new version of its AS/Entry, a machine that uses System/36 technology and is incompatible with the rest of the AS/400 family. Unlike its predecessor, however, this computer looks the same as other AS/400s, and can be upgraded to a regular AS/400 by replacing a few circuit boards.

IBM also will announce the availability of a speedy, high-capacity disk drive for all models of the AS/400. These new drives will replace a slower drive that accounted for at least $3 billion in revenue last year, said consultant David Andrews of ADM Inc., Cheshire, Conn.

The disk drive was announced in February for use in IBM's new RS/6000 workstations. "It's quite exciting technology, and it's very big business," said Mr. Andrews. Another technological advance last seen in the RS/6000 -- the four-megabit memory chip -- will also make its AS/400 debut on Aug. 21, he added.

An IBM spokeswoman confirmed that the Armonk, N.Y., computer giant will make several AS/400 announcements that will focus on the low end "in the very near future," but declined to give details.

In an interview last week, Robert LaBant, the new general manager of IBM's minicomputer operation, also refused to be specific about the new products. But he and other IBM executives were unreservedly upbeat about the prospects for the AS/400, which they said is now entering its "growth phase."

"We have tremendous demand," Mr. LaBant said. "We're all working overtime. . . . Demand is going to stay very high."

Mr. LaBant and the other executives sketched out an impressive first half for the AS/400, which they said achieved double-digit growth in both revenue and units in the U.S. World-wide, the AS/400 recorded only single-digit unit growth, but revenue grew in double digits because sales were skewed toward the high end. They added that in both the first and second quarters, AS/400 shipments were "supply-constrained," meaning machines weren't produced fast enough to fill orders.

Sales to sites with more than one AS/400 also have done well, with the installed base doubling since September. Mr. LaBant said one customer -- identified by consultants as Allstate Insurance Co., a unit of Sears, Roebuck & Co. -- is installing "hundreds of AS/400s a month." An Allstate spokeswoman said she couldn't confirm the report.

Such numbers suggest the AS/400, which many analysts have predicted will be outflanked by personal computers and workstations such as the RS/6000, has a long way to go before it's declared dead.
