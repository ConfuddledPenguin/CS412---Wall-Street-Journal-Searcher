Law: ABA Assembly Votes to Rescind Abortion Stand

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 08/07/90

CHICAGO -- Members of the American Bar Association voted by a narrow margin to rescind a resolution the organization adopted last February that endorsed a constitutional right to abortion. The 885-837 vote by the ABA Assembly was passed after two hours of heated debate, and will be considered tomorrow by the House of Delegates, the policy-making arm of the lawyers' group.

The House of Delegates, a representative body of 461 members, is the only group that can set ABA policy. While the Assembly's vote isn't binding, the House is likely to consider seriously any suggestion made by the ABA Assembly.

The abortion debate has divided the ABA, which has 365,000 members nationwide. Yesterday's vote is being closely watched by Washington policy makers because the ABA's judicial screening committee plays an important role in evaluating judicial nominees who will be voted on by members of Congress.

At a news conference yesterday after speaking to the ABA, Attorney General Dick Thornburgh underscored his concern that the ABA's pro-abortion stance could taint the ABA's public appearance as a neutral evaluator. He said, however, that since February there has been no evidence that the ABA screening panel has used abortion as a litmus test in evaluatating judicial candidates.

If the House of Delegates fails to adopt the Assembly's new position on abortion, the Assembly can take a second vote and force a referendum of the entire ABA membership. Such a move would be unprecedented, observers said.

February's resolution recognized "the fundamental rights of privacy and equality guaranteed by the U.S. Constitution," and opposed "legislation or other governmental action that interferes with the confidential relationship between a pregnant woman and her physician."

Approval of the pro-choice policy meant the ABA could file friend-of-the-court briefs in abortion cases. The ABA could also lobby against legislation cutting back on abortion rights. Since the vote, 1,500 ABA members have resigned in protest.

Anthony R. Palermo, a Rochester, N.Y., lawyer who proposed the resolution that would rescind the earlier decision, argued that the ABA should remain "neutral" in the abortion debate. His resolution held that the ABA should not adopt a policy supportive of a particular viewpoint "with respect to constitutional, moral, medical or other questions involved in a decision to terminate pregnancy."

Mr. Palermo's supporters have lobbied for their position, spending about $70,000 on pollsters, political advisers and mailings.

Proponents of the House's earlier resolution have painted the debate as "pro-choice" versus "no choice." Sara-Ann Determan, a Washington lawyer who lead the charge to pass an abortion resolution last February, said yesterday that Mr. Palermo's resolution "won't create neutrality; it would tell the nation's lawyers that the ABA no longer supports a woman's right to choose."
