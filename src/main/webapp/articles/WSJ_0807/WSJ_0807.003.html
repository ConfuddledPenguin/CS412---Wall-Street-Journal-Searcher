Heard on the Street: Many Stocks Can Offer Shelter In Mideast Storm, Analysts Say

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 08/07/90

NEW YORK -- If the twin dangers of war and recession dominate the market's outlook, are any stocks worth buying? Yes, according to Wall Street strategists, who say that investors can try to immunize their portfolios with stocks that should hold up reasonably well, or even rise, in the current troubled market.

Among their favorites are oil and energy-related companies that should benefit from higher oil prices and selected food, tobacco and drug companies whose products should keep selling even in an economic slump.

Oil and oil-service stocks have risen sharply in the past week as the rest of the market fell back. But stock strategists are nearly unanimous in their belief that bigger price gains will come as the world gets used to the idea of paying well over $20 a barrel for crude oil.

"The revaluation of oil stock prices is going to take a while, to think about the effects of $25-a-barrel crude oil vs. $15-a-barrel crude," says Edward Kerschner, chairman of the investment policy committee at PaineWebber. "If oil prices settle in the mid-20s per barrel, there's still another 10% to 15% left in the oils and more than that in the oil services," Mr. Kerschner says.

Texaco, Amerada Hess, British Petroleum, Unocal and Atlantic Richfield are Mr. Kerschner's favorites among the oil companies; Schlumberger, Halliburton, Baker Hughes, Smith International and Rowan are his choices in the oil-services group.

"We think oil stocks are attractive on a longer-term basis," says Michael Metz, chief market strategist at Oppenheimer. "The developments in the Mideast indicate that the secular direction of oil prices should be higher," Mr. Metz says. Arco is Mr. Metz's pick among the oils; he adds that Tosco, an oil refiner, has probably been beaten down too far and might be attractive.

There's less agreement about which stocks investors should buy to prepare their portfolios for recession. But one theme dominates: "defensive" stocks, or shares in companies whose products will be among the last that consumers give up when times get tough. These include food, drug and cigarette makers plus electric and telephone utilities.

"People will still need medicine and buy food," says Mr. Metz, who recommends Albertson's, a well-regarded supermarket chain, and Coca-Cola. Among drug manufacturers, Mr. Metz favors Schering-Plough and Syntex.

John Hoffmann, associate director of research at Smith Barney, says that firm is largely sticking with the recommendations it had in place before Iraqi tanks rolled into Kuwait last week. He says Smith Barney had been recommending that investors overweight their stock portfolios in energy, putting 17% into energy companies compared with the energy sector's 13% weighting in the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index.

Among energy stocks, Mr. Hoffmann likes Texaco, Amerada Hess, Amoco and Anadarko Petroleum, with Halliburton and McDermott International as oil-service plays.

Among the consumer-oriented stocks, Mr. Hoffmann suggests Philip Morris plus drug stocks such as Baxter International, Bristol-Myers Squibb, Warner Lambert and SmithKline Beecham.

As a defensive move, Smith Barney on Friday increased its recommended weighting for utility stocks to match the group's proportion in the S&P 500. Mr. Hoffman suggests Pacific Telesis and GTE among telephone companies and Commonwealth Edison and PacifiCorp among electric utilities.

But some strategists suggest avoiding utilities, despite their reputation as classic defensive stocks. They worry that long-term interest rates will remain stubbornly high this time, even if the economy falls into recession. Utilities tend to have heavy borrowing costs, so their stocks usually fare poorly when interest rates are high.

"We could have an unusual recession where longer-term rates are going to be slow to go down," says Mr. Kerschner of PaineWebber.

Instead of utilities, Mr. Kerschner prefers as defensive buys companies with a large percentage of their sales abroad, figuring their earnings will benefit from a weak dollar. As examples, he cites Merck, a drug maker, as well as Coca-Cola. He also views Philip Morris as a well-positioned defensive buy.

Carime Grigoli, chief portfolio strategist at First Boston, also is talking about defense, but he means the kind practiced at the Pentagon.

"The stocks I would be willing to step up and buy are either oil-related or defense-related," says Mr. Grigoli, who recommends Texaco, Unocal and Mobil among the oils; Coastal as a natural-gas play; and E-Systems, which makes defense electronics systems, to profit from increased demand for electronic military surveillance gear.

For long-term investors -- whom Mr. Grigoli defines as those willing to watch the prices of their stocks go down 25% "without losing any sleep," he recommends selected consumer-oriented stocks. They include food producer CPC International, Procter & Gamble and health care and personal-care giants Johnson & Johnson and Gillette.

Mr. Grigoli's near-term view of the overall market, however, is decidedly negative. Yesterday, he predicted that the Dow Jones Industrial Average will fall to 2500 because of rising oil prices and a weakening U.S. economy. In a full-blown recession, Mr. Grigoli expects the market would trade still lower.

Given the stock market's current volatility, Mr. Grigoli also believes a 50 or 100-point rally in the industrial average is a real possibility, especially if the U.S. launches a successful military strike at Iraq. But in that case, Mr. Grigoli says, "I would use it as an opportunity to sell" other stocks.
