International: Japanese Agency Calls Expansion Sustainable Despite Oil Worries

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 08/07/90

TOKYO -- Japan's current economic expansion is healthy and sustainable, notwithstanding the prospect of higher world oil prices and interest rates, a government agency said. But the Economic Planning Agency urged the cabinet of Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu to accelerate deregulation of the economy, enforce antitrust laws more stringently and guard against the polarization of the haves and have-nots in Japan's increasingly wealthy society.

The recommendations, contained in the agency's annual economic white paper, mirror some of the suggestions the U.S. put forward in recent bilateral talks on structural economic changes.

In large part, the report praises Japan's management of its current economic expansion. Now in its 45th month, the expansion is the country's second longest since World War II. A 57-month period in the late 1960s remains postwar Japan's longest boom. The average rate of growth during the current expansion is 5.7%, compared with 12.1% in the earlier cycle. As in the previous major expansion, strong consumer spending and capital investment are the leading factors.

"We see no domestic reason why the expansion would come to an end," said Shousuke Tanaka, director-general of the Economic Planning Agency's research division.

Even the impact on world oil prices of Iraq's invasion of Kuwait doesn't threaten Japan's health, he said. In the past, oil has been the Achilles' heel of Japan's economic juggernaut. The surge in crude prices during the oil crises of 1973 and 1979 set off inflation and curtailed growth. But today, Mr. Tanaka said, Japan's dependence on oil for economic productivity is markedly lower. Nowadays, he said, an increase of 10% in world oil prices would translate to a 0.1 percentage-point increase in consumer prices.

Japan's strengths, the agency said, lie in its corporate innovation and use of technology. But the economy also has benefited from intercompany relationships -- many Japanese concerns have close links to suppliers and distributors -- that help industry keep costs down.

The agency warned that outsiders shouldn't be excluded from that system and said that deregulation of the economy would help. But it also cautioned that Japan's antitrust authorities should be given more power to dismantle alliances that go beyond the boundaries of law and international acceptance. Mr. Tanaka suggested the country's Fair Trade Commission should have power to recommend criminal punishment for violations of antitrust codes.

Another big boost for Japan's industry has been a cooperative labor force. But the agency warned that equality among Japanese workers is diminishing.

"More than 50% of Japanese people live in houses with land, but the other half doesn't have land" in a country where property prices have skyrocketed, he said. "We are seeing a very large social issue beginning to emerge."

The planning agency also urged the government to do more abroad. Japan now spends about $9 billion, an amount equivalent to about 0.8% of its economic output, on official development assistance. The agency recommended that the proportion be increased.
