Merrill Lynch Said To Be Seeking Buyer for Unit

  • Author: Mitchell Pacelle
  • Date: 08/07/90

Merrill Lynch & Co. is searching for a buyer for Merrill Lynch, Hubbard Inc., its real estate investment and syndication unit, according to two people familiar with the company's efforts. Hubbard, which has about 105 employees, manages approximately $1.5 billion of commercial and residential real estate for more than 230,000 investors in 10 partnerships.

Merrill Lynch has been retreating to its core of securities brokerage and investment banking businesses for more than a year. The Hubbard unit has been for sale since early this year, according to people familiar with the efforts. But turmoil in the market for real estate limited partnerships has reduced the number of potential buyers.

Two syndicators who remain strong -- Atlanta-based Equitable Real Estate Investment Management Inc., and Chicago-based JMB Realty -- are considering the acquisition of Hubbard, according to people familiar with Merrill's efforts.

"Hubbard is under constant review, but no decision to sell it has been made as of this date," said Merrill spokesman William Clark, who would not comment on whether the unit is for sale.

Merrill officials decline to disclose how much they are asking for the unit, as do officials from both Equitable and JMB Realty.

Between 1981 and 1987, Hubbard raised $1.7 billion dollars to purchase real estate through a series of six MLH Income Realty Partnerships. Hubbard made cash-based acquisitions of commercial properties with strong cash flows and good potentials for appreciation, according to Larry Kay, who follows Hubbard for Standard & Poor's Corp.

After flourishing in the mid-1980s, the market for real estate limited partnerships was crippled by the 1986 Tax Reform Act. Previously, investors were able to write off losses from real esate limited partnerships against ordinary income. After 1986, the losses could only be written off against passive income, such as income from other limited partnerships. The collapse of real estate markets in the South, Southwest, and Northeast also stemmed the flow of investors seeking to capitalize on runaway real estate prices.

The deterioration of the market threw Integrated Resources Inc. in New York and Southmark Corp. of Dallas, two syndicators that relied heavily on investors seeking tax shelters, into bankruptcy-court proceedings. Unlike Hubbard, these two syndicators had highly leveraged portfolios.
