McDonald's Plans Tactics to Boost Its Sales Margins

  • Author: Richard Gibson
  • Date: 08/07/90

McDonald's Corp., pummeled by the stock market in recent days, sought to soothe nervous Wall Street analysts by promising to reduce the cost of new restaurants and using discounting as a sales tactic. Some analysts came away from the conference call with McDonald's disappointed at what they didn't hear.

"I didn't learn anything new with respect to the fundamental issues such as turning sales around in the U.S.," said Michael Mueller of Montgomery Securities Inc., San Francisco.

Similarly, Prudential-Bache Securities restaurant analyst Leslie Steppel said, "There wasn't any new strategy revealed -- just a rehashing. I didn't come away feeling that anything was going to change."

But McDonald's executives did say they intend to take perhaps $100,000 out of the cost of building a new domestic store, primarily in equipment and construction savings. The current average cost of about $1.5 million has come under fire lately for rising more rapidly than the sales rate.

The company, which blamed the softening economy and intense competition for no-growth domestic sales, reiterated it is willing to use discounting as a tactic -- but not a strategy -- to improve sales at the expense of margins.

Also, executives told analysts that despite the low current price of its stock, McDonald's is likely to reduce its share repurchase plan. McDonald's closed yesterday at $29.125, down $1.125 in composite New York Stock Exchange trading.

In the past two weeks the price of McDonald's shares have fallen nearly $10 a share, as its second-quarter earnings came in below some Wall Street estimates and as nervous investors have deserted even blue-chip issues such as McDonald's.

The company still plans to open more than 300 restaurants in the U.S. this year, Ms. Steppel said. But it struck her as a contradiction that McDonald's is relying increasingly on franchisees domestically while putting more company-owned stores overseas. Franchisees' profit margins typically are lower than that of company-owned stores.

"They indicated that the environment was competitive and difficult and was likely to remain so for the second half," Mr. Mueller said. And despite intense price-cutting by some fast-food competitors, he said that "discounting is not going to be employed as a strategy. It's one of an arsenal of tactics."

Nevertheless, McDonald's told analysts and portfolio managers it is comfortable with per-share earnings estimates of between $2.20 and $2.25 for the year.

As for McDonald's pizza, in test since last summer, executives said it is at least two years away from becoming part of the domestic menu, analysts reported. Earlier, some had thought the product would be rolled out much sooner.
