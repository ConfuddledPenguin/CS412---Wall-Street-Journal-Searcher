World Stock Markets: Tokyo Stocks Reel From Iraq Invasion; Some Expect Repeat of Earlier Plunge

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 08/07/90

TOKYO -- Iraq's conquest of Kuwait has stock markets reeling around the world. But in Tokyo, stocks are taking their second big drubbing this year. Indeed, some analysts now fear that the Tokyo stock market is headed for a repeat of the plunge that wiped out about 28% of the exchange's value in the first quarter.

Amid growing concern about the instability in the Middle East, the impact of higher oil prices and the possibility of a monetary tightening by Japan's central bank, the Nikkei average of 225 big stocks was off sharply Monday and Tuesday.

In Tokyo Tuesday, the indicator was down 1327.67 points, or 4.6%, at 27271.86 at the close of the morning session. Monday, the index had tumbled 916.23 points, or 3.1%, to 28599.53.

At its levels Tuesday morning, the index now is 11,644.01 points, or 30%, below its record high of 38915.87 set on the last trading day of 1989. It also is below the previous low of this year of 28002.07, reached April 2.

Volume on the first section was estimated at 150 million shares, down from an estimated 170 million shares Monday.

Of the traded issues, losers outnumbered gainers 563 to six, while 10 issues remained unchanged.

"It's tough to see anything right now," said Mike Morizumi, a strategist at Shearson Lehman Hutton Asia Inc. "There are no cyclical patterns to look at."

Japan's bond market also took a beating Tuesday morning, with yields on the No. 119 benchmark 10-year government bond climbing for the first time above 8%. That means the gap between the bond yield and the earnings yield on Japanese stocks, which now averages about 2.2%, is almost at an unprecedented 6%, Mr. Morizumi said, adding: "Hopefully, this won't last."

There are similarities between Tokyo's earlier downturn and the current one. Stocks have been highly volatile -- partly, some brokers say, because of computerized program trading. And the fear of higher interest rates is driving down the stock market.

It was the Bank of Japan's decision to raise the discount rate that turned a record-setting stock market rally in December into a nosedive in January. The decline accelerated in March after the central bank announced another increase in the discount rate, which is the fee it charges on loans to financial institutions.

Now concern is growing about yet another rate increase. Even before Iraq invaded Kuwait on Thursday, worries about higher rates were undermining Japanese stocks. Japan's economy is booming at about a 7% to 8% annual rate; money supply growth is accelerating; and there is an acute labor shortage in the country. In addition, a weak yen has been contributing to inflationary fears. In response to these factors, market interest rates have been climbing steadily to where the yield on the government's benchmark 10-year bond is at 7.94%, up from 6.75% in early July.

The invasion of Iraq has all but ensured an increase in the discount rate, economists say, by driving up world oil prices.

Japan depends on imported oil for 58% of its energy needs, and any increase in oil prices could translate into inflationary pressures. To avert those pressures, economists say, the Bank of Japan is likely to raise the discount rate -- it currently stands at 5.25% -- soon. The central bank has no policy meeting scheduled for this week, but analysts don't rule out an emergency decision.

The timing of a rate increase, though, is critical. "We don't know what would be appropriate," said Mikio Fujino, an institutional salesman at Nikko Securities Co., "but it's very, very important that it doesn't come too early to be effective or too late to stop inflation."

Analysts say the Japanese stock market has already adjusted fairly well for the expected increase, even if it proves to be as big as a full-percentage-point jump in the discount rate. Still, investors are alarmed by events in the Mideast because the investors fear that more conflict could lead to tighter oil supplies and higher prices. That would feed inflation in Japan -- and might prompt even another interest-rate increase.

"The invasion was bad timing for Japan," said Pelham Smithers, an analyst at Shearson Lehman Hutton Asia Inc. "The market had expected a short-term fluctuation in interest rates, but now we have to make a correction for the new bad news, plus continue the adjustment for what had already happened."

If the discount rate is raised, yen-denominated Japanese government bonds would become relatively more attractive to big Japanese institutional investors than U.S. Treasury bonds, which are denominated in dollars and thus carry a foreign-exchange risk for the Japanese. A quarterly auction of $32.5 billion in Treasury debt instruments is scheduled this week. To attract Japanese money, U.S. interest rates might have to move higher even if there isn't a discount rate increase in Japan, because market rates in Tokyo recently have been rising, analysts say.

With that background, reasons Mamoru Yamazaki, an economist at Goldman, Sachs & Co., "if the Bank of Japan will raise the {discount rate}, it will be after the U.S. Treasury auction."

An increase in Japanese rates would hamper any easing of credit by the U.S. central bank and might help send the U.S. into a recession. Japanese government economists said they aren't worried, however. Shousuke Tanaka, director-general of the Economic Planning Agency, said that "even if there were a recession in the U.S., I feel there would be very small direct impact on Japan." Domestic corporate investment and consumer demand are growing fast enough, he said, to sustain Japanese economic growth. Economists point out that Japan's economy is more efficient and more resilient than it was during the oil shock of the 1970s.

Mr. Tanaka also doubts that higher oil prices will have a long-term impact on Japan's economy, and other economists share his view. Stock market participants, however, remain wary.

"There is no ability to forecast what will happen in the Middle East, and people tend to take the worst scenario," said Yutaka Nakai, a strategist at Daiwa Securities Co. "We don't fear panic selling, but there aren't many fundamentals to be optimistic about right now."

In yesterday's trading, volume on the first section was estimated at 330 million shares, down from 431 million shares Friday. Decliners outnumbered advancers 997 to 30, while 34 issues were unchanged. The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section slumped 76.14 points, or 3.50%, to 2098.53.

The second section index plunged 198.40 points, or 4.51%, to 4199.05. Volume in the section was estimated at 11 million shares, down from 28.3 million shares Friday.

Meanwhile, the Frankfurt market's DAX Index dropped 5.4%, to 1740.93, off 100.01 points, and London's Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index was down 2.8%, to 2220.2, a decline of 64.4 points.

Although West Germany gets less than 2% of its oil from Iraq and Kuwait, the country still is heavily dependent on oil imports. Britain, as an oil producer-exporter, is somewhat cushioned from the current crisis.

"The potential downside risk on the U.K. is probably less than on any other major market," said Martin Paling, international investment strategist at James Capel & Co. in London.

But given the political and military uncertainties in the gulf, few experts would predict near-term stock market performance. "You don't know where an index goes in a war -- flip a coin, spin a wheel, throw the dice," said Thomas R. Holmes, manager of institutional sales at Schroeder Muenchmeyer Hengst & Co. in Frankfurt.

Added Mr. Paling: "The two big imponderables are how high the price of oil will go, and how long the price will stay up."

The declines in London and Frankfurt were broadly based, with few major stocks able to counter the trend. In late trading in London, British Petroleum gained 2 pence to 367 pence ($6.81), but big losers included Imperial Chemical Industries, down 52 pence to 958 pence; Rothmans, off 45 pence to 719 pence; Glaxo, down 25 pence to 762 pence; B.A.T, down 30 pence to 541 pence, and Thorn EMI, off 24 pence to 680 pence.

In Frankfurt, Deutsche Bank closed at 755 marks, down 44 marks ($474.54, down $27.66); Daimler-Benz, at 771.50 marks, down 63 marks; Lufthansa, at 136 marks, down 11 marks; Porsche, at 1076 marks, down 56 marks, and Siemens, at 688 marks, down 36.50 marks.

The steep drops prompted some big investors to venture bets that the bottom is near. "We did a wee bit of buying on the {price} falls -- we aren't unduly worried," said James C. Fisher, a fund manager for Scottish Amicable Investment Managers Ltd. in Glasgow.

Mr. Fisher said that on Friday and yesterday, Scottish Amicable bought B.A.T, Glaxo and Hanson shares. "We saw an opportunity to pick up cheap stock," he said. "Our market will be supported by fundamentals. It won't go much lower. I suspect we may get a bounce {upward} from here. But then again, a lot depends on the gulf situation."

Meanwhile, shares closed lower in Paris, Stockholm, Milan, Madrid, Zurich, Amsterdam and Brussels. South African gold stocks closed narrowly mixed. And stocks ended lower in Sydney, Taipei, Hong Kong, Singapore, Seoul, Manila and Wellington.
