Rubbermaid Signs Accord to Acquire Eldon Industries

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 08/07/90

Rubbermaid Inc. said it signed a definitive agreement to buy Eldon Industries Inc. in a stock transaction that will expand Rubbermaid's office products business. The acquisition, the company's largest to date, should enable Rubbermaid to take advantage of the growing service-related industry and the creation of more home offices. The purchase also gives Rubbermaid a strong brand name and more clout in selling to major retailers.

Under terms, each of Eldon's 9.3 million shares will be converted into no fewer than 0.5438 and no more than 0.6320 Rubbermaid share. In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Rubbermaid closed at $36.875, down $2.625, giving the transaction an estimated value of $186.5 million to $216.7 million. The transaction wasn't announced until after the market, which plummeted yesterday, was closed.

The number of Rubbermaid shares to be issued will be from 5,448,000 to 6,332,000. The Wooster, Ohio, company said the exact number will equal $23.75 divided by the price of Rubbermaid shares during the 10 trading days ending three days prior to Eldon's stockholders meeting. The meeting's date hasn't been set, but Eldon officials said they hope the meeting will be in November.

The agreement is subject to Eldon shareholder and regulatory approval. Rubbermaid said it has already received commitments from stockholders representing 29% of Eldon's stock. Rubbermaid also said the transaction would be accounted for as a pooling of interest and thus wouldn't affect 1990 per-share earnings.

Eldon, which earned $8.6 million on sales of $112.6 million in 1989, has a strong brand name in a variety of office products from plastic to leather and wood. The Inglewood, Calif., company has a stronger presence in the commercial market than Rubbermaid, which has relied more on the consumer side of office products with its wastebaskets, chairmats and files.

"Eldon has a larger commercial business," said Jay Freedman, an analyst with Kidder, Peabody & Co. "Now when Rubbermaid starts dealing with some of the larger retailers it can go in with a much broader product line."

Rubbermaid has been interested in acquiring companies that complement its own product lines, have strong brand names, and synergies in manufacturing. Eldon uses an injection molding process, which is a Rubbermaid hallmark.

Richard Agins, Eldon vice president and general counsel, said the agreement "maximizes shareholder value and gives Eldon more options for growth." He said the combination is partly a response to the emergence of office product "superstores." Analysts have said that such stores put strong downward pressure on wholesale office supply prices. Citing this trend among other things, Avery International Corp. and Dennison Manufacturing Co., two large office products concerns, agreed to merge in May.
