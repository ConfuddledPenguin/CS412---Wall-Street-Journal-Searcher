Defense Firm To Cut 2,000 In Consolidation --- General Dynamics Layoffs Are Latest in Big Wave Sweeping Arms Industry

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 08/07/90

General Dynamics Corp., in yet another sign of weakness among weapons makers, said it would cut up to 2,000 jobs by the end of next year as it consolidates two of its missile-making operations. The company's Valley Systems and Pomona divisions, both based in Southern California, currently employ a total of 9,200. Overall, St. Louis-based General Dynamics has a work force of nearly 103,000.

"While we all welcomed a diminished threat from the Soviet Union and the end of the Cold War, these changes have brought unfortunate personal economic consequences for many people in the defense industry," said Michael C. Keel, who has been named vice president and general manager of the new streamlined unit, the Air Defense Systems Division. "In order to remain competitive . . . General Dynamics must more efficiently use its existing resources and reduce overhead."

The layoff announcement at the nation's No. 2 Pentagon contractor is part of a giant wave that is rolling through the arms-manufacturing industry. It is also a reminder that even as world tensions flare with the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, defense contractors still face diminishing military budgets and eroding business bases.

The turmoil in the Persion Gulf "won't really have an effect unless it becomes a full-blown world incident," explained Peter Aseritis of First Boston Corp., adding that it still behooves companies such as General Dynamics to "tighten up and buckle down."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, General Dynamics shares bucked the trend of a widely falling market and closed at $30, up $1.

The layoff news came after the Big Board's closing bell, suggesting that despite the assessment of Mr. Aseritis and many other securities analysts, investors still believe defense stocks are a relatively good play now because of the conflict in the Middle East.

Just last week, on the eve of the Iraqi incursion, United Technologies Corp.'s Pratt & Whitney aircraft engine-building subsidiary said it would eliminate 4,000 jobs, or 11% of its domestic employment, over the next three years because of Pentagon spending cuts. Many other aerospace companies, including Lockheed Corp., Northrop Corp. and McDonnell Douglas Corp., also have been trimming their rolls of late.

At General Dymanics, a spokesman said, corporatewide employment already has dwindled by 1,150 positions in 1990, and another 1,700 to 2,000 slots are expected to vanish by the end of the year. Besides the Standard, Sparrow and Stinger missiles made at the Pomona and Valley Systems divisions, General Dynamics also assembles tanks, jet-fighters and submarines.

More than 600 of the layoffs at Air Defense Systems will be the result of disposing of duplicate responsibilities as the two divisions are joined.

The company said that between its sites in the Los Angeles bedroom community of Pomona and nearby Rancho Cucamonga, where the Valley Systems division is located, it will have more capacity than is required to meet its future needs. However, it has not made any decision at this point to move any of its production lines.
