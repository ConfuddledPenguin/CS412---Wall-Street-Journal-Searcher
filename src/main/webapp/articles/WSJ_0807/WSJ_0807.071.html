OTC Focus: Market Plunges 4.2% to Lowest Level Since March 1989; Larger Stocks Dive

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 08/07/90

Over-the-counter stocks dove 4.2% to their lowest levels since March 1989 as investors and professional dealers continued to shun smaller stocks amid anxiety about the economy and developments in the Middle East. The Nasdaq Composite Index plummeted 17.42 to 400.04, the fourth largest one-day point drop in the index's history. Volume eased but remained heavy as 174.8 million shares changed hands, down from 203.2 million Friday. Declining stocks trounced advancers, 2,058 to 421.

Investors continued to bail out of the OTC market's largest stocks and take profits in the technology, emerging growth and health care shares that had been their favorites until mid-July. The Nasdaq 100 Index of largest OTC issues plunged 22.30 to 402.91, a 5.2% drop; the Nasdaq Industrials of more than 2,000 larger issues fell 21.34 to 426.59, a 4.8% decline.

Among the day's largest losers, Nike fell 7 3/4 to 72 3/4; Liz Claiborne dropped 3 1/2 to 25; Amgen dropped 4 1/4 to 81 1/2; Sun Microsystems slipped 3 1/8 to 27 5/8; Microsoft tumbled 2 3/4 to 60 1/2, and Intel eased 2 to 37 1/4. Among foreign issues, Class B American depositary receipts of L.M. Ericsson Telephone, the Swedish telecommunications giant, plummeted 15 1/4 to 198 1/2; Reuters ADRs fell 3 1/4 to 52 3/4.

Trading remained particularly heavy among small investors using the Small Order Execution System operated by the National Association of Securities Dealers, said NASD President Joseph R. Hardiman. Known as "SOES," the system allows investors to automatically execute up to five orders of 1,000 shares each at the best available bid or ask price indicated by all the market makers in a stock. NASD considers the system to be a good indicator of activity among individual investors.

Volume on the system was 60% above normal yesterday and Friday, according to Mr. Hardiman. Volume on NASD's smallorder system and similar systems run by a number of brokerage firms accounts for more than 50% of all shares traded over-the-counter, he added.

Some portfolio managers and traders complained that the fall in OTC shares has been exacerbated by a herd mentality among market makers: They undercut each other's bids but refuse to step up and buy shares. The steep declines in some OTC shares is "due to the fact that everyone is acting in unison" rather than some market makers venturing to buy shares when others are marking down their prices, said Alan Radlo, manager of the $730 million Fidelity OTC Fund.

"Market makers are running down their bids. It's like two-year-olds running away from the market," said Mr. Radlo. He suggested that dealers who advertise their bids on the automated Nasdaq system should be obligated to commit a minimum number of shares to the prices they advertise.

For orders of 1,000 shares or less, market markers are indeed obligated to honor their advertised prices, Mr. Hardiman points out, while larger transactions are negotiated between investors and dealers. Mr. Hardiman defended the performance of the Nasdaq system yesterday and Friday. "We had no complaints about the inability to get trades made," he said, adding that rumors Friday that some traders weren't answering their telephones were unfounded.

"I don't think there's anything you can do to prevent these kinds of sell-offs," Mr. Hardiman said. "There's an awful lot of uncertainty out there, particularly among institutional investors and dealers as to what's going to happen in the Mideast and as to what's going to happen in our own domestic economy."

Redemptions at several mutual funds that trade OTC stocks were running slightly ahead of normal, but not excessively so. Both T. Rowe Price's $1 billion New Horizons Fund and its Science & Technology Fund each had net outflows Friday of about $1 million and didn't see any significant pick-up in redemptions yesterday, according to a T. Rowe Price spokesman.

Two other fund managers said redemptions were less than they anticipated. Binkley Shorts, manager of the Over-The-Counter Securities Fund, Boston, said redemptions were below his daily average. Lawrence Auriana, manager of the Kauffman Fund, New York, also said he received fewer redemptions than he expected. But both fund managers indicated the market has been tough.

"It's a painful, ugly market out there. There's no place to hide," said Mr. Shorts.

Among the day's other losers, American Software Class A shares plunged more than 25%, or 3 1/8 to 9 5/8. Friday, the maker of IBM-compatible software indicated that net income for the fiscal first quarter ended July 31 will be 11 cents to 17 cents a share, compared with 17 cents a year earlier.

Some energy issues remained strong as oil prices continued to rise. Falcon Oil & Gas rose 1 to 5 3/4; Baker Hughes warrants added 1/2 to 9 1/4, and Hamilton Oil advanced 1 1/4 to 43 3/4.
