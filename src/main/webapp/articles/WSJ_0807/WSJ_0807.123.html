Crude Prices Hit Highest Levels In Over 4 Years --- Many Analysts Say Oil Costs May Rise Much Further If Gulf Tensions Escalate

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 08/07/90

Petroleum prices exploded on crisis buying to the highest levels in more than four years. Prices surged $3 to $4 a barrel around the world as Iraqi troops occupying Kuwait appeared to be digging in on Saudi Arabia's border.

In the U.S., crude oil futures topped $28 a barrel, a level of domestic petroleum prices not seen since before the oil-price collapse of early 1986.

Some oil analysts turned cautious, suggesting prices may be near their peaks after the run-up of more than $10 a barrel in recent weeks. But most said that if Middle East tensions continued to escalate, prices could still go far higher to close in on the records of the early 1980s of $30 to $40 a barrel.

"It's a free-for-all," said Michael Rothman, vice president and a senior energy analyst at Merrill Lynch Capital Markets. "People are concerned not only about the loss of Iraqi and Kuwaiti oil but fear that Saudi Arabia's production may also be in jeopardy."

Global sanctions continued to tighten against President Saddam Hussein of Iraq. An embargo threatened to choke off from world oil supplies some four million barrels a day of crude oil and petroleum products that Iraq and Kuwait had been exporting.

While industry officials were skeptical that all the oil flowing from Iraq and Kuwait could be cut off, there were indications yesterday that the embargo had begun to work. Because some of the tankers that were to pick up Iraqi oil at the Mediterranean port of Ceyhan, Turkey, sailed away after the embargo began, Iraq stopped pumping crude through one of its pipelines there. It also reduced the flow of oil through another pipeline across Turkey.

These developments, combined with the rapid rise in oil prices, also pushed the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries further into disarray. Over the weekend, some of the OPEC oil ministers had indicated they might hold an emergency session to try to take steps to slow the run-up in oil prices.

The idea would be for some of those with spare producing capacity to begin producing above the quotas that have been so controversial until oil prices began rising. And some of the countries that want a more moderate approach to pricing to protect their market shares of the future are understood to have begun stepping up their output.

Yesterday, however, OPEC's Vienna headquarters reported that it hadn't been advised of any requests for an emergency meeting. "OPEC is not doing anything," an official said, "except to wait and see."

Several oil ministers called, meanwhile, for adherence to the production quotas that were re-established last month. The July agreement had set a ceiling of 22.5 million barrels a day for total OPEC production and brought the United Arab Emirates, a chronic overproducer, back into the fold with a higher quota.

It's generally assumed the July agreement was nullified by the fact that one Persian Gulf member of OPEC, Iraq, invaded and occupied another gulf member, Kuwait. But Mana Said Otaiba, the Emirates' oil minister, was among several ministers yesterday urging continued compliance with the agreement. He sent a message to Sadek Boussena, Algeria's oil minister and OPEC president, calling on all member states not to exceed their quotas "under the current circumstances in the {Persian Gulf} region."

Immediate oil shortages aren't expected even if Iraqi and Kuwaiti crudes are cut off completely by embargoes or by blockades. J. Robinson West, president of Petroleum Finance Co., noted that world oil inventories are at a nine-year high and that demand for OPEC oil is less than the 22.5 million barrels a day allowed under the production ceiling. But he suggested major differences between the "actual structure of the market and the psychology of the market."

Whether warranted or not, oil prices leaped record amounts yesterday. Some U.S. oil companies increased their field postings, what their refineries are paying, for West Texas Intermediate, the benchmark crude, by a record $3.50 a barrel to $26.50. On the New York Mercantile Exchange, the futures price of a WTI-like crude for September delivery jumped a record $3.56 to settle at $28.05 a barrel.

"What was a surplus in oil inventories only a week ago suddenly is considered balance today," said William H. Brown III, international oil analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co.

According to studies by Mr. Brown, a two-month disruption in Iraq and Kuwait exports would carry WTI to a new peak of $29 a barrel even if other nations such as Saudi Arabia produce more oil to fill the gap. If others in OPEC don't try to make up the lost supplies, "you are talking $37 plus for WTI," he said.
