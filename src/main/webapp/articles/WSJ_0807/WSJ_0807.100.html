International: ANC Puts Down Its Arms In Battle With South Africa

  • Author: Roger Thurow
  • Date: 08/07/90

JOHANNESBURG, South Africa -- The African National Congress suspended its 30-year armed struggle against the white minority government, clearing the way for the start of negotiations over a new constitution based on black-white power sharing. The announcement came in the early morning hours Tuesday after a full day of talks between the ANC and the government. ANC leader Nelson Mandela said his organization's infiltration of trained guerrillas and weapons into South Africa would be stopped immediately, as would all military activities inside the country. In return, the government said it would begin a phased release of political prisoners and would grant indemnity to some 20,000 ANC exiles so they can return to South Africa. A joint ANC-government committee will work out the details and the timing of these moves.

The marathon meeting in Pretoria -- the second face-to-face session between the two sides since the government unbanned the ANC and other opposition groups in February -- seems to have cleared away the major obstacles to full negotiations on a new constitution. Both the government and the ANC say they hope these talks can begin by early next year. In a joint statement, the two sides said they were acting "in the interest of moving as speedily as possible toward a negotiated peaceful political settlement."

At a news conference following the meeting, Mr. Mandela called the suspension of the armed struggle "a very significant concession" on the ANC's part. And South African President F.W. de Klerk said it marked an important turning point in bringing peace to the country, which has been wracked by racial conflict for decades. Since the unbanning of the ANC earlier this year and other moves by the government to end its policies of racial segregation known as apartheid, the level of violence has escalated, both from the heightened political activity of the blacks and from a right-wing white backlash.

Mr. Mandela and other ANC activists founded an armed wing, called Umkhonto we Sizwe, or Spear of the Nation, in 1960 following the government's banning of the ANC. A few years later, Mr. Mandela and many of the ANC leaders not in exile were arrested and sentenced to life imprisonment for conspiring to overthrow the government. Mr. Mandela was freed from prison after 27 years in February.

During the years of its armed activities, the ANC considered itself to be at war with the government. Its guerrillas mainly targeted their attacks on government installations and on those in the black community considered to be government collaborators. Still, occasional bombs in the segregated white residential areas spread fear among the white community. In many shopping centers and cinemas in Johannesburg, visitors are routinely body-searched before entering.

In recent months, the ANC's armed struggle has been largely dormant, though the organization has continued to move guerrillas and arms into the country. Last month, the government claimed it uncovered "Operation Vula," an ANC plan drawn up in 1987 to strengthen its underground in South Africa. The police made dozens of arrests and uncovered several arms caches, and alleged that it was part of a plot by the South African Communist Party to overthrow the government in case the present negotiations fail.

After yesterday's meeting, Mr. Mandela said the Communist Party, which is an ally of the ANC, also would be bound by the agreement to suspend the armed struggle. The current and former leaders of Umkhonto weSizwe are members of the Communist Party as well as of the ANC.

Despite the suspension of its armed activities, the ANC says it will continue with its protest politics, which include mass marches, boycotts and strikes, until apartheid is ended and the blacks have a share of the political power. Through these actions, the ANC is also hoping to keep the loyalty of many black youths, who have been the main supporters of Umkhonto we Sizwe. Mr. Mandela said the ANC also will continue to press the government to restrain the police in its dealing with black protesters.
