Economy: Fed Has Decided Against Easing Credit for Now

  • Author: Alan Murray and Peter Truell
  • Date: 07/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Reserve decided not to ease credit at its meeting this week, despite signs of a slowing economy, according to government officials. The Fed's lack of action was apparent yesterday when the central bank made no moves to lower short-term interest rates. However, officials said they believed the directive approved by the Fed at its meeting earlier this week provided Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan with the freedom to ease credit if further signs of economic slowing appeared. A key indicator of the economy's health comes out this morning when the Labor Department releases employment figures for June.

The Fed's lack of action prompted an unusual rebuke from Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady yesterday.

In comment to a small group of reporters, Mr. Brady argued, "In the U.S., it seems to me, it's not a wise idea to emphasize an undying, forever attack on inflation if it jeopardizes growth."

In recent months, the Fed has been unwilling to cut interest rates because of its continued concern about inflation. That has led to increasingly loud administration jawboning for lower interest rates.

"In our opinion, the inflation is in the services area," Mr. Brady said. "It's not clear you can get that down to zero without jeopardizing growth." He acknowledged that "others feel differently," but he declared, "I think they're wrong."

Troubles with the savings and loans and with real estate "are becoming more apparent," Mr. Brady continued. "It's quite clear that they would be helped by lower interest rates."

An agreement between the White House and Congress to narrow the deficit could get the Fed to move, according to Mr. Brady. "I think Chairman Greenspan has indicated that if we get a budget agreement, he believes interest rates will come down. I do too." But Mr. Brady added, "I don't think we should wait around."

Nevertheless, Mr. Brady noted that the Fed "carries on its own deliberations" and is "an enormously sensible group of individuals."
