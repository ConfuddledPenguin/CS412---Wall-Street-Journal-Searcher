Spain's New Gas Firm Plans to Offer Shares On Local Exchange

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/06/90

MADRID -- A Spanish gas utility being set up by state-controlled oil firm Repsol S.A. and savings bank La Caixa will offer shares to outside investors, in a public flotation that could be worth between 45 billion pesetas and 80 billion pesetas ($445.1 million and $791.3 million). Repsol Chairman Oscar Fanjul said the two partners are in discussions with Spain's National Stock Market Commission over details of a plan to merge their gas interests, announced last week.

Depending on the results of an audit and the size of the assets to be transferred by both partners, Mr. Fanjul said the new mega-utility is expected to be worth between 90 billion pesetas and 170 billion pesetas. Its shares will be listed on the stock exchange through a public flotation that could take place some time in the first half of 1991, he said in an interview.

The project calls for the formation of a holding company jointly owned by La Caixa and the state oil and gas agency Instituto Nacional de Hidrocarburos, which controls Repsol. INH will hold its 50% stake through Repsol and its sister company, Enagas S.A, the state gas firm.

The joint holding company, in turn, will own at least 51% of a mega-utility controlling local gas utilities whose capital will be open to outside shareholders.

Neither of the two companies to be created has yet been given a name, and details of the project will be determined after an audit of both firms' gas interests.

However, the new utility's principal assets will be controlling stakes in Barcelona-based Catalana de Gas S.A., of which La Caixa owns 24% and Repsol 14%, and Gas Madrid S.A., of which INH controls 87% directly and through Repsol.

A final decision concerning the size of the new utility's Gas Madrid stake will depend on an audit to value the Catalana shares to be transferred, Mr. Fanjul said.

Because the project involves the takeover of Catalana, the new utility will also be obliged to offer minority Catalana shareholders a chance to exchange their shares for shares in the new firm. The number of shares to be floated publicly will depend on the shareholders' response to this offer, Mr. Fanjul said.

Analysts said the operation could present an attractive opportunity for investors to gain access to a new Spanish gas utility on the model of British Gas or Gaz de France.

But they said the success of the operation is likely to depend on the terms offered to Catalana shareholders, many of whom traditionally have shown strong loyalty to the company.

An additional uncertainty is the attitude of British Gas, which recently acquired 10% of Catalana and which hasn't shown any interest at this stage in joining the Repsol-La Caixa project.
