Credit Markets: Bonds Fall on Surprisingly Strong Auto Sales, Creating Anxieties About Today's Jobs Data

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 07/06/90

NEW YORK -- News that car sales surged in late June gave big bond dealers the jitters yesterday, prompting them to sell bonds out of fear that today's employment report may also deliver surprising results. Prior to the release of the car-sales data, many bond dealers loaded up on long-term Treasury bonds. They were betting that the employment report would show an economy that's growing weaker and revive speculation among investors that the Federal Reserve would lower interest rates to stave off recession. That, in turn, would cause bond prices to soar.

But after the car-sales data were released, "the tone became a little more apprehensive" as players reconsidered just how weak the jobs statistics will be, said Joseph Liro, a vice president and money-market economist at S.G. Warburg Securities & Co. Inc.

Domestic auto sales were estimated yesterday to have been running at a seasonally adjusted annual rate of eight million units in the June 21-30 period, the highest rate since February. Industry analysts had expected an annual rate of about seven million units.

In response to the data, bond prices slid. The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond fell more than 1/4 point, or $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, rose to 8.40%.

Bond-market analysts said the auto data, which was the highlight of an otherwise dull session, inspired some market participants to liquidate their holdings of longer-term bonds. Most of the trading was executed by bond dealers, while retail investors remained quiet on the sidelines.

"Some dealers decided to lighten up their positions and take profits in response to the auto-sales report, which came in much stronger than expected. The report introduced a bit of caution to the market," said William Sullivan, director of money-market research at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. "If auto sales had come in weaker, then {dealers} may have decided to buy the market instead of sell it."

Mr. Sullivan said the price movements were exaggerated by the market's thin trading condition, which was curbed by the lingering effect of the Independence holiday on Wednesday and caution ahead of the jobs data.

Trading typically slows to a crawl on the day before the release of jobs statistics, and this session was no exception.

The data are considered the most important of the government's monthly economic releases because they provide the first official reading of how the economy fared in a given month.

Surprises in the report often jar the bond market. The softer-than-expected May employment report issued on June 1, for instance, pushed bond prices up more than a full point, a gain of about $10 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

The employment report, however, is taking on more significance this time because it comes on the heels of the Federal Reserve's two-day Federal Open Market Committee meeting that ended Tuesday.

There is speculation that members of the FOMC may have decided to ease monetary policy if the jobs data point up extremely sluggish economic growth.

"I think the Fed adopted an asymmetrical directive with a bias toward easing," said Mr. Liro of S.G. Warburg. But "to get an immediate ease, you're going to have to need some very weak numbers" in the jobs report, he adds.

Treasury Securities

Yields rose on long-term and intermediate-term Treasury securities yesterday, but short-term rates declined due largely to reports that Bush administration officials are considering a proposal to impose a tax on the sale of securities as a way of reducing the budget deficit.

The excise tax would apply to the sale of stocks, options and debt, but not Treasury securities. Some analysts said that money was leaving the stock market and flowing into Treasury bills, which is where some investors park cash temporarily.

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of about 103 20/32 to yield 8.40% compared with a price of 103 28/32 to yield 8.38% on Tuesday. The financial markets were closed Wednesday in observance of Independence Day. The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 103 1/32 to yield 8.395%, the price was down from 103 4/32 to yield 8.38% on Tuesday.

But short-term rates were lower. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.67% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.91%, while the rate on six-month bills ended at 7.58% to yield 7.97%.

Junk, Corporate Bonds

The junk bonds of Southland Corp., which operates the 7-Eleven convenience-store chain, rose sharply yesterday on news that the Securities and Exchange Commission approved its plan to refinance $1.8 billion in debt and some cumulative exchangeable preferred stock.

The exchange offers would provide bondholders with debt securities totalling about $550 million. In addition, bondholders and preferred shareholders are being offered approximately 18.5% of Southland's common stock in the exchange offers. Existing shareholders -- led by the Dallas-based Thompson family, which founded Southland -- would retain approximately 6.5% of the common stock and, following consummation of the exchange offers, the Japanese companies Ito-Yokado and Seven-Eleven Japan would purchase 75% of the stock.

Southland's 16.75% subordinated debentures due in 2002 ended the day up one point at 13. The 15.75% senior subordinated notes, due in 1997, were unchanged at 23.

The junk bonds of Charter Medical Corp. jumped on news that the company is considering proposals for the sale and lease-back of several of its hospitals.

The announcements were an attempt to assure bondholders that the heavily financed company had the ability to make interest payments on outstanding debt. The company also said it was "current with all debt obligations," and added that it had $50 million in cash and a $30 million line of bank credit available.

Charter Medical's 14% senior subordinated debentures, which reached an all-time low of 38 last week, were bid at 40 1/2, up 1/2 point on the day. The company's 14.25% subordinated debentures ended 1/2 higher at 22.

In the broader market, most junk bonds barely budged from Tuesday's level in activity that one trader described as "the slowest day of the year." Prices were also little changed among investment-grade bonds.

Foreign Bonds

Most major foreign government bonds ended lower yesterday.

British government bonds, called gilts, fell sharply despite continued firmness in the pound. Traders said profit-taking by overseas investors was mostly responsible for the huge price declines.

Selling also stemmed from concern about the government's budget outlook. Local newspapers reported that the central government may provide #2.5 billion ($4.44 billion) in additional grants to municipal governments to discourage increases in a politically unpopular new local tax charge.

Britain's 11.75% bond due 2003/2007 fell 1 3/32 point to 100 27/32 to yield 11.62% while the 12% notes due in 1995 fell 3/4 point to 99 1/8 to yield 12.26%.

In Japan, prices of government bonds followed the movement of the yen to lower levels.

At the 3 p.m. JST close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the price of the benchmark 10-year yen bond was 86.85, down 0.10 point to yield 7.22%.

In West Germany, government bonds ended about 0.35 point weaker in what traders term a technical correction, unrelated to economic events.

Even the new German Unity Bonds, which were launched on Wednesday, slipped yesterday. The 10-year 8.75% German Unity Bond fell 0.40 point to 100.60 to yield 8.66%.

(Foreign bond yields are quoted here in local terms and therefore differ somewhat from yields calculated for these markets by U.S. investment firms, which tend to use the semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets.)

Municipal Bonds

Actively traded municipal bonds finished unchanged to 1/4 point higher in snail-paced dealings.

With market participants sluggish after Independence Day and cautious before Friday's release of the U.S. employment data for June, there was little action.

But traders say the market is still in good shape because of talk about tax increases and speculation that Federal Reserve easing of interest rates is around the bend.

The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 ended 1/8 higher at 98 7/8, pushing the yield down to 7.27% from 7.28% late Tuesday.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

In what turned out to be the expected limp trading day, mortgage securities prices ended unchanged to slightly firmer.

Among actively traded secondary issues, Federal Home Loan Mortgage 9.5% securities for July delivery were unchanged at 98 30/32 and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities for July were flat at 99 14/32.
