Ernst & Young Barred From Some New Jobs During 45-Day Period

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/06/90

WASHINGTON -- Ernst & Young was barred by an administrative law judge of the Securities and Exchange Commission for 45 days from taking any new accounting business at its New York regional offices for alleged "improper professional conduct" in audits done by a predecessor firm. The order by administrative law judge Jerome K. Soffer was effective June 28, its issue date. The SEC's accounting division previously alleged that work done by Ernst & Whinney for U.S. Surgical Corp. in 1980 and 1981 resulted in unqualified audit reports on statements that significantly overstated U.S. Surgical's income. Ernst & Whinney merged last year with Arthur Young & Co.

In addition, the administrative order censured Michael L. Ferrante, managing partner of Ernst & Young's office in White Plains, N.Y., who was the firm's partner supervising the audits of U.S. Surgical, a Norwalk, Conn.-based maker of surgical stapling devices. The order doesn't affect the firm's work begun prior to June 28.

An Ernst & Young spokesman in New York said, "This is a very old matter in which civil litigation was concluded many years ago without any payment from the firm. We feel that after almost 10 years, it is time to put the matter behind us." He said the bar was only for new clients that are subject to SEC reporting requirements and only affected audit work. Calls to Mr. Ferrante at the White Plains office were referred back to Ernst & Young's main office.

One expert on the accounting profession said he saw little if any practical harm to Ernst & Young. "Clients or potential clients will only wait" until the 45 days runs out, said Jim Emerson, editor of the Professional Services Report in Seattle, which monitors the accounting profession. "The only injury is going to be negative visibility, which is not helpful in light of other things, like the S&L crisis. The climate right now is very touchy" with accounting firms, he said.
