Sales of Diamonds At De Beers Reach $2.48 Billion for Half

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/06/90

LONDON -- Diamond sales of De Beers Consolidated Mines Ltd. rose to a record $2.48 billion in the first half, an 7% increase from a year earlier, the company said. But sales in the second quarter were slower than in the buoyant first quarter. Dealers described the diamond market as quiet. They estimate that of the first half's sales, De Beers sold about 60% in the first three months.

The record sales partially reflect a diamond price increase of 5.5% at the end of March, the company said. De Beers, which controls about 80% of the world rough diamond market, decided to raise prices following buoyant sales in January and February.

De Beers reported sales of 2.32 billion for the first half of 1989. In the second half of the year, the company had sales of $1.77 billion. Second half sales tend to be lower than the first half because of seasonal buying trends, De Beers said.

Because the diamond market appears to be slowing, total sales in 1990 might be around $4.1 billion, about the same annual level as the previous two years, dealers predict.

Although sales of medium and high quality diamonds are still reasonable, the market in poor quality diamonds, priced at $350 a carat or lower, is weak, De Beers said.

The U.S. market, which accounts for about two fifths of world diamond jewelry sales, is particularly slow, dealers said. Japanese diamond imports surged in the first two months, but since then consumption has waned. Trade in the European markets, however, is fair, they said.

After the boom between 1986 to 1988, the market is returning to normal, De Beers said.

But Jacques Zucker, a director of Zucker-Berkovic PVBA, Antwerp diamond dealers, said, "Diamond manufacturers are being squeezed by falling margins." Prices of polished diamonds haven't matched the 21% rise in De Beers's rough diamond quotes during the past 18 months, he said.
