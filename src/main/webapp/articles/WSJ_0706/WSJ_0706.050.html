Don't Bother Looking for a Job In Their Complaint Department

  • Author: Junda Woo
  • Date: 07/06/90

FORT WORTH, Texas -- You won't find its fashions on the racks of Bloomingdale's or Saks Fifth Avenue, but Vera Lee Garment Co. makes clothes you'd just die to wear. Literally.

Founded in 1932, Vera Lee is one of the nation's oldest producers of what it tactfully calls "premade funeral fashions." In other words, clothes for dead people.

Operating out of a small yellow building in a residential neighborhood here, the company makes about 125 styles of burial apparel, ranging from frilly nightgowns and dresses ($37 to $100 wholesale) to sober business suits ($75 to $80).

And despite the gloom hanging over parts of the fashion industry this season, business for Vera Lee is, well, lively.

In 1989, the company sold about 400 dresses a week, racking up total sales of about $1 million for the year. Profit rose, says a Vera Lee accountant, although he wouldn't elaborate. And owner Kathye Kellow says the company intends to expand into new areas of the country this year and add five or six salespeople.

Why would anybody buy a Vera Lee original for a loved one's eternal rest, instead of, say, a favorite old dress or suit? Most people are buried in their own clothes, says Ms. Kellow, but others have been in nursing homes for years and don't have any dress-up clothes left. Buying a new outfit also saves family members from a painful search through the deceased's closet, she adds.

Good marketing helps, too. Some funeral homes display Vera Lee styles side by side with matching caskets. "A lot of times a family will just walk up and see a dress and say, `That's mom,'" says Ms. Kellow.

For obvious reasons, this branch of the apparel industry operates with its own set of rules. Fashion, for example, takes a back seat to practicality. Suit pockets and zippers are fake, and waists are elastic so that one size fits most. Dresses are fitted with Velcro fastenings down the back.

Pastels and lighter colors are recommended for women, Ms. Kellow adds. Men are generally attired in conservative navy or brown suits, available with or without pin stripes.

"In the '70s, we made a leisure burial suit because leisure suits were so cool back then," Ms. Kellow recalls. "But we can't get too fashion conscious because they're out the next year."

Not that anybody would notice.
