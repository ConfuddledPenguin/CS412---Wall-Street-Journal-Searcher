Abreast of the Market: Rally Is Snapped by Profit-Taking, Rounds of Futures-Related Selling

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 07/06/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market decisively broke its longest winning streak in two months, as prices finished broadly and sharply lower in light, post-holiday trading. Profit-taking in advance of today's release of June employment data, coupled with several rounds of futures-related selling, sent the Dow Jones Industrial Average tumbling 32.42 points to 2879.21. This was the largest decline since June 22, when the Dow industrials slipped more than 44.35 points.

News that aides to President Bush are considering a possible tax of as much as 0.5% on securities sales "overshadowed the market," said Richard Meyer, head of institutional trading at Ladenburg, Thalmann & Co. Though traders refused to attribute the market's decline solely to the tax issue, they said some big institutions now will increase hedges on stock positions by selling futures, for example, to insulate portfolios from the day-to-day swings that might come from Washington's tax debate.

Prices had finished higher in each of the previous five sessions, with the Dow industrials gaining a total of 69.30 points, or 2.4%, in that period. The performance was the market's best since the average posted seven consecutive advances in a late April-early May rally.

Traders reported little demand to support prices, because many institutional investors remained on vacation after the July Fourth holiday.

Also, uncertainty about the outcome of today's employment report kept buyers on the sidelines. Though the June data is generally expected to show a modest pickup in job creation, some expect to see signs of economic weakness, which could prompt the Federal Reserve to lower interest rates.

The report is expected to show that nonfarm payrolls rose 83,000 overall and went up 132,000 after excluding the layoffs of temporary census workers, according to a consensus of economists polled by Dow Jones. Payrolls rose 164,000 in May, but the hiring of census takers accounted for 145,000 of the increase.

Declining stocks routed advancers on the New York Stock Exchange by 1,058 to 397.

The breadth of the sell-off also could be seen in the declines in broad averages. The Standard & Poor's 500-stock index fell 4.48 to 355.68, the Dow Jones Equity Market Index lost 3.69 to 331.03, and the New York Stock Exchange composite index tumbled 2.16 to 194.45.

Big Board volume dropped to 128,320,000 shares from 130,050,000 shares on Tuesday.

The "mediocre up market" of the past five sessions gave way to "some profit-takers, ready to cash in" after the advance, said David Holt, director of technical research at Wedbush Morgan Securities, Los Angeles.

At least four rounds of sell programs tied to stock-index arbitrage hit the market during the session, putting further pressure on share prices. The first two flurries struck within the first half-hour of trading, when the Dow industrials dropped more than 23 points.

The market's leaders over the course of its rally-growth stocks and consumer issues with a history of steady earnings improvement -- weren't spared from the sell-off. Philip Morris dropped 1/2 to 47 1/2 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading of 1.7 million shares, Merck dropped 1 1/2 to 86 1/8, Coca-Cola went down 5/8 to 44 1/4 and General Electric slid 7/8 to 70 1/4 on slightly more than one million shares.

Auto stocks were hit especially hard, even though late-June sales reports showed that U.S. car sales climbed to their highest levels since February. General Motors fell 1 1/8 to 47 1/4 and Ford Motor slid 1 to 44, while Chrysler eased 1/4 to 15 1/4.

Avon Products placed first on the Big Board's list of most active issues, as the result of a block trade of 3.98 million shares, or 7% of the Avon shares outstanding. Chartwell Associates bought the block from a partnership of the investor Irwin Jacobs and Amway. Chartwell said its partners, who include the investor Gordon Getty and the Fisher brothers of New York, plan to buy an additional 1.2 million Avon shares from the Jacobs-Amway partnership within the next 30 days. The stock lost 3/4 to 37 on composite volume of 4.7 million shares.

Panhandle Eastern fell 2 1/8 to 21 3/8, on 1.4 million shares, reflecting speculation that the company may reduce its dividend as a result of lower-than-expected natural gas prices and its heavy debt load from its acquisition last year of Texas Eastern. Panhandle, which previously said it wouldn't lower the annual dividend of $2 a share, declined to comment on the latest talk, but said its board "is expected to thoroughly review" the payout policy at its July 25 meeting.

Genentech went up 1/2 to 29, on 1.2 million shares, and Eli Lilly gained 1 1/2 to 86 1/4, following the release of a study that found that elderly men can benefit from regular injections of human growth hormone, produced by both companies. Researchers in Wisconsin found that the drug can reverse atrophying of muscles and increases in fat tissue.

Another drug stock that avoided being caught in the market's sell-off was Rorer Group, which rose 1 to 72 5/8. Standard & Poor's upgraded ratings on Rorer's debt issues to reflect the company's planned merger with Rhone-Poulenc of France.

Gulf States Utilities added 1/8 to 11. Moody's Investors Service placed its ratings on the company's long-term debt and preferred stock under review for a possible upgrade, citing Gulf States' tentative agreement to resolve a dispute about power purchases from Southern Co.

British Steel dropped 7/8 to 24 3/8, on 2.6 million shares, with a set of dividend-related trades by institutions accounting for the bulk of the volume. The stock has a 5.1% dividend yield, and stock of record tomorrow will receive the next quarterly payout.

McDonnell Douglas climbed 1 7/8 to 40 7/8, amid indications the U.S. and South Korean governments have resolved differences that had blocked a proposed sale of the company's F/A-18 fighters to South Korea.

Motel Six, buoyed by recent speculation that Kohlberg Kravis Roberts may sell its 53% stake in the partnership, went up 1/2 to 17 7/8 in active trading. KKR declined to comment on its plans.

HomeFed fell 1 1/4 to 19 5/8, but closed well above its intraday low of 18. The bounce came after Merrill Lynch upgraded its short-term investment rating on the stock to "hold" from "below average," citing its drop in price.

Waban gained 3/8 to 8 1/8 and Circuit City Stores added 1/4 to 26 7/8, after releasing June sales reports. Waban's sales rose 19% from a year earlier, and sales at Circuit City rose 23% overall and 6% for stores open at least one year.

Tootsie Roll Industries gained 1 1/8 to 48 3/8, setting a 52-week high for the third consecutive session. Prescott, Ball & Turben featured the stock in the latest monthly issue of Investor News, a newsletter sent to the firm's clients on Monday. Elliott Schlang, an analyst, said his price target for Tootsie Roll over the next six months is $52 a share.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 2.18 to 358.49. Amex volume totaled about 8,520,000 shares, compared with about 11,730,000 a day earlier. Declining issues outnumbered those advancing, 321 to 216, and 234 finished unchanged.
