Family-Firm Advisers May Need More Training

MORE CONSULTANTS are rushing to help family-owned businesses.

But veteran family-business consultants say too many newcomers lack credentials for untangling complex family-business issues, such as succession and sibling rivalry.

Rod Correll, executive director of the Family Firm Institute, says, "The interest in family-business consulting seems to have doubled over the last two years. And there is no mechanism for credentials." Membership in the Johnstown, N.Y.-based institute, an organization of consultants and others serving family businesses, also has doubled, to 500 members this year from about 250 in 1988.

New programs for family-business owners are proliferating -- not to mention books, magazine articles and university courses -- in a nod to the growing awareness of family problems affecting small businesses. In September, the Center for Management at the Institute of Living, a psychiatric hospital in Hartford, Conn., will begin quarterly seminars to help 50 firms resolve family tensions.

No figures are available on the newcomers catering to family businesses, but they range from accountants to family therapists. Added to those are many big companies that didn't target family businesses a decade ago -- like the accounting concerns Laventhol & Horwath and Grant Thornton, as well as Wall Street firms like Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. -- which recently expanded into the booming field.

But John Messervey, longtime director of the National Family Business Council, a Northbrook, Ill., consulting firm, says many newcomers have "invested much in marketing and little in training. Frankly, many are not equipped to handle the problems."

The Family Firm Institute conference in Atlanta in October will discuss possible qualifications and credentials for the field.

Small-Business Issues

Swell PAC War Chest

POLITICAL GIVING by small-business owners is increasing in the wake of heightened congressional interest in issues important to small companies, says the National Federation of Independent Business.

The largest national small-business organization says contributions to its political action committee war chest jumped to $214,000 in 1989. While relatively small by PAC standards, that's still three times more than the group ever collected before in a year with no congressional elections.

The federation's PAC receipts have passed $300,000 for the two-year election cycle ending this year; the goal is $500,000.

As previously reported, small companies also have increased lobbying efforts. Their concern over matters that came before Congress this year -- such as federal regulation of fringe benefits, required company leave for new parents and an increased minimum wage -- also helped swell the PAC war chest, says John Motley, the federation's chief lobbyist. "People give when they're scared and angry," he says.

Mr. Motley says the federation ran its PAC "as an afterthought" until 1987, when it overhauled its political fund-raising machine to make it more effective. He says the federation now identifies members who are "proven givers," goes to them repeatedly for contributions and specifically acknowledges their donations.

But the group is helping candidates in other ways besides PAC contributions. For instance, Mr. Motley says the federation is recruiting its members to work in local races for candidates with pro-small-business backgrounds. "Now we're doing the things the unions have been doing for years," he says.

Franchise Rules Are

Back Off Drawing Board

THE REVISED DRAFT on legislation to regulate franchising was made public last month -- and the most controversial parts had been excised.

A committee of the North American Securities Administrators Association released the revision for public comment, says committee head Debra M. Bollinger, director of South Dakota's securities division. The original draft was released last July. "Substantial redrafting has been done in almost all of the sections," Ms. Bollinger says, and several sections were eliminated.

The committee hopes the revised draft will be adopted as a model for state franchising legislation. Ms. Bollinger says the association, whose members are involved in state regulation of franchises, is expected to consider the matter at a meeting in August.

Many franchisers opposed the group's original proposal for a Model Franchise Investment Act, says David J. Kaufmann, a franchising lawyer in New York. He says they particularly disliked provisions regarding termination of franchises. For example, the original draft would have required franchisers to buy goods and equipment of terminated franchisees. Mr. Kaufmann called that draft "another effort to kill -- or at least overregulate -- the goose that laid the golden egg."

But the newest draft contains no such provisions.
