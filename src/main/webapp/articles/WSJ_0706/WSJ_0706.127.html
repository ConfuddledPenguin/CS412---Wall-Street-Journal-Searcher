Columbia S&L Ex-Chief Spiegel Named In Administrative Charges by Regulators

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr. and Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 07/06/90

Federal thrift regulators brought administrative charges against Thomas Spiegel, the former chief executive officer of Columbia Savings & Loan Association, alleging a spending spree with federally backed deposits and seeking a record $24 million in restitution and penalties. The Office of Thrift Supervision alleged that Mr. Spiegel used funds from the Beverly Hills, Calif., thrift to pay for European travel for him and his wife, a $55,000 gun collection including Uzi submachine guns and automatic pistols, $7,000 in tickets to a Michael Jackson concert, and personal use of Columbia Savings aircraft and four resort condominiums.

The thrift office said one of the condominiums, in Jackson Hole, Wyo., was used almost exclusively by Michael Milken, the former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. junk-bond chief who pleaded guilty earlier this year to securities fraud, and his brother, attorney Lowell Milken, without reimbursing Columbia.

As part of the restitution, the office is seeking $6.4 million from Mr. Spiegel for losses allegedly suffered by Columbia in a partnership with Michael Milken that bought two 30-seat jets and built a luxury airplane hangar. Neither of the Milken brothers was charged with any wrongdoing. Columbia Savings bought several billion dollars of high-yield, high-risk junk bonds from Drexel.

In a statement, Mr. Spiegel said he was "outraged" at the thrift office decision and forcefully denies all of the charges. He said the office had refused to meet with his attorneys to discuss the allegations and to give him a "fair chance to respond."

His statement continued: ". . . historically, OTS and its predecessors have been abysmal failures and now, in panic and for purely political reasons, OTS is seeking scapegoats to divert public attention from its gross incompetence." Mr. Spiegel is being represented by New York criminal defense lawyer Robert Morvillo, who recently successfully defended Saudi businessman Adnan Khashoggi against charges linked to former Philippine First Lady Imelda Marcos.

The case against Mr. Spiegel is an important one for the thrift office. Not only was it initiated on the same day the office announced a new policy of making such administrative actions public, but it is the largest number of charges, the largest restitution, and the largest civil fine ever sought from an individual by the agency.

Also, Congress, responding to constituents' outrage about the costs of the S&L bailout, is in a paroxysm of election season denunciation of S&L crime and will be watching the case carefully. If the allegations are proven, Mr. Spiegel would join the list of S&L executives who have used thrifts as personal piggy banks.

Thrift office director Timothy Ryan wouldn't say whether any criminal action was being sought against Mr. Spiegel, but he said the Justice Department was involved in the investigation that led to the administrative charges. He also said the thrift office is investigating "at least three other institutions" and their junk-bond dealings with Drexel.

The administrative action comes as Columbia is being required, under last year's savings-and-loan bailout law, to liquidate its once hugely profitable junk-bond holdings. Currently valued at about $2.9 billion, the portfolio has declined in value by about $1 billion over the last six months, leaving Columbia insolvent and on the verge of federal takeover. Company officials have expressed confidence that they will be able to sell the bonds, however, to help minimize the institution's losses.

Mr. Spiegel, whose father Abraham is Columbia's chairman, resigned under pressure as Columbia's president and chief executive in December and was a consultant until this past spring.

In addition to the costs for the airplanes, the thrift office is seeking the following restitution:

-- $2.9 million for a bonus paid to Mr. Spiegel last year. Regulators objected when Columbia paid Mr. Spiegel a $3 million bonus, the thrift office said, and in response, Mr. Spiegel returned $600,000. The office is seeking the return of the remaining $2.4 million plus interest.

-- $5.7 million for losses allegedly incurred by Columbia on $28 million in loans made to Howard Schneider, a luxury auto dealer and friend of Mr. Spiegel. The office said Mr. Schneider lent Mr. Spiegel a Mercedes 560 SEL and a Bentley while the loans were being negotiated. The office alleged the loans were made at below-market interest rates and weren't properly documented. Mr. Schneider wasn't charged with wrongdoing.

-- $2.4 million for allegedly unauthorized payments made to a charitable foundation controlled by Mr. Spiegel. The thrift office said most of the foundation assets were invested in junk bonds and Columbia stock.

-- $1.1 million for the alleged personal use of four condominiums owned by Columbia, including two in Jackson Hole, one in Indian Wells, Calif., and one in Deer Valley, Utah.

-- $463,000 allegedly used for personal expenses, including four trips to Europe for Mr. Spiegel and his wife, $55,000 in guns, the Michael Jackson tickets and also tickets to a Steve Winwood concert, $2,000 for a French wine-tasting course and $1,700 for silverware.

The total restitution of $19 million would go to Columbia. The thrift office is also seeking $5 million in civil penalties that would go to the government.

The action freezes Mr. Spiegel's funds and assets with any financial institution until he establishes an escrow account or puts up other security to guarantee the restitution. He is also prohibited from voting his Columbia stock. Mr. Ryan of the thrift office said the agency is convinced Mr. Spiegel has the wealth to pay the restitution and fine. The office also is seeking to bar Mr. Spiegel from working in any federally insured bank or thrift in the future.

Mr. Spiegel has 20 days to reply to the thrift office and request a hearing.

Mr. Spiegel's compensation at Columbia has long been an issue for regulators. In 1985, for instance, he earned an estimated $9 million in salary and benefits, roughly 17 times what the chairman of the nation's largest S&L was making. Columbia was also the top-earning S&L in the country at that time, but the package drew a public rebuke from federal thrift regulators.

Earlier this year, regulators ordered that a $500,000 annual consulting fee that Mr. Spiegel was receiving from Columbia be reduced to $1 a year.
