Law: Architects Group Settles Charges By Justice Agency

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 07/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The American Institute of Architects, a Washington trade group, settled federal antitrust charges that it had discouraged competitive bidding and discounts by its 55,000 members. Although the AIA denied any wrongdoing, it agreed to a proposed court order that would block it from barring or deterring architects from competitive bidding, discounting fees and providing free services.

The proposed court order, or consent decree, was filed in federal court in Washington by the Justice Department and requires a judge's approval. The decree would settle civil charges by the department, including the allegation that the Chicago chapter of the AIA in 1984 adopted a policy statement prohibiting practices such as competitive bidding and discounting. Federal antitrust prosecutors had investigated the group for four years.

David Perdue, the AIA's associate general counsel, said such statements "reflected anxieties and concerns about financial returns of {architectural} firms" in the mid-1980s. But he insisted that the policy statements hadn't affected architects' fees. Mr. Perdue said the AIA agreed to the consent decree to prevent the cost and distraction of a trial. The decree would require the architects group to pay $50,000 to the government for the expense of its investigation. Mr. Perdue said the group has already spent $2 million in legal fees in connection with the investigation.

The Justice Department investigated alleged antitrust violations by the AIA in the early 1970s. That inquiry resulted in a narrower consent decree barring restrictions on competitive bidding.
