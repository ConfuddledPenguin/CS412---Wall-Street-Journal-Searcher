Who's News: Davis, Citicorp Corporate Finance Head, Steps Down

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 07/06/90

NEW YORK -- Citicorp's head of North American corporate finance, George L. Davis, resigned in what appears to be the first fallout from a shuffling of top officers in January. Mr. Davis, 55 years old, couldn't be reached for comment. However, a memo from Chairman John S. Reed to policy committee members said that "the business has changed considerably from that envisioned in 1987," when Mr. Davis rejoined the large banking company. Mr. Reed's memo added, "George feels that he can make a greater contribution elsewhere."

Mr. Davis had left Citicorp in 1981 to become executive vice president at First Chicago Corp. under Chairman Barry Sullivan.

Mr. Davis's resignation, Citicorp executives said, isn't related to the bank's rising levels of problem loans for corporate takeovers and commercial real estate, two troubled areas that Mr. Davis oversaw.

Rather, a bank executive who asked not to be identified linked the departure of Mr. Davis, who is noted for his credit analysis skills, to a political setback suffered by Vice Chairman Lawrence Small, his patron within Citicorp.

The January reorganization diminished Mr. Small's influence within Citicorp and, some executives said, hastened Mr. Davis's departure. In that shake-up, the traditional commercial and investment banking functions were merged, and the resulting wholesale bank split into two groups -- one managing the developed countries, consisting of North America, Japan and Europe; and the other managing the developing countries.

As part of that shake-up, Mr. Small, who had run the world-wide commercial bank, was placed in a lesser role as the top liaison with corporate clients, and Richard S. Braddock, head of consumer banking, was elevated to the long-vacant presidency.

Mr. Small, a hard-charging, ambitious executive, was considered by some the leading contender for the president's job. However, Citicorp executives said disagreements grew between Messrs. Reed and Small, especially over the tough bargaining stance advocated by Mr. Small toward debt negotiations with Brazil and other Latin American borrowers.

At the time of the reorganization, Mr. Reed and other Citicorp executives -- as well as Mr. Small himself -- insisted Mr. Small hadn't lost out. "With the age difference of only two years between John and myself, I've never thought that it was particularly logical for me to aspire to be chief executive officer," Mr. Small said at the time.

Michael A. Callen, who had been sector executive for investment banking, was put in charge of wholesale banking in the developed countries. Citicorp executives speculated that Mr. Callen wants to fill key posts, such as Mr. Davis's position, with personnel loyal to him.

Mr. Callen was traveling yesterday and couldn't be reached.
