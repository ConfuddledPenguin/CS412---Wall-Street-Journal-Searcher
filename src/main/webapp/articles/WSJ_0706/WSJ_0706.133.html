What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/06/90

A NEW ENERGY TAX is being pushed by the Bush administration as a way to help cut the budget deficit. The levy would bring in an estimated $20 billion a year, more than any other part of the revenue-raising package being considered. The tax would apply to various sources of energy, including coal and petroleum, and would add five or six cents a gallon to the price of gasoline. ---

Japanese auto makers grabbed a record 28% share of U.S. car sales in the first half of 1990. Sales of North American-made autos, meanwhile, surged 11.9% in late June to their highest rate in six months.

---

Hughes Aircraft Chairman Malcolm Currie received some of the classified data illicitly obtained by a defense-industry ring, court records assert. But a criminal probe of the GM subsidiary's top officer seems unlikely to lead to an indictment.

---

Thrift regulators brought administrative charges against former Columbia chief Thomas Spiegel. They are seeking $24 million for an alleged spending spree with insured deposits.

Charges against Bush's son, an ex-director of a failed S&L, will be heard in public, the government announced.

---

Avon holders Irwin Jacobs and Amway sold most of their stake to two Chartwell Associates partners. The deal appears to give Chartwell, which includes a Mary Kay officer, more leverage with Avon managers.

---

Stock prices declined sharply, in part on worry about a possible tax on securities sales. The Dow Jones industrials fell 32.42 points. Bond prices also sank, and the dollar was mixed.

---

The Federal Reserve decided not to ease credit at its meeting this week, despite signs of a slowing economy, government officials said.

---

Stock funds returned an average of 5.89% in the second quarter, while the S&P 500 index gained 6.29%. But growth and small-company growth funds both returned more than 7%.

---

Price Waterhouse could face contempt penalties if it violates securities laws, under a permanent injunction sought by the SEC. The agency claims the accounting firm is likely to break the law without such an order.

Accounting firms could do more consulting work for their auditing clients under an SEC staff ruling.

---

UAL halted talks with aircraft makers about the purchase of up to $15 billion in jets, under pressure from a union-led buy-out group. The group is offering preferred status to vendors in exchange for financing.

---

Gannett is expected to post a decline in second-quarter profit, its first such year-to-year drop in more than two decades. The reversal signals that an industry slump has finally taken hold at the newspaper concern.

---

An architects trade group settled U.S. charges that it violated antitrust law by discouraging competitive bidding and discounts by its members.

---

Bristol-Myers Squibb gave additional duties to Wayne Davidson, strengthening his position to be next chief executive and illustrating Bristol Myers's dominance over Squibb since their merger last year.

---

Anheuser-Busch plans to lead a lobbying effort by brewers to head off a boost in the U.S. excise tax on beer. The campaign will include ads condemning the levy as regressive.

---

Five Players International officials sold $2.5 million of the gambling-services firm's stock less than two weeks before its price plunged last month.
