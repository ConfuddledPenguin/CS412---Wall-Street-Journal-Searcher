Marketing & Media: Gannett Expected To End 22 Years Of Earnings Gains

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 07/06/90

NEW YORK -- Gannett Co. is expected to report its first quarterly earnings drop from a year earlier since the company went public more than 22 years ago, ending one of the longer streaks in corporate annals and signaling that an industrywide slump has finally taken hold at the country's largest newspaper company. Gannett's vice chairman and chief financial officer, Douglas McCorkindale said in an interview yesterday that he can't disagree with the conclusions of some Wall Street analysts, who expect the company to post second-quarter net income below the year-earlier $112.1 million, or 70 cents a share. In May, ad revenues fell 4.3% at Gannett's 82 daily newspapers, and "business continues to be soft. We aren't seeing any significant business in June," Mr. McCorkindale said. He declined to predict any specific figures and said the company will issue its second-quarter report next week.

Peter Appert, an analyst at C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc., expects Gannett to report earnings of about $109.7 million, or 68 cents a share, for the second quarter. "It's not a conclusion I'll disagree with," Mr. McCorkindale said of the analyst's forecast.

Mr. Appert said Gannett's earnings are being hurt by continued weak retail and classified newspaper linage at the company's dailies. "The simple fact," he added, "is that Gannett will continue to report disappointing earnings until the ad revenue environment improves."

Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette analyst Susan Decker also expects results to be down slightly, although earnings could be flat after counting a small gain from the recent sale of a Gannett radio station. She expects classified advertising will "continue to get worse" for Gannett and most newspaper companies. She added that major newspaper companies' earnings growth, which averaged about 15% through the past decade, will slow to about 8% for the 1990s.

The gloomy projections come at a crucial time for Gannett. The Gannett Foundation, the company's largest shareholder, plans to sell its 10% stake, raising the possibility that Gannett may be put into play as a possible takeover target -- particularly if its stock price falls on bad earnings news.

The foundation, headed by former Gannett Chairman Allen Neuharth, is sorting through several potential bidders and waiting for the company's stock price to rebound. Shares traded as high as $55.25 in 1987 and were at $36.75 when the foundation announced the planned sale on April 26. The stock has been slowly rising since. It closed yesterday at $37.125 a share, down 37.5 cents, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

In the past, Gannett executives have boasted of the string of earnings gains. Even in the recent ad slump, Gannett, with papers spread across 36 states, has managed to post some of the industry's strongest earnings. Earnings at most other major publishers have been weakened by declines in retail and classified ad spending, particularly in Northeastern states.

Gannett went public in October 1967 and since then has logged earnings gains from the year-earlier period for 89 consecutive quarters. Gannett barely kept the string alive in the first quarter of 1990, posting net earnings of $75 million, or 47 cents a share, only $100,000 higher than in the year-earlier quarter.
