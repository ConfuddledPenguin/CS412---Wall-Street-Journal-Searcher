Technology: Monsanto's Blood Pressure Drug Found More Effective for Blacks Than 2 Others

  • Author: Ron Winslow
  • Date: 07/06/90

A Monsanto Co. blood pressure drug is more effective among black patients than two competing drugs, University of Maryland researchers said. In a study of 394 patients, Verapamil SR, a calcium channel blocker, reduced hypertension in as many as 73% of cases, while captopril, an ACE inhibitor marketed by Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. was effective in 57.1% of patients and ICI Pharma's atenolol, a beta blocker, had a 59.6% effective rate, the researchers said. ICI Pharma is the U.S. arm of Britain's Imperial Chemical Industries PLC.

Verapamil SR is marketed under the trade name Calan SR by Monsanto's G.D. Searle unit, which funded the study, published in the current issue of Archives of Internal Medicine.

Previous research has shown that diuretics and the class of drugs known as calcium channel blockers appear to be more effective among blacks than the other types of blood pressure medicines. This study was the first to compare the three classes of medicines among black patients.

Drug companies often support clinical trials that compare the effectiveness of competing drugs to use in marketing their products. This study demonstrates that all three medicines worked well among blacks, says Matthew R. Weir, medical director of clinical research at the University of Maryland Hospital, Baltimore. Patients responded best to the calcium channel blocker, but the effectiveness of the other two drugs was higher than previous studies have shown, the study says.

Hypertension is more prevalent among blacks than non-blacks and such illness as coronary artery disease, stroke and kidney disease develop earlier among blacks. Researchers think differences in blood chemistry and other factors partially explain blacks' susceptibility to hypertension, as well as their varied responses to different classes of medicines.

Calcium channel blockers help lower blood pressure by reducing the heart's workload. Beta blockers block certain nerve impulses and slow down the heart's activity, reducing its need for oxygen. ACE (for angiotensin converting enzyme) blocks the action of a kidney enzyme that causes blood vessels to tighten. One reason researchers think blacks are less responsive to ACE inhibitors, for instance, is that blacks typically already have low levels of renin, the enzyme the drug blocks.

Doctors commonly prescribe diuretics to black patients, but researchers are increasingly concerned that these medicines increase the risk among both black and non-black patients for heart disease, high cholesterol and abnormal blood chemistry, Dr. Weir says. The study shows that "there are drugs that work well in blacks besides diuretics."

Diuretics, at a cost of about $5 a month, are much cheaper than the other drugs, which range from $20 to $30 a month per equivalent dose, he adds.
