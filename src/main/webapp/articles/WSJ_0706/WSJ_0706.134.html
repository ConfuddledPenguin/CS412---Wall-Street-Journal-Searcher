Thursday's Markets: Stocks Drop 32.42 Points On Tax Talk --- Trading Is Very Thin; Bond Prices Also Sink But Dollar Is Mixed

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 07/06/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices fell sharply in very thin trading, triggered by news that the Bush administration might back a 0.5% tax on securities sales as part of a budget deficit-reduction package. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 32.42 points to 2879.21. Bond prices also sank, but the dollar was mixed.

Much of the activity in stocks was generated by program traders intent on capturing profits from fleeting price discrepancies between stock-index futures contracts and the underlying stocks. Stock futures prices fell faster than stock prices, so program traders bought the cheaper futures and sold stocks. Their tactics caused the Dow Jones industrials to fall in spasmodic fashion during the morning.

Moreover, "Talk about a stock transfer tax provided some excuse to sell" following five sessions of rising stock prices, said James Solloway, chief economist at Argus Research. He added that "it's way too early to tell whether that tax will ever become a reality." In fact, the report may turn out to be a trial balloon that quickly gets shot down, he said.

Another reason program trading had so much influence is that many investors are either on vacation or sitting on the sidelines awaiting this morning's disclosure of June jobs data. Many investors are hoping that payroll employment growth will be weak enough to coax the Federal Reserve into lowering interest rates soon.

"I'm not buying that," said Mr. Solloway. "I think there may be the chance to see a surprising gain in payroll employment." If he is right, and if many investors already have bet on a near-term Fed ease, both stocks and bonds could be in for rough sledding.

Bond prices didn't react to the tax talk and were unchanged most of the day. Then, June car sales came in stronger than expected, leading some investors to wonder if today's employment report might follow suit.

Uncertainty about interest rates continues to affect the dollar. It closed only slightly higher against the mark, which is holding steady in the aftermath of monetary union between East and West Germany. The yen strengthened on the expectation of lower U.S. interest rates, which would make Japanese rates relatively more appealing.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell on volume of 128.3 million shares in New York Stock Exchange trading. Declining issues swamped advancers, 1,058 to 397.

Bond prices fell more than a quarter of a point, or more than $2.50 for each $1,000 of face amount, in light trading. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 8.40%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6517 marks and 150.60 yen, compared with 1.6505 marks and 150.90 yen Tuesday.
