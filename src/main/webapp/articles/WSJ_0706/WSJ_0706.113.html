Your Money Matters: Growth, Foreign Stocks Power Top-Ranking Funds --- Small Companies Also Won in Quarter

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 07/06/90

For investors in stock mutual funds, the message from the past three months is: Caution didn't pay. By venturing abroad or loading up on stocks of companies with fast-growing earnings, some funds picked up handsome returns for the quarter. But conservative fund managers who sat on cash found themselves sitting out a rally.

Growth funds and small-company growth funds both had total returns -- price change plus dividends -- of more than 7% for the quarter, according to Lipper Analytical Services. The average general stock fund returned 5.89% for the quarter while the Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index rose 6.29%.

The quarter's gains were an abrupt turnaround from the first quarter, when the average stock fund slipped 2.31% and the plodding money market funds ranked as the second-best performing category of mutual funds.

"Investors are looking for companies that have above-average earnings growth," says Gordon Fines, manager of the $800 million IDS Growth Fund. The economy is sluggish, but "inflation isn't a problem and interest rates are stable. Against that sort of benign backdrop, investors are drawn to growth stocks."

Mr. Fines's fund was up 16.45% for the past three months, helped by both big-company growth stocks such as Wal-Mart Stores Inc. and Waste Management Inc. as well as smaller growth stocks including Flightsafety International Inc., Adobe Systems Inc. and Novell Inc.

According to A. Michael Lipper, president of Lipper Analytical, funds that bought growth stocks -- whether they were big company growth stocks or small company growth stocks -- were the big winners in the quarter. "It paid to assume risk," notes Mr. Lipper. "Growth is back, and high growth is back even more."

Among general domestic stock funds, small-company growth funds were the top performers for the second consecutive quarter. But after seven years of lagging behind the market averages, most small-cap managers aren't yet ready to uncork the champagne.

"It's not size that's done it, it's growth," says Roy McKay, manager of Scudder Development Fund, a small-company stock fund that was up 16.36% over the past three months. "It's the growth that's doing well. Companies with consistently growing earnings have done well."

Not all stock fund managers are cheering. Many fund managers missed part of the rally because they were holding too much cash. As a result, over the past three months the average fund trailed the market by 0.4 percentage point. Very conservative fund managers did even worse. The equity income funds were up just 2.6%.

It's been rough going for some highly regarded fund managers. Mario Gabelli, who pulled in assets of $1.1 billion when he launched the broker-sold Gabelli Value Fund in September, was up just 1.41% in the second quarter. Mutual Shares and Windsor Fund, both of which trailed the market badly in 1989, continued to struggle. Michael Price's Mutual Shares was up 2.51% in the quarter, while John Neff's Windsor eked out a gain of 1.37%.

Just as the riskier stock funds did well in the second quarter, so too did the riskier bond funds, notes Mr. Lipper. Bond funds that hold longer maturity bonds did well, and high-yielding junk bond funds snapped their yearlong losing streak. Junk funds were down 5.57% for the past 12 months, and the list of the 25 poorest performing mutual funds now includes 11 junk funds. But for the most recent quarter, junk funds bounced back 4.1%.

International stock funds, which invest outside the U.S., outstripped their domestic counterparts over the past three months. Though funds that invest in the Pacific region -- and especially Japan -- bounced back smartly in the second quarter, the hottest international funds this year have been those investing in Europe.

Oppenheimer Global, which is up 13.7% this year, has 75% of its assets invested in Europe. "We emphasize `theme' areas," explains fund manager Kenneth Oberman. "Our theme areas are not very sophisticated. They're mostly things on the front page of the newspaper rather than the back page of the business section." Mr. Oberman says he was attracted to European companies by such themes as the 1992 economic integration of Europe, perestroika and the long-run rise in spending on health care.

Among specialty funds, the past three months were particularly kind to funds investing in technology, health care and biotechnology. Gold funds were the biggest losers in the quarter, down 10.27%, and the list of the second-quarter's poorest 25 performers includes 23 precious metals funds.

Daniel Leonard enjoys the dubious distinction of managing one of the quarter's best performing funds, Financial Strategic Portfolios-Technology, and one of the poorest performers, Financial Strategic Portfolios-Gold.

On the technology front, "we've been in the tank for seven years," says Mr. Leonard. "We've had a nice move, but the stocks are still at pretty attractive price-earnings ratios." Technology stocks, after performing well all year, took a beating at the end of May, but he says the group now is starting to rebound.

"The software stocks were particularly rewarding during the quarter," notes Mr. Leonard, who thinks software companies such as Electronic Arts and Software Toolworks Inc. should continue to perform well for the rest of the year.

As for gold, Mr. Leonard is pessimistic about the metal's price. "The two reasons you want to own gold," he says, "are inflation and uncertainty in the world." Mr. Leonard sees little reason for enthusiasm on either count.

So where has Mr. Leonard got his money? "I've got it in technology," says Mr. Leonard the investor. "I expect it'll stay there for a while."
