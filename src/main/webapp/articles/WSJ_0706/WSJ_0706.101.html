World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Ease Amid Weakness in Currency And Bond Markets; London Shares Also Drop

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/06/90

Tokyo stocks finished lower in dreary trading Thursday, hurt by investor reluctance to participate amid weaker currency and bond markets, traders said. London stock prices also were down.

At Tokyo, the Nikkei index of 225 selected issues was down 94.25 points at 32351.67. The index gained 31.32 points Wednesday.

At midmorning Friday in Tokyo, the Nikkei index was up 39.72 points to 32391.39.

Thursday's volume on the First Section was estimated at 460 million shares, down from 546 million Wednesday.

Winning issues narrowly outpaced losing issues, 476 to 463, while 187 remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index (Topix) of all issues listed in the First Section, which rose 13.87 points Wednesday, slipped 1.31 points, or 0.06%, to 2362.04.

The Second Section index, which added 19.17 points Wednesday, jumped 30.96 points, or 0.73%, to close at 4253.80. Volume was estimated at 31.4 million shares, up from 28.3 million Wednesday.

Julian Jones of W.I. Carr, Sons & Co. (Overseas) Ltd. said that the yen's retreat in Tokyo trading after a strong performance overseas overnight was the underlying cause of the quiet activity.

The dollar reached a high of 151.10 after closing at 149.75 yen in London late Wednesday and 150.30 yen in Tokyo Wednesday.

The currency rate's move above 150 yen to the dollar was bearish news for the Japanese bond market, with the yield on the benchmark No. 119, 4.8% coupon of 1999 remaining near 7.22% throughout the day, up two basis points on the day.

The bond market's weakness inhibited buying in futures, offering very limited opportunities for arbitrage, which has been keeping the market afloat over the past few days, Mr. Jones said.

Although large brokerage houses were spotted performing basket purchases on the Nikkei index, including some limited arbitrage buying, the adverse situation in currency and bond markets prevented any large gain in futures, putting a cap on profitability.

Mr. Jones said, meanwhile, that the recent increased participation of Japanese brokerage firms in conducting arbitrage is a new and significant factor for the market.

Oil stocks were one of the few advancing sectors in the market Thursday.

Kevin Haag, a trader in overseas operations at Nomura Securities Co., said that the rise was probably technically triggered, with share prices at relatively inexpensive levels lately.

But Stephen Good, a representative in Japanese equity sales at McIntosh Securities, noted that the rise may have been more fundamental, on speculation that oil prices may have bottomed out.

W.I. Carr's Mr. Jones lent support to the latter theory, making reference to still-higher inventory reports in the U.S. that have damped oil prices there and have helped weaken U.K. Brent prices.

Mr. Haag argued, however, that prospects for oil companies aren't strong for the mid-term, adding that 4% or 5% growth on earnings prospects isn't strong enough to justify fundamental-based buying.

Petroleum shares advanced broadly, with Mitsubishi Oil gaining 40 yen (26 cents) to 1,260 yen and Nippon Oil rising 30 yen to finish at 1,360.

Also higher were electricals and precision machinery makers. Aiwa was up 90 at 2,540 and NEC advanced 30 to 1,990.

Nikon increased 20 to 1,700 while Konica gained 60 to 1,630.

Construction companies generally finished the day lower after their strong advances Wednesday. Ohbayashi went down 30 to 1,660, while Kajima lost 40 to 1,950.

London share prices posted hefty losses at the close of trading, as the worsening outlook for U.K. corporate profits and the continued spate of company downgradings by analysts have taken center stage.

The market started the session easier and continued its descent. When Wall Street also reversed its recent trend and shares softened throughout the London afternoon session, the London market took its cue from that drop and ended mostly in the red.

But volume was relatively poor, with 386.8 million shares changing hands. This was only slightly above Wednesday's 353 million shares, when the U.S. market was closed for Independence Day.

Traders said it was too soon to tell whether the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index was establishing a new, lower trading range, but they conceded that negative sentiment is mounting.

The 100-index ended down 24.1 points at 2331.4. At the day's low the index was down 27.7 points. The intraday high was reached just after the start of official trading, when the index was 3.3 points easier.

The narrower FT-SE 30-share index ended down 22.9 points at 1858.2.

In the futures market, the September FT-SE 100-share index was quoted at 2393.0, down from its close Wednesday of 2424.0.

Shares to post large losses included Pearson, down 16 pence (28 cents) at 768, and RMC, down 24 at 665.

P&O continued to suffer from Wednesday's downgrading by James Capel, ending down 11 pence at 617. Reckitt & Colman also continued its descent, following its profit warning earlier in the week, ending down 21 pence at #1,244 pence ($22.09).

The only sector to show gains was the retail banks. One dealer said this was largely due to continued rumors of a merger between Midland and its 14.9% shareholder, HongKong & Shanghai Banking Corp. A dealer with County Natwest Woodmac, which issued a buy recommendation for Midland Wednesday, said the firm was convinced the merger would take place within 12-18 months.

Midland ended up 8 pence at 303.

The entire banking sector benefited from the talk, but the sector appears to be generally undergoing an upward move after its recent underperformance on debt worries.

National Westminster ended up 2 at 322, while Barclays was up 4 at 391. Lloyds closed 6 higher at 287.

Share prices closed higher in Stockholm, lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Brussels, Amsterdam and Madrid, and mixed in Milan. South African gold stocks were lower.

Share prices closed higher in Sydney, Singapore and Manila, were mixed in Wellington and lower in Hongkong, Taipei and Seoul.
