U.S. Will Conduct Thrift Case Against Neil Bush in Public --- President's Son Says Decision Is `Political,' but Agency Cites a Change in Policy

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 07/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The Office of Thrift Supervision said it has decided to make public its conflict-of-interest case against Neil Bush, the president's son and a former director of a failed Denver thrift, over his protests that the action is "political." The thrift office also announced that an administrative law judge has ordered a Sept. 25 hearing in the case involving Silverado Banking, Savings & Loan Association.

The thrift office announced the date of the hearing and released its administrative charges against Mr. Bush as part of a new policy under which more of the agency's enforcement cases will be made public. The September hearing will be open to the public as well.

Mr. Bush objected that making his case public "would be political," said Harris Weinstein, chief counsel of the thrift office. But Timothy Ryan, thrift office director, made the decision to open the case.

Mr. Ryan said the new policy "is necessary to reassure the public that as regulators we are doing everything possible to assure the integrity of thrift institutions and the protection of the taxpayer."

The thrift office has charged that Mr. Bush violated savings-and-loan regulations by voting to approve Silverado loans to a business partner, and by failing to disclose to the Silverado board his business ties to borrowers. The most important charges released yesterday had already been aired in House Banking Committee hearings on the Silverado failure.

Mr. Bush has denied any wrongdoing, arguing that while his business partners invested in his oil and gas operations, he didn't invest in their businesses and thus wouldn't benefit by helping them obtain loans. Mr. Bush served on Silverado's board from August 1985 until August 1988. He resigned after his father won the Republican presidential nomination. Silverado was taken over by the government in December 1988, at an expected cost to taxpayers of $1 billion.

The OTS is seeking an order barring Mr. Bush from violating S&L regulations in the future. He refused to sign a consent agreement in January, leading to the filing of the administrative charges in February. The administrative law judge will recommend action to Mr. Ryan, who will make the final decision.

Under the thrift office's new policy, administrative hearings on enforcement actions involving thrifts that have been closed or taken over by the government will be open to the public. The targets of the charges may ask that the thrift office close the proceedings, but the final decision lies with the agency's director.

The thrift office said enforcement hearings that involve officers of still-operating S&Ls will generally be closed, out of concern that publicity could cause a depositor run. But if the individual involved isn't a current officer, the OTS said it will be "inclined" to make the proceedings public.
