Letters to the Editor: Long List of Opponents To Telescope Project

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/06/90

In your May 23 Mount Graham editorial "A Species of Extremism" you said, "no one has shown persuasively that the project would harm the squirrel." For the record: All state, federal and University of Arizona biologists who prepared the official biological documentation have opposed it. Fish and Wildlife Service biologists, under oath, testified against it. Scientists nationwide have signed petitions opposing it. University of Arizona biologists risked tenure and signed. Max Planck and Ohio State scientists oppose their own institutions' participation. You also stated: "Telescope backers promise to reforest the logged areas." For the record, government biologists say it takes 200 years to create the old-growth forest the squirrel requires. It should be noted that only the University of Arizona would be allowed to cut trees in "critical habitat," having obtained congressional exemption from all our environmental laws. Do you suggest we spend millions of taxpayer dollars on a captive-breeding program so the astronomers can destroy the squirrels' homes and food supply?

You said the project requires 26 acres, but that is only the project "footprint." The government says the project would destroy some 130 acres of squirrel habitat -- mostly within the mountaintop's ecologically priceless 600-acre virgin spruce-fir forest.

What you did not say is that there are other places (New Mexico, Nevada, Utah, Arizona, South America, etc.) for advanced design telescopes that do not destroy endangered species or irreplaceable ecological treasures. Studies have ruled Hawaii's Mauna Kea far superior. Mount Graham was listed in a recent study as 37th worst of 58 sites in the Western U.S. Is a Univerity of Arizona astronomer's driving convenience reason to trash our environmental laws?

Jobs lost is pure hokum. There are institutions dying to steal the University of Arizona's collaborators. Jobs follow the collaborators.

Regarding your disavowal of subspecies that "are found elsewhere, have many subspecies or can be relocated" -- by that definition you demean human races, preclude plant varieties for lifesaving drugs and miracle grains, and ensure a barren North America without grizzly bear, Florida panther or Key deer since all the above are subspecies.

Robert Witzeman, M.D.

Conservation Chairperson

Maricopa Audubon Society

Phoenix, Ariz.
