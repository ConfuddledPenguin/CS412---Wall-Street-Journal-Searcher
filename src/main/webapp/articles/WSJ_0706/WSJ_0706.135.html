What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/06/90

NATO AGREED to revise its defense policy in view of changes in the East bloc. On the first day of a two-day London summit, the Western allies generally accepted a proposal by Bush to declare that NATO will use nuclear weapons only as a "last resort," U.S. officials said. The allies also agreed to an American proposal for inviting Soviet leader Gorbachev to address an alliance meeting. The invitation would be a way of showing that the group wants to enter an era of cooperation with its adversaries in Eastern Europe. The U.S. and Bonn apparently reached a compromise on a pledge for cutting back the army of a newly united Germany.

NATO wants to reassure the Soviets that there is nothing to fear from allowing a unified Germany to become a full member of the revamped organization.

---

Albania's foreign ministry pledged to grant passports to the hundreds of Albanians who have sought refuge in foreign embassies in the capital in an effort to flee their Communist homeland. The ministry appeared to deny a report that Albanians would no longer be able to apply for visas. The number of asylum-seekers rose to 600.

---

Gorbachev appeared to have staked an unassailable claim to remain head of the Soviet Communist Party as critics attending a party congress showed no indications of fielding any alternative candidates. The Soviet leader is facing re-election when the meeting comes to a conclusion next week.

---

Czechoslovakia's Parliament re-elected Havel as president. The election of the former dissident playwright to a two-year term culminated a seven-month transition to democratic rule. Havel had served as interim president after last year's peaceful revolution toppling the Communists. The Parliament was voted into office last month.

---

Israel's defense minister said he would cut troops in the occupied territories if violence eased in the 30-month-old Palestinian uprising. Arens, who was named to his post last month, made the comment during a tour of the Israeli-occupied Gaza Strip.

---

Poland's agriculture minister resigned in anticipation of a major cabinet shake-up by leader Mazowiecki, who is under pressure to dismiss holdover Communists from key posts. A government spokeswoman indicated the prime minister would announce policy changes today, but she declined to say how many ministers would be ousted.

---

India's governor in Kashmir gave security forces sweeping powers in their battle to quell a separatist revolt in the strife-torn region, where at least 700 people have been killed in six months. Leaders of the protests in Kashmir, India's only Moslem-majority state, have been demanding independence or annexation by neighboring Pakistan.

---

The U.S. and Britain plan to argue at next week's economic summit in Houston that a controversial plan for $15 billion in international aid to the Soviet Union is premature. Surprisingly, leading Soviet economists and officials agree. They say the help would be welcome, but that the country isn't ready to absorb it.

---

Kenyan police detained three more prominent individuals linked to recent calls for a legalized opposition in the one-party state. The detentions of a former vice president's son and two lawyers brought to 10 the number of people seized in a two-day sweep.

---

Defense attorneys filed an appeal in a Massachusetts court on behalf of David and Ginger Twitchell, a Christian Science couple found guilty Wednesday of manslaughter for withholding medical treatment from their son, who died in 1986 of a bowel infection. The couple, who said they relied on spiritual healing, face up to 20 years in prison.

---

Colombian security forces seized about 800 pounds of pure cocaine at an airport in northwestern Colombia as a wave of drug-related terrorism intensified in major cities, officials said. The cocaine was found aboard an airplane that was about to take off for the U.S., according to an army statement.

---

About 5,300 rebels fought Liberian troops in Monrovia's suburbs, according to an insurgent source, while soldiers ransacked stores and restaurants in the capital. More government advisers left the country as Doe clung desperately to power. The president has shown no sign of departing and has rejected a U.S. offer to help him flee.

---

Serbian authorities dissolved the local legislature and government in Kosovo, the troubled southern province administered by Yugoslavia's largest republic. Serbian officials also acted to gain control of Kosovo's media. The moves came in the wake of Monday's independence declaration by ethnic Albanian deputies in the local assembly.

---

Sri Lankan troops shot and killed 27 Tamil rebels in a new wave of violence in the northern and eastern parts of the Indian Ocean island, and the government said it planned to hold new elections in Tamil-dominated areas. At least 14 soldiers were also killed in rebel attacks. The guerrillas launched their latest offensive June 11.

Died: Mitch Snyder, 46, a leading advocate for the homeless, in Washington, D.C., an apparent suicide by hanging.
