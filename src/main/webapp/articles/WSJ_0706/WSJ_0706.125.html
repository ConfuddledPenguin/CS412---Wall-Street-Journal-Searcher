Heard on the Street: Carbide Attracts Some Admirers Despite Debt And Shaky Prices for Commodity Chemicals

  • Author: John R. Dorfman and Richard Koenig
  • Date: 07/06/90

Union Carbide is still paying the price for escaping the clutches of GAF Corp. in 1986. To avoid a takeover, Carbide went deeply in debt to pay a huge special dividend. It also sold its consumer products operations. Now Carbide is mainly a maker of commodity chemicals -- those made in large quantities by several manufacturers. The company is caught in a cyclical downturn of commodity-chemical prices and still bears a heavy, though reduced, debt burden. Its shares, which briefly rose above 30 in 1987 and 1989, closed yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange at 19 1/8, down 1/4 from Tuesday.

No question the stock is cheap. The question is whether cheapness is enough. Carbide is selling for only eight times estimated 1990 per-share earnings -- and a mere five times the past 12 months' earnings. It yields just over 5% in dividends.

At these prices, Carbide is picking up some fans. Five brokerage houses recommend it -- not a huge cheering section, but the biggest in quite a while. "The stock is arguably as cheap as it has ever been over the last 20 years," writes John L. Garcia, an analyst with Wertheim Schroder. It could double in two years, suggests Leslie Ravitz of Salomon Brothers. And Sasho Dalov, editor of Wall Street Wire, speculates that Carbide could be a takeover candidate.

But others who follow the stock closely say that Carbide is likely to stay cheap for at least a year or two. Earnings are being hurt by world-wide oversupply of Carbide's mainstay chemicals -- polyethylene (a common plastic) and ethylene glycol (used in antifreeze and polyester fabric). Carbide's profit is likely to fall to around $2.50 a share this year from $3.92 last year, analysts say.

That unfavorable outlook is already reflected in the stock price. But William Young, an analyst at Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette, says it is too early to buy on fundamentals. The balance between supply and demand for commodity chemicals began worsening a little over a year ago, he says, and "that down cycle tends to last more than one or two years."

Mr. Ravitz of Salomon Brothers thinks Carbide should appeal to patient investors. "Short-term, we don't think the stock's going to do anything," he says. But "the downside is moderate." Some time in the first half of 1991, he argues, the stock market will begin anticipating a recovery in prices of commodity chemicals.

Carbide's other major division -- industrial gases -- is growing faster than the chemicals-and-plastics division and enjoys improving prices. Unfortunately, it has major operations in Brazil, where an iron-handed anti-inflation program crushed economic activity earlier this year. So the industrial-gases division's 1990 results are expected to be flat.

Money managers and analysts have been hoping Carbide would spin off the industrial-gases business or sell a minority interest in it to the public to avoid the risk of a future takeover. Their appetites were whetted last year when Carbide set up separate divisions for its major business lines. But this February, Carbide said that it had decided not to do a spinoff or partial public offering.

Among the disappointed parties was the T. Rowe Price family of mutual funds, which had bought 600,000 shares early this year. "We thought that management was beginning to take actions to unlock the value" of the company, says Charlie Ober, a T. Rowe Price analyst. "We have been unpleasantly surprised that there hasn't been any follow-through."

The lack of follow-through, some money managers think, means Carbide no longer fears a takeover, now that such activity has cooled. A Carbide spokesman declined to go beyond the statement the company made in February or discuss the company's stock price.

Mr. Ravitz, for one, still has hopes. He thinks the marketplace may bludgeon Carbide into action. "Before the (February) announcement, the stock was at 24; now it's at 19," he says. He thinks Carbide could spin off its smallest unit, the carbon products division.

David A. Katz, chief investment officer at Matrix Asset Advisors in New York, says his firm won't buy Carbide because of its debt load. Currently, total debt is more than half of capital.

He prefers rival chemical makers B.F. Goodrich and Imperial Chemical Industries. With a good dose of revenue from specialty chemicals, Goodrich enjoys better pricing. It sells for about nine times the past 12 months' earnings and yields 4.7%. London-based Imperial's output is about evenly divided among commodity chemicals, specialty chemicals and pharmaceuticals. ICI, based in London, produces pharmaceuticals and specialty chemicals as well as commodity chemicals. It sells for less than 10 times earnings and yields 5.9%.
