Marketing & Media: Japanese Firms Jump Into Race To Make TV Films

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 07/06/90

TOKYO -- A major trading company and a leading supermarket chain have jointly launched Japan's latest entrant in the race to produce films and television programming. C. Itoh & Co., one of Japan's largest trading houses, and Seiyu Ltd., a member of the Saison retailing and hotel group, announced the establishment of Media International Corp. Takao Yoshiki, president of the new venture, said the Tokyo-based concern intends to co-produce television programming with foreign companies, to invest in film productions, to supply news from Japan and other Asian nations to foreign broadcasters, and to buy and sell audio-visual software.

With Japan's fledgling cable-television and direct-satellite broadcasting networks expected to proliferate in the next few years, there is widespread talk here of an impending shortage of "software," or television programs. So a variety of companies are hoping to fill the void.

American cable networks such as Turner Broadcasting System Co.'s Cable News Network and ESPN Inc. are supplying programming to the nascent market. Moreover, a number of Japanese companies have moved into the film-production business, with Sony Corp.'s $3.4 billion purchase of Columbia Pictures Entertainment Inc. the most prominent example.

Media International said that its aim is to help supply "high quality software" internationally by acting as a "software comprehensive trading company."

Exactly what that means won't be clear for at least a few months. Initially, Media International will be merely a pre-operational planning organization with capital of 550 million yen, or about $3.7 million. C. Itoh and Seiyu each is supplying 45.5% of that total, with Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank and Sumitomo Bank, two of the world's biggest banks, each providing 4.5%. Actual operations are expected to begin in November.

Mr. Yoshiki, previously vice president of NHK Enterprises, a subsidiary of Japan's public broadcasting network, said in an interview that Media International expects to increase its capital to about $30 million and hopes to achieve revenue of about $200 million in its first year. The new company said it plans eventually to draw in about 40 corporate participants from a cross-section of Japanese industry.

While a mammoth trading company and a supermarket chain would seem unlikely candidates for the entertainment business, both C. Itoh and Seiyu already have footholds in the industry. Seiyu has produced several film adaptations of Japanese literary works, and C. Itoh, one of several trading companies pursuing this new line of business, recently joined with ESPN to launch a cable television sports network in Japan.

While NHK Enterprises developed the idea for Media International, NHK won't have a direct equity participation in the new company, Mr. Yoshiki said. NHK will, however, stand to benefit by procuring programming for its two direct-broadcast satellite stations and for its traditional television and radio networks.
