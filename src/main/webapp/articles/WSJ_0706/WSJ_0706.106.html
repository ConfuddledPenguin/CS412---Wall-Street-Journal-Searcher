Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Dismissed Saatchi Executives Plan To Counter With Their Own Firm

  • Author: Joann S. Lublin
  • Date: 07/06/90

LONDON -- Two top Saatchi & Saatchi Co. executives dismissed in May intend to buy or set up their own ad agency, posing a potential competitive threat to the troubled communications giant. "We are still in the formative and design stage" for the would-be business, said Terry Bannister, ousted along with Roy Warman as joint chief executive officers of the company's advertising operations after a boardroom power struggle. "But in all probability, it will be in London" and will begin operations by year end, he said in a telephone interview yesterday. The pair had considered becoming ad industry consultants, Mr. Bannister said, but "you don't only want to make the bullets. You want to fire them as well."

Robert Louis-Dreyfus, Saatchi's new chief executive officer, fired the two men after attempting to demote them by slashing their corporate responsibilities. Messrs. Bannister and Warman were expected to receive contract settlements of roughly #1.25 million ($2.22 million) apiece that also would exclude them from the British advertising industry for four years. Each was earning about #345,000 a year before Saatchi's directors voted them a 10% pay cut earlier this year. Each executive had almost four years left on a five-year contract.

Mr. Bannister said the two now hope to complete a settlement with Saatchi by the end of July giving them the freedom to operate an agency. They likely will receive smaller severance packages, continue as Saatchi consultants and accept fairly loose limits on recruiting Saatchi clients and staffers. "We are moving forward with an agreement in principle with the company that's now being crafted by the lawyers," Mr. Bannister said.

The accord would prevent the ex-Saatchi executives from soliciting any current Saatchi advertising business "that we have been in charge of and highly influential over," he said. That prohibition might cover only parts of the world-wide Saatchi empire, Mr. Bannister noted. And it wouldn't prevent Saatchi clients from switching as long as the pair didn't actively recruit them.

Indeed, similar restrictions didn't stop one Saatchi client from awarding an approximately #1.25 million account yesterday to a breakaway shop formed in May by seven former officials of Saatchi's flagship London advertising agency. Reckitt & Colman PLC, a British consumer-products concern, said it chose Cowan, Kemsley, Taylor Ltd. to handle everything but media buying for its U.K. household-cleaner brand Dettox.

In the same way, any new agency created by Messrs. Warman and Bannister might lure unhappy staffers from Saatchi's London agency, Britain's largest, which they once ran. "There's always the threat that other disaffected people within Saatchi might find the opportunity {attractive} of joining Roy and Terry in a new agency {rather} than toughing it out at Charlotte Street," the London agency's nickname, suggested David Forster, an advertising analyst at U.K. brokers Kleinwort Benson Securities in London. "There's a danger that {Saatchi} would lose people as well as business."

A Saatchi spokesman said the company continues to negotiate a severance package with Messrs. Warman and Bannister, but declined to comment about details. He also dismissed the loss of the Dettox account, saying, "it's not terribly significant anyway."

Boyle Brands Moved

Reckitt & Colman, following its acquisition of American Home Products's Boyle Midway unit, pulled its newly acquired brands from Young & Rubicam and McCann-Erickson and placed them at a number of smaller shops.

Wayne, N.J.-based Reckitt & Colman, a unit of Reckitt & Colman PLC, said its ad budget, which includes a number of brands that aren't switching agencies, totals more than $40 million. A spokesman refused to say how much the brands it is moving will bill separately.

TBWA Advertising was perhaps the biggest winner, picking up the Woolite and Neet brands. According to Leading National Advertisers, a tracking firm, Woolite billed about $13 million last year; Neet billed about $2 million.

Bloom Agency, a unit of Bloom Cos., picked up Easy Off oven cleaner, which spent about $3 million in 1989, and two other small brands. Campbell-Mithun-Esty, a unit of Saatchi & Saatchi, won Wizard air fresheners; Partners & Shevack picked up the Holiday insecticide line.

Bloom, C-M-E and Partners & Shevack already handle other Reckitt & Colman accounts. The company spokesman said the changes reflected a desire to consolidate its advertising at fewer shops.

McCann-Erickson, an Interpublic Group agency, had Easy Off and also a large chunk of the Woolite business. Young & Rubicam had part of the Woolite account, as well as Neet, Wizard and a number of other small brands. It continues to handle American Home's Pam and Butter Buds brands.
