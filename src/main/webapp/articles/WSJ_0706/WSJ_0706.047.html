Letters to the Editor: Hidden Dangers in Species Extinction

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/06/90

Michael D. Copeland's June 7 editorial-page article, "No Red Squirrels? Mother Nature May Be Better Off," says that the use by environmentalists of the Endangered Species Act "is often a subterfuge for achieving other goals, such as the prevention of development." Subterfuge, as I understand it, implies dishonesty at worst, evasion at best. It doesn't fit. We use the Endangered Species Act -- along with the National Environmental Policy Act, the Clean Water Act, the Clean Air Act and scores of other laws -- to achieve environmental protection.

Mr. Copeland attacked my colleague Andy Stahl for acknowledging that the spotted owl was a useful ally in the campaign to save a little of the pitiful remnant of old-growth forest that still stands in the Pacific Northwest. "Evidently, Mr. Stahl is more concerned with old-growth timber than with the spotted owl," wrote Mr. Copeland, as if that were a crime. Of course our goal is to protect old growth: You can't have spotted owls without it. No one has ever denied, to my knowledge, that we are in favor of preserving old growth.

Mr. Copeland exhibits considerable bravery in advocating the extinction of species we know very little about. Bravery? Make that arrogance. Historic extinctions caused by nature are not necessarily equivalent to extinctions caused by man. The latter reflect decidedly unnatural upsets and imbalances in natural systems that we perpetuate at our peril.

Tom Turner

Staff Writer

Sierra Club Legal Defense Fund Inc.

San Francisco

Using Mr. Copeland's logic, it could be argued that the world would be better off without giant pandas, California condors, the American bald eagle and developmentally disabled people. Just who is to decide what species (or class) should be sacrificed?

Perhaps he should note that the past extinction of species as a natural byproduct of evolution was not generally something that happened overnight as a result of some developer's whim or greed.

Krisanne Edgcomb

Lynden, Wash.

---

I take exception to Mr. Copeland's contention that many of the world's species are irrelevant since they stand in the way of development projects.

He makes two specious arguments. The first is that natural mass extinctions, such as that of the dinosaurs, permit the human race to extinguish more species or certain subspecies. I assert on moral grounds that the human race has no such right. Mr. Copeland's second argument is that biological diversity, such as the existence of subspecies within one species, is of no value. That argument is in serious error, as demonstrated by the medical-research value of certain species to humans as well as the interdependence of various species within certain ecological systems. In certain cases, the loss of one species can cause the collapse of an entire system.

In conclusion, I agree that development decisions should not be totally blocked due to ecological considerations. However, such development must clearly carry the burden of proof that species extinction would not be the result. A mountaintop observatory is not worth the extinction of any species.

James H. Maitland

Darien, Conn.

---

In response to Mr. Copeland's squirrel bashing and greed-motivated rationalizations on the future of endangered species, he quotes Harvard Prof. Stephen Gould on his assessments of mass extinctions through time, stating that today's species represent less than 1% of all species that have ever existed. Extinctions of species through time were caused by natural forces, before man ever walked on this earth. Man is omnipotent when it comes to survival. He can easily wipe out anything of his choosing. If we allow bending of the rules of the Endangered Species Act, when would it stop? We should be doing all we can to save that 1%.

Donna Jean North

Dallas

---

Mr. Copeland's critique relies on arguments that belittle the benefits of "individual animal or plant categories." We can test the persuasiveness of those arguments if we substitute the phrase "individual humans or class of humans" for "individual animal or plant categories" and if we imagine Mr. Copeland is not disputing the wisdom of this law, but rather the wisdom of laws against murder and genocide.

His assertions could then be paraphrased as follows: (1) More than 95% of all humans who have ever lived are now dead; (2) despite these many deaths, the human population is actually increasing, past deaths making room for present numbers; (3) nature obviously entails death as well as life, so we may be wasting time, effort and money on humans who will die over time regardless of our efforts; (4) human traits that have helped folks survive in the past are not particularly important to future longevity, so preserving lives will not necessarily increase the life span of folks to come.

All these statements may be true. They are not necessarily meaningful, however. They fail regarding human exterminations for the same two reasons Mr. Copeland's arguments fail regarding animal extinctions. First, he implicitly assumes that teleological benefit-cost ethics are the only ones relevant to decision-making in this case. Such ethics serve nonmoral ends such as consumer welfare. He ignores imperative ethics that find rightness or wrongness intrinsic to deeds themselves, e.g., "Thou shalt not kill."

Mr. Copeland could defend himself by saying that imperative ethics should be ignored when it comes to keeping people alive; that we should, for example, calculate the benefits before spending added costs of, say $300 billion a year on more medical care for the expiring elderly; and that imperative ethics should likewise be ignored for species preservation. This would be a good answer for Mr. Copeland if by "species preservation" he meant efforts to keep alive species that would die naturally from causes unrelated to mankind's actions, just as it is a good argument when considering health care for hopelessly ill humans. But we are not talking about "species preservation" in this sense, which brings us to the second flaw.

The Endangered Species Act attempts to outlaw exterminations of species insofar as they are caused by human overharvesting or habitat destruction. People may find imperative ethics useful here -- even imperative. Such ethics are useful in assessing the genocide of the Tasmanian Aborigines and our laws against the painful slaughter of animals, not to mention more obvious matters. Similarly, the Endangered Species Act reflects our attempt to apply imperative ethics to the way we treat other species, not the way Mother Nature treats them.

These distinctions are critical. If Mr. Copeland could convince us that the conscious taking of life forever lies on the same moral plane as letting it expire naturally, then teleological ethics might overrule imperative ethics, and his arguments depreciating the benefits of individual species might be meaningful.

Douglas F. Greer

Professor of Economics

San Jose State University

San Jose, Calif.
