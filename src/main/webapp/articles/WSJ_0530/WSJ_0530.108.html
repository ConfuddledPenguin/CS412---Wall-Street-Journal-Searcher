Drexel Subsidiaries Seek Protection Under Chapter 11

  • Author: Wade Lambert
  • Date: 05/30/90

NEW YORK -- Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc.'s brokerage subsidiary and 14 other Drexel units filed under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code, joining the parent company and two other affiliates that previously had filed. Meanwhile, Drexel said that John F. Sorte, previously co-leader of the corporate finance department, is succeeding Frederick Joseph as chief executive officer of the brokerage unit, Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc.

The latest filings put practically all of Drexel empire's assets and liabilities under the control of the federal bankruptcy court in Manhattan. The filing by the brokerage unit also opens the door for creditors to more closely scrutinize -- and possibly recover -- the $250 million in bonuses Drexel executives received just weeks before the parent company filed for bankruptcy-law protection. The bonuses were paid by the brokerage unit and were off-limits to creditors as long as the unit didn't file a bankruptcy petition.

"The filing in bankruptcy court will make it easier for creditors to recover the millions in bonuses that were paid in the dying days of the empire," said Barry Dichter, of Cadwalader, Wickersham & Taft, an attorney for the central bank of Portugal, a major Drexel creditor.

The parent company filed under Chapter 11 on Feb. 13. At the time, Drexel said it would liquidate all its assets and go out of business. In the filing by the brokerage unit, however, Drexel raised the possibility that it may emerge from the bankruptcy proceedings as a going concern.

A Drexel spokesman said that under a Chapter 11 reorganization plan, the company now is planning to resume operating in at least one area of its past financial services. Although he declined to say what areas might be considered, he said it wouldn't be as a stock brokerage firm or commodities broker.

Mr. Sorte, 42 years old, also was elected a director and senior executive vice president of the parent; Mr. Joseph continues as president of the parent. Because Mr. Joseph has come under much criticism for Drexel's demise, the change in top management lends credence to talk of a Drexel revival after the bankruptcy proceedings. "This is an indication that they are serious about emerging as a going concern," Mr. Dichter said.

In its bankruptcy petition, the brokerage unit -- Drexel's largest subsidiary -- said it had $2.1 billion in assets and $1.09 billion in liabilities. The assets include $304 million owed by the parent company.

The filing said the brokerage firm's largest creditors, excluding any claims by other Drexel entities, are Equitable Life Assurance Society, which is owed $20.8 million; New England Mutual Life, $18.8 million; and Citibank, a unit of Citicorp, $13.2 million. The claims also include $1 million owed to Paul Weiss Rifkin Wharton & Garrison, the law firm representing Mr. Milken, who recently pleaded guilty to insider-trading related charges.

In connection with the filing, U.S. Bankruptcy Judge Howard Buschman III approved the separation of $88.7 million in funds from the brokerage unit's other assets. The Drexel spokesman said the funds represent the potential liability Drexel may owe in possible claims by former customers. In its filing, the Drexel unit said the Securities and Exchange Commission informed the company that it wouldn't object to the Chapter 11 filing as long as the customer funds were separated and other requirements met.

The filing also said that the brokerage unit and the other subsidiaries are named as defendants in about 400 lawsuits. The company said it wouldn't be able to resolve the mounting litigation unless it reorganizes under Chapter 11. It said the maturing of obligations to creditors also made the Chapter 11 filing now necessary.
