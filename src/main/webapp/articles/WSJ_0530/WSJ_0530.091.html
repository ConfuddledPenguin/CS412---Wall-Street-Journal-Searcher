Abreast of the Market: Stocks Soar, Sending Industrials And S&P's 500 Index to Records

  • Author: Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 05/30/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices rocketed higher, sending the Dow Jones Industrial Average and the Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index to records. The industrial average closed with a gain of 49.57 points to 2870.49.

Some analysts thanked Mikhail Gorbachev for the unexpected rally, while others thanked IBM.

The Soviet president arrives in Washington today for a four-day summit beginning tomorrow. Some market minders say summit meetings often spark stock rallies, and that Mr. Gorbachev has a particularly strong effect on the market.

But International Business Machines was clearly the leader in the stock market. IBM soared 3 1/2, or 3%, to 119 3/4, more than compensating for the 2 1/2 point loss incurred in Friday's sell-off. The stock's strength was attributable in part to this week's Barron's, which featured Big Blue in its cover story.

IBM's early strength appeared to rally stocks from their weak opening to move higher throughout the day. The gains surprised many, who had expected Friday's steep sell-off to carry over into yesterday.

"We deserved a down day," says Ralph J. Acampora, head of technical research at Kidder Peabody.

A late spate of computer-driven buy programs kicked the market to new highs.

The final upward surge also broke the S&P 500 index above its previous all-time high. The index of the market's 500 largest stocks closed at 360.65, on a 6.07-point advance, surpassing its high set last October.

The New York Stock Exchange Composite Index rose 2.86 to 196.57, and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 5.08 to 335.42.

Advancers led decliners by 1,107 to 434, with 462 stocks ending unchanged. Volume rose to 137,410,000 shares from 120,250,000 shares Friday.

Don R. Hays, director of investment strategy at Wheat First Butcher & Singer, said the S&P 500 record may give the market added assurance.

He said the rally might carry the industrial average above the 2900 level before it runs out of steam, despite the fact that many of the blue-chip stocks have risen too high, too fast.

A.C. Moore, director of research at Argus Research, said stocks may get another boost from economic data due at the end of the week.

On Friday, the Labor Department will release its report for May U.S. employment.

Mr. Moore believes that data will strengthen the belief that the economy is slowing and that interest rates ought to come down. The bond market should react favorably to the news, giving bonds further room to rally, he said.

Many market watchers noted that the one ingredient missing from yesterday's rally was strong volume. Trading was particularly quiet until the final half hour, they said.

IBM surged to a 52-week high for the stock. Other technology stocks followed. Computer maker Compaq rose 1 3/4 to 120 3/8.

Philip Morris was high on the most active list, ending unchanged at 42 1/2. The stock came under pressure late last week as some money managers began cutting their exposure to tobacco companies after a government agency proposed new restrictions on cigarette sales.

Time Warner gained 3 3/8 to 109. In this week's Barron's, money manager Michael Price calls the stock "undervalued." The stock had fallen with other cable television shares over fears of new regulation in that industry. But market players now expect any regulation to be less stringent than originally feared.

Merrill Lynch slipped 5/8 to 24 1/8. The company may need to add as much as $200 million to reserves this year to cover soured bridge loans and other forms of junk-bond exposure, The Wall Street Journal reported. The company took a $470 million pretax charge last year.

Tektronix added 1 to 15 3/8. The company said it expects to take a $70 million restructuring charge in its fiscal fourth quarter ended Saturday. The restructuring will involve the layoff of about 10% of its employees. The company also says it ended talks to sell its Grass Valley Group unit to Japan's Sony.

L.A. Gear fell 1 3/4 to 47 3/8 as investors began slicing fat off the stock. Kidder Peabody cut its rating on the athletic footwear maker's shares to "hold" from "current buy," saying that the stock has run about as far as it can go for now.

Rival Reebok International, meanwhile, added 5/8 to 19 1/4, while Nike rose 1 3/4 to 81 3/4 in over-the-counter trading.

Bank of New England eased 3/8 to 3 1/8. The Journal reported that James F. McNally, chairman of the bank's Connecticut Bank & Trust unit, intends to retire this summer. Mr. McNally's successor has yet to be named.

Tyco Laboratories added 2 1/2 to 50 3/4. The company said it expects to report sales of about $2.1 billion in the year ending tomorrow, up from sales of $1.97 billion last year. Tyco also agreed to buy the operating assets of an Australian maker of fire protection equipment for $350 million in cash. The added revenue from Wormald should boost fiscal 1991 sales to $3.45 billion, Tyco said.

Corning Glass Works dropped 1 3/8 to 48 3/4. Donaldson Lufkin Jenrette turned neutral on the stock, after having recently rated it as "moderately attractive," according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 2.72 to 362.56. Amex volume totaled 11,440,000 shares, compared with 11,400,000 shares Friday. There were 300 issues advancing, 239 declining and 241 issues were unchanged.
