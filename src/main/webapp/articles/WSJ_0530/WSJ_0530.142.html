Loan to Revlon Is Restructured By Four Banks --- Changes, Relatively Minor, Show Lenders' Concern For Debt-Heavy Firms

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 05/30/90

NEW YORK -- Four major banks have restructured a pending $1.8 billion loan to Revlon Inc., in the latest sign of lenders' nervousness about making new loans to heavily indebted companies. Although the changes in the Revlon refinancing are relatively minor, bankers say they occurred partly in response to regulatory pressure on banks to limit loans for highly leveraged transactions.

Revlon, controlled by New York investor Ronald O. Perelman, has operated under a heavy debt load ever since a company controlled by Mr. Perelman acquired Revlon in 1986 in a hostile takeover financed by high-risk, high-yielding junk bonds.

The Revlon debt restructuring, first disclosed yesterday in the American Banker, a trade publication, was forced on the company and the four banks after other U.S. banks balked at terms of the new loan. The four banks, units of Chase Manhattan Corp., Chemical Bank Corp., Citicorp, and Manufacturers Hanover Corp., were seeking to resell parts of the loan to other banks.

The changes in the borrowing will boost the fees paid to some banks that participate in the loan, and accelerate the repayment of part of the loan by Revlon, a maker of toiletries, cosmetics and other beauty products. The Revlon borrowing refinances existing Revlon bank debt.

The bulk of the new loans carry an interest rate of 1 1/2 percentage points above the prime rate, a common benchmark for bank loans; the prime is now 10%. The interest rate wasn't affected by the restructuring. Revlon has another $800 million in junk bonds outstanding, which yield more than 18% based on their market prices, indicating some uncertainty among investors about the company's finances.

The new Revlon debt package consists of $1.25 billion in term loans plus a $550 million revolving credit without any set maturity. Originally the term loans had a four-year maturity, meaning they had to be repaid or refinanced in 1994. To help the banks resell the loans, Mr. Perelman agreed to repay half the term loan in 1992.

A statement by the four banks said of the Revlon loan: "In order to respond to current market conditions, Revlon and the co-agents are making certain structural and pricing adjustments to the $1.8 billion transaction currently in the marketplace." The loan is expected to close in June.

Although Revlon confirmed that the terms of the loan have been changed, a spokesman stressed that the refinancing is voluntary and that the four lead banks have committed to lend all the money.

An individual close to the company said the new bank debt will reduce Revlon's bank interest costs by about one-half a percentage point. The same person said Revlon's projected 1990 revenue is $2.8 billion, with expected operating cash flow -- before interest, depreciation, and amortization -- of more than $400 million.

The amicable revamping of the loan's terms stands in sharp contrast to a $7 billion bank borrowing for a proposed takeover of United Airlines parent UAL Corp., which collapsed last October amid wrangling between two lead banks and UAL management.

The market for bank loans for leveraged transactions has grown increasingly wobbly in the past year, during which many heavily indebted companies have suffered financial reversals and some have been forced to seek bankruptcy-law protection. Bank regulators have since launched a crackdown on loans for highly leveraged transactions.

One banker familiar with the loan said, "There was tougher going than expected in placing the loan. It's a leveraged transaction with all the complications therein in a marketplace with a difficult tone."
