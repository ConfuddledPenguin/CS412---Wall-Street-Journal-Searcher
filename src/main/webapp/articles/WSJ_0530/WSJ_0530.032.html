OECD Officials Gather for Paris Meeting --- Effort Will Be Made to Reignite World Trade Talks

  • Author: Peter Truell and Tim Carrington
  • Date: 05/30/90

PARIS -- Economic officials from 24 major industrial countries will try to boost faltering multilateral trade talks when they gather here today for the annual meeting of the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development. The U.S. is especially eager to galvanize the talks. "Our window of opportunity is now," said White House Trade Representative Carla Hills in a meeting with reporters here yesterday. "It's just going to take the political will to do it."

Similarly, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady, speaking in London before flying to Paris, said, "We are determined to see these negotiations completed on time and with concrete results."

The trade talks, called the Uruguay Round, are designed to establish rules to govern the approximately one-third of world trade that isn't covered by the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade. Accomplishing that goal would mean bringing agriculture, services, intellectual property, textiles and trade-related investment under GATT's jurisdiction, toughening GATT rules and fully integrating developing countries into the 96-nation organization.

The Uruguay Round talks are scheduled to be completed by year end, but there are growing concerns that negotiators won't be able to meet that timetable. Under U.S. law, the talks must be finished by then or any legislation to put new rules into effect won't qualify for streamlined, "fast-track" consideration by Congress; that would be a severe blow to the prospects for an accord.

U.S. leaders are concerned that the European Community won't be willing to commit itself to reduce agricultural subsidies substantially. "I think it's important for {the EC} to understand that agriculture is a key to the success of the round," Mrs. Hills said.

Just last week, President Bush said the U.S. sees agricultural-trade reform as fundamental to the success of the GATT talks, which began in Punta del Este, Uruguay, in 1986. And he pointedly added that the U.S. would prefer having no agreement to a bad one.

Besides dealing with the GATT talks, economic officials meeting here are expected to review recent developments in the world economy, the transition to market economies in Eastern Europe and environmental concerns. They also are likely to discuss so-called tied-aid, which the U.S. is trying to curtail. Tied aid is foreign assistance that governments provide to developing countries on the condition they buy goods from companies in the donor country.

There also may soon be new members of the club of 24 rich nations; both South Korea and Singapore are possible candidates, according to OECD officials.

Meanwhile yesterday, Treasury Secretary Brady and his counterparts from about 40 other countries formally launched the new European Bank for Reconstruction & Development in a ceremony at the Elysee Palace. The EBRD, which will lend to the new democracies in Eastern Europe and, to a very limited extent, to the Soviet Union, is to be based in London.

French President Francois Mitterrand's adviser Jacques Attali will head the new institution, and an American is expected to fill the No. 2 slot. But at his breakfast address in London, Mr. Brady said "no decision has been made" on an American deputy for Mr. Attali.

President Mitterrand said he expects the bank to play a "catalytic role" in helping the East bloc nations build market economies. He called for the parliaments of the participating nations to ratify the articles of agreement promptly. The bank will begin formal operations once two-thirds have done so.

Separately, OECD Secretary General Jean-Claude Paye this week took a positive view of the state of the global economy, but he added that there are "dark spots in the picture." In its annual forecast released for the meeting, the OECD predicted the economies of the 24-nation group will grow 2.9% this year and next, after inflation. It forecast inflation at 4.4% in each of the next two years.

The OECD isn't worried that German unification will rekindle inflation. Indeed, the OECD predicted that even with full economic unification, German inflation would rise to 3% this year and 3.4% next -- comfortably below the OECD average.

But the OECD forecasts a rise in U.S. inflation to 4.2% this year and 4.5% in 1991 from 4.1% in 1989. The organization doesn't foresee a recession in the U.S. soon. It predicts the U.S. economy will grow 2.3% this year and 2.5% next year, after inflation.
