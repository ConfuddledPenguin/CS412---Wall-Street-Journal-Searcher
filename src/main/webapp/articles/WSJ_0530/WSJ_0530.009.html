Letters to the Editor: Limit Federal Terms At the State Level

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/30/90

Your April 18 editorial "Lawmakers for Life" was on the mark. If the principle underlying the American system of government could be summed up in a single sentence, it would be Lord Acton's: "Power corrupts." The S&L scandal alone is reason enough to limit the power of our Imperial Congress. I serve on the board of Americans to Limit Congressional Terms, and heartily endorse its efforts. But many state legislators who agree in principle with term limits are reluctant to embrace the ALCT's initiative because of the dangers, real and imagined, of a "runaway" Constitutional Convention.

In Colorado, we're trying a different approach. Coloradans to Limit Terms is attempting to put on the November ballot an amendment to our state constitution to limit Colorado's U.S. senators and representatives to 12 consecutive years in the same office, and to limit the governor, state executive officers and state legislators to eight consecutive years in the same office.

Term limits have been endorsed by two of the three candidates for the U.S. Senate in Colorado this year -- Republican Rep. Hank Brown and Democrats Carlos Lucero and Josie Heath -- and by all three candidates for the House seat Rep. Brown is vacating.

We're trying to bring about federal term limits in much the same way earlier Progressives obtained direct election of U.S. senators. The Congress then was as reluctant as the Congress now to reform. But Colorado and other progressive states passed laws requiring the legislature to select the Senate candidate who had won a popular election. Eventually, Congress bowed to the inevitable and ratified de jure what was already de facto the practice in much of the country.

If other states were to follow the Colorado approach, we could bring about federal term limits without the need to call a Constitutional Convention.

Terry Considine

State Senator

Colorado

Denver
