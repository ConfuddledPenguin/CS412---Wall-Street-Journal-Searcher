WPPSS's Legacy: Premium Yields Still a Fact of Life

  • Author: Frederick Rose
  • Date: 05/30/90

The credit-rating agencies that are cracking down on banks and overleveraged companies are boosting ratings on bonds issued by the biggest defaulter in the municipal market. These ratings boosts -- by Moody's Investors Service Inc. yesterday and by Standard & Poor's Corp. last week -- come just as Washington Public Power Supply System prepares to sell $600 million of bonds next week.

But even with the higher ratings, market analysts said WPPSS's offering of bonds maturing in two to 27 years will require premium yields to attract investors. That is because WPPSS, known on Wall Street as "Whoops," still bears the black eye of defaulting on $2.25 billion of bonds in 1983. And the onus persists even though the bonds WPPSS is selling with the higher ratings are for power plants for which there wasn't any default.

While WPPSS bonds have been well-received by institutions, individual investors still shy away from the name. "The average guy on the street still doesn't want to own {WPPSS} bonds," said Bruce Whiteford, a vice president and manager of the fixed-income department of Chase Manhattan Bank's U.S. Private Banking Group.

Prices of WPPSS bonds didn't move yesterday in reaction to the ratings news because of the impending offering, which adds still more new supply to the marketplace. WPPSS, a quasi-government agency that supplies electricity in the state of Washington, sold $721 million of bonds last September.

That offering was the first time WPPSS raised money in the public debt market since its default on nuclear units 4 and 5 bonds. The September sale consisted of refunding bonds for nuclear units 1 and 3. Premium interest rates offered on the refunding securities resulted in strong demand at the time.

Moody's said it upgraded to double-A from single-A about $6.32 billion of revenue bonds related to WPPSS's nuclear plants 1, 2 and 3. The new double-A rating also applies to the $600 million of refunding revenue bonds for nuclear units 2 and 3, scheduled to be sold next week through a group lead by First Boston Corp.

Also boosted to double-A from single-A was $26 million of miscellaneous securities of WPPSS -- $17 million issued for the Hanford Generating Project, and $9 million issued for the Packwood Lake Hydroelectric Project.

Bonds related to WPPSS's nuclear units 1, 2 and 3 are guaranteed by Bonneville Power Administration and weren't part of the 1983 default, the biggest municipal default ever.

Moody's said its upgrade reflects "continued strong performance at Bonneville Power Administration," the federally created agency left to pick up some of the pieces from WPPSS's giant miscalculation of the need for nuclear power in the Pacific Northwest.

Moody's noted that Bonneville's "role is a critical ratings consideration, given the fact that it operates in a region where public power issues have a long history of being particularly volatile and political." The ratings company said Bonneville has demonstrated a "capacity and willingness to establish itself institutionally as a competitive, self-supporting enterprise, with sound resource-planning policies."

Last week, Standard & Poor's raised the rating on WPPSS's revenue bonds for nuclear units 1, 2 and 3 and related securities to double-A from double-A-minus, citing similar conclusions as Moody's.
