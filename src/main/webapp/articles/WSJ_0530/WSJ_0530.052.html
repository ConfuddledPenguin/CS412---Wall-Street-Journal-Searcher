Farley May Have to Fight Bondholders Over Control of West Point-Pepperell

  • Author: Robert Johnson
  • Date: 05/30/90

CHICAGO -- William Farley, still pinched by a shortage of cash in his $1.56 billion acquisition of West Point-Pepperell Inc., may soon have to battle his bondholders over voting control of the company. Ironically, his most potent weapon in such a fight may be an outside evaluation suggesting he overpaid for the 1989 acquisition -- making it less advantageous for his creditors to force him into bankruptcy. West Point-Pepperell is a West Point, Ga.-based textile company.

Kidder, Peabody & Co., financial adviser to some of Mr. Farley's bondholders, suggested to its clients last week that they seek voting control, one person close to the situation said. Officials at Kidder declined to comment, except to say that "no consensus" on strategy has yet been reached among bondholders. Officials at Farley Inc. declined comment. Farley Inc. is Mr. Farley's closely held manufacturing concern, and the overall umbrella company for his business empire.

Further, Kidder has surprised some bondholders by estimating West Point-Pepperell's breakup value in the range of only $1.7 billion to $1.9 billion, or up to $500 million less than its total debt load, sources said. The low valuation may eliminate one of the bondholders' possible tactics: forcing Mr. Farley into bankrupcty and liquidating West Point-Pepperell to recover their investment.

Troubles worsened for Mr. Farley and his creditors in March when West Point Acquisition Corp., the unit he set up to buy West Point-Pepperell fell into default on $796 million in bank debt and missed bondholder interest payments.

Last month, Mr. Farley offered to surrender majority ownership of West Point-Pepperell, which he fought so hard to win in a takeover battle last year. But Mr. Farley's offer would leave him in control of West Point through his ownership of a series of so-called super-voting privilege common shares, sources said.

In April, according to people on Wall Street, Mr. Farley offered certain debt holders a combined stake of 65% in a restructured version of West Point-Pepperell. The transfer of the equity stake would help reduce and delay certain interest payments, sources said.

Though a major setback for Mr. Farley's once-burgeoning industrial and investment empire, the exchange offer to bondholders nevertheless may be the only strategy to keep him out of bankruptcy-law proceedings. But if Kidder's low valuation of West Point-Pepperell is accurate, Mr. Farley may avoid a bankruptcy-law filing for now because he apparently overpaid for his takeover target.

Mr. Farley has completed his tender offer on 95% of West Point-Pepperell, but he is about $83 million short of the cash needed to complete the transaction for 100% control. Sources close to the transaction said his bankers, led by Wells Fargo & Co., San Francisco, and Bankers Trust Co., are likely to loan him the cash needed to close the deal if holders of West Point Acquisition bonds agree to the debt-for-equity exchange. Bankers Trust is a unit of New York-based Bankers Trust New York Corp.

Kidder's recommendations to bondholders are generally conciliatory, sources said. They added that bondholders' negotiations with Mr. Farley over restructuring ownership of West Point-Pepperell may take several months.

Mr. Farley's financial strength ebbed earlier this year with the collapse of his investment banker, Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. His Farley Inc. had a pretax loss of about $40 million in 1989, analysts estimate.
