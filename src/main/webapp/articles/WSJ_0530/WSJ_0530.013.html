Postal Service Faces Hurdle in Switching To Private Financing

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/30/90

WASHINGTON -- The Treasury has created a major stumbling block for the Postal Service's plan to raise money in the private credit market. The department has told the service that it can't switch its borrowing to the private market from the Federal Financing Bank unless the service gives up its special ties to the government, including a $2 billion line of credit to the Treasury.

Comer Coppie, senior assistant postmaster general for finance, made it clear that the Postal Service isn't prepared to relinquish its Treasury credit line. Another benefit, the ability to request a federal guarantee for its debt, isn't something the Postal Service contemplates using, he said.

Mr. Coppie said the Treasury's position on Postal Service borrowing was spelled out in a recent letter from Under Secretary Robert Glauber. A copy of the letter wasn't available, but a Treasury spokesman confirmed the department's stance.

Mr. Coppie said the Postal Service remains committed in principle to changing its relationship with the Treasury, and reiterated the agency's desire to borrow in the private market rather than from the FFB, a financing entity within the Treasury that raises money for many agencies.

"We continue to have open communications" with the Treasury, Mr. Coppie said. Still, he said, it seems clear at this point that the Postal Service's next round of borrowing will be done from the FFB.

The Postal Service was taken out of the federal budget by last year's budget reconciliation bill. It maintains that borrowing in the private market would be consistent with the off-budget treatment, that it would have more flexibility in the private market, and that the discipline imposed by the marketplace would be beneficial.

The Postal Service is being supported in its quest by some Wall Street firms interested in potential business opportunities, officials said.
