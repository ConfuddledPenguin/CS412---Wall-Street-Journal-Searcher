OTC Focus: Market Surges as Technology Stocks Continue to Spur Gains; Adobe Rebounds

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 05/30/90

Over-the-counter stocks rebounded in quiet post-holiday trading, but trailed the gains of exchange-listed issues. The Nasdaq Composite Index rose 3.82 to 457.51, a 0.84% gain, compared with the 1.76% jump in the Dow Jones Industrial Average. Advancing shares led decliners, 1,014 to 868, as volume slipped to 133.9 million from 152.2 million Friday.

"It was a typical bounce back on a quiet day," said Richard Bruno, head of BUSY OTC trading at PaineWebber, adding that some investors appeared to still be out for the long Memorial Day weekend. Mr. Bruno said he saw a number of institutions buying technology shares in the wake of Friday's 1% decline in the OTC market, led by the 15 1/4 plunge of Adobe Systems.

Adobe rebounded 2 5/8 to 37 7/8 yesterday, joining other technology shares in a surge led by the 3 1/2 point advance, to 119 3/4, of International Business Machines. Apple Computer rose 1 to 41; Intel added 1 1/8 to 48 5/8; Oracle Systems gained 3/4 to 19 7/8; Seagate Technology advanced 1/2 to 14 1/4 and Sun Microsystems climbed 1/4 to 30 1/2.

Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst David Nelson said he is sticking with the brokerage firm's top investment rating on Adobe. Mr. Nelson said he believes the shortfall in the Mountain View, Calif., software maker's revenue is temporary.

The analyst, who Friday cut his 1990 and 1991 earnings estimates for the company by 30 cents a share, yesterday added 15 cents a share to Friday's estimates after reviewing the company's earnings outlook in more detail over the weekend.

Mr. Nelson said he now estimates Adobe will earn $2 a share this year and $2.75 in 1991, compared with $1.55 in 1989.

Cabot Medical rose 13/16 to 5 5/8 on more than 11 times average volume. Warren G. Wood, president and chief executive officer of the Langhorne, Pa., medical-instrument maker, told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report there weren't any corporate developments to explain the increased trading activity. W.H. Newbold's Son & Co. analyst Edward J. Stevetski suggested Cabot Medical's recent quarterly earnings report and an advertisement about the company in Monday's edition of Barron's might have drawn investors to the stock.

Harris & Harris Group gained 5/16 to 2 7/8 on more than 1.1 million shares after Worms & Co. reported that it cut its stake in the New York insurance brokerage and financial management group to 4.6% of its common shares outstanding. Worms didn't give a reason for reducing its holdings. Stocks sometimes gain after the sale of stock removes downward pressure on the shares.

Sun Sportswear tumbled 2 1/8 to 10 5/8 on more than 18 times average volume. Deborah Skiles, vice president of finance for the Kent, Wash., clothing designer and marketer, said in an interview the stock apparently fell in reaction to a report by Piper Jaffray Hopwood analyst Richard E. Pyle, who cut his earnings estimates for the company's second quarter by 33% and full year projection by 15% because of sluggish order rates, according to DJPIR. However, Mr. Pyle continues to rate the stock a "buy," DJPIR said. Ms. Skiles said she agreed with "the trend" of Mr. Pyle's downward revisions, but declined to comment on his specific estimates.

Big B dropped 1 3/8 to 9 7/8 after the Bessemer, Ala., operator of drug and food stores reported net income for the first quarter ended May 12 of five cents a share, down from 30 cents a year earlier. Big B attributed the profit fall primarily to financing costs and operating losses from 85 stores it acquired in February 1989.

Bio-Logic Systems stumbled 3/4 to 4 1/4 after the Mundelein, Ill., designer and marketer of computerized diagnostic systems reported net income for the 1990 fiscal year ended Feb. 28 of 13 cents a share, down from 21 cents a year earlier.

Statewide Bancorp slid 1 1/4 to 7 1/2 after the Toms River, N.J., bank said late Friday that it increased its allowance for loan losses by $30 million, suspended its quarterly 20-cents-a-share dividend and agreed to a close review of its main subsidiary under an agreement with the comptroller of the currency.

Silk Greenhouse slid 1/4 to 4 1/2 after PIR said that some analysts are concerned about inventory levels and credit conditions outlined by the Tampa, Fla., operator of artificial flower stores in its latest quarterly filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission.

Analyst Harry G. Katica of Raymond, James & Associates, which co-managed Silk Greenhouse's initial public offering two years ago, told DJPIR that he now recommends investors sell their Silk Greenhouse holdings. However, Alex. Brown & Sons analyst Fred E. Wintzer Jr. said he continues to hope the retailer can turn itself around, although he has a "hold" opinion on it. Silk Greenhouse Chairman and President Jere L. Bradwell declined to comment.
