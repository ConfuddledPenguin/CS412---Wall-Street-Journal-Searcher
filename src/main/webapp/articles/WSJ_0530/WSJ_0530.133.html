DHL International Stake to Be Bought By Three Concerns

  • Author: Susan Carey
  • Date: 05/30/90

BRUSSELS -- In an attempt to get a bigger piece of the booming global express-parcel business, Lufthansa, Japan Air Lines and Japanese trading company Nissho Iwai Corp. agreed as expected to buy minority stakes in DHL International Ltd., the world's largest air-courier company. Patrick Lupo, chief executive officer of DHL International, said the three partners also are negotiating to purchase small stakes in DHL Corp., the U.S. arm of the closely held company.

According to the agreement, the three companies will buy equal shares in DHL International, a Hong Kong holding company run out of Brussels, and Middlestown N.V., a holding company in Netherlands Antilles. Mr. Lupo said the two vehicles together control all DHL's international operations, which have annual revenue of $1.3 billion.

Initially, Lufthansa and JAL will buy 5% of the stock of both companies, while Nissho Iwai will take 2.5%. But the three have options to boost their stakes during the next 18 months. The two airlines can increase their stakes to 25% each and Nissho Iwai can buy as much as 7.5%.

In Tokyo, JAL said this total equity stake of 57.5% represents an investment of about $500 million. DHL and Lufthansa declined to comment on the figure.

The deal is subject to various government approvals.

Mr. Lupo said DHL Corp., the U.S. operation, has revenue of approximately $500 million a year. He said he believed the three partners would buy stakes smaller than those they will take in the international DHL. In any case, U.S. law would limit foreign investment in DHL Corp. to a total of 25% of the shares.

Founded in 1969 by three U.S. entrepreneurs, DHL has grown to become the world's largest international courier service. DHL serves 184 countries and carried more than 50 million shipments in 1989.

In recent years, the company has faced intense competition from Federal Express Co. of Memphis, Tenn., United Parcel Service of America Inc. of Greenwich, Conn., and TNT Ltd. of Australia. The capital infusion from Lufthansa, JAL and Nissho Iwai will help DHL keep its competitive edge, analysts said.

The new investors also could ease the pressure on DHL to become a major international air carrier, said Cotton Daly, director of cargo services of New York consultants Simat, Helliesen & Eichner Inc. DHL uses its own planes to fly packages within the U.S. and within Europe, but uses other airlines to carry its shipments long distances.

DHL's Mr. Lupo said JAL and Lufthansa together carry less than 20% of DHL's international shipments. He said the company intends to continue placing its international shipments on many airlines, based on price and scheduling.

For JAL and Lufthansa, the DHL tie-up is a way to gain easy access to an extensive world-wide distribution network. For Nissho Iwai, which has vast experience in shipping large volumes of goods, the deal is a way to gain experience in small aircargo shipments.
