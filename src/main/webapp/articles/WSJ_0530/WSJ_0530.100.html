Who's News: Judith Fellheimer Quits Equimark Post, In Suprise End to Husband-Wife Team

  • Author: James S. Hirsch
  • Date: 05/30/90

PITTSBURGH -- Judith E. Fellheimer resigned her top management position at Equimark Corp., bringing a surprise end to the bank holding company's controversial husband-wife management team. The departure of Mrs. Fellheimer, who is credited with helping her husband, Alan S. Fellheimer, save Equimark after he became chairman in 1985, comes several months after she was promoted to vice chairman of the bank's main subsidiary, Equibank. And it comes amid growing turmoil at the bank. In recent weeks, Equimark has had to abort its nine-month effort to acquire a Texas bank, has dismissed a half-dozen senior managers and has seen its stock price continue to erode. Rumors have also circulated that the bank will be put up for sale, but Mr. Fellheimer says he has no such plans.

Yesterday's announcement marks a stunning personal change for the Fellheimers. When the couple married 15 years ago, they pledged they would be business partners forever, and until yesterday had kept that vow.

In separate interviews, the Fellheimers said the resignation reflects broad cost-cutting measures that the bank, which had a $15.4 million loss in the last quarter, has been forced to take. A total of about 20 managers had already been dismissed, but more cuts were needed.

Mr. Fellheimer said his wife "technically agreed to be terminated, but without cause." He indicated that she would be given a yet-to-determined severance package from the board.

The Fellheimers said yesterday's decision was made jointly.

"What kind of pigs would we be in letting all these good people go and the Fellheimer family keeping two salaries?" Mr. Fellheimer asked. "It will be the first time since we've been together that we won't be working together, and I'm not happy about it."

Mrs. Fellheimer, who was one of the company's highest paid executives with a $319,952 annual salary, said: "It just felt wrong for both of us to stay at the company when it meant that someone else who is loyal wouldn't be able to stay. It just didn't have that evenhanded feel to it."

The 47-year-old Mrs. Fellheimer, a tough, self-confident and hard-driving executive, has served in numerous high-level positions at Equimark. Prior to her promotion to vice chairman of the bank's main unit, Mrs. Fellheimer, a lawyer by training, was head of two Equimark units: Equimanagement, which turned around several small ailing banks, and Treasure Valley Bank in Idaho.

The Fellheimers joined Equimark as turnaround specialists in 1985 when federal regulators were threatening to shut down the bank unit. Mrs. Fellheimer's high-level profile in the company stirred controversy among some investors and analysts who believed a husband-wife management team created a conflict of interest. The Fellheimers always defended the arrangement, saying that as principal shareholders in the company no one had a greater stake in its management than they. Together, the Fellheimers hold nearly 13% of the company's voting stock.

But Mr. Fellheimer acknowledged yesterday that criticism of the husband-wife team had intensified since the recent layoffs began. The retrenchment followed Equimark's aborted $116 million acquisition of National Bancshares Corp. of Texas. Equimark, which has about $3.8 billion in assets, had built a staff assuming the National Bancshares deal would be completed. When it fell through, the bank had to slim down.

Mrs. Fellheimer said she'll try to find a job in litigation or real estate. Her husband dismissed the notion that she left the company because of any problems in their personal lives. "Judy and I are very much in love," he said.

In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, Equimark shares closed at $8.125, unchanged.
