Technology: Tektronix Plans To Lay Off 1,300, Close Some Lines

  • Author: Susan C. Faludi
  • Date: 05/30/90

BEAVERTON, Ore. -- Tektronix Inc., the embattled electronics testing and measurement concern, said it will lay off at least 1,300 employees by August, sell or shut down several of its business lines, and take a $70 million charge in its fiscal fourth quarter as a result of this major restructuring. Tektronix also said that negotiations have fallen through in its efforts to sell its Grass Valley Group subsidiary to Sony Corp. The company couldn't get Sony to agree to an acceptable price.

"This is one of those companies that really got swallowed and chewed up by the '80s," says Stephen Balog of Shearson Lehman Hutton, who follows Tektronix closely.

The company failed years earlier to jump into areas like computer-aided software and workstations when it had the chance, Mr. Balog says, adding, "The whole Apollo-Sun Microsystems thing could have been them."

Instead, the company's earnings have been declining since 1985, and in its last year the company sustained its first annual loss since the company was founded in 1946. In its latest fiscal third quarter, Tektronix reported a $25 million net loss. Its stock fell to a 14-year low this winter, where it currently remains. And in April, its president David Friedley resigned under pressure, when the board decided he wasn't reorganizing the company fast enough.

Tektronix won't reveal which business lines it specifically intends to sell for several weeks, but a company spokesman said all will be in "non-core" fields in the company's test and measurement and non-television related communication groups. Depending on which divisions the company sells, the number of layoffs may be higher, the spokesman said.

The layoffs, which represent 10 percent of Tektronix's work force, comes on the heels of labor cuts of 1,400 other jobs in the last fiscal year. The company shut down its plant in Guernsey, one of the U.K.'s Channel Islands in January, dismissing 220 employees. Then its facilities in Clark County, Wash., were closed; about 50 employees lost their jobs and the rest were transferred.

The layoffs will be especially painful for the Portland, Ore., area, where 1,000 of the latest dismissals are expected to take place. Tektronix is the largest manufacturing employer in the state and the second largest private employer.

Laid-off employees, who will be notified by the end of June, will range from managers to production line workers to support staff, a company spokesman said. They will receive one week's pay for every year of service.

The last few years has brought the greatest travails for Tektronix as its new products generated killer costs, production snafus and a cool reception from customers. The company belatedly entered the workstation business, for example, only to be 18 months late in bringing its product to market.

The effort to finance new products has taxed the company's resources. "To cap it all off, the enormous levels of funding have probably hurt their ability to manage their bread-and-butter business," says Richard Chu, an analyst who follows the company for Cowen & Co. And the nearly $200 million a year the company is spending on research and development has so far yielded little in the way of lucrative business opportunities.

Ironically, in the 1970s, Tektronix designed what was one of the first engineering workstations ever, but the company never got it off the production line. "The product was reasonably successful and they went on to do a more powerful version, along the lines of Apollo," recalls Thomas Bruggere, chief executive officer of Mentor Graphics, who was a software engineer manager at Tektronix at the time, in the division designing the workstation. "They had, at least, enough vision to realize this was a product worth doing. But they didn't have enough execution to get it out the door."

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Tektronix rose to $15.375, up $1.
