Marketing & Media: Cadbury Schweppes to Pay $211 Million For Most of Perrier's Soft-Drink Business

  • Author: E.S. Browning and Tony Horwitz
  • Date: 05/30/90

Cadbury Schweppes PLC of Britain agreed to buy most of the soft-drink business of Source Perrier S.A. for #125 million ($211 million). Paris-based Perrier had put the business on the block long before contamination forced a world-wide recall of its trademark bubbly water in February. Cadbury, a major candy and beverage concern, acquired almost all of Perrier's non-cola soft drinks for what analysts say was a slightly higher-than-expected price. Although Perrier Chairman Gustave Leven said last year that he sought at least 1.7 billion francs ($300 million), Paris analysts said they hadn't expected the business to fetch more than one billion francs.

"I think that they {Perrier} got a pretty good price," said Alain Kerfourn of the brokerage house Sellier S.A., a Paris unit of Britain's National Westminster Bank. He said the sale would help Perrier cover the cost of its bubbly water recall and relaunch, but he added that the benzene contamination probably didn't affect either the decision to sell or the price received.

The deal, which is conditional on clearance by French authorities, is in line with the strategy of both companies. Perrier has been selling many of its smaller subsidiaries in recent years, concentrating in areas of strength such as water and Roquefort cheese. Cadbury, meanwhile, has been expanding aggressively, with the recent purchase of Procter & Gamble Co.'s Crush International and of soft drinks in Portugal and Spain.

Chris Milburn, a Cadbury spokesman, said the deal "gives us a critical mass in the French soft-drinks market," increasing the company's market share there to 16% from just over 6%. He added that soft-drink consumption in France is low by U.S. and European standards, "so there is room for us to develop it."

The acquisition, being made through the purchase of shares in a Perrier subsidiary, Cie. d'Exploitation de Boissons Rafraichissantes, includes noncarbonated drink brands called Oasis, Bali and Atoll, as well as the French bottling rights to Gini, a lemon-flavored brand Cadbury acquired as part of the P&G deal. These brands accounted for sales of #91.3 million in 1989, contributing #18 million to Perrier's soft-drink division. The deal doesn't include Perrier's license to bottle Pepsi, which is the subject of a legal tangle between Perrier and PepsiCo Inc.

Cadbury shares were up 13 pence to 347 pence ($5.87) in late trading in London yesterday.
