Canada Extends Line Of Credit to Assist Exports to U.S.S.R.

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/30/90

OTTAWA -- The Canadian government decided to extend a 500 million Canadian dollar (US$423.7 million) credit line to the Soviet Bank for Foreign Economic Affairs (Vnesheconombank) to assist the export of Canadian goods and services to the Soviet Union. The decision was announced yesterday, the first day of a two-day Canadian visit by Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev. The Soviet leader is to fly to Washington later today for a five-day U.S. visit.

The two-year credit line will be "a major asset to develop trade with the Soviet Union," said Canadian Trade Minister John Crosbie. It will be provided by a Canadian government export financing agency, the Export Development Corp.

The corporation will provide financing for as much as 85% of the value of Canadian goods and services sold to Soviet customers. Repayment will be made over periods ranging from three to 8 1/2 years at fixed or floating interest rates.

A Canadian trade official said the credit line will replace a less-flexible supplier credit protocol with the Soviet Union.

Deputy Prime Minister Don Mazankowski also disclosed that Canada and the Soviet Union have held preliminary talks on the renewal of a grain sales agreement. The existing five-year agreement, which expires July 31, 1991, calls for the Soviet Union to buy at least 25 million metric tons of Canadian wheat and feed grains during the period of the agreement.
