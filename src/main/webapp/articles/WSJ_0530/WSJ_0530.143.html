Federal Express Gets Clearance to Merge Its Pilot Seniority List With That of Tiger

  • Author: Peter Waldman
  • Date: 05/30/90

Federal Express Corp., overcoming a hurdle that has caused inefficiencies for the struggling company, said a government arbitrator cleared the way for the merger of its pilot seniority list with the seniority list of pilots acquired with Tiger International Corp. But the good news for the company isn't being greeted that way by some of the affected pilots.

The Memphis, Tenn., air express giant has been operating with two separate pilot lists since buying Tiger last year, pending arbitration. The company's inability to merge its roughly 2,000-person pilot force -- comprised of roughly half former Tiger aviators -- has hampered Federal Express's efforts to meld the Tiger fleet into its own air operations, costing the company as much as $150 million a year in possible savings, analysts have estimated.

"The decision will now permit the company to integrate the two pilot groups and achieve greater efficiencies in its air operations," said Frederick W. Smith, Federal Express's chairman and chief executive officer.

But arbitrator George Nicoloau's ruling hasn't ended the bitter strife within the company over the pilot seniority-list question. Many longtime Federal Express pilots fell hundreds of places on the new, merged list, fanning already bitter feelings over a promise they say Federal made to them several years ago to "end-tail" -- or put at the bottom of the list -- aviators acquired in future mergers or acquisitions. The issue has become a staple gripe at informal meetings being held on the possible organization of an independent pilots' union at the company.

"I feel like we got cheated again," said Richard Sowers, a Boeing 727 first officer who fell from 642 on the old list to 1,143 on the merged list. "The Fed Ex pilots are the nicest guys in the world to fly with, but they're so naive it's embarrassing."

Mr. Sowers has filed suit against Federal, claiming the company violated its employment contract with pilots by not end-tailing the Tiger aviators and by agreeing to let the arbitrator merge the new pilots into Federal's list. A U.S. district court judge in Reno, Nev., is expected to rule next month on a Federal Express motion to dismiss the case.

But yesterday, as the arbitrator's seniority list arrived at pilots' homes by mail, Mr. Sowers's lawyer, Leo Donahue of Sacramento, Calif., said he received calls from some 15 disappointed Federal pilots inquiring how they can join the suit. Yesterday's calls were on top of dozens of prior inquiries from pilots in recent weeks, he said.

"If we get through the motion {for dismissal}, we estimate we'll have the majority of the crew force in the suit," said Mr. Donahue.

A Federal spokeswoman declined to comment on the litigation, but Mr. Smith has said the contract with the company's pilots wasn't meant to limit Federal's flexibility in a merger.

Gregory Chappars, a 727 captain who went from 284 on the old list to 515 on the merged list, praised the fairness of the arbitrator's list, but said some Federal pilots are angry with the company's recent attempts to change work rules. In the wake of five consecutive quarters of falling year-to-year profits, the company has moved to revoke some of the very vacation perks that management praised so heartily during a union-affiliation drive last fall, Mr. Chappars said. Pro-union pilots were trounced in that election.

"As soon as the union vote was over," said Mr. Chappars, who said he voted against the union, "they started seeking to modify those various policies to make working conditions more onerous." Federal Express officials couldn't be reached for comment late yesterday evening.
