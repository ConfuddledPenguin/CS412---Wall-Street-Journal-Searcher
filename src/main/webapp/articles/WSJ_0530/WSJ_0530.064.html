Marketing & Media: Regional Groups Move to Regulate Recycling Labels

  • Author: Randolph B. Smith
  • Date: 05/30/90

Two regional government groups took another collaborative step toward regulating marketers' claims that consumer products and packaging are recyclable, recycled, reusable or waste-reducing. The Coalition of Northeastern Governors and the Northeast Recycling Council, which represent 10 states and various industry interests, proposed model definitions and rules on the use of recycling labels. The final regulations, likely to be adopted in some form throughout the Northeast, are expected to influence similar efforts in other states.

The state officials and industry representatives reached broad agreement on the need for uniform labeling standards to promote recycling and waste reduction while protecting consumers from false or misleading environmental claims. However, proposed definitions of what is "recyclable" and what is "recycled" have prompted disputes between industry officials who say they are too strict and environmentalists who say they aren't tough enough.

"There is no consensus right now," says Shelley Dresser, executive director of the recycling council. The council and the coalition are sponsoring a "labeling summit" meeting next month to resolve disputes and refine regulatory proposals that later this year will be submitted to the states for adoption.

Under current proposals, a product or packaging could wear a "recyclable" label if it is recycled in a given state at a 20% rate this year, 35% by 1995 and 50% by 2000. To be called "recycled," most products would have to contain 50% secondary material, including 15% to 40% consumer waste. Plastic packaging would have to contain 35% secondary material, including 15% consumer waste. Secondary material also includes manufacturing waste.

Procter & Gamble Co. says the proposals could actually discourage recycling because many products that are being recycled won't qualify for labels. Tom Rattray, a P&G packaging executive, says the 35% recycled content requirement for plastic packaging is too high because there isn't enough plastic waste being collected.

However, Environmental Action Foundation, a Washington, D.C., advocacy group, is pushing for a 50% recycling rate and higher consumer-waste content to force industry to invest in recycling. "We want the label to be hard to get so that manufacturers have an incentive," says Jeanne Wirka, foundation policy analyst.

There is also disagreement among the states. New York, for example, is proposing a 25% recycling standard, while Rhode Island would grant the label to any material covered by its mandatory recycling law. The groups are seeking to avoid state-by-state inconsistencies that would impede recycling.
