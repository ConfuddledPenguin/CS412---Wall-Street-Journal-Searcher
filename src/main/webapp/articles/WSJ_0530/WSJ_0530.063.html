Rule of Law: Lawrence Walsh, Drug Cases and The Costs of Selective Prosecution

  • Author: L. Gordon Crovitz
  • Date: 05/30/90

"Nothing is too trivial for these people to investigate," said the official giving the Justice Department's view of independent counsels. "The proof of that lies in what we already know about their expenditures." When Assistant Attorney General John Bolton made these comments in 1987, Lawrence Walsh's office was less than a year old and had spent $1.3 million. Mr. Walsh's office now reports that it's spent more than $20 million. Despite expectations that he'd take down his shingle after the John Poindexter trial, Mr. Walsh this month called a new grand jury. He may prosecute more officials for what he calls a "pattern of deceit."

Mr. Walsh's office begins to look like a permanent agency, but a government office with a difference. Freedom from accountability for independent counsels seems to extend to making it impossible to find out how much they've really spent. I know. I've tried.

Mr. Walsh's reported expenses of $20.8 million do not include costs that his prosecutions forced onto other government offices. On request, Attorney General Dick Thornburgh's staff disclosed nearly $8 million more, shown in the table "Iran-Contra Costs," for a total of $28.7 million. The FBI has spent more than $5.4 million helping Mr. Walsh, with security adding nearly another $1 million. Although the Justice figures include $29,000 to track the Walsh budget, officials admit these figures are seriously incomplete.

The White House, Defense, State, CIA, National Security Council and National Security Agency all have huge unreported expenses. So do the courts. In 1987 alone, the White House had to hire 60 lawyers and archivists to review classified material. The Washington newspaper Legal Times more than a year ago estimated that the total cost of the Iran-Contra cases then was $40 million, possibly $100 million. This sounds about right.

Let's put even the low-ball $28.7 million into perspective. This dwarfs the $3.8 million allegedly diverted from the Iranian arms sales to the Contras. Or the $13,800 "illegal gratuity" in the form of the home security fence for Oliver North.

And for what? We haven't learned much from the Iran-Contra prosecutions that wasn't disclosed by the presidential Tower Commission and the congressional hearings. Mr. Walsh never tried to prove that the diversion itself was illegal or that the Boland amendments applied to the National Security Council. Judges so far have decided the crimes warranted nothing more than community service.

Two Contra fund raisers copped tax pleas based on what a Justice official called a "wacko legal theory." Robert McFarlane, Mr. North and Mr. Poindexter, respectively, withheld information from Congress, aided and abetted in obstructing Congress and obstructed Congress. Middlemen Albert Hakim and Richard Secord pleaded to covering up Mr. North's security fence. Mr. Walsh had to drop the case against former CIA agent Joseph Fernandez, but still pursues former CIA official Thomas Clines on tax charges.

No one has looked into any "pattern of deceit" by Congress, but how do offenses by executive-branch officials fit into law enforcement more generally? Contrast Iran-Contra's $28.7 million with the $50 million budget for the office of New York District Attorney Robert Morgenthau. More than 400 prosecutors last year filed criminal cases in Manhattan against more than 120,000 defendants, including more than 11,000 alleged felons -- $400 per defandant.

Maybe it's more fair to compare the Walsh prosecution with other federal prosecutors. In pursuing nine former executive-branch officials and their private contractors, Mr. Walsh has spent more than any of the 93 U.S. Attorneys' Offices spent in 1989 (except for Washington, D.C., where the federal prosecutor has many local functions). The table "Cost Per Criminal Defendant" shows the performance of selected U.S. Attorneys. The U.S. Attorney's Office in Manhattan prosecuted more than 1,400 cases -- including complex Wall Street cases -- with a budget smaller than Mr. Walsh's.

According to a statistical report by the Justice Department, the top two crimes targeted by U.S. Attorneys were drugs and crimes committed with weapons. No one would say these prosecutions had anything to do with criminalizing the battle over separation of powers.

Such extraordinary prosecutorial resources devoted to a few defendants raise clear civil liberties issues. "How frightening it must be," Justice Antonin Scalia wrote in his dissent to the case upholding these prosecutors as constitutional, "to have your own independent counsel and staff appointed with nothing else to do but to investigate you." The average lawyer in a U.S. Attorney's Office handles 117 cases a year, picking them with a discretion that independent counsels need not exercise.

Congress created prosecutors who would show no restraint with executive-branch targets, but this game has gone too far. With drugs and violent crime tearing at the social fabric, this is no time to fund political prosecutions.
