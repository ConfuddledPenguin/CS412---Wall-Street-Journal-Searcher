Heard on the Street: Xerox Faces Fresh Round of Write-Offs

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 05/30/90

NEW YORK -- If you thought the bad news at Xerox was over, think again. Analysts and insurance-industry executives say Xerox faces new write-offs at its Crum & Forster insurance unit that ultimately could rival the $400 million charge that Xerox took in the first quarter to reflect deepening problems at VMS Realty Partners, a partnership promoter in which Xerox owns a 25% stake.

Leading the list of potential troubles this time is Crum & Forster's bread-and-butter property-casualty insurance business, whose results have been hurt by cyclical price-cutting. Xerox has said its operating profit this year will be less than last year's because of weakness at Crum & Forster. But at least one analyst believes the troubles are deeper than the company's mild statement would indicate.

"We suspect Xerox will need to strengthen reserves before the year is out, probably to the tune of $150 million to $200 million," says B. Alex Henderson, a Prudential-Bache Securities analyst. Reserves, which are funds set aside to make future claims payments, are deducted from earnings, and a boost of that size would cost Xerox about $1 to $1.50 a share after taxes.

Mr. Henderson isn't the only observer worried about Crum & Forster's reserves. Moody's Investors Service cited concerns about Crum & Forster's reserves and its "below-average profitability prospects" in lowering the insurance-financial-strength rating on Crum & Forster to single-A-2 from double-A-3 two weeks ago.

In response to these concerns, a Xerox spokesman says, "We believe our reserves are adequate at this time."

However, that's what insurance companies always say, right up until they boost reserves, as Crum & Forster did in last year's third quarter to the tune of $140 million.

Another problem that could cost Crum & Forster an additional $100 million or more in write-offs is transactions with other insurance companies known as reinsurers. Reinsurance is insurance coverage that insurance companies buy to reduce their risk of having to pay big claims. But if the reinsurer runs into financial trouble and can't pay its bills, the reinsurance becomes "unrecoverable," a euphemism for worthless. The company that bought the reinsurance then must pay the claims that it intended to pass along to the reinsurer.

Crum & Forster buys a lot of reinsurance, particularly for high-risk types of insurance such as legal and medical malpractice. "We suspect Xerox has some exposure to some European reinsurers that appear to be struggling," says Prudential-Bache's Mr. Henderson. "It is possible some reinsurance Xerox bought could become valueless."

Some of it already may be valueless. Crum & Forster was a customer of several reinsurance companies owned by London United Investments, a troubled British holding company whose management was put under the control of two court-appointed administrators last week, in a step similar to bankruptcy reorganization proceedings in the U.S.

A Xerox spokesman says Crum & Forster was owed $147.1 million in reinsurance recoverable from various London United subsidiaries and affiliates as of last Dec. 31. However, Mr. Henderson and several insurance-industry executives believe the amount that Xerox might ultimately have to write off as a result of London United's troubles could increase as claims continue to roll in from Crum & Forster policies that were reinsured with London United units.

Xerox says it is waiting for an independent study of London United to be completed before it takes any action. "It's just too soon for us to speculate on what financial impact this may have on Crum & Forster," Stuart Ross, chairman of Xerox Financial Services, says through a spokesman.

Whatever the final bill for London United's woes, it seems unlikely that Crum & Forster will be able to recover all of the $147.1 million it already is owed. Therefore, it seems likely that some of it will be written off.

In the past, Xerox has been able to prevent reserve increases or reinsurance write-offs from depressing net income by taking capital gains from its investment portfolio. Last year, for example, Crum & Forster took $358.7 million of capital gains, mostly from trading some of its $5.4 billion bond portfolio.

But this year, it will be much tougher for Xerox to take such gains because the value of its bond portfolio has been depressed by rising interest rates. At the end of the first quarter, Xerox's portfolio had a paper loss of $282 million, compared with a paper gain of $77 million at the end of last year.

The one bright spot at Xerox is the company's traditional strength, its business-equipment sector. But even there lurks an obvious but unquantifiable problem: Brazil.

The new Brazilian government's Draconian austerity plan, which is intended to bring long-term economic stability to the country, may in the short term depress Xerox's earnings.

Mr. Henderson of Prudential-Bache estimates that Brazilian operations contributed 35 cents a share last year to Xerox's earnings of $6.25 a share.

Though Brazil's austerity measures began in March, they weren't reflected at all in Xerox's first-quarter earnings because Xerox closes the quarterly books on its foreign operations a month early. The first hint of the Brazilian problems will come in Xerox's second-quarter earnings, which will include results of foreign operations for March, April and May.

Xerox declines to estimate the earnings impact of the Brazilian measures, which include a price freeze. A Xerox spokesman offers this assessment: "If prices remain frozen, all we can say is we'll have a difficult year. Brazil is likely to be a significant source of quarter-to-quarter uncertainty through the balance of this year."

Perhaps reflecting that uncertainty, analysts' estimates of Xerox's operating earnings, excluding any special write-offs, vary widely. Daniel Mandresh of Merrill Lynch is at the low end, forecasting earnings of $4 to $4.50 a share this year and $4.50 to $5 a share next year. At the high end is Eugene Glazer of Dean Witter Reynolds, who is expecting $5.80 this year and $6.70 next year. Mr. Henderson of Prudential-Bache is forecasting $5 a share this year and $5.65 next year.
