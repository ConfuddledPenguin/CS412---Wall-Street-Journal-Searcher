International: Mitsubishi, Daimler-Benz Hold Talks, Agree to Spur Mercedes Sales in Japan

  • Author: Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 09/20/90

TOKYO -- Japan's Mitsubishi Group and West Germany's Daimler-Benz AG said they are moving toward cooperation on about a dozen products, from cross-country vehicles to semiconductors, but disclosed basic agreement on only one new venture-boosting sales of Mercedes-Benz cars in Japan. "The effort expended in terms of getting to know each other has been greater than I had expected," Daimler-Benz Chairman Edzard Reuter said in a statement after the first set of top-level meetings since the two giant industrial groups began exploring a global alliance in March.

Shinroku Morohashi, president of the Mitsubishi Corp. trading house at the center of the Japanese group, said at a news conference after the three-hour meeting that the talks "will take considerable time to reach fruition." But he added that "given another six months we will be able to report further progress."

Some of the projects have been reported before, but yesterday's news conference provided the most detailed progress report since the first talks, in Singapore, six months ago. The most progress has apparently come in the automotive business, where each group concentrates on opposite ends of the spectrum. Gerhard Liener, Daimler-Benz chief financial officer, said Mercedes-Benz AG and Mitsubishi Motors Corp. were near completion of an agreement to design, develop and jointly produce cross-country vehicles.

The companies also said they expect to form shortly a joint venture along with Yanase & Co., a Japanese distributor of imported cars, to expand Mercedes-Benz's sales network in Japan, including arranging for some Mitsubishi dealers to sell the German cars. Mercedes says it is the leader in Japan's tiny import market, and with the new agreement hopes to boost sales to 60,000 a year by 1995 from the current 37,000.

Regarding previously disclosed discussions on sharing technology for an auto factory in what is now East Germany, Mr. Liener said Daimler-Benz must first complete its own plans for the site before negotiating Mitsubishi's involvement. He said economic conditions have delayed cooperation on an auto factory in the Soviet Union.

In the potentially significant area of aerospace, Koichi Danno, director of Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Ltd., said they were talking about working together on aerospace systems, ultrasonic measuring devices and medium-sized commercial aircraft. Mr. Liener said the companies were discussing three-way cooperation with United Technologies Corp., which already works with Mitsubishi Heavy Industries.

Executives said Mitsubishi and Daimler-Benz are exploring making versions of each other's chips, and that talks were focusing on semiconductors for the consumer electronics products of Daimler unit AEG AG.

On Oct. 1, Mitsubishi Corp. will set up an office in Stuttgart near Daimler-Benz headquarters.
