Letters to the Editor -- One Man's Family: Conquest in the Desert

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/20/90

Karen Elliott House's excellent article, "How Jordan's King Was Played for a Fool" (editorial page, Aug. 14), states that the British installed his grandfather Abdullah in a kingdom they had created for him "after they removed him from the Hejaz, now a part of Saudi Arabia." The British did not remove the Hashemite family from their hereditary domain in the holy cities of Arabia. They were removed by the desert tribal sheikdom of Ibn Saud, who conquered most of the Arabian Peninsula during the first two decades of this century and exiled the Hashemites when the conquest was completed in 1921. This gives the Hashemite family at least as much legitimate claim to Saudi Arabia as anybody has to any area of land in the Middle East. Some lingering coolness between the two royal families may be explained by their old enmity and the precarious legitimacy of both over the lands they rule.

After their conquest of Arabia, the Saud family named their new domain after themselves -- "Saudi Arabia." This would be about the equivalent of the King ranch family of Texas conquering the entire state and calling it "King Texas."

The other consolation prize given by the British to the exiled Hashemite family was Iraq, where they installed Abdullah's brother Feisal as king. He was murdered by an earlier version of Saddam Hussein.

M. Donald Coleman, M.D.

Mamaroneck, N.Y.
