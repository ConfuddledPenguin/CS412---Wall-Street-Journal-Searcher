Speaking of Business: Has the Long-Awaited Recession Arrived?

  • Author: Lindley H. Clark
  • Date: 09/20/90

In the wake of the October 1987 market crash, many economists were convinced the next recession was no more than a year away. Somehow, though, the economy has continued to expand. Saddam Hussein, however, may be producing the long-expected recession. If the 52 economists interviewed monthly by Blue Chip Economic Indicators, a Sedona, Ariz., newsletter, half now say an official recession will be under way by the end of this year; a similar percentage probably applies to economists generally. The economy was slowing even before Iraq invaded Kuwait, and rising energy costs and bigger budget deficits may push the economy over the brink.

It's likely but by no means certain.

Even those who vote for a 1990 recession have some doubts. Lawrence Meyer, an econometrician based at Washington University of St.Louis, expects a recession to begin in the fourth quarter. But he worries about the political uncertainties. "Our models," he says, "can't fully handle the problems. There's more need for judgmental analysis." In other words, there's more need for a few careful guesses.

Close to half of all analysts see no recession in 1990, and they offer some interesting reasons for their relative optimism.

Those who think the economy will muddle through obviously assume the oil shock will be handled, with increased supplies from Saudi Arabia and elsewhere offsetting most of the lost output from Iraq and Kuwait. They also don't expect full-scale war, shutting off a great deal of oil. Oil prices will settle at about $25 a barrel, about $5 above the pre-invasion level, and the market will stabilize.

There is general agreement that the oil price shock will keep upward pressure under general prices, at least through the end of this year. Some analysts see this as a sign of unsuspected economic strength. Robert Brusca, chief economist of Nikko Securities Co. International, for instance, notes that the consumer price index is rising at a year-over-year rate of 4.8%. "If the economy is so weak," he asks, "how can such price pressures persist?"

Michael Keran, chief economist of Prudential Insurance Co., thinks that high oil prices, and a fear of more of the same, can have a positive effect on the economy in the short run. "Increased energy costs are a factor of production which raises the cost of producing a whole host of other goods," he says. "Businessmen, aware that this creates a potential for future price increases, will add to their inventory holdings."

Some analysts were encouraged by the revision of the inflation-adjusted gross national product for the second quarter, although the gain remained at an overall 1.2% rate. Consumer spending and net exports were revised upward substantially, says Morris Cohen, a Hackensack, N.J., consultant. "Thus the economy turned out to be in better shape than the original estimates had shown." Mr. Cohen does expect the fourth quarter to be negative, but he looks for an upturn at the end of the year. (For the consensus views, see the accompanying table.)

The optimistic analysts expect the Federal Reserve eventually to provide more help in fending off a 1990 recession. Lacy Hunt, chief economist of CM&M Group, says, "Currently, the Federal Reserve is likely to pursue a relatively accommodative policy in an attempt to keep the economy growing, even at the expense of higher inflation."

Mr. Hunt notes three other props that could keep the economy out of a recession this year. Before the oil shocks of 1974 and 1979, business inventories had been very high, and the subsequent liquidations were heavily responsible for the recessions that followed. At the moment, business inventories are relatively low.

Second, the composition of the economy has changed. The now-dominant service sector is less affected by higher energy costs than manufacturing was in the 1970s. Finally, the weak dollar is helping to push up exports and limit imports.

Even the relative optimists don't foresee a business boom. "While the economy should avoid recession," Mr. Hunt says, "the growth rate should be a very sluggish 0.5% through late 1991."

Mickey Levy, chief economist of CRT Government Securities in New York, says the difference between a positive growth rate of 0.5% and a negative rate of 0.5% is so small the average businessman would be unlikely to feel it.

Mr. Levy likes to stress the difference between economic risks and economic uncertainty. "Risks," he says, "we can deal with by the way we plan and make investments. Uncertainties are just uncertainties -- all we can do is speculate." Seldom have forecasters felt so trapped in speculation.
