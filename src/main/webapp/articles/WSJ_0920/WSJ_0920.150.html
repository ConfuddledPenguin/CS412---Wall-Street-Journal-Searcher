Technology: New Compound Developed For Superconductivity

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/20/90

BOSTON -- Hitachi Ltd. said its researchers developed a compound that shows signs of superconductivity at a somewhat higher temperature than previously reported for the phenomenon. The new compound, unlike most "high-temperature" superconductors developed during the past few years, doesn't contain copper -- an aspect of the material that intrigues superconductivity experts.

They hope the new compound will provide clues about how to make superconductors that work at yet higher and more cheaply attained temperatures.

Superconductors, which conduct electricity without resistance when cooled, promise new electrical technologies, such as faster computers and cheaper power generation.

The Japanese concern said its new material contains the elements thallium, strontium, vanadium and oxygen; the company also reported fabricating a closely related superconductor in which part of the strontium is replaced with the element cerium.

A sample of Hitachi's compound showed a sharp drop in electrical resistance -- indicating superconductivity -- between 130 Kelvins and 160 Kelvins, or minus 225 degrees Fahrenheit to minus 171 degrees Fahrenheit, Hitachi said. Currently, the highest known temperature for superconductivity is about 125 Kelvins, or minus 234 degrees Fahrenheit.

The finding hasn't yet been confirmed, and some scientists said they were skeptical about it. During the past few years, several research teams have reported similar superconductivity temperature advances that apparently were spurious.
