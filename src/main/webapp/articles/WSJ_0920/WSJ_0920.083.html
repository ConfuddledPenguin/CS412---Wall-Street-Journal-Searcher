Court Allows Officials To Pursue Nevada Site For Nuclear Dumping

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/20/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- The 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals ruled Wednesday that federal energy officials may pursue Nevada as a potential nuclear waste dump site, despite the state Legislature's adamant opposition. The uananimous court decision allows government officials to proceed with a massive environmental study of Nevada's Yucca Mountain, about one hundreds miles northeast of Las Vegas, as a burial site for radioactive wastes. The state has mounted several legal challenges to the federal plan since 1987, and the state Legislature has passed hostile resolutions and refused to supply the government with necessary environmental permits.

The three-judge panel rejected state arguments that the federal government was violating constitutional protections of state rights, as well as provisions of the U.S. Nuclear Waste Policy Act. The state attorney general had argued in August before the court that Congress failed to override Nevada's disapproval notice within the 90 days required under the 1982 act.

In his decision, federal Judge Arthur Alarcon asserted that the U.S. energy secretary was correct in arguing that the U.S. Constitutions's 10th amendment "does not protect a state from being outvoted in Congress."

Originally the federal government was considering five sites in Mississippi, Utah, Texas, Washington and Nevada. Since 1987, the government has narrowed the list to three -- in Hannaford, Wash., Deafsmith, Texas, and Yucca Mountain -- and more recently dropped all but the Nevada location.

Nevada can appeal the decision to the U.S. Supreme Court or ask the appellate court for reconsideration. State Deputy Attorney General Harry Swainston wouldn't say whether the state will appeal until he reads the decision. But in August, state Attorney General Brian McKay vowed "to use every tool possible" to keep the dump site out of the state.

The federal energy officials have said that its "site verification study" would take about six to ten years to complete. So far, government researchers have bored several exploratory holes in the mountainside, but their work largely halted in the face of state resistance.
