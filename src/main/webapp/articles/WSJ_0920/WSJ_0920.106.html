Politics & Policy: Public Opinion Divided on Republican, Democratic Budget Plans, Poll Shows

  • Author: David Shribman and Jackie Calmes
  • Date: 09/20/90

WASHINGTON -- Neither the Republicans nor the Democrats have a political advantage in the current budget standoff, according to a new Wall Street Journal/NBC News poll. The poll, which underscores public concern about an economic recession, shows that the public is equally divided over whom to blame if no agreement is reached to reduce the federal budget deficit. If the talks fail, 34% say the Bush administration would be mostly to blame, while 31% say that the Democrats in Congress would be mostly to blame.

Top congressional and administration officials continued budget talks at the Capitol yesterday, seeking to break the impasse over taxes and other issues.

The WSJ/NBC News poll, taken by Peter Hart, a Democrat, and Robert Teeter, a Republican, was based on telephone interviews Sept. 15-18 with 1,508 registered voters across the nation. The poll carries a margin of error of 2.6 percentage points.

The results show virtually no public preference between the Democratic plan, which, among other elements, includes an energy tax and an additional income surtax on the very wealthy, and the Bush administration plan, which includes a smaller energy tax, limits on deductions for state and local income taxes and a cut in the capital-gains tax.

"There is as much division on the issue among the public as there is between the president and Congress," Mr. Hart and Mr. Teeter say.

The poll shows that 43% of the public prefer the Democratic plan, with 41% preferring the Bush administration plan.

A Journal/NBC News poll in January showed the public split over cutting the capital-gains tax, which assesses levies on the profits from the sale of stocks, bonds and real estate. In that poll, 47% opposed a capital-gains tax cut and 45% supported it.

Meanwhile, the poll shows there remains strong public concern about the prospect of a recession. A majority (51%) believes there will be a recession. The Iraqi invasion of Kuwait triggered a major upsurge in public apprehensions about the prospect of a recession; those apprehensions haven't disappeared in the past six weeks even though the initial shock of the invasion has worn off. Thirty-five percent believe there won't be a recession. Republicans are slightly less sure of the prospect of a recession than are Democrats.

The budget talks that continued behind closed doors yesterday were again limited, as they had been Tuesday, to the five highest-ranking congressional leaders of both parties and three top administration officials, in an effort to cut down on acrimony and information leaks. At Tuesday's session, House Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.) and Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell (D., Maine) asked to meet with President Bush, but administration officials said negotiations had to show more progress before the president got involved again, congressional aides said.

Meanwhile, House Democratic leaders began considering the need for an emergency government-funding bill, including a possible provision to suspend across-the-board spending cuts that would be triggered in October by the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law absent some budget accord.

Without an agreement in place after Oct. 1, the start of fiscal 1991, government programs would be unfunded and at least $85 billion in automatic cuts would be imposed by the law. Senate GOP Leader Robert Dole began circulating a letter to President Bush promising to support his veto of any measure waiving the cuts. But given the pain such cuts would impose on federal workers and beneficiaries of numerous programs, Sen. Dole was having trouble getting signatures.

The four-month-old budget impasse and threatening fiscal breakdown has fed frustration among lawmakers. Some House Democrats now propose to draft their own budget that would meet the budget negotiators' goal of $50 billion in budget cuts for fiscal 1991, while protecting Democrats' favorite social programs. But proponents acknowledged any such budget would be difficult to pass. And House Democratic Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.) said he told the group that the summit negotiators "have got to keep talking and see what we can accomplish."

Despite congressional leaders' recommitment Tuesday to bargain in "good faith," the troops from both parties mobilized to put their own partisan spin on the impasse through news conferences and floor speeches. Democrats complained that only President Bush's insistence on lower capital-gains taxes for the rich blocked a budget agreement. Republicans in turn stressed that many other differences remained, including the amount and kind of energy taxes and Medicare cuts.
