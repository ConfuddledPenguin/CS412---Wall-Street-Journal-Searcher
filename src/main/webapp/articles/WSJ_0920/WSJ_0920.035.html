World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Finish Lower in Light Trading; U.K. Shares Edge Up on Late Bargain Hunting

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/20/90

Tokyo stocks finished lower in continuously light trading Wednesday, with little investor participation because of uncertainty about the direction of domestic interest rates. London shares ended slightly higher, up modestly from intraday lows as a late round of bargain hunting helped boost prices.

Tokyo stocks fluctuated mostly in tandem with futures prices. Last-hour arbitrage selling pressured down both the Nikkei Index and the broader-based Topix index to the lowest closing levels this year, traders said.

The Nikkei Index of 225 issues fell 158.65 points, or 0.66%, to 23726.17, below the previous lowest close of 23737.63 posted Aug. 23.

In Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index retreated another 231.06 points to close the morning session at 23495.11.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at about 360 million shares, down from 401 million shares Tuesday. Decliners outnumbered advancers 565 to 358, while 170 others were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section was down 16.61 points, or 0.91%, to 1799.72.

The second section index dropped 25.25 points, or 0.73%, to 3449.74. The section's volume was estimated at 7 million shares, up from 6.5 million shares Tuesday.

Ahead of the fiscal midyear book closing at the end of September, participation of both brokerage houses and institutions remained sluggish, with activities centering mainly on profit-taking, traders said.

Because of thin overall volume, stock prices were swayed by the pressure of arbitrage trading, observers said.

The Nikkei Index rose 247 points to its intraday high in early trading and moved narrowly above 24000 points during the first hour of the morning session.

The index then fell below Tuesday's close under pressure of arbitrage selling that continued through the early afternoon.

The index once again bounced up above 24000 points in the last hour before dropping quickly on rounds of arbitrage selling, which appeared to be linked to Wednesday's expiration of the Nikkei September futures contract on the Singapore International Monetary Exchange, traders said.

Concern about the impact of the volatile bond market as well as that of rising oil prices on the Japanese economy also prevented investors from hunting for bargains, traders said.

Rumors that the Ministry of Finance spoke to the Big Four brokerage houses Tuesday afternoon to show its concern about the stock market's decline weren't effective in rekindling investors' interest in buying stocks, traders said.

An official at Wako Securities said, "Investors aren't interested in such lip service. The authorities won't do anything after all." He continued: "Investors' distrust on the stock market is really intense. It'll take a long time for the market to regain credibility."

Some pharmaceutical shares ended firmer, with Sankyo up 40 yen to 2100 yen (29 cents to $15.23) and Shionogi gaining 30 yen to 1090 yen.

While most steel makers ended lower, Kobe Steel gained 19 yen to 535 yen, as the strong earnings outlooks for its aluminum products attracted investors, traders said.

As the London market wandered in a narrow range either side of the day-earlier close, two securities houses, Kleinwort Benson and Shearson Lehman, said U.K. shares are now oversold and due for a short-term bounce as market makers find themselves short of stock.

However, neither the late bargain hunting nor the brokerages' assessments are likely to have much of an effect on the immediate direction of the market, traders said.

"There is no strong feeling in the market, people are just trading on a whim and market makers are playing about with numbers," one trader said.

In the U.S., a statement from Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan that higher inflation is probable sent Wall Street lower during the late London session, and damped the U.K. market, traders said.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-Share Index closed up 1.8 points at 2065.8. The day's high of 2075.9 came at the opening of trade; the day's low was 2061.6.

The Financial Times 30-Share Index closed down 0.7 point at 1576.1. Volume was 386.8 million shares, under Tuesday's 379.2 million shares.

Among company interim results disclosed Wednesday, Tesco, a U.K. supermarket chain, said half-year pretax profit was 36% higher at #188 million ($359.7 million), ahead of market forecasts. The shares ended 2 pence lower at 229 pence on the back of profit-taking, traders said.

Willis Faber gained 9 pence to 210 pence after U.S. insurance broker Corroon & Black rejected Aon Corp.'s takeover bid, saying it will proceed with plans to merge with Willis Faber.

British Telecom fell 6 pence to 270 pence after recent downgradings from two London securities houses.

After a downgrading from securities house Hoare Govett Ltd. cutting a forecast for Cable & Wireless' year-end pretax profit to #630 million from #665 million, the shares of the telecommunications company closed down 5 pence at 434 pence.

In the building sector, RMC was unchanged at 479 pence prior to Thursday's disclosure of interim results. Traders said the market was not optimistic following poor results elsewhere in the sector over the past few weeks. Blue Circle, another building-supplies company, ended a penny higher at 190 pence.

Elsewhere, shares closed higher in Madrid, and lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Brussels and Milan, flat to higher in Stockholm, and unchanged in Amsterdam. South African gold stocks fell.

Also, stocks ended lower in Sydney, Singapore, Taipei and Hong Kong, were mixed in Wellington, and higher in Manila and Seoul.
