Letters to the Editor: Unattractive Genius Made Beautiful Science

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/20/90

In his book review of "A Literary Guide to Science" (Leisure & Arts, Aug. 17), Jim Holt writes: "A particularly unattractive physicist named Otto Warburg accepts the status of `honorary Aryan' from the Nazis so that he can remain in his Berlin laboratory even though he is half Jewish." The Otto Warburg to whom Mr. Holt refers was of course not a physicist but a biochemist, indeed the most notable biochemist of his time, perhaps of any time. The body of work for which he received a Nobel Prize includes his famous determination of the action spectrum of the "respiratory enzyme." This was not only a technical tour de force but also on the purely aesthetic level one of the most beautiful experiments of the 20th century.

Warburg is the subject of a brief, highly readable biography "Otto Warburg: Cell Physiologist, Biochemist and Eccentric" (Oxford University Press, 1981) by Sir Hans Krebs. Sir Hans, himself a Nobel Prize-winning biochemist, was a Jewish scientist forced to flee to England from the Nazi butchers. He knew Warburg as well as anyone could claim to know him, having worked for some years in Warburg's institute. His criticism of certain aspects of Warburg's personality and career is unsparing but he is also generous in his admiration of Warburg's genius and his devotion to science.

Eugene P. Kennedy

Professor of Biological Chemistry

Harvard Medical School

Boston
