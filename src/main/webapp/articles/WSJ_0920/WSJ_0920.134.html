Giving a Hand: Ernst & Young Gives Olympic Effort To Put U.S. Athletes on Right Track

  • Author: John A. Conway
  • Date: 09/20/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

POSITION WANTED: Super-healthy college graduate, powerfully motivated, highly competitive, totally inexperienced, seeks executive post. Will require minimum three months' time off for athletic pursuits.

A WANT AD LIKE THAT SOUNDS like an adolescent pipe dream but it fits any American hoping to compete in the Olympic Games. The average age of a U.S. team member is now a mature 25 years and training expenses for a shot at the gold can hit $40,000 a year. Unlike state-supported foreign competitors, most U.S. athletes foot their own bills. Now Ernst & Young, a $2 billion auditing and consulting firm, is putting them in the starting blocks.

In 1977, the U.S. Olympic Committee set up its Olympic Job Opportunity Program and by 1984 had put 276 athletes on career paths. That year, Ernst & Young, which handled scoring for the Los Angeles Games, volunteered to help. By 1988, the firm had placed 162 more athletes. Since September 1989, 164 Olympic strivers have been placed, a year that topped any previous non-Olympic year. By the time the 1992 Summer Olympics start in Barcelona, Spain, Ernst & Young hopes to have 400 Olympians at work.

James Taylor, Ernst & Young's marketing director, runs the placement program. The Olympians, he points out, are incredibly efficient at sports but naive outside the arena. To help place the 176 athletes now in the program, he is using 500 volunteers from the firm's 25,000 employees. "Mentors" in 28 offices go one-on-one with the athletes, review their resumes, and use the firm's good offices to match them with employers.

Ed Brodie, a senior manager in the New York executive search operation, has three Olympians on his string, a 1988 gold-winning sprinter and two top fencers. One, runner Andrew Valman, is working at Eastman Kodak as a sales representative. Mr. Brodie estimates that he puts in 10 to 20 hours a week on his volunteer job, an important consideration in a profession that starts the meter when the phone rings.

In Los Angeles, Elizabeth Hammond spends 25% of her time on her nine charges. Her job, she says, is to "find a label" for a candidate's skills. "The athletes," she says, "must analyze themselves, something they are good at."

The Olympians offer more than glamour and muscles, Mr. Taylor points out. "Athletes learn from their failures," he says, a valuable trait in any employee. The grueling training, he adds, takes dedication, organization and staying power, qualities most employers long for.

Olympic rower Kevin Still, a bronze medalist in the 1988 Seoul Games, is on the fast track at New York insurance broker Johnson & Higgins in New York. "These guys are our future," says J&H director Norman Barham. Kayaker Mike Herbert of Arkansas got to the 1988 Olympics because of his job at J.C. Penney; the company has hired 27 other athletes through the OJOP program. "I couldn't have done it without their help," Mr. Herbert says. Anheuser-Busch has taken on 50 Olympians over the years and vice president Jack McDonald says "everyone's a winner" in the program.

What does Ernst & Young get out of all this? The tangibles are publicity, special invitations to U.S. Olympic Committee events and the right to put a special torch-and-Olympic-ring symbol on the company's stationery. But the real plus is the satisfaction of knowing that the firm is finding post-Olympic careers for the best amateur athletes in the U.S.
