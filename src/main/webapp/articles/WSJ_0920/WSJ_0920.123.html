Marketing & Media: Time Warner to Create Unit Combining Book Publishing, Magazine Operations

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 09/20/90

NEW YORK -- Time Warner Inc. is consolidating its publishing operations into a new unit, Time Warner Publishing, which will include its magazine business and its book publishing operation. The new entity will be run by Reginald K. Brack, who was named chairman of Time Warner Publishing. Mr. Brack will continue as chairman, president and chief executive of Time Inc. Magazines Co., but will now oversee Time Inc. Book Co. as well. Kelso Sutton, president of that unit, will now report to Mr. Brack.

The company has been expected to consolidate some operations to cut costs at the media giant created last year by the merger of Time Inc. and Warner Communications Inc. However, Mr. Brack said that while merging the two units is a streamlining measure, it will not result in any staff reductions and is not aimed at cutting costs. Mr. Brack will continue reporting directly to Time Warner president Nicholas J. Nicholas Jr.

Separately, Time Warner said that Peter R. Haje, a senior corporate partner with the law firm of Paul, Weiss, Rifkind Wharton & Garrison, will become executive vice president and general counsel of Time Warner. Mr. Haje assumes the general counsel's duties from Martin Payson, who will continue as vice chairman of the company. Mr. Haje was the longtime outside counsel to Warner Communications Inc. prior to its merger with Time Inc.

Mr. Brack, who ran Time Inc. Books for a few years in the 1980s, is expected to focus in part on Time Warner's plans to create new products drawn from both magazines and books. In the past year, some of those products have included Sports Illustrated for Kids Books, published by Little, Brown and Warner Books; Warner Books paperbacks written by Time magazine reporters; and an upcoming cookbook series from Warner Books based on Cooking Light magazines.

But some analysts and industry observers were perplexed by the decision to consolidate books and magazines, especially since there are no savings. Jessica J. Reif, a First Boston Corp. analyst, said "the synergy they talk about, they can do now as separate companies, and if there are not savings, why put it together?"

In an interview yesterday, Mr. Brack said the unit's first moves would be to use an established book distribution system in Western Europe to push subscriptions of international editions of Time and Fortune. Mr. Brack said book distribution could be expanded in Asia and Australia, where Time Warner has established magazine distribution. The company will bring together 30 million customers culled from magazine subscribers and book club members, Mr. Brack said.

Mr. Brack now oversees two operations whose businesses have been battered in a slowed-down economy. Almost all of Time Warner's major magazines are down in advertising pages through the first half of 1990. Although Time Warner's Book-of-the-Month Club is the industry leader, the book club business in the U.S. is suffering. Like other publishers, profit margins are under pressure at the Little, Brown and Warner Books divisions.

Time Inc. Magazines in 1989 had an operating profit of $318 million on revenue of $1.9 billion. Time Inc. Books had a loss of $23 million because of the sale of its Scott, Foresman unit. Revenue was $1.1 billion.

At one point in the mid-1980s, Messrs. Sutton and Brack were peers. But in 1986, Mr. Brack, then president of the book company, swapped jobs with Mr. Sutton, who was then running the magazine company.

Mr. Brack's track record at Time Inc. Magazines is mixed. He dismantled the Magazine Development Group, which had produced only expensive duds such as TVCable Week and Picture Week, and put magazine development into the hands of editors at established Time magazines. Sports Illustrated editors spawned Sports Illustrated for Kids, and a Time for Kids is under development by the staff of Time magazine. Fortune, meanwhile, is developing a student version.

Mr. Brack has also successfully moved Time Warner into joint ventures which include joint ownership in new magazines, or investments in publications started by entrepreneurs. Time Warner has partial ownership in Whittle Communications L.P., Hippocrates and In Health magazines, and Emerge magazine. It recently took over full ownership of Parenting magazine, where it initially had a half-interest.

But bigger, more expensive projects -- notably the eight-month-old Entertainment Weekly -- have had shaky starts, and some critics question Time Warner's decision to make such investments in the first place, considering its merger-related debt. To date, analysts estimate Entertainment Weekly has cost Time Warner more than $50 million. The magazine's founding editor resigned early on, and the publication was redesigned and redirected after early issues turned off both readers and advertisers.
