Real Estate: Reichmanns Act To Sell Chunk Of Real Estate

  • Author: Neil Barsky and Mitchell Pacelle
  • Date: 09/20/90

The Reichmann family, which owns Olympia & York Developments Ltd., is seeking to sell almost a 20% partnership interest in its massive U.S. real estate portfolio. John Zuccotti, Olympia & York USA president, declined to estimate the value of the stake, but real-estate professionals said the interest could sell for between $200 million and $800 million. Mr. Zuccotti denied speculation that the company, as with many real estate concerns, has a cash-flow problem. He added that the planned transaction had been in the works for many months.

Mr. Zuccotti said the move is intended to put in the hands of a single institutional investor minority holdings in properties controlled by a variety of Reichmann family members. The corporate entity will be unaffected by the sale, said O&Y officials.

The properties, which include commercial projects in New York City, Boston, San Francisco and other major U.S. cities, are being shopped to a variety of institutional investors, although the ultimate buyer is expected to be foreign.

Lazard Freres & Co. has been retained by the Reichmanns to handle the sale.

Observers of O&Y's activities are keenly interested in the sale because they think it may signal that the Reichmanns are protecting themselves against a more severe downturn in the real-estate market.

One individual who has spoken to family leader Paul Reichmann said that while Mr. Reichmann does not believe in the "gloom and doom scenario, it's enough of a possibility that he's hedging his bets."

In the past, the Reichmanns have made fortunes by correctly anticipating swings in the real estate and credit markets. In perhaps its most prescient move, the company purchased the Uris family's New York City real-estate portfolio in 1977 for $320 million. Those properties were said to have increased tenfold in value during the 1980s.

O&Y also developed the successful World Financial Center in the 1980s. Its tenants include the world headquarters of American Express Co., Merrill Lynch & Co., and Dow Jones & Co., publisher of this newspaper.

Lately, however, the O&Y's fortunes have taken a slight turn for the worse, in part because of its heavy investment in the troubled Campeau Corp.

Olympia & York also has spent more than $1 billion building the world's largest real-estate development in the London docklands. That project, called Canary Wharf, is 50% leased, but its success is far from certain. The company is in the midst of securing long-term bank financing from British and Japanese institutions.

Some of the company's older, Wall Street-area buildings, such as one once occupied by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., sit largely empty.

Nonetheless, during the past two decades, O&Y has diversified into a number of industries that provide hedges against major drops in the real-estate market. It owns a controlling interest in newsprint producer Abitibi-Price Inc. and Consumers' Gas Co. It also owns a 73% interest in Gulf Canada Resources Ltd., which has benefited from the recent rise in oil prices.
