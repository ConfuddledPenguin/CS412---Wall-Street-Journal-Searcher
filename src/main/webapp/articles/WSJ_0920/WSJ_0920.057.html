Viewpoint: Democracy Wins in Court -- Three Cheers]

  • Author: Michael Gartner
  • Date: 09/20/90

Three cheers for the court system. Cheer Number One: To the Appellate Division of the New York State Supreme Court, which last week overruled a lower court's ban on the publication of a book by a former member of the Israeli intelligence service.

Israel had claimed that the safety of its intelligence agents would be endangered by the publication of "By Way of Deception: A Devastating Insider's Portrait of the Mossad." Michael Dontzin, a New York judge, agreed, and he temporarily blocked publication of the book in the United States.

That was just an awful ruling.

The glory of democracy is that anyone can say anything. Sometimes, those things that are said are lies. Sometimes they're evil. Sometimes they hurt people. Sometimes they're obscene. Sometimes they're inane.

But never, never in this land can anyone stop us in advance from saying what we want.

That was established in 1931 when the court ruled that Jay Near, a bigot and extortionist who ran a sleazy newspaper in Minnesota, couldn't be barred from writing what he thought. That doctrine prohibiting so-called prior restraints of the press was reaffirmed decades later in the Pentagon Papers cases.

And last week it was reaffirmed again. I have no idea whether Victor Ostrovsky, the former Israeli spy, is a good guy or a bad guy. But that makes no difference. For, as Justice Felix Frankfurter wrote in 1950, "It is a fair summary of history to say that the safeguards of liberty have frequently been forged in controversies involving not very nice people."

Cheer Number Two: To the Judicial Conference, which is the policy-making body of the federal court system.

Last week, the Judicial Conference voted to allow cameras into federal courtrooms. It's just a three-year experiment. It's just in six district courts and two appellate courts. And it's just for civil trials. But, still, it's good news.

The hallmark of democracy is openness. But for 50 years, the federal court system has barred cameras and broadcasting equipment from its courtrooms. In the past 10 years, more than 40 states have opened their own courts to cameras, and that has been informative and instructive. Sure, TV stations go in and pick out the dramatic confrontation, the telling sound bite. But they also show longer segments, they familiarize Americans with the system, and they show democracy at work.

Our judicial system is, after all, the real people's court. But few people have the time or inclination actually to visit a court just to watch a trial. Now we can do that on TV, we can see how our people's court outshines "The People's Court."

Cheer Number Three: To Glenn E. Pille, a state district judge in Des Moines. He ruled that my friend Gil Cranberg is a journalist and that the county attorney should quit messing around with him.

Gil Cranberg is the retired editor of the editorial pages of the Des Moines Register, and he kept his sense of outrage -- a must for any good editorial writer -- after he retired. One thing he was outraged about was a race track built near Des Moines with money that was backed by the county without any votes of the people, so he continued to write about it for the Register. The county people and the race-track people think Mr. Cranberg is a nag and they're especially defensive about the deal because the race track has been finishing out of the money ever since it opened. Mr. Cranberg points this out with a certain regularity, maybe even a little glee.

On Aug. 14, the county subpoenaed Mr. Cranberg and told him to bring all documents he had regarding absolutely any matter relative to the Prairie Meadows track. The subpoena was tangential to a class-action lawsuit about the track, a lawsuit that Mr. Cranberg is not involved in except as a resident of the county.

The subpoena was clearly just harassment, and it worked: it messed up Mr. Cranberg's life and work, causing him to devote precious time to fighting the subpoena and pre-empting him from gathering any more information on the track because of the fear he would have to disclose sources and hand over documents and notes.

But last Thursday, Judge Pille saw through the whole thing.

He quashed the subpoena and re-iterated the role of a free press. It was quick justice.

The country is a little better off today because of the appellate decision in New York, the vote of the Judicial Conference, and the ruling by Judge Pille.

The press can be jerks, but that's not the point. The point was made 20 years ago by federal Judge Murray Gurfein when the government tried to stop newspapers from printing the Pentagon Papers.

"The security of the Nation is not at the ramparts alone," he wrote. "Security also lies in the value of our free institutions. A cantankerous press, an obstinate press, a ubiquitous press must be suffered by those in authority in order to preserve the even greater values of freedom of expression and the right of the people to know. . . . These are troubled times. There is no greater safety valve for discontent and cynicism about the affairs of Government than freedom of expression in any form."

---

Mr. Gartner is editor and co-owner of the Daily Tribune in Ames, Iowa, and president of NBC News in New York.
