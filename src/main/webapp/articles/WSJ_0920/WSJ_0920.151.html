Nuclear Plants Don't Boost Cancer Deaths, Study Says

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The National Cancer Institute found that people who live near nuclear facilities don't face a greater risk of death from cancer than those who live elsewhere. The NCI compared mortality data in 107 counties surrounding all 62 U.S. nuclear facilities -- 52 commercial power plants, nine Department of Energy research and weapons plants, and one commercial fuel-reprocessing plant -- in operation before 1982 with data from demographically similar counties that weren't close to nuclear facilities. Researchers found "no evidence to suggest that the occurrence of leukemia or any other form of cancer was generally higher in the study counties than the control counties."

Both proponents and opponents of nuclear power agree that the NCI survey is the nation's most comprehensive assessment of the relationship between proximity to nuclear facilities and cancer mortality.

But Sen. Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.), who requested the study, said the study "isn't a clean bill of health for the nuclear industry" because it didn't go far enough. In contrast, the U.S. Council for Energy Awareness, a nuclear trade group, said the study "should put the public at ease that living near a nuclear plant isn't harmful to their health."

No matter how reassuring its findings, the study isn't likely to improve the prospects for the construction of new nuclear plants. Because of concerns about cost, nuclear waste disposal, safety and regulatory delay, there hasn't been an order for a new nuclear plant in the U.S. since 1978.
