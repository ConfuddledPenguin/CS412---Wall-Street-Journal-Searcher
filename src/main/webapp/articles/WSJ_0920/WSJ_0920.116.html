Wednesday's Markets: Stocks Decline And Bonds Rise On Fed Goals

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/20/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks fell and bonds rose on comments by Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan that investors deciphered to mean the Fed won't be turning on the money spigot soon. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 13.86 points on expanding volume to 2557.43. Bond investors, reassured that Mr. Greenspan is a determined inflation fighter, bid prices higher, and the dollar rose against most major foreign currencies for the same reason.

Stock investors have been hoping the Fed would ease interest rates to keep the economy from slipping into a recession. But in testimony before the House, Mr. Greenspan indicated he remains less concerned about the threat of recession than he is about surging inflation.

Though the market still is focused on oil prices, said Jack Conlon, head of equities at Rothschild Inc., Mr. Greenspan called the tune yesterday. "The market didn't get what it expected," he said. "It was looking for more significant easing. Instead, the Fed seems to be locked in neutral. That's disappointing."

Some stocks tumbled. Merck, for example, plunged as much as 4 on an analyst's negative report about a promising new drug; the stock ended down 3 1/8 at 76 7/8. And American General sank 6 3/8 to 33 5/8, affecting other takeover stocks. The company said it was having trouble selling itself to fend off an unwanted suitor.

The result: "Two gaping holes" in the limelight stocks that "certainly doesn't help the cause," Mr. Conlon said.

Stocks didn't react to a drop in oil prices; the October crude-oil futures contract fell 23 cents a barrel to $33.18. As long as oil isn't making new highs, "stocks won't be following oil," said Sal Gilbertie, oil-futures trader at Merrill Lynch. "The stock market is getting used to intraday volatility."

In major market action:

Stock prices fell. Volume was 147.5 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues led advancers, 883 to 599.

Bond prices rose one-half point, or $5 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell to 9.01%.

The dollar rose against most currencies. In late New York trading, the dollar was up to 1.5715 marks from 1.5530 marks Tuesday. But it edged down to 137.52 yen from 137.85 yen a day earlier.
