Letters to the Editor -- Highway Safety: Some Statistics Lie

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/20/90

Regarding your Aug. 22 "Tracking Travel": There are lies, damned lies and statistics. I propose we add a new category called "damned statistics." Damned statistics should be reserved for any statistic by the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety, or any other self-serving insurance-industry propaganda mechanism. The fact is, the most reliable measurement of traffic fatalities is the number of people killed per million miles driven. The IIHS never quotes that one. It drops every year and is at the lowest level since transportation statistics were first collected for motorized vehicles. The number of fatalities that are reported rise in proportion to the number of miles driven. Another statistic conveniently left out by most of that group (IIHS and the like) is that the deaths are rising equally on highways not affected by the 65-mph speed limit. In addition, studies have found that states that wish to keep their hands buried deeply into the pockets of their citizenry by maintaining the preposterous 55-mph speed limit (i.e. Massachusetts) have the same average speeds on their highways as states with the 65-mph speed limit, despite ridiculous fines for speeding.

Whether you wish to travel at 55 mph or 65 mph (conditions permitting), you should know that the posted limit has had no effect on anything other than revenues for the beneficiaries of the 55-mph speed limit. States have lost ticket revenues and insurance companies have less excuse to raise your premiums due to tickets received for driving at a speed dictated by common sense, rather than arbitrary politics.

William D. Brown

Assistant Director

Brownson Technical School

Anaheim, Calif.
