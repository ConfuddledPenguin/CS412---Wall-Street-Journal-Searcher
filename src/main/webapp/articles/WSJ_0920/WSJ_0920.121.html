Corroon's Stock Plunges as Aon Backs Off Its Bid

  • Author: James P. Miller and Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 09/20/90

Corroon & Black Corp. shares dropped sharply after Aon Corp. reiterated that it will abandon its $40-a-share bid if Corroon's board continues to oppose it. Shares of the New York insurance broker tumbled $3.50 in composite New York Stock Exchange trading yesterday, closing at $32.

Tuesday, Corroon rejected Aon's $840 million bid, and said it would proceed with its earlier plan to merge with Willis Faber PLC of London. The announcement of the rejection of Aon's bid was made after the close of trading Tuesday.

Investors received conflicting signals yesterday concerning Aon's intentions: Prior to the company's threat to walk away, people close to the Chicago concern said Aon hadn't ruled out a hostile tender offer for Corroon. But an Aon official said that a release by the company specifically meant that "we will not go hostile" -- the market apparently concluded that Aon is serious about not pursuing a bid in the face of a rejection by Corroon.

Aon reiterated that it will withdraw its bid if Corroon's board "denies us a hearing," or if the company, "after a hearing, persists in its position that the Willis Faber transaction . . . will serve the best interests of Corroon's stockholders."

Aon had said previously it was "not interested in pursuing any transaction which is opposed" by Corroon's board. But after Corroon's rejection of the bid Tuesday, Aon immediately said it wouldn't pull its proposal. The company said it was "incredible that (Corroon) came to this conclusion without even talking to us or to our investment bankers about our proposal."

The earlier decision not to withdraw the proposal "was part of our request to meet with the Corroon board -- if it wasn't on the table, there wouldn't be anything to meet about," said Patrick G. Ryan, Aon's chairman, chief executive officer and president in a release.

Traders said Aon's statement yesterday that it would back away from its bid significantly increased the chances that Corroon will be able to proceed with its plan to merge with Willis Faber. Under that deal, Corroon holders would receive Willis stock currently worth $30.87 for each of their shares.

Some traders, though, held out a slim hope that some Corroon directors might be willing to listen to Aon's proposal.

The stern criticism Aon leveled at Corroon "is designed to give Corroon holders more ammunition for a lawsuit against the company for rejecting a higher bid," said Allan Nichols, an insurance analyst with James Capel & Co. in London.

"I can't believe the board was united in turning a deaf ear to Pat Ryan. The view of shareholders is that this is truly outrageous conduct on the part of the Corroon board," said Michael Smith, an analyst with Lehman Brothers, a unit of Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc.

He added that the board is "putting faith on (a) Time Warner ruling" that allows the board to reject the Aon offer on the basis of what it considers to be the best long-term benefits to shareholders. Corroon & Black declined to comment on Aon's statement.

A relieved Willis Faber, responding to Corroon's Tuesday decision, said "We're pleased at the outcome and are looking forward to getting the merger completed."

In London trading, Willis Faber shares rose 9 pence, to 210 pence -- a price that values the Willis Faber merger at $648.2 million.
