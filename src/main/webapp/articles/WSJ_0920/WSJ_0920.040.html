Economy: Greenspan Says Oil Prices Curb Fed Moves; Poll Finds Mideast Crisis Slowing Economy --- Central Bank Chief, Citing Inflation, Doesn't Signal Any Interest-Rate Cut

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 09/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The 1990 oil shock is pushing the U.S. economy closer to recession while limiting the Federal Reserve's ability to resist a recession by cutting interest rates, Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan said. Mr. Greenspan told the Joint Economic Committee of Congress that "inflationary pressures have increased concurrently with the weakening of the economy," frustrating the Fed's goal of averting a recession while gradually bringing down the inflation rate.

The always cautious Fed chairman went to unusual lengths to avoid foreshadowing Fed policy moves in his testimony. He gave no hint that a long-anticipated Fed move to lower interest rates is in fact imminent, though he didn't rule it out.

At one point, he exasperated some committee members by stating: "Whether an efficacious policy response to current developments would seek higher, lower or unchanged interest rates will depend on the specifics of the situation, which are shifting day by day."

For that statement, Sen. Paul Sarbanes (D., Md.) offered "the obfuscation award of the month."

The weakness of the U.S. economy -- which was growing more slowly than the Fed anticipated even before the Aug. 2 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait touched off a surge in oil prices -- argues for lower interest rates. But Mr. Greenspan said that "as of last week" the U.S. economy was "sluggish" but wasn't showing signs of "a cumulative unwinding of economic activity."

He conceded that economists who forecast a recession aren't being unreasonable. But he warned that nothing the Fed can do will "prevent the transfer of wealth, and {the} cut in our standard of living, that stems from higher prices for imported oil."

If oil prices average $30 a barrel over the next year, he said, economic models suggest that the economy's growth rate will be cut by about one percentage point and that consumer prices will be 1.5 to two percentage points higher than they otherwise would have been.

Mr. Greenspan explicitly identified two events that might lead the Fed to ease credit: an intensification of the credit crunch that he cited when the Fed last made a move to reduce interest rates in July and a "credible, long-term, enforceable" agreement to reduce the federal budget deficit.

One constraint on the Fed's ability to rescue the economy is the recent rise in long-term interest rates in the bond market -- a reflection of fears of inflation, among other things, Mr. Greenspan said. He said that any Fed move to push down the short-term interest rates that it controls directly would be "counterproductive" unless the markets responded by bidding down long-term rates.

Long-term rates are important because they influence business investment decisions as well as consumers' ability to buy homes, he said.
