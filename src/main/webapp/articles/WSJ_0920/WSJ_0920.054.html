House Approves Nearly $283 Billion For Defense Authorization Measure

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 09/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The House approved a nearly $283 billion defense authorization bill after adopting a $978 million amendment to expand the military's sealift capacity and boost spending for equipment and training to protect U.S. troops from chemical weapons. Spurred by the current Iraqi crisis, the action came as a House Appropriations subcommittee separately approved an estimated $2 billion supplemental defense bill to cover more-immediate costs incurred by the deployment of U.S. forces to the Middle East.

The Appropriations leadership hopes to rush the supplemental measure through Congress before the fiscal year ends Sept. 30, but the administration faces serious obstacles in its related bid to help Egypt by forgiving $6.7 billion in back debts on military sales. At a hearing yesterday, senior lawmakers argued that the White House had understated the true cost of the transaction, and despite personal lobbying by President Bush, there appears to be little appetite in the committee leadership to move quickly on the Egypt issue.

Instead, House Appropriations Committee Chairman Jamie Whitten (D., Miss.) is proposing to incorporate the military supplemental bill with a short-term spending resolution to keep the government funded through the first weeks of the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1. The measure would buy time to complete action on annual appropriations bills backed up in Congress, and Mr. Whitten appears to support a provision that would delay the effective date of the Gramm-Rudman law's across-the-board cuts as budget negotiations continue.

Delay could make the administration's task that much more difficult, but lawmakers are reluctant to commit themselves until they have a better understanding of the costs and what concessions will be given to other nations, such as Israel and Poland, on finances or aid. The postponement is embarrassing at a time when the White House is most eager to forge a strong alliance with Egypt, yet the administration may have hurt itself by appearing to engage in budgetary legerdemain to play down the costs of the U.S. commitment in the Persian Gulf.

For example, William Diefenderfer, deputy director of the White House Office of Management and Budget, testified in Congress yesterday that canceling Egypt's debt posed no cost to the Treasury as the U.S. had given up any hope of being repaid. But in answer to a separate question, Deputy Secretary of State Lawrence Eagleburger said the administration had considered having Kuwait or Saudi Arabia pay the debt for Cairo, but this would have reduced what these Gulf kingdoms could do to aid front-line nations in the region and help pay for the burgeoning U.S. military commitment.

"It would be inappropriate to put this in the supplemental {bill} at this point," said Rep. David Obey (D., Wis.), chairman of the foreign operations subcommittee. "There are too many questions to be answered."

Approved on a 256-155 roll call, the massive defense authorization bill would cut the Pentagon's fiscal 1991 budget 8% and reflects a wholesale shift of funds from strategic weapons to more conventional systems. As seen in yesterday's amendment, the current crisis has contributed to this emphasis, but the bill also reflects a calculated strategy to protect lawmakers' parochial interests at a time of retrenchment for the defense industry.

The bulk of the new sealift-money yesterday, for example, comes at the expense of cuts from the Strategic Defense Initiative. And while the measure terminates production of additional B-2 Stealth bombers, it authorizes $165 million for start-up procurement of the V-22 tilt-rotor aircraft backed by the Texas and Pennsylvania delegations over the objections of the Pentagon.

Similarly, the bill adds $437 million to the Navy's budget for the F/A-18 Hornet jet built by Missouri and Massachusetts manufacturers, and in an effort to embarrass Democrats, Republicans offered an unsuccessful motion to reapply these funds to reduce the need for troop cuts elsewhere in the measure.

The bill also contains the first provision to clear the House or Senate for helping workers who lose their jobs in defense cutbacks. The House voted 288-128 for an amendment by Rep. Nicholas Mavroules (D., Mass.) authorizing $200 million to extend unemployment compensation for affected workers, provide retraining and education funds and beef up job placement. The amendment would also offer funds to help communities plan for converting defense projects to civilian work.

Under the threat of a White House veto, the bill goes to a conference with an estimated $289 billion Senate measure, and all sides expect major adjustments in both the weapon and personnel accounts. In light of the current crisis, some influential lawmakers expect Congress this year to approve troop cuts of closer to 80,000, or well below what the two chambers have approved.

The $2 billion defense supplemental bill approved yesterday fully meets the administration's requests for Operation Desert Shield, but the panel refused to give the Pentagon the free hand it wanted in accepting and spending billions in contributions pledged by foreign nations to help pay for the U.S. forces. Instead, all expenditures would be subject to the appropriations process, requiring approval from the committee leadership or full Congress.

The House action came as the Senate Appropriations Committee approved a nearly $51.2 billion fiscal 1991 farm and rural development bill, which rejects prior House-approved cuts calling for the termination of the federal crop-insurance program.
