Letters to the Editor: Turkey Takes a Big Risk, for the West

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/20/90

Your Aug. 10 editorial "The Forgotten Ally" was a welcome recognition of Turkey's unflinching defense of Western interests. Turkey has been a staunch ally since the formation of the Turkish republic following World War I. Shutting off the Iraqi pipeline without even a blink and immediately beginning the embargo against Iraq is yet another example of Turkey's devotion to Western ideals. That the path taken by Turkey is an extremely costly one both for the short term and for the long haul is unquestioned. A casual look at the map reveals that Turkey has enjoyed a trusting relationship with Iraq. Why would Iraq build a pipeline that goes north for miles to enter Turkey before heading west to reach the Mediterranean when a much shorter route was available directly west through Syria? The Iraqis trusted their lifeline to the dependable Turks in preference to their fellow Arabs, the Syrians. Aside from the immediate loss of $2 billion to $3 billion a year in business with Iraq and $3 billion in back payments, Turkey faces an uncertain future with its erstwhile trusting neighbor.

Following your laudatory editorial, you became the target (not unexpectedly) of anti-Turkish voices emanating from individuals with Greek surnames (Letters, Aug. 30). Turkey's alliance with the West is anathema to these folks who are still fighting World War I against the Ottoman Empire. Every time somebody says something nice about Turkey, they come out slinging mud, with misstated facts, exaggerated half-truths and absolute lies.

For the record, Turkey did not invade Cyprus. Turkey fulfilled its lawful obligation, as Greece and Britain could also have done, to protect the integrity of Cyprus, which had fallen into the hands of the military junta then ruling Greece. Ironically, the Turkish action benefited Greece immensely by giving the junta a bloody nose and opening the way for democracy to return to its birthplace in Athens.

For the record, torture is not condoned in Turkey. It is against the law. What we call police brutality, illegally practiced by certain overzealous officers in the U.S., becomes government-sanctioned torture in Turkey as related by those who are still fighting World War I.

For the record, there were no pogroms in Turkey to diminish the minority populations. Any non-Turkish citizen of Turkey who left Turkey left for the same reason we Turks left -- to seek better economic opportunities abroad.

Ali F. Sevin

President

Assembly of Turkish American Associations

Washington

---

Your editorial stated, "The Senate passes resolutions wallowing in the Armenian tragedies of the old Ottoman Empire. . . ." I resent the use of the term, "wallowing" and wonder whether my Jewish friends have been wallowing also in the tragedies of the Holocaust.

Any infection, left untreated, continues to be destructive to the body. Any conflict, left unresolved, continues to erupt in strife. Any tragedy, left unrecognized, continues to haunt future generations. Oedipus or the Old Testament stories of David and Absalom (to name a few literary sources) are but a reminder of such truths.

Pauline Melikian House

Somerset, Ky.
