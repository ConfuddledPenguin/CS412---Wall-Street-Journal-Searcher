Law -- Legal Beat: Instron's Management May Suffer Bad Side Effects From Poison Pill

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 09/20/90

Like all companies with "poison pill" provisions, Instron Corp. hoped that its takeover defense would protect the company from hostile suitors. But now Instron may be getting a taste of its own medicine.

Under the provisions of Instron's pill, stockholders gain the right to purchase additional shares at below-market prices whenever an individual or group gains control of 20% or more of the company's shares. This makes the company more expensive for a raider to buy.

But what happens when the existing management group, rather than a raider, breaks the 20% barrier with no intent to change the status quo?

A lawsuit filed earlier this month in a Massachusetts state court claims it means that the rest of the shareholders must be granted the lucrative stock-purchase rights guaranteed by the pill -- even if that outcome turns the obvious intent of the anti-takeover provision on its head.

In this case, the lawsuit alleges, Instron's own founders triggered the poison pill when they declared in a news release in July that they and family members owned about 39% of Instron's stock. Ironically, the purpose of the news release was to oppose a takeover, not to start one.

While many companies' poison pills explicitly state that officers and controlling shareholders are exempt from the pill's provisions, Instron's pill contains no such exemption. Stephen Carr, a partner at Goodwin, Procter & Hoar, which drafted the pill, said such a provision wasn't necessary. He said no "group" of shareholders owns 20% or more and that the news release didn't create such a group.

Takeover experts said the lawsuit apparently marks the first time that controlling shareholders or managers have been accused of triggering a poison pill.

"This certainly is a new wrinkle," said Alan Bromberg, a professor of securities law at Southern Methodist University in Dallas. Mr. Bromberg said that if the Instron founders in fact authorized the news release or were acting as a "controlling group," as defined by the poison pill, the novel suit might actually succeed.

Stephen Rabinovitz, a Boston investor, filed the lawsuit on behalf of himself and other Instron shareholders in Superior Court in Boston. Mr. Rabinovitz's group had said in a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission in July that they were interested in acquiring control of Instron, a Canton, Mass.-based maker of materials testing instruments.

Michael Schell, a partner at the New York firm of Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom who is defending Instron, said that Mr. Rabinovitz is notorious for "facilitating his own personal objectives" by "making noise" and filing suits. Mr. Schell said that Instron intends to vigorously defend against the suit, although he won't say on what grounds.

Mr. Schell added that Skadden Arps had no role in writing the news release or the poison-pill provision.

PLANNED PARENTHOOD loses appeal over limits on federal funds abroad.

The U.S. appeals court in Manhattan upheld a federal policy banning the use of U.S. funds to "perform or actively promote abortions" in foreign countries. The federal aid involved is family-planning assistance from the Agency for International Development. The money is distributed to foreign organizations through Planned Parenthood and other nongovernmental groups in the U.S.

Planned Parenthood claimed that the rule, adopted by the Reagan administration in 1984, violated its free-speech rights by requiring it to enforce restrictions on speech in order to qualify for the program. But the Second U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals said no rights were violated because Planned Parenthood wasn't prohibited from using its own money for the activities.

"A policy of not subsidizing the exercise of a fundamental right differs in an important respect from a prohibition on the exercise of a fundamental right," the appeals-court panel said. The ruling upheld a lower-court decision dismissing the case.

A Planned Parenthood spokeswoman said the organization is considering whether to appeal.

BAN ON SOME TIMBER SUITS is rejected as unconstitutional.

The ban was contained in the so-called Northwest Timber Compromise. Congress enacted the law in 1989 to allow for limited timber harvests for a year in certain old-growth forests in California and Oregon that are populated by the northern spotted owl. The law was designed as a temporary, if forced, truce between warring environmental and timber interests while a presidential commission developed a final plan for protecting the owl, recently designated a threatened species.

But the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals rejected a provision of the legislation requiring federal courts to dismiss suits challenging the timber sales. Reversing a lower-court ruling, the appeals court said Congress had violated the constitutional separation of powers by requiring the courts to rule in a particular way without changing the underlying laws that would permit such suits.

The act expires on Sept. 30; Congress has been considering extending the law for another year because the presidential panel report itself has been delayed.

Todd True, an attorney in Seattle for the Sierra Club Legal Defense Fund, said environmental groups were still assessing possible suits. He said the ruling would be a useful precedent in fighting other attempts by Congress to bar judicial review of environmental laws.

Mark Rutzick, an attorney in Portland for the Northwest Forest Resource Council, a timber industry consortium, added, however: "To tie up a sale, one must prove that it will have an unlawful impact on the spotted owl." He also said the timber group is mulling an appeal.

FRANCHISEES bring civil racketeering suit against American Vision.

In a suit filed in U.S. District Court in New York, eight franchisees in New York and Georgia allege that American Vision Centers Inc. profited from store-opening services that it should have provided at cost and misrepresented the success of company-owned stores that it sold to franchisees. The suit also alleges that American Vision claimed franchisees would make money renewing contact-lens insurance, then started a division to renew the policies itself.

The franchisees also claim that American Vision Centers didn't obtain regulatory approval to sell franchises in New York state and failed to account for advertising fees -- equal to 6.5% of a franchisee's sales -- that it collected from franchisees. The suit seeks to recover alleged losses of more than $1 million, plus treble damages.

Seth Poppel, American Vision Centers president and chief operating officer, says the suit is a response to legal and other actions American Vision Centers has taken against several of the plaintiffs who allegedly owe his company money. "The suit is totally without merit," Mr. Poppel says. "The bulk of our franchisees are profitable and happy." Of American Vision Centers' 82 stores, 36 are franchisees.

Mr. Poppel says the failure of numerous franchisees to pay royalties and other fees has contributed to American Vision Centers' financial problems.

The company has reported losses of more than $2.5 million annually since 1987. During the first half of this year, it reported further losses of $1.8 million on $18.7 million in revenue.

NEWSPAPER loses bid to keep unpublished photographs confidential.

The Albuquerque Journal's pictures were taken of a 1985 highway accident in which 62-year-old Eleanor Barton was killed. Her husband, John Barton, is suing Ford Motor Co. for negligence and product liability, alleging that a defect in her car caused the accident. Both Mr. Barton and Ford sought the newspaper's unused photos and negatives, saying they were needed to help determine the relative position of the vehicles involved in the accident.

Attorneys for the Albuquerque Journal argued that producing the pictures interfered with the news process in violation of the paper's First Amendment rights. But State District Judge Art Encinias in Santa Fe, N.M., ruled that the photos are "relevant, essential and material" to deciding key issues in the case. And the New Mexico Supreme Court refused without comment the newspaper's request to block the lower-court order.

Kent Walz, assistant editor of the Albuquerque Journal, said the paper planned to comply with the order, but it was still considering an appeal aimed at clarifying the First Amendment question.

HARVARD LAW SCHOOL reinstates public-interest law counseling post.

Harvard Law School Dean Robert C. Clark has appointed Stacy DeBroff, a lawyer who isn't a member of the faculty, to the position of public-interest law counselor. Elimination of the position last year prompted protests from students.

A Harvard Law School spokesman said "over 100 students" have already met with Ms. DeBroff. The keen interest in finding jobs in the public-interest field seems to be an indication that "there is a new activism among students here," he said.

The counseling position was first established in the late 1970s to help students tap the network of increasingly scarce public-interest jobs.

Public-interest law includes such areas as legal aid for the poor, consumer advocacy and congressional staff work, all paying much less than associate positions at top private law firms.
