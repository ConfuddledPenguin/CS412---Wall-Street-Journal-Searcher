Postal Service Moves To Boost Use of Mail Carrying Bar Codes

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/20/90

WASHINGTON -- The Postal Service is moving to speed up the use of mail with address bar codes, which enable post offices to use efficient automated equipment. Postmaster General Anthony Frank announced several actions aimed at encouraging business customers to put bar codes on letter envelopes, large flat mail pieces and even packages. The agency also said it will send brochures encouraging mailers to use complete addresses, with ZIP codes on the line next to the city and state, to the more than 116 million U.S. addresses.

In addition, the agency awarded two contracts totaling $27.2 million for modified equipment that will read address bar codes almost anywhere on the face of envelopes. Automated bar code sorters currently in use can only read bar codes printed in the lower right-hand corner.

The awards include a $14.2 million contract to Westinghouse Electric Corp., Pittsburgh, and a $13 million contract to Bell & Howell Co., Skokie, Ill. Each contract is for modification of 614 bar code sorters that currently are being built and for changing an additional 694 sorters already being used by the Postal Service.

By 1995, the service hopes to have all letters bar coded with 11 digits, which narrow delivery to the recipient's address. It hopes to have 40% of all letters coded by customers, 40% by post offices and 20% by contractors who do the coding from remote locations, or using other technologies.

Mr. Frank said the agency proposes to begin offering rate incentives for barcoded letters next June, and to extend them to all of the nation by three months after that. The service originally had proposed starting the discounts in 1992, then moved the plan up to September 1991. While the agency has proposed that first-class mail be charged 30 cents, up from the current 25 cents, mailers who bar code letters could pay as little as 24.3 cents under the rate proposal pending before the Postal Rate Commission.

The agency will file for a bar-coding rate discount for flats, possibly more than one cent per piece, next spring, Mr. Frank said. It has contracted with several vendors to develop a flats bar code reader. Finally, the Postal Board of Governors has approved $52 million for a package bar coding system to process packages, Mr. Frank said.
