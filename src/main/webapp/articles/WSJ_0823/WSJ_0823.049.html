`Early Bird' Nasdaq Plan Hits a Snag

  • Author: Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 08/23/90

LONDON -- The effort by the National Association of Securities Dealers to extend its automated quotations computerized trading network to London trading hours has hit a regulatory snag, which could delay the planned September start-up or even scuttle the plan. The proposal, a step toward global 24-hour trading, must be cleared by the Securities and Exchange Commission, because it involves predawn trading in the U.S. The hitch arises because the SEC is pressing the new Nasdaq system to publish U.S.-style market data, principally trade-by-trade price and volume figures.

Less stringent publication requirements have been proposed by Nasdaq, which is striving to woo business in U.S. stocks away from London's International Stock Exchange.

The NASD fears that dealers in the relatively thin London market, accustomed to reporting skimpier information, will balk at using a program with American-style rules, Joseph R. Hardiman, NASD president, said in a telephone interview from Washington, D.C. The NASD is the self-regulator for the U.S. over-the-counter market.

The disagreement highlights a larger issue, as world stock markets become increasingly entwined and U.S. markets expand overseas: Can a system meeting tough U.S. regulations compete with more lenient foreign systems? Or must a system be bent to meet local custom?

"Our job is to further the integrity of the marketplace and protect investors," Kathryn Natale, the SEC's assistant director for OTC matters, said in a phone interview from Washington. "We've discussed our concerns with the NASD, including real-time information -- or rather, the lack thereof."

Ms. Natale said it is "clearly within the realm of possibility" that the SEC would allow the program to publish less market data. But she added: "We don't know if that's the best thing for markets today. We don't know if it's advisable to take a step backward."

Mr. Hardiman seemed to be setting the stage for a compromise on the Nasdaq plan for a two-year pilot program, saying the NASD might propose shortening the pilot period in return for less strict disclosure requirements.

As previously reported, the program would involve predawn trading of more than 300 Nasdaq issues, mainly stocks of U.S. companies, including about 70 that are listed on the London stock exchange. The trading would start at 3:30 a.m. Eastern time in the U.S., which is five time zones behind London.

Traders said the NASD plan could be threatened from both sides of the Atlantic. They doubt that London traders and customers would use the program if it required U.S.-type disclosure. And they said that without such disclosure, it might be illegal for certain U.S. fiduciaries to participate -- mainly pension funds, which operate under the federal Employee Retirement Income Security Act.

Mr. Hardiman is hopeful that the SEC will see things the NASD's way. "Commission members recognize the need for U.S. markets to be competitive world-wide," he said. But he added that because of the discussions with the SEC, "We may not get started in September." And he indicated the program might be dropped if the SEC rules against Nasdaq and dealers boycott the system, saying rhetorically, "We can bring up the best system in the world, but if no one commits their capital. . . ."

Under the NASD plan, Nasdaq would collect from dealers all price and volume data on each trade, storing it for surveillance purposes. But rather than report individual trade data on Nasdaq's ticker in the U.S., the NASD would issue periodic quotes and volume information, said J. Lynton Jones, the NASD's London-based executive director for Europe.

Mr. Hardiman explained: "It's not like in the U.S., where you have 50 dealers trading Apple Computer. In the U.K., you have maybe two or three in most stocks, and people can quickly sense what the position of a competitor is."

Actually, the Nasdaq plan would offer London dealers slower disclosure than that provided by the London exchange for British stocks. The London exchange's ticker reports British trades with a three-minute delay, except for large trades, which are reported after 90 minutes. But there isn't any organized market data system now for U.S. stocks traded on the London exchange.
