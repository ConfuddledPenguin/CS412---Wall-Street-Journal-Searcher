Abreast of the Market: Industrials Plunge 43.81 Points On Jump in Crude Oil Futures

  • Author: Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 08/23/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices dove in late trading on a spike in crude oil futures, while a report that Iraq claimed its airspace had been invaded added to the selling. The report from the Middle East, taken from Iraq's official news agency, was unconfirmed by Western sources. But the news hit a market already jittery from the rise in crude oil futures.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at its low for the day at 2560.15, down 43.81.

The broader market also fell. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index slid 5.31 to 316.55, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index lost 2.72 to 174.22 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index tumbled 4.92 to 293.10.

Declining issues swamped advancers, 1,202 to 360, with 426 stocks unchanged. New York Stock Exchange volume eased to 175,550,000 shares from Tuesday's 194,630,000 shares.

Stock prices surged at the opening, pushing the Dow Jones average up about 17 points within the first half hour of trading. But the gains quickly evaporated as crude oil futures rose through the morning.

The average drifted lower through the afternoon, finally ending in a flurry of selling as October crude oil futures rose to their highest levels since 1985, closing at $31.22 a barrel, up $2.51. Traders say computer-driven sell programs contributed to the final sharp drop at the end of the day.

"The market is adjusting to a new world," says Edward J. Shopkorn, director of equity services at Mabon Nugent. He says the market had adjusted to oil prices in the mid-$20 range, but now is dropping again as oil prices rise above $30 a barrel.

Mr. Shopkorn noted that a surge in the price of the various Nikkei 225-Stock Index put warrants trading on the American Stock Exchange yesterday means traders are expecting the selling to carry over to the Japanese market.

The put warrants rise in price as the Tokyo Stock Exchange bellwether index falls, and a surge in the warrants often anticipates selling in Japanese stocks overnight.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues fell 1086.93 points to 25210.91 yesterday. A further drop in overseas stock markets could add pressure to stocks in the U.S., traders say.

Dale Tills, head of institutional trading at Charles Schwab, says traders are getting nervous as the industrial average approaches a key support level at around the 2550 level. "As we get closer and closer to this support, it gets very dangerous," he says.

He adds that if the Dow Jones industrial average falls below the support level, it will have broken an upward trend that has been in place for about six years.

"If we do break that support, it could mean 2,200 on the Dow {industrials}," Mr. Tills says. "That's got a lot of people nervous."

But Harold MacKinney Jr., head of the investment policy committee at Fleet National Bank, remains hopeful that a resolution to the Mideast crisis will save the market from further losses.

He says the market's bottom is more likely to be 2500. And the Dow Jones industrials could jump 100 points in a day on any positive news, he says.

"I think if we resolve the {Mideast} problem effectively, this thing is off and running and next year we could see the market above 3000," Mr. MacKinney says.

Some oilfield equipment stocks were lower, after having risen sharply following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait. Dresser Industries lost 3 1/4 to 46 1/2, Halliburton was off 1 3/4 to 53 1/2, and Schlumberger was down 1 3/8 to 63 1/4.

Caesars World fell 1 7/8 to 11 7/8, adding to its 7/8 point loss on Tuesday. The casino operator reported fiscal fourth quarter profit fell to 31 cents a share, less than half of what it earned in the year-ago quarter.

But not all stocks fell.

MGM/UA Communications added 3/8 to 13 1/4. The company said it received checks totalling $50 million from Pathe Communications as the latest of a series of payments toward Pathe's $1.3 billion acquisition of MGM/UA. The acquisition pact calls for MGM/UA shareholders to receive a total of $21.50 a share, including a $4-a-share payment that was distributed on July 20. Payment of the remaining $17.50 a share is set to occur before Oct. 23.

National Intergroup gained 1 1/2 to 16 1/8. The company said it is seeking buyers for itself and its Foxmeyer drug distribution unit as part of an accord reached with dissident shareholder Centaur Partners.

Wilcox & Gibbs added 3/8 to 6 7/8. The company said it would buy back up to one million of its 13.8 million common shares outstanding.

Computer Associates added 1/4 to 7 1/8. On Monday, the company said it would buy back up to 15 million of its 187 million shares outstanding.

Comprehensive Care finished unchanged at 2 1/8. A dissident shareholder group has apparently succeeded in removing the companies current directors.

USAir Group was off 1 3/4 to 15 1/8. The company said it will fire about 1,500 probationary employees in the next few days as part of the company's program to constrain expansion and to reduce costs. The company also said it would delay a planned fare increase.

Dreyfus fell 2 1/8 to 27 1/4 in heavy trading. The company said it will continue to waive its management fee for the world-wide dollar money market fund until June 30, 1991. Dreyfus has waived the fee since the fund was created. The waiver was set to expire on Sept. 30. The normal fee on a money market fund is about 50 basis points, or 0.5%, of the fund's assets. Dreyfus's world-wide dollar money market fund currently has about $8.6 billion invested.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index lost 2.95 to 324.39. Volume totaled 16,570,000 shares.

On the American exchange, CSS Industries edged up 1/4 point to 16 1/2 after the company said it would repurchase up to 100,000 of its 5.2 million common shares.
