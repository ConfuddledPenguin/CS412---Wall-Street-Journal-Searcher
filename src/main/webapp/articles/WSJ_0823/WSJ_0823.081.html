Stocks, Bonds Tumble as Oil Soars --- Wednesday's Markets: Dow Slumps 43.81 As Broad Indexes Fall To 52-Week Lows

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 08/23/90

NEW YORK -- A sharp jump in oil prices pounded stock prices so hard that broad market indexes tumbled to their lowest levels in more than a year. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 43.81 points in active trading to 2560.15, its low for the day but still above the 1990 low set in January. But both Standard & Poor's 500-stock index and the New York Stock Exchange composite index fell to their lowest levels since May 1989.

Bond prices also fell sharply, pushing the yield on the long-term U.S. Treasury bond to 9.05%, its highest level this year. The dollar skidded against most major foreign currencies amid a continuing flight to currencies of countries with relatively high interest rates. The Swiss franc continued to gain stature as the perceived safe-harbor currency if war breaks out in the Mideast.

Underlying the rise in oil prices to above $30 a barrel and the drop in stocks was the increasing conviction among investors that Iraq's invasion of Kuwait will result in open warfare.

"The market believes there will be a shooting war," asserts Jack Solomon, market technician at Bear Stearns. "A lot of people are saying stock prices will head straight up" if war breaks out, but he isn't so sure. "War is such a traumatic event, the market has to react no matter what," he said.

Buyers were notable by their absence yesterday, traders said; even bargain-hunters stayed away. Kenneth Spence, technical analyst at Salomon Brothers, noted that even oil-service stocks such as Halliburton and Schlumberger got hit in the sell-off.

"Traders are taking profits where they have them," Mr. Spence said, adding that the rise in the long-term bond yield increased the selling pressure in stocks. As the yield rises in bonds, stocks lose appeal for investors.

Because of the impact of rising oil prices on the already weak U.S. economy, Mr. Spence noted, "analysts are beginning to downgrade their profit estimates for the third and fourth quarters and for 1991."

The dollar's weakness came largely on the strength of the Swiss franc, which jumped to 1.2780 from 1.2917 against the dollar.

Bob Morrissey, senior currency trader at the Bank of Boston, said the sinking stock market helped push the dollar lower. "We're definitely on the brink of recession," he said, "and we should see higher inflation. Because of that, we're not seeing any kind of investment flows into the U.S."

In major market action:

Stock prices fell on volume of 175.6 million shares on the Big Board, where declining issues far outnumbered advancers, 1,202 to 353.

The dollar fell. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5590 marks and 146.30 yen, compared with 1.5645 marks and 146.65 yen Tuesday.
