RJR Refinancing Is Challenged In Court Filing

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 08/23/90

Securities lawyers have begun a court challenge to RJR Nabisco Inc.'s current $6.9 billion refinancing, arguing that it "deprives" the company's bondholders of certain rights. The new court filing, in Delaware's Court of Chancery, continues a long series of suits and legal complaints involving just about every financial transaction RJR has undertaken in the past three years. So far, none of the court cases has produced any awards for damages.

Whatever the outcome, the latest legal filing is likely to be watched closely by holders of RJR's high-yield junk bonds. These bellwether bonds have undergone wild trading swings this year, creating more than $1 billion of paper losses and paper gains for investors at different stages. In large part, RJR's $6.9 billion refinancing seeks to end the financial havoc caused by its junk bonds.

The new court filing is a proposed amended complaint to a large class-action suit brought in late 1988 on behalf of RJR's then-shareholders. That original suit tried and failed to block the February 1989 acquisition of the New York tobacco and food company by Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co.

The proposed amended complaint would expand the 1988 shareholder suit to cover RJR's current refinancing, alleging that it hurts bondholders. The proposed amended complaint hasn't yet been accepted by the Delaware court, and RJR's attorneys could challenge it. Usually, though, plaintiffs are allowed to amend their complaints to include fresh developments.

RJR declined to comment on the latest filing, as did attorneys at Abbey & Ellis, New York, who helped draft the proposed amended complaint.

RJR on July 16 raised the interest rate on its 14.07% bonds of 2007 to 17%, and boosted the rate on its 14.07% convertible bonds of 2009 to 17.375%. Both those changes were made to meet an obligation that the bonds trade at par, or face value, on a "fully distributed basis." RJR also announced plans to buy back $2.05 billion of junk bonds and retire an additional $2.4 billion through an exchange offer.

Shortly after RJR's announcement, the bonds of 2007 traded as high as 95 1/2 cents on the dollar, while the bonds of 2009 traded as high as 97 cents on the dollar. It was their best showing in nearly a year.

More recently, though, both bond issues have slumped. Yesterday, the bonds of 2007 were quoted at 84 1/2 cents on the dollar, while the bonds of 2009 finished at about 89 cents.

RJR has "failed to cause the securities to trade at par, as warranted and covenanted," the proposed amended complaint asserts. The complaint also objects to RJR's plans to retire most of its convertible bonds of 2009, asserting that to do so "diminishes the former shareholders' interest in the equity value of RJR."

The proposed amended complaint seeks the repeal of RJR's refinancing moves so far, and unspecified monetary damages.

Curiously, RJR's big bondholders generally haven't been upset with their treatment in the refinancing. "I'm almost satisfied," said Jack Utter, portfolio manager of the IDS Extra Income mutual fund. Added Howard Marks, a managing director at Trust Co. of the West: "I'm satisfied. I believe the company made a good-faith effort to get the bonds to par."
