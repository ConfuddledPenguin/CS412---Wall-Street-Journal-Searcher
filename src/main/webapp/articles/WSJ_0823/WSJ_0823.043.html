Technology: Washington Post Co. Agrees to Finance Experimental Wireless-Phone System

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 08/23/90

NEW YORK -- Washington Post Co., an early investor in the cellular-phone industry, agreed to finance an experimental wireless communications system that one day could rival existing phone services. The agreement calls for Washington Post to set up a limited partnership with Baltimore-based American Personal Communications Inc., which earlier this year received licenses from the Federal Communications Commission to build and test the system in the Washington area.

In addition, Washington Post will invest $6 million to be used for "research, technology exploration and labor costs over a two-to-three-year period," said Wayne Schelle, APC's chairman.

If the test is successful, the partners said they will ask the FCC for an operating license to provide service in the Washington area and will pursue licenses for other U.S. markets.

Like cellular networks, the new network will use transceivers placed around the city to transmit and relay calls. However, the new system is expected to require far less electricity than cellular systems. This, proponents say, will allow for phones that are lighter than current cellular portables, because their batteries are smaller.

Mr. Schelle's network is still experimental. But if such networks test successfully, analysts have said, they could pose a serious long-term challenge not only to cellular-phone networks, which provide portable phone services today, but also to local phone companies that have long enjoyed a monopoly on hard-wired residential and business phone services.

Mr. Schelle envisions a phone that will sell for about $75 once full-scale networks are operating, plus a monthly service charge of $35 to $45 a month by 1995. Portable cellular phones, by contrast, sell for $1,000, and service costs about $90 to $100 a month. Mr. Schelle and Washington Post were partners in the Washington-Baltimore area's first cellular network in 1983. He ended up selling his 40% interest the following year for $52.5 million. The Post left the cellular business in 1986, realizing a $200 million after-tax gain on its investment in properties that also included networks in Detroit and Miami.

"This gives us a successful partner with sufficient resources," Mr. Schelle said. "It's like starting the game in the fourth inning." A spokesman for the Post said the company will set up a new division to provide and market pocketphone services if the Washington tests succeed.

"We're not a company that likes to flip assets," he said. "Our aim is to do this broadly and systematically in a way in which we can have a sizable stance."

The Post is not alone. A growing number of companies are either planning or have begun to erect networks that will provide pocketphone services. In England, several companies already provide a service called Personal Communications Network, or PCN, that allows customers to place calls on pocketphones. And several companies, including partnerships involving regional Bell companies, have begun building networks in England to provide two-way phone services.

In the U.S. the FCC has awarded about 20 licenses to test "personal communications services" or PCS. Companies such as APC, Millicom Inc. and Graphic Scanning Corp., have received permission from the FCC to test their PCS systems at various frequencies.

Nynex Corp. recently announced plans to install an adjunct pocketphone-type system to its cellular network, using network equipment from American Telephone & Telegraph Co. and Qualcomm Inc.

Motorola Inc. plans a satellite-based service. The company wants to offer portable-phone service through a network of more than 70 low-orbit satellites by the end of the decade.

The Post and APC expect to begin testing the system by year end and to have it working fully in suburban Maryland and Virginia by the end of 1991.

The Washington test will involve transmission and reception in two frequencies -- 900 MHz, which current cellular networks use, and 1800-1900 MHz, Mr. Schelle said.

In the first stage, as many as 2,000 customers will get a one-way service, with which they can place calls after receiving a message on an attached pager. In the second stage, expected to be available by the end of next year, customers will get two-way service allowing them to send and receive calls.
