Japan May Provide Economic Assistance To Countries Hurt by Blockade of Iraq

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 08/23/90

TOKYO -- Japan will consider economic aid requests from Persian Gulf countries that are hurt by the international blockade of Iraq, a Foreign Ministry spokesman said. The spokesman said the policy, which he called "unprecedented," was decided after Egyptian President Hosni Mubarak asked visiting Japanese Foreign Minister Taro Nakayama to help Cairo offset foreign-exchange losses that eventually could total $2 billion a year. Mr. Nakayama is on a tour of several nations in the region.

In addition to Egypt, Turkey and Jordan probably would be eligible for aid, the spokesman said. Last week, Iran asked Japan for $1.5 billion in fresh loans, but that request wasn't directly tied to the crisis in the Gulf.

New money to countries in the region would take the form of both grants and loans, the Foreign Ministry official said.

In addition to its pledge to provide financial aid, Japan yesterday said it would dispatch medical and communications equipment to Gulf countries hurt by the confrontation with Iraq.

Japan, the world's second-largest importer of oil, has been agonizing over how to respond to the Aug. 2 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait. Although it joined in the international boycott of Iraqi and Kuwaiti oil and froze Kuwait's financial assets in Japan, the government considers itself constitutionally barred from providing military assistance in the region. Under the country's constitution, drafted by the occupied Allied forces after World War II, Japan is prevented from maintaining a military except for its own defense.

Over the past several weeks, some officials have said they might consider revisions in the country's laws to provide some kind of support to the multinational force in the Gulf. But the country's political opposition, led by the Japan Socialist Party, vehemently opposes such a move.
