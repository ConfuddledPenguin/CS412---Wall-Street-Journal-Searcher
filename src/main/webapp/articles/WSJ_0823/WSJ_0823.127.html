Justin Industries Inc. Agrees to Takeover Of Tony Lama Co.

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/23/90

DALLAS -- Justin Industries Inc. said it and Tony Lama Co., both makers of Western footwear, signed a definitive agreement for Justin to acquire Tony Lama for about $18 million, or $9 a share, plus the assumption of about $34 million of debt. But an investor group holding about 12% of Justin and eager to acquire the rest sharply criticized the merger, calling it "a completely defensive maneuver, a blatant attempt to make {Justin} undesireable." The two leaders of the investor group are Perry Sutherland, vice president of Kansas City, Mo.-based Sutherland Lumber Southwest Co., and Barry S. Rosenstein of New York, a former associate of corporate buy-out specialist Asher Edelman.

John Justin, chairman and chief executive officer of Justin, denied the assertion by the group, saying the proposed acquisition had "no connection whatsoever" to the investor group's attempts to acquire Justin. He said, "we've talked with Tony Lama off and on for several years," long before the investor group acquired its stake.

Justin, which also makes building materials and industrial equipment, posted 1989 footwear sales of $142 million. El Paso-based Tony Lama, which makes Western boots and leather accessories exclusively, had 1989 sales of $75.6 million.

Yet Tony Lama isn't as financially-fit as its boots are famous. For 1989 the company posted a loss of $1.8 million, or 95 cents a share, and for the first half of this year it had a loss of $901,000, or 47 cents a share. As for its stock, after a planned $14-a-share acquisition by a Houston leveraged-buy-out company collapsed in March, the price fell to below $5 a share.

In national over-the-counter trading yesterday, Tony Lama stock surged $3.50 to $8. Justin stock fell 75 cents to $14.25 in national over-the-counter trading.

The investment group said it feared that Tony Lama's losses, combined with its $34 million in debt, overshadowed the synergy for Justin of acquiring another bootmaker. "On the surface, it looks like Justin is offering a very high price for a company that lost {millions} on a pretax basis over the last 12 months," said Mr. Sutherland.

Last March, Justin's board rejected an $18.50 a share, or $140.5 million, offer from the investment group. Last month, in a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, the investors said that they were still trying to acquire Justin.
