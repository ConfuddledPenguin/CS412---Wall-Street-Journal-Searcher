CrossLand's Plan To Boost Capital Is Rejected by U.S.

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 08/23/90

NEW YORK -- Federal regulators turned down a plan by CrossLand Savings for meeting new capital requirements but gave the thrift until the end of 1991 to devise a new plan. Under its proposal, CrossLand, the biggest thrift outside California, would have converted $380 million in preferred stock, which can't be counted as core capital, into common shares, which do count toward the new capital standards. With a 1.6% ratio of capital to assets, CrossLand falls at least $335.6 million short of satisfying the 3% level required by law. The conversion of the preferred into common shares would have satisfied the 3% requirement.

However, CrossLand, which has $12.8 billion in assets, said that the Office of Thrift Supervision has indicated that it wants an even higher level of capital for CrossLand because of its relatively large exposure to commercial real estate loans. Nonperforming assets, largely commercial real estate loans, stood at $563 million as of June 30.

The OTS gave CrossLand until Dec. 31, 1991, to come up with a new plan to meet the capital requirements.

Maurice Reissman, chairman, president and chief executive officer, said that it was his understanding that the OTS didn't object to the conversion of the preferred shares into common but rather wanted CrossLand to raise more capital.

Mr. Reissman called the new deadline "an appropriate amount of time." He added, "The game plan, candidly is to wait until the OTS tells us the capital requirement that they want us to meet. It is still my intent to go back to the preferred shareholders and ask them to convert into common equity."

CrossLand could meet the higher capital standards through asset sales as well as raising new equity.

An earlier plan to have Chicago's Pritzker family and New York investor Alvin Dworman invest $150 million in CrossLand in return for 85% of its common stock was scuttled in March because of shareholders' objections.

If the OTS calls for a significantly higher capital level for CrossLand than the rest of the industry, analysts said that the Brooklyn-based thrift faces an uphill fight, despite its attractive consumer franchise with 82 branches in New York, Florida, New Jersey and elsewhere. Henry Peltz, a securities analyst with Keefe, Bruyette & Woods Inc., said, "They'll have a hard time. CrossLand is more or less being kept alive by sufferance."

In addition, the federal Office of Thrift Supervision imposed new restrictions on CrossLand, including directives not to make any new loans other than residential mortgage and consumer loans without federal permission.

The OTS loan restrictions won't pose a serious problem for CrossLand, Mr. Peltz said. In the last six months, CrossLand has shed $1.4 billion in assets and in in the past 18 months has pared its junk bond portfolio down to $19.5 million from $193 million.

In the second quarter, CrossLand posted a loss of $40 million, or $3.64 a share, after taking a $50 million special charge for problem real estate loans.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, CrossLand closed unchanged at 87.5 cents.
