Trump Takes Airline Shuttle Off the Market

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 08/23/90

Donald Trump said he has taken the Trump Shuttle off the market. Hours after signing the final closing documents on his $65 million bank bailout, Mr. Trump said in a telephone interview that he pulled the shuttle from the market because it was now a moneymaking operation.

The sale of the shuttle, which Mr. Trump purchased from Eastern Airlines last year for $365 million, had been sought by his bankers to ease the drain on his cash flow and help restructure his troubled empire. Until recently, the shuttle was showing losses even before debt payments were factored in. In the past few months, Mr. Trump has said the shuttle operations have improved and, under the bank deal, some of the debts payments will be deferred. The improvement in operations, however, could be stalled by recent increases in oil prices.

As part of Mr. Trump's agreement with his bankers, interest payments on about $135 million of the debt service on the shuttle will be deferred. Mr. Trump said that even though the shuttle must continue making payments on its remaining $245 million in debt, it is still making money. "It's a great asset," he said.

Mr. Trump's bankers have said in the past that the sale of the shuttle would be an important component of getting Mr. Trump back on his feet. Previously obtained documents show Mr. Trump has already had losses of about $100 million since purchasing the airline.

In June, former Trump Shuttle President Bruce Nobles said no bids had been received for the airline, though Mr. Trump disputed that claim. Mr. Nobles was subsequently dismissed from his position.

Documents prepared by Mr. Trump's accounting firm, Kenneth Leventhal & Co., estimate that as a going market concern, the shuttle has a value of $340 million. If sold as part of a liquidation, the accounting firm estimated, the Shuttle would only fetch between $150 and $300 million.

Meanwhile, Mr. Trump announced that the "vast transaction" with his bankers was completed, and suggested his recovery is tied to a turnaround in the Northeast's economy. "Interest rates must come down sharply, and credit, which is squeezing every business, must be made available," he said.

With the bank deal behind them, Mr. Trump's advisers said they were now preparing a restructuring of his $1.3 billion in casino debt. "This has been the most grueling assignment I have ever been involved with," said Kenneth Leventhal comanaging partner Stan Ross, who served as Mr. Trump's chief liaison to the nearly 100 banks involved in the bailout.
