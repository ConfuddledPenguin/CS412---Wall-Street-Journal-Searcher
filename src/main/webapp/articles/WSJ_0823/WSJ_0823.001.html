Oil Prices Top $30 a Barrel, Their Highest Since 1985 --- Quotes for Fuel Also Surge During Frantic Trading Spurred by Fears of War

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 08/23/90

Thirty-dollar oil is back. Mounting war scares drove futures prices of crude in London and New York beyond $30 a barrel -- the highest level for the North Sea Brent and the U.S. West Texas Intermediate since 1985. Some oil prices reached as high as $40 a barrel in the early 1980s.

On London's International Petroleum Exchange, Brent crude for October delivery broke through the psychologically important level only moments before the close and then settled $2.56 a barrel higher at $30.19. In the U.S. futures market, October crude traded above $30 by early afternoon and ended at $31.22 a barrel, up $2.51.

Fuel prices also leaped in frantic futures trading. On the New York Mercantile Exchange, September heating oil jumped 7.64 cents to pass 91 cents a gallon. Motor fuel wasn't far behind. Unleaded gasoline for September delivery rose 2.85 cents to $1.04 a gallon.

The petroleum-price surge helped drive down stock and bond prices in U.S. markets.

Growing Middle East tensions contributed to both developments, with the oil-price climb being accelerated by news that Saudi Arabia's exports of refined products may be drying up because the fuels are needed by military forces within its borders.

Before European and U.S. markets opened, Japanese customers of Saudi Arabia reported they had been notified that deliveries of so-called middle distillates, which include fuels such as kerosene and diesel oil, will be suspended for September. That led to the run-up in European and U.S. markets in prices of heating oil, which is closely related to diesel fuel and other middle distillates.

Oil industry officials confirmed that supplies of some petroleum products are beginning to tighten in some areas of the world, particularly in such countries as Pakistan, which formerly depended on fuels from the now-closed refineries of Kuwait. This is due in part to the fact that the U.S. military is seeking additional jet and diesel fuels in the Persian Gulf region. Also, some oil-producing countries are beginning to stockpile some of the fuels their refineries produce in case of eventual domestic needs.

Overall, Saudi Arabia's reduction in product exports isn't expected to have a big world-wide impact, however. Saudi oil officials said that the cuts probably will be limited and that crude-oil exports aren't affected.

The kingdom's main petroleum exports are crude oil, not fuels. Also, industry officials said that even though there may be some dislocation of destinations for some Saudi petroleum products, the overall volumes of fuels to be processed remain the same.

There were new indications, meanwhile, that Saudi Arabia's total exports will be increased in the next few days in line with its prior commitment to produce more to help offset the losses to world markets of four million barrels a day of Iraqi and Kuwaiti crudes.

"What the markets may be ignoring is that the Saudis have followed through" on their plan to sell more crude to their customers, said Marshall Thomas, editor in chief of the Oil Daily. Mr. Thomas, an oil-marketing expert, said that in the past few days the Saudis have responded to customer requests for more oil. "This oil can be available starting in four or five days," he said.

Hisham Nazer, the Saudi oil minister, officially confirmed last Saturday that the kingdom would produce as much as two million barrels a day more than its OPEC quota to help stabilize oil markets. Such a step would be taken with or without the approval of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, Mr. Nazer said.

Venezuela also is understood to be increasing its production now after waffling over whether it would act without OPEC's approval in providing as much as a promised 500,000 barrels a day in additional oil to world supplies. But both Venezuela and Saudi Arabia still hope for some endorsement of their plans from others in OPEC.

Yesterday, some OPEC sources suggested that the oil ministers of at least six countries -- Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, Algeria, Indonesia, Iran and Nigeria -- might meet as soon as Sunday to consider the oil supply situation.

Sadek Boussena, Algeria's oil minister and president of OPEC, is understood to be sending letters to each of the others suggesting the meeting. But late yesterday an official of the Saudi petroleum ministry said such a letter hadn't yet been received.

In Caracas, Celestino Armas, Venezuela's oil minister, told reporters that OPEC "will possibly hold a consultation meeting" in Vienna on Sunday to discuss a temporary re-distribution of oil quotas. He said such a meeting would be attended by "all 13 members" but didn't clarify how Kuwait, currently occupied by Iraq, would be represented.
