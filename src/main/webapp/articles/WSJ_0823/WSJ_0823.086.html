Judge Places Restrictions On Keating's Transactions

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/23/90

LOS ANGELES -- A federal judge ordered Charles Keating, the chairman of Lincoln Savings & Loan's parent company, to provide regulators with up-to-date financial statements, to refrain from transferring any assets overseas and to give regulators 48 hours notice before making any transaction involving assets of more than $5,000. The order had been requested by the Office of Thrift Supervision, which is trying to force Mr. Keating to return $40.9 million to the failed S&L as restitution. Mr. Keating has maintained, however, that he is broke and hasn't committed any wrongdoing.

OTS officials said Mr. Keating is now required to provide an accounting of his finances within five days. "From our standpoint, we would characterize this as an absolute victory for the American taxpayer," said Timothy Ryan, OTS director.

Brad Boland, a spokesman for Mr. Keating, countered that the chairman of American Continental Corp., of Phoenix, Ariz., already had volunteered not to move assets out of the country. He also asserted that federal regulators already know what Mr. Keating's financial condition is. "They're out chasing shadows," Mr. Boland said. "This is nothing more than a waste of taxpayer dollars."

Lincoln, of Irvine, Calif., was seized by the government in April 1989, one day after parent American Continental filed for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code.

U.S. District Judge Stephen V. Wilson's ruling will remain in force for 10 days, and he will rule next week if the order should be continued in the form of a preliminary injunction.
