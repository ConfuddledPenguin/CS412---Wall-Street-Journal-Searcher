Electrolux to Idle 10% of Its Staff; Earnings Plunge

  • Author: Stephen D. Moore Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/23/90

STOCKHOLM -- Stung by a 56% plunge in second-quarter pretax profit, AB Electrolux said it plans to lay off 10% of its 150,000 employees world-wide by the end of next year under a sweeping cost-cutting drive. The Swedish appliance concern said about 8,000 of the 15,000 job cuts would occur this year. Anders Scharp, chief executive officer, said the biggest reduction would come in the U.S. where market demand has softened so far this year and isn't expected to recover for at least the next 18 months.

Electrolux said it will sell certain "nonstrategic" loss-making operations, which weren't identified, as well as certain real estate assets in the U.S. and Western Europe to finance the estimated 700 million Swedish kronor ($121.52 million) cost of the restructuring moves. Those sales probably won't be completed until next year, company officials said.

Mr. Scharp remained guarded in his assessments of economic prospects, especially in the U.S. where he said "there's already a recession in our markets." And while he remained confident that Electrolux would maintain its strong market positions in both North America and Western Europe, he didn't hold out prospects of rapid recovery for the company's sagging profitability.

"We don't anticipate any improvement in the economic outlook through the end of next year. If anything things could turn out worse than we're expecting, especially in Sweden," he said.

Electrolux said second-quarter pretax profit plunged to 500 million Swedish kronor from 1.12 billion kronor a year earlier. Sales during the period declined 4% to 22.33 billion kronor from 23.25 billion kronor the previous year.

Company officials said the Industrial Products division accounted for more than half the second-quarter profit drop. Spiraling raw material costs and falling prices for finished products slashed profit at the Graenges AB aluminum unit.

Mr. Scharp declined to discuss which of the "nonstrategic operations" Electrolux planned to put on the block. In recent years, the company has expanded its outdoor-products division through aggressive acquisitions. Besides Appliances, which account for roughly 50% of sales, divisions for Commercial Appliances and Commercial Services are also usually viewed as core operations.

Lennart Ribohn, chief financial officer, cites the case of a U.S. dishwasher factory to illustrate the delays Electrolux has encountered. The Swedish concern acquired a small dishwasher plant as part of the 1986 purchase of White Consolidated Industries Inc.; bought another U.S. dishwasher company to further boost volume; and then prepared to shift the combined operations to a new plant in North Carolina.

But the plant construction project dropped behind schedule, forcing Electrolux to continue parallel production at the inefficient old plants. A new dishwasher model redesigned according to specifications from a big buyer, Sears, Roebuck & Co., still hasn't reached the market.

Similar snags have occurred at almost all 15 major plant overhauls Electrolux has undertaken, Mr. Ribohn said. "In retrospect we obviously were too ambitious and tried to do too much at once."
