Obscure Law That Gives GIs a Break On Rates Stirs Concern Among Banks

  • Author: Arthur S. Hayes
  • Date: 08/23/90

A nearly forgotten law that allows servicemen to reduce interest rates on consumer loans has become a hot topic in the military in recent days and is causing concern in the banking community. The law, part of the Soldiers' and Sailors' Civil Relief Act of 1940, allows individuals in the armed services to limit the interest rates they pay on credit cards, car, student and other personal loans to 6% as long as the debts were incurred before they joined the military. The provision gives reservists who are called to active duty the same benefits.

No one knows why the obscure provision has suddenly come to the attention of servicemen. But as the Persian Gulf conflict escalates and thousands of reservists are called up, banking lawyers are worrying about the scope of the law's impact.

The American Banker, which first reported the concern over the law, said no one is sure how many loans might be affected. But Laurence E. Platt, a Washington, D.C., attorney who represents consumer banks, speculates that if tens of thousands of reservists are called to active service as President Bush has proposed, "it's going to be a major problem" for banks. "My clients tell me their phones are ringing off the hook," he said.

Mr. Platt says these reservists are typically older than most enlisted personnel and are thus more likely to have taken out loans. They are probably paying interests rates ranging from 10% for mortgages to 18% for credit-card loans, Mr. Platt says.

The potential problem for the banks is that the law doesn't require the federal government to compensate them for the money lost from the interest-rate reduction. Mr. Platt says that banks now may attack the law as taking of property without just compensation, which is barred by the Fifth Amendment.

But Mark Burneko, spokesman for the American Bankers Association in Washington, which represents about 10,000 banks, says, "We tend to believe the overall effect will be slight."

Mr. Burneko says the association's lawyers believe borrowers will have to show that they suffered a financial hardship because they left their higher-paid, private-sector jobs for the military. But Mr. Burneko says he thinks that proving financial hardship will be difficult because the reservists probably won't see lengthy action and because the salary gap between the private sector and the military isn't as wide as it used to be.

Mr. Platt, however, says he believes the law places the burden of disproving financial hardship on the lender, which will have to take the borrower to court. All the borrowers have to do is apply for the reduction, and Mr. Platt says that began happening 10 days ago.

The interest-rate provision of the law hasn't been updated. When the law was passed 50 years ago, a 4% interest rate was the norm. Congress, Mr. Platt says, intended only to prevent banks from boosting rates above 6%.

A spokesman for the House Committee on Veterans Affairs says the panel is reviewing the law with an eye toward updating it.
