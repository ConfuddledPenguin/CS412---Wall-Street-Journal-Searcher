U.S. and Canada To Begin Talks On Acid-Rain Pact

  • Author: Michael McQueen
  • Date: 07/09/90

HOUSTON -- Leaders of the U.S. and Canada agreed to begin negotiations on a treaty to reduce acid-rain emissions. The discussions will begin July 16 in Ottawa, said Prime Minister Brian Mulroney and President Bush in a joint appearance following a brief meeting. President Bush said he decided to go ahead with the talks even though Congress has yet to pass a comprehensive Clean Air Act.

The measure has passed both houses of Congress, and discussions to resolve differences between the two versions begin next week. Both versions include specific provisions for sulfur-dioxide emissions, which produce acid rain. In the past, the reluctance of the U.S. to enter into such discussions has been a sticking point in the generally warm relations between the U.S. and Canada.

The treaty could include specific timetables for reducing acid-rain emissions, details for a joint research and monitoring program and provisions for settling disputes.

Although the two leaders, meeting before the start of the economic summit of industrial nations, emphasized the progress in resolving environmental issues, their talks came amid lingering disagreement among the summit partners over environmental issues.

A draft copy of the environmental portion of the summit communique obtained by this newspaper shows that more than one-third of the document remains in dispute, with pre-summit negotiators unable to decide on such issues as whether global climate change is the most important environmental issue facing humanity, or only one of several environmental priorities.

Summit leaders are also expected to discuss the importance of preserving forest land and helping underdeveloped nations protect their environments while promoting economic development. But they disagree on how extensive this assistance should be.
