Boeing Lands British Airways Order for 21 747s --- Deal, Which Also Includes 12 Options, Has a Value Of at Least $3.8 Billion

  • Author: Jill Bettner and Barbara Toman
  • Date: 07/09/90

British Airways PLC ordered 21 Boeing Co. 747-400 jumbo jetliners valued at about $3.8 billion, and took options for 12 others, which could bring the total value of the deal to $6.4 billion. Deliveries under the order, which Seattle-based Boeing described as its third-largest ever when options are included, will begin in 1992 and would stretch through 1999. In London, British Airways Chairman Lord King said the airline's biggest order ever "will ensure that we have a sufficient flow of 747-400 aircraft to meet the growth of our intercontinental network" forecast for the 1990s and beyond.

The firm order for 21 of the 386-passenger, long-range planes -- plus British Airways' conversion of two options to firm orders late last year -- would boost the airline's 747-400 fleet to 42, larger than that of any other carrier, Boeing said. The planes will be powered by Rolls Royce RB211524G engines. In 1986, British Airways ordered 19 of the jetliners and took options for 12 more, including the two that were converted to firm orders in December. The remaining 10 options are still outstanding. Were all its options converted, the British Airways 747-400 fleet could swell to 64 by the end of the decade. British Airways already has taken delivery of 13 747-400s from that 1986 order.

British Airways said it had negotiated the right to convert the 747-400s it ordered into any new variant of the aircraft that Boeing might develop in the 1990s.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Boeing stock closed at $60.50, up $1.25 a share. British Airways shares traded late Friday on London's Stock Exchange at 211 pence, up 2 from Thursday.

A spokeswoman for the airline confirmed it also is talking with Boeing about a possible purchase of the aerospace concern's proposed 767-X airliner, a new 350-seat plane it hopes to launch as the 777. The spokeswoman said British Airways also is talking with McDonnell Douglas Corp. about its MD-11 aircraft and to European aircraft maker Airbus Industrie about its A-330, which are much further along in the development process.

"All three options are being studied. All three are in contention," she said, adding that the airline expects to choose "very late" this year. She declined to say how many of those planes British Airways plans to order.

The 747-400 order brings Boeing's total orders so far this year to 242 aircraft valued at $22.7 billion, the company said. Last year at this time, the company had orders for 602 aircraft valued at $33.2 billion.

Separately, Boeing said it expects to reduce its work force in the state of Washington by about 2,700 during the second half of the year, making the total 1990 reduction about 5,600. Such a reduction would exceed by 600 the size of the employment decline that Boeing had projected in January. Reductions generally are related to a lower volume of military work, Boeing said.
