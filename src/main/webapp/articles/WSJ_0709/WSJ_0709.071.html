Stone Container Mills in Quebec Ratify Labor Pacts

  • Author: G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 07/09/90

MONTREAL -- Workers at three Stone Container Corp. paper mills in Quebec narrowly ratified new labor contracts, but the settlement, which sets the pattern for contracts covering 30,000 pulp and paper workers in eastern Canada, may run into trouble at other mills. "We still face some difficulties in the rest of Canada," said Don Holder, president of the Canadian Paperworkers Union. He said some companies may be reluctant to grant workers perquisites, such as bonus vacation pay, which are included in the contracts at Stone Container, a Chicago-based paper and packaging concern. Given the slim margin of approval at the three Stone Container mills, rejection of the contract at some other mills is a possibility, Mr. Holder said. He said the union leadership is satisfied with the settlement.

Just 56.4% of workers at the three Stone Container mills voted in favor of the contract. The three-year agreement includes an hourly wage increase of 85 Canadian cents (73 U.S. cents) in the first year and 5.5% pay increases in each of the following two years. The current base wage at mills in eastern Canada is 15.37 Canadian dollars (US$13.22) an hour.

The three paper mills are operated by the Stone-Consolidated Inc. unit of Stone Container. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Stone Container shares closed at $15.75, up 12.5 cents.

Analysts said the settlement may face a rough ride at certain mills but generally expect that it will be adopted without major problems throughout the region by the end of the summer. They said the agreement at the Stone Container mills removes the threat of an industrywide strike in eastern Canada, because in pattern bargaining, other sites usually fall in line with the initial settlement. This in turn may force North American newsprint producers to give up a June 1 price increase. "There's definitely going to be pressure," said Stephen Atkinson, an analyst at Deacon BZW Ltd.

The threat of a strike helped most major North American newsprint producers push through price increases of about 5% last month. Eastern Canada is a major source of newsprint for U.S. and Canadian publishers. The benchmark Eastern U.S. list price for newsprint rose 5.4% to $685 a metric ton from $650. A metric ton is 2,204.6 pounds. A similar increase took effect in other markets, where the pricing system is slightly different. Newsprint now is sold at about a 19% discount from list price.

In anticipation of a possible strike, publishers built up their inventories of newsprint this spring. Analysts said publishers now will probably reduce inventories.

Separately on Friday, the Canadian Pulp and Paper Association said Canadian production of newsprint in May rose 2% from a year earlier, as the industry operated at 97% of capacity.

The industry group said Canadian mills produced 847,000 metric tons of newsprint in May, compared with 830,000 tons a year earlier. For the first five months of this year, production fell 1.6% to 3,971,000 metric tons from 4,037,000 tons.

The 97% operating rate in May was up from 93% in April and 96% in May 1989.

The association said U.S. consumer stocks at the end of May totaled 1,299,000 metric tons, a 40-day supply, up from 1,276,000 tons at the end of April, a 37-day supply. Canadian mills' stocks at the end of May totaled 358,000 metric tons, down from 372,000 tons at the end of April.
