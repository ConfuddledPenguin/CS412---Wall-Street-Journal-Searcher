Banks Rush to Commit $7 Billion to RJR, Triple What Firm Needs for Refinancing

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 07/09/90

NEW YORK -- Bankers said they have deluged RJR Nabisco Inc. with $7 billion of loan commitments for its debt refinancing plan, more than triple what the tobacco and food company needs. The banks' rush before a deadline of last Friday suggests that big money is once again becoming available for leveraged-company transactions, after a yearlong halt. Citibank's head of leveraged lending, Todd Slotkin, predicted that the RJR loan would help "bring back confidence" in bank lending to heavily indebted companies.

RJR is seeking to retire more than $4 billion of high-yield junk bonds whose interest rates must be reset by April. Those bonds had been seen as potentially ruinous to RJR because their rate must be reset so that the bonds trade at face value. The bonds had sunk as low as 58 cents on the dollar last winter, raising the specter that RJR might have been forced to reset their interest rate at 20% or higher.

In its refinancing package, RJR plans to use $2.25 billion of fresh bank loans to help buy back bonds. Other money for the refinancing will come from an equity infusion by Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., new preferred stock and existing bank-credit facilities. In total, bankers calculated, only about 32% of the $7 billion that banks pledged to RJR will actually be borrowed. The remaining loan commitments will go unused.

As recently as three weeks ago, bankers said they didn't know how much difficulty RJR and KKR, the company's major shareholder, would face in borrowing money from the banks. "This loan really snowballed," said a Japanese banker who worked on the RJR refinancing.

Several bankers said they were attracted to the RJR loan by the big fees involved, as well as a belief that the refinancing package will improve RJR's creditworthiness. RJR last month agreed to pay fees as large as 4%, some of the most lucrative bank fees on record, after protracted negotiations with the banks.

The banks' rush into the loan the past two weeks may cause some chagrin for RJR's chief financial officer, Karl von der Heyden, and KKR. The two companies declined to comment. It's understood, however, that KKR and Mr. von der Heyden grudgingly accepted the high fees this spring on the belief that banks wouldn't lend otherwise.

Still, bankers insisted they weren't bluffing KKR and RJR during the fee negotiations or trying to make up for practices during the great debt boom of the 1980s, when banks put up much of the money but got little of the profit. Instead, bankers said the high fees were needed to get banks to relax the leveraged-lending limits they had imposed on themselves in the past year.

"Getting this thing moving was a lot like breaking the ice on a frozen river," said a veteran leveraged-company lender. "You need something drastic to break the ice. Once you do that, the river flows along quite nicely."

Bankers said the RJR loan will be made by nearly 30 banks, led by seven agent banks that have each pledged $350 million. The agents are: Security Pacific Corp., Fuji Bank, Long-Term Credit Bank, Chase Manhattan Corp., Citicorp's Citibank, Bankers Trust New York Corp. and Manufacturers Hanover Corp. Formal loan documents are expected to be signed in the next week.
