Politics & Policy: Ex-S&L Official Avoids Making Escrow Deposit

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt and Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 07/09/90

A Los Angeles federal judge spared Thomas A. Spiegel from a government demand to immediately deposit $19 million in an escrow account, dealing the Office of Thrift Supervision at least a temporary setback in its landmark case against the former Columbia Savings & Loan Association executive. Columbia, based in Beverly Hills, Calif., is currently insolvent, reflecting roughly $1 billion in losses and write-downs of its once lucrative junk-bond portfolio, developed over the years because of a close relationship between Mr. Spiegel and former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. junk-bond exponent Michael Milken. Columbia is currently attempting to sell the bonds to minimize further losses, although most people believe the government will eventually seize the thrift.

Federal Judge Stephen Wilson agreed to a government-requested freeze of the assets of Mr. Spiegel and his wife and ordered Mr. Spiegel to post a $1 million bond by this Wednesday. The order allows the couple to continue paying reasonable living expenses but subjects single expenditures of more than $5,000 to government review and possible court challenge.

Last week, the OTS alleged in an administrative complaint that Mr. Spiegel improperly diverted $19 million in funds belonging to Columbia for such disparate personal uses as airplanes, condominiums, guns and concert tickets. The action, believed to be the largest attempt to obtain restitution from a thrift executive, also sought a $5 million civil penalty against Mr. Spiegel. Originally, the OTS had ordered Mr. Spiegel to meet the escrow demand by noon PDT last Friday.

At a Friday court hearing, however, lawyers for Mr. Spiegel argued that the law under which the OTS was proceeding didn't authorize the agency to obtain funds in advance of a hearing, especially where the individual being attacked wasn't any longer an employee of the institution and a direct threat to its financial well-being. Mr. Spiegel resigned as Columbia's president and chief executive officer last December and as a consultant in May.

"Congress intended to give the OTS authority to issue these kinds of extraordinary orders when it was necessary to protect the financial integrity of a thrift. That {authority} does not exist when you have a former officer," asserted Dennis Perluss, a Los Angeles lawyer for Mr. Spiegel.

While deferring final judgment on the ultimate legal questions, Judge Wilson said there were enough grounds to temporarily enjoin the government from requiring the $19 million escrow because "this case clearly raises serious questions and that the balance of hardships tips sharply in plaintiff's favor," according to his order. He set a hearing for July 30 to consider Mr. Spiegel's request for a further preliminary injunction.

An OTS spokesman said the government was nonetheless pleased with the financial restraints that the judge imposed on Mr. Spiegel.
