OTC Focus: Some Investors Venture Into Shares of Thrifts And Analysts Like the Looks of Certain Issues

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 07/09/90

NEW YORK -- A few fearless investors are venturing out among the wreckage of the savings institutions. Nobody is predicting that investors will suddenly embrace the savings industry as they did a year ago, when thrifts were among the darlings of the over-the-counter market. But there's hope for at least a handful of thrifts' shares, market watchers say.

Investors are in no hurry to repeat the mistake of last year, when they expected the thrift-bailout bill to cleanse the scandal-ridden industry and groom it for acquisitions by bank holding companies. Those hopes dimmed as problem real estate loans multiplied and big banks' shares sagged, diverting their attention from the acquisition field.

"A lot of people were burned, anticipating massive acquisitions and takeover activity directly after the signing of the bailout bill" last August, says E. Gareth Plank of San Francisco, thrift analyst for Dean Witter Reynolds.

In fact, this year's first half brought a 12% decline in the Nasdaq "other financial" index, which includes many small savings-and-loans. (The year-ago half saw an increase of about the same size.) As the thrifts sagged in the six months through June, the broader Nasdaq Composite Index of OTC stocks climbed 1.2% and the Nasdaq Industrial Index jumped 10.9%.

"There's nothing safe about {investing in thrifts} unless you take a lot of time to differentiate among the few companies that are survivors, the few companies that are well-run and the few companies that have successfully balanced risk and return," says Nick Adams, portfolio manager of New York's $70 million First Financial Fund, which invests largely in thrift issues.

Among 400 publicly traded thrifts, about half have met the tough new minimum-capital and other requirements of the government's thrift bailout legislation, says Allan G. Bortel, a Sutro & Co. vice president and veteran thrift industry analyst. Before recommending a stock, Mr. Bortel and some other analysts apply higher standards than the government does.

"An investor should first look at capital," advises Mr. Bortel. "Everything else flows from that."

New federal regulations require thrifts to maintain 3% of their total assets in "core capital," notably common stockholders' equity. Mr. Bortel says he looks for savings concerns with 4% to 6% of their total assets in core capital. He also values management expertise, preferring 20 to 30 years of experience in the highly cyclical thrift industry, and inside ownership of stock and stock options. And he keeps an eye on expenses, preferring to see them held to 1% or 1.5% of total assets for a traditional portfolio lender.

As a general rule of thumb, he says, a thrift with core capital equaling 4% to 6% of total assets shouldn't have more than 1.5% to 2% of total assets tied up in nonperforming loans. Other factors to consider are the kinds of loans made by a thrift (single-family loans are safest); whether a portfolio is well-matched in terms of maturities of deposits and loans; and whether spreads -- the gap between what a thrift pays out on deposits and what it earns on loans -- are expanding or shrinking.

Sound thrifts may prove to be more attractive takeover candidates than the laggards seized and auctioned by the federal government, says Dean Witter's Mr. Plank. Many suitors are likely to have more appetite for healthy thrifts than for the "freebies from the government," he says.

Investing in S&Ls as takeover possibilities is risky, Mr. Plank says, as thrift mergers can easily unravel during the lengthy regulatory reviews required. "You have to buy {thrift stocks} based on fundamentals," he says. "If you can't buy them on fundamentals, you have no business looking at them on a takeover basis."

Among the various analysts' favorite thrifts are:

Bay View Capital of San Mateo, Calif. With a core capital ratio of just under 6% at year's end and about 1% of its total assets tied up in nonperforming loans, Bay View is "quite a contrast to the horror stories you read about," says Mr. Plank. Last October's San Francisco-area earthquake not only rattled Bay View's headquarters but pummeled its stock, Mr. Bortel notes. At 17 7/8, the stock is trading below book value of about $26 a share and selling at less than six times Mr. Plank's estimated 1990 earnings for the thrift of $3.50 a share.

Washington Mutual Savings Bank in Seattle is benefiting from the relatively strong economy and housing market in the Pacific Northwest, says Mr. Plank. The thrift has a core capital ratio of just under 6%. A rumored takeover candidate, Washington is trading at a small premium. It was at 16 7/8 Friday, more than seven times Mr. Plank's estimated 1990 earnings of $2 to $2.30 a share.

JSB Financial, New York. The holding company for Jamaica Savings Bank, JSB Financial recently converted to a federally chartered stock savings bank from a mutual savings bank, raising $160 million from the public, depositors and employees. "People have gone crazy about the huge amount of capital they're sitting on," says Mr. Bortel, noting that the thrift has a core capital ratio of about 18%. Its nonperforming loans are "virtually non-existent," at about 0.14% of total assets at the end of 1989.

America First Financial Fund 1987-A. A partnership that went public in 1987, America First owns EurekaBank, of Foster City, Calif., which it bought from the government. As of March 31, its core capital was 6.5% of total assets. Mr. Bortel calls its Northern California market an "excellent franchise."

Friday's Market Activity

Over-the-counter stocks managed to recover from Thursday's loss in slow trading.

The Nasdaq Composite Index climbed 1.35 to 460.54, a 0.3% gain, as volume rose to more than 101 million shares from 97.8 million Thursday. Advancing issues outpaced decliners, 956-843.

St. Paul Bancorp dropped 1 1/2 to 9 5/8 after the Chicago thrift reported a loss for the second quarter of $1.72 a share. It had net income of 45 cents a share a year earlier. The loss reflects a $30 million loan-loss provision, including a $26 million addition to reserves at the thrift's principal subsidiary, St. Paul Federal Bank for Savings, directed by the Office of Thrift Supervision. Without that reserve, St. Paul said second-quarter net would have been 43 cents a share.

Sun Sportswear fell 1 1/4 to 8 1/4 after the Kent, Wash., maker of casual sportswear said sales for the second quarter were 12% below the year-earlier period, pushing net "considerably below" a year earlier.

Mack Trucks surged 1 1/4 to 6 1/2 after France's Renault said it will launch a $6-a-share bid Thursday for the 55% of Mack that it doesn't already own. The French auto maker currently owns about 44.5% of the Allentown, Pa., truck manufacturer. Mack also said it expects to report a $130 million loss this year and may soon be in default of certain provisions of its credit agreement with Citibank and Credit Lyonnais.
