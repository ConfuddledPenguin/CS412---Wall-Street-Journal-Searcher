Shearson Lehman Is Fined $500,000 By the Big Board

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 07/09/90

NEW YORK -- The New York Stock Exchange censured American Express Co.'s Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. unit and fined it a record $500,000 for Shearson's trading of E.F. Hutton & Co. stock during 1986 takeover talks. Shearson, without admitting or denying the allegations, consented to the exchange's findings that it violated Big Board rules in its purchases and sales of Hutton stock between August and October 1986, while Shearson was making an unsuccessful effort to acquire Hutton. About a year after that aborted effort to acquire Hutton, Shearson succeeded in buying the firm for nearly $1 billion.

A separate Securities and Exchange Commission investigation of the matter has been dropped, people familiar with the case said. The SEC declined to comment.

The Big Board's investigation involved Shearson's purchase of Hutton stock before the two firms began exploratory talks on a merger in late September and October 1986. By Sept. 19, according to the Big Board, Shearson had bought about 1.5 million shares, or 4.9%, of Hutton's common, just under the 5% level that requires public disclosure.

Shearson sold about a third of its Hutton holdings Oct. 15; the next day, Hutton announced the talks were off, and its stock price plunged.

The Big Board found that Shearson had engaged in "conduct inconsistent with just and equitable principles of trade" in connection with the Hutton stock sale. Separately, the Big Board said Shearson had broken rules in its acquisition of the Hutton stake by failing to mark certain Hutton purchases as trades for the firm's own account, rather than for customers.

Besides the record fine, the Big Board ordered Shearson to appoint a lawyer who for at least three years will advise it on rules involving securities transactions for its own account.

Shearson, in a statement, said: "We did not violate any law or existing interpretation of the exchange rules. We determined to settle rather than going through a protracted process over actions that took place years ago."

As for the alleged violations involving the marking of trades for its own account, Shearson said: "We took steps before there was any indication of a disciplinary proceeding to help prevent similar processing errors from occurring again."
