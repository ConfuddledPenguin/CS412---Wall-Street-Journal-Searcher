Letters to the Editor -- Us in Relation to Them: Real Cause for Alarm

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/09/90

In her haste to bash the Japan-bashers, Susan W. Liebeler ignores some of the disquieting implications of the Graham and Krugman study (review of "Foreign Direct Investment in the United States" by Edward M. Graham and Paul R. Krugman, Leisure & Arts, June 11). They argue that the competitive strengths of American firms have declined relative to those of foreign firms; thus the growth of foreign direct investment (FDI) is "a part of the general decline of U.S. economic pre-eminence." While Ms. Liebeler and Messrs. Graham and Krugman want to draw a sharp line between FDI and the trade deficits the truth is that a troubling connection does exist. Both FDI and the trade deficits reflect a declining ability of American firms to compete with their Japanese and European rivals. If there were no costs to this decline, it would perhaps be easier to accept the do-nothing policy prescription of Messrs. Graham and Krugman. But there are real costs. Family incomes in the U.S. have stagnated since 1973. The wages of production workers continue to fall by roughly 1% a year. Our growing international debts have limited American influence in international affairs.

My question for Messrs. Graham and Krugman and Ms. Liebeler is: Why shouldn't we be alarmed at the prospect of reduced economic and political opportunities?

Martin Kohli

Industrial Cooperation Council

State of New York

New York
