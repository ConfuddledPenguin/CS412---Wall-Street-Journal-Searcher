Economic Summit (A Special Report): On the Agenda --- The G7: Past Its Prime?

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 07/09/90

WASHINGTON -- Is it possible to suffer from hardening of the arteries at the age of five? Not likely, maybe, but that is precisely what some financiers think is happening to the Group of Seven. The G-7, as it's commonly known, is not to be confused with the the summit group meeting this week, which is led by heads of state and which frequently is called the Seven. The G-7, by contrast, has 15 members -- the finance ministers and central-bank chieftains of those same seven countries, plus the head of the International Monetary Fund.

Formally established in 1986, the G-7 is the world's most exclusive and powerful economic club. It will play an important part in translating into action some of the initiatives that the heads of state decide upon at this summit, but its writ runs well beyond that into regulation of a huge hunk of the global economy, and its pronouncements frequently move world markets.

In its first years of life, G-7 used its clout effectively in dealing with major economic problems. It did much to stabilize an unruly U.S. dollar, it coordinated interest-rate moves on an unprecedented global scale, and it intervened speedily to help ensure that the world-wide stock-market dive in 1987 didn't drag the planet into recession.

But it hasn't done an awful lot lately, and people are beginning to wonder whether the club members aren't napping in their wing chairs, victims of early senescence. G-7's statements, critics point out, have grown more and more predictable, constantly calling for lower inflation and higher savings without spelling out concrete steps to achieve either. Over the past couple of years, its meetings have produced no major initiatives beyond some relatively modest fiddling with the industrialized nations' strategy for handling Third World debt.

"Has the G-7 become irrelevant?" asks Thomas Berger, a former U.S. Treasury Department official and now director of a unit of Swiss bank Lombard Odier & Cie. Increasingly, yes, he and others believe. In a recent essay, he concluded that "the essential elements of the current G-7 strategy were formulated in 1985. Like an automobile of this vintage, it is time for an overhaul."

Balderdash, say the club members. If something isn't broken, they ask, why fix it? If G-7 is quiet now, they maintain, that's because its policies have worked so well.

Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady says the U.S. and its partners have been "successful in maintaining growth, reducing trade and current account imbalances, containing inflation and promoting exchange-rate stability." He contends that G-7 strengthened and lengthened the economic expansion, now into its eighth consecutive year, and increased its resiliency in the face of periodic financial market instability.

There is a great deal of truth in this, and even the critics do not contest it. But there is also a growing feeling the G-7 is not being vigorous enough now in managing currency-exchange rates and other undramatic financial chores -- work that, if left undone, could lead to bigger problems later. "The wolf isn't at the door," says Robert Solomon of Brookings Institution, a Washington think tank. "It's just that we've got termites in the woodwork."

The G-7 may not agree, but it knows that in the world of international finance perception can be as important as fact. So, at its next meeting -- a fall conclave is probable -- a new item is likely to be on the agenda: a discussion of charges that the group has been resting on its laurels. For now, the critics can only hope that the response will mean closer management of the world economy by the members of the club.
