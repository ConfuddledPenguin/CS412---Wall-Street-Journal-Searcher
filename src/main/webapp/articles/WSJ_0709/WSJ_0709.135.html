Taiwan's New Finance Chief Pushes To Open Stock Market to Foreigners

  • Author: James McGregor
  • Date: 07/09/90

TAIPEI -- Taiwan's new finance minister says he would like to open the country's stock market to foreign institutional investors "as soon as possible," but the move could be delayed a year or more while Taipei considers major political reforms and revamps market regulations. Wang Chien-shien, who took the finance post last month, said in an interview that because of the government's current state of flux and the need to draft regulations for the market opening, the best he can predict is that it will come "within one or two years."

"This is my personal goal," Mr. Wang said. "But our market is so small that we have to be careful about limiting them {foreign institutional investors} when they first come in."

In the past week, officials of Taiwan's Securities and Exchange Commission told foreign brokers that a limited number of foreign institutional investors may be allowed to invest in the stock market within a year, and perhaps as soon as six months from now.

Mr. Wang said another reason it was difficult to specify when the opening would take place was because the country is on the verge of major political changes. At a National Affairs Conference in Taipei that began June 28 and ended Wednesday, political and business leaders drew up a blueprint for revamping the government. Their suggestions have included direct presidential elections and holding new elections for all members of the three-chamber Parliament as soon as next year.

"So many things are unpredictable at this time," Mr. Wang said. "We don't even know what kind of political system we will have in the future."

The stock exchange's weighted price index has plummeted 62.6% since the market peaked in mid-February. Because of the market's overall bearish nature and Mr. Wang's pledge to rein in local market speculators, a consensus has been building both within and outside the government that it is time to let in foreign institutional investors.

Government officials believe that foreign investors will be long-term holders of stocks and so bring more stability to the market. The government also expects that foreign institutions will help modify the violent swings the Taiwan index has experienced in the past few years by acting as contrary investors: selling as the market reaches new highs and buying during downturns. Taiwan investors tend to do some of their heaviest buying near market peaks, and then try to bail out as the index falls.

Institutional investors account for less than 10% of the market volume, which averages between 800 million and one billion shares a day. The individual investors -- who constitute one of every five people on this island -- are for the most part speculators who trade solely on rumors. Often these rumors are spread by about two dozen big investors who "ramp" the market, buying large blocks of shares and then selling out to unsophisticated local investors who jump in as the prices rise.

Mr. Wang said he is determined to eliminate the influence of these big investors. He also has announced a crackdown on insider trading that is aimed at Taiwan company directors who ramp their own shares. Mr. Wang concedes that the crackdown won't be easy to accomplish because more than a dozen Taiwan legislators serve as executives at brokerage houses, and many of the big investors are politically influential.

Because of joint ventures and foreign investments in Taiwan companies, foreigners currently own almost 10% of Taiwan's shares outstanding. But trading these blocks of shares is a cumbersome process that requires approval by the Ministry of Economic Affairs.

The SEC is currently studying how other countries have opened their markets. One of several methods under consideration would be to split off separate blocks of shares for foreign investors so that they couldn't buy a majority of a company's shares. Another would involve allowing foreigners to buy only nonvoting stock.

Mr. Wang said that whatever system is adopted, the market at first is likely to open to selected institutional investors who meet standards set by the SEC.
