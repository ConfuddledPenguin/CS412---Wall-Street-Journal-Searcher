Letters to the Editor: Analogies Don't Vindicate Poindexter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/09/90

I cannot agree with your argument for leniency toward John Poindexter with regard to his lying to Congress ("Poindexter's Punishment," Review & Outlook, June 8). Your example of Robert McNamara's lying to Congress about the deal to remove missiles from Turkey does not vindicate the practice of lying. In "Vietnam: A History," Stanley Karnow reveals that Mr. McNamara also lied to Congress over American involvement in the South Vietnamese naval operations that led to the Gulf of Tonkin incident. This pattern of deceit by the executive branch led to the passing of the War Powers Resolution, the type of clumsy regulation that the Journal frequently argues against. The best way to prevent this type of regulation is to facilitate truthful communication to the public of executive operations where propriety is in question. In this manner, information can act as a catalyst for democracy to check specific executive policies. This type of check is more efficient than the long-term regulations resulting from congressional dissatisfaction with duplicitous and deceitful executive communications. Testimony before congressional committees not only informs the committee members but also is a process to mobilize the check of democracy.

Mark D. Robins

Chestnut Hill, Mass.

---

Once again you mounted your bully pulpit to put forth another Alice-in-Wall-Street-Land argument based on patently absurd analogies.

Notwithstanding the fact that the Iran-Contra affair and the Cuban missile crisis are about as historically analogous as the invasion of Grenada and the bombing of Hiroshima, you have gone off the deep end in using such an analogy to attempt to exonerate Mr. Poindexter for his management role in a scheme in which millions of taxpayer dollars were misdirected, and from which the profits are still missing.

Richard Newton Meyer

Los Angeles
