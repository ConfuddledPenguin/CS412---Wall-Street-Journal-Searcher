World Markets: Milan Stock Market's Rise Is Expected to Slow This Quarter as Foreign Buyers Look Elsewhere

  • Author: Sara Gay Forden Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/09/90

MILAN -- After having outpaced its European counterparts in the first half, the Milan stock market is likely to see its advance slowed in the third quarter, analysts say. Although sentiment hasn't turned sour, the mood is already a bit lethargic, and buying interest will become increasingly selective, analysts predict. The market's leading industrials are expected to lag behind the market, while telecommunications, cement, food and selected textile stocks will still be attractive, they added.

Analysts predict the general MIB index will maintain itself above 1060 through the July-September period. This would be 6% above its level at the beginning of the year, but down about four percentage points from its 1990 high of 1113 reached on June 14. The MIB closed at 1065 Friday after a weak session.

The strong inflow of foreign buying that lifted the index during May and June could be diverted to other European markets over the summer as international investors look for greener pastures, the analysts said.

"International money follows performance, and we may see it shifting from Italy to France, which has been quiet lately, or back to Germany if the market perceives a unified German market will pose no threat to inflation," said Albert Alonzo, Italian market analyst with Barclays de Zoete Wedd in London.

A major factor spooking foreign buyers recently is the sudden escalation of labor unrest just as Italy takes on its role as president of the European Community. Government attempts to head off a general strike by Italy's three leading unions set for Wednesday have been unsuccessful so far. The conflict is expected to test a delicate political balance in Italy's governing coalition headed by veteran Christian Democrat Giulio Andreotti.

"We're going to see relatively slower progress over the third quarter largely because the conflict between Confindustria (Italy's manufacturers' association) and the unions will probably not be resolved until the end of the quarter at the earliest," Mr. Alonzo said.

Confindustria wants to abolish the "scala mobile" automatic wage index by next year and is urging that wage increases be linked to performance instead of the current "escalator," which is tied to inflation.

Until the social tensions are resolved, the cost of labor remains a wild card in Italy's macroeconomic picture, with major implications for inflation and interest rates. Although inflation is on a downtrend, "it still hasn't been beaten," Mr. Alonzo asserted.

Italy's cost of living rose 5.6% in June from a year earlier, down from 5.7% in May, and the Bank of Italy predicted last week that it will fall to around 5% by the end of the year if wage costs can be kept under control.

Mutual funds are heavily invested in blue chips Fiat and Generali, two shares that drive the Milan market. Together, they represent some 20% of the Milan market's total capitalization.

Analysts said the telecommunications sector is still hitting strong notes and shows little sign of abating.

"There's nothing to cloud the horizon" on telecommunications shares, said Suzanne Miller, sector analyst for Barclays de Zoete Wedd in London. "You keep expecting some sort of short-term consolidation, but it never materializes."

Friday's Market Activity

Tokyo stocks finished higher on thin volume Friday, with interest flocking instead to the secondary markets, particularly the Tokyo Stock Exchange Second Section.

The Nikkei index of 225 selected issues was up 93.45 points at 32445.12. The index lost 94.25 points Thursday.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the Nikkei index fell 2.56 points to 32442.56.

Friday's volume on the First Section was estimated at 450 million shares, off slightly from 473 million Thursday.

Winning issues outnumbered losing issues, 512 to 438, while 174 remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index (Topix) of all issues listed in the First Section, which dropped 1.31 points Thursday, added 2.16 points, or 0.09%, to 2364.20.

The Second Section index, which advanced 30.96 points Thursday, soared 69.23 points, or 1.63%, to close at 4323.03. Volume in the Second Section was estimated at 48.6 million shares, compared with Thursday's 31.4 million.

Electrical parts shares were strong advancers, helped by local news reports that the industry remains healthy. Hirose Electric soared 250 yen ($1.66) to 7,350 yen, and Yokogawa Electric rose 20 to 1,630.

Among blue-chip winners were Kyocera, up 110 to 8,970, and Sony, up 60 to 8,800.

London share prices closed on their intraday highs Friday, buoyed by a firm performance on Wall Street and a broad-based program trade by an unidentified securities firm.

Sentiment remains nervous on the almost daily release of profit downgradings and Thursday's comment by the U.K. Chancellor of the Exchequer that interest rates would have to remain high because of the excessive amount of credit in the economy.

Friday, the end of the current two-week trading account, saw brisk volume, with 739.8 million shares traded compared with 386.8 million Thursday. Dealers ascribed the hefty volume in part to a share trade in Cable & Wireless. Dealers reported that 50 million shares were traded, but this couldn't be confirmed.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index closed up 8.6 points at 2340.

The narrower FT-SE 30-index ended up 6.8 points at 1865.0, while the September FT-SE 100 futures index closed at 2412.0, up 19 points.

Shares gaining Friday included the banks, which continue to receive favorable comment after being downgraded on debt worries. Analysts reckon that all the bad news is now factored into the prices.

Midland gained 6 pence (seven cents) to 397, Lloyds was up 12 pence at 299, Barclays gained 7 to 398 and National Westminster was up 7 at 329.

Shares losing ground included Rank Organisation, which shed 8 to close at 833, and Hawker Siddeley, which lost 6 to 610.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Frankfurt, Zurich and Brussels, lower in Stockholm and Madrid, were mixed in Amsterdam and were little changed in Paris. South African gold stocks closed marginally higher.

Share prices closed higher in Taipei and Manila, mixed in Hong Kong, Singapore and Wellington, lower in Seoul and little changed in Sydney.
