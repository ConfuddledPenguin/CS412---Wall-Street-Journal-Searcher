They're All Thankful the Client Isn't the Liver Marketers Council

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 07/09/90

NEW YORK -- If they can't deliver the public, at least they can deliver their employees. The public relations gurus at Burson-Marsteller, trying to restore some of the fizz Perrier lost in the benzene contamination crisis, haven't met with unqualified success since the brand's re-entry into the U.S. market two months ago. A number of restaurants, which accounted for a big chunk of the brand's sales, have refused to put Perrier back on their menus, and their patrons aren't complaining.

Now, Burson-Marsteller officials are taking matters into their employees' hands. In an internal memo distributed late last week, Tom Mosser, president of Burson's U.S. operations, directed the firm's 1,500 staffers to "help Perrier regain its prominence" by "expressing disappointment" when patronizing watering holes that shun the brand. Employees have been instructed to grill restaurateurs who don't offer the once-chichi import and to forward their explanations to a Burson executive in New York.

Some rival PR honchos swear they would never resort to such a technique. "Usually you try to reach out to the public to create the demand," says Howard J. Rubenstein, president of Howard J. Rubenstein Associates in New York. "You don't go out and ask your employees to create it."

Richard Edelman, president of Edelman Public Relations Worldwide, says Burson is resorting to "desperation tactics." He adds: "That's not conducive to employee morale, nor frankly is it going to be useful in raising sales. . . . People resent this kind of thing."

Burson officials counter that the appeal is nothing out of the ordinary. "To characterize this as desperate is totally nonsense," says James E. Murphy, vice chairman at Burson, a unit of Young & Rubicam Inc. "We customarily endorse all of our clients' products. It's not unusual for us to encourage our employees to use them."

Perrier, meanwhile, insists that the brand is going gangbusters and that retail sales are strong. And despite reports that some Perrier executives are unhappy with the price of Burson's PR balm, the watermen publicly gush with gratitude.

Kim Jeffery, Perrier's senior vice president of marketing, characterizes Burson's hands-on approach as "very nice." His verdict: "I think they're very, very good."
