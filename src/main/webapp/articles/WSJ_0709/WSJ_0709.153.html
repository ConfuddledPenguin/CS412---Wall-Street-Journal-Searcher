BUSH IS FOCUSING on agricultural issues at the economic summit in Houston. The president will urge leaders of the major Western nations -- especially the European Community -- to commit themselves to certain changes in agriculture in order to guarantee success in the current round of global trade-liberalization talks, U.S. officials said. Bush's comments will ensure a contentious opening day at the three-day summit. The U.S., a major agricultural exporter, favors sharp cuts and the eventual elimination of farm subsidies, while the EC has argued that it can't do away with the subsidies without damaging its social fabric.

The group isn't likely to agree on economic aid for the Soviets. The U.S. believes the aid would be wasted without certain preconditions -- such as a commitment to free-market principles.

GORBACHEV TOLD farmers and workers to back him or find a new leader.

The Soviet president, addressing a gathering of workers on the eve of nominations for Communist Party leader, asked the group, "Am I needed by you? You will decide." Delegates at a party congress will vote on the nominations later this week, and both hard-liners and moderates say Gorbachev appears certain to weather the sharp criticism of his policies and be re-elected general-secretary of the party. But the rest of the ruling Politburo, which the congress also will elect, could be completely overhauled, with a new generation of conservatives rising to power.

A Soviet spokesman said Moscow's assistance to Cuba will "fade away, but not tomorrow." The U.S. has said aid to the Kremlin depends on such a move.

---

Louisiana's Legislature passed a bill that would send doctors to prison for up to 10 years for doing abortions. The bill, which also sets a $100,000 fine, would allow abortions, however, in cases of rape or incest. The Senate earlier yesterday had failed to override Gov. Roemer's veto of a similar bill that didn't include the exceptions.

---

The U.S. and Canada agreed to begin negotiations on a treaty to cut acid-rain emissions. Canadian leader Mulroney and President Bush, both in Houston for the economic summit, said the talks would start next week. Bush said he decided to proceed with talks even though Congress has yet to pass a Clean Air Act.

---

The U.N. secretary-general sent an envoy to Albania to try to help solve the crisis in Tirana, the capital, where up to 6,000 dissidents have taken refuge in foreign embassies. Perez de Cuellar, who is acting as an intermediary, said he was hopeful some people would be allowed to leave Albania but that "most should stay in their country."

---

Bulgarian student leaders demanded that more of the country's ruling Socialist Party leaders step down after public protests forced President Mladenov to resign. The 53-year-old leader, who ousted Communist hard-liner Zhivkov in November, quit Friday because of charges that he requested tanks to crush a December demonstration.

---

Greek and U.S. officials signed a defense treaty allowing two major U.S. military bases to remain in Greece for at least eight years. Washington sweetened the pact by providing Athens with 62 warplanes and four naval destroyers. The U.S. agreed to sharply reduce American military personnel in Greece and to close two other bases.

---

Bush will permit U.S. commercial satellites to be launched by Soviet rockets, according to Baker. But the secretary of state said he didn't know when the first American satellite would be carried by a Soviet rocket. Plans call for the rocket to be launched from a base in northern Australia.

---

More than 22,000 youths die each year from injuries, with homicides and suicides accounting for one-fifth of the fatalities, according to a report produced for Congress by the Centers for Disease Control. The study calls for schools, local governments and families to put an increased emphasis on injury prevention.

---

Nine white extremists were detained in connection with a series of recent bombings aimed at anti-apartheid targets, South Africa's minister of law and order said. He stated that they hadn't yet been charged.

---

Two Kenyan lawyers were given refuge in the U.S. Embassy in Nairobi, according to a newspaper owned by the ruling party. The lawyers were described as leading opponents of the country's one-party rule.

---

Stefan Edberg of Sweden won the Wimbledon men's singles tennis title for the second time, defeating defending champ Boris Becker of West Germany, three sets to two. On Saturday, Martina Navratilova of the U.S. captured a record ninth women's Wimbledon title by winning her match against American Zina Garrison in straight sets.

---

West Germany won the World Cup soccer title for the third time by beating Argentina, 1-0, in Rome. The West Germans had lost to the Argentines in the 1986 final in Mexico.

---

Died: Bill Cullen, 70, television personality and game show host whose broadcast career spanned more than 40 years, Saturday, in Los Angeles, of lung cancer.
