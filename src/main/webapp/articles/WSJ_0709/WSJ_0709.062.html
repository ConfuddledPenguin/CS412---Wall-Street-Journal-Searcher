Bank Results In Southwest Look Weaker --- Lower 2nd-Period Net Seen Due to Drop in Loans, Credit-Quality Problems

  • Author: Michael Allen
  • Date: 07/09/90

Southwestern banks, plagued by continuing credit-quality problems and sluggish loan demand, generally are expected to report weaker earnings for the second quarter. "A lot of banks are planning to take an unusually heavy provision for loan losses, trying to build up reserves," said Bruce Hoyt, an analyst with Boettcher & Co., Denver. "This will probably be one of the toughest quarters of the year."

Analysts are even starting to question the health of some banks that have already been bailed out, such as First City Bancorp. of Texas and the former Texas banking network of First RepublicBank Corp., which was bought by NCNB Corp., Charlotte, N.C., in 1988. (First RepublicBank isn't related to First Republic Bancorp of San Francisco.)

Both rescues included massive infusions of federal aid that purged the books of sick loans and drastically lowered the risk to the new owners. But now, concern is rising about possible problems in the loans extended by the new managements since they assumed control. Analysts are particularly worried that the bailed-out banks have been risking too much of their capital in nationally syndicated loans financing highly leveraged companies.

"We think that a story that has been viewed as being without risk is now taking on a different flavor," writes Charles W. Peabody, an analyst with Kidder, Peabody & Co., in a recent report to investors.

He notes, for example, that loans in NCNB's Texas bank grew 14% in the year ended March 31, 1990, adjusted for bad credits that were sloughed off to the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. as part of NCNB's takeover agreement. This increase came despite weak loan demand in Texas. During the same period, credits extended by banks in the state fell by 13%, according to regulatory statistics.

Mr. Peabody views this as evidence that a significant portion of the NCNB-Texas loan growth occurred outside of Texas in syndicated loans. "We expect additional problems to surface in the NCNB-Texas bank's portfolio" in the second quarter, Mr. Peabody writes.

But Timothy Hartman, vice chairman of NCNB-Texas, denied Mr. Peabody's assertions. He said that bank's loans actually grew more than 20% during the period and that "the vast majority of the loans were to businesses and consumers in Texas." Mr. Hartman said NCNB-Texas sold more national credits than it acquired during the period and said nonperforming loans as a percentage of total loans will be less than 0.8 of 1% in the second quarter, or below the percentage at the end of the first quarter. He also said he expects the Texas unit's earnings to be "comparable" to the first-quarter figure of over $75 million.

At First City, a Houston-based bank holding company acquired in 1988 by an investor group led by A. Robert Abboud, some problems with out-of-Texas loans have already bobbed to the surface. Since the beginning of the year, four major out-of-state borrowers that owe the bank a collective $72.5 million have filed for bankruptcy-court protection, including Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. and Federated Department Stores Inc.

Mr. Abboud, former chairman of Chicago-based First Chicago Corp., says he expects to collect the full amount due on the loans and believes the rise in nonperforming assets is slowing. But the problems helped contribute to disappointing earnings during the first quarter. At the end of June, First City said it expects to report that second-quarter net fell to between 75 cents and 95 cents a share from $1.08 a year earlier.

The weaker earnings also reflect the expense of consolidating First City's disparate operations. Before the takeover, First City had 59 separate banks. With the advent of branch banking in Texas, the new management team has slimmed that number down to 20, and has consolidated many back-office functions. Mr. Abboud said he expects to further reduce staff by about 700 during the next year, or 10%, through a combination of dismissals and attrition.

A final reason for First City's poor quarter was a slowdown in sales from the company's collecting bank, a separate institution set up to liquidate bad loans that were on the books before Mr. Abboud's group took over in April 1988. First City, which receives an incentive fee for selling the assets, said gross collections were around $45 million in the second quarter, less than half the $104 million collected in the year-earlier period.

Mr. Abboud said collections slowed in part because buyers are waiting to see whether sales by government regulators of real estate seized along with failed thrifts will drive prices lower. He said First City held some property off the market to allow time for demand to rise again. "We will not discount," he said.

But some analysts suggest a gloomier explanation: The collecting bank may have already unloaded most of its easy-to-sell assets, they say, and further sales might require deeper price cuts. First City says it doesn't expect that scenario to happen.

Elsewhere in the Southwest, several other banks also reported bad news during the second quarter. First Commerce Corp., New Orleans, said it expects second-quarter earnings to fall to between 35 cents and 40 cents a share from 58 cents a share last year.

First Commerce blamed the decline in part on a rise in nonperforming assets caused by continued weakness in the New Orleans and Alexandria, La., markets. The bank holding company said it doubled its provision for loan losses for the quarter to $12 million from $6 million in the comparable period a year ago.

In Albuquerque, N.M., Sunwest Financial Services Inc. said it recorded additions to loan-loss reserves and write-downs totalling $7 million due to a regulatory exam in April. The bank holding company said in May that it expected "additional adjustments" due to the exam, as well. The problems "reflect the continuation of the extremely weak real estate sector and the slow-growing Albuquerque economy," the company said.

Meanwhile, Standard & Poor's Corp. recently lowered its ratings on First Security Corp.'s senior debt to triple-B from triple-B-plus, citing worries that the Salt Lake City bank holding company has put creditors at greater risk by warehousing problem credits at a nonbank subsidiary and using debt to fund the unit.

"Although First Security's exposure to commercial real estate and construction loans has decreased dramatically since 1985, and little new business has been booked, problems within the portfolio continue to arise," the rating agency said.
