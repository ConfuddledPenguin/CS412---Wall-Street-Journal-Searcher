Safety Training Under Scrutiny In Arco Blast

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 07/09/90

CHANNELVIEW, Texas -- Investigators of a fiery Arco Chemical Co. plant explosion that killed 17 workers here late last week are trying to determine whether contract workers were a factor, but the cause of the blast isn't yet known. The explosion is the latest in a recent rash of petrochemical and oil refinery blasts that has prompted closer scrutiny of plant operations by government, industry and union officials and tougher enforcement of safety rules. A Phillips Petroleum Co. explosion killed 23 workers in October at a chemical plant about a dozen miles from here. Hundreds of other workers have been injured or killed in the past two years as such accidents seem to be on the rise around the country.

"We are concerned about what is happening in the petrochemical industry," said Gerard Scannell, head of the Occupational Safety and Health Administration, in an interview. Mr. Scannell and OSHA investigators were on the scene at Channelview within hours of the explosion.

Industry officials are searching for answers and many have reviewed their safety practices. "We can't explain this bunching {of accidents}," said Lodwrick Cook, chairman of both the chemical company and Atlantic Richfield Co., the oil giant that owns 83% of the chemical company. "It's troubling."

"This situation is accelerating," said Joseph Misbrener, president of the Oil, Chemical and Atomic Workers union in Denver. "It is definitely a crisis." The union has been distributing a videotape narrated by Ralph Nader called "Out of Control" on the industry's problems.

The Arco blast occurred at a waste-water treatment tank where maintenance workers were about to fix a compressor, said Arco officials, who wouldn't speculate on the cause of the accident until the company and federal authorities complete their separate investigations.

The eruption and ensuing blaze that began around 11:30 Thursday night destroyed an area about the size of a city block at the plant. Tremors from the blast were felt for miles. It took firefighters four hours to put out the fire at the facility here, about 20 miles east of Houston.

OSHA and industry critics have been focusing on the increased reliance of contract workers in chemical plants and refineries who, they say, often don't get the same training as company workers. Mr. Scannell said OSHA commissioned a study, due by the end of summer, to see if there's a correlation between the use of contract workers and safety problems.

Of the 17 who died here, five were Arco employees, 11 were contract employees working for Austin Industrial Inc. and one was a truck driver, Arco said. The plant employs about 350 Arco workers and 80 contract workers full time.

Arco officials said the contract workers they use receive the same training as Arco employees. "We treat the contract employees, who are routinely part of our work force and part of our plant, as we do our own employees," said Jack Johnson, senior vice president. Austin Industrial officials couldn't be reached for comment.

Union officials maintain that companies use contract workers because they receive lower wages and benefits than full-time company employees. While acknowledging that contract workers are less expensive, Arco officials insisted the plant uses them to maintain flexibility, not to cut costs.

Past accident investigations have also focused on a lack of preventive maintenance, excessive overtime, inadequate safety practices and the running of equipment at or above capacity.

OSHA has stepped up penalties against companies it believes made willful and flagrant violations. The agency levied a $5.7 million fine against Phillips, which the company is contesting. In the past, OSHA penalties involving worker deaths often totaled in the hundreds of dollars.

Arco officials said it could take several months for the entire plant to restart but that some portions could be brought on line much sooner. The plant makes a much-in-demand gasoline additive, methyl tertiary butyl ether, which has received a big environmental push because it reduces gasoline evaporation. Arco officials said production could resume within a few weeks, which analysts said shouldn't affect the market.

The plant also makes propylene oxide, used to make foam in seat cushions and mattresses, and styrene monomer, used as a food-packaging material. An extended shutdown could boost prices of these chemicals, analysts said.
