International: NATO to Overhaul Military Structure; Economic Lifeline to Soviets Is Unlikely

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 07/09/90

LONDON -- The U.S. and its allies have offered Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev a helping hand by agreeing to overhaul the West's military structure, but they aren't likely to agree this week to offer him an economic lifeline. Secretary of State James Baker reiterated over the weekend that economic aid would be wasted unless certain conditions -- such as a full commitment to free-market principles -- are in place.

Furthermore, in meetings before this week's economic summit in Houston, Japanese Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu told President Bush that Japan will oppose any aid until it resolves a dispute with the Soviet Union over ownership of four islands in the Kuril chain.

At the end of their summit here last Friday, Mr. Bush and other leaders of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization endorsed a package of historic changes in the alliance's military structure. They agreed, among other things, to use nuclear arms only as a "last resort," to soon withdraw American nuclear-tipped artillery shells from Europe and to shrink the army in a newly united Germany.

Those steps are designed to make NATO look less threatening so that the Soviets will accept a united Germany inside the Western alliance. They are intended also to allow Mr. Gorbachev to tell his domestic critics that the West is responding to his reform program.

"I would think he could say, `We've been right to reach out as we have tried to do to the United States and indeed to improve relations with countries in Western Europe,'" Mr. Bush told reporters after the NATO meeting.

But Mr. Bush reiterated that the helping hand he is offering Mr. Gorbachev won't hold any direct U.S. economic aid, setting up a debate between the president and other leaders of the seven industrialized countries who gather this week in Houston for an economic summit.

West Germany, in particular, is prepared to make an impassioned argument that the West should give Mr. Gorbachev immediate and direct economic help to ensure his political survival.

Mr. Bush said at the close of the NATO session that if the Germans want to go ahead with aid, "that's their business."

White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said President Bush had received a letter from Mr. Gorbachev, dated July 4, that outlines steps Mr. Gorbachev has taken in the Soviet Union and "asks the summit countries to consider providing technical assistance and credits" to help his reform efforts.

In his comments Friday Mr. Bush hinted that there could be a crack in the door for aid to the Soviets. "Perhaps there are ways that we can assist them as we go forward," he said, "with credit or other matters, before we go to direct government loans."

Secretary Baker, appearing on ABC News' "This Week" program yesterday, said that credits are "not right now in the cards." But officials said the administration is considering trying to dismantle longstanding legal barriers to direct U.S. bank loans for the Soviet Union.

But Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney said on the same television program, "We will provide credits . . . . We want to send out a signal that this leadership in the Soviet Union is valid and valuable."

The remarks were later clarified by Derek Burney, who is Canada's ambassador to the U.S. and Mr. Mulroney's special assistant for the summit. He said the prime minister was referring to two existing Canadian programs for the Soviet Union -- one by the Canadian Wheat Board and the other by Canada's Export-Import Bank. The Soviets aren't making full use of either program, indicating that Canada could extend credits to the Soviet Union without any new measures.

The U.S. and its NATO allies did succeed in settling disagreements over how to begin revamping the military alliance. They agreed to thin out its forces on the alliance's front line and begin putting them under new multinational commands.

In addition, the U.S. worked out an agreement declaring that nuclear arms are weapons of "last resort" but endorsed in the summit's final communique a statement that nuclear arms will continue to "fulfill an essential role in the overall strategy of the alliance." Britain and France wanted such a statement to make clear that the alliance wasn't suggesting that nuclear weapons could be discarded.

The allies also agreed on pulling nuclear-tipped artillery shells out of Europe. The U.S. originally had proposed taking out the shells -- whose short range makes them nearly useless as East European countries leave the Soviet military orbit -- only when the last Soviet soldier is withdrawn from Europe. West Germany wanted to move more quickly.

So NATO agreed that the weapons will be taken out as soon as talks begin next year on a treaty cutting short-range nuclear arms in Europe and the Soviets take the "reciprocal action" of withdrawing their own nuclear artillery.

The U.S. and West Germany agreed to make a general pledge to shrink the army in the united Germany when a conventional-arms agreement with the Soviets is signed late this year, while putting off a formal agreement on the army's size until a follow-on conventional-arms deal with the Soviets.

The allies, as expected, also agreed to invite Mr. Gorbachev to address a future NATO meeting and to invite countries of the Warsaw Pact to establish a "diplomatic liaison" with NATO.
