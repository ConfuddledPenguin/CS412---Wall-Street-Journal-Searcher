Poring Over Recycling Of Soda-Bottle Plastic

UNCLE SAM may open up plastic beverage bottles for recycling.

Kenneth Falci, a consumer safety officer and recycling expert with the Food and Drug Administration, says the agency is leaning toward permitting soft drink bottles and water jugs to be made from recycled polyethylene terephthalate (PET) and high-density polyethylene (HDPE), respectively. Each currently is made from the virgin material.

A ruling is still several months away, says Mr. Falci, and will depend in part on whether the agency is convinced there is a consistent supply of uncontaminated recycled plastic available. "It can probably be done successfully," he says.

Others aren't so sure. Dennis Sabourin, vice president of Wellman Inc., the largest U.S. plastics recycler, says there's an acute lack of plastic collection and sorting facilities. Wellman, he says, could use twice as many bottles as it can buy for the fiber products it makes. Moreover, he says, recycled bottles could cost much more to make than virgin bottles because it's necessary to break the bottle down into its base chemicals, then go through a costly repolymerization.

Still, a spokesman for PepsiCo Inc. says the company is working with its packaging suppliers on a recycled-PET pop bottle, and hopes to have one ready within 12 to 24 months. Coca-Cola Co. didn't return repeated phone calls.

Power-Line Radiation

DOES POWER-LINE radiation cause cancer? The debate spreads to the Environmental Protection Agency.

The controversy over powerline radiation has been building for a couple of years, spurred by a spate of inconclusive but suggestive new epidemiological and animal studies. Unwilling to wait for definitive proof, some communities and individuals have sued their local electric utilities to stop construction of overhead lines.

Earlier this year, EPA analysts recommended that the agency classify power-line radiation a "probable" human carcinogen. They were overruled, however, by the EPA's director of Health and Environmental Assessment, William Farland, who decided there isn't yet enough evidence to justify such a warning. The analysts' recommendation was then leaked to Microwave News, a New York newsletter.

An EPA spokeswoman calls the disagreement "an interoffice squabble." She says Mr. Farland "chose very wisely," adding that, "it can serve no purpose . . . to jump out there {with a warning} right now."

In their written recommendation, the analysts acknowledged there is only limited evidence linking power-line radiation to cancer. They also acknowledged that nobody yet understands the interaction between power-line radiation and human cells.

The deleted recommendation was part of an EPA report that reviewed the scientific literature on low-level radiation's health effects. That report, minus the recommendation, is to undergo scientific peer review, the spokeswoman says, adding that the reviewers could decide that there's enough evidence to warrant reinstating the analysts' warning.

Landfill in New York

Dumps Its Higher Prices

NEW YORK CITY wants its garbage back.

In December 1988 the Big Apple more than doubled the fee at its huge Fresh Kills landfill on Staten Island. Haulers balked, diverting 25% to 30% of the landfill's intake -- a monstrous 10,000 tons of trash a day -- to less expensive landfills in Ohio, Pennsylvania and other states.

The diversion inflamed a border war originally touched off by New Jersey's policy to ship vast amounts of its garbage to other states. Last month, Indiana Sen. Dan Coats tried to attach an amendment to a chemical-weapons bill that would have let states ban or otherwise impede the flow of out-of-state garbage. (It was killed by a cloture vote.)

With New York buried under a fiscal crisis, a spokesman for the city's sanitation department says the department has proposed rolling back the Fresh Kills dumping fee, to $25 a cubic yard from $40 for garbage from which recyclables have been removed. Though that's still higher than the $18.50 haulers were paying before the fee increase, sanitation officials hope they can woo back 3,500 tons, enough coffee grinds and chicken bones to raise an extra $20 million a year in revenue.

Whether or not the plan works, however, it isn't going to douse the border war. An aide to Sen. Coats says, "States should have the ability to control their overall destiny." Will the amendment be reintroduced? "We live to fight another day," says the aide.

Odds and Ends

SOUTHWALL Technologies Inc. of Palo Alto, Calif., claims its "Superglass" window glass insulates as well as wooden walls. The glass consists of two panes, lined with two polyester films pulled tight. Each film has a metallic coating that reflects the heat in or out, depending on the time of year. The pockets between the panes and the films are filled with argon and other insulating gases. . . . Next summer's mulch could come from this winter's Christmas trees. Residents of Kingsport, Tenn., with an assist from Waste Management Inc., collect Christmas trees and grind them into mulch, which is then spread on city property. The program won an award last year from Keep America Beautiful, a nonprofit group, which says other communities have copied the idea. . . . Midnight Oil, an Australian rock group whose songs loudly voice social consciousness, uses recycled-paper packaging for its latest album.
