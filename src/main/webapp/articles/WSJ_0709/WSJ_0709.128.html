Letters to the Editor: Kemp Became His Own Doppelganger

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/09/90

Thanks to Congressman Jack Kemp's legislative program of tax cuts for millionaires, the U.S. hasn't the fiscal wherewithal to follow HUD Secretary Jack Kemp's new plans to help the poor out of poverty ("Liberate America's Other Economy," editorial page, June 12). Urban homesteading, housing vouchers, an expanded earned-income tax credit and magnet schools all cost money. Yet with his unrelenting call for a capital-gains tax cut, Secretary Kemp would ask for even less support for his program than those with the greatest ability to pay currently provide.

In real dollars, the median U.S. family income has been stagnant for more than a decade. The poorest fifth have lost 14% of their buying power since 1978. Meanwhile, the average real-dollar, after-tax incomes of those in the top 1% have more than doubled. Would that Secretary Kemp's 1990s advocacy for the poor also includes a plan to end the debt binge and the upward wealth transfer that Rep. Jack Kemp loosed upon the country in the 1980s.

Bruce L. Fisher

Research Director

Citizens for Tax Justice

Washington

---

Mr. Kemp's thoughtful, evocative article should be must reading for all members of Congress and their staffs. It makes such good sense; it might cause some to stop and think. Implementation of his suggestions for a national agenda could, over time, so truly and dramatically improve the lot of countless millions that even those most responsible for leading the nation into this sorrowful dilemma will stand and cheer.

Hugh B. Lee Jr.

Terre Haute, Ind.

---

The highlighted text from Mr. Kemp's article referred to a mother and two children. Among other topics, Mario Cuomo's solutions, apparently, included redistribution of wealth.

How about a better form of redistribution? How about a redistribution of the children? Fathers typically make more than mothers, so why not routinely grant the custody of their own children to them? We live in a society of strange values, in my opinion, when we automatically grant custody to the parent earning less income (or none at all), and then bleat that our nation's children are living in poverty.

Trevor N.F. Crick

Associate Professor of Finance

University of Detroit

Detroit
