Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Inflation Is in the Spotlight as Reports On Prices, Credit, Retail Sales Are Due

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 07/09/90

NEW YORK -- Trickle-down from June's so-so jobs report should damp industrial-output data and focus attention on the next economic bugaboo: inflation. Economists surveyed after Friday's employment-report release said they expect June industrial production, due out next week,to fall far short of May's 0.6% gain. Meanwhile, attention turns toward the June producer price index, which is expected to show a slight increase. The PPI is due out Friday along with June retail sales. This week's other major indicator is May's consumer credit, due out today.

Much attention lately has focused on whether the Federal Reserve will ease credit, giving the flagging 7 1/2-year-old economic expansion a shot in the arm. Economists generally viewed the June jobs data as inconclusive: soft, but not weak enough to prod the Fed to lower interest rates.

Lawrence Kudlow, chief economist at Bear, Stearns & Co., New York, said the next checkpoint for possible Fed action may come with the June inflation figures.

"That's where the rubber could meet the road," he said. Mr. Kudlow expects only a 0.1% increase in the June PPI after May's 0.3% advance. And he envisions another tame inflation reading from next week's June consumer price index. He holds that lower inflation, against the backdrop of a slower economy, could encourage the Fed to ease credit and help stimulate the economy. "I think this economy is really on the cusp of recession," he said.

Of course, not everyone agrees with Mr. Kudlow about recession. David Resler, chief economist at Nomura Securities International Inc., New York, said that the "best read on the {jobs} number" is simply that it confirms "that the economy isn't growing very much." The slowdown in manufacturing jobs has been discernible for some time, he said. And the recent slackening of job growth in the important services area "doesn't seem to be causing widespread weakness in the service sector as yet -- with the emphasis on yet," noted Mr. Resler.

He expects industrial production to show a rise of 0.4% for June, with half the gain coming from stronger mining and utility production. The consensus view calls for only a 0.2% increase in June output, according to MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York. In May, industrial production rose 0.6%. Capacity utilization is expected to remain unchanged at 83.6%, according to the MMS survey.

The consensus view on the June PPI calls for a rise of 0.2%, down slightly from May's 0.3% advance. Consumer prices are expected to show a rise of 0.3% for June, up slightly from May's 0.2% increase.

Economists said producer prices should benefit from another drop in energy prices, helping to offset upward pressure from capital-goods prices. Higher tobacco prices are expected to tug upward on the index.

Donald Fine, economist at Chase Manhattan Bank, New York, believes "the threat of a recession right now is fairly small," although he noted there aren't any great signs of vigor, either. "The economy is sort of bopping along at a 1.5% growth rate and that, in the long run, will help moderate inflation," Mr. Fine said. He expects both the June PPI and CPI to increase 0.3%.

A rebound in auto sales in June is thought to have helped retail sales move into the plus column after two months of fairly steep declines. The consensus view calls for a 0.6% gain in retail sales, according to MMS. Excluding auto sales, that gain narrows to 0.3%, it said.

In May, sluggish auto sales, along with subdued general-merchandise buying, are thought to have contributed to another sluggish consumer-credit report. In April, consumer credit expanded $600 million.
