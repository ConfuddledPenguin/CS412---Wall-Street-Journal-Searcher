Marketing & Media: Warburg Pincus Says It Won't Shed Any Ingersoll Papers Despite Problems

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 07/09/90

E.M. Warburg Pincus & Co., the new owner of the newspaper empire that belonged to Ralph Ingersoll II, isn't considering the sale of any papers, though it faces potentially drawn-out negotiations over debt payments to bondholders. Warburg severed its seven-year partnership with Ingersoll Publications Co. last Monday, agreeing to acquire all of Mr. Ingersoll's stock in his three U.S. newspaper companies, while handing over control of British and Irish properties to Mr. Ingersoll.

Robert Jelenic, who had been No. 2 as Ingersoll Publications' president, was quickly made chief executive officer and is already planning changes. Mr. Jelenic said he will rename the company as early as this week and will move the company headquarters as of Nov. 1 from Princeton, N.J., to Trenton, N.J., where Ingersoll publishes the Trentonian.

Warburg, a New York-based investment banking firm, will control companies whose daily newspapers are concentrated in Northeastern states, where an economic slowdown has battered newspaper profit margins. The companies include Ingersoll Newspapers Inc., which also publishes the weekly Suburban Journals in St. Louis; New Haven Acquisition Co., publisher of the New Haven Register; and Community Newspapers Inc., which publishes papers in Ohio and New York and faces a possible default on interest payments to bondholders.

The new owners said they will work out any problems with bondholders and seek to bolster the companies with new cash investments. Lionel Pincus, chairman of Warburg, said they would "improve and expand the operations of Ingersoll Newspapers Inc. and New Haven" and that Warburg has equity capital of more than $3 billion to work with.

"Since the acquisition, I've had plenty of calls from people wanting to buy our papers," said Mr. Jelenic. "That's natural in this situation, but nothing's for sale. Warburg wants to grow the properties."

Warburg has a thorny financial problem with the payment of debt on Community Newspapers. CNI didn't make the semiannual interest payment due July 1 on the $125 million of 13% senior subordinated reset notes, and didn't meet the July 7 deadline to reset interest rates on those bonds.

CNI is working under a 30-day grace period in which to work out payment arrangements, or otherwise be considered in default. After that, CNI could be pushed into bankruptcy law proceedings by bondholders.

Bondholders aren't expected to go that far. One bondholder, who asked not to be identified, said: "Bankruptcy is the ultimate threat, but we would like to avoid it. Bankruptcy is expensive and very slow."

A steering committee representing CNI bondholders has worked with financial adviser, Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. and legal adviser, Weil, Gotshal & Manges, to place sale values on Ingersoll properties in the case of liquidation. While it's not clear what alternatives the bondholders group has offered CNI's management, two possibilities include lowering the interest on the bonds in exchange for equity, or increasing the dollar value of a tender offer to holders of CNI's junk bonds.

Warburg Pincus's decision to miss the interest payment rankled some bondholders, but other believe it gives them even more leverage to force a solution. "I am not happy they didn't meet my interest payment but it gives us leverage to pick it apart if it comes down to it," said a bondholder.

But bond analysts doubt Warburg will ever let CNI fall into bankruptcy. Analysts expect Warburg to make the interest payment, estimated to be $8.1 million, just before the 30-day period runs out in an attempt to keep negotiations alive. Should CNI fall into bankruptcy proceedings, analysts said, it would largely wipe out the value of Warburg's equity in the company.

In general, bondholders are happy the newspapers have new ownership. "We are generally happy that Ralph {Ingersoll} is out of the picture," said one bondholder. "There is no given that Warburg will do better by us than Ralph did, but Warburg has deeper pockets and Ralph has zip."
