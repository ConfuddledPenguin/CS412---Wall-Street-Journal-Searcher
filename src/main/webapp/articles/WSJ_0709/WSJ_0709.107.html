Credit Markets: Bond Rally Now Seems Unlikely --- Economic News Ends Speculation of Easing By Federal Reserve

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 07/09/90

NEW YORK -- Bond investors are giving up hope that the long-predicted bond market rally will soon materialize. Friday's employment report, coming on top of stronger than expected auto sales data on Thursday, has convinced investors that interest rates won't fall significantly and that the Federal Reserve will probably keep credit policy on hold.

For the bond market, that bodes an extended period of trendless trading activity providing little room for investors to profit.

"It's going to be a dreadfully slow summer," said Charles Lieberman, director of financial-markets research at Manufacturers Hanover Securities Corp. If bond-market players "had any sense they'd all go away on long vacations." Mr. Lieberman said the only excitement could come on days when there is news out of Washington regarding congressional efforts to reduce the size of the budget deficit.

"If you want to make money in a hurry, the Treasury bond market is not the place to be right now," said Sung Won Sohn, senior vice president at Norwest Corp. "Rates aren't going anywhere. This is what we call a hiatus and it might continue through the summer."

Call it the bond-market blahs, and note that Messrs. Lieberman and Sohn aren't the only ones infected. Up and down Wall Street, money managers, traders and economists alike are grumbling about what they expect will be a slow summer in the bond market.

The Department of Labor's June employment report set the mood. Although employment growth was sluggish in June, many economists said the report overall wasn't weak enough to prompt the Federal Reserve to ease monetary policy any time soon.

The government said the unemployment rate declined to 5.2% from 5.3%, while only 40,000 new non-farm jobs were created. But perhaps more stunning was the huge upward revisions of the May data, which were revised to show employment growth of 355,000 instead of the previous estimate of 164,000.

Shortly after the employment report was released, bond prices plunged. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell 1 1/8 point, a loss of about $11.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, slid to 8.5%.

Though he expects the market to settle into a trading range now that Friday's rout is over, Mr. Lieberman said the market could see a few exciting days later this month if Congress "comes up with a budget deal" before the summer recess. Although Congress will return in September, politicians will be off hitting the campaign trail in October, not leaving much time for budget negotiations.

"That leaves a small window to achieve progress on the budget package; during that small period of time the bond market will be very vulnerable to information," said Mr. Lieberman.

Meanwhile, money managers are busy restructuring their portfolios.

Edward A. Treichel, investment strategist at Van Kampen Merritt Inc., said that many institutional investors are "shortening" their fixed-income portfolios in an effort to match their holdings to the major bond-market indexes, such as the Salomon Brothers Broad Investment Grade Index or the Shearson Lehman Hutton T-Bond Index.

Shortening, which simply means that money managers are buying shorter-term bonds and possibly selling longer-term bonds, is a sign that portfolio managers are bearish or neutral on the market.

Mr. Treichel said that prior to Friday, many money managers had "lengthened" their fixed-income portfolios relative to the indexes. Lengthening is a sign that investors are bullish. Because long-term bonds are more volatile than short-term bonds, lengthening a portfolio can boost performance when the market is rising but it can also be a drag on performance when the market is falling.

An example of this took place during Friday's market decline, when the benchmark 30-year bond fell 1 1/8 points, or $11.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. But two-year notes fell only 1/4 point, or about $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

When investment managers aren't sure about the direction of rates, they often simply match their portfolios to the major indexes, seeking to perform as well as the market. Some of Friday's portfolio shuffling was meant to achieve this end.

Michael M. Spencer, vice president at One Federal Asset Management, Boston, was among the managers who were busy going shorter on Friday.

"This is the third time this year that we thought the Fed would ease and short-term rates would fall and it didn't happen," said a disappointed Mr. Spencer, who manages only high-grade bonds. He believes that to make money in the third quarter will require investors to go for yield since he doubts that the market will move strongly enough to provide capital appreciation in the way of higher bond prices.

On Friday, Mr. Spencer sold two-year Treasurys and bought high-quality commercial paper of companies such as General Motors Corp. Yields on commercial paper, short-term IOUs that in many cases mature in 30 days, are about the same as yields on short-term Treasurys. But because they are held for such a short period, investors incur very little interestrate risk.

"It's time to stop expecting short-term rates to fall and just go back to collecting income" from interest payments, said Mr. Spencer.

---

Friday's Market Activity

Bond prices plunged Friday after the latest employment data dashed hopes for an immediate easing of interest rates.

"It was a day of unfulfilled expectations," said F. Ward McCarthy, a managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates Inc.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond tumbled 1 1/8 point, a decline of $11.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The bellwether bond was quoted late at a price of 102 18/32 to yield 8.5% compared with 103 22/32 to yield 8.40% on Thursday. Shortterm rates jumped. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills, for example, rose 0.10 percentage point on Friday to 7.76% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.0%.

Most of the selling in the bond market occurred in the first hour after the release of the jobs data. After that, bond prices held steady.

In the corporate bond market, negative comments by a securities analyst hurt the junk bonds of Quantum Chemical Corp. Avi Nash at Goldman, Sachs & Co. was said to have cut his second-quarter earnings estimate and recommended that holders sell their Quantum stock, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Officials at Goldman wouldn't confirm the reports.

Quantum stock dived on the news, falling 8% or 1 1/4 points to 15 3/8 in volume more than triple the stock's daily average.

The junk bonds fell in sympathy with the stock. The 12.5% senior subordinated notes ended the day 87 1/2, down 1/2 point, while the company's 13% senior subordinated debentures also slipped 1/2 on the day to 86.

Quantum's bonds have been under pressure in recent weeks as a spate of analysts have reported gloomy earnings forecasts for cyclical and recession-sensitive industries.

Most other junk bonds, which are high-yield, high-risk corporate securities that have low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, barely budged Friday.

In the investment-grade market, bonds slipped 1/8 to 1/4 point in thin trading.

Tax-exempt municipal bonds outperformed the Treasury market on Friday as they did for most of the week, buoyed by talk out of Washington that taxes may be about to rise. Some long-term municipal bonds fell just 1/8 point on Friday.
