Your Money Matters -- Investment Dartboard: SciMed Wins Contest; Fan Sticks With It

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 07/09/90

How's this for loyalty? Six months ago, John Tauer recommended SciMed Life Systems Inc. in this column's stock-picking contest. The stock has since gained more than 51%, beating out those issues selected by three other investment professionals in January. And that earns a return visit for Mr. Tauer, who is director of research for Minneapolis brokerage firm Piper, Jaffray & Hopwood.

So what stock does Mr. Tauer pick for the next six months? SciMed. The company, based in Minneapolis, makes balloon angioplasty catheters -- tubes with a tiny expandable balloon attached that can be inserted into arteries in the heart to unblock clogged passageways.

Mr. Tauer is the first Investment Dartboard winner under new rules that make it strictly a six-month contest. The change was made mainly to minimize the impact of short-term jumps in the recommended stocks immediately after publication.

In the six months through June, the four investment pros who picked in January outpaced a random "dartboard portfolio," which was selected by Journal staff members tossing darts at stock listings. The pros averaged a 12.7% gain. The four dart picks were unchanged (on average) for the period, while the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 2.5%.

Mr. Tauer's decision to stick with SciMed flies in the face of many investors' belief that chasing big gainers isn't wise. But Mr. Tauer thinks SciMed still has a long way to go. He'd buy it at anywhere near Friday's closing price of $38.50, up 87.5 cents on the day, and says it "certainly could go into the 50s by the end of the year."

Sales and earnings are growing quickly. Sales were only $27 million in the year ended in February 1989. Sales grew to $67 million in fiscal 1990, and Mr. Tauer expects them to hit $126 million in the current year. Meanwhile, per-share earnings have shot up to $1.29 from 33 cents. Mr. Tauer looks for $2.15 this year.

He cautions that SciMed is an "aggressive" pick that carries real risks. SciMed has only one major product. The stock sells at a high multiple of earnings (18 times this year's estimated earnings, 25 times the past 12 months' earnings). And there are some patent-infringement suits pending against the company.

Still, he says, "They've got an extremely good product. This is a pure play in . . . a fast-growing area of medicine."

Finishing second for the past six months, with an 8% gain in Snap-On Tools Corp., was Randall Eley, president of Edgar Lomax Co., a money management firm in Alexandria, Va. Mr. Eley's new pick is V.F. Corp., a clothing maker in Wyomissing, Pa., best-known for its Wrangler and Rustler brand jeans and Jantzen sportswear.

But its jeans are the part of the business that's been struggling a bit, Mr. Eley says. V.F.'s three other lines -- sportswear, intimate apparel and occupational apparel -- are all "experiencing steady growth."

Steady earnings appeal keenly to Mr. Eley as the economy sputters. He doesn't necessarily expect a recession this year, but he is picking investments "on the basis that a recession could occur -- or could be under way."

In June, Standard & Poor's Corp. and Moody's Investors Service Inc. both said they were considering downgrading V.F.'s debt. S&P cited "manufacturing, marketing and distribution problems" in jeans and sportswear. Mr. Eley says the announcement "is a concern" but that the bad news has caused the stock to drop to a bargain level.

As for those jeans, Mr. Eley says V.F. has lost some market share to competitors but has taken steps to bolster profitability by closing some plants and cutting costs. This "ought to keep the line profitable," he says. "My team is projecting earnings of $2.50 a share as the worst possible case."

V.F. closed at $26, up 75 cents Friday. Mr. Eley would pay as much as $28 for it, with a target price of $38. He thinks the target might be hit within six months "if everything falls into place."

The other two contestants this month, Alan Bond and Jonathan Steinberg, would have been winners under the previous rules.

Mr. Bond, equity portfolio manager at W.R. Lazard & Co. in New York, likes Nike Inc. The Beaverton, Ore., athletic shoe and apparel maker sold Friday for $80, up $2, but Mr. Bond thinks a fair target is $95 within a year.

He says Nike is doing very well with "cross training" shoes (usable for more than one sport) and hiking shoes and is increasing its percentage of revenue derived from clothing other than footwear. Though the stock has performed well, it isn't too high-priced, he says, selling for 14 times the past 12 months' per-share earnings.

As a kicker, Mr. Bond says Nike's board is likely to approve a stock split and listing on the New York Stock Exchange, two steps that may help the stock attract a wider group of buyers.

Mr. Steinberg, publisher and editor of Individual Investor magazine, recommends Iomega Corp., a South Roy, Utah, maker of computer equipment, especially disk drives. The drives, used in some IBM and Apple Macintosh computers, are "very cheap, very reliable," Mr. Steinberg says.

"This was a company founded in 1980. At one time it was a high-flyer," Mr. Steinberg says. "It stumbled in 1986 and 1987 {overestimating demand and having to write off excess inventories}. The stock was hammered. It has not received much attention since then."

Mr. Steinberg says Iomega quietly turned profitable in 1988 and 1989 and showed dramatic growth in the first quarter, with earnings up 57% over the previous year's first quarter as sales grew 17%. The balance sheet is strong, and the stock -- which closed at $4.9375, up 6.25 cents Friday -- trades at only about six times earnings.

"The average {price-earnings} multiple for companies like this is 13," Mr. Steinberg says. If Iomega even comes close to that, he says, "it's an $8 stock."

Through the end of the year, the pros' picks will compete against a dartboard portfolio consisting of Clark Equipment Co., Great Western Financial Corp., Northrop Corp. and Wells Fargo Co.
