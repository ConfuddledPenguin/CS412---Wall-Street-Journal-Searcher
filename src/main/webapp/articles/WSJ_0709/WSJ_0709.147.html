U.S. Says Satellites May Be Launched By Soviet Rockets

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/09/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush will permit, for the first time, U.S. commercial satellites to be launched by Soviet rockets, Secretary of State James Baker said. Speaking on ABC-News' "This Week" program yesterday, Mr. Baker said he didn't know when the first American satellite would be carried by a Soviet rocket, which would be launched from Australia, but that the president had decided "an initial launch can go forward." The president's decision was first reported by the New York Times.

American rocket companies have opposed the competition that allowing U.S. satellites on Soviet rockets would bring. But the Soviets offer services to U.S. satellite companies at prices the companies feel they should take advantage of to stay competitive.

Meanwhile, USBI Co., a unit of United Technologies Corp., reportedly is in line to receive an export license from the State Department to operate the Australian space port. Andrea Shea-King, a USBI spokeswoman, confirmed yesterday that United Technologies has been involved in studying and planning the initiative for about three years. But she added that United Technologies has yet to be notified about the license approval.

USBI, with operations in Cape Canaveral, Fla., and Huntsville, Ala., is a major contractor to NASA for the Space Shuttle program. Mrs. Shea-King said United Technologies was one of three aerospace giants invited by the Australians to submit proposals, although she added that she didn't know the identity of the rivals. "To my knowledge, we are the only entity in the running now," she said.

While declining to provide details, the spokeswoman said any forthcoming contract to serve as the program manager in Australia would "have a significant financial" impact for the company.
