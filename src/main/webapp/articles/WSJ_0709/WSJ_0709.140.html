Economic Summit (A Special Report): Getting Ready --- For Houston, a Chance to Prove It's Hot

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 07/09/90

HOUSTON -- This city has been preparing, too -- but not just for a cadre of world leaders and their erudite aides. Still smarting from the collapse of its oil and real-estate industries in the mid-1980s, America's fourth-largest city is determined to prove it is alive and well. So, in the belief that as many as 4,000 media representatives will flock to this week's summit, Houston has prepared a public-relations extravaganza.

Months ago, a host committee of 270 top civic, business and cultural leaders marshaled time, services and products donated by 10,000 volunteers and prepared to scrub the city's image. An extraordinary 10-week cleanup drive removed two million pounds of trash from targeted streets and neighborhoods. Trees were planted, and special red-and-white flowers were added along some routes leading to the summit site, Rice University. (Those colors look best on TV, experts decided.)

A "Media Fest" party has been organized, and the night before sessions begin, favorite son President Bush will play host to summit luminaries -- and, of course, the press -- at a barbecue and rodeo.

The Houston Friendly Committee even devised a special summit slogan "as a way of encouraging Houstonians to be friendly to . . . the news media and other dignitaries." The phrase? "Houston's Hot."

Which it is. World leaders and reporters willing to venture outdoors may encounter daytime temperatures well above 90 degrees and morning humidity around 90%. But civic boosters insist that's dandy, providing the opening for well-coached citizens to assure reporters and other visitors they encounter that Houston is also "hot" when it comes to its business climate, port facility, energy industry, and aerospace, medical and convention facilities. (Houstonians got a pre-summit version of the campaign.)

"We will convert that negative {the climate} to not only a positive but to a focused sales opportunity," promised oil man George Strake, co-chairman of the host committee, who announced the slogan on a June day when temperature had already hit 95 degrees.
