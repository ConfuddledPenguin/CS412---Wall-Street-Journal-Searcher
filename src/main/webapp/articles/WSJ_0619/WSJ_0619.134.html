OTC Focus: Stocks Post Fourth-Biggest Loss of Year, Tumbling With Exchange-Listed Shares

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 06/19/90

Over-the-counter stocks posted their fourth-largest loss this year as they tumbled with exchange-listed shares. The Nasdaq Composite Index fell 6.76, or 1.45%, to 460.79, compared with a 1.83% drop in the Dow Jones Industrial Average. That's the largest decline since a 7.39-point drop Jan. 30. Declining shares more than doubled advancing stocks, 1,424 to 582, as volume slipped to 157.6 million shares from 168.5 million shares Friday.

The day's fall in share prices was orderly and marked by a lack of buyers, rather than a rush of sellers, traders agreed. "There was no panic selling," said E.E. "Buzzy" Geduld, president of Herzog, Heine, Geduld Inc., a New York dealer for other market professionals. Investors sold stocks across-the-board and when they did buy, "they were very particular," Mr. Geduld said.

Professional dealers were active in the market, according to William A. Rothe, head of OTC trading at Alex. Brown & Sons. "The dealers are selling to take advantage of the momentum," Mr. Rothe suggested, adding that other investors seemed to be cautious about their next moves.

At least one OTC trader said he found some signs of encouragement in yesterday's fall. "I think the fact that more people are bearish is bullish for the market," said Richard Bruno, director of OTC trading for PaineWebber. Mr. Bruno added that he doesn't expect the market's decline to be prolonged.

Software Toolworks was the day's most active stock, dropping 4 7/8 to 19 on nearly 3.5 million shares. A negative article in Barron's flagged a number of concerns about the high-flying stock.

The growing Chatsworth, Calif., software company has seen its stock price triple in the first five months of 1990, after tripling in 1989 from a low of $2.50 a share, amid the prospect the company will sell Nintendo-related products. Barron's cited the maturation and increasing competitiveness of the Nintendo market; Software Toolwork's lackluster outlook; insiders' plans to cash out nearly 30% of their holdings through a secondary offering; the company's history of growing by acquisition rather than through operating acumen, and its possible use of aggressive accounting practices.

However, Piper, Jaffray & Hopwood analyst Robert F. Kleiber advised his clients that the article painted an "unfair picture" of the company and recommended investors buy the stock on weakness, according to a detailed rebuttal of the Barron's piece he sent to clients.

Potential mergers or buy-outs sparked surges in Healthco International, which jumped 2 1/2 to 18, and Fiserv, which rose 2 7/8 to 29 3/8.

After hiring an investment banker last month, Healthco, a Boston distributor of dental supplies and equipment, said its board decided to explore all possible alternatives, including a possible merger or acquisition. Healthco also said there have been recent expressions of interest in a transaction involving the company, but didn't elaborate. The company scheduled a shareholders meeting for Sept. 6.

Last month, Gemini Partners L.P., which holds a 9.96% stake in Healthco, said it intended to seek control of the company by nominating a slate of candidates to replace the current board.

Fiserv, a Milwaukee provider of data processing services, said it has been approached by a company it wouldn't identify about a possible corporate combination. The two companies had preliminary discussions, but Fiserv said it couldn't predict the outcome of the talks.

American Steel & Wire was among the day's percentage gainers with a one-point gain to 7 1/4. Friday afternoon, the Cuyahoga Heights, Ohio, maker of steel rods and wire declared a special cash dividend of $3.25 a share, payable June 29. The company also said it closed on $95 million of new credit facilities.

On the other hand, a number of issues were pummeled after companies reported negative earnings news.

Silk Greenhouse slid 1 1/8, or 34.6%, to a new 52-week low of 2 1/8 following a series of adverse news. The Tampa, Fla., operator of artificial flower stores reported a loss of 60 cents a share for the first quarter ended April 30, down from net income of four cents a share a year earlier, reflecting "substantial" increases in expenses. To curb those costs, the company said it cut "several hundred" employees from its work force over the past few months.

Silk Greenhouse also announced that Chief Financial Officer John Stokely, who joined the company in May, resigned on June 8 and that two of its outside directors have resigned since April 30. No replacements were named. Chairman and President Jere Bradwell said salaries of certain senior management, including his own, had been "reduced significantly." Silk Greenhouse had traded as high as 10 1/8 this year and 24 1/4 within the past 52 weeks.

IFR Systems plunged 3 1/4 to 9 1/2, or 25.4%. The Wichita, Kan., maker of communications testing equipment predicted a decline in profitability for the fiscal year ending June 30, 1991. The company said fiscal 1990 earnings should be in line with estimates. However, next year's profits will be pressured by changes in the timing of deliveries under its Sincgars Army contract, higher production costs for initial deliveries under the contract, and an acceleration in research and development and related manufacturing expenses.

J&J Snack Foods tumbled 2 1/8 to 7 7/8, a 21% decline, after the Pennsauken, N.J., maker of frozen snack foods said late Friday it expects earnings for the third quarter ending June 30 will be lower than the 21 cents a share earned a year earlier, although sales will be higher. J&J said higher costs and disappointing retail supermarket sales have hurt earnings.

First Commerce dropped 2 1/8 to 19 after the New Orleans-based bank holding company said late Friday it expects earnings for the second quarter to be 35 cents to 40 cents a share, down from 58 cents a share a year earlier. The bank also said it expects provisions for loan losses will be about $12 million for the second quarter, up from $8 million in the first quarter and $5 million in the year-earlier period.

CSP fell 1 to 5 5/8 after the Billerica, Mass., maker of array processors and related computer software and hardware processors reported net income for the third quarter ended May 31 of a penny a share, down from eight cents a share a year earlier.

Multibank Financial eased 3/4 to 11 1/4 after the Dedham, Mass., commercial bank said late Friday that about $30 million of additions to its loan-loss reserves for the second quarter ending in June will result in a loss of $11 million for the second quarter and a loss of $8.1 million for the first six months of 1990. Multibank reported net income of $7.4 million, or 79 cents a share, in the year-earlier quarter and of $14.1 million, or $1.51 a share, for the first six months of 1989.
