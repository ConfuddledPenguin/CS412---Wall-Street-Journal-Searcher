Monday's Markets: Dow Falls 53.71 Points As Buying Pace Wanes

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/19/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices plunged in thin trading, suggesting to some analysts that the market has sopped up as much cash as portfolio managers are willing to commit before the quarter ends. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 53.71 points to close at 2882.18. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices fell three-eighths of a point, or $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount, in weak trading. The dollar sagged against most major currencies amid rumors of a European move to let the dollar weaken.

Institutional investors weren't big stock sellers, but the steep drop suggested investors are less enthralled with certain assumptions that have helped drive the market to records -- particularly expectations of improving corporate earnings and falling interest rates.

"The amount and intensity of selling did not warrant a 50-point decline," says market strategist Laszlo Birinyi, of Birinyi Associates Inc. By his calculations, only about $21.2 million flowed out of the New York Stock Exchange in trades of less than 10,000 shares, which Mr. Birinyi doesn't consider a strong signal of an impending market decline. Selling pressure didn't push prices lower so much as did the absence of buyers, he says.

One unabashedly bearish market strategist, Abby Joseph Cohen of Barclays de Zoete Wedd Research, isn't surprised the buyers stayed home. "People simply have run out of reasons to buy," she says.

But while Mrs. Cohen thinks the drop is merely a foretaste of as much as a 25% drop in stock prices, Mr. Birinyi sees the market making no major moves either up or down for a while; but then it will head higher on the next piece of good news.

To detect changes in the direction of the market, Mr. Birinyi watches money flow in and out of the Big Board. He focuses on the dollar amounts of "non-block" trades under 10,000 shares each. He thinks such trades tend to set prices, making them a more accurate measure of price moves.

After Jan. 2 of this year, for example, when the Dow Jones Industrial Average was heading lower, the net money flow into the market was flat at first and then turned sharply higher just before the market itself started rising. The money inflows, and the industrials, have been rising fairly steadily ever since.

The time to watch for a market downturn, Mr. Birinyi says, is when the Dow Jones industrials keep rising while money starts flowing out of the market. That hasn't happened yet, he says.

James Solloway, chief economist at Argus Research, saw the decline as "some residual disappointments to last week's news." Hopes that the Federal Reserve Board may ease interest rates soon are fading because of strength in the industrial sector, he says, and some companies signaled disappointing earnings.

The drop in bond prices also appeared to be continuing fallout from this past week's economic news. The dollar fell on unconfirmed rumors that some European central banks wanted to abandon a 1987 stabilization agreement.

In major market action:

Stock prices plunged in anemic trading of 133.5 million shares on the Big Board.

Bond prices fell. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 8.46%.

The dollar fell. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6758 marks and 153.66 yen, compared with 1.6875 marks and 153.90 yen Friday.
