Foreign Exchange: Technical Factors Depress the Dollar, Which Gains Only Against Canada Unit

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/19/90

NEW YORK -- Technical factors achieved what economic fundamentals haven't for weeks now: The dollar was pushed out of its narrow trading bands against the mark. The U.S. currency ended lower against all other major currencies except the Canadian dollar. In trading late in the morning in New York, the dollar on its way down crossed a line at 1.6830 marks where complex charts told currency traders to sell it. Traders gave differing reasons for the dollar's move to that point. Some cited manipulations of a speculator, others pointed to the preceding drop in U.S. stock prices, while still others pointed to rumors suggesting the European Monetary System will soon be realigned and that European central bankers want to dump the Louvre Accord.

What happened afterward isn't in doubt.

"It dropped like a stone," said Robert Ryan, foreign exchange manager for Bank of New York. "People got caught the wrong way, and they panicked."

As they did so, the currency price passed levels -- at 1.6770 marks, for instance -- where stop-loss orders to sell dollar were tripped.

In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.6758 marks, down from 1.6875 marks late Friday in New York. The U.S. currency also was changing hands at 153.66 yen, down from 153.90 yen. Sterling was trading at $1.7145, up from $1.7095.

In Tokyo Tuesday, the dollar was trading at 153.70 yen soon after opening at 153.45 yen; it closed Monday in Tokyo at 153.88 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6755 marks, down from Monday's Tokyo close of 1.6855 marks.

Monday's plunge shook many dealers who had grown complacent during the quiet morning hours. After some volatility on Friday, said Alexis Egan, corporate foreign exchange trader for Banque Indosuez, "people were comfortable with the dollar settling back into its range {of 1.68 to 1.70 marks}. A big move was the last thing people expected, and it took everyone by surprise."

It also left participants looking for a "story" -- what started it all. "People not willing to accept technical answers look around for fundamentals," said David Gilmore, foreign exchange analyst for McCarthy, Crisanti & Maffei.

Mr. Gilmore said he doubted fundamentals provoked the plunge, but he wouldn't discount another explanation. A speculative player, knowing the market wanted to buy dollars until 1.6810-30 marks and sell dollars thereafter, may have decided to take advantage of the thin, non-aggressive dealings to swing the price there by selling large amounts of dollars, he said.

"So they go for it," Mr. Gilmore said. "They don't need a story if they can get {the dollar} through support, and there's nothing underneath it."

The most likely fundamental to have precipitated the decline is a late morning decline on the New York Stock Exchange. The Dow Jones industrial average, which was down 20 points in late morning trading, ended 53.71 points lower.

The decline in the Dow contributed to "a soft sense that things are going wrong {in the U.S.}," suggested Mr. Ryan of Bank of New York.

With recent indicators suggesting a slowing U.S. economy, dollar traders have recently begun to take more seriously such worries as higher estimates of the cost of the savings-and-loan bailout and missed debt payments Friday by Donald Trump.

But Banque Indosuez's Ms. Egan, a self-described "chart person," said she doubts fundamentals played a role in yesterday's retreat. "There wasn't a tremendous amount of interest in pushing the dollar lower," Ms. Egan said, noting that the support it found at 1.6720-30 marks was only "soft."

The dollar still could fall below its bottom yesterday in the days ahead, Ms. Egan said. The market is technically poised for a dollar rebound after the decline, she said -- "If everyone is short dollar, you can't go shorter" -- but the fundamentals for the dollar are mixed.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $349.30 an ounce, down 20 cents. Estimated volume was a light two million ounces.

In Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $348.15 an ounce.
