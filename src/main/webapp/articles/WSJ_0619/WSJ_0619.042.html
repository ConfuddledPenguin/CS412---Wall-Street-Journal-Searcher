Prudential-Bache Canada Unit Plans To Sell Retail Line

  • Author: Suzanne McGee
  • Date: 06/19/90

TORONTO -- Prudential-Bache Securities Canada Ltd. said it agreed to sell its retail brokerage business to Burns Fry Ltd., becoming the second U.S.-owned investment dealer this year to retreat from the Canadian securities market. The Canadian unit of Prudential Insurance Co. of America said it expects to shift most of its other Canadian operations, including its corporate finance, institutional sales and capital markets divisions, to New York.

"Forecasts showed us that the retail business {in Canada} will not become robust until at least this time next year," said George McGough, president and chief executive of Prudential-Bache Canada. Terms of the sale to Burns Fry weren't disclosed.

In the three years that Prudential-Bache Canada has operated as a full-service broker, it hasn't reported a profit. Exact losses haven't been disclosed but a company executive said that even if retail activity picked up next year, it would take until the end of 1992 for the company to make "a real profit."

Mr. McGough said no decisions have been made on the future of the Canadian unit, which will retain only about 35 of its 700 employees.

About 400 of the employees will be offered positions with Burns Fry, all of them retail brokers and support staff. About 200 others will be laid off. Burns Fry executives said they will interview some Prudential-Bache Canada traders and research analysts for other positions.

In January, Merrill Lynch Canada Inc., then the largest U.S. investment dealer in Canada, agreed to sell its retail brokerage operations to Toronto-based Wood Gundy Inc. for an undisclosed sum, citing the closer links between banks and brokerage firms as a reason for their move.

Prudential-Bache Canada executives didn't liken their decision to that of Merrill Lynch Canada, a unit of Merrill Lynch & Co. "This was a purely business move," Mr. McGough said.

Canada's retail brokerage business still hasn't recovered from the 1987 stock market crash. Only one-third of Toronto Stock Exchange member firms reported profits for the first quarter of 1990, the exchange said last week.

Michael Fusco, president of Dean Witter Reynolds Canada Inc., now the only U.S. securities firm offering retail services in Canada, said he sees Canada as continuing to offer many opportunities for companies with a clear strategic focus.

"We're purely and simply a retail firm, with only a small involvement in capital markets and other areas," Mr. Fusco said. He declined to say whether Dean Witter Canada, which also has reported losses, will show a profit this year. But he said the firm's results to date are "on plan." Dean Witter is a unit of Sears Roebuck & Co.

Burns Fry executives said they aren't worried about the effects of the current slump on retail brokers. "In five to 10 years, there is no area of the industry that should grow faster than individual-client services," said Terry Cooper, executive vice president of the Toronto-based investment dealer.

The Prudential-Bache transaction will put Burns Fry, which is 30%-owned by Security Pacific Corp. of Seattle, into the ranks of Canada's largest retail investment dealers. Burns Fry said it will have at least 1,900 employees, including 500 retail brokers, following the acquisition of Prudential-Bache Canada's operations.

In New York, Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. Chairman George Ball told employees in an internal memo that the firm's revenue, based on first-quarter results, project to $1.27 billion for 1990, 11% above 1989 revenue of $1.14 billion, when the firm sustained a net loss of about $51 million.

Revenues are rising partly because of an influx of brokers after Prudential-Bache's takeover of Thomson McKinnon Securities Inc.'s brokerage network last summer. However, revenue per broker is down from $223,986 last year to a pace of $202,232 for 1990, apparently reflecting lower production from the McKinnon brokers, the memo states.
