Economy: Curbs Relaxed For Exporters Of Technology --- U.S. Concerns Won't Need Licenses for Shipments Beginning in Early July

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 06/19/90

WASHINGTON -- The Commerce Department announced that starting in early July it will allow U.S. exporters to ship all but the most sensitive high-technology goods to major U.S. allies without first obtaining licenses. Dennis Kloske, undersecretary of commerce for export administration, said the change will obviate 20,000 export licenses a year and help speed up deliveries of $30 billion in annual shipments to America's best high-tech customers. "This means giving U.S. businesses an even better chance to compete in the international marketplace," he said.

The new rule will free exports of most computers, semiconductor-manufacturing equipment, precision instruments, telecommunications equipment and other high-technology products from licensing requirements.

The department said it will maintain existing licensing requirements for shipments of some critical items, such as supercomputers, X-ray lithography systems, certain underwater sensing equipment and cryptographic devices. The new rule, nonetheless, brings the U.S. and the 16 affected allied countries a step closer to creating what their authorities call a "license-free zone" within their borders. All the countries belong to the Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls, which oversees joint efforts to keep sensitive technology out of the hands of potential adversaries.

The relaxation in licensing requirements is designed to eliminate a disadvantage U.S. exporters face in trade within Cocom; currently, they have to plow through more paper work than their competitors in Japan or Western Europe.

The rule doesn't eliminate all paper work; exporters still will be required to maintain some records in case authorities decide they need it. But the department said it might end even the remaining restrictions within a year if all the Cocom members agree to raise their standards for enforcing export controls to a common level. Mr. Kloske said Cocom has set an April 30, 1991, deadline for completing this task.

The Commerce Department action comes on the heels of Cocom's decision earlier this month to release previously controlled Western technology to the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe. Despite that relaxation, the U.S. is trying to get the other Cocom members to agree to uniform licensing requirements for shipping the most strategically sensitive goods among Cocom members. The Commerce Department commended Japan, Canada, Norway and Australia for agreeing to follow the U.S. lead.

The U.S. still requires licenses for shipments of certain items even to Cocom destinations because they are considered high-risk targets for illegal acquisition by the Soviet Union or by Third World countries with clandestine programs to build nuclear devices or ballistic missiles. Moreover, the department said it will maintain existing controls on shipments of chemical and biological weapons. Also unchanged is the current U.S. practice of requiring buyers in other Cocom countries to apply for re-export licenses before shipping licensed U.S. products to other countries.
