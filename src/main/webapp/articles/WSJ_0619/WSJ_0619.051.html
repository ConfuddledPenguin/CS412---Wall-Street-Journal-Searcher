African Nations Urged to Stick To Commodities

  • Author: Neil Behrmann Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/19/90

LONDON -- African nations should concentrate on production of commodities rather than industrialize, a United Nations study said. Reversing previous policy recommendations that advised stricken African countries to reduce their dependence on commodities, the report said it would cost billions of dollars and be "extremely difficult" for the countries to diversify into manufacturing.

"We studied the problem for a year, and despite world commodity price trends there just isn't any alternative," said Malcolm Fraser, a former Australian prime minister who chaired the UN Secretary-General's Expert Group on African Commodities.

The study said African nations "must use commodity production and trade as a motor for expansion." Commodity production and distribution should be privatized or decentralized, it said, adding that local and foreign investment would improve transport, storage, agricultural research and education facilities.

Producers would help counter commodity gluts by allowing local prices to fluctuate in line with international quotes and by cooperating with other African nations and consumers, the report said. Meanwhile, the U.S. and European countries should reduce barriers to African produce, Mr. Fraser added.

Africa's population will grow to about 740 million in 10 years from 550 million at present, the report said. Yet debts have surged to about $220 billion from $108 billion in 1980 and economic growth has fallen. Africa is more dependent on commodities as export income than any other region, the report said, and raw materials normally account for about 90% of the total.

In 1988, commodities earned $18 billion for Africa, the study said, but adjusted for inflation that was 26% lower than in 1980 and 35% below 1970. The decline reflects lower price levels and a much smaller international market share.
