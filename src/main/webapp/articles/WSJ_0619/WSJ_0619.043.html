They Better Send In the Clowns, If Anyone Can Still Find Them

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 06/19/90

If Mikhail Gorbachev thinks he's on a tightrope trying to keep the Soviet Union together, he should try uniting the Great Circus BimBom From Russia. About 10 of the Soviet circus's aerialists, known as the Flying Stankeevs, are in Las Vegas, Nev., at the Landmark Hotel. Thirty-plus acrobats are fishing and swimming at a West Virginia ski resort, while more than 60 illusionists and musicians are holed up at an Atlanta Travelodge motel. Meanwhile, the circus's 13 horses, 12 soccer-playing Boxer dogs, five bears, five chimps, three lions and a cart-pulling goose are in New York.

The supposed two-year U.S. goodwill tour fell apart in April, when Kuwaiti financial backers did a disappearing act following an opening performance in Hershey, Pa. It's been a zoo ever since.

Unpaid bus drivers and truckers quit but kept equipment as ransom. The broke BimBom was evicted from a handful of motels. And now promoters and Americans who have befriended the troupe can't agree on getting the show on the road.

Andrew Truman, a circus tour director, says a two-year contract has been lined up in Las Vegas, which is why the aerialists are there. But Laurie McLeod, a circus truck driver in Atlanta, where the group split up, says the performers there, who are wary about the Las Vegas arrangements, are adamant about not going to Nevada. Instead, they're trying to work out a contract, possibly in Kentucky.

Mr. Truman says the Atlanta contingent has no choice: "It's Vegas or Moscow." He says the circus's Russian sponsor, who controls the terms of the troupe's American stay, has an agreement with Nevada investors to begin performances in July, albeit under a different name: the Great Russian Circus. Evidently the Nevada investors decided to drop BimBom -- the name of a Russian entertainer -- after clowning radio stations called it the Bimbo circus.

Meanwhile, the West Virginia performers have been told nearly daily by the Nevada group to have their bags packed for Las Vegas.

"They were supposed to leave on four different occasions. Something always comes up," says Steven Drumheller, marketing director for the Silver Creek Ski Resort, who heard of their plight and invited circus members to West Virginia.

He doesn't mind, he says, but the lodge is running out of donated food. And feeding people nicknamed "Grizzly" costs more than peanuts.
