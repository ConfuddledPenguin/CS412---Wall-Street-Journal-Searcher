Commodities: Corn and Soybean Futures Contract Prices Spurt In Wake of Heavy Rains in Midwest at Weekend

  • Author: Scott Kilman
  • Date: 06/19/90

CHICAGO -- Prices of corn and soybean futures contracts rose in the wake of heavy weekend rains in the Midwest. Analysts said the rains prevented farmers from completing their rain-delayed planting of the soybean crop. The rains also washed out some fields and further soaked already waterlogged corn fields.

The rally follows the steep price declines that occurred late last week, which were brought on by profit-taking. On Thursday and Friday, the prices of some soybean futures contracts dropped a total of about 20 cents a bushel.

In trading at the Chicago Board of Trade yesterday, the soybean contract for July delivery rose 10 cents a bushel to settle at $5.96 a bushel.

The price of the corn contract for July delivery rose 3.5 cents a bushel to settle at $2.865 a bushel.

Today, traders will study government reports released late yesterday that showed that planting of the soybean crop is only 79% complete nationwide. Usually, soybean planting is 88% finished by this time of the year. Corn planting wraps up around the first of June.

Robert Lekberg, an analyst at Farmers Grain & Livestock Corp., Chicago, said the Agriculture Department's reports generally fell in line with analysts' expectations.

Sowing of soybeans is lagging particularly in Illinois, where only 78% of the crop is planted. Normally, the soybean planting season is finished in that state by this time of the year.

Because of planting delays caused by the unusually wet spring, most crop analysts have already shaved their original projections for a corn harvest exceeding 8.1 billion bushels. Estimates now range from 7.4 billion to 7.8 billion bushels.

Some traders have been expecting to see a sudden shift in the weather this summer to hot and dry conditions. But there isn't any sign of it yet. After the close of trading yesterday, the National Weather Service's six to 10-day forecast for the Midwest predicted below-normal temperatures and normal precipitation.

Prices of wheat futures contracts settled mixed. According to Victor Lespinasse, a floor broker with Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., Chicago, China is interested in buying 500,000 metric tons of subsidized U.S. wheat for shipment in October.

Under a program designed to boost U.S. grain exports, the Agriculture Department compensates exporters that ship U.S. wheat to certain countries such as China and the Soviet Union.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

ENERGY: The price of crude oil for delivery during July fell below the important psychological level of $16 a barrel, finishing yesterday at $15.94 as overproduction and hearty supply continue to plague that market. The price for the OPEC basket of crudes fell last week to $14.24, far below the $18 level cartel members had pledged to support at recent meetings. And there doesn't appear to be any signs that either Kuwait or the United Arab Emirates plans to reduce production to quotas assigned them. Both have for months flagrantly pumped far more than their alloted quotas. "Only Saudi Arabia {of the major producers in the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries} is holding back on their output," said Nauman Barakat, first vice president, futures, for Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc.

COPPER: Futures prices rose. The July contract ended with a gain of 2.10 cents a pound at $1.1390 after trading as high as $1.1540. Prices were initially strong on unconfirmed reports that the Ok Tedi mine in Papua New Guinea was experiencing labor difficulties. One analyst said there didn't appear to be any published reports but that talk of the labor problems circulated in London and then in New York. When the July price rose above $1.1250, fresh buying orders were triggered, and a quick rally developed to the high of the day. Also helping prices rise, the analyst said, were reports that Asarco Inc.'s negotiations with the union representing its workers had slowed over local issues. The current contract runs out June 30. The analyst said there was no known major risk of a strike taking place. Two other major U.S. mining companies with contracts that also end on June 30 have recently come to agreement, greatly minimizing the potential for any supply disruptions. Asarco also is expected to reach agreement soon. A spokesman for Asarco said the negotiations had been dwelling a little longer than expected on local issues but said he had no other comment.

ORANGE JUICE: Futures prices again declined. The July contract was off 3.20 cents a pound to $1.7630, for a two-day loss of 13.85 cents. Prices traded over a 9.70-cent range, as low as $1.70 and as high as $1.7970. At the low, the decline represented a two-day loss of 20.15 cents. An analyst said the selling continued from Friday, when a report circulated that Brazilian juice processors would cut their price. The report was later denied by the processors, but the selling continued yesterday mostly on a technical basis, the analyst said. However, he noted, good support developed at the $1.70 level, and the July contract recovered some of its losses.
