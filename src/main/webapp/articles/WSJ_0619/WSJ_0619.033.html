Letters to the Editor: All Good News Is Often Bad News

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/19/90

It was with sadness that I read your "Chilling Tale" (page one, May 7) about GE's disaster with the rotary compressor. It's tragic that such a valiant effort to upgrade manufacturing in the U.S. failed because of a layered corporate structure that told the boss what he wanted to hear. It also is astounding to learn that GE was the inventor of the rotary compressor for appliances and in 1986 "designed a revolutionary rotary compressor for refrigerators." When I joined Frigidaire in 1955, it had been using the rotary compressor exclusively in its "Fridges" for many, many years. In 1967 we brought the new RI compressor into production without a hiccup. This new unit had many technological advances.

In those days, as today, it was customary for appliance manufacturers to exchange factory visits. These exchanges of ideas and technology have been a significant factor in keeping America the world leader in major-appliance manufacturing. Where else can you buy a 21-cubic-foot Frost Proof Fridge for $687 when the first 1957 15-cubic-foot models sold for $700?

Hayward Thomas

Manager

Frigidaire Refrigerator and Compressor Plant,

1964 to 1970

Palos Verdes Estates, Calif.

---

Thank you for the fine article on General Electric. Having worked in both the refrigeration department and the corporate manufacturing staff of GE, I can say you caught the flavor of GE's operating style: Act as if there are no problems and do not, above all, give the boss any bad news.

This fiasco is, I am told by many GE graduates, a repeat of a very similar problem in the early 1950s. The parts of the compressor were different but the results were the same. Sometimes we Americans are so busy shooting ourselves in the foot that we don't have enough time for Japan-bashing.

Thomas S. Fiske

Arcadia, Calif.

---

We were surprised to read that in December 1987, Tecumseh Products Co. "had quit" the domestic refrigeration-compressor business. GE must have been equally surprised by this statement as we were an on-line supplier of compressors to its Decatur, Ala., plant at that time.

Throughout our history, the G.E. Motor Group has been a key vendor to Tecumseh Products and continues to supply us with motors for all types of compressors. Tecumseh is alive, well and working hard to satisfy industry demand for energy-efficient compressors.

Jack Duncan

Vice President Sales and Marketing

Tecumseh Products Co.

Tecumseh, Mich.

---

The GE refrigerator-compressor fiasco is a symptom of Jack Welch's management style. In the 27 years I worked at GE and at the countless technical meetings I attended, there was no mention of Reginald Jones or Ralph Cordiner when they were GE's CEOs. Decisions were made on technical and economic merits. Mr. Welch's name and his "vision" now predominate meetings, as if he were the guru of turbine-generators. No wonder the Louisville management did not have the courage to confront Mr. Welch with any negative news.

Mr. Welch's 1987 vision for Schenectady's turbine-generator business was emphasis on customer service while concurrently nuking the Turbine Technology Laboratory and hundreds of experienced engineers. These were the engineers and scientists who provided the product reliability, engineering, quality control, research and development, manufacturing support and true customer-service support to the business. We are now working feverishly to contain the damage caused by Mr. Welch's actions because no manager had the courage to tell him that his vision for the business was flawed.

Mark Markovitz

Turbine-Generator Engineer

Schenectady, N.Y.

---

Powder metal is not a new material as you implied. Powder metallurgy (P/M) parts have been used in appliances and automobiles for more than 50 years. The average passenger car contains about 21 pounds of P/M parts in critical applications in the automatic transmission and engine, a four-wheel-drive light truck about 30 pounds. Powder metallurgy is probably one of the fastest-growing sectors within the world-wide metalworking industry, because of unique parts properties and production efficiencies.

Our investigation of the circumstances surrounding the design of the new compressor leads us to believe that the real culprit was an unfortunate overall design and inadequate field testing. We also have learned that the lubricant failed at a low temperature, causing subsequent part failures. To the best of our knowledge, the same compressor, used in air conditioners, currently contains two powder metallurgy parts that are performing without any problems.

In the design and manufacture of any product, the proper selection, specifying and testing of any material are critical. In this particular application, an incorrect P/ M material in combination with the wrong lubricant seems to have caused the failure.

P. Ulf Gummeson

President

Metal Powder Industries Federation

Princeton, N.J.
