Justices Rule Against LTV In Pension Case --- U.S. Agency May Require Company to Reassume $2 Billion in Liabilities

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/19/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court ruled 8-1 that the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp. may require LTV Corp. to reassume liability for more than $2 billion in employee pension plans that the company stopped funding in 1987. The ruling strongly reaffirms the authority of the troubled pension agency, which is operating with a $1 billion deficit, to protect the federal pension-insurance program that it administers. An adverse high court ruling could have increased the deficit to $7.8 billion if other companies followed LTV's lead of filing for protection under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code and stopped funding of pension plans, agency officials warned Congress last week.

The ruling "does send a strong message to all companies," said James Lockhart, the agency's executive director, that they must fund plans adequately and not dump them, "unless there's a real need."

The ruling is also likely to impede LTV's efforts to complete a reorganization plan and emerge from Chapter 11. An LTV spokesman said the situation "still has not changed -- we couldn't afford the plans then, and we can't afford them today."

The high court decision, written by Justice Harry Blackmun, focused on the complex -- but not unusual -- situation in which a company stops funding pension plans, either because of a Chapter 11 filing or some other inability to keep up with the growing liability, and then institutes new pension plans to accommodate union demands.

When a company stops funding pension plans, the pension agency, which would become liable for the shortfall, has authority to terminate the plans so that the liability doesn't continue mounting.

But the agency has adopted a policy that prohibits companies from instituting what the agency calls "follow-on" plans that effectively reinstitute the same benefits in new plans after shifting the old liability to the agency. The agency views such plans as an abuse of the insurance program and responds by ordering companies to restore the old plans and the liability that goes with them.

That was the scenario that evolved between the agency and LTV. But agency officials have warned that they could face similar problems with other steel companies, and with companies in the auto and airline industries as well. Agency officials have declined to name the other companies. Mr. Lockhart said the agency is currently negotiating with "a handful of companies that want to terminate their pension plans" and that most "would have liked to have had some sort of follow-on plan."

Yesterday, agency general counsel Carol Connor Flowe said the high court ruling was "extraordinary" in how far it supported the agency, even ruling that it need only consider the policies of federal pension law and not of bankruptcy law in deciding whether to make companies liable for abandoned plans.

LTV filed for reorganization in federal bankruptcy court in 1986, partly because the three pension plans run for employees of its LTV Steel Co. subsidiary were underfunded by $2.3 billion, including $2.1 billion in benefits insured by the agency.

In January 1987, as LTV Steel continued to operate while in bankruptcy court, the agency terminated the three pension plans to avoid continued underfunding. Agency insurance assumed the liabilities for benefits owed under the plans.

A few months later, under pressure from the United Steelworkers of America, LTV instituted a program to provide retirement benefits similar to those in the terminated plans. The agency, however, viewed the new plan as an abuse of federal pension law and an attempt to transfer the liability from LTV to federal insurance. In September 1987, the agency ordered LTV to reassume liability and funding for the three original plans.

LTV challenged the order, and a federal district court ruled in June 1988 that the agency improperly ordered LTV to reassume the plans' operations. In May 1989, a federal appeals court in New York agreed that the agency exceeded its legal authority.

Yesterday, the Supreme Court reversed the appeals court ruling. Justice John Stevens dissented.

The agency's Mr. Lockhart said LTV has suggested it may try to terminate the three plans again, but the agency doesn't think the steelmaker can do that legally. Moreover, Mr. Lockhart said, "We're convinced that it has the viability to afford these plans." The agency will continue to insure the three plans in the event that problems arise, he said.

The pension agency said the ruling will benefit 60,000 LTV retirees and 40,000 active workers. Agency officials said LTV must assume liability both for the $2.1 billion and for hundreds of millions of dollars more that hasn't been paid since 1987.

In response to the Supreme Court decision, LTV issued only a terse statement saying it will continue searching for a solution that recognizes its inability to fund the plans. In practical terms, that means negotiating with the agency to seek partial funding.

Alternatively, the LTV spokesman said, the company could terminate the plans again. Still, he said, LTV is taking the position that negotiations will succeed and that the court decision "is an unfortunate bend in the road, but it's only a bend -- it's not a dead end."

LTV's creditors, meanwhile, took a less philosophical view. The ruling means the agency's claims will take precedence over theirs. "It comes as bad news for creditors, that's for sure," said Larry Handelsman, an attorney for the unsecured creditors' committee. Any funding of the pension plans, he said, "comes directly from the pockets of the creditors." (PBGC vs. LTV Corp.)
