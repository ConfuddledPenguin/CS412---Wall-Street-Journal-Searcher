World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Fall 161.60 Points as a Lack Of News Keeps the Investors Off Trading Floor

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/19/90

Tokyo stock prices finished lower in thin dealings as lack of any fresh news kept investors off the trading floor. Meanwhile, London stock prices finished near intraday minimums in dull trading, after a slow start and a poor early showing on Wall Street.

In Tokyo, after closing the morning session with a moderate gain, the Nikkei Index sagged toward the end of the day on a few rounds of minor selling by arbitragers, traders said. The Nikkei Index of 225 issues fell 161.60 points, closing at 32376.80.

"There was no incentive at all to trade," said Masami Okuma, a senior trader at UBS Phillips & Drew International. "All kinds of investors fully remained on the sidelines," he said.

While the first section lacked the energy to boost large-capital shares, investors, including institutions, continued to hunt for high-priced, small-capital shares with bright earnings prospects, traders said, pushing up stocks on the second section as well as those on the Osaka and Nagoya stock exchanges.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the Nikkei index fell 318.56 points to 32058.24.

On Monday, an estimated 300 million shares changed hands on the first section, down from 460.3 million shares Friday. Decliners outnumbered advancers 632-300, while 190 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section dropped 15.29 points, or 0.64%, to 2381.03.

The second section index gained 24.99 points, or 0.60%, to 4178.59. Volume in the section was estimated at 36 million shares, in line with 35.3 million shares Friday.

Trading volume on the first section has been constantly thinning, from more than one billion stocks traded May 28 to yesterday's 300 million shares.

"Basically it's all because of {domestic} interest rates," which haven't shown a sign of declining, said an official at Yamaichi Securities.

To many participants' disappointment, U.S. economic indicators disclosed Friday didn't help to provide any hint for direction of either U.S. interest rates or the dollar, traders said.

Another set of U.S. indicators is due later this week, including final first-quarter gross national product data on Thursday, but those reports are unlikely to be of much help in giving the currency market any direction, traders said. As a result, participants expect institutional investors to continue to hold off from active trading ahead of German monetary union, now two weeks away, the traders said.

A few sectors that received what little buying there was yesterday were pharmaceutical and environment protection-related stocks.

Pharmaceuticals gained ahead of the annual conference of the cancer academy beginning July 3.

Yoshihiro Nomura, an phamaceutical industrial analyst at Nomura Research Institute, said the market is moving on expectations of some new products and testing results scheduled to be introduced at the conference.

However, none of the products or results are at a stage to make sales or profitability forecasts, the analyst said.

Dainippon Pharmaceutical, which advanced strongly this past week on the potential earnings prospects of the company's anti-cancer agent continued to be bought yesterday. But active profit-taking in the afternoon pushed the stock down from its intraday high of 3460 yen ($22.47) to close at 3240 yen, up 30 yen from Friday's close.

Also heavily traded was Yamanouchi Pharmaceutical, which added 10 yen to 3040 yen, down from an intraday high of 3110 yen. Mochida rose 50 yen to 4000 yen, while Taisho gained 40 yen to 2390 yen.

Chiyoda Corp., which holds a strong market share in anti-air pollution equipment, gained 80 yen to 2660 yen. Toyo Seikan Kaisha advanced 240 yen to 4120 yen on the strong earning prospects for its beverage cans with safety fliptops.

London shares were marked down early in the session, as the beginning of the week, a lack of major corporate news, and the upcoming Royal Ascot race meeting kept fresh interest at a minimum.

Later in the session, London stocks continued to be pressured by a drop on Wall Street.

Furthermore, market professionals suggested that institutional equity investors were beginning to assess the negative influence that may arise from membership in the exchange-rate mechanism of the European Monetary System, after last week's euphoria over the beneficial consequences of entry.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index fell 21.8 points to 2370.5, slightly better than the intraday minimum of 2367.4. The index reached a high of 2394.5 near the start of trading.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index dropped 14 points to 1911.9. Volume was a mild 403.4 million shares after inclusion of a 120 million-share program trade of second-line stocks, little changed from Friday's 400.2 million shares.

Among issues, BAA, the airports management concern, rose 7 pence to 423 pence (12 cents to $7.23) after announcing a 29% rise in pretax profit for the year and the appointment of Jaguar's chairman, John Egan, as the company's new chief executive.

Dealers said the banking sector was generally weaker following news and speculation about banks' exposure to the troubled British & Commonwealth Holdings.

Barclays, which said it has made a #100 million provision against exposure to the financial services concern, fell 6 pence to 387 pence, Midland dropped 3 pence to 297 pence, and National Westminster Bank shed 9 pence to 334 pence. Lloyds, which has said it is selling its business interest in Portugal to Banco Bilbao de Vizcaya, fell 2 pence to 287 pence.

There were also worries in the banking sector that some U.K. banks might be exposed to losses stemming from loans to Donald Trump, dealers said.

Elsewhere, Reuters declined 13 pence to #12.81 on profit-taking, after the stock advanced 7.6% this past week, dealers said. Additionally, along with the American Stock Exchange, the Chicago Board Options Exchange and the Cincinnati Stock Exchange, the British company announced a definitive agreement to develop a world-wide after-hours electronic system to trade listed equities, equity options and other products.

Meanwhile, prices closed higher in Frankfurt; lower in Paris, Amsterdam, Zurich and Madrid; mixed in Brussels and Stockholm; and unchanged in Milan. South African gold stocks closed marginally higher.

Also, stocks closed lower in Seoul, mixed in Wellington, and higher in Singapore, Sydney, Taipei and Manila. Hong Kong was closed.
