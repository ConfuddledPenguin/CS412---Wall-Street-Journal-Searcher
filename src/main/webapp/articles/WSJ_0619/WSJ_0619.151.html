Who's News: First Boston Head Of Junk Bonds, Others Reassigned

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 06/19/90

NEW YORK -- First Boston Corp. replaced the head of its junk-bond department and reassigned other executives amid a deep slump in its bond financing business. The big securities firm named managing director William R. Reid to head its junk-bond operations, succeeding David M. Malcolm, who was reassigned to head the firm's real estate group.

Several other First Boston executives were also moved out of the junk-bond group, which like most of Wall Street's departments specializing in high-yield, high-risk bonds, doesn't have much to do these days.

Wesley Jones, a managing director, will move to the new products group, according to people familiar with the firm. Andrew Fisher, a vice president, is going to the restructuring group. The moves are the latest signs of turmoil at First Boston, which has been hit hard by the slump in the $200 billion public junk-bond market.

The firm hasn't led a junk-bond underwriting so far this year. By contrast, in 1989's first half, it raised nearly $1.4 billion through junk issues, generating $45 million in fees, according to Securities Data Co. For all of 1989, First Boston ranked No. 6 among junk-bond underwriters, with an 8% market share. (Industrywide, Wall Street has raised just $1.1 billion from new junk-bond issues so far this year, a 93% drop from the year-earlier period.)

First Boston also has stumbled in refinancing some temporary "bridge" loans it made to help corporate clients finance takeovers. Earlier this year, the firm sold to CS Holding more than half of a "hung" $450 million bridge loan to Ohio Mattress Co. when First Boston couldn't easily sell junk bonds in the market to refinance the loan.

First Boston's parent, CS First Boston Inc., is 44.5%-owned by CS Holding, which owns the Swiss banking giant Credit Suisse. First Boston earned just $11 million last year.

The executive shuffling comes amid a slump in First Boston's overall bond-financing activities. This year through Friday, the firm slumped to No. 5 from No. 2 in debt underwriting, raising $12.4 billion through 61 issues for a 9.7% market share, according to Securities Data. Even with a $1 billion financing deal yesterday, that's still a 16% drop from the year-earlier level of $15.9 billion raised through 78 issues and a 12% market share.

Mr. Malcolm, who joined First Boston in 1979 from Kidder, Peabody & Co., declined through a spokesman to comment on his new post. "It's premature to talk about anything," the spokesman said. Mr. Malcolm, who will oversee the firm's 20-member real estate group, began his career at First Boston by launching the firm's leasing operations. He was named managing director in 1982 and has headed the junk-bond group since December 1984.

Mr. Malcolm, 43 years old, succeeds William Dickey, a managing director, who will launch a new real estate division in Los Angeles, the First Boston spokesman said. Mr. Dickey will report to Mr. Malcolm; Mr. Malcolm will report to Charles Murphy, co-head of investment banking.

Mr. Reid, 34, who joined First Boston's junk-bond department in 1985, was traveling and couldn't be reached for comment. He joined the firm in 1980 from the Stanford Graduate School of Business. After serving as an account officer, he was named a director in 1987 and a managing director in 1988.

Messrs. Jones, Fisher and Dickey couldn't be reached.

The recent moves aren't the only significant personnel changes at First Boston lately. Earlier this month, the firm lost David C. Batten, a managing director since 1978 who played a major role in building First Boston's capital markets business, to Blackstone Group.
