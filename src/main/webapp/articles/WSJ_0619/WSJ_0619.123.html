Credit Markets: Treasurys Lose More Ground as Anxiety Grows Over Prospect of Heavier Government Borrowing

  • Author: Craig Torres and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 06/19/90

NEW YORK -- Investors continued to back away from Treasury bonds, which fell about 3/8 point, or $3.75 for each $1,000 face amount. Traders said the sell-off in Treasurys was a continuation from Friday when bond prices fell sharply in reaction to stronger-than-expected May industrial production data.

Investors are expecting the U.S. government to step up its debt issuance this summer, and that's one reason why they're turning cautious on the market, said Joseph Liro, economist at S.G. Warburg & Co.

"For the third quarter, we're talking awesome numbers for Treasury borrowing," said Mr. Liro. "Counting Resolution Trust Corp., it looks like $65 to $70 billion for the third quarter alone," he said. The RTC is the government agency overseeing the bailout of the thrift industry.

Government demand for debt is growing due to expenses associated with the savings and loan bailout, and to weak corporate tax payments, Mr. Liro said. Next week, the first signs of swelling U.S. debt offerings could be seen in the Treasury market. Mr. Liro expects the Treasury to announce a record $11 billion 2-year note offering, and $8.5 billion 4-year note offering. Mr. Liro also expects the Treasury's weekly bill auctions to swell from about $17.2 currently to $18 billion sometime later in the third quarter.

The thrift bailout has created new uncertainty about the size of the government's credit demands, traders say. Expectations of higher Treasury-bill issuance have narrowed a traditional money market gauge known as the Treasury-Eurodollar or TED spread.

Typically, Eurodollars, or dollar deposits in banks abroad, trade at higher interest rates than Treasury bills because they are viewed as less secure. But lately, the spread between Eurodollars and Treasurys has narrowed as investors have anticpated higher Treasury-bill rates to accomodate larger federal borrowing.

The TED spread has narrowed to about 0.79 percentage point recently from its usual spread of about 1.2 percentage points to 1.5 percentage points, Mr. Liro notes. (The TED spread is based on trading in futures contracts for both Treasury bills and Eurodollars.)

What's ironic about the narrowing of this financial market indicator is that usually it widens in times of financial distress as investors flock to Treasury bills, commonly viewed as the safest of all investments. A so-called flight to quality in Treasury bills usually pushes their rates lower.

Lately, credit market traders have marvelled at investor complacency over the deteriorating credit quality of several banks, corporations, and, of course, Donald Trump.

"Normally, you would expect to see people buy Treasury bills because if Trump goes under it's an example of how overextended people are," said Steven Kordas, assistant vice president of proprietary trading at Carroll McEntee & McGinley. "People are very complacent because the government is bailing out everybody."

Junk Bonds

Overall, junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, ended the session 1/4 to 3/8 point lower.

Among the notable losers were junk bonds of Trump Taj Mahal Funding Inc. and related casino bonds issued by real estate developer Donald Trump.

Trump Taj's 14% first-mortgage bonds, due in 1998, dropped 2 1/8 points to end at 51 7/8 on the American Stock Exchange.

Trump Castle Funding Inc. 13.75% first-mortgage bonds, due in 1997, shed 2 points to end at 53.

But junk bond traders said activity in Trump debt was relatively light and calm. One reason might be is that holders of bonds on which Mr. Trump has defaulted are at the front of the creditors' line. Mr. Trump is in 10-day grace period to meet interest payments on the bonds.

"If he defaults, we own it; we'll take over the buildings," said Edward D'Alelio, managing director at Putnam High Yield Funds, which owns some of the Trump Castle bonds.

Meanwhile, junk bonds of Trump Plaza Funding Inc., considered the crown jewel of the Trump empire, continued to show strength.

Trump Plaza's 12 7/8% first-mortgage bonds, due in 1998, ended at 83, up a couple points from Friday's closing level. Trump Plaza is in the best financial shape of all Mr. Trump's casinos, traders said. Investors, they added, are speculating that Mr. Trump may be forced to sell Trump Plaza.

Treasury Securities

U.S. Treasury securities fell slightly in light trading.

Analysts and traders attributed the selling to a continued negative sentiment following Friday's bond market rout and jitters about upcoming supply. The 30-year bond fell 13/32 to 103 2/32 compared with 103 15/32 Friday. The yield rose to 8.46% from 8.42% late Friday. Prices on the 10-year note fell to 102 15/32, down from 102 23/32 on Friday. The yield on the 10-year rose to 8.48% from 8.45% Friday.

At the government's weekly Treasury bill auction, the average discount rate for three-month bills was set at 7.74%, while the average rate for six-month bills was set at 7.64%. The rates were slightly higher to unchanged from last week's auction when they were 7.73% and 7.64%, respectively.

Foreign Bonds

British government bonds, or gilts, ended sharply lower on a deterioration in the government's budget position and other data suggesting building inflationary pressures.

Among benchmarks, the Treasury's 11.75% issue due 2003/2007 was down one point at 101 5/8 to yield 11.51%. The Treasury 12% bond due 1995 was off 14/32 at 99 5/8 to yield 12.11%.

French Treasurys finished slightly lower after a quiet session.

The West German bond market was also quiet. The actively traded 7.75% 10-year note due February 2000 was quoted at 92.70 to 92.85, down slightly from Friday's levels.

Municipal Bonds

Lacking any impetus to move prices higher, tax-exempt issues ended 1/8 to 1/4 point lower in sluggish dealings.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 were off 1/8, at 98 1/4 to yield 7.33%.

Looking ahead on the municipal new-issue calendar, today Minnesota will sell $200 million of general-obligation bonds via competitive bid.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Prices of mortgage-backed securities ended with losses of 1/4 point or less, after barely moving from early levels as participants brace for an influx of new supply.

Among actively traded mortgage securities issues, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities for July delivery were down 7/32 at 99 2/32, while the 10% securites were down 1/4 at 101 14/ 32.

Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities for July delivery were off 5/32 at 98 11/32 and the 10% securities were down 1/8 at 100 13/32.

Fannie Mae July 9.5% securities were 5/32 lower, and the 10% securities were lower by 1/8 at 100 29/32.
