Trump Shuttle Plans to Cut Some Weekend Flights

  • Author: Jonathan Dahl
  • Date: 06/19/90

In a move signifying the continued weakness in the once lucrative Northeast shuttle market, the Trump Shuttle is quietly planning to cut more than 25% of its flights on Saturday and four other round-trips on Sunday. The change, which takes effect July 1, will likely inconvenience hundreds of travelers. The airline isn't planning any formal announcement, even though shuttle service has been operated on an hourly basis for years.

"I assume we'll have some people showing up for canceled flights," says Bruce Nobles, president of the Trump Shuttle. But Mr. Nobles said the airline may compensate these people with extra frequent-flier mileage points or some other bonus.

So far, Trump's arch rival in the shuttle market, Pan Am Corp., hasn't matched the change. Harris Herman, president of the Pan Am Shuttle, said he was considering some schedule adjustments, but added he didn't want to confuse the public. The airlines have traditionally matched each other's fare changes.

Together, the two carriers carry about four million passengers a year between New York and Washington and New York and Boston in what had been considered one of the industry's most prized routes. Fares are high -- $119 one-way -- and the passenger clientele is among the industry's richest.

But since acquiring the shuttle from Eastern Airlines last year for $365 million, real estate mogul Donald Trump has watched the carrier struggle -- primarily because business travel has slacked off in the Northeast. As reported, the Trump Shuttle lost at least $85 million since starting up. Much of the loss was from debt service, and the carrier earned over $2 million from operations last April, according to documents obtained by this newspaper. But Trump officials concede the carrier's passenger loads have been below expectations.

In response, the shuttle recently laid off about 70 employees and cut back a few frills, such as its concierge desk. Now it is planning to cancel four daily round-trips in both the Boston-New York and Washington-New York market on Saturday, as well as two other flights in each market Sunday. Those two days are easily the slowest ones for the shuttle and Mr. Nobles said he was trying to "match capacity with demand." He said the move wouldn't lead to more layoffs, because the carrier would use the planes for more charter flights.
