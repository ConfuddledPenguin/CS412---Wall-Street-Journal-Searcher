Who's News: Harcourt Appoints Finance Chief, Heads Of Publishing Firms

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/19/90

ORLANDO, Fla. -- Harcourt Brace Jovanovich Inc. named a new chief financial officer and new presidents for three of its publishing companies. Gary J. Arnold, senior vice president and secretary, said the moves are designed to get a "new team in place" for John S. Herrington, who was recently named chairman of the publishing and insurance concern, and Peter Jovanovich, who was recently named chairman of the board's executive committee.

Harcourt has been struggling under debt linked to its 1987 recapitalization. Along with the personnel changes, Harcourt said, it is consolidating its corporate finance department with its publishing finance department to cut costs.

Robert E. Evanson, 53 years old, was named chief financial officer. He succeeds John Berardi, who left the company to pursue outside opportunities. Mr. Evanson most recently directed Harcourt's university, professional and international publishing.

Marcus L. Boggs, 42, was named president of Harcourt's Academic Press Inc., an international publisher of books and scholarly journals for the natural, physical and social sciences, filling a vacancy. Mr. Boggs had been executive editor, humanities and social sciences, in Harcourt's college department.

Bill M. Barnett, 59, was named president of the college division of Harcourt's subsidiary, Holt, Rinehart & Winston Inc., in Fort Worth, Texas, succeeding David Dusthimer. Mr. Barnett had been president of Harcourt's college department.

Robert A. Pawlik, 49, was appointed president of Harcourt's college department, based in San Diego, which specializes in the fields of accounting, English, history, political science, computer science, mathematics and social sciences. Mr. Pawlik had been national sales manager in the college department.

Mr. Arnold, the senior vice president, said the company doesn't anticipate any changes in management at its other major publishing arm, the elementary and high-school textbook division. That division has done poorly in the past 18 months, after failing to meet changing demands from educators. Last year, Harcourt took an $88 million write-down to cover obsolete inventory in the division.
