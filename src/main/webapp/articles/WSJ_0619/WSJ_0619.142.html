Technology: Motorola Faces Ban on Selling Leading Chip

  • Author: Stephen Kreider Yoder
  • Date: 06/19/90

In a decision that poses a grave threat to Motorola Inc. and several U.S. computer makers, a federal judge in Texas has decided to impose an earlier ruling that ordered Motorola to stop selling its most successful computer chip. The decision harks back to a ruling made in March, in which a federal district judge in Austin, Texas, ordered Motorola to stop selling its 68030 chip because it infringed on patents held by Japan's Hitachi Ltd. The ruling also found Hitachi in violation of certain Motorola patents. The judge then issued a stay of the injunction, allowing Motorola and Hitachi to keep selling their chips.

The judge yesterday disclosed his intent to lift the stay, Motorola and Hitachi officials said. The judge hadn't yet signed the order yesterday but is scheduled to do so today, a Hitachi official said.

If enforced, the decision could be disastrous for Motorola, which would be forced immediately to stop selling its flagship 68030 microprocessor chip, used in powerful computers made by such companies as Apple Computer Inc. and Hewlett-Packard Co. The 68030 chip is the "brains" of many of Apple's Macintosh machines.

Motorola officials said in a written statement that it will file an emergency appeal with the U.S. Court of Appeals to stay the injunction. The ruling "might temporarily prevent the company from selling its market-leading 68030," the statement said. Motorola is "confident" that the appeals court will "promptly stay the lower court's decision, pending appeal."

A Hitachi official said that "Hitachi is continuing to meet {with Motorola} in an effort to reach a negotiated settlement" with Motorola. "We want to do all we can toward reaching a settlement," he said.

Hitachi has little to lose. The giant conglomerate, which requested the lifting of the stay, won't be hurt much by the injunction because it sells few of the chips in the U.S. that were found to be violating Motorola patents. "There will be very little effect on Hitachi," the Hitachi official said. In fact, many U.S. chip executives say Hitachi has the upper hand in any negotiations and may be using the injunction to get more favorable treatment from Motorola.

The ruling could also hurt Apple and others that use the chip, which only Motorola makes. Companies in March said they had several months' worth of stockpiles of the vital chip, but some warned Motorola that they would be angered by a cutoff of supply. An Apple spokesman said the company couldn't comment on yesterday's news until it had seen a copy of the formal ruling.

A Hewlett-Packard spokeswoman said the company wouldn't be affected because it has a license to use the Hitachi patent and to commission chip makers to make semiconductors for Hewlett-Packard that use the patent.

The ruling is the latest twist in an 18-month trans-Pacific patent battle, started by Motorola, in which Motorola and Hitachi have launched a barrage of suits and countersuits. The March ruling, in which the judge chided the companies for not agreeing out of court, backfired against Motorola, shocked the computer industry and even caused one user of the chip to guard its stockpiles with extra security guards.

Motorola also said yesterday that the U.S. Patent Office agreed last week to reexamine Hitachi's patent, which Motorola claims is invalid on the basis of a 1966 patent. The patent office has found that "a substantial new question about {the} Hitachi patent has been raised which has a direct bearing on the outcome of this case," the Motorola statement said.
