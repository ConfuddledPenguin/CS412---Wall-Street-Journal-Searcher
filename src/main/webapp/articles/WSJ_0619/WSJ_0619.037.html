Letters to the Editor: Listen, You Might Hear Something Good

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/19/90

Among the many commencement ceremonies Hodding Carter III has attended ("Commencement Speaking: Bloviating to the Bored and Boorish," Viewpoint, May 10), mine was not one of them. This is too bad for Mr. Carter, for if he had, his cynicism might be tempered just a bit. I graduated in May 1982 from Gonzaga University, a small Jesuit university in Spokane, Wash. The commencement address was given by William Bennett, then chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities. His address was a typical Bennett performance; very straightforward and to the point.

The point was common-sense advice on how to approach your first job. The overall theme was plunge yourself into your work, get to know every aspect of your job, get excited about even the most mundane tasks, and become a true asset to your company. It is based on the belief that good things happen to people who work hard for the sake of a company. This was well-timed advice that I've tried to follow throughout my career.

To Mr. Bennett's credit, he did not use the event as a platform to announce a new policy or political opinion. But rather, he used it simply to address the graduates. Perhaps if more speakers followed William Bennett's role, Hodding Carter would be less apt to scrap the entire tradition.

Jim Triggs

Edina, Minn.
