Bankers Trust's Finance Division Announces Layoffs

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 06/19/90

NEW YORK -- Bankers Trust New York Corp. said it laid off 75 employees in its flagship bank's corporate finance department, reflecting the slump in mergers and acquisitions. The layoffs, which represent about 10% of the corporate finance department's professional staff, cut across all lines of business and include not just New York, but also regional offices in Atlanta, Chicago, Houston and Los Angeles, said Ralph L. MacDonald Jr., managing director and head of the corporate finance department. He said the staff reductions will save about $10 million annually.

Additionally, Mr. MacDonald said Bankers Trust created two new units -- a corporate finance group and a banking and finance group -- to advise corporate clients on methods of restructuring and financing. The corporate finance group will act as advisers to senior corporate clients on such matters as strategic restructuring and mergers, while the banking and finance group concentrates on specific methods for financing corporate clients.

Mr. MacDonald said the changes and layoffs "recognize that the volume and form of corporate finance restructuring will be driven primarily by strategic rather than financial considerations in . . . the '90s." Although total volume of such transactions clearly has slowed, he said global competition will continue to force companies here and abroad to become more efficient.

"Financial institutions that will be successful in this new environment will be characterized by both leaner staffing and a greater emphasis on relationships with senior corporate managers," he said. He conceded that Bankers Trust hadn't done as much to cement relationships with corporate clients as it might have and that the reorganization is aimed at increasing the company's commitment to its corporate clients.

Bankers Trust isn't alone in trying to cope with reduced restructuring and merger activity. Most investment banks and money center banks that participated in the merger mania of the late 1980s, building expensive teams of talented dealmakers, now are struggling for pieces of a much smaller pie. According to Securities Data Co., the value of domestic mergers and acquisitions in the first quarter of 1990 totaled $49.7 billion, less than half the $102.6 billion in deals announced in the first quarter of 1989.
