Technology & Health: Novell Plans to Tighten Security Features To Protect Computer Users From Snoops

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 06/19/90

Novell Inc. plans to toughen the security features of its network software products, responding to growing concerns that personal computer networks are vulnerable to disruption by electronic intruders. The Provo, Utah, company already offers limited security features in its current networking products, which are widely used by industry. But it is starting to incorporate into Netware, its main software family, a more sophisticated approach to security called public-key encryption. The method enables many people to encrypt messages in such a way that only one person can decipher them. It also allows one person to send an encrypted message that numerous recipients can understand.

To improve the security features of its products, Novell said yesterday that on Friday it signed a license agreement covering public-key technology from RSA Data Security Inc., a closely held Redwood City, Calif., concern. RSA is generally believed to sell the most efficient public-key software, and it controls a key U.S. patent underlying its products.

"We considered doing this on our own . . . for about 10 minutes," said Richard King, Novell's vice president of software development. "There are some things you can roll your own on, but not this."

Mr. King said Novell would release its first product using public-key techniques by the end of July. The product, called Netware NameService, is designed to shorten the time it takes for personal-computer users to access central computers on the network where shared files and sensitive data are stored. Novell's products now require a user to punch in a new password for each server they wish to log onto. NameService will make a single password work for all the network's central computers.

Novell is the first important supplier of networking software to embrace public-key cryptography, which has been a controversial subject since its creation by a group of academic researchers in the late 1970s.

Because it can afford security against intrusion normally reserved for highly-sensitive government operations, public-key has been an irritant to the National Security Agency, which has complained that spread of public-key cryptography will make it harder for the agency to monitor secret transmissions. U.S. law allows the sale of public-key products but restricts their export.

Supporters say public-key can reduce the threat of computer network disruptions by limiting the spread of rogue programs, and making it possible for recipients of electronic messages to be sure they have received an authentic communication.

"Right now there's nothing in the way of privacy or authentication" for users of many PC networks, said Ralph Merkle, a researcher at Xerox Corp.

Nevertheless, some security specialists are skeptical about the commercial prospects for products using public-key techniques, such as the ones planned by Novell. "Significant customer demand for (improved security) has just not appeared yet," said Donn Parker, an electronic security consultant at SRI International in Menlo Park, Calif. Mr. Parker predicted it would take several years for public-key demand to mature.

Novell, however, hopes its aggressive stand on security will give its products an advantage. "It's a feature that will stand out," said Mr. King. "It shows people we're really concerned about the integrity of their systems."

While the use of public-key remains small compared with traditional "one-way" cryptography, it is catching on with high-technology firms. Last year, Digital Equipment Corp. signed a licensing deal with RSA Data and Lotus Development Corp. introduced a software package called Notes that partly relies on RSA Data's techniques.
