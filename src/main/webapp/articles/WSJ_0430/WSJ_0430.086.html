Politics & Policy: Pennsylvania's New Anti-Takeover Law Fuels Controversy, Faces Fight in Court

  • Author: Vindu P. Goel
  • Date: 04/30/90

Pennsylvania Gov. Robert P. Casey signed the nation's toughest anti-takeover statute on Friday, just in time to protect Armstrong World Industries Inc. against Canada's Belzberg family in the proxy fight at Armstrong's annual meeting this morning. Within minutes of the law's enactment, two lawsuits challenging its constitutionality were filed in U.S. District Court in Philadelphia, promising that the controversy over the statute's provisions may not be over for months or even years.

"This law fundamentally alters the relationship between the corporation and its shareholders," said Richard Greenfield, an attorney for the plaintiffs in one of the lawsuits. "Under this law, there is no way to attack corporate decisions."

The new law, first introduced at Armstrong's behest to fend off a possible bid by the Belzbergs, has four major provisions designed to dissuade a takeover.

Most immediate to the Armstrong-Belzberg conflict, the law would "disgorge" the profits of short-term shareholders who sell within 18 months after a failed attempt to "control" a company through a buy-out or proxy fight. The profits would be turned over to the target company. The law also withholds the voting rights of shareholders who control 20% or more of a company's stock until "disinterested" shareholders agree to restore their votes.

Another provision redefines the fiduciary duty of directors, allowing them to base decisions not only on shareholders' interests, but also on the interests of employees, customers, suppliers and other "stakeholders." Under the provision, directors would also be insulated from most lawsuits by aggrieved stakeholders. Finally, the law guarantees labor contracts and severance pay in the event of a takeover.

Many states, such as Indiana, New York, Massachusetts and Ohio, have some form of anti-takeover statute, and Pennsylvania last adopted such protections two years ago. The new law's backers, which include the state's business chamber and largest labor unions, say that it will further dissuade raiders from making runs at Pennsylvania companies and attract even more companies to the state. Currently, only about 4% of New York Stock Exchange firms are incorporated in Pennsylvania, compared to nearly 50% for neighboring Delaware.

But critics, including the Belzbergs, institutional investors, and the Securities and Exchange Commission, say that the new law so seriously infringes upon the rights of shareholders that it will drive them away from Pennsylvania. In any case, they argue, the law is probably unconstitutional.

Indeed, two individual Armstrong shareholders filed a suit against the company, its officers and state officials, alleging that the law diminishes the value of their holdings and deprives them of their constitutional rights. The Belzbergs, who own 11.5% of Armstrong, filed a similar lawsuit, seeking to block enforcement of the law, particularly with respect to its battle today for four seats on the board of Armstrong, a Lancaster, Pa., flooring and building-products concern.

The suits say the statute conflicts with federal securities laws, unduly burdens interstate commerce in shares, curtails free-speech rights of shareholders, interferes with contracts and violates property rights.

Legal scholars said the law is most vulnerable on interstate commerce and supremacy grounds. The Supreme Court has upheld the right of states to regulate internal corporate affairs. "But who gets the gain on a transaction between two non-Pennsylvania residents?" says Ronald Gilson, a Stanford University law professor. "That is a transaction many states would have an interest in," particularly for taxation purposes.

In addition, the voting restrictions appear to conflict with the Securities and Exchange Act of 1934, which regulates proxy contests.

But given the Supreme Court's recent support of control-share voting limits and merger restrictions, the outcome of the constitutional challenges is not clear, said Gregg Jarrell, former chief economist of the SEC and a finance professor at the University of Rochester. "The courts have to give the benefit of the doubt to the state," he said. "After all, the legislation passed almost unanimously."
