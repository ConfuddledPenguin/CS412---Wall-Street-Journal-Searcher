Another Large California Thrift Is Seized by U.S. --- S&L Unit of Financial Corp. Of Santa Barbara Calls Step `Complete Surprise'

  • Author: William Celis III
  • Date: 04/30/90

Federal regulators seized yet another large California thrift, Santa Barbara Savings & Loan Association, just weeks after the company's parent, Financial Corp. of Santa Barbara, completed a recapitalization that left it nominally solvent. Santa Barbara Savings is one of several of California's biggest S&Ls that have failed recently, or are in danger of failing, because of a second wave of problems brought on by ill-fated diversification either outside the state or outside traditional home-loan lending.

Columbia Savings & Loan Association of Beverly Hills and Valley Federal Savings & Loan of Van Nuys, for instance, currently are insolvent but haven't yet been seized; other thrifts, such as Far West Savings & Loan of Newport Beach, a unit of Far West Financial Corp. and Great American Bank of San Diego, have lost most of their tangible capital and are struggling to survive.

In an earlier round of problems, some of the state's biggest thrifts, including Lincoln Savings of Irvine and Gibraltar Savings of Beverly Hills, were taken over by regulators.

In contrast, about four or five other giant California thrifts are thriving, partly because their troubled competitors are being restricted in bidding for deposits or loans.

On Friday, the Financial Corp. thrift unit called its seizure by the Office of Thrift Supervision "a complete surprise." Just last week, the parent Financial Corp. reported first-quarter net income of $37.7 million, or $1.71 a share, including a $36.5 million extraordinary gain from a debt-for-equity swap.

Philip Brinkerhoff, Financial Corp.'s chairman and chief executive officer, called the debt swap "an incredible boon to our capital position," adding: "We will continue to aggressively pursue opportunities to increase capital, including capital infusion or sale."

But while the one-time gain eliminated a tangible-capital deficit, it still left the thrift unit with only about one-third of the capital required by regulators, and little or no operating profit. The parent, which has $4.7 billion of assets, incurred a loss of $60.3 million, or $10.01 a share, for all of 1989.

Moreover, the parent company recently let go its auditor, Deloitte & Touche, after the accounting firm refused to issue an opinion concerning the 1989 financial statement due to "material weakness in internal controls," according to a Financial Corp. news release.

Regulators are apt to move swiftly against an even nominally solvent S&L if they find evidence that the thrift has insufficient controls to monitor and stanch losses. The OTS already had prohibited Santa Barbara Savings from making new loans, an order considered by some regulators to be tantamount to a death sentence. Regulators were unavailable for comment over the weekend.

In composite trading Friday on the New York Stock Exchange, Financial Corp. closed at 25 cents a share, off 3.125 cents.

Separately, Resolution Trust Corp., created under the thrift-bailout bill to manage failed institutions, said it approved the sale of First Federal Savings & Loan in Bakersfield, Calif., for $1.5 million to Bank of America National Trust and Savings Association, a unit of BankAmerica Corp. Under the sale, the BankAmerica unit will assume $125.2 million in deposits and secured liabilities.

The BankAmerica unit will acquire $78.3 million of the failed Bakersfield institution's assets, which include $21.5 million in cash and $56.8 million in loans.
