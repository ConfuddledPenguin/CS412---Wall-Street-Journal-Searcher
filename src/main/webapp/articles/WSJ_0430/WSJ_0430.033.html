Marketing & Media: Has `Twin Peaks' Peaked Already? ABC Says Ratings Drop Isn't a Mystery

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 04/30/90

NEW YORK -- Everyone knows the mystery engulfing Twin Peaks, the fictional town in the Pacific Northwest: who killed Laura Palmer, the high school cheerleader who led a double life. But there is another, more intriguing, puzzle: What is happening to the viewers?

"Twin Peaks," on Capital Cities/ABC Inc.'s TV network and arguably the most original prime-time series in years, is suffering audience erosion. When the show had its special two-hour movie premiere earlier this month, it was watched by 35 million viewers.

The audience has declined since then, however, and last Thursday's episode, the third weekly episode, had about 16.7 million viewers, according to figures released by A.C. Nielsen Co. Specifically, "Twin Peaks" finished with an 11.3 rating and 18 share. A rating point represents 921,000 television households and a share is the percentage of sets in use.

Not surprisingly, rival National Broadcasting Co., a subsidiary of General Electric Co., reacted with glee. It continued to win the 9 p.m. hour with the situation comedies "Cheers" and "Wings." The shows had a combined 19.4 rating and 31 share. "An 18 share is what `The Young Riders' was doing when ABC pulled it," said Preston Beckman, vice president of audience research at NBC.

Alan Wurtzel, ABC's senior vice president of research, said he was not surprised at the audience falloff for "Twin Peaks," which has come from women, 50 years old and older and men 35 years old and older.

"The show has basically taken up a traditional ABC profile, which is comprised mostly of younger women" he said. "This isn't a surprise. What was a surprise was the initial broad appeal of the show."

Thursday has evolved surprisingly into one of the most competitive evenings on television. For the past five years, NBC has owned Thursdays with its lineup beginning with "The Cosby Show" and ending with "L.A. Law." But ABC has made inroads.

And to further illustrate the battle for audience, CBS Entertainment, a subsidiary of CBS Inc., is considering moving the CBS News series "48 Hours" from its so-called protected place on the schedule, Thursdays at 8 p.m., where it has been for two seasons, according to network officials.

"48 Hours," anchored by Dan Rather and featuring Bernard Goldberg and a rotating roster of correspondents, had consistently finished second behind "The Cosby Show" and "A Different World" on NBC. But since ABC placed "Father Dowling Mysteries" last winter, the CBS News series has slipped to third place.

According to two network officials, who did not want to be quoted by name, "48 Hours" may be moved to 10 p.m. on another night, if it returns as a weekly series.

The possible move puzzles some at CBS, which just completed its third consecutive season in the ratings basement. "48 Hours" was considered protected against "The Cosby Show" because ratings expectations are so low for any competitor of the NBC situation comedy.

But the 1990-91 TV season could be the last for comedian Bill Cosby and CBS might want to develop an entertainment show for that time period.
