Leading Securities Firms, Bond Brokers Are Forming Venture to Sell Price Data

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and John Connor
  • Date: 04/30/90

The nation's biggest securities firms are forming a joint venture with leading bond brokers to distribute government-securities price data 24 hours a day. The decision to create the new venture, which will represent all 44 primary dealers and four of the major so-called interbroker dealers, was announced at a Virginia meeting of the Public Securities Association.

"We are going to move very fast" and hope to launch the service this summer, said William Brachfeld of Daiwa Securities America Inc., chairman of a task force that recommended forming the venture.

The New York-based PSA said the venture, as yet unnamed, will negotiate to sell "real time" price data to several news services and electronic information systems. These include Telerate Inc., a subsidiary of Dow Jones & Co., which publishes the Wall Street Journal; Reuters Holdings PLC; Knight-Ridder Inc.; Bloomberg Financial Markets and Associated Press.

Real time or "live" quotes would allow subscribers to see prices as they occur. Securities firms once kept a close grip on their own trading data. But the government has been pressing them to make more price information widely available in the market, and the value of each firm's knowledge has diminished somewhat with the growth of the electronic data suppliers. Round-the-clock quotes are feasible because the bond market doesn't close, as the stock market does; government bonds are traded globally from New York to Tokyo to London.

A PSA spokesman said the price data wouldn't be disseminated on an exclusive basis to a limited number of vendors, but instead would be available to anyone willing to pay for the service. Mr. Brachfeld said he could even envision a tape of government bond prices running on the Financial News Network.

A PSA spokesman said the venture should be up and running in two or three months. One potential sticking point is how the profits from the venture will be divided. To decide that, the venture participants must first agree on who really owns government bond price information -- the primary dealers who buy and sell bonds or interdealer brokers who handle some of those trades.

Primary dealers are an elite group of securities firms and big banks that the Federal Reserve calls upon to buy and sell government securities as part of its activities to regulate monetary policy. These dealers can also bid to buy bonds directly from the Treasury at government bond auctions.

Interdealer brokers execute so-called blind trades for securities firms. (Although these brokers buy and sell bonds for securities houses, they don't transact business with the general public.)

Fran Bermanzohn, the PSA's general counsel, said the four interdealer brokers that will participate in the new venture are Fundamental Brokers Inc., RMJ Securities Corp., Liberty Brokerage Inc. and Garban Ltd.

Mr. Brachfeld said the new venture won't prevent market players from pursuing their own efforts to disseminate government bond data. He cited in this regard the recently reported plans of the so-called Group of Six securities firms to distribute bond prices and other market information. That venture, called Electronic Joint Venture, was formed by CS First Boston Inc.'s First Boston Corp. unit, Goldman, Sachs & Co., Morgan Stanley Group Inc.'s Morgan Stanley & Co., Salomon Inc.'s Salomon Brothers Inc., American Express Co.'s Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. and Citibank, a unit of Citicorp.

At the PSA meeting, all but one of the Group of Six firms, Shearson, voted to allow the new joint venture to proceed. The willingness of the Group of Six members to participate in the PSA venture raises questions about the viability of the Electronic Joint Venture. Officials at Electronic Joint Venture couldn't be reached for comment.

The new venture could also threaten the exclusivity of several existing bond quotation services, such as the service that provides "live" Cantor Fitzgerald quotes on Telerate screens. Also, Merrill Lynch provides bond quotes to Bloomberg. Cantor Fitzgerald, an interdealer broker, abstained from Friday's PSA vote.

The General Accounting Office several years ago recommended that information on government securities brokers' screens be distributed more broadly, beyond the primary dealers and aspiring primary dealers. The GAO said market participants should be given a chance to do this voluntarily before the government steps in. A new GAO report, expected to be released soon, will note that voluntary progress is still awaited. It is expected to recommend government action to require broader dissemination of price data if voluntary efforts don't reach fruition by a "date-certain" deadline.

The PSA said it wouldn't be involved in the new business venture, but would continue its consultative efforts to encourage expanded dissemination of government securities price information. "Our goal is to walk in the office Monday morning and start the next step," said Ms. Bermanzohn.
