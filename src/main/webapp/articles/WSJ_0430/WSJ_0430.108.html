Foreign Exchange: Gloom Surrounding Dollar Will Vanish By End of May, Market Observers Predict

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 04/30/90

NEW YORK -- In May, the yen will confirm it has hit bottom, the mark will narrowly survive economic-union jitters, and gloom surrounding the dollar will vanish. That's the outlook of 10 economists and currency dealers in Tokyo; Frankfurt, West Germany; London and New York interviewed by Dow Jones International News Service. By the end of May, these experts' average forecast calls for the dollar to stand at 159.20 yen and 1.6980 marks. The mark is seen slipping to 93.76 yen.

In late Friday afternoon New York trading, the dollar was at 159.30 yen, up from 159.23 yen late Thursday, and at 1.6775 marks, down from 1.6803 marks. The mark was trading at 94.96 yen up from 94.76 yen.

Where and whether interest rates will rise remains a source of widespread speculation and disagreement. While most see real U.S. rates rising relative to Japanese and West German rates, others see them falling or holding even.

But there is a consensus that the yen is undervalued and the mark overvalued, suggesting both deserve correction.

At the beginning of April, a similar survey predicted the dollar would be about 156.73 yen by the end of April, with estimates spread evenly between 162 yen and 150 yen. One bear said 136 yen.

The early April survey saw the dollar at 1.7150 marks by the end of April, with estimates ranging from a low of 1.65 marks to a high of 1.75 marks.

"Real" interest rates -- nominal rates minus anticipated inflation -- were cited by most in the survey as the key to predicting exchange rates. While acknowledging problems with the political and economic unification of the two Germanys, most of the respondents said the mark would be the main currency of Europe in the 1990s. The survey's two West German experts, however, were relatively bearish on the mark's prospects for May.

Ingo Kuller, chief trader at Westdeutsche Landesbank Girozentrale in Duesseldorf, said reunification will cast a shadow over the mark for many months to come, and the energies of the Bundesbank will be diverted away from holding the dollar down as it grapples with the specter of rising inflation. He sees the dollar climbing to 1.7640 marks by the end of May and higher, to 1.78 marks, in 90 days.

Klaus Groner, chief trader at Bayerische Vereinsbank in Munich, differs over the 90-day term, expecting the U.S. currency to fall against the mark to 1.65 marks later in the spring as worries about the monetary union with East Germany abate. "I see a renaissance for the mark," he said. "The way the government in Bonn is systematically tackling the problems with monetary union is encouraging."

Most of the respondents said the Bundesbank can keep inflation under wraps, predicting a 3% or lower rate for the year. Though most survey respondents said a boost in West German interest rates was in the cards, one economist suggested that nation's rates would ease because politics has outrun economics.

"I recognize it's a new ball game for the Germans," said Irwin Kellner, chief economist for Manufacturers Hanover Trust in New York, "but I'd say the markets have already discounted the effects of this and then some," leaving the dollar room to correct.

Mr. Kellner said trade-related pressures have led to an undervaluation of the dollar against European currencies and an overvaluation against the yen. The dollar should be buying fewer than 140 yen, he said, instead of 159 yen.

The U.S. posted an unadjusted $959.6 million trade surplus with Western Europe in February, compared with a $309.8 million deficit in January, putting the U.S. ahead $650 million through March. Against Japan, meanwhile, the unadjusted trade gap widened in February to $3.12 billion from $2.86 billion in January.

Survey participants generally agreed the Japanese base interest rate wouldn't fall, but were reluctant to say it was certain to rise in the month or 90-day period. The yen, in this scenario, likely will stay weak but not slip further.

Russell Sheldon, senior economist at Mellon Bank, Pittsburgh, said Japan may have an emerging inflation problem. "I don't think they'll be tightening policy to the degree they'd need to" to control inflation, he said. Japan's consumer prices, a key index of inflation, rose 2.9% in the fiscal year ended March 31, the largest gain since 1981.

As long as Tokyo stock and government bond markets stay shaky and prospects for European growth are good, "strong demand for dollars and other currencies will keep the yen weak," said Shigeito Yanase, manager of foreign-exchange trading at Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank Ltd. in Tokyo.

Political problems still dog the yen, Mr. Yanase noted. Talk in Tokyo that Liberal Democratic Party elders could force Japanese Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu to resign before a July economic summit in Houston will hinder any sort of yen recovery, he said.

Prospects for a tightening of U.S. monetary policy, which would tend to boost the dollar, are at least as tenuous as those for Japan and West Germany. Again, no one saw the U.S. Federal Reserve lowering interest rates, but only two said flat-out that rates would rise.

Supporting that view were sharply higher inflation readings released Friday with U.S. provisional first-quarter gross national product data. The implicit price deflator rose at a 5.7% rate, the largest increase since the third quarter of 1982, and the fixed-weight deflator, another inflation gauge, was up 6.5%, the largest leap since the fourth quarter of 1981.

"U.S. consumer price inflation should be around 4.5% in the second quarter, an unacceptable level for the Federal Reserve Board," said a senior corporate dealer with a large Tokyo-based bank.

The same dealer said growth in Japanese institutional investor demand for dollar-denominated investments should fall off, but dollar investments will still grow in absolute terms.

Forecasts for the pound averaged $1.613 in 30 days and $1.606 in 90 days, and were the most tightly-grouped forecasts among the currencies surveyed. All 10 participants said the pound would be about $1.6 in both periods, ranging from five $1.6 estimates to a low of $1.65 in the 30 days; for the longer period, estimates ranged from a bearish $1.53 to $1.67. Late Friday in New York, sterling was at $1.6350, below $1.6343 late Thursday in New York.

On New York's Commodity Exchange Friday, current-delivery gold settled at $371.10 an ounce, up 20 cents. Because of computer failure, trading was limited. Estimated volume was only 700,000 ounces.

In Hong Kong Monday, gold was quoted at $371.60 an ounce.
