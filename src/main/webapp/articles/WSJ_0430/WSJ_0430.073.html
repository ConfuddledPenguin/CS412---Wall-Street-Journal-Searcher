AIDS IN CUBA

Three-quarters of Cuba's sexually active population has been tested for AIDS, the official Communist Party newspaper Granma said.

The tests revealed 434 carriers of the AIDS virus to date, of which 63 had developed the disease and 27 died from it. Of those who tested positive, 315 were men and 119 were women. Their average age was 26, the youngest a girl of 15 and the oldest a man of 57.

The Cuban government says it has managed to control the spread of the disease on the island by means of massive testing and obligatory segregation of AIDS patients in a special clinic. Patients can work and receive a salary while being treated at the clinic.

The importation of blood products has been banned in Cuba since 1983.

SOUTH KOREA'S `FX' PROJECT

South Korea will spend $77 million on developing a facsimile machine to compete with similar products in Japan and other countries. Yonhap, the South Korean news agency, said the "FX" project was approved by government officials because Japan and other nations were reluctant to transfer their advanced technology. Yonhap said South Korea wants to begin exports of the machine in 1995.

DRASTIC MEASURES IN GREECE

The newly elected Greek government announced drastic price rises and higher indirect taxes. The measures are intended to increase state income by 400 billion drachmas ($2.43 billion) this year but will increase the annual inflation rate by 4 percentage points, Economy Minister George Souflias said; last year's rate was 15%. Said Mr. Souflias: "Of every 100 drachmas the state spends it has to borrow 40. This has to come to an end."

NEW ZEALAND EMIGRATION

New Zealand's net population loss through emigration has slowed sharply, the Department of Statistics said. The department said that in the 12 months ended March 31, net loss through migration movements fell 83.6% to 4,040 persons, down from a net loss of 24,708 persons in the 12 months ended March 31, 1989. The net loss in the 12 months to March was the smallest net loss in six years. New Zealand last recorded a net gain in population through migration in the 12 months ended March 31, 1984. At the last census, in 1986, New Zealand's population totaled 3.3 million people.

NORWEGIAN TV PROPOSAL

Norway's government proposed opening national airwaves to private television broadcasts and lifting an almost-total ban on TV commercials. The proposal to parliament calls for the creation of a second channel, funded by up to 30 minutes a day of advertising. The one existing channel, financed by viewer licenses and state transfers, has had a near monopoly on radio and TV broadcasts since it was founded in 1933.

A NO-TAX PLEDGE IN GERMANY

Helmut Seiters, the chief of staff of the West German government, said the German economic and monetary union will be financed with the help of strong economic growth, a rise in the budget deficit and spending cuts -- but without any tax increases. Speaking in the West German parliament, he said: "Tax increases aren't necessary." The strong rise in gross national product, which is expected to climb 4% this year, should boost tax revenue by 30 billion marks (about $18 billion) in 1990 and 1991, Mr. Seiters said.

CHINA CHANGES COURSE

China has dropped its earlier opposition to Hong Kong's plan for a multibillion dollar port and airport complex, Chinese officials said.

The Hong Kong-based officials said the economic-research units of China's state-owned companies in the British colony were examining the project to see if China could take part. Experts from the New China News Agency, the de facto Chinese embassy in Hong Kong, and the Bank of China group, China's major financial arm, are monitoring and analyzing the plan, they said.

Hong Kong's governor, Sir David Wilson, unveiled plans last year for a $16.28 billion complex, including a new international airport, a high-speed rail link, a six-lane highway and a new port. The project, to be financed by the government and the private sector, is seen as demonstrating confidence that Hong Kong will remain a vibrant international center after China regains sovereignty in 1997.

Chinese officials have said they oppose using Hong Kong's cash reserves for the airport, saying the money was intended for territorial government after 1997. A Bank of China official said: "Basically we have no objection to the plans because it eventually serves Hong Kong. But we must ensure it will not become a burden to us when the British are gone."
