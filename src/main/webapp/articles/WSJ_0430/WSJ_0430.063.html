BCCI Had Huge 1989 Loss, Will Unveil Infusion of $500 Million, Sources Assert

  • Author: Peter Truell and John J. Fialka
  • Date: 04/30/90

Bank of Credit & Commerce International, which pleaded guilty to money-laundering charges this year, is expected to announce a huge loss for 1989, according to individuals close to the Luxembourg-based bank. In addition, these individuals say, the bank will unveil major changes in its management and ownership, including a capital injection of well over $500 million from the ruler of Abu Dhabi.

The bank, which has grown to almost $21 billion in assets from $10 million at its founding in 1972, had an operating profit of about $200 million in 1989, the same individuals said. But the need to substantially increase provisions for bad and doubtful loans -- following pressures from auditors and regulators -- led to its 1989 loss, estimated to total about $500 million. The changes, which could be announced as early as today, also are said to include a sharp reduction in staff and the possible closure of some offices world-wide.

The bank's capital, and its ability to make such huge provisions, will, however, be bolstered by an infusion of more than $500 million from Sheik Zayed al-Nahyan, president of the United Arab Emirates and the ruler of Abu Dhabi. One estimate has Sheik Zayed, who is already a major shareholder in BCCI, injecting as much as $1 billion. Abu Dhabi, a big oil producer, is the richest of the small sheikdoms on the southern shore of the Persian Gulf.

During a recent interview in London -- where BCCI has 45 branches -- bank representatives refused to discuss the institution or its plans. But recently, company officials and people close to the bank have indicated that they planned major announcements that would transform it. Yesterday, bank representatives couldn't be reached for comment.

The massive infusion of capital -- made necessary by losses from unsecured or poorly collateralized loans -- also will be accompanied by substantial changes in the bank's ownership. Sheik Zayed and the state-owned Abu Dhabi Investment Authority, which the sheik and his family effectively control, would become the bank's majority shareholders, making the bank a government-owned institution.

BCCI, which is closely held, doesn't disclose its ownership; its parent is a Luxembourg holding company, BCCI Holdings (Luxembourg) S.A. But, according to recent reports, and according to estimates based on interviews with several people close to the bank, the bank is roughly 36%-owned by Sheik Zayed and the Abu Dhabi investment agency. Khaled Bin-Mahfouz, of the family that owns National Commercial Bank of Saudi Arabia, is the next largest shareholder with about 20%.

In an apparent link with the U.S., BCCI has several common shareholders with First American Bankshares, the Washington-based bank holding company. Sheik Zayed, the ADIA and Mr. Bin-Mahfouz are estimated to own close to one-third or more of First American.

BCCI also is expected to name a new chairman to succeed Hasan Abedi, the bank's founder and mentor who is an invalid after a heart transplant in 1988. Meanwhile, Zafar Iqbal, who is managing director and chief executive officer of BCCI's unit in the UAE, would become co-president with Swaleh Naqvi, BCCI's current acting president and chief executive. The two presidents and three other appointees from Abu Dhabi would form the bank's executive committee.

BCCI also will slash its staff as part of a restructuring it has worked out with its shareholders and discussed with its regulators in Luxembourg and London, according to people close to the bank.

They said that the bank is taking a close look at its far-flung branch network that now extends to 73 countries, and that it is expected to close some offices. According to one individual close to the bank, BCCI will close its Latin American and Caribbean branches, and may even close up in the U.S.

After BCCI earlier this year pleaded guilty in Tampa, Fla., to charges that it had laundered more than $14 million of drug money, state banking authorities in Florida have said they won't renew its banking license there.

According to its annual report for 1988, BCCI's U.S. assets totaled $390 million, a small part of overall assets of $20.67 billion.

According to one drastic retrenchment plan that top management has considered, BCCI would relocate its headquarters to the United Arab Emirates and restructure itself as an Abu Dhabi-based bank with most of its offices in the Middle East, Africa and Asia.
