International: Developing Nations To Receive Expanded U.S Trade Benefits

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/30/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. expanded the trade benefits it gives to developing nations -- especially to Mexico -- following an annual review of the so-called Generalized System of Preferences. The GSP, a system of tariff reductions and quota increases designed to benefit developing countries, will now cover goods from developing countries that had a total of nearly $2.4 billion of U.S. imports in 1989.

The moves will particularly help Mexico, which the White House Trade Office termed "the major beneficiary of this year's decisions." Mexican goods valued at $1.97 billion in 1989 will be provided new, restored, or expanded GSP eligibility, the trade office said.

The Bush administration has been trying hard to help Mexico, which has shown more commitment to economic policy changes than most other Latin American countries. The Mexicans also are trying to work their way out of a formidable foreign debt problem, and were the first country to negotiate a package that includes cuts in bank debt and interest payments under the Bush administration's international debt strategy.

The administration had the room to expand the GSP program because of the graduation from the program of Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong and South Korea on Jan. 1, 1989. Countries graduate from the GSP program when they reach a certain stage of economic development. The Andean nations also benefited from this year's review: Goods that totaled $44.3 million in U.S. imports from Bolivia, Colombia, Ecuador and Peru will soon be given duty-free status.

The administration also is delaying until June 1 GSP decisions on eligible Brazilian goods totaling $517 million in annual trade. By that date, President Fernando Collor de Mello, who took office in March, will have instituted trade and economic reforms, the White House Trade Office said.
