GE Aerospace, Responding to Decline In Defense Outlays, Will Cut 4,200 Jobs

  • Author: Amal Kumar Naj
  • Date: 04/30/90

General Electric Co.'s aerospace division, responding to defense spending cuts, said it will eliminate 4,200 jobs, farm out production of standard parts and consolidate several plants. Earlier this year, GE had said the defense cuts might prompt it to eliminate as many as 10,000 jobs over the next three years. The announcement Friday gave the first indication of the diversified concern's extensive restructuring plans.

The GE unit said it expects to lay off about half of the 4,200 workers. The rest will be absorbed in other GE operations or their positions will be eliminated through attrition and retirement.

"We're entering a decade that will provide a difficult challenge for defense companies," said Jon Rittenhouse, senior vice president of GE Aerospace in Valley Forge, Pa. "In this difficult environment, only the most-efficient, highest-quality manufacturers will survive and win."

Tassos Philippakos, an aerospace analyst at Moody's Investors Service Inc., applauded GE's action. He said it was the first such major move by a defense contractor. "Unfortunately, the U.S. defense contractors are responding slowly to the reality. That will definitely hurt their profitability," he said.

Mr. Philippakos said a major reason for most defense contractors' slow response is that it isn't clear whose programs are going to be axed. But if the companies don't begin to make broad plans, many of them will find themselves with excess capacity and other high fixed costs. "The European defense industry is moving a lot faster. We're seeing European companies consolidating their products and forming consortiums," he said.

A GE Aerospace spokesman said that if the defense budget continues to decline the way the division anticipates, "we expect that some of the plants will be operating at 75% to 80% of capacity, or less." He said the cutbacks and consolidation will eliminate the potentially unused capacity of 20% to 25%, thus bringing the operations closer to full capacity.

GE, which is based in Fairfield, Conn., said the 4,200 hourly and salaried jobs will be eliminated over the next 2 1/2 years in five locations: in Binghamton, Syracuse and Utica in New York state, and in Camden and Moorestown in New Jersey.

GE will purchase standard parts, such as printed wire boards and cables, from outside suppliers. It said this would allow the aerospace division to concentrate primarily on final assembly, testing and systems integration, enabling it to reduce inventory and overhead costs, as well as to cut excess capacity. Currently, 70% of GE Aerospace's business is devoted to final assembly and testing, and the rest to the manufacture of parts.

GE also will consolidate its dozen plants that manufacture advanced electronic parts into two centers. One center, which will be located in Conklin, N.Y., will serve six businesses in the New York and New England area. The other, for which GE is considering Logan Township, N.J., as a site, will supply six GE operations that are primarily in Pennsylvania and New Jersey. These two centers will become operational in the spring of 1991.

Separately, Lockheed Corp. said it will lay off 2,750 employees in its Lockheed Aeronautical Systems Co. unit by the end of June. Like GE, Lockheed cited the shrinking defense budget, as well as its completion of several military aircraft production programs.

A spokesman for the unit, based in Burbank, Calif., said that of the layoffs, 2,000 will be in Southern California and 750 in Marietta, Ga. The layoffs will be "across the board" and include manufacturing, finance, administrative and clerical employees, the spokesman said. Supervisors will be included, he said.

Lockheed Corp., based in Calabasas, Calif., is a military, aerospace and electronics concern.
