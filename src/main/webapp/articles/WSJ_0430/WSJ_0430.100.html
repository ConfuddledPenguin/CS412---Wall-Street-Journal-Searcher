Shearson Placed a Big Bet on Junk Bonds -- and Lost

  • Author: Jonathan Clements and Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 04/30/90

NEW YORK -- Who are some of the biggest victims of the junk bond market's collapse? Nobody can say for sure, but here's one particularly noteworthy candidate: American Express Co.'s Shearson Lehman Hutton Holdings Inc. unit.

Earlier this month, Shearson announced it was bailing out of $480 million of junk bond holdings -- taking a $115 million writedown in the process -- and ending a spectacularly unsuccessful foray into the high-yield junk market by the big securities firm.

The fiasco raises questions about management controls at Shearson and the judgment of former chief executive officer Peter A. Cohen, analysts say, and shows the pitfalls of taking steep short-term market risks.

In early 1989, just as the junk bond market was reaching its peak, Shearson and its money management subsidary, Boston Co., made a big bet on the junk market. Boston Co. plunked down $290 million to buy shares in seven junk-bond mutual funds. And Shearson bought $190 million of individual junk bond issues through an unusual "special purpose investment subsidiary."

The big securities firm funded the long-term junk bonds -- which were then yielding well above 12% -- by issuing short-term floating-rate paper with yields of less than 9%.

As Shearson saw it, "If I can gross 12% and pay 9%, that's a return on assets equal to four times what I'm getting on my normal business," says James Hanbury, an analyst at Wertheim Schroder & Co. "It was a simple arbitrage play." Other analysts called it more of a casino gamble.

Trouble is, Shearson's timing -- both buying and selling -- probably couldn't have been worse.

A junk bond manager familiar with Boston Co.'s mutual-fund purchases says that the buying largely occurred last spring when the prices of many junk bonds were at their peak. "They may not have hit the bottom {with their selling}," the manager says. "But they couldn't have done much worse."

A Shearson spokesman says all the mutual fund shares were sold this month, in addition to part of the individual junk bond holdings. Shearson declined further comment about the junk holdings.

Which funds were the beneficiaries of Boston Co.'s largesse? A Boston Co. spokesman refuses to say but says none of the money ended up in Shearson's own broker-sold mutual funds.

Boston Co. put most, if not all, the money into no-load funds, which don't charge upfront fees and are often sold directly to investors by telephone and mail, and not through brokers. "I'm sure they had some in Fidelity High Income, and maybe some in T. Rowe Price and Vanguard," says the portfolio manager familiar with the fund purchases.

Both Vanguard Group and T. Rowe Price Associates said there would have been net inflows into their junk bond funds in April, but for hefty redemptions by a single financial institution, which they refused to identify. A Fidelity Investments executive declined to comment.

Shearson has tried to blame the trip-up largely on its former chairman Mr. Cohen, who resigned under pressure earlier this year. A Shearson spokesman notes, for instance, that the trades took place on Mr. Cohen's watch. Shearson declined further comment.

But other top Shearson and American Express executives, including current Shearson chief executive Howard L. Clark Jr., clearly knew about the junk holdings -- or should have, analysts say.

"It was a failure of management," says Mr. Hanbury, the Wertheim Schroder analyst. "The buck has been passed about, but the ultimate responsibility of the business still was with {Peter} Cohen."

Mr. Cohen, who hasn't spoken to reporters since his resignation, declined to comment.

Boston Co.'s decision to bet $290 million of its $5 billion investment portfolio may have reflected pressures at the firm for short-term results. And, "You can't beat junk bonds for short-term income," says a fund manager at a competing Boston firm.

Shearson's $115 million charge against first-quarter earnings were part of a massive $630 million restructuring charge: $70 million in reserves were for the Boston Co. junk portfolio, and $45 million went toward Shearson's special purpose investment unit. All told for the quarter, Shearson posted an industry-record loss of $915 million.

Although securities firms long have used special-purpose units to buy mortgage-backed obligations, it's very unusual for them to buy the more risky junk bonds. And, in Shearson's case, says Wertheim Schroder's Mr. Hanbury: "It was a major miscalculation."
