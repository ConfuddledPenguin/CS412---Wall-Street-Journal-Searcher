Social Problems At Colleges Studied In Carnegie Report

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 04/30/90

WASHINGTON -- College social problems, such as excessive drinking and racial tension, reflect murky standards of conduct and administrative ambivalence about enforcing the rules that do exist, a new report on campus life concludes. The study by Ernest Boyer, president of the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching, comes amid growing reports that colleges across the country are struggling to deal with heavy student drinking, as well as with increases in racial and ethnic slights, including anti-semitism.

"The idyllic vision so routinely portrayed in college promotional materials often masks disturbing realities of student life," the report contends. One reason is that "on most campuses, expectations regarding the personal conduct of students are ambiguous, at best," the report says. "What we found particularly disturbing is the ambivalence college administrators feel about their overall responsibility for student behavior."

The report cites efforts to control drinking as one example. The study was based in part on a survey of 382 college and university presidents conducted by the foundation and the American Council on Education. In the survey, 67% of the presidents rated alcohol abuse as a problem on their campuses. Many college administrators say drinking contributes to a number of campus problems, including vandalism, crime and sexual harassment.

Yet many colleges don't take a firm stand against alcohol use, even by minors. In part, that's because they would rather have students drink on campus than force them to drink off campus and then drive back.

"If state laws say alcohol use is illegal for those under 21, colleges should make this fact clearly known to students and declare that they will support the law, rather than ignore it," Mr. Boyer says, adding that students "need models of integrity, not equivocation."

Many colleges are crafting firmer anti-drinking policies, driven in part by several well-publicized drinking-related deaths on campus and increasing fears of liability.

While Mr. Boyer praised these programs, his report, entitled "Campus Life: In Search for Community," recommends that colleges and universities adopt basic principles on which to base their policies. The first principle, it suggests, should be to pay more attention to education. He notes with some despair that many students simply don't study very much. According to one nationwide survey, only 23% of college students study 16 or more hours a week.

Other principles stress the importance of discipline, justice and open debate in campus communities.
