FDIC Loses on Claim To Over $50 Million In Texas Firm Assets

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/30/90

DALLAS -- An uncompleted stock transfer cost the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. its claim to more than $50 million in cash from the assets of First RepublicBank Corp., a bankruptcy judge ruled. The ruling by U.S. Bankruptcy Judge Steven A. Felsenthal will allow the creditors of the Texas bank-holding company to share the cash under a reorganization plan that could be approved as early as July.

The cash comprises the proceeds, and interest earned, from the holding company's 1989 sale of its Republic Money Orders Inc. unit to Greyhound Corp. of Phoenix.

The FDIC, which seized First RepublicBank's bank units in late July 1988, claimed the proceeds because the holding company had assigned its Republic Money Orders stock to the company's Dallas bank in 1978.

But the bank never took possession of the stock certificate or assignment, and the holding company continued to list the stock as an asset held in a safe-keeping account at the Dallas bank.

"Since the holding company did not deliver and the bank did not obtain possession of the stock certificate with attached assignment, First RepublicBank Corp. remained owner of the Republic Money Orders stock as a matter of law," Judge Felsenthal ruled.

The judge rejected the FDIC's argument that the agreement establishing the safe-keeping account should be set aside because it wasn't approved by the bank's directors and wasn't part of the records available to federal examiners before the bank's seizure.

"Absent the {safe-keeping} agreement, there was delivery of the stock," said Jeffrey W. Hurt, a Dallas attorney for the FDIC in the dispute. Mr. Hurt said he expects the FDIC to appeal Judge Felsenthal's decision.

The FDIC is the largest creditor of First RepublicBank Corp., which entered Chapter 11 reorganization proceedings Aug. 1, 1988, just days after the FDIC seized its bank units. The holding company isn't related to First Republic Bancorp of San Francisco.
