U.S. Is Warned About Federal Home Loan Banks

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 04/30/90

Thrift regulators and Congress should be wary of further erosions of the earnings of the Federal Home Loan Banks, according to separate warnings from the president of the Dallas bank and thrift analysts at Salomon Brothers Inc. "A further drop in the (banks') dividend yield and, in turn, the value of (bank) stock could have horrendous implications for the savings institutions we cover," Salomon Brothers analysts Bruce Harting and Susan Silverstein said in a report.

Thrifts, which own stock in the 12 regional banks equal to at least 1% of their mortgages outstanding, count the holdings as part of the tangible net worth they must maintain to stave off regulatory actions. As dividend yields have fallen, many of the thrifts have been reducing their holdings in the Federal Home Loan Banks.

Part of the decline in dividend yields is caused by a ceiling imposed by the banks' regulatory board. The ceiling is roughly one percentage point above the federal funds rate, placing the current dividend yields between 8.2% and 8.5%. The funds rate is what banks charge each other on overnight loans.

But yields are also suffering from provisions of the federal thrift-bailout legislation that took effect in August. Among those provisions is a requirement that the Federal Home Loan Banks contribute $300 million annually, regardless of earnings, to pay for the cost of failed thrifts.

In addition, the banks are watching their major source of revenue -- loans, or advances, to member thrifts -- dry up as thrifts cut borrowings in an effort to shrink to meet the law's stringent capital standards. The decrease in borrowings is also helping push down dividend yields.

"As this bank shrinks, it is going to be more and more difficult to pay reasonable dividends," said George Barclay, president of the Federal Home Loan Bank of Dallas. For the first time, the Dallas bank will tap a reserve fund to keep its dividend yield at about 8.5% for the second quarter, he said.

Mr. Barclay said the Dallas bank has fed the reserve by deliberately keeping dividends low during the past several quarters to counteract the loss of revenue as borrowings by member thrifts decline. Advances from the Federal Home Loan Bank of Dallas fell from a peak of $27.5 billion in June 1989 to the current level of about $19 billion, Mr. Barclay said.

Other more-profitable banks, such as the one in San Francisco, had been paying member thrifts substantially higher dividend yields than the Dallas bank. But that has ended. The San Francisco bank dropped its yield from about 12% in early 1989 to the current rate of about 8.5%.

"The real concern about this is that net worth be preserved," said Ray Martin, chairman and chief executive officer of Coast Savings Financial Inc., parent of Coast Savings & Loan Association of Los Angeles. Coast Savings is a shareholder of the San Francisco Federal Home Loan Bank.

Mr. Martin said Coast has reduced the amount of its holdings in the San Francisco bank by about $240 million to about $100 million. Those holdings represent about 49% of Coast's tangible net worth, according to the Salomon Brothers report.

Coast isn't concerned as long as Congress and the regulators don't do anything to erode the earnings of the Federal Home Loan Banks further, Mr. Martin said.
