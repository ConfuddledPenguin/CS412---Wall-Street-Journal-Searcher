World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Move Higher on Strength In Blue-Chip Electricals; London Ends Lower

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 04/30/90

Tokyo stocks finished higher in quiet trading Friday, helped by investment trust buying of blue-chip electrical shares, traders said. The Nikkei index of 225 selected issues, which lost 139.78 points Thursday, closed 160.22 points higher at 29584.80.

Volume on the first section reflected pre-holiday sluggishness, estimated at only 480 million shares, down from 766 million shares Thursday.

Advancing issues outnumbered declining issues, 503 to 395, while 198 remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index (Topix) of all issues listed in the first section, which slipped 0.41 point Thursday, added 5.86 points, or 0.27%, at 2205.96.

The second section index, which gained 6.89 points Thursday, jumped 15.23 points, or 0.43%, to close at 3530.95. Volume in the second section was nine million shares, up from 7.2 million Thursday.

Traders said that the most notable buying of the day came from investment trust funds, which supported blue-chip electrical shares toward the afternoon close.

The substantial rally in many of these high-priced issues boosted the Nikkei index, they said.

But Christopher Leighton, manager of international equity sales at Schroder Securities (Japan) Ltd., noted the advances weren't sectorwide, as many electricals were hit by profit-taking.

Moreover, many observers doubted the possibility that index-linked buying of the Nikkei 225 issues was responsible for the afternoon rise, saying that they would expect a broader-based upward movement if that were the case.

Meanwhile, incentive-backed shares, particularly Osaka Gas, were struck by a wave of selling in the afternoon. While a company spokesman had said Thursday that its new materials, which might be used in high-efficiency batteries, are still in the experimental phase, the stock was heavily bought Friday morning, only to be sold when investors' excitement fizzled.

"These were really clobbered down, as investors were obviously just looking for some quick money," said one trader at a foreign brokerage firm.

"The market has been trading on stories rather than fundamentals," said Kevin Haag, a trader in international equity sales at Nomura Securities Co. "It's just a little game for the market."

Investors are putting off big strategic positions and are concentrating on speculative trading, which is fine as long as the currency and bond markets hold up, Schroder's Mr. Leighton said.

But the yen's weakness hasn't abated, and bond prices are still under pressure, traders noted.

Kyocera ended 90 yen (57 cents) higher at 7,310 yen ($45.91), while Fanuc jumped 170 to 7,140.

Sony continued its recent strength, up 120 to 8,470. Sharp was up 20 to 1,710, while TDK rose 40 to 6,880. Advantest gained 50 to 5,150.

Hirose Electric rose 200 to 6,500, and Tokyo Electric Power was up 50 to 4,140.

But Pioneer fell 20 to 5,960 and Fujitsu also fell 20, to 1,400.

The top story in the market Friday afternoon concerned talk that Middle Eastern companies are considering buying a stake in a Japanese oil refinery. Showa Shell soared on the rumor and closed 90 higher at 1,150.

Investors lost their incentive to back Suzuki Motors, which lost 79 to 931. Isuzu was also hit by profit-taking, falling 40 to 1,100.

Osaka Gas ended 15 lower at 635.

Alps Electric, which reported worse-than-expected earnings Thursday night, closed 180 lower at 2,040.

London share prices ended a second day of sharp losses in light dealings Friday, pressured by overhanging economic and political gloom, markdowns on the end of the three-week trading account and a dearth of buyers.

"Market-makers didn't want to be stuck with any stock," one dealer said. "Sentiment is just as bad as it could be," he added.

Additionally, a poor performance on Wall Street added to the market's woes. Wall Street and London were hurt by the release of U.S. first-quarter figures for the gross national product, which showed a lower-than-expected growth rate and a higher-than-expected inflation measure.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index finished down 27 points at 2106.6, slightly above its intraday low of 2105 and greatly lower than its intraday high of 2140.2, reached in the first hour of official trading.

At the close, the narrower FT 30 share index was down 17.4 points at 1658.7. Volume was 536.7 million shares compared with 510.9 million a day earlier, a relatively healthy number in light of the dismal trading volume during the past month.

In the futures market, the June FT-SE 100 index contract finished at 2135.0, down from its close of 2156.0 Thursday.

Banking, property and insurance stocks were hit particularly hard by the bearish tide, as negative statements by Midland and Barclays banks earlier in the week and a profit warning by construction company George Wimpey Friday were poorly received, traders said.

Among bank shares, Barclays shed 7 pence (11 cents) to 500, Lloyds was off 2 to 250 and Midland sunk 8 to 279.

In the property sector, MEPC dove 2 to 479, Land Securities skidded 14 to 465 and Hammerson lost 3 to 727. George Wimpey slipped 8 to 227 after its bearish statement.

Composite insurers Commercial Union lost 12 to 447, General Accident dropped 16 to #10 ($16.33) and Sun Alliance shed a penny to 298.

Food and drinks group Grand Metropolitan plunged 16 to 554 after Britain's Monopolies and Mergers Commission said Friday it would investigate the pending merger between the brewing and beer retailing operations of Grand Met and Australia's Elders.

Elsewhere, stock prices closed lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Amsterdam, Stockholm and Brussels, and higher in Milan. South African gold stocks closed firmer.

Share prices declined in Hong Kong, Sydney, Wellington and Manila, and were higher in Seoul and Taipei. Singapore was closed.
