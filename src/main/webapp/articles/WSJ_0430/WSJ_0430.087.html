Technology & Health: Genetically Made Hormone to Raise Cow Milk Output Is Banned in Wisconsin

  • Author: Richard Koenig
  • Date: 04/30/90

Gov. Tommy Thompson of Wisconsin signed legislation to temporarily ban the commercial use and sale in the state of a genetically engineered hormone that increases milk production by dairy cows. The governor's action was claimed as a victory by opponents of the hormone, who had argued that it would hurt dairy farmers by causing a milk glut and forcing milk prices lower.

But whether a temporary ban would delay the marketing of the hormone for long remained unclear, since it still must get marketing approval by the Food and Drug Administration anyway.

For several manufacturers -- Monsanto Co., Eli Lilly & Co., Upjohn Co. and American Cyanamid Co. -- the hormone is the first potentially big product to emerge from agricultural biotechnology. Some estimates of the world-wide market range as high as $1 billion annually.

A Monsanto spokesman said, "We'll continue battling and are confident that in the long run the product's merits will carry the day."

Mr. Thompson approved a ban on the hormone, called bovine growth hormone (BGH) or bovine somatotropin, until June 1, 1991. At the same time, he vetoed another measure that would have extended the ban until July 1, 1991, or six months after FDA approval, whichever came later. The FDA is expected to approve BGH this year or next.

Whether to ban BGH has been a hot issue in Wisconsin for several years. Mr. Thompson, a Republican who is up for re-election in November, had indicated his opposition to stronger bans but hadn't publicly disclosed until Friday how he would respond to the latest bills to pass the state legislature.

The governor said his action would provide "sufficient time to allow for additional farmer and consumer education on the use" of BGH "without compromising Wisconsin's ability to compete" in milk markets.

Opponents of BGH have argued that any drop in milk prices because of its use would particularly hurt small dairy farmers. Among the opposition have been small-farm groups and the Foundation on Economic Trends, which is headed by the anti-biotechnology activist Jeremy Rifkin.

State Sen. Russ Feingold, a Democrat who sponsored anti-BGH bills, called the product part of a "technological treadmill" that is forcing small farmers out of business. In addition to the economic issue, the opponents say, some questions about the safety of BGH need more study.

The companies that developed the hormone maintain that its use wouldn't favor big farmers over small, and that it wouldn't disrupt the dairy-farm economy more than have previous innovations, like artificial insemination. Farmers in various states have volunteered to take part in BGH trials.

As for safety, the companies note that FDA officials have already said milk from BGH-treated cows is safe for human consumption; continuing studies focus on the health effect on dairy herds.

BGH, a protein that is nearly identical to a growth hormone that cows produce naturally, is manufactured by fermentation. The gene encoding the protein is inserted into bacteria, which then produce the protein in quantity. Injected into cows, BGH has boosted milk production 10% to 25%.

Opponents of BGH said the they expect the temporary ban in Wisconsin to help their efforts in other leading dairy states. For one thing, the Wisconsin law appears strong enough to activate a one-year ban in Minnesota, which was contingent on action in Wisconsin or other states.
