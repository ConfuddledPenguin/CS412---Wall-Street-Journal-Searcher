SEC Officer Suspends Blinder Robinson For 45 Days, Bars It From Underwriting

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 04/30/90

WASHINGTON -- A Securities and Exchange Commission hearing officer suspended Blinder, Robinson & Co., once the nation's largest penny-stock underwriting firm, for 45 days and barred the firm from underwriting any securities offerings for two years. Administrative law judge Brenda Murray also barred Meyer Blinder, founder and president of the Denver-based firm, from associating with any brokerage firm, with the proviso that he can seek reinstatement as a registered broker after two years. The unusually harsh sanctions stem from a 1980 casino stock-underwriting in which the firm and Mr. Blinder were charged with fraud and manipulative practices.

In issuing her decision Friday, Ms. Murray said that the evidence in the long-running case "indicates a high probability that Mr. Blinder cannot ever operate in the securities industry in compliance with the law."

The decision becomes final within 15 days unless the firm and Mr. Blinder ask the five-member SEC to review it. Both were expected to file such petitions.

Attorneys for Mr. Blinder and Blinder Robinson declined to comment Friday afternoon.

Under the 45-day suspension, Blinder Robinson will still be able to make unsolicited trades for customers, complete transactions outstanding, and make deliveries and transfers of securities.

Ms. Murray rejected the SEC's request for a permanent bar against Mr. Blinder, opting instead for two years which, she said, "will give Mr. Blinder an opportunity for rehabilitation."

The administrative case has been fiercely fought for years. In 1985, another SEC hearing officer ruled that Blinder Robinson and Mr. Blinder had violated securities laws by, among other things, failing to disclose the firm's purchase of American Leisure Corp. securities to close out a $25 million underwriting for the Atlantic City company. In 1986, the commission imposed the same sanctions as Ms. Murray did Friday. Attorneys for Mr. Blinder and the firm appealed the sanctions to the federal appeals court here in 1987, arguing that the SEC deals more harshly with newer brokerage firms than "old-line houses."

In January 1988, a three-judge appellate panel remanded the case to the SEC for further hearing, saying the hearing officer should have allowed the introduction of certain evidence about Mr. Blinder's relationship with his legal advisers on the casino-stock offering.

Mr. Blinder had consulted with securities lawyers before he decided against disclosing in the American Leisure prospectus that Blinder Robinson was buying the last unsold securities in the "all or none" offering. In such an underwriting, if all of the securities aren't sold by a specified date, the investors must be reimbursed.

In her decision, Ms. Murray said that Mr. Blinder had rejected the lawyers advice to disclose the American Leisure purchases "because it would have damaged Blinder Robinson's business reputation," indicating an inability to complete a deal. "Taken as whole," she added, "the evidence is that Mr. Blinder didn't rely on anyone's advice but did what he wanted to do."

Ms. Murray also rejected the argument that the SEC has a double-standard of enforcement, meting out more severe sanctions to firms, such as Blinder Robinson, that aren't members of the New York Stock Exchange.
