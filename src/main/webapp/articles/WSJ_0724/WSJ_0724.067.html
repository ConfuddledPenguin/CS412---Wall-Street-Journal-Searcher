OTC Focus: Market Plunges as Sell-Off Continues; Intel Falls 5/8 to 44 1/2 on 3.1 Million Shares

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 07/24/90

Investors hammered over-the-counter stocks in the worst one-day pasting since last year's "mini-crash" on Oct. 19. "The OTC market is getting its brain beat in," said William Sulya, director of OTC trading at A.G. Edwards, St. Louis. Despite a "few signs of life" at the end of the day, Mr. Sulya said he wasn't convinced the selling spree had ended. "We hope it will bounce {today} so we can sweep up the debris, but that remains to be seen."

The Nasdaq Composite Index plunged 10.63 to 444.64, or 2.3%, surpassing yesterday's 1.9% drop in the Dow Jones Industrial Average. Declining shares swamped advancers, 1,857 to 431, as 186 stocks fell to new lows and only 21 reached new highs. Volume eased slightly to 168.9 million from 174.6 million Friday.

The steep plunge in smaller stocks in lock-step with larger shares was unusual, said Jack Laporte, president of the $1 billion T. Rowe Price New Horizons Fund, who noted that smaller issues usually trail big drops in large stocks by a day.

"What bothered me was that the secondaries underperformed," he said. "Normally, they get whacked the next day." Mr. Laporte called "disturbing" the recent poor performance of OTC stocks relative to the broader Standard & Poor's 500 stock index. The OTC market has fallen 5.32% in the last five sessions, compared with a 3.4% decline in the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index.

Traders and portfolio managers blamed the OTC bloodbath in part on program trading of larger stocks, nervousness about corporate earnings, fears about the economy and concerns that the market for initial public offerings might lose some steam.

Alan Radlo, manager of the $734 million Fidelity OTC Portfolio, grumped about program trading. "They might as well be playing with jelly beans instead of the market," Mr. Radlo said, adding that he finds such influences on the market discouraging not only to himself but also to his mutual fund clients. Both Mr. Radlo and Mr. Laporte said they bought shares during yesterday's fall.

Scattered among the day's most-active shares were some well-known stocks that ended higher after a day of erratic trading.

Nike rose 3/4 to 90 3/8 after shares of the athletic footwear maker traded as low as 84 1/2. Apple Computer, which Friday announced a five-million share buy-back program, gained 1/2 to 41 1/2 after falling to 40.

MCI Communications closed unchanged at 38.

Intel, the day's most active stock, fell 5/8 to 44 1/2 on 3.1 million shares. Among other technology shares, Sun Microsystems slipped 1 3/8 to 30 7/8; Conner Peripherals slid 1 to 26 3/4; Oracle Systems dropped 1/2 to 17 3/8; Lotus fell 3/4 to 24 1/2; Microsoft declined 1 1/4 to 72, and AST Research tumbled 1 1/4 to 20 3/4. The Nasdaq Industrial Index, which includes many computer-related issues, dove 12.60 to 479.36, a 2.56% slide.

Although a big queue of sellers lined up before the opening, traders contended that activity throughout the day was orderly. "The way it started it out, I thought it would be a disaster," said Jerry Mullins, head of OTC trading at Dean Witter Reynolds. Mr. Mullins attributed the steady erosion in smaller stocks to a lack of support among buyers. "I guess nobody wants to take positions," he said.

Investors remained cautious throughout the day, according to E. E. "Buzzy" Geduld, president of Herzog, Heine, Geduld, New York. "It's like cattle," he said. "They can go either way here. If someone starts a run on the down side, they'll hit them again."

Among the day's prospectors, Mr. Radlo said he bought about 75 issues, among them shares of banks with strong earnings, oil-service related companies and specific companies with robust operations. Those included stocks of included Bancorp Hawaii, a Honolulu commercial bank; Novell, a maker of personal-computer network systems; and Medical Care International, which Friday reported net income for the second quarter rose to 37 cents a share from 24 cents a year earlier. Medical Care fell 1 7/8 to 38.

Despite having their "fingers burned" by disappointing earnings among some technology companies, T. Rowe Price's investment managers still have faith that long-term bargains remain in the shares of some technology companies and other OTC concerns with double-digit earnings growth, said Mr. Laporte.

"We feel strongly that valuations remain compelling," he said, adding that the New Horizons Fund hasn't changed its level of cash. Among the stocks he favors are Medco Containment Services, a provider of prescription services by mail; Autodesk, a maker of computer-aided design and drafting products; and bubble-gum card maker Topps.

Earnings reports managed to lift the price of a few stocks. American Power Conversion rose 2 1/2 to 40 1/4 after the Peace Dale, R.I., maker of power supply products reported net income for the second quarter more than doubled to 53 cents a share from 26 cents a year earlier. The company also announced that it would split its stock 3-for-1.

Dynatech gained 1 1/2 to 18. The Burlington, Mass., scientific-instrument company reported net income for the first quarter ended June 30 rose to 45 cents a share from 37 cents a year earlier.

BB&T Financial added 1/2 to 18 1/2. The Wilson, N.C., thrift was the subject of a positive profile in the current issue of Barron's.

Amgen gained 3/4 to 85 1/2. Friday, the Thousand Oaks., Calif., biotechnology company reported net income for the first quarter ended June of 57 cents a share, before a 2-for-1 stock split, compared with five cents a share a year earlier. Also, analysts at First Boston and Prudential-Bache Securities repeated their "buy" opinions on the stock, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.
