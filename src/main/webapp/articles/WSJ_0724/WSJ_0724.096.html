Abreast of the Market: Industrials Plunge 56.44 on Worry Over Earnings, Rates and Inflation

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 07/24/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices suffered their biggest setback in six months as disappointing corporate earnings and other worries took their toll on the market. An unsettled outlook for interest rates and inflation, news of financial difficulties facing the largest guarantor of student loans and waves of futures-related sell programs also pressured share prices. The Dow Jones Industrial Average tumbled 56.44 points to 2904.70.

Most of the damage was done early in the session as the market picked up where it left off late Friday, when an earnings-related slide thwarted the industrial average's latest attempt to finish above 3000 and left the average with a loss of 32.67.

In the first hour of trading, the Dow industrials tumbled about 60 points as prices steadily eroded. Less than 20 minutes later, the 30-stock measure was down more than 105 points at what proved to be its session low.

The average then jumped more than 50 points within minutes after the second wave of selling subsided, but the recovery was short-lived and prices drifted below those levels until just before the close.

Among broader measures, Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index skidded 6.30 to 355.31, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index tumbled 3.43 to 194.22 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index dropped 6.01 to 329.86.

Declining issues outnumbered advancers by 1,430 to 227 on the New York Stock Exchange, where volume swelled to 209,030,000 shares from 177,810,000 Friday.

The drop in the industrial average was the third largest point decline this year and the largest since Jan. 22, when it plummeted 77.45 points. The second-largest drop was recorded Jan. 12, when it fell 71.46 points.

On the earnings front, the latest shock came from the second-quarter report released by McDonald's late Friday. The stock plunged 3 to 33 in Big Board composite trading of 4.2 million shares in the wake of the report; although net income rose 10% from the year-earlier level, earnings per share of 59 cents didn't meet analysts' projections.

An increase in Japanese interest rates overnight put additional pressure on the market, analysts say, as the move cast further doubt over whether rates in the U.S. would be likely to decline soon. Those concerns had surfaced last week following news that June consumer prices rose 0.5%, which stoked fears of renewed inflation.

Also, worries about debt problems in the economy intensified following a report in this newspaper that the Higher Education Assistance Foundation, the largest guarantor of student loans, is facing a possible bankruptcy filing unless it merges with another guarantor or receives other federal aid. Student Loan Marketing Association, one of the foundation's largest creditors, tumbled 4 1/8 to 49 7/8.

Amid all those developments, sell programs related to stock-index arbitrage pummeled the market at midmorning as index futures fell to discounts to the cash indexes. The arbitrage trading, which involved the sale of baskets of stocks and the purchase of futures contracts, resurfaced periodically in the afternoon.

The Dow Jones Utilities Average, which closed Friday at its lowest level in 13 months, went down another 1.95 to 200.20. The average hasn't posted a gain in the past seven sessions and has closed higher only twice in 15 sessions this month.

The Dow Jones Transportation Average hit its lowest level since late June by retreating 23.18 to 1136.13. Among its component issues, airline and air-freight stocks were especially hard hit; USAir Group fell 1 1/8 to 23, Alaska Air Group lost 1 to 21 1/2 and Airborne Freight fell 7/8 to 25.

The earnings report from McDonald's -- one of the many consumer stocks to benefit over the past few months from a focus on issues viewed as most likely to deliver earnings growth -- came on the heels of a series of disappointing results last week for technology companies, another market favorite for some time.

Taking those reports together, "The growth stock mystique has been pretty well destroyed," said Michael Metz, chief market strategist at Oppenheimer & Co.

Mr. Metz said the market's steep drop on the heels on its late sell-off Friday represents "a really traumatic blow to confidence" from which investors will need time to recover, making an immediate rebound unlikely.

"You're going to have a market that is risk-averse and gun-shy, at least for the next few weeks," Mr. Metz said. "Now that confidence has been shaken, you have a market where there's no great conviction and there's a high degree of anxiety."

While expressing greater confidence about the potential for an immediate recovery, Eugene E. Peroni Jr., director of technical research at Janney Montgomery Scott Inc., Philadelphia, said stocks have probably seen their highs "over the next several weeks, and possibly over the next several months."

Only two of the more than 80 Dow Jones industry groups closed higher -- precious metals, traditionally a "safe haven" for investors in uncertain periods, and trucking.

Among precious-metals stocks, Battle Mountain Gold gained 1/4 to 11 1/8, American Barrick Resources added 5/8 to 20 3/4, Newmont Mining advanced 1 3/8 to 47 and Placer Dome rose 3/8 to 16 3/4.

The trucking sector's rise resulted from a rally in Consolidated Freightways, which gained 1 3/4 to 15 after issuing its second-quarter earnings report. The company reported a loss of $3.6 million, or 19 cents a share, but analysts had generally expected a bigger loss.

Union Carbide gained 5/8 to 19 5/8 on 1.1 million shares. The company reported net income of 81 cents a share for the second quarter, down from $1.33 a year ago but near the upper end of the range of analysts' estimates.

Texas Instruments, whose second-quarter earnings report touched off the market's plunge late Friday, rose 1/2 to 33 5/8 after plummeting 5 7/8 in the prior session.

Storage Technology, another victim of heavy earnings-related selling Friday, rebounded 5/8 to 26 3/8.

Kansas Gas & Electric climbed 4 5/8 to 24 3/8 after Kansas City Power & Light began a $27-a-share takeover bid for the company, along with an offer for three preferred-stock issues. Kansas City Power dropped 7/8 to 31 1/2.

Kansas City Southern Industries gained 1 5/8 to 48 1/8. A report in the newsletter "Mergers & Corporate Policy" quoted a source as saying management is considering a plan to sell an asset, most likely its railroad unit, and then take the company private for $80 a share. The company declined to comment.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 5.53 to 354.92. Volume totaled 17,170,000 shares.
