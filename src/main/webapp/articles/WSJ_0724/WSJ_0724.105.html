Heard on the Street: `Trump Futures'? Unique Way to Bet On the Financier

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 07/24/90

NEW YORK -- For those who follow the ups and downs of Donald Trump, there may soon be some new SEC-approved securities. Call them Trump futures, though they don't yet have a name. The new securities, currently in the process of being hatched, would rise and fall with Mr. Trump's perceived ability to satisfy potential claims arising from his 1988 sale of Resorts International to Merv Griffin.

Resorts, a gambling concern that later fell on hard times, will be in U.S. bankruptcy court in Camden, N.J., today proposing a plan of reorganization. The plan has the backing of creditors and is expected to be confirmed by Judge Rosemary Gambardella.

For the record, the plan would halve Resorts' debt to $435 million from $931 million. Creditors would receive about three-quarters of the company's stock; Mr. Griffin, who would remain as chairman and who would invest an additional $23 million in cash, would get 21.5%. This much, in the arcane world of bankruptcy, is standard operating procedure.

But a novel feature of the plan "provides that a trust will be established and funded to pursue for the benefit of {Resorts creditors} all claims the debtors and their affiliates may have against Donald Trump, Trump Taj Mahal Associates L.P." and various Trump affiliates.

Holders of nearly $600 million of the company's debt securities have argued that the sale of Resorts, and a related transaction with Mr. Trump, constituted a fraudulent conveyance -- in laymen's terms, that is, so weakened Resorts as to leave it insolvent, leading to the company's bankruptcy-law filing.

Under fraudulent-conveyance law, former shareholders of a company can be ordered to return the cash they received for their shares in these circumstances.

The reorganization plan, which is opposed by Mr. Trump, transfers the purported causes of legal action against Mr. Trump to a separate trust. Ten million units of the trust will be distributed to Resorts creditors, and the value of these units will consist of whatever settlement or award the trust can win from Mr. Trump.

Marc Kirschner, an attorney for the creditors committee at Jones, Day, Reavis & Pogue, says he expects that the units will be tradable securities. "We have applied for a `no-action letter' from the Securities and Exchange Commission," meaning a letter stating that trading the units wouldn't violate U.S. law.

"We believe they comply with applicable laws," he says. "If we get the letter, they will be tradable securities."

It's uncertain who would make a market in this special sort of Trump securities, but it's expected that somebody would. "They wouldn't be listed. It would probably be some sort of over-the-counter market," says Arthur Newman, a Chemical Bank restructuring specialist hired to advise the Resorts creditors.

"Creditors feel there is a very significant cause of action which should yield a large return," he says.

Mr. Trump declined to comment.

But an attorney for Mr. Trump says "Mr. Griffin structured a takeover that piled on more debt. It's as clear as can be that Griffin and not Trump was responsible for the financial debacle at Resorts." Under the reorganization plan, however, debenture holders would release Mr. Griffin from acquisition-related claims.

To put some teeth into the trust, Resorts will endow it with a litigation kitty of $5 million. And the trust has hired lawyer Kenneth Feinberg, a heavyweight partner of Kaye, Scholer, Fierman, Hays & Handler, to serve as trustee and to spearhead any litigation.

Meanwhile, Mr. Trump and Resorts debenture holders have been trying to negotiate a settlement. Both sides may have an incentive for doing so now.

Currently, the creditors committee could approve any settlement. But once Mr. Feinberg takes over as trustee, any deal would need the approval of a majority of unit holders. Both sides are thus likely to spend much more time and money on the case if a settlement isn't reached fairly soon.

Creditors were once dreaming of collecting $100 million or so, but that was before Mr. Trump's financial distress became household news. Some people close to the matter now say the litigation-related units could be worth a total of $20 million to $50 million (including any unspent portion of the litigation kitty).

Mr. Trump, whatever his net worth, still controls hundreds of millions of dollars in assets. But he is believed to be using his troubles to his advantage. "He can argue that even if we prevail, what do we have?" one creditor says. "That's how talks have been going."

If the matter isn't settled out of court, it's anybody's guess where the units will trade.

"They are clearly worth something, but I haven't figured out how much," says William Teitelbaum, a Resorts debenture holder and a member of the creditors committee.

"There are two issues. Is there a significant judgment that can be won? It's my belief that there is. The real issue is: Is it collectable? I haven't seen Trump's financial statements."

Mr. Trump sold his controlling interest in Resorts stock for $96 million; he also received roughly $60 million from Resorts in exchange for terminating his management contract with the company. And, in addition, Mr. Trump purchased back from Resorts the then-unfinished Taj Mahal casino for $273 million in cash.

"What the bondholders have on their side is common sense," one bondholder claims. "Resorts was sold in November of 1988. The deal put $325 million of additional debt on its books. By June of '89 they were saying they couldn't make interest payments." And six months after that, the company filed for bankruptcy-court protection.

An additional possible claim is that Mr. Trump underpaid for the Taj. However, given the Taj Mahal's uncertain state, that claim is considered weaker.

One point in Mr. Trump's favor is that while fraudulent-conveyance suits have been a hot topic, the area, as it applies to modern leveraged buy-outs, is uncertain and relatively new.

Another point is that $50 million of the money used to purchase Mr. Trump's stock was invested by Mr. Griffin. Thus, Mr. Trump could argue that this portion of the payment didn't directly weaken Resorts.

If the units do begin trading and are offered cheaply, bankruptcy specialists note that Mr. Trump might want to acquire some and reduce his exposure.

"If they get cheap enough, I'll want to buy them," one debenture holder says. "But I don't know what cheap enough is."
