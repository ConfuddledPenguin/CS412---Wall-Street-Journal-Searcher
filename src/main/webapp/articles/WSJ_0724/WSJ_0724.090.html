Fed Governor Seger, Staunch Supporter Of Easier Credit, May Resign This Year

  • Author: Alan Murray
  • Date: 07/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Martha Seger, the Federal Reserve Board's most consistent advocate of easier money, said she may resign this year for personal reasons. "I'll probably be making a decision by the end of the year on whether or not I have to leave," Ms. Seger said in an interview yesterday. "I'm going to play it by ear."

Ms. Seger said she may have to resign to care for her elderly mother, who lives in Arizona and has recently undergone hip-replacement surgery and suffered a stroke.

If Ms. Seger resigns, the Bush administration is unlikely to find another reserve board governor who will be more consistent in pushing for lower interest rates than Ms. Seger. But they may be able to find a replacement who has more clout with her Fed colleagues. Ms. Seger is considered a maverick by most of her fellow policy makers at the Fed, and is thought to have little influence in the policy-making process.

Appointed in 1984, Ms. Seger still has eight years to go in her 14-year term. But according to friends, she has increasingly complained of getting poor treatment from top Fed staff, saying they sometimes don't even bother to inform her of important meetings. In the telephone interview yesterday, Ms. Seger acknowledged her differences with the Fed staff, but said that isn't the reason she's considering leaving.

Her resignation would give President Bush an opportunity to make two new appointments to the Fed's seven-member Board of Governors. Fed Vice Chairman Manuel Johnson already has announced he plans to resign early next month.

The Senate earlier this year confirmed Mr. Bush's first appointee to the Reserve Board, former Treasury official David Mullins.

In the interview yesterday, Ms. Seger put herself at odds with Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan by saying she believes the economy now stands on the brink of recession. "I think we are at risk," she said. "Whenever you get growth very, very, very slow, it's easy to tip from modest black ink into red ink."

She also said she's very concerned that a credit crunch may be having widespread effects on the economy. "This is one of the drums I've been beating for some months now," she said. "I first started to hear it from builders. . . . But it has spread way beyond that. I'm hearing stories from bigger businesses in the Northeast whose banks are reacting to tough regulatory treatment. And a number of car dealers, who are not exactly teeny, tiny businesses, have found their bank lines cut."

Ms. Seger, 58 years old, has a Ph.D. in finance and business economics from the University of Michigan. Before coming to the Fed, she was a professor of finance at Central Michigan University and banking commissioner for the state of Michigan.
