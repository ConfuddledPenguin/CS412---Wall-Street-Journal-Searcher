Marathons Keep Track Of Runners With Tags

THE PHOTO FINISH may become a quaint anachronism as sports events and auto races begin using digital radio-tag technology.

Contestants wear wristwatch-sized devices that emit a unique low-power signal used to identify and time them. The tags already have been tried on a test basis in the Los Angeles Marathon, in triathlons and in cycling and wind-surfing races.

William Burke, president of the marathon, says several companies offered to supply radio-tag systems, from giants such as American Telephone & Telegraph Co. to a start-up called American Sports Timing Inc. of Topsfield, Mass. He says they chose the start-up's system because it "seems to be the one that is farthest along, with the best accuracy." Mr. Burke says correctly timing the 20,000 entrants whom the race attracts has become "a logistical nightmare."

David McGillivray, technical coordinator of the Boston Marathon, calls radio-tag technology "the wave of the future" and says the nation's oldest marathon is considering it for future races.

Davy McKee Corp., a British company that makes radio tags for industrial and transportation applications, says its customers have come up with some surprising uses. Hospitals use the system to keep track of wandering patients, while others have begun using them to prevent mix-ups or even kidnapping of babies.

Relief Is Coming Soon

For Lousy PC Graphics

NEW CHIPS in the pipeline promise big enhancements to the power of PCs.

Researchers at Edsun Laboratories Inc., a chip designer in Waltham, Mass., have developed a device that squeezes stunning graphics performance out of an ordinary $400 color computer monitor. The chip, which plugs into the standard graphics boards inside most computers, displays as many as 790,000 colors -- compared with just 256 for the board by itself -- and smooths out the jagged edges that appear when curved or diagonal lines are drawn on most screens.

The chip lets pixels, individual points of light on a screen, display two colors at once. In conventional designs, pixels display just one color, causing the jagged edges and limiting the color palette. The chip makes PC screen resolution almost as good as that of a $10,000 workstation, producing almost photo-quality images.

Steven D. Edelson, chairman, says several computer makers are negotiating to use the chip, which would cost about $15 each, in forthcoming products. He wouldn't name the companies.

Another chip maker, Chips & Technologies Inc., has developed add-in PC accelerator boards to speed up specific software programs such as Lotus 1-2-3 and Microsoft Windows. While accelerators aren't new, tailoring them for specific mainstream software packages is unusual. As software becomes more complex and slower-running, demand for these turbochargers could take off.

Industry insiders say Chips & Technologies is discussing some novel marketing approaches with major software makers. The company hopes to be ready to show a Windows booster at Comdex, the big fall computer exposition.

Large Computer Buyers

Found a Software Clique

THE STRUGGLE over software standards has computer makers forming cabals and consortiums of every stripe.

Now, fed up with the fractious process of setting standards, a few big corporate computer purchasers have formed a clique of their own.

Dubbed the Houston 30, for the city where the first meeting took place, the informal group is made up of computer-systems managers from some of the nation's largest purchasers of computers, including General Electric Co., Eastman Kodak Co., General Motors Corp. and the National Aeronautics and Space Administration. Together, they represent billions of dollars in buying power.

"There's a growing frustration among corporations over the slow progress toward achieving open systems," says Bud Huber, a Hughes Aircraft Co. network systems manager. The group, which meets again in September, hopes to force faster progress toward standards that allow different companies' computers to run the same software.

There's a lot at stake for these companies -- and for computer makers -- in the shift to open standards. Buyers want standards so they don't get locked in to a particular company's hardware design. Computer makers, some customers suspect, want to slow the emergence of true standards to protect fat profit margins.

The group was especially critical of the impasse between AT&T's Unix International software unit and the Open Software Foundation, a group backed by International Business Machines Corp., Hewlett-Packard Co. and Digital Equipment Corp. They called the failure of the groups to agree on a standard "a classic example of vendors making their {interests} paramount to the detriment of the customer base."

In Software Industry,

Big Shots Get Bigger

CONSOLIDATION continues apace in software, as mergers have erased half of the companies that were in the top 10 of Software Magazine's Top 50 list five years ago.

But the Big Three just got bigger: Computer Associates International Inc. was tops in 1989 with $1.29 billion in world-wide sales, while Microsoft Corp. surged into second past Lotus Development Corp. with sales of $691 million. Lotus, with sales of $556 million, came in third in the $25 billion world market for packaged software, the magazine said.
