Amax's Net Fell 57% in 2nd Period; Asarco's Was Flat

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 07/24/90

NEW YORK -- Amax Inc. posted a 57% decline in second-quarter net income, reflecting a slide in aluminum prices. Meanwhile, Asarco Inc.'s earnings were nearly flat, as increases in lead, zinc and gold prices were offset by a decline in copper prices.

Amax, an aluminum, gold and energy company, reported net fell to $56.9 million, or 65 cents a share, from $130.9 million, or $1.53 a share, a year earlier. Operating profit declined 44%, to $102 million from $181 million.

Sales fell 8.9% to $984.7 million from $1.08 billion. Aluminum accounted for nearly two-thirds of Amax's total sales. Amax said industry ingot prices declined about 26% but added that the prices appear to have bottomed out.

Allen Born, Amax chairman and chief executive, said the company was "encouraged" by its performance so far in light of the continuing sluggish economy. The quarter's results included several one-time items, whose net effect was an after-tax gain of $6.1 million, a spokesman said.

The second-quarter results were slightly lower than analysts had predicted. Vahid Fathi, an analyst with Prescott, Ball & Turben in Cleveland, said in a phone interview that 1989 was a record year for Amax earnings.

For the six months, earnings fell 56%, to $106.9 million, or $1.22 a share, from $245.1 million, or $2.87 a share, a year ago. Sales fell 7.3%, to $1.91 billion from $2.06 billion.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Amax shares fell 62.5 cents to $25.50.

Asarco, a nonferrous metals company that also has interests in specialty chemicals, reported second-quarter net of $50.2 million, or $1.21 a share, nearly flat with $50.8 million, or $1.20 a share a year ago.

Sales rose slightly, to $543.1 million from $539.3 million.

Richard de J. Osborne, chairman, president and chief executive, said the results "reflect continued good market fundamentals" for the primary metals Asarco produces.

Mr. Fathi said he doesn't believe the "strength in copper is sustainable on a fundamental basis." The analyst said the recent rise in copper prices, to $1.30 a pound, was a result of disruptions in supplies by strikes and political unrest in several copper-producing countries.

For the six months, Asarco posted a 28% decline in net, to $86 million, or $2.07 a share, from $119.3 million, or $2.83 a share, a year ago. Sales fell about 8% to $1.06 billion from $1.16 billion.

Asarco also said union members at its Ray unit in Arizona overwhelmingly ratified a wage agreement. About 650 workers struck the mine July 1 after the previous contract expired; workers began returning to work last weekend.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Asarco shares also fell 62.5 cents to $27.50.
