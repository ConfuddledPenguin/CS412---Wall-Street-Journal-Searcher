Student Loan Guarantors End Talks For Merger, Citing Federal Opposition

  • Author: Gary Putka
  • Date: 07/24/90

An effort to solve the financial woes of a big student-loan guarantor was dropped in the face of federal opposition, leaving open the possibility of bank losses unless the government agrees to a full rescue. The guarantor, Higher Education Assistance Foundation, said it ended merger talks with Nebraska Student Loan Program Inc. The merger was aimed at transferring some or all of HEAF's $9.6 billion in guarantees of high-defaulting student loans to the Nebraska agency. The Education Department is developing its own plan to resolve HEAF's financial crisis, but details aren't known.

HEAF, a nonprofit corporation based in Overland Park, Kan., is incurring deficits because of high defaults on loans it guarantees. It portrayed itself as near collapse last week to try to sell the Nebraska transaction in Washington, according to people HEAF briefed.

The Nebraska agency also disclosed that "two or three" other guarantee agencies have approached it seeking to unload loan guarantees but that these discussions have also been terminated. The disclosure supported speculation in the student-loan industry that financial problems are spreading because of high defaults and aren't limited to HEAF. There are about 55 guarantee agencies in the aid program, standing behind about $51 billion in educational loans.

A spokeswoman for the Education Department said the department rejected the HEAF merger proposal because the Nebraska agency could have been overwhelmed by HEAF's size and because the proposal would have unfairly extended a valuable exemption to HEAF under the loan program.

When students default under the loan program, the guarantee agencies must usually pay lenders 100% of the unpaid principal, which qualifies them for reimbursement from the Education Department. But when agencies' default reimbursements hit certain levels, the government pays them only 80% of loan value. This is how HEAF is losing money.

The Nebraska agency, because it is relatively new, receives 100% reimbursement unconditionally and will continue to for a year. HEAF wanted to transfer a large portion of its riskiest guarantees to the agency to secure full repayment.

HEAF says it has met payments on all lenders' default claims but that its position has been worsened by the Education Department's disclosure of its financial troubles last week.

"The public representation of this information has caused, for understandable reasons, members of the lending community to lose confidence in the HEAF guarantee," Mr. Westbrook said. "Such loss of confidence obviously puts us in an untenable position. We have been told (by the Education Department) that the option of merging with Nebraska is unacceptable and we will receive a proposed solution imminently. We have not received that solution."

Nebraska Student Loan Program said one of the reasons it was talking to HEAF was to protect Nebraska Higher Education Loan Program -- a buyer of loans that repackages them as bonds -- against possible losses if HEAF fails to honor its guarantees. Nebraska Higher Education Loan Program "holds a large volume" of HEAF-guaranteed paper, Nebraska Student Loan said. "Any difficulty HEAF may have had in honoring its commitments would be of significant concern" to the Nebraska loan repackager, it added.

One of HEAF's largest creditors is Student Loan Marketing Association. Sallie Mae, which extended an $865 million credit line to HEAF last December, is one of the country's largest holders of student loans, which it purchases wholesale from lenders. Sallie Mae's stock plunged $4.125 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday to close at $49.875 a share.

A spokeswoman for Sallie Mae yesterday reiterated a statement that the company "would not suffer any material loss" because of HEAF's problems. "We don't believe there is any doubt we would be able to collect 100% of what we've advanced to HEAF," she said.
