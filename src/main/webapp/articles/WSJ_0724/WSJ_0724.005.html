Exxon Profit Rose in Second Quarter, But Amoco and Arco Earnings Declined

  • Author: Allanna Sullivan and Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 07/24/90

Second-quarter earnings increased at Exxon Corp., while profits declined at two other major oil companies, Amoco Corp. and Atlantic Richfield Co. Overall, oil companies registered sharply higher profitabililty in their gasoline refining and marketing businesses in the quarter. Results from oil-production operations were mostly lower as crude prices declined, and the petrochemicals business also remained in the doldrums.

Unusually long maintenance turnarounds at some refineries helped reduce inventories and keep product prices high. Also, a sharp drop in the price of crude oil, the raw material from which refined products are made, "gave a boost to product prices as well," said William Brown III, oil analyst for Kidder Peabody & Co.

Mr. Brown and other analysts predict, however, that the strong refining margins of the second quarter will decline this quarter, leaving major integrated oil companies with flat to lower results.

Exxon missed out on the second-quarter profit runup in the refining and marketing side of the business, despite an overall increase in earnings. As other companies reaped profits from this sector, one of Exxon's major refineries was closed for an unexpectedly long time, and two others were idled for routine maintenance. An Exxon spokesman said that a portion of the decline could be blamed on the closures, but he declined to elaborate.

Exxon's large Bayway refinery in New Jersey was closed for several months. In January, a break in an underwater pipeline there dumped more than 500,000 gallons of oil into New York Harbor, and several other incidents at the Bayway docks prompted Exxon officials to close the facility for an internal study of the operation in addition to scheduled maintenance. Refineries in California and Texas were also down for planned maintenance in the period.

Exxon said profit rose to $1.1 billion, or 87 cents a share, from $140 million, or 11 cents a share, a year earlier. The 1989 figure includes an $850 million provision to cover cleanup of the giant Alaskan oil spill. Earnings before the provision were $990 million, or 78 cents a share. Revenue increased 10% to $26 billion.

Refining and marketing operations overseas were especially profitable, with earnings climbing to $498 million from $141 million.

Amoco, based in Chicago, said net fell 24% to $379 million, or 75 cents a share, from $497 million, or 96 cents a share, a year earlier. The latest quarter included a gain of $471 million from a settlement with Iran that was offset by a $477 million charge for expected environmental clean-up costs, mostly in the company's gasoline refining and marketing business. Revenue rose 9.4% to $7.45 billion from $6.81 billion.

Because of the environmental costs, Amoco had a $125 million loss in refining and marketing. Without the charge, the company's refining sector's profit rose to $292 million from $213 million because of higher margins and sales. Chemical results declined because of lower prices and charges for environmental remediation.

Oil prices that were more than $2.50-a-barrel lower than a year earlier sharply reduced U.S. exploration profits, to $82 million from $143 million. Exploration and production results were also hurt by higher expenses overseas and unfavorable currency effects.

Arco net fell 16% to $391 million, or $2.36 a share, from $467 million, or $2.66 a share, a year earlier, as record refining profits weren't enough to offset lower exploration and production results. Revenue declined 6.9% to $4.04 billion from $4.33 billion.

The company, based in Los Angeles, said the average price it received for domestic crude fell $3.57 a barrel to $10.61 in the quarter. Natural gas prices also fell. As a result, exploration and production profit dropped to $96 million from $229 million.

But Arco's refining and marketing results nearly doubled to $175 million from $91 million because of strong gasoline prices and the lower oil prices.

In addition, Arco increased its first-quarter net income $45 million, or 27 cents a share, to $592 million, or $3.55 a share. Because of an unusual accounting situation arising from Arco's equity investment in Lyondell Petrochemical Co., the Securities and Exchange Commission advised Arco to recognize some dividend income differently.

The wide refining margins boosted earnings for Ashland Oil Inc., almost exclusively a refiner and marketer, to a record $100 million, or $1.79 a share, in the third quarter ended June 30, more than double the year-earlier $43.5 million, or 78 cents a share. Sales rose slightly to $2.24 billion from $2.21 billion.
