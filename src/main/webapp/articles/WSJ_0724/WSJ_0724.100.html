World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Fall, Due to Bond Price Weakness; London Shares Sink in Tandem With Wall Street

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/24/90

Tokyo stocks withdrew further, with prices displaying widespread bearishness on the Japanese government bond market's weakness, as well as cautiousness amid busy arbitrage selling, traders said. Meanwhile, London share prices sank in tandem with Wall Street to close sharply lower, as few issues were able to escape the general onslaught.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues fell 526.73 points to 31894.79. The drop represents a retrenchment of 1277.49 points, or 3.9%, in the past week.

At the end of the morning session Tuesday in Tokyo, the Nikkei index was down 67.66 points to 31827.13.

Volume on the first section Monday was estimated at 300 million shares, down from 503.6 million shares Friday. Losers widely outpaced advancers 861 to 151, while 110 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section fell 33.51 points, or 1.42%, to 2329.92. The Topix also was under selling pressure by arbitragers, though the average still managed to outperform the Nikkei Index.

The second section index fell 39.45 points, or 0.90%, to 4360.11. Volume was estimated at 13.5 million shares, down from 17.7 million shares Friday.

Traders said they knew before arriving in the morning that the bond market was bound to remain weak after Friday's move by the Bank of Japan to call 1.35 trillion yen ($9.07 billion) of bank loans had jostled yields out of a tolerable range.

In an unusual and reverse move Monday morning, the Bank of Japan purchased overnight commercial paper twice for a total of 500 billion yen, but the attempt to put a cap on yields was rather ineffective, traders said.

The stock market paralleled the bond market's unwinding, although a lack of volume indicated more uncertainty than panic, traders said.

At the close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the yield on the benchmark, the No. 119, 4.8% coupon of 1999 was up 9.5 basis points, or hundredths of a percentage point, at 7.47%, the weakest closing yield for the No. 119. The price was down 0.42, at 85.75. The yield traded to a six-year high of 7.475% before the stock exchange close.

Moreover, front-month September bond futures prices were also hitting lows not seen since early February, traders said. Indicating further concern about the direction of interest rates, the move in bond futures encouraged greater liquidation by stock market participants, traders added.

J. Graham Biggart, head of futures and options trading at Schroder Securities, said that one of the major bearish conditions of the market right now is the vast number of investors holding long positions and who are being forced to liquidate because of margin calls.

Buying opportunities are limited as it is, with interest rate concerns damping such sentiment. But with the margin calls, any small rallies will immediately be taken as a chance to sell off, he said.

Meanwhile, the yen is beginning to gain interest with rates at high levels, although there hasn't yet been a notable advance. Rumors and anxieties that the arrest of the head of a major Tokyo stock speculator group Thursday could lead to another Japanese political scandal have prompted some dollar-buying.

Should the yen manage to strengthen substantially, however, stock prices could receive some help, traders said, as domestic-demand related stocks might rally.

Financial shares were broadly lower.

Mitsui Trust fell 20 yen to 1560 yen, and Mitsubishi Trust fell 40 yen to 1830 yen.

Nomura Securities dropped 30 yen to 2150 yen, while Nikko Securities slipped 30 yen to 1180 yen.

Construction shares were lower. Nippon Hodo fell 200 yen to 3610 yen, Ohbayashi Corp. slipped 30 yen to 1650 yen, Taisei eased 30 yen to 1260 yen, and Sekisui House fell 40 yen to 2070 yen.

Many high-technology shares also fell. Sony dropped 90 yen to 8800 yen, and Fanuc fell 60 yen to 7350 yen.

In London, most of the session was listless, as investors found scant reason for getting involved in the market during the usually stagnant summer.

But once New York opened, London stocks sagged under the weight of Wall Street's losses. U.K. stocks rebounded from lows just before the close.

"It came quickly and sharply and obviously sentiment has been knocked in our market. But I wouldn't say there was an awful lot of selling pressure," one market maker said.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index settled at 2359.7, just slightly better than the late afternoon low of 2352.5 and well below the opening intraday high of 2406.3. The index was off 40.4 points on the day.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index fell 31 points to 1867.1. Volume stood at a relatively meager 369.8 million shares compared with 437.6 million Friday.

There were few stocks that went against the general downward bias. Of these, Lasmo added 12 pence to 434 pence (22 cents to $7.89) ahead of its interim earnings tomorrow, and Burmah Oil gained on a buy recommendation from a small regional-based U.K. brokerage firm.

Also bucking the lower trend was food maker Ranks Hovis McDougall, which advanced 7 pence to 347 pence. Anglo Group, one of Sir James Goldsmith's bid vehicles, said it will offer holders the opportunity to receive listed Ranks Hovis shares in exchange for their now-unlisted investment in Anglo.

The day's biggest loser was Reuters, which shed 38 pence to #12.29 ahead of interim figures set for disclosure today.

After the close of trading, U.K. conglomerate Hanson said it has held discussions with the government that could lead to the private sale of PowerGen, an electricity generator. Lord Hanson, chairman of Hanson, confirmed the company has a "firm interest" in making a bid for PowerGen. Hanson fell 7.25 pence to 233 pence.

Thorn EMI shed 24 pence to 735 pence. Dealers said the stock was sold on rumors of an imminent acquisition.

Elsewhere, shares closed lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Madrid and Milan. South African gold stocks closed higher. Brussels was closed for a holiday.

Also, stocks closed lower in Sydney, Taipei, Seoul, Wellington and Manila, mixed in Singapore, and higher in Hong Kong.
