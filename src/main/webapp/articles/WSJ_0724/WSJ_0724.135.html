What's News -- World-Wide

  • Date: 07/24/90

BUSH SAID he will nominate appellate judge David Souter to the Supreme Court. Just three days after liberal Justice Brennan resigned for health reasons, the president selected Souter, a former New Hampshire Supreme Court jurist, for his first appointment to the nation's highest court. Bush, in introducing Souter at a White House news conference, said he hadn't asked the 50-year-old judge's opinions on such key topics dividing liberals and conservatives as abortion and civil rights. Souter's name is believed to have been given to Bush by his chief of staff, former New Hampshire Gov. Sununu.

Souter, though a conservative, lacks any reputation for the kind of conservative judicial activism that aroused such fierce opposition to Reagan's nomination of Robert Bork in 1987.

---

Aspin announced he'll join an effort to kill funding for the B-2 bomber, representing a serious blow to Bush's effort to build more of the radar-evading planes. The chairman of the House Armed Services Committee, a Wisconsin Democrat, said he will press to stop production of the B-2 bomber beyond those that are already in development.

---

The Bush administration told makers of six semiautomatic assault-style rifles banned from import last summer that new designs of the weapons conform to U.S. laws, according to the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms. A bureau spokesman didn't know if makers had sought import permits for any of the new weapons.

---

All 79 opposition lawmakers quit South Korea's National Assembly as the governments of South Korea and North Korea wrangled over a plan to open their border to free travel. The opposition politicians warned that President Roh was trying to use relations with North Korea to divert the public from domestic problems.

---

The House passed a bill requiring broadcasters to provide children's television programming and to limit commercial time during children's shows. The Senate passed a similar bill last week, but that version included a provision to establish an endowment for creating educational shows for children.

---

Greece's parliament ratified a new defense treaty with the U.S. that will allow two American military bases to remain on the island of Crete for at least eight more years. The decision ends more than 18 months of uncertainty about the U.S. bases following the expiration of a prior treaty.

---

Liberian rebel fighters waded across a swamp and shot their way into the center of Monrovia, surprising government troops who had been defending two bridges leading into the capital. A U.S. official quoted President Doe as saying he was being held hostage by his own troops.

---

West German Chancellor Kohl will meet East German Prime Minister de Maiziere on Thursday to discuss a controversy over pan-German elections that has shaken East Germany's coalition. De Maiziere insists that separate votes in the two Germanys precede unification, while two coalition partners want a single election after the union.

---

The Pentagon said it was ready to begin moving U.S. chemical weapons stored in West Germany to a remote Pacific island where the Army is testing an incinerator for large-scale destruction of the arms.

---

The CIA denied involvement in Italian terrorism and the assassination of Swedish Prime Minister Palme as investigations into allegations of the agency's role got under way in Sweden and Italy. A CIA spokesman called the charges "absolute nonsense."

---

A last-minute electrical glitch forced NASA to postpone its launch of an unmanned Atlas rocket with a government satellite to study the Earth's magnetic field. NASA said the launch was delayed because the agency couldn't tell whether the rocket had switched over to internal power.

---

The U.S. and EC delayed a showdown over farm subsidies as negotiators launched a bid to make progress at Geneva talks aimed at a more open global economy. But Washington and European officials said there are still policy differences, and the U.S. said it would press for elimination of the trade-distorting export subsidies.

---

Five North African nations ended a two-day summit, vowing to speed up economic integration. Algeria, Libya, Morocco, Tunisia and Mauritania also called for stronger ties with the European Community.

---

More than 250,000 Bulgarians marched in Sofia to honor the late Communist hero Dimitrov in a massive show of support for the ruling former Communist Party.
