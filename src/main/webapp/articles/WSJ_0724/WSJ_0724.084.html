Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Xerox Drops Backer Spielvogel for Y&R

  • Author: Joanne Lipman
  • Date: 07/24/90

Backer Spielvogel Bates lost one of its most prestigious clients yesterday, as Xerox Corp. pulled its $25 million-plus account and handed it over to Young & Rubicam. The switch followed a highly-guarded two-month review pitting all four of Xerox's U.S. agencies, including Backer, against one another. The scope of the change came as a surprise to many in the industry, who had expected Xerox to yank only the corporate portion of its business, not its product advertising. Backer had handled both types of advertising; its "We document the world" campaign ended in November, while its newest campaign centers on Xerox's recent Malcolm Baldrige National Quality award.

Xerox and the agencies wouldn't publicly discuss billings, but industry estimates range from $25 million to $40 million. According to Leading National Advertisers, Xerox spent about $18.2 million on U.S. advertising last year.

The account move deals yet another blow to Backer Spielvogel Bates, the Saatchi & Saatchi Co. unit that had handled Xerox since 1986. Backer earlier this year suffered a painful loss when Prudential Insurance pulled its $60 million account. The agency also has been a poor new business performer in the U.S.

Carl Spielvogel, Backer Spielvogel's chairman and chief executive officer, declined to comment on the Xerox loss. But he defended his agency's record saying: "Our business is strong. On a global basis we have picked up $460 million in new business this year, and our year isn't over until September 30." He said most of the wins came in Europe, followed by Asia, but he added that the U.S. agency is ahead of last year in billings due to "good growth from existing clients. We have to gear up our new business efforts, and that's what we're doing."

The account loss couldn't come at a worse time for Backer. With parent Saatchi hit by declining earnings paired with a heavy debt load, rumors have abounded that Backer would be put on the block.

But in an interview yesterday, Robert Louis-Dreyfus, Saatchi's new chief executive, dismissed the speculation as "rubbish." He said that in the past three weeks, he has heard he is selling Backer to at least four different buyers, but that he has no plans to do so, nor has he ever: "I haven't changed my mind since the beginning."

"I echo the sentiment," Mr. Spielvogel said. "At a certain point, if the parent company decides to sell, they said they would talk to me and my management group first. I have no reason to doubt that." Mr. Spielvogel has been quite vocal about his desire to buy back his agency from troubled Saatchi.

For Young & Rubicam, meanwhile, the Xerox account is an important win. The privately held firm has handled Xerox in Europe for more than 20 years, and has long wanted to get its hands on the U.S. business.

A Xerox spokesman said Young & Rubicam's European experience was a clear plus, because "Xerox wants more integration between our corporate campaigns throughout the world." He said Xerox wants ads that can be adapted for different countries world-wide. The company's strategy, he added, will be to position itself as "the document company," offering everything from copiers to fax machines to computer networks. The new campaign is expected to break in September.

The win was the first following a reorganization at Young & Rubicam in May, under which Joseph DeDeo, formerly president of the agency's European operations, became president of the entire agency. Also pitching the account were Ogilvy & Mather and Lowe Marschalk, both of which will continue to handle direct response advertising for Xerox.

Ayer Gets Puritan Oil

Procter & Gamble pulled its Puritan Oil ad account from Grey Advertising and awarded it to Ayer, New York. According to Leading National Advertisers, a tracking firm, P&G spent about $14 million advertising the brand last year.

P&G, based in Cincinnati, said it switched the brand because it wanted to have its two trademark cooking oils handled by separate shops. Grey, based in New York, will continue to handle the Crisco brand.
