Politics & Policy: Measure to Investigate Thrift Debacle And Stiffen Fines Clears House Panel

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 07/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Judiciary Committee, propelled by fear of voter anger over the costly savings and loan crisis, approved legislation aimed at investigating the causes of the debacle and invigorating punishment of those responsible. The package of thrift-related measures passed by the committee in a voice vote includes increased spending for enforcement, new criminal penalties for fraud and the creation of a national commission to determine the origins of the S&L debacle and recommend solutions.

An amendment offered by Rep. George Sangmeister (D., Ill.) and approved by voice vote would require life sentences for the kingpins in S&L fraud schemes.

The thrift measures were added to a broader anti-crime bill that received final approval from the committee last night. The Senate earlier this month approved its own package of S&L legislation, which has similar goals but differs in many specifics from the measures approved by the House panel.

The Bush administration has backed many of the congressional proposals, but not all. For example, the White House strongly opposes the creation of a bipartisan investigative commission as an unnecessary new bureaucracy.

The House legislation would create new criminal offenses for concealing assets from a receiver of a failed thrift and for obstructing a government examination of a financial institution. The bill would also authorize $153 million for each of the next three years to hire more prosecutors and agents devoted strictly to cases involving financial institutions. That amount is $9.5 million a year less than what the Senate approved.

These provisions reflect lawmakers' tendency to stress that criminal activity caused many of the failures in the S&L industry. Charging that the Bush Justice Department has moved too slowly to investigate thrift fraud, Rep. Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.), lead sponsor of the House measures, said, "It is critical that the administration prosecute, convict and punish those who committed crimes against the S&L industry."

Most law enforcement officials, however, contend that the vast majority of thrift failures resulted from mismanagement, lack of government supervision and loosening of industry regulations by Congress in the early 1980s. Moreover, whatever new criminal statutes Congress ultimately passes won't apply retroactively to acts committed before the sanctions are signed into law by the president.

On the civil side, the House legislation would give prosecutors and bank regulators the authority to undo fraudulent transactions by thrift executives. Under the bill, S&L defendants would be barred from using the bankruptcy laws as a shield from court judgments, and regulators could prohibit "golden parachute" severance payments to thrift executives.
