Credit Markets: Bonds End Flat to Lower, Recovering From Losses As Investors Move Out of a Tumbling Stock Market

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 07/24/90

NEW YORK -- The bond market was whipsawed yesterday, initially battered by rising interest rates abroad then rebounding as U.S. stock prices plunged. Ultimately, the bond market ended unchanged to moderately lower with the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond posting a 1/4 point decline, or a loss of $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

"It was a very volatile and choppy trading day," said James Capra, senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc.

The bond market opened sharply lower with prices falling as much as 3/4 point in reaction to rising interest rates in Japan. Yields on Japan's benchmark 10-year bond rose to a six-year high of 7.475% yesterday, even though Japan's central bank tried to stabilize rates.

The increase in Japanese bond yields revived fears that Japanese investors, traditionally big buyers of U.S. Treasury bonds, may not participate aggressively in next month's multibillion-dollar quarterly Treasury-bond auction.

The Treasury's 10-year bond now yields just one percentage point more than Japan's 10-year bond, which some analysts say is too small of a spread to encourage Japan's big institutional investors to buy U.S. Treasurys.

The bond market then abruptly shifted gears as a plunge of more than 100 points in the Dow Jones Industrial Average took center stage.

"When you got the swan dive in the stock market, traders were climbing all over themselves to buy bonds," said F. Ward McCarthy, a managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates, Princeton, N.J.

The stock sell-off prompted some "flight-to-quality" buying among nervous institutional investors who sold stocks and reinvested the cash in the bond market. The Dow Jones Industrial Average staged a partial rebound later in the day and ended at 2904.70, down 56.44 points.

"If investors are getting out of stocks, then governments are the safest available alternative," said Charles Lieberman, a managing director at Manufacturers Hanover Securities Corp.

The bond rebound was not only the product of modest flight-to-quality buying, but also of traders reversing their bets on the market.

Alan McClymonds, co-head of government trading at First Boston Corp., described yesterday's market as "vicious" because many bond speculators lost large sums of money.

In early trading, many speculators took on short positions. In a short sale, dealers and speculators sell borrowed securities and later seek to make a profit by replacing what was borrowed with cheaper securities.

But when bond prices rose yesterday, these speculators were forced to cover their money-losing short positions by buying bonds at higher prices.

The losses didn't stop there. Mr. Capra at Shearson Lehman said speculators continued to buy bonds in a bet that stocks would fall further and lead to even higher bond prices. They were wrong again.

"The speculative side was in and out and lost money both ways," said Shearson Lehman's Mr. Capra.

The bond rally stalled out late in the day, however, as investors turned their attention to the bond market's numerous negative fundamentals. In addition to rising Japanese bond yields, investors are worried that budget-cutting negotiations between Congress and the White House have stalled and that the Federal Reserve appears to be abandoning its battle against inflation.

Treasury Securities

Treasury bonds ended little changed to lower in volatile trading.

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 102, down from 102 7/32 on Friday. The issue's yield rose to 8.55% from 8.53% on Friday. Two-year notes were quoted at 100 15/32 compared with 100 16/32 on Friday. The yield was unchanged at 8.07%.

Meanwhile, at the government's weekly Treasury bill auction short-term rates were set sharply lower.

The average discount rate on three-month Treasury bills was 7.49% at yesterday's auction while the rate on six-month bills was 7.40%. Those rates were down from last week's auction when they were 7.62% and 7.52%, respectively.

The average rate on three-month bills was the lowest since the average of 7.37% at the auction on Oct. 16 while the rate on six-month bills was the lowest since the average of 7.30% at the auction on Dec. 4, 1989.

Corporate Bonds

The tumble in stock prices rattled the junk bond market, sending the high-yield speculative-grade securities moderately lower.

Most junk bonds finished the session down 1/4 point to 3/8 point, the declines were modest compared to the sell-off in the stock market.

Among the high-yield bonds which ended the day lower were pay-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. Pay-in-kind bonds pay interest in the form of additional securities. The company's 17 3/8% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, which previously carried an interest rate of 14.07%, finished down 3/4 points to close at 117 on the New York Stock Exchange. The 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, which also had a coupon rate of 14.07%, ended down 1 point at 94 1/4.

The junk bonds of R.H. Macy & Co. continued to gain after news that the New York-based retailer asked investment banks to suggest ways for it to reduce debt and raise money. Macy's 14.5% senior subordinated debentures added 1 point to close at 76.

Time Warner Inc.'s convertible preferred stock slipped after it reported a second-quarter loss of $51 million. The company's 8 3/4% convertible preferred stock Series C ended down 1/4 point at 42 1/8.

Among new issues in the investment grade market, Spain received an enthusiastic welcome when it tapped the corporate debt market with its first underwritten U.S. debt offering.

Spain offered $300 million of 10-year notes which were priced to yield 9.125%.

Mark Seigel, managing director in the syndicate department at lead underwriter Morgan Stanley & Co., said the offering received such a rousing response from investors because it represents "the first opportunity for institutional investors to purchase a discrete Yankee bond offering" from the Kingdom of Spain.

Municipal Securities

Tax-exempt issues ended unchanged to 1/8 point lower, with some issues off 1/4 point in spots.

In secondary trading, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 were unchanged at 99 1/2 to yield 7.22%.

No large new municipal issues were priced during the session.

Looking ahead on the calendar, New York City will sell $1.1 billion of tax anticipation notes via competitive bid today.

Foreign Bonds

Japanese bond yields hit a six-year high yesterday as investors reacted negatively to a move by the Bank of Japan, the nation's central bank.

The BOJ bought a total of 500 billion yen ($3.36 billion) of overnight commercial paper in two separate purchases. A Bank of Japan official said the central bank was trying to offset a rise in rates after the markets misread a 1.2 trillion yen bank loan call the central bank made last Friday. He said the loan call was meant to drain excess bank reserves, and not a signal that short-term interest rates should rise.

But investors interpreted yesterday's action as a flip-flop in policy. "The BOJ reversed the move they made last week, which made the market even more upset because {the Bank of Japan} is flopping back and forth and is untrustworthy," said a dealer at a foreign-based securities firm. "The market is still negative about rates," said another dealer.

At the official 3:00 p.m. JST close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, Japan's benchmark No. 119 10-year bond was quoted at a price of 85.75, down 0.45 points, to yield 7.47%, according to Japan's Daiwa Securities Co. That yield was the highest for a benchmark 10-year bond in six years. During over-the-counter trading, after the Tokyo Stock Exchange closed, the yield on the benchmark bond rose further to 7.48%. (Japanese bond yields are quoted here in local terms. However, when calculated using the semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets, the yield on the No. 119 bond was 6.97%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc., New York.)

Japanese rates on new three-month certificates of deposit were unchanged yesterday at 7.7%. Corresponding Euroyen deposits, which are securities sold in Europe that pay interest in yen, were also flat at around 7.69%.

Mortgage, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended little changed.

Among actively traded secondary issues, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were off 1/32 at 99 3/4 to yield 9.67%. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% issues were unchanged at 99 3/ 32 and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were flat at 99 19/32.

In the primary market, trading began in two new issues, a real estate mortgage investment conduit and an asset-backed offering.

Fannie Mae unveiled details of a $500 million Remic mortgage issue, offered by Kidder, Peabody & Co. and backed by 9.5% Fannie Mae mortgage-backed securities.

And Sears Receivables Financing Group, a unit of Sears, Roebuck & Co., is offering $250 million of securities backed by credit-card receivables with a coupon rate of 8.70% that were priced at 99.855 to yield 8.75%.
