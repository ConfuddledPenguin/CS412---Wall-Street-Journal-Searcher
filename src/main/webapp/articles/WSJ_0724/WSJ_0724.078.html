REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Justice for Ollie -- and Walsh

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/24/90

Justice Brennan's retirement and the nomination of Judge David Souter to the Supreme Court have almost eclipsed what might have been the legal news of the month. So before elaborating on the Supreme Court, we'd like to pause a moment to note that in reversing Oliver North's convictions, the Federal Court of Appeals has ruled that the Iran-Contra prosecutions were indeed kangaroo trials. The court's tight reasoning Friday reversed all three North convictions -- one outright and the other two pending theoretically possible reinstatement after seemingly impossible further hearings. It ruled that the North convictions, and by implication presumably the John Poindexter conviction as well, may have been tainted by the misdeeds of Congress and Special Prosecutor Lawrence Walsh.

Congress forced Mr. North to testify in public during the summer of 1987. His testimony didn't go the way his inquisitors had planned; he became a national hero instead of an excuse to impeach President Reagan. The legal significance was that Congress forced Mr. North to abandon his right against self-incrimination under the Fifth Amendment. When a defendant is given immunity and forced to testify in public, any trial must proceed without any use in any way of the coerced testimony. No witnesses, prosecutors or jurors can be "tainted" by Mr. North's testimony to Congress.

At Mr. Walsh's urging, trial Judge Gerhard Gesell failed to hold a hearing on this issue. Now the appeals court says if anyone wants to keep pursuing Mr. North, there will have to be a hearing to "inquire into the content as well as the sources of the grand jury and trial witnesses' testimony." The court added that the inquiry "must proceed witness-by-witness; if necessary, it will proceed line-by-line and item-by-item."

"We readily understand how court and counsel might sigh prior to such an undertaking," the court said, in a not-very-subtle message to Mr. Walsh and/or Judge Gesell to call the whole thing off. The judges said such a hearing "could consume substantial amounts of time, personnel, and money, only to lead to the conclusion that a defendant" cannot get a fair trial.

Many witnesses admit hearing Mr. North's congressional testimony; one witness, Rep. Lee Hamilton, had earlier been one of his interrogators. As we wrote two years and millions of taxpayer dollars ago, the immunity issue was one of many that would have stopped any normal U.S. Attorney from bringing this case.

The Fifth Amendment here is no Miranda-style technicality. While reversing on narrower grounds, the appeals court saw here the same principle that led the Founding Fathers to include constitutional provisions banning ex post facto laws or bills of attainder. This was a political battle masquerading as a criminal case. The court wrote, "We do not countenance political trials in this country, and this matter is not styled Independent Counsel v. Executive Branch or even Congress v. Executive Branch."

It is now clearer than ever that Mr. Walsh should cut his losses and go home. Or President Bush should send him packing. Congress should forswear its triple punch of forcing a presidential aide to testify, then sicking a loose-cannon special prosecutor on him, then having the congressional testimony used in criminal court. We can hope that Mr. North's wrongful trial might be this country's last experiment with political prosecutions.
