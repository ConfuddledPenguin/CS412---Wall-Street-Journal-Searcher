Foreign Exchange: Dollar Falls to Lowest Level Against Mark In 2 1/2 Years on Big Decline in Industrials

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/24/90

NEW YORK -- A plunge on the New York Stock Exchange pulled the dollar to its lowest level against the mark in 2 1/2 years. The U.S. currency ended nearly 1% lower against its German counterpart, posting its worst close since the beginning of 1988. The dollar also fell substantially against the Swiss franc and other continental European currencies and to a lesser degree against the pound.

Meanwhile, profit-taking on long, or overbought, Canadian dollar positions -- led by trade in futures -- actually boosted the U.S. currency's price in Canadian dollars.

The dollar almost held its own against the yen, retreating only slightly. The Japanese currency was facing pressures similar to those on the dollar after a second consecutive sell-off of Tokyo stocks.

As yen holders sold the Japanese currency for dollars and then changed those dollars into marks, the U.S. currency was supported against the yen while its descent against the mark was exaggerated.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.6220 marks, well below 1.6365 marks late Friday in New York.

The U.S. currency held above 148 yen and was trading at 148.20 yen, down from 148.60 yen in New York late Friday. The mark rose to 91.30 yen from 90.80 yen.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the dollar was quoted at 148.20 yen, down from 148.50 yen there late Monday. The dollar was trading at 1.6230 marks.

The dollar also fell to 1.3815 Swiss francs Monday, 5.4445 French francs and 1188.3 Italian lire from 1.4010 Swiss francs, 5.4945 French francs and 1199.8 lire late Friday.

Sterling was trading at $1.8225, up from $1.8190 late Friday.

The last time the U.S. currency bought so few marks was Jan. 4, 1988, when the dollar ended at 1.5858 marks.

"We are getting to levels that are very significant," said Bernd Becker, chief New York dealer for Oesterreichische Laenderbank. "We've been dealing for so long in this narrow range. This is the biggest move I've seen in a long time. No one wanted to miss the boat, so everyone jumped on."

After emerging from a listless Asian day and European morning, the dollar felt steady selling pressure in New York as the Dow Jones Industrial Average lost ground right from the opening bell.

But only when the industrial average was down 102 points during the session did currency trading rooms awaken from their midsummer slumber to sell the dollar out of its accustomed range.

As the dollar crossed a divide at 1.63 marks, stop-loss selling sped its descent, said Andy Robertson, Chase Manhattan's corporate foreign exchange sales manager. In 10 minutes, the dollar fell an entire pfennig against the mark.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $369.10 an ounce, up $7.80. This was the highest close since May 22. Estimated volume was a very heavy six million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $368.45 an ounce.
