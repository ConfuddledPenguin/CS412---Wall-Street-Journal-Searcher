Marketing & Media: House Clears Bill Requiring Broadcasters To Provide Children's TV Programming

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 07/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The House passed a bill requiring broadcasters to provide children's television programming and to limit commercial time during children's shows. The Senate passed a similar bill last Thursday, but the Senate version includes a provision to establish a National Endowment for Children's Educational Television. The endowment would provide grants for creating and producing educational shows for children. Differences will have to be worked out with the House before the bill can be sent to President Bush.

It's uncertain whether the president would sign such a bill. Former President Reagan, citing First Amendment concerns, vetoed legislation to cap advertising time during children's shows and to require broadcasters to provide certain amounts of children's programming.

The legislation, which passed the House on a voice vote, requires the Federal Communications Commission to consider a broadcaster's track record on providing educational and informational children's programming when the broadcaster's license comes up for renewal.

While not threatening a veto, the administration said that evaluating children's programming as part of the license-renewal process "raises serious First Amendment concerns by permitting the government to deny license renewals if a station doesn't carry what the government views as adequate programming for children." The administration also said it opposed capping ad time, saying that limits could hold down revenues and perhaps even defeat the purpose of the bill.

Broadcasters didn't seem to share the Bush administration's concerns. Instead, they enthusiastically supported the measures. "The bill protects children from overcommercialization in their TV and cable programs, while acknowledging that advertising is what makes these programs possible," said James C. May, executive vice president, government relations, for the National Association of Broadcasters. "It spells out, for the first time in statute form, broadcasters' public-interest obligations to the child audience but avoids unnecessary and unconstitutional programming standards."

The bills would require broadcasters and cable operators to limit advertising during children's programming to 10 1/2 minutes an hour on weekends and 12 minutes an hour on weekdays. The FCC could review and modify those caps after Jan. 1, 1993.

Further, the House and Senate legislation directs the FCC to conduct a study of program-length commercials such as "G.I. Joe" and "Thundercats," with characters that are sold as toys.

The bills don't specify the number of hours that broadcasters must devote to children's educational shows but leave the evaluation of quantity and quality of children's programming up to the FCC at license renewal time.

"What's going to make this bill work is that it gives a hook -- to parents, teachers and others who are concerned about children -- to hang their concerns on," said Peggy Charren, head of Action for Children's Television Inc. "We believe that President Bush -- and Mrs. Bush -- will like it," she said.

Ms. Charren, who has been working to improve children's TV for more than 20 years, said the bill was even more necessary after the Reagan-era deregulation at the FCC. "With deregulation, what got lost was that television has to be meaningful for children, too," she said.

Andrew Schwartzman, head of Media Access Project, a public-interest law firm, also applauded the legislation. "I'm sure it will make a difference in programming because it sends a message that broadcasters must be responsible. Broadcasters should be concerned less with what they can get away with and more with how they are going to serve the public."
