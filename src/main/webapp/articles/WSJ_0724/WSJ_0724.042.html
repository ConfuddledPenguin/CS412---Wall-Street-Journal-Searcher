S&L Bailouts Put June Budget Into a Deficit

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 07/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Savings-and-loan bailout costs shoved the federal budget into a deficit in June, a month when corporate tax payments usually produce a surplus. The government ran an $11.22 billion deficit during the month, compared with a $7.79 billion surplus in June 1989, the Treasury said.

Weak corporate tax receipts were also to blame. They failed to reach year-earlier levels, declining to $18.57 billion from $20.88 billion. Overall tax receipts edged up to $110.61 billion from $108.25 billion. At the same time, outlays surged to $121.84 billion from $100.46 billion.

The slowing economy as well as the cost of taking over failed S&Ls and paying off insured depositors have wreaked havoc with the federal budget this year. Just last week, the Bush administration widened its estimate for the fiscal 1990 budget gap to $218.5 billion, including the thrift bailout costs. Nine months into the fiscal year, the deficit stands at $163.09 billion, the Treasury said.

"I was expecting about a $2 billion surplus, which would have been bad enough," said Stanley Collender, director of federal budget policy at the accounting firm Price Waterhouse. "This is close to extraordinary. I don't know how much longer you can get bad numbers without someone sitting up and taking notice."

The Bush administration and congressional leaders have stepped up the pace in their talks to reach an agreement to slash the ballooning budget deficit in fiscal 1991, but there were few signs of progress yesterday.

In June, the government spent $15.82 billion to resolve failed S&Ls, the busiest month to date for the bailout. Without that expense, the government would have posted a $4.6 billion surplus -- a modest number considering that June is a deadline for quarterly corporate income tax payments.

The government expects eventually to get back some of the money it spent taking over failed thrifts by selling off loans, securities, real estate and other assets inherited from the institutions. For that reason, some members of Congress argue that bailout costs shouldn't be included in the deficit tabulations.

But even without counting the thrift costs, the deficit would probably be larger than last year's $151.86 billion gap, partly because the slowing economy has crimped corporate profits, leading to lower-than-expected corporate tax receipts.

More than $42 billion of federal spending last month went to pay interest on the federal debt.
