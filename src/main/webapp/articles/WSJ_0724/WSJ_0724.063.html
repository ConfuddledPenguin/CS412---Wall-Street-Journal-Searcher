Kansas City Utility Seeks Kansas G&E --- Hostile Tender Offer Priced At $857 Million; Move Is Termed `New Ground'

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 07/24/90

Kansas City Power & Light Co. launched a surprise $27-a-share, or $857 million, tender offer for a neighboring utility, Kansas Gas & Electric Co. of Wichita. While other utilities have put unsolicited proposals before the boards of target companies, yesterday's bid appears to be the first major hostile tender offer in the heavily regulated electric industry.

The bid represents "new ground for the industry," said Prudential-Bache analyst Barry Abramson. "I don't think (Kansas Gas & Electric) will go down easily," he added, noting that the offering price contains a smaller premium to book value than have other recent acquisitions in the industry.

The move was born out of Kansas City P&L's frustration over its failure to obtain a friendly deal with its neighbor. The Kansas City, Mo., concern has made unsuccessful overtures for a combination over the past two years. The two companies already jointly own 94% of a nuclear generating plant.

Kansas City P&L said the proposed acquisition would allow it to make use of Kansas Gas & Electric's excess capacity, thus deferring the potentially costly expansion Kansas City P&L otherwise faces to satisfy its growing customer base. In addition, it projected advantages accruing from better utilization of plant assets, joining two complementary transmission systems and economies of scale.

Such calculations are behind a recent spate of acquisitions and mergers in the industry, where enlightened managers are increasingly seeking combinations to boost efficiency.

In March, for example, eyeing a similar discrepancy in capacity requirements, Iowa Resources Inc. and the slower-growing, capacity-rich Midwestern Energy Corp. definitively agreed to form a holding company that will combine the two utilities.

But unlike the situation in that friendly deal, Kansas City P&L opted for a tender offer, with a bid for Kansas Gas & Electric that was 37% above the Friday close for shares of the Wichita company. Friday's price represented a 52-week low, however, for the Wichita company, which is still financially recovering from earlier difficulties with its nuclear operations.

In Wichita, a spokesman for Kansas Gas & Electric declined to comment, saying the company was still reviewing the offer.

In composite New York Stock Exchange trading yesterday, Kansas G&E shares jumped $4.625, to close at $24.375. Yesterday's closing price shows investor wariness over the lengthy time a deal could take, and the numerous conditions on which it is predicated.

But the fact that the bidder has lined up bank financing and is actually making an offer indicates, as one trader put it, "these guys are very serious."

"Wilson, I want to emphasize our strong desire to achieve a negotiated transaction . . . Personally, I would have preferred to proceed with negotiations prior to commencement of our tender offer, but we were strongly advised to commence our offer before making a proposal to you," wrote A. Drue Jennings, Kansas City P&L president and chief executive officer, in a letter to Kansas Gas's chairman and president, Wilson K. Cadman.

The adviser on the bid is a team of investment bankers led by David Hedley, who left Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. in January, just before that firm collapsed. They now work for Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp.

Kansas City P&L said it estimated that savings of $85 million, over a five-year period, could be obtained from combining the company. In an interview, Mr. Jennings called that estimate "conservative."

But the company's short-term borrowings to fund the buy-out -- which could be avoided if the parties were involved in a friendly, stock-swap acquisition -- would work to offset a big part of those savings.

Salomon Brothers analyst Gregory B. Enholm finds much more significant the savings that would be available through Kansas City P&L's access to the target company's excess capacity. "If you look at the interest cost on borrowing a billion dollars" for the acquisition, buying extra capacity in the form of another utility "is cheaper than building a new power plant," he asserted.

Under Kansas law, an unsolicited bidder is restricted from voting its shares above 20%, unless, at a special shareholder meeting called for that purposes, stockholders of the target company confer voting rights on the suitor for the full amount of its shareholdings. Kansas City P&L filed suit yesterday in federal court in Kansas City, Mo., asking the court to order Kansas G&E to call such a meeting by no later than 50 days.
