Schwab Subsidiary Is Fined, Censured By the Big Board

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 07/24/90

NEW YORK -- The New York Stock Exchange censured and fined Charles Schwab & Co. $375,000 for allegedly misusing customers' stock for the firm's profit. The fine against the nation's largest discount broker is among the stiffest ever levied by the Big Board. And the case, similar to recent regulatory action against Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp., underscores the increased scrutiny regulators are placing on brokerage firms' back-office operations.

"In any examination of member firms, we'll be looking for these practices," said Regina Mysliwiec, a vice president in the Big Board's enforcement division.

Without admitting or denying wrongdoing, Schwab, a unit of San Francisco-based Charles Schwab Corp., agreed to a finding that it surreptitiously used customers' stock to participate in more than 150 tender offers or other reorganizations between 1985 and 1988, booking about $2.4 million in profits for the firm.

As part of the settlement, two Schwab executives were censured without admitting or denying the allegations. Barry Snowbarger, a senior vice president, agreed to a censure, a one-month suspension and a supervisory bar for 18 months from the firm's operation and credit areas. Stephen Cass, a vice president, consented to a censure and supervisory bar of 12 months, with the exception of direct project support staff, the exchange said.

Neither man was available for comment, a Schwab spokesman said.

Schwab, which said the practices involved less than 1% of its customers, repaid nearly $3 million to customers in early 1989 after company officials discovered the problem. In a statement, Charles R. Schwab, the firm's founder and chairman, said, "We have undertaken strong internal measures to prevent such practices in the future, including new policies governing each type of reorganization transaction, separation of functions and a complete restructuring of the reorganization department."

The firm's reorganization department, a back-office processing unit, handles tender and exchange offers, stock purchase rights and warrants, and convertible securities.

In at least 40 partial tender and exchange offers, Schwab's reorganization department tendered part of its customers' stock without permission from the investors, allegedly generating about $1.4 million in profits. This amount was said to come from keeping the difference between the funds it received for the tendered shares and the typically lower cost of repurchasing shares to replace the customers' stock.

In a similar fashion, Schwab allegedly made about $250,000 in profit through at least 47 conversion offers, and about $550,000 in profit through at least 79 rights and warrants offers, the Big Board said.

The firm also was required to conduct an outside review of the reorganization department and report the results to the exchange.

The action was made public after the market closed yesterday. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, Schwab stock closed at $15, down 37.5 cents.
