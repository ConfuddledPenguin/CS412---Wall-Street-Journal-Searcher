Federal Investigators Fail to Find Cause Of '89 Aircraft Crash

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/24/90

ATLANTA -- Federal officials investigating the fatal 1989 crash of a Southern Co. plane said they were unable to determine the cause of the on-board fire that brought down the aircraft. In its final report, the National Transportation Safety Board said it could find no evidence that a lightning strike, a mechanical malfunction or a bomb triggered the blaze aboard the Beech 200 April 10, 1989.

Jacob F. Horton, senior vice president of Southern's Gulf Power unit, died in the crash, along with the plane's two pilots. Transcripts from the cockpit voice recorder showed that the three men became aware of a fire at the plane's rear and the pilots attempted to radio for help about a minute before the aircraft slammed into an apartment complex in Pensacola, Fla.

The NTSB report said that the failure of the plane's crew to put on oxygen masks, to cut the effect of smoke and fumes in the cockpit, "contributed to the accident."

In a statement responding to the report, Southern Company Services Inc., the Southern unit that owned the plane, said it was disappointed that the fire's origin remains unknown. But Allen Franklin, the unit's president, said "perhaps additional information will come to light through the legal process."

Last year, lawyers for Mr. Horton's estate filed suit against the Southern subsidiary, alleging that sloppy maintenance and operation of the plane led to the crash. Southern Co. has since filed a counterclaim, alleging that actions taken by Mr. Horton himself caused the fire.
