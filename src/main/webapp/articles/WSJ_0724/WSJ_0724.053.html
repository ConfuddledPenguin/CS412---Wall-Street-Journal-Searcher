Sudden Slide: Growth Stocks Take A Pounding, Showing Market's Vulnerability --- In Quest for Safety, Investors Had Pushed Up the Prices Of Some Favored Issues --- Memories of the `Nifty Fifty'

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease and Craig Torres
  • Date: 07/24/90

NEW YORK -- If consumers aren't eating enough Big Macs, will we miss Dow 3000? The question isn't as far-fetched as it might seem. Late last Friday, McDonald's Corp., one of a select cadre of seemingly invulnerable growth stocks whose six-month-long rise drove the Dow Jones Industrials within a fraction of 3000, disappointed Wall Street with lower-than-expected earnings.

Yesterday, Wall Street took its revenge. Acting like jilted lovers, investors dumped McDonald's and other growth stocks, knocking the industrial average down 56.44 points to 2904.70 in heavy trading. The question now is whether yesterday's big decline is a mere interruption of the upward movement or something more serious, the start of a shift in sentiment that would kill any thought of a 3000 Dow for the rest of this year.

For months, investors have had an almost insatiable appetite for big, household-name growth stocks, such as McDonald's, Coca-Cola, Walt Disney and Procter & Gamble. And although these issues have begun to look expensive by some measures, many experts still see them climbing further. Much further.

"There's a very select group of stocks with good earnings-growth stories that are being pushed up," says Charles Clough, chief market strategist at Merrill Lynch & Co. "We're not convinced that's over yet. Since it's in such short supply, the price of growth is likely to keep going up."

But other analysts who only a week ago were predicting a surge above 3000 now see an ominous portent. "There's been a change in investor psychology," contends Kenneth Spence, director of the technical-analysis group at Salomon Brothers. "With weakness in earnings for autos, hamburgers and semiconductors, the earnings rebound that investors expected in the second half of the year is under a great deal of suspicion."

If nothing else, the Dow's dive yesterday -- it was down more than 105 points early in the day -- illustrates what can happen in a stock market dangerously dependent on a handful of supposed winners. Abby Joseph Cohen, the chief U.S. market strategist for Barclays Bank, puts it this way: "If the market starts to decline, what stocks will investors sell? They can only sell the stocks they own, and the growth stocks are what they own."

Of course, it isn't any surprise to most investors that as prices rise, which the growth stocks have been doing for the past several months, so inevitably do the risks. But the irony of the Dow's climb to 2999.75 last week, and the sudden plunge yesterday, is that investors have pushed up the average partly in a quest for safety. Amid fear late last year that the economy might slide into recession, investors sought out stocks that seemed relatively immune to economic cycles.

Big companies such as Merck, Gillette, Home Depot, Wal-Mart, Anheuser-Busch and Philip Morris became the darlings of Wall Street. Whether because of these companies' marketing muscle, unique products or their innovative management, investors became convinced that the firms would thrive whatever the environment. Consequently, prices of such companies' stocks rose much faster than anyone expects their earnings to grow. While the average Dow Industrials stock sells at a per-share price of about 14 times its annual earnings (for the Standard & Poor's 500, it's about 16), some of the growth stocks investors were snapping up through last week were selling at as high as 40 times earnings.

No matter. Some money managers argue that sustained growth in earnings is a valuable commodity, well worth the high prices they pay to own a piece of these companies. If this all sounds reminiscent of the "Nifty Fifty" phenomenon of the early 1970s, it is.

"My style is to be in what used to be known as the Nifty Fifty," says Roger Engemann, who heads a Pasadena, Calif., money-management firm. "We expect these stocks' earnings to grow at 20%-plus per year for the next five years. What's that worth? At the very least, it would be a 20 multiple."

Indeed, if the Nifty Fifty upsurge is repeated, earnings growth will be worth a lot more than Mr. Engemann's minimum. In the fervor of the market's fling with growth stocks in the early '70s, a price-earnings ratio of 20 was a bargain. In April 1972, Polaroid commanded a multiple close to 65 times earnings; Digital Equipment had a P/E ratio of 80; Walt Disney, a P/E of 78, and McDonald's, a P/E of 70. International Business Machines looked like a laggard at only 41.

By those standards, most growth stocks today still look awfully tempting, perhaps especially to investors who, stunned by the October 1987 stock-market crash, sat out much of the market's nearly 1,300-point recovery. "As these people come back, they're saying they want the very best," says Jack Orben, president of American Family Services, which manages money for wealthy individuals. "They find great comfort in high-quality growth stocks, a situation not terribly different from the 1972-73 period."

There's no denying the impressive returns to investors who purchased growth stocks a year ago. Then, before the growth-stock frenzy, an investor could have bought a portfolio of growth stocks at prices about equal to the overall price-earnings ratio of the broader market, says Ms. Cohen, Barclays Bank's strategist.

"That means you could have bought the "creme de la creme" of corporate America at no price premium at all," she says. Even after yesterday's slump, that same portfolio would cost a big premium.

James LaTorre, a portfolio manager at the Ivy Fund, has watched his Ivy Growth with Income Fund rise 7.5% so far this year, more than double the total return -- price plus dividends -- of 3.03% for the Standard & Poor's 500 stocks at the end of June. The fund is loaded with such growth stocks as Home Depot, Motorola, Boeing and Wal-Mart, all of which sport substantial premiums for their earnings.

"Price-earnings ratios are a moving target," Mr. LaTorre says. "As long as the stocks are sufficiently popular with institutions, you have to be in."

Many companies are lumped under the heading of growth stocks, and many of them deserve premium prices, analysts argue. Although Boeing, for example, carries a relatively rich price-earnings ratio of 25 times the past four quarters' earnings, analysts such as Hugh Johnson, chief investment officer at First Albany Corp., don't mind holding the stock in hopes of further gains. Not only does the aircraft maker have a huge order backlog, it also has managed in recent years to focus on the lucrative commercial market while paring its dependence on military sales.

"Boeing is a good example of a company that has changed enough to justify moving into uncharted waters," Mr. Johnson claims.

Gillette is another solid play, according to some investors. Mr. Orben says that in view of its new Sensor razor and its long-demonstrated marketing expertise, investors can be comfortable with Gillette's stock price despite a lofty P/E ratio of 23.5 times earnings. "It and stocks like it are just going to continue hitting new highs," he predicts.

But not every so-called growth stock will grow fast enough to justify fat premiums. What happens when such a stock, for whatever reason, loses a bit of its luster? A look at what has just happened to McDonald's shares shows just how fragile a reputation as a growth stock can be.

Basically, the news that McDonald's reported Friday didn't sound bad at all. Second-quarter net income rose 10%. International sales and earnings were robust. And although analysts had expected quarterly earnings of 60 cents a share, the company earned 59 cents -- hardly a major disappointment.

But the average company-owned restaurant did $4,000 less business than a year ago, and the average franchisee-operated one did about $10,000 less. McDonald's said it isn't satisfied with the performance and is weighing new menu strategies. To analysts, that news suggested stepped-up price discounting. McDonald's shares dropped $1.50 Friday and another $3 yesterday to close at $33. The declines brought the stock's price-earnings ratio down to 16.4 times the past four quarters' earnings, from 19 just before the financial results were announced.

Although that P/E is still at or above average, McDonald's prolonged stock rise and its plunge in a few days is somewhat reminiscent of the rise and the much-longer fall of the Nifty Fifty in the early '70s. Back then, skyrocketing oil prices, soaring labor and food costs and a tumbling dollar brought stagflation. Even the big growth companies couldn't stand up to the painful combination of a severe recession and surging inflation. By late 1974, Polaroid had tumbled to less than $8 a share from $74, and Xerox to $50 from about $150 earlier.

"You had a tremendous concentration that blew stocks to unrealistic values" in the early '70s, recalls John M. McCarthy, a 63-year-old portfolio manager of the $3.7 billion Affiliated Fund, who was running the fund during the 1970s growth-stock boom. "When you bought Polaroid in April 1972 at 65 times earnings, you were buying infinity."

Now, Mr. McCarthy worries about a rerun. "I'm staring at a pit out there," he frets. "If the market has been going straight up, with a concentration in a limited number of stocks, you know that sooner or later it's going to walk into a black hole."

The virtual certainty that something, sometime, will close down the party isn't far from the minds of many analysts. "A lot of these are stocks that I've happily recommended, and I've enjoyed the ride up," First Albany's Mr. Johnson says. "But now they've reached levels that make me uncomfortable."

His discomfort stems from a simple question: What justifies the recent high prices? The answer, in his view: Nothing. "It isn't because interest rates are falling sharply, and it isn't because the level or quality of earnings are going to improve a lot," he says.

Yet Mr. Johnson isn't selling even now. Most of yesterday's drop, he believes, was due to program trading, not selling by long-term investors. He is betting that speculative fever is still at large among investors and that they will ultimately drive still higher the prices of growth stocks, even those that he deems the most overpriced, such as General Electric, Merck, Philip Morris and Browning-Ferris.

"We're stretching the rules, and you have to realize that the risks are higher when you stretch the rules," he says. "It's OK to stretch the rules as long as you know you're on thin ice, and that's why I'm going to stay with Merck and General Electric."

Despite the battering from investors yesterday, even McDonald's still has fans among growth-stock aficionados. Hugh Zurkuhlin, a consumer-products analyst at Salomon Brothers, contends that despite the sluggish U.S. economic growth, McDonald's is poised for striking growth abroad. "McDonald's looks like Coca-Cola looked in the 1950s, when international growth was limitless," he says.

And the most bullish of analysts contend that investors needn't continue to concentrate their attention on the supposedly-safe big growth stocks. "When interest rates peaked, investors got a little afraid of a recession and were looking for safe stocks like Disney and Procter & Gamble," says Peter Canelo, chief market strategist at Bear Stearns. "But with rates coming down now, people are not quite so afraid of a recession, so they don't need the safe stocks anymore."

He expects the Federal Reserve to continue easing monetary policy, slowly, for several months. Done correctly, he says, such a strategy could inspire confidence that the economy will continue to grow while inflation remains in check. And that might well open the door to big gains for industrial and technology stocks.
