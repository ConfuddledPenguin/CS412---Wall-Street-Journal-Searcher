Florida Sets Out to Restore Wetlands By Refilling a Canal Inadvisably Dug

  • Author: Peter Waldman
  • Date: 07/05/90

MIAMI -- Though man's possible alteration of South Florida's climate is neither well understood nor widely discussed here, wetlands drainage is a major public issue for other environmental reasons. Last week, Republican Gov. Bob Martinez, who is fighting for re-election, stood on the bank of a central Florida river and signed legislation authorizing the sale of $3 billion of state bonds to acquire wetlands. The state, counting on heavy federal support, is also planning two of the largest land restoration projects ever undertaken -- official recognition, environmentalists say, of the shortsightedness of past water-management policies.

"Water resources are the critical issue of the next decade for Florida," says Brian Ballard, Gov. Martinez's chief of staff. "Everything else you can manage, if you have water."

In one project, Florida has earmarked $7 million to begin filling in a 60-mile canal built in the 1950s that shunted water from Lake Kissimmee, south of Orlando, to Lake Okeechobee, bypassing the once-meandering Kissimmee River. The deep trench drained the flood plain adjoining the Kissimmee and dried out the old river bed. In addition to draining a marsh once rich in wildlife, the canal polluted Lake Okeechobee by feeding it high doses of nutrients from cattle-ranch runoff. Before the canal, water was cleansed by the leaching process of the slow-flowing and winding river.

State officials put the cost of restoring the Kissimmee River at $300 million. "We're looking for the {Army} Corps of Engineers and the federal government to restore the river, since they're the ones who came in and built the canal," says Mr. Ballard.

The other project is the expansion of Everglades National Park to encompass key watershed areas to its northeast. Park officials say the project, to cost an estimated $90 million in state and federal money, is essential if the park's ecosystem is to survive.

The added territory would allow state water regulators to better simulate the "river of grass" that once washed over the Everglades and South Florida. Currently, because the land to the park's northeast is private and thus protected from flooding by the water district, water pumped into the Everglades from the north flows over only about half of the terrain it once drenched. The difference, scientists have found, has had a jarring effect on the area's wildlife.

Yet some scientists and environmentalists say Florida's piecemeal efforts at restoring wetlands are doomed to failure. Robert Livingston, director of Florida State's Center for Aquatic Research and Resource Management, is one. "Most scientists are saying that there isn't enough water any more," he says. "And if there isn't enough water, how are you going to restore wetlands?"
