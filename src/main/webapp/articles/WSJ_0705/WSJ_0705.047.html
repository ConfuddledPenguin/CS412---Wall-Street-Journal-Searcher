Abreast of the Market: Market Rise Extends to 5 Sessions, With Focus on Profit-Jolt Safety

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 07/05/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market on Tuesday rose for the fifth consecutive session, as investors loaded up on stocks they hope will be safe from negative earnings surprises. But trading was moderate. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 12.37 to 2911.63, the first close above 2900 since June 21. The current winning streak in the Dow industrials is the longest since early May.

Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 130,050,000 shares, compared with 130,200,000 on Monday. Advancing issues on the Big Board outpaced decliners 834 to 650. The financial markets were closed yesterday in observance of Independence Day.

Profit-taking depressed technology issues: Compaq Computer fell 2 to 123 1/8; Digital Equipment Corp. fell 5/8 to 83 3/4, and International Business Machines slipped 7/8 to 117 5/8.

Traders expect little change in stock prices until the wave of corporate earnings reports begins next week.

"I would be hard-pressed to call it anything other than a trading-range market right now," George D. Adler, director of portfolio management at Banc One Asset Management Corp., said. "We are all sitting on our hands waiting for something to happen."

Still, stocks of companies that maintain earnings growth throughout the ups and downs of economic cycles were clearly in favor Tuesday. Tobacco, waste-management and beverage stocks advanced. For example, American Brands rose 7/8 to 65 5/8, Waste Management gained 1 to 42 7/8, and Anheuser-Busch added 5/8 to 43 3/4.

Among advancing growth and consumer issues in the Dow industrial average were Philip Morris, which gained 7/8 to 48 and was the most active Big Board stock, with more than 2.6 million shares changing hands; Merck, which gained 5/8 to 87 5/8; General Electric, which rose 5/8 to 71 1/8, and Woolworth which jumped 1 to 34 1/8.

Periodic stock-index arbitrage buy programs, in which traders buy stocks and sell futures to lock in fleeting price differences, also lifted these shares.

Among declining stocks, Dow Jones slipped 1/2 to a new 52-week low of 22 1/2, continuing a monthlong slide. Since early June, shares of the information and publishing company, which publishes this newspaper, have fallen 20%. Several analysts, including Susan Decker of Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette, have recently lowered their earnings estimates for Dow Jones. Ms. Decker said she is concerned about a "softer profit outlook" at Dow Jones's Telerate subsidiary.

General Instrument rose 1/2 to 45 5/8 on volume of more than 1.9 million shares, an indication that market players believe another suitor for the company will surface with an offer higher than Forstmann Little's buy-out bid of $44.50 a share. Analysts said potential bidders include U.S. telecommunications companies and foreign concerns such as Matsushita Electric Industrial and N.V. Philips.

But Kay Jewelers, another takeover target, slipped 1/2 to 13 3/4 as Ratners Group's offer drew a cool reception from some holders of the company's two "junk" bond issues. The bid calls for the British concern to pay 75% of face value for Kay's high-yield bonds.

Mattel, which said it expects to report that second-quarter sales rose about 16% from a year earlier, rose 3/8 to 22 1/2 on 1.3 million shares. The company didn't provide an earnings estimate.

TCBY Enterprises dropped 1 5/8 to 17 5/8, after Robinson-Humphrey downgraded the stock's short-term investment rating and cut earnings estimates. Separately, a company spokeswoman acknowledged that a Florida-based franchisee with about 50 stores had filed for bankruptcy-law protection, but she said the filing in "no way will impair" TCBY.

Dividend-related trades by institutions swelled volume in Central Maine Power, which rose 1/8 to 18 3/4, as 2.3 million shares were traded, and SCEcorp, which added 1/8 to 37 5/8 on 1.4 million shares. Another utility stock, Entergy, also was high on the NYSE's list of most active stocks; the issue gained 1/8 to 20, as two million shares changed hands.

Signet Banking, which said it expects to post "minimal earnings" for the second quarter after adding to its reserve for loan losses, regained 5/8 to 19 7/8, after plummeting 3 3/4 on Monday. But selling spread to issues of other Southeast-based bank holding companies: Sovran Financial dropped 1 1/4 to 26 3/4, Citizens & Southern slipped 5/8 to 22 3/8, and Southeast Banking fell 3/8 to 9.

Dallas Semiconductor lost 5/8 to 7 1/8, after Shearson Lehman Hutton, in resuming its coverage of the company, downgraded the stock's short-term investment rating and removed it from two listings of recommended growth stocks. The firm also lowered earnings projections for 1990 and 1991 by four cents a share to 55 cents and 70 cents, respectively.

Genuine Parts slid 1 3/8 to 38 1/8, after Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette cut its rating on the stock to "unattractive" from "neutral" and lowered earnings estimates. The firm now expects the company to earn $2.70 a share in 1990 and $2.90 a share in 1991; its earlier forecasts were $2.85 and $3.20, respectively. Hillenbrand Industries fell 2 1/8 to 42 3/4. The company reported that net income for the quarter ended June 2 dropped slightly from a year earlier.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.53 to 360.67. Volume totaled about 11,730,000 shares, compared with about 9,850,000 on Monday. Advancing issues edged decliners, 295 to 263, and 240 stocks ended unchanged.

Greenman Bros. jumped 1 7/8 to 7 1/8, after agreeing to sell its Circus World toy-retailing unit to Melville for $90 million to $100 million; the price will be set upon completion of the sale, which is expected in early August. Melville added 3/4 to 53 in Big Board trading.

Fruit of the Loom dropped 3/8 to 12. Standard & Poor's lowered its ratings on debt issues of the company and two other concerns controlled by the investor William Farley.

P. Leiner Nutritional Products dropped 1 3/8 to 10 1/8. The company estimated that sales for the June quarter fell to about $27.2 million from $32.2 million a year earlier, and said it expects net income to be substantially lower, as well.

John Fluke Manufacturing, which said orders for the quarter ended June 29 were below its expectations, slipped 2 1/8 to 19 7/8. Net orders for the period amounted to $53.7 million, the company said.

Sanifill rose 3/4 to 20 3/4, after revising its first-quarter earnings upward to reflect an acquisition. The company declined to identify the takeover that led to the restatement.
