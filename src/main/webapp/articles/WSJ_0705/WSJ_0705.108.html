Japan's Vision for 1990s Spotlights `Human Values'

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/05/90

TOKYO -- The Ministry of International Trade and Industry released its vision for the 1990s, with a general theme of "creating human values in the global age." The document, as expected, professes a shift from visions for the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, which focused on Japan's economic growth and international competitiveness. Skeptics, however, question whether the agency, which many credit with helping coordinate Japan's rapid industrial rise, has really changed its basic policies.

The statement says the ministry's three main objectives for the 1990s are to "contribute to international society and promote internal reforms;" to "improve the quality of life for the Japanese people;" and to "secure the foundations of long-term economic growth."

To support those goals, the ministry calls for major changes in Japan's domestic economy, such as shortening working hours, cutting land prices and encouraging imports.

It also calls for Japan to play a more active international role, particularly in environmental and Third World development issues. It says Japan should become "a new, powerful, and creative standard bearer" for the world.

To help sustain Japan's domestic economy, the vision statement calls for improving Japan's "information infrastructure" by promoting the more widespread use of technology.
