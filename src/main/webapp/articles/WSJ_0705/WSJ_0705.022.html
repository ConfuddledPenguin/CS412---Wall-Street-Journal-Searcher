Want a Check, Mate? Bankers Trust Seeks Chess, Bridge Players as Traders

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 07/05/90

NEW YORK -- Do you spend hours a week playing bridge? Or are you a neighborhood chess master? Bankers Trust may have a job for you.

In a bid to discover new foreign-exchange and bond-trading talent, Bankers Trust New York Corp., the U.S.'s 10th-largest bank, is advertising in magazines for chess and bridge enthusiasts for players who may be interested in turning their game skills into cold cash -- for themselves and the bank.

According to the ads, chess and bridge "require certain finely-honed skills," among them the ability to predict an opponent's moves, courage to take short-term risks for long-term gains, and the patience to assess every option available.

"Interestingly enough, these are the exact skills necessary to succeed as a trader in the global currency and securities markets," the ad in Chess Life says.

"It doesn't really matter what your background is" the ad in Contract Bridge Bulletin emphasizes, "and it could be the best move you'll ever make."

Bankers Trust won't talk about how it came to advertise for chess and bridge players. "We really have nothing to say," says a spokesman for the bank. "We've just placed the ads and we haven't had any response yet."

But the ads say that the idea came from several of the bank's best traders, including one highly successful currency trader who is an international master chess player. "He is still moving pieces to form a calculated game plan, but now it is yen and Deutsche marks as well as rooks and knights," the ad says.

Competitors have mixed feelings about trolling for trading talent in hobby magazines. "We just hired some traders, but we didn't hire them from a bridge club," says the head currency trader at one bank.

But Carmine Rotondo, chief corporate foreign-exchange dealer at Security Pacific National Bank, says good traders have to keep their wits about them. Bridge players, he says, "tend to be more calm, more acclimated to changing conditions" than other games players, especially gamblers. "You wouldn't want a blackjack player on the desk," he says. "They tend to self-destruct."

Yet another currency trader says bridge and chess players may be fine, but the people he really wants on his trading desk "are the guys that scalp tickets outside the stadium."

In any event, traders are well rewarded when they produce big profits for their employer. While a trader straight from a bank's training program would probably be paid about $60,000 a year, senior traders with five years or more experience earn six-figure salaries with generous bonuses. That's in return for producing $2.5 million or more in annual profits for the bank.
