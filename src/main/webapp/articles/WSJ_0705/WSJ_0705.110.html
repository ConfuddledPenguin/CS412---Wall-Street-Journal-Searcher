World Markets: Tokyo Shares End Higher After Late Sell-Off; Stocks Fall in London, Advance in Frankfurt

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/05/90

Tokyo stock prices were slightly firmer Wednesday, declining from midday highs on index-linked selling in late afternoon trading. Stocks in London closed lower on mounting concern about the strengthening pound and the continued spate of corporate profit downgradings, while Frankfurt shares rose on support of a successful start to monetary union with East Germany.

In Tokyo, the Nikkei index of 225 selected issues, which gained 254.37 points Tuesday, rose 31.32 points to close at 32445.92.

In early trading Thursday, the Nikkei index was up 21.14 points at 32467.06.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 550 million shares, up from 466 million shares Tuesday.

Advancing issues outnumbered declining issues 641 to 324, while 160 issues remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which gained 0.78 points Tuesday, was up 13.87 points, or 0.59%, at 2363.35.

The second section index, which advanced 13.77 points Tuesday, was up 19.17 points, or 0.46%, to close at 4222.84.

Volume in the second section was estimated at 28 million shares, up from 21 million shares Tuesday.

The Nikkei index rose 213 points during the first half hour of the morning session. The strength of the yen favored domestic demand-related shares, while prompting profit-taking on export-oriented high-technology shares, which had been bought in the past weeks on expectations that the yen's weakness would push up their earnings, traders said.

"But buying didn't continue," said Hiroyuki Murai, general manager of stock trading at Nikko Securities.

Gains made for the domestic demandrelated shares prompted selling by institutional investors, who hold these issues by heavy volume, traders said.

Concern about an official discount rate increase by the Bank of Japan has gradually been slackened this week, helped by the central bank's recent stance, which is perceived as neutral in controlling market interest rates, said a senior trader at Wako Securities.

Meanwhile, most investors -- institutions in particular -- continued to hold off from buying, traders said, because the future of German economic conditions after Sunday's monetary union and results of the Soviet Communist Party conference, which will last until July 13, are unclear.

High-priced, high-technology shares were broadly lower. Fanuc fell 120 yen to 7,700 yen (80 cents to $51), and Kyocera lost 230 yen to 8,850 yen.

Sony lost 120 yen to 8,750 yen, while TDK fell 100 yen to 7,400 yen and Pioneer dropped 90 yen to 6,350 yen.

Construction and development stocks were firmer on the day. Ohbayashi rose 30 yen to 1,690 yen, Taisei added 20 yen to 1,360 yen and Shimizu rose 20 yen to 2,130 yen.

Kajima rose 30 yen to 1,990 yen, and Kumagai Gumi rose 20 yen to 1,160 yen.

In London, the market started the session easier, with initial losses ascribed to Barclay's de Zoete Wedd downgrading RTZ's profit forecast.

The pound's continued strength, with the currency standing at a 16-month high vs. the dollar, heightened nervousness about stocks with considerable overseas business, prompting some sell activity.

"People are concerned that, on a currency translation basis, a number of overseas earners will be disadvantaged by the strong pound," said one dealer.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-Index, which fell 0.3 points Tuesday, ended down 16.2 points at 2355.5.

The narrower Financial Times-Stock Exchange 30-Index, which fell 1.9 points Tuesday, ended down 13.6 points, at 1881.1.

Dealers said if company profit warnings and downgradings continue at the current pace, the uncertainty in the market will continue to prevail.

Leading stocks to fall on currency worries included Reckitt & Colman, which eased 29 pence to #12.65 (51 cents to $22.40). RTZ ended down 22 pence at 541 pence.

A profit downgrading of P&O by James Capel sent that company's share price down 21 pence to 628 pence. A James Capel source said that P&O's operations were similar to Trafalgar House, which was downgraded earlier in the week.

The only sector to show gains Wednesday was clearing banks, with Midland leading, ending up 6 pence at 295 pence on rumors of a bullish statement by the chairman of Hong Kong & Shanghai Banking Corp. about a possible merger with Midland by year end. The report was refuted by both banks.

Total turnover during the session was 353 million shares, down from an equally modest 449.9 million shares on Tuesday.

Share prices on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange gained 1% in lively trading. The German Stock Index, or DAX, jumped 18.90 to 1925.13.

Traders said the market was led higher by financial issues, which were basking in the glow of a stronger bond market. Market operators are optimistic about the acceptance of the German Unity Bond, and say that a successful launch of the unity bond could be a signal for even higher prices in coming weeks.

Dresdner Bank jumped 15 marks to 449.50 marks (9 cents to $272.08), Deutsche Bank added 10.50 marks to 818.50 marks, and Commerzbank gained 7 marks to 293 marks.

The banking sector had lagged the market this year as fears of a rapid surge in interest rates at the start of 1990, due in large part to worries about the costs of unification and monetary union, weighed against their share prices.

But with monetary union passing its first start-up hurdles without any major hitches, and with German central bank officials sounding much more optimistic about inflation being under control, pressures are easing, dealers say.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Zurich, Stockholm and Madrid; lower in Paris and Milan; and mixed in Brussels and Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed mixed.

Share prices closed lower in Taipei, Manila and Seoul, higher in Sydney, Wellington and Singapore and stable in Hong Kong.
