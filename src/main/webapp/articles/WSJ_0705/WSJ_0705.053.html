REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Another Shoe Drops

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/05/90

We have no fondness for former dictators or their self-indulgent widows, but the acquittal of Imelda Marcos is a reminder that criminalizing political doesn't pay. Whatever else the jurors may have thought during the three-month trial of the woman with all the shoes, they easily concluded that Mrs. Marcos wasn't a racketeer. The real mystery is how then-U.S. Attorney Rudolph Giuliani persuaded the Justice Department that the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations law was appropriate for this case -- just as he managed to RICO other notorious "gangsters" such as investment bankers.

Marcos juror Thomas O'Rourke, a Transit Authority worker, said Mr. Giuliani was "only trying to make a name for himself" with this "totally silly case." Those are remarkably critical words from a juror. So much for a reported State Department cable that Mr. Giuliani had "guaranteed" a conviction.

The case became more complex when Ferdinand Marcos died in September; none of the case's allegations involved Mrs. Marcos alone. But there were problems with the case as early as the indictment two years ago after the Marcoses gave up power and were given refuge in Hawaii. The case claimed to be about property in the U.S., but it was obvious to everyone that the prosecution was brought because of what the Marcoses did in the Philippines.

The charges were based on alleged stealing of some $200 million from the Philippine treasury that the couple then spent on jewels, art and New York real estate. The defense that the money was from a gold hoard Mr. Marcos discovered may not have been easy to credit. Still, after almost 100 prosecution witnesses and thousands of pages of bank records, even the judge wondered what a case that belonged in a Philippine court was doing in ours. Judge John Keenan asked out of the jury's hearing, "What's an American court, what am I, doing here, trying a case involving the theft of money from Philippine banks?" The jury apparently agreed.

The collapse of this prosecution is surely a telling example of prosecutorial hubris. We knew that with RICO an aggressive prosecutor could indict a ham sandwich, but it now appears that convictions may no longer be so easy. Coming after a judge vacated the RICO sentences of Princeton/Newport securities officials, this acquittal leaves us wondering how a Michael Milken might have fared with a New York jury if RICO's power hadn't rubber-hosed him into a settlement. The embarrassing Marcos acquittal should be another nail in RICO's coffin.

There is also a serious foreign policy point here. We worried that this case might deter other tyrants from giving up power bloodlessly if they saw that they'd still end up RICO'd into a U.S. jail cell. We doubt that the State Department will be pressured again by U.S. Attorneys who want to indict foreign heads of state, at least without clear evidence of legal wrongdoing in this country.

This prosecution didn't do the Philippines any favors either. Whatever Mr. Marcos's venal crimes, the real scandal remains that his reign delayed any mature system of democracy and free markets. Surely the embattled Corazon Aquino has enough on her mind without having to worry about more internal divisions caused by any unintended rehabilitation of the Marcoses in a U.S. court.
