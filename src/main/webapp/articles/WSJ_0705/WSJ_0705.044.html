Technology & Health: Microcom Slashes 4th-Quarter Results, Raises Projections of 1st-Period Losses

  • Author: John R. Wilke
  • Date: 07/05/90

Microcom Inc. slashed its previously reported fiscal fourth-quarter and year-end results and said it also expects to report sharply higher than anticipated losses for the first quarter. The announcement suggests deepening problems at the Norwood, Mass., software and data communications equipment maker. In June, the value of Microcom's stock was reduced by nearly half in frenzied trading after the company warned investors it would post a loss in its fiscal first quarter ended June 30.

In a statement issued after the close of the market Tuesday, Microcom said it revised its fourth-quarter results downward to net income of $1 million, or 11 cents a share, from the previously reported net of $3.2 million, or 32 cents a share. Revenue for its fourth quarter, which ended March 31, was revised to $18.25 million from $21.8 million.

Microcom also said it expected to report a loss of between 43 cents and 48 cents a share for the first quarter ended June 30, on sharply lower revenue of $10.5 million. Just last month, the company had estimated the loss at between 15 cents and 20 cents a share, on revenue of $14 million. In the year-ago first quarter, Microcom reported net income of $2.5 million, or 29 cents a share, on revenue of $17 million.

Microcom attributed the unexpected adjustments of the fourth-quarter results to "overstocking of key North American distributors" during the period.

James M. Dow, president and chief executive officer, said first-quarter revenue was substantially below estimates "due to tightened credit policies with distributors and resellers." He also said sales reserves were increased and that purchases of two key product lines, LAN Bridge and Protocol modems, were delayed. International sales, however, were in line with estimates, he said.

Michel Guite, an analyst at Salomon Brothers Inc., called the problems in the first quarter a "serious distribution and packaging blunder" by the company, which caused a large number of software product returns. He also said sales of modems had fallen short of expectations. Sales of equipment such as modems -- which transmit computer data over phone lines -- have accounted for about two-thirds of revenue, Mr. Guite said, though they contributed only about 35% of earnings.

Microcom's stock still hasn't recovered from the pounding it took last month when the company issued its initial forecast of a loss for the quarter. The stock, which dropped $8.50, to $9.75, with more than 1.7 million shares changing hands on June 11, closed at $8.75, unchanged, in national over-the-counter trading Tuesday.

For the full year, Microcom said it revised net income to $9.5 million, or $1 a share, from a previously reported $11.66 million, or $1.22 a share. Revenue was adjusted downward to $71 million, from a previously reported $75.1 million. In 1989, Microcom reported net income of $7.6 million, or 92 cents a share, on revenue of $59.3 million.

The revisions to the fourth quarter and full year did not amount to a formal restatement, because they had not yet been filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission, the company indicated.
