BUSH AIDES are considering a plan to impose a tax of as much as 0.5% on the sale of stocks, bonds, futures contracts and other instruments. The securities industry is already lobbying against the idea, which the White House sees as a possible way of making a capital-gains tax cut palatable to Democrats without boosting the top income-tax rate. Among tax writers in Congress, Bentsen is interested in the proposal, but Rostenkowski opposes it. ---

Falling interest rates will help avert a recession for at least a year, according to a Wall Street Journal survey of 40 economists. But most forecasters warn of painfully slow growth, increasing unemployment and tough times for many industries.

Factory orders increased 2.1% in May, making up for most of April's decline. Durable goods, especially transportation equipment, accounted for May's strength. Orders for nondurable goods were flat.

---

The dollar declined against most currencies in European dealings yesterday, largely on technical selling. On Tuesday, the U.S. stock market gained for the fifth day in a row, while bonds rose modestly and gold rallied. The Dow Jones Industrial Average went up 12.37 points to 2911.63.

---

U.S. brokerage firms were profitable in the second period, after two quarters of losses, analysts say. But earnings were down from a year ago.

---

Chrysler gave stock awards to its 1,800 top officials on the condition that they stay with the auto maker until January. The move, an effort to stem a surge in management turnover, is intended to make up for the omission of traditional cash bonuses this year.

---

AT&T agreed to buy most of Western Union's electronic-messaging business for $180 million. The deal, which requires regulatory approval and the completion of Western Union's debt restructuring, would make AT&T dominant in the electronic-mail industry, with a 40% market share.

---

McDonnell Douglas moved closer to selling 120 fighters to Seoul, as South Korea and the U.S. appeared to resolve their dispute over the deal.

---

Microcom sharply lowered its results for the quarter ended March 31, and said losses in the June quarter appear to be much higher than expected. The disclosure suggests deepening problems at the software and data communications concern.

---

United Airlines unions recruited John Edwardson, a onetime finance specialist at NWA, to be chief financial officer in their bid to buy UAL.

---

Massey named Don Blankenship, a tough anti-union manager, to be president of the coal producer and heir apparent to the company's chairman.

---

S&P downgraded $1.14 billion of debt issued by William Farley's investment and manufacturing empire.
