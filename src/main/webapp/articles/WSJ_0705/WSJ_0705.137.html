Giving a Hand: Daniel Lieberman Makes Sure That Wishes Can Come True

  • Author: John D. Williams
  • Date: 07/05/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

DANIEL LIEBERMAN MAKES HIS LIVING selling bagels and cheese in Mount Kisco, N.Y. But he lives his life devoting 30 hours or so every week to fulfilling the wishes of children near death.

Operating from the small office at the back of his "Danny's Cheese Shop," the 39-year-old Mr. Lieberman presides over the Hudson Valley chapter of the Make-A-Wish Foundation. He also serves as one of 21 volunteer board members of the national group, a nonprofit organization with 7,300 volunteers that has made wishes come true for more than 10,000 children since its founding in 1980 in Phoenix.

Mr. Lieberman came to Make-A-Wish five years ago after hearing about the group from a friend. The description of the joy brought to seriously ill children, often near death, by the grant of a single wish touched a nerve with the bagel-shop proprietor. His own daughter, Rachel, had barely survived major surgery at the age of five months, and the 45-day hospital vigil following her operation gave Mr. Lieberman new appreciation for the life of a child.

His chapter's first wish award went in January 1986 to Joey, a 12-year-old suffering from leukemia, whose wish was to visit his grandparents in Czechoslovakia. An airline donated two tickets to Prague, and Mr. Lieberman's chapter contributed $4,500 for two more tickets plus expenses for Joey and three family members. Joey, seen smiling in a picture on Mr. Lieberman's office wall, is 17 this year, his leukemia in remission.

Since then, the Hudson Valley group has worked to grant the wishes of 350 children. Their stories don't always have a happy ending, and there seems to be more of them every year. It's a challenge for Mr. Lieberman and the 200 other volunteers in his chapter. Two of the six phone lines in his store are dedicated to "wish work"; he's even got a facsimile machine, donated by a retired advertising executive, to keep the wishes coming.

It isn't hard for a child to qualify. A candidate must be under age 18 and afflicted with a life-threatening illness; nominees come from a network of medical and social workers and clergy. Financial hardship isn't a criteria. Parents who could afford an elaborate last birthday party for their stricken child can't cope with hiring a clown, explains Karla Blomberg, president of the national group and owner of a real estate company.

Mr. Lieberman asks candidates, "If Christmas or your birthday were tomorrow, what would you like?" Or, "If you could do anything, what would it be?" Candidates can make another wish if the first one is rejected. An 11-year-old boy's wish for a rifle was turned down because of its potential harm, but he got his second wish, a trip to Disney World. Also, the wish granted may not always be the wish sought. The New York Mets, citing Major League Baseball rules that restrict the dugout to team personnel, turned down a boy's wish for a seat with the players but gave him four season tickets instead.

There's some formality: Volunteers have to be certain the wish is the child's own; and a doctor must certify that the child's life is threatened by an illness.

Wishes run the gamut. David, 17, exchanged gifts with President Bush in the Oval Office. Some ask to meet star athletes or entertainers; others want a doll, a computer or a shopping spree. There often isn't much time to comply. The cost isn't high. Excluding donated items such as clothing and transportation, the average cost of fulfilling a dying child's dream is about $2,400.

When he isn't granting wishes, Mr. Lieberman is raising money to fund them. One big event is a June "bagel day" in which Mr. Lieberman and other volunteers show up at 39 commuter train stations for three hours to hand out bagels and cream cheese and accept Make-A-Wish donations. This June's "bagel day" raised about $100,000, compared with $4,100 in 1985.
