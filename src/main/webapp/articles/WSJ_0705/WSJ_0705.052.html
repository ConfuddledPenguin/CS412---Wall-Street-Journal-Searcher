Baker Proposes Central American Aid at G-24 Parley

  • Author: Mark M. Nelson
  • Date: 07/05/90

BRUSSELS -- Secretary of State James Baker proposed that 24 Western nations establish an aid program to assist Central America. Mr. Baker gave few details of the proposal, which came as the two dozen countries that have pooled $14 billion in financial aid for Poland and Hungary agreed to offer similar help to four other Eastern European countries.

Though there was no official reaction to Mr. Baker's proposal, which he said would help "broaden the support for democracy and development in Central America," several countries said they would consider the idea. Mr. Baker said President Bush had received a favorable response to the idea when he discussed it recently with EC Commission President Jacques Delors. And Mr. Baker said several European foreign ministers had indicated they would support the idea.

Regarding Eastern Europe, the ministers of the so-called Group of 24 agreed that Czechoslovakia, Bulgaria, East Germany and Yugoslavia were making progress toward market economies and democratic societies and thus should be included in the joint aid programs.

"The door is now open for direct, coordinated assistance," said Frans Andriessen, the EC Commissioner for external relations who chaired the G-24 meeting.

But the ministers expressed widely divergent views about creating a separate aid program for the Soviet Union. And they refused to offer any aid to Romania until that country does more to create a democratic political system.

The discussions among the G-24 ministers, the second since they agreed one year ago to coordinate their financial assistance to Poland and Hungary, were described as a major step toward creating a more stable and prosperous Eastern Europe. The meeting came less than a week before the summit of leaders of the seven major industrial countries in Houston, where the controversial subject of aid to the Soviet Union is likely to be brought up again.

The ministers didn't estimate how much additional aid would be necessary to help the four new members of the G-24 aid program. But in their joint statement, they said it would require "further financial efforts from the whole Western Community."

"The amount of money needed to give help to these countries is enormous," added Mr. Andriessen.

The ministers also asked the EC Commission, which is administering the aid program for the G-24, to study the possibility of a large financial "safety-net" fund, that could be used to help the Eastern countries create convertible currencies, finance short-term balance-of-payments shortfalls and meet other emergency needs.

Mr. Andriessen and several other commissioners had suggested the fund, which would be similar to the $1 billion stabilization fund that the G-24 already has put into place for Poland. The Polish fund is credited with helping that country put into place radical changes in its economic and monetary system. Mr. Andriessen said that such a fund for all of Eastern Europe should amount to 10 billion European Currency Units ($12.47 billion).

But the ministers were cautious about endorsing the fund before it was clear whether it would duplicate the efforts of the International Monetary Fund, which is the principal organization that tries to help countries with balance-of-payment problems or other short-term financing needs.
