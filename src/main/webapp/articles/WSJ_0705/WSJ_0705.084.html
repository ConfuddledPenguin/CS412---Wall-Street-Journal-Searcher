Economy: Factory Orders Were Up 2.1% During May --- Figure Follows Erratic Path Of Earlier This Year; Durables Sector Strong

  • Author: Tuesday Georges
  • Date: 07/05/90

WASHINGTON -- New government figures show the manufacturing sector continuing on its bumpy path, with factory orders rising 2.1% in May after declining 2.1% in April. New orders to U.S. factories increased to $240.98 billion in May, the Commerce Department said, after falling to $236.03 billion in April and jumping 4%, to $241.07 billion, in March.

"These figures are consistent with what we have been seeing for most of the year," said Neal Soss, chief economist for First Boston Corp. in New York. "Over the last six months, orders are up only one-half of a percent . . . What this says to me is that nothing has changed."

The department said that much of the strength in May was concentrated in orders for durable goods -- big-ticket items, such as cars and aircraft, expected to last at least three years. Orders for durable goods rose 4% in May to $128.56 billion after declining 4.1% in April and climbing 6.7% in March. In a preliminary report late last month, the department had said durable goods rose 3.9% in May.

Orders in May were strongest for transportation equipment, which rose 10.1%, with motor vehicles and parts accounting for most of the gain. Also showing large rises were nonelectrical machinery, which includes computers, up 3.5%, and primary metals, up 1.7%.

"Good thing there is a transportation industry in America," said Mr. Soss. The manufacturing sector, which he said is too dependent on transportation equipment, is currently "at a high level of activity but fairly stagnant" in growth.

However, Robert Dederick, chief economist for Northern Trust Co. in Chicago, said the report is "a small positive because orders are edging upward" but said he questions the manufacturing sector's ability to maintain its growth.

"This is a modest uptrend, one that I view as being quite vulnerable," said Mr. Dederick. "Unless we get some strength in consumption and capital-goods spending, it will be vulnerable to aborting its gains."

Orders for nondurable goods were relatively flat in May, after a 0.2% gain in April and a 1.1% rise in March. Unfilled orders -- factory work that is in the pipeline -- edged up 0.4% in May, following a 0.3% increase in April and a 0.7% rise the month before.

May shipments, also concentrated in durable goods, increased 2%, after a 1.3% downturn in April and a 1.2% rise in March. Inventories of durable goods were unchanged after April's 0.5% increase, while inventories of nondurable goods edged up 0.2% in May.

The numbers are adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
