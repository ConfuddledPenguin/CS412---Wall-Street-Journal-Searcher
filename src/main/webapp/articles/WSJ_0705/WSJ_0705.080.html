THRIFTS IN LIMBO: S&Ls hobble along while awaiting government sale. Some 256 S&Ls thrash in conservatorship, a kind of no-saver's-land between receivership and going on the block. "It's like a ship with no rudder" staying open pending sale, says Richard White, president of Midwest Federal Savings, Minot, N.D. Resolution Trust Corp. sends a managing agent who often curtails activity. Generally not allowed: new loans; maturities beyond six months; top rates or ad promotions. That makes it tough to keep even the most loyal customers. "We're not competitive," sighs Mr. White. Deposits fell 10% to $510 million at Midwest since the end of 1989.

George Bragg, new president of Sooner Federal in Tulsa, Okla., visited every one of the thrift's branches to stem outflows last year. "I talked with 900 customers," says Mr. Bragg, who also has had political aspirations. Sooner lost $80 million of its $1.2 billion in deposits. Managers also fled, but he says the thrift "spread responsibility" and now has "a more efficient staff." RTC, meanwhile, speeds up sales.

RTC figures 60% of the thrifts were "victimized by criminal activity."

FIDO'S A FATSO, say pet food companies pushing new "lite" products.

"Lots of cats and dogs sit with their owners and watch TV when they used to be out chasing birds," says Elizabeth Hodgkins of Hill's Pet Products. To combat an "epidemic of overweight," the Colgate-Palmolive unit offers high-fiber, low-fat "maintenance" pet food as well as prescription products. Ralston Purina adds Pro Plan Lite for cats on the paws of Tender Vittles Lite. Purina's Fit & Trim for dogs -- its pioneer product -- is still a big seller in the $5.8 billion supermarket pet food field.

Snacks are always a problem: one extra-large Milk-Bone has 254 calories. Ms. Hodgkins suggests withholding some of doggie's canned food, baking it and "cutting it into little bites" for treats. Wesley Bayles, a Washington, D.C., vet, is more generous, but he confesses to being overweight himself. "My evaluation of a dog's weight may not be the same as some skinny veterinarian's." His own black Lab manages to stay a trim 72 pounds on dog food and the odd pizza crust.

FIRST-DAY DOCS lay hands on patients this month. Should you be one?

The academic year begins in teaching hospitals as interns fresh from medical school join newly-advanced residents in a changing-of-the guard. Turnover of the so-called house staff means a patient can have one team of doctors on June 30 and another on July 1 -- provoking anxiety all around. But a recent study led by Dr. Eugene C. Rich of the University of Kentucky College of Medicine, found that quality of care didn't suffer during the summer months. However, the length of hospitalization rose -- and so did the bill.

The report, published in the Journal of the American Medical Association, drew criticism from Dr. Jeffrey L. Kaufman, of Albany (N.Y.) Medical College, for counting readmissions, death and nursing home placement rather than more relevant criteria like quality of written orders or correctness of drug dosage. His advice? Ask your doctor if the hospital has a transition strategy. If not, "I'd say wait a little bit." If so, "it's perfectly reasonable to go in." Dr. Sol Sherry, former chief of medicine at Philadelphia's Temple University Hospital, says overlapping the teams helps greatly.

"It's a pressure cooker," Dr. Sherry says of the transition. But "we never had a great problem."

HAZARDOUS TO YOUR LOAN: Concern over toxic materials clouds lending.

Commercial loans and multifamily housing projects have been subject to environmental audits for some time. Now, as lists of hazardous substances and potential liabilities grow well beyond asbestos and radon, some lenders feel the home mortgage may be the next big risk. There aren't the typical business-site hazards, "but more lenders are becoming more sensitive to other kinds of problems," says Warren Tyler of State Savings Bank, Columbus, Ohio.

Mr. Tyler, a former state EPA director, predicts lawn chemicals and drinking water are the next potential concerns. Others point to backyard, underground fuel-storage tanks. In Massachusetts, state law says prospective homeowners must agree to remove lead-based paint from certain surfaces if a child under six will live in a house built before 1978. But enforcement has been feeble. Part of the problem: budget cuts.

Chemical Bank's first-stage environmental audit for a multifamily project costs $2,000 to $3,000.
