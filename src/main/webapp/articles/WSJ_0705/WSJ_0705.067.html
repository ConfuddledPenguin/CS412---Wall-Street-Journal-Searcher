Mistrial Declared In WPPSS Suit Filed Against GE

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 07/05/90

A federal judge in Phoenix, Ariz., declared a mistrial Monday after eight jurors failed to reach a verdict in a $989 million suit brought by Washington Public Power Supply System against General Electric Co. The suit, filed in 1985, charged General Electric with fraud and breach of contract in the design of the containment vessel at WPPSS's No. 2 plant near Richland, Wash. The containment vessel is the thick concrete enclosure designed to prevent the escape of radiation after a nuclear accident.

WPPSS will probably request a retrial, subject to board approval later this month. "We've come this far, I expect we'll pursue it," said a spokesman for WPPSS, a quasi-government agency that supplies electricity in the state of Washington.

"We were pleased the jury didn't accept the allegations," said a spokesman for General Electric, Fairfield, Conn. "If they want to pursue another trial, we think the outcome would be favorable to us." General Electric has denied all of WPPSS's allegations.

Other utilities with nuclear reactors and containment vessels designed by General Electric have apparently been watching the WPPSS case. Pennsylvania Power & Light Co. and Cleveland Electric Illuminating Co., among other utilities, sent lawyers to observe the trial, WPPSS said.

It wasn't clear when or if Judge Alan McDonald, who presided over the trial, would unseal his separate ruling on the validity of a damage-limitation clause in General Electric's 1971 contract with WPPSS. The contract capped General Electric's liability at the face amount of the contract, or about $110 million. Judge McDonald had sealed his decision to avoid biasing the jury.

During the 4 1/2-month trial, jurors were presented with detailed engineering data and analysis about structural loads on concrete and pipes during a nuclear plant accident. WPPSS has been trying to recover its costs for redesigning and reconstructing the containment vessel. WPPSS says the "rework" caused an 18-month delay in completing the plant, which began commercial operation in December 1984.

General Electric said that the only lawsuit against the company that is similar to the WPPSS case is a $600 million action brought by Long Island Lighting Co., which owns the Shoreham nuclear plant in Brookhaven, N.Y.

"We feel we have even a stronger case than in the WPPSS situation," the General Electric spokesman said. The suit is expected to go to trial this fall in federal district court in Pathogue, N.Y.

WPPSS's claims in its General Electric suit aren't related to the 1983 default by the agency on $2.25 billion of bonds issued for construction of its nuclear units 4 and 5. Those units were abandoned after being partially built.
