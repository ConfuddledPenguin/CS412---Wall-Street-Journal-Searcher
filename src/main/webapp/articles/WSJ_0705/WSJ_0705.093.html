Baby Boomer People Make Less, but Make Do

THE BABY BOOM generation is faring better economically than many studies suggest.

Considerable research has indicated that the huge size of the cohort born between 1946 and 1964, plus the recession of the early Reagan years, have depressed the boomers' earnings compared with those of earlier groups at similar ages. But, a new analysis argues, that's just part of the story.

Richard Easterlin and Christine Macdonald of the University of Southern California and Diane Macunovich of Williams College agree the earnings picture isn't too good. Their data show that average real earnings of young men between the ages of 20 and 40 in 1987 were lower or just barely above the earnings of men in that age range in 1974. For example, men 30 to 35 in 1987 had average earnings $2,300 a year less than the comparable age group in 1974.

But the researchers maintain that the boomers made life-style adjustments giving them a higher economic standard of living than the earnings figures imply. They continued to live with parents, cohabited or doubled up with other adults, stretching their buying power. They postponed marriage and children until they were older and making more money. Wives went to work and then kept on working after having kids.

The Easterlin team calculates an "average income per adult equivalent," a figure that takes into account the number of adults and children sharing each household's income and economies of scale; they conclude that this figure was sharply higher for young people in 1987 than for comparable age groups in 1974. In 1987, for instance, the average annual income per adult equivalent for men between 30 and 35 is figured to be $2,700 higher than it was for those 30 to 35 in 1974.

"For the baby boom generation," the analysis says, "altered demographic behavior has been the key to transforming adverse labor market conditions into favorable living levels."

Parents' Relationships

Shape Sexual Attitudes

PARENTS' MARITAL history influences their children's attitudes toward sex and marriage, a University of Michigan professor reports.

Arland Thornton drew on a large sample of white Detroit families who had children in July 1961, interviewing the mothers repeatedly between 1962 and 1985 and interviewing the children when they were 18 and again when they were 23. He found that children whose mothers had married young generally had more accepting attitudes toward premarital sex than children whose mothers married older. They also tended to enter more quickly into cohabitation or marriage.

Children whose mothers were pregnant before marriage were substantially more sexually active themselves than children conceived after marriage.

Parental divorce and remarriage had similar impact. Young people whose parents divorced during the years they were growing up were substantially more likely to have extensive premarital sex and to cohabit before marriage. The cohabitation rate among children whose childhoods were marked by family disruption was more than double that for children from intact families.

"One particularly interesting finding," Mr. Thornton says, was that children whose mothers divorced and remarried were far more sexually active than those whose mothers didn't remarry.

Non-Traditional Homes

Can Hurt Kids' Health

FAMILY STRUCTURE has much to do with children's health and development, a new survey documents.

With so many more children living in single-parent or stepparent families, the subject takes on new urgency. Deborah Dawson of the National Center for Health Statistics examined a 1988 government survey of the health and emotional status of some 17,000 children ranging in age from infancy to 17.

After adjusting for the children's age, sex, race, family income, whether the mother worked outside the home and other factors, she found that on a series of health tests, children living in non-traditional families had substantially greater problems than those living with both natural parents.

For example, the risk of having had an injury or accident during the year before the survey was 20% to 30% higher for children living with a mother and stepfather or with a divorced mother only; peculiarly, children living with a never-married mother had no higher injury rates. Children in mother-headed families, whether never-married or previously married, were 50% more likely to have asthma.

Children living in mother-headed families or with mothers and stepfathers were anywhere from 40% to 75% more likely to have had to repeat a grade in school than those living with both biological parents. Children from disrupted marriages were over 70% more likely to have been suspended or expelled, and those living with never-married mothers more than twice as likely.

Odds and Ends

MEN MADE UP 73% of the full-time faculty in U.S. higher education in 1988 and 56% of the part-time faculty, according to the Department of Education. Whites accounted for nine-tenths of all faculty members, while 4% were Asian, 3% black, 2% Hispanic and 1% American Indian. . . . More arrestees in the Northeast test positive for multiple drug use than in any other part of the U.S., National Institute of Justice researchers report. Compared with males, female arrestees show as high or higher multiple drug use in every section of the country.
