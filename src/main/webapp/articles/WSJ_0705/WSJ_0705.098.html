Foreign Exchange: Dollar Falls Overseas on Technical Sales As Support Level Against Yen Is Broken

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/05/90

The dollar ended European dealings Wednesday broadly lower after technical selling was drawn into the market when a key chart support level against the yen was erased in Asian dealings. In London, the dollar was quoted late at 1.6464 marks, down from 1.6505 marks late Tuesday. The U.S. unit ended at 149.70 yen, down from 151.10 yen. The pound rose to $1.7840 from $1.7790.

In Tokyo early Thursday, the dollar was quoted at 150.68 yen, up from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 150.30 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at 1.6516 marks, up from 1.6477 marks on Wednesday.

The dollar's biggest move Wednesday was against the yen, although dealers are generally discounting the significance of Wednesday's action given extremely thin trading conditions.

Dealers noted that outside a flurry of morning dealings in Europe, little business was done in the afternoon. They pointed to the Independence Day holiday in the U.S., Moslem observations in the Middle East and lack of interest among European players in taking overnight positions.

Laudy Bahl, head of corporate trading at Bank of Tokyo in London, said that even though technical factors played a clear role in the dollar's decline on the day, the level of short-covering seen in late Asian dealings indicates that the downturn action hasn't quiet signaled a fresh trend.

She and other dealers expect the dollar to recover modestly Thursday when Asian markets open given the lack of clear fundamental trends and some disagreement in the market over the near-term direction of U.S. and Japanese interest rates.

But a dealer with a large U.S. bank added that he agrees with Ms. Bahl that the dollar may again move to test lower levels depending on Friday's potentially pivotal U.S. employment report for June.

Some technical chart watchers maintained that because of the dollar's move through long-term chart support levels, the U.S. currency is more likely to stay under pressure against the yen Thursday.

But dealers suggest that the dollar's weakness against the yen has also taken pressure off movement toward higher Japanese interest rates, a factor that may translate into dollar stability once the technical-based selling exhausts itself.

In Tokyo, the dollar pitched lower Wednesday as fear of lower U.S. interest rates set off a wave of selling.

Japanese players, caught off guard by the dollar's fall overnight, sold heavily in Pacific and early Tokyo trading.

While neither U.S. nor Japanese interest rates have moved substantially, participants are trading on speculation that the Federal Reserve will ease its tight monetary policy soon. Thus, Japanese institutions are becoming wary of holding long dollar positions, dealers said.

The dollar was pressured lower Tuesday against all major European currencies amid more activity than most traders expected ahead of Independence Day. The dollar fell out of its comfortable range against the mark and through technical supports vs. the Swiss franc.

The dollar gained marginally on the yen and Canadian dollar. Early Asian dealings set the stage for the squaring of short, or oversold, dollar positions, following the U.S. unit's rapid decline against these two currencies over the past week.

In New York Tuesday, the dollar ended at 1.3930 francs, below 1.3965 francs late Monday. It was quoted late at 1.6505 marks, unchanged. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 150.90 yen, above 150.83 yen.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Tuesday, gold for current delivery jumped $3.30 to $362.10 an ounce, the highest close since May 31.

In early Hong Kong trading Thursday, gold was quoted at $361.60 an ounce.
