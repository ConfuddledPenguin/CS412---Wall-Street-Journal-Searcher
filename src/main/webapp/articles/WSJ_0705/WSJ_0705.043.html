Panel Seeks Killing Gulf Canada Plan For Ocean Drilling

  • Author: Rose Tamburri
  • Date: 07/05/90

OTTAWA -- A Canadian environmental panel recommended that the federal government reject plans by Gulf Canada Resources Ltd. to drill in the western Arctic Ocean, citing the potentially devastating effects of a spill. The recommendation, if accepted by the federal government, would result in a suspension of operations in Canada's Beaufort Sea region, which leads into the Arctic Ocean. Gulf Canada, a unit of Toronto-based Olympia & York Developments Ltd., which is controlled by the Reichmann family, is the only company that planned to drill there this year.

The Environmental Impact Review Board said Gulf Canada, an energy concern, and federal regulators had demonstrated "complacency" toward environmental protection and wildlife preservation. The regulators, the Canada Oil and Gas Lands Administration, will rule on Gulf Canada's drilling plans.

Gulf Canada had proposed to drill four wells in the Beaufort Sea in the next three years.

The environmental board, made up of representatives from the federal government, the governments of the Yukon and Northwest Territories, and Western Arctic Inuit natives, said Gulf Canada hadn't been able to estimate the effects of an oil spill on the area's coastline or to give details of how the company would carry out cleanup operations in the event of a spill. It said Gulf Canada didn't provide adequate evidence at a public hearing last month to enable the board to estimate the cost of cleaning up a hypothetical worst-case oil spill.

Gulf Canada officials estimated that a worst-case well blowout could release 40,000 barrels of oil a day for 66 days, or 10 times more oil than that released when Exxon Corp.'s Valdez tanker ran aground in Alaska last year. The board said such a spill could have "devastating impacts on sensitive shorelines and on wildlife that could last for decades."

Frank Mitton, manager of Gulf Canada's northern operations, said the board's comments were even "harsher than anticipated." Gulf Canada has "all the plans and precautions to make sure we don't have an environmental disaster," he said.

The review board said it blamed the lack of preparedness for dealing with oil spills on "a complacency" on the part of Gulf Canada and the government, which is "driven by an ultimate belief that a blowout is so unlikely to occur that preparing to meet it is largely an academic exercise."

It added: "This is the kind of complacency that has been blamed for the confused response to the Exxon Valdez oil spill."

Maurice Taschereau, administrator of the Canada Oil and Gas Lands Administration, said he was "shaken and puzzled" by the board's criticism. He said the agency has "lots of contingency plans" in place to deal with environmental disasters. Mr. Taschereau declined to indicate how his agency will respond to the board's recommendations. It has 30 days to reach a decision.

Stephen Hazell, executive director of the Canadian Arctic Resources Committee, an environmental group, said the federal regulator can't reject the board's advice without calling into question the government's own environmental review process.

No wells are being drilled in the Beaufort Sea now. Amoco Canada Petroleum Co., a unit of Chicago-based Amoco Corp., recently canceled its plans to drill two wells in the Beaufort Sea, because of insufficient interest from its partners to proceed with the project. An Amoco spokesman said the company now intends to carry out some seismic work this summer.
