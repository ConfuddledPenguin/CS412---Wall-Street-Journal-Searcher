Farley Empire's Ratings on Debt Are Cut by S&P

  • Author: Robert Johnson
  • Date: 07/05/90

CHICAGO -- Standard & Poor's Corp. lowered its ratings on about $1.14 billion of debt issued by the investment and manufacturing empire controlled by beleaguered William Farley. Separately, Mr. Farley's troubled, closely held holding company, Farley Inc., disclosed in its annual report that its 1989 net loss widened to $814.7 million from $41.8 million in 1988.

A recently obtained auditor's report to Farley Inc. directors by the accounting firm of Ernst & Young cites the company's previously reported failure to make interest payments on certain debt. "These conditions raise substantial doubt about Farley Inc.'s ability to continue as a going concern," the report said.

Farley Inc.'s latest annual report states current assets of $1.57 billion and current liabilities of $3.25 billion.

S&P lowered Farley Inc.'s subordinated debt rating to single-C from triple-C, and lowered ratings on the senior debt of Farley's 95%-owned West Point-Pepperell Inc. unit to triple-C-minus from single-B-minus. The ratings concern said Farley Inc., based in Chicago, "remains in a strained financial condition due to its heavy debt load, and the general weakness of some of Farley's operating units."

Mr. Farley hasn't been able to complete last year's protracted $1.56 billion tender offer for West Point-Pepperell, a textile company based in West Point, Ga.

A Farley spokeswoman had no immediate comment.

Mr. Farley's financial strength was based on junk bonds. That strength is ebbing in the wake of the collapse of his investment banker, Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc.

Meanwhile, Mr. Farley continues to negotiate with his bondholders and bankers over voting control and majority ownership of West Point-Pepperell, according to sources close to the situation. Kidder, Peabody & Co., financial adviser to some of Mr. Farley's bondholders, suggested to its clients in May that they seek voting control, according to a person close to those discussions.

Troubles worsened for Mr. Farley and his creditors in March when West Point Acquisition Corp., the unit he set up to buy West Point-Pepperell, fell into default on $796 million in bank debt and missed bondholder interest payments.
