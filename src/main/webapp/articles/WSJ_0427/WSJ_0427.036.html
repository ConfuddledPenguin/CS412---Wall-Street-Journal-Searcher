Xerox Had Loss Of $254 Million In First Quarter --- Bigger-Than-Expected Gap Reflects Deterioration In Financial-Services Line

  • Author: Laurence Hooper
  • Date: 04/27/90

Xerox Corp. posted a bigger-than-expected first-quarter loss of $254 million, including a one-time charge of $375 million, reflecting a rapid deterioration in the company's financial-services business. One analyst called the business's performance "disastrous." But the loss also masked solid results from the company's core business equipment operation, which had a 20% profit gain.

The Stamford, Conn., company's loss compared with profit of $158 million in the year-ago period. The loss was $2.93 a share, compared with profit of $1.47 a share. Revenue grew 2.8% to $4.26 billion from $4.14 billion.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Xerox dropped $1.25 to close at $51.

The one-time charge, disclosed April 3, was related to the company's abandonment of its investments in VMS Realty Partners, a Chicago real estate concern. The previous day, Vice Chairman Melvin Howard, who heads Xerox Financial Services, had said he would take early retirement May 1. Industry watchers said both moves signaled Xerox's growing impatience with costly attempts to diversify away from its strength in copiers and other office machines.

Analysts said that the latest dismal showing by financial services -- revenue sagged 5%, and profit plummeted 84% from $67 million to $11 million, on woeful results from insurer Crum & Forster Inc. -- should strengthen Xerox's resolve to deemphasize this area.

"My guess is that we've already seen the first in a series of steps," said Alex Henderson of Prudential-Bache Securities Inc., noting that Crum & Forster stopped writing personal lines of insurance this past week. "Allaire has given marching orders to put a tourniquet on the cash flow going into financial services," he said, referring to Paul Allaire, Xerox president, who will become chief executive officer Aug. 1.

On a brighter note, analysts praised gains in cost control, rental and service revenue on the business equipment side, though the analysts tagged copier sales as weak. "But in document processing, at least, things are going reasonably well," said Eugene Glazer of Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

Still, he and other analysts spent yesterday figuring out new estimates of Xerox's 1990 earnings. Not counting the $3.99-a-share charge, most guesses had hovered from $6 to $6.50 a share. The consensus is now likely to settle well below $6.
