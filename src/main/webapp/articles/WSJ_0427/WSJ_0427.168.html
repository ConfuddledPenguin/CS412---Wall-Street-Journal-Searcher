Citizens Savings Sues 2 U.S. Units; Breach Of Contract Alleged

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/27/90

MIAMI -- Citizens Savings Financial Corp. said it sued two federal agencies for breach of contract involving Citizens' acquisition of an Illinois thrift. The suit, filed in U.S. District Court in Washington, charges that the U.S. government broke an agreement made with the company when Citizens acquired American Savings Bank, a failing thrift, in December 1988.

According to Citizens, the Federal Home Loan Bank Board and the Federal Savings and Loan Insurance Corp. agreed to provide financial assistance to Citizens, including a cash contribution, when it made the acquisition. That cash contribution -- $86 million -- was to be credited to Citizens' regulatory capital account and amortized over 25 years.

In 1989, however, those two federal agencies were abolished under the Financial Institutions Reform, Recovery and Enforcement Act. With the passage of that act, according to Citizens, all previous regulatory agreements were voided -- including Citizens' ability to count $86 million toward its capital account.

Citizens filed its suit against the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. and the Office of Thrift Supervision, the successors to the two abolished agencies. Citizens said it is asking the court to declare that the Financial Institutions Reform Act resulted in a breach of contract and, thus, took Citizens' property without just compensation and due process in violation of the Fifth Amendment. The suit also seeks an injunction prohibiting enforcement of those provisions of the act that breached the original agreement.

Officials at OTS said they hadn't seen the suit and couldn't comment. The FDIC said that while it also hadn't seen the litigation, it believes the spirit of the act is correct.

Citizens is a $4 billion, five-state federal savings bank, with 92 offices in Florida, Illinois, Ohio, California and Virginia.
