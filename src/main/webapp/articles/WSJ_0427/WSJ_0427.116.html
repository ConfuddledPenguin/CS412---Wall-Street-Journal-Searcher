U.S. Clears Routes For Continental And American Air

  • Author: Laurie McGinley and Bridget O'Brian
  • Date: 04/27/90

The U.S. Transportation Department granted Continental Airlines, a unit of Houston-based Texas Air Corp., authority to fly passengers and cargo between Houston and Tokyo. The agency also gave final approval to the sale by Eastern Airlines, another Texas Air unit, of its Latin American routes to American Airlines, a unit of AMR Corp., Fort Worth, Texas.

Continental has said it expects to earn $25 million a year from the Houston-Tokyo route. The route calls for a stopover in Honolulu, which also gives Continental a chance to compete for Honolulu-Tokyo travelers. About 40% of U.S. traffic to Tokyo flies through Honolulu. Continental said it will begin flying the route daily on July 2.

The department's decision is unrelated to a U.S.-Japan route case that will greatly expand service to Asia. In that case, which won't be decided until the fall, nine U.S. carriers are competing for six new routes to Japan -- three to Tokyo and three to other Japanese cities.

The route authority granted to Continental once belonged to Federal Express Corp., Memphis, Tenn., which had to relinquish it because Flying Tiger Line, acquired by Federal Express, already had a U.S.-Japan route. The Transportation Department had said it would allow the succeeding carrier to fly passengers, as well.

One reason that Continental was chosen over five rivals was that it proposed service without a plane change to Houston, the only one of the nation's four largest cities that doesn't have single-plane service to Tokyo, said Patrick Murphy, the Transportation Department's deputy assistant secretary for policy and international affairs. The other candidates were American Airlines, Delta Air Lines of Atlanta, the Aloha Airlines unit of Aloha Airgroup Inc. of Honolulu, America West Airlines of Phoenix, Ariz., and the Hawaiian Airlines unit of HAL Inc., Honolulu.

In approving the transfer of Eastern's routes to American, the department concluded that the sale is in the public interest and won't conflict with aviation policy objectives.

The agency's approval is the last hurdle for the transaction, which will add $349 million to Eastern's bankruptcy-law reorganization efforts. Eastern and American have tentatively scheduled July 1 as the date American will begin flying the routes.

The department also approved the sale to American of Eastern's Miami-Madrid route and of Continental's Miami-London certificate. But the agency refused for now to approve the sale of Eastern's Miami/Tampa-Toronto route to American. It said it will begin a review to determine whether American or another carrier should be given the authority.
