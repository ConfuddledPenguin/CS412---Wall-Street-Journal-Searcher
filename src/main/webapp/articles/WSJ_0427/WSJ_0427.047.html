Rival Paper's End Fails to Aid Pulitzer Stock

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 04/27/90

ST. LOUIS -- Pulitzer Publishing Co.'s flagship St. Louis Post-Dispatch paper has regained its monopoly as the city's sole daily, following the failure of the upstart St. Louis Sun. But the day after the late-afternoon announcement of the Sun's fate, Pulitzer shares fell 25 cents to close at $27.25. Analysts said the market's indifferent reaction reflected just how little of a factor the Sun actually was, in comparison to the ongoing softness in advertising sales that is plaguing the Post-Dispatch, along with many other newspapers.

Kenneth F. Cook, an analyst with Burns, Pauli & Co. in St. Louis, said, "I think an awful lot of the Sun's demise was in the stock already. There was an expectation that this thing wasn't working, that it was only a matter of time" before Ingersoll Newspapers Inc. folded the Sun. The Sun began printing in September and lasted only seven months.

In recent quarters, Pulitzer has conceded that the new competition had helped push down Post-Dispatch earnings, although the media concern has stressed that a weak advertising market played a bigger role. Largely because of the downturn at the Post-Dispatch, Pulitzer's earnings for the first quarter tumbled 70% to $906,000, or nine cents a share, from the year-earlier $3 million, or 29 cents.

The Sun's "impact has been primarily on {Pulitzer's} cost side" in terms of promotional and other efforts to counter the new competitor, said Mr. Cook. He noted that the Sun never hurt circulation at the Post-Dispatch, and said it's unclear just how many ad dollars it had drawn from the Post-Dispatch.

At least for now, Mr. Cook said, he is holding to his earlier projection that Pulitzer will have per-share earnings of about $1.40 for the current year. In 1989, the company had earnings of $31.4 million, or $3 a share, but the figures were boosted by a net $12.7 million gain from sale of a broadcasting property.

A.G. Edwards analyst Michael Kupinski said, "Advertising support for the Sun wasn't that significant." The Post's recent earnings declines "have been 60% the result of the economy, 40% the Sun," he said. Although "when you return to a monopoly status, that's always good" because of the increased leverage over advertising rates, he doubted there's much near-term advantage to be gleaned from the death of the Sun. "Looking forward," however, "it's a positive" for Pulitzer, he said.
