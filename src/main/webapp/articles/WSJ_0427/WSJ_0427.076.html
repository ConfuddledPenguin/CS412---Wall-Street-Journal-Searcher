Vickers Defeats Bid for Spinoff Of Rolls-Royce

  • Author: Barbara Toman
  • Date: 04/27/90

LONDON -- Vickers PLC shareholders overwhelmingly defeated Sir Ron Brierley's attempt to force the engineering concern to spin off its Rolls-Royce luxury-cars unit. By a 2-to-1 margin, holders voted against the New Zealand entrepreneur's proposal at Vickers's annual meeting yesterday. The proposal, strongly opposed by Vickers executives, had been expected to fail.

But Sir Ron evidently doesn't intend to back down. Yesterday morning, about an hour before the annual meeting started, Vickers said Sir Ron's IEP Securities Ltd. had raised its stake in the company to 20.05% from 19.54%. And Sir Ron, speaking to reporters later during a lull in the meeting, didn't rule out buying more Vickers shares. IEP will persist in its efforts to "unlock the value of Rolls-Royce," he said, envisioning a timetable of "years, rather than months."

Sir Ron has spent about two years amassing the stake in Vickers, an engineering conglomerate with interests in defense contracting and marine and medical equipment, as well as its Rolls-Royce Motor Car Ltd. unit. Last March, he proposed that Vickers issue to existing holders shares in Rolls-Royce as a separate public company.

The investor argues that a separately quoted Rolls-Royce would trade at a price of at least 12 times this year's earnings, compared with Vickers's price-earnings ratio of about 8.8. Spinning off Rolls-Royce would result in "an immediate gain for all shareholders of at least 50% in the value of the Rolls-Royce component of their shares," he told Vickers holders.

But Vickers executives insisted Rolls-Royce needs the security of the parent. Sir David Plastow, Vickers's chairman and chief executive officer, criticized Sir Ron for having "a really short-term" attitude. Though it is successful, Rolls-Royce "has a long way to go before it reaches any form of maturity," Sir David said.

Holders voiced more qualms about the company's plan to buy Carlton Communications PLC's Cosworth division, a specialty automotive-engineering unit, for #163.5 million ($267.7 million). Some appeared to agree with Sir Ron, who criticized the agreed price as "awfully expensive." But the resolution approving the purchase eventually passed.
