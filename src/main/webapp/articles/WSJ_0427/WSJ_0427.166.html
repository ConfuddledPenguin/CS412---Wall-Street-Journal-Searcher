Citicorp Securities Totaling $30 Billion Are Downgraded by Standard & Poor's

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 04/27/90

NEW YORK -- In another blow to the nation's banking industry, Standard & Poor's Corp. downgraded the long-term and most short-term debt of Citicorp, the nation's largest bank. The downgrading affected $30 billion of debt and preferred securities of Citicorp, the parent of Citibank. The lone obligation that escaped the downgrading was Citicorp's commercial paper, an important source of short-term funds that was affirmed at A1-plus.

Standard & Poor's cited Citicorp's "aggressive" posture in maintaining levels of capital and reserves for possible loan losses well below those of competing banks.

While Citicorp debt remains investment-grade, the downgrading is bound to raise the cost of money for the bank and to intensify pressure on Chairman John S. Reed to boost capital and reserves for loan losses through asset sales and issuance of new common shares.

In a statement, Citicorp said, "We are disappointed in S&P's action. We are confident that the strength of Citicorp's core businesses and well-positioned global franchises will continue to provide excellent returns and growth."

The announcement of the downgrading came after the close of trading yesterday. orp shares ended unchanged at $22.375 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Citicorp is the 44th and largest U.S. banking company to have its credit ratings downgraded in recent months. The downgradings reflect worries about the banking industry's rising level of bad loans, particularly for commercial real estate in the Northeast and Southeast, and about bank loans for highly leveraged transactions, such as leveraged buy-outs and recapitalizations.

Just last week, the other major rating agency, Moody's Investors Service Inc., announced a review of Citicorp obligations for a possible downgrade. In the wake of that announcement, yields on Citicorp debt have climbed.

Citicorp, with $230 billion of assets, is by far the largest U.S. bank holding company. Long noted for its aggressive banking practices, Citicorp has kept its capital and reserves for loan losses at levels below those of most competitors. At a recent meeting for securities analysts, Mr. Reed said, "I'm as concerned about capital efficiency as capital sufficiency."

At the end of the first quarter, Citicorp reported equity of 3.6% of assets, well below the industry norm of 4%. That places it near the bottom of the nation's 50 largest banking companies. Moreover, analysts noted that percentage drops to 2.9% when they factor in good will, or the premium paid over book value in acquisitions, and an adjustment for an accounting change that Citicorp hasn't yet adopted.

Meanwhile, Citicorp's nonperforming loans, especially on commercial real estate and highly leveraged transactions, have climbed sharply, but reserve levels have lagged well behind.

A Citicorp official, however, expects the downgrading to have a "marginal" impact on Citicorp's earnings this year because the banking company gets about 60% of its funds from deposits, which generally aren't affected by ratings changes. Last year, Citicorp raised about $2.5 billion in long-term debt and expects to sell even less long-term debt this year.

As of March 30, Citicorp's $4.5 billion reserve for possible losses stood at 61% of its loans that are more than 90 days past due and 2.8% of total loans. Most competitors' reserves generally are 70% to 80% of past-due loans and about 5% of total loans.

A Citicorp official maintained that those industry norms may be appropriate for some banks but not for Citicorp. The company's global reach and diverse product lines, the official said, give it an inherent stability that allows it to run with leaner reserves and capital.

S&P reduced the double-A rating on Citicorp's long-term debt to double-A-minus and the rating on its subordinated debt to single-A-plus from double-A-minus. Citicorp's preferred stock was dropped to single-A from single-A-plus. The rating on its flagship Citibank's letters of credit, certificates of deposit and deposit notes was reduced to double-A/A-1-plus from double-A-plus/A-1-plus.
