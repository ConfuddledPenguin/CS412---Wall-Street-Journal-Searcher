Economy: Building Contracts Grew 4% in March, F.W. Dodge Reports

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/27/90

NEW YORK -- Construction contracting grew 4% in March from February, reversing a five-month slide that had been heavily concentrated in the Northeast, F.W. Dodge said. The gain stemmed from a 15% rise in nonresidential projects -- commercial, industrial and institutional building -- said the forecasting division of publisher McGraw-Hill Inc. The value of residential building, which includes single-family and apartment units, slipped 2%; while so-called non-building construction -- public works and utilities -- eased 1%.

Institutional building, the nonresidential sector that includes schools and health-care facilities, continued strong, rising 29% in March.

But office construction, included under the commercial heading, "has been declining for four years and is still on the way down," said George A. Christie, vice president and chief economist at Dodge. "It's the single most overbuilt category of construction since the mid-'80s boom, and it's going to be the longest one to dig it's way out."

March construction equaled a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $244.98 billion, compared with $235.97 billion, in February.

Dodge reported that its index grew to 156 in March, from 150 in February. The index, a seasonally adjusted leading indicator of construction, uses a base of 100, which was set in 1982.

The value of first-quarter construction fell 6%, from first-quarter 1989, Dodge reported, to an unadjusted $52.65 billion, from $56.11 billion.

Despite the overall gain in non-residential building, the Northeast and South still lagged behind first-quarter 1989 in construction -- contracting value by 25% and 8%, respectively, Dodge said. In the North Central region and the West, construction gained 5% and 3%, respectively.
