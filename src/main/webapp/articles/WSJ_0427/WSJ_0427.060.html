BTR Has Sold Its 1.7% Stake in Norton In Market in Wake of Saint-Gobain Offer

  • Author: David Stipp
  • Date: 04/27/90

BTR PLC, whose hostile bid for Norton Co. was topped Wednesday by friendly suitor Cie. de Saint-Gobain S.A., disclosed that it sold its Norton stake soon after Saint-Gobain's $90-a-share offer was announced. London-based BTR had said it didn't plan to top Saint-Gobain's $1.9 billion offer for Norton. In a Securities and Exchange Commission filing, BTR reported that it sold its 325,000 shares, or 1.7% stake, in Norton for $88.6615 a share in the open market. BTR, which last month launched a $75-a-share, or $1.64 billion, hostile bid for Norton, bought its Norton stock for between $49.54 and $53 a share.

Meanwhile, Norton held an anticlimactic annual shareholders meeting in Worcester, Mass., where it is based. Before agreeing to be acquired by Saint-Gobain, a French maker of glass, packaging and building materials, Norton faced the prospect of a prolonged takeover battle against BTR that Norton was expected to lose.

"I feel like a condemned prisoner who has received an 11th-hour pardon from the governor," John M. Nelson, Norton's chairman, president and chief executive officer, told those at the meeting. Mr. Nelson received a standing ovation from the audience. Many who attended were Norton employees who had staged rallies to support Norton's resistance to BTR. They were clearly pleased with a commitment by Saint-Gobain, made in its acquisition agreement with Norton, to retain Norton's existing policies on employment and other matters.

In a news conference after the meeting, Mr. Nelson disclosed that when trying to fend off BTR, Norton had approached some 20 companies in hopes of finding a friendly suitor to buy a minority stake in Norton. Six to eight of them had taken a detailed look at Norton, but only Saint-Gobain made a "concrete" offer, he added. Saint-Gobain, however, wanted to acquire Norton outright, which Norton's directors agreed to in order to avoid a "war of attrition" with BTR, Mr. Nelson said.

Mr. Nelson also said Saint-Gobain had agreed to pay $90 a share for Norton "very early" in the negotiations between the companies, and the talks had centered mainly on concessions Norton wanted from Saint-Gobain aimed at keeping Norton in its present form for at least five years.

While Norton's purchase by Saint-Gobain is expected to be completed, at least one sticky wicket remains: the question of whether the transaction threatens national security. Norton makes abrasives, ceramics and other products, and its customers include various branches of the U.S. military. When battling with BTR, Norton and its supporters raised national security issues about Norton's acquisition by a foreign company. At least one congressman, Rep. Silvio O. Conte (R., Mass.) has stated that he supports an investigation by President Bush into national security issues surrounding Norton's purchase by Saint-Gobain, although he termed it a "far superior option" compared with a takeover by BTR.
