Australian Economy Must Limit Demand, Says OECD Report

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/27/90

SYDNEY -- Australia must restrain demand in its economy to reduce consumption, inflation and imports, the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development said in its latest survey on the country. That implies Australian interest rates should be kept relatively high, a recommendation likely to be accepted by the Australian government and central bank.

Both have warned recently that demand, particularly from consumers, must be suppressed for several years to restrain imports while exports increase.

Australia's main economic goal during the 1990s is to narrow its current-account deficit, halting the growth of a still-rising foreign debt of 148 billion Australian dollars ($114.95 billion), and then to reduce that debt.

Suppressing consumer demand also would help combat the nation's high 8% inflation rate. Only last week, the central bank called for a major effort against inflation by the Labor Party government. Labor won a national election March 24, and doesn't have to face the voters again for three years. That provides an opportunity for the government to introduce unpopular anti-inflation measures and maintain high interest rates.

The OECD report issued this week is based on its study of the economy last December. It says demand in the economy surged in recent years following a recovery of world prices for Australia's farm and mineral exports, which lifted national income.

Tight government fiscal and monetary policies started to slow demand late in 1989, the OECD says, but a severe recession is unlikely. "The challenge now is to ensure that the necessary weakening of domestic demand growth continues, while pursuing policies conducive to higher, sustainable output growth and lower inflation over the longer term," the OECD advises.
