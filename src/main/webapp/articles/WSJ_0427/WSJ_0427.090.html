International: Some Japanese See Thaw in Trade War, But Others Fear It's Just Temporary Lull

  • Author: Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 04/27/90

TOKYO -- The Bush administration's apparent decision to refrain from again branding Japan as an unfair trading partner stems a year-long erosion in relations between the two countries. But while some government officials here are proclaiming a thaw in the trade war, many observers fear this is only a temporary lull, with contentious issues looming a few months away.

"The immediate problems seem now to have been sorted out," Michael Armacost, the U.S. ambassador to Japan, said at a news conference. But, he added, "we've got a lot of follow-up work to do."

The Bush administration continued to play its cards close to the chest yesterday. White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said the president still hasn't decided whether to name Japan as an unfair trader under the so-called Super 301 clause of the 1988 Trade Act. But Mr. Fitzwater did praise Trade Representative Carla Hills's congressional testimony Wednesday, in which she indicated the U.S. wouldn't name Japan for a second year.

The president's Economic Policy Council met yesterday, and administration officials said it was clear from the meeting that Japan wouldn't be cited as an unfair trader. The council will relay its findings to Mr. Bush in time for him to make a decision by Monday, Mr. Fitzwater said. The administration officials said the decision could come as early as today.

But with the writing already on the wall, there was a sigh of relief in Tokyo this week, in sharp contrast to the bitter outpouring a year ago when the U.S. used the Super 301 provisions to cite Japan for protecting three domestic industries.

"One cannot but be impressed by the change and how far we have come," said a senior Foreign Ministry official.

But there have been periods of premature euphoria before. In 1988, after Japan agreed to open its beef, citrus and construction markets, U.S. officials said publicly that there were no trade disputes outstanding between the two countries.

The U.S. and Japan still face discussions this year on construction, intellectual property, telecommunications equipment and on Allied-Signal Inc.'s ability to sell a new kind of metal product in Japan. In mid-1991, the five-year U.S.-Japan semiconductor agreement expires, and the American industry remains disappointed with its access to the Japanese market.

"Although the sophisticated way of handling things is not to name Japan {under Super 301}, the fact remains that all these issues will be taken up anyway between the countries," noted Hajime Ohta, director of international economic affairs for Keidanren, a business organization.

Still, America's trade deficit with Japan, a subject of heated political debate in the U.S., is likely to widen later this year if the yen remains weak, making U.S. exports to Japan more expensive and Japanese imports to the U.S. less so.

But Japanese officials hope the recent events augur a basic change in the traditional trade relationship, under which the U.S. pressures Japan, which then caves in at the last minute under threat of sanctions. This year they settled negotiations on satellites, supercomputers and wood products as much as two months before the official deadline, the Japanese said.

Mr. Armacost, the U.S. ambassador, said at the news conference that "we have a chance to work on them a little more quietly." The atmosphere was anything but quiet when Washington initially invoked the 1988 Trade Act. Japan said that the Super 301 provisions were illegal and that it would refuse to negotiate under them.

But as the months dragged on, the anger went beyond the specific trade issues to a deep fear that the U.S. had turned hostile to Japan and that the overall relationship was fraying. The showdown came in the past few weeks, with a string of deadlines for U.S. retaliation.

The U.S.-Japan trade talks had been going so poorly on several fronts that President Bush invited Japanese Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu to an emergency summit meeting in early March. Since then, Japan has hastened to tidy up more than a half-dozen pending and potential trade battles and has written a rough draft for a plan to make its economy more open to imports.

Japanese government officials, grown paranoid about the level of anti-Japan rhetoric in Washington, say that for the first time in months they feel "the atmosphere {there} has changed in such a way that those people who like to stand up and say positive things about Japan can do so," the Foreign Ministry official said. "That wasn't the case two months ago."
