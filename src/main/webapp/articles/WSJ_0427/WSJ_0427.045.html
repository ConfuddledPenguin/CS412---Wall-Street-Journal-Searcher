Japan's Kaifu Sets Out for South Asia In Effort to Assure Link to the Region

  • Author: Kathryn Graven
  • Date: 04/27/90

TOKYO -- Riding a wave of popular support at home, Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu sets out tomorrow for a nine-day tour of South Asia to show Japan's commitment to Asia and to helping developing economies. While the rest of Japan celebrates the annual Golden Week of holidays, Mr. Kaifu will pay official visits to India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Sri Lanka and Indonesia. It is the first time in six years that a Japanese head of state has visited the region.

Foreign Ministry officials say the purpose of his trip is to reassure Japan's neighbors that despite all the recent changes in Europe, Tokyo still places a high priority on South Asian economic development. Officials say Mr. Kaifu will stress that whatever Japanese aid and investment goes to Europe it will not come at the expense of South Asia and the Association of Southeast Asian Nations, or ASEAN.

Japan is already South Asia's biggest foreign aid donor, handing over $1.1 billion in official development assistance and loans to India, Bangladesh, Pakistan and Sri Lanka in 1988, up from about $970 million in 1987. Indonesia, the single largest recipient of Japanese aid, received about $984 million in aid and loans in 1988, up from $707 million in 1987.

But since there are no major burning political issues between Japan and these countries at the moment, political analysts say Mr. Kaifu is likely to use the diplomatic junket to further bolster his domestic standing.

"Increasingly, Japanese prime ministers have limited room for action at home. So they rely on foreign policy to enhance their domestic power," says Takashi Inoguchi, a professor of political science at Tokyo University.

Already, Mr. Kaifu appears to have grasped this tactic. In his early days in office, he was seen as a puppet prime minister, easily manipulated by former Prime Minister Noboru Takeshita. But since the Lower House elections in February, Mr. Kaifu has won high marks for his delicate handling of heated bilateral trade issues with the U.S. A recent poll by the Yomiuri Shimbun showed his approval rating has risen to 56.1%, up from 52.9% in March and the highest since he was inaugurated last August.

At the same time, political analysts say Mr. Kaifu wants to widen Japan's foreign policy field beyond ties to the U.S. "He wants to show that he has contacts in Asia," says Hiroichi Yamaguchi, a senior researcher at the Institute of Developing Economies in Tokyo.

While the timing of the trip takes advantage of the holiday lull in Japan, observers say it also gives Mr. Kaifu the chance to sound out South Asian leaders on their views on global issues before the summit of advanced industrialized nations in Houston this July. Increasingly, developing countries in Asia are relying on Japan to voice their concerns at international meetings.

Analysts also expect that the governments of India, Bangladesh, Pakistan and Sri Lanka will all ask the Japanese government to encourage more private investment in their countries. Currently, less than 1% of Japan's overseas investment goes to South Asia.

For all the expected toasts to improving relations, few political analysts expect Japan to suddenly start playing a more active role in the region's conflicts. Foreign Ministry officials say Mr. Kaifu will encourage the governments of India and Pakistan to continue their efforts towards peace, but that Japan has no intention of playing a role as a mediator.
