Foreign Exchange: Dollar Ekes Out Gains Against Mark On Unease Over German Monetary Union

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/27/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar eked out some gains against the mark and yen, benefiting from a resurgence of unease over the Germanys' monetary union and selling of the yen against the mark late in the New York trading day. Traders said the U.S. currency was helped by development of a "show-me" attitude toward the Bundesbank's stated intention to keep the mark strong.

"It will take more than talk {to preserve a strong mark}. The Bundesbank needs to follow through, to do something," said Bob Hinton, a vice president at Harris Trust & Savings Bank in New York.

In Frankfurt, Bundesbank President Karl Otto Poehl said the West German central bank "will do everything in its power" to maintain the mark's stability, reaffirming what the Bundesbank said Wednesday in its 1989 annual report.

The market takes follow-through action to mean a rise in West German interest rates, but as there probably won't be any news on that until at least after Thursday's Bundesbank Central Council meeting, there's no reason to sell the dollar aggressively against the mark, Mr. Hinton said.

Other participants doubted the Bundesbank would act quickly to protect the mark. Any such action isn't likely to come until after monetary union takes place July 2, the traders said.

The West German currency was also hurt a bit by concern that the Bundesbank might go too far in raising interest rates to protect the mark, damaging West German industry, said Peter Iversen, a trader at Shawmut Bank of Boston.

The U.S. unit received some support against the mark and yen from a modest shift to long dollar positions ahead of the scheduled announcement today of preliminary data expected to show a healthy increase in U.S. gross national product for the first quarter, said Hubert Pedroli, vice president and manager of foreign exchange at Credit Suisse in New York.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.6803 marks, up from 1.6785 marks late Wednesday, and at 159.23 yen, above 158.75 yen late Wednesday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6343, down from $1.6403 Wednesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Friday, the U.S. currency was quoted at 159.25 yen, up from Thursday's Tokyo close of 158.77 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.6798 marks.

The data are predicted by economists to show GNP at an annual growth rate of 2.7%. GNP grew at an annual rate of 1.1% in the fourth quarter. Growth for 1989 as a whole was 2.2%.

The yen declined again around midday in New York, when traders reversed their previous sales of marks for yen.

After falling to 94.44 yen late in the London day from 94.51 yen in Tokyo, the mark ended the New York day around 94.763 yen, above the Wednesday close in New York of 94.59 yen.

Yen sales for marks and the Swiss franc, which Japanese corporations continued to buy to pay for maturing Swiss franc-denominated bonds, helped the dollar break 159 yen and remain there in the second half of New York's day, traders said.

Aside from the Japanese purchases of Swiss francs that have brought the currency new attention, the Swiss unit is benefiting from the Swiss National Bank's successful effort to convince the market that the bank is determined to do whatever is necessary to keep the franc strong, said Credit Suisse's Mr. Pedroli.

The Swiss franc finished the New York day at 109.226 yen, above 108.58 yen in Tokyo and 108.91 yen late Wednesday in New York. The franc finished at 107.12 yen in Tokyo Wednesday.

In Europe, the dollar ended moderately lower against most major currencies from its Wednesday closing levels.

Dealers said the market was more than likely to wait for the U.S. GNP data before committing aggressively in either direction on the dollar.

In Tokyo, the dollar finished higher as dealings once again focused on non-dollar trading. The dollar finished at 158.77 yen, below 159.25 yen late Wednesday in Tokyo.

Many Japanese market participants are waiting for the end of next week's holidays before taking any new positions against the U.S. currency, according to dealers.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $370.50 an ounce, down 20 cents. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.3 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $371.05 an ounce.
