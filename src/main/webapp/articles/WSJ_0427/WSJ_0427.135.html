Robert Campeau to Repay Loans in Deal To Regain Control of His Troubled Firm

  • Author: Robert Melnbardis
  • Date: 04/27/90

TORONTO -- Robert Campeau has worked out a deal to regain control of his troubled retail and real-estate empire. The embattled chairman of Campeau Corp. agreed to pay his major personal creditor, Montreal-based National Bank of Canada, 80 million Canadian dollars (US$68.9 million) to retire C$153 million of personal loans, according to a filing with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission. The repayment works out to about 52 cents on the dollar.

Under the agreement, if Mr. Campeau pays C$40 million by May 31, National Bank will return Campeau securities it seized from him in January. The bank had registered shares and convertible debentures representing 18.9 million Campeau common shares, or a 37.7% fully diluted stake in the company, when it said that Mr. Campeau had missed interest payments on the C$153 million of debt.

The filing didn't indicate the source of Mr. Campeau's financing for the transaction, or whether he has obtained the necessary funds. The seizure by National Bank decreased his fully diluted stake in Campeau to about 21% from 43%.

If Mr. Campeau regains those securities, he again will be the largest shareholder in the company. The Reichmann family's Olympia & York Developments Ltd. currently has a 38.4% fully diluted interest in Campeau. Olympia & York officials couldn't be reached for comment.

The agreement between National Bank and Mr. Campeau also stipulates that he will pay another C$40 million plus interest after U.S. bankruptcy courts approve the restructuring of Campeau's retailing units, Federated Department Stores Inc. and Allied Stores Corp. Federated and Allied sought protection from creditors in January under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code, and it could take many months for the units to emerge from the proceedings. Mr. Campeau must make the second C$40 million payment by Jan. 31, 1992 or National Bank will take back C$60.4 million of Campeau convertible debentures.

The agreement between National Bank and Mr. Campeau could mean that he has the funds to settle the loans or has found a financial backer. A spokesman for the Reichmanns said they don't have any connection with financing related to Mr. Campeau's accord with National Bank. Lawyers for Mr. Campeau and National Bank declined to comment.

In February, two other Canadian banks sued Mr. Campeau because he missed payments on personal loans totaling C$39.2 million. Those banks declined to say whether Mr. Campeau has approached them with a proposal to repay the debt.

Lawyers close to the banks' court actions against Mr. Campeau speculated that his agreement with the National Bank might be an attempt to settle with all the banks for less than full value of the loans.

The Campeau securities that National Bank would return to Mr. Campeau include 13 million common shares, the C$60.4 million of convertible debentures and four million preference shares. The securities would be held by a holding company controlled by the Campeau family. Mr. Campeau's stake in the real estate and retailing concern includes about 3.6 million shares held in trust for his son Jacques and daughter Rachelle. Mr. Campeau previously sued them to retain voting control over the shares. Jacques Campeau didn't know about the agreement between his father and National Bank, a lawyer said. Campeau Corp. has about 44.3 million shares outstanding.
