Rigorous Rules On Safety Planned For Chemical Sites

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 04/27/90

WASHINGTON -- The Labor Department plans to require that chemical plants institute rigorous "process safety management plans" to guard against disasters. The plan to propose a new safety standard for chemical plants, incorporating policies already followed voluntarily by many companies, was disclosed in a department report to President Bush covering the explosions last October at a Phillips Petroleum Co. plastics complex near Houston. The catastrophe, one of the worst chemical-facility disasters since World War II, killed 23 workers and injured more than 130.

The department's Occupational Safety and Health Administration plans to propose that chemical companies be required to take such steps as evaluating chemical-plant valves to see if any pose a safety hazard, training company and contractor workers properly, ensuring that valves can be closed easily if chemical material is clogged in pipes, and managing changes in procedures that could pose new safety problems, Assistant Labor Secretary Gerard Scannell said.

Mr. Scannell, who runs OSHA, told a news conference, "I think we've got to do something right away" to make sure that similar disasters don't occur. He said the procedures encompassed by the coming proposal are ones that Phillips plants are supposed to follow, but didn't at the Houston complex.

While the petrochemical industry has had a lower injury rate than many other industries, major disasters have occurred in the industry, including a 1984 accident that killed more than 2,000 in Bhopal, India, and a 1985 explosion in Mexico City that killed about 650 that same year, the report said. It said accidents in the industry resulted from such factors as inadequate attention to the recognition of hazards, poorly maintained equipment, poor planning, and unsafe work practices.

Last week, OSHA cited Phillips for $5.7 million in fines on charges of willful violation of OSHA rules, including failure to follow company-prescribed methods of protecting workers against fire and explosion hazards. Fines of $730,000 were also proposed against Fish Engineering & Construction Inc., a Houston-area company doing maintenance work on the Phillips site when the explosions took place.

OSHA traced the Houston explosions to Phillips's failure to provide a backup valve or other means of preventing the escape of gases when a single ball valve wasn't closed during repairs. Polyethylene particles clogged settling legs in a pipe system, and were being cleared, when a buildup of highly flammable gases escaped from the unattended settling legs, OSHA said.

OSHA's plans for a chemical-process management rule were already in the works before the Houston disaster, officials said. Now, the agency also will revise its priorities, to better identify "catastrophic risks" in the petrochemical industry, such as those posed by the use of contractors, Mr. Scannell said.

The agency also released a preliminary report by the John Gray Institute, which concluded that petrochemical companies are relying more on contractors, and that contract workers have a high turnover rate, sustain more injuries, get less safety training than permanent workers, and work under looser safety supervision.

Mr. Scannell said injury reports for chemical plants cover only permanent workers, a situation that may mask higher injury rates for on-site contractors.
