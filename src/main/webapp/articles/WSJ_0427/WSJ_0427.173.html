McDonnell Douglas to Furlough 3,000 At Its Transport-Aircraft Subsidiary

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 04/27/90

LONG BEACH, Calif. -- McDonnell Douglas Corp., on the heels of an $84 million first-quarter loss at its transport-aircraft unit here, said it would lay off roughly 3,000 workers at the site, probably all of them salaried personnel. The cost-cutting move, which would affect about 6% of the subsidiary's work force, reverses the unit's previously stated goal of hiring new employees, even as the U.S. military budget shrinks in coming years. The company had thought that the development of its new commercial jetliners, the MD-11 and MD-90, would allow it to add about 1,000 this year to its rolls here, already at 50,000 or so.

Yet an admittedly "poor financial performance" has led McDonnell Douglas to reconsider. The company said it has undertaken "extensive efforts" to trim non-payroll expenses for such things as utilities and supplies. However, "those cuts have proved insufficient," the company explained in a statement, "leaving little choice" but to lay people off.

For the employees here, most of whom have already experienced considerable hardship because of a massive restructuring in February 1989 that forced them to redesign their jobs and then reapply for those slots, the prospect of a reduction was stunning news. "I think some people are shellshocked," one employee said late yesterday afternoon.

McDonnell Douglas stressed that the announcement of layoffs, the first at the Long Beach factory since 1983, had nothing to do with Defense Secretary Dick Cheney's proposal yesterday to chop the Air Force's planned fleet of C-17 cargo planes, which are built here. "It's an unfortunate coincidence," one company official said.

Doug Griffith, president of a United Auto Workers local that represents about 19,000 hourly McDonnell Douglas production-line workers, said he was promised that his membership "wouldn't be impacted" and that only general and administrative functions would be eliminated.

He said, in fact, that there would probably be more hires on the MD-11 line as it matures and moves to fill the 344 orders for the tri-jet booked to date. But, according to a company spokesman, any addition there would now have to come as the result of intracompany transfers. "There are a lot of instances where people have changed from blue collar to white collar over the years, and they may elect to accept one of those opportunties that remain open," he said. Moreover, there is a possibility of workers coming to Long Beach from McDonnell Douglas's jet-fighter facility in St. Louis, where several lines are slated for closure by the mid-1990s.

Late yesterday, McDonnell Douglas disclosed that to keep the employment level at its St. Louis factory as stable as possible, it was even going to begin assembling MD-11 nose sections, which it will then ship here.

McDonnell Douglas is counting on the Long Beach plant to be its big breadwinner by the end of the decade. But the transport unit, which has grown from fewer than 15,000 people in 1984, hasn't been able to capitalize on its huge backlog, and is suffering from inefficiencies. "They're straining under the load," Mr. Griffith said. "There has literally been a hemorrhaging of money."

The action at McDonnell Douglas adds pressure to an industry that already is experiencing downsizing. The Machinists union said a few weeks ago, for instance, that Calabasas, Calif.-based Lockheed Corp. was going to lay off about 2,500 workers, the bulk of them also white collar, in an effort to reduce overhead costs. Lockheed, though, has said no definite figure has been determined. General Motors Corp.'s Hughes Aircraft Co. division, Northrop Corp. and Boeing Co. all have been reducing their work forces in recent months.

McDonnell Douglas employed a total of almost 128,000 at the end of 1989.
