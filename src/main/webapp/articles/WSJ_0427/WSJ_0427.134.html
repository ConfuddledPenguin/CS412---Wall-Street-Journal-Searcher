UAL and USAir Post Quarterly Losses; Delta, Southwest Have Slender Profits

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani and Bridget O'Brian
  • Date: 04/27/90

Four major airlines confirmed predictions of a dismal March 31 quarter, reporting poor financial results compared with the year-earlier period. UAL Corp. and USAir Group Inc. recorded net losses while Delta Air Lines and Southwest Airlines each eked out small profits.

It is the second consecutive quarter that airline results have plunged. The primary culprit was an unexpected surge in the cost of aviation fuel.

UAL said the average price per gallon of fuel in the first quarter rose to 73.3 cents, compared with 60.7 cents a year earlier. The company said the rise contributed to a 23% jump in fuel expense to $386 million from $315 million. Delta said that fuel expense rose 27% and that it consumed 5% more than in the year-earlier fiscal third quarter.

"The thing that just kicked everybody in the pants was fuel," said Vivian Lee, an airline analyst at Merrill Lynch & Co. She estimated that in some cases fuel costs rose 30% from a year earlier.

Comparisons with the year-earlier period also were hurt because the busy Easter weekend this year didn't come until after March 31. Last year, airlines also reaped a windfall because of the strike at Texas Air Corp.'s Eastern Airlines. Even with its return, however, Eastern hasn't recaptured that many passengers, particularly the high-paying business traveler who is the meat-and-potatoes flier for most carriers.

Carriers attempted to boost their financial performance this year by raising fares several times, but the increases apparently didn't offset the higher cost of fuel. USAir also said it was hurt by the number of free tickets it shelled out under its frequent-flier program.

UAL, parent company of United Airlines, had a loss of $36.4 million, or $1.68 a share, compared with year-earlier profit of $65.4 million, or $3.03 cents a share.

The Chicago-based carrier was particularly hurt by a one-time, nonoperating charge of a $77.5 million settlement it paid to Texas Air's Continental Airlines unit. The settlement covered several lawsuits in which United was charged with unfairly competing in the computer-reservation business.

Revenue rose 8.1% to $2.52 billion from $2.33 billion. At a shareholders' meeting, UAL Chairman Stephen M. Wolf said he was "clearly disappointed" by the results. Without the one-time charge, results would have still been poor, he said.

UAL stock fell 25 cents yesterday to close at $152.50 a share in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

USAir reported a first-quarter loss of $38 million, or $1.05 a share, compared with net of $15.5 million, or 35 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue barely increased to $1.54 billion from $1.48 billion.

Jack T. Ciesielski, an airline analyst at Legg Mason Wood Walker Inc. in Baltimore, said improvements in on-time performance and pilot scheduling kept USAir's results from looking worse. Problems in both areas hampered the carrier through the fourth quarter.

USAir stock rose 25 cents to close at $27.50 in yesterday's Big Board trading.

Although both Delta and Southwest reported profits, earnings were sharply down from a year earlier.

Delta said net income fell 63% to $31.3 million, or 48 cents a share, from $85 million, or $1.73 a share, a year earlier. Like its competitors, Delta blamed the decrease primarily on the soaring cost of jet fuel. But the carrier acknowledged it would be hard-pressed to match the year-earlier numbers, when traffic was inflated by the strike at Eastern that sent travelers scurrying to Delta.

Still, Delta was able to increase its revenue 4% to $2.12 billion from $2.04 billion. Strong business traffic, higher fares and more cargo business helped keep revenue high, said Thomas Roeck, Delta's chief financial officer.

In the first nine months, Delta net fell 15% to $228.7 million, or $4.15 a share, from $270.1 million, or $5.49 a share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 9.8% to $6.34 billion from $5.78 billion.

In the quarter, Delta bought back 2,988,200 common shares for $202.3 million, bringing the total number of shares bought back to 6,255,662, at a total cost of $438.8 million. Last year, in an effort to make a takeover of the company more difficult, Delta placed 14% of its shares in an employee stock option plan and sold 5% stakes each to friendly carriers, Singapore Airlines and Swissair.

Delta stock rose $1.125 a share yesterday to close at $74 in Big Board composite trading.

Southwest's first-quarter net fell 74% to $5.1 million, or 18 cents a share, from $19.6 million, or 64 cents a share, a year earlier. Dallas-based Southwest said higher fuel prices pushed its operating expenses up 21.7%.

In addition, the carrier said profit was further squeezed by a decrease in nonoperating gains of $7.2 million. The first quarter of 1989 was a record for Southwest.

Still, revenue rose 14% to $260.6 million from $229.4 million.

In a statement, Herbert D. Kelleher, Southwest's chairman, president and chief executive officer, said he was grateful "to report even a meager first-quarter profit." He called the most recent period "one of the most difficult quarters in the history of the airline industry."

Southwest's stock rose 25 cents yesterday to close at $24.25 a share in Big Board trading.

AMR Corp., American Airlines' parent and the first carrier to release March 31 quarter results, set the tone for the industry last week when it reported a $19.3 million loss.

Of the major airline holding companies, only Pan Am Corp. and Texas Air have yet to report results. They aren't expected to buck the industry trend; analysts figure their results will disclose big first-quarter losses.
