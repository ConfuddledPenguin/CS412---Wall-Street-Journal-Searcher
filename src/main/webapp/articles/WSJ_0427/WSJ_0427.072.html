Many Gas Stations Mislabel Octane, GAO Study Finds

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 04/27/90

WASHINGTON -- Drivers are being cheated out of more than $150 million a year as a result of inaccurate octane labels on gasoline pumps, according to a congressional report. The report, based on a two-year study by the General Accounting Office, found that drivers who thought they were paying for high-octane gasoline, which can cost as much as 20 cents more a gallon, received low-octane fuel. The problem is especially prevalent in the 28 states that currently don't have octane-testing programs; GAO findings for four of those states showed mislabeling in 22% to 53% of the gasoline sold.

"Although the vast majority of gasoline distributors and dealers are honest, the report shows too many instances of motorists being cheated for us to ignore," said Rep. Phil Sharp (D., Ind.), who released the report at a news conference with Rep. Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.).

A fuel's octane rating refers to its resistance to engine knock, a pinging sound caused by improper combustion that can lower engine efficiency, reduce mileage and increase emissions. Gasoline is generally sold in three octane levels. Fuel with a high-octane rating burns more smoothly and is more expensive than low-octane gasoline. Although most cars run as well on regular gasoline, many people buy high-octane gasoline in hopes of improving performance. The car's octane requirements also vary depending on driving conditions.

The Environmental Protection Agency and the Federal Trade Commission are required by law to test gasoline for its octane content and enforce federal labeling requirements. The agencies, however, citing staff and budget cuts, haven't carried out the tests since 1981. The GAO report noted that neither agency has asked Congress for additional funds to carry out the testing requirements.

The lack of an enforced federal testing program makes it hard to determine how extensive the mislabeling is. Victor Rezendes, GAO director of energy issues, said the problem may be costing consumers more than $500 million annually.

The states that test for octane content are Alabama, Arkansas, California, Colorado, Delaware, Florida, Georgia, Illinois, Iowa, Louisiana, Maryland, Massachusetts, Missouri, New Mexico, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oklahoma, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Virginia and Wisconsin. The GAO obtained test results for 11 of these states. It found mislabeling of less than 2% of the gasoline sampled in seven of the states and mislabeling of more than 2% of gasoline in the four other states.

Michigan, New York and Washington plan to put statewide testing programs into place; New York City already conducts tests. Rep. Schumer released figures from New York City's consumer affairs department showing that last year, 146 of 280 gasoline stations in the metropolitan area were cited for octane violations.

Rep. Sharp said he is drafting legislation that would broaden the testing and enforcement powers of the states. Some state officials have said that efforts to prosecute individuals or companies for mislabeling octane gasoline pumps are hampered by uncertainty about whether their laws are pre-empted by the federal requirements.

Mr. Sharp also plans for his legislation to make clear that octane-labeling rules apply to reformulated gasoline and the other alternatives to conventional fuels that are likely to become more widely used as a result of pending clean-air legislation.

The GAO said that most of the mislabeling occurs at distribution points and retail stations, rather than at refineries and bulk terminals where there is frequent testing. Some of the mislabeling may be unintentional, the agency said. Robert Kelley, deputy director for enforcement of New York City's consumer affairs department, said, "It's unintentional when you get high-octane gasoline when you pay for low octane. It's intentional when you get low octane after paying for high."
