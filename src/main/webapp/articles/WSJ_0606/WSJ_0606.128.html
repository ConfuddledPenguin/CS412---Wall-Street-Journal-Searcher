Big Coke Bottler Plans to Shuffle Some Territories

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 06/06/90

ATLANTA -- Coca-Cola Enterprises Inc., in a shuffling of franchise territories, said it agreed to buy a bottler in Arkansas for $250 million and to sell properties in Ohio for $120 million. Coca-Cola Enterprises, a major Coca-Coca bottler, agreed in principle to buy Coca-Cola Bottling Co. of Arkansas for cash from Coca-Cola Co., the Atlanta-based soft-drink giant. Coca-Cola owns about 49% of Coca-Cola Enterprises.

At the same time, Coca-Cola Enterprises agreed in principle to sell Portsmouth Coca-Cola Bottling Co. and a majority interest in the Coca-Cola Bottling Co. of Ohio to Johnston Coca-Cola Bottling Group Inc., a closely held independent bottler based in Chattanooga, Tenn.

For Coca-Cola Enterprises, the sale of the Ohio businesses will result in a second-quarter pre-tax gain of $55 million. The gain will be partly offset, however, by a $10 million increase to reserves for potentially uncollectible accounts receivable. A Coca-Cola Enterprises spokesman said the company believed that adding to the reserves was prudent in light of the increasingly leveraged nature of some of its retail customers.

Also in the second quarter, Coca-Cola Enterprises will take about $9 million in charges related to standardizing its computer information systems.

The net result of the special items will be a per-share gain of about 15 cents to 16 cents in the quarter, the company said. In the year-ago second quarter, Coca-Cola Enterprises earned 26 cents a share, when net was $38.8 million.

Coca-Cola said it expects to report a gain of less than one penny a share from the sale of the Arkansas bottler. A Coca-Cola spokesman said the soft-drink company is selling the bottler for the price it paid for it last year, plus some related interest costs.

In addition to parts of Texas, the territories covered by the Arkansas bottler include the cities of Little Rock, Ark., and Shreveport, La., and other areas in Arkansas and Louisiana. The purchase could provide benefits to Coca-Cola Enterprises because those areas are contiguous to some of the company's existing territories, particularly around big markets like Memphis, Tenn., and Houston. Patching together contiguous territories can allow a bottler to cut costs by consolidating production, among other things.

"Coca-Cola Enterprises has tried to concentrate its efforts in the South and on the West Coast," said George Thompson, an analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "This is just another effort in that direction."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Coca-Cola Enterprises closed at $15.875, down 12.5 cents.
