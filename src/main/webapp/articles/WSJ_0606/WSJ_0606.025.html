What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/06/90

FED OFFICIALS APPEAR to have no intention of cutting short-term interest rates, despite signs of a stagnant economy. The officials seem convinced that last week's weak employment report doesn't presage a recession. Furthermore, they are concerned that their tight-money policies have so far failed to head off an acceleration of inflation. ---

Tomkins of Britain agreed to acquire Philips Industries, a maker of windows, bathtubs and other products, for $550 million. The deal follows the collapse of a buy-out of Philips.

---

National Intergroup's chairman agreed to step down next year after the firm sells everything but its drug distributor. Dissident holders expressed satisfaction, but said the whole company should be liquidated.

---

Sales of U.S.-built cars and light trucks fell 3.3% in late May. But auto makers dismissed the possibility of huge discounts at the end of the model year in September, because stocks of unsold vehicles are relatively modest.

---

Stock and bond prices declined as investors took profits after two days of strong gains. The Dow Jones industrials fell 10.19 points to 2925.00.

---

The cable TV industry indicated it would accept a reregulation of its basic rates, and limits on the size and activities of major operators.

---

Drexel is expected to present a proposed reorganization plan to its creditors today that would pay them 100 cents on the dollar.

---

Saudi Arabia moved to damp speculation that it is launching an oil-price war, after the kingdom's discount plans triggered a petroleum sell-off.

---

Businessland is forming a Japanese unit with Canon, Fujitsu, Sony and Toshiba as investors. The move by the biggest U.S. dealer of personal computers supplements a similar push into Europe. But the Japanese PC market may prove perilous.

---

Equimark's Claire Gargalli quit as head of its main banking unit amid speculation that the parent intends to put itself up for sale. Equimark's chairman denied any sale plans.

---

Three Campbell Soup holders pooled most of their 17.4% stake and called again for a sale of the firm. Campbell stock jumped on the news.

---

The Bush administration unveiled its plan to give the SEC jurisdiction over stock-index futures. The proposal would stiffen regulation of the products and also give the government power to set margin requirements.

---

Saatchi posted an $11.8 million fiscal first-half loss, citing heavy debt, a British ad slowdown and consulting losses. Separately, the firm agreed to sell Hay Group to the consulting unit's management for $90 million.

---

Limited antitrust liability for certain joint production ventures was endorsed by the House. Triple damages would no longer apply if the activities were disclosed to the government.

---

Greyhound Lines may be forced to make peace with its striking drivers if it is to emerge successfully from Chapter 11, some creditors believe. They may demand such a settlement.

---

Increasing SEC power to gather Wall Street data was approved by the House. The bill, which must be reconciled with a Senate version, would also let the agency take steps against program trading, a provision opposed by the Bush administration.

---

Coca-Cola Enterprises agreed to purchase a bottler in Arkansas for $250 million and to sell bottling interests in Ohio for $120 million.

---

Adolph Coors agreed to buy its first brewery outside Colorado from Stroh, giving the buyer its first large group of union workers in 13 years.

---

Paramount Communications posted a 67% drop in fiscal secondquarter profit, but reversed its loss from continuing operations.
