You Can Bet Someone Out There Is Already Scripting `Airport '90'

  • Author: James S. Hirsch
  • Date: 06/06/90

As urban problems go, groundhogs don't rank that high in most places. In Columbus, Ohio, however, the fat, furry creatures have overrun the airport, and the authorities there want to take up arms -- literally -- against them. But turning prairie land next to the paved runways into a sort of Wild West makes some people uneasy. The issue came to a head Monday night, when the City Council voted on whether to allow airport personnel to use firearms against several thousand of the burrowing rodents.

The problem stems from a population explosion beneath the grassy 180-acre field. Officials attribute the groundhogs' fertility to a spate of warm winters.

The plump creatures "don't hibernate as long as they did in colder weather," says John Carpenter, operations officer for the Port Authority International Airport. "They get bored and have to do something, so they have more kids."

And a mischievous lot they are. Among other things, they burrow underground and gnaw on buried electrical cables. Mr. Carpenter also fears a gamboling groundhog may collide with a small aircraft and perhaps mangle the landing gear.

Federal regulations being what they are, Mr. Carpenter says the groundhogs cast a shadow over the future of the airfield. Airport authorities have tried to kill the critters by dropping cannisters of poison gas into burrows and setting traps above ground.

"Somone suggested mothballs, but with 180 acres, you're talking about a lot of mothballs," Mr. Carpenter says.

Desperate, officials asked the city if the airport could arm several maintenance employees with shotguns and so-called varmint slugs, which have a hollow point and spread out upon impact, thus tending to kill rather than wound. "It's a very humane way to go," says Mr. Carpenter.

But the council rejected the idea by a 5-2 vote. Members said that the plan sounded extreme and that there might be better ways to control the population.

Like contraception. Animal-rights representatives argued at the council meeting that Columbus should consider wildlife birth control, which entails putting chemicals into animal food.

"In the case of nuisance animals, man's first solution is `let's kill it,'" says Robin Russell, director of Protect Our Earth Treasures, an animal-rights group. "But if you use birth control, after a couple of years, you've eradicated the problem."
