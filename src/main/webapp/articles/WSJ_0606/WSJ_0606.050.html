Economy: Fed Officials Unlikely to Ease Credit Grip --- Despite Recent Indications Of a Slowing Economy, Inflation Remains Target

  • Author: Alan Murray
  • Date: 06/06/90

WASHINGTON -- Despite signs of a stagnant economy, Federal Reserve officials appear to have no intention now of cutting short-term interest rates. Interviews with a number of top Fed officials, some speaking for the record and some insisting that their names not be used, suggest the central bank isn't likely to ease its grip on credit anytime soon. The comments came despite a discouraging employment report Friday, which showed private payrolls in the past three months expanded at their slowest rate since the recession of 1982.

"I am comfortable with policy as we sit here today," said Fed governor Edward Kelley, describing the current policy stance as "probably moderately restrictive."

"I think the best analysis might be that we are accomplishing precisely what we set out to do," agreed Roger Guffey, president of the Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City. "The economy is slowing into the area of 2% growth."

In the past, weak employment reports have prompted the Federal Reserve to ease its grip on credit and allow short-term interest rates to fall. So when the Labor Department said Friday that private payrolls barely grew in May, many market analysts and some Bush administration officials began to speculate that the Fed would be moved to ease.

But Fed officials seem convinced that stagnant job growth doesn't presage a recession. Economic data watched closely by Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan, such as the level of inventories and the lead times between orders and deliveries, show no signs of a cumulative weakness in the economy.

Moreover, top Fed officials also seem relatively unperturbed by recent slow growth in the money supply, arguing that those numbers have been artificially depressed by the government's accelerating pace in disposing of failed savings-and-loan institutions.

And perhaps most importantly, Fed officials worry that their tight-money policies haven't yet had the desired effect in heading off a gradual pickup in inflation.

Mr. Guffey, for instance, says he still sees no signs that the slowing economy has brought down inflation. "When you look at the sectors, you don't get the sense that there is anything other than a Mexican standoff," he said.

To be sure, further signs of deterioration in the economy, or convincing evidence that inflation has slowed, could lead the Fed to change course. But for now, it seems clear no policy change is in store.

Fed policy is set by the 12-member Federal Open Market Committee, which is made up of the seven Fed governors in Washington and five of the 12 regional Federal Reserve Bank presidents. That committee is scheduled to meet in Washington July 2 and 3.

In the past few years, the regional presidents have tended to favor a tighter Fed policy than their colleagues in Washington. Cleveland Fed President Lee Hoskins, for instance, has been the most consistent anti-inflation hawk. And in an interview, he said the employment numbers hadn't changed his views. "We shouldn't react to short-term numbers," he said. "There is a lot of noise in the economic numbers that come out, and our ability to forecast is quite weak."

But for now, Fed Chairman Greenspan, Mr. Kelley and other members of the Fed's board in Washington also appear willing to keep their current relatively tight grip on credit. Mr. Kelley noted there is "always the possibility" the economy could slip into recession, but said "I don't see that in the cards at this time and I don't expect it."
