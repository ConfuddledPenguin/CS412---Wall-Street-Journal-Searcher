Technology: Du Pont Develops Substitutes for Halon Fire Extinguishants

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/06/90

WILMINGTON, Del. -- Du Pont Co. said it developed two prospective substitutes for Halon fire extinguishants that don't cause depletion of the stratospheric ozone layer. Based on preliminary tests over the past year, the new bromine-free agents cause "zero to extremely low" ozone depletion, said Donald P. Dunn, a Du Pont marketing manager. Halons, which can do severe damage, are being phased out by international agreements that call for a production freeze by 1992.

Du Pont, a chemicals and energy company, currently sells about $8 million to $10 million a year of fire extinguishants. The world-wide market is about $80 million. Du Pont's new products could be used in aircraft, ships, computer rooms, museums, hospitals and petroleum-production facilities, Mr. Dunn said.

Du Pont has invested more than $170 million on research into alternatives to chlorofluorocarbons and Halons, which are both substances widely blamed for damaging the ozone layer. Du Pont is concentrating on substitutes made up of hydrofluorocarbons, or HFCs, and hydrochlorofluorocarbons, or HCFCs.

By excluding the bromine compound, the active agent in Halon, Du Pont's new products are safer for the environment, Mr. Dunn said. But he added that they would be two to three times more expensive, and would require using more volume for comparable effectiveness.

Du Pont's products will be produced commercially at its Maitland, Ontario, facility and in small quantities at a pilot facility in Deepwater, N.J.

Approval of the products are subject to separate applications and standards testing by the U.S. Army, Air Force and Navy, as well as the National Fire Protection Association. Mr. Dunn said the company expects to market the products commercially beginning in 1995.

Du Pont's main competitors in manufacturing fire extinguishants are Imperial Chemical Industries PLC in Britain and Atochem in France as well as Great Lakes Chemical Co. and several smaller companies in the U.S.
