Bankruptcy Judge Advances Southmark Reorganization Plan

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/06/90

DALLAS -- Southmark Corp. said a federal bankruptcy judge has approved its disclosure statement, allowing the company to seek creditor and stockholder approval for its reorganization plan. The real estate and thrift concern plans to reorganize around its San Jacinto Savings Association thrift and to liquidate most of its remaining assets. The company, which filed last July for protection under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code, said it will mail the disclosure statements next week to about 127,000 creditors and security holders, and collect votes until July 6.

Terms of the plan call for Southmark to pay about $300 million -- $76 million in cash, $75 million in secured notes, $100 million in preferred stock, and the remainder in common stock -- to settle approximately $2 billion in debts. As a result, creditors are expected to receive a few pennies to perhaps 32 cents for each dollar owed.

As part of the reorganization, Southmark said, creditors have chosen Glen Adams, a former executive of Great Western Sugar Co., to become the new chief executive officer after the plan is approved, succeeding Arthur G. Weiss. Mr. Weiss, an Atlanta businessman, has led the company since its top two officers were ousted in January 1989.

The company said Mr. Adams is expected to join the company soon. In addition, creditors have chosen as Southmark's new directors Mr. Adams; Arthur Collins, founder and president of a Connecticut real estate development company; Philip Montgomery, president of a Dallas real estate management company; and Richard J. Vossler Jr., an executive of a money management firm that owns Southmark bonds.

A confirmation hearing on the reorganization plan is scheduled for July 11. The company has said it hopes to have its reorganization plan approved before July 17, when an agreement among the company, San Jacinto and federal regulators expires. That agreement calls for Southmark to put about $100 million more in assets into the Houston-based thrift, though the company said recently that even those assets won't bring the thrift into compliance with federal regulations.
