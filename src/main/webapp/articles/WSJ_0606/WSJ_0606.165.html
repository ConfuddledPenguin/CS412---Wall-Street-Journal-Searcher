Eurovaleur Offers New Team Approach For Big Investments in European Stocks

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 06/05/90

The invest-in-Europe bandwagon is getting a new calliope. It's one more sign of the rush to capitalize on the presumed gold mine of unifying Western Europe and rebuilding Eastern Europe.

The latest wrinkle comes from New York-based Eurovaleur Inc., a new money management firm specializing in publicly traded European stocks. The firm is starting a new venture headlined by a "star" money manager, Felix Zulauf, a Swiss who is well known in U.S. investment circles.

But this isn't a solo venture. While Mr. Zulauf, in Zurich, makes broad asset allocation decisions, such as how much to invest where, 11 different money managers in as many countries will run portfolios in 14 European stock markets.

Commerz International Capital Management G.m.b.H., a unit of Commerzbank AG of West Germany, will evaluate and supervise the country managers. To maintain independence, the West German bank doesn't hold a stake in Eurovaleur.

"It's a unique way for Americans and Asians to invest in European equities," said Mr. Zulauf, who is resigning as chief investment officer of Zurich-based Clariden Bank, a unit of Credit Suisse Holdings S.A. "You don't have to go to 10 or 15 individual countries or managers."

U.S., Japanese and other Asian money invested in multiple European markets is usually managed from a major money center such as New York or London. But Mr. Zulauf and his partners at Eurovaleur contend this technique is faulty.

"An American operating out of the U.S. or London talks to brokers in individual countries," he said. "Brokers' first interest is in volume, turnover, and not good, long-term performance." Moreover, the American "has to compete in each market with a professional domiciled in that market who knows the market inside and out," he said.

A minimum investment with Eurovaleur is $5 million; investing $20 million wins the right to have the funds independently managed. Participation is restricted to institutional investors.

European markets have been attractive, especially because of the weak dollar, euphoria surrounding the planned 1992 abolition of trade and investment barriers among the 12 countries of the European Community, and the recent collapse of communist regimes in Eastern Europe.

In the five years to May 31, Morgan Stanley Capital International's Europe index rose 200.3% in dollar terms. In the same period, the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 118.69%. The two European markets that posted the biggest gains in those five years were Sweden, up 396.1% in dollar terms, and Austria, up 382%.

In the first five months of this year, the Europe index climbed only 2.8% vs. the Dow's 4.48% increase. But the Austrian market has climbed a hefty 24.1% and the Norwegian market 22.1%.

Nonetheless, Sonja Kohn, Eurovaleur's president, who also remains president of Windsor I.B.C., a privately held New York broker-dealer that specializes in European business, said that investing in Europe can be tricky. "To really know what's going on in Europe, you've got to be part of the establishment," she said. Compared with the U.S., "There is less regulation, the flow of information is different, there are no Chinese walls between investment banking and brokers."

For example, she said, the Swiss pharmaceutical giant F. Hoffmann-La Roche & Co. carries its real estate holdings -- valued at as much as an estimated three billion Swiss francs ($2.09 billion) -- on its books at one franc (70 cents). "You can't sit in New York and London and expect to understand what's going on," she declared.

The emphasis at Eurovaleur will be on performance. Daily, Commerzbank and Mr. Zulauf will be able to judge how well the 11 country managers -- each has outpaced his domestic market over the past five years -- is doing, Mrs. Kohn said.

A U.S. investor can't afford to ignore European markets, Mr. Zulauf said. "You have a lot of momentum going on 1992, and Eastern Europe should extend that momentum," he said. "The greater Germany will be on a major fiscal expansion program for the next couple of years," and the demand created by that spending "will spill over throughout Europe," he predicted. What's more, "The long-term theme is capital goods," Mr. Zulauf said, noting that West Germany is "the major capital goods producer in the world."
