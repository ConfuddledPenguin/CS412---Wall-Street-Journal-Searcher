Media: Cable Industry Indicates It Will Accept Reregulation Bill

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 06/06/90

WASHINGTON -- In a compromise that could break the logjam over cable TV legislation, the cable industry indicated it would accept a proposed Senate bill to reregulate monthly rates for basic service and let federal regulators limit the size and activities of large operators. The new draft legislation, which calls for regulation of rates for a basic tier of service consisting of broadcast stations, is to be introduced tomorrow at a Senate Commerce Committee session. The legislation is tougher than a proposal circulated in the House by Chairman John Dingell (D., Mich.) of the Energy and Commerce Committee, but is less restrictive than earlier Senate measures.

James Mooney, head of the National Cable Television Association, said the industry has "serious problems" with the new draft, but signaled that his organization wouldn't oppose the measure. The proposal, he said, "clearly is less onerous than the previous staff draft. That being the case, we will focus on opposing adverse amendments to it."

Under the proposal, the Federal Communications Commission would be required to monitor monthly rates for special cable channels, and could reverse abusive rate increases. The FCC would also devise rules for limiting horizontal and vertical integration of large cable operators. In addition, it requires cable companies to provide programming to competitors, such as satellite or wireless cable services.

While the measure is aimed at encouraging competition, it wouldn't allow telephone companies to enter the cable business. Phone companies, however, have been lobbying intensely for the opportunity, and Sen. Conrad Burns (R., Mont.), is planning to introduce an amendment to let phone companies provide cable service within their franchise territories. The amendment would substantially reverse the 1984 Cable Act ban on such activity.

In the House, Rep. Edward Markey (D., Mass.), chairman of the telecommunications subcommittee, began circulating draft cable legislation and has set a markup for June 25. Like the Senate measure, the Markey plan also calls for reregulating a basic tier of broadcast stations, establishing customer-service standards and encouraging competition by setting up guidelines for satellite or wireless cable rivals to obtain cable programming.

For the past year, lawmakers have been under pressure from cable subscribers to rein in rates, which have risen more than 100% in many cities since they were largely deregulated in 1987. But even if both the Senate Commerce Committee and the House telecommunications panel pass legislation this month, there is still a good chance that proponents of reregulation will run out of time before the session ends, preventing lawmakers from bringing home a trophy by November. A crowded agenda could bog down lawmakers in phone-company or competition issues that the industry is certain to fight.

Legislative efforts could be blunted by the FCC, which is getting ready to decide whether to give municipalities more say over rates and will recommend a package of legislative changes in July.

The Bush administration is preparing to express strong opposition to reregulation in a letter from Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher and James Rill, head of the Justice Department's Antitrust Division. They caution lawmakers that legislation already introduced goes too far.
