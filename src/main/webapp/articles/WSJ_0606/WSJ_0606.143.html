TOKYO'S NUCLEAR-POWER PLANS

Japan's Ministry of International Trade and Industry unveiled a plan to build 40 nuclear-power plants in Japan over the next 20 years to almost double the country's reliance on nuclear power to 16.7% of total primary energy supplies. It already has 38.

But energy analysts called the plan impractical because resistance to nuclear plants means such a number cannot be built and Japan will continue to rely on pollution-causing coal and oil.

"Building that many nuclear plants is just a political target which is almost impossible to achieve," said an analyst at the Japanese Institute of Middle Eastern Economies.

Critics say Japan is unlikely to complete even its existing plan of building another 16 stations by the year 2000 because of the anti-nuclear movement.

LEBANON'S DESTROYED GENERATION

The Unicef representative in Lebanon said the Lebanese civil war has killed 40,000 children and destroyed an entire generation. The representative, Andre Roberfroid, said: "In this country, in 15 years of war, no less than 40,000 children have been killed, many more wounded, handicapped or crippled. Fifteen years is the age at which childhood ends . . . This means that one complete generation of children in Lebanon has been actually living all its life in an environment of war and aggression."

BRITAIN'S LORDS SAY NO

Britain's House of Lords defeated a bill that would have allowed prosecutions of suspected World War II criminals. The 207-74 vote to throw out the measure came after it had been overwhelmingly adopted by the House of Commons. The Lords argued that it was wrong to make retroactive laws, that fair trials would be impossible because of the age of witnesses and that the legislation would lead to "show trials." But the Lords' powers are limited to delaying bills for a year, and the government now must decide whether to reintroduce the bill in the autumn and force it through the Lords.

IRAQ TO END INHERITANCE TAX

Iraq's National Assembly voted to abolish the inheritance tax, the state-run news media reported. The Al-Jumhouriya daily said Deputy Prime Minister Saadoun Hamadi advocated the move because "the abolishing of this tax will work as a catalyst to encourage wealthy people to invest their money and help the growth of the economic cycle." The tax was levied at a rate of between 5% and 50% of the value of legacies. In 1988, the government collected the equivalent of about $60 million from the tax.

JAPANESE SPACE STAKE

Six Japanese companies -- Mitsubishi Corp., Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Ltd., Japan Air Lines, Mitsubishi Trust & Banking Corp., Shimizu Corp. and Toyo Engineering Corp. -- will buy a 10% share of a U.S. firm planning to lease room in space missions, a Mitsubishi Corp. spokesman said. The six companies will buy $10 million of a new stock issue of Washington, D.C.-based Spacehab Corp., the spokesman said. He said Spacehab plans to lease containers on spaceships to let companies and others conduct experiments under weightless conditions.

VENICE CONTROVERSY CONTINUES

Italy said it would not withdraw its controversial bid for Venice to be the site of Expo 2000 but added that much of the world's fair would be held outside the city's historic center.

Prime Minister Giulio Andreotti told parliament that if the Paris-based International Exhibition Bureau chose Venice the fair would be centered on the mainland cities of Marghera and Mestre and other parts of the Veneto region. The Paris exposition bureau is scheduled to make a decision between Venice, Toronto and Hanover, West Germany, on June 14.

The proposal to hold the fair in Venice sparked an uproar from lovers of the city around the world. Opponents argued that Venice would be irreversibly damaged by new construction and a massive influx of tourists.

The European Community's environment commissioner, Carlo Ripa de Meana, said Italy, due to assume the rotating presidency of the EC July 1 for six months, will face major problems in this role if it doesn't withdraw its bid.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

On average, every British man, woman and child over age 10 drinks three cups of tea daily, according to the Tea Council's annual report. The report said tea accounts for 43.9% of everything the British drink, not counting water. . . . About two million workers in Greece are expected to participate in a 24-hour general strike today. Public services, banking, commerce and transportation are expected to be seriously disrupted.
