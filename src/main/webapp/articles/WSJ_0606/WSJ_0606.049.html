Tuesday's Markets: Stock Prices, Bonds Drop After Climb --- Investors Take Profits After 2 Days of Gains; Dollar Ends Mixed

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/06/90

NEW YORK -- Stock and bond prices, after a brief surge in early trading, declined under pressure from investors taking profits after two sessions of strong gains. Bond prices rose sharply on news that Saudi Arabia was cutting oil prices. But a wave of profit-taking quickly followed, fueled by news of stronger-than-expected car sales in late May, a rebound in oil prices as the day wore on, and an increase in commodity prices.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average retreated 10.19 points to finish at 2925.00 in brisk trading. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices fell more than a 1/4 point, or more than $2.50 for each $1,000 of face amount, in moderate trading. The dollar was narrowly mixed against major currencies as traders hunted vainly for market-moving news.

"Stocks took their cue from bonds, and in both cases you had profit-taking," said Jack Solomon, market technician at Bear Stearns. Helping buoy both markets lately was the widening perception that lower interest rates are inevitable because economic growth is weakening and inflation seems to be reined in. But when expectations of lower interest rates becomes a topic of household conversations, he said, "you know we're going to get a correction."

Later, in fact, the prospect of the Federal Reserve Board lowering rates any time soon dimmed with Fed Gov. Edward Kelley's comment that he is comfortable with the current "moderately restrictive" monetary policy. Without the immediate threat of recession, the Fed is likely to hold to its policy, aimed at keeping inflation under control.

Mr. Kelley's comments, combined with news indicating economic strength and higher prices, was "enough bad news to justify some profit-taking," said Philip Braverman, chief economist at DKB Securities Corp. "If you've got it there, you want to make sure it doesn't disappear on you."

Technology stocks that helped spark the market's most recent run-up were being sold yesterday, noted Joseph Feshbach, director of technical market analysis at Shearson Lehman Hutton.

But some investors were taking advantage of the selling to get into the market. Andrew Riley, head of U.S. equity research at Yamaichi International, said he noticed a pickup in Japanese buying of U.S. stocks yesterday.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell in heavy trading, with 199.7 million shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange. Declining issues outnumbered advancers, 813 to 774.

Bond prices fell. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond edged up to 8.45%.

The dollar ended mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6850 marks and 152.45 yen, compared with 1.6855 marks and 152.40 yen Monday.
