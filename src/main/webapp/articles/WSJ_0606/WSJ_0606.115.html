Ex-Official of A&P, 5 Others Are Accused Of Insider Trading

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/06/90

NEW YORK -- The Securities and Exchange Commission accused a former official of Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Co. and five others of securities-law violations involving three A&P acquisitions. The civil suit, filed in federal court in Manhattan, accuses Alan C. Goulding, formerly an A&P senior executive vice president and chief operating officer, of passing on inside information that the others used in purchasing stock. The insider-trading scheme allegedly lasted from May 1986 through March 1987. Mr. Goulding allegedly tipped the other defendants off about A&P's plans to acquire Shopwell Inc., Delchamps Inc., and Waldbaum Inc., as well as tipping them off about higher-than-projected A&P earnings.

Mr. Goulding, 60 years old, of Allendale, N.J., and the other defendants signed consent orders settling the case. In the consent orders, the defendants neither denied nor admitted any wrongdoing. The other defendants are Robert J. Foley, 68, a food-marketing consultant in Redwood City, Calif.; Joel Lopate, 56, of Miami; Murray Verlin, 68, part owner of a food-distribution company, of Miami; Kurt Goldman, 65, of Boca Raton, Fla.; and Peter Goldman, 62, also of Boca Raton.

In the consent order, the defendants agreed to turn over alleged insider-trading profits totaling $513,881, plus interest of $189,880. They also will pay penalties totaling $1.3 million. Mr. Goulding, who isn't accused of trading on the inside information, will pay a penalty of $635,000, the largest portion of the disgorged profits and penalties.

Michael Himmel, an attorney for Mr. Goulding, said his client left A&P for health reasons, and that he settled the case in order to put it behind him.

An A&P spokesman said Mr. Goulding's departure from A&P had nothing to do with the SEC allegations. He said the SEC's investigation began after Mr. Goulding had taken early retirement in July 1987.

Pamela Rogers Chepiga, an attorney for Kurt Goldman and Peter Goldman, said she doesn't believe her clients committed inside trading because they received their information second-hand, rather than directly from Mr. Goulding. But she said the two men decided that the settlement "was economically advisable."

Lawrence Horn, an attorney for Mr. Lopate, declined to comment. Attorneys for the other defendants couldn't be reached for comment.
