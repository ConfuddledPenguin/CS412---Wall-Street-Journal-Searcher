Abreast of the Market: Stocks Fall as Early Advance Gives Way to Profit-Taking in Busy Day

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 06/06/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market's latest move into uncharted territory was short-lived, as an opening advance gave way to profit-taking that sent prices lower in active trading. Reports that Saudi Arabia had lowered crude-oil contract prices for July set off a bout of heavy selling among oil-field services stocks, which paced the market's slide. Also, the technology sector erased some of its gains over the past few weeks.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which had advanced a total of 4.1% in the five previous sessions since Memorial Day and had set records in four of them, fell 10.19 to 2925.00.

At the opening, the average rallied to a gain of about 12 points with the help of buying that spilled over from the market's rally Monday afternoon. The industrial average had jumped 34.22 points Monday, and nearly all of the gains came as a result of that advance.

But after the initial rise, the market drifted back into a narrow trading range and then retreated as market players moved to take advantage of its recent rally.

"The selling which is coming in," said Paul Hennessey, a vice president of trading at Boston Co. in Boston, "is definitely aimed at taking some profits after a big run-up here."

Broader market measures fared better than the industrial average, as the profittaking left more of a mark on blue-chip issues. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index dropped 0.76 to 366.64, the New York Stock Exchange Index lost 0.28 to 199.93 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index fell 0.68 to 341.30.

Declining issues edged advancers by about 813 to 774 on the Big Board, where volume swelled to 199,720,000 shares from 175,520,000 shares Monday.

"What we're seeing is a little bit of consolidation, which is totally to be expected. You could see some continuation of this for the next session or two," said John A. Conlon Jr., a managing director at Rothschild Inc.

However, traders said buying interest at prices just below current levels appeared to be widespread, providing an indication that stocks aren't likely to fall sharply from their peaks right away. "People with cash positions that are higher than they would like them to be have been looking for an opportunity to get into the marketplace," Mr. Hennessey said.

In the oil-field services sector, Schlumberger plummeted 2 3/4 to 55 3/4 on Big Board composite volume of 1.8 million shares, Baker Hughes slid 2 to 27 5/8 on 1.7 million shares and Halliburton tumbled 2 3/4 to 46 1/4 on 1.4 million shares. In addition, McDermott International eased 1 to 30 1/2 after reporting that its loss from continuing operations for its fiscal fourth quarter, ended in March, widened from a year earlier.

Stocks of major oil companies were less affected by the developments in the oil market, where July crude-oil futures contracts fell 46 cents a barrel to $16.68 in New York trading. Exxon closed unchanged at 48 1/4, Chevron was unchanged at 70 3/8, Texaco fell 5/8 to 59 3/8, Mobil slid 3/8 to 63 1/8, Amoco dropped 3/8 to 52 3/4 and Unocal fell 1/8 to 30.

AMR climbed 1 3/8 to 68 7/8 as two million shares changed hands. Merrill Lynch raised its second-quarter earnings estimate for the owner of American Airlines by 25 cents a share, to $2.40, and reiterated its buy recommendation.

Other airline stocks rallied as well, reflecting expectations that the drop in oil prices will translate into lower prices for jet fuel. Delta Air Lines rose 5/8 to 80, Southwest Airlines gained 3/8 to 26 1/2, USAir Group added 3/8 to 30 1/8 and Alaska Air Group climbed 3/4 to 24 3/8.

Meanwhile, financial issues continued to attract buyers despite a downturn in the bond market, which gave back early gains linked to the Saudi reports. Among money-center bank stocks, Citicorp rose 3/8 to 25 on 2.3 million shares and BankAmerica added 1/2 to 33 1/8 on 1.9 million shares. Elsewhere in the sector, Primerica climbed 1 1/8 to 34 3/8, PNC Financial added 1 to 37 1/2, NCNB went up 1 1/2 to 43 1/2 and Golden West Financial rose 7/8 to 33 1/8.

Philips Industries jumped 3 1/4 to 17 1/4 on 2.5 million shares. Tomkins PLC of Britain agreed in principle to acquire the company for $18.50 a share, or $550 million.

Campbell Soup surged 3 3/8 to 60. Three dissident members of the founding Dorrance family formed a voting trust, holding 16.5% of the company's common stock, and reaffirmed their belief that a sale of Campbell Soup would be in the best interest of its shareholders.

Kellogg surged 3 3/4 to 72. Goldman Sachs put the stock on its list of recommended issues and raised its second-quarter earnings estimate by five cents a share, to $1.05. Food analyst Nomi Ghez told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report the stock "has been a major laggard" and the company is "high-quality."

Fluor fell 1 7/8 to 46 7/8 following the release of its earnings report for the fiscal second quarter, ended April 30. Although its net income for the quarter exceeded analysts' forecasts, a tax-related gain accounted for about half its earnings.

Greyhound Dial went down 5/8 to 28 3/8. The company said it would take a $100 million charge to set up a reserve for losses tied to Greyhound Lines, which filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection. Greyhound Dial owns 22.5% of GLI Holding, which owns the bus company, and leases buses to the line.

National Intergroup rose 2 1/2 to 17 3/8 after disclosing that it plans to conclude its restructuring by selling its Ben Franklin Stores unit and either paying a special dividend to its shareholders or buying back stock. The company also said Howard M. Love would step down as chairman and chief executive officer following completion of the restructuring.

Tribune rose 1 to 44 7/8 after British publisher Robert Maxwell acknowledged he would be interested in acquiring the New York Daily News from the company if contract negotiations with the paper's union reach an impasse.

Morrison Knudsen, scheduled to join the S&P 500 this week, advanced 1 1/2 to 52 3/4. Stocks added to a major index typically attract buying upon their inclusion from managers of index funds, or portfolios designed to mirror the performance of a selected market measure.

Saatchi & Saatchi gained 3/8 to 5 5/8. In reporting that first-half pretax profit dropped 29% from the year-earlier level, the company said it agreed to sell its Hay Group consulting unit for $80 million to a group including the unit's managers and institutional investors.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 0.57 to 363.83. Amex volume totaled 13,310,000 shares, compared with 12,700,000 shares Monday. There were 275 issues advancing, 326 declining and 231 were unchanged.

Team Inc. gained 7/8 to 17 1/8. The Wall Street Journal reported in its "Heard on the Street" column that R. Jerry Falkner, research director at Southeast Research Partners, favors the company as a possible beneficiary from the expected passage of the Clean Air Act.

Ecology & Environment rose 1 to 18 1/4. The company reported that net income for the fiscal third quarter, ended April 28, more than doubled from a year earlier.
