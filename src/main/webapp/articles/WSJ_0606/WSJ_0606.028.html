Letters to the Editor -- Agony of Alzheimer's: Help Is on the Way

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/06/90

Your page-one article about the challenges faced by families and other care givers of Alzheimer's disease victims ("Life With Alzheimer's Is Often the Hardest on Families of the Sick," May 2) was exceptional. As the son of an Alzheimer's victim, I know the agony and pain faced by millions of American families. A bipartisan coalition in Congress is introducing legislation that details the funding increases necessary for research into Alzheimer's and outreach to its victims. Named the CARE bill (Comprehensive Alzheimer's Assistance Research and Education Act), it will authorize $300 million for research funding in fiscal 1991, more than a twofold increase in the current funding of $149 million. It also authorizes family support demonstration projects throughout the country and federal matching grants for states to develop such programs.

Alzheimer's is nothing new, but comprehensive authorization legislation with the support of a broad coalition of policy makers has never been introduced in Congress. In years past, funding for research and outreach has been done on a piecemeal basis by the appropriations committees. The CARE bill will change that, signaling that the federal government is making Alzheimer's a top priority.

Sen. Mark O. Hatfield

(R., Ore.)

Washington

---

Having lost my husband to this dreadful illness, I am aware of the tremendous burdens placed upon not only the patients, but also their families and care givers. It is distressful to learn that the government's research budget is only $149 million for this fiscal year. For those involved in this crusade against the cruelest and most destructive of all diseases, we find it difficult to understand why the funding for this type of dementia is still pitifully small.

Dorothy Kirsten French

Founder

The French Foundation for Alzheimer Research

Los Angeles

---

Your article is outstanding in describing the suffering of all involved in the care of their destroyed family member.

Very poor federal support is being given to the efforts of the researchers at the National Institute of Mental Health in their efforts to find the cause, the cure and ultimately the prevention of Alzheimer's.

Unmentioned was the continued refusal of the government to allow fetal-tissue research, which has shown the most immediate promise for restoring those with Alzheimer's. When will all of us rise up and insist that this promising line of research be supported by our government?

Abraham M. Fuchs

Ambler, Pa.
