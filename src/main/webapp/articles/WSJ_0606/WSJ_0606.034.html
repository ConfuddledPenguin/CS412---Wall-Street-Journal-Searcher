Equimark Bank Unit Chairwoman Quits Amid Speculation Firm to Go on the Block

  • Author: James S. Hirsch
  • Date: 06/06/90

PITTSBURGH -- The chairwoman of Equimark Corp.'s main banking unit resigned amid increased speculation that the parent company is preparing to put itself up for sale. The departure of Claire Gargalli, one of the nation's highest-ranking woman bankers, as chairwoman of Equibank comes one week after the resignation of another top executive, Judith E. Fellheimer. The moves are part of a cost-cutting program in which more than 20 middle and senior managers have been fired, four Philadelphia branch banks have been sold, and executive perks have been trimmed.

The moves have prompted speculation that Equimark's embattled chairman, Alan S. Fellheimer, who together with his wife owns 13% of Equimark, is trying to trim the bank to make it more attractive for sale. The departure of the bank unit's two most senior executives has also fueled rumors that Equibank may have unacknowledged problem loans. But in an interview, Mr. Fellheimer vigorously denied that either the company was for sale or that the executives were fired.

Wall Street reacted favorably to the news as buyers bid up the stock on takeover speculation. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, Equimark rose 75 cents to $8.625 in heavy trading.

For his part, Mr. Fellheimer insisted he has no plans to sell the bank. He contends the recent cost-savings were made necessary when Equimark's $116 million acquisition of National Bancshares Corp. of Texas fell through. Equimark, which has about $3.8 billion in assets, had added staff assuming that the deal would come to fruition.

Moreover, Mr. Fellheimer said, personnel cuts were needed because the focus of the bank has changed. With earnings down, Equimark won't be looking for acquisitions, which have been a source of growth over the past several years.

Even without diversions, the bank has plenty of problems at home. The bank had a loss of $15.4 million in the first quarter and is dogged by rumors that it will have to take further write-offs this year to cover bad real estate loans, particularly in the Philadelphia area.

Mr. Fellheimer wouldn't comment on the possibility of more write-offs or on earnings projections.

The 57-year-old Ms. Gargalli said she walked away from her job, which paid about $600,000 a year before bonuses, because she agrees that the company had too many senior executives for its current size. Her decision to leave clearly surprised many at the bank. At one department meeting where the announcement was made, employees gasped when they heard the news.

Ms. Gargalli, who will receive a severance package, said the decision to leave Equimark was her own. She worked at the bank unit since 1984 when federal regulators were threatening to shut it down, and eventually became Equimark's No. 2 executive. She had held the top post since 1987, and will be succeeded in that post by James McLaughlin, currently the bank's president.

Her departure leaves Mr. Fellheimer without two longtime executives who were instrumental in helping to bring Equimark back to financial health after he became chairman in 1985. Nevertheless, he insisted that the changes were necessary. "I'm doing everything I can to slim down" the company, he said. "We had no sacred cows."
