Mulroney Detects Progress in Talks Over Constitution

  • Author: John Urquhart and G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 06/06/90

OTTAWA -- Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney reported a "small degree of progress" on the third day of negotiations on Canada's constitutional impasse. Mr. Mulroney declined to disclose details of the negotiations, which involve himself and 10 provincial government leaders. But government officials said that much of yesterday's negotiations involved differences over a proposed constitutional amendment that would set new rules for changing the federal Senate.

The proposed amendment is part of the constitutional accord that was negotiated three years ago at Meech Lake, Quebec. The accord, as drafted, is opposed by two provinces, Newfoundland and Manitoba.

The New Brunswick government dropped its opposition to the accord Monday after receiving assurances from other provinces that its constitutional concerns would be satisfactorily addressed.

Mr. Mulroney welcomed New Brunswick's move as a "very welcome development." But at the start of yesterday's negotiations several provincial premiers said they were pessimistic about prospects for settlement of the impasse. "Today my greatest fear is for Canada," said Alberta Premier Don Getty. Prince Edward Island Premier Joseph Ghiz said "We're at an impasse and I don't see a way out of it."

Quebec Justice Minister Gil Remillard told reporters that Quebec won't accept any changes to a Meech Lake proposal that would recognize Quebec as a "distinct society" within Canada. Such a designation would be used by the courts in interpreting the constitution, but wouldn't give Quebec any new law-making powers, federal officials said.

Newfoundland and Manitoba have strong concerns about giving Quebec special status in the constitution. They also object to a proposed amendment that would give all provinces a veto over reform of the federal Senate. Smaller provinces have been seeking to gain greater influence over federal government policies through a reformed Senate. Mr. Ghiz said he disagreed with some of the provinces who consider that "Senate reform is the key to the economic salvation of the country." Ontario and Quebec currently have the greatest representation in the Senate, whose members are appointed by the prime minister.

If the proposed Meech Lake accord is to take effect, it needs to be ratified by all provincial legislatures by June 23, a legal deadline. If the accord isn't approved, many in Quebec have said they will view the failure as a rebuff to Quebec and press for the province's separation from the rest of Canada.

The Canadian Business Council on National Issues, which represents the chief executives of some of Canada's largest companies, said resolution of the constitutional impasse "has now become critical" for the country.

"The political costs of failure, so far, have been heavy," the council said. "The economic costs to all Canadians are already apparent -- and rising with alarming speed." Some businessmen have said some investments in Canada are being postponed because of the uncertainty.
