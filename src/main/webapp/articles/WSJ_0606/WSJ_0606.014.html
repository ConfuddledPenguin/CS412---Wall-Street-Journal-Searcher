Foreign Exchange: Dollar Is Mixed on Technical Factors; Traders Are Mulling Politics, Economy

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/06/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar was mixed as the market concerned itself primarily with the potential impact of future political and economic developments. The dollar was slightly higher against the yen and lower against the mark and the pound in trading inspired mainly by technical considerations amid a dearth of market-moving news, participants said.

The market is waiting for next week's release of May U.S. inflation indicators, for an outcome to negotiations on Canada's constitutional deadlock and for news on progress of U.S.-Soviet talks on the military status of a unified Germany.

In particular, the dollar's failure to reach 1.7000 marks during European trading led to selling of the U.S. currency below 1.6900 marks, said Charles Cossentino, a vice president at Deutsche Genossenschaftsbank in New York.

Mr. Cossentino and others said their outlook for the dollar against the mark remains bullish, with nervousness over the domestic economic consequences of unification again leading the list of factors weighing on the West German currency.

Traders are seeking signs of a U.S.-Soviet compromise on the military status of a unified Germany, but the influence of that factor has waned on the perception that any deal will be a long time coming and isn't likely to delay unification.

Sterling was broadly higher as participants went to currencies backed by high interest rates amid a lack of news to move other exchange-rates aggressively, traders said. In late afternoon New York trading, the pound was at $1.6870, up from $1.6810 late Monday.

The U.S. currency rose slightly against its Canadian counterpart, as market nervousness over the outcome of negotiations on Quebec's constitutional status negated the potentially positive factor of high Canadian interest rates, participants said.

Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney and 10 provincial premiers are conducting talks in a last-ditch attempt to forge a compromise on the constitutional amendment giving French-speaking Quebec status as a "distinct society" within Canada. All 10 provinces must ratify the measure by a deadline of June 23 for it to become law. Two provinces are still opposed and leaders are pessimistic about the chances for a compromise.

Quebec has threatened to secede from Canada if the measure isn't passed.

In late afternoon New York trading, the dollar was at 1.6850 marks, down from 1.6855 marks late Monday, and at 152.45 yen, up from 152.40.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the dollar was quoted at 152.55 yen soon after opening at 152.45 yen; it closed at 152.43 yen in Tokyo on Tuesday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6835 marks, down from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 1.6884.

Participants said the market is beginning to look beyond the interest-rate implications of the current attempt to reduce the huge U.S. budget deficit, focusing instead on the economic benefits that a lower government debt could bring.

Traders said the shift in focus is creating a more positive view of the short-term effects of the budget "summit" between the U.S. administration and Congress. During past weeks, many in the market said they expect the dollar to fall on expectations of lower interest rates if a deal is reached to reduce the budget deficit.

Meetings on the budget deficit in Washington are scheduled to resume today after a holiday break for Congress last week.

Nonetheless, Congress is expected to approve an increase in the Treasury's $3.12 trillion debt limit sometime in the next few months, observers said.

Foreign-exchange dealers said they expect greater market attention on the budget talks in coming weeks because of a growing awareness of the economic damage that unrestrained growth in the deficit could cause.

In Europe, the dollar was modestly weaker, although the day's action was largely defined by technical dealings and featured dollar moves within previously defined trading bands. In Tokyo, the dollar drifted lower in quiet dealings.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $359.80 an ounce, up $1.20. Estimated volume was a light 2.5 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $359.50 an ounce.
