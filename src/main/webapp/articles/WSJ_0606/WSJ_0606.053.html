U.S. Sales of Cars and Light Trucks Fell 3.3% in Late May From a Year Earlier

  • Author: Bradley A. Stertz
  • Date: 06/06/90

DETROIT -- U.S. auto sales stayed in the doldrums in May, with sales of domestically built cars and light trucks falling 3.3% in the last 10 days of the month. But auto makers dismissed the possibility that lagging sales will prompt huge discounts at the end of the 1990 model year in September. The reason: Each of the Big Three auto makers have relatively modest supplies of unsold cars on dealer and factory lots.

"We believe that our lower inventory position will signal to consumers that GM is not planning a costly model-year-end sale," said James B. Fitzpatrick, vice president for General Motors Corp.'s marketing and product planning staff.

Overall, Americans bought 459, 109 cars and light trucks in the nine selling days of the May 21-30 period. In the eight selling days a year earlier, sales totaled 422,134 vehicles. The percentage change is based on the daily sales rate.

The late-May car sales translated into a seasonally adjusted annual selling rate of 7.3 million cars, according to a Commerce Department formula. That compares with a 7.8 million-car rate a year earlier, and it left May with a 6.7 million-car rate.

Although sales have been sluggish since February, the Big Three auto makers have compensated by scaling back production. Now, Chrysler Corp. has a 55-day supply of cars, GM has a 59-day supply and Ford Motor Co. has a 68-day supply. Auto makers usually prefer to keep supplies at about a 65-day to-70-day level.

The relatively lean stocks of many models have emboldened auto makers to start trimming sales incentives. David Healy, an analyst with Barclays BZW investment banking group, estimated that discounts offered on new cars decreased by an average of $100 to $200 a car in May to about $900.

Dealers, however, are still wary of getting stuck with unsold vehicles late in the model year, and are holding back on ordering substantial numbers of new vehicles from the factories. As a result, it remains unclear whether auto makers, particularly the Big Three, can cut discounts back much further this summer and fall.

That may be especially true in the face of continued strong sales by Japanese auto makers, which increased their share of the U.S. passenger car market to 30% in May compared with 24% a year earlier. Japanese brands had a 28% share for the first five months of this year.

Some Japanese auto makers have taken advantage of the weaker yen by sweetening the incentives they're offering on their cars and trucks in the U.S.

Toyota Motor Corp. made the strongest gains in May, increasing its car and truck sales 31.3% and boosting its U.S. car-market share to 9.8% from 6.1% in May 1989.

Honda Motor Co. also increased its share of the car market -- to 9.5% from 8.5% -- even though its sales nudged up only slightly.

Chrysler's combined car and truck sales fell 16.9% in May -- the most of the Big Three. Chrysler's share of the U.S. car market dropped to 9.7% from 10.9% a year earlier. Ford also continued to lose ground as its combined car and truck sales for May were off 14%. Ford's share of the passenger car market slumped to 18.8% in May compared with 21.5% a year earlier.

One weak spot for Ford is the restyled 1991 Ford Escort model, which is getting off to a slower-than-expected start, according to Ford dealers. Combined sales of the new subcompact and the old model it's replacing fell 17.2% to 24,867 cars in May. Dealers said a big problem for the new Escort is that it doesn't come with any discount, while many rival Japanese and domestic models do. Ford is also offering rebates on the discontinued Escort models.

On the plus side, Ford saw a big increase in sales of its new Ford Explorer and the discontinued Bronco II compact sport utility vehicles; production of the Bronco II has been discontinued.

The Explorer and Bronco II recorded a 67.8% increase to 15,706 vehicles. They seem to have taken a bite out of Chrysler's rival Jeep Cherokee and Wagoneer. Sales of those models declined 20.1% in May.

GM, meanwhile, benefited from strong sales of its Geo line of Japanese cars. In May, GM domestic car sales fell 13.6%, but its imported cars -- primarily the Geo vehicles sold through Chevrolet dealersjumped 10.8%. Still, GM's car-market share slipped to 35.5% in May from 37% a year earlier.
