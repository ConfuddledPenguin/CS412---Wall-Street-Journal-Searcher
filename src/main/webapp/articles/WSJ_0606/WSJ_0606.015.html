Tomkins Agrees To Buy Philips Industries Inc. --- London Company Is to Pay $550 Million After LBO By Merrill Lynch Failed

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter and Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 06/06/90

Britain's Tomkins PLC agreed in principle to acquire Philips Industries Inc. of Dayton, Ohio, for $18.50 a share, or $550 million. The acquisition accord ends months of debate on the Philips board over the company's course, following the collapse earlier this year of a leveraged buy-out by Merrill Lynch Capital Partners Inc.

"There was a healthy discussion by the board whether to stay independent, do an LBO or sell to an industrial company," Gregory Hutchings, Tomkins's chief executive officer, said in London. In Dayton, Jesse Philips, the company's 75-year-old founder and retired chairman, said the board voted unanimously to accept the Tomkins offer because it represented the "best value" for shareholders.

Last September, Merrill Lynch offered $25.50 a share for Philips, a maker of windows, bathtubs, air-moving equipment and other products, but was unable to line up financing for the buy-out, even after Philips agreed to reduce the price to $24 a share. Further discussions failed to produce an alternative transaction. Talks were initiated with Tomkins soon after the Merrill Lynch buy-out collapsed last January, according to individuals involved in the negotiations.

The proposed price for Philips stock, though significantly lower than the initial $25.50-a-share buy-out proposal, is a premium of 32% above Monday's closing price of $14 a share in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. Yesterday, Philips stock surged $3.25 to close at $17.25 on volume of 2,483,800 shares.

For London-based Tomkins, which already makes lawnmowers, bicycles and handguns in the U.S. as well as other metal products in Britain, the acquisition would be the biggest yet in a program to expand in the U.S. Tomkins was attracted to Philips because it operates basic businesses involving relatively low-risk technology, said Richard G.G. Carr, president of Tomkins Corp., the U.S. subsidiary of Tomkins PLC.

In 1987, Tomkins bought Smith & Wesson Corp., the gun maker, for $112.5 million. The following year it bought Murray Ohio Manufacturing Corp., a maker of lawnmowers and bicycles, for $224 million.

Acquisition of Philips would give Tomkins 66% of its earnings from U.S. sources, according to Mr. Hutchings, up from 45% currently. "Philips would be very similar to our other U.S. acquisitions -- a company with low-risk, technical products and good cash flow," he said.

Tim Harris, an analyst with Nomura Research Institute in London, predicted that Philips would add significantly to Tomkins's already positive future earnings outlook.

Tomkins stock, though, slipped 14 pence (23.5 cents) to 288 pence in late trading in London, reflecting concern over the 58% dilution in the company's shares that would result from the #320 million ($537.6 million) rights offering to finance the acquisition. Tomkins conditioned its buy-out offer on a successful underwriting of the rights offering.

If a definitive agreement hasn't been executed by the close of business June 20, the agreement in principle will lapse, the companies said. And Philips is permitted to discuss a merger with any other party that indicates an interest prior to completion of the Tomkins transaction.

Philips was very successful until recently, acquiring companies in related industries and showing consistent profit gains by improving operations of the new businesses. However, the company reported a net loss of $14.6 million, or 51 cents a share, for the fiscal year ended March 31 because of after-tax charges totaling $51.3 million related to planned disposition of three units and costs of the failed Merrill Lynch buy-out. Sales were $927.5 million.
