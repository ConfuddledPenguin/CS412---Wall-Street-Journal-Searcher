Limit on Thrifts' Lending To Be Relaxed by Agency

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The Office of Thrift Supervision said it intends to relax temporarily a new regulation that limits the amount savings and loans may lend to an individual borrower. The move, which will ease the regulation for strongly capitalized thrifts for the next year and a half, comes in response to complaints by thrifts and the housing-construction industry. They said the regulation was forcing lenders to break off lending relationships with economically sound projects.

Ordered by Congress in last year's savings-and-loan bailout law, the regulation originally flatly prevented any institution from lending more than 15% of its capital to a single borrower. But under a phase-in plan the thrift regulation agency intends to propose in the next few weeks, well-capitalized thrifts initially will be allowed to make loans equal to as much as 60% of their capital to single borrowers engaged in housing construction. The limit would be reduced gradually to 15% by the end of 1991, said a spokesman, confirming a report in the American Banker newspaper.

Only thrifts that currently meet 1995 capital standards enacted in the bailout law will be allowed to make the larger loans during the phase-in period. About 1,185 of the country's thrifts currently meet those capital standards.

Previously, thrifts could lend up to 100% of their capital to single developers, even though their net worth would be jeopardized if such a loan resulted in big losses.
