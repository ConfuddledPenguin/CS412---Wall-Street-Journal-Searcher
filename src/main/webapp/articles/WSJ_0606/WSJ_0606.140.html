Columbia S&L's New Chief Criticizes Senior Management --- Harshfield Says Sick Thrift Is Candidate for Seizure By Federal Regulators

  • Author: David J. Jefferson
  • Date: 06/06/90

BEVERLY HILLS, Calif. -- In a thrift industry version of a celebrity roast, Columbia Savings & Loan Association's new chief executive officer blasted his predecessors at yesterday's annual meeting, as former chief executive Thomas Spiegel and his father, Chairman Abraham Spiegel, watched. But shareholders of this insolvent thrift weren't laughing.

"All of us are victims," said shareholder Charlotte Levine, who bought Columbia stock on April Fool's Day 1987 at the urging of her Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. stockbroker. "This company was used as a personal piggy bank" for the Spiegel family, which owns about two-thirds of Columbia, she said.

"The association was placed in harm's way by direct senior management actions," said Edward G. Harshfield, a longtime financial services industry executive who was named president and chief executive of Columbia in March.

Mr. Harshfield was particularly gloomy about the thrift's ability to avoid seizure by federal regulators, saying that "remedial medicine could be too late, since the patient was already comatose -- insolvent -- when the doctor arrived."

Columbia has been scrambling to sell off its deteriorating junk bond portfolio and certain preferred stock. Mr. Harshfield said the portfolio has declined in value by roughly $1 billion since the thrift purchased the bonds. The portfolio had a value of about $2.9 billion as of March 31.

"The reality of the matter is what price we get for the bonds, to see what solution is left" for the S&L, Mr. Harshfield said, noting that the company has had "progress on that front." The executive has said he believes the company can get book value, "if not better," for the bonds. However, thrift experts have said that even a sale at book value wouldn't put a dent in Columbia's insolvency, and, therefore, they expect regulators to take over the thrift.

"We are capable of being taken over by federal regulators at any moment, as it now stands," Mr. Harshfield acknowledged in response to a shareholder's question.

Although federal legislation changing the way a thrift's assets are valued contributed to Columbia's insolvency, "only 3% of the loss was the cause of the government's action," Mr. Harshfield said. Columbia had a $591.1 million loss in 1989 and a $293.3 million loss in the first quarter.

After the meeting, Thomas Spiegel bristled at Mr. Harshfield's suggestions of mismanagement, saying, "You can't just sit there and point in time and say that 97% of the losses are attributable to this. . . . Ed's been here for three months, and he has his perspective. I have mine, being here 13 years."

The issue of alleged mismanagement was foremost on most shareholders' minds yesterday, particularly in light of a continuing probe by the Office of Thrift Supervision into "certain expenses and activities of former association officers."

Mr. Spiegel confirmed the OTS investigation but declined to discuss specifics.
