Credit Markets: Firms, Municipalities Go on Borrowing Binge And Bullish Investors Stampede to Greet Them

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Kevin Donovan
  • Date: 06/06/90

NEW YORK -- Corporate and municipal treasurers went on a borrowing binge yesterday as they issued more than $2.7 billion of new notes and bonds. The flood of new supply is a sign that issuers find current interest rates -- which have been falling for more than a month -- now at very attractive levels. At the same time, bullish bond market sentiment greatly increases the success of new offerings. Traders said that issuers are rushing to tap the debt market for fear that these favorable conditions could change.

Investors, meanwhile, are snapping up the new notes and bonds in an effort to lock in current rates before they fall any further.

The bulk of yesterday's new supply consisted of tax-exempt municipal bonds, including an $875 million offering by Washington Public Power Supply System. The nuclear power agency, which is irreverently known as `Whoops,' is best remembered as the issuer behind the biggest default in municipal-bond history. In 1983, WPPSS defaulted on $2.25 billion of Project 4 and 5 bonds.

But despite its infamous past, demand was so strong yesterday for the WPPSS bonds that underwriters led by First Boston Corp. restructured the offering by increasing the size from the originally planned $600 million and reducing yields. After the restructuring, the bonds, which were refunding revenue bonds for Project Nos. 1, 2 and 3, were priced to yield from 6.30% for one-year notes to 7.78% for 27-year bonds.

Brad Higgins, managing director of public finance at First Boston, said the strong demand partly reflected the recent credit rating upgrade on WPPSS bonds. Last week, both Moody's Investors Corp. and Standard & Poor's Corp. boosted ratings on some WPPSS debt to double-A from single-A.

Junk Bonds

Junk bond prices continued to rise yesterday, outperforming the broader bond market.

Most junk bonds, which are speculative-grade corporate bonds with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, climbed 1/8 point to 1/4 point.

The major market movers yesterday were again the bonds of casinos operated by Donald Trump and those issued by retailer R.H. Macy & Co.

Junk bonds of Trump Taj Mahal Funding and Trump Castle Funding continued to slip on news that real estate developer Donald Trump is facing an acute cash shortage.

Trump Taj Mahal's 14% bonds, due in 1998, slid 2 7/8 points to 54 1/8 yesterday after shedding eight points Monday. Meanwhile, Trump Castle Funding's 13.75% bonds, maturing in 1997, declined 2 1/2 points to end at 58 1/2. These bonds also fell eight points on Monday.

The recently battered junk bonds of R.H. Macy jumped on news that the company is "actively examining" opportunities to refinance or reduce its debt.

Macy's 14.5% senior subordinated debentures, due in 1998, gained 2 1/2 points to close at 67 while Macy's 14.5% subordinated debentures, maturing in 2001, advanced three points to 56 1/2.

Treasury Securities

Prices of Treasury bonds fell yesterday after a day of seesaw trading.

The benchmark 30-year bond ended more than 1/4 point lower, or $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, rose to 8.45% from 8.41%. The benchmark bond was quoted late yesterday at a price of 103 5/32 compared with 103 15/32 Monday.

The latest 10-year notes ended at 102 21/32 to yield 8.45% compared with 102 26/32 to yield 8.43%. Short-term rates were little changed.

In early trading, prices of bonds moved higher on expectations that oil prices would plummet following overnight news wire reports that Saudi Arabia will cut some of its crude oil prices to customers in July.

Even though crude oil futures slipped substantially at the New York Mercantile Exchange, "oil didn't fall as much as hoped for and the bond market subsequently lost its momentum," said James R. Capra, a senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc.

Municipal Bonds

Washington Public Power Supply's $875 million bond offering was clearly the highlight of the market.

However, in addition to WPPSS, several other municipalities tapped the market, including Hawaii, which issued $100 million in general obligation bonds and Maricopa County, Ariz., which issued $202 million in bonds.

Despite the heavy supply, municipal bonds held up pretty well.

The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bonds due in 2018 finished unchanged at 98 5/8 to yield 7.28%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Prices of mortgage-backed securities ended lower, with prices retreating after posting gains early in the day.

Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities for June delivery were down 1/8 on the day at 99 7/32, with the bellwether issues having posted a 1/8 point gain shortly after the opening.

The spread between the Ginnie Mae 9.5% securities and the 10-year Treasury was at 129 basis points, unchanged from late Monday.

Ginnie Mae 9% securities were down 6/32 at 96 27/32, and the 10% securities, at 101 20/32, were down 4/32.

In other mortgage-backed markets, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities were down 5/32 at 98 18/32, as were the 10% securities, at 100 15/32. Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were down 5/32 at 99 2/32, as were the 10% securities, at 101.

Corporate Bonds

It was busy for the investment-grade new-issue market as more than $1.1 billion of debt was offered.

The highlight of the session was a two-part debt offering by two units of American Electric Power Co. The units, AEP Generating Co. and Indiana Michigan Power Co., each sold $218.3 million of bonds.

The bonds, which mature in 2022, were priced at par to yield 9.82%. Among other new offerings: Morton International Inc. issued $200 million of "credit-sensitive" debentures priced to yield 9.335% and Boise Cascade Corp. issued $125 million of 12-year notes priced to yield 9.85%.

Foreign Bonds

Most foreign bond markets were relatively quiet yesterday, apparently because investors had little economic news on which to trade.

In recent sessions, some foreign government bonds have taken their cues from the U.S. bond market.

Among them are Japanese bonds, which rallied Monday but lost ground yesterday.

Japan's benchmark 10-year issue ended at a price of 88.19, down 0.14 point, to yield 6.93%. (Bond yields quoted in this column are calculated in local terms, but would show different results if calculated by the semiannual compound measure that is used in the U.S.)
