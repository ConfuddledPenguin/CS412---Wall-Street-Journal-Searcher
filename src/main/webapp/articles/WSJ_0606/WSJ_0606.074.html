Your Money Matters -- Getting Started: Index Funds Are Hot -- but Which One? --- Popular S&P 500 Loses Some Luster

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 06/06/90

{An Occasional Look at Financial Fundamentals}

First, the good news: If you invest in a mutual fund that tracks the U.S. stock market as a whole, you are likely to outperform the vast majority of all stock mutual funds.

Now, the bad news: Although a lot of folks think they own funds that track the whole market's performance, they don't.

So-called index funds are the hottest mutual-fund product of 1990, with industry giants Dreyfus Corp., Fidelity Investments and Vanguard Group slugging it out for market share.

Unlike most stock funds, which are actively managed in an effort to achieve superior returns, index funds don't try to outguess market swings or hunt for bargains. They simply buy and hold all or most of the stocks that make up a market index. Dreyfus, Fidelity and Vanguard all now offer funds that seek to track the performance of Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, the most popular benchmark among index fund investors.

So far this year, Vanguard's top selling stock fund is its $2.3 billion Index Trust -- 500 Portfolio, the oldest and largest of the stock-index mutual funds. The fund buys the 500 stocks that make up the S&P-500 index, and thereby hopes to closely track the index's performance. This passive strategy served shareholders well in the 1980s, when it generated 17% annual returns, better than 73% of all stock funds.

But now fund analysts and industry executives are warning that the S&P-500 stocks account for only part of the U.S. stock market. And it is starting to look like an increasingly pricey part.

"We've been concerned about that for some years," says John Bogle, Vanguard Group's chairman. "There's certainly been a bias {toward bigger stocks} in the market. The issue is, will Standard & Poor's 500 funds be as dominant in the '90s as they were in the '80s? We don't know the answer to that."

Index funds get their edge partly from staying fully invested in stocks at all times. That was a big plus during the raging bull market of the 1980s. Index funds also are cheap to run. Vanguard's 500 Portfolio has annual operating costs of 0.2%, vs. 1.5% for the typical actively managed stock fund.

Lured by Vanguard's success, in terms of both investment performance and attracting investors' dollars, Dreyfus and Fidelity are trying to muscle into the index-fund market. Earlier this year, both companies launched funds that track the S&P 500.

The trouble is, S&P-500 stocks no longer seem to be representative of the entire U.S. stock market. While S&P-500 stocks posted sparkling returns during the 1980s, other U.S. stocks trailed badly. How badly? Take a look at the Wilshire 4,500, which is the Wilshire 5,000 index of all U.S. stocks minus the S&P-500 stocks. During the 1980s, the Wilshire 4,500 posted a cumulative total return of 300%, vs. more than 400% for the S&P 500 stocks.

As a result of this lopsided performance, by the end of 1989 the stocks in the S&P 500 accounted for 73% of the market value of all U.S. stocks, up from 68% a decade earlier. The benchmark for measuring the market value of all U.S. stocks is the Wilshire 5,000, which doesn't restrict itself to 5,000 stocks, but rather includes all U.S. stocks traded on the New York Stock Exchange, the American Stock Exchange and Nasdaq. Over the course of the 1980s, the number of stocks in the Wilshire 5,000 ballooned from 3,785 to 5,870.

As critics of indexing are quick to point out, a big reason for the strong stock-market performance of the S&P-500 companies -- and of S&P-500 index funds -- is indexing itself. "When people are buying a stock because it's listed in an index, that creates inefficiency," notes Donald Phillips, editor of Morningstar's Mutual Fund Values, a Chicago newsletter.

Does that mean that the success of indexing in the 1980s was a fluke, a self-fulfilling prophesy caused by the huge influx of indexing money into the S&P-500 stocks? Not at all. Even a strategy of indexing the whole market would have paid big dividends over the past decade. Vanguard Group estimates that a Wilshire 5,000 index fund would have returned 16.4% a year after expenses during the 1980s. That would have put it ahead of 65% of all stock funds.

For those who want to index the whole market, the only current choice is Vanguard. In 1987, the company launched a fund that tracks the Wilshire 4,500, called the Extended Market Portfolio. Combine this fund with Vanguard's 500 Portfolio, and you would get exposure to the entire U.S. stock market. To own each index in the right proportion given their relative market values, you would currently have to put $26 in Vanguard's Extended Market Portfolio for every $74 you put in the 500 Portfolio.

Later this year, IBM Credit Corp. plans to launch two index funds. While they will be aimed at employees of International Business Machines Corp., they also will be available to the general public directly from IBM Credit. Taken together, the two IBM funds will index the 3,000 largest U.S. companies. But IBM's funds currently propose charging hefty annual expenses of 0.8% a year. By contrast, the annual expenses charged by Dreyfus, Fidelity and Vanguard are all under 0.3%.

How much of your money should you have in an index fund? Morningstar's Mr. Phillips says that for many investors, indexing half their stock portfolio would make a lot of sense. "You forsake the chance of really doing well, but you also won't do really badly," he says. "It's a strategy for people who don't have a lot of time."
