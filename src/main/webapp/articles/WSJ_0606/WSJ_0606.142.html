Coors, Its Output Crimped, to Buy Plant From Stroh

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 06/06/90

Adolph Coors Co. said its brewing unit agreed to buy a Memphis, Tenn., brewery from Detroit-based Stroh Brewery Co. Terms weren't disclosed. The deal would give the Golden, Colo., company its first large group of union workers in about 13 years and its first beer ever brewed outside Colorado.

The acquisition, which is expected to be completed about July 3, would alleviate the severe packaging-facility shortages and imminent brewing-capacity shortages that Coors faces because of its recent rapid growth. In the first quarter, Coors shipments rose nearly 30% to four million barrels from a year earlier. In 1989, its shipments grew faster than those of any other major U.S. brewer, expanding 6.5% to 17.7 million barrels.

Coors brews only in its Golden facility, which can brew about 20 million barrels a year, and packages beer in Golden and Elkton, Va. Coors's major problem now is its limited packaging capacity, as the company struggles to fill its bottles and cans for the peak summer season. The packaging facilities at both locations are being operated at or above design capacity to meet demand, said Peter Coors, president of the brewing unit, who noted that Coors distributors aren't getting all the beer they want in the packages they want.

Coors said it plans to increase its Golden brewing capacity to about 28 million barrels a year, but this expansion won't be completed until 1995. Mr. Coors said Stroh's 1971-vintage Memphis brewery has a capacity of 5.5 million barrels and can probably be retrofitted and ready to produce and package Coors products for next year's summer season. He said the company hasn't determined what beer or how much it would brew in Memphis.

Coors would inherit a unionized work force and a union contract at the Memphis brewery. Coors beer was the subject of a 10-year boycott by the AFL-CIO after a 1977 strike, and now its only union workers are some Operating Engineers in Golden. The Teamsters organized a vote of Coors brewery workers in December 1988, after the union boycott was ended, but 73% of the employees rejected union representation.

"We have no aversion to having unions," Mr. Coors said. Coors managers began holding talks with the Teamsters and the Brewery and Soft Drink Workers Conference representatives in Memphis yesterday, he said.

The planned sale would leave troubled Stroh with five breweries. Stroh's 1989 shipments fell nearly 10% to about 18.4 million barrels from a year earlier. The company acquired many brands and some breweries throughout the 1980s by buying other brewers such as Schlitz, but the family-owned company wasn't able to catch up with the market leaders, Anheuser-Busch Cos. and Miller Brewing Co., or match the marketing dollars of the two giants. In 1989, Stroh, the nation's fourth-largest brewer in terms of shipments, retained Morgan Stanley & Co. to try to help sell the company or some of its assets.

Last September, Coors, the third-largest brewer in the U.S., signed a deal to buy most of Stroh for about $425 million, but that agreement expired in December without a final deal. People close to the negotiations have said that the agreement fell apart during due-diligence investigations, which disclosed a substantially weaker Stroh than Coors executives had anticipated.
