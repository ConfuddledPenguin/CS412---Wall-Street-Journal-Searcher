Plan to Shift Oversight of Index Futures To SEC Is Unveiled by Administration

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 06/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration unveiled its long-awaited proposal to shift oversight of stock-index futures to the Securities and Exchange Commission, and would place the products under the agency's stricter approach to enforcement. The measure also would grant the SEC the power to oversee stock-index futures margins, or the good-faith money needed to trade the products. The commodity exchanges currently set those margins, with federal oversight only in emergencies. Treasury and SEC officials have cited low index-futures margins as causes for the 1987 and 1989 market drops.

Index futures, currently regulated by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, enable investors to bet on the movement of a basket of stocks without owning the actual securities. Trading in stocks and stock options is overseen by the SEC. While index futures are widely used by large institutional investors to control stock-market risk, they also are used in computer-aided program-trading strategies.

"The fact that we have separate regulators at this time is history," said Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady. "It's the way things started, but it's not the way things should be."

The proposal now goes to Capitol Hill, where Treasury officials say it will be introduced in the House at the administration's request by Rep. John Dingell, the Michigan Democrat who heads the Energy and Commerce Committee. In the Senate, Slade Gorton (R., Wash.) and John Heinz (R., Pa.) will sponsor both a free-standing bill and an amendment to either pending SEC or CFTC legislation, the Treasury aides said. A Senate source said it probably will be substituted for an amendment Mr. Gorton already has proposed to a bill beefing up the CFTC.

"The Treasury proposal improves on my original idea of simply changing jurisdiction," Sen. Gorton said. "It will allow for innovative new products and strengthened enforcement."

The Treasury measure, which took weeks longer to craft than officials there had expected, faces something of a tough road, particularly in the Senate. While the Banking committees are expected to support it and the Agriculture committees will oppose it, many Capitol Hill leaders are current or former members of the Agriculture committees. Those include Senate Minority Leader Robert Dole (R., Kan.) and House Speaker Thomas Foley (D. Wash.).

Sen. Patrick Leahy (D., Vt.), who heads the Agriculture Committee, said he opposes the proposal and plans to fight it vigorously on the Senate floor.

The Treasury said the measure is designed for "minimum disruption to the markets." Stock-index futures would continue to be regulated as commodities under the Commodity Exchange Act.

But the SEC would "enhance" regulation with securities anti-fraud provisions, such as the prohibition of insider trading and rules that require a "suitability" test for a salesperson to sell a product to a customer. The agency, created after the 1929 crash, is designed to focus on protecting the individual investor. CFTC oversight is largely based on the premise that most futures-markets investors are institutions that need fewer protections.

A CFTC spokeswoman said the futures markets already have their own provisions similar to the insider-trading rules and securities-markets suitability rules. "We are opposed to the jurisdictional changes," she said.

The Chicago Board of Trade also opposed the measure, calling it "creeping regulatory imperialism."

But Mr. Brady, who is supported in the jurisdiction fight by the SEC, said, "This is good common sense. It's good for the American people, and it ought to be law." He said that the measure would allow for closer coordination among the stock, options and futures markets and that it wouldn't have much effect on program trading.

The bill also would carry out another long-promised Treasury plan to change a clause of the Commodity Exchange Act to allow products with futures elements to be traded on securities exchanges.

The Treasury also said that if the bill isn't passed, "it is more likely that minor events will trigger major market disruptions like the ones we experienced in October of 1987 and October of 1989."

That statement immediately brought complaints from Sen. Leahy, who called it "false advertising."
