Dissident Faction At Campbell Soup Pools Voting Stake

  • Author: Vindu P. Goel
  • Date: 06/06/90

Three members of Campbell Soup Co.'s controlling Dorrance family who pressed for a sale of the company last winter disclosed they pooled most of their 17.4% stake into a voting trust. They also said their feelings about a sale are "substantially unchanged." In a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Dorrance Hamilton, Hope van Beuren, Diana Norris, and their spouses said they "continue to believe that the best interests of the shareholders would be served by a sale of the company."

Wall Street reacted sharply to the statements. In heavy trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Campbell shares rose $3.375 to close at $60.

The newly formed voting trust replaces an earlier trust that included several other family members. The previous trust was dissolved in December when the Dorrances publicly split over the independence of the Camden, N.J., food company.

The latest filing threatens to reignite the family feud, which pitched the three dissidents against the three children of late family patriarch John T. "Jack" Dorrance Jr., with three other family members in the middle. Jack Dorrance's children control about 31.5% of the company. The other family members have a combined 9.8% stake.

In January, Campbell appointed a new chief executive officer, David W. Johnson. The dissident shareholders agreed then to suspend temporarily their drive for a sale to give Mr. Johnson a chance to improve the company's mediocre financial performance.

In yesterday's filing, the dissidents said they support Mr. Johnson's "strategic measures" to bolster returns. The dissidents also said their latest call for a sale of Campbell "does not reflect dissatisfaction" with the new chief.

"But Mr. Johnson has clearly not embraced the sale of the company as his objective," said Peter Haje, an attorney with Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison, which represents the three dissidents.

The formation of the trust is "basically the formalization of a situation that has existed for some months," added an adviser to the group.

A Campbell spokesman said the company's 15-member board, which includes six family members, is committed to the company's independence and sees no reason to change its position. Mrs. Hamilton is considered the only acknowledged dissident on the board.
