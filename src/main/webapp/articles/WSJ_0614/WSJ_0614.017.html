Increase in Child-Abuse Cases Is Linked To Rising Cocaine Problem, Study Says

  • Author: Cathy Trost
  • Date: 06/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Cocaine and crack use is straining the nation's child welfare agencies with child-abuse and neglect cases and contributing to an increase in the number of children placed in foster care, according to a congressional report. "Drug-exposed parents and children are placing growing demands on the child welfare system," said the report released yesterday by the Human Resources Subcommittee of the House Ways and Means Committee. "The upsurge in crack use has been blamed for the increase in the number of reported cases of child abuse and neglect, the increased severity of child abuse, and the number of children placed in out-of-home care."

The child welfare system is "so overloaded by crack cocaine that it is about to short circuit," warned Rep. Thomas Downey (D., N.Y.), the subcommittee's acting chair. He and Rep. George Miller (D., Calif.), who heads the Select Committee on Children, Youth and Families, introduced legislation to provide states more resources for prevention, treatment and other services for drug-affected families.

The report parallels growing concern over increasing births of drug-exposed babies and their ability to cope in school and beyond. It draws together wide-ranging information about the effects of cocaine and its fiercely addictive derivative, crack cocaine, on families and child welfare, drug treatment and health-care systems.

More than 100,000 cocaine-exposed babies were born in 1988, according to the report, a figure that could rise to between 500,000 and four million in 10 years.

Crack has been linked to an increase in the number of abuse and neglect cases reported since the mid-1980s, when the drug first emerged. The report said drug abuse is a significant factor in more than 70% of the child welfare cases in some agencies. It has become "the dominant characteristic" in case loads of child protective-service agencies in 22 states and the District of Columbia. One survey cited in the report said 675,000 children a year are seriously mistreated by an alcoholic or drug-abusive caretaker.

The shortage-plagued foster-care system is also feeling the impact of drug-exposed babies and their often fragmented families. Data cited in the report said foster-care placements rose nationally by 29% to 360,000 in 1989 from 280,000 in 1986. Though no link can be conclusively drawn between drug use and the rise in placements, the report said increased placements are concentrated in areas heavily affected by crack abuse, such as California and New York. Those two states were responsible for 55% of the increase in foster placements nationally.

Despite these problems, there is a severe shortage of drug-treatment programs for pregnant women. Just 16% of New York City's drug-treatment programs, for instance, serve pregnant women addicted to crack.

The report also documents increased costs of health care for cocaine-involved pregnancies, including abandoned or "boarder" babies, and future effects on educational, mental health and juvenile justice systems.

Mr. Downey said legislation to address the problem would cost $5 billion over five years. But "we will save many lives and billions more than this bill costs" through early intervention efforts, he said. "We believe this bill has the same place that the budget summit has in the priorities of this country."
