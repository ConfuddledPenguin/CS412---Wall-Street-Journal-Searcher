Aide Tells Congress Pension Agency's Solvency Depends on Outcome of Cases

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 06/14/90

WASHINGTON -- The government agency that insures pension plans delivered a good-news, bad-news message to Congress. The good news, said James Lockhart, executive director of the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp., is that the agency "could be solvent in a few years" if several major pension cases it is negotiating or litigating go its way. Currently, the agency has a deficit of about $1 billion.

But the bad news is that the agency's deficit could balloon to as much as $7.8 billion if it sustains major losses in those cases, Mr. Lockhart warned in testimony before the House Ways and Means Committee's oversight subcommittee.

Despite recent statutory changes designed to strengthen the federal pension insurance program, including a sharp boost in insurance premiums paid by pension plans, the agency "still has problems that may require further reform," Mr. Lockhart said. The Bush administration is reviewing issues confronting the agency, and it may recommend legislative action early next year, he said.

Meantime, he said he hopes that the pension agency, the Labor Department's Pension and Welfare Benefits Administration and the Treasury Department can work with Congress to simplify pension regulations.

The insurance program and the defined-benefit plans it insures may be too complicated, Mr. Lockhart said. He said the complexity may be a reason why the number of pension-plan participants covered by defined-benefit plans has declined. Under defined-benefit plans, employers guarantee a certain level of retirement benefits for retirees.

Two of the pension cases that concern the agency involve LTV Corp. The PBGC has been locked in a long-running legal battle over a decision by the steel company, which is in bankruptcy proceedings, to terminate several of its pension plans and stick the agency with responsibility for $2.2 billion in the funds' liabilities.

An appeals court ruled in favor of LTV, and the Supreme Court is expected to rule on the matter any day now. The PBGC argues that LTV is trying to use agency insurance payments as a corporate subsidy, and officials worry that a high-court ruling for LTV would set a precedent for other bankrupt firms to take the same tack.

In the other LTV case, a New York federal bankruptcy court recently disallowed any priority for PBGC claims over those of other creditors and rejected the agency's authority to determine employer liability for pension underfunding. Mr. Lockhart said the agency is challenging the bankruptcy court's ruling, which he said could limit the agency's ability to collect on its claims.

He also voiced concerns about employer attempts to shift plan liabilities to weaker sponsors while in corporate reorganization; lack of employer funding for plant-shutdown benefits, which sometimes are guaranteed by the PBGC; and rules that let firms with inadequately backed plans pay out more in benefits than they must contribute to those plans the same year.

The agency is trying to protect the insurance program by encouraging better employer funding of pension plans, discouraging termination of underfunded plans and maximizing its recoveries when plans are ended, Mr. Lockhart said. He said the agency uses such tools as liens, along with tougher negotiating, to reduce its liability.
