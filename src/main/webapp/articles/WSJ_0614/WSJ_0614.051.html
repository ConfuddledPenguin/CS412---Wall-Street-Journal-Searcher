P&G to Buy Some Assets of American Cyanamid Co.

  • Author: Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 06/14/90

Procter & Gamble Co. agreed to buy most of American Cyanamid Co.'s men's toiletries and fragrance business for between $300 million and $370 million. The purchase gives P&G the popular Old Spice brand, along with the Sante Fe brand of toiletries, to add to its cabinet of personal and beauty-care products.

For American Cyanamid, a Wayne, N.J., chemical company, the sale is a major step in its efforts to shed its Shulton Group of consumer products, which has posted lackluster results in recent quarters.

The final pricetag for P&G will depend on how much American Cyanamid can get in a separate sale of its Breck hair-care and Lady's Choice personal-care products business. P&G said it guaranteed American Cyanamid $370 million for the combined sales.

American Cyanamid said it is negotiating to sell Shulton's household-products business, which includes Pine-Sol cleaner and Combat insecticides. Shulton's Pierre Cardin fragrances aren't included in the P&G deal.

The Old Spice brand of deodorant builds P&G's strength in that market, where it already holds a 24% share with its Sure and Secret brands. Old Spice has about 4% of that market, analysts said.

The Old Spice and Sante Fe men's fragrances mark P&G's first move into that market. With its purchase last year of Noxell Corp., P&G moved into the women's fragrances market with the Navy brand of perfume.

Analysts said the Old Spice brands share distribution to drug stores and mass marketers, where Noxell sells its Cover Girl and Clarion lines of makeup. "It's a good fit with other mass-market cosmetics," said Bonita Austin, analyst with Wertheim Schroder & Co. in New York.

P&G is sure to address Old Spice's advertising. American Cyanamid had tried to revamp the brand with new ads to move away from the sailor image of previous campaigns. One recent commercial, shot in Paris, featured a Frenchman with "a passion for American things."

But the company curbed spending last year as it looked to control costs in the division, analysts said. That should change under P&G's ownership. "P&G may not have the most dynamic ad copy, but it has the ability to create some excitement" with the brand, said Lynne Hyman of First Boston.

P&G also is expected to expand the brand's world-wide sales. Edwin L. Artzt, P&G chairman and chief executive officer, called the Old Spice brand "a strong foothold" in a market with growth potential.

Some analysts figure the purchase will mildly dilute P&G's 1991 earnings. Ms. Austin put the dilution at three or four cents a share, with her earnings estimate at $5.20 a share for the year.

The acquisition is subject to regulatory approval.
