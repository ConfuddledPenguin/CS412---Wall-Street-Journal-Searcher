OTC Focus: Market Ends Higher as Technologies, Utilities Advance; Microsoft Gains 5/8

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 06/14/90

Over-the-counter stocks ignored late-day declines in big exchange-listed issues and closed higher, like the bond market. The Nasdaq Composite Index rose 2.30 to 468.86, a 0.5% gain. Volume remained brisk at 163.5 million shares, up slightly from 163.3 million Tuesday. Advancing shares again outpaced decliners, 1,021 to 910.

"I think secondary stocks are finding their own way," said William Sulya, director of OTC trading at A.G. Edwards & Son, St. Louis. "Business {in OTC trading} in general is much better than it was in the first quarter."

Yesterday's brisk trading of smaller stocks surprised Lance Zipper, head of OTC trading at Kidder Peabody. Mr. Zipper said he expected caution from investors in advance of several economic indicators due out today and tomorrow.

Institutional buying of OTC stocks was strong across-the-board, he said, suggesting that portfolio managers are favoring smaller stocks as they approach the end of the quarter.

Utility shares surged, pushing the Nasdaq Utility Index up 6.17 to 674.84. Cable television shares, which are included in the index, got a boost from County NatWest analyst Lisa Donneson, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. The analyst upgraded her investment opinion on the group to a "buy" from a "hold," citing an easier regulatory climate and prospects for changes in evaluating cable industry loans. Tele-Communications, one of the day's most-active stocks, rose 1/2 to 15 5/8, and American Television & Communications gained 1 1/4 to 39 1/4.

Technology shares also were strong, pushing the Nasdaq 100 Index of larger issues, including many computer stocks, up 3.89 to 484.11 and lifting the larger Nasdaq Industrial Index 3.59 to 499.09.

Microsoft rose from early weakness to a gain of 5/8 to 76 1/2. The company extended its stock buy-back program through June 30, 1991, and Intel gained 1/8 to 48.

Westmark International fell 1 to 38 1/4 on nearly 1.6 million shares. Robert T. DeGavre, chief financial officer of the Seattle maker of ultrasound and other medical equipment, told DJPIR that investors have been nervous since a Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst lowered his rating on the stock last week. Mr. DeGavre, who called the stock's weakness an "over-reaction," said he hopes to provide investors next week with specific guidance about second-quarter earnings.

Also yesterday, Hambrecht & Quist analyst Neal Bradsher repeated cautious comments on Westmark's near-term outlook. However, Mr. Bradsher said in the long term he thinks the company is well-positioned in its markets, according to DJPIR. Westmark was scheduled to appear last night at a Ragen & Mackenzie investment conference in Seattle.

International Microelectronic Products was the most active, adding 5/16 to 2 7/16 on volume of 3.7 million shares. Most of that volume came from the sale of a block representing 7.5% of the company's shares outstanding.

Market sources say British Petroleum sold the 1.8 million-share block, which it had inherited through its acquisition of Standard Oil (Ohio). Sohio had acquired the stake through an original venture capital investment in International Microelectronic, a San Jose, Calif., maker of integrated circuits.

The sources say the sale was part of BP's divestment of investments in the electronics industry. An International Microelectronic spokesman said there have been no corporate developments that may have prompted the sale.

U.S. HealthCare jumped 1 3/4 to 19 3/8. Leonard Abramson, president of the Blue Bell, Pa., operator of health maintenance organizations, said in an interview the company expects to report earnings for the second quarter ending in June of more than 25 cents a share, compared with 14 cents a share in the year-earlier quarter. Mr. Abramson also forecast that the company will have record operating earnings and revenue in 1990, as analysts expect.

Gendex climbed 3/4 to 14. John McDonough, president and chief executive officer of the Milwaukee maker of dental and medical X-ray equipment, told a meeting of the New York Society of Securities Analysts that he's "comfortable" with analysts' estimates that Gendex will earn 90 to 91 cents a share for fiscal 1991 ending in March, compared with 81 cents a share in 1990.

Cell Technology added 3/8 to 2 5/8 after the Boulder, Colo., drug company said it will receive its first U.S. patent covering the company's anti-cancer drug for adult brain cancer.

Amgen advanced 1 1/2 to 74. Shortly after the market closed, the Thousand Oaks, Calif., biotechnology concern said its board approved a two-for-one stock split and the July 25 redemption of the company's 8% convertible subordinated debentures.

National Savings Bank of Albany tumbled 3 3/8 to 28 5/8 after the Albany, N.Y., bank rejected a buy-out proposal from TrustCo Bank Corp. of Schenectady, N.Y. National Savings also doubled its quarterly dividend to 30 cents a share from 15 cents. TrustCo shares fell 3/4 to 30 1/4.

Elcotel dropped 5/8 to 2 1/8 after the Sarasota, Fla., marketer of privately owned pay telephones reported a loss for the fourth quarter ended March 31 of six cents a share, compared with year-earlier net income from continuing operations of five cents a share. The company also restated sales revenue for the fiscal second and third quarters of 1989, which resulted in operating losses for those quarters, rather than profits as originally reported.

Analysts' comments fueled activity in several stocks, according to DJPIR.

Nike gained 1 7/8 to 80 1/2 after Merrill Lynch analyst Brenda Gall raised her intermediate-term rating on the Beaverton, Ore., athletic footwear maker to "buy" from "above average." Merrill Lynch also selected Nike as its weekly "focus 1" stock pick.

American Management Systems jumped 1 1/2 to 16 3/8 after Alex. Brown & Sons analyst Cato D. Carpenter raised his investment rating on the stock to "buy" from "hold" and increased his 1990 and 1991 earnings estimates for the Arlington, Va., information systems services company. DJPIR reported that Mr. Carpenter now estimates American Management will earn $1 a share in 1990 and $1.20 a share in 1991, compared with 56 cents a share in 1989, and is optimistic about the company's earnings momentum and introduction of new products.

Rouse fell 1 to 23. Merrill Lynch analyst Jordan Heller cut his near- and intermediate-term ratings on the Columbia, Md., real estate concern to "hold" from "above average," DJPIR said. Mr. Heller, who cautioned that negative market sentiment about problem real estate markets will prevent the stock from outperforming the market, maintains his long-term "buy" opinion on the stock and didn't change his earnings estimates for the company.
