Technology: Smog-Cutting Regulations Are Announced by EPA

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/14/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency issued rules to cut smog-forming emissions from chemical plants and hazardous-waste facilities. The regulations will cost industry more than $54 million a year. The agency has been trying since 1983 to impose certain rules on synthetic organic chemical plants, but was delayed during the Reagan administration by opposition from budget officials.

The regulations are among a series required by clean-air and hazardous-waste laws to reduce volatile organic chemicals, which are emitted by both industry and vehicles and are blamed for producing smog. Smog causes health problems and reduces visibility.

The proposals will require new chemical plants to cut pollution from vents either by using new combustion equipment or through manufacturing changes that recover and recycle chemicals before they are vented. Certain regulations that originally were opposed in 1983 will apply to plants built or modified since then. The EPA estimates the changes will cost industry $35.8 million in capital costs with annual operating costs of $21.6 million.

The regulations also mark the first time the government is regulating air pollution from hazardous-waste sites. Some 1,400 facilities that handle hazardous wastes will be required within two years to control leaks from pumps and valves, and in many cases to install pollution-control equipment on vents. The agency put the cost to the industry at $33 million to $46 million a year.

Agency officials said the rules shouldn't be affected by new clean-air legislation now before Congress.
