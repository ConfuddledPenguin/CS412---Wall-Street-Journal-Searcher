Top Bush Aides Say Japan Is Stalling In Trade Talks, Press for Quick Action

  • Author: Peter Truell and Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 06/14/90

Senior Bush administration officials, accusing Japan of foot-dragging, say talks aimed at improving trade and economic relations are stalled and that President Bush and Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu may have to meet again soon to defuse tensions. The White House is sensitive about the recent erosion in the bilateral economic relationship because President Bush earlier this year decided against naming Japan for unfair trade practices under the tough Super 301 provision of the 1988 Trade Act, which carries the threat of trade retaliation.

U.S. critics charge that ever since that decision Japan has stonewalled U.S. attempts to gain better access to its markets. For example, a recent high-level U.S.-Japan meeting in Hawaii resulted in little or no progress in resolving issues that threaten to delay a final report -- due at the end of the month -- on ways to resolve problems.

The two countries began this process, called the Structural Impediments Initiative (SII), last summer in an attempt to reduce bilateral friction and address the U.S.'s stubbornly high bilateral deficit with Japan. "We were shocked at the stonewalling in Hawaii," said one senior administration official who asked not to be named.

Japanese officials, in return, argue that the U.S. is pushing commitments that weren't required by the interim report. "The main architecture is already on the table," a foreign ministry official said. "Neither side is supposed to add new buildings to the blueprint."

The Japanese seem to sense that fast repair work is needed. Foreign Minister Taro Nakayama and Vice Minister for International Finance Makoto Utsumi are in the U.S. this week to meet with their U.S. counterparts to discuss the SII and other bilateral issues. Shintaro Abe, a powerful, high-ranking leader of the Liberal Democratic Party, will soon visit the U.S. for meetings on the 30th anniversary of the U.S.-Japan security treaty, but will also likely hold talks on the SII.

Both sides agree that the most contentious issues preventing the SII from succeeding are Japan's future level of public spending; procedures for following up and ensuring progress in implementing the SII report's recommendations, and how Japan will toughen its antitrust procedures.

Recent statements from the Japanese government saying that the country intends to continue running a surplus equivalent to 2% of national output have angered the Bush administration, particularly when the U.S. is trying to address a $49 billion deficit with Japan. A continued Japanese surplus of that magnitude "creates a dislocation in the trading system," U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills said in a meeting with reporters yesterday, adding that it's "a statement from the {Japanese} ministry of finance that needs to be corrected."

Others are more direct. "We're damned if we're going to let {Japan} accumulate another half trillion dollars in trade surpluses at our expense over the course of the next decade," said the senior administration official. That, he continued, will happen if the Japanese don't open up their markets further and increase public investment.

Japan's recent insistence on retaining its surplus "is purely a negotiating ploy on the part of the Ministry of Finance of Japan," David Mulford, Treasury undersecretary for international affairs, told reporters this week. He said it was an attempt "to take pressure off itself to comply with understandings it reached with the U.S. earlier this year to increase public expenditure."

Congress also is growing impatient. "The government of Japan must understand that if SII doesn't meaningfully address bilateral trade concerns, the Congress may have no choice but to explore additional measures to address these concerns," Sens. Robert Packwood (R., Ore.), Max Baucus (D., Mont.) and Jack Danforth (R., Mo.), said in a joint letter sent to Mrs. Hills this week.

The Japanese insist they have made progress since a lengthy interim SII report was published in April. On May 30, Japan revised its large retail store regulations, saying stores must receive a permit to open within 18 months of application. Previously, that process often dragged on for 10 years, effectively excluding many foreign stores. Also, Japan's Fair Trade Commission has increased its staff and is drafting antitrust changes that, the Japanese say, answer many U.S. requests. Japan's major ministries also are mulling further proposals for meeting U.S. concerns about the SII, officials in Tokyo say.

And foreign ministry officials say that the notion Japan should preserve its surplus is a minority position advocated by the ministry of finance. "We are aware of that view, but the government as a whole doesn't have that as a policy," one Japanese official said.

But difficult problems remain. In particular, the U.S. wants Japan to increase public spending to 10% of the gross national product over the next 10 years. But the Japanese say they want to use a yen total rather than a percentage of national output. "They raised the 400 trillion yen figure," another senior U.S. administration official said, but he said that "just continues the {Japanese} surplus at its current level." Mr. Kaifu himself, in remarks to reporters over the weekend, also said Japan was opposed to specifying percentages of GNP, indicating strong resistance to the U.S. approach.

There also are disagreements over what the penalties should be for punishing those who violate antitrust regulations; the U.S. maintains that Japan merely imposes small fines that amount to little more than wrist-slapping.

The U.S. and Japan plan to exchange proposals June 20 and then meet June 25 to finalize the SII report. But U.S. officials question whether that will be possible. "If there's to be a final report by the time of the Houston summit on July 9, then we'll probably have to meet again {after the end of June}," this official said.

If those SII meetings do falter, President Bush and Prime Minister Kaifu will at least get a chance to address these issues soon. They are scheduled to hold a private bilateral summit before the main gathering in Houston of the heads of state of the U.S., Japan, West Germany, France, Britain, Italy and Canada.
