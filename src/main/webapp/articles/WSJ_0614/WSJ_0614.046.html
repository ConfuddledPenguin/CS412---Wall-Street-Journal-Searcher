SEC Votes to Seek Comment on Plan For Mutual Funds

  • Author: Sandra Block Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/14/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission voted to seek public comment on ways to update the 50-year-old statute that governs mutual funds. The comments are being solicited in connection with a study started recently by the SEC's investment-management division to suggest legislative and regulatory changes to the Investment Company Act, which governs regulations of mutual funds, said Kathryn McGrath, the division's head. The study is scheduled to last at least until next spring.

The SEC will request comments on such issues as distribution of mutual fund shares, closed-end funds and repurchases of shares, transactions in options and futures contracts, and the regulation of securities products sold by insurance companies.

SEC members strongly support the plan to update the statute regulating mutual funds. "I'm afraid the Investment Company Act has become cumbersome and outdated and even anti-competitive," Commissioner Philip Lochner said.

The agency also voted to seek comment on a proposal that would allow issuers of initial public offerings to distribute an abbreviated version of the offering's prospectus to investors. A summary prospectus provides investors with a condensed version of key information in the full registration statement, enabling investors to determine whether they are interested in more information about the offering, the SEC said.

While summary prospectuses have been permitted for issuers who met certain criteria, the proposal would for the first time allow their use in IPOs. Issuers usually don't take advantage of the procedure unless their prospectus exceeds 100 pages, said Linda Quinn, head of the SEC's division of corporation finance.

The proposal to extend the procedure to IPOs stems from a recent inquiry by an issuer of an IPO, SEC officials said. Because of the length of the prospectus, the cost of soliciting investor interest in the offering would be substantial.

Ms. Quinn acknowledged that the use of abbreviated prospectuses to generate investor interest "has the potential for abuse." But since IPOs are always reviewed by the commission staff before they are offered to the public, "I don't think that will figure in," she said.

The SEC also voted to seek comment on a proposal aimed at assuring that regulators have access to records of investment companies that invest in foreign securities. However, several commissioners, including Chairman Richard Breeden, indicated that they may not support the proposal when it comes up for a final vote. "I don't want to suggest support for comment on this issue indicates support for adoption of the rule," he said.

The proposal would direct investment companies to keep certain books and records in the U.S. and require companies to preserve all original books and records in English. The rule would ensure that the SEC staff can carry out its responsibility to inspect investment companies, Ms. McGrath said.
