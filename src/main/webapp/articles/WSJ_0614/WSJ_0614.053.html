Wednesday's Markets: Retail Data, Rate Hopes Boost Bonds --- Investors Debating Possible Fed Move As Stocks Ease a Bit

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/14/90

NEW YORK -- Worse-than-expected retail sales for May drove bond prices higher as investors happily contemplated lower interest rates. Lower rates play well in the stock market, too, but investors worried that sluggish economic growth might deteriorate to the point of hurting profits and, in turn, stock prices. The Dow Jones Industrial Average climbed briefly into record territory in midafternoon, then eased back to close at 2929.95, down 3.47 in moderately active trading.

Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices rose nearly seven-eighths of a point, or nearly $8.75 for each $1,000 of face amount, in active trading. The dollar was down against the West German mark and up against the Japanese yen, but much of the action in this global market involved currencies other than the dollar.

The government report on May retail sales -- showing a sharp decline rather than an expected weak rise -- raised expectations that the Federal Reserve Board may ease interest rates in order to stave off recession.

Stock prices probably would have fallen further on the news of lower consumer spending, but investors in this market also like to fantasize about the Fed lowering rates. In addition, the drop in bond yields that accompanies a rise in bond prices makes stock investments look comparatively more attractive.

In the stock market, "it wasn't a bad day for a down day," said Joseph McAlinden, Dillon Read research director, noting that the volume of trading in declining stocks wasn't much greater than in advancers.

Gary Ciminero, chief economist at Fleet/Norstar Financial Group, Providence, R.I., said the retail-sales report is "bad news for the growth indicators, and the stock market is reacting in a way that says, `Whoops.' Investors like to celebrate lower interest rates, but not at the expense of economic growth."

Sung Won Sohn, chief economist at Norwest Corp., Minneapolis, said he feels the retail-sales figure is a more important indicator than the inflation indicators due out today and tomorrow. "After the weak employment numbers we've seen, a decline in retail sales means the economy continues to be weak and raises concern about recession," he said.

The dollar didn't move on the retail-sales report, however, Mr. Sohn said. "Foreign exchange is much more sensitive to trade and inflation numbers," he said.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell. Moderately active trading totaled 158.9 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues outnumbered advancers, 786 to 722.

Bond prices climbed. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell to 8.38%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6865 marks and 154.75 yen, compared with 1.6965 marks and 154.55 yen Tuesday.
