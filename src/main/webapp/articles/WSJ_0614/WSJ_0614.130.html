Chase Manhattan Weighs Asset Sales, Trimming Some Units, Cutback in Staff

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 06/14/90

NEW YORK -- Faced with slack corporate financing needs, Chase Manhattan Corp. is considering selling assets and paring back some activities, which could possibly include layoffs. Chase bankers said that the review is still under way and that no decisions have been reached. Two areas that are reportedly being considered for cutbacks are foreign-exchange trading and European corporate finance. But although pruning is possible, Chase isn't expected to pull out of those businesses altogether.

"We are clearly determined to improve profitability," Richard J. Boyle, vice chairman in charge of global banking, said in an interview. "The level of deal flow is markedly lower . . . we're not looking at something that is all that radical. It's an ongoing process of renewal."

Among the assets that the bank holding company might sell are its buildings in Europe as well as One New York Plaza, which it has tried to sell in the past.

Chase's primary dealership in U.S. government securities and its recently won securities-underwriting powers aren't on the block, according to Chase insiders.

The hunt for ways to reduce overhead costs is apparently an outgrowth of a recent retreat attended by Chase's top managers. At that meeting, some of those present said, President Thomas G. Labrecque outlined the prospects for revenue growth for the remainder of the year and concluded that expenses need to be reduced.

The earnings outlook is understood to be especially tight in Mr. Boyle's global banking unit. Its North American corporate-finance unit is trailing results posted a year ago, people in the bank say. Last year the unit posted record revenue, largely on the strength of corporate acquisitions and restructurings. Since then, such deals have largely vanished with the collapse of the junk bond market.

Meanwhile, Chase, like other U.S. banks, is having little success grabbing its share of deals being done by European corporations in anticipation of the unification of the European Community in 1992.

Last year, Chase reported a loss of $665 million, or $7.94 a share, as a result of $1.26 billion in special charges related to past-due developing-country loans, bad real estate loans in Arizona and the closing of its London securities dealer. In 1988, Chase had earnings of $1 billion, or $11.55 a share.

Earlier this month, Moody's Investors Service Inc. lowered its ratings on Chase's senior debt to Baa-2 from Baa-1, as its levels of problem loans have risen.

Robert Albertson, a securities analyst with Goldman, Sachs & Co., said Chase has done a "very good job" of reining in its growth in overhead. As of Dec. 31, Chase had 41,610 employees, down 2% from 1987 levels. Analysts find that encouraging in light of the fact that Chase's profitable consumer and transaction-processing businesses continue to grow and add employees.

Chase recently centralized all of its European back office operations into a single location in Bournemouth, England; pared back the scale of its U.S. government securities operation and sold off its underperforming banks in Belgium and the Netherlands.

"Every money center is having reductions in staff -- Continental Bank, Citicorp and, bless their hearts, J.P. Morgan," said Mr. Albertson. "These aren't some shocking event. They're just paring away."

Despite the difficulties encountered in Europe, Mr. Boyle said Chase remains committed to corporate finance on the continent. "Our opportunities in Europe center very much on the fact that we are a pan-European bank. Nevertheless, there are activities that no longer offer attractive products for us. With the lower levels of activity, it's tough all over the place."

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Chase closed at $25.25, down 75 cents.
