Business Bulletin: A Special Background Report On Trends in Industry And Finance

  • Author: Lynn Asinof
  • Date: 06/14/90

CAR LOANS get shorter, as lenders seek better credit quality. Average length of a new bank car loan dropped to 51 months in 1989, down from 53 months the previous two years, says the Consumer Bankers Association. Having found that 60-, 66- or 72-month loans often leave people owing more than their cars are worth, lenders have been pushing shorter loans. "There has been some increase in repossessions and delinquencies," says Fritz Elmendorf of the CBA, who calls the trend "a conservative reaction to controlling possible problems."

Loan length was "getting out of hand," says John Andrews of General Motors Acceptance Corp., noting that the longer loans were also keeping buyers out of the market for up to six years. Sixty-month loans still dominate the market, he says, but percentages are falling -- from 70% of all GMAC loans in 1988 to 59% last year. "That is a tremendous drop in one year," he says.

Many lenders aggressively market leasing as car loan interest deductibility phases out, says Jack Shipman of Liberty National Bank, Louisville, Ky.

FATHERS DAY GIZMOS range from the practical to the extravagant.

The Sharper Image in Pittsburgh says its hot seller this Father's Day is a $40 mirror that attaches to the shower head, allowing water to circulate behind the mirror to prevent fogging. Also in demand: the $35 motorized tie rack, which the store says is selling "like popcorn." Hammacher Schlemmer in New York says it can't stock enough $115 Swiss Army sunglasses, which feature a "unique torsion system" that grips the head instead of the nose. But it has sold only two of its $59,000 reproductions of a 1929 mahogany speedboat.

Stress-reliever gifts are big sellers at Broadway, a Los Angeles chain. Priced at $20, tear-apart dolls -- a baseball umpire, football referee or golfer whose arms, legs and head attach with Velcro -- allow Dad to vent his frustration while watching Sunday sports. Another sports-viewing accessory: a beer mug with a bell that can be rung for a refill. One popular gift from Herrington Co., a Londonderry, N.H., catalog company: a $22.95 car wash/polish called Klasse that replaces eight car-care compounds.

OLD BATTERIES attract growing attention as an environmental problem.

In an effort to keep lead-acid batteries out of landfills and incinerators, some 22 states have passed laws to encourage recycling, says the Battery Council International. Legislation is pending in five more states and at the federal level. Some of the laws merely require retailers to take back used batteries; others establish a deposit system. Currently more than 80% of lead-acid batteries are recycled, says Robert Garino of the Institute of Scrap Recycling Industries, Washington.

But household batteries -- which contain mercury or cadmium -- are only just beginning to be recycled, says William Brown of Waste Management Inc. David Cohen of Mercury Refining Co., Latham, N.Y., says his company is signing "a tremendous number" of contracts to begin accepting household batteries. "It is a relatively small part of our business now, but it could become a very large segment."

Button-size batteries are the most profitable to recycle; nickel-cadmium rechargeables are break-even, he says.

UPSIDE-DOWN LABELS will appear on beer bottles shipped from Dock Street Brewing Co., Philadelphia, this month to protest import duties on American beer. Noting that U.S. duties are only 13.5 cents per case, it says foreign duties range from 24 cents a case in Canada to $14.64 a case in China.

FAX HUMOR gets a boost from cartoonist John Caldwell, who offers 200 illustrated cover sheets in his "Fax This Book." All are faxable and perforated for easy removal. One example: "My report follows" shows a man being followed by six pages.

WILD BULBS exported by Dutch flower bulb companies will bear a special label starting in July, due to an agreement with environmental groups. The goal: to help protect endangered species, such as snow drop, that come from Turkey.

THE MILKMAN makes a modest comeback as consumers opt for convenience.

Welsh Farms Inc., Long Valley, N.J., says home delivery now makes up about 25% of business "and it seems to be growing." The largest customer group: families with children. Roger Haadsma of Quality Creamery in Grand Rapids, Mich., is seeing 5% annual growth in home delivery. He credits use of returnable bottles and growing environmental awareness. So does Marie Oberweis of Oberweis Dairy Inc., Aurora, Ill. "On Earth Day, I personally signed up 12 new customers, but we've been getting three to five calls every day," she says.

Today's milkman isn't the bottle dropper of yesteryear, but an entrepreneur. H.B. Hood in Boston sold its home delivery routes to individual operators more than 10 years ago. Madison Whitehead of Welsh Farms describes the milkmen who work with his dairy as independent business people with expensive equipment who service up to 200 homes a day and make "a fair living."

Still, less than 1% of milk sales are through milkmen, says Christine Meissner of the American Dairy Association and Dairy Council.
