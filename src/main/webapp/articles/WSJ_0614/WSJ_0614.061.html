U.S. Car Sales Declined 13.1% In Early June

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 06/14/90

DETROIT -- U.S. auto sales continued on a downward spiral as sales of domestic cars and trucks fell 13.1% in early June. The Big Three U.S. auto makers accounted for all of the market's decline. The seven major Japanese auto makers who build cars in the U.S. all reported sales gains, although some of those increases were related to higher domestic production this year.

Car dealers said consumer demand continues to be spotty. Consumers are snapping up some newer models like Ford Motor Co.'s Explorer sport utility vehicle introduced in March and General Motors Corp.'s new 1991 four-door Buick Regal introduced June 4. But other lines, particularly those that have been carried over with relatively little change from this past year, are lagging. Sales of Chevrolet's Beretta-Corsica compact cars, for example, plunged 24% in the period.

"You've got to have something different from everybody else. Having the new models helps," said Ed Polito, new car manager at William Lehman Buick Inc., Miami. Sales of the 1990 two-door version of the Regal, for example, are "sluggish" compared with the new four-door model, even though the discount package on the coupe is $500 more generous than that on the sedan, he said.

Overall, Americans bought 243,297 domestic cars and trucks in the eight selling days of the June 1-10 period. In the nine selling days a year earlier, sales totaled 314,942 vehicles. The percentage change is based on the daily sales rate.

U.S.-built passenger cars sold at a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 6.3 million units in the period, according to a U.S. Department of Commerce formula. That lagged behind both the 7.2 million-car rate a year ago and the 7.3 million rate for late May.

The weak sales in the recent period suggest consumers are putting up some resistance to auto makers' efforts to cut back on the heavy discounts that depressed profits earlier this year. Car makers have relatively lean inventories now, but the companies are hoping to boost production later this year. Those beefed-up schedules could come under pressure if sales don't pick up.

Among the Big Three, Chrysler had the worst early June performance. Combined sales of Chrysler cars and trucks fell 20.1% from a year ago. Sales of Chrysler cars skidded 18.3%, while sales of the auto maker's trucks, minivans and Jeeps fell 22%.

Ford posted a 0.5% increase in domestic truck sales largely attributed to a doubling in sales of the new Explorer sport utility and discontinued Bronco II compact sport utility vehicles. But the No. 2 auto maker's total car sales fell 20.6% during the latest 10 days.

GM's domestic car sales were off 19.2%, as the Cadillac luxury division was the only one of five GM car divisions to post a gain from a year ago. GM's domestic truck sales fell 11.3% in early June from a year earlier.

Japanese auto makers who build and sell cars in the U.S. continued to show gains over a year ago. Toyota Motor Corp. boosted sales of its domestically built Camry and Corolla models by 35%. Sales of Honda Motor Co.'s U.S.-built Accords and Civics rose 30.8%.

Nissan Motor Co. posted a 30.5% increase in domestic car sales but reported a modest 1.1% gain in trucks.

At Mitsubishi Motors Corp., sales of U.S.-made cars jumped 59%, largely reflecting low production a year ago.
