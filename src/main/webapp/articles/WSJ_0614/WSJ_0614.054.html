Battle for Listings May Follow Ruling On `1 Share, 1 Vote' --- Big Board, Most Vulnerable, May Adjust Requirement Due to Court's Decision

  • Author: Craig Torres and Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 06/14/90

A major court ruling striking down the Securities and Exchange Commission's "one-share, one-vote" rule could reignite a war among U.S. stock exchanges over which one gets to list the shares of major American corporations. Although exchanges were reluctant to tip their hands yesterday, people familiar with exchange rule-making say listing requirements may be rapidly adjusted to attract an expected round of new stock issues by companies seeking to create several classes of stock with different voting rights, principally as an anti-takeover defense.

Most at risk is the New York Stock Exchange, which currently has a rule on its books that, with some exceptions, prevents companies from listing new shares of stock that have different voting rights.

A federal appeals court Tuesday struck down SEC rule 19c4, which required corporations to have equal voting rights for different classes of stock. A rule similar to 19c4 has been incorporated into the New York Stock Exchange's rule books.

With the court decision, many companies could seek to create multiple classes of stock with different voting rights. But they are potentially barred from listing these new shares on the Big Board because of its current rules.

The American Stock Exchange and the National Association of Securities Dealers Nasdaq over-the-counter market never adopted a version of 19c4 for their rule books, according to one attorney familiar with the exchanges. "Companies traded on the American Stock Exchange or the NASD are no longer subject to the SEC's regulation of the allocation of voting rights among shareholders," said Robert D. Rosenbaum, and counsel for the Business Roundtable, which successfully challenged the SEC.

Meredith Brown, head of mergers and acquisitions at Debevoise & Plimpton said: "The question is, `Will there be a race among {exchanges} for greater permissiveness?'"

In the mid-1980s, the Amex and Nasdaq used their more liberal listing requirements to try to lure from the Big Board companies seeking to create classes of stock with different voting rights. That fight ultimately led the SEC to enact its rule requiring one-share, one-vote for companies that didn't already have multiple stock classes.

Now, in order to compete with rival exchanges, it could "be necessary for {the Big Board} to amend its own rules to eliminate the effect of rule 19c4," Mr. Rosenbaum said.

Perhaps not. Some people familiar with the SEC think the agency may try to abort the battle by cajoling exchanges into adopting uniform listing regulations.
