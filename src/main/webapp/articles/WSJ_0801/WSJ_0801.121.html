Group Led by Morse Sells F.A.O. Schwarz To a Dutch Concern --- Recovering From a Slump, Toy Retailer Has Chance To Expand Into Europe

  • Author: Udayan Gupta
  • Date: 08/01/90

NEW YORK -- F.A.O. Schwarz has a new owner again -- the sixth since the founding family sold the famous toy-store chain in 1963. Koninklijke Bijenkorf Beheer N.V., Amsterdam, purchased the 17-store chain from an investor group led by the Philadelphia investment banker Peter Morse. That group had acquired F.A.O. Schwarz in 1985 and engineered its current recovery.

Terms of the latest sale weren't disclosed, but an industry executive estimated the price at about $40 million, nearly four times what the Morse group paid in 1985. F.A.O. Schwarz said its sales will exceed $50 million this year.

The transaction won't change the way F.A.O. Schwarz operates, said Peter Harris, its chief executive officer. The current management team will continue to operate independently, he said, but "KBB's financial strength will give F.A.O. new capital to grow and fuel its expansion into Europe."

The purchase of F.A.O. Schwarz gives KBB, a Dutch retail conglomerate with sales of $2.2 billion in 1989, its first major acquisition in the U.S. The high-end retailing business is a niche that has proved extremely attractive to foreign buyers. Investcorp International Inc., a partnership of Arabian investors, recently purchased Saks Fifth Avenue for about $1.5 billion. Investcorp earlier had made more than $100 million in profit buying and selling Tiffany & Co.

"The acquisition gives F.A.O. access to KBB's capital and international expertise," says Carol Farmer, a Boca Raton, Fla., industry consultant who helped put the deal together. "In return KBB has acquired a premier brand name in the selling of toys and gifts," she says.

When Mr. Morse and the investor group purchased F.A.O. Schwarz in 1985, the 123-year-old company had lost much of its glitter. With sales of only $20 million a year, and with many of the stores in poor locations, F.A.O. Schwarz was losing money.

The new investors closed down some of the unprofitable stores, moved the company's New York flagship store to a larger location and opened a giant new store in San Francisco. They also revitalized the company's catalog operations and added a new line of products sold by mail. The changes were instrumental in attracting about $7 million in new capital from such venture capitalists as Prudential Venture Partners, New York, and Boston's Charles River Ventures. By the end of last year, sales at F.A.O. Schwarz had more than doubled from 1985 and the company was highly profitable, the company says.

Frederick August Otto Schwarz, an immigrant from Germany, opened the first Schwarz store in 1862, in Baltimore. After running the company profitably for more than a century his family sold it in 1963. The company then had a succession of owners, including Parents Magazine Inc., W.R. Grace & Co., and Christiana Cos., a San Diego real estate and specialty retailer.
