Politics & Policy: Senate Panel Refuses to Fund Drug Center

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 08/01/90

WASHINGTON -- A Senate Appropriations subcommittee rejected fiscal 1991 funding sought by the Bush administration to establish a National Drug Intelligence Center under the Justice Department and drug czar William Bennett. Mr. Bennett is seeking an initial $46 million for the center, but the Senate panel chose instead to apply the money to expand an existing anti-drug program aimed at areas of intensive narcotics traffic. Four metropolitan areas -- Houston, Los Angeles, Miami and New York -- and the Southwest border region have been designated as targets for the funding, which would triple to $82 million, including an estimated $32 million for state and local aid.

The proposed intelligence center has been a central part of Mr. Bennett's drug strategy. But critics in Congress have argued that the project represents a financial burden to the Justice Department and could prove disruptive to drug intelligence operations by other agencies.

"If we don't get this, we'll be partially blind in the war on drugs," said John Walters, Mr. Bennett's chief of staff and national security adviser. But despite its stated goals, the administration's initiative appears weakest with the very lawmakers who sit both on the Appropriations panel and the Senate Intelligence Committee.

The decision to deny the funding yesterday had the backing of Sens. Dennis DeConcini (D., Ariz.) and Alfonse D'Amato (R., N.Y.), both on the Intelligence Committee. Moreover, within the full Appropriations Committee, Sen. Ernest Hollings (D., S.C.), himself a member of the Intelligence Committee, has taken the lead in challenging funding for the new center.

Budget and local politics appear to play a part as well. By reallocating the funds to cover state and local grants, lawmakers would directly benefit their constituencies in the Southwest and New York, for example.

"We still think it is a high priority," said Joseph McHugh, legislative liaison for Mr. Bennett. But he acknowledged that the Senate strategy could prove attractive to Southwest and Los Angeles-area lawmakers who dominate the comparable House subcommittee, which has so far supported funding for the intelligence center.

The debate over drug policy comes as part of a larger $20.7 billion bill funding the Treasury Department and Postal Service for the new fiscal year beginning Oct. 1. An estimated $5.9 billion is provided for the Internal Revenue Service, a 7.2% increase over current spending but still less than the $6.1 billion requested by the administration.

Unlike the House measure, the Senate bill sets no specific target for increased IRS tax enforcement against foreign-owned U.S. companies. But in response to a request by the agency, the Senate bill would give the IRS new authority to hire outside experts in support of investigations and litigation efforts against foreign-owned corporations.
