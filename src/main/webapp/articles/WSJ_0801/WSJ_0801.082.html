Ex-Official Regrets 1988 Sale of Thrift To Arizona Executive Indicted for Fraud

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 08/01/90

WASHINGTON -- A former government official said he regretted the sale of an ailing thrift, in a lucrative government-assisted deal, to an Arizona insurance executive who had been indicted for fraud 12 years earlier. George Barclay, president of the Federal Home Loan Bank of Dallas, said that the government staff should have looked more deeply into the background of James Fail, the insurance executive. Mr. Barclay said that he was aware of the fraud indictment at the time of the sale but that by itself it wasn't enough to disqualify Mr. Fail from bidding. However, in testimony before a Senate Judiciary subcommitee investigating government-assisted thrift sales in 1988, Mr. Barclay admitted that his staff failed to inform senior officials that an insurance company owned by Mr. Fail was convicted of fraud as well.

"If we had the time to work in a more orderly basis, people like James Fail would not have slipped through the net," said Mr. Barclay. "Had I known the facts, I would have objected," he added.

But Mr. Barclay and other regulators denied that Mr. Fail was given preferential treatment in the transaction because of White House ties to Mr. Fail's attorney, Robert Thompson, a lobbyist. Danny Wall, the former top S&L regulator, testified yesterday that any hint of political pressure "is totally absurd."

The sale of 15 Texas thrifts, which were merged into Bluebonnet Savings Bank, is the subject of a lengthy inquiry by Sen. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio) and a panel of the Senate Judiciary Committee. The Bluebonnet sale occurred in an end-of-year rush in 1988, in which dozens of insolvent thrifts were sold to buyers who reaped billions of dollars in tax benefits and government assistance in compensation for the poor thrift investments.

The Bluebonnet case has attracted particular scrutiny because Mr. Fail contributed just $1,000 of his own capital to acquire the S&Ls, while the U.S. pledged $1.8 billion in assistance.

Mr. Thompson, under subpoena by the subcommittee, refused to appear, contending that the committee lacks the authority to investigate the transaction and that the committee staff disclosed private financial information to a reporter. Mr. Thompson's attorney said he would fight the subpoena in court.

Sen. Metzenbaum has charged that Mr. Fail was able to win regulatory approval even though a competing bid would have cost the government $91 million less. "This all would appear as though Mr. Thompson had the inside track," said Sen. Metzenbaum.

But the former regulators said that the competing bid flopped on its merits. "It was a novel, cockamamie idea," said Stuart Root, former executive director of the thrift agency. He argued that the competing bid would have selectively pooled assets and tax breaks of several thrifts under the supervision of a limited partnership, which isn't permitted under thrift regulations. "The proposal was not feasible," said Mr. Root.

The regulators also defended the 1988 sales, overall, saying they had little choice but to offer lucrative long-term assistance in order to attract buyers because the savings-and-loan insurance fund was low on cash in the short term. "They weren't brilliant, or strokes of genius, but they were responsive to the marketplace," said Mr. Wall. "We had no alternative."
