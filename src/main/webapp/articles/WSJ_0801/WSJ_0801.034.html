Xerox Profit Declined 27% In 2nd Quarter

  • Author: Lawrence Hooper
  • Date: 08/01/90

Xerox Corp. said profit dropped 27% in the second quarter, but the company produced some encouraging results in both its major business areas. Most of the profit lag came from two factors, Xerox said: Austerity measures imposed by the government of Brazil, where the company sells a lot of copiers, and lower capital gains at its Crum & Forster Inc. insurance subsidiary.

Analysts focused, however, on indicators they consider more fundamental to the company's financial health, such as gross margins, expense control and Crum & Forster's "combined ratio," a measure of the insurer's financial state -- all of which moved in the right direction.

Excluding the Brazilian results, Xerox's profit from office equipment advanced a robust 19% from the year-ago period. And Xerox signaled that the company's recent disastrous performance in financial services -- the business contributed only $2 million in profit during the quarter, a 96% drop -- may finally be bottoming out.

"There are some things going on here that could make Xerox look attractive," said Eugene Glazer of Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. "I'm not yet convinced, but they seem to be doing a lot of the right things."

For the quarter, Xerox reported net income of $130 million, or $1.16 a share, compared with $179 million, or $1.67 a share, in the year-ago period. Revenue rose 3%, to $4.52 billion from $4.38 billion.

Those results came in below analysts' predictions, which averaged about $1.25 a share, but estimates were so scattershot and unsure that the disappointment wasn't meaningful. Xerox's much-battered stock took a 25-cent drop at the start of trading, but recovered to close at $45.625, up 50 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

"I think this stock is as burned out as it's going to get," said Alex Henderson of Prudential-Bache Securities Inc., who raised his rating on Xerox to a "buy" yesterday. "There were some very nice numbers this quarter, and I'm optimistic about the second half."

That optimism was shared by most Xerox watchers, but tempered by Xerox's poor track record in recent years, sluggish revenue growth and leeriness about a major new-product push slated for this fall.

Peter Enderlin of Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. said he cut his estimate of Xerox's 1990 earnings to $4.90 from $5.10, excluding write-offs, and is maintaining his "hold" rating. Still, he has started advising clients to buy the stock "on weakness."

But Daniel Mandresh of Merrill Lynch & Co., who predicts Xerox will earn between $4 and $4.50 this year, remains unconvinced. "I'm absolutely impressed by how Xerox has controlled its expenses, but I look at the top line first," he said. "I don't see any reason to adjust my thinking yet."

For the first half, Xerox posted a loss of $124 million, or $1.77 a share, compared with a profit of $337 million, or $3.14 a share, in 1989. The 1990 results include a first-quarter write-off of $375 million for bad real estate investments. Revenue rose 3%, to $8.77 billion from $8.52 billion.
