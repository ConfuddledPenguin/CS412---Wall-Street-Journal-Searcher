Politics & Policy: Senate Votes to Ban Forming of PACs By Foreign Firms

  • Author: Jill Abramson
  • Date: 08/01/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate approved an amendment to the campaign-financing bill that would prohibit foreign-owned companies in the U.S. from forming political action committees. The amendment, sponsored by Sen. Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas), a critic of increasing foreign investment and influence in this country, would outlaw PACs operated by companies that are more than 50% foreign-owned. It was approved by a vote of 73-27 during the Senate's second full day of debate on legislation to overhaul the financing of federal elections.

Senate Democrats and Republicans have both embraced a total ban on PACs, but are worried that such a stringent step might not withstand constitutional challenge. The Bentsen amendment would apply only if such an outright ban were ruled unconstitutional.

Federal law prohibits foreign nationals and corporations from directly contributing money to U.S. political candidates. Since 1978, through a series of advisory opinions, the Federal Election Commission has said that as long as a company is incorporated in the U.S, it isn't a foreign entity and may sponsor a PAC if it follows certain rules.

In June, however, the commission reversed course and voted to open a rule-making proceeding to prohibit PAC sponsorship by U.S. corporations owned or controlled by foreign interests. That could lead to regulations that would be almost identical to the amendment passed by the Senate. A similar amendment will probably be introduced when the House takes up campaign-finance legislation later this week.

Sen. Bentsen said his amendment would "limit what has been a rapidly growing source of foreign influence on domestic American politics." He cited a recent study done for his office by the Congressional Research Service that showed PACs of companies with significant foreign ownership gave $2.8 million to federal candidates in the 1987-88 election cycle, representing 5% of the total given by all corporate PACs.

Opponents of the amendment said it discriminated against U.S. citizens who happen to work for companies that are acquired by foreign conglomerates. "The effect will be to create two classes of Americans," warned Sen. Mitch McConnell (R. Ky).
