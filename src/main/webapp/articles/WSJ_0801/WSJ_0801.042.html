Chrysler's Net Plunged 47% In 2nd Quarter --- Cost-Cutting and Strength Of Financial Subsidiary Helped to Prevent a Loss

  • Author: Bradley A. Stertz
  • Date: 08/01/90

HIGHLAND PARK, Mich. -- Chrysler Corp. said second-quarter earnings skidded 47% from a year ago, and would have plunged into a loss without aggressive cost-cutting and surging results from financial services. The No. 3 U.S. auto maker said it had net income of $180 million, or 81 cents a share, in the period. That compares with net of $341 million, or $1.46 a share, a year earlier.

Chrysler's second-quarter 1990 results included one-time charges of $22 million for canceling plans to build a small Jeep with French auto maker Regie Nationale des Usines Renault, and $13 million paid to some union employees who took early retirement. Analysts said the charges cut Chrysler's second-quarter earnings by about 10 cents a share.

The 1989 second-quarter earnings included a gain of $8 million, or four cents a share, from Gulfstream Aerospace Corp., a unit Chrysler sold earlier this year.

Chrysler's second-quarter profit, while paper thin, showed that the company's efforts to eliminate $1.5 billion in operating costs are bearing fruit. Chrysler trimmed $250 million from second-quarter pretax operating costs compared with a year ago, while pretax savings in the first six months of the year reached $470 million. These figures are slightly ahead of the company's goals.

Meanwhile, net income at the company's Chrysler Financial Corp. subsidiary leaped 49% to a second-quarter record of $85 million. Chrysler Financial attributed the increase to higher interest-rate margins and gains from the sale of receivables.

Without the cost-cutting success and the record contribution from financial services, Chrysler would have shown an after-tax loss of $55 million, analysts said.

That's largely because the company sold fewer cars and trucks while spending substantially more on buyer incentives for each sale.

Jerome B. York, Chrysler's executive vice president, finance, said the company spent an average of $1,200 per vehicle on incentives in the second quarter, compared to just $700 a year earlier. John A. Casesa, an analyst with Wertheim Schroder & Co. in New York, estimated the higher incentives cost Chrysler about $130 million, or 55 cents to 60 cents a share.

Despite this extra spending, Chrysler pushed about 110,000 fewer vehicles out the doors of dealer showrooms, a decline of 16%. Chrysler sold 585,615 vehicles in the quarter compared with 695,552 a year earlier.

The auto maker blamed one-fourth of that decline on the loss of the Dodge Omni and Plymouth Horizon subcompact cars. Chrysler stopped making those aged models in January after their sales had fallen by more than 60%.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Chrysler stock closed unchanged at $15.125.

Although Chrysler finished the first six months of the year with a negative cash flow of about $400 million, it strengthened its actual cash holdings. Chrysler did this by selling off its Gulfstream unit and by completing the $1.1 billion sale and leaseback of the Chrysler Technology Center still under construction in Auburn Hills, Mich.

As a result of those deals, Chrysler had $4.3 billion in cash and marketable securities on June 30, compared with $3.4 billion a year earlier and $2.1 billion at the end of last year. Mr. York told analysts in a meeting yesterday that Chrysler can raise an additional $2.5 billion by selling "assets not related to the automotive business" and $1.6 billion by not funding its pensions in the next two years.

Chrysler has a total unfunded pension liability of $2.8 billion. But Mr. York told analysts that Chrysler has been whittling down the balance, and thus could skip its planned pension-fund payments of $800 million a year for 1990 and 1991 if that becomes necessary. But he described that as a "worst-case scenario" and added that Chrysler expects to fund its product-development programs without resorting to that step.

"It's a cloudy picture at Chrysler," said Mr. Casesa. "They're walking a tightrope there now, but they seem to be getting their finances in order to become a lean and mean organization like they were before" acquiring American Motors Corp. in 1987.

Chrysler's earnings fell within the range expected by industry analysts. But analysts said Mr. York hinted yesterday that the company expects a third-quarter loss larger than the consensus 53 cents-a-share loss predicted by analysts and closer to the worst estimate of a 79 cents-a-share loss.

Chrysler will suffer a third-quarter loss partly because it is closing the St. Louis and Windsor, Ontario, assembly plants that build its most profitable vehicles, minivans, for four weeks to retool for the 1991 models coming out this fall. Scott Merlis, an analyst with Morgan Stanley & Co., estimates Chrysler will lose about $177 million in after-tax profit from the lost minivan production alone.
