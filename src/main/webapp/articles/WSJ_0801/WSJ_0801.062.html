International: Ford Sells Farm-Gear Unit To Venture Headed by Fiat

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson and Guy Collins
  • Date: 08/01/90

Ford Motor Co., in its second major divestiture move in a week, said it will sell its New Holland farm equipment subsidiary to a joint venture headed by Fiat S.p.A. of Italy. The agreement, announced yesterday, calls for Fiat and Ford to merge their tractor, farm and industrial equipment businesses into a new company that will be 80%-owned by Fiat. Terms were not disclosed, but analysts said Ford is likely to receive more than the $330 million that it paid for the New Holland operations in 1987.

The New Holland deal, in the works since spring, comes just a week after Ford announced it will sell its aerospace operations to Loral Corp. The two sales mark an about-face for Ford, which acquired New Holland three years ago as part of an aggressive diversification strategy.

But earlier this year, Ford decided to pare its operations to automotive and financial services. The New Holland agreement virtually completes Ford's divestiture program, and will give the auto maker additional cash to help finance a big capital spending push in its auto operations.

For Fiat, the Milan auto and industrial products maker, the New Holland acquisition positions it to become one of the three biggest farm equipment makers in the world.

Fiat will pay Ford an undisclosed amount of cash and will also have an option to buy Ford's remaining 20% stake in New Holland within four years. Ford officials said they expect Fiat to exercise that option.

Ford is looking to bolster its cash reserves to finance heavy investments in its automotive business. The No. 2 auto maker boosted its capital spending during the first half of this year to $3.3 billion, up 26% from a year ago. Much of that money is being spent for new engines and transmissions, and on the recent acquisition of Jaguar PLC.

Ford and Fiat said yesterday they are continuing separate talks related to a possible joint venture involving commercial trucks.

As part of yesterday's agreement, Fiat will get a Ford New Holland diesel engine plant at Basildon, England, which currently produces 65,000 truck and tractor engines a year, and has a capacity of more than 100,000 engines a year. Fiat said it had sought additional engine-making capacity in the separate truck negotiations with Ford.

The link-up marks a giant step forward for Fiat in the buoyant U.S. tractor market, where its annual sales of 1,500 tractors, barely 1% of the market, compare with Ford New Holland's 26,500, a 22% market share. The combined sales will make the new company one of the big three U.S. suppliers, along with Deere & Co. and the JI Case unit of Tenneco Inc.

Fiat currently builds farm implements in the U.S. in joint ventures with Case, and builds industrial machinery in a European joint venture with Deere. Fiat doesn't expect those to change.

"We have reason to believe that our existing joint ventures can co-exist with this agreement," a Fiat spokesman said.

The new company will combine Fiat's farm and construction machinery subsidiary FiatGeotech with Ford's New Holland agricultural-equipment division. The units will retain their separate market and product identities.

Last year, New Holland and FiatGeotech had combined sales of 95,000 tractors and a further 30,000 tractors produced in joint ventures or under license. Sales of other agricultural and industrial products totalled 54,000 units.

Ford New Holland, with revenue of $2.8 billion last year and close to 18,000 employees, is slightly larger than FiatGeotech, which had sales of $2.3 billion and 13,000 employees. FiatGeotech has plants in Italy, Brazil, France and the U.S., while the Ford unit, based in New Holland, Pa. has plants and offices in the U.S., Canada, Britain, Belgium and Brazil.

Fiat officials said FiatGeotech and Ford New Holland had complementary markets. While Fiat is the market leader in Europe for tractors, with close to 16%, or 33,500 tractors, much of its strength is in Italy, where it accounts for 38% of the market. Ford New Holland has 9% of the European market, equivalent to 16,500 tractors.

Fiat said it will name the management and the headquarters location for the joint venture after the transaction is finalized. Ford officials said they were uncertain how long it would take to reach a definitive agreement and to obtain governmental approvals.

The merger isn't the first time that Ford and Fiat have sought to work together. In the mid-1980s, talks on combining their car operations in Europe took place but ultimately led nowhere. However, for the past four years the two companies have successfully run a heavy-truck joint venture in Britain.
