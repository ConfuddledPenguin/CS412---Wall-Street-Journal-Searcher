House Passes, 424-4, Bill to Stimulate Pursuit and Punishment of S&L Crime

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 08/01/90

WASHINGTON -- The House, anticipating voter anger over the expanding savings-and-loan mess, overwhelmingly approved legislation aimed at invigorating the investigation and prosecution of thrift-related crime. The bill, which passed 424-4, would increase maximum prison terms for S&L offenses and would make it a federal crime to conceal assets from the receiver of a failed thrift or to obstruct a government examiner. The legislation also would authorize greatly increased funding over the next three years to hire hundreds of additional investigators and prosecutors specifically to handle fraud cases involving financial institutions.

The bill includes provisions that would vastly expand the government's civil authority to freeze or take over assets of those suspected of S&L fraud.

Focusing on the imminent summer recess and election-year constituent meetings, representative after representative rose to declare that the bill would help "put the S&L crooks in jail."

Rep. Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.), who caught some of his colleagues by surprise when he hastily introduced the measure under a suspension of normal House procedures, called it "an effective blueprint" to "bring S&L criminals to justice."

In fact, none of the bill's criminal provisions would apply retroactively. That means that the increased prison terms and new categories of federal crimes couldn't be used against anyone associated with the S&L debacle of the 1980s. Moreover, the bill would create redundant criminal statutes covering acts already prosecutable under existing law, according to Justice Department officials and defense lawyers.

Rep. Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas), one of the four dissenters in the vote, complained that the measure hadn't been sufficiently debated and was being rushed through the House because of "election-year fears." Lawmakers, he contended, "are very zealously eager to prove that we want to get these crooks in jail."

The Senate has approved S&L-related enforcement provisions with goals similar to those of the House bill but some differences in the mechanics. The Bush administration has backed some of the congressional proposals but strongly opposes a provision in the House bill that would create a bipartisan investigative commission to study the causes of the thrift crisis.
