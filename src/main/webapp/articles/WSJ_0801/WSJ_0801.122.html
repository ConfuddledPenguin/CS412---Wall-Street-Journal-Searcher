Saturday Scramble At Virginia S&L Spurs a Takeover --- Thrift Regulators Shut Down United Savings Bank After Run on Deposits

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 08/01/90

WASHINGTON -- In an unusual midweek takeover, thrift regulators seized United Savings Bank, after the Vienna, Va., thrift, with $415 million in assets, experienced an old-fashioned run on its deposits. Following United's report last week that it posted losses of $11.5 million for the first six months of 1990, nervous depositors lined up and withdrew $13 million from the thrift on Saturday, industry sources said.

"United was not likely to have been able to meet the demands of its depositors or to pay its obligations in the normal course of business," a statement from the Office of Thrift Supervision said.

United will operate under the management of the Resolution Trust Corp., which will seek a buyer. All deposits at United are insured up to $100,000, and government officials dismissed the possibility of runs at other institutions.

Thrift regulators waited until 4:15 p.m. yesterday to shut the doors at United's offices.

"Keep in mind, people around here may still relate to the Maryland situation, where a state insurance fund ran out of money," in the mid-1980s, a spokesman for the thrift office said. In that case, some depositors had funds tied up for weeks or months, while the mess was sorted out.

In the federal thrift cleanup so far, government officials say, depositors have received their funds in three to four days even when an institution is liquidated. The far more common practice is for the thrift to close over a weekend and reopen Monday under a different name; depositors never lose access to their funds.

The takeover also was another signal that the Washington-area real estate market, once considered invulnerable to recession, is being hit by a slump, particularly in the commercial sector. United's rapid-growth strategy was fueled by heavy commercial real estate lending, the thrift office said.

The thrift office said the thrift was operating in an unsafe and unsound condition, failed all three capital requirements under the S&L cleanup law, and relied heavily on expensive brokered funds for its investments. United has operated under strict federal supervision for 15 months, but as of June 30, it classified nearly one quarter of its assets as nonearning, troubled or losses.

Shareholders will retain no interest in the institution under government management. It was previously traded over the counter.
