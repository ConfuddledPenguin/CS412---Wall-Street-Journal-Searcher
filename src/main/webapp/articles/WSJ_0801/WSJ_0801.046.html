Technology & Medicine: German Study Finds Eye `Sculpturing' With Lasers Helps Fix Near-Sightedness

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 08/01/90

German ophthalmologists reported they had successfully corrected near-sightedness in 10 of 13 patients by "sculpturing" their eyes with an American-made laser. The eye researchers called the eye sculpturing a success if the patient was able to see without eye glasses at least as well as he or she did with glasses before the laser treatment.

The German scientists, from the Charlottenburg Clinic in Berlin, are the first to report the results of laser eye sculpturing in near-sighted patients although there have been previous reports of tests of the technique in blind patients. They reported the results in the May/June issue of Refractive & Corneal Surgery, an American medical journal.

Copies of the medical journal article were distributed to reporters by Summit Technology Inc., Watertown, Mass., which made the ultraviolet excimer laser used at the Berlin clinic. Summit is in a race with two other small U.S. laser makers to bring to market excimer lasers that can correct near-sightedness and far-sightedness. The two competitors, Taunton Technologies Inc. in Monroe, Conn., and VisX in Sunnyvale, Calif., recently agreed to end a protracted patent dispute and to merge.

The lasers are designed to reshape the cornea of the eye in a fraction of a second, flattening it to correct near-sightedness or increasing the curvature to correct farsightedness. To do this, the lasers can almost instantly vaporize layers of the cornea less than a millionth of an inch thick.

Theo Seiler and his colleagues at the Berlin clinic reported the results of laser sculpturing on eight women and five men, aged 26 to 48 years, who had varying degrees of near-sightedness. They said the results six to eight months after treatment were equal to or better than those achieved with radial keratotomy, in which the cornea is surgically flattened by cutting tiny radial slits around the edges of the cornea.

Six months after treatment the degree of near-sightedness was reduced in all 13 patients, but in three the correction fell short of the correction afforded by eye glasses. This undercorrection was deliberate in two of these patients who decided not to risk an overcorrection that would have made them somewhat far-sighted. Hence, in only one patient did the technique fall short of expectations.

All the patients had an ache and a feeling of a foreign body in the treated eye for 24 to 36 hours after the laser treatment, the researchers reported. Glare was a common problem for about two weeks afterwards and two patients reported having "ghost images" in their eyes for about two weeks.

The eye doctors said they could see a haze in the eyes of the treated patients during the first three months after treatment but the haze gradually cleared up in the subsequent three months.

The main problem was predicting the degree of correction. The researchers deliberately sculptured the corneas to produce initially a degree of far-sightedness. As expected, the corneas moved toward near-sightedness as they healed. The rate at which this happened was slowed by medications to prevent scarring. In three cases, the medication was stopped to permit sufficient compensation for the deliberate overcorrection, a move that proved successful.

"In the present series of eyes, the predictability {of the degree of correction} was good but not excellent," Dr. Seiler and his associates reported. Results should improve with experience, they said.
