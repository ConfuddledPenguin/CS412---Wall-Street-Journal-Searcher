Politics & Policy: Bush, Democrats Seek to Set Dates For Deficit Votes

  • Author: David Wessel and Michel McQueen
  • Date: 08/01/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush and top congressional Democrats, surrendering any hope of reaching a deficit-reduction accord in August, are inching toward agreement to try to negotiate a pact immediately after Labor Day. A White House official said President Bush and top congressional Democrats yesterday tentatively agreed to set firm dates in September for floor votes on an agreement, even though they haven't reached one. But some Democratic budget negotiators are resisting what Sen. Wyche Fowler (D., Ga.) termed making any "agreement to agree" on spending cuts and tax increases.

House Budget Committee Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) said budget negotiators are talking about an ambitious schedule of resuming talks immediately after Labor Day with a goal of reaching an accord by Sept. 10, the day the rest of the Congress returns from its summer recess. The goal would be to get Congress to enact the agreement by Oct. 1.

Mr. Bush and congressional Republicans want the entire Congress to ratify that timetable, but a parliamentary mechanism for that hasn't been agreed to yet.

Preoccupied with tactics, schedules and the pending August recess, budget negotiators have spent little time in the past week talking about the substance of what spending to cut or what taxes to increase. Nevertheless, the White House so far has displayed no sign that Mr. Bush has accepted advice from some congressional Republicans that he criticize Democrats for not putting forward a deficit-reduction proposal of their own.

But a White House official said that Mr. Bush and top Democrats have agreed to "break the code of silence" that was supposed to surround the talks. The new posture would allow both sides to "debate publicly the merits of ideas," and that could open the door to more partisan debate in the next few weeks.

Mr. Bush invited congressional Republicans to breakfast this morning, and some of them expect the president to begin criticizing the Democrats soon after that.

Republican budget negotiators continue to insist that Rep. Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.), the House majority leader, indicated to budget negotiators on July 24 that Democrats would present a deficit-reduction plan of their own within a few days. Democrats deny that he made any such promise. "I don't think there was any reneging," Rep. Gephardt said. "There may have been some miscommunications about what was to happen."
