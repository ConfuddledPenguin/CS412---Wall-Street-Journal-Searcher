Some Southwestern Real Estate Is, Unfortunately, a Liquid Asset

  • Author: Junda Woo
  • Date: 08/01/90

Along the Red River, people always knew where they stood; north was Oklahoma, south was Texas. But soon, that may not be so.

The problem is that the river is a wanderer, shifting course every time it floods -- which it often does.

When the river overflowed in 1987, for instance, its shifting course took 140 acres belonging to Thomas Henderson, a Byers, Texas, farmer, and moved it to Oklahoma from Texas. With the border relocated, the land no longer belonged to Mr. Henderson. His Oklahoma neighbors filed a lawsuit to claim it, but the courts, reviewing deeds and historical records, handed it instead to the U.S. Bureau of Land Management, which owned land nearby.

The imbroglio didn't matter much to Mr. Henderson's bank, which didn't see fit to excise the land from Mr. Henderson's loan. He pays the bank $13,000 a year for those vanished acres. "It's really kind of a joke," the beleaguered Mr. Henderson says. "If you weren't financially involved, you'd have to laugh at it, really."

But now the Bureau of Land Management is pushing a plan to set a fixed border that would ignore the shifting river. Instead, the line would be determined by latitudes and longitudes. The plan requires the support of both states and approval by the U.S. Congress. Next week, a committee of Oklahoma lawmakers is scheduled to begin discussing the issue.

Farmers seem pleased with the plan. "Wherever they put it is fine with me, so long as it's set," says Charles McCullough, a cattle rancher in Devol, Okla., who at various times has owned as few as 450 acres and as many as 600 acres on the north side of the river.

Past efforts to set a permanent border have been easier said than done. Since 1819, the two states have considered the river's south bank the border. Unfortunately, both Texas and Oklahoma have used sharply different surveying methods, with nobody agreeing on the exact location of the south bank.

In 1987, Texas thought it had a solution. It proudly marked off the south bank with stakes to show officials from both states. When unveiled the next day, however, the stakes were about 100 feet off the mark -- and under water. During the night, the fickle river had shifted again.

"It was a pretty graphic demonstration," says Paul Tanner, area manager for the Bureau of Land Management.
