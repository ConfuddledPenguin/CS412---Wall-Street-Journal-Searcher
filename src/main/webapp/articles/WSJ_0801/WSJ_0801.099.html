Politics & Policy: Valdez Decision Spreads Blame For Alaska Spill --- Safety Board Cites Exxon, Skipper, His Third Mate, Coast Guard and Officials

  • Author: Laurie McGinley
  • Date: 08/01/90

WASHINGTON -- The National Transportation Safety Board -- spreading around blame for the nation's worst oil spill -- cited an array of failures by the Exxon Valdez's captain, his third mate, Exxon Shipping Co., the Coast Guard, and state and local authorities. The board's findings, which were unanimous, end a 16-month investigation into the March 24, 1989, accident in which a 900-foot tanker smashed into Bligh Reef near the Alaskan port of Valdez, rupturing eight cargo tanks and spilling 258,000 barrels of oil into Prince William Sound. The oil caused extensive environmental damage and $2 billion has been spent so far to clean up the spill.

The board said the third mate, who was navigating the vessel at the time of the grounding, didn't maneuver the ship properly "because of fatigue and excessive workload." It said his boss, Capt. Joseph Hazelwood, wasn't supervising because of his "impairment from alcohol." The captain left the bridge minutes before the third mate began a crucial right turn -- a turn in which improper execution resulted in the vessel hitting the jagged submerged reef.

The board said that other direct causes were the failure of Exxon to "provide a fit master and a rested and sufficient crew," the ineffectiveness of the Coast Guard's traffic-tracking service in the area and the lack of effective piloting services.

The board said that tests on blood samples drawn from Mr. Hazelwood 10 hours after the accident indicated his blood-alcohol content was high at the time the Valdez ran aground. But Mr. Hazelwood, who has a history of drunken-driving convictions, has steadfastly denied being drunk.

Yesterday, his attorney, Thomas Russo, said that the board's conclusions about his client's alcohol use were "ridiculous." He reiterated contentions that the blood samples taken from Mr. Hazelwood were mishandled by a California laboratory. But James Kolstad, chairman of the safety agency, dismissed those contentions, saying that the "samples weren't tampered with."

In March, an Anchorage jury convicted Mr. Hazelwood on the misdemeanor charge of negligent discharge of oil. But it acquitted him of charges of being under the influence of alcohol, reckless endangerment and felony criminal mischief. He is appealing his conviction.

Last week, Mr. Hazelwood's license was suspended for nine months after he pleaded no contest to charges of negligently leaving the tanker bridge and violating a ban on drinking under four hours before sailing. The Coast Guard dropped charges of drunkenness.

The safety board also criticized Exxon Shipping for failing to monitor Mr. Hazelwood's drinking problem, and said the Coast Guard did an inadequate job of tracking ships and icebergs in the area. The agency faulted Alaska's piloting rules, saying that in the case of the Valdez, the tanker shouldn't have been allowed near Bligh Reef unless a federally licensed pilot familiar with the waterway was aboard. Such a requirement is now in effect.

The safety board's findings aren't admissible in court. However, facts uncovered as part of the investigation can be admitted as evidence.
