Blinder Robinson Has Assets That Exceed Liabilities, Filing Says

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/01/90

ENGLEWOOD, Colo. -- The brokerage that penny-stock king Meyer Blinder once boasted would be bigger than Merrill Lynch, Pierce Fenner & Smith Inc. listed $17.9 million in assets and $13.4 million in debts in its Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law petition filed in Denver Monday. Although the brokerage claims more assets than debts, the company couldn't raise enough cash to meet immediate needs.

Blinder, Robinson & Co., a unit of Intercontinental Enterprises Inc., listed office-building leases and lawsuit judgments as its principal unsecured debts. Even in its demise, the Blinder Robinson was controversial. "It's evident that the filing is a maneuver to default on a $5.8 million judgment I have against the firm," said Denver attorney Steve A. Miller. "I intend to pursue this."

Mr. Miller's client, Donald L. Walford, won the judgment in a malicious prosecution suit against the firm, which disputes the claim.

In filings with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Blinder Robinson said it held for customers $70 million in securities and $4.8 million in cash, and had 250,000 accounts. Blinder Robinson said it ceased securities transactions before it filed.

Blinder also said it expected to transfer its accounts to another brokerage. The company had not disclosed the identity of the brokerage and did not return repeated telephone calls. The Securities Investor Protection Corp. insures the accounts to a maximum $100,000 in cash and $500,000 in cash and securities. A SIPC attorney said the insurance firm was "gathering information."

Operating problems were the immediate threat to Blinder Robinson. From its peak of 1,800 salesmen in offices around the world, the brokerage had dwindled to 43 offices and perhaps 300 salesmen. Its parent reported losses of $553,000 in the quarter ended April 27 on revenue of $9.5 million.
