One Bancorp Posts Loss for 2nd Quarter, Ends Stock Offer Plan

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/01/90

PORTLAND, Maine -- One Bancorp reported a second-quarter net loss of $29.8 million, scuttled a preferred stock offering and said it hasn't had acceptable offers for Connecticut and Massachusetts subsidiaries that it had agreed to sell in a pact with regulators. The second-quarter results include a $15.5 million provision for loan losses, loss of interest income on bad commercial loans and an adjustment of $6.2 million to the company's investment in Southstate Bank for Savings, which has been offered for sale, the company said.

The $29.8 million loss, equal to $3.63 a share, compares with a loss of $10.5 million, or $1.28 a share, in the 1989 second quarter.

One Bancorp's nonperforming assets rose during the second quarter by $7 million to $297 million, a sign that growth of nonperforming assets is stabilizing, the company said. First-quarter additions to nonperforming assets were $15 million, following an average increase of $40 million a quarter for all of 1989.

In partial accordance with the plan, the company said it reduced its total assets to $2.2 billion at June 30, down from $2.3 billion at year-end. The company must still sell its Bank of Hartford and Southstate units, retire $36 million of convertible debentures, restructure nonperforming assets and sell foreclosed real estate.

One Bancorp said it is committed to injecting additional funds into its Maine Savings unit, but that the ability to make these required capital infusions "is dependent entirely" on the sale of the Connecticut and Massachusetts units. The company said it hasn't been able to get the aggregate selling price of $55 million for the two units and that appraisals indicate that the fair market value of the units is now $42.2 million, less than called for in the plan.

Vincent E. Furey, president and chief executive, said in a statement that preservation of any value in the holding company's securities will not be possible unless it can substantially reduce its nonperforming assets and restore Maine Savings capital to required levels in a time frame acceptable to savings regulators.

He said, however, that it is "highly unlikely, given current market conditions, that the company will be able to satisfy all of the requirements of the capital plan" signed in January with regulators. The company's ability to continue as a going concern "will therefore depend upon the level of progress it can show in meeting the goals of the capital plan and the willingness of its regulators to grant the holding company additional time."

In national over-the-counter trading yesterday, One Bancorp closed at 44 cents a share, down 12.5 cents.
