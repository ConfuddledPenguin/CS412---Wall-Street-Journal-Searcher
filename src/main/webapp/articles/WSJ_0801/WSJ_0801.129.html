Official Says Canada Opposes Varity Plan To Incorporate in U.S.

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/01/90

OTTAWA -- The Canadian government is opposed to Varity Corp.'s plan to reincorporate in the U.S., said a senior official of Canada's Department of Industry, Science and Technology. William Terry, director of machinery in the department, said Varity undertook to maintain its head office in Ontario and to remain a Canadian corporation in a 1986 agreement with the federal government and the province of Ontario. As part of that agreement, the two governments invested 200 million Canadian dollars (US$173.5 million) in Varity to help it stave off bankruptcy-law proceedings.

Victor Rice, chairman and chief executive officer of Varity, told the company's annual meeting Monday that he plans to seek shareholder approval to reincorporate the industrial concern as a Delaware corporation and simultaneously conduct a 1-for-10 share consolidation. He said after the meeting that the company's legal advisers believe that a move to the U.S. doesn't violate any covenants with the government.

"We expect Varity to live up to {its} legal obligation" to remain a Canadian concern, Mr. Terry said. He said Canadian government officials have met with Varity executives to discuss the planned move, but that the talks haven't changed the government's position.

If Varity proceeds with the plan, Mr. Terry said the government "would have to consider the full range of alternatives open to us."

One alternative could be to vote against the proposal at a shareholders meeting. After injecting capital into Varity, the provincial and federal governments received all the Class II preferred shares of Varity. According to Varity officials, the reincorporation and share-consolidation measures require approval of at least two-thirds of each class of shares.

Mr. Rice, of Varity, has said that it would be difficult for the company to implement a share consolidation as a Canadian corporation. Under Toronto Stock Exchange regulations, a company conducting a share consolidation must change its name. Mr. Rice said that isn't very feasible for Varity, which changed its name from Massey-Ferguson four years ago.

A spokesman for the Toronto Stock Exchange said an exemption from the regulation can be granted to a non-Canadian company when most of its shares are traded on foreign exchanges.
