International: For Soviet Citizens, `Hard' Currencies Are Finally Legal

  • Author: Elisabeth Rubinfien
  • Date: 08/01/90

MOSCOW -- Bowing to the inevitable, the government is officially condoning the dollar-based economy that has burgeoned in the Soviet Union in the past year. Starting today, Soviets will be allowed to spend and save convertible currency without having to state the source of their income. The government plans to open a network of stores that will sell imported and high-quality domestic goods to individuals and organizations for hard currency.

The ruble isn't a convertible currency, but the Soviet Union requires foreigners to pay hard currency for anything from hotel rooms to utilities according to so-called "hard-currency ruble" prices. Prices are stated in hard-currency rubles on anything foreigners might buy, and payment is made in hard currency.

The hard-currency ruble doesn't exist in any tangible form, but the government sets its value at about $1.65. (The more realistic tourist exchange rate introduced last year is six rubles to the dollar.)

The new system marks the reversal of a decades-old policy under which it has been illegal for Soviets to earn or spend convertible currency in their own country. Now the government wants to draw in what it estimates to be hundreds of millions of dollars of hard currency, and wants it to be spent at home. To do so, it is tacitly acknowledging that a hard-currency economy operating in parallel to the ruble economy has grown rapidly in the past year.

"It's just the latest case in which the weakness of the Soviet ruble has been demonstrated," said an article titled "Ruble, Our Poor Relative," last week in Komsomolskaya Pravda, the nation's largest circulation daily.

It still isn't clear exactly how the new system will operate. For one thing, although the government decree goes into effect today, it doesn't address the fact that the law making the holding of foreign currency illegal hasn't been changed.

Many economists and bankers oppose the move, fearing it will undermine the already battered ruble. "I am afraid," said Viktor Gerashchenko, chairman of the U.S.S.R. State Bank. "It is the wrong move in the long run because people first of all should be motivated here to use the local currency."

For years, only people who had worked overseas, had foreign relatives, or dared to deal in the black market had access to hard currency. Those who acquired their dollars legally could open bank accounts in the Soviet Union, but could only withdraw money if they presented an exit visa as proof that they needed the money for travel abroad.

That system still exists, but control over it has eroded rapidly in the past several years. The increase in Soviets traveling overseas and the opening of joint ventures and cooperative businesses aimed at foreigners in the Soviet Union has opened the floodgates to hard currency. And as shops are nearly bare of things to buy for rubles, more people are demanding payment in dollars, marks or pounds.
