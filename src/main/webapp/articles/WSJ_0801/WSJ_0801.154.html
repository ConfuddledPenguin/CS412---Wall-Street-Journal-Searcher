Japanese Securities Firms Told to Pay Penalty Taxes

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/01/90

TOKYO -- Some top Japanese securities companies said they received instructions from the Tokyo Regional Taxation Bureau to pay penalty taxes for not reporting what the bureau calls compensation paid to major clients for losses incurred from the "Black Monday," global, stock market plunge. "Big Four" firms Yamaichi Securities Co. Ltd., Nippon Kangyo Kakumaru Securities Co. Ltd. and New Japan Securities Co. Ltd. were among 14 securities companies that were reportedly ordered to pay a total of 7.5 billion yen ($50.7 million) in penalty taxes for a total of 16.5 billion yen that was viewed as compensation.

When major institutional and individual clients suffered huge losses in the stock market crash, the securities companies allegedly compensated their clients by selling convertible bonds, warrants and government bonds at discounted prices and later buying them back at higher prices.

Each company had supposedly reported costs of such transactions as losses caused by ordinary equity transactions. But the tax agency identified the reported deals as compensation, which should have been reported as entertainment expenses or donations, subject to taxation.
