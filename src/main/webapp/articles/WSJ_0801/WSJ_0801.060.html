Who's News: Davis Is Named New President Of Taj Mahal

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 08/01/90

I.G. "Jack" Davis Jr., an experienced casino executive who ran Resorts International for 29 years, was named president of the Trump Taj Mahal casino. Mr. Davis is the fourth individual named to the position within the past year. He will also serve as the facility's chief operating officer. The move was announced by Edward M. Tracy, the president of Donald Trump's casino operations.

The new position marks a return of sorts for the 65-year-old Mr. Davis, who helped conceive the Taj Mahal project when it was launched by Resorts International a decade ago. Mr. Davis's relationship with Mr. Trump dates back to 1987 when, as president of Resorts, he helped persuade the estate of the company's late founder to sell its stock to Mr. Trump. After gaining control of the company, Mr. Davis confirmed, Mr. Trump gave him a $1 million-a-year salary.

"Jack obviously knows a lot about the project," Mr. Tracy said.

Mr. Davis resigned from Resorts after the company was purchased by Merv Griffin in 1988, but he is believed to have remained friendly with Mr. Trump. "The casino business stays in your blood," he said. "I feel I have some unfinished business to take care of."

Mr. Davis will have his work cut out for him. The Taj Mahal, which opened to great fanfare on April 2, has not been the smash some casino analysts predicted, and appears to be a drain on Mr. Trump's already distressed holdings. In its first quarter of operations it had a net operating loss of about $15 million, according to people familiar with the casino. In July, the facility is expected to gross about $40 million, which would be a record month for any casino, but would also fall short of what the Taj must gross in the busy summer months to meet annual debt payments of about $100 million.

The Taj Mahal's disappointing performance has led Trump officials to prepare to propose an exchange offer for holders of $675 million in junk bonds that Mr. Trump sold to pay for the property. The offer, which could be announced by the end of August, would require bondholders to agree to exchange debt for equity in the casino.

Mr. Davis will succeed Willard "Bucky" Howard, who will become the Taj's executive vice president and concentrate on casino operations, Mr. Tracy said. Shortly after the Taj opened, Mr. Howard replaced Walter Haybert as president of the casino and hotel facility. Mr. Haybert had taken the place of Mark Etess, who died in a helicopter crash along with two other Trump Organization officials in October.

Mr. Tracy said he expected that Mr. Trump's bankers, who recently agreed to a restructuring of his $2 billion in bank debt, would be pleased with the decision to hire Mr. Davis. But, he added, they had not been consulted. For regulatory reasons, Mr. Trump's lenders have no say in day-to-day casino operations, Mr. Tracy said.
