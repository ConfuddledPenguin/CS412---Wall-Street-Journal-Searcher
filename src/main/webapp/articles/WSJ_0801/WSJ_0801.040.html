Letters to the Editor: Immigration Is No Open-and-Shut Case

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/01/90

In response to your July 3 editorial-page article "Through the Open Door: What Is It Like to Be an Immigrant in America?" Of the immigrants interviewed, eight were from other than an English-speaking country. Of these eight, five made comments worth reviewing for the reasons they came to America. These are shortened and excerpted as follows: 1. ". . . Hitler would invade Switzerland."

2. ". . . from Nazi Germany in 1937."

3. ". . . fleeing Castro and his communist thugs."

4. ". . . the Nazis broke into our house."

5. ". . . for the same reasons as everyone."

The thought occurred to me that these people's families had some place to come to in order to escape totalitarianism.

If America loses its freedoms, where would the immigrants of today go?

A.R. Patten Jr.

Arvada, Colo.

---

Your article hardly shows an awareness of, or sensitivity to, the broad spectrum of immigrants who make up the diversity of the U.S. population.

Contrary to your statement, virtually every American does not know where he or she comes "from." The largest part of the American black population, involuntary immigrants whose families were torn apart before and after they reached our shores, cannot trace where they come from (or can do so only with great difficulty and diligence).

I realize the thrust of your article was statements by 13 naturalized prominent Americans and was not meant to be representative. I respect your right to make an editorial judgment. However, why five Germans and three Canadians out of a group of 13 "immigrants"? (If you count the British-born Bob Hope and Swiss-born Madeleine Kunin, that is two more white Anglo-Saxon types in the top-heavy majority.) Where are the Asians, Italians and Latin Americans who contribute so heavily to the monetary and cultural wealth of our country? There must be a few who are "prominent."

Lucy Brody

Teaneck, N.J.

---

I was puzzled by your selection. Note the following breakdown:

-- Five German-born

-- Three Canadian-born

-- Three other European-born

-- Two Caribbean-born

-- All Judeo-Christians

No Oriental-born, no South American-born, no African-born. Also, no representatives of large immigration countries such as Ireland and Italy.

The professional breakdown also puzzles me:

-- Seven politicians

-- Two artists

-- Three academics

-- One business person

Are you saying America is the land of political, rather than business opportunity? That artists and capitalists are less relevant to our country's development?

Alain M. Breyton

Immigrant-Entrepreneur

Syracuse, N.Y.

---

May I point out that nearly every one of the immigrants in the article came to this country when the door was not quite so open, because certain standards of decency and selectivity were applied.

For the benefit of those too young to remember them, let me enumerate the three principal requirements for admission:

1) You had to prove that you would not become a public charge within the foreseeable future, either by being personally solvent or by obtaining an affidavit of support from a responsible and solvent person.

2) You had to give evidence of a clean criminal record in every country where you had resided before coming to the U.S.

3) You had to produce a medical certificate that you were not afflicted with any contagious or disabling disease.

In other words, we had an immigration policy in those days.

Paul G. Kafka

Bellevue, Wash.

---

The immigration experience also includes the African-Americans. We, too, know and remember that our ancestors were from a foreign land. We, too, have been transplanted, although not by choice. The only difference is that we were never entitled to a naturalization ceremony. Too bad you didn't choose some African-Americans to tell about their or their relatives' immigration experiences -- they would have a very different story. The weight of slavery's shackles will never leave our memories.

Mikiko Murakata

Neptune, N.J.
