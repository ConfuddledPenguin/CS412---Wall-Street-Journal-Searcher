EPA Threatens To Limit Driving In California

  • Author: Jim Herron Zamora
  • Date: 08/01/90

LOS ANGELES -- The Environmental Protection Agency said it may be forced to limit the days Southern California commuters can drive their cars, if existing state and local plans fail to cut smog by 86% by the end of the century. The federal agency's plan, which includes many anti-pollution measures approved more than a year ago by the regional South Coast Air Quality Management District and the California Air Resources Board, seeks to cut carbon monoxide emissions in this part of the state by 86% by the year 2004, with the same reduction in ozone emissions by 2010.

Daniel W. McGovern, EPA's western regional administrative director, said the agency "reluctantly" included guidelines for so-called no-drive days as a "backstop measure" for reducing carbon monoxide emissions if the state and local plans are unsuccessful by their own deadline, the year 2000. The no-drive plan would use odd or even numbers on auto license tags to alternate driving days.

"It is in the spirit of presenting all possibilities that we are presenting the no-drive day," Mr. McGovern said. "It is not one we are endorsing."

The EPA was forced to issue the plan as a result of a 1988 lawsuit by the California Coalition for Clean Air and the Sierra Club. An EPA official termed timing of the new plan "awkward" because state and local authorities were developing more stringent pollution plans for the Los Angeles area that may render the federal plan unnecessary.

Mr. McGovern said that since Congress and the president were revamping the federal Clean Air Act, the new plan soon may become obsolete and require another revision.

Environmentalists who brought the lawsuit called the EPA plan insurance that will help make the state and regional governments stick to their guns, although they noted that the federal plan takes longer to achieve the goals set by the state.

"We're disappointed that they did not adopt the state's timetable," said Jan Chatten-Brown, president of the California Coalition for Clean Air.

Most measures in the EPA plan don't take effect until after 1996, and then only if state and regional efforts have failed to reduce pollution sufficiently.

The federal proposals that could become effective before 1996 include winter and summer reductions in gasoline volatility, increases in the oxygen content in winter gasoline and enforcement of federal guidelines to reformulate gasoline to further reduce emissions.
