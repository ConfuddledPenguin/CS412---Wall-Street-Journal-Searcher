What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/01/90

A U.S. PANEL cleared the first human tests of gene therapy to fight disease. The National Institutes of Health committee approved the use of gene therapy to treat a fatal form of skin cancer and an inherited immune-system disorder that affects children. The panel's chairman said that the approvals move gene therapy "from abstract science to research and patient care." Scientists have traced some inherited disorders, including hemophilia and cystic fibrosis, to malfunctions by genes, which instruct cells to perform functions. As a result, researchers are focusing on therapies to replace missing genes or to enhance the genes' performance as a possible treatment for disease.

But gene therapy has attracted criticism from groups who worry that it could lead to tinkering with genetic structures to improve the human race.

AN OIL SPILL SPREAD off Texas, disrupting shipping and threatening wildlife.

The spill from a collision between a tanker and two barges in Galveston Bay blocked most vessels for the third day from the Houston Ship Channel, though some ships went through last night. The Coast Guard said only a few dead birds and fish were reported, but that more were expected as the oil moved ashore. Initially, Coast Guard officials said only about 50,000 gallons of oil spilled, but they later raised that to 500,000 gallons.

Local maritime shipping experts estimated that the shipping channel's closing is costing $1 million to $1.5 million a day to owners of idled ships.

A defense spending bill was approved by the House Armed Services panel that guts the Pentagon's top-priority strategic programs, shelves production of other weapons and cuts three times as many troops as Bush proposed. In clearing the $283 billion bill, the panel cut $24 billion from Bush's spending blueprint.

---

Scientists said they are on the trail of a gene that raises the risk of developing lung cancer, especially for smokers. Two research reports are the firmest proof yet that a susceptibility to lung cancer can be passed from generation to generation, and may explain why lung cancer is relatively common in some families.

---

Moslem rebels freed Trinidad's Prime Minister Robinson, but other hostages seized during Friday's coup attempt were still being held, according to Trinidad's attorney general. He refused to say what conditions led to Robinson's release. According to other officials, the premier had been shot in both legs and beaten during his captivity.

---

Federal investigators spread blame for the March 1989 Exxon Valdez oil spill off Alaska, criticizing the captain, his third mate, the Coast Guard and state and local authorities, as well as Exxon Shipping. The National Transportation Safety Board's findings ended a 16-month probe concerning the nation's worst spill.

---

Liberian government troops staged a counterattack on rebels fighting to oust Doe and recaptured parts of Monrovia. Meanwhile, the president vowed to "fight until the last soldiers in the Liberian army die." The offensive came a day after loyalist troops were accused of killing hundreds of civilian refugees in a Lutheran church.

---

A Senate subcommittee rejected fiscal 1991 funding sought by the Bush administration to establish a National Drug Intelligence Center under the Justice Department and drug czar Bennett. Instead, the Appropriations panel chose to apply the requested $46 million to expand an existing anti-drug program.

---

The Irish Republican Army said in a statement that it killed British lawmaker Ian Gow because of his friendship with Prime Minister Thatcher and his role in developing Britain's policy concerning Northern Ireland. Gow, 53, died Monday when a bomb exploded under his automobile.

---

Kuwaiti and Iraqi negotiators began talks in Saudi Arabia on their oil and border dispute, while Persian Gulf-based diplomats said Iraq had massed 100,000 troops on Kuwait's border. The diplomats said the Iraqi force, triple the number earlier reported, included about 300 Soviet-made tanks.

---

A federal jury in Los Angeles found a Mexican businessman guilty of racketeering, kidnapping and conspiracy in the 1985 death of U.S. drug agent Enrique Camarena. Ruben Zuno Arce, a brother-in-law of a former president of Mexico, was the third defendant found guilty in the trial.

---

Soviet citizens will be allowed, as of today, to spend and save convertible currency without stating the source of the income. The government also plans to open stores selling certain imported and domestic goods for hard currency.
