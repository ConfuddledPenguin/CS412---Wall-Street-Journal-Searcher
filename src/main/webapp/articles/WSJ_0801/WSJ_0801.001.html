Who's News: Edley of United Negro College Fund To Retire After 17 Years in Top Posts

  • Author: Leon E. Wynter
  • Date: 08/01/90

Christopher F. Edley, a Harvard trained lawyer who led the United Negro College Fund to become one of the nation's most successful black charities, will retire after 17 years as president and chief executive officer, the college fund said. Mr. Edley, 62 years old, cited a desire to rest and some health concerns as the reasons for his move. The college fund began a quiet national search for Mr. Edley's successor about two months ago, according to college fund chairman Joe Williams, who is chairman and chief executive officer of Warner Lambert Corp. Mr. Williams said the college fund is looking for a successor with "stature in the black community, stature in the corporate community, someone who can interface with the White House, someone who is dedicated to his race."

The New York-based college fund, which supports 41 private black colleges and universities, raised about $9.5 million in revenue annually when Mr. Edley took over in 1973. For the year ended March 30, the fund took in $48.5 million. Mr. Williams said Mr. Edley was also instrumental in securing a recent $50 million donation from publisher and philanthropist Walter H. Annenberg, the largest single donation in the fund's history.

Mr. Edley said the fund was "like a mom and pop kind of store that was getting bigger," when he came. "People were so busy waiting on customers that no strategic thoughts were being given." The year before Mr. Edley's arrival, the fund began its first national advertising campaign with the slogan, "A Mind is a Terrible Thing to Waste." The campaign and slogan, among the most successful in the history of public service advertising, gave the fund a national visibility previously unknown for a black charity.

But, Mr. Edley said, "People do not give money just because they see your ad on TV. Advertising helps soften people up." Mr. Edley said he had to focus the fund on creating a wide range of opportunities for people to actually give money after seeing an ad. Among the most successful of these efforts is an annual telethon and other special events closely associated with sports and entertainment celebrities.

The award-winning college fund ads have become the most requested of all public service spots handled by the Advertising Council, a nonprofit organization that produces and distributes such advertising using member advertising agencies. Cathy Mitchell, who handles the fund's campaign for the Advertising Council, said the ads succeed in part because they convey the basic appeal of the college fund's mission.

"I think that Chris {Edley} is quite a leader. He's directly involved with the advertising and he knows his organization. He knows his market and what he wants to convey," Ms. Mitchell said.

Mr. Edley is a former Philadelphia prosecutor who also served as counsel to what is now the federal Housing and Urban Development Department. He joined the college fund after a 10-year stint with the Ford Foundation. "Really, all my professional life has been heavily laden with the things that would advance the minority cause," Mr. Edley said. His son, Christopher Edley Jr., is a professor of law at Harvard and was a principal adviser to Massachusetts Gov. Michael Dukakis in his presidential bid.

The college fund, founded in 1944, has gained broad recognition in the market of sympathetic middle-class givers, which is about 60% white and 40% black, according to Ms. Mitchell. And the fund can point to a 13% increase in enrollment since 1986 at the four-year schools it supports. The 1988 gift of $20 million to Spelman College in Atlanta by entertainer Bill Cosby, the largest individual donation to a black college in history, heralded the next major focus for the college fund, Mr. Edley says: hitting up black millionaires.
