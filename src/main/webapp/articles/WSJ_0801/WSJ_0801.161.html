REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): A Reunified Korea?

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/01/90

It is tempting to read great significance into next month's proposed meeting between the prime ministers of North and South Korea, given the rarity of high-level contacts between the two Koreas in the violent 45 years of their separate existence. But while hope is a natural response, we have learned to be skeptical that such events, in themselves, offer promise of Korean reunification. Kim Il Sung probably is genuinely nervous about his godfathers in Moscow. Mikhail Gorbachev's meeting with South Korean President Roh Tae Woo in California earlier this year suggested that the Soviets are taking up with Kim's worst rival. Those fears have been fanned by Soviet-South Korean talks to normalize relations and trade overtures to Seoul from Beijing as well.

But Kim knows how to play symbolic games. His agreement to the prime minister get-together probably is another of his well-practiced exercises in dissemblance.

Something more substantive, a proposed temporary breach of the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ) to allow some direct contact between North and South Koreans, apparently won't happen. Both governments agreed in principle to a joint rally in the Panmunjom truce village August 15 to celebrate Liberation Day, which commemorates Korea's 1945 liberation from Japan. But in a last-minute spate of demands, gripes and accusations, North Korea seems to have scuttled that plan.

Our own guess is that for any progress toward reunification on terms acceptable to the civilized world, the North's communist regime must go. So far that doesn't seem to be on Pyongyang's agenda.

South Korea, however, is growing bolder. President Roh not only approved the prime ministers' meeting and the August 15 rally, but suggested that all Koreans be allowed to travel freely throughout the peninsula for five days next month. He even agreed to allow North Korean officials and South Korean dissidents to meet in Seoul to discuss arrangements for the rally.

Pyongyang's response was less than gracious. State radio broadcasts called the offer of travel "fraudulent propaganda." North Korean diplomats imposed absurd conditions. To Seoul's suggestion that the Liberation Day rally be open to everyone, Pyongyang replied that only South Korea's radical students and dissidents, many of them sympathetic to the North, would be welcome. To allow anyone else, North Korea said, would be "an act of challenge we cannot permit." When the time came late last week for five North Korean officials to go to Seoul to meet the dissidents and fix the details of the Liberation Day rally, they found an excuse not to go.

The contrast between the two Koreas grows more profound every day. Since 1985 -- the last time a few North Koreans were allowed in -- South Korea has held free presidential and legislative elections, hosted the Olympics, and become a regional economic power. North Korea, under Great Leader Kim Il Sung, meanwhile has been rounding out its long record of poverty and international isolation.

Mr. Roh's proposals for breaching the DMZ signify South Korea's growing self-confidence. It is unlikely, for instance, that South Korea's increasingly sophisticated citizens are going to be overwhelmed by a visit to Pyongyang's Department Store Number 1 or the Victorious Fatherland Liberation Museum.

The South is not yet a model democracy. The entire opposition in Seoul's National Assembly walked out after the ruling Democratic Liberal Party rudely rushed through 25 bills in less than a minute during the closing session last week. But every step toward political and economic maturity in South Korea represents another blow to Pyongyang. The North can only respond to the South's real progress with gestures, and over time, symbols are no match for substance.
