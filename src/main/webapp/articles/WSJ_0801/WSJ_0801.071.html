Law -- Legal Beat: Cap on AIDS Benefits Wins Legal Round

  • Author: Amy Dockser Marcus and Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 08/01/90

A federal judge has ruled that an employer can cap AIDS benefits for its employees under the federal statute regulating employee benefits known as the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974, or ERISA. The decision has been appealed. It is believed to be the first case to address the issue of whether ERISA allows employers to amend their health plans in response to medical claims submitted for a particular illness.

John McGann was diagnosed as having acquired immune deficiency syndrome, or AIDS, in December 1987 and told his employer, H & H Music Co., Houston, of the diagnosis. Seven months later, the company became self-insured and changed its group medical care plan.

Under the previous plan, employees diagnosed with AIDS were entitled to as much as $1 million in benefits during their lifetime. The new plan capped AIDS benefits at $5,000 and also discontinued benefits for chemical dependency.

After learning about the changes, Mr. McGann filed a discrimination lawsuit alleging that his employer's action violated his rights under ERISA. He also sued Brook Mays Music Co., Houston, which administers the plan, and General American Life Insurance Co., St. Louis, Mo., which provided excess risk coverage.

U.S. District Judge Norman W. Black dismissed Mr. McGann's lawsuit. In essence, the judge's ruling means that under ERISA employers have the right to change or terminate their medical plan even if they know about a particular employee's illness. Judge Black found that the financial strain caused by the plan had forced the company to make changes or end health benefits for everyone in the company.

And Judge Black said that H & H Music had complied with ERISA by providing employees with notice that the group medical plan was subject to change from year to year.

Employment lawyers say the case highlights a troubling issue currently confronting corporations. The AIDS crisis has left many companies unable to cope with the rising health costs associated with the catastrophic illness, these lawyers say.

"If you have 40 people in a company and one has AIDS and it's so expensive that no one can have insurance, then what are you going to do? It's a disaster for a small mom-and-pop operation faced with a $60,000 hospital bill for one employee. And companies are facing that question every day," says Robert C. Christenson of the Atlanta firm of Fisher & Phillips, who has represented employers in such cases.

A handful of states require health insurance policies to include AIDS coverage. And, about 50% of the states have laws barring discrimination in employment solely because a person has AIDS. But employers who are self-insured are governed by the federal ERISA law, which pre-empts those laws and doesn't require employers to provide specific benefits in their health plans.

"This decision appears to validate a dangerous trend under which employers attempt to circumvent the obligations otherwise imposed on them by state law by going the self-insurance route," says Thomas B. Stoddard, executive director of Lambda Legal Defense and Education Fund, a legal organization for lesbians and gay men and co-counsel for Mr. McGann.

Under the law, "H & H had the right to do what they did. They followed the law," says Mark Huvard of the Houston law firm of Engelhardt Harberg & Huvard, a lawyer for H & H and Brook Mays. A lawyer for General American said he wouldn't comment because the case is pending. The plaintiff filed an appeal last week in Houston.

Mr. McGann's lawyers say they believe that ERISA bars companies from discriminating in the granting of health benefits.

Says Donald Skipwith, a Houston solo practitioner who is co-counsel for Mr. McGann: "In order to protect the group, the defendants left an employee who had been part of that group for years and years out in the cold."

PROCTER & GAMBLE SUED two Amway Corp. distributors.

The Cincinnati-based consumer-products giant, eager to quash rumors linking the company to Satanism, sued the distributors for spreading the tales and launching boycotts of P&G brands.

The lawsuit, filed in a federal district court in Topeka, Kan., seeks an order to stop the distributors, James and Linda Newton of Parsons, Kan., from circulating the rumors. Procter & Gamble also seeks compensatory damages of more than $50,000 and unspecified punitive damages.

Previously, P&G has filed a dozen lawsuits related to the devil-worship rumor, including actions against three other Amway distributors. All were settled out of court, the company said.

The Newtons distributed brochures that accuse the company of supporting the Church of Satan, P&G alleged. The couple also told others that P&G's moon and stars logo is a Satanic symbol and encouraged them to boycott P&G products, according to P&G officials. The Newtons couldn't be reached for comment.

A spokeswoman for Amway, Ada, Mich., said its distributors operate independently and declined to comment on the suit. She said Amway has cooperated with P&G in the past to squelch rumors about Satanism.

Distributors for Amway sell numerous products, including detergents, cleaning and personal-care products. P&G sells leading brands in all those categories, such as Tide detergent, Spic and Span cleaner and Pert Plus shampoo.

A $100-A-YEAR ASSESSMENT is being fought by New Jersey lawyers.

New Jersey lawyers are upset about the state's new automobile insurance reform package. The law, which went into effect last March, requires all attorneys licensed in New Jersey to pay a $100-a-year assessment for the privilege of practicing in the state.

The idea came from recently elected Gov. Jim Florio, a Democrat who campaigned heavily on the promise to reform the state's auto insurance system. Last March, he signed into law a new system designed to cut premiums by 20%, partly by eliminating the state-operated insurance pool for high-risk drivers known as the Joint Underwriting Association.

Where do the lawyers come in? Because of bad management, the pool was $3.1 billion in debt. Insurance companies are required to pay off $1.4 billion of the debt. But the new law requires all groups that benefited financially from the prior system also to chip in -- including doctors, podiatrists, chiropractors, physical therapists, auto-body shops and each of the state's 42,000 lawyers.

The lawyers countered with a lawsuit. Now pending in New Jersey State Taxation Court in Trenton, the suit contends that the law is overbroad because it affects all lawyers, not just those who practice personal injury and automobile negligence cases. Law professors, judges, inactive attorneys, even second-year judge's law clerks are all required to make the payment, according to court pleadings filed by Newark attorney Frederick B. Lacey; he is a former federal judge and U.S. attorney who is representing the New Jersey State Bar Association in the case.

Last month, a New Jersey tax judge issued a temporary restraining order requiring that all payments made by lawyers to be held in an escrow until after a final hearing on the new law's legality, tentatively scheduled for September.

The Medical Society of New Jersey, which regulates doctors, has a separate suit pending and a federal class-action suit on behalf of all the professional groups affected by the law is pending in a federal district court in Camden, N.J.

Several insurance companies, including Allstate Insurance Co., the state's largest automobile insurer and a Sears, Roebuck & Co. unit, also are challenging the law.

INSURER ORDERED to pay for AIDS treatment.

In another case involving insurers and AIDS, a New York state court in Manhattan ordered Empire Blue Cross & Blue Shield to pay for a bone-marrow transplant for a man who is infected with the HIV virus, which causes AIDS. The insurer had refused to pay for the $150,000 treatment, claiming it was experimental. But Justice Elliott Wilk agreed with the patient's attorneys that although the procedure may be new for HIV patients, it is an appropriate therapy for rebuilding the immune system.

The transplant isn't expected to rid the patient, 47-year-old Tom Bradley, of the HIV infection, but doctors believe it could improve his health for an additional two years, said Evan Wolfson, an attorney with Lambda Legal Defense and Legal Education Fund.

Bone-marrow transplants are unlikely treatments for most HIV patients because the procedure is usually performed only when the patient has an identical twin who can act as donor. Also, the treatment can be done only before the patient has one of the opportunistic infections that often afflict HIV patients.

A spokesman for Empire said the company hasn't decided whether to appeal the decision. He said the transplant hasn't been used widely enough to have been proved "beneficial and appropriate" for Mr. Bradley's condition.
