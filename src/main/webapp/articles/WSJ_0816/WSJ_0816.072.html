Economy: Industry Output Leveled Off During July --- Operating Rate Decreased; Inventories Fell by 0.4% In June After May Gain

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 08/16/90

WASHINGTON -- Production by U.S. industry leveled off in July after rising for two months, the Federal Reserve reported, an indication that the economy continues to stumble. Manufacturing production slipped 0.1% and utility output dropped 0.4%, but mining posted a 1% increase.

American industry has been encumbered by high interest rates, and the new numbers show that it continues to be frail, despite production increases of 0.4% in June and 0.6% in May. Nevertheless, some economists took last month's flat production as a sign that the industrial economy was still strong enough to dodge a downturn.

The Fed also said that variations in motor vehicle production have been influencing overall output numbers. "Excluding motor vehicles, industrial output rose 0.2% last month, about the same rate of growth that has been evident so far this year," it said.

"When you put motor vehicles to one side, the industrial economy keeps crawling upward," said Robert Dederick, chief economist at Northern Trust Co. in Chicago.

But S. Jay Levy, a partner at Levy Economic Forecasts in Chappaqua, N.Y., said, "I think this little spurt in production a few months ago was interpreted as stronger demand for manufactured goods than was the reality."

The Fed also reported that industry used less of its capacity in July production than it had in June, another indication of slack. July's industrial operating rate was 83.4% of capacity, down from 83.6% in June. Manufacturing operating rates dropped to 82.6% from 82.9%.

The lower operating rates could temper some of the inflation pressure being created by oil price increases. When factories are running flat-out they often encounter production snags and shortages that can lead to price increases.

Any sign of relief from price pressures will be welcome news for the economy, which now faces an acceleration of inflation provoked by rocketing oil prices in the wake of Iraq's invasion of Kuwait.

A report from the Commerce Department also contained some encouraging news for the prospect of continued economic growth. Business inventories fell 0.4% in June, nearly erasing a 0.5% rise in May, the department said.

With the economy growing very slowly these days, it is particularly important for businesses to hold down inventories. If unsold stocks pile up too high companies are likely to cut back orders or production and even lay off workers. The chain reaction can tip the economy into a recession.

The drop in inventories left stocks of retailers, manufacturers and merchant wholesalers at $797.30 billion in June. At the same time sales of all businesses rose 1% during the month, to $544.43 billion.

At that sales pace, it would have taken 1.46 months to sell off June inventories, down from 1.49 months in May.
