Southland Offer On Debt Expires; Extension Is Likely

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 08/16/90

DALLAS -- Southland Corp.'s debt exchange offer and the buy-out offer from its Japanese suitor both expired at midnight last night without completion, but most observers expect both offers to be extended at least a week. Investment bankers involved in the deals wouldn't indicate how many of the bondholders have agreed to accept the exchange offer. John Gordon, the chairman of the bondholder committee, said he expects the offers to be extended. Furthermore, he said, holdouts who oppose the latest debt swap, while troublesome, won't kill the deal. "One way or another, this deal will be done," said Mr. Gordon of Deltec Securities Corp.

Meanwhile, the parties negotiating the deals have contemplated the possibility of a "pre-packaged" Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceeding as a way to eliminate the problem of holdouts without substantially changing the terms of the agreement. The offer, the fourth put forth by Southland and the first approved by the bondholder committee, would give bondholders about 25% of the common stock of the company. The founding Thompson family of Dallas would get about 5% of the stock and Ito-Yokado, operator of 7-Eleven stores in Japan, would get about 70% for $430 million.

Southland, the troubled parent of the 7-Eleven convenience store chain, went into default on its $1.8 billion publicly traded debt in mid-July, but avoided being forced into bankruptcy at that time by getting a tentative approval from the bondholder committee for the most recent exchange offer. Banks and other creditors would receive full payment under the plan.

The Thompson family, which now owns slightly more than two-thirds of the company, would see its stake shrink under the offer to 5%, or about a $25 million investment from about $163 million. The family members took about $200 million out of Southland in the $4.9 billion leveraged buyout that took the company private in late 1987 to avoid a possible hostile bid by the Belzberg family of Canada.

According to the offering documents filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission, John P. Thompson, Jere W. Thompson and Joe C. Thompson Jr. will receive five-year employment contracts from the new owners providing annual base salaries of $600,000 each, plus an annual bonus equal to about $360,000 each. In addition, Jere and John will receive the benefits given other executives. Also, five-year recourse loans of about $32 million will be provided to the Thompsons at no interest.

Southland owns about 6,700 7-Eleven and other convenience stores in the U.S. and Canada. Increased competition eroded sales and profit margins and made it difficult for the company to service the huge debt from the buy-out.
