Salomon Seeking To Market Issue That Bets on Oil

  • Author: William Power and Stanley W. Angrist
  • Date: 08/16/90

NEW YORK -- Salomon Inc., one of the brokerage firms that scored a success earlier this year with "Nikkei warrants" to bet on the Japanese stock market, is trying to do the same thing with the oil market. Salomon filed for Securities and Exchange Commission approval for a new security that appears to be the first equity issue, as opposed to a futures contract or option, that allows investors to bet on the future price of oil. The Salomon Phibro Oil Trust units would trade on the American Stock Exchange.

In May, Salomon tried to come out with a more complicated version of the trust, but that offering was pulled when the oil markets plunged. But now, with soaring oil prices caused by Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, Salomon's timing seems better for a big sale.

A Salomon spokesman declined to comment, citing regulations against public statements when an issue is awaiting SEC approval.

The units, which are being underwritten by Salomon Brothers Inc., the company's securities subsidiary, and Bear, Stearns & Co., are designed for individual and institutional investors. Each unit entitles the investor to a cash payment based on the price of a quarter of a barrel of oil five years from now.

But the offering isn't without big risks. At a time when many oil experts aren't sure what oil prices will be tomorrow or next month, the Salomon offering is geared to predicting where oil will be in 1995. The trust will pay a single cash distribution in November 1995, based on the price of oil at that time.

If the price of oil isn't well over $20 a barrel then, the investor won't make any money and could lose heavily if oil prices have plunged from current levels.

Investors can cash out early by selling their units on the Amex, although it's unclear yet how active trading will become.

The units could have some advantages over oil futures. Unlike oil futures contracts, with lifespans ranging from a month to two years, the Salomon units can be bought and put away by investors for five years. Also, if oil prices go down, there won't be additional margin payments required as there can be in the futures market.

With plans to offer 12 million units at a maximum of $5 each, Salomon could raise as much as $60 million from the public in the offering, in which its Phibro Energy Inc. unit will play a role and in which an oil broker for Phibro, PVM Oil Associates Inc., will be paid as an adviser.

The Nikkei warrants that began trading earlier this year were fortuitously timed. They were initially issued by Salomon and several other U.S. securities firms as the Tokyo stock market began falling sharply, and the value of the warrants soared.

The first Nikkei warrants were designed by Goldman, Sachs & Co. and issued by the Kingdom of Denmark on Jan. 12. Five days later, Salomon issued its warrants, and others followed. en

In its registration statement for the proposed oil offering, Salomon warns that investors "should understand the risks of investments related to crude oil prices," which have gyrated in recent years, from nearly $40 a barrel in the early 1980s to as low as $11 later in the decade and back to more than $20 last year.

Salomon isn't the only player coming up with new ways to play oil prices. Bankers Trust New York Corp. is expected to announce in London a U.S.-style warrant with a life of 18 months, which permits the holder to elect whether to bet oil prices will rise or fall. The investor has 90 days from the issue date to decide which way to bet. The offering isn't likely to be available to Americans.
