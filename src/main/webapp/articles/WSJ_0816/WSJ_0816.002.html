Politics & Policy: Energy Department Unveils Strategy To Cut U.S. Dependence on Mideast Oil

  • Author: Barbara Rosewicz
  • Date: 08/16/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration unveiled its first energy strategy to cope with oil-supply disruptions in the Middle East and identified ways to reduce U.S. dependence on foreign oil as much as 550,000 barrels a day by year end. But the reductions, which would replace much of the 610,000 barrels a day the U.S. bought from Iraq and Kuwait, rely heavily on voluntary cooperation from industry, local governments and consumers.

The plan, which puts inflating automobile tires at the top of its list of energy-saving measures, steers clear of mandating consumer behavior and of the most controversial means of reducing the use of foreign oil, such as opening up an Alaska wilderness area to oil drilling or tightening fuel-economy standards for new cars.

"It sounds good, but it's marginal and it doesn't go far enough," said Eli Bergman, head of the non-profit policy group Americans for Energy Independence.

The measures announced by the Energy Department envision that by the end of the year domestic energy supplies can be increased 270,000 barrels a day and demand can be reduced 280,000 barrels a day. Energy Secretary James Watkins said much of the savings could be made within 90 days, quickly replacing the equivalent of 400,000 barrels of imported oil.

Mr. Watkins called the steps "prudent" but said there is no energy crisis. "Today's supplies are adequate," he told reporters.

He said the plan, which comes two weeks after Iraq shocked world oil markets by invading Kuwait, is only the first installment of steps under consideration by energy officials and the White House. More could come as soon as two weeks, he said.

Officials reiterated the White House's stand that there is no need to dip into the government's Strategic Petroleum Reserve, and they expressed confidence that other nations will pump enough extra oil to make up for embargoed Kuwaiti and Iraqi oil.

Still, officials called on U.S. consumers to do their part to head off an oil shortage by making a few simple changes in driving practices.

Inflating tires on vehicles to the proper pressure would improve gasoline mileage and save 100,000 barrels of oil a day. Surveys show about half of drivers' tires are underinflated.

The government is also urging drivers to do more car-pooling and van-pooling, saving 90,000 barrels a day with only 20% more ride-sharing; observe speed limits to save 50,000 barrels a day; and drive the most energy-efficient car if there's a choice. If one out of five two-car families in the U.S. drove the car that gets the higher gasoline mileage, the U.S. would save 40,000 barrels of oil a day, the government said.

"It's the kind of things that are not really asking you to turn off your air-conditioning or turn off your heat in winter, but simple things that can have an impact," said Deputy Energy Secretary Henson Moore.

On the supply side, officials said that British Petroleum Co., Exxon Corp. and Atlantic Richfield Co. have agreed to boost production by 50,000 barrels a day from the North Slope of Alaska, where they already produce 1.8 million barrels a day. Those producers also said that an expansion project under way in the Alaska oil fields can be counted on to boost production a further 90,000 barrels a day starting in November and as much as 140,000 barrels a day in six months.

The government will increase its production from U.S. naval petroleum reserves by 5,000 barrels a day by October, Mr. Watkins said.

But the bulk of measures the government is counting on to replace Kuwaiti and Iraqi oil in the short term -- a total of 215,000 barrels under its plan -- aren't guaranteed to succeed.

The Energy Department, as reported earlier, will mediate a California dispute that is keeping 200 million to 300 million barrels of oil locked under the ocean floor. Mr. Watkins said top-level officials of Chevron Corp. "are encouraging our coming in and acting as a facilitator" between oil producers and Santa Barbara County officials.

Mr. Watkins said he hopes that the dispute over the Point Arguello project can be resolved within six months and that the three completed offshore oil platforms, which have been sitting idle for 2 1/2 years, can be pumping 75,000 barrels a day by year end. They eventually could produce as much as 100,000 barrels a day. The oil is caught up in a controversy over whether to move it by tanker or pipeline to refineries 130 miles down the coast.

The Energy Department is ordering all its vehicles switched to gasohol -- a blend of gasoline and 10% alcohol fuel -- and other government agencies will be approached to make the switch. The department is urging producers of ethanol, an alcohol fuel, to produce at full capacity. The department isn't taking any other steps to mandate wider use of gasohol, but is counting on increased usage to replace 10,000 barrels of oil a day.

The government said it also intends to work with the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission and state utility commissions to remove obstacles and possibly increase pipeline capacity to allow more industries to switch from oil to more plentiful natural gas. They are banking on the shift to offset 100,000 barrels a day of foreign oil. Some dual-fuel plants have already switched.

Officials also are counting on persuading oil-producing states to relax regulations on "allowables" that restrict certain well production, for an extra 30,000 barrels a day.

"It's wildly optimistic," said Ed Rothschild, head of a Washington lobbying group on consumer and labor issues. "If you want to talk about where the oil is and where you can get immediate access to it, it's in the Strategic Petroleum Reserve. It was designed for this purpose and it ought to be used for this purpose."

The Energy Department also urged independent oil producers to increase production. But Mr. Watkins said he doesn't anticipate a reversal in declining U.S. oil drilling unless oil prices of about $25 a barrel persist for six months or so and give drillers the incentive to make long-term investments.
