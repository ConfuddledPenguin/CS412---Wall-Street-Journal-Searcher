International -- Foreign Insight: Kohl Is Willing to Order Ships to Gulf, Signaling Readiness for New World Role

  • Author: Frederick Kempe
  • Date: 08/16/90

BERLIN -- West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl, increasingly at ease with his nation's new political clout, is willing to send German ships to a trouble zone for the first time since World War II to help settle the Mideast crisis, a senior government official said. Mr. Kohl privately told Italian leader Giulio Andreotti by telephone that he would be willing to send five West German minesweepers to the Persian Gulf, but only as part of an international fleet that would be approved at next Tuesday's meeting of the West European Union.

The Bonn government has already dispatched the ships to the eastern Mediterranean, replacing North Atlantic Treaty Organization ships sent to the gulf in response to Iraq's invasion of Kuwait.

But Mr. Kohl's apparent willingness to go even further underlines Germany's readiness to play a more active world role, a readiness that is likely to increase after East and West Germany's scheduled unification late this year.

"We are the largest country in Western Europe, and we believe we have to join the common solidarity of the Western world" regarding Iraq, says Horst Teltschik, Mr. Kohl's national security adviser.

Mr. Teltschik wouldn't comment on what he called the "theoretical possibilities" of sending the minesweepers into the gulf, an act that would be expected to encounter political opposition as well as constitutional impediments to the use of German troops outside the territory of NATO. But he said, "We have to prove the possibilities of what we can do more than ever before because of the expectations of the international community."

A year ago, Bonn would have faced a storm of internal opposition for actions it has already taken: allowing the U.S. to deploy forces to the gulf from German bases, deploying its own ships and joining the sanctions imposed on Iraq after its Aug. 2 invasion. But sending forces to a potential war zone is a far more sensitive matter in Germany, and Mr. Kohl might not be able to pull it off.

His own foreign minister, Hans Dietrich Genscher, says he believes the country's constitution forbids it. The opposition Social Democratic Party's contingent in the Bundestag is likely next week to oppose any such deployment but at the same time suggest a change in the constitution that would allow German troops to be used outside NATO's territory as part of a United Nations contingent.

No matter what becomes of the minesweepers, however, the decision marks the growing pains of a new political power. The Soviet Union and Eastern Europe turn to West Germany first for economic assistance, the U.S. leans on it to rally European support for Washington's actions, and Germany itself is increasingly unmatched in political and economic power in Europe.

Most of all, Germany's attitude has changed. After years of being psychologically and politically handicapped by its militaristic past and the resulting postwar division of the nation, it has become more willing to stand up for its national goals.

"Germany is going to be a more prickly country when she defends her interests," says Thomas Kielinger, editor of the West German weekly Rheinischer Merkur. "We will be less meek, less like a lamb, more assertive -- not domineering, but in a natural sense."

But where Germany's increased influence can be best measured isn't in the gulf but in Eastern Europe, where its formerly Communist neighbors are all seeking help. Here, Bonn is held back only by its unfamiliarity with its new role and by fiscal limitations imposed by the cost of absorbing East Germany.

"The role that Germany is playing in the world has a new quality," Mr. Teltschik says. "The problem is that most Germans haven't recognized it yet. We must take more responsibility ourselves. The world expects more of us. However, we are of the opinion that the expectations are too great. World-wide, people expect we can finance everything. We just don't have the economic and financial resources to meet those expectations."

That said, Germany has seldom been so well positioned economically. In the first quarter alone, it was able to expand its exports by 18% to nearly $100 billion and surpass the U.S. as the world's largest exporter. After unification, which will ultimately expand the country's potential by 25%, experts say the country could hold onto that position for years. West Germany's trade surplus in the first quarter was nearly double that of Japan's at $22 billion.

Some officials say Mr. Kohl is using his minesweepers less to flex muscle and more to pay a debt to President Bush for his support of German unification. "There isn't a political debt, but a moral and political obligation," Mr. Teltschik says. "The Americans have shown strong solidarity for the Germans not just during the last year, but over the last 40 years. Now we can't say it doesn't matter."
