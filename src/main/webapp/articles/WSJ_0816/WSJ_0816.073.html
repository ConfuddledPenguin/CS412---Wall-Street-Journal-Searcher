The Value of Science (Non)Fiction

  • Author: Ben Bova
  • Date: 08/16/90

By now I'm pretty sick and tired of people asking, "Who would have thought a few years ago that Eastern Europe would overthrow communism, and the Soviet Union would be in danger of breaking up?" I would have thought of it. In fact, I did, in the novel "Voyagers II: The Aliens Within," published in 1986. It is a science fiction novel, of course.

It has always surprised me that people who pay attention to stock market analysts and weather forecasters refuse to read science fiction. They are missing the best technique available for examining the future.

There is virtually nothing that has been discovered or invented in this century that was not predicted in a science fiction story. From computers to space travel, from organ transplants to genetic engineering, robots, nuclear power, the population explosion, the energy crunch, urban sprawl and greenhouse warming -- all were subjects of science fiction stories 20, 40, 60 years ago and more.

I am speaking of science fiction as it is published in books and magazines, not the "sci-fi" of Hollywood. Most "sci-fi" movies bear the same relationship to science fiction that Popeye does to naval history. Even films based on solid science fiction, such as the Schwarzenegger epic "Total Recall," bury the science fiction ideas beneath an orgy of mindless violence. Would you give the cops machine guns if you lived in a glass dome? Come on]

(Incidentally, the term "sci-fi" is loathed by most science fiction writers. It is a term of derision, used mainly by publicity agents and desperate headline writers.)

Futurists, the professional scientists who make a career of technological and/or sociological forecasting, are like science fiction writers who have had the imagination beaten out of them. If you have ever read a futurist's report five years after it was published you know that their forecasts are usually worthless. This is because they must labor under a tremendous handicap: They must stick to the facts that are known at the time the report is written. This means their forecasts tend to be straight-line extrapolations of the present situation that become more and more inaccurate the farther into the future we go.

Science fiction writers, on the other hand, are not hampered by the limitations of the present. They can create crazy ideas, wild cards. They are free to invent anything they can imagine, as long as it does not violate the basic laws of science. (And, in some cases, even if it does.) If you wait long enough, the wild card eventually shows up. The Arabs do put an embargo on petroleum. The Soviet system does crack apart. Ray guns become lasers. Invisible airplanes become Stealth bombers.

Does this mean that you can foretell the future by reading science fiction? Yes. And no.

Science fiction writers are really not even trying to predict the future. Most of us do not believe there is a single, immutable THE future to predict. The future is plastic, unformed, capable of being shaped by all the things each of us does -- and all the things we fail to do.

Science fiction stories examine possible futures. The writer takes a set of ideas and asks, "What would happen if . . ." And the game begins. What would happen if a flying saucer landed on the White House lawn and gave us an energy source that ran on water and produced no harmful pollution? In science fiction, that is the beginning of a story. How would the world change? Who would benefit? Who would get hurt? Where should you invest your money?

By reading science fiction, you get a kaleidoscopic view of many possible futures. The one that actually happens is in there someplace. It may become reality because enough readers liked that future well enough to make it come true. Most of the men who walked on the moon started by reading science fiction. So did most of the people who got them there.

This is not to say that every tale is a literary gem. Far from it. The late science fiction writer Theodore Sturgeon years ago coined Sturgeon's Law: Ninety percent of science fiction is crud; but then, 90% of everything is crud.

At its best, science fiction acts as a sort of simulations laboratory in which we test ideas about the future. "What if?" is the question constantly being examined.

What if Saddam Hussein succeeds in his quest to develop nuclear arms for Iraq? What if AIDS or a similar immune-system disease spreads to the general population? What if the greenhouse effect melts the polar ice caps?

Can a world divided into nations and blocs create an effective International Peacekeeping Force with the power and authority to prevent any nation from attacking any other nation?

Will electronic books replace paper?

Will private enterprise finally move into space development and create the industries in orbit that government programs cannot? Or will space be left entirely to politically motivated government efforts?

Will advances in biotechnology allow us to live for centuries? Or become virtually immortal?

These are the kinds of questions that good science fiction considers. Surely, if any field of literature can be said to have any practical value, this ongoing examination of the future's many pathways is as important as it is entertaining.

---

Mr. Bova, the author of more than 75 futuristic books, fiction and nonfiction, is president of the Science Fiction Writers of America and former editorial director of Omni magazine.
