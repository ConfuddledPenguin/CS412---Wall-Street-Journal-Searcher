Enterprise: Harrowing Ordeal for a Nightclub Owner --- Robbed and Threatened, Entrepreneur Fought Back

  • Author: John R. Emshwiller
  • Date: 08/16/90

{The second of two articles on small businesses struggling against crime.}

LOS ANGELES -- Dennis Lidtke found out how rough it can be to run a small business.

Two years ago, a gunman robbed $40,000 from the safe of the Palace Nightclub here, which Mr. Lidtke operates. Only two months later, masked gunmen held Mr. Lidtke and his wife hostage at their home while accomplices cleaned out the club safe again.

Next, two men allegedly told Mr. Lidtke to give up partial control of his business and fork over $50,000 -- or face a beating.

Such horrifying experiences aren't unprecedented. Small businesses have long been targets for such crimes as extortion, fraud, loan-sharking and money laundering -- sometimes committed by individuals and sometimes by organized crime. Entrepreneurs often wonder how -- or if -- they can fight back.

Mr. Lidtke paid the money the criminals demanded. But, unlike many entrepreneurs, he also went to the police. He didn't get total satisfaction, but he didn't waste his time either. Three men were eventually indicted for extortion and other crimes against Mr. Lidtke and other businessmen.

In a plea bargain, one defendant, John Scotto, pleaded no contest to a felony count, possession of a forged document with intent to defraud. He also agreed to resign from the state bar and sell his interest in two restaurants, says John Monaghan, the deputy district attorney who handled the case.

The other two defendants, Leonard Passafaro and Natale Cardino, pleaded no contest to attempting to extort another businessman. Each of the three agreed to prison terms of up to 16 months and face formal sentencing within the next several weeks.

Law enforcement authorities apparently fear that the alleged extortion of Mr. Lidtke may have been part of a wider Mafia move into Los Angeles in recent years.

According to an affidavit by a Los Angeles Police Department organized-crime investigator, filed in connection with the Lidtke case, police here three years ago launched an investigation into "the suspected infiltration" by the Mafia into Los Angeles area businesses, particularly restaurants, nightclubs and bars.

Early on, the police came across Mr. Scotto, one of the defendants in the Lidtke case, says the affidavit, filed in county municipal court here. The 29-year-old Mr. Scotto, a local attorney, is the son of Anthony Scotto, a former New York labor leader, a convicted criminal and a captain in the Gambino crime family, according to the affidavit.

In January, 1987, Los Angeles police stopped John Scotto while he was driving with a reputed East Coast Mafia figure, the affidavit states. The alleged crime figure told an undercover police informant that he was interested in Los Angeles night clubs as a way to launder cocaine profits, the document adds.

An attorney for Mr. Scotto declined to comment on any aspect of this story.

The 54-year-old Mr. Lidtke says thoughts of mobsters were far from his mind when he got involved with the Palace more than a decade ago. Mr. Lidtke owned a graphic-design business and had long been active in efforts to restore Hollywood's badly faded glamour.

The Palace was built in the 1920s as one of Hollywood's first legitimate theaters. "Just about every legend from Sinatra and Garland to Armstrong and Crosby performed at the Palace," says Mr. Lidtke.

Mr. Lidtke says he bought it in 1979 to help save it from the wrecking ball. He turned it into an entertainment complex with a nightclub, restaurant and film and video production facilities.

Mr. Lidtke says that besides investing most of his personal wealth in the project, he raised $1.5 million through a limited partnership. The partnership, whose investors were mainly New Yorkers, took title to the building.

Despite its aesthetic successes, the project has had serious financial problems. The company owned by Mr. Lidtke that manages the Palace recently filed for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the federal bankruptcy code, its second such filing in the past six years. The company is hundreds of thousands of dollars in arrears on rents to the limited partnership, a matter that is part of litigation between Mr. Lidtke and the partners in a Los Angeles state court.

But Mr. Lidtke contends he was making progress in getting the Palace on firmer footing when "all hell broke loose" in early 1988.

For one thing, the limited partnership hired Mr. Scotto to represent it in the festering rent dispute. He and Mr. Lidtke initially worked out a new rent schedule and Mr. Lidtke agreed to resign as general partner of the building partnership if his company fell in arrears.

Subsequently, the Palace suffered the two major robberies. In the first, Mr. Lidtke says his wife, Beverly, came to the office one Monday morning to find an armed robber waiting, who forced her to open the Palace safe.

As a result, Mr. Lidtke says he couldn't keep current on the Palace's rent, and Mr. Scotto filed an action to evict Mr. Lidtke's company. However, according to lawyers for Mr. Lidtke and the limited partnership, the eviction effort failed because Mr. Scotto served the wrong type of notice on Mr. Lidtke.

About two months after the first robbery, the Lidtkes came home to find two masked robbers with semi-automatic weapons, says Mr. Lidtke. The Lidtkes were bound and held for eight hours while the robbers ransacked the house and phoned a confederate at the Palace with the new combination to the safe.

During the ordeal, the two robbers cracked open a few beers and chatted with their victims. "They told us they were hired out of New York and paid $10,000 to clean us out," says Mr. Lidtke. He says the two were familiar enough with the Palace situation to suggest at one point that Mr. Lidtke sell out to his partners in New York.

Though no one has been charged with the robberies, Mr. Monaghan, the deputy district attorney, says he believes the thefts were connected to the extortion effort. However, he adds, there isn't any evidence that the limited partners were involved in any wrongdoing. Mr. Monaghan says he believes Mr. Scotto eventually hoped to take over the Palace himself.

In September 1988, two men claiming to represent the interests of the limited partners contacted Mr. Lidtke and requested a meeting, says the police affidavit. In his office conference room, Mr. Lidtke and a partner met with the men, whom police later identified as Mr. Passafaro and Mr. Cardino.

The two demanded that Mr. Lidtke resign as general partner of the Palace limited partnership and pay $50,000, says the police affidavit. The two men told him to go see Mr. Scotto to settle things, according to the affidavit. One also told Mr. Lidtke: "I know where you live, where you go, your wife, everything," adding that Mr. Lidtke might face a "beating." Mr. Lidtke says he was scared enough to pay the $50,000.

But he and his partner also had the presence of mind to provide the police with hard evidence of extortion. The two secretly tape-recorded the meeting with Mr. Passafaro and Mr. Cardino. Mr. Lidtke says he also had the conference table's glass top cleaned beforehand to better pick up fingerprints from the men.

After a lengthy preliminary hearing on the charges against Mr. Scotto and Mr. Passafaro, a state court judge found sufficient evidence of guilt to order a full trial. Then, however, the three reached a plea bargain with the district attorney.

Meanwhile, Mr. Lidtke says he is still nervous enough to keep an armed guard at his home.
