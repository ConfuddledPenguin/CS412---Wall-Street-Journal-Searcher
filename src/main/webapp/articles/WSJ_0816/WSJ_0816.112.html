SEC Is Seeking Comment On Issues by Foreign Banks

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/16/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission voted to seek comment on a proposal that would make it easier for foreign banks and insurance companies to offer securities to U.S. investors. The proposal would exempt foreign banks and insurers, as well as their finance subsidiaries, from the Investment Company Act, which regulates mutual funds. U.S. banks and insurance companies already are exempt from the act.

Three years ago, the SEC exempted from the Investment Company Act issues of debt securities and nonvoting preferred stock by foreign banks and insurers. The proposal would expand the exemption to include offerings or sales of all equity securities by foreign banks and insurers.

Foreign banks and insurers would still be required to file registration statements and periodic reports under other securities law. Those filings are far less onerous than the requirements under the Investment Company Act.

For practical purposes, however, the rule hasn't prevented foreign banks and insurance companies from offering their securities in the U.S. The SEC typically grants them exemptions from the current rule.

The agency is expected to approve the proposal after a 60-day comment period.

The agency also voted to issue an interpretive release stating that U.S. branches and agencies of foreign banks are exempt from the Investment Company Act for the limited purpose of issuing securities in the U.S.
