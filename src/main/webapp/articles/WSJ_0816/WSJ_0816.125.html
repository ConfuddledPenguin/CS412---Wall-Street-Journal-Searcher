Imperial Oil to Sell Refining Assets On Canada's Coast

  • Author: Suzanne McGee
  • Date: 08/16/90

TORONTO -- Imperial Oil Ltd. agreed to sell the refining and marketing assets it acquired in Canada's Atlantic Coast provinces and other downstream assets in three separate transactions that analysts valued at about 300 million Canadian dollars (US$262.3 million). Most of the assets to be sold were acquired as part of Imperial's C$5 billion acquisition of Texaco Canada Inc., completed earlier this year.

Montreal-based Ultramar Canada Inc. agreed to buy the bulk of the assets in the Atlantic Coast provinces, including 199 service stations, three marine terminals and a refinery. The deal must still be approved by Canadian antitrust regulators.

Imperial Oil, 70%-owned by Exxon Corp., pledged to shed all of Texaco Canada's downstream oil and gas assets in Canada's Atlantic provinces as part of its commitments to the Canadian government's antitrust agency, the Bureau of Competition Policy. Those commitments were required before regulators would approve the Texaco Canada acquisition.

Imperial wouldn't disclose the price Ultramar agreed to pay for the assets, but analysts valued the transaction at about C$200 million.

Imperial also said it agreed to sell 25 retail gas stations and its other assets in Newfoundland to a private consortium, Island Petroleum Co. An additional 96 company-owned service stations outside Canada's Atlantic provinces will be sold to 35 individual bidders, pending government approval, Imperial said. The company didn't disclose the value of those two transactions, which analysts said could fetch C$100 million.

Profits from these asset sales will help Imperial cut its debt load, which soared to C$5.8 billion after the Texaco Canada acquisition. Asset sales, a C$1.3 billion equity issue and cash from operations helped reduce the company's debt to C$3.5 billion by June 30, 1990.

Still on the block are about 300 service stations throughout the rest of Canada and between C$500 million and C$750 million of oil and gas producing assets in Western Canada. These are slated to be sold over the next 18 months.

Dennis Baxter, a spokesman for Imperial declined to speculate what the remaining downstream assets might fetch, but he said the company has received 529 bids for the remaining service stations.

Mr. Baxter added that Imperial is also getting offers on its upstream production properties, but acknowledged that "that's a very difficult market at the moment."

Analysts said the acquisition would be a positive move for the Montreal-based unit of Britain's Ultramar PLC.

"Until now, they've had a minimal exposure in that market," said Philippe Hervieu, an analyst with Nesbitt Thomson Deacon Inc. in Montreal. "This is a breakthrough for them."

Ultramar's acquisition more than doubles the company's retail outlets in three of the four Atlantic provinces to 337 from 138. An Ultramar executive said the company didn't bid for the Texaco stations in the fourth Atlantic province, Newfoundland, because Ultramar already has 221 outlets there.

The company emerged early as a logical buyer for the assets because antitrust regulators probably wouldn't have approved offers by other major integrated oil companies.

In trading on the Toronto Stock Exchange yesterday, Imperial Oil closed at C$64, unchanged.
