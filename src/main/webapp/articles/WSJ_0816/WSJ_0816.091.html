REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): The Bush Economy

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/16/90

George Bush has a bigger problem than Saddam Hussein: The economy, slowing down even before the Iraqi oil shock, now is slouching toward recession on his watch. Ronald Reagan inherited stagflation, turned it around and kept the recovery going. President Bush inherited a healthy economy, but now it's grinding down. Granted that recoveries weaken with age and that rising oil prices hurt, but the looming pain also says something about the Bush team's economic policies. After all, the Bush administration has supplanted the Reagan economic policies with its own.

The differences are of nuance, of course, but that's what makes markets. The centerpiece of the Reagan economic policies was tax cuts to provide incentives for growth; other objectives were secondary. The Bush people want a capital-gains tax cut too, but this is secondary to a different holy grail: reducing the deficit.

This was evident even in President Bush's press conference Tuesday. He was courageous in promising spending vetoes, and made the right revenue-enhancing arguments for a lower tax rate on capital gains. But even with the economy flirting with recession, these measures were tethered to a deficit-reduction agreement with the nabobs of Congress. Treasury Secretary Brady, no doubt reflecting the private sentiment of the administration as a whole, couples this with a tie for easier money. We should have learned from the '70s that monetary policy should not validate oil-price increases.

Now, preoccupation with deficits and interest rates might be fine in an ideal world of brisk growth in the economy and good will in Congress. Sooner or later the federal government needs to get a handle on its liabilities, as the Brady-Darman policy axis keeps insisting. A smaller deficit also would increase the world's savings pool, a good thing.

But Brady-Darman has bought into a debt paranoia that defies economic logic. The nearby chart compares the public debt burdens of the major industrial nations. The U.S. debt is hardly beyond the pale. And while the U.S. burden has grown in the 1980s, it actually was much higher in the post-World War II decades of rapid economic growth. Besides, if Japan's debt experience is any guide, neither interest rates nor growth depend on government debt.

Other things count for more, such as tax policies that reward investment. Rather than force-feed savings to induce investment, the better policy is to increase the opportunities for return on investment, and savings will follow. If some of those savings come from overseas, so what? In today's global economy, savings will go where the best opportunities are.

Jawboning the Fed for easy money, meanwhile is downright harmful, curbing the Fed's flexibility to ease if it wanted to. Secretary Brady's public call last week to "avoid an over single-minded emphasis on inflation" sounded to us a lot like Secretary Blumenthal of the Carter administration. To the markets too, apparently, since gold shot over $400, while the dollar sank to trade at a record-breaking low against the German mark. As for interest rates, after the Fed nudged short-term rates down a fraction of a point last month, the bond market slumped. If nothing else, this is no way to fight a war.

If the current White House debt preoccupation is economically marginal, it may prove politically suicidal. While the President tried to seize the offensive Tuesday, the Democrats have played the budget summit beautifully, appearing to be responsible by talking but opening up the spending spigots on Capitol Hill. Despite veto threats, appropriations bills have been coming in with double-digit increases. Meanwhile, Republicans debate a tax-a-week among themselves. The President's press conference offers some reason to hope he will spotlight spending, but the administration has often sounded as if it's talking itself into a tax increase veiled with smoke-and-mirror spending cuts. We would be a lot more confident that it won't revert to this mode if it talked less about trimming the deficit to lower interest rates and more about spending itself, to lower the government's claims on the private economy.

The Iraqi invasion changes everything, providing the occasion to get general economic policy back on track. The oil shock pushes at once toward inflation and recession, and the White House needs a strategy to push back against both. Our answer is the same one we and others promoted to cure the stagflation of the '70s. On the monetary side, that means resisting the siren song of easy money. On fiscal policy it means putting tax cuts back to the center of the policy debate. While 28% is low enough as a top rate, the capital-gains cut would do a lot to curb recession. If the Democrats' price for a capital-gains cut is the Moynihan proposal to cut the payroll tax, that would help low-wage workers.

The Reagan policy mix helped elect George Bush on a national ticket three times. By abandoning that mix, he risks the fate of the candidates who offered high taxes and easy money under the rubric of "responsibility."
