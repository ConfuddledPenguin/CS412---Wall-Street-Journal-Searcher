Politics & Policy: Pentagon Widens High-Definition Display Projects

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica and Robert L. Rose
  • Date: 08/16/90

The Pentagon, signaling its continuing interest in high-definition display systems, announced the selection of six more contractors to work on military applications of the advanced technology. The contractors are to pursue various aspects of this technology, which can produce finely detailed images or high-volume information through conventional electron tubes or flat-panel screens.

Zenith Electronics Corp. of Glenview, Ill., one of the largest of the companies, was chosen to work on enhancing the capability of cathode-ray tubes that are commonly used in television sets and computers. Ovonic Imaging Systems Inc. of Troy, Mich., will make active-matrix liquid-crystal displays for aircraft cockpits and other military uses.

The other companies selected are Norden Systems Inc., Norwalk, Conn.; MRS Technology Inc., Chelmsford, Mass.; Microelectronics & Computer Technology Corp., Austin, Texas; and Tektronix Inc., Beaverton, Ore. The amount of awards for each of these companies will be announced after they complete negotiations with the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency, the Pentagon unit overseeing the program.

Though the Pentagon intends to use the advanced displays only for its own purposes, the underlying technology potentially has enormous commercial value in the future manufacture of computers, medical imaging systems, and high-definition television.

The selections confirm statements by senior Pentagon aides that this and other programs associated with Darpa's former director, Craig Fields, will continue. Mr. Fields reportedly lost his job because his Pentagon superiors objected to his advocacy of a strong U.S. effort to promote new technologies to meet the challenge of other industrial nations.

The Pentagon said the selections complete the process of identifying U.S. companies that are qualified to work in the high-definition display field. Ten other U.S. companies, including Xerox Corp. and Texas Instruments Inc., were admitted to the program earlier.

Zenith said the federal government plans to spend at least $1 million to finance part of the company's research on displays for military computers. Though the award is relatively small, it will be a boost for Zenith, which is spending $10 million of its own money this year for research on high-definition television systems, which promise sharper pictures and sound than current television sets deliver.

The grant gives Zenith help in its effort to come up with larger-screen versions of its so-called flat tension mask color picture tubes. Zenith's clear, flat displays now are used for computers, but the company hopes to use them for HDTV sets in the future.

Zenith is battling larger and better-financed foreign companies to come up with an HDTV system for the U.S.
