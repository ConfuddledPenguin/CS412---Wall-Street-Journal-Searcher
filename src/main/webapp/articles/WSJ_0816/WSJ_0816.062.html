Wednesday's Markets: Bond Prices, Stocks Post Small Gains --- Mideast Continues To Influence Trading; Dollar Ends Mixed

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 08/16/90

NEW YORK -- Stock and bond prices continued to dance to the tune of the Mideast, gyrating through the day before ending with tiny gains. Long-term U.S. Treasury bonds ended up 1/8 point, or $1.25 for each $1,000 of face amount, in thin trading. Stock prices followed the bond market in lock step. The Dow Jones Industrial Average eked out a 0.50 point rise to 2748.27. The dollar ended mixed.

Bonds jumped more than half a point early in the day on reports that Iraq might consider leaving Kuwait in return for a freeze in the U.S. military buildup in the Persian Gulf. Oil prices drifted lower on the news. Then President Bush said he sees no hope of a diplomatic solution until Iraqi troops leave Kuwait. That helped halt the slide in oil prices and sent bonds tumbling nearly a full point.

"The bond market is on a teeter-totter," said Roberta Olson, fixed-income portfolio manager at Banc One Asset Management Corp. "One minute it's focusing on political developments, the next on what's going on in our economy," she said.

Bond prices rose early in the day in part on the news that July industrial production was unchanged from the June level. That cheered investors who expected a slight rise. Any evidence pointing to a weaker economy tends to calm fears that inflation will rise and erode the value of fixed-income investments.

But as soon as Mr. Bush came out later in the morning with his hawkish comments, Ms. Olson said, traders decided that "we still have a hot potato on our hands" in the Mideast and took profits.

The notion that peace was just around the corner helped the stock market as well early on, pushing the Dow industrials up more than 20 points. "There was this breath of fresh air," said Hugh A. Johnson Jr., chief investment officer at First Albany Corp., "and investors came out of the bunkers." But Mr. Bush's comments and the subsequent bond market reaction quickly erased the gain.

The dollar started the day by resuming the previous day's sharp decline, then turned around and rose on indications that the Mideast turmoil wasn't over.

But several currency traders said they wouldn't bet that Mr. Bush's saber rattling will protect the dollar long from further declines because of weakness in the U.S. economy.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose on volume of 135.2 million shares in New York Stock Exchange trading. Advancing issues outnumbered decliners, 887 to 619.

Bond prices rose. The yield on the Treasury's 30-year bond eased to 8.76%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5660 marks and 147.65 yen, compared with 1.5645 marks and 148.78 yen Tuesday.
