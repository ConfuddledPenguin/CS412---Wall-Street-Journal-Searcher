Cut in Ratings Of Japan Debt Spills Into U.S.

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 08/16/90

NEW YORK -- Fallout from the downgrading earlier this week of two of the largest Japanese banks' long-term debt ratings spread to the municipal debt market. Moody's Investors Service Inc. downgraded $13.3 billion in the debt of many U.S. municipalities backed by the Japanese banks' credit. The municipal debt downgradings were touched off by Moody's downgradings earlier this week of long-term debt ratings for Fuji Bank Ltd. and Dai-Ich Kangyo Bank Ltd. to double-A-1 from triple-A. The reduction of long-term debt ratings in turn triggered a similar reduction in the letter-of-credit ratings at the two banks.

Typically, American cities and towns that have poor credit ratings find it worthwhile to pay a fee to better-rated banks to back their debt with the banks' own name. The banks do so by issuing letters of credit.

Moody's action yesterday affects only the debt backed by the two Japanese banks and does not necessarily affect the overall credit rating of the municipalities. However, the downgrading of these two Japanese banks and several others earlier reduces the number of triple-A rated institutions available to back debt issued by American cities. As a result, those cities may have to pay higher rates when they borrow in future.

Dai-Ichi is the world's largest bank by asset size; Fuji is the fourth largest.

Laura Levenstein, assistant vice president at Moody's, said the letter of credit and subsequent municipal debt moves were the logical outcome of the long-term debt changes.

"The letter of credit ratings rank equally with the long-term debt ratings, so when long-term ratings come down so do the letter of credit ratings," she said.

Moody's said earlier that it downgraded Dai-Ichi Kangyo's long-term debt rating because of its relatively high real-estate exposure in Japan, rising interest rates and high overhead ratio. The credit-rating service reduced Fuji's debt ratings because of its lower core profitability and higher risk profile compared with its peer banks.

Japanese banks have been among the most aggressive issuers of letters of credit and other guarantees in the U.S. in recent years. To a large extent they capitalized on their superior credit ratings to provide letters of credit at prices that undercut U.S. banks, most of which aren't top-rated credit risks.
