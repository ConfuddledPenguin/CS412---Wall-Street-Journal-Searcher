Two Ex-Owners Of Failed Thrift Guilty of Fraud

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 08/16/90

DALLAS -- The former owners of the defunct Guaranty Federal Savings & Loan Association of Dallas were convicted of bank fraud and other charges after a three-month trial that included 18 days of jury deliberations. Paul Sau-Ki Cheng and Edward Heath were convicted of charges related to Guaranty Federal's financing of a Florida property owned by the two men's closely held company, Pacific Realty Corp. Guaranty Federal was an affiliate of Pacific Realty until the thrift failed in September 1988 with almost $2 billion in assets.

Federal regulators attributed the thrift's failure to risky real estate ventures, such as the Florida deal, and Messrs. Cheng's and Heath's failed attempt to acquire Houston-based U.S. Home Corp. in 1986 through Pacific Realty.

The attempted takeover of U.S. Home resulted in about $68.5 million in losses at Guaranty Federal, according to a civil lawsuit pending against Messrs. Cheng and Heath and now-defunct Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., which was U.S. Home's investment banker.

The lawsuit, filed in February in Dallas federal court by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., also alleges that Messrs. Cheng and Heath diverted profits from trading in government securities to entities they controlled while saddling Guaranty Federal with losses from the trades.

The allegations in the civil lawsuit are unrelated to the criminal charges on which Messrs. Cheng and Heath were convicted. The criminal charges included bank fraud, misapplication of thrift funds and wire fraud stemming from Guaranty Federal's $10 million loan for the Florida property.

A portion of the charges dealt with false information provided by Messrs. Cheng and Heath in order to obtain an inflated appraisal of the Florida property to justify the loan. U.S. Justice Department prosecutor Robert Hauberg, of the Dallas Bank Fraud Task Force, said the convictions of the two men are significant because false-appraisal charges typically are difficult to prove to a jury.

Mr. Cheng, 35 years old, was found guilty of 10 separate charges and faces a maximum possible sentence of 70 years in federal prison. Edward Heath, 36, was convicted of 10 charges and faces a maximum possible sentence of 80 years in federal prison.

Both men were acquitted of conspiracy and a handful of other, lesser charges.

Guaranty Federal and two other failed Texas thrifts were sold in September 1988 to a joint venture led by Temple-Inland Inc. of Diboll, Texas. Temple-Inland has since bought out its joint-venture partners and is sole owner of the thrift, now known as Guaranty Federal Savings Bank.
