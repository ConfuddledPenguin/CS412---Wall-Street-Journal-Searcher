Bank of New York's Petition to Acquire Northeast Bancorp Is Denied by Fed

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease and Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 08/16/90

The Federal Reserve Board denied Bank of New York Co.'s petition to acquire Northeast Bancorp, ending a bitter dispute between the two banks over what constituted a fair price for Northeast. The Fed said that even at the $195.6 million price that Bank of New York has said it would be willing to pay for Northeast, the acquisition would hurt Bank of New York's ability to maintain a strong capital position.

When the banks first reached a tentative agreement on the purchase last year Bank of New York said it would pay 250% of Northeast's book value. Northeast, which is the parent of Union Trust Co., Connecticut's third-biggest commercial bank, claimed a book value of $33.53 a share as recently as March 30, 1990, which would indicate a purchase price of about $500 million.

But Bank of New York has been arguing for some time that Northeast's true book value was something more closely akin to $12.31 a share, based on the deteriorating outlook for its real estate loan portfolio. Some analysts suggested that Bank of New York was more interested in calling off the deal than proceeding. Earlier this year Northeast sued Bank of New York in an effort to force it to complete the merger agreement or pay damages.

During the course of the dispute Northeast's shares have fluctuated from a high of $72, when the deal appeared ironclad, to a low of $14.50. Yesterday's decision came after the stock market closed. Northeast stock closed yesterday at $17.50, up $2, in national over-the-counter trading. Bank of New York said that in light of the Fed decision "the acquisition cannot proceed." Officials of Northeast couldn't be reached.

The Fed decision ends the dispute between the two banks, but raises questions about the ability of Bank of New York and other large banks to make acquisitions. The Fed noted in its decision that it "expects banking organizations contemplating expansion proposals to maintain strong capital levels substantially above the minimum levels specified in the board's capital adequacy guidelines. . . ." Bank of New York's proposed acquisition of Northeast "is inconsistent" with that policy, the Fed said.

Yet bankers and bank analysts agree that one of the basic solutions to the banking industry's mounting problems is a massive wave of consolidation that permits banks to slash overhead costs while expanding their market reach. But that consolidation may be jeopardized by the inability of many proposed mergers to meet the Fed's capital guideline tests.
