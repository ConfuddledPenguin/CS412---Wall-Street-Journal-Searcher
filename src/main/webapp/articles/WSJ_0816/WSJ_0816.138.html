Negative Net Worth Of $294 Million Is Shown for Trump

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 08/16/90

The New Jersey Casino Control Commission disclosed previously confidential documents concerning Donald J. Trump's empire that show that the developer could have a negative net worth of as much as $294 million. The documents also show that even with a $65 million bank bailout and partial suspension of debt payments, Mr. Trump's operations are expected to have a negative cash flow of $106 million between May 1990 and April 1991.

The report lists Mr. Trump's debts as totaling $3.21 billion and his assets ranging in value from $2.91 billion to $4.62 billion. The lower number is the value of the assets if they were forced to be sold today, while the higher number anticipates values in a few years if the economy improves. If the higher figure were applied, Mr. Trump would have a net worth of $1.41 billion.

Essentially the report spells out just how leveraged Mr. Trump became in amassing over the past decade the assets he fondly calls his "trophies." While the Plaza Hotel, Trump Shuttle and Taj Mahal contributed to building Mr. Trump's image as a purveyor of glitz, the documents show that the purchases contributed to his near ruin.

The report could also be embarrassing to Mr. Trump's bankers because it details exactly how much each of them lent to him. On Tuesday, the banks signed the final documents securing the $65 million bailout.

Through a spokeswoman, Mr. Trump declined to comment.

The documents were filed as part of Mr. Trump's petition to have the commission, which must approve any major changes in a casino's financing, clear the $65 million bailout. Mr. Trump's lawyers, who tried to stop the release of the documents, had claimed that the information contained in the report could prove damaging to Mr. Trump if it became public.

The commission is scheduled to hold hearings today and tomorrow, and is expected to issue a decision tomorrow.

If the commission rejects the bank deal, the banks would then either have to put together another loan or declare Mr. Trump in default of numerous loans on which he is by now overdue.

Among the thousands of pages of released documents, the most sensitive material appears to be a review of Mr. Trump's assets that was performed by the accounting firm of Kenneth Leventhal & Co. on June 14, 1990. That firm is working for Mr. Trump and the banks to devise a financial plan to put the Trump Organization on sound financial footing.

The accounting firm arrived at the numbers based on its independent analysis and data provided by Mr. Trump. "We were asked by the company at the request of the banks to come up with a plan for the Trump Organization," said Stan Ross, co-managing director of the firm. "Mr. Trump is not unique among developers with this kind of problem."

The Leventhal report shows that, without the bailout, Mr. Trump would be expected to spend $6.2 million on personal and household expenses from May 1990 to April 1991. Under the bank arrangement, his personal spending is limited to $450,000 per month in 1990 ($5.4 million), $375,000 a month in 1991 and $300,000 a month in 1992 and thereafter.

The report also anticipates that the cost of maintaining Mr. Trump's yacht, the Trump Princess, would be $9.3 million a year. The Trump Regency Hotel is expected to have a loss of $5.6 million, the Trump Castle Hotel and Casino is slated for a loss of $21.5 million, and the upkeep on a 70-acre real estate vacant parcel in Manhattan is expected to cost Mr. Trump $18.7 million.

Of 22 assets owned by Mr. Trump listed, three, including the Trump Plaza Hotel and Casino in Atlantic City, are expected to show a profit in the next 12 months.

The documents anticipate that Mr. Trump's recently published "Trump: Surviving at the Top," will net the developer $1.1 million. Royalties from his board game will amount to $261,000.

One person close to Mr. Trump said that were the Leventhal documents to be prepared today, the valuations of his assets would likely be even lower.

As for the banks, the Leventhal report indicates that Citicorp's Citibank unit is by far Mr. Trump's biggest creditor, having lent Mr. Trump $993 million. Of that amount, about $320 million was believed to be personally guaranteed by Mr. Trump. Citibank sold the majority of its loans to over 50 other lending institutions.

Mr. Trump also took out a total of $135 million in unsecured loans, of which $104 million was lent by Bankers Trust Co., a unit of Bankers Trust New York Corp. All told, the amount Mr. Trump is believed to have borrowed that is either unsecured or personally guaranteed is about $800 million.

He also borrowed $47 million from Boston Co.'s Boston Safe Deposit & Trust Co. unit for the purchase of his Florida residences, his yacht and "personal use."
