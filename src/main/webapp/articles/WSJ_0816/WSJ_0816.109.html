Technology: IBM Sets Patent Pact With Sun Microsystems

  • Author: Marilyn Chase
  • Date: 08/16/90

Sun Microsystems Inc. became the latest company to feel the heat from International Business Machines Corp.'s aggressive patent-enforcement policy. The maker of high-end workstations signed a cross-licensing agreement covering IBM's entire patent portfolio plus all patents filed before July 1, 1995.

While Sun and IBM never went to court over their patents, the move marks the end of negotiations that began two years ago, when IBM wrote Sun a letter suggesting that it "needed a license to some of their patents," said Michael Morris, vice president and general counsel of Sun, based in Mountain View, Calif.

IBM's vast portfolio of technology encompasses 10,000 U.S. utility patents, and it grows at a rate of 600 applications a year. "With a portfolio of such breadth and depth, you run the risk of inadvertent infringement," Mr. Morris acknowledged. Sun's portfolio, he added, numbers just 25, with 106 applications pending.

IBM has been insistent about requiring that smaller companies take licenses to its technology. In 1989, IBM struck a similar cross-licensing pact with Compaq Computer Corp., requiring the Houston-based maker of PC clones to pay an estimated $100 million over five years.

While its terms with Sun weren't disclosed, some analysts believe Sun paid considerably less. "It's very different," said Thomas T. Rooney, an analyst with Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. in New York. "Compaq shipped a lot of product perceived to be infringing," whereas Sun doesn't engage in the same head-to-head competition. Sun said it will pay a net royalty payment of a fixed dollar amount to IBM in five annual installments.

"Normally, such agreements amount to 1% to 3% of related revenues," Mr. Rooney said. "It's not going to be significant to Sun." Sun said in its statement that it doesn't expect materially adverse impact on its financial performance. In national over-the-counter trading yesterday, Sun stock declined early in the day and closed at $28.25, down 75 cents. IBM stock rose 62.5 cents to close at $105 a share in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

While the companies didn't specify which products or technologies were at the heart of the deal, some analysts believe it has to do with IBM's original RISC -- or reduced instruction set computing -- technology dating from the 1970s. RISC has since been widely adopted throughout the computer industry.

Other possible points of contention, according to Mr. Rooney, are IBM's semiconductor-fabrication technology and its microchannel-based products, which relate to its PC bus architecture, which is a sort of data highway into which all information from chips and other parts flows.

"It may be that Sun is looking to bring RISC technology to the desktop in the form of microchannel-compatible products," Mr. Rooney speculated. "That would help Sun further penetrate the low end of the market." Most of Sun's products sell for $20,000, but it has recently introduced products in the $5,000-to-$10,000 range.

An IBM spokesman called the deal "a typical arrangement," asserting such cross-licensing pacts "help keep one's assets in a certain amount of control." The Armonk, N.Y., computer giant now has an unspecified amount of cash to help defray its massive research and development budget.

Some also speculate that IBM, in gaining access to Sun's patents, might find a use for Sun's proprietary file-server technology.

But on balance, Mr. Rooney of Donaldson Lufkin says, "It's a face-saving device to call this cross-licensing. What does IBM need from Sun?" Probably not much, apart from its possible interest in Sun's file-server technology, he added. "It's a mistake to blow this out of proportion."

The agreement is expected to remove a potential liability from Sun's books. Since IBM served notice with its 1988 letter, Sun's annual report has routinely disclosed the matter as a "contingency," while downplaying its potential impact on Sun's books.

Mr. Morris, Sun's general counsel, said the licensing agreement won't change Sun's primary business focus. "Our philosophy from the start has always been to pursue an open systems architecture that can communicate with products of IBM and others." he said.

With one important difference, he adds. "This agreement gives us access to patented technology that we can incorporate into our products." He didn't elaborate on potential applications.

Mr. Morris refused to respond to any speculation about possible applications of IBM technology. "We didn't focus on any specific technology in our part of the negotiations," he said.

"We don't have any current plan to incorporate IBM technology into our products," he added, whether it be RISC or microchannel products. "Legally, we can. But whether or not we do is subject to further evaluation."

Sun's fiscal 1990 profit rebounded strongly from a depressed 1989 performance to $11.2 million, or $1.21 a share. Revenue grew 40% to $2.47 billion. During the year ended June 30, Sun logged both record bookings and shipments, and increased its world-wide installed base of workstations and file servers to 285,000 units.
