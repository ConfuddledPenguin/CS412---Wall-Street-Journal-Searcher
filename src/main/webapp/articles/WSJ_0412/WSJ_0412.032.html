LEISURE & ARTS: TV: The Circus, the Choir, a Civil War Love Story

  • Author: Martha Bayles
  • Date: 04/12/90

Friday, April 13, 8-9 p.m. EDT, on CBS: "120th Edition of Ringling Bros. and Barnum & Bailey Circus." The black panther and the white rhinoceros, Johnny Peers and his clowning dogs, the spinning whips of Argentina's Los Gauchos Latinos -- they'll all be there, kicking up the traditional sawdust.

Friday, April 13, 10-11 p.m. EDT, on PBS (PBS air dates and times vary, so check local listings): "The Mormon Tabernacle Choir: An Easter Gift of Music." Dame Kiri Te Kanawa is the soloist in this program of inspirational music ranging from Bach to Wagner to Sir Henry Bishop.

Sunday, April 15, 4-6 p.m. EDT, on ABC: "MacDonald's All-American High School Basketball Game." Another talent search for the nation's hottest high school players will culminate in a game that also is a charitable fund-raiser -- this year's proceeds will go to institutions in the Indianapolis area.

Monday, April 16, 8-9:45 p.m. EDT, on TNT (repeated April 16, 17, 21 and 22): "The Rose and the Jackal." According to this fictionalized version of Civil War history, Allan Pinkerton (the founder of the American Secret Service and the famous detective agency) fell in love with Rose O'Neal Greenhow (a beautiful Confederate spy) while prosecuting her. No one knows whether such a romance actually occurred. But this wonderfully stirring film, starring Christopher Reeve and Madolyn Smith Osborne, will make you wish it had.

Monday, April 16, 8-9 p.m. EDT and PDT, on HBO (repeated April 19, 24, 29, May 2 and 11): "History of the NBA." Ever since Dr. James Naismith took that first rim shot a century ago, the game has been getting better -- as evidenced by this lively film, produced with the cooperation of the NBA.

Monday, April 16, 8-9 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "Profit the Earth." The return of Earth Day (April 22) doesn't necessarily mean the return of 1960s-style environmentalism. This film offers some hope that the problems can be addressed in ways that move beyond reflexive anti-business attitudes.

Friday, April 20-Sunday, April 22: Earth Day programming. TDC will present a group of films exposing various problems and issuing warnings: "Amazonia" (Friday, 9-11 p.m. EDT); the three-part series "Earth" (each night, 8-9 p.m. EDT); and "Where Have All the Dolphins Gone?" (Sunday 9-10 p.m. EDT). ABC takes the concerned-celebrity route with an entertainment special: "Time Warner Presents the Earth Day Special" (Sunday, 9-11 p.m. EDT). And A&E offers the 27-part documentary, "Challenge of the Seas" (beginning Sunday, 8-9 p.m. EDT and continuing on Saturdays, 7:30-8 p.m. EDT).

Friday, April 20, 9-10:30 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "Largo Desolato." Written (appropriately enough) in 1984, this gloomy but wry play by Vaclav Havel stars F. Murray Abraham as a dissident Czech writer about to be interrogated by the secret police. If it makes you curious about the playwright, recently elected president of Czechoslovakia, stay tuned for "Havel's Audience With History" (10:30-11:30 p.m. EDT), a documentary that tries to capture the essence of two different productions (one Czech, one American) of Havel's best-known play, "The Audience," while also tracing his life and character.

Saturday, April 21, 10-11:30 p.m. EDT, on Showtime (repeated April 26, May 2, 8 and 13): "Showtime Coast to Coast: All Star Edition." Most popular-music specials on TV are too packaged and categorized. The same cannot be said of this smart, eclectic series, now launching its third season with a post-Grammys jam session featuring B.B. King, Herbie Hancock, Bonnie Raitt, Michael Bolton and Rickie Lee Jones, among others.

Sundays, April 22-May 20, 9-10 p.m. EDT (the first episode is two hours long), on PBS: "Traffik." We Americans are so accustomed to thinking about the drug crisis in terms of cocaine, Central America and the U.S. that it comes as a shock to see the same tragic scenario being played out in terms of heroin, Pakistan and Europe. Few anti-drug films are as starkly riveting as this Channel Four production filmed on location in northwest Pakistan, Karachi, Hamburg and London. More in Monday's column.

Sunday, April 22, 10 p.m.-midnight EDT, on HBO (repeated April 25, 30, May 3, 8 and 11): "The Investigation: Inside a Terrorist Bombing." More starkly riveting stuff from the British Isles. Only this time, the subject is the 1974 IRA bombings that killed 21 people. This docudrama, starring John Hurt and Martin Shaw, means to stir up controversy about the guilt of those convicted, the so-called Birmingham Six.
