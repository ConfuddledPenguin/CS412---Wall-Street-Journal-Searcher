As Economy Crawls Along, Old-Fashioned Stock Picking Picks Up

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 04/12/90

NEW YORK -- In a bull market, it's easy for investors to grab a bunch of stocks in a strong industry and watch the returns soar. But in an uncertain market like today's, that strategy may not work. With the economy crawling along in 1990, some money managers are opting for eclectic portfolios of carefully selected stocks. With old-fashioned stock picking, the money managers hope to outperform their sector-betting peers.

"We're in a slower economy; a rolling recession not only affects industries, but individual companies within industries," says Ellen Harris, chief domestic investment officer at Mitchell Hutchins Asset Management Inc., the adviser to PaineWebber Inc. mutual funds. "You need strong economic signals to create sector trends -- down or up."

No doubt, industry groups fall and rise even when markets and the economy are moving sideways. Air freight and semiconductor stocks, for example were two top performers in the first quarter, with gains of 19.6% and 14.33%, respectively. (Standard & Poor's 500 stock index fell 3% in the quarter.) But lately, there is divergence among companies within many industry groups, and that's why some stock pickers are excelling this year.

Currently, "investors are focusing a lot more on company-specific issues, rather than the group," says John Lazlo, an analyst who covers semiconductor stocks for PaineWebber. "There used to be group moves, but now stocks move company by company."

For example, Mr. Lazlo notes that Intel jumped 21% in the first quarter, while National Semiconductor gained a more modest 7%. Why the difference? Intel dominates the market in certain chip products, Mr. Lazlo says. National Semiconductor competes in chip products that are also produced by other suppliers.

Also, the technical shape of the stock market this year seems to favor stock pickers. Though the market has been rising in recent weeks, the gains are concentrated in fewer stocks lately. The advance-decline, which adds up the difference between declining and advancing issues on the New York Stock Exchange, "has been falling since August, showing internal market weakness," says Robert Dickey, technical analyst at Dain Bosworth Inc.

Skillful sector players did well last year by picking a few well-performing industry groups, like oil or beverage stocks. Sector players are "top-down" investors. They believe they can spot macroeconomic trends in the world economy that will benefit a specific industry.

Sometimes, the top-down strategists try to predict shifts in the supply and demand of certain commodities, like copper or oil. Other times, they try to be the first to spot a major trend in consumer habits, such as greater use of personal computers. A top-down strategist's tool box is full of forecasts and statistics on interest and exchange rates, the money supply and economic growth around the world, to name a few.

For a "bottom-up" strategist, it's secondary if a company is big or small, makes shoes or gasoline. These investors thumb through stock guides and research reports in search of companies that have strong franchises, unique products, dominant market share and a host of other criteria that might make the company special.

Typically, most money managers use a blend of these two strategies, but are biased toward one discipline or the other.

George Bennett, the portfolio manager of Massachusetts Managed Sectors Trust, must be invested in five or fewer sectors of the stock market at any one time, according to his fund's charter. Yet Mr. Bennett says he is a stock picker at heart and has brought a "bottom-up" discipline to a "top-down" fund. This past year, this combined approach earned the fund a roaring 42% gain. But the first quarter has been tougher. To date, Mr. Bennett says he lags the market by "two or three percent."

"Sector investing becomes difficult when the market is going through a transition period like it is now," Mr. Bennett says. "You don't have a momentum in sectors; I can't really move in this market."

Mr. Bennett still argues that the current muddle in the market and economy make top-down analysis all the more important. "I have been hurt by macroeconomic factors," such as the rise in interest rates and the scare over credit quality in financial stocks, he says. Many companies in his portfolio, he says, are fundamentally sound, even though they've been trampled by the herd.

Graham Tanaka, president of Tanaka Capital Management Inc., keeps a macroeconomic outlook in mind, but it's not his main focus when he is hunting for stocks. "Buying sectors is almost as tough as predicting the direction of the market," he says. Mr. Tanaka is a bottom-up investor, and his main investing criterion is earnings growth. His largest holding is Airborne Freight Corp., up 35% this year. Other holdings among his 18 stocks include Intel, Humana Inc., Federal National Mortgage Association and American Cyanamid Co.

"The common theme among these stocks is that we believe earnings will be in three years 40% to 100% higher than they are now," he says. Mr. Tanaka's accounts were up about 2% in the first quarter, about five percentage points better than the S&P 500.

Stock pickers don't always win big, of course. One of Mr. Tanaka's holdings, National Data Corp., is down 27% this year. The company yesterday reported third-quarter earnings fell 4.4% from the year-earlier quarter. But Mr. Tanaka is sticking with his bet. Current earnings problems are related to the cost of the company's effort to build a new business, he says. National Data closed down $1 at $24.50 in over-the-counter trading yesterday. "We see it as a major buying opportunity," Mr. Tanaka says.
