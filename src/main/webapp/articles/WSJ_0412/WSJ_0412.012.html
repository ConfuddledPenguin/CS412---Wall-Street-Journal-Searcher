Credit Markets: Bonds Gyrate With Oil Prices, End Unchanged To Modestly Higher; Note Sale Goes as Expected

  • Author: Andrew Bary and Susan Scherreik Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/12/90

NEW YORK -- Bonds gyrated with the price of oil before ending unchanged to modestly higher. "It was the first time in a long time that the bond market tracked oil," said Kevin Flanagan, money-market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

While the bond market always has some concern about the inflationary implications of oil prices, it was the gyration in energy prices that caught the market's attention.

Bond prices reached their peak levels of the day when oil prices slumped. When oil prices later reversed direction and recouped those losses, bond-price gains were trimmed.

Traders said the erratic energy markets held center stage because there were few other developments to focus on during the last full trading day of the week. Most bond trading desks will close at 2 p.m. EDT today and will remain closed through tomorrow in observance of Good Friday.

The Treasury's sale of $7.52 billion of new seven-year notes went "as advertised," according to one trader. The 8.62% average yield was in line with expectations. However, dealers and analysts said there was some disappointment that institutional investors and the Japanese didn't show much interest.

The bulk of the issue was bought by Wall Street dealers for their own accounts, especially arbitrage desks, which purchased the new seven-year notes while selling other securities.

In trading, the benchmark 30-year Treasury bond ended nearly 1/4 point higher, a gain of about $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. In when-issued trading, the new seven-year notes were quoted at a yield of 8.63%, down from 8.64% immediately after the results were announced.

The mortgage-backed securities market received some bearish news from Lewis Ranieri, one of the market's founders who is chairman of Hyperion Capital Management Inc.

In a speech to institutional investors, Mr. Ranieri said the government's bailout of the thrift industry could result in huge sales of mortgage-backed securities. He noted that the Resolution Trust Corp., the government's conservator for insolvent thrifts, now has about $185 billion in assets and so far has sold very little.

Mr. Ranieri said most of the assets, which are whole loans and not securities, will be packaged into mortgage securities as thrift liquidations proceed. He estimates that the RTC could sell between $60 billion to $70 billion of mortgages in the next six months and a total of $100 billion over the next 18 months.

Based on the speed at which the government is seizing troubled savings and loans, Mr. Ranieri said about half of the $500 billion of mortgage securities currently in thrift portfolios could be sold over the next five years. The added supply, he said, could expand the size of the mortgage-backed securities market by 100%.

Junk Bonds

Prices of junk bonds, or high-yielding, speculative-grade securities, were mixed in trading that was driven by rumors.

The junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. surged on talk that Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. was snapping up the bonds.

But R.H. Macy & Co. bonds got clobbered on reports that one Wall Street firm was short-selling the bonds.

A spokesman at RJR, which is controlled by KKR, declined to comment on the speculation.

But dealers named Los Angeles-based Jefferies & Co. as a major buyer of RJR bonds yesterday. David Feinman, a junk bond trader at Jefferies, said the firm was "both a buyer and seller of the {RJR} securities." Mr. Feinman declined to comment on whether the firm was buying the securities for KKR, saying only that the firm handled trades in RJR securities "for a variety of customers."

Other traders said the buying by Jefferies caused other dealers to scramble to cover short sale positions. In a short sale, dealers sell borrowed securities and later seek to make a profit by buying the securities back at a lower price.

RJR's 13.71% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, finished up 3 3/4 points at 81 1/4 on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely held RJR issue, the 13.71% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, ended 4 3/4 points higher at 68 3/4.

RJR pay-in-kind bonds have been volatile for several weeks, falling on concerns that the coupon on the bonds would be reset at more than 20% and rising on speculation that the company will either launch a tender offer for the pay-in-kind issues or exchange them for a new security plus cash or equity or both.

Macy's 14 1/2% senior subordinated debentures due in 1998 fell two points to 75 while its 14 1/2% subordinated debentures due in 2001 were off two points at 62.

Merrill Lynch Capital Markets was rumored to be short-selling Macy bonds to hedge a long position in Ames Department Stores Inc. Bonds of Ames Department Stores plunged nine points Tuesday on news about the company's distressed financial condition. A Merrill spokesman declined to comment on the rumors.

Separately, Moody's Investors Service Inc. said credit rating downgrades of U.S. corporations outpaced upgrades by four to one in the first quarter. Moody's said ratings of 95 corporations representing about $75 billion of long-term debt were lowered, and ratings of 23 corporate issuers representing $26 billion of debt were upgraded.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 99 4/32 to yield 8.57%, compared with 98 30/32 to yield 8.59% on Tuesday. The latest 10-year notes ended at 99 5/32 to yield 8.61%, compared with 99 2/32 to yield 8.63%.

Short-term rates were slightly lower. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills was 7.76% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.00%, while the rate on six-month Treasury bills was 7.76% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.17%.

The Treasury's seven-year note auction attracted the largest amount of individual investor buying at such a sale since last April. The reason was the relatively high yield.

The 8.62% average yield on the notes was up from 8.02% at the previous seven-year note auction on Jan. 10 and was the highest since 9.39% on April 12, 1989.

The robust buying by individual investors, however, didn't totally offset the lack of interest by institutions.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

The $2.9 billion of Franklin Savings Association zero-coupon mortgage securities that were the subject of the recent decision by the Resolution Trust Corp. regarding post-insolvency interest are trading at close to their accreted value because of investor concerns about an early call.

The $800 million of bonds due in 2014 trade at 6 1/4; the $1.6 billion of bonds due in 2019 at about 4; and the $500 million of bonds due in 2024 at roughly 2 1/2, according to one trader.

These prices are slightly above their accreted values based on the yields of about 12% at the pricing in 1984, but the prices are down several points from levels before Franklin Savings' troubles emerged earlier this year. The institution is being operated under the conservatorship of the RTC after being seized in mid-February.

Zero-coupon bonds are sold at a deep discount to face value. The bonds then accrete, or slowly rise in value, until they reach their full face value at maturity.

The current price of the Franklin zero-coupon securities is a reflection of the investor belief that the bonds will be called.

In trading yesterday, most mortgage securities rose as much as 1/8 point.

Foreign Bonds

Japanese government bonds weakened as short-term interest rates and the value of the dollar rose.

In morning trading, the Bank of Japan bought 300 billion yen ($1.9 billion) of overnight commercial paper at 7%. Prior to this purchase and since the last discount rate rise on March 20, the central bank has bought commercial paper three times, once at 6.84% and twice at 6.88%.

Dealers said the increase in the rate at which the central bank bought commercial paper seems to indicate that the Bank of Japan is willing to let short-term rates go higher, thus making long-term debt with a lower rate a less-attractive investment.

Rates on new three-month certificates of deposit were 7.5%, up from 7.45% on Tuesday. The price of Japan's benchmark 10-year bond fell 0.28 to 86.23 to yield 7.31%.

Meanwhile, Canadian bonds rose between 3/8 and 3/4 point on improved sentiment. Traders said the better tone is an indication that investors believe rates have stabilized, at least for now, after rising for the past three weeks.

British bonds ended moderately lower after a seesaw day, as participants awaited today's release of several key economic indicators, including the March retail sales and employment reports. Britain's 11 3/4% benchmark bond due 2003/2007 fell 1/4 to 97 28/32 to yield 12.04%.

West German bonds ended stronger with the value of the mark, but trading was lifeless. Bonn's benchmark 10-year bond rose 0.38 to 94.30 to yield 8.63%.

Municipals

Tax-exempt issues were unchanged to 1/8 point higher in late trading, having barely budged.

What little life there was in the market was in the new-issue arena, where $187 million of new debt was priced.

Underwriters led by Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. reported an unsold balance of about $46 million for $119 million of Baltimore County consolidated public improvement bonds and metropolitan district bonds.

Meanwhile, an unsold balance of about $23.2 million remained for $68 million of Dallas limited tax bonds, won by a Prudential-Bache Capital Funding group.

Looking ahead, $53 million of Milwaukee general obligation corporate purpose bonds will be priced via competitive bid today. No large negotiated issues are expected to be priced today.

Among actively traded bonds, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was unchanged at 97 3/8 bid to yield 7.40%.
