Unisys, Honeywell Face Lawsuit by U.S. On Defense Job Costs

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/12/90

NEW YORK -- The Brooklyn U.S. attorney's office filed suit, charging Sperry Corp., Unisys Corp. and Honeywell Inc. with defrauding the government by inflating cost estimates on defense work. In 1986, Sperry and Burroughs Corp. merged to become Unisys. Later that year, Honeywell bought the Unisys division cited in the lawsuit, a maker of flight simulators to train pilots.

Spokesmen for Unisys, a Blue Bell, Pa., computer maker, and Honeywell, a Minneapolis business-machines company, said the companies hadn't been served with the suit and couldn't comment.

The suit, which seeks at least $48 million in damages, charges that in 1979 and 1980, Sperry underbid on one flight-trainer contract by $1.5 million to get a leg up on competitors when two succeeding contracts came up for bids. Sperry was awarded the next two pacts as well, but in its cost proposals prepared after the awards, it secretly inflated its anticipated costs, the complaint charges.

Earlier, a similar suit was filed by a former Sperry executive, Ralph F. D'Avino, under the federal "whistleblower" provision of the False Claims Act, according to the law firm that represents him, Baden Kramer Huffman & Brodsky here. Last Monday, U.S. Attorney Andrew J. Maloney's office filed a superseding complaint.

In a Securities and Exchange Commission filing, Honeywell disclosed "preliminary discussions" with the government about alleged "false claims and statements" by Sperry and Unisys. Honeywell said that the government indicated its single damage claim could total $48 million, but that the complaint seeks treble damages. Honeywell's filing said the action "stems from facts and circumstances arising, in significant part, prior to the company's acquisition of this portion of its business from Unisys." Thus, Honeywell said it believes it is covered by contractual indemnification provided by Unisys.

Separately, Unisys and partner Westinghouse Electric Corp., Pittsburgh, lost a contract to build part of the Navy's Aegis radar system.

In late March, Unisys said, the Navy "decided to discontinue second sourcing," dropping Unisys and Westinghouse, a defense, electrical-products and broadcasting concern. The first source is General Electric Co., a Fairfield, Conn., broadcaster and defense and industrial products company.

Unisys received the contract in April 1988. The company said it expects to pare some of the 4,000 employees at its Shipboard & Ground Systems unit in Great Neck, N.Y., but isn't sure how many. The first phase of the project was valued at $10 million for Unisys; the second phase, which was under way when the Navy dropped the second sources, had a $90 million value to Unisys. A Westinghouse spokesman couldn't be reached to comment.
