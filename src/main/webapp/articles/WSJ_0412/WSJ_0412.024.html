International: France's Economy Gets Strong Grades In Report by OECD

  • Author: This article was prepared by William Echikson in Paris And Tim Carrington in London
  • Date: 04/12/90

The French economy, shaking off its reputation for being unpredictable and inflationary, won a glowing assessment in the latest report by the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development. France has "surpassed expectations on many fronts," the Paris-based economic institution said. "The French economy could thus outperform the average of the other countries in the OECD on both the inflation and growth fronts for the first time since 1970." The OECD consists of the world's 24 major industrial economies.

The OECD's accolade coincides with changing perceptions in the investment community. Increasingly, France is seen to offer a measure of both sizzle and stability: It can expect healthy growth in the early 1990s but without succumbing to the inflation virus that's currently sapping Britain and threatening West Germany.

The French economy should grow at a rate of 3.1% in 1990, and 2.8% in 1991, with inflation kept well under control, the report said. Moreover, this follows two years of growth of greater than 3%. Political stability also is a hallmark these days, with legislative elections not scheduled until 1993 and a presidential vote until 1995.

Among the major European economies, France looks particularly strong. Britain is bracing for its inflation rate to surge above 9% this spring, amid anemic growth outside the oil sector. West Germany, Europe's economic engine and its historic monetary anchor, faces disruptions and considerable inflationary pressure from the pending economic and monetary union with East Germany.

According to the OECD, the recent international confidence in the French franc stems largely from its participation in the European Monetary System, which links currencies together within set exchange-rate bands.

Not everything is perfect in Paris. The OECD and independent economists point to the continuing aftershocks from a poor investment record over the past two decades, which they say prevents French industry from taking full advantage of new growth opportunities around Europe.

Another danger sign is the shortage of skilled labor despite relatively high unemployment of 9.4%. The OECD noted that France created 500,000 jobs in the past two years, but it worries that the increase will slow because of a lack of qualified workers.

For the future, the OECD recommended reform in tax and social benefits policy. French social insurance costs eat up the highest percentage of any major industrial country, 44% of GNP compared with 30% in the United States and 37% in Germany. Pension and health costs have risen particularly fast in France.
