Letters to the Editor: Census Undercounting Adds Up to a Big Loss

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/12/90

We commend you for your special supplement on the 1990 census (March 9), but would like to call your attention to the fact that "Census '90" virtually ignored the minority communities in this country and the issues they face in the coming census. While these issues most directly affect Hispanics, African-Americans and Asian-Americans, they also will greatly affect the general public once the counting is done. Major problems include undercounting of the Hispanic community and other minority groups, an insufficient number of Hispanic enumerators to go into those communities where Hispanics will be missed, and the lack of an outreach program by the U.S. Census Bureau. As a result, Hispanics and other minorities will once again face an undercount. An undercount in the census means decreased political power and fewer federal dollars going into communities across the country.

Arturo Vargas

Director

National Census "Make Yourself Count" Program

Mexican American Legal Defense and Educational Fund

Los Angeles

---

I was fascinated by your observation in "Census '90" that "computer whizzes have flourished in this statistical hothouse." Exactly a century ago, inventor Herman Hollerith saved the 1890 census from disaster and in doing so spawned a revolution in data processing. In 1880, the Census Office had been overwhelmed. It took seven to eight years to produce detailed reports by largely manual methods. If nothing were done by 1890, one census might overlap another and the whole process fall into chaos. Fortunately, Hollerith's punched-card tabulation system, developed for the 1890 census, saved the day.

Employing the first practical data-processing system, the Census Office completed tabulating most of the 1890 census in only two to three years, although Hollerith's system was used only for the population portion of the job. For the first time, it had been possible to draw every possible combination of data from the census schedules. And the machines saved the Census Office $5 million in labor costs.

Hollerith's punched-card machines made quite a hit with industry, too. By the time he sold his Tabulating Machine Co. in 1911, Hollerith's firm had about 100 commercial customers. His firm was merged with several others making butcher scales, time clocks and coffee grinders. In 1924, the new company changed its name to IBM.

Witnessing Hollerith's machines at work in the 1890 census, journalist T.C. Martin noted, "The apparatus works as unerringly as the mills of the Gods, but beats them hollow as to speed."

Geoffrey D. Austrian

Auburndale, Mass.
