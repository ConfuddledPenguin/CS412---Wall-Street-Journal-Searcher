Environment: Shell Pumps Cleaner Gas In `Dirtiest' Cities in U.S.

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 04/12/90

HOUSTON -- Shell Oil Co. began selling an environmentally cleaner gasoline at 2,000 stations in the "dirtiest" U.S. cities. But motorists may have to pony up more green to make their cars run clean. The effort by Shell is the broadest attempt by any oil company to sell so-called reformulated gasoline designed to reduce air pollution.

The introduction of the new fuel at this moment by the nation's biggest marketer of gasoline also comes just as Congress is hammering out new clean-air legislation that could force unwelcome changes on the oil industry.

Shell is selling its new gasoline in the nine metropolitan areas often cited as having the most severe air-quality problems. Moreover, the company is supplying its stations in Washington, D.C., which isn't near the top of the dirty-air list. Asked about the decision to add the nation's capital to its roster, Shell said Washington is part of the same "air shed" as Baltimore, which is high on the list.

Some other oil companies applauded Shell's effort, but downplayed the significance, in part because the industry has had the ability to produce such gasoline for a while. "It's not particularly revolutionary," said Allen Kozinski, vice president of research and development of Amoco Corp.'s gasoline operations. "It's no technical breakthrough," he added. Like many oil companies, Amoco has been testing new auto fuels.

But so far, other companies selling reformulated gasoline similar to Shell's are doing so only in select cities or only for old-model cars. Almost any car can use Shell's formulation.

That's because it will replace Shell's current super unleaded product in the markets involved, its highest-octane and most-expensive grade of gasoline. Not surprisingly, super unleaded fuels also carry the fattest profit margins in the industry.

Shell officials said it costs the company an extra two cents a gallon to make the new product, called SU 2000E, but they wouldn't say whether they will pass that cost on to motorists. "It will be priced competitively in the marketplace," according to Frank Richardson, chief executive officer of Shell, a unit of Royal Dutch/Shell Group.

Motorists who already buy top-of-the-line gasoline, generally with octane levels above 90, may only feel a price impact if market conditions allow Shell to pass along its added costs, or if enthusiasm runs so high that it can charge a premium over competitors' high-octane gasoline.

But for motorists who normally buy regular or midgrade gasoline and are motivated by environmental concern, a switch to the new formula could pinch the pocketbook. In effect, Shell is attempting the classic marketing tactic of trying to induce customers to "buy up."

Super unleaded -- Shell's and everyone else's -- can easily cost 15 cents to 20 cents a gallon more than regular unleaded at the pump. On a 15-gallon fill-up, an environmentally conscious motorist would pay as much as an extra $3 for the Shell fuel.

Shell officials defended their selection of the high-price end of the gasoline market for introduction of their cleaner fuel. By making it a high-octane product, Shell said, the fuel could be used by owners of the most powerful cars, economy cars, old cars and new cars. If it were a lower grade, it wouldn't be suitable for some of these categories.

Also, "it's something we think the customer wants and demands," said Steven Miller, president of Shell's refining and marketing operations. He expects a much more enthusiastic response than Shell received in 1970, when it launched a cleaner, lead-free gas and the public didn't care. "It was a technical success but a commercial failure," he said. Later, lead-free fuels became a requirement for new cars.

This time, he said, Shell views its new product as "only a first step. The reformulation of gasoline has only just begun."

In Washington, William Rosenberg, a Bush administration advocate of alternative fuels, praised Shell's efforts but noted the gasoline isn't as clean as fuels being proposed for 1995 and beyond in clean-air legislation now moving through Congress.

Mr. Rosenberg, head of the clean-air office in the Environmental Protection Agency, added that Shell's decision to market to disparate cities should dispel logistical concerns expressed by others in the oil industry about distributing special fuels.

Currently, other oil companies that are selling reformulated gasoline have limited themselves. Atlantic Richfield Co. sells a reformulated gas just on the West Coast and only for older vehicles. The Marathon Petroleum Co. unit of USX Corp. markets a new gasoline in all three octane grades, but only in Detroit. In Denver, the Conoco Inc. unit of Du Pont Co. sells reformulated gas; Amoco is testing liquified natural gas for some fleet vehicles; and Phillips Petroleum Co. is testing propane for others.

Like others who have announced reformulated gasolines, Shell said its version will reduce hydrocarbon and carbon monoxide emissions. It said, for example, a car operating in the summer in Los Angeles, which has the nation's worst problem, would emit 10% less hydrocarbon vapors and 13% less carbon monoxide. In New York, where the climate differs, the reductions would be about 6% in each category.

On the other hand, emissions of nitrogen oxides, an ingredient in smog, will rise, though less than 2%. Hydrocarbons are unburned fuel and contribute to ozone and smog, which in turn can cause respiratory irritation. Carbon monoxide is partly burned fuel and is poisonous.

Shell uses an additive called methyl tertiary-butyl ether, or MTBE, in place of butane to cut the gasoline's volatility while enhancing its octane. The resulting fuel helps reduce tailpipe emissions without hurting performance, and also cuts the amount of fuel that evaporates from a vehicle, especially on sunny days or in warm weather. Many areas of the country have already required some reductions in volatility in the summer.

Shell hasn't touched two other controversial components of gasoline, aromatics and olefins. A joint study by the auto and oil industries is trying to determine whether reducing their levels will benefit the environment.

Officials of Shell said the company was considering expanding production of the new gasoline, but that it can't right now because of physical constraints. The company said that while it's able to produce and distribute the new gasoline with only a "modest" capital investment, any expansion would be costly.
