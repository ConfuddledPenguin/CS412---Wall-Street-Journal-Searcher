Heard on the Street: Kodak Seems a Pretty Picture to One Analyst But Patent Suit by Polaroid Scares Off Others

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 04/12/90

PITTSBURGH -- A veteran Eastman Kodak watcher named Eugene Glazer had a nightmare the other night. He dreamed that Kodak was ordered to pay $3 billion in damages to settle the patent case won by Polaroid in 1985. Mr. Glazer, a Dean Witter analyst, has reason to toss and turn. After two years of keeping a "neutral to negative" rating on Kodak, he recently took a warmer view of the shares of the Rochester, N.Y., marketer of photographic, chemical and drug products.

Most of his colleagues disagree. They see the Polaroid legal situation as sufficient reason to avoid the stock for now. A decision on damages is expected soon, as a judge appears nearly ready to impose an award if a settlement isn't reached. And the Street consensus is that Kodak will have to pay between $1 billion and $2 billion for infringing on Polaroid's instant-photography patents.

Other negatives loom: Revenue remains sluggish in the face of weak foreign currencies. A lackluster economy is dragging down Kodak's chemical division, which posted excellent results last year. The information segment, which includes copiers and electronic printers, was plagued by high costs and production problems last year, and still isn't generating as much revenue as expected. Savings from the company's current pruning programs, which some believe are too little, too late, won't come until the second half.

David Nelson, analyst at Shearson Lehman Hutton, figures that Kodak's stock could fall an additional 10% to 15%. "We think Kodak is a cheap stock but there's no reason to think it will be less cheap in the immediate future," he says. "A trust officer at a bank would have a tough sell pushing investment in Kodak, particularly in advance of the damage settlement."

The only thing that would change his outlook is surprisingly strong earnings -- and that isn't in the cards, Mr. Nelson asserts. Wall Street, too, is doubtful. The consensus forecast for full-year earnings has been falling. Now, most analysts predict Kodak will earn less than $4 a share, down from the $4.30 they were estimating last fall. Profit for 1989 was $3.32 a share before $875 million in restructuring charges.

So why is Mr. Glazer so optimistic? Based on his profit estimate for this year, Kodak trades at a 35% discount from the price-earnings ratios of the market's industrial stocks in general -- mainly because, in his view, it already reflects all the bad news.

At yesterday's close of 39 7/8 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Kodak is near its 52-week low of 36 7/8. Mr. Glazer believes Kodak could trade at 49 a share within a year in a flat stock market, based on estimated 1990 earnings of $4.10 a share.

He sees good news in Kodak's fundamental operations. Cost-cutting programs should yield savings soon, he says, raising the potential for an upside earnings surprise in subsequent quarters. He estimates savings at $350 million in 1990, and $450 million to $500 million annually -- about $1 a share -- in years beyond.

Moreover, pricing pressures on the crucial film and photographic paper businesses seem to have eased. Kodak, which has been unable to put through price increases in recent years because of tough competition, recently raised prices as much as 5% on amateur film. And rivals have seemed willing to follow Kodak's lead.

There are a lot of "ifs" in Mr. Glazer's scenario. For one thing, he doesn't factor in any changes in the currency. He also assumes the economy will slow but won't plunge into a recession.

As for the black cloud of Polaroid, he says, Wall Street already has discounted Kodak stock to account for a $1 billion to $2 billion award.

But Wall Street seems to feel that investors are braced for an award of as much as $2 billion. In fact, if the settlement is below $2 billion, Kodak's stock could rise, Mr. Glazer says. (Of course, he concedes, Kodak's stock could drop $2 a share for every $1 billion above a $2 billion determination.)

Once the damages are determined, he thinks other contrarians, who have sat on the sidelines for fear of a huge settlement, will begin buying Kodak stock. Particularly prime for investment are institutional investors, which hold only 48% of Kodak stock compared with a more typical 65% to 70% for large-company stocks.

"The only thing holding {the institutions} out is the fear of an upside surprise on damages," he contends. "It's an ideal contrarian type of stock."
