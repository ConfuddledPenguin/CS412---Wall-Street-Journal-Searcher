Finding Charity Just Too Taxing, They Make Some Nice Donations

  • Author: Linda K. Nathan
  • Date: 04/12/90

Where can you get something for nothing in New York? With April 15 around the corner, the answer seems to be lying in the streets. Since the Tax Reform Act of 1986, donating personal property to charity doesn't seem to pay anymore. With that in mind, more upper-crust residents are forgoing deductions and simply putting their discards out on the street.

"If you donate a $500 table, for example, you have to indicate when you bought it, how much you paid, and the fair market value," says Stanley Person, senior partner in Person & Co., an accounting firm. All that hassle "puts people off." Moreover, Stephen Seltzer, another CPA, says today the effective rate for tax savings on personal property deductions for a New York City resident is only 37%, compared with 60% before 1986. In other words, the incentive is gone.

To the delight of tasteful scavengers. Ruth Manning, president of a personnel company and a hunter of terrific trash in her fashionable Upper East Side neighborhood, observes that "New Yorkers are more capricious about the way they dispose of goods; they're not as concerned with giving to charity anymore because it's not as much to their benefit." Ms. Manning says she has amassed chairs, tables, lamps and an antique bird cage, thanks to her "good eye" and her habit of early-morning jogging.

Sometimes the best stuff doesn't even make it to the curb. A Park Avenue resident, an art dealer who declines to be named, describes the "private enterprise" of a small cartel of building superintendents. "When someone dies in these posh buildings," he says, "the heirs want to get rid of things fast" without cumbersome paper work required for donations. The supers take it off their hands -- and promptly sell it to neighbors in the know.

Jonathan Roskes, an international bank examiner with the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, praises the pickings in his Upper West Side neighborhood, partly because he puts out his own things. After all, he reasons, it's easier placing them on the street "than selling them, packing and labeling them, or arranging garage sales."

He cautions that some finds are better left behind. The Columbia graduate claims classmates of his got more than they bargained for with a found rug. "They lugged the carpet back to our dorm, unrolled it, and discovered a dead body inside."
