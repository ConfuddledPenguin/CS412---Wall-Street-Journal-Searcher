Default Rate Falls Slightly For Federal Student Loans

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/12/90

WASHINGTON -- The default rate on federally guaranteed student loans fell slightly, but Education Department officials said the decline probably doesn't reflect the impact of a new program to reduce defaults of tuition loans. The default rate fell to 15.6% in fiscal 1988 from 17.3% in fiscal 1987. Officials said that about 2.5 million loans, totaling $9.2 billion, entered the repayment phase in fiscal 1988 and that about 385,000 loans, totaling $1.1 billion, were in default by the end of fiscal 1989, which ended Sept. 30. A loan doesn't fall into default until a payment is 180 days overdue.

Last June, the government launched a default reduction program aimed at schools with default rates of more than 20%. Of the 5,226 post-secondary schools tracked by the Education Department, nearly 1,600 have default rates of more than 20%. If these institutions don't lower their default rates, their ability to offer guaranteed student loans will be limited or suspended. Last year, the department reported that vocational schools had the highest default rates, followed by two-year colleges and then four-year colleges.

Some of the reduction in the default rate reflects technical changes in the way the rate is calculated. Without these changes, the default rate would have been 16.5%, rather than 15.6%.
