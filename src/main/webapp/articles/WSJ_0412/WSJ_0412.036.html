Exxon Shareholders Seek An Environmental Audit

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/12/90

The state of Washington's investment board has asked Exxon Corp. to undergo an independent audit of all its facilities and operations to determine if they are environmentally sound. The request by the board, which owns or controls about 1.1 million of Exxon's 1.3 billion shares outstanding, is the latest in a swelling chorus of complaints from major shareholders about the oil company's recent string of environmental catastrophes and management's response to them. The most notable disaster was the 11 million gallon oil spill caused when the Exxon Valdez supertanker ran aground off Alaska in March 1989. Exxon has indicated it expects to eventually spend at least $2 billion on a cleanup.

In a letter to Exxon Chairman Lawrence G. Rawl, investment board chairman Joseph A. Dear said the spills and other environmental problems have "imposed an extraordinary financial burden" on holders. Mr. Dear said the "value of our holdings could be jeopardized" if Exxon management doesn't improve its environmental record. He called for the audit to be completed by the next Exxon annual meeting.

The board's action came in response to a proposal by State Treasurer Dan Grimm. Noting that a lot of Exxon tankers ply the Puget Sound and other state waters, Mr. Grimm said that "if Exxon rejects this request {the state} will have no choice but to consider other approaches to assuring the safety of oil tanker operations in Washington waters." He didn't elaborate.

Exxon couldn't be reached to comment on the board's proposal.
