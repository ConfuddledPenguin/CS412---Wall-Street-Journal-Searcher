Abreast of the Market: Stocks Drop as Widespread Selling Of Oil Issues Weighs on the Market

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 04/12/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks drifted slightly lower as widespread selling of oil issues, stemming from the recent decline in crude-oil prices, weighed on the market. While the price of oil abruptly reversed course during the session on reports that the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries might hold an emergency meeting to address the slump in world oil markets, the rebound failed to stem the weakness in energy stocks.

With blue-chip oil issues under pressure, the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed with a decline of 1.35 at 2729.73 after spending the session in a narrow range.

Energy-related stocks captured 10 of the top 18 places on the New York Stock Exchange's list of most active issues, but trading overall remained lackluster. Big Board volume amounted to 141,080,000 shares, a modest improvement over Tuesday's total of 136,020,000 shares.

The pace of activity slowed substantially during the afternoon as the market started to wind down in advance of the observance of Good Friday, for which U.S. stock exchanges will be closed.

Among broader market measures, Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index slid 0.15 to 341.92 and the NYSE Composite Index dropped 0.06 to 187.57, while the Dow Jones Equity Market Index gained .04 to 318.32. Decliners edged advancers on the Big Board by 762 to 720.

John D. Connolly, chairman of the investment policy committee at Dean Witter Reynolds, described the session as "a pretty listless day, with the exception of the oils having their problems."

Oil stocks contributing to the decline in the industrial average included Exxon, which fell 3/8 to 45 5/8; Chevron, which tumbled 1 3/4 to 65 7/8; Texaco, which dropped 1 to 57 3/8; and USX, which dropped 5/8 to 34. All but Chevron also ranked among the most active on the Big Board; USX set the pace for the group, as 1.7 million shares changed hands in composite trading.

Other oil issues taking high places on the Big Board's most actives list included Amoco, which went down 7/8 to 50 1/2; Mobil, down 1 to 59 5/8; Phillips Petroleum, down 1/2 to 25 3/8; Occidental Petroleum, down 1/4 to 26 7/8; and Unocal, down 1/2 to 29 3/4.

Crude-oil futures plummeted in early New York trading, sparking the sell-off in the oil sector. May futures on West Texas Intermediate crude skidded to a low of $16.25 a barrel before recovering to settle at $18.06 a barrel, up 38 cents on the day.

But several oil analysts warned that oil prices are likely to decline further over the short run, leading to further pressure on oil companies' shares. Against the backdrop of those forecasts, "the recovery in the crude market had very little to do with oil stocks," Mr. Connolly said.

Two oil-field services stocks, Schlumberger and Baker Hughes, rounded out the list of the 10 most heavily traded energy issues. Schlumberger fell 1 to 50 as 1.4 million shares were traded, while Baker Hughes declined 1 3/8 to 25 7/8 on 1.2 million shares.

Rowan, whose first-quarter results fell short of most analysts' expectations, dropped 1/2 to 12. The company reported a loss of nine cents a share for the period. Elsewhere in the oil-services group, Halliburton dropped 5/8 to 43 1/2 and Helmerich & Payne fell 1 to 32 1/2.

But there were some exceptions to the rule in energy issues, including Ashland Oil, which climbed 7/8 to 36 5/8, and Sun, which rose 3/8 to 37 1/4. Kidder Peabody reaffirmed its buy recommendations on the two refiners, based on "very fat and healthy" margins between the prices of crude oil and petroleum products despite the price declines in both.

Three airline stocks posted gains after Prudential-Bache Securities initiated coverage of the companies with buy recommendations: AMR, the owner of American Airlines, which went up 7/8 to 66 1/4; Delta Air Lines, up 1 1/8 to 73 5/8, and Midway Airlines, up 1/2 to 13.

Although airlines stand to benefit from lower oil prices through reduced costs for jet fuel, other issues in the sector finished mixed. UAL, the parent company of United Airlines, fell 1 to 160 1/4; Southwest Airlines rose 3/4 to 25; Pan Am dropped 1/8 to 2 7/8; and Alaska Air Group added 3/8 to 23 1/2.

Federal National Mortgage Association, which reported that first-quarter earnings surged about 68%, rallied 7/8 to 35 on 1.2 million shares. The results were better than analysts had generally expected.

United Telecommunications retreated 1 7/8 to 40 1/4 on two million shares after rising a total of 2 3/8 Monday and Tuesday on the news of MCI Communications' agreement to acquire Telecom USA, a rival in the long-distance phone market.

United Telecommunications told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report that it expects to report its first-quarter earnings on Tuesday. PaineWebber analyst Jack Grubman noted that the company has indicated it expects to post net income for the quarter of about 50 cents to 55 cents a share, down from an earlier projection of 55 cents to 60 cents.

Woolworth went up 3/4 to 62 1/4. The company's board declared a 2-to-1 stock split and increased the quarterly dividend by 11%.

TCBY Enterprises gained 1 1/8 to 22 1/8. Shearson Lehman Hutton reiterated a favorable rating on the stock, based on early indications that McDonald's introduction of frozen yogurt in certain areas of the U.S. doesn't appear to be posing a serious threat to the company.

Norton jumped 3 1/8 to 76. A federal judge ordered the company, which is fighting a $75-a-share tender offer from Britain's BTR PLC, to hold its annual meeting April 27 as it had originally scheduled. After BTR made its offer, Norton canceled the meeting, saying it needed more time to weigh alternatives.

Apple Bancorp advanced 1 7/8 to 43 3/4. Its directors rejected a $38-a-share takeover offer from New York real estate investor Stanley Stahl, its largest shareholder, and directed its management to seek other possible bids.

West Point-Pepperell surged 5 to 42 5/8. The Wall Street Journal reported that investor William Farley plans to surrender his majority ownership of the company in connection with a debt restructuring.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index, weighted heavily with oil stocks, fell 1.98 to 357.54. Volume totaled 13,520,000 shares, compared with 11,770,315 shares Tuesday. There were 233 issues advancing, 282 declining and 292 were unchanged.

Among energy-related Amex issues, Imperial Oil Class A retreated 1 5/8 to 50 1/2, Mitchell Energy & Development dropped 1 to 19 1/8, Global Natural Resources fell 1/2 to 7 3/4 and Energy Service dropped 1/4 to 4 3/4.

Jan Bell Marketing tumbled 1 1/2 to 8 3/8 on 1.8 million shares. Goldman Sachs cut its 1990 earnings estimate by 20 cents a share to 75 cents a share, citing its belief that the company's first-quarter sales were about even with the year-earlier level. The company declined to comment.

ENSR jumped 2 1/8 to 8 after agreeing to be acquired for $8.25 a share by NuKEM G.m.b.H., a West German company. The accord permits ENSR to hold talks with any other potential bidders that may emerge.
