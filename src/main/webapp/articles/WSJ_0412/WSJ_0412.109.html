CBS Profit Rose In First Quarter; Outlook Uncertain

  • Author: Dennis Kneale
  • Date: 04/12/90

NEW YORK -- CBS Inc. posted a 52% increase in first-quarter net income, boosted by the January telecast of the Super Bowl. But CBS warned of an "uncertain" outlook for the second half of this year amid a sluggish economy and rising costs for sports contracts. CBS, the third-place network in prime time, has bet $3.6 billion over several years for big-time sports events in the hope of sparking a ratings turnaround. The first two big payments on that gamble come due later this year, the first year of CBS's four-year contracts for major league baseball and professional football, priced at $1.06 billion for each four-year package.

In the first quarter, when neither sports package had yet kicked in, CBS earned $85.3 million or $3.31 a share, up from $56.1 million or $2.18 a share a year ago. Revenue rose 16% to $851 million from $730.6 million.

The earnings far exceeded the consensus estimates of Wall Street analysts, who had expected CBS to earn about $2.70 a share instead of the $3.31 the company turned in. CBS stock rose $2 a share immediately following the company's earnings report, then fell back a bit to close at $180.125, up 62.5 cents, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

CBS didn't break out results for the CBS network. A spokesman said, however, that the network would have posted higher earnings even without the help of football's Super Bowl. Analyst Raymond Katz of Mabon, Nugent & Co. estimates the Super Bowl added $20 million in pretax profit to the network's results.

The company's broadcast group, which includes the network, TV stations and radio stations and is CBS's only major subsidiary, logged a strong 89% gain in pretax operating profit in the quarter. CBS also was aided by continuing interest income on its $2.9 billion in cash and marketable securities, gaining $41.7 million compared with $39.4 million a year ago.

But how CBS fares later in the year is another matter. Mr. Katz of Mabon Nugent said the first-quarter surge "isn't indicative of a bullish year." He had predicted full-year earnings of $12.50 a share (vs. $11.51 last year) but now plans to reduce the forecast. By the fourth quarter, unless the World Series features teams in large TV markets and runs for more than four or five games, "they're going to get hurt," he said.

Peter P. Appert of C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc. is more upbeat, predicting that CBS will earn $13 a share this year. He sees strong ad sales in the second half, despite worries to the contrary expressed by CBS President Laurence A. Tisch, and says robust sales can "hide a lot of cost problems."
