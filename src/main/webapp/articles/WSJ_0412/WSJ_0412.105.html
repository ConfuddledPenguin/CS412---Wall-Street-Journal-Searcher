Norton Loses Bid to Put Off Holder Meeting --- Court Ruling Is Major Blow To Company's Attempt To Thwart BTR Offer

  • Author: John R. Wilke and Randall Smith
  • Date: 04/12/90

BOSTON -- A federal court judge here dealt a major setback to Norton Co. in its effort to fend off BTR PLC's $1.64 billion hostile bid, ordering Norton to hold its annual meeting as scheduled April 26. Norton immediately appealed the decision, saying in an affidavit that it "will have no choice but to put Norton up for sale" if the meeting isn't delayed.

London-based BTR is expected to proceed with a proxy fight to unseat Norton's board. Norton, a Worcester, Mass.-based chemicals and abrasives maker, had canceled its annual meeting and scheduled a special shareholders meeting June 26, citing the need for time to pursue other options such as a "white squire" minority partner.

The ruling is a major blow to Norton because the company itself conceded in a hearing last week that it was unlikely to prevail in a proxy fight if the annual meeting were held as scheduled.

In an interview in London, John Cahill, BTR chief executive officer, said BTR "absolutely" will go ahead with its proxy fight in light of the court decision. He added that initial indications showed that "between 50% and 70%" of Norton holders had tendered their shares to BTR under the offer that began last month. Speaking of the court decision, Mr. Cahill said: "We are very pleased about the result. It changes the ballgame."

Holding the meeting April 26 will force shareholders to vote "before the board has had a reasonable opportunity to explore alternatives," said John M. Nelson, chairman and chief executive officer.

BTR, which launched its $75-a-share cash bid March 15, said that shareholders "now will have a timely opportunity to take steps to have Norton Co. remove the obstacles" to its tender offer.

In his ruling, U.S. District Judge Joseph Tauro cited Norton's own bylaws that prohibit changing the date of an annual meeting within 60 days of its scheduled date. The case "clearly tips in (BTR's) favor because its legitimate interest in enforcing Norton's bylaws -- a contract made for the shareholders' benefit -- must trump the (Norton) board's interest in circumscribing them."

Norton has insisted that BTR's offer is inadequate, but analysts are skeptical that Norton can come up with a recapitalization or other alternative worth enough to top the BTR bid. They think Norton could get only $65 to $70 even by loading the company up with debt. "I don't see what basic defense they could have against the fact that $75 is a lot more than the stock has ever sold for," said David Sutliff of S.G. Warburg & Co. "I can't conceive of an alternative the company could put in place to get that kind of return to shareholders."

One Wall Street source said Norton will try to have its appeal heard as early as next Tuesday. Takeover stock traders and Wall Street people said that if the April 26 date is upheld, it's likely to force Norton to try to find a white knight or negotiate a friendly deal with BTR.

Traders said an April 26 meeting would eliminate a possible Norton defense of selling a block of stock to a friendly holder, or employee stock ownership plan, that could block a takeover. Because only Norton shareholders as of March 2 may vote at an April 26 meeting, new friendly investors couldn't vote to block the purchase.

Traders said yesterday's $3.125 jump in the stock price, to $76 a share in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, reflected the betting that a friendly transaction would be negotiated in the range of $78 to $80 a share. BTR has said it would be willing to pay more if Norton would open its books and show that the value is there.

Norton has painted BTR as a heartless foreign raider and rallied state legislators to its cause. A bill now before the state Senate would make it more time consuming to prevail in a proxy fight against Massachusetts-based companies. The bill would require all companies in the state to have staggered directors' terms, unless the board votes otherwise. Under the proposed law, installing a new slate of directors through a proxy fight would take at least a year or more.

But traders and Wall Street sources don't believe the Massachusetts law can be passed until April 21 or later, which might be too late for Norton. One Wall Street source said Norton's directors probably couldn't prudently wait until the last minute before seeking white-knight buyers, a step that could effectively put the company up for sale.

A Senate aide said the bill had been returned to committee this week for amendment after opponents raised constitutional questions about the measure. "We were hoping to see the bill out by early next week, and assuming we can overcome the technical objections, we still hope to see fast action on the bill," the staff member said.

Separately, Norton said it completed the $550 million sale of its Eastman Christensen Co. to Baker Hughes Inc. Under the agreement, in addition to the cash payment, Baker is to assume $8 million in debt and issue a five-year warrant to Norton to purchase as many as eight million shares of Baker's common at $36.75 a share.
