Politics & Policy: U.S. Undervalued Lincoln's Assets, Keating Charges

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 04/12/90

WASHINGTON -- Regulators vastly undervalued Lincoln Savings & Loan Association's loan portfolio and other wide-ranging investments to back up their takeover of the thrift, charged Charles Keating, the chairman of the thrift's former parent. In testimony in Mr. Keating's legal battle to regain control of Lincoln, attorneys for the Arizona executive introduced federal documents showing that in an analysis of how much it would cost to liquidate Lincoln, regulators slashed the value of loans and investments by as much as 75%.

The analysis, dated one week after the government seized Lincoln and placed it in conservatorship last April 14, suggested Lincoln's assets would be worth only $2.1 billion if liquidated. Lincoln was carrying the assets on its books at $5.2 billion.

"It's absolutely arbitrary," John Lundin, an attorney for Mr. Keating, said in an interview outside the federal district courtroom here. "These big write-downs were done only a week after the takeover."

But an attorney for the government said the document wasn't the regulators' final word on the value of Lincoln assets. "It's just a first cut at the value," said James Murphy in an interview. He said that regulators normally take such a quick, broad-brush look at a thrift's value, writing down assets by using "percentages that in the past they found to be useful."

Mr. Murphy said it wasn't until last August that the regulators, after doing many appraisals of individual assets, declared Lincoln insolvent and placed it in receivership, wiping out the ownership interests of Mr. Keating and other shareholders.

Nevertheless, Judge Stanley Sporkin seemed surprised by the big write-downs assumed by the document and asked yesterday, as he did Tuesday, for testimony from the government explaining its valuation. "You don't write down 50% of a mortgage portfolio," said Mr. Sporkin, a former enforcement director at the Securities and Exchange Commission.

The liquidation document said nonperforming mortgage loans, or loans on which interest isn't being paid, totaled $1.4 billion and assumed losses of $703 million on those loans. Mr. Keating asserted that Lincoln's entire mortgage portfolio, aside from $600 million in real estate loans to Lincoln subsidiaries, totaled $1.4 billion and that only 3% of that was 60 days late in payments.

The document also assumed a 75% loss on Lincoln's investment in certain subsidiaries such as hotels and insurance companies, as well as stockholdings. Such investments in so-called service corporations were written down to $170 million by the regulators from the $700 million Lincoln used. "It's an impossible number," Mr. Keating, who is chairman of American Continental Corp., said of the government's figure.

Attorneys for Mr. Keating also released a memo showing regulators were upset with Roger Clark, the executive they had hired to run Lincoln after taking it over. Though Mr. Clark was paid a $300,000 annual salary, the memo from the government's managing agent for Lincoln said Mr. Clark had "failed to perform" and wasn't taking part in running Lincoln. Mr. Keating's attorneys said the memo showed that Lincoln is being managed incompetently by the government. Lawyers from the government argued the memo is irrelevant to the case.

A spokesman for the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., whose employees are overseeing Lincoln, said Mr. Clark was leaving his post as Lincoln's chief executive officer today because his one-year contract hasn't been renewed. Mr. Clark didn't return calls seeking comment.
