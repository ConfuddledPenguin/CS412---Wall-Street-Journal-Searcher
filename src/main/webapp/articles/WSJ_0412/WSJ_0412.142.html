Giving a Hand: Riordan Becomes the Third `R' In Mississippi Reading, Writing

  • Author: John A. Conway
  • Date: 04/12/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

RICHARD J. RIORDAN is a 59-year-old lawyer, investment banker, restaurateur, real estate man and venture capitalist with a passion for education. He wants every child to be reading and writing in the first grade. "If children cannot do that," he says, "we have lost them for life."

His efforts have been applied to more than 500 schools and 50,000 five- and six-year-olds and he is now helping to put the system into every elementary school in Mississippi, a state laboring under an appalling illiteracy rate.

Mr. Riordan's crusade was sparked by a 1983 Carnegie Foundation report arguing that language was the key to all learning and that children should master it by the fourth grade at the latest. After exploring what schools were doing, Mr. Riordan concluded that computers could help achieve that goal.

At first, the Riordan Foundation, the vehicle used for the Los Angeles lawyer's philanthropies, concentrated on parochial schools in that city. Mr. Riordan is education chairman for the archdiocese of Los Angeles and thanks to his fund-raising efforts, 103 of its 200 parochial schools now have a computerized learning system in which children and personal computers "talk" to each other, letting children write the words they can say and read the words they can write.

Public schools have been added to the program and it has now spread to New York, Trenton, N.J., Orange County, Calif., and Kansas City, Mo. Mr. Riordan's role has been to combine his own funds with those of local sources to finance the programs.

Mississippi's statewide program is Mr. Riordan's most ambitious by far. This phase began after the state's 41-year-old governor, Raymond Mabus, met Mr. Riordan while on an industry-promotion tour in California and invited him to the Executive Mansion for lunch. The governor has big plans for education in Mississippi and the computer-in-the-classroom approach, dubbed Writing-to-Read, is part of them.

Mr. Riordan's approach is to act as a catalyst, putting up his own money but raising the ante by enlisting the aid of others with similar views. For the Mississippi program, he put up $1 million. He then called on Robert Dowling, a New York entrepreneur who has been a close friend since grade-school days in New Rochelle, N.Y. Mr. Dowling responded with a $500,000 gift of his own. Mr. Riordan also pledged to raise $5.5 million more over four years.

The privately raised money went to International Business Machines Corp., which installed 72 Writing-to-Read labs in 60 schools during the summer of 1989, a job that entailed not only computers but new electrical outlets, desks, security devices, lettered tiles for word-making, and a host of other items. By the time the 1989 fall terms began, the Writing-to-Read program was a fact. The goal is to have it working in all 532 Mississippi grade schools by 1993.

Mr. Riordan's lunch with Gov. Mabus and the program it produced did not go unnoticed. The lawyer has had calls from the governors of Louisiana, Indiana and West Virginia asking for help in developing their own computer programs. The Riordan Foundation has also held meetings with business groups from Arkansas and Chicago.

For Richard Riordan and his partners, the interest of the business groups is especially gratifying. "Illiteracy is not only evil and sinful," Mr. Riordan says, "it is extremely bad business."
