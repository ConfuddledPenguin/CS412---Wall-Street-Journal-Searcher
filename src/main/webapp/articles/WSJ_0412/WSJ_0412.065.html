Letters to the Editor: Weakening the Dollar Is an Irrational Policy

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/12/90

I want to commend you for your March 9 editorial "Why the Fire Sale?" You are quite right in pointing out the counterproductive nature of U.S. government actions against the dollar. This irrational policy helps foreigners at the expense of Americans. In addition to being a bad bargain when Americans sell assets to foreigners, a cheap dollar makes it more expensive for U.S. businessmen to invest abroad in such promising markets as the emerging democracies in Eastern Europe. The main argument in favor of a lower dollar, that it supposedly helps reduce the foreign trade deficit, does not stand up under critical examination. A lower dollar means higher inflation rates, which increase interest costs, which undermine the competitiveness of the U.S. in the international marketplace. In contrast, a strong dollar reduces inflationary pressures and results in lower interest rates, which have a favorable impact on the operating costs of the capital-intensive enterprises that account for most U.S. exports.

Historical evidence supports the view that a strong dollar is good for the U.S. trade balance. In the period 1945-1972, the dollar was the strongest currency in the world, and the U.S. had the lowest interest rates of any major industrial country and a highly favorable trade balance.

Ernest J. Oppenheimer

New York
