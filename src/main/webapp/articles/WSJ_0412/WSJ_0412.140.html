British Columbia Reaches Compromise on Land Use

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/12/90

VICTORIA, British Columbia -- The British Columbia government said it plans to preserve as parkland more than half of the 26-square-mile Carmanah Valley, a battleground between environmentalists and loggers that reputedly contains Canada's tallest trees. The decision, following months of debate between environmental groups and MacMillan Bloedel Ltd., a forest products firm that has cutting rights to the timber, was an attempt at compromise that apparently satisfied neither side.

MacMillan Bloedel said the move would cost 150 jobs and could set a precedent for other land-use disputes, which currently affect about 30% of a license area on Vancouver Island that includes Carmanah, and about 3% of the province's total timber-cutting land.

The Western Canada Wilderness Committee, an environmental group, said it is considering court action to block the plan, which requires legislation before it can proceed.

MacMillan Bloedel, 49.9%-owned by Noranda Forest Inc., a unit of Toronto resource giant Noranda Inc., had proposed to set aside a 1,300-acre tract on the valley floor that contains the oldest trees, including a 311-foot Sitka spruce that is believed to be the world's tallest.

Forests Minister Claude Richmond said compensation that must be paid to MacMillan Bloedel for the loss of timber-cutting rights "could amount to millions of dollars."
