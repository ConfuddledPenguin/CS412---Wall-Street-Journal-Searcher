Japan and U.S. Make Some Gains in Talks On Wood Products

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Japan has offered to lower tariffs on some wood products but not quickly enough to ensure the early completion of an accord with the U.S. to eliminate Japanese trade barriers in these commodities. U.S. officials, acknowledging Tokyo's increasing flexibility on the tariff issue, said the chances of a comprehensive forest-products accord currently "look a little better" than they did when negotiations started in Tokyo this week. But they also indicated that the talks may have to be extended until after the Easter holidays.

Individuals in the forest-product industry, who are following the talks closely, said the Japanese offer includes the lowering of wood-product tariffs that currently range from 5% to 20%. However, the reductions would be phased in over periods of one to two years.

Sen. Max Baucus (D., Mont.), chairman of the Senate subcommittee on international trade, said he is concerned about the proposed "extremely long-term phaseout period." U.S. producers, he added, want "meaningful and immediate access to the Japanese market."

If the wood-products issue isn't settled by the middle of June, the U.S. could impose trade sanctions on Japan under the so-called Super 301 provision of the 1988 U.S. trade act.
