Letters to the Editor: Data Don't Reveal Women's True Goals

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/13/90

I was troubled by your March 15 item "White Married Mothers, Working for Better Life" in the People Patterns column. I find the assumption of Penn State researchers David Eggebeen and Alan Hawkins that "if a married mother's husband earns enough to provide the family with an `adequate' income, the wife must be working chiefly to make things still better" both offensive and questionable. To analyze economic data and make statements about women's motivations is simply not good science. Women, like men, are motivated to work for many reasons not reflected in census data, such as personal fulfillment and a desire to contribute to one's job and one's profession. Mary C. Gilly

Assistant Professor

Graduate School of Management

University of California, Irvine

Irvine, Calif.

---

Apparently the Penn State researchers don't believe that career satisfaction or professional recognition applies to people of the female sex with children. They also apparently never heard of wives, even those with children, out-earning their husbands. It is not always possible to tell who makes the incremental income.

Shelley Begg

Orinda, Calif.
