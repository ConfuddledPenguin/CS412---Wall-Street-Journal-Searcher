International: Britain's Rate of Inflation Jumped to 8.1% in March

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/13/90

LONDON -- British inflation jumped to 8.1% in March from 7.5% in February, and the rate for this month is expected to climb to well over 9%. The rate -- the second-highest level since 8.2% in July 1989 -- isn't expected to trigger further tightening in British base rates, as the trend results largely from earlier mortgage rate increases and the government's community charge introduced this month.

But analysts warned that if the government is forced to increase base rates beyond the current 15% level because of external reasons, such as a decline in the value of the pound, the inflation rate could reach double digits this summer.

The government blamed the rise largely on the effect of mortgage rate increases in February. But it said that with the underlying inflation level, excluding mortgage rates, rising to 6.3% from 6.2% in February, other factors were at work, including higher seasonal food prices. It estimated that annual food inflation alone rose to 8.7% in March -- the highest level since May 1982 -- from 8.6% in February. Higher food prices and the community charge, among other factors, are expected to push inflation higher next month.

Other government data confirmed that while the economic slowdown will soon lead to a rise in unemployment for the first time in nearly four years, wage demands remain high. Unemployment fell by 6,800 in February, as expected, but this followed an unusually small decline of 1,700 in January. Wage settlements continued to rise, with the underlying annual rate of average earnings boosts jumping to 9.5% in February from 9.25% in January; a rise to 9.75% is expected by midsummer.
