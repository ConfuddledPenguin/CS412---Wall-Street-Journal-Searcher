FCC Votes to Give Formal Clearance To Airline Phones

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 04/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Communications Commission formally established a service that enables airline passengers to make phone calls during flights. The action, on a 4-1 vote, lets Airfone Inc., a unit of GTE Corp., continue operations, but also opens up the four-megahertz spectrum to competitors, including a new service, In-Flight Phone, owned by Jack Goeken, Airfone's founder.

Airfone has been operating under an experimental license since 1984 and has installed air-to-ground phones in about 1,260 aircraft, or about a third of the U.S. airlines' domestic fleet.

In recent months, Airfone has begun installing phones in each row of seats, a move that has led to a fourfold increase in calling. So far, Airfone has installed the additional phones on 93 aircraft, and has signed contracts with several carriers, including United Airlines, to equip their fleets, a spokesman said.

The commission said it adopted an "open entry" plan because the panel believes the service would develop faster and be more innovative. The agency envisions that individual carriers will contract with phone-service providers.

A GTE spokesman said the company was pleased with the outcome of the lengthy FCC proceeding. In recent weeks, other companies had proposed alternative uses for the spectrum, including using it for cellular-phone service.

Sandra Goeken, a vice president with her father's company, said the Goeken Group is "delighted." The FCC's action clears the way for competitors to apply for licenses to operate phone systems, and the In-Flight Phone service could be ready by the fall, she said.

The new Goeken service is digital, compared with the older analog technology used by Airfone, and Ms. Goeken said she expects to provide airline passengers with a wide range of information services, facsimile service and computer links. "As an airline passenger, you could have access to everything you have at your desk," she said.

To discourage speculative applications, the FCC requires applicants to have a service agreement with at least one airline and insists that construction of the systems are completed within five years, with a minimum of 25 base stations before service can begin. In addition, licenses can't be transferred until a year after construction is completed.

Separately, the commission approved plans by the regional Bell telephone companies for breaking out the basic services in their network so that all information-service providers can obtain individual services on an equal footing with the Bell companies themselves.

The move was a step forward in the fight to open up the networks so that business and residential telephone users can have more choices of special services.

The commission required seven Bell companies to file minor changes by July and update their plans by April 15, 1991.

The Bell companies were generally pleased with the commission action, but expressed concerns about the changes the commission may require. Geni Porti, a specialist in the field at Nynex Corp., called the commission's action "a significant step forward in the whole process."

On another telephone matter, the commission voted unanimously to make all credit card phones and phones in workplaces, hotels, motels, hospitals and other public facilities compatible for persons with hearing aids.

The commission decided that credit card phones must be accessible to the hearing-impaired by May 1, 1991, and those in workplaces and other facilities by May 1, 1992. Under law, phones made for use in the U.S. after August 1989 must be compatible with hearing aids.
