Arbitration Settles A Lot, Unsettles a Few

THE VERDICT is still out on the booming field of "dispute management," in which disagreeing parties go to an arbitrator instead of the courts.

Robert Coulson, president of the American Arbitration Association, calls dispute management "the buzz word for the 1990s."

With the heavy backlog in both federal and state courts, it's not surprising that independent arbitration filings grew to some 90,000 in 1989. The arbitration association, the largest of some 600 socalled alternative dispute-resolution groups, reported 55,520 case filings last year, a jump of more than 36% from 1980.

Business arbitrations with the association almost doubled to 12,220 last year from 6,140 in 1980, and represented $2.41 billion in claims and counterclaims. Disputes over construction, securities, computer software, labor and insurance make up the bulk of business cases. Increasingly, contracts spell out that any dispute will go to arbitration.

Not everyone is excited about the rush to settle disputes outside the courts. Judith Resnik, professor of law at the University of Southern California, argues that independent arbitration raises "serious questions about power and justice" and that the privatization of justice doesn't necessarily translate into the better administration of justice.

Arbitration, she says, favors the well-to-do, such as large companies, which "can afford to go out and hire an arbitrator or set up a system of arbitration."

And studies recently done by Rand Corp. show that while court-supervised arbitration programs are popular, they may not actually save time or money, a key reason for avoiding the courthouse in the first place.

Paralegal Jobs Thriving

In Atmosphere of Thrift

LAW FIRMS and corporate law departments continue to bring paralegals on board at swift rates.

The number of paralegals, or legal assistants, in law firms increased 18% in 1987 and 16% in 1988, says a study by Ernst & Young's legal consulting group.

The 300 law firms surveyed had an average of one paralegal for every four attorneys and paid paralegals an average salary of $31,000. Paralegals logged an average of 1,350 billable hours a year, and clients were billed $41 to $60 an hour for their time finding documents and carrying out other tasks that don't require a lawyer.

With the dropoff in big-dollar mergers and other deals, large law firms are facing a new economic reality, according to Robert J. Berkow, national director of legal consulting services for Ernst & Young and chief author of the study. "Paralegals are a key ingredient in achieving savings in cost, timeliness and even quality," Mr. Berkow says.

In corporate law departments, the number of paralegals increased nearly 10% in 1988. Company legal departments employ one paralegal for every two attorneys, perhaps because of limits on the numbers of attorneys corporations can hire and the more routine nature of corporate legal work, the study theorizes.

Law Schools' Fall Down

On Minority Recruiting

DESPITE GUNG-HO recruiting efforts by the nation's law schools, the number of black, Mexican-American and Puerto Rican law students has increased only slightly in the past 12 years.

According to the American Bar Association, members of these three minority groups account for only 7%, or 8,456, of the 120,694 students enrolled at the 174 ABA-accredited law schools.

While the number of black students rose about 5% in the past six years to 6,321, and the number of Puerto Rican students increased about 10% to 478, the number of Mexican-Americans in law school actually dropped about 100 students to 1,657 from its peak in the early 1980s.

By contrast, the number of Asian-American students more than doubled in the past decade, from fewer than 1,400 in 1977 to 3,133 in the '88-'89 school year.

Considering the much-ballyhooed recruitment efforts of schools, what's the reason? Some law professors point to the scarcity of minority faculty members at law schools, saying it's a turnoff for prospective students. "Of the more than 7,000 law professors in the country, only 28 or 29 are Chicano or Puerto Rican," says Richard Delgado, a law professor at the University of Wisconsin at Madison.

Harvard Law School Prof. Derrick A. Bell says the dearth of minority faculty members "is even greater at the so-called elite law schools." The University of Chicago Law School, he points out, "has no minority faculty presence whatsoever."

Kathryn R. Stell, an admissions dean at the school, says the absence of minority faculty "may be a factor for some students" in whether to attend the school. She is also quick to point out that 35 of the schools 550 students are black, up from 15 in the 1985-1986 academic year.
