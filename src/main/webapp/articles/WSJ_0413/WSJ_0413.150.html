Foreign Exchange: Dollar Finishes Mixed in Thin Trading; Market Has Lingering Doubts About G-7

  • Author: Randolph Walerius Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/13/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar finished narrowly mixed in thin trading, with dealers reluctant to take positions for fear of having them jeopardized by unexpected news during the long Easter weekend. The U.S. unit edged higher against the yen, the mark, the Swiss franc and sterling, while it slipped against its French, Italian and Canadian counterparts.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6724 marks, up from 1.6715 marks late Wednesday, and at 158.18 yen, up from 158 yen late Wednesday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6415, down from $1.6433 Wednesday.

Early Friday in Tokyo, the dollar was trading at 158.30 yen, up from Thursday's close of 158 yen in Tokyo.

The market remained cautious after last Saturday's meeting of the Group of Seven industrial nations. Traders are still struggling to grasp the probable results of the meeting, speculating that heavy coordinated intervention to defend the yen is yet to come, or that the Bank of Japan will raise interest rates as early as this weekend. Another possibility is that Japan has agreed to raise rates in exchange for coordinated intervention by the other G-7 members, dealers said.

James Merrill, senior international economist at McCarthy, Crisanti & Maffei in New York, contended that the G-7 sent a signal when it talked about a currency -- the yen -- other than the dollar, and then said it wouldn't be helpful for the Japanese unit to weaken. "The market was maybe left in doubt about what would be done, by whom, and when," he said.

Because of the recent G-7 meeting, a quick increase in the Japanese discount rate can't be ruled out, according to Chris Manno, chief dealer at Midland Bank PLC in New York. The other G-7 members may have forced Japan to take that step, he said.

The discount rate is the Japanese central bank's base rate on loans to commercial banks.

Mr. Manno said dealers are consolidating after the U.S. unit's recent gains in preparation for another sharp climb by the dollar against the Japanese currency. He said the dollar could reach 165 yen by early summer, a level that some traders had expected this week in the absence of G-7 action to stop its rise.

But Robert White, vice president and manager of corporate trading at First Interstate of California in Los Angeles, said the absence of G-7 intervention was reflected in the yen's slide against the West German currency. "You could make a point that there are problems in Japan and there are problems here {in the U.S.} and the mark is benefiting," he said.

The mark crept to 94.58 yen in late New York trading from 94.47 yen late Wednesday in New York.

The Commerce Department said that the nation's retail sales for March fell 0.6%, contrasted with a consensus estimate for a gain of 0.2%. The revision for February showed a decline of 0.3%.

Most of the recent U.S. economic numbers have been worse than expected, said Mr. White. Although he doesn't expect the Federal Reserve to ease monetary policy immediately, Mr. White said the market is switching its perception more and more to the possibility that the next Fed move could be in favor of lowering rates.

Noting, too, the pressure of the Treasury and the White House on the Fed to lower rates, Mr. White said a drop in commodity prices could allay the inflationary fears of the Fed's policy-making Open Market Committee.

European dealers said the market is growing anxious over the dollar's range trading in recent weeks. And while the dollar has been locked in a range to a degree because of skittishness over potential G-7 action to prop up the yen, some dealers said that there are signs of growing disenchantment over the dollar's chances for making significant gains against the yen and other currencies.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $375.90, up $1.60, on light volume of an estimated two million ounces.
