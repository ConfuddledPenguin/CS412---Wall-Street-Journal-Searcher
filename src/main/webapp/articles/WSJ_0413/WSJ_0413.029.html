Marketing & Media: News Corp. Unit Is Seeking to Sell J.P. Lippincott Co.

  • Author: Meg Cox
  • Date: 04/13/90

NEW YORK -- Harper Collins Publishers, the book publishing arm of Rupert Murdoch's News Corp., said it is putting J.P. Lippincott Co. up for sale to reduce debt and focus Harper Collins more tightly on book publishing. Publishing analysts said Lippincott, which publishes specialized newsletters, journals and books on medical and nursing topics, is believed to earn between $15 million and $20 million a year on revenue of about $100 million. The analysts said it could bring between $200 million and $250 million.

George Craig, president and chief executive officer of Harper Collins, declined to comment on the sum Lippincott is expected to fetch. But he said that in combination with several other divestures made recently Harper Collins will collect about $500 million in all. News Corp. has said it plans asset sales of about that much to reduce the publishing subsidiary's debt, which totals nearly $1 billion.

Industry analysts said potential buyers include a number of large, diversified media concerns, such as Paramount Communications Inc. Paramount officials declined to comment. Smaller publishing boutiques might also be buyers, such as K-III Holdings, a year-old publishing concern headed by William Reilly, ex-chief of Macmillan Inc., and financed by Kohlberg, Kravis, Roberts & Co. "We're not in journals, and we'd love to be," said Mr. Reilly. "It's a terrific business."

Mr. Craig said other recent divestitures by Harper Collins include Gordon & Gotch, a magazine distributor in New Zealand and Australia, and the Angus & Robertson Bookshops, a chain of about 100 stores in Australia. There were several other small properties, he said, which he didn't identify.

Although Lippincott is quite profitable, "it is the farthest away (of Harper's subsidiaries) from our mainstream businesses," said Mr. Craig. "We've been evaluating our portfolio recently and we decided to concentrate our efforts on textbooks (elementary school through college), religious books and general book publishing. We'll continue to grow and we'll need money to grow."

News Corp. has quickly built a huge book-publishing empire, piling up debt along the way. In 1987, it acquired Harper & Row, a big but sleepy publishing house, for about $300 million. News Corp. also bought a British publisher, Collins; a religious publisher in the U.S., Zondervan; and textbook publisher Scott, Foresman, for $407 million in November.

Mr. Craig said the sale of Lippincott is being conducted by investment adviser Munroe Pofcher of New York. The Philadelphia-based publishing company, founded in 1792, is regarded as an established name in an area of publishing that is steadier than general-interest trade books. Harper & Row bought Lippincott in 1978 for about $16 million and the assumption of about $15 million in debt. Mr. Craig said it has been quite profitable in recent years, growing in such areas as electronic transmission of data to pharmacies.
