GM's Hughes Aircraft Wins Brazilian Pact To Supply Satellites

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/13/90

RIO DE JANEIRO -- Hughes Aircraft Co., in partnership with Brazil's Promon Engenharia S.A., won a contract to supply Brazil with a new generation of telecommunications satellites, according to Telebras, the state telecommunications company. Hughes Aircraft is a subsidiary of General Motors Corp. McDonnell Douglas Corp. was defeated by France's Arianespace in a separate bid to launch the two satellites, Telebras said.

The two contracts have a combined value of $244.3 million -- $155.5 million for the satellites and $88.8 million for the launchers, Telebras added.

The awarding of the contracts caps over one year of international lobbying, high-level diplomacy and internal political disputes. The dispute became so fierce that there was speculation that Brazil would simply reopen the bidding rather than make a decision -- raising fears that the country's telecommunications system could collapse.

The fight centered on who would supply Brazil with two second-generation satellites having both civil telecommunications and military applications.

To win the satellite contract, Hughes outbid a consortium formed by Canada's Spar Aerospace, France's Matra S.A. and Brazil's Victori International. While Hughes's price was lower than the consortium's final offer, opponents said they offered more generous technology transfer conditions.

The previous government avoided a decision and passed the problem to the newly installed government of President Fernando Collor de Mello.

In bidding for the launchers, Arianespace offered to sell Brazil technology to produce large rocket engines. The U.S. government asked France to provide assurances that these engines would be used for peaceful purposes. Brazil said it was interested in the engines to develop an independent space business. McDonnell Douglas said that U.S. laws on technology transfer barred it from matching Arianespace's offer.
