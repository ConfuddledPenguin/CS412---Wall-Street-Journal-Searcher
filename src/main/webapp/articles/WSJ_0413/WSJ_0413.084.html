Politics & Policy: Texas Prosecutors Tell House Committee That S&L-Fraud Restitution Is Chimera

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 04/13/90

DALLAS -- Taxpayers shouldn't hold their breath waiting for the perpetrators of thrift fraud to repay the millions of dollars they stole, according to the top federal prosecutors in Texas. "I don't think there's a lot of restitution to be had on the criminal side," said U.S. Attorney Marvin Collins of Dallas during testimony here yesterday before the House Banking Committee.

Courts in Texas ordered about $100 million in restitution last year from people convicted of fraud related to banks or thrifts, or almost one-third of the $361.5 million in restitution ordered nationwide, according to William S. Sessions, director of the Federal Bureau of Investigation. Mr. Sessions testified before the committee Wednesday.

But collecting on those orders is another matter. When the money is available, restitution is often spread out over the term of a defendant's probation, slowing collections. Of the $2.2 million in restitution ordered in the Southern District of Texas in 1989, for example, only $29,000 has been collected, according to U.S. Attorney Henry Oncken of Houston.

Besides, the restitution ordered is minuscule when compared with the total cost of the thrift bailout, which, it was estimated last week, would cost a minimum of $325 billion.

The committee members' questions about restitution were the sharpest leveled during the two days of hearings on the progress of prosecution in thrift-related fraud cases. "We haven't had any S&L fraud in Delaware," said Rep. Thomas Carper (D., Del.). "Yet we're having to help you pay for it. We don't like it."

But Mr. Collins and his fellow U.S. Attorneys from Texas offered little hope to Rep. Carper and intimated that long prison terms were a higher priority for prosecutors. "People in jail don't pay a lot of restitution," Mr. Collins said. "Another complication is the length of time it takes for these cases to come to investigation."

In the case of the defunct Sunbelt Savings Association of Texas, which was singled out by the Banking Committee, investigators have been worked for 2 1/2 years and won guilty pleas from five officers of Sunbelt or affiliated companies.

But the investigation has yet to produce an indictment against Sunbelt's former owner, Edwin T. McBirney III, who was paid an annual salary of $252,000 on top of wildly expensive perquisites, according to evidence given to the House Banking Committee. Thrift regulators attribute almost $20 million in Sunbelt losses to expenditures for airplanes, parties, gifts and overdrafts on insiders' accounts.

Mr. Collins, when asked by Committee Chairman Henry B. Gonzalez why Mr. McBirney hasn't been charged yet, said he couldn't respond because of the secrecy of the grand jury process. Mr. McBirney declined an invitation to testify before the committee.

A civil suit to collect some of Sunbelt's losses is currently being pursued against Mr. McBirney and other former Sunbelt officers and directors by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.

Such civil actions by the FDIC offer a better chance of collecting restitution than criminal cases, Mr. Collins said. He resisted Rep. Gonzalez's suggestion that U.S. Attorneys take on more restitution chores through their civil sections.

"We cannot begin to match what the regulators spend" on outside lawyers to pursue restitution, Mr. Collins said.
