Technology: Scientists Say Alaska Oil Spill Cleanup Would Have Benefited From Microbe Use

  • Author: Amal Kumar Naj
  • Date: 04/13/90

Alaskan shores contaminated by oil from the tanker Exxon Valdez could have been cleaned far more effectively with a new detergent-producing microbe than the hot water spray that was used, according to a group of microbiologists. The researchers from the biotechnology division of the U.S. Army Chemical Research and the University of Illinois said the new microbe performed successfully in a test on oil-soaked gravel and stones collected from Prince William Sound, the site of the Exxon Valdez spill.

"Our results show that the microbial surfactant can remove three times as much oil as water can," said A.M. Chakrabarty, a microbiologist at the university, who led the experiment. Mr. Chakrabarty, while at General Electric Co., developed the first genetically engineered microbe to be patented.

The experiment with Valdez oil, however, didn't involve a genetically altered microbe. A public unease over the release of such man-made organisms in open environment has prevented the use of such bacteria. The researchers instead used a strain of natural bacteria, called Pseudomonas aeruginosa, that was forced through a natural selection process to live on solid and liquid hydrocarbons that are the building blocks in oil.

Mr. Chakrabarty also said that by the time his group finished its experiment Exxon was already far into its cleaning process with warm water jet sprays and an army of scrubbers. He said his team nevertheless suggested the microbial solution to the oil company, but "we didn't get a favorable response."

Robert Mastracchio, technical manager at Exxon Alaska Operations in Anchorage, said that only 74 miles of the 1,089 miles of coastal lines Exxon cleaned last year were suitable for biological remediation. The company has been using special chemical fertilizers there to stimulate the local bacteria to speed up the degradation nature does on its own. He said that Exxon didn't think "it was necessary" to introduce foreign bacteria. "It raises questions about what impact it will have on the environment," he said.

The bacteria clean up the oil just as common dishwashing soap pulls grease out of water. Oil is insoluble in water. But the soap emulsifies the grease into tiny droplets that can be grabbed by water molecules and dragged down the drain. Using the same trick, the Pseudomonas aeruginosa first releases a soap-like surfactant to emulsify the oil into tiny droplets. That allows the bacteria to take up the oil, which they then convert into carbon dioxide and water.

In their experiment, the researchers washed paired batches of the oil-coated Alaskan gravels, one batch in each pair with water and the other with a 1% solution of Pseudomonas aeruginosa surfactant. They then repeated the process at several different temperatures on a scale from 10 to 80 degrees Celsius. In their report, published in the latest issue of Bio/Technology journal, the researchers said the surfactant removed at least twice as much oil as did water at every temperature tested.

The surfactant removed three and a half times as much oil as water at 40 degrees, and 2.4 times at 50 degrees.

The report suggested that surfactant may be particularly useful at low temperatures, which make oil dispersal with water difficult. At 30 degrees, for instance, water removes only 5% of the oil. At that temperature, the surfactant solution removes three times as much oil within a minute after contact. Mr. Chakrabarty said that higher concentration of the microbial solution cleans more oil, but adds significantly to the cost.

He said a big advantage the microbial surfactant has over chemical surfactants is that it's nontoxic and biodegradable. The oil released with warm water tends to go back into the sea unless special measures are taken to contain it.

"We think that microbial surfactant is the only ecological acceptable way to tackle oil spills in coastal areas," Mr. Chakrabarty said.

The use of microbes to treat oil pollution and hazardous waste, as well as for oil recovery from deep wells, is getting increasing attention from researchers. That's because traditional remedies, which involve chemical treatment or incineration, are either expensive or environmentally unsafe. But environmentalists and ecologists' fear that altered bacteria, however natural, may upset the balance of nature. The opposition is even more fierce to the use of genetically engineered microbes, which can do specific tasks more effectively than natural bacteria can.

Mr. Chakrabarty's genetically altered microbe, which won the first patent in 1981 after a celebrated Supreme Court decision, is languishing in a GE laboratory. Herman Finkbeiner, manager of GE's biological sciences laboratory, said GE decided to suspend further work on the microbe largely because of public concern.
