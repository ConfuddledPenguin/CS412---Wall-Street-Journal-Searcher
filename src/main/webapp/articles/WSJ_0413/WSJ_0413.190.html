Newspaper Publishers Expected to Post Flat to Negative Results for 1st Quarter

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 04/13/90

Newspaper publishers, facing one of the deepest declines in newspaper advertising in a decade, are expected to report modest to negative first-quarter results. New York Times Co., for example, cited a drop in ad linage in reporting that net income fell 51%.

While there are pockets of positive advertising growth, largely in Southern California and Florida, newspapers face a dismal year for advertising and earnings. One positive note for most publishers is continued favorable newsprint-price picture -- often a quarter of a newspaper's costs -- which will hold down increases in operating expenses.

In the newspaper industry's three major sources of advertising revenue, the outlook is bad. Continued instability among the nation's retail chains and sluggish retail sales in general are expected to lead to further declines in retail linage. National advertising in newspapers is expected to remain flat at best. Classified advertising linage, robust in the late 1980s, is expected to decline, largely because of declining help-wanted advertising, an economic slowdown, and continued growth of low-cost competitors such as community shoppers and weekly newspapers.

"This is the worst quarter for publishers in a long time," said Edward Atorino, media analyst for Salomon Brothers. "Most newspaper stocks are underperforming. There's some good long-term value there."

Lisa Donneson, analyst for County NatWest, said based on reports for January and February, none of the major newspaper companies showed year-to-year increases in total advertising linage.

Like many companies with major newspapers in the Northeast, New York Times Co. saw its earnings get pounded by the drop in ad linage at its flagship newspaper, the New York Times. Net fell to $16.6 million, or 22 cents a share, from $33.5 million, or 42 cents a share, from a year earlier. The year-ago quarter included profit of $2 million, or two cents a share, from discontinued operations. Excluding the year-ago gain, profit was off 47%. Revenue rose 2% to $431.2 million from $423.1 million.

The company cited weakness in the New York region's economy as a major factor for the decline. There was also an eight-cent-a-share reduction in earnings caused by increased depreciation and interest expense related to completion of the building for the New York Times's new automated color production and distribution facility in Edison, N.J., and a new facility for the Gwinnett (Ga.) Daily News.

"The linage drop is worse than what any one expected," said Salomon Brothers' Mr. Atorino.

Tribune Co. is expected to report lower earnings for the quarter, between 40 to 45 cents a share, compared with 49 cents a share a year earlier when net was $36.7 million on revenue of $553 million. Growth in Tribune's broadcasting operations was probably offset by declines in newsprint profits and weakness in newspaper ad linage. In New York, Tribune's Daily News linage has been dragged down by the sagging New York City economy, and the situation could worsen. The paper, now in the midst of crucial labor negotiations, could be facing a strike soon.

Washington Post Co. is expected to report one of the few per-share gains, more than $3.30 for the quarter, compared with $3.22 a year earlier when net was $41.5 million on revenue of $342 million. County NatWest's Ms. Donneson noted, however, that linage for the company's flagship Washington Post was off 5.6% in March. "They had a strong year last year, but they were hurt by a late Easter this year," she said. "The underlining tone of the Post's business is not good."

As previously reported, Dow Jones & Co.'s first-quarter net fell 88% to $24.9 million, or 25 cents a share, from $200 million, or $1.99 a share, a year earlier. The 1989 quarter included a $164.1 million, or $1.63 a share, gain from sale of an investment in a cable-TV system operator. Excluding the gain, profit was off 31%.

Dow Jones, publisher of The Wall Street Journal, said increases in operating profit from its business publications segment were offset by a decline in operating profit from information services. The decline was caused mostly by the costs of introducing a foreign exchange trading service by the Telerate Inc. subsidiary. Ad linage was down 3.9% at the Journal, up 2.1% at Barron's magazine, and down 8.8% at the 23 daily community newspapers in the Ottaway Newspapers Inc. unit.

At Times Mirror Co., earnings are expected to be about 40 cents a share, compared with 53 cents a share a year earlier when net was $68.8 million on revenue of $846.9 million. The company's major problem, analysts said, is its East Coast papers, Long Island, N.Y.'s Newsday, Baltimore Sun, and Hartford Courant, where classified and retail ad linage has declined severely. Despite competition from Freedom Newspaper Inc.'s Orange County Register, the Los Angeles Times' ad linage has been on the rise.

Knight-Ridder Inc., buoyed by a recovery at the Miami Herald and cost savings from the Detroit newspaper merger involving its Detroit Free Press, is expected to report an earnings increase to about 48 cents a share, compared with 39 cents a share last year when net was $20.5 million on revenue of $541 million.

Gannett Co. is expected to remain flat or possibly show a slight increase in per-share earnings to about 48 cents for the quarter, compared with 47 cents a year earlier when net totaled $75 million on revenue of $818 million. Despite falling linage and flat revenue for Gannett's newspaper group, analysts pointed to positive factors such as termination of its joint venture with GTG Entertainment and cancellation of its "USA Today on TV" show.
