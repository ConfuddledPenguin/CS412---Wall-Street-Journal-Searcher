Abreast of the Market: Stock Prices Rally on Busiest Day Of Trading This Holiday Week

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 04/13/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices rallied as market investors found reasons to be less pessimistic about the first-quarter earnings reports starting to flood the market. Upward revisions of some first-quarter earnings estimates for International Business Machines spurred the gains, traders said, while Chemical Banking moved higher as its results for the quarter proved better than expected.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 22.07 to 2751.80 -- only its second close above 2750 in the past three months. The first came March 19, when the 30-stock average reached its recent high of 2755.63.

Among broader measures, Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index climbed 2.42 to 344.34, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index went up 1.23 to 188.80, and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 2.41 to 320.73. Advancing issues topped decliners on the Big Board by 908 to 573.

Prices steadily advanced through midday, when they peaked as trading began to slacken in advance of the observance of Good Friday. U.S. stock exchanges will be closed today for the religious holiday.

Despite the slowdown, brisk activity in a handful of issues resulted in the busiest Big Board session of the holiday-shortened week. Volume totaled 142,470,000 shares on the Big Board, compared with 141,080,000 Wednesday.

For the week, the industrial average recorded a gain of 34.68 points, or 1.3%. The S&P 500 and the Dow equity index also rose 1.3%, while the New York Stock Exchange Composite gained 1.1%.

Several Wall Street analysts have forecast that first-quarter earnings overall could show double-digit percentage declines from the level of a year earlier, and stock prices have stayed within narrow trading ranges since mid-March as investors awaited the earnings reports.

"People are kind of steeled for a really tough first-quarter round of earnings," said Eric T. Miller, chief investment officer at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette.

As a result, Mr. Miller said, any positive earnings surprises are likely to have a more broadly based impact on the market than negative ones. "For the short term, the path of least resistance is up," he said.

But Jack Baker, head of capital commitments at Shearson Lehman Hutton, said the market isn't likely to break out of its trading-range pattern immediately. The industrial average has remained within a range of about 60 points since hitting the March 19 high.

The latest advance is "just another run-up to the top end of the trading range, and I don't see any reason to get overly excited about it," Mr. Baker said. "I don't see the urgency that (suggests) institutions are going to rush in here on Monday."

IBM gained 3/4 to 107 1/8 and traded as high as 108 during the session. Analysts at Sanford C. Bernstein and Smith Barney, Harris Upham both raised estimates of the company's first-quarter earnings by 10 cents a share to $1.70, citing indications that demand for IBM products may have been stronger than they had anticipated. Bernstein also raised its 1990 estimate by 10 cents a share to $10.60.

Chemical rallied 1 1/8 to 24 1/2 on Big Board composite volume of 1.3 million shares after issuing its first-quarter earnings data. Net income went up 28.7% from the year-ago level, partly because of a gain in the latest period from the sale of its stake in Florida National Banks of Florida.

Bank of New York, which reported a 1.2% increase in first-quarter net, dropped 3/8 to 31 3/8. Analysts had generally anticipated a sharper rise. Other regional banks to report first-quarter results included Barnett Banks, which gained 1/4 to 30, and First Wachovia, which added 1 1/4 to 39 3/4.

Ames Department Stores, the Big Board's most active issue by a wide margin, tumbled 5/8 to 1 7/8 as more than 7.3 million shares changed hands. Some manufacturers have stopped shipping goods to the discount department-store operator as a result of its financial problems, and the delays could worsen its difficulties, The Wall Street Journal reported.

TJX, which holds a major stake in Ames, went down 1 to 13 1/2 as 2.1 million shares were traded. Activity was also brisk in Public Service of Colorado, which rose 3/8 to 21 3/8 as trades designed to capture the stock's dividend pushed volume to 5.2 million shares, and Federal National Mortgage Association, which added 1 3/4 to 36 3/4 on 2.2 million shares in the wake of a better-than-expected earnings report Wednesday.

Carter Hawley Hale skidded 1/2 to 7 after reporting that its sales were down 1.9% in March from a year earlier, but several other retailing issues rallied. K mart, whose March sales were up 8.6%, climbed 1 to 35 1/4; J.C. Penney added 1/2 to 68 3/4; Dayton Hudson rose 1 1/4 to 70 3/4; Wal-Mart Stores advanced 1 3/8 to 51 3/8, and Dillard Department Stores gained 2 1/4 to 75 7/8.

Oil stocks rebounded from their sell-off Wednesday as the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries confirmed it is considering an urgent meeting and other options to stop the current decline in world oil prices. Mobil rose 1/2 to 60 1/8, Chevron added 7/8 to 66 3/4, Texaco gained 1 to 58 3/8, Amoco tacked on 1 1/4 to 51 3/4, and Phillips Petroleum went up 3/8 to 25 3/4.

Glaxo Holdings added 1 to 27 1/8 after a presentation in London by a Canadian company, Biochem, which is developing an anti-AIDS drug. Glaxo holds U.S. distribution rights for the drug and is funding Biochem's research.

Marion Merrell Dow gained 3/4 to 25 3/8. Dow Chemical, which acquired about 67% of the company's common stock through the merger of its drug operations with Marion Laboratories last year, said it plans to purchase an additional four million shares -- a stake of about 1.5%.

Mylan Laboratories fell 1 1/4 to 22 after Salomon Brothers lowered its investment rating on the stock and its earnings estimate for the fiscal year ending next March. Analyst Robert Uhl told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report he based the changes on a cutback in his 1990 sales forecast for Eldepryl, a treatment for Parkinson's disease that Mylan and Bolar Pharmaceutical produce in a joint venture. Bolar lost 1/4 to 8 7/8 in American Stock Exchange trading.

Norton rose 1 1/8 to 77 1/8 on 1.5 million shares amid speculation that the company could be forced to deal with its hostile suitor, BTR PLC of Britain, or attempt to find another bidder. A federal judge on Wednesday ordered Norton to hold its annual meeting April 26 as scheduled; the company, which is appealing the decision, has already conceded that it was unlikely to prevail in its proxy fight with BTR if the meeting isn't delayed.

Control Data gained 1 1/8 to 21 1/8. The company is expected to report its first-quarter results later this month, and analysts said they expected the figures to show that it returned to profitability after posting a loss of $16.11 a share last year.

RMI Titanium finished at 12 5/8 on its first day of trading, up 1/8 from the price at which Quantum Chemical's entire 50% stake in the company was sold in an initial offering of 7.5 million shares. Quantum, which gained 1/4 to 23 1/2, said it expects to report a gain of about $15 million on the sale.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index gained 0.34 to 357.88, cutting its loss for the week to 0.9%. Volume totaled 12,560,000 shares compared with 13,520,000 Wednesday.
