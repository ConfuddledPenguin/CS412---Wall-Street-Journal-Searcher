Elsevier N.V. Says Pearson Merger Is Long-Term Goal

  • Author: Bob Hagerty
  • Date: 04/13/90

AMSTERDAM, the Netherlands -- Elsevier N.V.'s chairman, Pierre Vinken, said the Dutch publisher still regards a merger with Pearson PLC as a long-term goal. Mr. Vinken also indicated that Elsevier, an Amsterdam-based publisher of scientific journals, trade publications and newspapers, is likely to show strong profit growth again this year.

Elsevier and Pearson, which is based in London and owns the Financial Times, announced an alliance in September 1988, saying they might eventually merge. Last week, however, the two companies confirmed that the idea of a merger had been shelved.

Even so, Mr. Vinken emphasized in an interview this week that the two companies remain "a happy, engaged couple," adding that they are looking "for ways to tie these companies together, rather than to divorce them." For its part, Pearson said recently that it still regards Elsevier as "our long-term European partner."

Pearson owns about 23% of Elsevier, and the Dutch company holds 9.3% of Pearson.

Mr. Vinken said merger considerations had been blocked at least temporarily by "enormous technical problems." For example, he said, a merger would give rise to huge capital-gains taxes in Britain.

Meanwhile, a drop in the pound's value and a surge in Elsevier's share price over the past two years has made the Dutch company prohibitively expensive for Pearson, the two companies have said. When the alliance was announced in 1988, Pearson's market value was nearly twice that of Elsevier. Now Elsevier is valued on the stock market at the equivalent of about $2.7 billion and Pearson at around $2.9 billion, partly reflecting the market's view that Pearson is no longer a likely takeover target.

Another worry for the two companies is that a bid for Elsevier from Pearson effectively would put the Dutch company on the block, opening the way for a third party to make a higher offer.

Thus, Mr. Vinken said, the two will need to seek "creative" solutions in their effort to forge closer ties. For the moment, he added, they are seeking ways to combine their medical-publishing operations and are considering joint acquisitions. The companies are particularly interested in acquiring leading financial newspapers in European countries, but none are on the market at present, Mr. Vinken said.
