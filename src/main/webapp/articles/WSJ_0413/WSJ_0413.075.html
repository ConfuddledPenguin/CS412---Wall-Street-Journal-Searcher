FCC Approves Plan to Tune Up Ailing AM Radio Band

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 04/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Communications Commission launched a crusade to rescue ailing AM radio. The commission, in a series of 5-0 votes, adopted a package of engineering changes and proposals to improve the quality of AM service, including opening new bandwidth, 1605-1705 kilohertz, for AM stations. With 5,000 stations, AM radio has been plagued with congestion and interference, particularly during nighttime hours, and has been battered by competition from FM stations. The agency plans not only to expand the bandwidth, but also to weed out the overgrown AM garden over the next 10 years.

Adding bandwidth would require radio manufacturers to modify their products because receivers on the market today stop picking up signals between 1600 and 1620 kilohertz. Manufacturers have already begun planning for the additional bandwidth because the FCC signaled its intention to authorize its use in the U.S., and Canada and Mexico have already approved its use for AM radio, said William Hassinger, assistant chief for engineering of the FCC's Mass Media Bureau.

Under the proposals, the commission would encourage 25 to 30 existing AM stations nationwide that are causing the most static to move into the expanded band. It would also give broadcasters incentives to improve AM service, such as allowing licensees to negotiate among themselves to reduce interference. The commission suggested it could provide tax breaks to AM licensees who receive payments from other stations to reduce their service areas or turn in their licenses.

Further, the FCC said it could ease its multiple-ownership rules to allow licensees to own AM stations with overlapping service areas if one of the stations cuts down its interference with other AM stations. And it said it is considering banning AM-FM simulcast stations.

Among other steps, the commission changed some rules to improve AM reception, such as modifying the way it calculates skywave and groundwave field strength. The new skywave model -- a key to improving AM service during nighttime hours -- gives engineers more accurate information on the likelihood of interference.

The FCC's actions drew immediate praise from the National Association of Broadcasters, which called the proposals "sweeping and historic." The NAB said broadcasters "are encouraged by the FCC's far-reaching changes and proposals and look forward to reviewing" the plans.

William Sanders, president of KICD/ KICD-FM, in Spencer, Iowa, and chairman of the NAB's radio board, said the commission's actions will improve all classes of AM stations. The changes, he said, "look to the future of possibly radical changes in the currently dense population of the AM band" to reduce interference and make AM radio a more competitive service.

For years, broadcasters have been complaining about interference on the AM band. Earlier this year, the FCC conducted a daylong hearing to explore ways to improve AM service. Yesterday's proposals grew out of that hearing and voluminous proposals from the industry.

The commission will accept public comments on all the proposals before deciding on final rules.

FCC Chairman Alfred Sikes noted that Congress is also taking up legislation to improve AM radio, and that the agency "is looking forward" to working with lawmakers. As the AM industry moves toward stereo broadcasting, he said, the commission's actions help "set the technological stage." In a competitive, high-tech market, the chairman warned, "being second best isn't good enough."

Commissioner Andrew Barrett, a former state public utilities regulator from Illinois, raised concerns about shifting channels from the existing AM band to the expanded AM band. "I understand the desire to remove interference and congestion from the existing AM band," he said. "However, the cure for AM may be the denial of opportunity for new entrants, including minorities, women and public broadcasters." The commission, he said, needs "to ensure that minorities are not precluded from ownership opportunities as this commission allocates new spectrum."

Commissioner Barrett said minorities own only about 3% of the nation's 11,000 broadcast properties. Just what the FCC can do to change this isn't clear. The FCC's minority-preference programs, which gives members of minority groups and women an edge in competing for broadcast licenses, currently are under review by the Supreme Court.
