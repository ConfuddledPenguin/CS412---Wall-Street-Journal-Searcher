S&P's Cuts in Ratings Exceeded Its Upgrades During First Quarter

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/13/90

NEW YORK -- Standard & Poor's Corp. lowered the credit ratings of 155 companies in the first quarter -- nearly three times as many as the number of companies given higher credit ratings. S&P said preliminary figures show it lowered ratings on $75 billion of debt in the first quarter, while raising ratings for $41 billion of debt issued by 55 companies. The credit-rating service cited negative trends for junk-bond issuers as one reason for the downgrades affecting industrial companies.

Among U.S. financial-services companies, reduced credit ratings exceeded the number of improved ratings for real estate, life-insurance and health-insurance companies. Rating changes for banks were "decidedly negative," S&P said, because of sharp increases in bad loans for real estate-related ventures.

Separately, S&P said municipal credit quality deteriorated in the first quarter, largely reflecting downgrades on debt ratings of New York State and Connecticut and their agencies.

S&P said ratings on $23.8 billion of municipal debt were lowered, while ratings on $1.4 billion of debt were raised. In the quarter, 87 ratings were lowered, while 29 were raised, compared with 61 lower ratings in the fourth quarter and 50 upgrades.
