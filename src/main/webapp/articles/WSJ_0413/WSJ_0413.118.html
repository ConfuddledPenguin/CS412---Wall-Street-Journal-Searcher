Marketing & Media: Paramount Makes Peace With Discount Theaters

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/13/90

Paramount Pictures made peace with bargain-theater owners by slightly modifying a pricing formula adopted last summer that exhibitors said would force them to charge more at the box office. After the policy took effect with the release of Paramount's "Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade," some discount movie theaters stopped screening Paramount films. Now most of them are expected to resume showing Paramount films without raising prices.

It looks as if the hiatus hurt the theater operators more than Paramount, one industry observer said.

Practically speaking, the change will make most Paramount movies available to discount movie houses four months after they are released, a month sooner than under last summer's policy.

Paramount Communications Inc.'s film unit said the revised policy strikes a balance that meets discount movie theater owners' needs and "allows us a fair return on our enormous investments" in the films. Movie makers have complained that discount theaters cannibalize first-run theaters. Discounters say their customers wouldn't be going to the movies at all if their prices, $1 to $2, weren't so low.

Paramount's revised pricing formula specifies that films are available on a percentage of box-office gross basis four months after theatrical release. The old formula required a per-capita payment up until 30 days before videocassette release. Under the per-capita payment requirement, a theater owner must pay the studio $1.05 for each adult admission (75 cents for children and senior citizens) or 35%, whichever is higher.
