Four Store Chains to Set Strict Standards For Environmental Claims of Products

  • Author: Randolph B. Smith
  • Date: 04/13/90

Four retail and supermarket chains in Western states said they will adopt strict standards for products making environmental claims to address consumers' mounting concern about misleading advertising. The retailers said they have designated an independent consulting company to review product claims and award a "Green Cross" seal of approval to products that promote recycling and reduce waste and pollution.

The chains sponsoring the program are Campeau Corp.'s Ralphs Grocery Co., based in Compton, Calif., with 143 stores in Southern California; Sacramento, Calif.-based Raley's, with 58 stores in Northern California; Phoenix, Ariz.-based Abco Markets Inc., with 75 stores in Arizona; and Portland, Ore.-based Fred Meyer Inc., with 125 stores in seven Western states.

The retailers said they planned to announce the program at a news conference scheduled for this morning in Washington.

Kevin Davis, vice president of advertising for Ralphs, said retailers are adopting the program because customers are confused by conflicting advertising claims and are pressing stores for guidance in choosing products that are truly "environment friendly."

"We are interested in making sure that claims are authenticated before we support them with in-store advertising," Mr. Davis said.

However, some environmentalists expressed concern that retailers were rushing to launch a national environmental seal program as a marketing ploy riding on publicity surrounding Earth Day.

Lisa Collaton, staffer with Environmental Action Foundation, a Washington-based advocacy group, said she was concerned that product standards adopted by the retailers might not be valid or could conflict with efforts by other agencies. She noted that Earth Day organizer Denis Hayes plans to launch a similar national program known as Green Seal next month, and that government-sponsored programs are under consideration.

"Our concern is that the retailers may be rushing this program to get a marketing advantage," before a consensus has been reached on definitions and standards for labeling green products, Ms. Collaton said.

Mr. Davis said retailers felt compelled to respond quickly to consumers' concerns, but that Green Cross could be modified later to conform to national standards that might be developed. He said he expected other retail chains would join the program and that products would be submitted for review within 30 to 60 days.

The four retailers have designated Scientific Certification Systems Inc., of Oakland, Calif., to develop standards, review products and issue a "Green Cross" seal. To qualify for the seal, products would have to contain at least 50% recycled material, or other materials, such as wood or cotton, that are considered "sustainable." The recycled content would have to be from consumer waste, not plant scrap.

In addition, product and packaging manufacturers would have to meet all federal, state and local environmental regulations to get the seal. The manufacturers would also have to nearly eliminate plant emissions of any substances known to cause cancer or reproductive damage.

Products making advertising claims would be encouraged to submit those for review by Scientific Certification, Mr. Davis said. Stores would promote products whose claims have been verified, he said.

Manufacturers would pay fees to Scientific Certification to have their products, labeling and production facilities reviewed for compliance with Green Cross standards. Linda Brown, a vice president of Scientific Certification, said the company is employee-owned and has no financial ties to retailers or manufacturers. A division of the company, Nutriclean, certifies to retailers that produce meets FDA standards for pesticide residues, is organic or meets other advertising claims.

Craig Merrilees, of the National Toxics Campaign Fund, a Boston-based environmental advocacy group, said the standards proposed by the retailers would be tougher than any existing labeling programs used by manufacturers or retailers, such as Wal-Mart Stores Inc. in the U.S. and Loblaws Supermarkets Ltd. in Canada. He said his group helped in developing tough standards that could lead to "significant changes" in product design and manufacturing.

"This is not going to be an easy test for products to pass," Mr. Merrilees said.

Cheryl Perrin, vice president of public affairs for Fred Meyer stores, said: "We must provide an incentive for manufacturers to integrate recycled materials into their products whenever possible." Julie Weeks, quality assurance manager for Ralphs, said the retailer wants to "communicate our customers' concerns back to the manufacturers."
