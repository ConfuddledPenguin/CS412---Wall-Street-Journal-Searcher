Union Valley Corp. Makes Disclosures Of Financial Woes

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/13/90

HOWELL, N.J. -- Union Valley Corp. said it may miss a May 15 payment to certain of its debenture holders as it races against the calendar to complete a revised bank-credit agreement. The real estate concern also said a "liquidity deficiency" has already caused it to default on a payment of $967,000 because of another creditor. In addition, the company has missed interest payments to its lead lender, First Fidelity Bank, for March and April. But the bank hasn't declared Union Valley in default, though it also hasn't provided the company with a written waiver.

In making the disclosures, Herbert E. Wishnick, Union Valley's president, said he couldn't offer assurances that the company can restructure its senior debt or its 14 1/4% subordinated debentures on which it owes the May 15 payment.

The disclosures were further signs of the company's deepening financial woes. Union Valley previously announced that First Fidelity Bank had declined to renew a $100 million revolving credit agreement and had demanded repayment by Aug. 1 of $70.5 million outstanding. Union Valley said it has relinquished all of its cash flow from its delivery of housing units to Fidelity Bank since March 1, which hindered its ability to pay other creditors.

Union Valley designs, builds and sells retirement and pre-retirement communities, houses and commercial real estate in New Jersey, Delaware and Pennsylvania. In 1989, it earned $300,000, or eight cents a share, on revenue of $86.6 million.

"The company may encounter further difficulties in meeting the payment terms of its outstanding debts until more permanent sources of financing can be worked out or until market conditions in the homebuilding industry improve," Mr. Wishnick, the president, said.

He said that Union Valley and First Fidelity have agreed on significant changes in the loan arrangement but that full agreement is unlikely before June 30. As a result, Union Valley is seeking interim financing, which it said will be in place within a few days. This will allow the company to meet some of its obligations, Mr. Wishnick said.
