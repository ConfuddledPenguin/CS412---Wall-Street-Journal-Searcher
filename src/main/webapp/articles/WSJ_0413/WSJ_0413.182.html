BTR PLC Begins Proxy Fight in Bid For Norton Co.

  • Author: Lawrence Ingrassia
  • Date: 04/13/90

BOSTON -- BTR PLC launched a proxy fight to unseat the board of Norton Co. and demanded it be allowed to inspect any confidential information Norton is allowing other potential bidders. BTR, a British conglomerate, nominated a slate of nine directors for the April 26 annual meeting of Norton, which is seeking to fend off an unsolicited $1.64 billion, or $75 a share, bid by BTR. There are 12 directors on Norton's board, but the board will be reduced to 11 at the meeting.

Norton, a maker of abrasives and engineering materials based in Worcester, Mass., has said that BTR's offer is inadequate. In demanding that BTR be allowed to review confidential information, BTR Chief Executive John C. Cahill said in a letter to Norton: "Ironically, at the same time that management apparently feel it is in Norton's best interests to ignore us . . . the board has authorized the company to provide certain confidential information to interested parties." It added, "The board, consistent with its fiduciary duties, should provide us promptly with the same confidential business and financial information."

Mr. Cahill reiterated that "BTR would be willing to consider increasing its offer" if the confidential information showed that a higher price is warranted.

Norton is appealing a federal court ruling requiring it to hold its annual meeting April 26, as initially scheduled; Norton's board had canceled the meeting and set a special shareholders meeting for June 26 after BTR's bid was announced. A U.S. appeals court granted an expedited hearing of the Norton appeal of the lower court ruling.

A bill being considered by the Massachusetts Legislature still could help Norton's defense, although it's unclear whether it will be passed before the April 26 annual meeting. The bill would require Massachusetts companies to have staggered terms for directors, with one-third of the board elected each year, unless the board votes otherwise. If the bill were to take effect prior to the annual meeting, it could prevent BTR from electing a majority to the board at the meeting.
