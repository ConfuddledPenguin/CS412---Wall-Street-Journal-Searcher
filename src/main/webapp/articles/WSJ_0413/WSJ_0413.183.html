Two Kirby Brothers Battle Over Measure Against Takeovers --- Proxies Are to Be Solicited For Alleghany's Meeting Of Holders on April 27

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 04/13/90

NEW YORK -- Two Kirby brothers are at odds over just how much anti-takeover protection their Alleghany Corp. financial services company needs. Director Allan P. Kirby Jr., 58 years old, said in a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission that he will solicit proxies for the April 27 annual meeting. He opposes a proposal to extend an anti-takeover measure that otherwise would expire after the meeting.

Mr. Kirby, who controls a bit more than 10% of the company, said he wants only the flexibility "to vote my stock in the future in concert with anyone I want to if I want to."

The Kirby family as of March 1 controlled about 39% of the company's shares, valued at about $213 million. F.M. Kirby, 70 years old, Allan's brother, is chairman of the company, which has made millions from investments and has made an occasional high-profile acquisition.

In 1984, Alleghany sold Investors Diversified Services Inc., the big mutual fund and investment company, to American Express Co. for more than $700 million, at the time making Alleghany the largest shareholder of American Express. In 1985, Alleghany was one of the final bidders seeking to buy Consolidated Rail Corp. from the federal government. From 1954 until 1968, Alleghany owned a controlling interest in the New York Central railroad, subsequently merged into the Penn Central system which later collapsed and was taken over by the government.

In recent years, Alleghany has expanded into financial services, acquiring several title and other insurance companies. It bought Sacramento (Calif.) Savings & Loan Association in 1989 and also operates a steel fastener business.

In July 1988 the SEC adopted a rule barring from national stock exchanges the securities of any company that acts to reduce unequally the voting rights of existing common shareholders. But companies with such plans adopted before mid-1988, such as Alleghany, were permitted to continue the plans.

Alleghany's directors, other than Allan Kirby, have proposed to extend the restriction to 1995. The company's proxy statement says directors believe the restriction "is still necessary to achieve the company's goals," and will "deal with an active takeover environment in general and with its possible disruptive consequences."

Allan Kirby's letter to shareholders asserts that keeping the restriction could limit the market value of the company and insulate directors and senior management from "accountability to all stockholders."

He also noted that the company has in place a variety of other measures to protect against unsolicited takeover offers. Mr. Kirby stressed that he isn't part of an investing group and doesn't want to see the company sold.
