Dutch Economy Seen Continuing Its Growth; German Impact Cited

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/13/90

THE HAGUE, the Netherlands -- The unification of the two Germanys will help the Dutch economy continue to grow through 1991 with a subsequent reduction in unemployment, but the government deficit will continue to widen, according to the official economic forecast for 1991 issued by the Dutch Central Planning Bureau. "For this year and the next, the economic prospects for the Netherlands are fair. Economic growth is steady, employment continues to increase, unemployment is on the wane and inflation remains within limits. One note out of tune seems to be the high real interest rate, associated in particular with the happenings in Germany," the CPB wrote in its annual spring forecast.

"Not only does it have negative consequences for residential construction, it also combines with the effects of the tax setback of 1989 to aggravate the financial problems facing the government in 1991," the CPB said.

The country's total output of goods and services will expand 3.25% in 1990 and will subsequently slow slightly in 1991 to a rate of growth of 3.0%, according to the CPB projections. Employment, which is expected to increase by 100,000 jobs in 1990, is expected to grow by a further 75,000 jobs in the following year.

However, the CPB projected that the Dutch government will fail in its efforts to reduce the government deficit.

In fact, because of shortfalls in taxes, a higher interest-rate burden and no further income for the sales of governmental stakes in businesses currently planned, the public financing deficit is likely to expand as a percentage of net national income, the CPB said.

Even though the overall Dutch economy will show healthy growth through 1991, not all sectors of the economy will benefit.

In particular, construction, agriculture and food industries all are expected to show below-average expansion through 1991, although for different reasons.
