Saudis Trying to Get Foreigners Interested In Its Bond Market

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/13/90

MANAMA, Bahrain -- Saudi Arabia has opened its fledgling domestic bond market to offshore banks in the Persian Gulf in a new move to increase trading, bankers said. The Saudi Arabian Monetary Agency (Sama), the kingdom's central bank, has told domestic banks that it will permit trading in its so-called "development bonds" in offshore centers such as Bahrain and Dubai.

Previously the secondary market in the government bonds had been restricted to domestic banks.

"It's a fairly major concession," one Saudi banker said. "Any major financial institution with an outlet in Bahrain and Dubai will now be able to trade the bonds," he added.

Bankers said the concession reflects Sama's determination to make a success of the bond program, which has been used since 1988 to finance the kingdom's substantial annual budget deficits.

Saudi Arabia began considering changes in the program last year after the kingdom's banks showed increased reluctance to take up all the fortnightly issues.

For the moment, dealing will be open only to banks with a branch presence in one of the Gulf Co-operation Council states, or overseas banks that have a Saudi shareholding. However, bankers said they believe the change is only a first step towards the full internationalization of the bond program.

Bankers say the bond program will likely appeal to offshore banks already active in the Saudi riyal deposit or currency markets and to the major Gulf institutions such as Arab Banking Corp., Gulf International Bank and National Bank of Abu Dhabi. However, they say it may be some time before the offshore banks get heavily involved in Saudi bond dealings.

Earlier this year six Saudi banks started acting as market makers in the bonds, quoting continuous prices on screen-based communications systems during banking hours. Domestic Saudi banks are being encouraged to market the bonds to state companies and private citizens.

Saudi Arabia plans to issue 25 billion riyals ($6.6 billion) of bonds in 1990 to finance this year's expected budget deficit. The kingdom has run substantial deficits in every year since 1983, but the bond program wasn't introduced until 1988 when it became clear that Saudi Arabia needed an alternative to drawing on its foreign reserves to finance the deficits.

Diplomats estimate that the kingdom has issued about 80 billion riyals of bonds since the program began.

Officially the bonds pay a return linked to the profit on various development projects in Saudi Arabia, thus overcoming the Islamic prohibition on interest payments. In practice, however, the bonds have a fixed spread over equivalent U.S. Treasury instruments.
