Intel Net Rose 48% And Revenue 25% In the First Quarter

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/13/90

SANTA CLARA, Calif. -- Intel Corp. reported that first-quarter net income surged 48% to $144 million, or 73 cents a share, despite a prolonged slump that has hurt the semiconductor industry. The results topped Intel's projection of two weeks ago, when the company predicted it would earn about $130 million. In the year-ago quarter, Intel had profit of $97 million, or 52 cents a share.

Revenue rose 25% to $894 million from $713 million.

The results were the latest in a string of strong performances by Intel, particularly in light of industrywide woes caused by Japanese competition and problems in some basic fields served by chip makers, such as the automobile industry. Recently, chip makers such as Advanced Micro Devices Inc. have had losses or profit declines because of the slump.

But Intel rolls on, primarily because of demand for its latest microprocessor chips, used in many of today's personal computers. Intel hasn't been able to keep up with demand for its 80386 chip, causing shortages the company expects to last into the second quarter. Its 80486 chip also is popular. Both chips rely on what's known as "32-bit architecture," a chip design that speeds up computing.

"The transition to 32-bit architecture has picked up momentum in the past six months," said Andrew S. Grove, president and chief executive officer. He said first-quarter 80386 chip shipments doubled from year-earlier levels. "Further growth is expected in the second quarter," Mr. Grove said.

An Intel spokesman said the results beat the company's earlier projection because the company sold more microprocessor chips, which have higher margins than its other products.

In over-the-counter trading yesterday, Intel closed at $42.50, down 25 cents.
