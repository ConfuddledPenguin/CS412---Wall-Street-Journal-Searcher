Pacific Gas Sheds Nuclear-Plant Woes --- Accord With Regulators Brightens Utility's Outlook

  • Author: Frederick Rose
  • Date: 04/13/90

For years, Pacific Gas & Electric Co. suffered the corporate equivalent of a migraine: a $5.8 billion nuclear plant reviled by environmentalists and criticized by regulators. The Diablo Canyon facility, nearly 20 years in the making, became a fixation at PG&E, preoccupying the company's management and its board. "We were totally absorbed," confesses Richard A. Clarke, chairman and chief executive. "We were worn out."

In a striking about face, the nation's largest investor-owned utility has shed its woes lately, turned potential financial disaster into a profit, and is trying hard to rework its tarnished image.

Thanks to an unconventional pact with state regulators-in which the company's revenue from Diablo Canyon depends on the plant's performance rather than a fixed return on investment -- PG&E last year coaxed $225 million in after-tax profit from its one-time white elephant and may make more this year if the plant continues its current output.

Moreover, the San Francisco-based utility is enjoying considerable public good will from its quick restoration of services after the city's Oct. 17 earthquake. The company expects to recoup nearly all of its estimated $100 million in earthquake-related outlays from insurance and from rates.

With regulatory treatment of Diablo Canyon settled, "we feel liberated," says Mr. Clarke. Now PG&E is wooing the same environmentalists who once hectored it. It's also striving to operate Diablo Canyon efficiently and safely, and broaden investments in non-regulated businesses with which it says it feels familiar.

A number of utilities have enjoyed a similar burst of cash flow, as heavy borrowing for nuclear construction suddenly has reversed and become a stream of revenue when a plant comes on line. The sudden wealth has been badly spent in some cases. Pinnacle West Capital Corp., for instance, squandered anticipated profits from its Palo Verde nuclear station by investing in the thrift business, nearly bankrupting itself in the process.

PG&E is determined to avoid such pitfalls, says Mr. Clarke. "We're not diversifying; we're taking advantage of the business that we know," he asserts.

Mr. Clarke says the company expects first-quarter net income to be lower than the year-earlier 53 cents a share. A scheduled refueling outage of the Unit 2 reactor at the company's Diablo Canyon nuclear plant makes it "reasonable to assume that our earnings will be lower than they were last year," he said.

But the executive said 1990 net income likely will exceed the $1.90 a share reported for 1989. However, he said an average of analysts' estimates of $2.07 a share is "a little high."

PG&E had net income of $244.4 million, or 53 cents a share, in the 1989 first quarter on revenue of $2.11 billion. Net income last year totaled $900.6 million, or $1.90 a share, on $8.59 billion in revenue.

To expand its non-regulated business, Mr. Clarke said PG&E may substantially boost its investment in natural-gas exploration and production. The company's PG&E Resources Co. subsidiary presently holds interests in exploration leases covering about 600,000 gross acres in Texas, Louisiana, Arkansas, California, and the Rocky Mountain region. The holdings amount to about 220,000 acres, net of other investors' interests.

"It's not infeasible that in resources you might see a huge acquisition of reserves," Mr. Clarke added.

PG&E, late in 1988, acquired Wessely Energy Co.'s U.S. oil and gas assets for about $100 million. At the time, it said further acquisitions were planned. However, purchases haven't materialized yet. Mr. Clarke said the company is constantly reviewing possibilities.

In another gas-related venture, PG&E is opening refilling stations for vehicles powered by compressed natural gas. The company expects to have nine such stations open to the public by the end of the year.

PG&E also plans to about double the capacity of a pipeline that carries natural gas from western Canada to the Pacific northwest and California, adding 900 million cubic feet daily of new transmission capacity. Operation of the $1.5 billion project is scheduled for 1993. Its capacity is already contracted for, Mr. Clarke says.

Jointly, the three natural gas projects are part of an overall strategy. "Our whole premise is that . . . gas is a good business to be in," Mr. Clarke says.

PG&E's environmental thrust is focused on future power developments, where the company is working at least as hard on the generating capacity it won't build as that which it will. Of about 3,300 megawatts of forecast potential new demand over the next decade, PG&E thinks it can forestall at least 2,500 megawatts -- roughly equal to the output of two nuclear generating units -- through energy conservation.

The utility plans to spend about $170 million this year and about $200 million annually after that on financial inducements to reduce demand among a wide range of electricity consumers. Plans include educational efforts for energy-efficient lighting, inducements for industry to use more efficient motors, and better residential insulation programs.

The California Public Utility Commission currently is considering proposals under which PG&E would receive a return on these investments.

Mr. Clarke said about 800 megawatts of capacity not achieved through customer efficiencies may be provided in part by wind and solar power. A substantial portion, however, likely will be come from updating existing gas and oil-fired generating stations, he said.
