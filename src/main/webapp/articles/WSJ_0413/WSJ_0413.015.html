Morton Results Aided by Airbag, Rock Salt Sales

  • Author: Brett Pulley
  • Date: 04/13/90

CHICAGO -- Morton International Inc. expects to report earnings of about $39 million, or 82 cents a share, for its third quarter ended March 31, on sales of $450 million, Chairman and Chief Executive Officer Charles S. Locke said in an interview. Harsh winter weather that increased demand for ice-controlling road salt, along with rising demand for the company's automobile airbags, more than offset an earnings drop at Morton's biggest segment, its specialty chemical operation, Mr. Locke said.

In the year-ago period, in which Morton took a variety of charges associated with the July 1, 1989 divestiture of its Thiokol Corp. aerospace operations, the company had restated net income of $9.6 million, or 20 cents a share, on sales of $400 million.

Thiokol has been hurt by its association with the fatal explosion of the space shuttle Challenger, which used a Thiokol-manufactured rocket motor. The spinoff has permitted Morton to focus on its specialty chemical division, Mr. Locke said, adding that he expects that operation will soon enjoy a turnaround.

"Specialty chemicals is down. It seems to be getting a little better . . . but we're still down," Mr. Locke said. But the demand for road salt, "particularly in Canada," is contributing to "clearly the best year ever" for Morton's salt operations, Mr. Locke said.

For the third quarter, pretax operating profit in Morton's salt business segment climbed to $30 million from the year-earlier $23 million, he said.

After 15 years of researching and developing its airbag business, the company is just beginning to realize a profit from the operation, Mr. Locke said. He estimated that third-quarter operating profit for the airbag segment totaled $3.7 million, compared to a loss of $1 million last year. Sales more than tripled in the latest period, to about $40 million from the year-earlier $12 million.

The recent growth in the airbag business, of which Morton holds nearly a 50% market share, is due largely to new federal safety regulations requiring automobile manufacturers to include driver-side passive restraint systems in 1990 model cars. Regulations also call for restraint systems on the passenger side in coming years.

Analysts estimate that Morton's airbag sales will rise from about $150 million for the current fiscal year, ending June 30, to about $350 million by fiscal year 1992.

Rising costs for raw materials, an electronics industry that has been "flat on its bottom" and cuts in auto output have caused the company's specialty chemical business to fall off, Mr. Locke said.

For the third quarter, the company estimates specialty chemical operating profit slipped to $36 million from $37 million the year earlier.

Morton's recent $220 million purchase of Whittaker Corp.'s specialty chemical segment will raise the company's specialty chemical sales to about $1.2 billion annually, Mr. Locke said.
