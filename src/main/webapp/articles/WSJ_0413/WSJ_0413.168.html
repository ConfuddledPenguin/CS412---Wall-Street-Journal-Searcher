B.F. Goodrich Posts 69% Decline in Profit For the First Quarter

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/13/90

AKRON, Ohio -- B.F. Goodrich Co. reported a 69% drop in first-quarter net income, mainly because of weak prices for polyvinyl chloride, a plastic that is the company's most important product. Nonetheless, the profit was somewhat higher than analysts had expected. The plastics, specialty chemicals and aerospace concern earned $16.2 million, or 56 cents a share, down from a first-quarter record of $53 million, or $2.02 a share, a year earlier. The 1989 first-quarter profit includes $2 million from discontinued operations.

Sales fell 4.2% to $589.8 million from $615.9 million.

Operating profit from polyvinyl chloride, or PVC, and related materials dropped 85% to $10.1 million from $68.1 million, on a 22% decline in sales to $272.1 million. Prices for PVC fell sharply in the 1989 second half and remained weak until the end of the 1990 first quarter. Meanwhile, the price of propane, an important feedstock or raw material, soared following the extremely cold weather in December.

Operating profit from specialty chemicals rose 33%, Goodrich said. Aerospace reported a 57% rise in operating profit, but more than two-thirds of this increase resulted from reversal of a previously established reserve, following Goodrich's victory in a patent infringement suit brought by a competitor.

Analysts expect a sharp improvement by Goodrich in subsequent quarters because of rising demand and higher prices for PVC. Leonard Bogner of Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. said that for 1990, he expects Goodrich to earn $3.75 a share, which would be a decline from 1989 net of $172.4 million, or $6.48 a share. But last year's results reflected a first-half boom in PVC, and 1989 profit was partly shielded from taxes by investment tax-credit carryforwards, which were exhausted by year end.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Goodrich shares closed at $42.375, up 12.5 cents.
