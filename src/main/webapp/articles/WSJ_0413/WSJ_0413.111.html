Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Toyota Picks OMON for Image Campaign

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 04/13/90

Toyota Motor has selected OMON, a tiny New York agency staffed by Australian creative executives, to develop a corporate-image campaign. The account, which will bill about $10 million this year, is a big victory for OMON, which beat out several larger agencies for the business, including Saatchi & Saatchi. OMON's parent agency in Australia is well regarded there, but the New York office opened only a year ago and has only about 15 employees.

OMON and Toyota, which has never done image advertising in the U.S., declined to comment on the new campaign. Executives close to Toyota said the auto maker is interested in, among other things, promoting its record as a large employer of Americans to gain favor with U.S. consumers. The campaign will begin next month.

The other agencies that bid for the account were Ingalls, Quinn & Johnson in Boston and Saatchi & Saatchi DFS/Pacific, Toyota's main agency, which created the long-running "I Love What You Do For Me" campaign.

OMON has announced only a few small business wins since it opened. But earlier this year it scored a quiet coup when Philip Morris invited it to pitch the $60 million Benson & Hedges account in competition with four much larger agencies. Philip Morris wound up retaining the account at Wells, Rich, Greene, but OMON walked away with a significant consolation prize that it didn't announce: the ad assignment for a new cigarette brand.

In an interview last year, OMON executives said the agency plans to hire some U.S. staffers, but said the creative department would be led by Australians. Its co-creative directors, George Betsis and Rodd Martin, opened the office last April after holding the top creative spots at Mojo's New York office.

But it is Joanne M. Davis, a former Wells Rich executive tapped by OMON last June to be president, who is credited with bringing in high-profile clients, industry executives said. As head of new-business development at Wells Rich, she led teams that helped win major business from International Business Machines and MCI.

Executives say Ms. Davis and her colleagues have worked to make the shop's Australian heritage a point of difference from other agencies. It's not a conventional Madison Avenue agency: At its arty headquarters in Manhattan's SoHo district, staffers answer the phone with a rousing "It's a great day at OMON]" greeting.

Shortly after it opened, OMON began a hunt for new business by sending selected companies copies of their ads that OMON judged to be of poor quality -- along with a note about why they should hire OMON. On each ad, the agency slapped a sticker with the headline, "It's quite possible that we're the only ones who noticed this ad." Some advertising people criticized the approach as brash, but others commended the shop for taking a gutsy stand.

"They're not going to be the agency for everybody," said Leslie Winthrop, president of Advertising Agency Register, which helps clients with ad agency searches. "But I think they'll probably bring opinions and ideas {to clients} that haven't been done here. They're not suffering from the Made-in-the-USA syndrome."

Chrysler Safety Campaign

Chrysler is launching an ad campaign that uses actual crash survivors to endorse the company's air-bag-equipped cars.

The campaign is the most aggressive step yet in the No. 3 U.S. auto maker's effort to make a marketing plus of outfitting all its 1990-model U.S.-made cars with air bags. Chrysler's campaign is unusual because it features testimonials from customers who have survived wrecks. It even shows pictures of smashed-up cars to dramatize the benefits of air bags.

In one of the new television spots, Karen McGowan of Columbia, Md., declares: "A Chrysler air bag saved my life." Chrysler Chairman Lee A. Iacocca appears with her and says: "I could give you a dozen reasons why you should consider a Chrysler product. But today I'll give you just one: Karen McGowan."

The television and print ads will continue the "Advantage: Chrysler" theme begun earlier this year by pointing out that a greater share of Chrysler cars come with standard air bags than any major Japanese car maker. Bozell created the new ads.
