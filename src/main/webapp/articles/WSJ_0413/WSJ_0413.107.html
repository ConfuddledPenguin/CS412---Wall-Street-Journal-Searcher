SEC Ready to Ease Private-Placement Rules

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi and Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 04/13/90

Regulators are poised to usher in a new era of selling bonds and stocks in the U.S. that could sharply swell the $165 billion market for privately placed securities. After a 1 1/2-year debate, the Securities and Exchange Commission next Thursday is expected to pass a rule that could exempt billions of dollars in new securities offerings from disclosure rules.

The chief beneficiaries will be big institutional investors, corporate issuers and Wall Street investment bankers. Individual investors, on the other hand, won't gain; they will be barred from buying any of the expected crop of new securities. And there are risks for buyers in a market with less-stringent disclosure requirements.

The SEC's new Rule 144a would exempt U.S. and foreign institutions from registration requirements for the sale of bonds and stocks to any institution already holding at least $100 million in securities.

These securities are sold in the private-placement market, which is limited to institutions; individual investors are shut out of this private market, which is largely used to sell corporate debt.

In effect, the SEC is expected to allow large players to trade among themselves without going back to the registration and disclosure process. This step could vastly enlarge the volume of bonds and stocks sold in the private-placement market, although the market isn't likely to mushroom overnight.

The rule would put a stamp of approval on a process that the SEC has tacitly allowed over the years among many institutions in the private market. By codifying these informal practices, the SEC will create a safe harbor that is expected to draw into the market institutions that had been reluctant to venture forth without regulatory guidance.

"The whole character of the private-placement market will change," says Sara Hanks, who until recently headed the SEC's international corporate finance unit.

The rule has several important implications. It's expected to attract droves of foreign companies to the U.S. markets to raise capital; foreign concerns have largely avoided the marketplace here because of stringent American disclosure rules.

And the rule could make U.S. markets more competitive, by lowering financing costs and keeping U.S. companies from heading overseas to finance themselves.

Equally important, the rule is likely to bolster liquidity -- the ease with which securities can be bought and sold in the secondary, or resale, market for private placements.

But the rule will be no panacea. That's because restrictions on snapping up private securities still exist among many of the expected buyers.

Mutual funds generally can't invest more than 10% of their assets in so-called restricted securities such as private placements. And state law doesn't allow insurance companies to buy private securities in a big way: New York state, for example, prohibits insurance firms from putting more than 1% of their assets in foreign securities. Until the restrictions are lifted, some say, the rule's impact will be blunted.

"The $64,000 question is: How long is it going to take to get buyers to come to the party, and how many will come once they decide what the party is?" says Curtis Welling, a managing director at First Boston Corp.

Says Robert Pozen, general counsel for Fidelity Investments, the nation's biggest mutual-fund group: "It's not going to be a huge revolution, but a modest evolution."

The rule may create yet another barrier for small investors, who can't trade these securities. By greatly expanding the market to which individual investors lack access, critics say, it will allow the big boys to use their size and clout to create even more favorable deals than they already get.

There are risks, too: Just because an institution holds a boatload of money doesn't necessarily make it a smart investor. Many large thrifts proved that. And the money that's at risk in a big pension fund ultimately belongs to small investors.

As a result, SEC Chairman Richard Breeden is considering seeking to impose an additional barrier for banks and thrifts, by setting minimum net capital standards. Mr. Breeden, a veteran of the thrift bailout, is said to be concerned about the sophistication of banks and thrifts. The SEC won't offer this proposal next Thursday, however.

Says Monte Wetzler, an attorney for Breed, Abbott & Morgan, a New York law firm: "The underlying assumption is that institutions are sophisticated enough to deal with the lack of disclosure."

Despite a lack of regulation, there is often more disclosure in the private market than for publicly traded securities, some securities executives contend. Jerome Greene, a managing director at Merrill Lynch & Co., says "the very personal process" of negotiating deals in the private market ensures that institutions receive adequate information. Of course, institutions that want more disclosure can demand a better rate or simply walk away.

The rule couldn't come at a better time for Wall Street. Stung by a deep recession, securities firms hope the rule eventually will be a shot in the arm. It also could help revive the private-placement market by removing the regulatory uncertainties that have deterred some companies from using it.

After booming throughout the 1980s, private placements have slowed recently amid the collapse of the junk-bond market. Nearly 82% of the $165 billion in securities sold through private placements last year was corporate debt, according to IDD Information Services.

But there will be outcasts from this market under whatever rule the SEC passes: companies whose stock or debt already is listed on an exchange or quoted over the counter.

The result nevertheless will be a chance for institutions to freely swap massive blocks of securities. "The impact is already occurring, much like {preparations for} 1992 in Europe," says John Miller, an executive vice president at Equitable Capital Management Corp. in New York. "Foreign issuers already are coming into the market more often. The pace has picked up in the past year or so."

The firms most likely to benefit from the new rule will be those with the biggest international presences, including Morgan Stanley Group Inc., First Boston and Salomon Inc., specialists say.

Among other things, the new rule is ideal for sovereign and semi-sovereign debt, executives say, because of the high quality of the issues. A debt offering in the private market likely to test the effect of the new rule is one by the State Bank of India. Philip Bennett, a director for capital markets at Salomon Brothers, says the India debt placement, and an expected issue for the State Bank of South Australia, "will be precursors of 144a offerings."

Moreover, the rule also might open up a market for easily trading controlling interests in companies that have been taken private in leveraged buy-outs. In that way, it could provide an alternative for LBO owners who don't want to take the companies public again to get their money out.

Frederick Zuckerman, vice president and treasurer at Chrysler Corp., said he thinks finance companies could emerge as the most frequent users of the new private market. He says the market would be ripe for "the guys who come to the party every other week for $100 million or so." He adds: "I just think it makes life a little easier for people who use the capital markets."

The New York Stock Exchange, meanwhile, may face pressure to upgrade its trading systems. That's because the SEC also is expected next Thursday to approve a new automated trading system by the National Association of Securities Dealers that the NASD hopes will serve as the central marketplace for privately placed securities.

The system, dubbed Portal -- for Private Offerings, Resales and Trading through Automated Linkages -- will be able to handle any bond or stock exempted from SEC registration under Rule 144a.
