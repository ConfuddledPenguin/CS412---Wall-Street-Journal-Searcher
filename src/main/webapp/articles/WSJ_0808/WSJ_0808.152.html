SAUDI ARABIA AGREED to boost its oil output to help make up for the loss of supplies from Iraq and occupied Kuwait, U.S. officials said. The Saudis haven't yet pledged to a specific figure, but the Bush administration was seeking a commitment to increase production by about two million barrels a day. Meanwhile, global sanctions against Iraq began to cut into its petroleum exports.

U.S. oil drillers are ill prepared to boost output in response to a world petroleum shortage because of a lack of equipment and experienced staff.

A federal budget accord is likely to come sooner because of the Mideast crisis and its likely economic effects. But the deficit-cutting goal for the first year will probably be lowered.

---

Tokyo stock prices tumbled Tuesday on concerns about the Mideast. The Nikkei fell 946.46 points, or 3.3%. In morning trading Wednesday, the index recovered 359.08 points.

---

American Airlines is expected to disclose plans to raise ticket prices by 10%. The move could supply the necessary momentum to sustain industrywide fuel-related fare increases.

---

Guarantees on Iraqi debt could cost the governments of the West, Japan and the Soviet Union tens of billions of dollars. Some international banks, meanwhile, could lose billions of dollars lent to Kuwait's banks.

---

Domestic coal producers could enjoy a comeback by expanding output at existing mines if the oil crisis pushes up coal demand and prices.

---

Emerson Electric plans to spin off to shareholders its defense units because they aren't profitable enough, despite $600 million in annual sales. The move is another sign of hard times ahead for military contractors.

---

The growth in consumer credit slowed to a 0.8% annual rate in June from 6.1% in May, indicating wariness among consumers. Auto-purchase debt declined, while bank-card and retail-store credit rose.

---

Pfizer plans to set up a registry of more than 55,000 patients who received a potentially defective heart valve sold by Pfizer's Shiley unit. The registry would be one of the first ever developed for a medical device.

---

The Manville personal-injury trust would be combined with other firms' funds and turned into a national facility for settling all asbestos cases, under a proposal by a federal judge.

---

A former Texas S&L owner and three ex-officers were indicted on charges stemming from the alleged misapplication of $121.1 million.

---

Quaker Oats expects to post lower profit for the current quarter. The food firm cited rising marketing costs.

---

Bank of New York withdrew as a primary dealer of U.S. government securities, citing declining profits.
