AMR Fare Rise Is Expected

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani and Jonathan Dahl
  • Date: 08/08/90

The push within the airline industry to raise fares because of rising oil prices might pass its biggest hurdle as early as today. Industry people said they expect AMR Corp.'s American Airlines to announce that it plans to raise ticket prices by 10% in the near future. The move is important because the momentum for a fuel-related fare rise probably would die without the acquiescence of the powerful Dallas-based carrier.

An American spokesman wouldn't comment on the reports, but said the carrier plans to make a fare-related announcement early today.

In recent days, Pan American World Airways and Northwest Airlines have released plans to raise their fares because of a surge in the price of aviation fuel: one of the biggest expenses of an airline. The Pan Am Corp. unit plans a 10% domestic fare increase effective tomorrow. The same day, NWA Inc.'s Northwest plans to institute an $8 ticket fuel surcharge on a one-way basis.

Yesterday, Trans World Airlines said it plans to implement a $20 fuel surcharge on trans-Atlantic fares on a one-way basis, Aug. 15. Pan Am plans to raise international fares 10% effective that same day.

The airlines are trying to compensate for price increases in aviation fuel that have occurred since Iraq invaded oil-rich Kuwait. TWA said its fuel costs have already risen 33%.

It isn't clear whether the fare rises will fly with consumers.

The carriers are trying to implement the fare increases at a time when traffic has been unusually soft. Traffic will also deteriorate with the approach of fall, when vacation travel evaporates and carriers often must resort to discount fares.

"With the climate the way it is, it's obviously not a good time to push through a fare increase," said Cornish Hitchcock, legal director at the Aviation Consumer Action Project.

But the airline industry has little alternative. Fuel costs account for about 15% to 20% of an airline's operating expenses.

One airline consulting firm projected yesterday that $25-a-barrel oil over a period of just a few months would result in one of the biggest hemorrhages of red ink for the airline industry since it was deregulated in 1978.

The firm, Washington, D.C.-based Airline Economics Inc., scrapped its projection that the industry would generate a $400 million to $500 million profit this year. It said it now expects the industry to post a $1 billion operating loss in 1990.

Last year, the industry had an operating profit of $1.8 billion, according to Airline Economics. Its best year since deregulation was 1988 with an operating profit of $3.4 billion.

The proposed fare increases would raise prices significantly. For example, under Pan Am's plan the cheapest round-trip fare between New York and Miami would increase to $216 from $196. American is expected to follow suit. In Northwest's proposal, the fare would increase to $212.

"I think it's horrible," says JoAnn Alesci, manager at the Travel Bug Inc. in Cleveland. "I've had people just sit in here and just be astonished at the prices everywhere."

If the planned fare increases go into effect, it would be the second time this year that the airlines have instituted boosts as a result of rising fuel costs.
