Economy: Consumer Credit's Growth Slowed Sharply in June

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 08/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The growth in consumer credit slowed substantially in June, the Federal Reserve Board reported, edging up $463 million following a rise of $3.65 billion in May. The June figure represents a 0.8% annual rate, compared with a 6.1% rate in May.

"Consumer spending is sluggish, so people are not extending themselves as far as credit is concerned," said Rosalind Wells, chief economist of the National Retail Merchants Association. "I don't think credit is really going to grow strongly."

Revolving credit, which consists mainly of bank card and retail-store accounts, rose $1.30 billion in June, trailing May's $3.19 billion.

Debt from the purchase of automobiles fell by $1.58 billion in June. Lending for mobile homes fell $85 million, while the miscellaneous category, which includes cash loans and certain retail financing contracts, increased by $827 million. As of June 30, consumer credit outstanding stood at $724.95 billion.

Economists say the figures indicate a wariness on the part of consumers. David Meer, a senior vice president at DYG Inc., thinks consumers are still cautious in the present economic climate. "We're still predicting that overall in this kind of consumer climate there will be a continuing effort by consumers to control spending."

Mr. Meer was "a little bit surprised" by the rise in revolving credit, but cautioned against unwarranted optimism over the figure, saying that the trend indicates that consumers are unsure about the future. "People are trying to come down from the binge of the 1980s," he said, adding: "The overall outlook is a sober one."

The figures are adjusted for changes in seasonal borrowing habits.
