Technology: Intel, Advanced Micro Each Claim Victory After Judge's Ruling

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 08/08/90

Intel Corp. and Advanced Micro Devices each claimed victory in a U.S. District Court judge's preliminary ruling in a copyright dispute over the use of Intel microcode technology in Advanced Micro's products. Since 1976 Intel has allowed Advanced Micro to use some microcode technology for internal office use, but last March the company filed a lawsuit to prohibit Advanced Micro from proceeding with a plan to distribute the technology in its own computer chips. Microcode is the software that tells a chip how to perform its function.

While not ruling on that final issue, U.S. District Court Judge William Ingram yesterday issued a preliminary injunction that bars Advanced Micro from using the Intel product name as an advertising tool or including computer chips bearing the Intel name in its product line. The judge, however, said Advanced Micro would be permitted to continue using a numbering system that mirror's Intel's own line of chips.

In Santa Clara, Calif., Intel's chief counsel said the court ruling just affirmed their position, and that the preliminary injunction was filed to "maintain the status quo" until a trial scheduled for February decides the copyrights of the company. In Sunnyvale, Calif., a spokesman for Advanced Micro said the company was equally satisfied because the judge refused to halt sales of the company's mathematics coprocessors with the "87" numbering system attached to the covers. Advanced Micro has been selling the products for about six weeks.

"The only issue we are interested in is the conflict over the numbering system used to designate the models," an Advanced Micro company spokesman said. He said the company had previously agreed to remove the Intel name from its computer chips and in its advertisements.
