TRADING RUBLES

The Soviet government announced a plan to allow some businesses and organizations to trade rubles for foreign currency at market rates.

The action, a step toward the ruble's convertibility, would establish currency markets in the Soviet Union beginning Jan. 1, 1991, the official Tass news agency said. Tass said the Soviet Council of Ministers made the decision on Saturday.

Legal enterprises and organizations, but apparently not individuals, would be permitted to buy and sell foreign currency in exchange for rubles at rates established by banks, currency auctions and other operations, Tass said. The report didn't say what the other operations would be or what other limits and controls would be placed on such markets.

Tass said the decision to set up a currency market was prompted by preparations for the Soviet Union's transition to a regulated market economy.

CHINA-INDONESIA AGREEMENT

Chinese Prime Minister Li Peng met Indonesian President Suharto in Jakarta yesterday and promised not to use ethnic Chinese in Indonesia to serve Beijing's own purposes. Mr. Li was in Indonesia to sign an agreement to resume diplomatic relations between the two countries. Relations were suspended after Indonesia accused Beijing of supporting an attempted coup by Communist rebels in 1965.

BANANA WORKERS BACK ON JOB

In Honduras, thousands of banana workers returned to their jobs after ending a strike that began June 25 against Chiquita Brands International Inc. The strike caused an estimated $60 million in losses to the Honduran economy. Threatened with dismissal after the government sent in troops to end the strike, the workers accepted a 25% pay raise; they had earlier demanded more than 50%.

MATSUSHITA-IBM AGREEMENT

Matsushita Electric Industrial Co.'s Taiwan personal-computer subsidiary will make PCs for International Business Machines Corp. after the subsidiary starts operations this October, a Matsushita spokesman said. The Japanese company's spokesman said the agreement to make 32-bit PCs for IBM on an original-equipment-manufacturing basis represents a further strengthening of the relationship between the two companies. Matsushita already manufactures IBM's PC2 at the company's facilities in Illinois.

JAPANESE IMPORT PANEL

Japan will set up a 30-member Import Board to help slash its trade surplus. Establishment of the panel, comprising government officials, Japanese businessmen and foreign business executives, was approved by the Trade Conference, the Ministry of International Trade and Industry said. The conference is chaired by Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu and includes cabinet members and Japanese business leaders. The new board will meet twice a year, beginning this fall.

WARSAW NEAR BANKRUPTCY

Warsaw is almost out of money because Poland's economic-reform program has left thousands of businesses facing bankruptcy and unable to make tax payments, the city government reported.

Both large enterprises and small factories have had trouble meeting their obligations, causing a dramatic drop in revenues collected by the Warsaw government, the Polish media reported. In the province of Warsaw, which includes the city and surrounding villages, local governments have paid only 3% of their annual dues to the provincial government, the Zycie Warszawy newspaper said.

The local governments owe 481 billion zlotys ($50.6 million) -- money they are unable to raise because they managed to collect only 40% of the taxes owed to the village administrations, the newspaper said.

"The {Warsaw} province is on the edge of bankruptcy," a provincial official said, adding that a precise budget review would be completed this month.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

With most of Europe in the grips of a drought, water restrictions are widespread. Yesterday, a woman caught watering her garden in southwest France was fined $250. In Britain, drought orders are in force in 26 districts, and those who disobey can be fined up to $750. . . . Switzerland's Federal Court agreed with retiree Andre Cornu that cowbells worn by neighboring farmers' herds can be a form of noise pollution. Mr. Cornu said the court ruled that bells must come off all cows right next to his property "and bronze bells must not be used at all."
