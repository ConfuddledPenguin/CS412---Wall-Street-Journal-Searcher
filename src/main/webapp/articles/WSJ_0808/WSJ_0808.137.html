End to Antitrust Exemption On Airline Fares Is Urged

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The Justice Department urged the Department of Transportation to rescind the antitrust exemption under which U.S. airlines participate in fare-setting for international flights. The department said that the fare-setting practices of the Geneva-based International Air Transport Association, the trade group for the major world airlines, are anti-competitive and result in higher fares.

IATA's members, acting under a grant of antitrust immunity first granted in 1946 by the U.S. government, hold tariff conferences to set fares. In 1985, the DOT extended the exemption, but said it would review the policy in five years. The Justice Department's recommendation was in response to IATA's application for a renewed exemption.

The recommendation wasn't unexpected. The Justice Department had sought to end the exemption in the late 1970s and early 1980s, according to Bert Rein, one of IATA's Washington attorneys. "The aeronautical experts rejected it," he said.

The Transportation Department declined to comment on the filing.
