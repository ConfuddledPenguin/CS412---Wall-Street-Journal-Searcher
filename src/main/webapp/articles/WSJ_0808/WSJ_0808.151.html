The Mideast Conflict: U.S. Seeks to Guard Itself Against Impact of Boycott

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib and Barbara Rosewicz
  • Date: 08/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The clock has begun ticking for Iraqi President Saddam Hussein, whose access to world oil markets has been closed by a multinational embargo. But the U.S. is scrambling to minimize the damage the boycott could do to its own economy. Saudi Arabia yesterday agreed to increase its oil production in a move that could cushion much of the coming embargo-related oil shock, according to senior Bush administration officials. The Saudis didn't commit to a specific increase in production, although there were indications that talks would continue. The White House had been seeking an increase of about two million barrels a day in the kingdom's current output of 5.4 million barrels a day.

The agreement represented a major breakthrough in the growing global campaign against Mr. Hussein. The Saudis in recent years have forsworn their one-time role as the "swing producer" in OPEC, and have always resisted appearing sensitive to Western influence. In the delicately balanced world-wide oil market, two million barrels a day of extra production could cut several dollars from the price of a barrel of crude.

Also yesterday, Vice President Dan Quayle met with Venezuelan President Carlos Andres Perez, who reversed his earlier position and agreed to increase his nation's oil production by 500,000 barrels a day, according to an administration official traveling with the vice president.

In addition, the U.S. decided to send a representative to a meeting of the International Energy Agency tomorrow to discuss plans for the U.S., Germany, Japan and other nations to use jointly their strategic petroleum reserves to ease the pain of the embargo. Officials say the White House has not yet decided whether it will use the U.S. reserve but wants to "feel out" the other nations.

The embargo of Iraqi oil began to show its full force yesterday. Turkey said it is turning away ships loading oil from an Iraqi oil export pipeline. And the flow of oil through Iraq's pipeline across Saudi Arabia also is likely to be shut down by Sunday, oil experts say, because storage tanks will be full and tankers aren't loading the oil. That means Mr. Hussein will lose, for now, the oil revenues he needs to maintain his military machine and feed his army.

"Realistically, he ought to be recalculating," says Richard Murphy, the State Department's top Middle East diplomat in the Reagan administration. "It is hard to believe, though we don't know, that he had expected the opposition that was generated in the last few days."

But U.S. government estimates show Iraq has enough food to last into the fall, even without oil money. "I think the food situation is better than it has been for some years," says Jonathan Crusoe, an Iraqi specialist at the Middle East Economic Digest who just returned from that country. "There's more local produce in the shops than before."

In the meantime, soaring oil prices could cut deep into the economies of the rest of the world. That means that unless the Mideast standoff explodes into a military confrontation, Iraq and the industrialized world will be caught in a high-stakes war of nerves to see who flinches first.

Mr. Hussein is a hardened risk taker, who has shown that he has incredible staying power and the ruthlessness to win. When he tried, at the age of 22, to assassinate Iraq's military leader, he was shot in the attempt and dug the bullet out of his thigh as he fled across the desert. And he proved he could endure a decade of intense economic pain during the Iran-Iraq war. "I think in a sense he does have more patience than we do," says Kenneth Katzman, a defense consultant who studied Mr. Hussein as a Central Intelligence Agency analyst. "Just choking him off won't deflate him."

Moreover, Mr. Hussein may get some help from neighboring Jordan, which has supported Iraq's move and could provide an overland route for bringing in food and other vital imports. In addition, Turkish truck drivers, whose workload has declined since the Iran-Iraq war ended, will undoubtedly be itching to sneak loads of goods across the long Iraqi-Turkish border.

Mr. Hussein is also likely to try to intimidate other oil sheikdoms, particularly Saudi Arabia, into giving him money. "Even if the embargo works 100%, they (the Iraqis) will still have revenues because the Saudis are going to pay bribes," says Patrick Clawson, an economist at the Foreign Policy Research Institute who has dealt extensively with the Middle East. The U.S., however, is sure to try and stop the Saudis from making any such payments.

In the meantime, the test for the boycotting nations will be to maintain their resolve in the face of high oil prices. Over time, Mr. Hussein is likely to find a way to sell some of his oil to energy-hungry nations. Indeed, without the formation of an effective international naval blockade, the embargo almost certainly will leak as oil prices rise. "There are always cheaters," says Michael Ameen, a Houston consultant who formerly worked for Mobil Corp. "There are always people who say they're not going to buy, and then they turn around and buy."

The industrialized world, meanwhile, is going to be badly hurt by the oil-price increase resulting from the embargo. "It will slow the rate of growth considerably in Europe and Japan, although they can probably avoid recession," says Robert Hormats, vice chairman of Goldman Sachs International. "In the U.S., however, if oil prices stay at these levels, it's probably impossible to avoid recession."

Japan is the nation most dependent on imported oil. But it is in some ways the best-positioned to deal with a price increase -- thanks to the energy-conservation measures and industrial restructuring that have taken place in the past decade. The U.S., on the other hand, has been less aggressive in reducing its dependence on imported oil. Moreover, the oil price spike comes at a particularly bad time for the U.S., because its economy is so weak.

The U.S. has been hoping to ease the pain of a jump in oil prices by convincing Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, Venezuela, Nigeria and Ecuador to produce more oil.

Venezuela's President Perez promised Mr. Quayle to help Brazil, which has been receiving large quantities of Iraqi oil. And he said Venezuela will do everything it can to keep the price of oil at the $21-a-barrel price agreed to recently by the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries.

John Easton, assistant energy secretary for international affairs, will represent the Bush administration at the IEA meeting in Paris. The U.S. has 590 million barrels of oil in its reserve, while Japan has 140 million barrels and Germany 100 million barrels. Those three nations alone could make up for the lost Iraqi and Kuwaiti oil for about 200 days.

Administration officials say they would prefer to act in concert with other nations. "Obviously using (the strategic reserve) unilaterally . . . wouldn't be nearly as beneficial for American consumers as if we can arrange for coordinating a multinational drawdown of stocks," said Linda Stuntz, deputy undersecretary of energy.

Ms. Stuntz said the U.S. Energy Department is preparing a list of options for President Bush to respond to the crisis. The list doesn't include any proposal for price controls, but does include such measures as encouraging industries to switch from oil to natural gas, and encouraging conservation measures -- for example enforcing speed limits and increasing tire pressure in vehicles.
