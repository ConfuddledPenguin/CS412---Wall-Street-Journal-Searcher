CPA Body to Allow Some Limited Use Of Contingent Fees

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Trade Commission issued a final consent order under which the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants agreed not to restrict accountants from accepting contingent fees and commissions from non-audit clients. The governing council of the institute, the major professional body for U.S. accountants, effectively had lifted its ban on most such practices in May. But the institute was awaiting a final FTC order before formally changing its rules.

The institute had agreed in August 1988 to settle FTC charges that its ban on contingent fees restrained competition.

The institute has a membership of nearly 300,000 CPAs in public practice, but its rules don't override state law or the rules of state CPA societies.

An FTC complaint issued with the order charges the institute illegally restrained competition and deprived consumers of information about "the availability, price and quality of CPA services." The order, issued on a 3-2 vote, doesn't constitute an admission of a violation, but carries the force of law regarding future actions.

Under the final order, the institute agreed not to restrict CPAs from working for non-audit clients for contingent fees, or accepting commissions, as long as the CPAs disclose them to their clients. The institute also agreed not to ban fees for referring clients to CPAs, or to bar nondeceptive advertising. Disclosure of referral fees and commissions on services and products also may be required.

Contingent fees are those in which accountants take an engagement where the final fee is based on getting a benefit for the client. Commissions are paid for services such as advising a client to buy specific computer hardware, or taking an audit or consulting job when the final fee is based on the benefit derived by the client.

Under modifications by the commission, the order allows the institute to prohibit CPAs from accepting contingent fees for preparing tax returns. It also limits acceptance of the fees and commissions to non-audit clients, and allows the institute to prohibit CPAs from providing audit services to clients where they have provided services for a contingent fee or commission. The earlier proposal would have removed restrictions on doing such work for audit clients.
