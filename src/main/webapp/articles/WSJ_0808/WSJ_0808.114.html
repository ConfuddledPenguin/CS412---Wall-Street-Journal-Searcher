Mutual Funds: Track Records That Signal Peace or Peril

  • Author: Ellen E. Schultz
  • Date: 08/08/90

Mutual fund investors who are worried about what will happen to their stock funds if the recent market drop continues and the economy slides into recession might step back and take a look at the record. While past performance is no guarantee of future results, looking at how well funds held up in past market declines may provide valuable clues about what to expect.

Many small investors have been busily shifting their money out of stock funds in recent days, as news of the Middle East conflict added new fuel to fears of recession and inflation. Some major mutual fund groups said redemptions slowed somewhat yesterday, though official tallies won't be available for a couple of days.

Typically, some types of stock funds hold up better than others when the market falls. Equity-income and balanced funds, for example, tend to drop less than the market as a whole, because they contain bonds and high-dividend stocks; growth and growth-and-income funds tend to perform in line with the market.

But just looking at categories won't tell investors what may happen to their funds, because there are many exceptions. Getting an idea about how a specific fund is likely to perform requires looking at its own performance history.

For example, take Acorn Fund, a small-company growth fund with assets of nearly $1 bil-lion. Because small-company growth funds are among the most volatile stock mutual funds, it would be easy to assume that Acorn can get squashed when the market falls. But a look at figures compiled by Lipper Analytical Services Inc. shows this isn't the case.

Acorn not only has outpaced other small-company growth funds over the past decade, but it did better than the market in three out of four downturns. In the week ended last Thursday, the latest week for which statistics are available, Acorn declined 2.9%. This compares with a loss of 3.4% for small-company growth stock funds as a group.

Investors who want to determine down-market performance of their funds won't find it easy. Mutual funds generally quote performance figures on an annual basis, while market drops occur at untidy intervals. Investors will have to ask the fund representative what happened during specific down cycles.

Taking the trouble to ask can be well worthwhile to investors, however, in helping them judge the desirabil-ity of various funds. While different fund groups and different individual funds may have similar long-term returns, they can achieve those results with vastly different gyrations. This can be important to investors who are easily scared when the going gets tough.

For example, equity-income, growth-and-income and growth mutual fund groups all have 10-year annualized results of 15.3%. But over the decade, growth and growth-and-income funds fluctuated widely, while equity-income funds gave investors a smoother ride, tending to fall only about half as much as the market in downturns and to rise less in market climbs.

The same holds for individual funds. For example, consider Vanguard Group's Windsor Fund, a widely held no-load fund that now is closed to new investors, and Washington Mutual Investors, a broker-sold fund managed by Capital Research & Management Cos. Both are growth-and-income funds that gave investors an average annual return of 19.1% in the 10 years through June 30, according to Lipper.

But while Windsor had a loss of 3.8% in the week ended last Thursday, Washington Mutual was off only 1.1%. Looking back at previous market downturns shows that Washington Mutual had smaller losses than Windsor in three of the four deepest declines in the 1980s.
