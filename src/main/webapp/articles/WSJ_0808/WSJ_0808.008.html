Foreign Exchange: Dollar Rises on Fears of Military Clash In Mideast and Slowing Oil Price Boosts

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/08/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar rose against all major currencies on profit-taking, fears of a new military clash in the Middle East and slowing price increases in the oil markets. Foreign exchange traders gingerly consolidated earlier price movements, lifting the dollar across the board and aiding the yen against Continental European currencies. Dealings were kept edgy by the tense situation in the Mideast.

Iraq's invasion of Kuwait has been considered a mixed blessing for the dollar, but everything worked in the currency's favor yesterday. Fears that a military clash would send the world's safe-haven currency soaring ensured the dollar was never wanting for buyers.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.5875 marks, up from 1.5765 marks late Monday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 150.95 yen, above 150.32 yen in New York late Monday. Sterling was trading at $1.8720, marginally below $1.8725 late Monday.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the dollar was trading soon after the opening at 150.27 yen, down from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 150.60 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at 1.5880 marks; in Tokyo Tuesday, it closed at 1.5767 marks.

Also lending support for the dollar was a deceleration in the rise of crude oil prices. Until news reports hit the market late in the New York session that U.S. troops were moving into Saudi Arabia, profit-taking by commodity traders had encouraged currency dealers to chalk up some gains of their own from the dollar's decline Monday.

The prospect that higher oil prices would pitch the U.S. into recession had been the dark side of Iraqi adventurism for the dollar. But by the end of trading yesterday, the September benchmark crude oil contract was up only 26 cents a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange at $28.31 a barrel.

That helped slow declines in New York stock prices, which ended mixed. The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed down 5.70 points to 2710.64.

Dollar dealers have tried to be especially nimble recently and were ready to cast aside economic considerations at a moment's notice.

The dollar hit its peaks yesterday of 1.5870 marks and 1.3365 Swiss francs when rumors whirled through the market that British Broadcasting Corp. reported Iraqi troops had crossed into Saudi Arabia. The BBC denied making such a report.

Near the end of trading, news reports that U.S. troops were moving into Saudi Arabia pushed the dollar near its intraday peak of 151.10 yen reached much earlier at the opening of Asian trading. Initially, the White House wouldn't comment on any troop movements, but after the close of New York trading, U.S. Defense Department sources confirmed it.

Earlier, a U.S. State Department comment that Iraq was improving its troop readiness along the Saudi border gave the dollar a similar boost.

"One moment everything seems like it's getting better. The next moment it looks like it's blowing up," said Marc Cohen, a New York vice president for corporate foreign exchange with Fuji Bank. "There isn't a lot of strategic thinking going on. Everyone is sort of trading on air. You take a position and then hope that everyone will react right way to the next headline."

"I'm not really looking at currency rates," said Donald Hubbard, a New York treasury officer with the U.K.'s Midland Bank. "I'm just looking at the price of oil and the news headlines."

Mr. Hubbard said the Iraqi invasion has made the market busier than it normally is in August and has also altered its character. "It's a different type of trader that was in (yesterday). The jobbers that come in for a few (small prices moves) were a bit shell shocked by the big moves last week and are on the sidelines. Now we're seeing more genuine commercial flows."

Commented a dollar dealer in Wellington, New Zealand: "The market fundamentally would like to sell dollars, but it is so nervous, it doesn't want to get too short. The trend is really to stay out of the market because no one knows what's going to happen next" in Kuwait.

Lynn Tierney, a vice president at Shawmut Bank of Boston, said talk in the North American morning that the U.S. Federal Reserve Board would call an emergency meeting later in the day to discuss looser credit helped pressure the dollar. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York wouldn't comment on the rumor.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $384.50, down 20 cents. Estimated volume was a very heavy 6.5 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $384.95 an ounce.
