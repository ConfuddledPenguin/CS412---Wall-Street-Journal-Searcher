Ex-Owner of Texas Thrift, Three Others Indicted in Case Involving $121.1 Million --- By Christi Harlan Staff Reporter of The Wall Street Journal End

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/08/90

The former owner and three ex-officers of the defunct Lamar Savings Association of Austin, Texas, were indicted on federal criminal charges related to the alleged misapplication of $121.1 million of thrift funds. Former owner Stanley E. Adams Jr., who once sought permission to open a Lamar Savings branch on the moon, was named in all 14 counts of the indictment.

The indictment, returned late yesterday by a federal grand jury in Austin, also charged former Lamar Savings officers Milton R. Perry and Reuben Coleman, both of Austin, and Frederick Scheibe of San Antonio.

The indictment is the latest in the Justice Department's stepped-up effort to prosecute the people believed most responsible for the billions of dollars in losses at the nation's thrifts. The crackdown so far has been focused primarily on Texas S&Ls.

Lamar Savings collapsed in late 1985 after having ballooned to become the 12thlargest thrift in Texas. The thrift, which had about $2 billion in assets when it failed, fueled its growth by offering some of the highest rates of interest available in the U.S. for deposits, which drew millions of dollars to the institution. The deposits were used to finance real-estate ventures that later soured.

The indictment accuses Mr. Adams and the three former officers of conspiring to conceal Lamar Savings' troubled financial condition from federal thrift regulators. As part of those efforts, the indict ment alleges, the thrift officials would arrange for borrowers to sign up for "sham loans" to finance purchases of repossessed real estate from the thrift's portfolio. The borrowers, in many instances, weren't required to repay the loans, but the thrift was able to book immediate gains for making the loans and for "selling" troubled real estate, according to the indictment.

Two Lamar Savings borrowers, real estate developers Louis G. Reese III and Robert Hayden Brown, both of Dallas, were each indicted on conspiracy and five other charges. Mr. Reese was a borrower at several now-failed Texas thrifts, including Sunbelt Savings Association of Texas and Western Savings Association.

Mr. Reese's attorney, William M. Ravkind of Dallas, said his client was forced to take on the loans for Lamar's repossessed real estate in order to obtain financing for his own real-estate projects. Mr. Brown's attorney couldn't be reached for comment.

None of the three former officers could be reached for comment.

The indictment of Mr. Adams had been expected for several weeks, mainly because of an unusual public-relations campaign by Mr. Adams himself.

Beginning in mid-May, an Austin public-relations operative retained by Mr. Adams arranged news conferences to needle federal investigators for overlooking alleged wrongdoing by Lamar borrowers and others; the operative, Garry Entress, was subpoenaed to appear before the federal grand jury hearing evidence in the case.

Yesterday, the Justice Department issued a news release in Washington, D.C., before Mr. Adams's attorney, David Botsford of Austin, was informed of the indictment.

Mr. Botsford said he couldn't comment on the specific charges because he hadn't seen the indictment, but said he would "be very surprised if that indictment alleges that Stanley Adams profited by one dime from any of these alleged crimes."

"He gave his heart, soul and resources to that institution," the attorney said, adding: "I rather suspect that Mr. Adams has been singled out for selective prosecution for his reputation in the industry as a maverick."

Mr. Botsford was referring to Mr. Adams's celebrated application to federal thrift regulators for permission to open a Lamar Savings branch on the moon, going so far as to name a specific crater where the National Aeronautics and Space Administration had indicated it would build a space station.

Federal thrift regulators didn't act on the application.
