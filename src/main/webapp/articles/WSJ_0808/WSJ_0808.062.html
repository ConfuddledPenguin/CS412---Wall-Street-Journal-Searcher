First Boston Corp. Repackages Bonds Into Lower Risks

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 08/08/90

NEW YORK -- First Boston Corp. said it repackaged $245 million of low-rated junk bonds into new securities with credit ratings as high as double-A. The First Boston transaction marks a bit of revival for such repackagings, which are known as collateralized bond obligations. CBOs briefly flourished last year, when they were hailed as a way to make junk-bond investing palatable to mainstream, credit-conscious investors. After a few boom months, however, CBOs fell into obscurity during the general junk-bond collapse of early 1990.

Robert Long, director of high-yield research at First Boston, said the CBO is his firm's second such offering this year; the first occurred in February. "I expect we'll see significantly more of these," he said.

The CBO is being sold to First Boston Asset Management, a unit of First Boston that invests money for pension funds and other clients. It was structured as a private placement that didn't need to be registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission.

First Boston is a unit of CS First Boston Group.

Mr. Long said the CBO draws upon a freshly acquired $245 million portfolio of bonds that are rated double-B and single-B. Interest and principal payments from those bonds will be divided into three sets of new securities. Two of these new classes of securities, totaling $117 million and $49 million, will be "over-collateralized," with more than $117 million and $49 million respectively of junk bonds backing them up, Mr. Long said.

That way, even if some of the individual bonds default, these two classes still would collect adequate cash flows to meet their stated yields of 1.8 percentage points and 2.1 percentage points above rates on comparable Treasury securities, First Boston said. This overcollateralization also allows those two classes to qualify for double-A ratings, First Boston said.

The CBO's final class, however, will be far riskier. It will have far less specific junk-bond collateral. Instead, it will be entitled to whatever interest income is left over from the junk bonds that back the first two classes, once the two double-A segments have made their obligatory payments. If default rates are what First Boston expects, the bottom class could yield more than 20%, Mr. Long said. If default rates are a lot higher, the bottom class could yield very little or even be a source of losses for investors.
