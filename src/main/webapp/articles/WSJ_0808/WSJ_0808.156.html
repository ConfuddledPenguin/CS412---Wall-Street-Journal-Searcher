Oil Firms Fret as Tempers Boil, Prices Jump --- PR Challenge Being Fumbled, Experts Claim

  • Author: Amanda Bennett
  • Date: 08/08/90

Turmoil in the Mideast has handed American oil companies a big public relations challenge: How to explain why prices at the pump jumped so fast. But so far, industry experts and crisis management consultants say, the oil companies are fumbling, shouldering the brunt of Americans' anger even though the situation may be beyond their control.

"The oil industry must enjoy wearing the black hat," says Paul Fullmer, president of the Chicago-based public-relations firm Selz, Seabolt & Associates. "They have again dropped the ball."

But others say that, given the circumstances, the oil companies are doing the best they can. "Given the uncertainties in the Middle East, it's probably inappropriate for them to do much more than they are doing," says Robert Dilenschneider, chief executive of Hill & Knowlton, a public relations firm that advises major oil companies. "It's like driving on a mountain in a heavy storm. You're not sure what's around the next bend."

Still, any mistakes could prove costly in the long run. U.S. oil companies are already reeling from such public relations disasters as the grounding of the Exxon Valdez. In the early 1970s, oil shortages and price increases after the Arab oil embargo helped lead to the loss of some industry tax credits, the passage of a windfall profits tax and an antitrust complaint against the eight biggest oil companies.

This time, industry officials fear that the public outcry could set back industry efforts to regain drilling rights in environmentally sensitive areas -- at a time when the industry needs them the most.

The oil companies say they are doing all they can to explain why price increases are necessary. "Our policy is to be prompt, straightforward, sincere and honest with all inquiries," Shell Oil Co. said in a written response to questions yesterday. "We recognize we are dealing with an extremely volatile and consumer-sensitive area."

Douglas Elmets, director of media relations for Atlantic Richfield Co., says that between the time of the invasion and yesterday afternoon, he was interviewed 92 times, while the president of the company's marketing arm was involved in six interviews with TV stations, one with radio and three with major newspapers.

But even those who have tried to get their positions out to the public say the industry could do better. Dale Basye, manager of corporate communications at Chevron Corp., says the company had up to 10 people at once answering inquiries, and made senior economists available for a televised rebuttal of an industry pricing critic's charges.

Still, he says, the company hasn't yet decided whether to involve senior corporate officers: "It's something we've been looking at," he says. "Unfortunately, it's been reactive up to this point. Now we are looking at ways to take the initiative."

And privately, some industry spokespeople bemoan the industry's passivity. "You always say we wish we had {done more}," says one. "I'm not the decision maker." Another complains that competitors were "unwilling or reluctant" to address the topic directly, while yet another berates industry officials for "burying their heads in the sand."

Since the industry takes the position that immediate price increases are necessary and unavoidable, public relations and crisis experts say, the industry should have moved much more quickly and actively to explain that. In particular, they say, a crisis in which oil supplies were interrupted and prices rose quickly was predictable, so the oil companies should have prepared their dealer network to deal with it.

"They should have had illustrations available on how the whole domino effect takes place, and posters and handouts available for dealers," says Mr. Fullmer of Selz Seabolt. "If they were done ahead of time, they could have been printed within a day or two."

Mary Woodell, a crisis management director at consultants Arthur D. Little Inc., agrees. "It's the dealer or retailer who takes the heat. I would advise them to give dealers as much advance notice as possible, a short statement of purpose, of why we are doing this, so that they have something to say to the people who go to the gas tank." Otherwise, she says, "the retailer can be left flat-footed and bearing the brunt of the blame."

Mobil Corp. says such materials for dealers are "in the pipeline."

Others suggest that oil companies should have tried harder to align themselves with the American public against a common, outside, enemy. Otherwise, says Ian Mitroff, director of the Center for Crisis Management at the University of Southern California's business school, the blame falls on the oil companies by default: "When you can't do much about an external threat, you have to project and put that threat somewhere. That's Freud 101. The anger has to go somewhere, especially when you feel impotent."

Herbert Schmertz, a former senior public affairs official with Mobil Corp., now heads a private consulting company bearing his name. He believes that oil companies should have quickly seized the initiative to try to affect U.S. energy policy: "There is a need to explain to the American public {that} by misguided governmental policies we have put ourselves in a position where we are dependent both politically and economically on imported oil. They should be making that case to the American public," he says, adding, "I don't see any sign of it."

Mr. Schmertz, long an advocate of lobbying aggressively directly to the public, recommends a combination of paid advertising and sending oil company executives out to speak.
