Letters to the Editor: Forget the Dream of Military Primacy

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/08/90

Martin Feldstein's article "Trade Economic Aid for Soviet Arms Cuts" (editorial page, June 28) described how the U.S. could reach "clear military superiority" over the Soviet Union. From his point of view, it would be foolish not to spend "a few tens of billions of dollars" to obtain a major reduction in Soviet military capacity in exchange for massive economic aid. In other words, he suggests using recent Soviet economic difficulties to fulfill an old dream in the West -- military superiority over the Soviet Union.

Even though he mentions the necessity of some U.S. military reductions, the U.S. superiority thus obtained supposedly would ensure peace for the rest of the world. Can you imagine what the American reaction would be if some people in the Soviet Union aspired to the role of "world peacekeepers" in such a manner?

Mr. Feldstein is wrong in thinking my country could ever sell its security for consumer goods and know-how. Recently, the Communist Party Congress in Moscow adopted a resolution on national defense policy saying in particular that a military threat to the Soviet Union persists as long as there are no guarantees of the irreversibility of positive changes in the military and political situation. Under these conditions the Party Congress considers it a major task to strengthen and maintain the Soviet Union's defense capacity at a reasonably sufficient level.

As one can see from the example of the postwar period, the desire to have military superiority has fed the arms race and increased international tension and the threat of war. The belief in my country is that the existing rough parity between the Soviet Union and the U.S., and between the Warsaw Treaty Organization and NATO, is the decisive factor preventing wars. This parity should be maintained, but at steadily decreasing levels.

The Soviet Union is already applying this principle. From 1989 to the end of this year the Soviet armed forces will be unilaterally reduced by 500,000 men and officers, 10,000 tanks, 8,500 artillery systems and 820 combat aircraft. In 1989 it unilaterally reduced its armed forces by 265,000 men and officers, withdrew 27,400 servicemen from the Northern, Western, Central and Southern groups of forces in Europe and 22,900 from Mongolia. The number of Soviet weapons in Europe went down by 9,320 tanks, 5,050 artillery systems and 835 warplanes.

More than 59,000 servicemen, 1,370 tanks, more than 1,040 artillery systems and 173 combat aircraft have been withdrawn from Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Poland and Mongolia this year.

The Soviet navy has scrapped 12 submarines and 28 surface ships, and moth-balled three submarines and 11 surface ships. Besides, 500 tactical nuclear charges have been withdrawn from Warsaw Treaty countries. The Soviet Union is prepared to pull all its nuclear weapons out from allied countries, if the U.S. acts likewise.

The restructuring of the Soviet armed forces is proceeding under the sign of defense. But realization of defense sufficiency is possible only on the basis of reciprocity.

Reciprocity is the only way to curb the arms race and disarm. This is the best way we have of achieving, using Mr. Feldstein's words, "greater military security" and "greater global stability." And it is also the best way for the U.S. to reduce safely its own military spending.

Alexander Malyshkin

Senior Information Officer

Soviet Embassy

Washington
