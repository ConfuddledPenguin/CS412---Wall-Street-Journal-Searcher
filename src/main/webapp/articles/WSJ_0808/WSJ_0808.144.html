The Mideast Conflict: Bank of England to Let Kuwait Agency Manage Holdings During Asset Freeze

  • Author: Tim Carrington
  • Date: 08/08/90

LONDON -- The Bank of England said it plans to allow Kuwait's massive investment arm to manage its holdings on a normal basis, so long as assets inside the U.K. don't leave the nation. Bank of England officials adopted this relatively flexible approach as they set out rules governing Britain's freeze on Iraq and Kuwait assets in the U.K. The freeze was imposed after Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, but the terms of the policy weren't spelled out until yesterday.

British officials said that while assets of the London-based Kuwait Investment Office are included in the freeze, normal portfolio management should continue.

"We're working with the KIO to try to devise for them a regime where they can operate their portfolio management as freely as they choose so long as their assets don't leave this jurisdiction," said one bank official. The main goal of the asset-freeze is to keep the Iraqi government from seizing control of the investment arm, whose holdings are estimated at $100 billion.

The KIO has been a major player in world markets, owning shares in dozens of British and Spanish companies, as well as several West German concerns, including Hoechst AG and Daimler-Benz AG.

Also included in Britain's asset-freeze is Kuwait Petroleum Co. Bank of England said that consistent with the goal of keeping these assets beyond the reach of the Iraqi forces occupying Kuwait, local offices of the oil concern would be able to carry on normal commerce.

The British freeze on assets covers both residents and institutions from the two countries. Kuwaiti institutions can draw on U.K. accounts to make pension and salary payments to Kuwaiti citizens living in Britain.

The rules governing the freeze of Iraqi assets don't provide for the same leeway. Bank of England officials noted that Iraqi assets in the U.K. are far smaller than the Kuwaiti assets. The Bank of England said it will consider giving permission for withdrawals of Iraqi funds needed to cover medical emergencies or travel costs of Iraqi citizens wishing to return home.

The Bank of England estimated that British banks have about $1 billion of claims on Iraq currently. The current rules don't provide for Iraqi assets to be confiscated in support of these claims. But bank officials said that in the event of a default, "they would be subject to the normal legal process."

The Bank of England has established a 10-person "emergency unit" to administer the rules. Violations could be prosecuted as criminal offenses.

The U.S., Japan and other major European nations have all imposed their own freeze-orders on Kuwaiti and Iraqi assets. Bank of England officials said there will be future efforts to coordinate the procedures and regulations each country adopts.

One problem, for example, is that the Bank of England rules don't apply to foreign branches of U.K. banks. However, officials said that U.K. banks won't be allowed to transfer Kuwaiti assets to foreign branches. "We don't want this to become a bypass," said a Bank of England official.
