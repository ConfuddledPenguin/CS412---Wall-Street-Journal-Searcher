Letters to the Editor: Passing the Buck On a Colossal Scale

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/08/90

If the White House is seriously contemplating responding to the S&L scandal by slashing federal deposit insurance coverage from $100,000 to $50,000 (page one, July 3), then the scheme represents one of the most colossal pass-the-buck scenarios ever devised. Such an "overhaul," as the Journal generously refers to it, smacks of an effort by the Bush administration to assess blame for the $500 billion crisis on depositors in the failed banks -- when, of course, it was the bankers who mismanaged their funds and the federal regulators who blithely turned a blind eye as if nothing untoward was happening, until it was too late.

If Washington is serious about creating an atmosphere under which this kind of scandal can never reoccur, it will turn away from scapegoating depositors and instead create a system of oversight that compels stricter compliance with the rules that remain on the books.

For example, when the Feds deregulated the banks they also cut back on their staffs of examiners. But prudent deregulation does not call for the total withdrawal of oversight. Quite the opposite. This was a terrible mistake, and to correct it the regulatory staffs ought to be beefed up immediately. Washington should also create a new, separate, independent entity -- apart from the bank regulators and accountable directly to the White House -- to manage and liquidate the S&Ls' real-estate assets for the best value possible. Such an entity ought, moreover, to have private-sector involvement, as well as a strong presence in each region of the country, since local real-estate dealings require local expertise.

Finally, the Feds ought to be thinking not about weakening safeguards for depositors but about overhauling the way they regulate banks in the first place. Replace the potpourri of existing regulatory agencies with a sole, centralized regulator, armed with a strong enforcement staff, and responsible for total oversight over the banking system at the federal level.

There is one reform we can institute where deposit insurance is concerned, as New York Gov. Mario Cuomo has already proposed: limit insurance to $100,000 per depositor, instead of $100,000 per deposit, so the so-called "sharpshooters" can't buy extra protection with extra accounts.

Vincent Tese

Director of Economic Development

Former State Superintendent of Banks

New York State

New York

I'd like to suggest the possibility of enabling legislation to cause federal deposit insurance to compete with insurance provided to banks by the insurance and financial communities, similar to the manner in which workers' compensation insurance systems work.

Over time, this should provide incentives to the financial system to be self-policing in regard to risks. I would expect under such a system that the risks associated with banking activities eventually would be reflected in the interest costs to borrowers. This is certainly fairer than creating high tax rates for taxpayers who may or may not be participants in the credit system that created the problem. In addition, those taxpayers who are also savers would be rewarded with higher returns on their savings rather than higher taxes on their earnings.

Robert B. Tormey

Los Angeles
