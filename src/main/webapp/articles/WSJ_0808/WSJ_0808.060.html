OTC Focus: Market Rebounds 0.5% In Lower Volume Despite Losses of Exchange Listed Issues

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 08/08/90

Over-the-counter stocks rebounded slightly and hung on to their gains, despite a fall in exchange-listed shares triggered by reports that U.S. troops were moving into Saudi Arabia. The Nasdaq Composite Index rose 2.04 to 402.08, a 0.5% gain, compared with a 0.2% loss in the Dow Jones Industrial Average. Volume eased to 165.5 million shares from 174.8 million shares Monday as advancing stocks overtook decliners, 1,106 to 964.

"We're not out of the woods yet," said Eric Siefert, a senior OTC trader with Shearson Lehman Brothers, who kept a close eye on televised reports of developments in the Middle East. Calling the day's gains a "technical bounce," Mr. Siefert agreed with other traders that professional dealers and investors were making investment decisions based on unfolding developments in the Middle East and the direction of the bond market and exchange-listed shares.

"Everybody's trading from a defensive point of view and staying close to the vest," said Robert Antolini, head of OTC trading for Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette. Mr. Antolini added that OTC trading desks are keeping their inventories of shares low.

Many of the OTC market's largest stocks reclaimed their lead after Monday's plunge, but larger issues ended the day mixed. Intel added 5/8 to 37 7/8; Conner Peripherals gained 1 to 22 7/8; Sun Microsystems rebounded 1 to 28 5/8; Liz Claiborne regained 1 1/8 to 26, and Nike advanced 2 1/8 to 74 7/8. The Nasdaq 100 Index added 2.40 to 405.31, while the Nasdaq Industrial Index gained 2.45 to 429.01.

Apple Computer closed unchanged at 39 1/2, as did Oracle Systems at 14 7/8. Shortly after the market closed, Oracle introduced a new database server called Oracle Server for Apple Macintosh computers.

Amgen slipped 1 1/4 to 80 1/4. The executive secretary of a Food and Drug Administration advisory panel told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report that scheduling problems prevent the panel from meeting in November. Analysts had expected the panel to meet then to review the Thousand Oaks, Calif., biotechnology concern's G-CSF product used to stimulate white blood cell production. The executive secretary said she hopes the meeting will occur by year end.

However, airline shares turned in a mixed performance: American West Airlines gained 1/4 to 7 1/2 and Mesa Airlines edged up 1/8 to 7 1/4, but commuter airline Air Wis Services eased 1/8 to 8 5/8.

Utility shares, hammered Monday on fears of higher interest rates, regained some lost ground. The Nasdaq Utility Index climbed 2.76 to 553.10 as market leader MCI Communications added 5/8 to 34 5/8. Among natural gas utilities, Delta Natural Gas advanced 3/8 to 12 1/4; Northwest Natural Gas climbed 1/4 to 22 7/8, and Public Service Co. of North Carolina added 1/8 to 16 3/8. Magma Power, a San Diego, developer of geothermal resources, jumped 1 3/4 to 35 1/2.

Seattle Filmworks added 1 to 7 5/8 after the Seattle mail-order film processor said it plans to buy back up to 200,000 shares of its nearly 1.8 million common shares outstanding.

Central Fidelity Banks climbed 3/4 to 24 after the Richmond, Va., bank said late Monday it plans to buy back up to 750,000 shares, or about 5%, of its common stock.

Buffets rose 1/2 to 18 3/4. Late Monday, the Eden Prairie, Minn., restaurant chain reported net income for the second quarter ended July 18 rose to 24 cents a share from 17 cents a year earlier.

Gish Biomedical jumped 1 1/2 to 11 7/8. Monday, analyst Steven E. Crowley of Craig-Hallum, Minneapolis, raised his investment opinion on the Santa Ana, Calif., medical-products maker to an "accumulate" from a "hold," DJPIR said.

Gish Biomedical's chief financial officer, Jeanne M. Miller, told DJPIR she had "no problem" with Mr. Crowley's estimate that the company earned 15 cents a share in fourth quarter ended June 30, up from 12 cents a year earlier. Those earnings are scheduled to be reported between Aug. 15 and Aug. 30, she said.

Ms. Miller also said Gish was listed as one of 500 top growth stocks Aug. 7 issue of Financial World magazine. She added that "a parade of corporate finance people" have recently visited the company, trying to interest it in a transaction such as a secondary public offering. However, Ms. Miller said the company has no interest in such a transaction.

Some other medical products concerns also rebounded after Monday's thrashing: Shares of heart valve maker St. Jude Medical jumped 2 3/8 to 29 and specialty medical-device maker Stryker leaped 2 3/8 to 25.

Sigma-Aldrich plummeted 4 5/8 to 55 5/8. The St. Louis producer of biochemical and organic products reported net income for the second quarter ended June 30 of 73 cents a share compared with 66 cents a year earlier. That profit was below analysts' expectations of 75 cents a share, according to First Call, a unit of Thomson Financial Networks.

Cetus fell 1/4 to 9 1/4 after the Emeryville, Calif., biotechnology concern reported a loss for the fourth quarter ended June 30 of 53 cents a share on 30.8 million shares outstanding, versus a year-earlier loss of 53 cents on nearly 27 million shares outstanding.

W. R. Berkley slid 1/4 to 35 3/4. After the market closed Monday, the Greenwich, Conn., insurer estimated it could face a charge of $10 million, or 84 cents a share, in the third quarter ending Sept. 30, following arbitration of a reinsurance dispute. In the year-earlier third quarter, Berkley reported net income of $1.03 a share.
