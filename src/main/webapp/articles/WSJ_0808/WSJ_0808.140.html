Inside Track: Health-Care Group Leads the Pack In Insider Sales

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 08/08/90

NEW YORK -- Health-care stocks are traditionally considered good investments for a recession because no matter how bad the economy gets, people still need medical care. But recession-wary investors now tempted to stash some of their money in health-care stocks may want to note that executives, directors and other industry "insiders" have recently been selling.

In fact, Securities and Exchange Commission filings show that the health-care industry has recently been leading all other industries in stock sales by insiders, overtaking the computer industry, which had long held the dubious distinction of "most sold."

Forty-four of the 167 health-care and hospital companies tracked by Invest/Net, a North Miami, Fla., firm that keeps tabs on such matters, were involved in recent insider sales.

All together, 134 insiders were net sellers of about 5.6 million shares since April 1, according to reports filed with the SEC. While the insiders were largely taking profits after run-ups in the sector's stocks, many top executives sold significant percentages of their holdings.

"Across the board, the picture for this group" of stocks "indicates it's at the higher level of valuation," said Robert Gabele, Invest/Net's president. Even with the stock market's overall decline of the past few days, he said, "the jury's still out on whether there's a buying opportunity in these stocks yet."

Right now, the health-care sector includes "a number of stocks that were overpriced, and, in some cases, there are some problems in the companies," said John Kaweske, portfolio manager of Financial Programs Inc.'s Financial Health, a mutual fund that invests in health-care, biotechnology and pharmaceutical stocks.

With the possibility of a recession, Mr. Kaweske said he recently sold all of his health maintenance organization stocks because he predicts they won't thrive in a period of federal budget constraints. But he bought shares of Healthdyne Inc. yesterday because the company is working on moving medical procedures out of the high-cost hospital setting.

The health-care company with the largest number of recent insider sellers is U.S. Surgical Corp., a Norwalk, Conn., manufacturer of surgical staples and instruments, according to Invest/Net and SEC filings. All told, 13 U.S. Surgical insiders have sold company stock since April 1, the filings show.

Leon Hirsch, the company's chairman, said the 13 sold the stock to "enjoy a better standard of living." He said he sold 27,000 shares to expand his ranch in Wyoming, but retains stock and options representing more than two million shares.

The insiders sold a total of 246,964 shares at an average price of about $34.78 a share. The stock closed in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday at $40.25, up 62.5 cents.

"You couldn't find a more bullish group," Mr. Hirsch said, because whether there's a recession or not, the company is insulated. "Somebody doesn't have their gall bladder out because they're in or out of a recession."

At National Medical Enterprises, a Santa Monica, Calif., owner and operator of hospitals and medical-care facilities, eight insiders have sold 305,591 shares valued at about $10.7 million since April 5, SEC filings show. They sold at an average price of $34.98 a share; the stock closed on the Big Board at $34.625, up 37.5 cents.

The largest sale was by the company's president, Leonard Cohen, who sold 200,000 shares on May 15, retaining about 750,000 shares, according to the company. About a third of the shares he sold were directly related to the exercise of options six months earlier, a company spokesman said. He added that Mr. Cohen sold the stock to pay taxes.

At United Healthcare Corp., a Minnetonka, Minn., company that owns and operates 15 HMOs and other health-related businesses, Chairman Kennett Simmons, has sold all his holdings, SEC filings show. He sold 92,982 shares in May at an average price of $13.04 a share. In national over-the-counter trading, the stock closed at $17.50, up $62.50.

"I took care to sell at a time when I was particularly confident of the company's future," Mr. Simmons said. The executive, who said he has restricted stock grants and options equivalent to 500,000 shares, said he sold the stock to make investments and to build a house.

At Healthdyne, a Marietta, Ga., healthcare provider, Terry Dewberry, the company's president, sold all of his 60,450 shares in May, SEC filings show. Two other insiders sold 5,000 and 15,000 shares, respectively, according to the filings.

Mr. Dewberry declined to comment, but a company spokesman said the president still has options to purchase 210,000 shares. Healthdyne shares closed at $12, up 87.5 cents, in over-the-counter trading. The insiders sold at an average per-share price of $10.73.

Martin J. Raynes, a director of Medco Containment Services Inc., a Fairlawn, N.J., company specializing in providing prescription drug programs to corporate health-care plans, sold 97% of his holdings in May, SEC filings show. He sold 281,235 shares for $5.6 million, or an average price of about $19.80 a share, the filings show. Mr. Raynes declined to comment. Medco Containment shares closed at $23.50, up 75 cents, in over-the-counter trading.
