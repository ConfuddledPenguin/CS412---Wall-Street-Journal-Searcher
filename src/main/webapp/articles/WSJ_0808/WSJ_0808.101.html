Unions Miss the Mark In Slighting Small Firms

UNION INROADS have been greater at big companies than at smaller ones, but many small companies are sitting targets.

So say researchers Charles Brown, James Hamilton and James Medoff in their recently published book "Employers Large and Small" (Harvard University Press). Employee dissatisfaction with wages and working conditions tends to be greater at smaller companies, they say.

Moreover, their evidence shows unions help close the gap between the often skimpy fringe benefits at smaller companies and those at big employers. "Small employers today have many characteristics that should make them prime targets for organizing drives," the authors find.

They say the share of unionized companies climbs from 8% for employers with fewer than 50 workers to 66% for companies with more than 1,000 workers. Overall, 18% of the U.S. work force is unionized.

Unions tilt their organizing efforts toward large companies, even though the bigger the target, the less likely unions are to win acceptance, the study indicates. Complaints by labor officials of illegal opposition to organizing campaigns also occur more often at small companies, according to the researchers.

Why do unions tend to zero in on bigger firms? It's a matter of economics, say the authors. Organizing costs don't rise in proportion to a company's size, but the potential payoff in the form of increased member dues is much larger at big employers.

Rehearsing Executives

Before a Roadshow

GIVING LESSONS to companies going public is a growing business for a California coach.

Jerry Weissman of Power Presentations, Santa Clara, Calif., helps top executives of young businesses deliver convincing pitches as they try to spark interest in their companies' initial public offerings.

Executives know the "roadshow" isn't easy. They must convince analysts, investors and portfolio managers that a company will make lots of money. All eyes are on the speaker; sweaty palms are common.

Top executives often compound the task with inept performances. "They empty the file when they speak," boring listeners with unnecessary data, says Mr. Weissman. Also, "they speak the way the prospectus reads: `It is hoped that the company flourishes in this market. . .'" Though many media-shy executives go to public-speaking coaches, Mr. Weissman's program is more specialized.

Mr. Weissman videotapes an executive's message, then critiques the performance. "It's exactly like studying their golf stroke," he says. "They see themselves as others see them." Finally, at "dress rehearsals," Mr. Weissman peppers the client with questions. Despite a $10,000 fee, he says, there is a waiting list.

Paul Grayson, chairman of Micrografx Inc., Richardson, Texas, tells of former problems, "shifting my weight back and forth," among other things, before Mr. Weissman cured him. "He showed us how to handle the tough questions from the audience . . . and there were a lot of tough questions," he says.

Mr. Grayson says the training helped Micrografx to sell 1.3 million shares in June in its first public offering, at $16 a share. Before the roadshow, he had hoped to get a price of about $13 a share.

New England Is Paying

For Its Overconfidence

SMALL BUSINESSES in New England can blame part of their current problems on overconfidence generated by the region's economic boom of the 1980s, some economists say.

A recent rise in business failures there follows several years of prosperity for Northeastern entrepreneurs, with unemployment rates touching the lowest levels since World War II.

"Two years ago, one out of every two {New England} firms had a job opening it couldn't fill," says William C. Dunkelberg, chief economist of the National Federation of Independent Business and dean of Temple University's business school. "That kind of excitement generally breeds overexuberance."

Dun & Bradstreet Corp. found Massachusetts failures in the first four months nearly quadrupled from the year-earlier period. Failures were up 156% in Vermont, 100% in Rhode Island and 97% in New Hampshire.

Large and small companies are included in the totals. But "we know that most of the failed firms are small," says Douglas Handler, a Dun & Bradstreet economist. "Smaller companies tend to operate at the margins. Any increase in economic stress brings about a disproportionate increase in their failures."

Lynn Browne, vice president and economist of the Federal Reserve Bank of Boston, says real-estate companies are in trouble and retail space that used to house boutiques is conspicuously empty. "Two years ago, everybody was talking labor shortages," Ms. Browne says. "It really does make you doubt the worth of long-range forecasting."
