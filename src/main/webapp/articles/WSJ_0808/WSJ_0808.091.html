U.S. Postal Service Deficit To Be Less Than Expected

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/08/90

WASHINGTON -- The Postal Service said it's making progress in boosting productivity and narrowing losses, but not enough to affect its plans for a new round of rate increases. The estimated postal deficit for the fiscal year ending Sept. 30 will be less than $1 billion, compared with the $1.6 billion loss originally projected, Postmaster General Anthony Frank told his board of governors and a news conference. He credited the narrowing to productivity gains, which have been escalating over the past 12 months, reaching 3.4% in the quarter ended June 30.

The benefits of a major postal automation program and a recently imposed ceiling on employment levels are responsible for improving productivity, Mr. Frank said. The Postal Service has been installing automatic equipment that reads envelopes and sorts them according to nine-digit address codes. Hiring curbs also have helped reduce its work force by 23,000, to 764,000, since June 1989, officials said.

But Mr. Frank said the improvement isn't sufficient to alter the plan to boost rates by an average of 19%, including an increase of five cents to 30 cents for first-class service. The rise would occur early in 1991, after consideration by the Postal Rate Commission.

In defending the rate increase's size, Mr. Frank noted the uncertainty over how well the agency can do in holding down labor costs in talks with unions, which begin late this month, and the White House budget office's efforts to require the service to contribute $1.3 billion to U.S. deficit reduction.
