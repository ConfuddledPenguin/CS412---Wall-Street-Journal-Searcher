Unocal Plans Work On California Plant, Bowing to Regulators

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/24/90

LOS ANGELES -- Unocal Corp., bowing to pressure from California's smog regulators, said it will make $515 million in additions to its Wilmington, Calif., refinery, allowing it to produce less polluting "reformulated" gasolines. Unocal had steadfastly refused to join the pack of other oil companies -- including Atlantic Richfield Co., Chevron Corp. and Shell Oil Co. -- who rushed to the market with reformulated gasolines over the past year in anticipation of tougher new air pollution standards.

Company executives had argued that Unocal already introduced a reformulated gasoline when it converted its stations to all-unleaded pumps a few years back and that they didn't want to introduce a gasoline that would be only slightly cleaner. The company instead launched a $10 million public relations campaign, buying up smog-belching old cars in the Los Angeles area and scrapping them, offering free emission checks for aging autos and having a small fleet of Unocal trucks cruise the freeways to help stranded motorists.

But the need to blend cleaner gasolines became an inevitability in September when the California Air Resources Board adopted the strictest smog laws in the nation, requiring oil companies to provide alternative fuels beginning in 1994 and calling for mass production of low-polluting cars, including electric cars. Next September, the board will consider regulations to make the fuel restrictions even tougher.

"I'm sure this was a factor in what Unocal is doing now," a spokesman for the Air Resources Board said. "They were intimately involved in the development of our new rules."

The new facilities, set to be completed in late 1993, will include a 55,000 barrel-a-day fluid catalytic cracker feed hydrotreater, a hydrogen plant and related sulfur recovery units and will result in substantially reduced sulfur oxide emissions from the cracker unit, Unocal said. Refinery production will rise by about 8,000 barrels a day from the current 80,000.
