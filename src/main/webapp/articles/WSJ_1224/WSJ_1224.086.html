Marketing & Media -- Advertising: `Green' Marketing Gets Tougher

  • Author: Joann S. Lublin
  • Date: 12/24/90

New state laws policing environmental pitches will give big advertisers heartburn -- and could force some to drop their "green marketing" claims altogether. The latest law, effective next week in California, sets strict standards for terms such as "environmentally friendly," "biodegradable" and "recyclable." In April, Rhode Island starts enforcing an even tougher statute that bans most green marketing as misleading. Companies risk fines of up to $1,000 a day if they label packages "environmentally safe," for example.

Less-sweeping legislation to oversee environmental advertising has passed recently in New York, Wisconsin and New Hampshire. New Jersey and Pennsylvania are considering bills, too.

The measures aim to end consumer confusion and cynicism about green marketing, but a proliferation of conflicting statutes could exacerbate the skepticism. The laws undoubtedly will widen the clamor for the U.S. government to adopt uniform standards. Federal action isn't imminent -- despite pressure from both a task force of 10 attorneys general and a new working group of 20 big trade associations.

Ad-industry executives and major manufacturers fear a patchwork of state laws will disrupt national marketing efforts. Large corporations can't afford to package goods differently for a particular state, argues John Kamp, a vice president of the American Association of Advertising Agencies. They will "have to follow the strictest {state} law" nationwide if they want to claim a product benefits the environment.

Few companies may choose such a course. Disparate state laws mean "there won't be a whole lot of options left except to refrain more and more from making environmental claims for our products," says Donald Theissen, director of corporate product responsibility at Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing. He adds: "The consumer is the loser in the end."

This fall, 3M started selling a recycled version of its popular, removable Post-it Notes. But the new notes won't qualify as "recycled" under the California law because they are made from paper discarded during production rather than paper used by consumers. And 3M can't find a reliable producer whose recycled paper would pass muster.

At the least, 3M "will have to refrain from selling {Recycled Paper Post-it Notes} in California," Mr. Theissen says. "The pressure is toward not making any {recycled-paper} claims at all" in promoting the product throughout the U.S.

Similarly, Lever Brothers wonders about its recyclability claim for a new package design being tested in Seattle and the Twin Cities. The "Bag in the Box" package, labeled "100% recyclable," consists of plastic pouches filled with fabric softener or liquid detergent inside a corrugated carton. The Unilever unit won't be allowed to introduce the label in California unless it knows the cartons can be "conveniently recycled" in the state's 15 counties with over 300,000 residents apiece.

"We would have to have something strictly geared for the California market," says Melinda Sweet, Lever's director of environmental affairs. "That's not something we are willing to consider." Lever may refrain from rolling out the package nationwide until national green guidelines emerge. "We don't support state-by-state regulation," Ms. Sweet says. "It is an impossible task."

State officials say the laws protect consumers against what they dub "green-collar fraud": misleading and deceptive environmental advertising.

American Enviro Products reached a settlement with the task force of attorneys general in mid-October by agreeing to stop making biodegradability claims about its disposable diapers. The concern, which didn't admit any wrongdoing, had promoted its Bunnies products as "biodegradable" in its packaging and ads. The attorneys general contended the diapers couldn't degrade quickly in landfills.

Seven states sued Mobil Chemical Co. last summer charging that Mobil exploited popular misconceptions by making environmental claims for Hefty trash bags that it knew were false. And the Federal Trade Commission expects to take action soon against other manufacturers as a result of the agency's 20 investigations into possibly deceptive claims for "environmentally friendly" products.

Too many companies "thought they could just stick that {green} label on like candy," says Sarah Johnston, an environmental policy assistant for New York's attorney general.

Kip Lepper, chief of staff for California Democratic Assemblyman Byron Sher, the statute's sponsor, shares her view. "The intent of this law is not to discourage environmental advertising," he says, "but rather to bring some truth to environmental advertising -- something which has been woefully absent."

WPP Skips Dividend

WPP Group, in the latest sign of slowing revenue and weakening cash flow at the British ad firm, said it won't pay an interim dividend to shareholders.

In a letter to shareholders, WPP Chairman David Ogilvy wrote that it remains WPP's priority to conserve cash. "We consider that it would not be in the best interests of the company to pay the interim dividend for 1990 and therefore this dividend will not be paid." WPP, which owns the J. Walter Thompson and Ogilvy & Mather ad networks, had said in August it would pay 13.7 pence (25.9 cents) as an interim dividend. Shares of WPP fell 11 pence to 50 pence Friday on the London Stock Exchange.

WPP was downbeat about 1991 earnings, saying it could be "some time before the group can resume the pattern of growth . . . experienced up until a few months ago." WPP added that it is reviewing loan arrangements with its creditor banks to reach satisfactory repayment terms, should earnings be weak in 1991. The company declined to elaborate on the talks.

Ad Notes. . . .

NEW ACCOUNT: Shearson Lehman Brothers awarded its ad account to Chiat/Day/Mojo's New York office. The account is expected to bill $20 million, somewhat more than in the past year, a Shearson spokesman said. It covers ad responsibilities for the brokerage company's domestic retail business and asset management activities. Chiat already handles the Gold Card account for American Express, which owns Shearson. The win capped a good week at Chiat, which a few days earlier won a big chunk of NutraSweet's national ad business.

ACQUISITION: Tatham-Laird & Kudner, Chicago, said it acquired Peter Rogers Associates, a New York agency with about $35 million in billings. Terms weren't disclosed. Peter Rogers Associates, whose clients include Baccarat and Vidal Sassoon, will operate independently.
