JPS Textile To Restructure Its Debt Load --- Odyssey Unit to Carry Out The $460 Million Plan In Chapter 11 Proceeding

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 12/24/90

NEW YORK -- JPS Textile Group Inc. announced a $460 million debt restructuring plan, which it said it proposes to carry out through a "prepackaged" Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law filing. The prospect of a bankruptcy filing by JPS, the successor company to much of J.P. Stevens & Co., underscores the growing financial difficulties of much of the U.S. textile industry. At least eight major U.S. textile companies took on big debts in the 1980s because of takeovers, recapitalizations and leveraged buy-outs. A deepening recession in the textile industry now is making it difficult for many of these companies to service their debts.

JPS's troubles also are a setback for Odyssey Partners, the New York investment bank that acquired the Greenville, S.C., textile company in late 1988. Odyssey spent most of last summer trying to negotiate an exchange offer for JPS's bonds that didn't involve a bankruptcy-law filing, but couldn't overcome bondholders' resistance.

"We feel a reduction in the debt service of the company is desirable," said Jerry Hunter, president of JPS. He asserted that "the most efficient and least disruptive way to implement our financial restructuring is through a prepackaged Chapter 11 filing."

In a prepackaged filing, a company wins creditors' approval of a restructuring plan before filing for bankruptcy-law protection from creditors. Such filings, in theory at least, can be resolved much faster than ordinary bankruptcy cases. JPS said holders of 62.3% of its bonds have already approved its restructuring plan. It also said it hopes to emerge from Chapter 11 within 60 to 90 days of filing.

In Chapter 11, a company gets court protection against creditor lawsuits while it attempts to come up with a plan to pay its debts.

In its proposed restructuring, JPS is asking bondholders to swap high-interest, cash-paying bonds for lower-interest bonds that pay interest in a mixture of cash and more bonds. It also is offering bondholders a total of 49% ownership in the company. Meanwhile, JPS's owners, chiefly Odyssey, plan to invest another $7.5 million in the company to achieve 51% ownership.

In a typical provision of the proposed restructuring, holders of 15 1/4% notes due in 1999 are being offered 10 1/4% notes due in 1999 and 15% equity in the company.

In an interview, JPS executives insisted that the company's operations have been doing reasonably well this year, but they acknowledged that the company still couldn't handle its heavy debt load. For the nine months ended July 28, JPS posted a $26.2 million loss, as operating profit of $34.7 million was overwhelmed by $60.9 million of interest expense.

Odyssey bought JPS in 1988 for $578 million, a price that analysts say appears high in hindsight. Odyssey's original game plan appears to have been to sell off major pieces of JPS to pay down debt and turn a profit. That strategy was quickly thwarted, though, as financing sources for potential buyers have dried up over the past 18 months.

JPS also has suffered because of high interest rates on its high-risk, high-yield junk bonds, which were underwritten by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. The rates of 14 1/4% to 15 1/4% were significantly higher than Odyssey had expected, an Odyssey executive said.

Bond traders Friday afternoon declined to quote prices on JPS's junk bonds, but investors appeared to have marked down the bonds in advance of news of the proposed Chapter 11 filing. Recent quotes for JPS's junk bonds have ranged from 20 cents on the dollar to 32 cents on the dollar.

Major JPS bondholders include Drexel, First Executive Corp., Allstate Life Insurance Co. and First Capital Corp., according to Odyssey and JPS executives.
