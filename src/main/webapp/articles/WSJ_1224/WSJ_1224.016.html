Economy: Bush Threatens To Veto Planned House Rule Change

  • Author: David Wessel and Jeffrey H. Birnbaum
  • Date: 12/24/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush, supporting an arcane provision in the deficit-reduction law, said he would veto a proposed change to House rules that "would begin to undo the recently enacted budget agreement." In a letter to House Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.), Mr. Bush said he opposed the rules change, which would require that any bill specify that cost estimates by the Congressional Budget Office or Congress's Joint Tax Committee be used to determine if a bill complies with the new budget law. The proposed change has been endorsed by House Democrats and is due for ratification by the full House next month.

Although arcane, the issue is important because the law blocks any spending increase or tax cut that isn't offset by a spending cut or tax increase elsewhere. Unlike the proposed rule, the new budget law gives primacy to White House budget office estimates, a provision the Bush administration fought to include.

White House aides and House Republicans see the pending rule as a way for the Democratic-controlled Congress to circumvent the strict spending limits imposed by the budget package. The Democrats see the GOP position as a partisan power grab, which could undercut their ability to legislate effectively.

However, Mr. Bush last week seemed to suggest that the provision lacked teeth. Explaining why the administration might decide against proposing a cut in capital-gains tax rates next year, he cited congressional cost estimates that such a tax cut would lose money -- and therefore require a compensating tax increase. Administration estimates differ.

In his letter, released by the White House last Friday, the president asked Mr. Foley: "If specifically negotiated and agreed provisions are to be undone before the ink is dry, how can we expect the agreement to be taken seriously?"

House Minority Leader Bob Michel (R., Ill.) plans to challenge the rule change on the House floor, aides said. But such challenges rarely prevail in the Democratic-dominated House. Mr. Michel also plans an attempt to force a vote on a balanced-budget amendment to the Constitution.
