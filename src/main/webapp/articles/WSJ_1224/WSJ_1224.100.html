Tucson Electric Hit by Court Decision That May Mean Charges of $350 Million

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 12/24/90

Tucson Electric Power Co. appeared to move closer to a bankruptcy-protection filing after an adverse state court decision raised the possibility that the utility may be forced to take $350 million in immediate one-time charges. The state court in Phoenix upheld two regulatory orders issued last year that had been challenged by Tucson Electric. The first involved a purchased power contract with a former Tucson Electric subsidiary. The second involved Tucson Electric's gain on the 1982 sale of an option to purchase an interest in a New Mexico power plant.

The Tucson, Ariz., utility prevailed in a third dispute with regulators related to $20 million of fuel expenses for a four-month period last year.

The Arizona Corporations Commission, which regulates utilities, had disallowed certain Tucson Electric charges for power purchased from its former Alamito unit, which was spun off to shareholders and later taken private as Century Power Corp. Tucson Electric has said that the difference between its estimate of costs and the limits set by regulators is $250 million over the life of the 28-year contract, which expires in 2014.

The commission also disputed Tucson Electric's treatment of a gain on the sale of an option to buy a 28.8% interest in Unit 4 of the San Juan Generating Station near Farmington, N.M. The commission argued that money from the sale to the Modesto, Santa Clara and Redding Public Power Agency should have benefited ratepayers rather than being passed on to shareholders as a one-time gain. Tucson Electric has estimated its pretax liability in the event of an unfavorable outcome in this dispute to be $100 million as of Nov. 1, 1990.

In a statement, Tucson Electric said it hasn't decided whether it will appeal the decisions. The utility also said it is "reviewing whether recognition of losses or write-offs related to the Century Power purchase contract and the {option-gain} adjustment is required at this time."

Regarding the 28-year Century Power contract, a Tucson Electric spokesman said: "We don't know if accounting treatment would require immediate" write-downs for the full $250 million rather than amortization over the life of the contract.

In two recent filings with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Tucson Electric has cautioned that it may file for Chapter 11 protection from creditors if it is unable to maintain liquidity. In October, a spokesman said such an action wasn't imminent. The spokesman declined to speculate on what the company will do now.

In a bid to improve liquidity, Tucson Electric omitted indefinitely all of its preferred-stock dividends earlier this month. The company said the omission was one of a number of steps designed to conserve cash. The company omitted its quarterly dividend on common stock in January.

Tucson Electric has been plagued by excess generating capacity throughout the Southwest and by fallout from the company's disastrous diversification into the thrift and real estate businesses. In the year ended June 30, the utility had a net loss of $210.9 million, or $8.64 a share, including write-downs from disposal of discontinued operations, on revenue of $480.9 million.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading Friday, Tucson Electric shares closed at $7.375, down 25 cents.
