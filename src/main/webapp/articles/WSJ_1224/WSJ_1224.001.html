It's a Bit Hard to Tote That Barge When You're on the Wrong River

  • Author: Carlee R. Scott
  • Date: 12/24/90

ST. LOUIS -- Does Old Man River really just go rollin' along here, or has he been shoved aside by a pushy interloper? The answer depends on whether a river is its water or its name.

Here's the problem: St. Louis is on the Mississippi River; textbooks, road maps and Mark Twain all say so. But can you, from here, dangle your feet (if you are hardy enough to want to at this time of year) in the muddy Mississippi? There are some who say no, because the water that flows past St. Louis is traveling under an assumed name.

Water specialists and river engineers agree that the water alongside the city limits isn't water from the Mississippi after all; it's from the Missouri River.

The U.S. Geological Survey and the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers maintain that although the Mississippi and Missouri rivers merge about 16 miles north of St. Louis, the waters probably don't completely mix until at least 30 to 40 miles south of the city. The wide stream of the silt-laden Missouri River forces the Mississippi water toward the Illinois shore and East St. Louis, with the two separate currents traveling along side by side.

Adding insult to injury, "there are people who say that the entire river {from the confluence on south} should be called the Missouri, because it constitutes the majority of the flow," says David A. Visintainer, an engineer executive for the city's water division.

But that's a "ridiculous" contention, asserts Lloyd Waite, a hydrologist for the Geological Survey's district office here. "They can say the currents haven't mixed totally, but it's still the Mississippi River."

Claude Strauser, chief of the U.S. Army's potamology (study of rivers) section, calls the occurrence "one of those mind-grabbers that's very interesting and fun" and will continue to generate lighthearted debate for years.

A spokeswoman for the Geological Survey says the situation isn't unique and exists in many other streams where one is the tributary to another. Still, this is the mighty Mississippi that's in question. Would the familiar song be as popular if the lyrics were "Down the Missouri to New Orleans?"

As far as this city's tourism promoters are concerned, though, Old Man River can answer to any name he likes. A name change "wouldn't make any difference," declares a tourist board spokeswoman.
