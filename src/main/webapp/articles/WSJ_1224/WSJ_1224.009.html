New England Bank To Restate Results For '89 3rd Period

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 12/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Bank of New England Corp. agreed to restate its third-quarter 1989 earnings as part of its settlement of fraud charges with the Securities and Exchange Commission. As previously reported, the Boston-based bank, without admitting or denying wrongdoing, agreed to settle charges that it filed false quarterly reports and registration statements with the SEC, hiding the deterioration of its loan portfolio.

The SEC voted two weeks ago to accept the settlement, but didn't file the case in Boston federal court until Friday.

In its settlement with the agency, Bank of New England agreed to restate its third-quarter 1989 earnings to reflect the loan problems more accurately. The banking company also agreed to have independent accountants conduct a review of internal accounting controls, and it agreed not to violate fraud provisions in the future.

A Bank of New England spokesman said, "We're pleased that we were able to arrive at a satisfactory settlement with the SEC and can put this matter behind us. The settlement has no effect on our current business operations and will in no way interfere with our ongoing affairs."

Separately, the SEC was granted a temporary restraining order after filing charges against Latin Investment Corp. and several principals of the Washington firm. The order largely freezes the assets of the three principals, Fernando Leonzo, Leonel Salinas and Jose Cortes.

The agency charged that since 1982, the company illegally sold securities in itself to about 3,500 people, mostly immigrants from El Salvador and other Latin American countries. The firm took money into what it described as passbook savings accounts, but since it wasn't a bank the money actually was invested in securities of Latin Investment. Moreover, the SEC charged that the firm defrauded investors by telling them Latin Investment was a bank.

The agency charged that the three men used much of the roughly $6.8 million for their own purposes. Earlier this month, the company was forced into Chapter 7 involuntary bankruptcy proceedings.

The principals couldn't be reached for comment.
