Economy: Fed's Key Panel Was Unanimous On Cut in Rates

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 12/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal Reserve policy makers were unanimous in deciding to lower interest rates in mid-November, amid mounting evidence of recession and indications that the inflation rate was stabilizing, the Fed disclosed. Despite some differences, all 11 voting members of the Fed's open market committee agreed that the softening economy called for cutting rates immediately and for tilting toward lower rates in the following weeks, according to a summary of the Nov. 13 meeting.

The Fed pushed down the federal funds rate, the rates that banks charge on loans to each other, by 1/4 percentage point after the meeting and by another 1/4 percentage point in early December.

Following a meeting of the committee last week, the Fed pushed down the fed funds rate by another 1/4 percentage point, bringing it to about 7%. Also last week, the Fed cut the discount rate, the rate it charges on loans to banks, by 1/2 percentage point to 6.5%. The Fed will release a summary of last week's deliberations in February.

In mid-November, Fed economists were projecting "a mild downturn," but they anticipated that economic growth would resume during the first six months of 1991. Not all the Fed policy makers were so optimistic, though. "Many of the {committee} members noted that, while the most likely outcome was a relatively mild and brief downturn, there were risks of a more severe or prolonged contraction," the summary said.

One continuing concern was the credit crunch or, as the Fed put it, "the increased reluctance of institutional lenders to accommodate less-than-prime business borrowers." Also figuring in the decision was weaker-than-anticipated growth in the money supply.

The summary said that a "few" of the committee members were urging the Fed to move gingerly. Though they were willing to ease credit conditions slightly, they were reluctant to give Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan leeway to make a second move toward lower rates. Still, those who had objected earlier in the year to easing credit conditions, including Fed governor Wayne Angell and Cleveland Fed president Lee Hoskins, didn't dissent.

The voting members of the Fed's open-market committee are the six current Fed governors and five of the 12 district Fed bank presidents. The other seven presidents participate in the discussions.
