Foreign Exchange: Dollar May Climb Higher in Near Term As Investors Seek a Haven, Traders Say

  • Author: Candace Cumberbatch Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 12/24/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar is gathering steam and could rise further, at least in the near term, traders say. Now that the Soviet Union has joined the Persian Gulf as a center of political turmoil, it's hard to say how high the dollar could rise on flight-to-quality buying, currency specialists say. Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze resigned last week, warning that a dictatorship was taking shape in his nation.

"There really is no downside. I only see upside," says John Hickey, treasurer at Kansallis Osake Pankki, a Finnish bank.

Late Friday here, the dollar was quoted at 1.5289 marks, up from 1.5115 marks late Thursday in New York. Against the Japanese currency, the dollar closed at 135.55 yen, off from 135.85 yen late Thursday. Tokyo markets were closed Monday for a holiday.

Friday's intraday dollar peaks of 1.5335 marks and 136.35 yen were the highest levels since early October. For the week, the dollar was up 4.6 pfennigs and 2.60 yen. The dollar gained more against European currencies than it did against the yen, because investors believe Europe is more exposed to troubles in the Soviet Union than Japan is.

The greenback's fortunes have changed sharply.

Only a few months ago, it was spiraling downward because of the Federal Reserve's easier monetary policy and worries about the woes of the U.S. economy and banking industry.

While those bearish fundamentals are still in place, they are being outweighed by the political crisis in the Soviet Union and rising tension in the Gulf. These developments have re-established the dollar's role as a "haven" for investors.

Mr. Shevardnadze's resignation put into question the future of the struggling Soviet government and economy. It has also cooled much of the cheer about German unification and the democracy movement that swept Eastern Europe this year.

As for the Gulf, odds for a war seem to be growing because of Iraq's refusal to leave Kuwait.

In such a scenario, the dollar may very well be on its way to 1.56 marks and 140 yen or higher, says Mr. Hickey.

David Horner, senior financial strategist at Merrill Lynch & Co., says dollar levels of 1.5750 marks and 137.50 yen are within reach. The year-end comeback is "a meaningful bounce," says Mr. Horner, adding that "it may take us months to get back down" to lower levels.

However, because of the bearish fundamentals, there's some skepticism about the dollar's potential for further gains. For example, the U.S. still has stubbornly large budget and trade deficits. Friday's news of a $48.05 billion November budget gap, larger than expected, prompted predictions of a $300 billion deficit for the fiscal year ending Sept. 30.

"I would be on the skeptical side. I honestly believe {the dollar} will fall back," says Bruno Zbinden, manager of foreign-exchange and precious metals dealings at Swiss Bank Corp. It's possible that the dollar will test 1.45 marks and 130 yen in the first quarter of 1991 if traders "go back to fundamentals" after focusing on politics, he says.

One negative factor may be further credit relaxation by the Fed, Mr. Zbinden says. He forecasts that the federal funds rate will slip to 6.5% in the first quarter from the current 7% norm. The federal funds rate is the charge on reserves traded among commercial banks for overnight use.

Lower U.S. rates are bearish for the dollar because they lessen the attractiveness of U.S. debt instruments, particularly at a time when rates in Germany, Japan and other nations are steady to higher.

Merrill Lynch's Mr. Horner agrees that the dollar could run into trouble quickly if its flight-to-quality support crumbles and traders zero in on the troubled U.S. economy again. "We could fall awfully far awfully fast," he says.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery settled at $382.50 an ounce, down $2.70. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.5 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Monday, gold was quoted at $382.45 an ounce.
