International -- Washington Insight: Japan and Germany Shirk Gulf Duties With Nary a Peep From the White House

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 12/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Japan and Germany are barely lifting a finger to help the U.S. in the Persian Gulf crisis, and the Bush administration appears to be letting them get away with it. As a result, economic resentment against the two powerful nations likely will merge with anti-war sentiment in a formidable challenge to President Bush's Gulf policy. Even though Congress is in recess, lawmakers already are lining up to complain. And the House and Senate budget committees are planning to tackle the question of the allies' contribution in high-profile hearings next month.

The issue is already beginning to reflect badly on two of President Bush's closest confidants: Secretary of State James Baker, who negotiated the German support, and Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady, who negotiated the Japanese contribution. Neither man has publicly chided those nations for their performance. And behind the scenes, aides to each man accuse the other of making mistakes.

So far, criticism has focused on the meager pledges of aid Mr. Baker and Mr. Brady secured from the two economic giants. As neither Germany nor Japan is committing troops to the Gulf, the only way to measure their contribution is in financial terms. Germany is dipping into its $1.4 trillion-a-year economy for just $1 billion for the U.S. and about $2 billion for other nations. And Japan's pledges of $2 billion for the U.S. and $2 billion for poor nations are infinitesimal given its $2.8 trillion gross national product.

So far, the U.S. has spent about $6.5 billion; and estimates of the total cost of Operation Desert Shield top $30 billion.

But the story is even worse than it seems. Soon, attention will focus on lesser-known details of the allied aid, which show that, in certain respects, it's even paltrier than it seemed at first glance.

For instance, it turns out that much of the supposedly new military equipment being sent by Germany to U.S. forces is actually cast-off gear from the defunct East German army. "It's perfectly good equipment," says German embassy spokesman Hans-Henning Horstmann, "and the Pentagon has approved it."

The $2 billion pledged by Japan to aid poor Moslem nations -- Jordan, Turkey and Egypt -- is nearly all in the form of loans, not grants as had been expected. "As a matter of principle, we generally don't believe in giveaways in the matter of economic assistance to developing countries," explains Kenzo Ohshima, political counselor at Japan's embassy.

Furthermore, what aid the two are providing is flowing like molasses.

So far, Japan has delivered to the U.S. just $585 million in cash and gear, such as fax machines and four-wheel-drive vehicles. Tokyo's "emergency loans" to frontline states have totaled just half of the $600 million promised by now, U.S. officials say. Germany has provided less than half of the $700 million in military gear it promised in September. Of the 60 highly publicized poison-gas-detecting armored vehicles it pledged, only 30 have been sent.

"This will do nothing but sour allied relations," asserts Rep. Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.). "Things are already strained over trade disputes, but this is even worse. . . . They need the Gulf oil more than we do, and they're not sending troops."

The Japanese and German embassies here are scrambling to put out the building political fire. Japanese Ambassador Ryohei Murata has been working the phones to members of Congress, and his deputy called a news conference the other day to try to put a gloss on Tokyo's performance. The German embassy has sent an upbeat "fact sheet" to members of Congress.

The Bush administration seems oblivious to the domestic political reaction. It hasn't asked either nation yet for more money for 1991, despite the huge increase in U.S. forces going to the Gulf and the looming prospect of war.

Privately, many mid-level officials are angry at the allies, especially the Japanese. But the only senior official willing to criticize Tokyo on the record is Treasury Undersecretary David Mulford, who heads an international committee funneling economic aid to hard-hit poorer nations.

"We have been disappointed that the Japanese have not disbursed funds as quickly as promised," he said in an interview.

By contrast, Richard Boucher, a spokesman for Secretary of State Baker, said, "We are satisfied with the response that we've gotten from all our allies." He added: "We are, of course, engaged with them in continuing discussions about the effort, and the secretary has made clear, very clear, that we would be looking for further support."

Takayuki Kimura, Japan's No. 2 diplomat here, says Japan is giving "the figure worked out with Mr. Brady. We have not been asked for more." A German official says, "We could give $10 billion and it wouldn't make any difference. It would still be symbolic. The only thing which would count in American eyes is German troops in the Gulf."
