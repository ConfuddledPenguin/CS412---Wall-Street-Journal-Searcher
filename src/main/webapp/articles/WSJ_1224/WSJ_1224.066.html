Marketing & Media: End of Strikes at Mills May Wipe Out Jump In Newsprint Prices

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/24/90

MONTREAL -- The end of long strikes last week at several newsprint mills in eastern Canada could wipe out a scheduled 5% newsprint price increase in the U.S. and Canada, analysts and industry executives said. Downward pressure on prices would cheer struggling newspaper publishers and hurt the North American newsprint industry, which is trying to climb out of a cyclical slump.

The price increase will probably take effect Jan. 1 as planned, analysts and industry executives said. But after the reopened Canadian mills return to full operations in January, producers will likely have to offer bigger discounts from the list price or formally roll back the price increase, they said.

"The world is going to be afloat in newsprint," said Homer Taylor, vice president, supply at Knight-Ridder Inc. "I can't imagine how the price increase will go through."

Led by Stone Container Corp., most major North American newsprint producers announced this fall that they would raise the benchmark eastern U.S. list price to $720 a metric ton from $685 Jan 1. A metric ton equals about 2,204.6 pounds.

Newsprint isn't sold at list price. Standard discounts from list range from 16% to as much as 22%, analysts and industry executives said. At an 18% discount, a buyer currently paying about $562 a metric ton would pay about $590 a metric ton if the Jan. 1 increase holds.

Newsprint producers raised list prices by about 5% June 1, their first increase in more than two years. They made that price increase stick largely due to the threat of strikes at mills in eastern Canada, many analysts and newsprint buyers said.
