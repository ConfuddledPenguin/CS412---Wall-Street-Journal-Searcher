Philadelphia Banks Push to Form Panel To Oversee Budget

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/24/90

PHILADELPHIA -- Banks are pressing for the creation of a fiscal-oversight board that would have veto power over Philadelphia's budget under certain circumstances as part of a $150 million loan proposal to help the city avoid bankruptcy. The demands of eight Philadelphia banks were outlined last week in a confidential 10-page document that bore the logo of CoreStates Financial Corp., the lead negotiator for the eight-bank consortium.

In addition to the oversight board, the banks also urged spending cuts, tax increases, sale of city assets and privatization of some services to end the city's chronic deficits. The administration of Mayor W. Wilson Goode is facing a deficit of $229.5 million this fiscal year.

Under the loan proposal, the banks would buy $100 million in notes as early as next week and be repaid by March 15. The other $50 million in notes would be sold by Jan. 31 "upon the satisfactory occurence of certain events," the proposal said.

Among those events would be approval by city council of the oversight board. Similar legislation would have to be introduced by Jan. 31 in the state House and Senate.

The source familiar with the document said that, while the banks "never intended to dictate" the specifics of what the oversight board would do, such an entity would be necessary to "restore the confidence of the city's Wall Street lenders and state legislators."

Seperately, Fidelity Investments of Boston, the nation's largest mutual fund company, joined negotiations on an emergency loan package for that city, which is running low on cash and could begin missing payrolls on Jan. 4. But a Fidelity spokesman said that "no investment decisions have been made."
