President Extends Rail Board Deadline, Postponing Possible Nationwide Strike

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 12/24/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush took action that postponed the threat of a possible nationwide railroad strike until mid-February. The president again extended the reporting deadline for a three-man emergency board, from yesterday until Jan. 15. In May, Mr. Bush named the board to try to resolve health-care costs, crew size and other contract issues, and later extended its reporting date to yesterday.

Under the Railway Labor Act, the newest extension put off for another 30 days, beyond the Jan. 15 deadline, any strike by the unions and any move by railroads to adopt their own labor-contract terms unilaterally.

The White House has said that a rail strike, besides idling railroad workers and stranding riders, could cause more than 500,000 layoffs in the economy within two weeks. This winter, such changes would occur when the economy is already likely sinking into a recession.

The emergency board finished its deliberations about a week ago and began work on a report. Its recommendations won't be binding, but Congress has typically acted to avert long rail strikes in the past by enacting board recommendations into law. That last happened in 1982, when Congress ended a four-day strike by the Brotherhood of Locomotive Engineers.

This time, large railroads and 11 unions are far apart on a range of issues that they have been negotiating for more than two years, and chances of a strike are considered high. That could be complicated if the U.S. becomes involved in a war in the Middle East, rail unions admit.

One of the biggest issues is health costs. Railroads want workers to start paying some of the premiums. And for the first time in 20 years, railroads are seeking to boost the deductible amounts that employees must pay on claims.

The railroads also want to trim operating crew sizes to a maximum of two from a current three to five, or else reduce wages of each crew member by 25% or more. Further, the railroads seek work-rule changes such as boosting the mileage that crews travel for a day's pay to 160 miles from a current 108 miles.

The unions oppose all these industry demands and insist they can continue to resist them. The railroads aren't "going to impose something we don't want, because we can lick 'em in Congress," says Richard Kilroy, president of the Transportation Communication Union, which represents railway clerks.

For their part, the unions seek wage increases of 30% over three years, boosts in cost-of-living pay and job protection in sales of short lines, among other demands.

Railroads say they need more cost cutting so they can compete with trucks, and to improve their return on investment. Unions counter that they've given enough concessions in the past, and now need improvements.
