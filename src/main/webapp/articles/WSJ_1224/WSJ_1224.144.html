Sharing the Wealth: Gulf Crisis Inflames Tension Between Rich And Poor in Mideast --- Saddam Hussein's Promise To Distribute Oil Wealth Appeals to Many Arabs --- Living a Harsh Life in Egypt

  • Author: Barbara Rosewicz
  • Date: 12/24/90

CAIRO -- The contrast isn't lost on the people of this city: 25,000 mostly dirt-poor Egyptian troops dispatched to the Saudi Arabian desert to protect a wealthy way of life unknown back home. In today's Gulf crisis, Arab "have-nots" are defending the "haves." Shabaan Abdel Kader, an unemployed father of four in Cairo, reflects on the gap between rich and poor and shakes his head. The oil nations "used their money to raise the quality of living of their own people and to invest in Europe and the U.S. -- and gave crumbs to the Egyptians," he says bitterly.

Mr. Kader's is just one in a chorus of Arab voices frustrated by the disparity. Saddam Hussein's populist rhetoric about forcing the Gulf states to share their wealth may be self-serving, but it hits a raw nerve in many Arab countries, particularly this one. In business and newspaper offices, government ministries and cafes, people here are sniping at the "insensitive super rich of the Arab world" and the "rotten monarchies" for not investing more of their oil windfalls in Arab countries. In the minds of many Arab and Western leaders, regional stability in the post-crisis era will partly depend on resolving these tensions.

The contrast in life styles in the Mideast is indeed stark, especially when the comparison is with Egypt. In Cairo's streets, a curly-haired urchin carries a naked infant and begs for food from tourists. A river of sewer water clogs a downtown street for days. A retiree explodes at a butcher over the price of meat. In the oil-rich realm of the Gulf, such scenes are virtually absent among the tidy villas, the superhighways and the brimming store shelves. In the wealthy United Arab Emirates, per capita income is $15,720 a year and in Qatar it is $11,610; in Egypt, the figure is just $650.

Sipping coffee at a restaurant overlooking the Nile River, a retired Egyptian diplomat named Tahseen Basheer sees a need for redistributing some of the Arab World's wealth. He believes the oil states should allot 10% of their annual income to an Arab development and defense fund. "The money the rich Arabs were dumping in buying houses in Beverly Hills and Cannes and indulging in massive, atrocious superconsumerism only alienated the people and led to continued destabilization," Mr. Basheer says.

Ideas like his are also getting plenty of currency outside Egypt, notably in Jordan, where King Hussein has called on the Gulf states to share the wealth. The U.S. State Department, too, is said to be considering ways to persuade the Gulf Arabs to better distribute their oil earnings after the crisis, in hopes that doing so will foster development and stability in the region. Stability is on everyone's mind. "If there is no real solution to the problems of the economy of this country, nobody can guarantee the future of the political regime," warns Phillip Gallab, editor of a leftist newspaper in Egypt.

Egyptians imagine that Saddam Hussein is gloating in Baghdad over the new economic frustration he is causing here. Hundreds of thousands of Egyptian workers have fled home to Egypt from jobs in Iraq and Kuwait, and many aren't finding work.

"The children are now asking me questions, like `why can't we have that kind of food?'" says Saeed Rifaah, a 40-year-old father of five who left Kuwait and is out of luck in Cairo. He can't get a job, much less a bank loan to restart his construction business or open a shop. He has switched his children from private schools to inferior, government-run ones and is borrowing money from relatives to make ends meet. "It's very embarrassing for a father," Mr. Rifaah says.

It doesn't take long for these economic frustrations to pick up political overtones. Abdel Basit Mohamed, 52, supported three families back home in Egypt with his construction job in Kuwait. When bread got hard to find following Iraq's invasion, he went back to Egypt, carrying only a few clothes and about $150. Now he looks for work, along with three million unemployed people in the country, and wonders how he will make the next payment on his new flat, which he proudly notes even has a kitchen.

"It's the government's responsibility to create these jobs," fumes Mr. Mohamed, clutching a grocery sack to his long robe. "There's such a shortage of housing here that young people can't find houses to get married. The government should use all of this desert land to build houses, and this would create jobs. I'm a taxpayer and I should be paid back."

His solution isn't far from what academics and business officials propose in agitating for a bigger share of the Gulf's oil income. What they envision is long-term investment and development aid and an economic integration of Arab nations similar to the European Community.

"It's not a matter of cash being transferred from Riyadh to Cairo -- that doesn't mean development," says Ali Dessouki, a political scientist at Cairo University. "We need serious industrialization, education, joint ventures with public and private-sector companies." And that holds true not just for Egypt, he says, but for those cash-strapped Arab countries now sympathizing with Iraq.

To be fair, the Gulf Arabs -- with the notable exception of Iraq -- have donated considerable amounts of money since they struck oil. According to the World Bank, Saudi Arabia and Kuwait lead the world in the percentage of gross national product dedicated to aid to poorer nations (not all of them in the Arab world).

In addition, the Saudis in particular are famous for handing out occasional huge sums under the table to buy favors or protection in the Mideast. Just before Saddam Hussein invaded, the emir of Kuwait helped finance the purchase of boatloads of wheat for Egypt. Saudi officials said early this month they are giving President Hosni Mubarak $1.5 billion in financial assistance to help pay Egypt's debts and finance development projects.

But as Butrous Ghali, Egypt's minister of state for foreign affairs, notes, Arab aid to Egypt traditionally has been "nothing to compare with what we receive from non-Arab countries" such as the U.S. and Japan. (In fiscal 1990 alone, the U.S. gave Egypt $2.2 billion in economic and military grants and $360 million in loans and credit guarantees.)

Most Egyptians have learned about the extent of the wealth in the Gulf from friends or relatives who have worked there. Sherif al Mazen was one of those workers. Serving guava juice and chocolates in his Cairo parlor, he reminisces about the life he led as a computer science professor at Kuwait University: no traffic jams, no housing shortage, no air pollution and little difference between what the poor and rich ate.

He also recalls -- with some fondness -- the rife consumerism of many Kuwaitis. There were the elaborate villas for extended families -- "you can actually call them palaces" -- and the appetite for Mercedes-Benzes and BMWs. "If you went to the Kuwait University campus, you'd find the most elegant and prestigious cars were those of the students, not the professors," he says. It was a far cry from life in Cairo.

Without some help, life in his native country is likely to simply get worse. Egypt's population of 54 million is ballooning about 3% a year. The country still owes $36 billion in foreign debt and hasn't been able to make all of its debt repayments. Inflation is sprinting along at more than 20% a year. And with foreign exchange in short supply, the country sometimes has been unable to buy enough raw materials for its factories. Before receiving recent aid, Egypt was even scrambling to find credits to import wheat for bread, the mainstay of the diet here.

The country's relatively modest oil industry is benefiting from the Gulf crisis, of course. But even if the price of crude oil surged to, say, $40 a barrel, and stayed there for a year, the windfall would offset only $1.8 billion of the $4.5 billion the government estimates it is losing annually because of the crisis. The costs to Egypt of the rising tensions have been immediate: Tourism dropped, toll-paying traffic through the Suez Canal fell and the amount of money sent home by Egyptians working abroad plummeted.

In the flood of workers back from the Gulf, the hardest to accommodate will be the professionals. There simply aren't enough well-paying jobs. Mostafa al-Ridi, an Egyptian lawyer who fled Kuwait, says he has been offered a position as a sales manager in Cairo, making just 4% of what he earned as a financial account manager in Kuwait. For now, he prefers to wait in hopes of getting back his Kuwaiti job.

The thousands of returnees like him will further tax this country's already strained housing, school and medical programs. So it is that more Egyptians are calling for a longer-term solution to their country's chronic financial problems, and a bigger role in that solution for the oil-rich Gulf states.

But will the wealthy Arab nations go along? Thanks largely to the current crisis, exiled Kuwaiti officials have lately sounded more egalitarian than ever. "I assure you that our intention is to have more economic cooperation with Egypt," says Kuwait's ambassador in Cairo, Abdel Razak al Kandari. "A new Arab system should be established from all angles: political, economic and security." Perhaps as a sign of good faith, Kuwait's exiled government, along with Saudi Arabia, Qatar and the United Arab Emirates, now have written off more than $7 billion in Egyptian debt that they had stubbornly refused to forgive for years.

Makram Mohammed Ahmed, editor of Egypt's al-Musower magazine and a confidant of President Mubarak, says there ultimately is no other path for the rich Gulf states but to share more of their wealth. "What kind of choice do they have for their security?" he asks. "They have a big fortune but few people. . . . They must change or they will be changed."
