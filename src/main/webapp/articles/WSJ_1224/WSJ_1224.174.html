REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Berkeley Remnant

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 12/24/90

One of the late Prof. Paul Seabury's last great deeds was to commission a small plane to fly over this year's Earth Day parade in his hometown of Berkeley with a banner telling the Mayor to forget radical politics and fix the potholes. Voters gave Mayor Loni Hancock much the same message this past Election Day. Her radical-left allies on the City Council and rent-control board were routed. But she has apparently survived the bid by a moderate Democrat to oust her, by about the narrowest margin conceivable.

A trial-court judge blessed her 77-vote victory, even though 386 absentee ballots remain uncounted. More than 35,000 votes were cast, with her challenger polling 60% among the absentee ballots that were counted. But the uncounted ballots arrived after Election Day, apparently because of a delay processing applications, and Judge Howard Schwartz said rules are rules.

The rule in Berkeley city government for nearly two decades has been relentless radicalism. The University of California's mother campus has mellowed back into a bastion of studiousness, where the only major political strife involves admissions policies that seem to keep out qualified Asian-Americans. But the town astride Telegraph Avenue struck the usual sistercity arrangements with revolutionary communists, and sponsored various festivals and exhibits of zoology.

What's really hurt the community has been draconian rent control, enforced to protect a favored class of barnacle renters familiar to college towns. The law reduced liquidity in the housing market, hurt students, and exacted a punitive tax on small landlords, many of them black.

Berkeley is on its way to overcoming the entrenched left, though the symbolic Mayor may remain, holed up like the czar in his winter palace. Our departed friend Paul Seabury would be amused.
