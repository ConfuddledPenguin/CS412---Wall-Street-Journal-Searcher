Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Economists, Not Waiting for New Data, Brace for Plunge in 4th-Quarter Activity

  • Author: Kathryn Graven
  • Date: 12/24/90

NEW YORK -- The economy is ending this year and beginning the new year on a decidedly downward note. Even before all the data for December are collected and tallied, economists are bracing for a sharp drop in fourth-quarter economic activity. That is because a spate of negative numbers in October and November has convincingly demonstrated the economy's weaknesses.

Moreover, though generally pleased with the Federal Reserve's moves to lower key interest rates last week, economists warn that the beneficial effects of lower rates won't show up in the economic statistics for months.

Bruce Steinberg, an economist with Merrill Lynch & Co. in New York likens the economic situation to a balloon that has been deflated. "The air is still being let out." It will be midyear, at least, "before the lower rates kick in," he says.

Others argue that the benefits of the initial easing of monetary policy will be muted during this recession. The reason: Banks and other lending institutions, with plenty of their own headaches, are choosing their customers more carefully. Alan Levenson, a director of financial services for the WEFA Group, an economic forecasting and consulting firm, puts it in a more cynical context. "The banking sector is like a roach motel. The reserves come in but the money won't go out."

Economists don't expect this week's indicators to add much, if any, brightness to the already dreary economic picture. Wrapping up the November data are durable goods orders, scheduled for release Thursday, followed by the leading indicators index on Friday.

The WEFA Group's Mr. Levenson says durable goods orders are so volatile he doesn't even bother to try to predict them.

Indeed there tends to be a wide range of predictions floating around. Gary Ciminero, an economist at Fleet/Norstar Financial Group in Rhode Island, sees durable goods orders dropping as much as 6.2%. In contrast, the consensus, as surveyed by MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York, sees durable goods orders falling about 3%. In October, durable goods rose a surprising 3.6%, mostly on the strength of aircraft orders.

There is less disagreement over what the leading indicators index is likely to do. The consensus view holds the index will decline by 0.5%. If so, that would mark the fifth consecutive monthly decline. In October, the index fell 1.2% to 141.6.

A report released last Friday by the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia suggests that the economic weakness is spilling over into December. The survey revealed that 54% of the companies polled said business was slowing in December from November, while only 15% said it was improving. Moreover, 40% of the concerns surveyed said they are cutting back on working hours and trimming payrolls, moves that will show up as negatives in future data.

In another measure of the economic outlook, a survey of U.S. corporate controllers shows that many believe the recession will be short-lived. According to the survey by the National Association of Accountants, 87% of U.S. corporate controllers predict a recession in 1991, but only 17% say it will continue into 1992. Asked what factors would have the greatest impact on the economy over the next six months, the controllers rated the Iraq crisis first, followed by interest rates and the budget deficit.
