Economy: May's Leading Indicators Jumped 0.8% But Economists Call Outlook Precarious

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The government's chief economic forecasting index flip-flopped again, rising solidly last month after declining slightly in April, the Commerce Department reported. In its second about-face in four months, the index of leading indicators, which is intended to foreshadow the economy's health six to nine months in advance, jumped 0.8% in May, more than recuperating from a 0.1% decline in April.

Despite the strong increase, many economists believe the wobbly performance of the leading indicators this year means the economy's future remains precarious. The fluctuations in the index over the past year leave it only two percentage points above its level a year ago.

"That tells me that the economy is moving sideways," said Richard D.C. Trainer, senior economist at Bank of Tokyo New York Group. "It's not rebounding, it's not about to topple into a recession. But it's moving at a very slow pace, a snail's pace."

The economy has weakened over the past year, largely the result of a Federal Reserve campaign to fight inflation by keeping interest rates high. Manufacturing and housing have been particularly bruised because purchases in those sectors often involve borrowing.

Still, many economists believe the economy doesn't face an imminent recession. The latest rise in the leading indicators gave further evidence to support that conclusion. But the index flashed some worrisome signals, too.

Building permits, one of the 11 economic indicators making up the index, fell for the fourth consecutive month, hitting a disturbingly low level. And an index of consumer expectations dropped more than any other of the index components, another in a string of recent signs that Americans' personal spending is tapering off.

But at the same time, factory workers put in a longer average work week than the month before and manufacturers' orders for capital and consumer goods increased. Both suggest the beleaguered manufacturing sector could be picking up a bit.

"The May leading indicators show that except for housing and construction, the production side of the economy is beginning to recover," said William K. MacReynolds, director of forecasting at the U.S. Chamber of Commerce in Washington. "But consumers are becoming more pessimistic, leaving future economic growth in doubt."

Some analysts believe three consecutive declines in the leading indicators forewarn an impending recession. But many economists dismiss this rule of thumb, noting, for example, that the index dropped for five consecutive months after the stock market crashed in 1987 but the economy escaped a downturn.

Most analyst believe the relative strength of the U.S. economy in the coming months will depend on the direction of interest rates. Federal Reserve officials are scheduled to meet in Washington Monday and Tuesday to set policy for the ensuing six weeks, but they are expected to leave rates alone for now.
