Equimark Sees Deficit Wider Than Expected For Second Quarter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/28/90

PITTSBURGH -- Equimark Corp., which has seen the top three executives at its main banking unit depart in recent months, said it sees higher-than-expected losses in the second quarter. The expected net loss of between $12 million and $17 million reflects an ill-fated acquisition attempt in Texas, bad commercial and real estate loans and litigation-related expenses, Equimark said. Equimark expects that loss despite an anticipated one-time $14.2 million gain from the sale of its credit-card business.

In 1989's second quarter, Equimark earned $4 million, or 33 cents a share.

The forecast of wider losses comes amid speculation that Equimark wants to clear its balance-sheet problems now to attract buyers. But Equimark Chairman Alan S. Fellheimer said there are no plans to sell the bank.

Chris Kotowski, an analyst at Oppenheimer & Co., said that he had expected a loss of $6 million to $7 million and that Equimark's consumer credit problems were worse than anticipated.

Mr. Fellheimer said Equimark released its earnings forecast at the request of the New York Stock Exchange. In composite trading Monday, the stock plunged 15.3%, closing at $6.25. Yesterday, the share price closed at $6.50, down 12.5 cents.

In the first quarter, Equimark had a loss of $15.4 million, compared with a profit of $5.7 million, or 50 cents a share, a year earlier.

In addition to a high provision for possible loan losses, Equimark said it had losses in its unsuccessful effort to acquire National Bancshares Corp. of San Antonio, Texas.

Equimark also incurred costs related to litigation, but Mr. Fellheimer declined to indicate what the litigation entailed.
