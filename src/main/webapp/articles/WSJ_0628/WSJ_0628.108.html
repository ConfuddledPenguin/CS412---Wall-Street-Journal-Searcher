Law: Vernon's Dixon Pleads Innocent To S&L Charges

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 06/28/90

DALLAS -- Don R. Dixon, former owner of the defunct Vernon Savings & Loan Association, pleaded innocent to charges that he illegally used thrift funds to reimburse employees for expenditures on political contributions and female escorts. Mr. Dixon's arraignment yesterday in federal court here came one day after his former accountant was convicted of tax fraud in connection with two loan transactions at Vernon Savings. One transaction involved the financing of the yacht "High Spirits," a vessel that was based in Washington, D.C., and frequented by congressmen, thrift executives and lobbyists.

The accountant, Wellington O. Rothwell, was convicted late Tuesday by a federal jury here on eight charges. His investment partner, real estate salesman Rosswell H. Westmoreland, was convicted of three charges. The charges against both men, which included conspiracy, attempted tax evasion and subscribing to false tax returns, were based on the concealment of taxable commissions earned by Messrs. Rothwell and Westmoreland in connection with the two Vernon loan transactions.

Defense attorneys Barry Sorrels and Gary Udashen indicated in court that they would ask for a new trial on behalf of their clients, who are scheduled for sentencing Aug. 2. Mr. Rothwell, 55 years old, faces a maximum possible sentence of 30 years in federal prison and a $500,000 fine. Mr. Westmoreland, 42, could receive a maximum sentence of 13 years in prison and a $250,000 fine.

Mr. Dixon, who was indicted earlier this month, is scheduled for trial Aug. 20 before U.S. District Judge A. Joe Fish here. Mr. Dixon is charged with 38 separate counts related to the allegedly improper reimbursement of Vernon employees' expenditures for political contributions and female escorts on thrift junkets. The charges aren't related to those against Messrs. Rothwell and Westmoreland.

Federal thrift regulators took control of Vernon Savings in 1987 and found that as much as 96% of its $1.2 billion-plus loan portfolio was in default.
