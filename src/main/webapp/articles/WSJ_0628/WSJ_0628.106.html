Anti-Collusion Law Wasn't Written For Era of Computer Communications

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani
  • Date: 06/28/90

Has airline pricing become so sophisticated that it outstrips antitrust law? The country's cornerstone of antitrust legislation, the 1890 Sherman Act, was written to rein in the industrial revolution's robber barons and monopolists. But the law may be beginning to show some limitations as the information age flowers.

The airline industry is the leading case. It has poured hundreds of millions of dollars into developing computer systems that not only allow carriers to track millions of reservations, but also, in essence, let them vote on whether they will go along with fare increases or cuts, critics say.

Antitrust specialists say that the only way to prove collusion is to discover a secret agreement. In order to prosecute a signalling case, the Justice Department has "a long row to hoe," says Charles F. Rule, former assistant attorney general of the Justice Department's antitrust division.

Years ago, the courts had a broader interpretation of what constituted an agreement. In a case against American Tobacco in the mid-1940s, the court ruled that an antitrust agreement could be inferred when companies had a "meeting of the minds" and were "working together understandably" to achieve a common objective.

In more recent years, the courts have narrowed their view. In a 1974 case against General Motors Corp. and Ford Motor Co., the Sixth District Court in Michigan ruled that companies could legally come to the same decision independently based on competitive reasons. It held that companies are acting legally as long as their actions are "consistent with rational business behaviour and prevailing economic conditions."

Also, the court ruled that it isn't illegal for a company to raise its prices in the hope that the action will prompt competitors to "go further or for the purpose of testing the marketplace to determine whether further unilateral action is possible."

When matching another carrier's fares, airlines say they are doing so for "competitive reasons." That type of behavior, the courts say, is "mere conscious parallelism."

In another 1974 government case, this one against Du Pont, the court said that the companies charged with price fixing weren't guilty because they were able to defend their actions with competitive explanations.

Enforcement can be difficult even in cases that involve direct contact. The Justice Department didn't get a guilty verdict in the celebrated case in which American Airlines' Robert Crandall used a telephone to propose a fare hike to competitor Braniff. Instead, American settled the case without denying or admitting guilt.

In that case, Mr. Crandall told Howard Putnam, then chairman of Braniff, that it was "dumb as hell" for the carriers to pound each other with cutthroat low fares in Dallas, according to a transcript of the conversation.
