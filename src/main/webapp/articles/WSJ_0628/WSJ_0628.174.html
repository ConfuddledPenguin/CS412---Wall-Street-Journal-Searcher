Marketing & Media -- Advertising: South Koreans Try to Build Image in U.S.

  • Author: Damon Darlin
  • Date: 06/28/90

SEOUL -- South Korea's conglomerates have a problem: Most Americans don't know a Sunkyong from a SsangYong or a Samsung. So the largest South Korean companies, including the three sound-alike companies above, are planning to spend some $10 million on image ads in U.S. business publications this year. Although not a lot of money, the spending boost marks a growing awareness among South Korean companies that they have to build a distinctive corporate image in the U.S., their most important overseas market.

"We find that most Western guys don't know the names of the groups," says Oke Dal Hyuk of LGAD Inc., the advertising affiliate of Lucky-Goldstar Group. "We are all at the first stage." Only Hyundai Group, which has thousands of cars on U.S. roads acting as rolling billboards, has significant name awareness in the U.S.

Image advertising has become crucial to South Korean companies because the U.S. prices of South Korean-made products are quickly rising to match those of Japanese imports. So South Korean companies must get consumers to trust their brands the way they trust Japanese competitors' names. "It is possible that foreigners might think that Koreans are unreliable," says Lee Sang Chan of the public relations and advertising department at SsangYong, a company just beginning to sell a few of its products in the U.S. under its own name. "Our goal is to plant a subconscious image in the mind of the general public," he says.

Because the South Korean companies' No. 1 goal is to find overseas business partners, most of the image advertising is aimed at business executives, not consumers. They want overseas executives to use South Korea as a source of supply, and even more importantly, to sell advanced technology to South Korean companies so they'll be better able to compete.

Making South Korean companies household names will be an uphill battle. The companies tend to look alike because they make many of the same things, each afraid to risk letting another domestic company outflank them. Moreover, they tend to run look-alike ads. A typical South Korean ad will mention that the conglomerate is young, fast-growing, large and in almost every business from petrochemicals and construction to electronics and finance. And they usually use a bit of nondescript but old-fashioned-looking artwork. "We don't have much know-how in international marketing," says Oke of LGAD, which recently spruced up its ad campaign for Lucky-Goldstar.

As the companies spend more, they are becoming more sophisticated. Once content to let the in-house staff design the ads, more South Korean conglomerates are seeking outside help. Samsung, for example, used Bozell Inc. to produce its new $3 million world-wide ad campaign that features photographs of four people from around the world who have benefited from Samsung products.

The best example of the improvement in South Korean ads is Daewoo Group. Its first international campaign began in 1983, the first for any South Korean company. "In terms of advertising, the U.S. was a strange market for us," says Lee Sung Bong, Daewoo's manager of international advertising and public relations. So it sought the help of J.W. Thompson.

This year, Daewoo took a giant leap forward. It decided that instead of spending a certain percentage more each year, it would spend as much as it takes to reach 50% of its intended audience in major business publications. As a result, it's increasing spending on image ads in the U.S. 100% to $4.5 million.

Some South Korean companies aren't sure that image ads are the way to go. Hyundai, which says it spent about $100 million on auto advertising last year, spends only $1.7 million telling people that "if you think we only make cars, you're 21% correct." Samsung and Lucky-Goldstar, major exporters of televisions and other electronic goods, each spend about 5% of their $50 million overseas ad budgets on corporate image ads.

But Richard Winger, a partner at Boston Consulting Group in New York, says, "For Korean companies that have just come into the mainstream, image advertising makes a lot of sense."

Cadillac Backs Conner

General Motors Corp.'s Cadillac division is putting $3 million behind Dennis Conner's 1992 quest to defend the America's Cup.

Cadillac in 1987 supported the New York Yacht Club's bid for the prestigious cup but this time around decided to put its name and technical support behind Mr. Conner, a four-time America's Cup winner who was the skipper of Stars & Stripes, which recaptured the cup from Australia three years ago.

"We want it to stay in the U.S.," said Peter Levin, Cadillac's director of advertising. Cadillac chose to support Mr. Conner because of his "focus, experience and ability to successfully defend the cup," Mr. Levin said.

Cadillac's early announcement may encourage other sponsors to pledge their support sooner rather than later, Mr. Conner said. He intends to build three boats in preparation for the 1992 yacht races, which will require some $30 million in cash and other types of support from about 400 companies. "We have several other equally important commitments, but Cadillac is the first to announce," he said.

Mr. Conner said he's "optimistic the goals will be reached in a timely manner" but acknowledges that raising money for these types of events is difficult these days. "Believe me, corporate America is watching their pennies," he said. "Cadillac looked at me very closely. There are a lot of other worthy marketing opportunities out there, and they put me through the hoops," he said.

Ad Notes. . . .

SAATCHI BREAKAWAY: An agency formed by eight defectors from Saatchi & Saatchi's London agency has won its first account. The breakaway agency, Cowan Kemsley Taylor, will handle the launch of Vanity Fair's U.K. edition next March. Billings weren't disclosed.

GARTNER TALKS: Saatchi's negotiations with its Gartner consulting group unit, which is trying to buy itself back, have been thrown into turmoil. Executives close to Gartner said that the consulting firm had been progressing with plans for a management buy-back, but that Saatchi -- without notifying Gartner -- began negotiating with a third party. "Things were coming together, and without any warning, a third party got involved. Saatchi told Gartner at the last minute," one person close to the talks said. Gartner declined to comment. A Saatchi spokesman wouldn't comment on the third party but said, "It isn't correct to say anything has been broken off. Let's hope we don't have long to wait."
