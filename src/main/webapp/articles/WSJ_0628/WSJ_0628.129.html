Giant Pension Funds' Explosive Growth Concentrates Economic Assets and Power

  • Author: James A. White
  • Date: 06/28/90

NEW YORK -- The remarkable growth of the nation's biggest pension funds is concentrating stock ownership and voting power in fewer and fewer hands. While much attention has been given to the rapid growth of U.S. pension funds, including almost a tripling of their assets in the 1980s alone, new statistics show the biggest of the big funds have expanded even more significantly.

Between 1985 and 1989, for example, assets of all pension funds climbed to $2.47 trillion from $1.59 trillion, an increase of 55%. During the same time, the 20 biggest corporate and public employee pension funds grew to $621.76 billion from $336.53 billion, an 85% jump, according to statistics compiled by the Columbia University Law School Institutional Investor Project.

The accelerated growth among the giants of the pension world means that the 20 biggest retirement funds now account for more than 25% of all pension assets, including stocks, bonds and other investments, up from slightly more than 21% in 1985.

"We see a tremendous concentration of economic power in the biggest institutional investors," says Carolyn Kay Brancato, economist for the Columbia project. While institutional investors number in the thousands, a disproportionate share of their power "is being crystalized in a small group" of the biggest funds, she says.

The growing clout of these pension giants is particularly evident in the stock market and, especially, their holdings of America's biggest companies. For example, the 20 biggest pension funds owned 9.1% of International Business Machines Corp. and 10.6% of General Motors Corp. at the end of 1989.

And the rapid growth of the biggest pension funds is putting more power in the hands of those institutional investors most likely to try to use it.

Last year, institutional holders showed their clout by managing to defeat anti-takeover proxy proposals made by Honeywell Inc. management. And Texaco Inc. last year agreed to name a director from candidates submitted by the $54 billion California Public Employees Retirement System.

The biggest institutional shareholders say their growing size shouldn't be viewed as a threat to companies' managements. "There is more concentration, but there is more sophistication" among the biggest shareholders, says Roland M. Machold, director of the New Jersey Division of Investment, which oversees $24.5 billion in state pension money. "That is an opportunity to work together," he says.

Still, this growing concentration of stock ownership in a few hands has the potential to shift the balance of power between shareholders and corporate managements.

"The model of {publicly traded} companies as owned by thousands of anonymous stockholders simply isn't true," says Bernard Black, a former Securities and Exchange Commission aide who now is a Columbia law professor. "There are a limited number of large shareholders, and they know each other."

But even the biggest institutional investors don't move in unison. GM sparked ire from some institutional holders when it included a measure to increase pension benefits sharply for Chairman Roger Smith, who is to retire Aug. 1, and other GM executives. While New York state's public employee pension system voted against the GM proposal, the California fund voted to approve it.

The fastest-growing retirement systems are those for state and local public employees. These public funds have taken the lead in challenging corporate managements in proxy votes and pressing their own suggestions about the way companies should be run. State systems such as Wisconsin's $20.03 billion fund are doubling in size every five years, and the California public employees fund is expected to reach $1 trillion by the year 2014.

Public funds account for 13 of the 20 largest pension funds, according to the most recent tally by Pensions and Investments, a trade publication.

In addition to outpacing the asset growth of corporate funds, public funds are allocating much of their new wealth to stocks, reducing their traditional reliance on more conservative bond holdings. As late as 1968, public funds in California and 15 other states didn't hold any stocks in their portfolios. In 1970, according to New York Stock Exchange statistics, stocks equaled less than 17% of the assets of state and local pension funds. But by 1988, their stockholdings more than doubled to 37% of their portfolios. Today, California's two giant state funds hold a total of $38 billion in stocks.

Corporate pension funds, meanwhile, cut their portfolio allocations to stocks to 45% in 1988 from 60% in 1970, according to the Big Board. Corporate plans remain much bigger stock investors in dollar terms, however, and held shares equal to 20.8% of the exchange's total capitalization at the end of 1988, compared with 9.1% for public plans.

With fewer conflicts of interest than other institutional investors, public pension funds -- as well as mutual funds, the second-biggest accumulators of stock in the 1980s -- "will be more likely to advance their own proposals and oppose management proposals," Mr. Black, the Columbia professor, says.

Pension fund officials often argue that their ability to gang up on companies is greatly exaggerated. Their forces are divided, because corporate pension funds play almost no role in anti-management fights, and many, if not most, public pension funds have neither the stomach nor the resources to enter proxy frays, fund officials say.

New York State Comptroller Edward V. Regan, who controls the state's largest pension fund, says that "even though there appears to be a lot of power on the institutional side, there is a lot of countervailing power" for companies' managements in the form of state anti-takeover laws, the advantages that the SEC proxy voting system gives managers and in poison-pill and other corporate defenses.

Yet another constraint is the maze of SEC regulations that prevent a pension fund from acquiring more than 5% of a company's stock without incurring complicated disclosure requirements. Still other rules effectively ban funds from discussing proxy matters with more than nine fellow shareholders unless they want to begin a formal proxy-solicitation process.

But pension funds are pushing proposals to get the SEC to overhaul its rules for proxy contests, and the agency says it is considering changes.
