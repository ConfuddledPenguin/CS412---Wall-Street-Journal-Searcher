Technology: Bill on Pesticide Residues Approved by Senate Panel

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Labor and Human Resources Committee approved a bill designed to standardize regulation of pesticide residues in raw and processed food. The measure, approved by voice vote, would establish a tolerance level under which pesticide residues would be allowed as long as they wouldn't expose a person to a greater lifetime cancer risk than one in a million.

This would eliminate the so-called Delaney paradox. Under the Delaney clause, which applies to processed food, a chemical is banned if it causes cancer in laboratory animals. Under other laws applying to pesticide use, however, that same chemical could be allowed to be used on fresh food if it fell within the Environmental Protection Agency's tolerance level.

Although the bill was approved, lawmakers are continuing to discuss modifications with the Bush administration and hope to work out a compromise before the measure reaches the floor. Among possible changes are whether to allow health or economic factors to be taken into account when deciding whether to allow pesticide use and whether to let federal rules override state standards.

In the House, the Energy and Commerce Committee hasn't yet taken up a similar bill.
