AT&T Says Net Likely Will Fall In 2nd Quarter --- Lower Sales of Equipment, Credit-Card Costs Cited; Stock Declines by $2.125

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 06/28/90

NEW YORK -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co., citing lower equipment sales and the costly startup of its new credit card, said second-quarter earnings will likely fall below those in the year-earlier quarter. AT&T's announcement knocked the wind out of the company's stock and led analysts to lower their estimates for the second quarter, as well as full-year forecasts for 1990 and 1991. Many also downgraded AT&T stock to "hold" from "buy."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, AT&T fell $2.125 to $39.125 on volume of 11.5 million shares, making the stock the most-active Big Board issue yesterday. AT&T's normal daily volume is 1.8 million shares. The decline also lopped about $2.34 billion off AT&T's market capitalization, given the company's nearly 1.1 billion shares outstanding.

While AT&T said second-quarter earnings will probably be lower than the $699 million, or 65 cents a share, in the 1989 period, the company still expects 1990 earnings to surpass 1989's profit of $2.7 billion, or $2.50 a share.

"We expect to have a record year this year in terms of earnings and our objective continues to be a mininum 10% to 12% {annual} increase in earnings," said Morris Tanenbaum, AT&T vice chairman and chief financial officer. While "we think it will be difficult to hit our objective, we will make every effort to do that."

AT&T's profit problem underscores the magnitude of Chairman Robert E. Allen's high-wire challenge at AT&T. His campaign to slash costs, refine AT&T's management structure and protect the company's "golden-goose" long-distance business has been highly successful. AT&T had record profit in 1989, and the company recently boosted its dividend for the first time since the court-ordered breakup in January 1984.

However, problems in AT&T's office phone systems and computer businesses continue to drain profits. Also, potentially lucrative ventures such as credit cards and international businesses are demanding huge start-up funds and are years away from delivering the kinds of profits that can offset losses elsewhere.

Analysts were quick to point out that AT&T's problem in the second quarter doesn't portend a calamity. "This is far, far, far from being any kind of a catastrophe," said Jack B. Grubman, telecommunications analyst at PaineWebber Inc. "If there's a silver lining in this news it is that the core business, long-distance, is healthy as ever. . . . And while the new credit card will cause short-term blips in earnings, AT&T has been signing up a boat load more customers than it thought it would by now, meaning it will reach critical mass to make money faster."

AT&T has signed up more than two million cardholders -- even after rejecting 40% of all applicants, notes Mr. Grubman. The company recently said purchases with its Universal MasterCard and Visa cards totaled more than $100 million, excluding revenue from phone call charges.

Nonetheless, Mr. Grubman lowered his per-share estimates for the second quarter to 58 cents from 72 cents, and to $2.65 for 1990 from $2.90. For 1991, he now expects AT&T to earn $2.95 a share instead of $3.40. Mr. Grubman was one of several analysts who cut his recommendation to a "hold" from a "buy."

"Investors have been paying a hefty premium {based on} AT&T's long-distance business," said Robert B. Morris III, an analyst at Goldman Sachs. The announcement "refocuses the market's attention on AT&T's exposure to product life-cycles and the economy {in addition to being} just a long-distance company."

Mr. Morris, who has had AT&T stock as a "hold" for the past six months, lowered his second-quarter per-share estimate to 60 cents from 72 cents, and cut his forecast for 1990 to $2.60 a share from $2.80. For 1991, he expects AT&T to earn $3 a share, down from $3.15 a share.

In the first quarter, AT&T's revenue from product sales, including telephone and computer equipment, increased 1.2% to $2.72 billion. In 1989, equipment sales rose 1.7% to $11.5 billion, while profit margins flattened out and costs rose 6.1% to $6.97 billion.

These numbers have led analysts to speculate that AT&T will have to dump some of its equipment businesses. Many have long held AT&T's communications equipment operations, which include office phone switches and computers, to be expendable. Mr. Grubman estimates that AT&T's losses are $400 million to $500 million a year on business systems and $300 million on computers.

"The equipment business basically stinks," said Mr. Grubman. "AT&T should take a look at exiting some of these businesses."

"A driving factor in PBX {office phone switch} sales is company relocations," said George Kelly, an analyst at Morgan Stanley & Co. "When a company moves it usually buys a new phone system, and with the real estate market down . . . this is affecting AT&T's sales."

Mr. Tanenbaum said there are "absolutely no plans to exit or sell" any of AT&T's equipment businesses. And Mr. Allen said: "Be assured, the anticipated lower second-quarter earnings will not lead to any short term `fixes' that will handicap the business over the long haul."
