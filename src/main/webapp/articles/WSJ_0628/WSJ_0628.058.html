Politics & Policy: Federal Agency Improperly Denied Disability Claims

  • Author: Wade Lambert
  • Date: 06/28/90

NEW YORK -- The U.S. Health and Human Services Department improperly rejected thousands of heart-disease patients who applied for disability benefits, a federal appeals court ruled. The decision will require the department to reconsider the applications of at least 150,000 New York state residents, according to the New York Attorney General's office, which brought the case. Although it applies only to New York residents, the decision could be used to challenge the department's policy in other states.

The court in Manhattan ruled that since 1980, the department has unfairly relied on treadmill tests to determine whether heart-disease patients were disabled.

"The treadmill test, while recommended by many medical experts as a good diagnostic tool, results in misdiagnosis of ischemic heart disease on more than one-third of the occasions," the appeals court said. Ischemic heart disease, caused by the narrowing of the arteries, is the most common heart ailment. "An individual who doesn't show signs of heart disease during the treadmill test may still be severely disabled from ischemia," the court said.

HHS has great leeway in deciding how benefits are granted, but it overstepped its authority in using only the treadmill test to evaluate heart-disease patients, the court said. Instead of solely relying on the treadmill test, the agency should also consider doctors' evaluations, angiograph readings and other tests that give a more complete picture of whether the patient is disabled by the disease, the court said.

"As a result of this decision, the federal government will no longer be able to cruelly deny justly deserved benefits to thousands of vulnerable disabled people," said New York Attorney General Robert Abrams in a statement. "The policy was just another example of the Reagan administration's efforts to balance the budget on the backs of the weakest in our society."

The decision will also require the U.S. to pick up the tab for millions of dollars in disability benefits that New York state has been paying, Mr. Abrams's office said. New York estimates that it pays $8 million annually in such benefits, and that the U.S. owes it $50 million for payments made since 1980.

Diogenes Kekatos, an assistant U.S. Attorney who represented HHS, said no decision has been made on whether to appeal the decision.

David Udell, an attorney with Legal Services for the Elderly in New York, said the case was the first class-action to challenge the agency's reliance on the treadmill test. He said the decision is likely to be used in challenging the agency's policy in other states as well.
