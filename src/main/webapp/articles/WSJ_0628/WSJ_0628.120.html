Landmark Land's Agreement to Sell Property Collapses

  • Author: Ken Wells
  • Date: 06/28/90

Landmark Land Co.'s problems deepened when developer Barry G. Hon terminated a previously announced agreement to buy one of Landmark's California developments for $275 million. Landmark, of Carmel, Calif., declined to say why the contract had been terminated; Mr. Hon couldn't be reached for comment.

The plan had been the latest in a series of attempts by Landmark to divest itself of real estate held by its Oak Tree Savings Bank unit. Landmark has been under pressure to boost Oak Tree's capital position to comply with increased capital requirements of the Financial Institutions Reform and Recovery and Enforcement Act of 1989, or FIRREA.

Separately, Landmark said that the federal Office of Thrift Supervision ruled that the Oak Tree thrift must boost "general and specific reserves" by $47.5 million. The real estate concern said that at least $30 million of these reserves relate to Oak Tree's loans outstanding for the La Quinta Hotel resort in Southern California.

Landmark said it was considering appealing the reserve ruling; the company said it has obtained appraisals from two national concerns that indicate the value of La Quinta properties far exceeds Oak Tree's loan exposure. Nonetheless, the OTS determination means that Oak Tree technically doesn't meet all of the capital tests established by FIRREA and is subject to "certain growth restrictions," according to Landmark. These restrictions include the renewal or acceptance of brokered deposits, without regulatory approval, the company added.

Oak Tree, within 60 days, must also submit a plan to regulatory agencies outlining how it will meet capital requirements in the future, according to Landmark. In the interim, Oak Tree's "status as a federally insured institution remains unaffected," the company said.

Landmark last month said it had retained Salomon Brothers Inc. to find "a strategic partner or asset purchaser" for Landmark. In April, Landmark thought it had solved its problems when it struck an initial agreement to sell to Mr. Hon, a Southern California developer, all of its real estate holdings for $967 million. But that plan unraveled after federal regulators objected to provisions under which New Orleans-based Oak Tree would have provided 75% of the financing for the purchase. Regulators said the move would have committed more than 20% of Oak Tree's assets to a single borrower.

Landmark is a thinly traded real estate concern 28% owned by its chairman, Gerald G. Barton, and 24% owned by Olympia & York Developments Ltd., a Toronto conglomerate controlled by the Reichmann brothers. In American Stock Exchange trading yesterday, Landmark closed at $8.125, off 37.5 cents.
