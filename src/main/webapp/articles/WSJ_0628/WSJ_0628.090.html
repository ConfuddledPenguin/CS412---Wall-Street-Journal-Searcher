Abreast of the Market: Industrial Stocks Advance 19.80 Despite Concern Over AT&T News

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 06/28/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks finished higher as investors bought up blue-chip shares despite disappointing earnings news from American Telephone & Telegraph. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 19.80 to 2862.13. Volume totaled 146,620,000 shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where advancing issues outpaced decliners 851 to 597.

AT&T stunned analysts yesterday morning when it announced that second-quarter earnings, to be reported in midJuly, would be below the $699 million, or 65 cents a share, reported in the year-earlier quarter. Analysts had been anticipating earnings improvement; in a recent poll of 11 analysts by Zacks Investment Research, the lowest estimate was 68 cents.

The stock finished down 2 1/8 to 39 1/8 on huge volume of 11,528,900 shares. AT&T's composite volume was the third highest of the year for any Big Board issue, and the highest since 14.3 million shares of Amax were traded Feb. 5.

"Everybody thought the AT&T numbers would pull the market down," said Richard Meyer, head of equity trading at Ladenburg, Thalmann & Co.

But to the surprise of many traders, the AT&T sell-off didn't spread to other stocks. As a result, investors' confidence rose, and they started buying health, drug and consumer issues and other stocks of companies with a history of solid earnings growth. Stock-index arbitrage buy programs, where traders buy or sell stocks and offset the position in futures to profit from price differences, also lifted shares.

Among broad averages, Standard & Poor's 500-stock index rose 3.08 to 355.14; the New York Stock Exchange composite index climbed 1.40 to 193.87 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 2.59 to 330.29.

"Some of the consumer nondurable stocks, which look overvalued, are getting more so because it is the safe place to put money on a short-term basis," said Robert Stovall, president of Stovall/Twenty-First Advisers.

Drug stocks, many of which are trading at a higher price-earnings ratio to the overall market, advanced. Bristol-Myers Squibb added 1 7/8 to 63 1/4 on 1.3 million shares; Merck advanced 1 3/8 to 84 1/4; Johnson & Johnson rose 1 3/4 to 66 5/8; Warner-Lambert gained 1 7/8 to 64 1/8, and Eli Lilly climbed 1 5/8 to 81.

Prudential-Bache Securities analyst Barbara Ryan told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report she expects second-quarter earnings for the group to be up 14% from a year earlier on a 10% sales rise.

Humana surged 3 to 48 1/2 to lead a rally among health-care issues. Manor Care rose 1/4 to 14 1/4, Community Psychiatric Centers gained 1/2 to 26 3/4 and National Medical Enterprises added 1/2 to 37 1/8.

In the pollution-control sector, Waste Management rose 1 3/4 to 41 on one million shares. Browning-Ferris Industries rose 3/8 to 43 1/2; the company agreed to sell used aluminum cans to Aluminum Co. of America for recycling. Alcoa closed unchanged at 63 7/8.

Amid the slump in AT&T, two other notable casualties of earnings-related selling this week extended their declines: Caterpillar, which fell 1/4 to 52 1/4 as 1.5 million shares were traded, Dow Chemical, which dropped 3/8 to 57 on 1.4 million shares.

But United Telecommunications, which went through a bout of selling two weeks ago after PaineWebber lowered estimates of the company's earnings, gained 1 3/8 to 38 3/4 on 1.2 million shares. Other telephone stocks also fared well despite the selling in AT&T; GTE climbed 1 1/8 to 32 and Bell Atlantic added 7/8 to 50.

L.A. Gear rose 1 1/8 to 27 1/4 after the company reported earnings of 51 cents a share for the quarter ended May 31, in line with its projection earlier this month of 50 cents to 60 cents a share. The stock had lost 1 3/8 points Tuesday as market players awaited the results.

PPG Industries retreated 2 7/8 to 46 3/8. This newspaper detailed the environmental problems facing the company because of chromium-waste pollution in Jersey City, N.J., where PPG once processed chromium ore.

Fluor gained 7/8 to 46. Saudi Arabia's state-owned oil company selected the company's Fluor Daniel unit to manage the expansion of its oil and gas production facilities. Terms weren't disclosed.

Sotheby's Holdings dropped 5/8 to 16 3/4 after the 1989-1990 art auction season ended with a disappointing sale in London, in which 58% of the artworks put up for bids failed to sell.

Elsewhere on the earnings front, Family Dollar Stores added 1/2 to 13 after reporting that net income for its fiscal third quarter, ended May 31, was up 65% from a year ago.

West Co. lost 1 3/4 to 17 3/8. The company said its second-quarter earnings could be more than 50% lower than a year ago because of production problems in the U.S. and a curtailment of business in Brazil.

Times Mirror dropped 1/8 to 31 1/2 after saying that operating results for the second quarter "will show no improvement" from its first-quarter results.

Hunt Manufacturing fell 2 to 13 3/8. The company said its earnings for the fiscal quarter ended June 3 were down 48% from the year-ago period, and its hopes for record net income this year have been "all but dashed."

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.25 to 357.05. Volume totaled 12,350,000 shares.

Bolar Pharmaceutical fell 3/8 to 5 1/8. Certain sellers of SmithKline Beecham common stock filed a class-action suit against Bolar over its alleged submission of fraudulent data to the Food and Drug Administration to obtain approval for a generic version of Dyazide, SmithKline's blood-pressure drug.
