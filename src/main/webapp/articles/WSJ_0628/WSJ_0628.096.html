Rate Weapon Gives Canadian Dollar Defiant Strength

  • Author: John Urquhart
  • Date: 06/28/90

OTTAWA -- The Canadian dollar is demonstrating again what attractive interest rates can do for a shaky currency. Economists have been predicting a decline in the Canadian dollar for more than a year, and with good reasons: The country has a huge foreign debt. The government is running a massive budget deficit. The trade balance is deteriorating. And there is the persistent push for independence in Quebec, which accounts for one-fourth of the population.

The Canadian dollar, on the basis of its purchasing power, "is overvalued about 8%," says Francis Scotland, managing editor of International Bank Credit Analyst, a Montreal-based forecaster of currency trends.

Traders were braced for a fall in the Canadian dollar, after a constitutional plan to appease Quebec was killed over the weekend. But the currency again defied predictions and rose every day this week, closing in Toronto yesterday at 85.50 U.S. cents, up from 85.38 cents Tuesday.

The Canadian dollar's strength owes to a relentless fight against inflation by the Canadian central bank. The Bank of Canada has pushed short-term Canadian interest rates about 5 1/2 percentage points above U.S. rates, double the margin of two years ago.

The interest spread is keeping the Canadian dollar firm, says Jody Foulks, vice president, foreign exchange, at Harris Trust & Savings Bank in Chicago. Mr. Scotland, the editor, says Bank of Canada Governor John Crow has become the Western world's fiercest inflation fighter.

Rates are so high that they make it very pricey to sell the Canadian dollar short, betting the oft-predicted decline will materialize soon. It costs about half a cent a month for each Canadian dollar to run a short dollar position, says Rod Fowler, foreign exchange director at the Bank of Montreal.

Some high rollers took the risk Friday, betting that the constitutional agreement with Quebec would fail. But on Monday, the Canadian dollar rose one-tenth of a U.S. cent. This forced speculators to buy back Canadian dollars that they had sold with borrowed funds in anticipation of a Monday decline that would have produced profits on the difference.

With Canada's constitutional setback, traders "bought on the rumor, sold on the news," says Richard Witten, vice president and chief of foreign exchange trading at Goldman, Sachs & Co. in New York. He says traders decided that the news wasn't bad enough to do without the big interestrate differential favoring the Canadian dollar.

Aron Gampel, Bank of Nova Scotia economist, agrees that "the Bank of Canada's anti-inflation stance continues to be a beacon for foreign capital inflows" into Canada. But he says the 5 1/2 percentage-point premium of Canadian rates over U.S. rates "can't persist." He says the Canadian economy is "close to stalling out, with serious strains in manufacturing and construction."

The anti-inflation policy is beginning to show positive results, as well. Most inflation indicators are moving in the "right direction," Finance Minister Michael Wilson said in an interview. But both Mr. Wilson and Gov. Crow remain concerned about rising Canadian wage settlements and their damage to Canada's competitive position in the U.S.

The U.S. and Canada trade more with each other than do any other pair of countries, and the U.S. takes about three-fourths of Canada's total exports. Canada's unit labor costs -- the most commonly used measure of how competitive a country is on production costs -- grew 6.9% in the 1989 fourth quarter. The growth of U.S. unit labor costs remained close to 5% throughout last year.

Mr. Wilson said the government won't relax its monetary policy until inflationary pressures are clearly under control. "We know that financial markets will be watching very closely to see if we ease in a premature way," he said.

Mr. Wilson is concerned about the potential inflationary effect of a planned 7% consumption tax that is to take effect Jan. 1. While he doesn't expect the tax itself to boost the consumer price index by more than 1.25 percentage points next year, he is worried that it could trigger a new round of inflationary wage settlements.

Canada's recent inflation record has been encouraging, says Michael Gregory, Royal Bank of Canada economist. Its consumer price index in May rose a seasonally adjusted 0.2% from April, the same rate as in the U.S.

Mr. Gregory predicts that the downturn in inflation will allow short-term Canadian interest rates to decline modestly about late August or September. This retreat in turn would trigger a drop in the Canadian dollar to about 82 U.S. cents, he says.

Sylvain Leith, National Bank of Canada economist, says the Bank of Canada "has set its sights on joining the ranks" of strong-currency countries, such as West Germany and Japan. But he says the Canadian dollar will remain shaky until the Canadian government is able to reduce significantly its big budget and trade deficits. Canada's current account deficit in its balance of payments totaled a record 5.08 billion Canadian dollars (US$4.33 billion) in the first quarter.

Canada has built up a foreign debt totaling C$229 billion, or about 35% of its gross national product. "Not only must Canada pay a large premium for attracting foreign capital, but the value of its dollar can also be significantly affected by the whims of foreign investors," Mr. Leith says.
