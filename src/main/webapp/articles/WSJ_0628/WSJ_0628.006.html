Building Contracts Increased 6% in May Due to Public Works

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/28/90

NEW YORK -- New construction contracts rose 6% in May from a four-year low in April, reflecting a surge in contracting for public works projects, according to F.W. Dodge, the forecasting unit of McGraw-Hill Inc. Contracts for nonresidential building rose 6% from April to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $82.89 billion. Residential building contracts, for newly started one-family homes and apartments, edged up 4% to $110.55 billion. Nonbuilding construction-public works and utilities, climbed 12% to $50.88 billion, its best level so far in 1990.

Dodge said three waterway development projects being built by the U.S. Corps of Engineers in Oregon, West Virginia and Pennsylvania gave an added lift to the generally improved level of building activity.

The Dodge index, which compares current contracting levels against a 1982 base of 100, rebounded to 155 in May from 147 in April. Despite the stronger index, the year-to-date contracting total of $97.29 billion remained 7% below the comparable five-month period of 1989.

George A. Christie, vice president and chief economist for F.W. Dodge, said, "It appears that the recent slide of construction contracting may be stabilizing at about 10% below last year's value."

The steepest decline in year-to-date value was in the Northeast, where newly started construction was 24% below the comparable five-month period of 1989. The greatest gain was 5% in the North Central region. The West remained even, while the South was down 10%.
