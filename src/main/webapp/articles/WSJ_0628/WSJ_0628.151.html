Senate Passes Omnibus Housing Bill After Debate Centered on S&L Bailout

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate passed an omnibus housing bill after a day of rancorous debate that centered more on the regional inequities of the savings-and-loan bailout than on the merits of housing legislation. The bill, approved 96-1 last night, is a compromise reached Tuesday by the Bush administration and key senators. But the floor debate quickly deviated to the S&L bailout as several senators representing the Northeast and north-central states charged that the bailout represented a transfer of wealth from their states to the Southwest.

"When was the last time New York had this kind of assistance?" asked Sen. Daniel Moynihan (D., N.Y.) as he brandished a copy of the federal water projects legislation that he maintained was heavily weighted to Texas and other Southern and Southwestern states. He answered: "When we built the Erie Canal."

Yesterday's action continued the debate over who pays for, and who benefits from, the $100 billion the U.S. expects to spend on the thrift-industry cleanup, not including interest.

Sen. Alfonse D'Amato (R., N.Y.) offered an amendment that would target community-development funds to housing jurisdictions based on a formula that takes into account the amounts individual states will receive in the S&L bailout. The D'Amato amendment was offered in response to an amendment by Sen. Phil Gramm (R., Texas) that would allocate funds based on population, similar to general revenue-sharing programs that were largely scuttled during the Reagan administration.

"To tie this to the savings-and-loan bailout is, pure and simple, outrageous," Sen. Gramm said. "I have been offended by the whole level of logic in this debate."

Sens. D'Amato and Gramm ultimately agreed to turn the matter of the formula for community-development grants over to Housing Secretary Jack Kemp, who is to study the current formula and report his recommendations to the Senate by July 1, 1991.

At a news conference before the debate, Mr. Kemp called the bill "the most significant step toward committing this country on a bipartisan basis to combating the conditions of homelessness, hopelessness, despair, and poverty that has taken place in this town since the original War on Poverty."

The compromise bill calls for outlays of $18.1 billion in fiscal 1991, which begins Oct. 1., $19.5 billion in fiscal 1992 and $21 billion in fiscal 1993. It includes agreement on four major provisions that had kept the two sides haggling for the past three weeks. The administration won on two of the four major points, including what it hopes will be full funding for a home-ownership initiative that would provide funds for public housing tenants to purchase rather than rent their homes or apartments. The administration also won changes in the Federal Housing Administration mortgage-insurance program to provide a stronger capital base for the fund.

Additionally, agreements were reached on the eligibility requirements for housing assistance, targeting more of the funds to the very poor, and allowing housing jurisdictions to fund new construction after they demonstrate special need for new housing.

Secretary Kemp acknowledged that the funding levels in the bill are subject to change. "It is obvious," he said, "that the budget summit will have an impact, not only on the final authorization, but the final appropriation of housing funds."

The House Banking Committee has approved a housing bill that would be considerably more expensive than the Senate version.
