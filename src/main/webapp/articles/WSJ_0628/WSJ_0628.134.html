World Markets: Tokyo Shares Rally on Interest Rate Hopes; London Stocks Weakened by Technical Factors

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/28/90

Tokyo stocks rallied on improved volume Wednesday, helped by technical and fundamental factors, traders said. The Nikkei index of 225 selected issues was up 740.98 points at 32312.75. The index gained 447.58 points Tuesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index fell 104.91 points to 32207.84.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 600 million shares, up from 396 million shares Tuesday.

Advancing issues outnumbered declining issues 751 to 214, while 156 issues remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues in the first section advanced 39.05 points, or 1.68%, at 2362.40.

The second section index rose 44.19 points, or 1.07%, to close at 4155.77. Volume in the section was estimated at 29 million shares, up from 18.3 million shares Tuesday.

Trading was helped by a shift in sentiment that the U.S. could lower interest rates, noted Kevin Haag, a trader in overseas operations at Nomura Securities Co.

Comments Tuesday by President Bush that he'd accept a tax hike as part of a deficit reduction plan could cause a change in the fiscal situation, putting less demand on the credit markets, Mr. Haag said.

"As a result, we could see lower yields in the {U.S.}, which means an opportunity for lower yields in Japan, which is encouraging for equities," Mr. Haag said.

Some stock market participants said they weren't overly optimistic that the U.S., followed by Japan, would see lower rates. "If everyone was so sure that rates were going to fall, the Dow {Jones Industrial Average} wouldn't be having so much trouble," one trader noted.

And while the Nikkei index managed to rise more than 2%, bond prices and the yen-dollar rate weren't so striking, they pointed out.

Also, volume, though improved from its lower levels of the past few weeks, wasn't strong enough to imply that buyers had returned in full force, traders noted.

However, the reappearance of institutional buying Wednesday was a bullish factor that shouldn't be underplayed, traders added.

While lower-rate hopes buoyed market sentiment throughout the day, the actual rise in stock prices was largely technically driven, traders said.

Wednesday was the first day of trading for July settlement, and investment trust funds began placing fresh cash on the market, they noted.

Moreover, arbitragers were seen buying steadily all through the day, as futures continued to trade at a premium to the cash indexes.

Buying was particularly heavy on high-priced, blue-chip issues. One trader at a leading U.K. securities firm speculated that talk among the Big Four domestic brokerage houses that the market had become too unfocused, with too wide of a range on interest in small shares, resulted in more directed buying.

That the Big Four were discussing such strategy "may well be," Nomura's Mr. Haag said, but Wednesday's rally wasn't dealer-led. Instead, institutional buyers were reacting to technical buy-signals, he said.

NEC was a prime reflection of institutional support, Mr. Haag said. The stock rose 120 yen to 2,020 yen (78 cents to $13.06).

Kyocera continued its recent strength with a 300 yen gain, to 8,630 yen. Fanuc gained 310 yen to 7,360 yen. Sony, meanwhile, rose 360 yen to 8,870 yen, and TDK rose 270 yen to 7,320 yen.

Hitachi rose 50 yen to 1,550 yen, Matsushita Electric Industrial gained 90 yen to 2,150 yen and Toshiba added 40 yen to 1,110 yen.

Fuji Photo Film soared 230 yen to 4,620 yen, and Advantest rose 220 yen to 6,290 yen.

London shares finished a stagnant session lower, weakened by technical factors ahead of the expiration of the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Stock-Index future contract.

Dealers said the broad-based index slipped back when the premium on the June futures contracted to near parity with the cash market. There are technical pressures to get the futures premium down in order to smoothly unwind basket trades that could dump a large amount of equities into the market come the Friday contract expiration, some analysts said.

"As the premium in the futures collapsed, the cash market decided to tumble in line with it," said one director of equities.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-Share Index closed down 26.3 points at 2373.5, a bit better than the 2369.1 intraday low. The market was at its best in the first 10 minutes of trading at 2394.1.

The Financial Times 30-Share Index was down 24.0 points at 1902.6. Volume registered a modest 389 million shares, down from the 461.2 million issues traded Tuesday.

Market watchers said there was concern over the vulnerability of companies that are largely dependent on their dollar earnings.

Dealers suggested that dollar-dependent internationals were dumped on worries over the new tax plans being fashioned by the White House. Dealers said the first impression on the tax news was that it would be unfriendly for the dollar, and hurt Britain's overseas earners.

Sterling's strength on anticipated imminent entry into the Exchange Rate Mechanism of the European Monetary System also bruised some members of the sector.

Imperial Chemical Industries shed 22 pence to #11.62 (38 cents to $20.21), B.A.T lost 11 pence to 652 pence, Reuters skidded 18 pence to #12.37 and Glaxo Holdings declined 11 pence to 799 pence.

BICC dropped 16 pence to 442 pence after announcing it was to raise #177 million from a rights issue to help finance the purchase of a larger stake in Grupo Espanol General Cable.

Rothmans International dropped 2 pence to 753 pence after announcing that its year pretax profit rose 24%.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Frankfurt, Zurich, Stockholm and Madrid; lower in Amsterdam, Paris and Milan; and mixed in Brussels. South African gold stocks closed marginally higher.

Also, prices closed higher in Singapore and Seoul, and lower in Hong Kong, Taipei, Manila, Wellington and Sydney.
