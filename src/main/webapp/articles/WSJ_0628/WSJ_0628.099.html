RJR Seeks to Reset Some Junk Bonds At Rate of 16%

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 06/28/90

NEW YORK -- RJR Nabisco Inc. is telling bankers that it hopes to reset the interest rate on $2.6 billion of its most troublesome high-yield junk bonds at 16 1/2%, perhaps as soon as this summer. The estimate -- which still has to win investment bankers' approval -- shows how much the tobacco and food company believes its giant refinancing plan should improve its creditworthiness. A month ago, bond-market specialists estimated that RJR would have to accept a rate of 20% or higher, if those bonds' interest rate could be successfully reset at all.

As previously reported, RJR has begun a $5.3 billion effort to reshape its balance sheet. RJR and its major shareholder, Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., plan to inject new bank loans, preferred stock and equity into the company, while retiring potentially high-cost junk bonds. News of RJR's plan has sent its junk bonds soaring nearly 20% the past month, to about 82 cents on the dollar.

Bankers say RJR aims to retire all of its $2.18 billion of 14.07% convertible reset bonds due 2009 and $1.95 billion, or about 45%, of its 14.07% reset bonds due 2007. Those reset bonds have threatened to create a financial crisis for RJR, because their interest rates must be reset by next April so that the bonds trade at full face value. The bonds have traded as low as 58 cents on the dollar this year, suggesting that RJR could be forced to pay a ruinously high interest rate to meet its reset obligations.

Even after its refinancing plan, RJR plans to leave about $2.6 billion of its reset bonds of 2007 outstanding, bankers say. Those are the bonds that RJR hopes to reset at a 16.5% interest rate, the bankers add.

Some bondholders are optimistic about RJR's chances of success. "The reset is starting to look more doable," says Lawrence Garshofsky, an arbitrager at Kayne Anderson & Co., Los Angeles, which owns some RJR bonds. "If they can complete it, that bodes well for RJR's financial health."

But an executive at a major bank lender to RJR cautions that the 16.5% reset rate is just a "ballpark figure" that could change. For one thing, he says, a major change in U.S. interest rates would disrupt RJR's calculations. So would any difficulties in putting the refinancing package together, or a downturn in RJR's basic businesses.

Furthermore, the exact reset rate won't be determined by RJR. The company's investment bankers, Merrill Lynch & Co. and Morgan Stanley & Co., are scheduled to confer with two independent Wall Street firms, Lazard Freres & Co. and Dillon Read & Co., to set the reset rate. If they can't agree, a fifth Wall Street firm will pick a rate between the ones they decide on.

Dillon Read vows it won't be pushed around by giant RJR and KKR. "Our job is to set the rate on the debentures," said Fritz Hobbs, an investment banker at Dillon Read. Those duties are "pretty clearly defined. And I don't know yet what's going to happen."
