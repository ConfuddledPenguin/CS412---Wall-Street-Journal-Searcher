Politics & Policy: Stiffer Sentences In Safety Breaches Clear Senate Unit

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Labor Committee approved a bill that would impose stiffer sentences for criminal safety and health violations by employers. The committee voted 11-5 for the bill, sponsored by Sens. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio) and James Jeffords (R., Vt.). All the Democratic committee members and two Republicans -- Mr. Jeffords and Sen. David Durenberger of Minnesota -- voted for the measure.

Separately, Labor Secretary Elizabeth Dole is expected today to urge that the maximum civil penalty for a violation of provisions of child-labor law be boosted to $10,000 from $1,000. In testimony prepared for delivery to the House Labor Standards subcommittee, Mrs. Dole will note that the department's Employment Standards Administration already has boosted penalties as much as fourfold by considering different violations involving one child separately, rather than as a single violation.

Two recent sweeps by the agency uncovered more than 2,800 child-labor violations, and more than $6 million in penalty assessments are expected, according to her testimony.

Under the bill approved by the Senate panel, employer violations of the occupational safety and health law that result in worker deaths would become felonies, rather than misdemeanors. The legislation also would boost the maximum jail sentence to 10 years from six months and the maximum fine for such criminal violations under the worker safety and health law to $250,000 from $10,000 for each violation. The Justice Department says maximum fines of $250,000 a violation for individuals and $500,000 for companies already can be imposed under general criminal code.

Under the Senate bill, willful violations that result in serious bodily injury would become a criminal offense subject to a jail sentence of as much as five years. And any federal preemption of often-tougher state prosecutions of such violations on murder or other charges wouldn't be allowed.

The Bush administration supports the increasing criminal penalties for employer violations that result in death and the bar on federal pre-emption. But it opposes making responsibility for serious bodily injury a criminal offense; the Labor Department has said it is impossible to define with any clarity what constitutes such an injury.
