What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/28/90

BUSH PROPOSED major initiatives for Latin America, including substantial relief on the nearly $12 billion in the region's debt held by the U.S. government. The policy also calls for as much as $300 million in multilateral grants to foster private investment, and foresees eventually turning the entire Western Hemisphere into a free-trade zone. ---

AT&T predicted a drop in second-quarter earnings because of lower equipment sales and the costly startup of its bank credit card. The company's shares sank $2.125 on the news, and many analysts withdrew recommendations to buy AT&T stock.

---

Democrats offered possible cuts in popular benefit programs, in a move to reciprocate for Bush's statement that he is willing to raise taxes.

---

Stocks and bonds rose in further reaction to Bush's statement on taxes. The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 19.80 points to close at 2862.13.

---

Japan and the U.S. settled most issues in talks on structural trade impediments. Negotiators were pushing to reach a final pact today.

---

Leading economic indicators rose 0.8% in May after a 0.1% decline in April. The index's second reversal in four months lends support to the belief that a recession is unlikely, but some worrisome signals persist.

---

Suspected price-fixing by airlines is the focus of a new Justice Department inquiry. United, Delta and America West have been asked for information on their ties to the industry's clearinghouse on fares.

---

Accounting rule makers agreed to delay by a year, until 1993, the effective date of a regulation requiring companies to account for the costs of non-pension retirement benefits.

---

Fluor and Parsons won contracts to help overhaul and expand Saudi petroleum facilities. Analysts valued each job at as much as $5 billion.

---

Siemens plans to invest more than $600 million in East Germany in the next few years. Separately, Allianz bought 51% of the nation's insurance monopoly for $163.1 million.

---

A bill to reregulate cable TV rates and encourage new competitors to challenge existing cable companies was approved by a House panel.

---

First Executive picked its ex-auditor to be chief financial officer in a move designed to tighten internal controls and to insulate Chairman Fred Carr from outside contacts.

---

The ties of all failed S&Ls to Drexel and its former junk-bond chief, Michael Milken, are being investigated by an FDIC task force.

---

The CFTC criticized sharply the SEC's conclusion that program selling in futures markets triggered a 6.9% stock-market drop last October.

---

RJR Nabisco hopes to reset the interest rate on $2.6 billion of its junk bonds at 16 1/2%, a much lower level than bond specialists estimated before the firm announced its refinancing.

---

Stiffer prison sentences and fines for safety and health violations by employers were approved on an 11-5 vote by the Senate Labor Committee.

---

Laidlaw's founder plans to step down as chief executive in August and resign as chairman at year end.

---
