Liberal Justices Are Losing Their Voice As Influence on Pivotal Decisions Fades

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- Despite the Supreme Court's surprise support for federal affirmative-action programs yesterday, the high court's liberal wing was almost entirely neutralized in the term just ended. The court's four liberal justices, who have combined experience of 91 years on the high court, wrote only a handful of important majority opinions. And many of those either preserved current law or were on subjects, such as business and regulatory issues, in which the court's deep ideological division didn't play a role.

In this regard, though the composition of the court remained unchanged, the latest term differed from the previous one. The court clearly became more conservative in the 1988-89 term, but the liberals were in the fight. They were able to eke out an occasional victory and write some significant opinions.

This term, says Walter Dellinger, a professor at Duke University Law School, with the possible exception of the affirmative-action ruling, "There is not a single significant case decided or written by the liberal justices in which major new ground was broken.

"In nearly every case that divides the court ideologically, there are now five justices for a conservative result," Mr. Dellinger adds. In case after case, the only suspense was whether the sometimes disparate views of the five conservatives would coalesce around a single rationale.

For the liberals, who are among the court's most senior members, this unusually passive role is a distinct change and a frustrating one, according to court insiders. The liberal wing consists of Justices William Brennan, 84 years old, Thurgood Marshall, 81, Harry Blackmun, 81, and John Stevens, 70.

Even some liberal victories this term were written by others. It isn't uncommon, especially in closely divided 5-4 decisions, to assign the majority opinion to the justice who is the least committed to the position, figuring that will produce a narrow product acceptable to all five justices.

That apparently was the strategy followed by Justice Brennan in the important 5-4 ruling upholding the authority of federal judges to require school districts to raise taxes to pay for school desegregation. With Chief Justice William Rehnquist in dissent, it fell to Justice Brennan as the senior justice in the majority to decide who would write the opinion. The assignment went to the usually conservative Justice Byron White, who probably wrote a narrower opinion than any of the liberals would have.

The decision was one of the most important liberal victories of the term. But it was a reaffirmation of federal court power to correct civil rights violations, not a new or expanded principle. Moreover, it wasn't written by the liberals.

To be sure, there were other liberal victories, but none that recognized new constitutional rights and only a few that expanded rights. Justice Brennan's opinion yesterday, upholding two Federal Communications Commission preference programs to aid minorities and women in obtaining broadcast licenses, was a broad endorsement of federal affirmative action. But the ruling, while unexpected, is an extension of a 1980 Supreme Court decision.

Similarly, Justice Marshall's opinion striking down North Carolina's death penalty law was foreshadowed by an earlier ruling. And Justice Brennan's decision striking down the federal flag desecration law was based almost entirely on the ruling a year ago invalidating the Texas flag law.

The abortion issue provides a long-term illustration of the pattern. Through most of the 1980s, a bare liberal majority held back state efforts to restrict the right to abortion. Now, for the second consecutive year, the liberal wing had to content itself with rulings that upheld new state restrictions, though the most severe aspects were struck down.
