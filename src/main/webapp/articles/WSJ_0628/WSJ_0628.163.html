SEC Authority to Impose Fines Clears House Panel

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Energy and Commerce Committee approved by voice vote a bill that would give the Securities and Exchange Commission the power to impose fines for a variety of securities-law violations. The SEC's authority to levy fines currently is limited to insider-trading cases. For other violations, such as short-selling offenses or fraudulent sales practices, the agency must choose between an injunction or barring the offender from the securities industry altogether.

In addition to giving the SEC the power to impose fines, the bill would allow the commission to impose cease-and-desist orders against securities-law violators. "This injunctive remedy would mean the SEC would no longer be required to go to court to secure a temporary restraining order or permanent injunction," said Rep. Matthew Rinaldo (R., N.J.).

The bill also would permit the SEC to ask courts to prohibit people convicted of a securities-law violation from serving on the board of a public company. Moreover, the bill would let the SEC impose on violators fines of up to $100,000 for individuals and $500,000 for firms, or the amount of their profits, whichever is greater. The bill is identical in most respects to a measure passed by the Senate Banking Committee.

The committee also passed by voice vote legislation aimed at curbing fraud and abuse in the penny-stock market. The bill would, among other things, substantially increase disclosure requirements for some penny-stock issues and expand SEC power to bar crooked penny-stock salespeople from the securities industry.
