Wednesday's Markets: Fresh Look At Tax Talk Spurs Prices --- Stocks, Bonds Sustain Their Gains on Hope Of Progress on Deficit

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/28/90

NEW YORK -- The more bond and stock investors thought about President Bush's willingness to do anything -- even raise some taxes -- to trim the U.S. budget deficit, the more they liked it. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices rose nearly 3/8 of a point, or $3.75 for each $1,000 face amount, though trading was light. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 19.80 to close at 2862.13, also in light trading. The dollar rose against the mark but fell against the yen; most of the action was in such high-yield currencies as the British pound and Canadian and Australian dollars.

The general impression left by the statement the president made Tuesday morning was that any economic harm from higher taxes wouldn't be immediate and that the Federal Reserve Board might ease interest rates if a deficit-cutting package nears congressional approval.

Helping bond prices rise was yesterday's surprisingly successful auction of U.S. Treasury four-year notes. Demand outstripped the supply by a large enough margin that investors' worries about a glut of government debt were eased.

Robert Smentkowski, a bond trader at Security Pacific National Bank in Los Angeles, said prices rose shortly after 1 p.m. Eastern time when rumors circulated that the note auction had gone well. The market already had been rising because of a growing sense that politicians "seemed to be serious about talking about reducing the deficit," Mr. Smentkowski said.

The bond rally helped push stocks higher, with investors continuing to buy the so-called defensive stocks of companies with secure earnings, said Philip Eagan, co-managing director of institutional services at Sanford C. Bernstein & Co.

The industrial average would have been higher if American Telephone & Telegraph shares hadn't been trounced after news that the company expects lower second-quarter earnings than many analysts had anticipated. But unlike similar disappointments earlier in the week, AT&T's news didn't scare investors out of other stocks. One reason, Mr. Eagan said, was that analysts saw good news for AT&T's rivals in its profit troubles.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose on volume of 146.6 million shares in New York Stock Exchange trading. Advancing issues outnumbered decliners, 851-597.

Bond prices rose. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 8.48%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6645 marks and 154.45 yen, compared with 1.6630 marks and 154.67 yen late Tuesday.
