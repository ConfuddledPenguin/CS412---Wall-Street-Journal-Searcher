Fluor, Parsons Get Contracts For Saudi Work --- Aramco Oil and Gas Projects Carry Estimated Value Of Up to $5 Billion Each

  • Author: Jill Bettner
  • Date: 06/28/90

Fluor Corp. and Parsons Corp. said they received contracts for chunks of Saudi Arabia's massive project to overhaul and expand its oil and gas production facilities. Analysts valued each of the contracts at between $2 billion and $5 billion. Aramco, the state-owned Saudi Arabian company, is boosting its energy-producing capacity at a time when world-wide demand is on the rise. The project, which oil-industry experts say could cost $40 billion over nine years, has been divided into three parts, by geographic regions of the country.

Fluor, based in Irvine, Calif., said its Fluor Daniel Inc. unit will act as overall program manager in the northern region of Saudi Arabia. It will expand and de-mothball Aramco's production facilities there, including its oil and gas plants, pipelines and offshore drilling platforms.

Parsons, a closely held Pasadena, Calif., engineering and construction concern, confirmed that it had received a similar contract naming it program manager of the southern region. A Parsons spokeswoman said she didn't believe that a central-region manager had yet been named. Aramco officials couldn't be reached for comment.

Les McCraw, Fluor vice chairman and chief executive officer, said, "In a prospective way, it's the most significant contract we've received in the last decade." He wouldn't disclose the contract value, however, saying it would be "premature to speculate on how the program will unfold. Over the next several months, we will be defining costs and determining schedules."

Parsons also declined to put a value on its contract or discuss other details of the award. The company's spokeswoman said it was equivalent in nature to the Fluor award, however, and will include both engineering and construction work.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Fluor's stock rose 87.5 cents to close at $46 a share on volume of 621,700 shares.

Fluor said it will start the project next month, working primarily out of its offices in Houston and in Al Khobar, Saudi Arabia. It will staff the effort with engineers already on the payroll, Mr. McCraw said.

Fluor may be renovating some production facilities it helped build in Saudi Arabia during the oil boom of the 1970s and early 1980s, he added. Unlike some other American engineering and construction companies that left the Middle East when oil prices fell, "we stayed and did other things," he said.

Analysts expect other U.S. contractors to be involved in the project, the largest by Aramco in nearly a decade. "The last time we were looking at a multibillion contract from Saudi Arabia was probably 1982," said Herbert Hart, an analyst at S.G. Warburg & Co. in San Francisco.

Companies he and other analysts mention as possibly vying for some of the new work include San Francisco-based Bechtel Group Inc.; the M.W. Kellogg Co. unit of Dresser Industries Inc., Dallas; Foster Wheeler Corp., Clinton, N.J.; and the Lummus unit of Asea Brown Boveri, a Swiss/Swedish industrial concern.

Bechtel declined to comment. None of the other companies could immediately be reached.

Mr. McCraw said he wouldn't be surprised to see several other members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries "do something similar, maybe not of the same scope and breadth, but similar. There is a desire for more production capacity, driven by the perception of energy growth over the decade of the 1990s."

Fluor's five-year contract includes options for four years of additional work on the project. The extent of the program is still being defined, Mr. McCraw said, but "we will be involved in planning, analysis and direct execution of the engineering effort" as well as defining the role of subcontractors.

Mr. McCraw said that because Aramco intends to release the work over several years, the financial impact it will have on "any single quarter or year for us is still uncertain. We intend to be conservative in reporting this work in our backlog." He added, however, that Saudi Arabia will pay Fluor in dollars, rather than in barrels of oil, as analysts claim it has paid other U.S. companies in the past.
