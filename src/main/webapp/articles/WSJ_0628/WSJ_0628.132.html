Economy: Democrats Respond to Bush Tax Move By Offering Possible Spending Cuts

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- Democrats, trying to reciprocate for President Bush's statement that tax increases are needed to reduce the deficit, began offering possible cuts in popular government benefit programs at bipartisan budget talks. "Tit for tat," said Senate Budget Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.). "They talked about their sensitive point yesterday -- taxes. We talked about our sensitive point -- entitlements."

Rep. Leon Panetta (D., Calif.), chairman of the House Budget Committee, listed a host of ways to trim spending on entitlement programs, including Medicare, federal employee pensions and agricultural spending. Programs for the poor weren't mentioned, but participants said there was discussion -- though no agreement -- about increasing federal income taxes on Social Security benefits paid to upper-income Americans.

The Democratic proposals would trim spending on entitlements less than President Bush and Republican congressional negotiators have suggested. Negotiators are aiming at a $50 billion reduction in next year's projected deficit. The Republicans want entitlement cuts of $10 billion or more.

Despite the continuing differences, budget negotiators yesterday engaged in what House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.) described as a "freewheeling" discussion of other ways to cut entitlement spending.

Republicans, even those who have criticized the Democrats recently, went out of their way to praise them yesterday. "We're moving in the right direction," said Sen. Phil Gramm (R., Texas).

When the budget talks resumed yesterday after a break of several days there was a markedly different atmosphere, which both sides attributed to Mr. Bush's statement on Tuesday that "tax revenue increases" are needed to reduce the deficit.

"The climate in there is pretty good -- for obvious reasons," agreed Sen. Wyche Fowler (D., Ga.).

"The whole tenor has changed. It is very determined," said Sen. Pete Domenici (R., N.M.). "Whatever it was that kept us from . . . seriously talking seems to have left the scene."

One sign that the talks have moved into a more serious phase was a demand from the Senate Finance Committee that it and the House Ways and Means Committee, rather than summit negotiators, decide what taxes to increase. But others, including the administration and congressional negotiators who don't sit on the tax-writing panels, insisted that the outlines of a tax increase be shaped at the summit table.

"We're going to work in tandem," said Senate Finance Committee Chairman Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas).
