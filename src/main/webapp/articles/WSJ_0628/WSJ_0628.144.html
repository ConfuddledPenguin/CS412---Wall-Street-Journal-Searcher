U.S., Japan Make Progress in SII Talks, Say Final Pact May Be Reached Today

  • Author: Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 06/28/90

TOKYO -- U.S. and Japanese officials said they settled most of the issues outstanding in the Structural Impediments Initiative talks by early Thursday morning and added that they were pushing to reach a final agreement by Thursday afternoon. After meeting all night, the negotiators broke off after 5 a.m. and agreed to reconvene at 11 a.m. Negotiators said there was still a chance they wouldn't complete a final report by Thursday afternoon, in which case they would probably resume talks in the U.S. next week.

The wide-ranging talks, advocating sweeping changes in macro and microeconomic policies in both nations, are aimed at altering the underlying causes of the $49 billion bilateral trade imbalance.

Attempting to resolve the most contentious issue of the SII discussions, the Japanese government during the night said it would spend 430 trillion yen ($2.780 trillion) on public works over the next 10 years, 15 trillion yen more than it stated last week. The Japanese government added that Nippon Telegraph & Telephone Corp. and Japan Railway Group, which are currently being privatized, will spend about 25 trillion yen over the next decade.

The U.S. argues that an increase in public spending will shift resources to Japan's domestic economy and cut its trade surplus, while Japanese officials have expressed fears that too much spending will fuel inflation.

At 10:40 p.m. Wednesday, Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu spoke with President Bush by telephone and told him he had made the "maximum effort" in putting together the package. The two sides are pushing to complete a report before the Houston summit meeting of the seven leading industrialized democracies in two weeks.

The major issue outstanding when the talks recessed Thursday morning, a member of the U.S. delegation said, was whether Americans thought the increase would have a sufficient economic impact. He said U.S. officials were analyzing the data before the late morning meeting. "We would have liked to have seen more," he said, but added that "it does clearly reverse a 10-year trend" of public spending declining relative to the size of the Japanese economy.

The two sides, according to the senior Bush administration official, did reach agreement on the other major policy issues. Most notably, he said, Japan agreed to "a significant shortening" of the time required to obtain a patent and gave "commitments" on increasing penalties for antitrust violations and strengthening the rights of private parties to bring antitrust lawsuits.

Japan also agreed to set up an import advisory council under the prime minister that will propose projects to boost imports and will also take measures to provide 24-hour clearance for imports, the U.S. official said. The U.S. is also satisfied with actions Japan has taken since an interim SII report was written in April to streamline its distribution system, most significantly to insure that large retail stores can get permission to open less than two years after applying for a permit. Currently the process can take more than 10 years.

The U.S. official said that the two sides were still haggling over the details of how to follow-up on the SII final report to make sure both sides adhere to their promises and that the actions have the desired effect. He said the U.S. was also pushing Japan to reaffirm clearly that it is committed to reducing its capital account surplus. In recent weeks, some Japanese officials have argued that Japan should preserve its surplus, in part to help aid needy countries.

While most of the focus has been on Japan, the process is supposed to be a "two-way street." U.S. negotiators said President Bush's announcement Wednesday that he would support an increase in tax revenue proved to the Japanese the U.S. is serious about reducing their budget deficit, action the Japanese say is crucial to reduce U.S. dependence on foreign capital.

Concerned with mounting trade tensions, and a seemingly intractable trade imbalance, the two countries agreed to set up the SII forum last July to supplement an endless series of sector-specific spats.

While many analysts have expressed cynicism about the value of the SII talks, U.S. officials say it could have a significant effect in reshaping the Japanese economy and bilateral economic relations within two to three years. If an agreement is reached amicably, both countries will also likely hail the pact as further evidence of the decline in tensions between the two countries.
