Marketing & Media: Bill to Reregulate Cable TV Clears Hurdle in House

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Telecommunications subcommittee passed a bill to reregulate cable television rates and encourage new competitors to challenge existing cable companies. Under the measure, which passed on a voice vote, the Federal Communications Commission would establish the maximum fee that cable operators could charge for basic cable service consisting of local broadcast signals and public education and government channels. Other cable programming would be subject to regulation as well, but on a case-by-case basis.

The legislation would direct the FCC to develop criteria to identify "unreasonable or abusive" rates and to establish procedures for reducing rates that are excessive. Local or state authorities could file complaints with the FCC alleging that rates are unreasonable or abusive.

The legislation also seeks to ensure that emerging competitors to cable companies -- such as so-called wireless cable operators and direct broadcast satellite service -- can obtain programming. Cable programmers that are owned wholly or in part by cable operators, for example, couldn't unreasonably refuse to sell programming to cable competitors. Also, cable operators couldn't require exclusive arrangements from cable programmers as a condition for carrying their programs.

But the future of the House bill and of a similar Senate measure is cloudy. Cable interests are fighting both measures. "We intend to remain involved in the process, but at this point, the question of whether a bill acceptable to the industry can be enacted this year remains very much unresolved," said the National Cable Television Association.

Consumer groups, however, claimed a victory because the House committee bill contains stronger consumer protections than the cable reregulation bill that the Senate Commerce Committee passed this month. "Consumers, satellite services, broadcasters and others got their pound of flesh -- all out of cable's hide," said Gene Kimmelman, legislative director for the Consumer Federation of America.

Meanwhile, telephone companies are trying to lift the ban on their entry into the cable business. An amendment that would permit cable competition from phone companies is expected to be considered when the bill reaches the House floor.
