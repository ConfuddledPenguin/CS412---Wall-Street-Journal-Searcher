Security Pacific Is Cleared to Buy Gibraltar Assets

  • Author: Marilyn Chase
  • Date: 06/28/90

Security Pacific National Bank emerged as the successful bidder, out of a field that included California's biggest banks and thrifts, to acquire certain prime assets and liabilities of Gibraltar Savings, Beverly Hills, Calif., from the Resolution Trust Corp. The Security Pacific Corp. unit will assume about $3.9 billion in core deposits and $2.7 billion of loans covering one-family to four-family residential housing. Security Pacific also will gain Gibraltar's premium network of 83 branches in California.

"This represents an important addition to Security Pacific's customer base in key California markets," said a spokesman for Security Pacific.

Terms weren't disclosed. In a recent precedent, however, Bank of America paid a price roughly equal to 4% of core deposits when it acquired Western Savings of Arizona's assets. By that yardstick, Security Pacific could be paying about $150 million or more for the thrift.

A Security Pacific spokesman said the bank expects to announce additional details of the transaction tomorrow, or as soon as a definitive agreement is signed with the RTC, the federal agency that took over the ailing thrift.

Regulators last year seized the thrift unit, declaring it insolvent. Gibraltar Financial Corp., the parent of Gibraltar Savings, filed for protection under Chapter 11 of the federal bankruptcy laws in February. Since then, nearly a dozen banks and thrifts have pored over the unit's records and considered bids but many were believed to have dropped out of the running earlier this month because of Gibraltar's deteriorating condition. Security Pacific may have been able to bid more because it can realize cost savings by closing down operations and branches that overlap with its existing facilities.

Gibraltar Savings, based in Beverly Hills, Calif., was placed in federal conservatorship after disastrous loans and investments in commercial real estate and mortgage-backed securites wiped out its capital. Even with the premium paid by Security Pacific, the case is expected to cost the government several hundred million dollars. The RTC retains all of Gibraltar's multifamily, land and commercial real-estate loans, amounting to about $2.5 billion.

Donald Crowley, an analyst with Keefe Bruyette & Woods Inc. in San Francisco, estimated that the sale price of the Gibraltar assets was in the range of $150 million to $200 million.

"In general, these deals tend to be running at about 4% of core assets," he said, citing the Bank of America deal.

Since Security Pacific bid on $3.9 billion worth of Gibraltar's total core deposits, without taking any questionable assets, Mr. Crowley added, "That means they got the good stuff."
