Justices, Upholding FCC License Policies, Broadly Support U.S. Affirmative Action

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court, upholding federal programs designed to increase the number of broadcast licenses held by women and minorities, broadly supported the power of Congress to adopt affirmative-action plans. By a 5-4 vote, the high court ruled for the first time that Congress may authorize affirmative action, not simply to correct the effects of past discrimination, but to promote other goals, such as racial and ethnic diversity.

The ruling, written by Justice William Brennan, upheld two Federal Communications Commission policies -- adopted by the agency and later ratified by Congress -- that give minority-owned and women-owned businesses preferences in competing for broadcast licenses. The court said the FCC's rationale -- that minority ownership is desirable because it promotes diversity in broadcast programming -- was sufficient to justify the affirmative-action plans.

The decision is the most sweeping endorsement of affirmative action ever issued by the court, but its practical effect may be limited for two reasons. First, the ruling applies only to federal programs and doesn't alter the effects of last year's high court decision restricting the ability of state and local government programs to give preference to minority-owned businesses. Second, the political climate in Congress isn't likely to bring about any major minority-business preference programs.

However, the decision will make it easier for Congress to preserve existing plans, including the FCC policies, and affirmative-action requirements in federal public-works transportation programs and Education Department projects. The decision also will help to bolster the transportation affirmative-action plans against pending court challenges.

The decision was an extension of a 1980 ruling in which a splintered high court upheld a minority preference program adopted by Congress for federal publicworks contracts.

Yesterday, the majority said that Congress may adopt race-oriented policies, as long as they are benign, meaning not intended to discriminate against anyone, and as long as "they serve important governmental objectives within the power of Congress and are substantially related to achievement of those objectives."

The decision provoked a strong dissent by Justice Anthony Kennedy, who said the court was permitting "racial classifications" like those that kept blacks and whites in "separate but equal" facilities until the desegregation of the 1950s and 1960s, and that led to the internment of Japanese-Americans during World War II.

"Policies of racial separation and preference are almost always justified as benign, even when it is clear to any sensible observer that they are not," Justice Kennedy wrote, citing as an example an apartheid policy statement of the South African government.

Justice Sandra O'Connor also wrote a dissenting opinion, joined by Chief Justice William Rehnquist and Justices Kennedy and Antonin Scalia.

The high court ruled on two FCC policies. In one, a federal appeals court for the District of Columbia in April 1989 declared unconstitutional the FCC's distress-sale policy, which allows a broadcast company that is in danger of losing its license to avoid that risk by selling the license to a minority-controlled business.

The appeals court ruled for Alan Shurberg, a computer consultant seeking a television license in Hartford, Conn., since 1982. The FCC awarded the license in a distress sale to Astroline Communications Co., a partnership led by Richard Ramirez. Astroline appealed the ruling, and the Supreme Court reversed the appeals-court decision.

Also in April 1989, a different threejudge panel of the same appeals court upheld the constitutionality of an FCC policy that gives minority and women applicants some preference in receiving new licenses. The appeals court ruled for Rainbow Broadcasting Co., a 90% Hispanic-owned company to which the FCC awarded a UHF television license in Orlando, Fla. The policy was challenged by Metro Broadcasting Inc. Metro appealed the decision, and the Supreme Court affirmed the appeals-court ruling.

FCC Chairman Alfred Sikes said the policies were "aimed at creating more diversity, and we think they have achieved that. We're pleased that the cloud of doubt has been removed from them."

Andrew Jay Schwartzman, executive director of the Media Access Project, a public-interest communications law firm, said the decision was "obviously a big surprise." Mr. Schwartzman called the decision "the most important, clearly positive affirmative-action ruling in some time." (Metro Broadcasting Inc. vs. FCC.)

Environmental Lawsuits

The justices, by a 5-4 vote, made it substantially more difficult for citizens' groups to mount broad legal challenges to federal government land-use and environmental policies. In a decision written by Justice Scalia, the justices barred the National Wildlife Federation, an environmental group, from suing to challenge Interior Department plans to open up millions of acres of federal land in Western states and Alaska to mineral leasing and mining.

The high court ruled that individuals or groups who propose to sue over federal land use must offer detailed evidence of how they would be hurt by the government's actions; moreover, the evidence must demonstrate injury from the proposed leasing of specific tracts of land, not for the overall, general land-use plan.

Justice Harry Blackmun dissented, joined by Justices Brennan, Thurgood Marshall and John Stevens. (Lujan vs. National Wildlife Federation).

Child-Abuse Witnesses

The justices, by a 5-4 vote, ruled that states may permit alleged victims of child abuse to testify on closed-circuit cameras or through other indirect methods intended to avoid their facing defendants in court. Such methods may be used only when absolutely necessary to protect against emotional trauma to a child, the court said, and when the defendant's right to have an attorney cross-examine the child witness is preserved. Under these circumstances, the defendant's Sixth Amendment right to confront witnesses isn't violated, the court said in an opinion written by Justice O'Connor.

The decision marks the first time the court has directly endorsed the constitutionality of alternative means of testifying for children in child-abuse cases. But it ratifies what most states have adopted for their courts. Thirty-seven states permit use of videotaped testimony of children; 24 states allow use of one-way closed-circuit television in such cases.

Justice Scalia dissented, joined by Justices Brennan, Marshall and Stevens. (Maryland vs. Craig).

By another 5-4 margin, in a second case, the court disallowed a physician's testimony in court about statements made to him by an alleged child-abuse victim who was considered incapable of testifying herself. The court said testimony about the alleged victim's statements can't be used unless prosecutors explain why the child is unavailable to testify and why the child's statements to the third party should be considered reliable. Justice O'Connor wrote the opinion. Justice Kennedy dissented, joined by Chief Justice Rehnquist and Justices Blackmun and Byron White. (Idaho vs. Wright).
