World Markets: Japanese Medium-Sized Securities Companies Could Be in Big Trouble as Stock Prices Shrink

  • Author: Clay Chandler and Masayoshi Kanabayashi
  • Date: 09/24/90

TOKYO -- Are Japan's medium-sized securities companies headed for trouble? As the Tokyo Stock Exchange sinks, the pubs and noodle shops around Kabutocho, the stock market district, have buzzed with worried talk of a shakeout in Japan's once-prosperous securities industry.

With stock prices down 40% in the past nine months and trading volume down by nearly two-thirds, Japan's brokers have good reason to be nervous.

The first clear signs that the market's reversal has taken its toll on the Japanese securities industry surfaced two weeks ago. Japan's Big Four -- Nomura Securities Co., Daiwa Securities Co., Nikko Securities Co. and Yamaichi Securities Co. -- drastically cut pretax earnings projections for the half-year ending Sept. 30.

Nomura said pretax profit would drop almost 50% to 125 billion yen ($908.1 million) in the first half. At Daiwa, officials projected a 62% decline, while Nikko and Yamaichi said earnings would plunge 73% and almost 75%, respectively. The projected profits are about a third of what the firms previously estimated. Pretax earnings are the clearest indication of the firms' operating results.

At the moment, few worry that the Big Four are in serious difficulty. Although it's also not clear sailing for the four: On Friday, Standard & Poor's Corp. said it now has a negative long-term outlook for the big securities companies, but added that it won't necessarily lower the houses' ratings. S&P said its new opinion reflects sharply lower earnings expectations and uncertainty about when and how long any rebound would last, but said the four maintain good financial positions.

But at midsized securities firms, which have high fixed costs and rely more heavily on equity brokerage commissions, the shock of a suddenly depressed market is proving harder to withstand. Many of the second-tier securities houses are projecting midterm earnings declines of more than 75%. Midsized brokerages -- many of which were in the middle of expanding operations -- will have trouble shrugging off those declines.

"The big brokers can take a hit more easily," says Stuart Matthews, a financial analyst at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Securities Ltd. "Medium-sized firms aren't as diversified, so when revenue from commissions dries up there isn't much for them to fall back on."

The plight of Japan's midsized firms may be best illustrated by difficulties faced in recent days by Sanyo Securities Co., Japan's only major family-run brokerage house.

Just a few years ago, the firm was flying high. Buoyed by soaring stock prices, Sanyo -- Japan's eighth-largest securities company in terms of revenue and trading volume -- racked up record profits. With Tokyo stocks climbing into the stratosphere, Sanyo's ambitious young president, Yoichiro Tsuchiya, decided the time was right for a bid to join the ranks of the major securities houses. He pushed Sanyo, which is 12.8%-owned by company controlled by Nomura Securities, into new types of trading, such as warrants and futures, increased staff by more than a third and significantly expanded international operations.

The centerpeice of Mr. Tsuchiya's bold vision for Sanyo's future was an eight billion yen trading center replete with giant wall-to-wall electronic display boards, lightning-fast computers and a daunting array of gee-whiz telecommunications equipment.

Mr. Tsuchiya -- who declined requests for an interview -- told a monthly magazine soon after the center was opened that he felt the trading center was necessary to bolster the confidence of his sales force. "When a strong tailwind is blowing, victory goes to the boat with the biggest sail," he was quoted as saying.

But Mr. Tsuchiya's strategy may have run aground. Earlier this month, the market was abuzz with unsubstantiated reports that Sanyo was about to lose as much as 50 billion yen in the futures market. Other rumors suggested that Sanyo was in danger of losing billions of yen from its holdings of commercial paper issued by a recently failed New Zealand investment bank. Reports about Sanyo's position in the futures market appear to have been false. The contracts were said to expire Sept. 13, but that deadline passed without noticeable interruption of Sanyo's business.

Sanyo officials confirm, however, that the firm holds $90 million in commercial paper issued by DFC New Zealand Ltd., a formerly state-owned investment bank that floundered after being privatized. But while acknowledging that they haven't been able to collect on the loans -- which came due in October 1989 -- Sanyo officials say they still believe the firm will get most of its money back. "The conclusion on this matter has yet to be drawn," said a Sanyo spokesman. "We aren't worried about this at all," he added, "because it is an issue with the New Zealand government."

As if the talk about Sanyo isn't bad enough, some traders have begun whispering that Sanyo's trading operations are sinking -- not financially, but physically. Rumors have it that Sanyo's giant trading floor, erected on a swath of reclaimed land along the Tokyo waterfront, is slowly receding into the muck. Sanyo officials say the reports are preposterous.

If Sanyo or any other securities firms are really floundering, it is doubtful that Japan's financial authorities would let them drown. When Yamaichi Securities teetered on the brink of collapse in 1965, officials at the Bank of Japan intervened to pump it full of emergency funds. Many here think regulators at the central bank and the Ministry of Finance remain ready to rescue the nation's securities institutions before they fail.

Friday's Market Activity

Tokyo stock prices rebounded strongly from midday lows in the last hour of the session Friday, posting the first daily gain in seven trading days.

The Nikkei Index of 225 issues was up 174.85 points at 23777.83.

Financial markets in Tokyo were closed for a public holiday Monday.

Friday's volume on the first section was estimated at 430 million shares, up from 407 million shares Thursday. Decliners outnumbered advancers 564 to 387, while 151 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section gained was up 1.80 points, or 0.10%, to 1769.77.

In London, stocks ended higher and at intraday peaks after staging a major recovery from morning lows triggered by widespread stock-index futures selling.

Once the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index dropped below 2000, "buyers just came out of the woodwork," a trader said. The FT-SE 100 closed up 8.6 points at 2025.5. The Financial Times 30 Share Index rose 6.3 points to 1537.3. Volume was 553.7 million shares, compared with Thursday's 517.7 million shares.

Elsewhere, shares closed higher in Paris; lower in Amsterdam, Brussels, Frankfurt, Madrid, Milan, Stockholm, and Zurich. Brussels hit a two-year low, while Amsterdam and Stockholm ended at 1990 lows. South African gold stocks closed narrowly mixed.

Stocks closed lower in Sydney, Wellington, Hong Kong, Manila and Singapore, mixed in Seoul and higher in Taipei.
