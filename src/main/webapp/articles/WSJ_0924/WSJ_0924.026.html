Marketing & Media: EPA Continues Ban On European Wines Containing Chemical --- Decision, Which Angered U.S. Beverage Importers, Stands Pending Studies

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 09/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency said it would continue to ban imports of European wine containing a fungicide while it studies whether the substance is harmful. The move, which drew an angry response from the National Association of Beverage Importers, came even though preliminary EPA analysis suggests that the levels of the chemical don't pose a health threat. Under EPA procedures, the soonest that wine containing the chemical could be back on the U.S. market is next summer.

The fungicide, procymidone, has been found in 20% of the French wines sampled by the Food and Drug Administration, 6% of the Italian wines and 2% of the Spanish wines. In Europe, where wine containing procymidone residues is legal and considered safe, the fungicide is used to kill the botrytis fungus on grapes. It isn't used in the U.S.

In an interview, EPA chief William Reilly said, "I have looked carefully for ways to avoid disruption of trade and still ensure compliance with the law and fully protect the public health against any risk, however remote."

The beverage importers association, however, said it is "very disappointed" by the EPA decision, which it said "could have a disastrous impact on international trade relations and invite retaliatory action by the Europeans." The group urged the U.S. "to fully consider the ramifications of the EPA action and the potential for European trade embargoes against U.S. food products."

Under federal law, the government must block the import of any food or beverage containing residues of a pesticide for which the EPA hasn't set a tolerance, or allowable level. The FDA began confiscating shipments of wine found to contain the chemical after it turned up during a routine test last February.

Mr. Reilly emphasized, though, that consumers needn't stop drinking European wine, even if it was purchased before the procymidone was detected. "People shouldn't worry about this," he said. "I intend myself not to worry about it."

But he was critical of Sumitomo Chemical Corp., maker of the chemical, which failed to obtain a tolerance for procymidone before shipments started. Wine industry officials also have criticized the company.

In April, the company did ask the EPA to set a tolerance level and sought expedited proceedings that would have allowed it to ship wine containing residue while the EPA studied the matter. The EPA declined, but said it is expediting its review of the data. Such a review normally takes several years, but the EPA is seeking public comment on whether to propose an interim tolerance level that would allow shipments to resume next summer.
