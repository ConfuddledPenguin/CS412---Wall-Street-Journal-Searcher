Economy: Machine Tool Orders Climbed During August --- Total Topped July by 23% And Year-Ago by 27%; Show Augurs Brisk Sales

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 09/24/90

Durable-goods producers are proceeding with plans to upgrade their factories despite the economic uncertainty. Machine tool orders in August rebounded briskly from the vacation-affected July lull, rising 27% from a year earlier and 23% from July.

Furthermore, buyer interest at the International Machine Tool Show in Chicago early this month indicates that business will continue brisk into the fall, if the economy doesn't actually plunge into recession, machine tool industry officials say.

Orders placed at U.S. machine tool plants in August jumped to $288.1 million from $227.1 million a year earlier and $234.3 million in July, according to NMTBA -- The Association for Manufacturing Technology, a McLean, Va., trade group.

The August total was bolstered by at least one large automotive order. Litton Industries Inc.'s Lamb Technicon unit at Warren, Mich., received an order for about $40 million of equipment to machine transmission cases for Chrysler Corp.

"We were very encouraged by the show," said Robert J. Siewert, president of Monarch Machine Tool Co., a Sidney, Ohio, producer of lathes, machining centers and equipment to process coils of sheet metal. "Many people came to us with plant-improvement projects, and some actually came with drawings. We were amazed; after a relatively slow summer, we didn't expect that much interest."

Salesmen and executives of Hurco Cos., Indianapolis, obtained twice as many sales leads at this year's machine tool show as they got at the 1988 show, said Michael K. Campbell, senior vice president and chief financial officer. "Manufacturers have decided that if they want to be in position to compete beyond 1991, they need to invest in equipment now."

Cincinnati Milacron Inc., the nation's largest machine tool concern, booked more orders at the show than officials had expected. A machine shop owner bought one of the machines Milacron was demonstrating and took it home in his pickup truck after the show.

Machine tool distributors, who handle imported as well as U.S.-made machines, also did far more business than usual at the recently concluded show, said Robert A. Gale, president, American Machine Tool Distributors' Association, Rockville, Md. He said some U.S. manufacturers have concluded that there's less risk in borrowing heavily to buy new machinery in uncertain times than there is in trying to compete with obsolete equipment. "They'd rather go out fighting" than to be slowly squeezed out by more efficient competitors, he added.

Part of Monarch's recent order rise results from a recovery in the oil patch, Mr. Siewert said, but Monarch has received inquiries and orders from "a very broad cross section of industry." September orders will be higher than for any of the summer months, he said, and "October looks like it will be good too."

Machine tool industry executives have been expecting the auto makers to resume active buying. Industry orders dropped noticeably in the spring of 1989 when auto companies began deferring decisions on equipment purchases.

The Middle East crisis, which is creating uncertainty for most businesses, may resolve some of the questions troubling engineers at the auto companies. They've been uncertain about whether to buy machinery to make six-cylinder engines, which the performance-oriented buyers have been seeking, or more fuel-efficient 16-valve, four-cylinder engines.

Auto companies are concerned that if they produce more of the six-cylinder cars, they'll exceed federal government limits on average fuel consumption per car. But they have also worried that the thrifty cars Uncle Sam wants them to produce wouldn't sell. If Saddam Hussein can hold the price of gasoline up in the present range, however, those four-cylinder cars may sell briskly, particularly if they're equipped with new four-speed automatic transmissions. Both the engines and transmissions would require large purchases of machine tools.

August orders for lathes, machining centers, milling machines, boring mills, grinders and other machines to shape metal parts by cutting totaled $240.5 million, up 54% from $156.3 million a year earlier, the NMTBA said. Even excluding the Lamb order from Chrysler, orders would have been up more than 25%.

Orders in August for metal-forming presses and other machines to shape metal with pressure were $47.6 million, which was down 33% from $70.9 million a year earlier. Figures for both years were reduced by unusually high cancellations of foreign orders.

Machine tool shipments in August totaled $242.3 million, a 7.3% rise from a year earlier, which is in line with an 8.7% shipment increase for the first eight months.
