Bush Gets Hoots From Timber Industry, Environmentalists for Plan to Save Owl

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld and Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 09/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration's long-awaited plan for preserving the northern spotted owl pleased neither the timber industry nor environmentalists, and left some of the thorniest issues for Congress to resolve. Agriculture Secretary Clayton Yeutter and Interior Secretary Manuel Lujan said the administration is asking Congress to allow new sales of 3.3 billion board feet of timber on Forest Service lands in Washington, Oregon and Northern California during the fiscal year that begins Oct. 1. Industry officials expressed disappointment that the proposed level wasn't higher, while environmentalists characterized it as excessive logging that would jeopardize the long-term survival of the owl's ecosystem.

"It's less overcutting, but it's still significant overcutting," said Frances Hunt, forestry resources specialist for the National Wildlife Federation. "They're cutting those forests at an unsustainable rate and that puts everything at risk."

Sen. Patrick Leahy (D., Vt.) chairman of the Senate Agriculture Committee, chided the administration for advancing only a one-year plan and "putting off the tough decisions until later."

In June, the administration declared the owl threatened and put it under the protection of the Endangered Species Act. At the time, the administration delayed announcing the bulk of its forest-management steps, saying it would wait for the recommendations of a task force headed by Mr. Yeutter. The latest steps are based on those recommendations.

But the administration didn't spell out in any detail how 3.3 billion board feet of lumber can be cut without jeopardizing the spotted owl's well-being. The administration also didn't present a plan for future years.

The administration is seeking to give a greater role in the spotted owl dispute to the endangered species committee, or so-called God Squad. The cabinet-level panel can overrule Agriculture and Interior Department decisions prohibiting economic activity that would jeopardize an endangered species, but it is rarely convened.

Under the administration's proposals, Congress would authorize a convening of the committee with authority to review all timber sales and land management plans related to the spotted owl. Environmentalists blasted this idea, and key lawmakers have suggested that Congress is unlikely to accept it.

The administration also is asking Congress to adopt language that "requires implementation" of its forest-management plans, which have been the subject of considerable litigation by environmentalists in the past. Officials said they don't recommend "precluding judicial review," though they didn't suggest any other way to ensure that timber sales aren't blocked.

Labor and timber interests complained that reducing Washington and Oregon timber harvests, which will total 4.5 billion in the year ending Sunday, would have serious economic effects. The labor and timber interests were equally unhappy about the proposal to allow only 310 million board feet in Northern California, about half of the average cut planned for national forests there.

The plan "represents a formula for economic depression, personal hardship and social turmoil throughout the Pacific Northwest," asserted Michael Draper, spokesman for the Western Council of Industrial Workers, which represents 23,000 timber-industry employes.

But the industry expressed hope that Congress would authorize, as recommended, an immediate convening of the endangered species committee. The endangered species law requires the committee to hold hearings; saw-mill towns and timber families see the hearings as an opportunity to have "their voices heard for the first time." Said Mark Rey, executive director of the American Forest Resource Alliance: "This deadly serious game over the fate of the Pacific Northwest economy has just begun."

Secretary Lujan said he would appoint a team to develop a long-term recovery plan for the owl as required by the endangered species act. This will include continuing the Yeutter task force and giving it the broader mission of reviewing the nation's forest-management needs.
