Apparel Firms Expected to Post Weak Earnings

  • Author: Teri Agins
  • Date: 09/24/90

Apparel companies are likely to report another quarter of depressed earnings in coming weeks, analysts say, as worries about the economy heighten already sluggish retail sales. In August, retailers failed to get the expected boost from early fall and back-to-school merchandise, causing analysts to become more negative in their third-quarter outlook for the apparel sector.

"The third quarter is going to be tough," says Jay Meltzer, an apparel industry analyst at Goldman, Sachs & Co. "There is still a discernible amount of inventory to work off at retail. . . . Most apparel makers are finding that retailers are playing it much closer to the vest than ever before, which has made it tough for {apparel companies} to make their plans."

Observes Josie Esquivel of Shearson Lehman Brothers: "Back-to-school didn't happen, and the first couple of weeks in September were weak. Things look very bleak for the third quarter."

For the 16 apparel companies she follows, Elizabeth Armstrong of Prescott Ball & Turben Inc. projects third-quarter earnings will rise an average of 4.1% from a year earlier. But the chasm between strong companies and the weak ones has become more pronounced this year, she and other analysts said.

Expected to continue posting decent third-quarter gains from a year earlier are Liz Claiborne Inc., estimated to gain about 15%; Fruit of the Loom, climbing between 15% and 20%; and Leslie Fay Co. increasing about 11%, according to estimates by Deborah Bronston, an analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc.

But stagnant retail sales in August and early September have clobbered weaker players in the apparel group, and those companies will report lower third-quarter earnings. These include VF Corp., where jeans sales continue to lag; Bernard Chaus Inc.; and Hartmarx Corp., where analysts expect a third-quarter loss compared with a year-earlier profit.

"During the first half of 1990, apparel and textile inventories were up, and manufacturer shipments were down," says Patricia L. Ryan of Raymond James Associates. "And now, with the problems of the Middle East heating up and reducing consumer confidence," retailers such as giant Wal-Mart Stores Inc. are holding back commitments for orders, she adds. For fleecewear maker Russell Corp., for example, she notes that the company has healthy orders and backlogs, but is waiting for retailers to commit to a shipment date.
