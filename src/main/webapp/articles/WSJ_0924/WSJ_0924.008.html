Enterprise -- Washington Update: SBA Deputy Is Departing; Congress Allows Breaks On Clean-Air Law; a European Market Worries Firms

  • Author: Jeanne Saddler
  • Date: 09/24/90

SBA'S NO. 2 gets the ax, but did her boss or the White House wield it? Kay Bulow, whose aggressive style as the Small Business Administration's deputy administrator alienated some people, was given her walking papers late last week. SBA chief Susan Engeleiter, who had fought to bring Ms. Bulow to the agency, says her deputy is leaving by mutual agreement because the two differ in management style. SBA officials claim Ms. Engeleiter took the initiative.

But others familiar with the situation say White House Chief of Staff John Sununu pushed the eject button after Ms. Bulow demoted and transferred some longtime staffers in the SBA's finance department. Members of the small-business lending community were so unhappy, they started calling the White House.

Pressure from all sides may have toppled the powerful SBA aide, whose influence grew, some staffers say, because of Ms. Engeleiter's frequent travel schedule. Ms. Bulow made enemies inside and outside the agency with her tough style. When staffers asked an SBA expert how to alert Ms. Engeleiter to the Bulow problem, he warned: "When you shoot, don't miss."

But some agency workers and outside officials have praised Ms. Bulow as a manager who would make decisions. Some said her rough and tumble style would play differently if used by a man. The new acting SBA deputy will be June Nichols, the agency's Atlanta regional director. Ms. Bulow couldn't be reached for comment.

One lender who deals with the SBA regularly says the Bulow ouster may make Ms. Engeleiter realize "she can't delegate leadership at the agency."

CLEAN-AIR BREAKS can be given to small firms by states, Congress decides.

A House-Senate conference committee settling differences on a final clean-air bill agreed that states can offer small companies extra technical help, time and flexibility in complying with the law. The committee adopted provisions saying each state must set up programs to tell small companies how they can cut pollution and how to handle the act's paper-work requirements. Each state would designate an official to be the small-business ombudsman and also create an advisory panel to monitor the assistance program.

If they wish, states also can set up a no-fault inspection system to check compliance with the law. State or private inspectors would do regular audits of small companies to point out illegal practices that must be corrected, but without assigning fines. However, establishing a no-fault system, as well as giving a concern flexibility and extra time to comply with the law, would be options, not requirements. Reps. Ron Wyden (D., Ore.) and Rick Boucher (D., Va.) pushed for the changes on the House side, while Sen. Rudy Boschwitz (R., Minn.) sponsored an amendment included in the Senate bill.

"We wanted to make sure firms like dry cleaners and furniture makers wouldn't be thrown into a bureaucratic ocean where it's presumed they have the lawyers and experts that bigger companies do," says Rep. Wyden.

RALLY ROUND THE CHAIRMAN: Several small-business groups will honor House Small Business Committee Chairman John LaFalce (D., N.Y.) at a dinner this week to show appreciation for his unsuccessful effort to broker a compromise on language in the civil-rights bill. He proposed a change limiting monetary damages for violations of the new legislation. This infuriated House civil-rights forces who had counted him as a friend. Rep. Major Owens (D., N.Y.) fumed that Rep. LaFalce had "crawled out of the sewer." The dinner's sponsors include the National Federation of Independent Business, National Association of Manufacturers, U.S. Chamber of Commerce and four other groups.

FRANCHISE FLAP: The Federal Trade Commission continues on the trail of errant franchisors. The FTC charged Lifecall Systems Inc. of Camden, N.J., whose franchisees sell emergency alert units for elderly or sick people, with failing to provide prospective franchisees with required information and misrepresenting the business's profit potential. Lifecall, without admitting or denying the charges, agreed to a consent order to change its practices and pay a $150,000 fine.

CONFUSION MARKS smaller firms' views on a unified European market.

As Washington policy makers ponder the impact of the 1992 advent of a single European market on U.S. business, a new survey of nearly 300 U.S. chief executives of smaller concerns shows that nearly half are confused about what the change will mean to them. More than half think it will either hurt or have little effect on profits. A third of those responding think the plan will create a fortress Europe atmosphere hostile to U.S. exporters.

The survey, conducted by Market Access Europe, a London-based research and public affairs company, also shows that 70% of the small companies understand the general principles of a unified common market, but not its detailed proposals.

American companies "have a serious information gap" about Europe's new single-market plan, says David Boddy, Market Access's chairman. Without detailed information on new regulations and technical specifications, he says, a firm could find itself barred from the 12 member countries instead of just from one. He also argues that now is the time for exporters to help shape the new rules. "If the U.S. wants this country to trade its way out of the economic slump, there's a lot of work to do," Mr. Boddy says. European concerns already are gearing up for the American market, he adds.

Respondents view the German and British markets favorably, except for high labor costs, but are more dubious about Italy and Greece. About a third believe the French are unsympathetic to the U.S. style of business.
