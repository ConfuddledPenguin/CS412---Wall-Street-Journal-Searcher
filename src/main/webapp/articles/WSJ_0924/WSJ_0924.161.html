Two Banks Acquire City Savings Bank Units in RTC Sale

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The Resolution Trust Corp. sold 36 branches of City Savings Bank, a Somerset, N.J., thrift with $8.3 billion in assets, to two financial institutions. City Savings' 27 branches in Florida were sold to Great Western Bank, Beverly Hills, Calif., a unit of Great Western Financial Corp. The nine offices in the Camden, N.J., area were sold to First Fidelity Bank, South Jersey, which is based in Burlington, N.J., and is a unit of First Fidelity Bancorporation.

In addition, City Savings' 66 remaining branches in New Jersey will be reopened today as a newly chartered federal mutual association. These branches will maintain normal business operations while the RTC negotiates a permanent resolution.

Great Western, which will assume about $938 million of deposits, paid the RTC a premium of about $9.8 million. First Fidelity will assume about $468 million of deposits and paid the RTC a $9.1 million premium. The RTC will advance $1.4 billion to the acquirers, and estimates that the transaction will ultimately cost the RTC $1.5 billion.

The RTC also announced that it sold most of the branches of failed Great American Savings & Loan Association, Oak Park, Ill., with $623 million in assets, to two banks. First National Bank of Chicago will acquire Great American's 13 Chicago branches, and First Financial Bank, Stevens Point, Wis., will acquire the thrift's three branches in Peoria, Ill. The RTC estimated its cost for the transaction to be $71.8 million.

Separately, the Office of Thrift Supervision reported that withdrawals at the nation's savings and loans exceeded deposits by $9.65 billion in April, the nation's thrift regulator said. A month earlier, withdrawals exceeded deposits by $958 million, the smallest deposit outflow in eight months.

A spokesman for the OTS said most of the outflows occurred in California, where banks competed aggressively for deposits in that month.

The outflow of deposits also reflects, in part, the thrift industry's shrinking efforts to meet new capital requirements. The S&Ls needn't hold as much capital if they reduce their deposits. The number of S&L associations also shrank to 2,492 in April from 2,716 a year earlier.
