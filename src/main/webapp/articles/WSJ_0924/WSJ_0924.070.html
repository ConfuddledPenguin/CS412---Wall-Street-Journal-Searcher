BREAKING BRITAIN'S SPEED LIMIT

Two Japanese car makers and one from France reportedly have been warned not to glamorize speed and high performance in advertising and selling their models in Britain.

According to a Japanese news agency, Britain's Advertising Standards Authority has criticized Nissan Motor Co. and Toyota Motor Corp., along with the French manufacturer Renault S.A., for emphasizing the ability of their cars to reach speeds of 155 miles an hour. This is more than twice the 70 mph speed limit that applies on all British motorways.

A spokesman for the authority was quoted by the news agency as saying that ads stressing speed and glamorizing power appeared to have come back in fashion after being dropped some years ago. "We will be keeping a very close eye on car advertisements in future," the spokesman reportedly added.

An official of the Royal Society for the Prevention of Accidents, noting that there was ample evidence that speed was a major factor in traffic fatalities, said: "We are very pleased the authority is getting tough with emotional language in car adverts which leads to aggressive driving."

A LOAN FOR SHANGHAI VOLKSWAGEN

Shanghai Volkswagen reportedly signed an agreement with China's Bank of Industry & Commerce to borrow 128 million yuan (about $27 million) to be used for Chinese-made parts. Shanghai's Liberation Daily said the loan also would serve as a "shot in the arm," enabling Shanghai VW to complete its 1990 production plan. The report called Shanghai VW the "pet of the Shanghai financial community." Shanghai VW is a joint venture of Shanghai Automobile & Tractor Industries and Volkswagen AG.

HELPING NORTHERN IRELAND

The European Community said it would give 35 million European currency units ($45.9 million) to projects in Northern Ireland. The money is being targeted for infrastructure projects as well as various social programs trying to reconcile the province's Roman Catholic and Protestant communities. The EC commissioner for regional policy, Bruce Millan, said: "The (EC) itself was born out of the need to resolve conflict and achieve reconciliation, and it is appropriate that it should today seek to help Northern Ireland in the same way."

GATT DOORS SHUT ON CHINA

A Beijing delegation failed in its latest attempt to gain China's entry into the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, the global trading organization. China, which has observer status in the GATT and has been trying to gain membership for some eight years, has been told that it has to open up its markets more to foreign trade and reduce tariffs before it can be considered. Beijing's bid for recognition also was set back considerably by last year's Tiananmen Square killings.

MORE PRIVATIZING IN PORTUGAL

The Portuguese government will sell its minority holdings in 34 companies, the news agency Lusa reported. The assets, in each case less than 10% of a company, are to be sold through direct negotiation with potential buyers, a Finance Ministry spokesman said; current shareholders may be granted preferential rights. The companies involved run the gamut from beer maker Companhia de Cervejas Estrela S.A. to the national zoo.

LINKING SEOUL AND BELGRADE

Yugoslavia and South Korea will establish the first-ever air link between the two countries, a Yugoslav news agency reported. Yugoslav Airlines plans to open a direct link between Belgrade and Seoul by the end of this year, and Korean Air is expected to fly to Belgrade soon thereafter, the report said.

THE FUTURE OF EUROPE'S ELDERLY

Europe's old people may have to go back to work, reversing the current trend toward early retirement, as families shrink and a labor shortfall looms, according to papers discussed at a recent Council of Europe workshop.

And as a trend toward very small families continues, the many over-65s expected by the year 2025 -- more than 20% in Sweden, West Germany and the Netherlands against half that figure now -- will likely experience increasing social isolation due to the absence of family ties.

"The picture isn't all black," said Italian demographer Raimondo Cagiano Azevedo. "The gray revolution is the result of medical improvements and a change in life styles, such as women joining the labor force. . . . But there will be problems if governments fail to adopt a long-term strategy."

Like other demographers at the seminar, Mr. Azevedo urged a quick overhaul of national welfare systems to cater to growing numbers of the "old old" -- those over 75 years. By 2025, they are expected to account for 8% of people in Britain, France and Switzerland.
