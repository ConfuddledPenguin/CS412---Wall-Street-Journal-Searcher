Economy: OPEC's Output May Forestall Energy Shortfall

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 09/24/90

Authoritative new estimates show that members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries are pumping enough oil to forestall -- at least until winter -- world-wide energy shortages because of the Middle East crisis. However, rising prices are building political pressures to tap stockpiles such as the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve even though these were established as buffers against physical shortages, not volatile markets.

Total OPEC output has rebounded from the August low of 19.7 million barrels a day to 22 million barrels a day or more for September. That level generally hadn't been expected until October.

The extra oil is coming mainly from Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates and Venezuela. It's enough to replace three-fourths of the four million barrels a day of embargoed Iraqi and Kuwaiti oil.

Demand for OPEC's oil still is higher than its output, partly as a result of panic buying. But rising flow rates combined with high world inventories make it unlikely that the International Energy Agency's emergency oil-sharing mechanism will be triggered when the governing board of 21 oil-consuming nations meets again in Paris Friday.

The increased output doesn't bar individual nations from dipping into their strategic oil reserves to alleviate high prices. Nor does it completely rule out a possible supply crunch later this year. "It's going to be a touch-and-go winter," said Thomas A. Petrie of Petrie Parkman & Co., a Denver investment firm specializing in energy. "We could still see some pretty tight conditions in November and December in some fuels."

It's true that supplies of some petroleum products -- including kerosene, jet fuel and naphtha, a petrochemical feedstock -- already are tight in some areas, particularly in regions of the Persian Gulf and Asia. "This reflects the loss of Kuwait refining capacity and the added demands associated with the military buildup in the Gulf," Cambridge Energy Research Associates reported Friday.

But crude oil supplies are ample everywhere even though some refiners worry that some oil replacing the lost Iraqi petroleum is of lower quality. In the U.S., according to the latest count by the Department of Energy, crude oil inventories total 373.1 million barrels, 35 million above the year-earlier level. And in Europe, according to Energy Security Analysis Inc., crude oil inventories are 25 million barrels higher than they were a year ago.

Energy Security Analysis is a Washington-based firm that monitors oil supplies world-wide. It estimates that at the end of August commercial onshore inventories of crude oil and chief petroleum products totaled 2.93 billion barrels. That was 87 million barrels above the year-earlier level. If government stockpiles are included, total global inventories were 113 million barrels higher than a year ago.

"We've seen no statistical evidence that inventories are being drawn down significantly," said Sarah Emerson, a vice president of Energy Security Analysis. Still, she said, the surplus is getting smaller just as demand increases seasonally. "No one is going to run out of crude," she said, "but in a month or two we could start having problems on products."

Oil markets appear to be expecting severe disruptions of supplies. Petroleum prices underscore that sentiment. They continued their run-up last week, reaching a nine-year high of more than $35 a barrel.

On the New York Mercantile Exchange, the futures price of crude oil for November delivery settled Friday at $35.43 a barrel -- up $2.01 a barrel for the day and $4.27 a barrel for the week. Gasoline and heating oil futures also continued to show sizzling gains, each advancing toward $1 a gallon.

U.S. and European energy officials foresee some problems in the distribution of petroleum products, as is happening already in Asia. Still, many are concerned that the potential for a supply crunch exists, even if war doesn't break out in the Middle East, because the global systems to produce, transport and refine oil have grown taut in the wake of the Persian Gulf standoff over Iraqi-occupied Kuwait."We are operating in a fragile environment," one official explained. "At every level, from the wellhead through the refinery, we are at capacity."

Still, energy representatives of oil-consuming nations have been pleasantly surprised at the rapid increases in production by Saudi Arabia and others. According to most estimates, the kingdom already is producing the additional two million barrels a day it had pledged, putting its current output at between 7.3 million and 7.5 million barrels. And it is understood to be pushing to expand its output to eight or 8.5 million barrels a day.

Actual "supplies" from OPEC -- exports of oil plus the volumes refined and used domestically -- exceed production at the moment. That's because Saudi Arabia and others are selling oil from storage as well as from current production. Petro-Logistics Ltd. of Geneva monitors both the volumes refined and exported by OPEC and estimates that such supplies currently total 22.2 million barrels a day.

But surveys of oil in tankers at sea by Petro-Logistics indicate that so-called producer-owned stocks afloat are beginning to shrink.

Further evidence of the declines in oil at sea comes from tanker charterings, which dropped sharply in recent weeks. However, according to Robert Boslego, a Winchester, Mass., oil consultant who monitors such charterings, there has been "a significant rebound in tanker chartering fixtures since the depths reached in August."
