Commodities: CFTC Rules Crude Sold as 15-Day Brent Isn't Futures Contract

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The Commodity Futures Trading Commission, as expected, approved a "statutory interpretation" that crude oil sold under so-called 15-day Brent oil contracts isn't a futures contract as defined in the Commodity Exchange Act. The commission, by a 3-1 vote, declared that the contracts describe cash sales of commodities for deferred shipment or delivery. As such, these contracts are excluded from the CFTC's regulatory jurisdiction.

The issue arose in April, when U.S. District Court Judge William Connor in New York ruled in a case brought by Transnor (Bermuda) Ltd. that forward trading in Brent, a widely traded North Sea crude, should be regulated in the same way as a U.S. futures contract.

Judge Connor's opinion initially placed a cloud over the entire crude oil market. Non-U.S. participants feared entering into Brent contracts that might subsequently be held unenforceable. U.S. participants shied away from the market, believing they could be held in violation of the Commodity Exchange Act.

CFTC Chairman Wendy Gramm and Commissioners Kalo Hineman and William Albrecht voted to approve the statutory interpretation, and Commissioner Fowler West dissented.
