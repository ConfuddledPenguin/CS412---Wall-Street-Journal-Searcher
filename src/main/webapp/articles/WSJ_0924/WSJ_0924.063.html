Make Them Search Washington For Anything That Makes Sense

  • Author: Suein L. Hwang
  • Date: 09/24/90

Search the caves of Postojna, Yugoslavia, for the mysterious "Brilliant." Scour Malacca, Malaysia, for Wah Aik, the last remaining artisan of Chinese bound-foot shoes. Find Juliet's balcony in Verona, Italy. Welcome to the Humanrace, competitive division -- an international scavanger hunt that promises challenge, exotic locales and glamour.

Spurred on by a world-wide, elbows-out, $5,300-entry-fee race among 30 adventurers last year, backers of the event are thinking up ways to expand. There's talk of local scavenger hunts, with contestants battling it out on the freeways of Los Angeles or the subways of New York, ending in a race for the national and international title.

Jonathan Bassan, a Montecito, Calif., traveler, one-time importer, and real-estate developer, organized the event last year to "take businessmen and professionals otherwise primarily stuck in offices all day and open them up in new ways. How you get there is the most significant thing."

But will it be any fun? Judging by last year's results, and with a $20,000 first prize at stake, not necessarily.

Instead of an enjoyable romp on, say, the Orient Express, last year's teams zoomed through cities in pursuit of brass ear-cleaning spoons (Singapore) and receipts from Cafe della Grazie (Italy).

"It was a nightmare," laments Kay Finegan, who at 78 was the oldest Humanracer. In Jakarta, Indonesia, Ms. Finegan raced up a temple's 354 stairs in 104-degree heat to take a photo from its top.

Another racer endured a 12-hour ride to Luxor on a packed Egyptian bus complete with live chickens and recorded Islamic prayers.

On the other hand, Humanrace's top finishers found the competition thrilling. "It was a great challenge," says Andy Valvur, a 36-year-old stand-up comic and writer who ran up the stairs to the crown of the Statue of Liberty and back in seven minutes. But his team had a few problems. "Right after the race my girlfriend said I looked 10 years older," he concedes. "We probably didn't expect not to sleep; we didn't expect not to eat."

The quick pace leaves little time for sightseeing. "We did Paris in 3 1/2 hours. We were in the Louvre for eight minutes. We did the Champs Elysees, Arc de Triomphe, Eiffel Tower, Notre Dame, Ile Saint-Louis and bingo] We were out of there," says Mr. Valvur.
