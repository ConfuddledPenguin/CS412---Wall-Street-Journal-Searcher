Index Technology To Unveil Software For Mainframes

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 09/24/90

CAMBRIDGE, Mass. -- Index Technology Corp. will introduce tomorrow a product to help corporate software experts update old mainframe software programs, Richard Carpenter, chairman and chief executive officer, said. The product, XL/Recover, marks a move into a new area of the software-engineering field that Index helped pioneer. It will be available next week. Index, the nation's biggest maker of products for the computer-aided software-engineering market, known as CASE, has previously made software tools to improve the productivity of programmers writing new programs.

In an interview, Mr. Carpenter said the XL/Recover software program, which will sell for $9,800 and runs on IBM-compatible personal computers, "is a major thrust in our offerings for 1991." He declined to predict sales but said it will "probably account for 10% to 25% of revenue" in the future.

Last year, Index earned $2.3 million, or 50 cents a share, on revenue of $38 million. During this year, Index has continued to grow rapidly, but earnings have declined. First-half net was $961,000, or 20 cents a share, down 21% from $1.2 million, or 27 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 39% to $24.4 million from $17.5 million.

Mr. Carpenter declined to predict earnings for the current quarter or the rest of the year. "This is continuing to be an investment year for Index. The good thing is we're beginning to get the results of all that investment into the marketplace," he said.

The company's new product addresses what should be a substantial market, Mr. Carpenter said. He added that in most companies, 60% to 80% of the software-development budget is spent adjusting old software programs to address new business conditions rather than designing new programs. For example, when a company eliminates a layer of bureaucracy in the credit-approval department, software has to be rewritten to make sure that bills don't still require approvals from those job titles.

XL/Recovery helps systems analysts look at the old COBOL software code that runs on most companies' mainframe computers. It can read the code, determine the logic and business rules used in the application, and draw charts of the structure of the code, making it easy for programmers to see how changing one part of the program will affect other parts.

Several other companies, including Cambridge-based Bachman Information Systems Inc., already make programs that automatically turn old COBOL "spaghetti code" that is extremely complex and hard for humans to follow into better-organized code. But Index said XL/Recovery goes beyond that by capturing information about both the data involved and the process by which it is manipulated.

John Dunkle, an analyst with WorkGroup Technologies Inc., Hampton, N.H., said, "Other CASE vendors are talking about this capability, but nobody is delivering it yet." He added that for Index to pioneer the technology is surprising because "usually the largest companies in any market are somewhat sluggish. The small companies usually tend to introduce technologies faster."

Mr. Carpenter said the company plans a major publicity campaign starting tomorrow. He said: "We're very eager to see how folks respond to it. We have to wait to see the market reception." He added that Index plans several other significant product announcements later this year, but he declined to comment on the products involved.
