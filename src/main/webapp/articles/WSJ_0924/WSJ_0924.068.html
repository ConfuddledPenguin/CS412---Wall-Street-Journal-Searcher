Nominee for SEC Supports CFTC On Index Futures

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 09/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Richard Roberts, President Bush's nominee to the Securities and Exchange Commission, voiced support for a plan that would leave regulation of controversial stock-index futures with the Commodity Futures Trading Commission. The plan, being floated on Capitol Hill, is an alternative to White House-sponsored legislation that would shift index futures to the SEC.

Stock-index futures, often used in computerized program-trading strategies, allow investors to bet on the move of a basket of stocks without owning the actual shares.

Mr. Roberts's stance, expressed at his confirmation hearing before the Senate Banking Committee, is significant because it would swing the balance on the SEC. Agency Chairman Richard Breeden strongly favors a jurisdiction shift, as does Commissioner Philip Lochner Jr. But Edward Fleischman has voted against any such power transfer, and Mary Schapiro has endorsed a third plan that would merge the two agencies.

The administration's plan, severely weakened by strong opposition from the Chicago futures exchanges and Capitol Hill Agriculture committees that oversee the CFTC, appears dead for this legislative session. Moreover, without solid backing from the SEC, it's unlikely Congress will press ahead with the plan and might focus exclusively on a compromise next year.

At the hearing, Mr. Roberts also offered support for another tenet of the Capitol Hill alternative -- a provision to shift the regulation of margins on stock-index futures to the Federal Reserve Board. Margins -- the good-faith money needed to trade the futures -- currently are set by the exchanges, with the CFTC allowed to intervene only in specifically defined emergencies. A similar switch is included in the administration's proposal.

Mr. Roberts also told the senators that he "intends to improve communications with the CFTC" and create a "better spirit of cooperation" between the futures agency and the SEC.

The committee is expected to approve his nomination this week, after which he isn't likely to have any difficulty passing the full Senate. Mr. Roberts, 39 years old, is a former top staffer for Sen. Richard Shelby (D., Ala.), a Banking Committee member who was one of only two senators to quiz Mr. Roberts at the hearing. The questioning was friendly and generally supportive of the conservative Democrat from both Sen. Shelby and committee Chairman Donald Riegle (D., Mich.).

Mr. Roberts wondered whether the SEC was doing enough to confront "front-running," which frequently involves trading in futures or stocks in front of a large order in the other market. He said he is concerned that the SEC isn't generating many of its own cases, instead relying on the exchanges to provide referrals. The SEC hasn't been able to bring front-running cases, and the agency contends that's because it can track only the stock side of the transaction and not the futures side.

Mr. Roberts focused largely on Wall Street-related problems in his testimony. Asked what his biggest enforcement priorities were, the nominee said they were "rooting out insider trading, stopping penny-stock fraud" and slowing fraudulent investment advisers.
