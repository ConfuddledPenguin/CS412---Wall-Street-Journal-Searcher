Auto Makers to Build 9.6% More Cars In 4th Quarter Than Weak Year Earlier

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 09/24/90

DETROIT -- U.S. auto makers plan to build just 9.6% more cars in the fourth quarter than they did in a weak year-earlier period, but analysts say those schedules won't hold if the economy continues to weaken. The 10 U.S. auto makers plan to build 1,741,955 cars in the fourth quarter, up from production of 1,589,215 in 1989's final quarter, according to industry sources. But the latest output plans are still the second-lowest since 1982, when a recession-wracked industry built just 1.2 million cars. Some analysts say auto makers will have to trim their fourth-quarter plans or face bloated inventories at year end.

Meanwhile, there are signs that higher fuel prices are prompting some Americans to buy smaller cars. That bodes well for Japanese auto makers who are boosting their U.S. output, and also for new small cars from Ford Motor Co. and General Motors Corp. GM's new Saturn division will launch its subcompact models late next month, and Ford is stepping up efforts to spur sales of its 1991 Escort subcompacts.

But sales of some of Detroit's larger models are starting to suffer. GM, blaming sluggish sales, said last week it will idle the plant that builds the big, V-8 powered Chevrolet Caprice until Oct. 8, and operate just one production shift for the following four weeks. GM had a 96-days' supply of the cars at the end of August, nearly 50% above the ideal level.

The slowdown in sales of the newly redesigned Caprice reflects a shift toward smaller cars in recent weeks. In August, compact cars accounted for 24.7% of all cars sold in the U.S., up from 23.3% a year earlier. Meanwhile, full-sized cars such as the Caprice saw their share of the market drop to 6.2% from 7.0% in August 1989, according to the industry trade publication Ward's Automotive Reports.

But analysts warned the auto industry could be headed for a broad slowdown as higher gas prices and uncertainty over the Persian Gulf buffet an already shaky U.S. economy.

"Consumers have led this economy into and out of recession," said John Casesa, an auto industry analyst with Wertheim Schroeder in New York. "And it looks to me like right now they're leading us into one."

If U.S. auto makers are to maintain their current production schedules without boosting inventories, cars will have to sell at a rate of seven million units in the quarter, says John Qualls, an analyst with Hill & Knowlton Inc. in St. Louis. That's roughly the pace sales have averaged since early July. But Mr. Qualls said more gloomy economic news could depress sales below that level.

Nonetheless, Japanese auto makers still plan to make gains, largely at the expense of the Big Three. Honda Motor Corp. is planning to build 26% more cars in the fourth quarter than a year ago, reflecting increased production from a new plant in East Liberty, Ohio. Honda also is planning to boost by around 30,000 cars the number of Civic sedans it imports to make up for production lost during the startup of the East Liberty factory.

As the third quarter closed, Detroit continued to shave its original output plans for the period. Ford last week said it would idle two of its U.S. car plants and one truck plant this week because of sluggish sales. In all, Ford has cut 45,000 cars out of its original third-quarter production schedules. The company now plans to build 311,199 cars in the July to September period, down 8.1% from a year ago. GM, meanwhile, cut about 20,000 cars out of its original third-quarter production schedule within the last month.
