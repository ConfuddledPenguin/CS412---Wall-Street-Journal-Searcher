Abreast of the Market: Analysts' Profit Estimates Grow Gloomier

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 09/24/90

NEW YORK -- Analysts, glimpsing how even the first shock waves from surging oil prices are souring consumer sentiment and crimping spending, are forging new corporate earnings estimates -- this time with a recession in mind. Since Aug. 15, 75% of all revisions on estimated 1991 earnings have been decreases, according to Zacks Investment Research, Chicago, which tracks some 4,000 companies.

"This is the worst set of readings since the last recession in 1981," says Benjamin Zacks, executive vice president of the company. "These numbers are getting very, very bleak."

And so are the market's reactions. Even minor adjustments in earnings forecasts are triggering torrents of sell orders. On Friday, for example, Scott Paper fell 17% after company executives told investors that third-quarter and full-year profit may fall below year-earlier results.

Analysts have even reversed their optimism in formerly bullish groups as recessionary trends become more apparent. From June 15 through Aug. 15, analysts raised 27 estimates on semiconductor companies and lowered 11, Mr. Zacks says. But in the past month, only five estimates were raised while 25 were lowered.

Reinforcing the "safe growth" theme, estimate revisions have gone up for hospital management companies, such as Humana, U.S. HealthCare and United Healthcare, Mr. Zacks says, as well as for drug makers and mining concerns. And in general, big makers of nondurable goods for consumers with dependable earnings growth, such as Warner-Lambert, Philip Morris, Johnson & Johnson and Procter & Gamble "are holding to slight earnings upgrades," Mr. Zacks adds.

Those are the few stocks that investors are holding onto as they run for cover, shares of companies in which they retain any confidence that profit projections will be fulfilled. Stocks that aren't as well known by investors and aren't as closely followed by Wall Street analysts are being hammered hard as safety-first investors dump them.

This pattern shows up in the market barometers. So far this year, on a total return basis, which includes both price change and reinvested dividends, the Dow Jones Industrial Average is down 6.16%, and Standard & Poor's 500-stock index is off 9.61%. But the rest of the market, as measured by the Wilshire 5000 index minus the 500 S&P stocks, has sunk 16.51%.

This isn't to say big brand-name stocks are escaping unscathed from this round of second-guessing earnings strength. This month, through last Thursday, the S&P 500 has fared a bit worse than the remaining stocks in the Wilshire 5000: On a total return basis the S&P 500 fell 3.24%, compared with a 3.07% decline in the Wilshire remainder.

"You have total capitulation -- no tolerance to own stocks with a question regarding the fundamentals," says James Awad, president of BMI Capital Corp., a money management company. This shooting down of stock by stock has occurred on such a scale that the overall damage "is as bad as if there had been an aggregate cleanout in the market, as in October 1987," he says.

Even the year's all-stars aren't being spared from the rash of earnings revisions.

Claire's Stores shares, which gained more than 34% in the second quarter, dropped 25% last week. On Wednesday, Alex. Brown & Sons analyst Chris Vroom trimmed his profit estimate for the women's specialty retailer's current fiscal year by no more than 10 cents a share to $1.20.

Sell-offs in stocks such as Scott Paper means money is shifting from those shares to either "safe growth" issues or out of the stock market altogether.

But even the big blue chips may not withstand selling pressures in coming days, says Stefan Abrams, chief investment strategist at Kidder Peabody. Investors already are selling stocks of companies with earnings prospects that are relatively invulnerable to economic troubles, he says.

"You can see it in top tier growth issues -- Coke, Pepsi, and hospital and drug stocks," Mr. Abrams says, asserting that the market is "in the early stages of capitulation."

Robert Stovall, president of Stovall/Twenty-First Advisers, observes that hoarding blue chips is typical behavior early in a bear market. But if a sell-off accelerates to stampede proportions, the big, easily traded shares are the ones investors are likely to sell first.

Mr. Abrams of Kidder says the S&P 500 is about 12% above "fair value," which is based on the yield of the one-year Treasury bill. "The first reasonable expectation of a stopping point in the current decline is fair value," he says. That would be roughly 273 on the S&P 500, which closed Friday at 311.32, down 0.16; a comparable decline in the industrial average would bring the average to 2210.89 from Friday's finish at 2512.38, down 5.94. "The greatest vulnerability will be in the most liquid stocks -- what people sell in a capitulation phase," he says.

What could precipitate such a sell-off? "It could be event driven -- such as an outbreak of war. It could be the continuing sand-papering of the investment community by a crude oil price that keeps edging up," Mr. Abrams says.

Whatever happens, Mr. Abrams advises investors to sell stocks of any company with "questionable" profit prospects and hang on to cash, perhaps investing in "a short-term instrument of impeccable quality."

He adds: "It is no time to go foraging around for a few extra percentage points of yield."

Friday's Market Activity

The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 5.94 to 2512.38 because of rising oil prices, declining hopes for a quick budget accord in Washington, and bad news in the banking industry. The loss for the week was 51.73, or 2%.

Friday's loss would have been greater, were it not for program buying related to the "triple-witching" expirations of September stock-index futures and options, and options on individual stocks.

Program activity also was responsible for pushing volume to 201,050,000 shares on the New York Stock Exchange, the busiest since Aug. 23 when the industrial average hit its low for the year. Average volume the previous four days last week was 136.1 million shares.

Iraq's expulsion of diplomats from the U.S., Egypt and several other countries helped boost November crude-oil futures contracts $2.01 a barrel to $35.43. That price jump hurt stocks, as did the news that the chairman of the House Appropriations Committee would seek a postponement of automatic budget cuts, which take effect next week if no deficit-cutting budget accord is reached by then.

Also weighing on the market was a broad sell-off in bank stocks in the wake of Chase Manhattan announcing sweeping restructuring moves. Shares of Chase fell 1 to 12 3/4 on Big Board volume of 2.6 million shares. The company set a third-quarter restructuring charge at $350 million and a loan-loss provision at $650 million, and said it plans to cut the dividend to 30 cents a share from 62 cents.

Among other money-center bank issues: Citicorp dropped 7/8 to 15 3/4; Manufacturers Hanover tumbled 2 7/8 to 23; Bankers Trust New York fell 1 3/4 to 34 1/4; BankAmerica slid 1/2 to 20 1/4, and J.P. Morgan fell 3/4 to 32 1/8.

But General Electric, which had lost 9% earlier this week amid concerns about possible problems at its financial-services unit, gained 3/4 to 55 7/8. While some critics say the company's profit growth will slow because of the prospects for the unit, GE Capital, this newspaper reported that analysts remain positive on the stock.

Scott Paper plunged 6 7/8 to 34 3/8 after the company said operating earnings for the third quarter and full year could fall short of year-earlier levels. Shares of Kimberly-Clark, a competitor in the paper-products business, fell 2 1/8 to 72 3/4.

Four utility issues that Smith Barney, Harris Upham cited as havens amid the market's current jitters moved higher. PacifiCorp rose 7/8 to 19 1/2; Public Service Enterprise Group added 1/2 to 23 1/4; American Electric Power rose 3/8 to 26 1/2, and Dominion Resources gained 1/4 to 42 7/8.

Hilton Hotels advanced 7/8 to 30 1/4. The company said it plans to buy back six million common shares, or about 12.5% of its shares outstanding.

Primerica added 1/8 to 20 3/4. The company's board approved the buy-back of as many as three million common shares.

Milton Roy jumped 3 3/4 to 26 3/4 after reaching a definitive merger agreement with Sundstrand, which is offering $28 a share for the company. Corroon & Black dropped 1 5/8 to 29 1/4.

Aon withdrew its $40-a-share takeover bid for the company, which had rejected the offer in favor of pursuing a stock-swap merger with Britain's Willis Faber.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.17 to 316.01, extending its loss for the week to 1.7%.

Howtek fell 3/4 to 7 1/2. Business Week magazine reported Dean Witter Reynolds, which controls about 20% of the company, is battling short sellers that collectively own more than one million shares, or a 13% stake.

Shares of Nichols Institute, which estimated that third-quarter earnings could be 10% below analysts' highest forecasts, tumbled 1 7/8 to 12 3/4.
