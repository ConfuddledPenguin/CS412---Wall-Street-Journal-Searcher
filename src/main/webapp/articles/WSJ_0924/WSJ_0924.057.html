Law: Legal Arguments Focus on Contents Of Memo on Milken

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 09/24/90

Michael Milken is scheduled to be sentenced for his crimes in a week and the battle over what the government may tell the public about the former junk bond king -- and when -- is well under way. A federal judge is expected to hear arguments today from lawyers for Mr. Milken, his brother, Lowell Milken, several media organizations and others about a sentencing memorandum that the government has filed with the court about Mr. Milken. The sentencing memorandum, filed under seal this month, contains information about the six securities fraud-related crimes to which Mr. Milken pleaded guilty in April, as well as a host of other alleged crimes that the government believes he committed.

The government's sentencing memo, which is understood to total more than 200 pages, is intended to persuade U.S. District Judge Kimba Wood to issue a stiff sentence when Mr. Milken appears before her next Monday. Mr. Milken's lawyers have had the opportunity to rebut the government's allegations in their own memo.

Lawyers for Lowell Milken asked Judge Wood late Friday to order prosecutors to delete those portions of their sentencing memo that refer to Lowell Milken. Lowell Milken was employed by the Beverly Hills, Calif., junk bond department that his brother headed for Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. Drexel filed for bankruptcy this year.

Lowell Milken had been charged with securities and tax fraud in an indictment filed last year. But the government agreed to dismiss all charges against Lowell Milken when his brother agreed to plead guilty. His lawyers said that "the government has pursued a meritless case against Lowell Milken," and that any mention of him in his brother's sentencing memo would violate his Fifth Amendment rights. The Fifth Amendment of the U.S. Constitution forbids the government from publicly charging a person with the commission of crimes unless he has the opportunity to defend himself at trial.

Meanwhile, Judge Wood also will decide whether any portion of the sentencing memo can be released prior to Mr. Milken's sentencing. Several media organizations, including this newspaper, have argued that the public has a right to view the memo before Mr. Milken is sentenced. However, Mr. Milken's attorneys oppose any release of the memo before next week, arguing that early release could lead to "undue public clamor and improper pressure" on the court.
