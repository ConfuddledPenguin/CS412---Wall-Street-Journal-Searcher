Stuart Hall Co. Says Profit Increased 50% In Fiscal 3rd Quarter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/24/90

KANSAS CITY, Mo. -- Stuart Hall Co.'s growing distribution base and "an exceptionally good" back-to-school season contributed to a boost in earnings for the fiscal third quarter, ended Aug. 31, Charles G. Hanson, chairman and chief executive officer, said. Mr. Hanson said net income for the office- and school-products company was "50% over last year's" third-quarter earnings of $544,000, or 14 cents a share. But the comparison benefits from a $188,000 net charge, from inventory write-down, that substantially trimmed year-earlier results. The 1989 quarter also was hurt by an unusually large $289,379 pretax bad-debt provision.

Per-share figures reflect a 4-for-3 stock split paid in December 1989.

Total sales for the quarter are expected to be $40.4 million, a 28% increase from $31.6 million for the year-earlier period. Mr. Hanson said he expects continued earnings growth of 30% to 40% next year.

"We are adding constantly to our distribution base. We estimate that some 46,000 outlets now carry a pretty broad base of our product," Mr. Hanson said in an interview.

Upgraded office-supply products, which he called "the backbone" of Stuart Hall's office product lines, also added to increased sales, but earnings growth was stunted nonetheless by the effects of a "depressed pricing structure throughout the industry," Mr. Hanson said.

"This year was probably the worst for pricing structure that I can recall," he said. "Had it not been for that we would have done substantially better."

Mr. Hanson said he expects to benefit from some "very fine equipment" purchased from a German-based paper-supply company that closed its American operation. The equipment purchased from Herlitz Co. will be placed in Stuart Hall's Kansas City headquarters, its Bessemer, Ala., facility, and in a new Woodland, Calif., facility scheduled to open in December, he said.

Mr. Hanson said the company will release within the next 30 to 45 days a new line of about 50 products made from 100% recycled paper products.

People are "caught up in the recycling movement but are not willing to pay more for a recycled sheet of paper," he said. "We are delighted" with the new recycled product, he added, because "the consumer will have an opportunity to buy a 100% recycled product at the same price he's spending for the product now."

Mr. Hanson wouldn't project how much the new products might affect earnings, but he said "it will have a favorable impact on our bottom line. It will be a major introduction, the largest thing that's happened to us in 1990."
