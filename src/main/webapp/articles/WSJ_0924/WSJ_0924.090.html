Who's News: Stanford Fires S&L Expert Following TV Controversy

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/24/90

STANFORD, Calif. -- An economist who recently incensed the banking establishment when he called the federal bank-insurance fund insolvent on national television has been fired from his post as an unpaid research scholar at Stanford University. R. Dan Brumbaugh, a thrift expert who was among the first to predict that industry's collapse, said yesterday that Stanford's director of the Center for Economic Policy Research notified him that the university was canceling his research project on banking in a letter dated Sept. 11.

Mr. Brumbaugh said he believes that, while the center's director has denied it, the termination is the result of his feisty words on ABC News' "Nightline" on July 31. On that news program, Mr. Brumbaugh said that six out of the 10 biggest banks were at or near insolvency, and the cost to close them and hundreds of other failed thrifts was going to outstrip the reserves of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., which insures bank deposits up to $100,000.

His remarks drew a firestorm of criticism and denials from bankers and federal regulators. FDIC Chairman William Seidman, who also appeared on the program, denounced the economists' predictions as "irresponsible" and "preposterous."

The center's director, John Shoven, did not return calls to his home yesterday, but he earlier denied in the press that outside pressure played any role in the ousting of Mr. Brumbaugh.
