Rare-Coin Dealers Call Summit Session On Self-Regulation --- Industry Said to Be in `State of Panic' Government Will Move to Regulate It

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 09/24/90

NEW YORK -- Rare-coin dealers want the government to know: We can police ourselves. Following a crackdown on rare-coin fraud by federal and state regulators, leaders of the coin business have called an unusual "summit" to launch the industry's first self-regulatory organization.

"The industry is in a state of panic that the federal government will regulate it," says Bruce Kaplan, president of Kaplan & Co., a Santa Monica, Calif., company that advises coin dealers. "They're scrambling to come up with a regulatory program that the {government} will tacitly approve," he says.

The planned summit follows last month's crackdown by the Federal Trade Commission on Professional Coin Grading Service Inc. -- which heralded itself as making the coin business safer for investors. The FTC accused the grading service of making false and misleading claims. Professional Coin settled the charges without admitting or denying them.

The summit, to be held early next month in Long Beach, Calif., is aimed at setting up a self-regulatory body that will establish safeguards for rare-coin customers and ethical standards for coin dealers, grading services, auction houses and other parts of the $5 billion-a-year coin industry.

There's been scattered talk in the past among coin dealers of a self-policing body. But the industry believes it has to act now because of the negative publicity generated by the recent FTC action and other FTC lawsuits against coin dealers.

"We better do something to get a positive image going, or else we're going to turn back into a little cottage industry and never have the chance to be a real financial market," says Frederic Tordella, president of MTB Banking Corp., a large New York coin dealer.

Besides trying to mollify regulators with its self-policing plan, the rare-coin industry wants to reassure a skittish Wall Street. Securities firms -- which have been moving into the coin business by selling rare-coin funds and partnerships -- now are worried about the regulatory pressure on the coin business.

"We want to make our industry more attractive to more traditional investors, particularly Wall Street," says Barry Stuppler, co-chairman of the summit organizing committee.
