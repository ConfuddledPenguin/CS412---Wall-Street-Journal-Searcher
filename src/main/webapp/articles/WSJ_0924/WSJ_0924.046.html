France's Military Shipbuilders Trying To Cut Bureaucracy --- Their Goal Is to Establish Commercial Export Unit For Flexibility and Profit

  • Author: E.S. Browning
  • Date: 09/24/90

PARIS -- Following in the army's footsteps, the French government's military shipbuilding bureaucracy is trying to become just a little less governmental, by setting up a commercial export subsidiary. It is all part of a push by arms makers world-wide to boost sales and improve sagging financial performance. It also reflects a growing effort by French state agencies to begin behaving more like companies.

Earlier this year, the French government's main maker of munitions, cannons, tanks and armored vehicles shortened its long, complicated name to Giat Industries. It ceased to be a state agency and became a corporation -- albeit still government-owned. It immediately announced plans to stem losses, expand slumping exports, and lay off employees.

The shipbuilders' goal is more modest. They don't want to become a corporation or lay anyone off. They just want the right to create a commercially registered sales subsidiary called DCNI, short for Direction des Constructions Navales-Industrie. Then they want to begin an export drive.

Until now, the DCN says, it has viewed its main job simply as supplying the French navy. It designs ships and oversees plants that build them.

It has been treated as another government bureaucracy, and had to go through a cumbersome process to make an export sale. The result has been poor export performance. The DCN won't say exactly how much of its 20 billion francs ($3.79 billion) in sales last year were exports, but says exports were well below Giat's figure of 10% of total production.

A spokesman says the DCN hopes the Finance Ministry will approve the new company within a few weeks. First, the DCN needs to calm official worries that the new company would mark a step away from full government control.

The DCN's idea is similar to Giat's: Boost efficiency and build cooperation with non-government companies, both French and foreign. And while the DCN doesn't calculate profits and losses, it would want its sales subsidiary to show earnings.

"Currently," the DCN official said, "we don't have the right to form joint ventures or to handle our own export sales. We must build and design ships and subs ourselves, and when we want to sell abroad, we have to work through one of the state export agencies. We are hoping to get more flexibility."

The official said the DCN has been lobbying for more than two years to create the company. The changes at Giat have made the change seem more urgent.

Giat Chairman Pierre Chiquet said in April that he hopes to cut staff more than 10% by 1992. He said he hopes to end losses, now 10% of sales, by 1994. And this month, he said Giat has agreed to cooperate with a division of General Electric Co. of Fairfield, Conn., (unrelated to the British company of the same name), to develop and make a cannon turret for light helicopters, which they hope to sell to the U.S. Army.
