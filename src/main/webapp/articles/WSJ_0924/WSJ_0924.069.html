Soviet Union's Chief Accountant Wants To Set Up Group Resembling U.S. CPAs

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 09/24/90

NEW YORK -- The Soviet Union's chief accountant expects to issue new standards for all enterprises by June, and he wants to set up a professional group resembling the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants. At a meeting organized by Ernst & Young here, Nicholai Panteleev, chief accountant for the Soviet Ministry of Finance, also said that by early next year he hopes to make some other rules changes regarding accounting for joint ventures. Mr. Panteleev heads the accounting rule-making body in the Soviet Union.

For the most part, Soviet accounting is predicated on a centralized form of government. The Soviet accounting system "now resembles an army code," Mr. Panteleev said. He said he would like to make the system "more democratic," and he wants to pass some of the functions that the government handles to an outside group.

With the current system, "if it directs to turn right, one can't possibly turn left," he said. The current accounting model was designed "strictly to forward the plans and direction of the government," Mr. Panteleev said. Current standards "are largely used for economic statistics as opposed to financial reporting," said James Searing, national director of Ernst & Young's international business services.

The government only recently allowed joint ventures to record revenue on an accrual rather than a cash basis. In the past, an enterprise would have to actually receive a cash payment before it could record a sale. Although Mr. Panteleev didn't specify what the joint-venture changes might be, one might be to also move expense accounting to accrual from a cash basis.

Mr. Panteleev said his goal is to bring Soviet accounting rules closer to internationally accepted standards. But it is difficult to envision a Soviet accounting system until questions about how a free-market economy will work are answered. And those questions, Mr. Panteleev said, are best left to the politicians.

Mr. Panteleev said the biggest challenge seems to be to establish a system of auditing and review, such as the one that operates in the U.S. The concept of auditing didn't exist in the Soviet Union until recently.

"Under new market conditions, there will be a great necessity to have an independent body to exercise control," Mr. Panteleev said. "The issue of accounting management is connected directly with the issue of any market standard," he said.

Mr. Panteleev visited the Securities and Exchange Commission and apparently was impressed. The SEC has statutory authority to establish accounting and reporting standards for public companies, though it has largely left that role to the private sector. The SEC also enforces the rules.

Mr. Panteleev said he would like to create a private organization similar to the AICPA for licensing. But he didn't say when that might happen.

Other issues involve developing standards that are more similar to those the rest of the world uses. A task force of the United Nations Centre on Transnational Corporations has been advising the Soviet Union on how to accomplish this goal, and it has provided expertise on international accounting standards.

Mr. Panteleev also sees re-educating professionals as a major goal. The Soviet Union has about two million bookkeepers and about 300,000 in-house chief accountants. "They have to move from a bookkeeping culture to an accounting profession," said Mr. Searing of Ernst & Young.
