Ford Canada Workers Ratify Contract Providing Gains in Wages and Benefits

  • Author: Lynne Kilpatrick
  • Date: 09/24/90

TORONTO -- Striking workers at Ford Motor Co.'s Canadian subsidiary yesterday voted overwhelmingly in favor of a generous wage and benefit package that the Canadian unit of cash-strapped Chrysler Corp. may find difficult to match. The agreement, reached after a marathon bargaining session Friday, met the Canadian Auto Workers' major demands. The 12,800 Ford Motor Co. of Canada workers were expected to return to work this morning, ending the week-long strike.

The agreement includes percentage wage increases in each year of the three-year contract, improved cost-of-living protection, added time off and a broad income-security program.

Wages will increase by 7.5% in the first year, 6.7% in the second year and 4.8% in the third. The increases include estimated cost-of-living allowances of 4.5%, 4.7% and 2.8%, respectively, designed to provide 90% protection against inflation.

Ford Canada also has established a 100 million Canadian dollar (US$86.6 million) fund to finance the generous early retirement, severance pay and income supplement programs agreed to in the contract.

Union President Robert White called the agreement "the best worker income-security program" in the industry and said he expects it to become a model for labor negotiations in other industries.

The agreement will be the basis for negotiations with the Canadian unit of General Motors Corp. and Chrysler Canada Ltd. The auto workers' contracts with the Canadian units of the Big Three auto makers expired Sept. 14. Ford Canada was the Canadian Auto Worker's target in the pattern-setting negotiations.

Mr. White said GM Canada probably will be the next company the union negotiates with. Nick Hall, a GM Canada spokesman, said the Ford contract looks expensive, but he declined to elaborate.

Chrysler Canada officials declined to comment on the Ford Canada contract, but analysts said the cash-strapped company would have difficulty affording such a settlement.

Chrysler is financially "very weak and there are questions of whether it could even withstand a strike," Toronto auto analyst Dennis DesRosiers said earlier in the Ford negotiations.

In separate U.S. auto talks, Chrysler already has warned that it may not be able to match the settlement the United Auto Workers reached last week with General Motors.

Ford Canada's chief negotiator, Donald McKenzie, declined to say how much the agreement will cost the company, but called it "a handsome settlement" as far as workers are concerned.

The contract requires that Ford provide one-year's notice in the event of a plant closing and six months' notice of job losses due to technological change or outsourcing -- the buying of products from outside sources, rather than producing them inhouse.

When jobs are lost for these reasons, the contract provides for early retirement at full pension for older workers with at least 10 years of service. Depending on length of service, laid-off workers also may receive 95% of their income for up to three years, or choose severance payments of up to C$65,000.

The union said the compensation plan is designed to encourage older workers to leave the work force ahead of younger ones.

Also in the new settlement is an annual C$500 vacation bonus for workers, and workers will also get three additional four-day weekends a year. Mr. White said the extra time off, a key issue in the talks, is likely to have an influence on the auto talks in the U.S.

Ford Canada also agreed to create a "social justice fund" to make contributions to such things as international development and relief projects and food banks. The company will contribute one cent per hour worked to the fund, up to C$250,000. It will also contribute to the Canadian Auto Workers' child care program.
