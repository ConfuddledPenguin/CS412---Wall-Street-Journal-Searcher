International: Lloyds Is Said to Be Seeking the Sale Of Banking Line in Asia-Pacific Region

  • Author: Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 09/24/90

LONDON -- Continuing to pare marginal foreign operations as it comes under pressure at home, Lloyds Bank PLC has put its Asian banking operations on the block, according to people familiar with the decision. These people said Lloyds has retained the investment banker Morgan Stanley & Co. in London to sell its four commercial-retail branch offices in Asia, and its Australian and New Zealand bank units. The four branches are in Tokyo, Hong Kong, Singapore and Seoul.

A Lloyds spokesman said that he had heard "market speculation" the operations were for sale, but that the bank, one of Britain's four largest, "has a standard rule about not commenting on market speculation." Morgan Stanley declined to comment.

Lloyds generally doesn't break out overseas results by country, but all international operations in the first half earned #71 million ($133.1 million) before taxes, double the year-earlier performance, and accounted for 17% of the bank's pretax earnings. But earnings of its National Bank of New Zealand unit, included in the overall international figure, were flat at #17 million.

Lloyds has divested itself of a number of peripheral units in recent months. The spokesman said this was "the general strategy of the bank. Where we aren't a strong player and aren't getting a reasonable return on equity, we have moved out." He declined to say whether this strategy applied to Lloyds's Asian operations.

The bank's international operations were built with an eye toward making Lloyds a world banking power. But like many other major U.S. and British banks with similar dreams, Lloyds was blindsided in the 1980s by the Third World debt crisis.

Though it has shored up that debt exposure with massive reserves, Lloyds, along with its British counterparts, has found another specter looming. Loans to British businesses, mainly in construction and real estate, have become increasingly risky, as high interest rates have hobbled building and accelerated business failures in several sectors.

In the first half, Lloyds more than tripled its domestic loan charges to #190 million from #60 million. The bank's first-half pretax profit of #408 million, though more than quadruple the year-earlier #93 million, disappointed many investors, mainly because of the riskier domestic loan exposure. Lloyds shares closed Friday at 235 pence, down 2 pence, equaling its 1990 low. The shares have traded as high as 316 pence this year.

In June, Lloyds sold its Portuguese operations to Madrid-based Banco Bilbao Vizcaya for an undisclosed sum. In February, it sold Lloyds Bank Canada to Hongkong & Shanghai Banking Corp. for 190 million Canadian dollars (US$164.5 million). That same month, it sold its stake in Yorkshire Bank PLC to National Bank of Australia. And last September, it sold its U.S. commercial banking unit to Daiwa Bank of Japan for $200 million.

The sales helped produce a #94 million extraordinary gain in the first half. Most of the gain came from the sale of the Yorkshire Bank stake, though the U.S. and Canadian sales contributed a total of #11 million.
