U.S. Is Removed As the Proprietor Of Nevada Brothel --- Refusal by Judge to Allow Trustee to Run Business Clears Way for IRS Sale

  • Author: Susan C. Faludi
  • Date: 09/24/90

RENO, Nev. -- It seems the federal government won't be operating one of the nation's most celebrated brothels after all: On Friday, a judge lifted the stay order that would have put a bankruptcy trustee in charge of running The Mustang Ranch. But the federal government will still ultimately be calling the shots here; by lifting the stay, the judge freed the Internal Revenue Service to put the brothel on the selling block.

An IRS official said the agency planned to seize the brothel as soon as possible and put it up for sale at a public auction, probably within about 30 days.

Mustang Ranch shut down last Tuesday, following a bankruptcy liquidation order. By the following day, the bankruptcy trustee was trying to reopen the ranch under federal management, with plans to run it until the government was able to find a buyer for the brothel.

At the hearing Friday, U.S. Bankruptcy Judge James Thompson refused to grant the bankruptcy trustee the clearance she needed to operate the brothel. The trustee, Jeri Coppa, told the judge that the county wouldn't allow her to run the brothel without court permission to assume the current owner's license. Getting her own license would take another 90 days.

But the judge declined, ruling that the seven-year battle between the brothel and the IRS, the brothel's largest creditor, had gone on long enough.

Even so, the brothel's owners -- Joe and Sally Conforte -- are hoping for a reprieve. "They can't foreclose yet," said Pete Perry, the Confortes' attorney, because earlier last week he filed yet another petition for bankruptcy protection under Chapter 11 -- this time using Mr. Conforte's name. (The last one was filed by Mrs. Conforte.) To foreclose, the federal government will have to petition the court yet again. "We will be filing all kinds of litigation," Mr. Perry added. "This is an attorney's field day."

Ron Smith, chief of special procedures for the IRS in Nevada, said the IRS will seek a "clarification" from the judge on whether Mr. Conforte's latest Chapter 11 filing can block liquidation. He said that while it may postpone the sale for a while, it is unlikely to prevent it.

Already, several prospective buyers have called the IRS office, Mr. Smith said, but "no one's mentioned money at this point."

In the last few years, the Confortes have attempted several times, unsuccessfully, to sell Mustang Ranch. They complain that the sales fell through because of IRS interference.

The bankruptcy trustee has maintained that the Confortes were generating interference themselves, by trying to thwart the government's efforts to operate the 105-room brothel.

The IRS says Mustang Ranch's owners owe $13 million in back taxes; the Confortes say they own less than half that amount.

"One of the real tragedies of the thing is that 200 people are going to be out of work because of this," Mr. Perry said. And the local economy will be out "millions of dollars," he said.

Mustang Ranch first opened for business in the late 1950s, when prostitution was illegal -- but winked at by Nevada officials. It was legalized by the county in 1971.
