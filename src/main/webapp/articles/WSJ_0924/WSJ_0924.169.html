Marketing: Cellular Phone Concerns Step Up Effort To Get Ordinary Consumers On the Line

  • Author: Julie Amparano Lopez
  • Date: 09/24/90

John and Jane Doe need cellular phones. The idea just hasn't rung a bell with them yet. But most mobile phone companies are hoping it will. They're looking for ordinary consumers as they aim to expand their market beyond business and professional customers. The strategies range from cuts in monthly charges to photos making comparisons to microwave ovens and TV ads featuring storms and smashups.

Companies yearning for Ordinary Joes offer free emergency road service and free long-distance calling on weekends. Others try to bait the hook with cheap equipment rentals.

Of the 4.4 million Americans with mobile phones, the vast majority use them for business. Last year, they laid out nearly $2 billion on cellular calls. This year, the revenue volume is expected to reach $3.2 billion. (For the average subscriber, that translates into a monthly bill of about $89.)

But a recent survey suggests how quickly the cellular landscape could change. Conducted by Yankelovich Clancy Shulman for Nynex Corp., the survey covered consumers with income of more than $25,000 a year; it found that one in every four, or about 27 million, will probably buy a cellular phone in the next five years. About six million of those expect to purchase one in the next 12 months.

But costs have kept lots of people off the cellular bandwagon. Drivers who would need a cellular phone only for an occasional quick call have little in common with the businessman who makes calls almost at will and passes the tab along to his employer.

Cellular phone owners find their wallets being drained in inventive ways. There are high monthly charges and pricey phone sets. For low-volume corporate customers, the cost of a cellular telephone call during business hours ranges from 60 cents to 75 cents a minute. Off peak, a minute can cost 10 cents to 35 cents. But customers don't just get charged when they make a call; they also get billed for each call they receive. Furthermore, they must pay about a dime for every busy signal and every unanswered call.

"The consumer market is very difficult," concedes Bruce Amundson, an official of U S West Inc.'s NewVector Group unit. "We're still conducting research to figure out what type of person would be a consumer and how much they're willing to pay."

Frederick Moran, president of Moran Asset Management Inc., with responsibility for heavy investments in cellular companies, cautions that a real consumer boom won't happen until calling rates drop rather sharply. "Then you'll find everyone using car phones," he says, "and the cellular companies will be carrying enormous amounts of traffic."

Those days look just too far off to some players. Nonbusiness use, they say, still generates far too few phone calls and far too little revenue. "We do not view cellular phones as a consumer device, nor do we market them that way," says Don Harris, vice president of corporate development for Pacific Telesis Group's PacTel Cellular unit. "For the foreseeable future, we see it as a business productivity tool. Maybe 30 years down the road, it will be for consumers. But it isn't yet."

The enthusiasts aren't waiting.

Take Metrophone, a joint venture of McCaw Cellular Communications Inc. and Metromedia Inc. It recently slashed the basic monthly service charge for casual users by 33% to $9.95. Metrophone's monthly fees for heavy-use business customers can run as high as $155.

Most of the Baby Bells are jumping in, too. Their new ad campaigns and department-store distributors talk up the themes that consumers like to hear: affordability, security and convenience.

In 1984, when cellular phone sets cost about $3,000 apiece, only about 5% of Bell Atlantic Corp.'s subscribers were nonbusiness users. To appeal to more consumers, Bell Atlantic now offers about three hours of talk time a month for $14.95.

Ameritech's Mobile Communications unit is swaying consumers with phone rentals that are practically free. For $5 a month, consumer can have an installed cellular telephone with speaker phone and memory dialing. A portable unit costs only a few dollars more.

Some phone companies appeal to another popular consumer emotion: fear. McCaw, Ameritech, Nynex, U S West and others run ads that all show a car careening off the road during a severe storm. Such ads swayed Bruce McGrath, a salesman in New York, to buy a cellular phone for his wife. "I didn't want her to get stranded in the car in some godforsaken place," he says.

Cellular companies also are using department stores, electronics retailers and auto-supply shops to zoom in on consumers. "We're making cellular phones available in places where consumers like to shop," says Steve Lamont, corporate director of marketing at McCaw.

Southwestern Bell Corp., which recently created some ads that show a mom on the phone in the car with her kids, also has shifted some of its print budget off the sports and business pages to reach a wider audience.

In April, BellSouth Corp.'s BellSouth Mobility tested a "Security Plus" program in South Florida that, for $35 a month, gave new customers 20 minutes of airtime, free emergency road service and night rates of 10 cents a minute. The program boosted new-customer sales by about 30%, and BellSouth wants to integrate it into other markets. McCaw says it may introduce similar plans.

One BellSouth ad shows a cellular phone below a VCR and a microwave oven. The appliances, now in most U.S. households, once were considered "gadgets that few people would ever need and even fewer could ever afford," the ad text says.

The ad concludes: "You get the idea."
