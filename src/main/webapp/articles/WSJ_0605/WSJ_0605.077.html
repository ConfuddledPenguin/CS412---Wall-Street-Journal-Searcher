Who's News: Freiman Joins List Of Top Executives Leaving Shearson

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 06/05/90

NEW YORK -- Herbert S. Freiman, a vice chairman of Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc., is retiring later this year after 16 years at the securities firm and its predecessors, company officials said. The retirement of Mr. Freiman, who turned 60 years old a few months ago, is the latest in a line of senior-level departures from Shearson this year, although it apparently is unrelated to the firm's recent troubles.

Shearson, an American Express Co. unit, in recent months has announced the resignations or retirements of its chairman and chief executive officer, Peter Cohen; its president, Jeffrey Lane; its head of public-brokerage business, Hardwick Simmons; its chief administrative officer, Robert M. Raziano; the chairman of Boston Co., a money-management unit, George Phillips; the head of its Balcor Co. real-estate unit, Steven Silverstein; and Vice Chairman Joel Margolies, a former head of the domestic-branch network.

Mr. Freiman, Shearson's trading and sales chief in the early 1980s, had until late last year been head of Shearson's capital markets division, before a management shuffle announced by then-chairman Mr. Cohen, which gave the job to Richard S. Fuld Jr. Since the shuffle, Mr. Freiman has been chairman of Shearson's capital-commitment committees.

Mr. Freiman this year had also taken on responsibility for the money-management businesses, but that chore has since been assumed by Jonathan S. Linen. Mr. Linen is a former American Express official who joined Shearson this week to head the firm's brokerage and money-management businesses, as part of a planned reorganization of Shearson into separate brokerage and investment banking halves.

Howard L. Clark Jr., Shearson chairman since January, told employees in an internal note that Mr. Freiman will remain a Shearson adviser. Mr. Clark added that Mr. Freiman had provided him with "invaluable assistance," and that his "insights into an industry undergoing tremendous change have helped to shape much of my own thinking about the Wall Street of the 1990s."

Mr. Freiman, who generally kept a low profile during his years at Shearson, declined to comment on his retirement plans.
