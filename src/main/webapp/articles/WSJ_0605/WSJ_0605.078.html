Accounting Body Effectively Clears Certain New Fees

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 06/05/90

NEW YORK -- The major professional body for U.S. accountants took a position that effectively allows its members to accept contingent fees and commissions from non-audit clients. The governing council of the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants, although it hasn't changed the institute's rules of conduct, voted to enforce the proposed changes to the ethics rules agreed to by the institute in August 1988 in a consent order with the Federal Trade Commission.

Since agreeing to the FTC consent order, the institute has deferred enforcing its rules on contingent fees and commissions pending final approval of the consent order by the commissioners of the FTC. The agency gave preliminary approval to the order in March 1989, but has yet to give final approval. The institute has said it would automatically change its rules, which prohibit all commissions and most contingent fees, once the commission gives final approval of the proposed consent decree.

"There's a lot of confusion among the members" about the issue, said Donald J. Schneeman, general counsel for the AICPA. "We felt there were members who don't have an {auditing} relationship; and we wanted to remove that cloud" of uncertainty; the change would allow them to accept commissions and contingency fees from non-audit clients without fear of an enforcement action by the institute, Mr. Schneeman said.

By the same token, the institute "wanted to send a clear message" to those accountants who have an auditing relationship with clients that the AICPA would enforce that part of its ban on accepting such fees. Violation of the institute's ethics rules can result in a member's suspension from the institute.

The profession's rules, for example, had prevented an accountant from taking a commission for advising a client to buy specific computer hardware, or taking an audit or consulting job if the final fee is based on the benefit derived by the client.

A spokeswoman for the FTC said she couldn't comment on when the commissioners might approve a final order or whether they have considered it.

It isn't clear how many accountants will be affected by the changes. Although the AICPA has a membership of more than 296,000 CPAs in public practice, the institute noted that its rules don't override state law or the rules of state CPA societies. Five states, including Texas and Maryland, have revoked prohibitions against commissions, the institute said. Five other states, including California and Florida, have prohibited accountants from accepting the commissions. "State law would be paramount" wherever there is a discrepancy, Mr. Schneeman said.

The dispute over the contingent fees has been controversial. The institute had long resisted efforts by the FTC to eliminate the group's ban on the fees. The AICPA had argued that the ban against contingent fees helped accountants remain independent and objective.
