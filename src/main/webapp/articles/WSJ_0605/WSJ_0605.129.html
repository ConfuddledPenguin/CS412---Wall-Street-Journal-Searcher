What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/05/90

STOCK PRICES JUMPED to record levels on brisk volume as investors bought issues sensitive to the recent fall in interest rates. The Dow Jones Industrial Average climbed 34.22 points to 2935.19, while the Big Board composite index broke a record high set in October. Bonds rose modestly, and the dollar was mixed. ---

Willis Faber of Britain agreed to acquire New York-based Corroon & Black for $712.3 million in stock. The deal would create the world's fourth-largest insurance brokerage and could encourage more such unions.

---

Junk bonds issued by Donald Trump's Taj Mahal and two other casinos operated by him sank on news of the developer's cash crunch. The price drop is likely to lead bondholders to pressure Trump to ensure they get their interest payments.

---

The Supreme Court expanded states' duty to give retroactive relief to those who paid taxes later deemed unlawful. The case could create billions of dollars in state liability.

---

Greyhound Lines entered Chapter 11, as did several other units of GLI Holding. Citing a "run on assets" by creditors, the strike-bound bus firm said the filing will enable it to keep serving all locations on its network.

---

Non-farm productivity plunged at a 2.7% annual rate in the first quarter, the largest decline since 1981 and much bigger than a 1% drop initially estimated. At the same time, per-unit business costs rose at a 7% rate.

---

Citicorp is ending its underwriting and trading of municipal securities, in a further sign of Wall Street's woes.

---

Morgan Stanley named Joseph Fogg head of investment banking, in the firm's latest management shuffle.

---

American Express quietly stopped giving free car-rental insurance to its corporate card holders at big firms.

---

A Shearson vice chairman is retiring this year, in the latest high-level departure at the securities firm.

---

Soybean futures prices skidded as a break in the season's wet weather allowed some progress in planting.

---

Nuclear workers who claim they were fired for pointing out safety problems may sue their ex-employers for damages, the Supreme Court ruled.

---

Several Japanese banks, under Chinese pressure, shelved their plans to enter the Taiwan market, and Bank of Tokyo is rethinking its Taipei move.

---

The main body of CPAs moved to effectively allow its members to accept contingent fees and commissions from non-audit clients.

---

Sony will start shipping digital audio tape recorders to the U.S. this month. Other firms may follow, despite recording-industry opposition.

---
