Corporate Focus: Mattel Again Is Heading the Toys Parade --- Firm Trumpets Comeback, Led by a Blond Barbie

  • Author: Pauline Yoshihashi
  • Date: 06/05/90

HAWTHORNE, Calif. -- Even as the Berlin Wall crumbled, officials of Mattel Inc. rushed in to size up the Eastern European market. But they were faced with a problem: How to introduce Western-quality and Western-priced toys to a market used to playthings that were generally simple and drab, but cheap? The solution: Friendship Barbie, a basic blond version of Mattel's long-running star that will be sold at cost, about $6, to introduce millions of consumers to the joys of U.S.-style shopping.

And where Barbie leads, can Hot Wheels and Mickey Mouse toys be far behind? Mattel, given up for dead six years ago, has steadily fought its way back by eschewing volume for net income, coming up with endless variations of its longtime staples, and pushing internationalization almost as a religion.

As the toy industry consolidates and once-hot companies vanish like spent hula hoops, Mattel has rebounded to become one of the fastest-growing and most fashionable toy companies on Wall Street.

Recently, the company drove home the point by reinstating its dividend, announcing plans to pare debt and landing toy-licensing rights to News Corp.'s Fox Broadcasting Inc.'s hit television cartoon show "The Simpsons."

Moreover, Mattel's stock is riding high on the New York Stock Exchange and investors and analysts are looking for the company to outperform the toy industry. Yesterday, in Big Board composite trading Mattel closed at $25.875, up 62.5 cents; the stock's 52-week high is $26.375.

It was spindly-legged Barbie that helped haul Mattel back to profitability, ringing up $590 million in world-wide sales in 1989; the doll, along with other basic toy lines, accounted for more than 75% of Mattel's consolidated sales. Mattel posted net of $68.7 million on sales of $1.2 billion in 1989.

John W. Amerman, Mattel's chairman, president and chief executive officer, and his handpicked executives hawk Barbie and her associates as if the toys were chewing gum or scouring powder. That comes naturally to Mr. Amerman, a veteran of Colgate-Palmolive Co. and Warner Lambert Co. who became Mattel's chairman in 1987 after heading its international operations for seven years.

"The toy business is a seat-of-the-pants business," says Robert Sansone, who came in to head Mattel USA in 1987 after 24 years with General Foods Corp. "But most of what we're doing here is from Marketing 101."

Mattel crash-landed in 1983, shot down by years of unbridled diversification, sloppy management and a disastrous plunge into electronic toys. Under former chairman Arthur Spear, the company bailed out of electronics and entertainment, but not before posting a staggering $394 million loss for fiscal 1984. The company continued to drift, even as a flood of competitors' blockbusters took the market by storm.

Mattel also has had its share of trendy hits. Its He-Man and Masters of the Universe action figure and vehicle line, built around a Saturday morning cartoon series the company paid for, rocketed to $400 million in sales by 1985. But when the show's costs edged upward and viewership drifted down, Mattel didn't cut back supply, so the toys languished on retailers' shelves and profits evaporated. "Inventory is a killer in this business," Mr. Amerman says.

Chastened, Mattel has trotted out new versions of its muscle-bound heroes and villains again. But now, Mattel won't foot the bill for a new cartoon series scheduled for the fall, and inventory will be kept lean. Mr. Amerman says He-Man's domestic sales this year may double to about $20 million.

Mr. Amerman streamlined Mattel's management ranks but has stepped up the company's spending on consumer research, trying to balance basic marketing with the gut instincts that toymakers traditionally used to identify promising products.

The company, which alienated some retailers before the management shakeout with a hunger for volume at almost any cost, now has senior managers working directly with important customers. Mr. Amerman oversees the team handling the K mart Corp. account, while Mr. Sansone works with top management of Toys `R' Us Inc.

"They treat us as a partner, and that wasn't so much the case years ago," says Michael Goldstein, vice chairman of Toys `R' Us, adding: "They've made tremendous strides."

That relationship may stand Mattel in good stead as it expands overseas. Toys `R' Us's ambitious plan to build a 170-store international chain by the end of 1992 could give Mattel an extra boost, particularly in cracking difficult markets such as Japan.

While Mattel is the largest and most profitable international toymaker with more than $1 billion in international sales last year, that doesn't prevent some flops and missteps here and overseas.

Barbie, for example, doesn't stack up to Hot Wheels in India because of the male-dominated culture. And the Japanese craze for superfast battery-driven cars didn't translate to the U.S., partly because Mattel and others misread the hobby as an at-home toy.

Still, Mattel's overseas business is growing. Lindsey Williams, president of Mattel International, says the Disney line should get a boost with the 1992 opening of Euro Disneyland, and Mattel's network of international offices is burgeoning. New offices have opened in East Berlin and Budapest, with another slated for Warsaw by year end.

Mr. Williams says the overseas markets are less volatile than the U.S., with products slower to peak and slower to decline. With the overseas appetite for basic products such as miniature cars, infant toys and large dolls, he says, Mattel intends "not to waste precious resources introducing fads."

Mattel and other big toymakers may be better served by the bread-and-butter approach, analysts agree. "The company may be losing a little sex appeal by focusing on what it does well," says John G. Taylor, an analyst with L.H. Alton & Co. "But they've traded that in for stability and profitability, and the possibility of sleeping better at night."

Mattel executives use the Simpsons licensing agreement to argue that the company can retain some sizzle and yet limit its risk. But because of Mattel's conservative bent, some industry experts say, it seems less likely that the next Cabbage Patch or Teddy Ruxpin will wear the Mattel logo.

That seems to be a trade-off Mattel is willing to make. "You really have to be careful with the mega-hits," Mr. Amerman says. David Leibowitz, an analyst with American Securities, agrees. "A blockbuster is such a rare phenomenon, you just can't plan for it," he says.

Mr. Amerman doesn't rule out acquisitions, and says the company will consider anything from board games to ride-on toys and other lines. The company recently bought Corgi Toys Inc. of Swansea, Wales, a maker of miniature die cast cars, to bolster its position in Europe and supplement its core Hot Wheels brand.

Mr. Amerman thinks his best seller can become "billion-dollar Barbie" by 1995. And the Disney line may be extended beyond infant and preschool toys. Eventually, "the Disney brand may do in the range of what Barbie does," Mr. Amerman says.

If not, there's always show business. Mr. Amerman, Mr. Sansone and Mr. Williams recently brought down the house in the company cafeteria with a soft-shoe tribute to Mattel employees for last year's results. The "Toy Boys," decked out in tuxedoes, glittery top hats and bow ties, sang and danced up a storm to "There's No Business Like Toy Business" and other ditties. The finale? A bonus of two weeks' pay to the unsuspecting employees; that brought a standing ovation.
