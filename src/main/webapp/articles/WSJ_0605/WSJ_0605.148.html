Economy: Revised Figures On Productivity Show 2.7% Drop --- Rate for First Period, Which Excludes Farm Workers, Is the Worst Since 1981

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 06/05/90

WASHINGTON -- The productivity of U.S. business plummeted in the first three months of this year at a far steeper rate than the government initially reported. At the same time, business costs escalated much faster. After reviewing new data, the Labor Department said productivity, excluding that of farm workers, plunged at a 2.7% annual rate in the first quarter, not at the 1% rate the department originally calculated. That marked the biggest decline since the fourth quarter of 1981, when the country was in a recession. In last year's fourth quarter non-farm productivity edged up at a 0.5% annual rate.

Meanwhile, the cost of producing a unit of output -- whether it was a television set or a financial service -- jumped at a 7% annual rate for nonagricultural businesses, the department said. In an advance report, the agency had calculated that unit labor costs rose at a 4.5% rate during the period. These costs grew at a 5.5% annual rate in the fourth quarter.

The deep revisions disturbed economists, although they have been worried for some time about the nation's inability to make significant advances in productivity.

"I thought it was a dismal report," said Allen Sinai, chief economist at Boston Co. in New York.

Gains in productivity -- or output per hour worked -- are vital to continued economic growth. The more workers produce per hour, the better businesses are able to compete across the globe. Improving productivity is also critical to raising the country's living standards because it allows businesses to boost wages without forcing a corresponding increase in prices or cut in profits.

As they have for some time, the numbers showed that most of the country's productivity problems are centered in the economy's vast services sector. Manufacturing productivity, which is far more precisely measured, actually improved even more than the big jump the government calculated in its advance report. It grew at a 4.9% annual rate instead of 4.1%, the department said. The fourth quarter rate of increase was 3.1%

Partly because of the big gains in output per hour, it cost manufacturers less to produce their wares during the period. Manufacturers' unit labor costs declined at a 1.3% annual rate in the first quarter, a bigger drop than the 0.9% rate the government initially calculated. Unit labor costs of manufacturers rose at a 3.7% annual rate in the fourth period.

That, at least, pleased economists. "Manufacturing is finally showing the benefits of a year of cost-cutting and employee cutbacks," Mr. Sinai said. "The non-manufacturing, non-farm sector, which largely includes services, is where the dismal record lies both on productivity growth and unit labor costs."

Generally the deep downward revisions reflected the fact that the gross national product -- the country's total output of goods and services -- was smaller in the first quarter than the government initially believed. The Commerce Department regularly makes revisions to its estimate of the GNP as it gathers more information. Based on new data the department reported earlier this month that the GNP grew at a 1.3% annual rate in the first quarter after adjusting for inflation, instead of 2.1%.

Gordon Richards, an economist at the National Association of Manufacturers, said the overall first-quarter productivity figures weren't consistent with the GNP report and called them "suspiciously low." He said "it would be reasonable to expect further revisions either in these productivity numbers or in the first-quarter GNP estimate."

The Labor Department figured that the output of non-farm businesses grew only at a 1% annual rate in the first quarter instead of 2.7%. At the same time it estimated that hours logged by workers rose at a 3.9% annual rate instead of 3.6%.

The department also released its initial measure of the productivity of nonfinancial businesses in the first quarter, a category that covers all corporations doing business in the U.S. except banks, brokers and finance and insurance agencies. The results there were grim as well: Output per hour dropped at a 2.1% annual rate. The department will revise those figures in the next few months. In the fourth quarter, productivity of nonfinancial business fell at a 0.6% annual rate.

Including farms, which economists believe is a less precise indicator of pressures in the economy, overall business productivity tumbled at a 2.1% annual rate last quarter, according to the new report, instead of the 0.5% rate of decline originally calculated. In the fourth quarter business productivity edged up 0.4%.

All the figures are adjusted for seasonal variations.
