Faber Agrees To Buy Corroon Via Stock Swap --- Value Put at $712.3 Million; Major Realignment Seen Of Insurance Brokerages

  • Author: Richard E. Rustin and Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 06/05/90

Willis Faber PLC, Britain's second-largest insurance broker, agreed in principle to acquire New York-based Corroon & Black Corp. in a stock swap valued at #424 million, or about $712.3 million. The marriage would create the world's fourth-largest insurance brokerage. The agreement could trigger a major realignment of insurance brokerages in the United Kingdom and the U.S. and stimulate more consolidation in the industry. An insurance broker specializes in assisting primarily corporate clients in seeking and arranging coverage with an insurance company.

But the engagement announcement itself was received coldly by the London and New York stock markets. Faber's stock slid 9% in London on uncertainty over whether the deal would be good for the British concern. The drop triggered a 7% decline in Corroon shares in New York, quickly removing a small premium for Corroon holders and sending the per-share offering price below what Corroon shares closed at on Friday.

The transaction, which is subject to approval by holders of each company, is targeted for the end of September. Faber said it would issue 7.8 of new ordinary shares for each of 20.5 million Corroon common, for a total of about 160 million new shares. The package would be worth #20.67 (about $34.72), based on Faber's closing price of 265 pence yesterday. The package was worth #22.69 ($38.13) when it was announced at the market's opening; Faber shares were trading then at 291 pence.

Some industry observers believe the transaction could prompt formation of a new set of alliances between major U.S. and British brokers.

"Everybody has to be reassessing their alliances right now," said Russell Miller, who heads a San Franciso-based investment-banking firm specializing in the insurance industry. He also believes this acquisition could increase consolidation among insurance brokerage firms that now feel they must be larger to compete. "You now have a new billion-dollar player on the scene," he said.

A Faber-Corroon merger would undo Faber's century-old, profitable relationship with Johnson & Higgins, another major U.S. broker. Moreover, the market uncertainty reflects concern about whether Faber would be getting more from Corroon than it would be giving up. Johnson & Higgins, which owns 5% of Willis Faber, contributed about 10% of Faber's earnings last year, Roger Elliott, Faber's chairman, said.

"It's quite a risky deal," said Julianne Jessup, insurance brokerage analyst at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Securities Ltd. in London. Ms. Jessup also said there is market concern over the share dilution that would result from the issuance of 160 million new Faber shares. "It's such a massive issue," she said.

Mr. Elliott acknowledged that Faber's ties with Johnson & Higgins would loosen. But he added: "We've been dealing with Johnson & Higgins for 100 years. People think everything will be canceled overnight. That's rubbish. It will take three to five years to cool down, and we still will be doing some business with them."

Robert Hatcher, chairman and chief executive officer of Johnson & Higgins, said the different strategies adopted by J&H and Willis Faber in recent years "caused some friction" between the two companies. J&H bought a London broker in 1988, which put the U.S. company, currently the fourth-largest in the world, directly in the London market and made it a licensed Lloyds of London broker.

While Mr. Hatcher agreed that J&H's working relationship with Willis Faber won't wind down immediately, he said he believes there will now be a conflict of interest because Willis Faber will be the owner of one of J&H's major U.S. competitors.

For the time being, J&H plans to beef up existing London operations by acquisitions of other small brokers and by increasing staff there. Mr. Hatcher said J&H doesn't know what it will do with its 5% stake in Willis Faber. The British broker hasn't offered to buy back its shares, he said.

Richard Miller, Corroon's chairman and chief executive officer, noted that there might be some compensation for Corroon shareholders because they will receive a 70% dividend increase when the combined company begins a quarterly payout early next year.

The two companies would be merged into a new entity, Willis Corroon PLC. Faber holders would own 60% of Willis Corroon, and Corroon holders the rest. Mr. Miller would be chief executive of the new company and Faber's Mr. Elliott would be chairman. Both men are 57 years old.

In an interview, Mr. Elliott said there isn't any thought of renegotiating the deal in light of the stock price drops. "A deal is a deal, and the deal's been done," he said, adding: "It's been approved by the boards of both companies."

Mr. Miller also said the two companies "signed a firm contract" late Friday evening. "There will be no changes in price."

Both Corroon and Faber officials said the combination of the companies ideally meets their individual strategies. There's no overlap between the two companies and the combination of the two operations won't require any physical mergers, officials said. Prior to the transaction, Willis was the fifth largest broker world-wide and Corroon was No. 6.

Faber proposed a 10% increase in its dividend, to 13.2 pence a share, conditional on the Corroon deal going through. Only Faber holders would get the first part of the payout -- 9.9 pence a share payable in November. Holders of the combined company would get a 3.3-pence-a-share dividend next January.

Faber said that Willis Corroon would apply to the Big Board to have its shares traded as American depositary receipts. An application for listing on London's stock exchange also would be sought.

In 1989, Faber earned #62.3 million pretax, up 15% from #54.4 million in 1988. Corroon's net income fell 14% to $36 million last year. Corroon's earnings have declined during the past two years because of the downturn in property/casualty premium rates.
