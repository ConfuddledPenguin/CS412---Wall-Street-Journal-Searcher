World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Gain 34.25 Points to 32925.37; London Shares Rise Slightly in Quiet Trading

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/05/90

Tokyo stocks closed slightly higher in light trading, helped by a minor rally in shipbuilding and construction stocks, traders said. But dealers lacked incentive to push the market aggressively, and institutions and individual investors were unenthusiastic about trading stocks on the first section, observers said.

Meanwhile, London stocks were lifted off their intraday lows at the close of quiet trading, with the market shadowing the fortunes of Wall Street.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues gained 34.25 points to 32925.37, after falling 239.68 points Friday.

At midmorning Tuesday in Tokyo, the Nikkei index had advanced 25.27 points to 32950.64.

Monday's volume on the first section was estimated at 650 million shares, down from 709.1 million shares Friday. Advancers outnumbered losers 521 to 417, while 188 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section added 0.69 point, or 0.03%, to 2427.24. The index fell 9.19 points Friday.

The second section index rose 18.32 points, or 0.46%, to 3992.94, after advancing 36.34 points Friday. Volume was estimated at slightly less than 20 million shares, down from 29.3 million shares Friday.

Interest in the second section remains strong, one trader at a foreign securities firm said, because recent earnings results offered attractive growth opportunities.

Moreover, prices on the second section are seen as easier to urge higher, another trader at a Japanese firm said.

With the exception of May 25 and May 28, the second section index has risen since April 26. From the recent low of 3313.92 points April 5, the index has recovered 20.5%.

The rebound compares with a 17.6% recovery on the Nikkei Index since its recent low of 28002.07 points April 2, and a 17.9% recovery on the Topix since its recent low of 2058.82 points April 5.

Reduced commission tables, which became effective yesterday, were one reason for fundamental softness in the first section, as well as another explanation for the second section index's strength, one trader pointed out.

Dealers probably were chasing large-capital issues, such as shipbuilding, construction and mining shares, in order to improve volume, and conceivably to make up for the decrease in their commissions, he said.

On the other hand, institutions, who have just finished reevaluating their book losses for the year ended March 31, would only increase costs by participating in the first section at current prices, the trader said.

As a result, institutions have been selectively participating in the higher-potential second section issues, as well as in better-performing Osaka Stock Exchange second section issues and over-the-counter shares, analysts said.

Market reaction to U.S.-Soviet Union discussions concerning a united Germany's possible membership in the North Atlantic Treaty Organization has been indecisive, as should be expected because the talks likewise were inconclusive, traders said.

The dollar firmed against the yen, but foreign-exchange market response to the talks was also limited, equity traders indicated.

Stock market participants are seeking a clearer move in currency rates before they will establish speculative positions on the U.S.-Soviet Union talks, traders said.

Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev's scheduled East Asian visit, meanwhile, is expected to stimulate a firmer response from the stock market, the traders added.

Despite some afternoon selling pressure, steel stocks ended mostly higher.

Daido Steel rose 26 yen to 989 yen (17 cents to $6.52), Nisshin Steel added 10 yen to 990 yen and Tokyo Steel advanced 200 yen to 4200 yen.

Oil stocks slipped on profit-taking, as Showa Shell fell 30 yen to 1320 yen, and Teikoku Oil Co. dropped 10 yen to 1270 yen.

In London, the slightly weaker pound kept the market under pressure. The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index gained 7.6 points to 2379.

Dealers said that London lost some of its recent steam, as the Dow Jones Industrial Average hovered slightly under the psychologically important level of 2900 late in the London afternoon.

"We've had such an amazing run, we would expect to see a period of consolidation," said one trader.

Some investors were reportedly distrustful of the recent sharp rally and weren't prepared to commit further funds to the market. Yesterday's volumes were consequently depressed, although start-of-the-week trading is typically slow.

Volume was 430 million shares, slightly under the 499.3 million shares Friday.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index rose 11 points to 1893.4, while in the futures markets, the June FT-SE 100 index contract ended at 2422, compared with Friday's close of 2411.

The market was also slightly depressed by afternoon suggestions that the food retail sector might start having supply problems. One unidentified retailer reportedly complained that its supplies were being disrupted, suggesting that other large retailers Argyll, Sainsbury and Tesco, might undergo similar difficulties.

However, this situation couldn't be confirmed with the individual companies. Argyll was off 6 pence to 234 pence (11 cents to $4.44), Sainsbury eased 4 pence to 290 pence, while Tesco was off 6 pence at 223 pence.

Among stocks to fall, Enterprise Oil posted the biggest losses. It fell 17 pence to 648 pence as speculation fades that Elf Aquitaine will mount a bid for the company.

Boots was up 13 pence to 310 pence, with its earnings later this week expected to include news that the company's heart drug, Manoplax, is selling well.

Meanwhile, markets were closed in Belgium, West Germany, France, the Netherlands, Switzerland and Sweden for Whit Monday. Prices in Milan fell, while South African gold stocks fell sharply.

Elsewhere, prices closed higher in Sydney and Seoul; lower in Singapore, Manila and Taipei; and mixed in Hong Kong. Wellington was closed.
