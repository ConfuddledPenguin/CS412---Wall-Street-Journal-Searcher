Politics & Policy: Moscow, Seoul Move to Renew Diplomatic Ties --- Korea's Roh Tae Woo Calls Talks With Gorbachev An `Epic-Making Event'

  • Author: David Shribman
  • Date: 06/05/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev and South Korean President Roh Tae Woo said that their two countries are moving toward establishing diplomatic relations. Last night's announcement foreshadowed an adjustment of East Asian political and economic relationships that could potentially be as dramatic as the overhaul of Soviet society that Mr. Gorbachev is struggling to nurture at home.

"The Korean peninsula remains the last area of tension in the Cold War," said President Roh. "The Cold War on the Korean peninsula now begins to crack."

Mr. Roh called his unusual meeting with Mr. Gorbachev -- the first between leaders of their two nations since the division of the peninsula in 1948 -- in a San Francisco hotel an "epic-making event" and "an important turning point." While setting no timetable for normalizing relations, he said it would provide a strong impetus for promoting a new Pacific order and reducing tensions between the two Koreas. "We do not want North Korea to remain isolated," he said.

Both the South Koreans and the Soviets -- frosty adversaries in four decades of mistrust -- now are hungry for closer trade relations. But Soviet initiatives until now have been stymied by the knowledge that any movement in that direction would trouble the hard-line North Korean regime of Kim Il Sung, who has maintained strong links with Moscow.

Soviet-South Korean trade amounted to only $31 million in 1981, and bilateral relations were all but nonexistent as recently as two years ago. They opened respective trade offices only 14 months ago, and the Soviet consular office in Seoul wasn't opened until April. But trade has been increasing substantially, and before last night's developments, the volume of trade was expected to reach $2 billion early in the 1990s.

Mr. Gorbachev's meeting with the South Korean president came at the end of a whirlwind, five-day U.S. tour that took the Soviet leader to the White House, the Maryland mountains, the plains of Minnesota and the Pacific coast. But when Mr. Gorbachev returns to the Soviet Union with his trade, environmental and defense pacts, he will be met with internal political disputes, ethnic passions, food shortages and economic distress.

In his last day here, Mr. Gorbachev, who has pursued "new structures" in his drive for perestroika at home, called on Americans to join him in developing "new structures" in international relations.

The Soviet leader urged the U.S. and the Soviet Union to move away from the "twin antipathies" of the West and East blocs. "They divided countries and peoples much more than they united them," he told students at Stanford University.

His final day included tea and sympathy with former President Reagan and Nancy Reagan, a stroll through Stanford, a drive through the storied hills of San Francisco and more of the impromptu sidewalk street seminars that have marked the Soviet leader's U.S. journeys.

Though the metaphor was mixed, the message that animated almost every public moment of the Gorbachev tour was clear: "The winds are now blowing," Mr. Gorbachev told members of the San Francisco Chamber of Commerce yesterday afternoon, "filling the sails of the process that places us on the path of cooperation."

Indeed, the thrust of Mr. Gorbachev's appeal yesterday was to stress the challenges the Soviet Union faces in battling "old thinking," to assert that he had "no illusions as to the possibility of the faster rapprochement of our societies," and to argue that the Western alliance itself would have to evolve to match changes that have occurred since the Iron Curtain shredded across Europe.

"Everyone must change," he said.

But Mr. Gorbachev, who began his mission as a commander-in-chief with geopolitics on his mind, concluded it as a chief executive with investment on his mind -- and revealed himself to be as shrewd a businessman as he is a rhetorician.

"We are now watching those who maybe are risking something but willing to cooperate and watching those who sit on the sidelines," he told the business executives. "Those who cooperate with us will obtain many more opportunities in our market. Those who stand on the side will stay there."

Mr. Gorbachev said the Soviet Union is moving more rapidly toward a convertible ruble and assured his audiences that, in all sectors of society, "we are changing in a serious way and not just sweeping the yard."

Declaring that the Cold War "is now behind us," Mr. Gorbachev told the Stanford students that setting aside the "exhausting and dangerous confrontation" of the postwar era would enable the Soviet Union to proceed down its new economic path. "As Soviet-American relations improve," he said, "military procurement and participation in the arms race will become a thing of the past."

Mr. Reagan emerged from his 45-minute meeting with the Soviet president with the conclusion that there "have been improvements" in U.S.-Soviet relations since Mr. Reagan left office only 16 months ago. "I'm pleased with all that's taken place," he said, expressing the hope that "it's going to continue."

As Mr. Reagan and Mr. Gorbachev -- one a doctrinaire anti-Communist for most of his life, the other a doctrinaire Marxist-Leninist for most of his -- spoke in the elegant brick Soviet consulate in the Pacific Heights neighborhood of San Francisco, their wives met separately.

Mrs. Reagan said the meeting with Raisa Gorbachev -- who was later pictured lingering at a display of bananas, oranges and Snickers bars -- went well, and that she had an easy rapport with the woman she once regarded as something of a socialist ice queen. "There has been a great misunderstanding about our relationship," she said.
