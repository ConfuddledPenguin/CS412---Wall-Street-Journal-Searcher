International: Japanese Banks Put Aside Plans To Enter Taiwan

  • Author: Cynthia Owens
  • Date: 06/05/90

HONG KONG -- Under pressure from China, several Japanese banks in recent days have shelved plans to enter the Taiwan market. As many as eight Japanese banks had been expected to follow Bank of Tokyo after it announced plans in May to open a representative office in Taiwan. But after criticism of those plans by People's Bank of China late last week, the other Japanese banks got the message "not to go to Taiwan now," according to the managing director of a large Japanese bank in Hong Kong.

A Bank of Tokyo spokesman said the bank still intends to open a Taipei office. But he said that the issue is "very sensitive" and that bank officials are conferring this week on those plans and China's reaction.

A Bank of Tokyo official in Hong Kong said the bank hopes the controversy dies down so the Taipei office can quietly open in July as planned, but he conceded that some executives in Tokyo want to reconsider the move.

Bank of Tokyo's move into Taiwan surprised banking analysts. Though Japanese banks long ago recognized the island's economic potential, relations with China had always been considered too sensitive to risk establishing direct commercial ties with Taiwan, which China continues to claim is part of its territory. Many Japanese banks have used Hong Kong subsidiaries to do business with Taiwan.

If Bank of Tokyo goes ahead, it would be the first Japanese bank to enter Taiwan since Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank set up an office in 1960. Dai-Ichi Kangyo's ties to Taiwan have kept it barred from doing any substantial business with China in the past three decades.

Still, Japanese authorities apparently thought the time might be right for a change in policy. Japanese banks have done little business with China in the past year because of a government curb on new loans since the Chinese government's massacre of pro-democracy demonstrators in Beijing last June. Meanwhile, Taiwan's economy has remained robust, and economic relations between China and Taiwan appeared to be improving.

In addition, some large Japanese securities companies had received clearance from Tokyo to buy into Taipei brokerage firms. But unlike the banks, which have extensive loans outstanding to China and hope eventually to resume substantial lending, the securities firms have little business with China and so can't be as easily pressured, a Japanese banker in Hong Kong said.

However, the Japanese banks haven't given up on Taiwan, he said. They are merely waiting "for a more appropriate time." A spokesman for Japan's Ministry of Foreign Affairs said going into Taiwan is a "private decision for the banks to make."
