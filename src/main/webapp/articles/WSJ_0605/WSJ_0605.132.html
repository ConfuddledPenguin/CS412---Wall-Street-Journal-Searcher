High Court Rules Whistleblowers Fired By Nuclear Industry Can Sue in States

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/05/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court ruled that workers in the nuclear industry who allege that their employers fired them for pointing out safety problems may sue for damages under state laws. In a decision written by Justice Harry Blackmun, the high court ruled, 9-0, that federal laws regulating the nuclear industry don't preclude so-called whistleblowers from suing for the emotional distress caused by their employers' treatment.

The ruling is a defeat for the nuclear industry, which now faces lawsuits under disparate state personal-injury laws instead of being held accountable under a single federal law.

The decision reinstates a lawsuit against General Electric Co. by Vera English, a former employee at the nuclearfuels plant in Wilmington, N.C. According to GE, the suit asks for about $1.5 million in compensatory damages and nearly $2.3 billion in punitive damages.

Ms. English was fired in 1984 after she complained that spills of radioactive materials weren't being properly cleaned up by workers. To prove her point, she left a radioactive work area uncleaned and showed her supervisor a few days later that it hadn't been cleaned or even noticed.

After her dismissal, she filed administrative claims under federal labor law with the Labor Department, but they were dismissed. So she sued the company for emotional distress.

Both a federal district court in North Carolina and a federal appeals court in Richmond, Va., dismissed the lawsuit, ruling that federal law pre-empted her claims. Yesterday, the Supreme Court reversed the appeals court decision.

"As a result of the Supreme Court decision the case will go back to the federal district court in North Carolina," a GE spokesman said. "GE is confident it will prevail on the merits of the state tort claim, which the Supreme Court said was not pre-empted by the federal law." (English vs. General Electric Co.)

Lawyer Advertising

The justices ruled, 5-4, that states can't prohibit lawyers from truthfully advertising themselves as specialists in certain areas. The high court ruled that although such advertising may be regulated by states to determine whether it is truthful, it is protected by the First Amendment and can't be entirely barred.

The high court ruled against Illinois officials for their 1989 censure of an Edwardsville, Ill., lawyer. The lawyer noted on his stationery that he is certified as a specialist in civil trials by the National Board of Trial Advocacy, a lawyers group that sets standards and maintains lists of those who have met them.

Illinois officials censured him because the state professional code for lawyers prohibits claims of specialization. Similar bans are contained in the codes of about 15 other states.

But in an opinion written by Justice John Stevens, the high court ruled that the letterhead is a form of advertising that is protected by the First Amendment. Justice Stevens's opinion only commanded four votes, but Justice Thurgood Marshall provided a fifth vote, agreeing for separate reasons that the Illinois ban was invalid.

Justice Byron White dissented. Justice Sandra O'Connor also dissented, joined by Chief Justice William Rehnquist and Justice Antonin Scalia. (Peel vs. Attorney Disciplinary Commission)

State Bar Dues

The high court ruled, 9-0, that the use of compulsory bar association dues for lobbying and to support political causes violates the First Amendment rights of lawyers who object to the positions advocated.

The decision, written by Chief Justice Rehnquist, was a defeat for the State Bar of California. But it will affect other states as well. In recent years, some 30 states have had mandatory bar associations, those that lawyers are required to join. Many of these bar associations use a portion of their compulsory dues to become involved in local political issues.

In California, the state bar used dues to lobby for legislation, file friend-of-the-court briefs, and adopt resolutions supporting gun control and a nuclear freeze. In a challenge by 21 lawyers, the California Supreme Court upheld the bar's activities.

Yesterday, the U.S. Supreme Court reversed that ruling. The court said that a state bar may spend dues to lobby on issues directly related to the profession, such as legislation on lawyers' ethics, but not for other, unrelated matters.

The decision subjects bar associations to the same limits on dues that the court has imposed on labor unions, although the American Bar Association had urged the high court to grant more flexibility. (Keller vs. State Bar of California)

Natural Gas Pricing

The justices agreed to decide whether Congress gave the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission the authority to revamp the pricing for natural gas from wells that were in production before November 1978. A federal appeals court in New Orleans ruled in September 1989 that the commission exceeded its authority in 1986 when it revamped the pricing system for so-called old natural gas.

If the justices uphold the appeals court ruling, the commission may order producers to refund $100 million to consumers, industry groups say.

Old gas is gas produced from wells that were already in operation when the federal Natural Gas Policy Act took effect in November 1978. Congress set higher price ceilings for so-called new gas, gas that would come from newer wells, and lower ceilings for old gas. But the 1978 federal law also preserved 15 different categories of old gas with varying price ceilings, based on the age of the wells.

In 1986, the FERC concluded that the ceilings for old gas were so low that producers were abandoning old wells, leaving huge untapped reserves and prompting greater reliance on imports and on higher-priced new wells. The commission consolidated old gas into one category, raised the price, and required producers to renegotiate their contracts.

Pipeline companies, consumers and distributors challenged FERC's authority for the orders and won in appeals court.

The case was appealed to the high court by the commission and by two dozen natural gas producers. They said the appeals court ruling will disrupt natural gas markets and 1,600 natural gas supply contracts that have been renegotiated. (Mobil Oil Exploration and Producing Southeast Inc. vs. United Distribution Cos.; FERC vs. United Distribution Cos.)

Airline Lawsuits

The high court agreed to decide whether a treaty that governs liability for international airline flights allows passengers to sue for damages for emotional distress because of near mishaps, even though there were no physical injuries. The justices agreed to hear an appeal by Texas Air Corp.'s Eastern Airlines Inc. unit.

The case stems from 25 separate lawsuits filed against the airline under Florida and federal laws by passengers who were on an Eastern flight in 1983 from Miami to the Bahamas. Soon after takeoff the engines shut down, and passengers were told the plane would have to ditch in the Atlantic Ocean. The pilots were able to restart one engine and return to Miami, but not before the plane sharply lost altitude.

Most international flights are subject to a treaty, approved by the U.S. in 1934 and modified in 1966, that limits airline liability for accidents to property and individuals.

Last year, a federal appeals court in Atlanta ruled that the treaty allows lawsuits for emotional distress, even when there was no physical injury. A federal district court had dismissed the lawsuits before trial. (Eastern Airlines Inc. vs. Floyd)

Alabama Business Tax

The high court let stand an Alabama Supreme Court ruling that the state's franchise tax for corporations doesn't discriminate against companies located in other states. The high court refused to hear appeals by General Motors Corp. and Reynolds Metals Co. General Motors challenged the $6.3 million that it and several subsidiaries paid from 1983 through 1988. Reynolds objected to $1.2 million it paid in 1982 and 1983.

The companies argued that the franchise tax, imposed on all corporations doing business in Alabama, is calculated differently for companies based in Alabama than for those based in other states. The result, they argued, is a much heavier tax on out-of-state corporations than on those based in Alabama. But the Alabama Supreme Court concluded that the tax doesn't violate either the 14th Amendment guarantee of "equal protection of the laws" or the Constitution's prohibition on interference with interstate commerce. (General Motors Corp. vs. Department of Revenue; Reynolds Metals Co. vs. Sizemore)
