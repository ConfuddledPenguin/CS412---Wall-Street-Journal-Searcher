REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): The Leveling of New Jersey

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/05/90

It doesn't seem to be very big news that the negotiations in the Bush-Congress budget summit are about to resume. Who can blame the public or even the media for losing interest? Farmers and Social Security recipients probably are the only segments of the American electorate who have any clear idea of what the federal budget does anymore. It's like a massive, exploding star of gas and dust, which now measures $1.2 trillion in diameter. Maybe it's a threat to real life, but how would you know? Many people still keep telescopes trained on Washington because there is talk of raising their taxes to close the distance between the budget's revenues and outlays at the edge of the universe. The summit astronomers say spending this money to shorten the measurements of the deficit will produce a prosperous new civilization. Let's come down to Earth. Let's visit New Jersey.

Unlike life with the federal government, the people of New Jersey more or less know what is going on around them. One might say the same about the people of New York, Connecticut and Massachusetts. All these jurisdictions have one thing in common with the federal government -- a big hole in their budgets. And all are now in the process of attempting to figure out what taxes to increase.

The accompanying table on per-capita spending over six recent years by these and other states offers a clear idea of why some states are struggling with their budgets. But something more important is reflected in this data than the fact that they've spent more than was prudent, and this is no more evident than in the case of New Jersey.

New Jersey's new Democratic Governor, former Congressman Jim Florio, has added a unique twist to the familiar belt-tightening, tax-raising scenario. Governor Florio, an unabashed liberal, has proposed to reorganize the way New Jersey pays for public education. It is a complex formula, which may be altered by the legislature, but its goal is to take about $150 million of the state's share of school budgets away from so-called "wealthy" school districts and transfer it primarily to inner-city systems, such as Camden's and Newark's.

The Governor expects the districts losing money to make up some of the shortfall by raising their property taxes. Also, Mr. Florio would transfer down to the local level responsibility for the teachers' pension and Social Security costs. Moreover, the Florio fiscal package includes, besides some program cuts, increasing sales taxes and doubling the top marginal income-tax rate (and using some of this to lower some property taxes.)

Governor Florio has been attracting some national attention as a liberal Robin Hood who's willing to transform the Garden State into Sherwood Forest. If the state's economy doesn't relocate to Pennsylvania or the citizens don't drive the legislature's Democrats from office, his national star will rise, at least in the Democratic firmament. But all this is New Jersey's peculiar problem.

We're inclined to think that Mr. Florio has done American politics in general a large favor. Unintentionally, his proposal will force a lot of people to finally come to grips with the cost and purpose of the public sector.

The plight of the school districts he plans to cut off mirrors that of the federal budget and many other state budgets. Local Jersey school systems say their hands are tied by the fact that most of their budget outlays are "mandated" or "untouchable" in the form of teachers' salaries and benefits, and so most of the cuts have to come out of vulnerable and "worthwhile" programs. This, of course, is the familiar Washington "entitlement spending" chorus. It is, in short, the bullet the system has refused to bite for about 20 years.

Now the choices, as with New Jersey's schools, are getting pretty stark: Either take on the untouchables -- public-employee salary and benefit structures, the cost of ever-increasing environmental "mandates" (for example, asbestos abatement), and federal entitlements -- or watch those things that are most valued slowly crumble.

The Jim Florios of the world, who are found at all levels of government, assume that in the crunch voters will again swallow new taxes to prop up the public sector. Our reading is that this process has about run its course, and that in places such as New Jersey, New York, Massachusetts and Washington, it's time to start searching for alternatives to the "untouchable" status quo.
