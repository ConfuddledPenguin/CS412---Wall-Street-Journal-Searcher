Technology: Foreign Market Share For Semiconductors Levels Off in Japan

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/05/90

TOKYO -- After a year of steady increases, foreign semiconductor market share in Japan leveled off in the first quarter, according to independent industry statistics. American officials said they were disappointed by the 13% share, a 0.1 percentage point increase over the fourth quarter of 1989. The five-year U.S.-Japan semiconductor agreement expires a year from now, and Americans say the Japanese promised to work for a 20% foreign share by the end of the pact.

"We're pleased with the changes in attitudes and practices of Japanese (semiconductor) users," said E. David Metz, executive director of the U.S. Semiconductor Industry Association's Japan office. "However, in light of the modest increase in this most recent quarter, we still need greater efforts."

In the fourth quarter, foreign share grew by nearly one percentage point over the previous three-month period, according to the World Semiconductor Trade Statistics (WSTS) organization.

Japanese industry and government officials, who deny ever agreeing to a market share target, played down the significance of the numbers, saying that a dip in the overall semiconductor market in the first quarter made it difficult for electronics companies to increase foreign share. They added that foreign share will likely increase sharply again in the current quarter ending June 30.

Japan's Ministry of International Trade and Industry, which measures market share differently than WSTS, showed foreign share increasing nearly one percentage point in the quarter, to 17.2% from 16.3%. A MITI official said the agency measures orders, while WSTS measures shipments.

When the bilateral trade agreement was first negotiated in 1986, foreign share of the Japanese chip market hovered around 9%. As the numbers remained stuck during the first year of the agreement, the U.S. imposed sanctions on certain Japanese imports using semiconductors.

But in the past two years, companies from both countries have started working more closely together, and the U.S. has curbed its criticism.
