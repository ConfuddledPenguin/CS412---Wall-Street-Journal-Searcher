Business World: The Golden State Assays the Golden Spike

  • Author: Tim W. Ferguson
  • Date: 06/05/90

In six weeks the first inner-city rail line to serve Los Angeles in half a century is to open. The 22-mile trolley between downtown and Long Beach is only an $871 million start. Tunnels for a far more costly subway are being dug in the central business district. And if California voters so choose today, mass transit will lay tracks all over the state for years to come. Two bond issues on the primary ballot aim to provide locomotion. One, Proposition 108 from the governor and Legislature, is tied to the fate of Proposition 111, a measure to double the gasoline tax, hike truck fees 55% and raise the limit on state spending. The other is Proposition 116, a more ambitious ($2 billion vs. $1 billion for Prop. 108) plan drawn up by fervent citizen-opponents of the auto culture.

Californians are promiscuous in agreeing to bond issues, so there's a good chance they'll be working on some sort of railroad starting tomorrow. Whatever happens, this megastate's choices are likely to affect national trends.

The rush to rail is still on in some American cities, despite horrendous cost overruns of the newer systems and continued grief with the older ones. All the lines are government owned, and not one pays for itself. Congestion relief is a hidden gain, but more of it per dollar can be had from other transit schemes, such as greater use of buses, jitneys and high-speed lanes for group vehicles. Yet the case for rail systems is consistently overstated at the time they are chosen over alternative modes, as the accompanying table shows.

Still, even in California, rail romances politicians, the press and much of the public. It seduced the San Francisco area into a partly underground system 20 years ago, San Diego a trolley line 10 years ago and Sacramento some street tracks more recently. And motorist resistance is down -- 25% population growth in the 1980s brought traffic delays real and exaggerated. (Periodic planning-agency surveys of commute times may reflect only drivers' worst memories.)

Prop. 108, to be supplemented by clones slated for the 1992 and 1994 ballots, is the first major concession to rail by the state Republican Party. Gov. George Deukmejian is on the hook for it, co-authoring a ballot argument that shouts it "will pave the way for A NEW ERA OF PROSPERITY." He likely did this to obtain bipartisan backing for Prop. 111, which is basically for road money he badly wants and has failed to get otherwise.

One might think, given the additional gallons of gas bought for all the new autos crowding the state's highways, that a static levy would suffice. But that doesn't seem to be true. More fuel-efficient cars are one reason. Escalating costs of maintenance as a road ages are another. Lots of money goes to build sound barriers and relocate residents in harm's way. Who knows where the billions in new federal highway dollars have gone?

So the statehouse cabal asks for road relief with a rail kicker. At least Prop. 108's projects -- as yet unspecified -- would be situated mostly in high-congestion corridors. Prop. 116 is more expansive, with more than half of its proceeds designated for long-distance travel between the state's metropolises. An Orange County monorail (not in Disneyland) and bicycle-commuter lanes also are included. Every major gubernatorial candidate has his or her name on this one, and visionaries on the left tout it as a way to convert the defense industry to peace in our time.

More-practical steps to mitigate traffic and the pollution that goes with it are receiving increasing attention outside the glare of politics. The Southern California-based Reason Foundation recently brought together players from the ranks of regulation, academia and business to discuss incentive-based ideas, such as peak-hour pricing and unsubsidized parking, that could alleviate congestion while actually supplanting taxpayer dollars.

It's debatable whether the road improvements of Prop. 111 would hasten more efficient traffic management. Michael McGill, director of a powerhouse San Francisco civic group that has emerged as a voice for market-oriented transportation policies, favors Prop. 111 because it would fund group-vehicle lanes, ramp meters and other half steps to pricing, as well as better engineering. However, some libertarians, along with the Sierra Club's LA chapter, argue that forcing a supply crunch by not building more roads is the best way toward alternatives.

Meanwhile, near the congestion capital of downtown Los Angeles sits a professor who disputes the whole notion that traffic tie-ups are getting worse. Peter Gordon, director of research at the University of Southern California's Planning Institute for urban and regional affairs, maintains that development sprawl and the flexibility of the personal car allow individuals and employers to find their way around jams. He points to local and national data showing shorter commute times where suburb-to-suburb drives replaced mass-transit trips to the central city. Artificial creation of high-density corridors will retard this, he says.

The reality of metro transportation may matter less than an impression of crisis as Californians decide. They truly may be dying to hop a commuter special, or simply wish others to "share the joys of rail," as the Los Angeles Times gushed in one of its weekly editorials backing the ballot measures.

Whichever, with the only real action in the Democratic primary this time, tax-and-spend items will be favored with a sympathetic turn out.

If both rail items pass, $3 billion in bonds will be authorized making California the engine that could pull along the nation. On the other hand, the ballot triangle could work against rail if voters choose the more moderate Prop. 108 over Prop. 116, but don't approve the Prop. 111 gas-tax increase on which 108 depends. It all gets as complicated as a schedule at Grand Central Terminal . . . except the polls should close on time.
