What's News -- World-Wide

  • Date: 06/05/90

GORBACHEV MET with Roh in a step toward Soviet-South Korean relations. Foreshadowing an adjustment of East Asian political and economic ties, the Soviet leader and South Korean president held talks in San Francisco and said their nations are moving toward establishing diplomatic relations. It was the first meeting between leaders of the two countries since 1948 and came at the end of Gorbachev's five-day visit to the U.S. In an address at Stanford University, he declared the Cold War "behind us," and called for "new structures" in international alliances. He also had a brief reunion with Reagan.

Baker traveled to Denmark to discuss with Shevardnadze a nine-point Western proposal on Germany's military future. The plan allows the Red Army to remain temporarily in East Germany.

---

THE SUPREME COURT RULED that public schools must allow religious clubs.

In an 8-1 decision, the justices said public schools generally must give student religious groups the same access to scheduling and facilities as other extracurricular activities. The ruling in a case from Omaha, Neb., where officials refused to permit a high school Bible-reading club, upheld a 1984 federal law aimed at allowing religious organizations in public schools. The court said the statute doesn't violate the separation of church and state.

The court ruled that police undercover agents may seek to elicit confessions from jailed criminal suspects without first giving Miranda warnings.

---

Rebels in Liberia captured a strategic military checkpoint near the international airport outside Monrovia, officials said, and President Doe appealed for U.S. assistance as the insurgents advanced upon the capital. Four U.S. warships, meanwhile, arrived off the West African nation's coast to help evacuate Americans and other foreigners.

---

Prime Minister Shamir defended the right of Soviet Jews to live in Israeli-occupied lands, saying a democracy couldn't limit where people live. His comments were in reaction to Gorbachev's statement Sunday that the Kremlin might restrict emigres to Israel if they continued to settle on land seized in the 1967 Mideast war.

---

Police in China blocked roads to Beijing's university area, and tight security elsewhere in the capital stifled dissent on the first anniversary of the government's crackdown on a pro-democracy movement. The public was barred from Tiananmen Square, center of last year's protests, and a few people trying to demonstrate were arrested.

---

A U.S. judge in Los Angeles ruled the county's supervisors violated the constitutional rights of the area's large Hispanic population when it drew voting boundaries in 1981. The decision came in a 1988 suit that alleged the supervisors illegally established boundaries to separate the Hispanic community, diluting its voting strength.

---

Jury selection began in the cocaine-and-perjury trial of Washington Mayor Barry. The official, 54, arrested Jan. 18 in an FBI sting operation, has accused U.S. prosecutors of pursuing him with the tactics of "a totalitarian kind of society." Sources said plea bargain talks between Barry's lawyers and the prosecutors were at an impasse.

---

NASA said the space shuttle Discovery sustained possible damage when an overhead bridge in a hangar at Cape Canaveral, Fla., snagged a payload door. Separately, space agency technicans prepared to make a last-ditch attempt tomorrow to find a fuel leak that caused last week's postponement of Columbia's astronomy mission.

---

Mandela traveled to Botswana, beginning a foreign tour. The black leader is to visit 13 nations, including the U.S., on his most extensive trip since being released from a South African jail in February. In Johannesburg, the government said it wouldn't force whites-only state schools to accept black pupils, despite its pledge to end apartheid.

---

Japanese officials worked to avert the collapse of Cambodian peace talks in Toyko. The discussions, aimed at ending the 11-year conflict in the Southeast Asian country, faltered after the leader of the Khmer Rouge guerrilla faction failed to attend the negotiations between the Phnom Penh government and a three-party insurgent alliance.

---

More than a million mourners gathered at the tomb of Iran's Ayatollah Khomeini to mark the first anniversary of his death. Some of those at the Islamic shrine on the outskirts of Tehran beat their chests in grief as loudspeakers at the compound blared anti-U.S. and anti-Israeli slogans.

---

The U.S. plans to begin destroying its stockpile of chemical weapons late this year, well ahead of a treaty signed Friday at the Bush-Gorbachev summit, officials in Washington said. The destruction of the weapons, currently stored in West Germany, would take place on a Pacific island.

---

Died: Jack Gilford, 81, dramatic actor, comic and vaudevillian who appeared on stage, TV and in films during a 55-year career, in New York, of stomach cancer.
