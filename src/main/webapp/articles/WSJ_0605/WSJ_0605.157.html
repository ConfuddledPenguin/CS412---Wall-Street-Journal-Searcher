Justices Back Public School Religious Club

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/05/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court upheld a federal law that requires public schools to give student religious groups the same access to scheduling and facilities that other extracurricular activities have. By an 8-1 vote, the justices ruled that a 1984 federal law requires an Omaha, Neb., high school to accommodate a Bible-reading club that students wanted to organize.

In an opinion written by Justice Sandra O'Connor, the court said the law treats extracurricular activities as part of a public forum created by the school. The federal law, the court said, means the school can't discriminate against use of the public forum based on a club's religious, political or philosophical views. This approach, Justice O'Connor said, doesn't violate the First Amendment's separation of church and state.

The eight justices in the majority were split into three opinions. Six of them, including Justice O'Connor, appeared to believe that the federal law is constitutional in all respects. Justices Anthony Kennedy and Antonin Scalia differed on how to analyze freedom of religion issues under the First Amendment, but not on the law's validity.

Justice Thurgood Marshall, joined by Justice William Brennan, agreed that the law was constitutional as applied to the Omaha school, but they warned that school officials must be careful to disassociate themselves from the religious message of the club.

Justice John Stevens dissented.

Officials at the Omaha school refused to allow the religious club to meet, but a federal appeals court in St. Louis ruled that the federal law required accommodation of the club. The high court affirmed the appeals court ruling. (Westside Community Board of Education vs. Mergens)
