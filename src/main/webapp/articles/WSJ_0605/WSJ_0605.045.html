Junk Bonds of 3 Trump Casinos Plunge On News Developer Faces Cash Crunch

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 06/05/90

NEW YORK -- The high-yield junk bonds of Trump Taj Mahal and two other casinos operated by Donald Trump tumbled sharply yesterday on news that the developer is facing a cash crunch. Trump's casino bonds, already hammered by weakness in the Atlantic City, N.J., gambling market, dived as much as nine points, or $90 for bonds with a $1,000 face amount.

The bonds recovered slightly later in the day but still wound up with heavy losses, especially for the bonds of Trump's costly new Taj Mahal casino.

While the prices of the bonds should not directly affect Mr. Trump's continuing negotiations with his bankers, they are an indication of the public's declining confidence in his ability to stay solvent.

The price decline is likely, however, to cause bondholders to put pressure on Mr. Trump to make sure that they receive their interest payments. In the past, companies have used their own depressed bond prices as a rationale for a restructuring whereby bondholders would receive less than par for their bonds.

The sell-off followed an article in this newspaper disclosing that Mr. Trump's bankers, who are owed $2 billion, are seeking a restructuring of the developer's assets that could force him to give up a chunk of his empire. The bankers fear some Trump properties aren't generating enough cash. As part of the negotiations, the bankers are opposing his making a mid-June payment of a relatively minor $30 million on certain casino bonds.

In a statement yesterday, Mr. Trump's principal holding company, Trump Organization, said the company is currently "meeting with major banks and . . . having cooperative and constructive discussions. Based upon the excellent asset values and their long-term earnings potential, we are confident we will arrive at a mutually beneficial solution."

Hardest-hit yesterday were the bonds of Mr. Trump's giant new Trump Taj Mahal, which is considered the weakest among the developer's three casino hotels because it is the newest and has the heaviest debt load. The Taj Mahal, which opened in April, requires $1.3 million in revenue a day to break even. Trump Taj Mahal Funding 14% bonds due in 1998 plunged eight points, or $80 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount, to close at a price of 57 on the New York Stock Exchange.

Trump Plaza Funding's 12 7/8% bonds due in 1998 fell four points to close at a price of 81. Trump Castle Funding's 13 3/4% bonds due in 1997 fell eight points to end at 61.

David Feinman, a junk-bond trader at Los Angeles-based Jefferies & Co., said investors dumped Trump bonds for fear they would continue to erode on any further news of financial strains for Mr. Trump. "When approaching any troubled situation, people would rather sell now" and cut their losses, he said, "rather than wake up two days from now" and find prices even lower. "This market is very sensitive to negative press."

Mr. Feinman said trading volume in Trump bonds yesterday wasn't especially heavy. He said the thin trading may have exacerbated the decline. Overall, the developer's interests have more than $1 billion in bond debt outstanding, in addition to the bank loans. The Wall Street Journal reported that bankers have been resisting the payment of roughly $30 million in interest due in mid-June to bondholders of the Trump Castle casino. The bankers were said to be opposed to Mr. Trump's making the payment until steps can be taken to ensure that payments due them will also be made.

Trump has a $50 million bank loan outstanding on the Trump Castle property to finance expansion of the site, analysts say.

Some high-yield junk analysts are skeptical that banks could prevent Donald Trump from making the approaching interest payments on the Trump Castle bonds.

"It's like Visa telling you not to make your home mortgage payment because you have a loan" on your charge card, says one high-yield analyst. "The only recourse the banks have is that they can put the obligation back to Donald personally."

Ann Lorsung, an analyst at McCarthy Crisanti & Maffei Inc., said the Trump Taj Mahal bonds were slammed the hardest because "it looks like the casino's requirements for external financing are greater than expected." The three casinos' bonds aren't personally guaranteed by Mr. Trump.

Mr. Trump declined to comment through a spokesman.
