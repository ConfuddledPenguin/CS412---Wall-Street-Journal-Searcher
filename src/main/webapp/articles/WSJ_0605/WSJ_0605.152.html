Citicorp Will End Its Underwriting Of Municipal Issues

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 06/05/90

NEW YORK -- In a further sign of Wall Street's ills, Citicorp said it is ending its underwriting, distribution and trading of municipal securities, a move that will cut 35 jobs from its payroll. Instead, Citicorp will focus on offering so-called derivative products -- instruments that enable issuers to hedge interest-rate and other risks -- for bonds issued by state and local governments.

In an interview, Glen Sergeon, managing director for Citicorp Securities Markets Inc., said: "To some extent, it is an excess capacity issue. It's a marketplace where there are a lot of players. We will focus on our strengths in derivatives. It's the place where we have an advantage."

Mr. Sergeon said the change won't affect various other services offered to municipal issuers, including investment agreements, short-term remarketing and distribution agreements and various credit and liquidity services. He said the company is attempting to place the 35 people in other jobs.

Citicorp is the latest big underwriter to defect from the municipals market. In 1987, Salomon Brothers dropped its municipals unit, amid complaints that the business's profitability had deteriorated severely.

The move also offers the latest evidence that Citicorp is no longer attempting to be all things to all customers. Instead, it is focusing on areas where it can make good returns. So far, that has meant limiting its underwriting of corporate debt securities, trimming its U.S. government bond business, and emphasizing fee-generating activities in its commercial real estate lending unit.

The municipals market has been suffering from severe competitive pressures since the passage of the 1986 tax act, which placed a ceiling on the amount of tax-exempt bonds that states can issue to support private activities, such as housing developments.

In 1986, the market's peak year, $222.2 billion in new municipals went to market, and underwriters on average made $20.95 for each thousand dollars of a bond's face value, according to the Public Securities Association. By last year, volume dwindled to $124.3 billion and the so-called spread fell to $13.44 for each thousand dollars in face value.

Citicorp last year ranked 16th in long-term municipal underwritings. In 1987, Citicorp got permission from the Federal Reserve Board to underwrite some types of municipal securities that had previously been off-limits to commercial banks.

Andrew Nybo, a research analyst for the securities association, estimated that 1990 issuance will rise to $135 billion for the industry. Longer term, he said, volume will climb as state and municipalities rebuild aging public facilities and federal revenue sharing tails off.

Citicorp's announcement came after the market closed. In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Citicorp closed at $24.375, up 37.5 cents.
