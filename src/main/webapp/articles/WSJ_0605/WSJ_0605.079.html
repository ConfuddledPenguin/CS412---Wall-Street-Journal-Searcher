Technology: Noyce Death Casts Shadow Over Group Designed to Boost Semiconductor Firms

  • Author: Andy Zipser
  • Date: 06/05/90

The death of Robert N. Noyce casts a shadow over Sematech, the semiconductor group that Mr. Noyce, an advocate for U.S. intervention in the semiconductor war with Japan, took over two years ago. Mr. Noyce, co-founder of both Fairchild Semiconductor and Intel Corp. and co-inventor of the integrated circuit, had campaigned for "a new culture" in response to Japanese semiconductor competition. For Mr. Noyce, that meant increased cooperation among U.S. companies and greater involvement by government, a position at odds with the Bush administration.

Industry observers said Mr. Noyce's death weakens such lobbying efforts, which had found few supporters outside the industry. "He carried an image that will be hard to replace, no question about that," said Richard Skinner, president of Integrated Circuit Engineering Corp., a consulting firm in Arizona. "But as for impact, there won't be any -- nobody was listening to him."

Mr. Noyce conducted his campaign as chairman of Sematech, an Austin, Texas, group of 14 technology companies including International Business Machines Corp., American Telephone & Telegraph Corp., Texas Instruments Inc. and Motorola Inc. The research group, whose goal is to bring U.S. semiconductor manufacturing methods into parity with the Japanese by 1992, receives half of its $200 million annual budget from the federal government.

But the unprecedented private-public partnership provoked criticism from companies that claimed Sematech was competing unfairly against nonmembers, as well as from conservative politicians. At times, it appeared that Sematech's strongest claim to legitimacy was Mr. Noyce himself.

More recently, Sematech officials say, Mr. Noyce, who took the top job when the search committee he was heading couldn't find a suitable chief executive, believed that the group had weathered political challenges and could survive without him. The research facility had been built, the plant had produced its first wafers, and the Bush administration had reversed its early opposition to continued funding of the effort. Indeed, say Sematech officials, Mr. Bush has hailed the effort as a leading example of U.S. competitiveness.

At the April board meeting, Mr. Noyce requested that a search committee be formed to find a new chief executive and indicated that he would like to retire in the fall. A few weeks later, he had a complete physical and received a clean bill of health. He died Sunday of a heart attack at age 62.

Charles Sporck, chairman of the Sematech board and president and chief executive of National Semiconductor Inc., declined requests for interviews but issued a statement acknowledging that a new chief executive must be found "expeditiously." Company officials, meanwhile, say the office of chief executive, which also included Turner Hasty, chief operating officer, and Peter H. Mills, chief administrative officer, continues to run the group.

"Bob Noyce was a master team builder and a master consensus builder," said Mr. Mills, contending that Sematech can function indefinitely until a new chief executive is named.
