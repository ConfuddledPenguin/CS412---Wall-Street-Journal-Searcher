Technology: Sony to Market Digital Tape Decks in U.S.

  • Author: Stephen Kreider Yoder
  • Date: 06/05/90

CHICAGO -- Sony Corp. said it will begin shipping this month in the U.S. a tape deck called the digital audio tape, or DAT, recorder. DAT machines record and play music with almost no distortion. Sony is the first major Japanese electronics concern to announce a firm price and shipping date for DAT recorders in the U.S. While DAT machines have a small following in Japan, most Japanese companies haven't sold the decks in the U.S. because of opposition from the recording industry, which argues that the decks will inspire pirating.

Sony's entry could change that sheepishness. Japan's electronics companies typically are quick to follow the lead of companies such as Sony into new markets.

Sony said it will begin shipping later this month two DAT models, the $900 DTC-700 and the $950 DTC-75ES. The decks, which can record digital music directly from compact disks, contain circuitry meant to prevent rampant copying. Called the Serial Copy Management System, the circuitry allows the user to make only one digital copy from a digital music source, such as a compact disk, that contains a special copy-prevention signal.

The machine is too highly priced to be accepted by the mass of U.S. consumers, who tend to put off purchases of electronic equipment until prices fall below $500. And there is little music that is pre-recorded on DAT cartridges, which are slightly smaller than conventional audio cassette tapes. Industry executives say there must be a critical mass of thousands of tapes available before sales of the decks take off.

But Sony said its machines will sell for a street price of under $800 and that future machines would come down in price rapidly.

"Once recording companies see DAT as a viable format, they'll come to the party," said Martin Homlish, president of Sony Audio Component System Co., the Sony Unit that sells audio equipment in the U.S. "It just may take a little time, as it did with the compact disk, which is now the bread-and-butter of the record industry."

Mr. Homlish said Sony expects Americans to buy about 100,000 DAT players over the next 12 months. In contrast, about 4.5 million compact-disk players are sold in the U.S. every year, he said.

Sony said its Sony Classical unit this month will distribute 10 prerecorded $19.95 DAT cassettes featuring such musicians as Vladimir Horowitz, Isaac Stern, Zubin Mehta and Yo-Yo Ma. Sony said its Sony Magnetic Products Co. unit will sell blank DAT cartridges for $12 to $18.

Sony's announcement wasn't unexpected: Last January it said it would begin selling the machines this summer, but didn't give a firm date or prices. Rival Matsushita Electric Industrial Co. also said in January that it would sell a DAT machine this year. Other audio heavyweights, such as Pioneer Electronic Corp., said this month that they don't plan to sell DAT machines until the prospects of their use are more certain.
