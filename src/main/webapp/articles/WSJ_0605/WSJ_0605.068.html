Morgan's Fogg Gets Investment Banking Post

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 06/05/90

NEW YORK -- Joseph G. Fogg III, one of Wall Street's best-known dealmakers, emerged as a major winner in the latest management shuffle at Morgan Stanley Group Inc., the big investment bank. Mr. Fogg, who saw his power clipped in 1985, although he remained a powerful figure at Morgan Stanley, yesterday was named head of the firm's investment banking division.

The head of the firm's world-wide corporate finance department since 1985, Mr. Fogg, 43 years old, now will oversee the firm's entire investment banking operation, including corporate finance, mergers and acquisitions, capital markets and real estate.

The appointment helps fill the gap left when Robert Greenhill -- former vice chairman and head of investment banking -- was recently slated by Morgan Stanley management to be president.

But Mr. Fogg won't get to devour the entire loaf left by Mr. Greenhill; as part of the reshuffling, Donald P. Brennan was named to head a newly created merchant banking division, which formerly had been folded into investment banking.

Under Mr. Brennan, the firm's five-year-old merchant banking operations have grown to be a major driver in the success of Morgan Stanley. The upshot of the moves: Morgan Stanley's investment banking division effectively will be split into two. (Mr. Greenhill's vice chairmanship will be left vacant.)

The changes cap a management shift at one of Wall Street's most successful investment banks. Three weeks ago, S. Parker Gilbert, chairman for six years, said he would retire at year end; Morgan Stanley's new chairman will be Richard Fisher, who as president has played a major role in the firm's strategic direction in recent years.

Both Messrs. Fogg and Brennan will report to Messrs. Gilbert, Fisher and Greenhill.

By focusing on its international strengths and high-profit areas such as merchant banking, mergers and acquisitions and trading for its own account, Morgan Stanley has blossomed into one of Wall Street's most profitable firms. Despite a sharp downturn on Wall Street, Morgan Stanley's return on equity currently stands at 27%, the highest among publicly traded investment banks.

Analysts said splitting up Morgan Stanley's investment-banking division makes sense.

"In merchant banking, you wind up becoming owner and partial manager; in the investment banking business, you're giving advice," says Alison Deans, an analyst for Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. "That requires different types of relationships."

For Mr. Fogg, the promotion is a homecoming of sorts. He joined the firm nearly 20 years ago as one of the founding members of Morgan Stanley's mergers and acquisitions department. He headed that area for several years before being shunted aside to head corporate finance, when Eric Gleacher took over the top mergers post. Several months ago, Mr. Gleacher left Morgan Stanley to form his own mergers and acquisition firm.

In an interview, Mr. Fogg said he was happy to "be able to play more of a role back in M&A." But he says he never really left it. "Even while at corporate finance, the business I've done separate from the administrative work very heavily focuses on M&A, particularly takeover defenses," he says.

Mr. Fogg has represented Phillips Petroleum, CBS Inc., American Brands Inc., Carter Hawley Hale Stores Inc. and Avon Products Inc., among others, in resisting unsolicited hostile takeover attempts.

So far this year amid a merger slump, Morgan Stanley ranks No. 2 behind CS First Boston Group in advising on takeover deals. Through yesterday, Morgan Stanley advised on 32 deals totaling $15 billion, for a 13% market share, according to Securities Data Co. In the year-earlier period, Morgan Stanley advised on 83 deals, valued at $19.6 billion, for a 9.4% market share.

Mr. Brennan's assignment reflects the increased importance of merchant banking within the firm. Launched five years ago, Morgan Stanley's merchant banking division, which invests both the firm's and clients' money in deals, currently has more than $20 billion in assets invested in about 20 companies. Morgan Stanley's investment represents about 15% of the firm's equity capital.

Mr. Brennan, 49, said in an interview that merchant banking, despite its potential pitfalls, will be even a bigger focus within Morgan Stanley. "We will be increasing by both the addition of investments to our portfolio as well as an appreciation of existing" investments, he said.

Despite his new title, however, Mr. Brennan's responsibilities largely will remain the same. As head of the merchant banking operations, he will oversee activities such as leveraged buy-outs, venture capital, the structuring of high-yield junk bonds and the investment of equity capital for the firm's own account.

Mr. Brennan also will remain chairman and president of Morgan Stanley's $2.25 billion leverage buy-out fund, which is currently about 50% invested.
