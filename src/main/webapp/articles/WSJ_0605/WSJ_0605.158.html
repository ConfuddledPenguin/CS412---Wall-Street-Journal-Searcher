Technology: Commodore Unveils System Linking CD, Computer Functions

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/05/90

WEST CHESTER, Pa. -- Commodore International Ltd. introduced a system that combines the capabilities of a personal computer and compact disk player. The Commodore system, called the CDTV player, consists of an Amiga personal computer -- without keyboard or monitor -- and a CD player. The system is hooked up to a television to display video and text.

The CDTV Player makes the personal computer maker an early entrant into a market that many computer companies consider to be vast. The companies think that the home market for computers is finally about to pick up with the introduction of multimedia machines. For instance, they expect people to buy encyclopedias on computer disks with video and audio capabilities, or instructional videos that they can interact with through the computer.

Commodore has also broken through a price barrier. Its CDTV Player, whose shipment date wasn't specified, will cost less than $1,000. Application disks will cost just $25 to $60.

Still, the market for such systems is very young and will take time to develop. The various companies involved haven't agreed on what technological standards they will follow, and it usually takes the market quite some time to sort out those issues. It's also not clear how people will use these systems, and whether they will want reference books or just more games. In any case, it may take a couple of years for companies to produce enough application disks to make these systems attractive.

"Commodore has a history of producing great hardware, but they don't have enough software available," said Greg Drahuschak, a securities analyst at Wheat First/Butcher & Singer. "That is where Commodore has always fallen short."
