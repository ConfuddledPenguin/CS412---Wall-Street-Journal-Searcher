Credit Markets: Lacking Major News, Treasurys Rise Slightly On Stronger Dollar, Lower Commodity Prices

  • Author: Susan Scherreik and Sharon R. King Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/05/90

NEW YORK -- A stronger dollar and falling commodity prices helped bond prices post modest gains yesterday. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose 1/4 point yesterday, or about $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, eased to 8.42%.

Traders said the prices started to firm as the value of the dollar rose against the yen. The dollar ended at 152.40 yen, up from 151.85 yen late Friday. Meanwhile, the Commodity Research Bureau's futures index, which comprises 21 commodities, fell 1.25 points to 239.23. The CRB index includes crude oil futures, which fell 36 cents to settle at $17.14 in the July contract.

The dollar and commodities held center stage largely because there is little else for players to focus on, the analysts and traders say.

"It's brutally quiet," said one dealer. The unusually light calendar of economic releases this week is largely responsible for the dull trading, which analysts say should continue for the near term.

"The dollar and lower commodity prices have helped out, but we're pretty much biding time until next week's data," says Kevin Flanagan, a money market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

Lynn Reaser, a vice president and senior economist at First Interstate Bank Ltd. agrees: "The market is digesting Friday's very large gains and waiting for more news on economic growth and inflation." And "the fact that the market has been able to retain the gains from Friday is positive," said Ms. Reaser. "There's been very little profit taking."

Traders said that investors aren't cashing in on the profits they reaped Friday when bond prices surged because they expect prices to rise further when key reports on last month's retail sales and producer and consumer prices are issued next week.

On Friday, bond prices rose as much as 1 1/2 points in response to the May employment report, which suggested a more sluggish economy than many had forecast.

Treasury Securities

Treasury bonds ended modestly higher in quiet trading. The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 103 15/32 to yield 8.42% compared with 103 8/32 to yield 8.44% on Friday.

The latest 10-year notes ended at 102 26/32 to yield 8.43% compared with 102 22/32 to yield 8.45%.

The government sold a total of $17.25 billion of three-month and six-month Treasury bills yesterday at its regular weekly auction.

The average discount rate on the three-month bills was 7.69% while the average rate on six-month bills was 7.62%. The rates were lower than at last week's auction when they were 7.80% and 7.74%, respectively.

The average rate on three-month bills was the lowest since the average of 7.67% at the auction on May 14, 1990. The rate on six-month bills was the lowest since the average of 7.58% at the auction on Jan. 22, 1990.

Junk Bonds

The junk bonds of Trump Taj Funding and two other casinos run by developer Donald Trump plunged yesterday on reports that the developer is in the throes of a cash squeeze. The report, which was in yesterday's Wall Street Journal, drove Trump bonds down as much as 8 points.

However, Trump casino bonds weren't the only junk bonds to take a beating in yesterday's bond market.

Although most junk bonds, or speculative-grade bonds with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, ended modestly higher yesterday, the bonds of Charter Medical Corp. sank.

Charter's bonds weakened on news that the psychiatric-care company will close about 25 outpatient counseling centers and idle about 600 employees across the country. Bids for Charter Medical's 14% senior subordinated debentures, due 2000, are off about two points at 52.

Meanwhile, the junk bonds of Harcourt Brace Jovanovich Inc. gained on a report that British publisher Robert Maxwell is still interested in acquiring the U.S. publishing giant.

Mr. Maxwell, chairman and chief executive officer of Maxwell Communication Corp., declined to comment on whether he has been in talks with Harcourt regarding a possible acquisition.

Harcourt's 13% senior notes, due in 1997, advanced 1/2 point to close at 95 3/4, while the firm's 13.75% senior subordinated debentures, maturing in 1999, also climbed 1/2 point to 81.

The giant publisher's senior securities have tended to advance whenever there is talk about a possible acquisition of the company.

Ben Bendre, a high-yield analyst at Salomon Brothers Inc., attributes the run-up to the expectation that the 13% senior notes would be bought back at par with proceeds from an asset sale.

According to covenants governing the 13% notes, proceeds from an asset sale must be used to pay off bank debt first and then for the buy-back of the company's senior bonds.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities were little changed after light trading.

Government National Mortgage Association 9% securities for June delivery ended at 97 1/32, unchanged; 9.5% issues were at 99 11/32, down 1/32; 10% issues were at 101 3/4, unchanged, and Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities stood at 98 3/4, up 1/32.

In derivative markets, the Federal National Mortgage Association issued two real estate mortgage investment conduits totaling $500 million and Freddie Mac issued one Remic for $300 million.

Fannie Mae issued a $300 million issue backed by its 10.5% mortgage securities through Kidder, Peabody & Co. and a $200 million issue backed by 9.5% 15-year securities through Prudential-Bache Securities Inc.

Freddie Mac issued the $300 million issue backed by 10% securities through Morgan Stanley & Co.

Municipal Bonds

Tax-exempt bonds ended unchanged to 1/4 point higher in quiet trading.

Bond dealers said market participants are bracing for New York State's annual spring borrowing, expected to consist of $4.1 billion tax and revenue-anticipation notes via negotiation on Thursday.

The 1990 spring borrowing will be one of the state's largest.

Because of New York's recurring fiscal problems, market participants expect that the state's short-term tax and revenue-anticipation notes may receive a MIG-2 rating from Moody's Investors Service Inc. -- one notch below the MIG-1 rating last year's spring borrowing received.

They also expect Moody's to downgrade the state's long-term debt rating, now at single-A-1. In addition, an SP-1 rating -- below last year's level of SP-1-Plus -- is expected from Standard & Poor's Corp., several traders say. Both agencies have the notes under review.

With a rating cut in mind, traders said yields on the New York bonds are expected to come in between 6.20% and 6.35%, although some estimates are as high as 6.50%. Underwriting officials declined to comment on price talk.

Late Friday, New York State Comptroller Edward Regan announced that he had certified the state's $49.8 billion budget, clearing the way for the spring borrowing to take place.

Despite the possibility of a credit rating cut, traders reported strong retail investor interest in the notes because of the expected higher yield.

"It's been sort of a blessing that it's taken so long" to adopt the budget, one trader says. "We've got orders lined up, a stack of them."

Foreign Bonds

British government bonds ended higher in quiet trading, with activity muted due to the Whit Monday holiday that was observed in most European countries.

Traders said that British bonds, called gilts, firmed during early trading in sympathy with Friday's rally in the U.S. Treasury market. The British market then dipped on an upward revision of U.K. retail sales data and drifted through the remainder of the session.

Britain's benchmark 11 3/4% bond due 2003/2007 rose 15/32 to 101 3/8 to yield 11.54% while the 12% notes due in 1995 rose 5/32 to 99 14/32 to yield 12.17%.

Friday's bond rally in the U.S. also bolstered Japanese bonds yesterday. The price of the benchmark 10-year Japanese government bond ended at 88.33 to yield 6.90%. (The yield is calculated in local terms, but would be lower if calculated by the semiannual compound measure that is used in the U.S.)

West German financial markets were closed.
