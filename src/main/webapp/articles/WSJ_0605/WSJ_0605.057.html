Working in Aerospace -- A Special Report --- Labor Letter: A Special News Report on People And Their Jobs in Offices, Fields and Factories

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 06/05/90

THE AX FALLS FASTER than most expected on aerospace workers. U.S.-Soviet detente and congressional budget-cutting hasten pink slips for white-collar and hourly employees alike. McDonnell Douglas, Lockheed, GE, General Dynamics and some small firms disclose plans to trim rolls. The Aerospace Industries Association tracks 16,000 job-cut notices among its 54 members in 1990, 28% ahead of its forecast. "This may just be the beginning," warns AIA economist David Napier.

The passenger-plane business, once viewed as a cushion, doesn't help much: Boeing isn't ramping up new programs, so it's laying off, too, and McDonnell Douglas's commercial unit flounders. Firms search for new niches to keep employment stable, but changing course is tough.

"There will always be some growth areas," such as electronics, says Karen Berk of the job-matching Aerospace Human Resources Network.

HANDLING HAZARDS lessen for use of aircraft "composites."

As plane makers add more of the light, reinforced fibers in their designs, they alert workers on how to better ply toxic chemicals that strengthen and coat composites.

Worker complaints decline with increased industry concern, better training and new cautionary signs posted in plants, the Machinists union says. A third of the cost of Lockheed's new Palmdale, Calif., composites center is for proper ventilation and other health controls.

An Occupational Safety and Health Administration crackdown last year at Boeing and Lockheed helped spur the effort. Now, manufacturers push suppliers of advanced composites to set specific exposure limits. "If we don't do it, they're not going to buy from us," says David Forest, head of BASF Structural Materials.

PEACETIME "CONVERSION" moves ahead at federal and state levels.

House members prepare wording for the defense authorization bill on the need to help defense workers brace for the changeover. It would list worker qualifications for relocation and other allowances when defense cuts hit. More controversial ideas, such as requiring weapons makers to pay for retraining and benefits, will be saved for floor debate.

"We want to make sure at least something gets on the table," says Rep. Nick Mavroules (D., Mass.), a member of a bipartisan House-Senate task force on the issue. California mulls a worker-retraining program; in Minnesota, stung by big layoffs by Unisys, Honeywell and other defense firms, a new 0.1% payroll tax raises worker aid. Over 40 aerospace firms met in Dayton, Ohio, last week to share tips on switching to non-military work.

One big challenge, says Ohio Gov. Celeste: meeting the civilian sector's tougher delivery standards.

LOWER YOUR WAGE RATES, the Pentagon tells Lockheed, Boeing and other companies. They must bring pay in line with other firms in their areas, or the government says it will find other sources. The Defense Contract Audit Agency contests $222 million in "unreasonable" labor charges. Unions grouse that contractors are exploiting the issue at the bargaining table.

BLACK PROGRAMS, WHITE FACES: Asians, Hispanics and blacks make up only 16% of workers in aerospace, with its often secret, or "black," programs, lagging behind the overall economy, the General Accounting Office finds. General Dynamics's space division agrees to offer employment and $270,000 in back wages to 21 minority engineers whom the Labor Department says were wrongly denied jobs.

BRAIN DRAIN: Of 30 companies surveyed by the AIA, two-thirds say they're short of scientists and engineers, and 85% expect such problems in the future. So firms do more to support education, with 40% of corporate contributions going for aid, such as scholarships.

OLD AEROSPACE WORKERS don't fade away -- they go back to work.

More cost-conscious jet makers take back retirees, for quick, complex assignments. "We know what they're capable of," says Vince DeBlaze, human resources director at McDonnell Douglas's jet-fighter factory in St. Louis, which employs about 200 of its retirees. While fast-food firms hire many retirees for low-skill work, "we've shown you can do it with high-tech," says ACS Management Group Inc.

Bob Stein, a manager at Fairchild Controls, says a former Hughes master scheduler he hired briefly for a new program to build environmental controls for jet-fighter components "was able to work faster and smarter -- and then press on."

ACS's Graybeard division, a job shop for former aerospace technicians, has 2,300 resumes.

THE CHECKOFF: Machinists at Lockheed's soon-to-be-shuttered, 62-year-old factory in Burbank, Calif., staged a rally last weekend to keep their jobs. . . . Boeing says that for each job created outside the U.S. by its "foreign sourcing," 20 domestic jobs are spawned. . . . Northrop holds job fairs to recruit workers for its B-2 Stealth bomber project, saying, "You just might wind up making history."
