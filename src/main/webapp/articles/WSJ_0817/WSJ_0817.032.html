Technology & Medicine: Hewlett-Packard Reports 5% Drop In 3rd-Quarter Net

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 08/17/90

Hewlett-Packard Co., citing stiff competition and cuts in defense spending, reported a 5% drop in net income for its third quarter ended July 31. The Palo Alto, Calif., maker of computers and electronic instruments posted net of $178 million, or 73 cents a share, compared with $187 million, or 79 cents a share, in the year-earlier period. Analysts expected HP to earn about 80 cents a share. Revenue for the quarter increased 8% to $3.2 billion.

HP said its results were hurt by weaker spending in defense, aerospace and health services and by loss of market share in personal computers and workstations, where competition is fierce and HP's offerings lately have lagged the industry.

In response, analysts cut slightly estimates for HP's fourth quarter and fiscal 1991 earnings, but said they were pleased with the company's efforts to hold down increases in operating expenses, which grew 6% in the third quarter.

"Expenses were the upside surprise," said Steven Milunovich, an analyst at First Boston Corp. "They're doing the job there."

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, HP's stock fell $1.875 a share to close at $35.875 a share.

However, growth in orders for the quarter slowed, with orders rising 9% from the year-earlier period to $3.3 billion. The company's growth in orders is generally higher. The sluggishness "led us to view the months ahead with caution," said John Young, HP's chief executive, in a prepared statement.

While part of the slowdown is due to global economic factors, the company's difficulties in PCs and workstations -- two expanding markets -- are especially worrisome. Last year, the company became the biggest supplier of workstations, desktop computers used by engineers and scientists, after purchasing Apollo Computer Inc. But this year Sun Microsystems Inc. has overtaken HP, which is in a battle for second place with Digital Equipment Corp.

Mr. Young, in an interview earlier this week, vowed that "Sun isn't going to run away with this marketplace" and that the company planned to introduce more competitive models later this year.

In PCs, HP had been gaining momentum in recent years, so the slowdown here is a surprise. While the company has never broken out of the "second tier" of PC suppliers, it hopes to win important niche markets with new models based on Intel Corp.'s state-of-the-art 486 microprocessor. But sales of those models are disappointing, company officials told analysts.

To counter slippage in PCs, Mr. Young yesterday named Jacques Clay as the PC group's new chief and said the group's headquarters will move from Sunnyvale, Calif., to Grenoble, France, where Mr. Clay has run the company's European PC division. HP's former PC chief joined Apple Computer Inc. recently.

Improving its market position in PCs and workstations is critical to HP's future because its minicomputer business, once a mainstay, is under siege. "The minicomputer business is faced with obsolescence," said Peter Rogers, an analyst with Robertson Stephens & Co.
