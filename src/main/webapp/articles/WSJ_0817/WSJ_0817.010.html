Letters to the Editor: Air Force, Secretary Don't Deserve the Flak

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/17/90

It's difficult to recognize the real U.S. Air Force and its secretary, Donald B. Rice, from your July 9 profile of them. You inaccurately single out the Air Force as the service most resistant to charge and inattentive to program management. On the one hand, Secretary Rice and the Air Force are depicted as stubbornly inflexible. Elsewhere, they are faulted for adjusting priorities and "turning their backs" on ICBM modernization.

In response to changing global circumstances and budget realities at home, the Air Force agreed to terminate its F-15E long-range fighter, cut the B-2 bomber and C-17 airlifter programs sharply, and make major reductions in its force structure. Your profile, however, does not tell us about these or other changes.

You do not mention that the Air Force has been the pace setter on the Total Force concept, transferring functions whenever possible to the Guard and the Reserve. We are not told the Air Force stood second to none in finding economies called for by the recent Defense Management Review.

You seem to hold Secretary Rice to account personally for imputed shortcomings. Few people who have seen this man at work would ever describe him as a prisoner of the past. In his 14 months in office, he has already gained a reputation as a tough-minded activist.

Jack C. Price

President

Air Force Association

Arlington, Va.

---

Just like the boundless vastness of the skies, Air Force research and development are limitless. This is a challenging area of imaginative strategies and constant innovation. The unending evolvement of superior aircraft and the ensuing conception of effective revolutionary strategies are all necessary to the formulation of a positive defense -- or, if necessary, offense.

The problems faced by Secretary Rice and Gen. Welch and their associates require support and understanding, not undeserved fire or the incendiary observations of critics, which have little substance under scrutiny and are products of jealous rivalry.

In this era of high technology, the conception, design, manufacture and utilization of superior equipment costs a lot of money. The days of simply making a spear longer or armor plate thicker are long gone.

Frederick Pederson

Major, U.S. Army Air Force

World War II

La Crosse, Wis.
