Politics & Policy: U.S. to Remove Some Trade Curbs With Austria

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 08/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. will extend to Austria nearly the same privileges in high-technology trade that it gives its European and Pacific allies, the Commerce Department said. The department granted Austria the new status after being assured that the country is satisfactorily enforcing export controls established by its revised foreign trade law. Until now, Switzerland, Finland, Sweden and Singapore have been the only neutral countries enjoying most of the export-licensing benefits that the U.S. normally accords its allies.

The U.S. move will allow U.S. vendors to ship higher-quality computers and other electronics products to Austria without a license. U.S. exporters also will be allowed to ship large numbers of even higher-level computers under single distribution licenses. Austria, moreover, will gain the right to import the most advanced U.S. mainframes without licensing paperwork, provided the equipment is bought by state-controlled enterprises and will remain on the enterprises' own premises.

Austrian diplomats welcomed the U.S. action, asserting that the new trade privileges will enhance Vienna's effort to attract U.S. companies interested in establishing European regional headquarters with sophisticated computer capabilities.

The U.S. granted Austria somewhat lesser benefits in 1987, shortly after Vienna began requiring export permits for certain strategic goods that previously had crossed its borders freely. But the U.S. said at the time that it would withhold other benefits until it was satisfied that Austria's busy free-free zones were being adequately policed.

Austria's new status also allows it to freely export to Western Europe, Japan and Australia computers containing most kinds of U.S.-made semiconductors and other components. Previously, Austrian computer makers have been required in many instances to get U.S. permission to export products containing such U.S. parts.
