Manufacturers Closes Its Swedish, Swiss Operations

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease and Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 08/17/90

NEW YORK -- Manufacturers Hanover Trust Co. said it closed offices in Zurich, Geneva and Stockholm as part of the bank's previously announced world-wide restructuring. The move comes as other big U.S. banks are reducing their presence in Europe because of the high costs of doing business there and intense competition. In recent years foreign banks have become more skilled at performing many of the corporate and investment services that U.S. banks had built their European businesses around. At the same time, most major U.S. banks have turned more of their attention to serving the domestic market, where competition from foreign banks isn't as stiff.

James Brokken, Manufacturers group vice president for Europe, said the bank's restructuring in Europe is designed to emphasize Manufacturers's investment banking expertise and reduce the bank's commercial banking operations. He said that Manufacturers already has reduced its presence in such countries as Belgium, Luxembourg and Greece. But at the same time the bank is expanding operations in Turkey, Portugal, Spain and Norway.

"We've done a good job of narrowing the focus in Europe," he said. "I don't believe we're perfect, and we're not earning what I want to, but we're not a big problem for the bank."

Mr. Brokken said Manufacturers has been scaling down its Swiss operations for some time and finally concluded that it could best serve Swiss clients from outside Switzerland.

"If you look at what we can do for our clients in Switzerland, it's not in Switzerland. It's pan-European, first-class risk management in currencies and interest rates and we can deliver that in a much more professional way in London."

The decision to withdraw from Sweden was also based on servicing clients from other locations as well as the return on the bank's investment there, said Mr. Brokken. "At the end of the day, in a world in which you have to be careful about every penny you spend, it wasn't worth the effort." He added that Manufacturers will maintain small representative offices in Switzerland and Sweden.

Manufacturers Hanover, a unit of Manufacturers Hanover Corp., hasn't been the only U.S. bank to pull back in Europe. In the past few years, Chase Manhattan Bank sold a Dutch bank with $5 billion in assets to France's Credit Lyonnais, and Bank America Corp. sold its Italian bank to West Germany's Deutsche Bank AG. U.S. banks have also been unloading operations in Asia.

Some European bankers are puzzled by the U.S. banks' retreat from abroad. They have contended that U.S. banks are surrendering markets and relationships that took years to establish and won't be easily built up again in the future. Foreign bankers also argue that such sales decrease the competitive position of U.S. banks globally.

U.S. bankers, on the other hand, retort that profitability is more important than maintaining costly overseas operations.
