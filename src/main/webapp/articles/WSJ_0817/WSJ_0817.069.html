Marketing & Media: Chrysler, Armed With New Survey, Challenges Toyota --- Ads Claim `Import Intenders' Prefer Its Compact Cars To Japanese Competitors

  • Author: Bradley A. Stertz
  • Date: 08/17/90

HIGHLAND PARK, Mich. -- Anxious to keep sales from slipping further this year, Chrysler Corp. is trotting out another survey that once again asserts its cars are better than Japanese models. This time, Chrysler is saying that two groups of California consumers -- consisting of people who own only domestic-brand cars -- preferred Chrysler models over competing cars made by Toyota Motor Corp. Specifically, Chrysler's advertising says, the consumers picked the Dodge Spirit and Plymouth Acclaim over the Toyota Camry, and chose the Dodge Shadow and Plymouth Sundance over the Toyota Tercel.

Last May, Chrysler began an aggressive advertising campaign that said another group of buyers picked the same Chrysler models over Honda Motor Company's Accord and Civic. As with the earlier claims, the latest surveys were conducted with groups of 100 "import intenders," defined by Chrysler as domestic-brand car owners "who are thinking about buying an imported car." The surveys didn't ask any owners of foreign cars for their evaluations.

Chrysler selected so-called import intenders "because these are the people we're trying to get to take a look at what we have these days," says Chrysler spokesman Tom Jakobowski. But some advertising executives and pollsters have criticized the Chrysler survey as being too limited in the types and numbers of consumers surveyed.

Chrysler is aiming its advertising squarely at Japanese car makers, who claimed a record 26% share of the U.S. market last year and have continued their gains this year.

Chrysler, on the other hand, has seen its share of the U.S. car market fall to 8.8% so far this year, from 10.4% a year earlier. When the year is over, Chrysler may wind up in fifth place -- behind Honda and Toyota -- in sales of cars alone, although Chrysler would still lead those companies in combined sales of cars, trucks and minivans.

While Chrysler's car-market share remained flat after the ads challenging Honda, the campaign seemed to help boost sales of the compact Spirit and Acclaim. Sales of those models, which have received high quality ratings, jumped 19% to 112,052 cars in the first seven months of this year. But some of the gain is attributable to slower production last year when the cars were just beginning to hit the market. Sales of the Shadow and Sundance were off 19.8% through the end of July.

In fact, so far this year Honda has sold more U.S.-built Accords and Toyota more U.S.-made Camrys than Chrysler has sold Spirits and Acclaims combined. Likewise, U.S.-made Honda Civic subcompacts have outsold the Shadow and Sundance.

Chrysler started airing two commercials with the Toyota comparison on network and cable television Wednesday, and it will run print ads beginning Monday. Unlike the Honda comparisons, the new broadcast campaign won't feature Chrysler Chairman Lee A. Iacocca. Instead an announcer will say during the ads: "The cars that beat the Honda Civic now beat Toyota. Who's next?"

Chrysler commissioned the survey from U.S. Testing Co. Market Research Inc., the same Wheeling, Ill., firm that conducted the Honda comparisons. Chrysler said the two groups of consumers, consisting of owners of General Motors Corp. and Ford Motor Co. cars, tested the Chrysler and Toyota models over a 2.4-mile route and were asked to compare 33 attributes. The consumers weren't told that the test was sponsored by Chrysler, the company said.

Of the 100 consumers in the Toyota Camry LE comparison, Chrysler said 80 picked the Spirit and Acclaim. In the Honda comparison, Chrysler said 83 preferred its two models to the Accord.

The group that tested the Shadow and Sundance against the Toyota Tercel Deluxe preferred the Chrysler vehicles 91 to 9, Chrysler said.
