N.V. Philips Unit Expects Rebound In Second-Half Net

  • Author: Bob Hagerty
  • Date: 08/17/90

LONDON -- PolyGram N.V. said it is preparing for several major recorded-music releases this autumn, bolstering expectations that the company's earnings will rebound in the second half. PolyGram, which is 80%-owned by N.V. Philips of the Netherlands, had reported that profit in the first half dropped 20% to 118 million guilders ($66 million) from 148 million guilders, largely because of the costs of recent acquisitions. Per-share earnings fell 26% to 69 Dutch cents from 93 cents, reflecting an increase in the number of shares outstanding. Sales, however, jumped 25% to a record 2.18 billion guilders from 1.75 billion.

The profit drop had been expected, and analysts predicted the company will still report an increase in net for the year. James Golob of S.G. Warburg Securities in London forecast net income for the year of 370.5 million guilders, up 11% from 333 million last year.

David G. Fine, PolyGram's chief executive officer, said in an interview that the first half was in line with the company's plan for the year and that prospects for the second half look strong. He declined to forecast results.

For the second half, the company plans releases by pop stars Jon Bon Jovi, Elton John, Van Morrison, Jean Michel Jarre, INXS and Robert Cray. It also promises several "major event" recordings, including one based on this summer's "Wall" concert in Berlin.

PolyGram had said results were hurt in the half by a drop in interest income to eight million guilders from 46 million guilders a year earlier. That decline reflected acquisitions over the past year of two record companies, Island International Ltd. and A&M Records Inc. In addition, PolyGram said, it had to delay releases of new recordings as it created a new distribution arm in the U.S. to integrate the acquisitions.

PolyGram said it benefited strongly from the boom in sales of classical-music compact disks. The company, whose classical labels include Decca/London, Deutsche Grammophon and Philips Classics, estimates its world market share in classical music is 45%.

One of PolyGram's biggest sellers in the latest half was "The Essential Pavarotti," a compilation of arias sung by Luciano Pavarotti. That recording has sold more than 700,000 copies in Britain alone, giving Mr. Pavarotti what Mr. Fine called "pop star status."

"It's become much more fashionable to play classical music," Mr. Fine said. "It's not fuddy-duddy anymore."
