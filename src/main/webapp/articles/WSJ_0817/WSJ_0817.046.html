What's in a Name? It Depends Which Source You Consult --- Iraqi Chief Is Saddam Hussein In Most of This Paper; Elsewhere, Its a Tossup

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 08/17/90

WASHINGTON -- Saddam Hussein's name is in the news. But the question is: which is correct when you're using only one? In the New York Times, Los Angeles Times, Chicago Tribune and the Baltimore Sun, the Iraqi strongman is called Hussein, as in the New York Times headline, "Hussein's Achilles Heel."

But at the Washington Post, the Associated Press, USA Today, Time and Newsweek, Saddam gets the nod. "Security Officials Foresee Saddam Turning to Proxy Terrorists," the Washington Post said.

So far, nobody is using one of the Iraqi leader's full names: Saddam Hussein al-Tikriti. Nor are they adding hosannas that his followers might use, such as "Hero of the Arabs" or "Sword of Islam."

Yahya Sadowski, a Middle East expert at the Brookings Institution here, says an Arab man can have up to four names. He will have his given birth name -- Saddam, in this case. He will have his family or tribal name -- such as Hussein, which literally means "little beautiful," Mr. Sadowski says.

The Iraqi leader's third name, "al-Tikriti," means "of Tikrit," a town in central Iraq. But that name was dropped after 1977 because too many members of Iraq's ruling Ba'ath party were also "al-Tikriti."

Still another moniker comes from the name of a first son. Around the Iraqi Embassy here, says press officer Abdulrahman Jamil, the Iraqi leader is sometimes called "Abu Uday," which means "the father of Uday," the name of his eldest son.

Most Arabs refer to the Iraqi president as "Saddam," Mr. Sadowski says. One reason is that Hussein is an extremely common name in the Middle East and "Hussein" alone would be confusing.

The U.S. media are trying to avoid the same kind of mix-up. One reason the Washington Post went with Saddam for the Iraqi dictator is that "just reading `Hussein' opens up the possibility that the casual reader would confuse him with King Hussein" of Jordan, says Michael Getler, the Washington Post's managing editor for foreign news.

But Ed Wright, an assistant foreign editor at the Los Angeles Times says, "It's our understanding that {Saddam} is not a legitimate second reference. It's more of an informal second reference . . . like the use of `Fidel' for Fidel Castro."

Even the same publication sometimes can't agree. To avoid confusion with Jordan's king on The Wall Street Journal news pages, reporters, editors and headline writers have been instructed to use "Iraq's Hussein" or "Saddam Hussein." "But you should not use Saddam or Hussein by itself." The paper's editorial page, however, uses "Saddam."
