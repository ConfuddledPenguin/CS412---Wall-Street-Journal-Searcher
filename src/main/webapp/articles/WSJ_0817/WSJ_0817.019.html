Gorbachev, Yeltsin Are Making Effort To End Discord Over Economic Reform

  • Author: Elisabeth Rubinfien
  • Date: 08/17/90

MOSCOW -- Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev and the leader of the vast Russian Federation, Boris Yeltsin, are cutting their travels short in an effort to halt internal battles that threaten plans for economic reform. Mr. Yeltsin broke off a working tour of Russia and returned to Moscow Wednesday. Yesterday, the official Tass news agency quoted Mr. Yeltsin as saying Mr. Gorbachev will return to Moscow from his holiday in Crimea in the next few days.

Mr. Yeltsin has long been urging more radical reforms than those proposed by the central government. Earlier this month, Mr. Gorbachev and Mr. Yeltsin agreed to create a joint team to work out a new proposal for shifting to a market economy, based on a more radical plan created by the Russian government. The agreement was a direct slap at Prime Minister Nikolai Ryzhkov, who is leading a central government effort at planning economic reform.

Mr. Ryzhkov's most recent proposal, presented in May, failed to win the parliament's approval because it was indecisive and would have started with an unpopular immediate rise in retail bread prices. The government was given until September to work out something better.

The new joint-team has been working in parallel to Mr. Ryzhkov's committee and another so-called alternative commission, and all three are racing to come up with draft plans for the Soviet and Russian parliaments to discuss next month. Now Mr. Gorbachev is under increasing pressure to choose between his prime minister's more moderate reforms and an alliance with the radical populist, Mr. Yeltsin.

Mr. Yeltsin indirectly criticized Mr. Ryzhkov for undermining the new team's efforts. According to Tass, he complained that the Federal ministries of defense and finance weren't giving the group certain essential data, defying presidential instructions to cooperate by doing so. He called for the government to abandon its parallel reform planning. "One should not duplicate work," he said. "It is necessary to concentrate on our program, which is trusted by the people and is both novel and realistic," he said.

Indicating that his intentions to cooperate only go so far, Mr. Yeltsin threatened that Russia will take its own course, no matter what. If the Soviet parliament is presented with two official proposals, he said, "The Russian parliament will adopt its own plan and the issue will be closed."

In his own republic, which covers the bulk of Soviet territory, Mr. Yeltsin has supported plans and declarations that directly contradict the rule of the central government. Various republics are seeking direct ties with one another as a way to achieve economic improvements faster than waiting for central government reform. Whatever economic reform program the government adopts this autumn will be crucial in Mr. Gorbachev's struggle to keep testy republics interested in remaining part of a unified nation.

Some political analysts say the decision to create a joint team reflected how weak Mr. Gorbachev is becoming, while others say the president intends to use Mr. Yeltsin's pressure to push through reforms he himself thinks are necessary. In any case, Mr. Gorbachev can only be helped if he wins the support of Mr. Yeltsin, among the most popular politicians in Russia.

So far the Soviet government has resisted taking radical steps that might cause immediate sharp inflation or unemployment. The plan presented by Mr. Ryzhkov in May was scheduled to take three to five years, and to start with price reform. The Russian 500-day plan takes about 18 months and emphasizes privatization of huge state-run industries. Local news reports indicate that the various economic reform commissions are now scrapping the idea of starting with price reform in favor of starting with privatization.

Mr. Yeltsin said he was hopeful that the new group will be able to complete its work this month, according to Tass. He said that yesterday the team agreed on a package of emergency measures to help limit the chaos of a transition to a market economy.
