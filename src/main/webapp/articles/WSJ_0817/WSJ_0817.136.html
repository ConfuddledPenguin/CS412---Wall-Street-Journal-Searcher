Credit Markets: Bonds Fall on Inflation, Mideast News as Yields Approach Attractive Levels for Small Investors

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 08/17/90

NEW YORK -- The bond market was knocked down with a one-two punch yesterday, staggering first on news of rising inflation then tumbling after a menacing speech by Iraqi President Saddam Hussein. The Treasury's 30-year bond, which plunged nearly two points during the day, rebounded slightly to end 1 5/8 points lower. The decline translates into a loss of $16.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

Top-quality corporate bonds followed Treasurys' lead, tumbling as much as 1 1/4 points. But municipal, mortgage-backed and junk bonds posted smaller declines, with prices ending between 1/2 and 5/8 point lower.

As prices deteriorated, the yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond jumped to 8.92%, the highest closing yield on a benchmark bond since May 3, when the yield was 8.98%.

Although institutional investors are bailing out of bonds, analysts say that yields are approaching levels that individual investors should consider attractive.

"You have a fragile stock market and bond yields that are close to 9%. That's a combination that has always attracted small investors to the bond market," said F. Ward McCarthy, a managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates. But buying on the part of small investors alone isn't enough to reverse the deeply bearish sentiment in the market.

Yesterday's sell-off began as soon as the market opened and was triggered by a larger-than-expected rise in the consumer price index, a measure of inflation. The Department of Labor reported that the CPI rose 0.4% in July. During the first seven months of the year, the department said the CPI was running at an annual rate of 5.8%. With oil prices now rising, some economists believe that 1990 could produce the highest inflation rate in nearly a decade.

"The core inflation rate is getting worse. This is very alarming," said Norman Robertson, chief economist at Mellon Bank Corp.

The CPI report was a disappointment because it reduces the chances that the Federal Reserve will soon cut short-term interest rates. Economists generally believe that reducing interest rates at a time when prices are rising tends to exacerbate inflation. To avoid that, economists believe the Fed may vote to keep credit policy unchanged at its next Federal Open Market Committee meeting, which is scheduled to take place next week.

"It will be very hard for the Fed to ease interest rates at this time," said Mr. Robertson. "In fact, the Fed may not ease unless or until there is clear evidence that we are in a recession," he said. In recent weeks, the value of short-term Treasury securities has appreciated due to expectations that the Fed would ease. But, short-term securities were the hardest hit in yesterday's early rout.

After the heavy round of CPI-related selling, the market quieted for a time.

But comments by Mr. Hussein clobbered the market again. In an open letter to President Bush, which was broadcast by Iraqi television and picked up by Cable News Network, the Iraqi strongman said President Bush was a "liar" who was trying to humiliate the Arab world by using military force in Saudi Arabia.

Saddam Hussein also threatened to battle Americans troops, saying that "you will receive American bodies back in bags."

Those ominous words shook up investors. "The part about sending Americans home in bags raised the prospect of war and right away people reached the conclusion that oil prices will rise," putting even more pressure on inflation, said James Capra, a senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc. Inflation is the bane of the bond market because it reduces the value of fixed-income investments.

Mr. Capra said that investors also fear that a war in the Middle East could disrupt the budget deficit-cutting process, which had been one of the few positive factors in the bond market.

Although yesterday's morning decline reflected heavy selling, Mr. Capra said that prices fell in the afternoon largely because dealers were cutting prices.

"The market is trying to reprice itself," he said. After Saddam Hussein's speech, "market participants reached a collective consensus that these prices are too high and yields are too low for this environment. We are seeking levels where we can attract interest."

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 98 2/32, down from 99 25/32 on Wednesday. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, jumped to 8.92% from 8.76%. The latest 10year notes ended at 99 22/32 to yield 8.78% compared with 100 21/32 to yield 8.63% on Wednesday. Two-year notes ended at 99 26/32 to yield 8.08% compared with 100 3/ 32 to yield 7.93%.

Short-term rates were sharply higher yesterday. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.50% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.73% while the rate on six-month bills ended at a rate of 7.47% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.85%.

Corporate, Junk Bonds

Investment-grade corporate bonds ended as much as 1 1/4 point lower yesterday while junk bonds, high-yield speculative-grade securities, ended with losses of about 1/2 point.

The biggest loser in the junk-bond market yesterday was AmBase Corp., which tumbled on concerns that the company's sale of its troubled Home Insurance Co. unit could be blocked.

Although AmBase's 14.875% senior subordinated notes rebounded slightly by the close, the debt securities still finished 13 3/4 points lower at 81 3/4 on moderate volume of $3.1 million of bonds.

Fears that the Home Insurance sale might be derailed were fueled by Shearson Lehman equity analyst Udayan Ghose, who told traders in a morning call that the deal could be stymied by regulators or disgruntled shareholders.

"It seems to me that shareholders are unlikely to favor the deal given what has happened to the stock," Mr. Ghose told Dow Jones Capital Markets Report.

AmBase, which had promised a special dividend to its shareholders, said it is unlikely to make any payout based on the expected proceeds from the sale.

Thursday's sharp declines in AmBase bonds come after the securities rose by a total of two points Tuesday and Wednesday on news that the company signed a letter of intent to sell its troubled Home Insurance Co. unit to Vik Brothers International, a European-based investor group, for $544 million.

The junk-bond market welcomed the asset sale because the company said it would use proceeds from the transaction to help pay off debt. Jack Plaxe, chief financial officer of AmBase, said the company would not have "done this deal had we not thought we could pay our debt." Mr. Plaxe also said he believes the sale will meet with required regulatory approval.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds followed the Treasury market with municipal issues ending as much as 5/8 point lower.

In competitive action, a Merrill Lynch Capital Markets group priced $100 million Metropolitan Seattle, Washington, municipality sewer revenue bonds to yield from 6.00% in 1993 to 7.45% in 2030.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% of 2018 were 3/8 point lower at 98 3/8 to yield 7.32%.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Prices of mortgage-backed securities ended lower. Higher-coupon mortgage securities posted smaller declines than the low-coupon issues, reflecting yesterday's news that housing starts in July fell 2.6% and that building permits, an indicator of future housing activity, fell 2.6%.

"That's why the high coupons are doing better," said a New York mortgage-backed securities trader. "To the extent that there's less housing, there are fewer people moving, and lower prepayments," which favor the high-coupon issues.

Among actively traded secondary issues, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues for August delivery were down 17/32 on the day at 98 25/32. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities were down 20/32 at 98 10/32, and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were down 20/32 at 98 27/32.

Foreign Bonds

Fears that oil prices may start to rise again undermined global bond markets again yesterday. Although the U.S. market took the biggest hits yesterday, prices also were lower in Japan, Britain, West Germany, France and several other countries.

In Britain, domestic economic data along with the prospects of higher oil hurt bond prices. The government reported that Britain's underlying annual rate of growth in average earnings rose to 10% in June from 9.75% in May. The trend suggests that wage inflation may continue to rise despite a firm pound and tight monetary policy, analysts say. In response, Britain's benchmark 11.75% bond due 2003/2007 rose 3/8 point to 98 30/32 to yield 11.89%.

West German bonds ended about 1/4 point lower while in Japan the yield on the benchmark No. 119 10-year bond rose to 7.92%. The price for the No. 119 fell 0.50 point to 83.52.

(Foreign bond yields are quoted here in local terms and therefore differ from yields calculated for these markets by U.S. investment firms, which use a semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets. When calculated using that semiannual measure, the yield on the No. 119 bond closed at 7.36%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)
