Southland Extends Debt-Swap Offer, Ito-Yokado Pact

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 08/17/90

DALLAS -- Southland Corp. extended its debt-swap offer and its deal with Ito-Yokado Co., its Japanese suitor, as expected, after falling far short of getting the approval from bondholders needed to complete either deal. According to the company's latest bond-exchange offer, dated July 16, the company needs to have 95% of each class of bonds tendered in order to consummate the deal. Only 28.2% of the company's 16.5% notes were tendered in the deal. The highest approval, 74.7%, came in the 13.5% senior notes, which are about 34%-held by investor Carl Icahn.

Mr. Icahn, of Mount Kisco, N.Y., said he tendered all of his Southland bonds in the company's exchange offer. Mr. Icahn owns an estimated $180 million of the senior reset notes, as well as smaller positions in other Southland bonds.

"We like the Southland offer," he said. "The Japanese are putting equity in. That's what it takes to make these kinds of deals work."

The deal required only two-thirds of the perferred stock to be tendered in the deal, and 83.5% were tendered, Southland said. The company also said its bank creditors approved a credit-agreement amendment essential to the deal. The debt-swap offer was extended to midnight Aug. 23 and the stock purchase agreement with Ito-Yokado was extended to Sept. 15.

The acceptance by bondholders, while short of the required 95%, was "extraordinary" for such deals, said James Heffernan of Whitman, Heffernan & Rhein Workout Fund, which tendered its 13.5% bonds. Usually, bondholders know that such offers will be extended and they don't tender by the first deadline date, he said. "These are among the best figures I've seen for a voluntary exchange," he said.

Analysts observed, though, that Southland's exchange offer was most popular with holders of its 13 1/2% senior notes, who would get more Southland equity than other bondholders under the restructuring plan. Holders of subordinated bonds, who wouldn't get as much equity, appeared to be holding out for better terms.

Some investors with lower-ranking bonds voiced concern that Southland may try a pre-packaged plan to quickly enter Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings to force the hand of bondholders who are holding out.

"I'm afraid it might turn out not to be a quicky bankruptcy. This could turn into just a regular bankruptcy," which would take many more months to settle, said William Eddleman Jr., director of fixed income research for Dallas-based Barre & Co., a major holder of three classes of the bonds along with its clients.

Mr. Eddleman, who declined to say whether his firm had tendered its 15 3/4%, 16 1/2% and 16 3/4% bonds, said questions over the seniority that was granted to some of the classes over others could become a major legal issue in a bankruptcy. That could extend the bankruptcy proceedings beyond the four months expected for a pre-packaged bankruptcy.

The exchange offer, the first approved by the bondholder committee, would give bondholders about 25% of the common stock of the company. The founding Thompson family of Dallas would get about 5% of the stock and Ito-Yokado, operator of 7-Eleven stores in Japan, would get about 70% for $430 million.

Southland, the troubled parent of the 7-Eleven convenience store chain, went into default on its $1.8 billion publicly traded debt in mid-July, but avoided being forced into bankruptcy at that time by getting a tentative nod from the bondholder committee for the most recent exchange offer. The company owns about 6,700 7-Eleven and other convenience stores in the U.S. and Canada. Increased competition eroded sales and profit margins and made it difficult for the company to service the huge debt from the buy-out.
