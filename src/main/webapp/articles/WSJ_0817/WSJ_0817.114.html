Court Calls Off Election For Los Angeles Board

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/17/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- A federal appeals court here called off the election for a seat on the Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors until the court reaches a decision on a Hispanic voting rights suit in the nation's most populous county. By a 2-1 vote, the appeals panel stayed a ruling by U.S. District Judge David V. Kenyon ordering a special primary election using the new district lines, which are designed to help elect a Hispanic to the county's five-member governing board. Judge Kenyon ruled in June that the county discriminated against Hispanics when redrawing district boundaries after the 1980 census. On Aug. 3, the judge rejected a redistricting plan drawn up by the county board and then accepted one developed by civil-rights groups.

Both sides were frustrated with the latest decision. Attorneys for Los Angeles County had argued that a November runoff election from a June primary in the old district boundaries should be allowed to go ahead until the appeal was decided. A Hispanic group and the American Civil Liberties Union wanted a new election to be held based on the map accepted by Judge Kenyon.

"Neither side has demonstrated that the equities strongly favor going forward with their chosen election on November 6," wrote the appeals court judges. A full hearing is scheduled for Oct. 1.
