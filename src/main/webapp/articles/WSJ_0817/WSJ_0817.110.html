NRC Probing Its Licensing Of Seabrook

  • Author: Joseph Pereira
  • Date: 08/17/90

The inspector general of the Nuclear Regulatory Commission is investigating the agency's licensing of the Seabrook nuclear power plant, amid lingering questions about adequate evacuation plans. The action, conducted by the commission inspector general David Williams, is unusual and throws at least some uncertainty on Seabrook's full-power start-up timetable. But some analysts remain optimistic that the investigation probably won't lead to a revocation of Seabrook's license.

The $6.6 billion power plant, in Seabrook, N.H., has demonstrated an ability to operate at 100% capacity in trial runs and is ready for regular full-power production.

The Seabrook plant is owned by 11 New England utilities, including Public Service Co. of New Hampshire, with a 35.6% stake. PS New Hampshire filed for bankruptcy-law protection in 1988 because it couldn't service its Seabrook debt. Another Seabrook holder, Northeast Utilities, has agreed to acquire PS New Hampshire in a deal valued at about $2.3 billion.

"This comes at a very awkward time for Seabrook, and it puts the financial community in a cautious mode," said Daniel Scotto, a bond analyst at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. "It's a hard one to call. We don't really have any precedent of NRC investigative activity to go by."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, shares of PS New Hampshire and Northeast Utilities were unchanged at $2.625 and $19.75, respectively.

In 1981 the commission conducted an investigation of seismic safety designs for the Diablo Canyon Nuclear Power Plant in Northern California. That resulted in a temporary suspension of a low-power testing license the facility had received. The plant eventually received a full operating license.

"I'd be very surprised if the Seabrook investigation leads to anything serious," said Paul Parshley, a bond analyst at Lehman Brothers. "The momentum was clearly on the side of Seabrook, and I don't think anyone would have wanted to jeopardize {getting} a license by doing something inappropriate at the time."

A public report of the investigation is expected to be issued in about a month.

If the inspector general finds wrongdoing, he can recommend certain actions to the full commission, which will decide whether or not to act on his findings.

At issue in the licensing inquiry is whether there was any wrongdoing by the Atomic Safety and Licensing Board, an advisory group to the commission. The board's recommendation that Seabrook be licensed was made public on Nov. 13, 1989. But, according to a memorandum from the board's chairman, Ivan Smith, Mr. Williams hypothesizes that the licensing board, only four days earlier, decided not to recommend an operating license.

The memo, released by the commission this week, said Mr. Williams is trying to determine whether the reversal "was corruptly induced by NRC officers and corruptly made by members of the licensing board." Messrs. Smith and Williams have confirmed the investigation, but wouldn't comment on it.

A Seabrook spokesman said the licensing was recommended Nov. 9 and that the recommendation wasn't made public until Nov. 13.

Anti-nuclear activists note that if the licensing board recommended licensing on Nov. 9, it came only two days after the Atomic and Safety Appeals Board, another NRC committee, outlined aspects of evacuation plans that still needed addressing.

They included identifying and mapping evacuation plans for critically ill patients and other disabled persons living within a 10-mile radius; providing shelter for beachgoers who might be stranded; and reaching an agreement with teachers for the care of school children. Most of these issues haven't been resolved.

"None of these issues could have been adequately addressed {during the two days} between the appeals board order and the time of the licensing recommendation," said Robert Backus, an attorney for the Seacoast Anti-Pollution League, a Seabrook opponent.
