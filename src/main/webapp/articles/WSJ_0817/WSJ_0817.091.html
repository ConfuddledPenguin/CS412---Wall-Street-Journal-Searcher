Abreast of the Market: Industrials Plunge 66.83 on News Of U.S. Inflation, Mideast Crisis

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 08/17/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices buckled under the pressure created by signals of increasing tensions in the Middle East and evidence that U.S. inflation may have been on the rise even before the recent surge in oil prices. Growth stocks and recession-resistant consumer issues paced the market's downturn, while oil and precious-metals stocks bucked the decline -- a pattern which has prevailed since Iraqi troops invaded Kuwait two weeks ago.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which had risen less than one point in the two previous sessions, posted its lowest close since the Mideast crisis began by plummeting 66.83 to 2681.44.

Among broader measures, Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index tumbled 7.67 to 332.39, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index skidded 3.81 to 182.73 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index fell 7.14 to 308.16.

Declining issues outpaced advancers by 1,300 to 330 on the Big Board. Trading activity remained fairly light, with 138,850,000 shares changing hands, compared with 135,210,000 Wednesday.

The market initially headed lower amid disappointment with the consumer price report for July, which showed that the so-called core inflation rate -- prices excluding the food and energy sectors -- rose 0.6% last month. The overall consumer price index, helped by a 0.7% decline in energy prices, rose 0.4%.

"The numbers should have been better than they were, and investors received a message that inflation is running at too high of a level," said A.C. Moore, director of research at Argus Research Corp.

Prices continued to fall as the session progressed amid two major developments which dashed hopes for any quick resolution of the Middle East conflict:

-- Iraqi President Saddam Hussein, in a televised address delivered by a spokesman, referred to a Jihad, or holy war, against the U.S. and threatened that Americans would die in the Middle East.

-- King Hussein of Jordan, who met with President Bush to discuss the crisis, denied afterward that he had carried a message from Iraq. Several reports said the king had been given a peace proposal by Saddam Hussein to be taken to President Bush.

The market plummeted to session lows in the final hour of trading, setting off one of the exchange's "circuit breakers" designed to put the brakes on market declines.

At 3:41 p.m. EDT, with the Dow Jones industrials down 50 points, the Big Board's "uptick" rule for program trades tied to stock-index arbitrage went into effect -- prohibiting arbitrage-related stock sales until after the issues moved higher.

But the "circuit breaker" didn't stop the market from ending the day at session lows, and selling in index futures following the close of stock trading laid the groundwork for possible arbitrage selling at today's opening.

September contracts on the S&P 500 ended the session at a discount of about 1/2 point to the cash index -- well below the level at which traders could lock in profits by purchasing the futures and selling baskets of stocks.

And with the Middle East situation still in flux, "Traders with positions aren't going to want to hold anything over the weekend while they're waiting for any kind of news," said W. Daniel Williams III, a vice president of trading at Dillon, Read & Co.

Blue-chip consumer stocks hit hard in the latest session included Philip Morris, which went down 2 to 45 1/4 in composite trading of 1.5 million shares; McDonald's, down 1 1/8 to 29; Procter & Gamble, down 2 7/8 to 80 5/8; Coca-Cola, down 1 1/2 to 43 1/8; Merck, down 4 1/8 to 83 3/4, and Eli Lilly, down 4 1/8 to 71 1/2.

Within the growth-stock category, Boeing fell 1 3/4 to 50 5/8 on 1.5 million shares, General Electric lost 1 3/8 to 64 7/8, Limited Inc. dropped 1 3/8 to 17 3/4 and Waste Management fell 1 3/4 to 37 7/8.

Oil stocks, bolstered by the surge in oil prices, closed little changed. Exxon lost 1/4 to 51 3/4, Texaco fell 1/8 to 62 1/4, Chevron slid 1/8 to 77 1/2, Mobil rose 1/4 to 66 3/4, Amoco slid 1/4 to 56 1/2, Unocal added 1/8 to 32 3/4 and Royal Dutch Petroleum gained 1/2 to 83 3/8.

Gold stocks showed strength across the board. Homestake Mining rose 1 1/2 to 23 1/8, Placer Dome rose 1 to 19 1/2, Battle Mountain Gold gained 3/8 to 12 3/4, Hecla Mining went up 3/8 to 13 and ASA Ltd. advanced 2 5/8 to 56 1/4.

Ohio Edison, the most active Big Board issue, plunged 2 1/2 to 17 3/8 on 2.2 million shares. Ohio regulators decided to grant the company a one-time rate increase of $142.3 million, rather than the three-step, $216 million rate boost it had requested -- a decline that led analysts to believe its common-stock dividend may be cut.

The utility said the decision would have "a materially adverse effect" on its results and its board could be expected to consider the payout at a meeting scheduled for Tuesday. Edward J. Tirello, an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham, suggested the board could reduce the annual payout to $1.50 a share from $1.96, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Hewlett-Packard, which reported that net income for its third quarter ended July 31 was down 5% from a year ago, dropped 1 7/8 to 35 7/8 on 1.3 million shares. Analysts generally had expected that the company would post higher earnings for the quarter.

Navistar International, second on the most actives list, dipped 3/8 to 2 7/8 as 2.2 million shares changed hands. The company posted net income of $6 million for the third quarter ended July 31, compared with $28 million a year earlier.

Security Pacific skidded 3 to 28 3/4. Analysts at several brokerage houses lowered earnings estimates on the company following a disclosure that it expects second-half results to fall short of the year-earlier level, citing weakness in the U.S. economy and the likelihood of slow growth in non-interest income, according to the Dow Jones investor report.

Issues of other West Coast-based bank holding companies also fell sharply. Wells Fargo tumbled 3 1/8 to 59 5/8, while First Interstate Bancorp fell 1 3/8 to 31 1/4.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 3.81 to 336.83. Volume totaled 14,160,000 issues.
