U.S. Offers Turkey Loan Guarantees For Arms Purchase

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica and John T. Norman
  • Date: 08/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. Export-Import Bank, reacting to the needs of a U.S. ally caught in the Persian Gulf crisis, said it is ready to provide more than $1 billion in loan guarantees to finance the sale of U.S. military helicopters to Turkey. Ex-Im Bank Chairman John Macomber said the board and a cabinet-level committee that oversees the bank's loan policies gave it the go-ahead to offer the guarantees if a U.S. vendor wins the huge award for 200 helicopters. The likely U.S. supplier is United Technologies Corp.'s Sikorsky division, which last month clinched another contract to supply the Turkish National Police with S-70A Blackhawk helicopters with the help of $37.7 million in loan guarantees from the Ex-Im Bank.

The latest prospective deal signals a turning point in U.S. arms-export policy. The bank traditionally has shied away from the defense trade, insisting that its limited resources be used to finance nonmilitary U.S. business. Its Turkey decision, however, shows the bank is willing to serve as financier for U.S. arms sales to middle-income allies that can't afford to pay cash for U.S. weapons.

Mr. Macomber said the gulf crisis surrounding Turkey was a factor in the bank's decision. "We had to do something faster than usual" because of the crisis.

The Bush administration has been quietly crafting a new policy to help U.S. defense contractors expand their export sales. Lawrence Eagleburger, the deputy secretary of state, last month ordered U.S. ambassadors to get on board by helping open doors for U.S. defense vendors, according to his aides.

The use of Ex-Im Bank financing, however, had remained a "sticking point" in forming the new policy, the aides said. A policy paper recommending its use for defense sales remains stuck in the Office of Management and Budget because of concerns by some White House aides about the propriety of expending the bank's resources in this manner.

Mr. Macomber said the prospective Turkish deal is specifically authorized by a law passed last year that authorizes such guarantees for military sales to Turkey and Greece. Democratic Sen. Christopher Dodd of Connecticut spearheaded the effort to pass this and other laws that effectively give such companies as United Technologies, based in Hartford, Conn., wider access to Ex-Im Bank financing.

The Ex-Im Bank's role of financing arms sales to other allies could become policy if an amendment to the Export Administration Act introduced by Sens. Dodd and Christopher Bond (R. Mo.) becomes law. The Senate is scheduled to consider changes to the export act when it returns from its August recess.

U.S. defense industry spokesmen said Sikorsky is facing tough competition for the Turkish sale from French and Italian vendors, which have the solid backing of their governments.
