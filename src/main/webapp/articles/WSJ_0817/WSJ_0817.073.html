JAPAN LOOKS TO THE SUN

Japan's Ministry of International Trade and Industry said it will begin basic research next year on a photovoltaic-power system in space to capture the energy of the sun and send it back to Earth.

Solar-power units stationed in space would be more efficient than ground-based photovoltaic-power systems, officials told a Japanese news agency. They explained that while much of the sun's energy is filtered out by the Earth's atmosphere and clouds, that wouldn't happen in space.

The project envisions a generator equipped with a solar-panel unit in geostationary orbit. A panel unit of three to six square miles would generate five million kilowatts, roughly equivalent to the output of about five nuclear-power stations, the officials said.

Energy captured by the solar-panel unit would be sent by microwave signals or laser to a ground-based receiver.

ASIAN TOURISM FEARS

Member countries of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations may have to adjust their tourism projections because of the anticipated drop in tourist traffic due to higher oil prices resulting from the Persian Gulf crisis, industry sources said. The 1992 Visit ASEAN Year working committee is scheduled to discuss its marketing plan in Kuala Lumpur this month.

MULTIPARTY MOZAMBIQUE

The ruling Frelimo party's central committee has endorsed resolutions calling for adoption of a multiparty system and new election laws, meeting a key demand of rebels fighting the government, the Mozambique government news agency said. President Joaquim Chissano said on July 31 he wanted a multiple party system in place for parliamentary and presidential elections next year.

PHILIPPINES LIFTS BAN

The Philippine government will lift an eight-year ban on Soviet ships at a shipyard near the U.S. naval base at Subic Bay, Justice Secretary Franklin Drilon told a Japanese news agency. Shipyard restrictions on vessels from the Soviet Union and other East bloc countries were imposed in 1982 at the request of the U.S.

MONKEY BUSINESS

An Australian city is considering using monkeys to pick coconuts from trees along its seafront walk to prevent the nuts from falling on tourists. "They'd splatter you if they hit you. It's bloody frightening," said Les Bass, a spokesman for palm-fringed Cairns City Council in northeast Australia. Using monkeys, possibly from Malaysia, was proposed by Alderman Bob Burgess, who suggested they would amuse tourists while protecting sightseers and picnickers from lethal cracks on the head.

SAMSUNG PLAN REJECTED

The South Korean Trade and Industry Ministry rejected a plan by Samsung Shipbuilding & Heavy Industries Co. to produce large trucks using Japanese technology. Ministry officials said the application to introduce technology from Nissan Diesel Motor Co. would be returned to Samsung.

MONGOLIAN MOTHERLAND

While China pleads with its teeming millions to have just one child per couple, across the border in sparsely populated Mongolia it's the more the merrier.

"Children are the flowers of our life," said "hero mother" Orsoo, expressing a common Mongolian sentiment. "They are our future." The 50-year-old mother of 10 and her husband received a government loan to build a new home as a reward for having a large family. Mrs. Orsoo, winner of a red star medal, said families of eight or more also get $300 a year in government subsidies, a princely sum in Mongolia.

Mongolia's communist rulers until recently prohibited birth control. Activists, such as prominent pediatrician and public health official Munkhuu, have pushed the authorities in the past year to legalize contraceptives, saying Mongolian women are risking their health because they have too many babies too quickly. Legalization of contraception is one of many reforms instituted since March, when demonstrations forced the communists to renounce their monopoly on power.

However, official approval of large families remains unchanged. "Our birth-control policy was and is one of encouraging population growth, because our population is so small," Mr. Munkhuu said.

Mongolia, a country the size of Western Europe, has a population of only two million. Its birthrate is high at 36 births per 1,000 people, and the average family has about five children.
