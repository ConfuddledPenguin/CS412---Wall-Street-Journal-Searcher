Who's News: Borden's D'Amato Named President, Operating Chief

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal
  • Date: 05/31/90

NEW YORK -- Borden Inc., continuing a tradition of tapping its chemical operations for top management, named Anthony S. D'Amato president and chief operating officer, effective July 1. The appointment of Mr. D'Amato, 59 years old, comes at a time when Borden, largely a food business, is going through a restructuring designed to streamline its food and consumer-products operations by consolidating production plants and cutting back its dairy business. The two posts have been vacant since early 1987, when R.J. Ventres became Borden's chairman. Mr. Ventres, 65, remains chairman and chief executive officer. It is clear, however, that the choice of Mr. D'Amato puts him firmly in line to take over from Mr. Ventres at his expected retirement at the end of 1991.

Mr. D'Amato's background as a corporate executive vice president and president of the packaging and industrial products division, which accounted for 23% of the company's $7.59 billion sales in 1989, gave food industry analysts little expectation that the company eventually would shed its films and adhesives business to concentrate solely on its food lines. A number of large food companies have been disposing of non-food operations in recent years.

Mr. D'Amato said he planned to continue the strategies that were put in place within the past year, including efforts to reduce costs through restructuring and investing significant capital in building more-efficient plants. Borden, the nation's leading milk producer, took a charge of $404.4 million in the third quarter for its restructuring program.

Mr. D'Amato characterized himself as "a mixed bag" between the food and chemicals segments, although he comes from the division that used to be called chemicals. He noted his division responsibilities included the company's Brazilian pasta products and other consumer-products businesses. He also pointed to the strength in the three other executive vice presidents who run the food businesses. Mr. D'Amato said he planned to become "much more aggressive in growth and acquisitions overseas," continuing to look for acquisitions and selling some businesses.

Mr. D'Amato, instrumental in Borden's divestiture of its commodity chemicals operations, also was elected a director. He has been with Borden 31 years and will be succeeded as corporate executive vice president and president of packaging and industrial products by Joseph M. Saggese. Mr. Saggese, 58 years old, had been senior group vice president of the division. He also was named to the office of the chairman, joining Mr. Ventres, Mr. D'Amato and the three other executive vice presidents.

John M. McMillin, a food analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities, was impressed by Mr. D'Amato's ability to explain the dynamics of the wall-coverings business and said he appeared "street smart" and "production driven."

Mr. McMillin said that although "Wall Street may be disappointed with the appointment of a chemical man," Mr. D'Amato would "slowly gain acceptance." He noted that many of the analysts who follow Borden are food analysts and know little about chemicals.

William Leach, a food industry analyst with Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette, said, "I've never understood the synergy between wallpaper and pasta," a reference to Borden's standing as the biggest company in the world in sales in both areas. Mr. Leach said most analysts feel Borden would be worth more if the two aspects of the company were separated.
