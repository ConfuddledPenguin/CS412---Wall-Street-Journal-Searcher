Private Placement Of Swedish Issue Set via New Rule

  • Author: William Power and Stephen D. Moore
  • Date: 05/31/90

Another first takes place tomorrow in the long-awaited transformation of the U.S. market for privately placed securities. An international stock offering for Atlas Copco AB, a Swedish industrial and construction equipment company, closes tomorrow. Investment bankers say it is the first stock offering under the Securities and Exchange Commission's new Rule 144a, which is designed to ease trading of privately placed securities in the U.S. by exempting billions of dollars in new offerings from U.S. disclosure rules.

Last month the SEC passed the new 144a rule, which is expected to benefit big institutional investors but not small investors. The new rule allows certain large U.S. institutions to sell and trade private-placement securities without having to fill out extensive financial disclosure forms. The new rule is meant to draw foreign companies and sovereigns to the U.S. markets to raise capital.

Bankers said Atlas Copco last week placed a 1.3 billion Swedish kronor ($214 million) offering that was oversubscribed, with 23% of the offering -- or about $50 million of common stock -- placed in the U.S. under the new Rule 144a. Lead manager of the issue was Enskilda Securities, a London-based unit of Sweden's Skandinaviska Enskilda Banken. CS First Boston Inc.'s First Boston Corp. unit headed a regional syndicate for North America.

The Atlas Copco offering is the first stock deal under Rule 144a, investment bankers said, but it isn't the first overall. A $100 million bond offering under Rule 144a for British Aerospace closed May 14; investment bankers led by Goldman, Sachs & Co. completed that transaction.

Elsewhere on the 144a front, Salomon Inc.'s Salomon Brothers Inc. is in the process of completing a large 144a bond transaction for Union Tank Car Co.

A CS First Boston spokesman declined to comment on the Atlas Copco deal yesterday, citing regulatory restrictions against public comment until the transaction closes.

While some kinks remain in the procedures for bringing such deals to market, the Atlas Copco offering is seen as a successful maiden test for 144a-related stock offerings, investment bankers said. Non-U.S. companies have shied from U.S. markets because of SEC disclosure rules. Rule 144a enabled Atlas Copco to avoid the extra paperwork. "We made no changes to the offering circular that was distributed in Europe other than stamping in red letters, `It's a 144a transaction,'" said one securities executive.
