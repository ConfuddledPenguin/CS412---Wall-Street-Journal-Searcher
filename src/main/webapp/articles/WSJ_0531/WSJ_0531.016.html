World Markets: Tokyo Shares Bounce Back to Finish Firmer; U.K. Prices Surge, Reflecting Wall Street's Gains

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/31/90

Tokyo stocks bounced back from early arbitrage selling pressure to close firmer on average volume. Traders said buying of domestic demand-related issues, as well as some arbitrage buying, buoyed the key index back from early losses.

Meanwhile, British stocks surged, boosted by the rising Dow Jones Industrial Average and new optimism about the U.K. economy and the Conservative government's prospects.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues gained 108.59 points to 32926.26. The index fell 373.94 points Tuesday.

In Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index rose 227.34 points to close the morning session at 33153.60.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 800 million shares, down from 825 million shares Tuesday. Advancers outpaced decliners 511 to 414, while 197 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section, which fell 22.39 points Tuesday, added 5.43 points, or 0.23%, to 2417.09.

The second section index advanced 15.95 points, or 0.41%, to 3902.32, after gaining 1.99 points Tuesday. Volume was estimated at 15 million shares, down from 16.4 million shares Tuesday.

"The market was buffeted by arbitrage all day," noted one trader at a foreign securities firm.

But although stock prices fluctuated a fair amount yesterday because of trading linked to futures and cash indexes by domestic and foreign arbitragers, the market ultimately was trading on fundamentals, observers said.

The Nikkei Index closely mirrored the currency's movements, falling early in the morning with a softer yen and recovering later when the yen firmed slightly against the dollar, one equity analyst at a U.S. investment trust bank said.

The market evidently is becoming two-tiered, with blue-chip electricals rising on yen weakness and domestic demand-linked shares rising on yen strength, the investment trust bank's analyst said.

"The (equity) market is a hostage of the currency market: As the dollar strengthens, the index falls but high-techs tend to get stronger, and vice versa when the dollar weakens," he said.

Yesterday's buying sentiment was also boosted by a slightly firmer bond market and various rumors about domestic demand-linked issues, traders said.

Environmental-control stocks received steady buying, focusing on a local economic newspaper's report that 15 types of chlorofluorocarbons may be restricted by the year 2000.

Ebara, which has been actively traded in the past week, rose 80 yen to 2200 yen (53 cents to $14.57).

Construction stocks continued to be bought as well, boosted by an article in a Japanese newspaper that said these companies are likely to post double-digit growth in the current fiscal year.

Very favorable earnings, which have persisted in attracting buyers in the sector, have been posted over the past few days by several construction firms for fiscal 1990.

Also yesterday, an unconfirmed rumor that Sumitomo Mining has discovered a high-yielding gold find in Japan's Kagoshima Prefecture was enough to ignite buying throughout the sector, one trader at a leading foreign securities firm said.

On the other hand, blue-chip electrical shares were lower, lacking any inspiring news to hold them up against profit-taking, observers said.

Chiyoda Corp. rose 150 yen to 2690 yen. An article in an economic newspaper said the plant engineering company received a large order from a European buyer.

Among mining stocks, Dowa rose 20 yen to 1000 yen, Mitsui added 80 yen to 1200 yen, Nippon gained 60 yen to 1070 yen, and Sumitomo rose 90 yen to 1650 yen.

Construction stocks included Taisei Corp., up 40 yen to 1420 yen, Ohbayashi, up 30 yen to 1740 yen, and Kajima, up 50 yen at 1970 yen.

In London, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index gained 50.6 points to 2346.20, just one point below its high for the day. Only four of the 100 stocks listed in the index fell during the day, with four others unchanged.

Traders and analysts said Wall Street's strong showing Tuesday and early yesterday was the major impetus for the London gain. But they said the British market also was driven by domestic factors, such as the possibility that early full membership in the European Monetary System could lower U.K. interest rates and bolster sterling.

"What we're seeing is a general rehabilitation of the U.K. market," said George Hodgson, U.K. strategist for S.G. Warburg Group, a U.K. stock brokerage firm. "People who were pouring their money overseas realized our market was looking cheap."

The market shrugged off a statement yesterday by British Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major that interest rates could rise. Analysts said base rates should fall by the end of the year. Traders also responded favorably to recent opinion polls indicating that the Labour Party's long lead over Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher and her Conservative Party has been cut to 15 points from about 20 points. Technical factors, such as a stock shortage because of firms scrambling to fill buy orders, also fueled the advance.

But analysts cautioned that the market, which has been flat for most of this year, won't hold up against bad news at home or from Wall Street. If it looks as though falling interest rates and full EMS membership won't happen until next year, Mr. Hodgson said, "the market won't sit and twiddle its thumbs." He added that London "still needs the comfort blanket of a strong New York market," with falls on Wall Street likely duplicated in London.

The rise yesterday on London's Stock Exchange was broad-based, though consumer sectors showed particular strength, while oil and property stocks lagged. Volume was robust, with 556.4 million shares traded, up from 330 million shares Tuesday.

Meanwhile, prices closed higher in Zurich, Amsterdam, Stockholm and Milan; lower in Paris and Frankfurt. South African gold stocks closed down.

In Asian and Pacific markets, stocks ended higher in Seoul, Hong Kong, Taipei and Wellington, and were lower in Sydney, Singapore and Manila.
