Credit Markets: Bond Prices Inch Up in Light Trading Ahead Of Reports; 30-Year Treasury Gains 1/4 Point

  • Author: Susan Scherreik and Sharon R. King
  • Date: 05/31/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices inched higher again in light trading activity as investors continued to mark time ahead of two key economic reports due out tomorrow. The most important of the two indicators is the May employment report, which is anxiously awaited because it will provide the first official reading on how the economy performed this month. The findings in the report are expected to set the tone in the bond market for the next several weeks.

"People are not willing to speculate in the bond market with a report of such importance coming out," said Michael Moran, chief economist at Daiwa Securities America Inc.

Also due tomorrow is the May survey of the nation's purchasing agents. Dealers and analysts said indications of a sluggish economy in data issued yesterday morning on April leading indicators and new home sales helped to buoy bond prices.

"The data that came out this morning were positive, and that set the tone for trading during the day," said Brian Fabbri, chief economist at Midland Montague Securities Inc. Leading indicators fell 0.2% last month, which was in line with market forecasts. New home sales fell more than expected to their weakest pace since December 1982.

Sales of new single-family houses fell 1.6% in April to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 546,000, the Commerce Department said.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended 1/4 point higher, or up $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, eased slightly to 8.59%.

The highlight of an otherwise dull session was the Federal Reserve's purchase of an estimated $3.5 billion to $4 billion of Treasury bills for its own account in an infrequent operation known as a bill pass.

The Fed's action, which added reserves to the banking system, had only a small impact on bond prices because it was viewed as technical in nature.

"The action meets a seasonal need for increased reserves and has no monetary policy significance," said Louis Crandall, an economist with R.H. Wrightson & Associates.

The Fed's bill pass did push Treasury bill rates lower because it helped to sop up some of the new supply in the short end of the market, analysts said.

In addition to yesterday's sale of $12 billion of cash-management bills, the Treasury will sell $10 billion of 52-week bills today and a record $17.2 billion of three-month and six-month bills Monday.

The bill pass "helped to relieve some of the congestion in the bill market," said Mr. Moran. Also in the short end of the market, the federal funds rate fell to 7%, well below the 8 1/4% perceived target.

However, the softness in the funds rate, which banks charge each other on overnight loans, had little effect on the market because it was attributed to yesterday being the final day of a two-week statement period, when funds often fluctuate widely as banks adjust their reserve positions.

Traders said that apprehension about tomorrow's data was reflected in rumors circulating in the bond market that the index of the Purchasing Management Association of Chicago, due to be released today at 10 a.m. EDT, would show a strong rebound in that region's manufacturing sector.

The Chicago survey is closely watched by the bond market because many players believe it can provide an early reading of the national survey, due tomorrow.

However, the composite diffusion index of the National Association of Purchasing Management is expected to have declined to 49.5% in May, according to the average of estimates of 10 economists surveyed by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report.

In April, the index was pegged at 50.2%, the first time in a year that it was above the 50% level. A reading above 50% indicates an expanding industrial sector, and a reading below 50% suggests the sector is contracting.

Elsewhere, employment growth is expected to have picked up considerably in May, with nonfarm payrolls expected to have increased 253,000, with a gain of 135,000 seen if newly hired census workers are excluded, according to a Capital Markets Report survey of 20 economists.

In the month, the civilian unemployment rate is expected to have been unchanged at 5.4%. The jobless rate rose 0.2 percentage point in April.

Municipals

Investors willing to gamble to get high yields went flying after a $201 million special project revenue bond issue by the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey.

Yields on the bonds, sold on behalf of Continental and Eastern airlines, topped 9%, about two percentage points higher than the average municipal bond. The new bonds posted the highest yields seen on a municipal issue in almost three years.

The yields were high because the bonds aren't rated and aren't obligations of the Port Authority, which means the authority is under no responsibility to repay the bonds if the airlines default on the debt.

The bonds, which are subject to alternative minimum tax, fall into the "speculative" category, which means they are essentially municipal junk bonds.

Although Continental, a unit of Texas Air Corp., is modestly profitable, Eastern is operating under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code. Eastern, a former Texas Air unit, has been put in the hands of a trustee by the bankruptcy court.

Despite these facts, underwriters led by Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. said the bonds drew strong demand from institutional and high net-worth individual retail investors.

The bonds were priced to yield from 9.38% for bonds that mature in 2005 to 9.63% for bonds that mature in 2015. In comparison, the Bond Buyer municipal bond index yielded 7.62% yesterday.

Proceeds from the sale will be used to build a new 10-gate passenger terminal at La Guardia Airport in New York.

Traders say Continental, based on its own credit, would have to pay exorbitantly high yields to do this type of unsecured bond deal in the corporate market. Also, this type of special project financing is usually done in the municipal market. Continental's corporate junk bonds generally yield between 17% and 19%.

Meanwhile, most actively traded municipal bonds ended between 1/8 and 1/4 point higher. New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bonds due in 2018 rose 1/8 point to 98 bid to yield 7.36%.

Corporate Issues

The junk bonds of a Bally Manufacturing Corp. subsidiary surged on news that the company is launching an exchange offer that analysts called favorable to bondholders.

Bally Health & Tennis's 13 5/8% senior subordinated debentures rocketed five points to finish the session at 58.

Under terms of the exchange offer, each holder of Bally Health & Tennis 13 5/8% subordinated debentures will receive 57 shares of Bally common stock for each $1,000 principal amount of debentures tendered. Based on yesterday's closing price of $10.625 for the common stock, analysts said bondholders would receive equity worth almost $606 for each $1,000 principal amount of debentures exchanged. That's considerably more than what the bonds were trading at before news of the exchange offer.

The junk bonds of R.H. Macy & Co. weakened after news that other vendors and lenders are concerned about the retailer's financial health.

Macy's 14 1/2% senior subordinated debentures due in 1998 ended at a bid price of 68, off one point from Tuesday. The 14 1/2% subordinated debentures maturing in 2001 finished at a bid of 58 1/2, down 1/2 point.

Meanwhile, Michelin Finance B.V. said its offers to buy Uniroyal Goodrich Tire Co.'s and UGTC Capital Corp.'s junk bonds expired Tuesday without any bonds changing hands.

Traders said investors are holding out, expecting Michelin to sweeten the offer. But some investors are doubtful that a higher offer will materialize, which explains why Uniroyal Goodrich junk weakened slightly. The company's 14 1/8% senior subordinated notes lost 1/4 point to end at 110 3/4.

Most junk bonds, or speculative-grade bonds with low credit ratings or no credit ratings, ended about 1/4 point higher.

Investment-grade corporate bonds also closed about 1/4 point higher.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 101 18/32, compared with 101 10/32 on Tuesday. The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 101 22/32 to yield 8.60%, compared with 101 15/32 to yield 8.63%.

Short-term rates moved lower. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills fell to 7.75% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.99%, while the rate on six-month bills ended at 7.67% to yield 8.07%.

Foreign Bonds

British government bonds, which have risen considerably in the past month, ended sharply higher again.

Prices of long-term gilts climbed more than one point during early trading, but they gave back some of the gains later to end about 3/4 point higher.

As in previous sessions, the impetus for the rally came from the foreign exchange markets, where the pound resumed its upward climb, especially against the mark.

Once again, speculation about the imminent entry of the pound into the exchange rate mechanism of the European Monetary System was the starting point for the pound's rise. Dealers said the latest speculation began ahead of a meeting between Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major and his French counterpart, Pierre Beregovoy. But no announcement on the exchange rate mechanism was scheduled nor was such a meeting expected by analysts.

In Paris, Mr. Major helped damp the market's euphoria by reiterating that monetary policy must remain tight for some time to come and that higher interest rates can't be ruled out.

The Bank of England contributed to cooling things off with its money market operations. Faced with an estimated shortage in the money markets yesterday of #1.15 billion ($1.95 billion), the bank quickly arranged only #15 million of repurchase agreements and then purchased through the rest of the day a total of only #948 million of short-term bills to help meet the shortage.

"That was a clear signal to the market not to be looking for lower interest rates soon," one dealer said.

Britain's benchmark 11 3/4% security due in 2003/2007 ended 3/4 higher at 100 1/8 to yield 11.72%, while 12% notes due in 1995 rose 9/32 to 98 29/32 to yield 12.29%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

After being stalled at their opening levels throughout the morning, prices of mortgage-backed securities steadily edged higher to stand at session highs late in the day.

Government National Mortgage Association 9 1/2% securities for June delivery were up 9/32 to 98 16/32. The spread between the Ginnie Mae issue and the Treasury 10-year note remained at 1.25 percentage points.

News that sales of new single-family homes in April fell 1.6% had little impact on the mortgage-backed market.

Ginnie Mae 9% securities were up 10/32 at 96 3/32, and 10% securities were at 100 29/32, up 6/32.

Meanwhile, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9 1/2% securities were up 6/32 at 98 1/32, and 10% securities were at 100 1/ 32, up 5/32. Federal National Mortgage Association 9 1/2% securities were up 6/32 at 98 17/32, and 10% securities were at 100 18/32, up 5/32.
