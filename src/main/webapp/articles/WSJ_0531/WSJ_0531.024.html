LEISURE & ARTS -- Film: Robert Zemeckis's Time Travel Ad Nauseam

  • Author: Julie Salamon
  • Date: 05/31/90

Shortly into "Back to the Future Part III," zany Doc Brown (Christopher Lloyd) realizes he and Marty McFly (Michael J. Fox) are about to embark on more time travel, and the Doc says: "I can't believe this is happening." That sentence may be the only one uttered in the entire film that contains an ounce of true feeling. Certainly that was the thought on my mind as I watched this depressing rehash of material that seemed original just five years ago, when it was. And "I can't believe this is happening" seemed to be what most of the actors were thinking as they gamely trudged through their paces yet again.

Director Robert Zemeckis ("Who Framed Roger Rabbit"; "Back to the Future" and its sequels) has a gift for seeing the world as it appears in Loony Toons. The usual laws of gravity don't apply to his imagination.

What's reined him in has been his ability to succeed with mediocrity. "Back to the Future Part II," the main purpose of which seemed to have been to sell advertising space, took in more than $300 million at the box office. In its first weekend, "Back III" grossed $23.7 million.

This time around Mr. Zemeckis and his producer-screenwriter, Bob Gale, have sent their show to the Wild West, in the year 1885. That's where Doc Brown landed at the end of the last movie, and where Marty goes to rescue him. The setting has allowed Mr. Zemeckis to stage some elaborate Western scenes. Like a television commercial shot in Monument Valley, the terrain looks right but you know it isn't "Stagecoach." Something is being sold, but it isn't story or character or emotion.

In much of the dialogue, the characters pass the time reminding one another what year it is. As part of the Western homage, Marty McFly calls himself Clint Eastwood and wears a poncho. Mary Steenburgen was brought in to fall in love with Doc Brown and to talk about H.G. Wells. This only adds to the misery, because it brings to mind an earlier Steenburgen film, "Time After Time," made a decade ago, when she -- and time travel -- were still fun to watch.

The ticket seller at a Manhattan theater showing Pedro Almodovar's "Tie Me Up] Tie Me Down]" informed patrons before they paid that the movie is in Spanish with English subtitles. "Of course it doesn't need subtitles," he added. "Heh. Heh. Heh."

The ticket man must have suspected that not everyone going to see this comedy about sadomasochistic sex/love has a deep interest in Mr. Almodovar's flamboyantly idiosyncratic vision of the New Spain. He'd figured out that at least some people were attracted by the picture's contested X rating, and were titillated by the chance to see a dirty picture made respectable by its director's reputation. Mr. Almodovar's cinematic virtuosity has been much acclaimed around the world, especially since the success of his kitschy "Women on the Verge of a Nervous Breakdown."

Those seeking pornographic thrills, however, will be disappointed by the relative brevity of the movie's one relatively explicit sex scene. But they won't be as disappointed as the viewers looking for the wayward bawdy humor many people managed to find within the convoluted plot of "Women on the Verge." The only thing in "Tie Me Up]" that may satisfy both camps is a bath toy, a motorized plastic scuba diver with a slyly naughty desire to dabble in human speleology.

Otherwise Mr. Almodovar takes a remarkably earnest approach to this story of a psychopath named Ricky who kidnaps a porn star named Marina and woos her by tying her up in bed. She disdains him completely until he gets drugs for her and is beaten up by thugs. Ricky's wounds touch Marina, and she touches them, and Ricky, and all of this leads to the X-rated moment of passion.

Mr. Almodovar has mingled this story of warped desire with the story of an old film director who has starred Marina in her first -- and last -- legitimate film. The "legitimate" film is a horror picture that uses Marina much the way the porn films do, as a prop with a firm and pliable body.

Victoria Abril, who plays Marina, has the right kind of hard beauty, worn around the edges, for the vulnerable porn princess. Antonio Banderas gives Ricky's madness a sweetness that somewhat diminishes the crude horror of what he is doing (brutally forcing a woman to love him). But the movie's ideas and Mr. Almodovar's handling of them are seriously unfunny.

VIDEO TIP: But does the movie deserve an X rating? Miramax Films, its distributor, argues that it doesn't, in a lawsuit filed against the Motion Picture Association of America. Miramax, as represented by William M. Kunstler, argues that the film deserves an R rating, which permits people under 17 years of age to see the film only when accompanied by a parent or guardian.

Miramax claims that the ratings board is being harsh on "Tie Me Up]" because it is a foreign film, and cites several examples of R-rated American films with equally explicit sex scenes. These include "Body Heat," "9 1/2 Weeks," "Looking for Mr. Goodbar," "The Postman Always Rings Twice," "Fatal Attraction," "An Officer and a Gentleman," "Lethal Weapon II," "No Way Out," "The Accused," "Dead Ringers" and "Blue Velvet." Every one of these is available on videocassette.
