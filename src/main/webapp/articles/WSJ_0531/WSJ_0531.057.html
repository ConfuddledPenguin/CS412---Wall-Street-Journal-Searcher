Economy: New Construction Fell 6% in April To Four-Year Low

  • Author: Tanya L. Bien
  • Date: 05/31/90

NEW YORK -- New construction contracts fell 6% in April to the lowest level in four years, reflecting a response to regulatory clampdowns on high-risk real estate lending practices. Nonresidential building contracts were down 12% from March to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $78.45 billion. The value of newly started one-family homes and apartments slipped 4% to $106.36 billion. Nonbuilding construction, which includes public works and utilities, edged up 1% to $45.38 billion.

F.W. Dodge, the forecasting unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., said total U.S. construction contracts awarded in April fell to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $230.2 billion from $245.47 billion in March. That brought the Dodge index down to 146 from 156 the previous month. The index compares current contracting with a 1982 base of 100.

After a temporary rebound in March, the latest month's setback continues a decline that began in late 1989. Construction contract values to date this year are trailing last year's total by 9%, on an unadjusted basis. Weakness was evident in the four major regions, with sharpest drops in the Northeast at 27%, followed by the South at 11%. The West was even with last year's rates. The only improvement was in the North Central region, which showed a 5% improvement, according to Dodge.

"The collapse of homebuilding in the first half of 1990, despite workable mortgage rates, indicates a problem of availability, rather than cost, of credit," said George A. Christie, vice president and chief economist for F.W. Dodge.
