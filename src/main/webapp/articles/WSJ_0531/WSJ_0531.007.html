Letters to the Editor: Yale Still Educates Tomorrow's Managers

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/31/90

In an April 13 article, you invited the reaction of Carnegie Mellon University's president, Richard Cyert, to Yale's "de-emphasizing the organizational approach" to studying management problems. He responded in kind. Both the question and the response appear to be outdated. We recently have changed our program to improve the education we offer to managers who wish to make a positive contribution to our society in the private, public or nonprofit sectors. We have diversified our Organizational Behavior offerings, emphasized professional, rather than Ph.D., education in this field, and changed the content of the required first-year course. We have not "de-emphasized" the school's commitment to understanding how organizations work and how people work within them, nor have we "driven . . . out" those who are not "discipline-oriented." We offer an innovative and interdisciplinary professional education to those who wish to be leaders in management. We continue to offer courses and to recruit faculty whose paradigms are drawn from the behavioral sciences. Our recent curriculum changes are meant to make sure that our education continues to offer an innovative response to the educational needs of managers for the 21st century. They should not be misunderstood as being hostile to organizational studies.

Michael E. Levine

Dean

Yale School of Organization and Management

New Haven
