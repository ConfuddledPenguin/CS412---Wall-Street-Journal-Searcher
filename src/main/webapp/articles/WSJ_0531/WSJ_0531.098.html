Judge Finds U.S. Violating the Law In Disability Cases

  • Author: Wade Lambert
  • Date: 05/31/90

NEW YORK -- A federal judge said the Department of Health and Human Services is violating the law in the way it processes many Social Security disability claims. The decision could force the U.S. to reconsider tens of thousands of applicants who have been turned down for disability benefits. Judge Leonard Sand said he will order Social Security officials to change their policy for processing the applications. The suit was filed in 1984 after the Reagan administration began taking a harsh approach to most applications for mental and physical disability benefits.

Under the Reagan administration approach, called non-acquiescence, federal officials rejected disability applications, although courts had ruled that the procedures they used were wrong. As a result, thousands of people were rejected for benefits without ever being informed that in similar cases a federal appeals court had ruled that the process used was inappropriate.

Because most of the applicants are poor people without lawyers, they usually never learn that they had grounds for challenging the benefit rejections, said Burt Neuborne, a New York University law school professor who helped bring the case when he was legal director for the American Civil Liberties Union.

Under the disputed policy, the applicants could be disqualified for benefits for various reasons. In some cases, for instance, the opinion of the Social Security Administration's own doctors were given more weight than the applicants' regular physicians. Judge Sand, in federal district court in Manhattan, found that and other guidelines to be illegal.

The judge said that although the department had revised its policy in 1985, apparently in response to the litigation, the procedure for granting claims "leaves a great deal of room for non-acquiescence." The judge said the policy allows the agency to continue avoiding complying with previous holdings by the U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals.

The agency has maintained that it hasn't engaged in any form of non-acquiescence in disability cases at least since 1985.

Judge Sand said a plan has to be worked out to remedy any harm caused by the policy. The remedy could involve reopening disability cases that were rejected as long ago as 1981. But Judge Sand rejected a proposal by the benefit applicants' attorneys to reopen all the cases. Instead, he ordered the attorneys to come up with a narrower plan. He said the plan should cover cases that were rejected for the specific reasons now found to have been in violation of previous court rulings.

Attorneys working on the case said it is impossible to predict how many cases might be affected and how much money might be involved. Prof. Neuborne said a small percentage of rejected applicants did appeal cases involving the disputed policy and that nearly all of them won. Generally the benefits at stake amount to about $500 a month for each individual.

Nancy Morawetz, an attorney for the plaintiffs, said she expects a remedy plan to be approved within two or three months.

A spokesman for the Justice Department, which represented the Health and Human Services Department, said attorneys are reviewing Judge Sand's opinion. He said they have no comment at this time.

Prof. Neuborne said the Social Security Administration adopted the policy of non-acquiescence because it believed that its interpretation of the law, rather than the federal appeals court's intrepretation, was correct. Instead of incorporating the court rulings into its policy, the agency required that each individual applicant go to court to challenge the decisions. Prof. Neuborne said the policy was one of the first "to use non-acquiescence against people who couldn't afford lawyers."

Judge Sand said the applicants had no reason to believe their requests were being rejected in ways courts previously had found to be invalid. Under Judge Sand's decision, the Social Security Administration's policy will have to be changed to state that the previous court rulings are binding.
