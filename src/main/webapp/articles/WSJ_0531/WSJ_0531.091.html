Canadian Pacific's Spinoff Is Blocked By Court Ruling

  • Author: Robert Melnbardis
  • Date: 05/31/90

TORONTO -- The Supreme Court of Ontario blocked a plan by Canadian Pacific Ltd. to spin off its real-estate unit to its common shareholders, saying that holders of preference shares who aren't included in the transaction would be hurt. In his ruling, Justice A.M. Austin said Canadian Pacific's proposed 767 million Canadian dollar (US$650 million) spinoff of Marathon Realty Co. wasn't necessary and wouldn't benefit Canadian Pacific. While the spinoff would enrich Canadian Pacific's holders of common shares, it would leave fewer assets in the company from which to pay preference-share dividends, Mr. Austin said.

The judgment was a surprise because Mr. Austin had ruled on April 23 that holders of preference shares weren't legally entitled to participate in the spinoff, which was devised as a dividend to Canadian Pacific's common shareholders. Holders of preference shares are appealing the April 23 ruling. But in his latest decision, Mr. Austin said Canadian Pacific also must show that the spinoff is fair to holders of preference shares.

Under Canadian law, Canadian Pacific must obtain court approval for a spinoff, which represents a "fundamental change" in the company's corporate structure.

A lawyer for Canadian Pacific said the Montreal-based railway, resources, telecommunications and real-estate concern is studying its options: whether to appeal the court's decision; submit a revised spinoff plan; provide other compensation for Canadian Pacific preference-share holders; or scrap the spinoff plan. The company has 30 days in which to appeal Mr. Austin's ruling to the Ontario Court of Appeal, the province's highest court.

As reported, at Canadian Pacific's annual meeting on May 2, its common shareholders voted 90% in favor of the company's proposal to spin off 80% of Marathon through a share distribution.

Under the plan, Canadian Pacific would keep 20% of Marathon, but the real estate unit's 99 million shares outstanding would make it Canada's most widely held public real-estate concern. Marathon's assets, which have a market value of more than C$4 billion, include office buildings in Canada and the U.S. and prime undeveloped land in railway corridors in downtown Toronto, Montreal and Vancouver.

At the annual meeting, common shareholders also narrowly approved a shareholder-rights plan that would make it prohibitively expensive to launch a takeover bid for Canadian Pacific. Observers saw the spinoff of Marathon as another important anti-takeover measure.

Holders of Canadian Pacific's preference shares are led by the Fielding family of Sudbury, Ontario, which holds about 82% of the shares.

The latest ruling could delay Marathon's debut as a public company for months if the decision is appealed. For several weeks, Marathon has been trading in the so-called gray market, where investors are exchanging promises to buy or sell the stock after it is listed on the Toronto Stock Exchange.

Marathon shares were quoted yesterday in gray market trading at C$7.86 to C$8, up from C$7.75 to C$7.86 on Tuesday when the court ruling was issued. In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Canadian Pacific common closed at $18.75, up 25 cents, on volume of 648,300 shares.
