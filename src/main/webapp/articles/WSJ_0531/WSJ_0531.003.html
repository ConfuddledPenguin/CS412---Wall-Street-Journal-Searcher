Letters to the Editor: Where There's Smoke There's Ire

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/31/90

Your April 23 article "Smokers Go Out of Their Way to Beat Restrictions on Lighting Up in Flight" distorted the reality of the overwhelmingly successful implementation of the smoking ban on virtually all domestic airline flights. You focused on a few addicted smokers and one "smokers' rights" activist funded by the tobacco industry, but failed to mention that 455 million people fly each year in the U.S., the vast majority of whom are thrilled with the new law. The measure has been a huge success since it went into effect on Feb. 25, surpassing even its proponents' greatest expectations. The almost complete lack of news about the public's adjustment to the new law is a sign of its warm welcome. Thus, by focusing on three or four of the millions of smokers and nonsmokers who have enjoyed your article, you drew an inaccurate picture of what has occurred.

Fran Du Melle

Coalition on Smoking OR Health

Washington

---

So the government has banned smoking on domestic flights, and passengers are sneaking smokes in the bathroom. Is anyone surprised? Sooner or later, there will be a bathroom fire, a plane crash and many deaths.

Will the government then admit its mistake and restore the smoking section as the lesser risk? Of course not. The government will ban bathrooms.

Norma S. Hass

Sleepy Hollow, Ill.

---

The current intolerant attitude of many Americans toward smokers shows with chilling clarity how easily a good propaganda campaign can create socially acceptable hatred of a minority. As the harassment, bullying and mistreatment escalate, it should surprise no one that more and more smokers are reacting angrily and impolitely.

The federal law banning smoking on flights of less than six hours is an excellent example of unnecessary government action that causes more problems than it solves. Decisions regarding smoking should have been left to the individual airlines. The marketplace would have decided the issue peacefully and efficiently. Why was it not allowed to do so?

David V. Walden

Chicago

---

I am sorry that smokers are trapped between their addictions and the regulations, but like many nonsmokers, the regulations mean that finally I am able to enjoy traveling. No longer do I get nauseated just at the thought of boarding an airplane. No longer do I arrive at my destination feeling slimed by smokers sitting 20 rows behind me. No longer does my wife make me shower and change my clothes the moment I walk in the door from a trip.

Dave Probert

Goleta, Calif.
