Technology and Medicine: Prenatal Surgery Saves Two Infants From Possibly Fatal Congenital Hernia

  • Author: Marilyn Chase
  • Date: 05/31/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- A team of physicians at the University of California Medical Center here performed delicate prenatal surgery that saved two infants from a potentially fatal congenital defect. It is the first time doctors anywhere have reported success in fetal surgery to correct the problem -- a diaphragmatic hernia, or hole in the muscle wall dividing the chest from the abdomen.

Such hernias occur in about one of every 2,200 babies born. Although children suffering from such hernias may undergo surgery after birth, they stand only a 25% chance of survival using this approach. This is because pressure forces internal organs up through the hernia and into the chest space, crowding and stunting the growth of the fetal lungs so they cannot support life outside the womb.

The prenatal surgery has previously been used to correct urinary obstructions, but doctors termed the hernia repair about 10 times more difficult to perform.

Drs. Michael Harrison and Mitchell Golbus and their colleagues at the University of California reported the first successful patient to undergo such surgery is now nine months old and doing well. A second infant now is six weeks old. Their report is published in the current issue of the New England Journal of Medicine.

"We're extremely proud of these kids. It was a terrible struggle and they were heroic," Dr. Harrison said, flanked by both families at a news conference.

At the news conference, Dr. Harrison added the team has spent 12 years of intense and sometimes frustrating work developing and testing the technique using fetal lambs and monkeys. Prior to June 1989, the surgery was attempted on six infants who died, four of them due to effects of the hernia on the liver and umbilical vein.

About a year ago, the team worked out problems in diagnosis, anesthesia and organ replacement. They had refined ways to preserve the fetal blood supply during surgery, and to prevent preterm labor afterward. Last June, what was to be the first successful hernia repair was undertaken to save the 24-week-old fetus of Beth and Rick Schultz of Chelsea, Mich.

"I was scared because I had never had surgery," Mrs. Schultz said yesterday. But chances of {the baby} surviving long enough to have the surgery after birth weren't good."

After anesthetizing mother and fetus together, surgeons made an incision in the mother's abdomen. They opened the uterus, partially removing the fetus from the womb and making a small cut under his ribs. The fetal stomach and other organs were taken out of the chest, and replaced in the abdomen where they belonged. Then the diaphragm was repaired with a small fabric patch. The fetal abdomen, too small to accommodate all organs in their proper place, also was enlarged with a fabric patch. The entire procedure took 54 minutes.

Seven weeks after surgery, Blake Schultz was born by Caesarian section, about six weeks premature. At nine months old, he weighs 14 pounds. Passed from Dr. Harrison to his parents, Blake squirmed, generally upstaged his surgeon, and punctuated the news conference with a series of squeals.

A second baby, Devona Anderson, the daughter of Stephen Anderson and Lora Scott of Tacoma, Wash., underwent the operation March 3. At six weeks old, she slept unperturbed through her press debut in her parents arms.

Drs. Harrison and Golbus said the fetal surgery, at $18,000, costs far less than the $50,000 necessary to perform surgery after birth due to the intensive-care-unit stay that follows the later procedure.

Asked about the debate over pitting such high-tech procedures against cheap and basic prenatal care, Dr. Golbus countered: "This in no way denigrates prenatal care. They're both necessary to have the healthiest possible cadre of newborns."

Still, Dr. Harrison said only one in 10 babies thus afflicted will become candidates for the rare procedure. "I don't think it will ever be routine. The disease is rare. {The procedure} is hard to accomplish," he said. "But it will save a few babies' lives."

Moreover, he said he was concerned that "as a surgeon and an admitted enthusiast, we might talk parents into it. They need to talk to the skeptics, too."

In the future, he said, the team may attempt to use such surgery to correct certain other congenital defects, such as tumors that steal the fetal blood supply, or malformations of the lung.

Dr. Golbus said fetal surgery may one day be combined with techniques of gene-therapy to perform genetic repairs before birth. "It's harder conceptually," he said, "but easier mechanically.
