Giving a Hand: At Lipton's Centennial Tea Parties, Attendance Is Strictly Voluntary

  • Author: John A. Conway
  • Date: 05/31/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

MONEY IS THE LEGAL TENDER of philanthropy, but it can take forms other than cash. Sir Thomas Lipton, for example, the Scottish-born master marketer and yachtsman who made tea-drinking big business in the U.S., willed $4 million to the poor of his native Glasgow. His successors at the Thomas J. Lipton Co. (now a Unilever subsidiary) who, unlike Sir Thomas, must explain their largesse to stockholders, are celebrating their U.S. centennial by giving time and expertise to a whole spectrum of causes.

When Blaine Hess, Lipton's president, called for "something special" to add to the conventional 100-year hoopla, his staff gave him the idea of using the company's considerable marketing and promotion muscle to urge consumers to volunteer for worthy causes.

The timing was right on the money. Volunteers can be as vital as cash, and their numbers have been shrinking. The Bureau of Labor Statistics found this year that one in five Americans was giving time to charity; in 1974, it was one in four. From 1981 to 1988, according to Independent Sector, a Washington, D.C., umbrella group for nonprofit organizations, the percentage of adults doing volunteer work dropped to 45% from 52%. Among women, traditionally the mainstay of charity drives, the falloff was from 56% to 47%.

With a cash outlay of $500,000, Lipton will stage half a dozen fairs at shopping malls across the country, the first in Chicago, the others in New Orleans, Kansas City, Philadelphia, Dallas and Tampa. At these "mall events," local agencies serving everything from AIDS to the Little League will be invited to make their pitches for volunteers among the crowds Lipton expects to generate.

To round up the agencies, Lipton teamed with an organization that has been recruiting volunteers for 11 years. This is the Volunteer National Center, a Washington, D.C.-based nonprofit group headed by former auto executive George Romney. The Center selected the six cities for the volunteer fairs and will handle the job of inviting the local charities. Lipton takes it from there.

As a food company, malls are Lipton's home ground, explains Anthony Montuori, vice president for marketing support. To keep the crowds coming, shopping centers are hungry for any event that will draw them. Four malls in Kansas City, Mr. Montuori says, began competing for Lipton's volunteer fair as soon as they heard of it.

Lipton's staff warmed up with a minifair at its New Jersey headquarters, rounding up 30 local philanthropic agencies to make their pitches to Lipton employees and those of neighbors like Lever Brothers (a sister Unilever subsidiary), publisher Prentice-Hall and other companies based along the Jersey Palisades. Five hundred potential volunteers showed up.

Before the first volunteer fair opened at Chicago's North Pier Mall, Lipton flew six regional directors of the Volunteer National Center to New Jersey for a two-day, all-expenses-paid seminar on public relations and media techniques. The six, in turn, were expected to conduct their own PR drills in each of the mall cities. To back up these amateur drumbeaters, Lipton will fly its own promotion people to each site to round up local media coverage, help conduct interviews and oversee the fanfare.
