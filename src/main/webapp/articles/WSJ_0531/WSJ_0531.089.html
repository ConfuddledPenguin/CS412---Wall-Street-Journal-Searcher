Law -- Legal Beat: Caseloads of State Courts Soared in 1988

  • Author: Edward Felsenthal and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 05/31/90

State courts, the workhorses of the nation's court system, added an astonishing total of 98 million new cases to their dockets in 1988, the most recent year for which data are available. While 68 million involved traffic and ordinance violations, more than 30 million were new civil, criminal and juvenile cases, according to a study by the National Center for State Courts in Williamsburg, Va.

In contrast, about 962,000 cases were brought into federal courts last year, nearly 5% more than in 1988.

State court civil filings in 1988 increased 4.3% from 1987, and new criminal cases rose more than 5%. The overall increase in new filings is consistent with the steady growth in state court caseloads since the center began the annual study in 1984, says David Rottman, project director.

Completion of cases filed in state courts in previous years has not kept pace with new filings, the study shows. The center estimates that the number of cases added to state court dockets in 1988 exceeds the number of files closed by about 5%.

In the state trial courts that hear the most serious felony and misdemeanor cases, filings of criminal cases rose an unprecedented 8.4%, a rate which, if it continues, could double the number of these cases every decade. Court experts say the upsurge in state-level criminal cases is largely an indication of a rise in drug-related prosecutions, which are also clogging the federal courts.

Defense lawyers, who have long complained about a so-called litigation explosion, view the data as confirmation that litigation is out of hand. Defense attorney James Morris of the Richmond, Va., law firm Morris & Morris says one reason for court congestion is judges' willingness to admit unreliable expert testimony. Many cases, he said, wouldn't "even be filed if it weren't for the fact that some paid expert loosely applying science is able to give an opinion" that the plaintiff should recover.

But the president of the mostly plaintiff-oriented American Trial Lawyers Association played down the caseload increase, saying it has generally kept pace with population growth and has also reflected the rise in business failures. The group's president, Russ Herman, said: "Even if all types of litigation were increasing, in a democracy that's good. The civil courts are the pressure valve for society."

George Milko of HALT, a Washington organization that seeks to promote the interests of consumers of legal services, notes that the persistent rise in case filings provides evidence that efforts to promote forms of dispute resolution outside the court are not succeeding. "Maybe we ask our courts to do more than they can handle," he says.

RETIREE BENEFIT termination is halted by court restraining order.

A federal judge in Connecticut ordered the energy conglomerate Societe Nationale Elf Aquitaine of Paris to refrain temporarily from terminating a health benefits plan for former senior executives of Texasgulf Inc., a U.S. oil company acquired by the French company in 1981.

Richard D. Mollison, who was chairman and chief executive officer of Raleigh, N.C., company at the time of the acquisition, sued Elf Aquitaine and its current chief executive officer, Michel Schneider-Maunoury, after he received a letter from Mr. Schneider-Maunoury that said the plan would be abolished May 31, 1990.

Marilyn Ford, a professor of law at the University of Bridgeport in Connecticut, who represented Mr. Mollison, asked the judge for a restraining order, arguing that the takeover agreement guarantees continuation of the benefit plan.

The suit is part of a recent trend of cases against companies that attempt to cut retirees' benefits in response to rising health care costs. The case appears to be one of the first in which a judge has put a restraining order on a company before any termination of benefits.

A spokesman for Elf Aquitaine said that the company decided "to provide the same medical coverage for senior officers as everybody else." He said although the takeover agreement stipulates that employee benefit levels would not be changed, the rising cost of health care has created a definitional dispute over whether "level of benefits" implies costs borne by the company or health services actually received by retirees.

Elf Aquitaine has retained the New York law firm Sullivan & Cromwell to represent it in the lawsuit. The judge scheduled a hearing for June 1, when he will consider granting a preliminary injunction, a stronger measure than the restraining order he issued last week.

Ms. Ford said that other former Texasgulf executives have expressed interest in joining Mr. Mollison as plaintiffs in the lawsuit and that there is potential for a class action against the company.

NEW YORK ELECTION law is challenged as unconstitutional in federal suit.

The lawsuit, filed by a group of labor unions, targets the state's requirement that voters must register at least 60 days in advance to be able to vote in a primary election. Deborah H. Karpatkin, an attorney for the unions and for 100% Vote/Human Serve Campaign, a national group seeking to make registration easier, said New York's 60-day cutoff "is one of the longest, if not the longest," in the U.S.

The suit, filed in federal court in Manhattan, claims the requirement is unreasonable and detrimental to voter turnout. "It places a harsh and unnecessary barrier in the way of citizens' ability to participate in the political process. . . . Many primary contests don't reach a high level of public attention until the last few weeks before the election, when it is too late to register for that primary," the suit said. For the general election, the registration cutoff is 30 days before the vote.

A spokesman for the New York State Board of Elections said the board and the state attorney general's office are reviewing the suit. He said 60-day requirement is meant mainly to deter "party raiding," in which a member of one party tries to influence the opposing party's primary results.

For instance, a voter might register in the other party's primary in an effort to get the other party's weaker candidate into the general election, the spokesman said.

CHINESE PROVINCE appoints U.S. lawyer as legal representative.

John T. Kesler, president of the 18-lawyer Salt Lake City law firm of Woodbury, Jensen, Kesler & Swinton, said he will represent Szechwan province in business dealings and in legal disputes between U.S. and Szechwan organizations. Szechwan province has more than 105 million residents and is in Western China. Many defense and aerospace companies are based there.

Woodbury Jensen specializes in international commercial work, as well as real estate, technology and corporate law. A visiting Chinese lawyer is an associate with the firm. The firm has also established ties with four Chinese law firms in different parts of China.

Mr. Kesler received the appointment after lecturing in the province's capital on international commercial law.

SALARY FREEZE: Jones, Day, Reavis & Pogue, one of the nation's largest law firms, said it will keep starting salaries this year at 1989 levels, ending years of annual increases. In all its cities except New York, the 1,160-attorney firm will pay new lawyers, who arrive during the summer and fall, a salary package worth $74,000. In New York, the figure is $83,000. Michael H. Carpenter, the Jones Day partner in charge of recruiting, said the Cleveland law firm decided that "this was an appropriate year to hold the line in terms of the concerns of our clients and in terms of outside legal costs and in controlling those outside legal costs." Jones Day's move could tempt other firms, many of them hit hard by the current slowdown in corporate deal work, to hold the line on salaries as well.

ROSENMAN & COLIN, New York, will merge its six-attorney Washington office with Casson & Harkins of Washington, whose 24 attorneys specialize in health care. "Rosenman had wanted to increase its presence in Washington for some time," said Casson & Harkins's Joseph Casson, who said his own firm would benefit from Rosenman's expertise in taxes and securities.
