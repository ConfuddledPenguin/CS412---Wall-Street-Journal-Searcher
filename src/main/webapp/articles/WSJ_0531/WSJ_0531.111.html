Kemper Corp. To Take Charge Of $126.5 Million

  • Author: Jeffrey Taylor
  • Date: 05/31/90

LONG GROVE, Ill. -- Kemper Corp. said it will take a $126.5 million charge to pare down its securities brokerage division, in what analysts said was a belated recognition of problems. The second-quarter after-tax charge will include $108.5 million to write down good will and other intangible assets of the company's brokerage operations and $18 million to pay for layoffs and trimming costs. In the year-ago quarter, Kemper had net income of $121.7 million, or $2.46 a share, including $67.2 million in profit from discontinued operations.

Kemper wouldn't forecast this year's second-quarter results.

In composite New York Stock Exchange trading yesterday, Kemper climbed $1.375, to $39; analysts said the rise reflected investor sentiment that Kemper had moved to address the problems of its brokerage operations.

The charge was widely viewed as an overdue effort by the insurance and financial services company to improve the performance of its regional brokerage concerns.

"We could have restructured sooner, but we believe that this plan is adequate to get the brokers in a stronger competitive position," said John Fitzpatrick, Kemper's chief financial officer.

Kemper acquired most of its regional securities concerns in the past decade at relatively steep prices. The brokerage business was itself attractive at the time, and Kemper viewed the units as sales outlets for its mutual funds and other financial services.

"The price was high, but not excessive," Mr. Fitzpatrick said. "We paid an entry price for regional broker dealerships, which at the time were disappearing off the face of the Earth."

Since then, Kemper has been struggling to reduce the operations' costs and improve their performance.

"They went into the business with a strategic plan that made sense to them, but the entire concept of diversified financial services has been changing over the last several years," said Carol Manning, analyst for Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. "Brokerages have less value today."

The charge comes just two months after David B. Mathis, former head of Kemper's insurance operations, edged out Thomas R. Anderson, president and chief executive officer of the financial unit, for the chief operating job of the parent company.

Earlier this month, Kemper was hit with a $38.2 million arbitration award to a former employee in a contract dispute. Though it is appealing the award, the company has taken a second-quarter after-tax charge of $19.5 million to cover the award, in addition to the $126.5 million for restructuring.

Kemper said the staff cuts and restructuring will bring annual after-tax savings of more than $30 million, once the plan is completed in 1991.

The company intends to lay off 600 employees at the regional brokerage operations, a cut of about 12%, and increase its authorized common stock buy-back program to 2.5 million shares from two million shares.

The restructuring plan also calls for centralizing risk management and consolidating back office functions at the broker firms Kemper owns.

"These steps should be a positive to those who look at cash flow, the competitive position of businesses and control of risk," Mr. Fitzpatrick said.
