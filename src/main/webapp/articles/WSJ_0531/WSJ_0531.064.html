Designated Chief At Westinghouse Has High Goals

  • Author: Gregory Stricharchuk
  • Date: 05/31/90

PITTSBURGH -- Paul E. Lego, Westinghouse Electric Corp.'s newly designated chief executive officer, is setting ambitious goals. The 60-year-old president, who succeeds Chairman John C. Marous Jr. in the No. 1 job effective July 1, said he expects the company's profit and revenue to double "long before the end of the decade." Last year, the company earned $922 million, or $6.31 a share, on revenue of $12.8 billion.

In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, Westinghouse closed at $35, down 62.5 cents.

"We will be a great company. We are very close to being that right now," Mr. Lego said in his first interview since being named to the job. He expects the company to continue to have a return on equity in the 18% to 21% range. Corporate revenues, meanwhile, should continue to grow at least 8.5% annually.

Some of that growth will clearly come from acquisitions. The executive noted, though, that while he doesn't rule out blockbuster acquisitions entirely, he would prefer smaller ones, in the $250 million to $500 million range. Mr. Lego also said Westinghouse won't make acquisitions outside its present portfolio of financial-service, broadcasting and diversified-manufacturing businesses.

Specifically, the executive said he wants to supplement Westinghouse's existing businesses in air-traffic control equipment as well as the production of news and entertainment programming that can be aired both in the U.S. and overseas.

He said he'd be "tickled to death" to acquire a company involved in international air-traffic control, which would enhance Westinghouse's Electronic Systems Group. That unit has been trying to expand its presence in the commercial sector in the face of likely cutbacks in military spending.

Citing legal constraints, Mr. Lego declined to comment on rumors that Westinghouse might be interested in making a bid for Sundstrand Corp., an aerospace parts maker based in Rockford, Ill. However, he cast doubt on the likelihood of a bid, mainly because Sundstrand probably would cost more than $2 billion, and therefore would dilute earnings.

He acknowledged that Westinghouse's growth path "scares" some people who follow the company because they fear it will make poor acquisitions. But he said Westinghouse is picky and has already screened "thousands of companies" as possible acquisition candidates. "We're very controlled," he said, reflecting his own analytical temperament. Confident and intense, Mr. Lego, is the son of a steelworker and joined Westinghouse in 1956 as an electrical engineer.

Increasing the company's revenue, however, "is an imperative," he said. In recent years the company has divested underperforming assets that produced about $1 billion of revenue and Westinghouse now wants to replace that revenue, he said.

As for future succession at Westinghouse, Mr. Lego clearly wants to establish a horse race for the No. 2 job. The executive said he would continue to carry the president's title for several years. The several possible presidential candidates, he said, need additional "seasoning" before they are ready to succeed him.
