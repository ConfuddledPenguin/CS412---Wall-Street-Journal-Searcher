Enterprise: Firms Hurt in U.S. Contracting Scandal May Get Help --- Federal Officials Will Examine Losses Coming From Surety Defaults

  • Author: John R. Emshwiller
  • Date: 05/31/90

For small businesses that have been victims of a nationwide federal contracting scandal, help may be on the way. Today, Sen. Orrin Hatch (R., Utah) is scheduled to preside over a fact-finding meeting in Washington that could eventually lead to Congress's appropriating funds to repay firms hurt by so-called individual sureties, says an aide to Sen. Hatch.

Subcontractors and government officials will discuss with Mr. Hatch surety problems they have encountered and means to help the victims. Some federal investigators estimate such sureties have cheated small businesses out of tens of millions of dollars in the scandal.

At the same time, however, a separate effort in Congress seeks to revive the individual-surety business -- just as it is dying out in the face of tough new federal regulations. This time, fans of the measure say, small businesses will be safeguarded.

An individual surety is supposed to be a well-heeled person who for a fee from the prime contractor provides financial-guarantee bonds on a government building project. If the prime contractor fails to finish the job, the surety is supposed to step in and make sure that the work gets done and that all the subcontractors get paid, even if some of the money has to come from his own pocket.

Government contracting officials say the use of individual sureties grew sharply in recent years as contractors found it increasingly difficult to get bonding from insurance companies.

As reported in this newspaper and elsewhere over the past year, the individual-surety market was invaded in recent years by individuals whose prime asset turned out to be an ability to vastly inflate their own net worth on financial statements. Many of these bogus financial statements slipped by federal contracting officers and weren't discovered until the building jobs went sour and subcontractors weren't paid.

In February, federal acquisition regulations were changed to substantially toughen rules on individual sureties. For instance, if a surety wants to claim real estate on his net worth statement, he has to prove his ownership and the value of the property. He also must give the government a lien on the property until the job is done, says J. William Green, a Navy contracting attorney.

Since the new regulations went into effect, "the number of contractors using individual sureties has shrunk to almost nothing" around the country, says Albert Proctor, an attorney with the Army Corps of Engineers. Before the rule changes, some government agencies estimated that individual sureties were appearing on more than 10% of their construction jobs.

Unfortunately, the new rules may be too effective, says Mr. Green, the Navy attorney who has been a vocal advocate of tougher individual-surety regulations. Now "all those small-business men who used individual sureties have a chance to argue that they can't get any of the {government contracting} action because the rules are too stringent."

Which is exactly what some businessmen and their representatives are arguing. The new individual-surety rules "have had a devastating impact on our members," says Ralph Thomas, executive director of the National Association of Minority Contractors, a Washington, D.C.-based trade group.

Partly because minority contractors tend to be smaller and newer than their white counterparts, they have had a more difficult time getting insurance-company bonding and have relied on individual sureties, he says. Mr. Thomas estimates that up to 75% of his members that do government construction contracting have been hurt by the new rules.

In response to such complaints, Rep. Cardiss Collins, an Illinois Democrat, is sponsoring a bill to authorize a pilot project that would allow individual sureties to form self-policing associations. These groups would ensure that claims against individual members got paid. Critics worry that such a plan would simply pool bad risks and wouldn't significantly reduce the risk of abuse. The House Government Activities and Transportation subcommittee, which Ms. Collins chairs, has scheduled a hearing next Wednesday on the individual-surety issue.

What makes the debate particularly vexing is that for every contractor who has been hurt by the tough new rules, there seems to be a subcontractor hurt by the old lax rule. For example, Camilo Ramirez says he is owed over $98,000 for masonry work his small firm did in 1988 on a building project for the National Aeronautics and Space Administration in California. When the prime contractor failed to pay, the individual sureties also failed to come up with the money.

The absence of that money left him unable to pay his workers or keep up payments on his equipment, which was eventually repossessed, says Mr. Ramirez. Creditors are hounding him for thousands of dollars of unpaid bills and his business, which he spent eight years building, is essentially closed down. The financial stress contributed to the breakup of his marriage, says the 37-year-old Mr. Ramirez. "This has ruined my life. I have nothing now," he adds.

He does have some unhappy company in the form of about two dozen other subcontractors who say they are owed a total of $2.8 million on the same NASA job. These firms have banded together to demand the federal government pay them, and representatives of some of these firms are to meet with Sen. Hatch today.

Getting Uncle Sam to pony up in such matters is a tall order, concedes John Tacke, the subcontractors' attorney. The government aimed to keep itself out of such payment disputes by requiring sureties.

A NASA spokeswoman said the agency believes it acted properly on the contracts.

Sen. Hatch is looking at introducing special legislation to pay these subcontractors and others "who fell into the pit before the surety rules were changed," says Robert Lockwood, an aide to the senator. Already, he adds, more than a dozen senators have expressed interest in co-sponsoring such a measure.
