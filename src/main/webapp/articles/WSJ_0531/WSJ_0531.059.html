Poland's Railroad Fares

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/31/90

WARSAW -- Poland's state-run railroad, recently hit by a crippling weeklong strike, will double passenger fares on Friday, the official PAP news agency reported. The fare increases, which were planned before the labor strike, result from higher fuel and other operating costs and the cut in state subsidies under the new government's economic overhaul program, a railway official told PAP.

The railroad had used government subsidies to keep fares unrealistically low.

For example, a first-class ticket for the three-hour express train ride from Warsaw to Gdansk cost about three dollars before the increases, and even less before the inflation surge of the second half of 1989.

Wildcat strikers snarled freight and passenger service to northwest Poland from May 20 until Monday, when Solidarity leader Lech Walesa persuaded the workers to return to their jobs and give the government until June 13 to consider their demands.

The government, not veering from its anti-inflation economic overhaul plan, had refused to consider pay raises for the workers while the strike was on.

However, officials said a plan, including new pay scales, was being drafted to restructure the railroad for Poland's post-Communist, market-driven economy.
