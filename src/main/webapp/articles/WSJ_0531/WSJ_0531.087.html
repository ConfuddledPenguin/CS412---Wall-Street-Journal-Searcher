Economy: Index of Leading Indicators Fell 0.2% in April --- Despite the Second Decline In 4 Months, Economists Don't Foresee Recession

  • Author: Tuesday Georges
  • Date: 05/31/90

WASHINGTON -- The government's premier gauge of expected economic strength declined slightly in April, indicating continuing weakness. The Commerce Department's index of leading economic indicators dropped 0.2% in April, diluting the optimistic outlook suggested by the 1.0% increase in March. It was the index's second decline in four months. The leading indicators, intended to foreshadow economic activity several months in advance, tumbled 1.0% in February after holding steady in January.

Although the figures do suggest a weakness in the economy, economic analysts said, they don't foreshadow a recession.

"This trendless indicator suggests that the economy is going to grow slowly," said David Berson, chief financial economist at the Federal National Mortgage Association.

Bob Dieli, a financial economist at Northern Trust Co. in Chicago, also forecast slow growth, saying the indicators' seesaw behavior reflects "a mixed economy that is still managing to move forward."

In another sign of sluggishness in the economy, the Commerce Department also reported that new home sales dropped 1.6% in April, the fifth straight month of decline.

Seven of the 11 statistics making up the index of leading indicators contributed to the April decline: average workweek, unemployment claims, factory orders, capital spending, building permits, unfilled orders and stock prices. Positive contributions were made by vendor deliveries, raw materials prices, money supply, and consumer expectations.

Many economists said the April drop doesn't mean the economy is deteriorating.

"Nothing has changed," said Fred Sturn, senior financial economist with Fuji Securities Inc. The numbers "have been bobbing up and down for the last year . . . we're looking at low growth."

April's 1.4% decline in new home sales, following a 8.4% drop in March, brought this key sector of the housing industry to its lowest level since December 1982, when the country had just emerged from a recession. Sales of new single-family homes fell to an annual rate of 546,000 in April, the department said, from a 555,000 annual rate in March.

Economists said increased mortgage rates were a major contributor to the April drop, but predicted a slow decline in the rates for the rest of the year.

In fact, mortgage rates have already begun to come down a bit in May, and Mr. Berson of Fannie Mae said he expects home sales to pick up during the next two months.

Sharing Mr. Berson's view, Mr. Sturn said, "A bottom may be forming in the home sales market."

Mr. Sturn said that although lower mortgage rates should help the market to level off, "the issue of whether banks will be willing to make loans" is still an important factor in the level of housing sales. "Interest rates are secondary to credit policy," said Mr. Sturn.

The numbers in both reports were adjusted for normal seasonal fluctuations.
