Who's News: McDonnell Sets Another Shuffle At Douglas Unit

  • Author: David J. Jefferson
  • Date: 05/31/90

LONG BEACH, Calif. -- In another shake-up at McDonnell Douglas Corp.'s Douglas Aircraft division here, the company named two executives to oversee its troubled C-17 military cargo plane program for the Air Force. John P. Capellupo, 55 years old, deputy president of Douglas Aircraft, was named to the additional post of interim program manager for the C-17 program. He succeeds Robert Clepper, who has taken "a medical leave of absence," Douglas said. Messrs. Capellupo and Clepper couldn't be reached for comment.

Mr. Capellupo was appointed deputy president of Douglas Aircraft in January in a move to help shore up performance on several major transport aircraft programs. He previously had served as president of McDonnell Douglas's missile-systems subsidiary.

The company also named Leonard F. Impellizzeri, 51, staff vice president for engineering and advanced systems at McDonnell Douglas's corporate headquarters in St. Louis, as vice president-deputy general manager for the C-17. He joins Jack Hayden, who was named to the same post last August. Mr. Impellizzeri will oversee engineering aspects of the C-17 program, while Mr. Hayden will continue to direct manufacturing operations.

"The C-17 has to be the area of greatest concern for Douglas, because if the cost goes over the ceiling price it would have a detrimental impact on Douglas's financials," said Lawrence M. Harris, an aerospace analyst with Bateman Eichler, Hill Richards in Los Angeles. Douglas took a $72 million charge in the second quarter last year to reflect cost overruns on the program.

The contract for the C-17 is a so-called fixed-price incentive contract, under which the company and the government share in any cost increases up to a certain ceiling, with the government assuming the larger share, about 80%, Mr. Harris explained. However, once the increases hit the ceiling, around $4.8 billion, the contract reverts to a firm fixed-price contract under which the company must absorb the cost overruns, he said. "Right now, Douglas is at or near the ceiling on the full-scale development portion of the program."

The executive changes on the C-17 program are just the latest in a series of shake-ups here that began last year, when Douglas eliminated four of nine management levels and forced all managers to reapply for their own jobs.
