From a speech on the invention of photocopying by Sol M. Linowitz, a Washington lawyer, given at the Bicentennial Celebration of United States Patent and Copyright Laws in Washington this month:

After Haloid {Xerox's predecessor} acquired the rights to xerography, the company set forth on its mission to develop the invention, and to explore and fulfill its promise. The crucial fact is that it took 14 years between the time Haloid obtained the first license on the Carlson patents and the successful introduction of the first truly commercial product, the Xerox 914. They were years of anguish, uncertainty, disappointment, frustration and only occasional satisfaction during which we were never entirely sure that xerography would ever be a success. It took truly tenacious commitment to bring it all to pass.

How did it happen? I think there were several qualities that gave Haloid and later Xerox its special character.

First, there was faith in the idea. From the beginning there was a belief in this untried, uncertain and still largely ephemeral concept about which many more sophisticated companies had misgivings. It took faith -- and some foolhardiness -- to pursue xerography with such commitment when we knew that other, more knowledgeable, experienced and competent companies had looked at and turned down. . . .

A second essential characteristic of the company was its enthusiasm -- a kind of corporate contagious enthusiasm about what was being done and what might yet be done. From the beginning there was a feeling of excitement which permeated Xerox. . . .

Third, there was a sense of idealism. We saw xerography as far more than a way to make better copies. We sensed not only a copying revolution but also the nascent information revolution. . . .

None of this could have happened without our antitrust laws and the patent system which provided the incentive for investment in an uncertain technology of potential value.

The protection provided by the patent laws enabled the international licensing and marketing of a technology that has brought benefits to the world and continues to reward improvement in xerographic processing.
