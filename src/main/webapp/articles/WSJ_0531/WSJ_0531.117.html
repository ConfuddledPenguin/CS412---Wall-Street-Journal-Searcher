International: EC Study Finds Fraud, Waste In Farm Policy --- Court of Auditors Criticizes EC Commission, States For Roles in Program

  • Author: Mark M. Nelson
  • Date: 05/31/90

BRUSSELS -- In a devastating condemnation of the European Community's agricultural policy, the EC's Court of Auditors found widespread criminal fraud, mismanagement and waste in its $33 billion-a-year farm-subsidy program. The court criticized the EC member states and the EC Commission for their roles in managing farm policy. The court said member states provided too few safeguards against financial abuse and that the commission had failed to satisfy "the requirements of public accountability."

John Carey, one of the court's 12 members, said he couldn't estimate how much money the EC was losing to fraud and mismanagement. Previous estimates from other sources have ranged between $3 billion and $10 billion a year. But Mr. Carey said court investigators had uncovered a series of multimillion-dollar cases in the past two years including rigged tendering of contracts, subsidies paid on misidentified or nonexistent products and other abuses. He also said the EC's policy was too complex and difficult to administer.

Guy Legras, the commission's top civil servant in charge of agriculture, defended the EC's farm policy and the commission's management of it. He said many of the cases the court uncovered were blown out of proportion. But the criticism of the EC farm policy comes at an awkward time for the commission; it is trying to defend its agriculture policy in world trade talks and at the same time convince member states to give more powers to EC institutions.

This isn't the first time the court has faulted EC farm policy, but the strong language of the report is likely to get more attention than previous investigations. A U.S. trade official said she hoped the Court of Auditors report "would finally wake EC consumers up to the fact that they are paying through the nose" for a program that is supposed to be helping farmers but instead is lining the pockets of big traders, shippers and other middlemen.

The court's findings also could make it more difficult for the EC to defend its agricultural subsidies program in the current Uruguay Round of world trade talks. In those talks, the EC's export-restitution program is blamed by most other countries for raising retail food prices and creating unfair competition. Experts say the policy is unfair to producers from less-developed countries who are often squeezed out of the market by the subsidized European products.

The independent Court of Auditors is charged with overseeing financial management of the EC budget, about 54% of which is composed of the agricultural subsidy program. The latest investigation covered only beef, dairy and grain exports. The total export subsidies program costs EC taxpayers more than $11.5 billion a year.
