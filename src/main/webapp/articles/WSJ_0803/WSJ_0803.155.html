EPA Won't Oppose Limits on Liability Tied to Foreclosures

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency, in a policy switch, said it wouldn't oppose legislation aimed at limiting the liability of banks and thrifts for environmental cleanups on foreclosed properties. But an EPA official, testifying before a House Energy and Commerce Subcommittee, said the agency will try to craft a regulation in the next few weeks that would make legislation unnecessary.

James Strock, EPA assistant administrator for enforcement, said the regulation would clarify EPA's opinion that under the 1980 Superfund law, financial institutions are liable for environmental cleanups of a borrower's property only if they were involved in operating the property. Financial institution trade groups are fearful that some recent court decisions have expanded that liability.

Congress currently is considering legislation that would more broadly exempt banks and thrifts from environmental liability, but the EPA said it had concerns about such "blanket immunity." Nevertheless, Mr. Strock said the administration wouldn't oppose legislation that is "narrowly focused on the lender-liability issue" and that still required banks to consider environmental liability in making loans.

The issue is particularly sensitive now, while the government is taking over hundreds of ailing thrifts and assuming responsibility for their properties. Officials of the main bailout agency, the Resolution Trust Corp., said they have identified about 300 properties on which the agency may have some liability. Mr. Strock said the EPA is discussing the issue with the RTC.
