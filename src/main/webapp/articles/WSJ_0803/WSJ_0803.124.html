Politics & Policy: To-and-Fro Budget Talks Recess With Hopes Dim For Reaching a Meaningful Pact by Election Day

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 08/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The bipartisan budget talks sputtered to a temporary halt as political maneuvering reduced the chances for a substantial deficit-reduction accord before Election Day. In a fitting conclusion to 12 weeks of unfruitful bickering, negotiators spent days trying to agree on a choice of secluded location where talks can resume after Labor Day for a last try at reaching a compromise.

If several days of hard bargaining in September fail, then President Bush and the Democrats in Congess will probably be forced to choose:

They can accept $85 billion or $100 billion in painful spending cuts triggered by the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law, which could cripple the government just before the elections. Or they can make the more likely choice of agreeing on a "fig leaf," somehow altering the Gramm-Rudman law to lighten the pain of spending cuts and delay the hard choices until a lame-duck session or even next year.

As the talks recess for a month, a tug-of-war between the urge to seek partisan advantage and the imperative to shrink the deficit continues -- both within the two parties and between them. House Democrats want to put forward a deficit-reduction plan; Senate Democrats block them. Mr. Bush concedes that tax increases are necessary; House Republicans vow to oppose them. Republicans demand that the Democratic-controlled Congress surrender some power over spending; powerful Democrats, particularly Senate Appropriations Committee Chairman Robert Byrd of West Virginia, say they will never go along.

If the talks are to succeed in September, the partisan urges will have to give way. But as the congressional elections approach, that seems unlikely. Indeed, some negotiators say only a new atmosphere of urgency -- perhaps prompted by looming automatic spending cuts or a deteriorating economy or an extraordinary presidential plea -- will force a compromise that wipes out the deficit over five years, which is the goal of the talks.

"Ultimately, there needs to be concurrence this really needs to be done," says House Budget Committee Chairman Leon Panetta of California, who is among the Democrats most eager to cut a deal with the White House. "If that's not there, it's not going to happen."

It's certainly not there yet. "There is not a sense of crisis in the land," says Sen. Wyche Fowler (D., Ga.). "I do not sense that -- outside of Washington -- they are waiting with bated breath."

The familiar obstacles to a compromise remain. Democrats want to tax the rich; Republicans don't. Republicans want to cut government benefit programs deeply; Democrats are reluctant. Democrats want a quick reduction in the Pentagon budget; Republicans don't.

A less prominent issue also stands in the way, namely Mr. Bush's insistence that Congress alter the budget process so the appropriations committees have less freedom to spend.

"Without process change, I don't know how you get something," says Senate Republican Leader Robert Dole of Kansas. But powerful Sen. Byrd, the former majority leader who now chairs the Appropriations Committee, is warning privately that he will fight any erosion of Congress's power over spending.

He has made the issue such a cause within Democratic caucuses that fellow Democrats have grown weary of his lectures, one of which he actually read. As his committee considers fiscal 1991 spending bills, Sen. Byrd's leverage is strengthened. He recently asked subcommittee chairmen to keep a list of those who ask for home-state projects while supporting Mr. Bush's proposal to give the president the ability to knock out individual items from spending bills. He was only half-joking.

The net result of all this politicking is that President Bush's attempt to negotiate a deficit accord with Democrats well in advance of a crisis and the fall election season has failed.

The administration tried to cajole the Democrats, but the bipartisan spirit was dissipated by anti-tax Republicans in the House. It tried to scare the Democrats with frightening figures about the spending cuts that loom should the budget talks fail, but that just persuaded them that Mr. Bush won't allow the cuts to take effect. And, most recently, the administration threatened a barrage of partisan rhetoric, but the message got garbled when House Minority Whip Newt Gingrich (R., Ga.) delivered it so harshly that the White House had to tone it down.

As a result, the Democrats have had a free ride for months -- and they are enjoying it. "The president and the Republican budget negotiators can't agree on what they want, and it shows," Sen. Fowler said on the very day that the administration was trying to put Democrats on the defensive.

Rep. Panetta and Sen. Fowler represent the two ends of the tug-of-war within the Democratic camp. Rep. Panetta has been arguing for months that Democrats ought to make concessions and begin bargaining.

Sen. Fowler, more of a poker player, figures Mr. Bush needs a budget agreement more than the Democrats do. He and his allies argue that their sit-on-our-hands strategy prompted the only significant move in the past 12 weeks: Mr. Bush's abandonment of his campaign promise not to raise taxes.

Among congressional Republicans, the roles are reversed. Sen. Pete Domenici (R., N.M.), who has been wrestling with the deficit for a decade now, keeps refining the deficit-reduction plan in the thick red binders his staff has been carrying around for weeks. But so far, it hasn't been endorsed by Republicans, let alone offered to Democrats.

His antagonist within the GOP is Rep. Gingrich, the No. 2 House Republican. "The White House has been far more patient than I would be," Rep. Gingrich says, explaining that Mr. Bush "truly believes that other people can be convinced to be reasonable." He adds: "The Democrats in Congress are doing what they can to convince him that that's not a very smart assessment."

Key Dates in Budget Negotiations

May 13 -- Bush initiates budget summit, declares "everything is on the table."

May 15 -- First meeting of summit negotiators at White House.

June 26 -- Bush abandons his "no new taxes" campaign pledge, admits "tax revenue increases" are needed.

July 16 -- Budget chief Darman says spending cuts of $100 billion loom in October if no pact is reached.

July 18 -- House Republicans vote to oppose "new taxes and all tax rate increases."

July 24 -- Last meeting of budget summit. House Majority Leader Gephardt, according to GOP, indicates Democrats will offer plan within days; Gephardt denies that.

July 26 -- Republican plan to raise taxes on beer, wine and liquor and to cap at $10,000 deductions for state and local income taxes leaks.
