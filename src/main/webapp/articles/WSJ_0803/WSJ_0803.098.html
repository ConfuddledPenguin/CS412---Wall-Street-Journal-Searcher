OTC Focus: Market Dives to Lowest Level Since May; Growth Stocks Continue Nagging Slump

  • Author: Anne Newman and Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 08/03/90

Over-the-counter stocks plunged to the lowest level since early May on news of Iraq's invasion of Kuwait. The OTC market's leaders extended their losses as investors' ardor for growth stocks continued to cool.

Yesterday's session extended the string of OTC market losses to 12 out of the past 13 trading days. The Nasdaq Composite Index fell 7.02, or 1.6%, to 428,99.

OTC losers swamped winners, 1,548 to 641. Volume rose to 146.5 million shares from 129 million shares on Wednesday.

Trucking, technology and consumer stocks fell farthest. But investors paid higher prices for energy-related shares that could benefit from sharply higher oil prices and the American Depositary Receipts of some South African gold companies.

William R. Rothe, head of OTC trading at Alex. Brown & Sons, Baltimore, said the Kuwaiti situation clouded a horizon already burdened by an apparent deadlock on the federal budget and a looming possibility of recession. He added: "You really didn't see massive selling pressure today as much as you did a slow and inexorable downward drift as buyers decided to sit on the sidelines."

Growth stocks continued to slide. Microsoft eased 3/4 to 66, Intel tumbled 2 1/4 to 40 1/4, Sun Microsystems dropped 1 to 30 1/2, Quantum slid 1 1/4 to 20 1/2, Conner Peripherals eased 3/4 to 26 3/8, AST Research fell 1 3/8 to 19 3/4 and Nike lost 3/4 to 84 3/4.

"They clearly threw the towel in on the growth stocks," said Jack Laporte, portfolio manager of the T. Rowe Price Associates New Horizons Fund. Mr. Laporte suggested that continued selling in those stocks was partly triggered by yesterday's "Heard on the Street" column in this newspaper reporting that some growth stocks have been ebbing steadily in recent days.

Robert O'Toole Sr., head of institutional OTC sales trading at Shearson Lehman, said he saw some bargain hunting near the close of the market. But he said prices remained soft enough for him to expect smaller stocks to open lower in the morning, with a "technical bounce" higher later in today's session after the sellers exhaust themselves.

A number of energy and gold stocks benefited from the developments in the Mideast. Baker Hughes warrants, which trade over the counter, surged 1 1/8 to 8 3/4; Forest Oil gained 1 3/4 to 11 3/4; Dekalb Energy jumped 1 1/2 to 27 1/2; Energy Ventures rose 1 1/4 to 19 3/4 and Tejas Gas climbed 1 1/4 to 29 1/4.

Among gold shares, Anglo American Corp. of South Africa added 3/8 to 31 3/8 and Buffelsfontein Gold Mining rose 3/8 to 15 7/8.

Tele-Communications eased 1/4 to 13 1/8 on 976,800 shares after the Denver cable operator announced it will invest $5 million in Video Jukebox Network and help expand that company's interactive music television channel. Tele-Communications' planned purchase of one million Video Jukebox shares at $5 apiece will complete the first phase of a planned $10 million Video Jukebox private placement. Video Jukebox closed at 7 bid.

Earnings news and outlooks again propelled some shares.

Sierra On-Line jumped 2 to 23 after the Oakhurst, Calif., maker of interactive entertainment software for personal computers reported late Wednesday that net income for the first quarter ended June 30 rose to 18 cents a share, from 17 cents a year earlier.

Statewide Bancorp tumbled 1 1/4 to 4 3/4 after the Toms River, N.J.-based company reported a loss for the second quarter ended June 30 of $9.43 a share, after an $83.4 million loan-loss provision, compared with net income of 49 cents a share a year earlier. However, Douglas Johnson, president and chief executive officer, said he believes the bank can return to profitability "immediately."

Centex Telemanagement lost 7/8 to 25 3/4, although the San Francisco provider of telecommunications management services reported net income for the second quarter ended June 30 rose to 25 cents a share from 17 cents a year earlier.

Pointing to Centex's profit gain, Morgan Stanley emerging growth analyst L. Keith Mullins said in an interview that the stock's decline shows how the market is indiscriminately pounding shares of smaller companies, regardless of how strong their earnings may be.

Cellcom added 3/8 to 2 5/8 after the Fairfield, N.J., communications company said it gained an exemption from the listing requirements of the National Association of Securities Dealers, the self-regulatory body that operates Nasdaq. Cellcom, which didn't meet NASD's listing requirements as of March 30, said it will meet those standards after it closes a previously announced sale of its franchise to operate a rural service area in Oklahoma. Its stock will continue to trade on Nasdaq.
