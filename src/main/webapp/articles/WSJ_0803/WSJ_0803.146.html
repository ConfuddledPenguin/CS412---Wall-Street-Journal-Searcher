Credit Markets: Bonds Fall as Iraq's Invasion of Kuwait Stirs Fears Of Rising Oil Prices and Higher Inflation Rate

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Stuart Drown
  • Date: 08/03/90

NEW YORK -- Iraq's invasion of Kuwait sent bond investors scrambling for cover yesterday on fears that war between the two Middle East nations will cause oil prices -- and inflation -- to soar. The sell-off started during overnight trading in Tokyo and continued in London and New York. Traders said activity was busiest in London, where dealers reported heavy selling by investors from all around the world, including those in the U.S., Britain, Saudi Arabia and Japan.

Even investors in smaller countries were big sellers. "We were extremely busy in Singapore," said Lawrence Leuzzi, a managing director at S.G. Warburg & Co.

The prospects of higher oil prices not only increase the odds that inflation will accelerate, but could force the Federal Reserve to change monetary policy.

News that oil prices are rising, "is coming at the worst possible time, when the economy is already extremely weak and when there is a major effort underway to reduce the size of the budget deficit," said Norman Robertson, chief economist at Mellon Bank Corp. He said that rising oil prices will make it difficult for the Federal Reserve to further reduce interest rates. At the same time, high oil prices will drag down already weak business activity.

"If not recession, this certainly carries the threat of stagflation, where oil prices rise and exacerbates inflation and at the same time reduces the economic growth rate. It's a very vicious brew here," said Mr. Robertson. The economist estimates that if oil prices rise to $25 a barrel, the nation's inflation could rise about 1/2 of a percentage point to between 4.5% and 5%.

On the New York Mercantile Exchange, September crude settled at $23.11 a barrel, up $1.57 on the day. The contract traded as high at $23.80.

The yield curve steepened in reaction to yesterday's news, as prices of short-term securities ended unchanged to slightly lower while prices of long-term bonds were clobbered.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell 1 1/4 point, a loss of about $12.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, jumped to 8.45%.

Two-year notes, on the other hand, fell less than 1/8 point to yield 7.88% while three-month bills ended unchanged to yield 7.66%.

Some traders detected signs of "flight-to-quality" buying of short-term Treasury bills yesterday, but were surprised there wasn't more of this, in view of the financial market's reaction to the Iraqi invasion. Because U.S. Treasury bills are considered the safest securities in the world, investors often sell out of other securities and aggressively buy Treasury bills when there is political or economic upheaval in other parts of the world.

But trading yesterday was dominated by investors making "yield-curve trades," essentially selling long-term Treasurys while buying short-term securities on expectations that these securities will outperform the market in the coming weeks and months.

In the wake of the Iraqi invasion, economic data released yesterday passed more-or-less unnoticed. Those reports were the latest data on claims for unemployment and factory orders. As trading quieted late yesterday afternoon, however, the market began to focus on the July employment report due this morning.

The market is looking for the report -- the first official indication of the economy during July -- to confirm signs of marked economic deterioration.

"The question is, if it is a good number, how high can the market go, now that they've fallen like this," says one trader.

Economists surveyed by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report estimate on average that nonfarm payrolls increased by 5,300, or by 130,000 excluding layoffs of census workers. Unemployment is seen rising 0.1% to 5.3% during July.

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 103 2/32, down from 104 8/32. The issue's yield climbed to 8.45% from 8.35%. Two-year notes were quoted at 100 6/32 to yield 7.88% compared with 100 9/32 to yield 7.83%.

Short-term rates were little changed. The discount rate on three-month bills was unchanged at 7.43% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.66% while the rate on six-month bills was modestly higher at 7.31% to yield 7.67%.

The Treasury Department auctioned $4 billion of 44-day cash management bills yesterday. The average rate was set at 7.60%.

Corporate, Junk Bonds

Junk bonds ended between 1/2 and 3/4 point lower. Trading was limited, however, and junk-bond dealers said that in most cases prices were simply quoted lower. Junk bonds are speculative-grade issues that carry low ratings or no ratings at all.

Among the high-yield bonds that suffered losses were pay-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc., which staged a partial rebound after falling more than two points earlier. Pay-in-kind bonds pay interest in the form of more securities instead of cash.

RJR Nabisco 17 3/8% convertible pay-in-kind debentures fell one point to 113 5/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. RJR's 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures finished one point lower at 91 3/4.

Despite yesterday's declines, First Boston Corp. said its junk-bond index showed a positive return of 3.23% in the month of July.

Advancers outpaced decliners by more than four to one, and the value of the First Boston high-yield index reached 149.45 in July, surpassing its July 1989 peak of 147.91.

"This is the first time the market has recouped all the losses from mid-1989 till February," said Sam DeRosa-Farag, a high-yield analyst at First Boston.

In addition, Mr. DeRosa-Farag pointed out that bonds, which are trading at between 60 to 80 cents on the dollar, are beginning to outperform bonds trading at par and above.

Bonds in the 60-cents to 80-cents on the dollar range produced positive returns of 4.6% in July compared with a 2.1% positive return generated by bonds trading at par and above, said Mr. DeRosa-Farag.

In the investment-grade market, prices of high-grade corporate bonds ended between 3/4 point to one point lower.

Municipal Securities

Municipal bonds finished 1/4 to 1/2 point lower in calm dealings.

Despite the slight losses, traders said the supply shortage in municipals continues to provide solid support and foster bullish sentiment.

Among actively traded secondary issues, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 finished 1/4 point lower at 99 1/2, pushing the yield up to 7.23% from 7.21% late Wednesday.

In the new issue sector, the Pennsylvania Housing Finance Authority's $80 million single-family mortgage revenue bonds were priced by a PaineWebber Inc. group.

The bonds, which are subject to the alternative minimum tax, were priced at par to yield from 6.30% in 1994 to 7.65% in 2023.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities ended lower.

Among actively traded issues:

Government National Mortgage Association 8.5% securities were down 13/32 at 95 17/32, and the 9.5% issues, at 100 12/32, were down 10/32.

Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% issues were off 9/32 at 99 25/32, and Federal National Mortgage Association 9% issues were down 11/32 at 97 31/32.

Foreign Bonds

Iraq's overnight invasion of Kuwait caused global bond prices to tumble.

In West Germany, prices were sharply lower. But traders said there has been little panic-selling. The trend much of the day was set in the Bund futures pit in London.

Bonn's 8.75% benchmark bond due 2000 fell 0.63 point to 100.32 to yield 8.69%.

British government bonds, called gilts, ended with losses of as much as 3/4 point, but they had been down by well over a point at midday.

Britain's benchmark 11.75% bond due 2003/2007 was quoted late at 102 15/32, down 22/32 to yield 11.37%.

In Tokyo, dealers said the market closely followed the movement of the U.S. dollar, with yen bonds recovering from their lows whenever the the dollar retreated from its highs.

At the 3 p.m. JST close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the yield on Japan's benchmark No. 119 bond was 7.575%. The price was down 0.34 point to 85.29.
