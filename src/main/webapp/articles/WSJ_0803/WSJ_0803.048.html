SOVIETS' CHEMICAL WEAPONS

The Soviet Union has a stockpile of 40,000 tons of chemical weapons, about 10,000 tons less than previous independent estimates, the country's commander of chemical forces was quoted as saying.

The weapons are all stored in Soviet territory, Stanislav Petrov was quoted as telling the Communist Party newspaper Pravda. Mr. Petrov told Pravda there are no facilities in the Soviet Union to destroy the chemical weapons, despite a superpower agreement to eliminate 80% of them immediately.

A destruction plant was built at Chapayevsk, 550 miles southwest of Moscow, but was closed last summer after local residents complained it would add to already high pollution levels. Mr. Petrov told Pravda the Chapayevsk site would become a training center for workers who destroy chemical weapons.

The Supreme Soviet legislature was asked to find a safe site to build a replacement for the Chapayevsk facility, but so far no lawmaker has volunteered a district in which to place the plant.

WORLD SUMMIT FOR CHILDREN

Thirty-eight presidents (including George Bush), 20 prime ministers and two kings have confirmed that they will participate in the World Summit for Children at the United Nations this September, organizers said, adding that it would be the largest gathering of world leaders in history. Some 80 leaders have yet to respond. The gathering, whose co-chairpersons are Prime Minister Benazir Bhutto of Pakistan and Prime Minister Brian Mulroney of Canada, will focus on such topics as disease prevention, nutrition, education and child labor.

STRIKE THREAT IN BRAZIL

Brazilian oil workers want a 280% pay raise from the state oil monopoly Petrobras and will strike in September if their demands are not met, a union leader said. "The 280% figure represents losses caused by inflation during the last year," said Mozart Queiroz, a director of the Oil Workers Union. "If we cannot reach an accord we will begin a strike on Sept. 4." Petrobras, which produces 665,000 barrels of oil per day, has about 60,000 workers represented by 19 unions nationwide. Mr. Queiroz said he expected Petrobras to offer a raise of between 60% and 70%.

EUROPEAN EXPORT CAMPAIGN

The European Community is planning a major export-promotion campaign in Japan. EC officials said a multimedia advertising campaign to improve the image of European products would be launched next year with a budget of well over $1 million. The campaign will focus on four industries: furniture, jewelry, health foods and testing and measuring equipment. The Japanese must realize that there is more to be found in Europe than Gucci shoes and Louis Vuitton bags, EC officials said.

AIDS IN UGANDA

At least one million Ugandans -- out of a population of about 17 million -- are believed to be infected with the AIDS virus, said Samuel Okware, head of Uganda's AIDS-control program. He said that as of July there were 17,000 diagnosed AIDS cases in Uganda, an increase of nearly 50% from the number recorded in December. Dr. Okware also said that heterosexual contacts were responsible for more than 90% of AIDS cases diagnosed in Uganda and that slightly more women were dying of the disease than men.

JAPANESE RESEARCH

Joint research projects between companies and universities are on the rise in Japan, jumping 21% in fiscal 1989, ended last March, from the previous fiscal year, the Education Ministry said. Ministry officials said the fiscal 1989 total, 705 projects, was five times the number in 1982 when the joint industry-university research system started. The officials said joint research projects in fiscal 1989 amounted to 3.37 billion yen ($22.9 million), a fivefold surge from fiscal 1982.

AN ORDER FROM THE EC

The European Community ordered Spain to allow private express-mail companies to compete with the state postal service for international deliveries.

The EC said Spain's post-office monopoly violates EC competition law and gave the government two months to change its rules. Meanwhile, the community is putting pressure on Danish authorities to abandon similar practices.

Because of EC action since 1984, West Germany, France, Belgium and Italy have abandoned most curbs placed on international courier firms. The community has also ordered the Netherlands to scrap restrictive practices, but that country is appealing the decision to the European Court of Justice.

The latest move is "further evidence of the {EC} commission's determination" to ensure that the express-mail market is open to competition, said Michael D'Arcy of the Dublin-based International Express Courier Conference.
