GATT Charges Japan With Maintaining Barriers to Imports

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/03/90

GENEVA -- Japan is maintaining a "hard core" of import barriers against many farm products and several kinds of industrial goods, a survey by the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade said. Analysts at GATT's secretariat said Tokyo has made "tangible progress" in opening its markets to foreign goods in recent years, under pressure from trade partners, led by the U.S., to reduce its surpluses.

"However, a hard core of high protection has remained," said the survey, which also criticized other parts of Japanese trade policy and predicted rising pressure from Third World exports.

Japanese Ambassador Minoru Endo said his country has taken "giant steps" to lower trade barriers.

Criticism of Tokyo often seems "to stem from an outdated perception about us," Mr. Endo told a session of GATT's 96-nation council, which commissioned the review.

Rice farming is "the most deep-rooted example" of how the Japanese government gives wide-ranging protection against foreign competition to agriculture, the survey said.

Developing countries are especially hit by restrictions on industrial products, which include leather, jute, clothing and footwear, the survey said.

A thicket of trade regulations and official channels was cited as another import obstacle.

GATT analysts said that while Japan says it reduced state aid to farmers 20% since 1986, it "continues to have a highly protected agricultural sector."

The report noted that Japan last spring urged major private companies to boost imports. But it said this approach could distort trade by promoting only certain kinds of imports.

GATT also warned Tokyo against making trade restraint deals with Third World producers wanting to gain a foothold in the Japanese market, saying such accords endanger the principles of international trade.

Japan has rarely used this method but is likely to face increasing pressure as developing economies become more diversified and competitive, the survey said.

Recent market-opening moves have led to surging shipments of cigarettes, wine and other alcoholic drinks, fruits, beef, passenger cars and semiconductors, the survey said.
