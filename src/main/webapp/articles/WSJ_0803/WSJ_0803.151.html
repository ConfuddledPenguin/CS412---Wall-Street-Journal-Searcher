Foreign Exchange: Dollar Rises in Hectic Day, Gives Up Part of Early Gain

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 08/03/90

The dollar quickly soared as Iraq's invasion of Kuwait raised the specter of war, but the advance slowly eroded as traders turned their attention back to weak U.S. economic growth. This wild ride produced hectic trading, and left many traders confused if not frightened.

"It was a very, very bloody day for most traders," said Manfred Totzauer, a senior trader at Citibank in Frankfurt. He explained that many of the market's large players had positioned themselves for further declines in the dollar, and the invasion news caught them off-guard.

Because these players -- large banks, institutional investors and multinational corporations -- have positions amounting to tens or even hundreds of millions of dollars, some losses yesterday easily ran into millions of dollars, traders said.

Initially, "there was panic; the market was caught short dollars and people had to buy," said Peter Stein, a trader at Commerzbank AG in Frankfurt. "It's always like this when there are international shocks."

In late New York trading, the dollar stood at 1.5915 marks and 149.25 yen, up from 1.5885 marks and 147.18 yen late Wednesday. The British pound fell marginally to $1.8545 from $1.8555 late the day before.

But over the preceding 24 hours, the volatile dollar rocketed from a low of 1.5818 marks and 146.50 yen in very early Thursday trading in the Far East to intraday highs of 1.6215 marks, and 151.58 yen -- up 2.5% and 3.5%, respectively -- in early European activity. By late afternoon in New York, the dollar had fallen back 1.9% from its high against the mark and 1.5% from its high against the yen.

In early trading in Tokyo Friday, the dollar was quoted at 149.75 yen almost an hour after the opening; it closed in Tokyo Thursday at 148.90 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5955 marks, down from Tokyo's Thursday close of 1.6038 marks.

Many traders expect the dollar to resume its recent decline unless the Iraq-Kuwait situation escalates. "The Iraq-Kuwaiti incident will not break the basic trend for the dollar," which has been falling recently because of a weakening U.S. economy, lower U.S. interest rates and higher rates abroad, said Friedrich W. Menzel, a risk manager for Citibank in Frankfurt. "What we are seeing will probably be very short-lived."

But some analysts think such interpretation is short-sighted or simplistic. "This isn't just an increase in prices," argued Lawrence A. Veit, an international economist at Brown Brothers Harriman & Co. "The territorial integrity of every state in the Middle East is now threatened. In a sense, there's a natural alliance between Iraq and Iran. This is a confrontation between the high-population countries in the Middle East that want higher oil prices in the near term and the low-population countries that want lower prices in the short term."

The dollar typically jumps during periods of international tension as international investors flee into the U.S. currency and dollar-denominated investments as a "safe haven." Yesterday, the dollar's initial climb was exaggerated. Many traders and investors, who had sold dollars in expectation that the currency would keep falling, began scrambling to buy them back either to cut losses or to lock in profits. Those purchases, in turn, triggered so-called stop-loss orders -- instructions that traders, investors and corporations leave with banks to buy or sell currencies at specific levels -- driving the dollar further upward.

"It kept going as each market opened; each market had to have a chance to react to the invasion," said Ian Spence, chief dealer at Manufacturers Hanover Trust Co.

Eventually, however, traders ended up holding more dollars than they wanted to, said Mr. Spence, "and following the invasion, nothing else really happened: The U.S. didn't declare war; Russia didn't declare war." Moreover, Iraq's embassy in Washington said the invasion forces might withdraw from Kuwait in a few weeks. As a consequence, traders and others spent the rest of the U.S. trading day undoing some of their previous purchases.

Roberto Bianchi, a first vice president at Union Bank of Switzerland in Zurich, said, "At the {dollar's} higher levels, there was selling by people who had missed the move in the past week and looked at this as an opportunity to sell dollars."

Meanwhile, traders began reassessing the consequences of higher oil prices that would result from Iraq's ability to control roughly 20% of the world's oil reserves. "With their strong currencies, the European economies look better equipped to handle stronger oil prices than the U.S. economy," said Mr. Bianchi of Union Bank. He added that higher prices would also make it more difficult for the U.S. to boost gasoline taxes as a means of closing the budget gap, as has been suggested.

The U.S. currency has been plagued by a softening economy, lower U.S. interest rates plus rising rates abroad. Such a combination normally reduces the dollar's allure to international investors and speculators. In addition, the large U.S. budget and trade deficits have hurt the dollar.

Despite the renewed bearish dollar sentiment, the yen remained relatively weak against the U.S. currency. "The yen should be the largest loser {from higher oil prices}. The Japanese are totally dependent on oil imports, and any threats to supply are going to be very threatening to the Japanese," said Jeremy Rumble, assistant chief dealer at Royal Bank of Canada in London. Because oil is priced in dollars, any weakness in the yen further increases petroleum costs to Japanese buyers and adds to Japanese inflation, which the Bank of Japan is already concerned about.

Some traders contended that while continental European countries and Japan could withstand higher oil prices better than the U.S. -- because their economies are more buoyant -- an actual cut in supply would hurt them more and also their currencies.

"Although the U.S. is a 50% importer," that's much less than most of Continental Europe and Japan imports, said Mr. Rumble. "The mark, yen and Swiss franc will be hurt." By contrast, the dollar and sterling would benefit. Britain as well as Norway control oil reserves in the North Sea.

Mr. Veit of Brown Brothers added that with West German production labor costs equal to $19.89 an hour compared with $14.88 in the U.S., "the mark is an overvalued currency."

Furthermore, "the potential impact of inflation is going to be a lot worse in Japan and Germany, particularly in Japan, where you also have a weak yen," said Mr. Veit. At current exchange rates and with Brent North Sea oil at $25 a barrel, he said that the cost of a barrel of oil in yen terms will be 50% higher than its 1989 average. That compares with a 37% rise for U.S., a 22% boost for Britain and only 18% for West Germany.

Nonetheless, "the fact remains that the U.S. economy is still fundamentally weak. There are a lot of fundamentals that argue for a weaker dollar, and those just don't go away because there's a war in the Middle East," said Mr. Spence of Manufacturers Hanover. "We can pretend today never happened. We had this one-day blowout; we're back to basics."

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $376.80 an ounce, up $3.50. Estimated volume was a heavy 6.5 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $374.95 an ounce.
