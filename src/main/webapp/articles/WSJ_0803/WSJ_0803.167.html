Compromise on Index Futures Regulation May Be in Trouble

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/03/90

WASHINGTON -- A compromise designed to resolve the regulatory dispute over commodities regulation could be facing trouble after a key senator said he was considering backing away from the accord. The compromise agreement, aimed at settling a squabble over stock-index futures regulation, has gotten "bogged down" as staff members try to work out the details of the plan, said Sen. Christopher Bond, who has been a catalyst of the compromise.

The Missouri Republican said that if details of the compromise can't be reached among Senate aides drafting the legislation, he would withdraw his support for the agreement and back the original proposal put forth by the Bush administration. That plan would shift regulatory authority for stock-index futures to the Securities and Exchange Commission from the Commodity Futures Trading Commission.

As currently discussed, the compromise would leave stock-index futures oversight with the CFTC but grant authority to the Federal Reserve Board for stock-index futures margins, the good-faith money needed to trade the product. Margins have become a key issue because they determine the amount of leverage traders can use.

"I would prefer the compromise," Sen. Bond said. "But if we continue to bog down in the specifics, we'll have to go back to the original proposal. We have to do something." Sen. Bond said he probably would make his decision when the Senate returns from its recess after Labor Day.

Stock-index futures allow investors to bet on the movement of a basket of stocks without buying the actual shares. They often are cited as pushing around the stock and options markets, which are regulated by the SEC.
