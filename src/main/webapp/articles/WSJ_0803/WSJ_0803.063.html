Free to Enjoy the Cruelty of the Streets

  • Author: J. Douglas Ousley
  • Date: 08/03/90

When a church opens its doors for worship, it opens them to everyone, and that should include the homeless, no matter their physical or mental condition. But this Christian response can also be dangerous -- witness last year's horrifying murder of a worshipper in St. Patrick's Cathedral by a deranged homeless man. At the church where I am rector, a midtown Manhattan Episcopal parish, just such people -- homeless and unmistakably very mentally ill -- do sometimes wander in during our Sunday services. One man in particular, swathed in torn and dirty clothes and unable to speak coherently, has come in several times; he walks quickly up a side aisle, enters a pew near the front, kneels and seems to pray, though it be only babbling.

He even came in briefly while the new Episcopal bishop of New York was conducting confirmation ceremonies. As on previous occasions, he knelt, seemed to pray, then jumped up and walked out down the center aisle, speaking frantically but causing only minimal disturbance.

But on a recent Sunday, his pattern changed radically. As my associate minister was giving her sermon, the man came quickly up the center aisle in a very agitated state. He entered a front pew, knelt and then stared at the woman in the pulpit. Suddenly, he scrambled from the pew and ran toward her, shouting.

My associate stopped speaking but held her ground in the pulpit. Fortunately, two laymen reacted quickly and ran up to either side of the man, touched his arms gently and quietly persuaded him to leave. They escorted him back down the center aisle as he raged and shouted.

"And we will pray for him, too," my associate told the congregation.

Though only a brief intrusion, it left us all a bit shaken, realizing how differently and violently such an incident might have ended. And, of course, thoughts of our own safety aside, we are concerned for the hundreds of people such as our anguished visitor who are living on the city's streets, most of them seemingly mentally ill. Our church, like so many in the city, has programs to aid the homeless. But our concern also leads us to a philosophical, legal and, sometimes, religious debate with ourselves: Should insane people who roam the streets be confined against their will or should they be allowed to remain free?

Many are on the streets as a result of the closing of mental institutions in the 1960s and '70s. Critics who campaigned against these institutions said they were more barbaric prisons than hospitals for healing; sadly, the community-based outpatient clinics that were supposed to have replaced them have never materialized.

When I began my ministry in New York City in the early 1970s, I, too, argued that these unfortunates were better off on the street than in some hellish asylum. Now, I am not so sure.

A friend who is both a professor of philosophy and an Episcopal priest doesn't accept the position that society's overriding concern is for the freedom of movement of the insane. "I am a member of the American Civil Liberties Union and I am a Christian," he told me. "The ACLU part of me says they should not be locked up; the Christian part of me says that it is not right to let them roam the streets in such terrible shape."

My friend's statement of the alternatives shows that it's not simply a question of confining the disturbed against their will or letting them live free -- for what does "free" mean in such cases? How could the man who lunged toward the pulpit in our church be described as exercising his free will if he is clearly unaware of what he is doing? How could he be said to have civil rights that include freedom of movement if his actions are driven by inner compulsions over which he has no control?

Surely not only the Christian would take a larger view that leaving the man in a degraded state not only doesn't help him but casts a moral shadow over the society that is guaranteeing him his putative freedom of movement. Surely not only the Christian would wonder if we would all be better off if the man were confined and had a better chance for a more human existence.

We need, then, to recognize that confining at least some of the mentally ill may be the best thing for them -- not just pragmatically, for the good of society, but in humanist and spiritual terms, for their own good. Already, in New York City at least, there appears to be wide public sympathy for measures to keep the homeless from bus and train stations. As these measures could reflect a coming wave of resentment of the homeless themselves, it may be time to find ways to get the mentally ill homeless off the streets.

As for those who are not insane but who are in danger of being committed by unscupulous relatives, their rights can be safeguarded, while those who clearly cannot care for themselves are cared for. While the indigency of the street people should make us careful about sweeping them out of sight for our convenience, we can maintain institutions for the mentally ill in humane and dignified ways rather than allowing the least of these, our brothers and sisters, to suffer the effects of their alleged freedom.

---

Mr. Ousley is rector of the Church of the Incarnation in New York.
