Letters to the Editor: Not Racist Then, Not Racist Now

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/03/90

Regarding "Davis-Bacon: Racist Then, Racist Now" by Scott Alan Hodge of the Heritage Foundation (editorial page, June 25): First, Mr. Hodge states that the "Davis-Bacon {Act} was written to prevent black workers, mostly from the South, from competing with Northern construction trades," when in fact on March 19, 1928, a letter from the Commissioner of Labor Statistics stated that a Southern contractor on Long Island had "brought with him an entire outfit of Negro laborers from the South, housed them in barracks and boxcars, permitting no one to see them." We submit this legislation was passed to prevent exploitation of all workers, and not, as Mr. Hodge suggests, to prevent black workers from competing for jobs. Second, Mr. Hodge asserts that "the Labor Department uses local union wage scales as a proxy for the `prevailing wage,'" when in fact a 1986 study of 3,358 wage decisions issued by the Labor Department showed only 881, or 26.2%, were based on union rates.

Third, Mr. Hodge's statement that the "debate on Davis-Bacon has focused primarily on its costs, the estimated $1.5 billion it costs U.S. taxpayers to pay union scale," is unprovable. We have already proved that these rates are not based on union scale; our study also showed that 44.7% of the 1986 decisions issued provided a wage rate below the poverty level recognized by the Census Bureau. In fact, we found prevailing wages issued in 77 decisions that were at or below the minimum wage.

Terry G. Bumpers

Director

National Joint Heavy and Highway Construction Committee

Washington
