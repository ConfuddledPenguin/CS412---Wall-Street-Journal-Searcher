Ford Motor Co. Plan Draws Praise From UAW Chiefs

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 08/03/90

DEARBORN, Mich. -- Ford Motor Co. officials hinted they may be willing to offer the iron-clad job protection its biggest union wants in return for concessions that help the auto maker boost quality and productivity. United Auto Workers executives praised Ford's proposal, outlined in general terms yesterday, as one setting a positive framework for negotiations. The union has made job security and pension benefits its top priorities in the talks.

"The message we got across the table today is that {Ford is} listening to us," said Ernest Lofton, the UAW vice president in charge of the union's Ford negotiations.

Although still in the early stages, the UAW's contract talks are going better at Ford than at General Motors Corp. or Chrysler Corp. All three companies are negotiating concurrently for new three-year pacts with the union. The three contracts expire Sept. 14.

Talks are moving more smoothly at Ford principally because the company's operations are leaner than its U.S. rivals. That gives Ford more flexibility to make job security promises.

"The people we're dealing with here at Ford are unlike those at GM or Chrysler," Mr. Lofton said.

Ford has objectives of its own, chiefly quality improvement. The auto maker also hinted that it will ask the union to accept less in the way of pay and benefits improvements in return for job security.

By signaling their willingness to negotiate strict job security provisions, Ford officials are angling to become the union's strike target. That would give Ford the chance to negotiate an agreement that would set the pattern for contracts at GM and Chrysler. In effect, Ford could set the terms for labor relations at the rival U.S. companies.

Many in the auto industry still expect the UAW to choose GM as its target and force a showdown over job security there. The No. 1 auto maker has laid off more than 30,000 UAW members in the past three years.

However, a UAW spokesman suggested Ford may have improved its chances.

"If Ford meets all of our requirements and the other companies are dragging their feet, then Ford will be the target," a UAW spokesman said.
