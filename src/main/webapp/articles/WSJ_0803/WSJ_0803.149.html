The Iraqi Invasion of Kuwait: U.S. Asset Freeze, Trade Embargo Likely to Do Little Harm to Iraq

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon and Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 08/03/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush's economic retaliation for the invasion of Kuwait isn't likely to hurt Iraq much. Mr. Bush issued two executive orders early yesterday, one imposing an asset freeze and trade embargo against Iraq, the other blocking transfers of official Kuwaiti assets in the U.S. to shield them from Iraqi takeover.

The combination of sanctions against Iraq is the most severe economic penalty the U.S. can impose against a nation. The order severs all transportation and financial links between the two countries and bars U.S. citizens from working in Iraq. But unlike the similar sanctions the U.S. placed on Iran in 1979 during the hostage crisis, the moves against Iraq aren't expected to have much impact on the Arab nation.

A Commerce Department official said there is a "limited" U.S. business presence in Iraq. He estimated there are less than 100 Americans working there now, mainly as technicians and engineers.

Also, U.S. officials say that Iraq has only minimal assets in the U.S., and U.S. companies export only about $1.2 billion in goods -- mostly farm products -- to Iraq. Iraq can quickly make up for the $2.3 billion in oil it ships to the U.S. from the price increases it will cause by taking over Kuwait's oil fields.

In the U.S., banks acted quickly on the president's orders. The manager of international money transfer at a major New York bank said that by 6 a.m. yesterday, his bank, and presumably other big U.S. banks, had shut down the transfers of Kuwaiti and Iraqi assets.

Handling the freeze against Iraq won't be a problem because of the country's small holdings in the U.S. Thomas Sams, who runs the Iraq desk at the Commerce Department, called Baghdad's holdings here "minuscule."

But dealing with Kuwait's huge assets could be more complicated, because they are divided between official, or government holdings, which are blocked, and private assets, which aren't covered by the president's order. U.S. officials say they don't know how big Kuwait's U.S. holdings are. According to the Commerce Department's tabulation of holdings in real estate and major corporate shares, Kuwait's direct investment in the U.S. totaled $4.2 billion last year.

But officials estimate that private and official Kuwaiti "portfolio" investment in stocks and bonds could amount to an additional $35 billion to $55 billion, for total U.S. holdings of about $40 billion to $60 billion. However, some private analysts estimate that the holdings are far smaller. Sharif Ghalib at the Institute for International Finance, for example, says his calculations show total Kuwaiti assets of $15 billion in the U.S.

Michael Bradfield, an attorney in the Washington office of Jones Day Reavis & Pogue who specializes in banking, says that "sorting out what's public and what's private in a place like Kuwait is extremely difficult. It will put judgmental burdens on banks that they certainly would rather not have. The Kuwaitis have assets all over the place and sorting all that out is going to be a long-term problem, assuming the situation remains as it is."

The Kuwaiti Investment Office, the financial arm of the government, has major stockholdings in a number of companies, including British Petroleum Co. In addition, private Kuwaiti investors have broad interests in real estate, retailing and other U.S. assets.

Santa Fe International Corp., Alhambra, Calif., a petroleum concern acquired by the Kuwaitis in 1981 for $2.5 billion, said it continues normal business operations.

John J. Mika, vice president administration, said in an interview that communications with the State and Treasury departments indicate that "the freeze isn't intended to be punitive to companies such as Santa Fe International." Mr. Mika said the company, with about 2,000 employees world-wide, issued its normal payroll yesterday.

Commercial relations between the U.S. and Iraq have been relatively slow to develop, aside from oil purchases and annual shipments of about $1 billion of rice, wheat, corn and soybean products to Iraq. Washington and Baghdad didn't have full relations until 1984, and then Iraq's bitter war with Iran limited commercial opportunity. After the war ended two years ago, U.S. interest began to pick up. General Motors Corp. negotiated to assemble cars in Iraq, and construction, irrigation and other companies began to look for business there.

The sanctions will end about $200 million in short-term Export-Import Bank insurance for shipments of U.S. manufactured goods, and agricultural credits that have ranged from $500 million to $1 billion a year recently. But yesterday, Iraq announced that it was suspending repayments on $2.2 billion of credits it owes the U.S.

Yesterday the House voted 416-0 to authorize an embargo on virtually all trade with Iraq and to impose new sanctions, including tighter standards on the export of manufactured goods and technology to Baghdad. The bill goes beyond the administration in some areas and would give Congress greater leverage in reviewing any future decision by Mr. Bush to lift the restrictions.

How the Crisis Boiled Over

July 17 -- Iraqi President Saddam Hussein denounces Persian Gulf countries that exceed their production quotas, accusing them of stabbing Iraq in the back.

July 18 -- Iraqi Foreign Minister Tareq Aziz tells the Arab League Kuwait has stolen $2.4 billion worth of Iraqi oil and has built military posts on Iraqi land. The U.A.E. says it will cut oil output as promised. Kuwait's parliament denounces Iraq's tirade.

July 22 -- Iraqi foreign minister flies to Cairo; Egypt says Gulf Crisis will soon be over.

July 23 -- Baghdad papers call Kuwait's foreign minister "a U.S. agent." Kuwait denies it seeks to draw foreign powers into the dispute and says it wants Arabs to settle it.

July 24 -- Iraq reportedly deploys thousands of troops to the Kuwait border and U.S. warships in Gulf are said to be put on state of alert. Egypt's President Hosni Mubarak visits Iraq, Kuwait and Saudi Arabia. Iraq says oil prices should rise to $25 per barrel.

July 25 -- Iraq demands $2.4 billion in compensation from Kuwait. Egyptian President Mubarak says Kuwait and Iraq will meet in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia, to discuss the crisis. Arab diplomats say Iraq has given Egypt assurances it will not attack Kuwait.

July 27 -- OPEC agrees to raise the cartel's target price to $21 per barrel of oil. Iraq warns Kuwait it must meet Baghdad's "legitimate rights" at the planned Jeddah talks.

July 31 -- Senior Iraqi and Kuwait officials meet for two hours in Jeddah; reports say that Iraq has almost 100,000 troops at the Kuwaiti border.

Aug. 1 -- Jeddah talks collapse without further meetings.

Aug. 2 -- Iraqi tanks and troops cross the border.

Source: Associated Press.
