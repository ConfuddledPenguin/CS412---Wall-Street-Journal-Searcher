Bear Stearns Cos. Profit Fell 30% In June Quarter

  • Author: Robert J. Brennan and Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 08/03/90

NEW YORK -- Bear Stearns Cos. posted a 30% drop in net income for its fiscal fourth quarter, capping a mixed earnings season for Wall Street securities firms. The big securities firm, citing the cyclical downturn on Wall Street, said net for the quarter ended June 30 was $33.5 million, or 32 cents a share, compared with $47.6 million, or 43 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue edged up to $640 million from $629.7 million. Many analysts had expected the firm to earn between 40 cents and 43 cents for the quarter.

Despite the results, however, Bear Stearns remains one of the few securities darlings among financial-services analysts.

"I'm not table-pounding any of these stocks, but Bear is the only one I have a buy on," says Larry Eckenfelder, an analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "It still has good value and not a lot more downside."

Yesterday, Bear Stearns shares closed at $12, down 62.5 cents, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

Bear Stearns's results bring to a close an uneven period for securities-firm earnings. For the first time in nine months, securities firms as a group broke into the black in the June quarter. But the results were mixed: Merrill Lynch & Co. surprised analysts with a jump in investment-banking revenue, while Morgan Stanley Group Inc.'s shares have dropped 18% since its disappointing earnings were released last week.

Bear Stearns's per-share results are adjusted for a 5% stock dividend declared yesterday, to be distributed with the regular quarterly cash dividend of 14 cents on Aug. 31.

Commissions were a little softer than some analysts were expecting; apparently an anticipated investment-banking deal didn't close before the quarter ended, some analysts said. Commission revenue for the quarter was down 22% to $77.9 million.

Investment banking revenue rose 4.3%. An increase in interest and dividend income was offset by an increase in interest expense.

For the year, Bear Stearns's net income fell 31% to $119.4 million, or $1.10 a share, down from $172.4 million, or $1.54 a share. Revenue was flat at $2.4 billion. Commission revenue was down 2% but investment banking revenue dropped 17%.

Strong performances in the areas of mortgage-backed securities, government bond and stock trading helped the fourth quarter, Bear Stearns said.

In a statement, Chairman Alan C. Greenberg said "fiscal 1990, particularly the first six months, was a tough year for the investment banking and brokerage industry." In light of that, he said, "we are proud of our results for the quarter and the year."

Indeed, Mr. Greenberg showed good spirit and a touch of humor about the results. "I have always heard that we are in a cyclical business. I am eagerly awaiting the next upward cycle," he said in the statement.
