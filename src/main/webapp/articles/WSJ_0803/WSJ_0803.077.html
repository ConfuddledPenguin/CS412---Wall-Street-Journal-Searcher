Mutual Funds: Benham to Offer Fund Transactions At Preset Prices

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 08/03/90

Benham Capital Management Group is hoping to lead the mutual fund industry into the future by drawing on its chairman's past. The chairman, James Benham, spent seven years as a Merrill Lynch stockbroker before launching the Mountain View, Calif., fund company in 1972. As a broker, he found that limit orders -- buy and sell orders that are triggered if a stock hits a predetermined price -- were a favorite trading technique with some customers.

Now Mr. Benham is hoping limit orders will prove equally popular with mutual fund investors. On Aug. 13, Benham, which has $6.3 billion in assets, will launch its "open order" service, which will allow investors to buy and sell mutual fund shares at predetermined prices.

Mr. Benham reckons the open order service will catch on with investors in Benham's gold stock index fund and its seven zero-coupon bond funds. "We probably have share prices as volatile as anybody's," Mr. Benham says. "Some of those {zero-coupon} funds move as much as 5% in one day." A zero-coupon bond pays no interest, so the investor instead can make gains from the narrowing of the bond's discount as the redemption date approaches.

Here is how the open order system will work. If a Benham money market investor wants to buy, say, one of the fund group's volatile zero-coupon bond funds, he will specify a buy price that is lower than the bond fund's current share price. If the bond fund falls to the specified price, money is automatically switched out of the shareholder's money market fund and into the bond fund.

Benham also will allow shareholders in its gold and bond funds to sell at predetermined prices that are above the current fund share price. If the fund share price hits this predetermined price, Benham will switch the shareholder's money into one of its five money market funds.

There will be no charge for switching among Benham's 21 no-load funds, though shareholders will be limited each year to six round-trip exchanges for each fund. Mr. Benham says, "We're not trying to turn people into traders."

Mr. Benham had also hoped to introduce a variation on the stop-loss order, which stock investors use to limit their losses in the stock market. With a stop-loss order, a stock position is automatically sold if the share price falls to a specified level.

But the Securities and Exchange Commission said it had some concerns about stop-loss orders, so Mr. Benham decided to seek approval only for the open order system. "In perhaps six months or a year, we'll go back and apply for stop-loss orders," he says.

News of the innovation has received a tepid response from others in the fund industry. "It is very innovative," says Neal Litvack, a marketing vice president with Fidelity Investments of Boston. "We'll be watching it very carefully. But from a conceptual standpoint, it does run contrary to the investment approach that we advocate to our investors, which is a buy-and-hold approach."

Kurt Brouwer, president of Brouwer & Janachowski, a San Francisco investment advisory firm, doubts that the idea will catch on. "You're kind of encouraging people to trade, and most fund groups don't want to do that," Mr. Brouwer says.

"I don't think we're going to lose any shareholders over this," says a spokesman for T. Rowe Price Associates, Baltimore, who reckons the open order system will encourage needless short-term trading.
