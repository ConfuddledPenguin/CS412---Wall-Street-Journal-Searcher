Economy: Factory Orders In June Slipped By a Steep 1.5% --- Durable Goods Take Brunt Of Decline in New Sign Of Slowdown in Industry

  • Author: Laurie McGinley
  • Date: 08/03/90

WASHINGTON -- June factory orders declined a sharp 1.5%, providing fresh evidence that the health of the U.S.'s manufacturing sector is deteriorating. The Commerce Department reported that orders fell to $237.4 billion in June after increasing a revised 2.2% in May to $241.1 billion.

Taken with other recent economic news, the orders data suggest that "there are renewed signs of weakness in manufacturing following some signs of firming" earlier in the year, said Norman Robertson, chief economist for Mellon Bank in Pittsburgh.

"The outlook for manufacturing employment is certainly bleak," he said. "The prospect for output is for lackluster gains at best over coming months." Mr. Robertson said he believes that the economy will squeak by without a recession but said it will be close.

Yesterday's report was consistent with three issued Wednesday that detailed an uninspired performance by the economy. The government's index of leading indicators was unchanged for June, while construction spending was flat for the same month, according to the Commerce Department. In addition, the National Association of Purchasing Management's July index of manufacturing health plunged.

The decline in June factory orders was concentrated in durable goods, which are items expected to last at least three years. Orders for these goods skidded 2.8% to $125.08 billion after rising 4.1% in May to $128.74 billion. The June drop reflected decreases in orders for defense capital goods, primary metals, electrical machinery and related products.

The 2.8% drop in durable-goods orders was smaller than the 3.2% decline estimated by the department last week. Orders for nondurable goods were about unchanged in June from May at $112.32 billion.

Brian Keyser, an economist at First Fidelity Bancorp. in Philadelphia, said the data confirm his view that the economy will grow at an anemic pace in the next few months. "We'll be in the same weak environment we've been in for the last year or year and a half," he said, adding that commercial and residential construction will continue to be a drag on the economy.

The figures are adjusted for seasonal variations.
