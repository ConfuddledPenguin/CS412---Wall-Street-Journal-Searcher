The Iraqi Invasion of Kuwait: U.S. Emergency Stockpile of Oil Might Be Increased Significantly

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 08/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. emergency oil stockpile already is big enough to counter any temporary supply disruption from the Mideast conflict, and Iraq's invasion increases the chances that it will get significantly bigger. The "strategic petroleum reserve," placed in salt domes in Texas and Louisiana, now contains almost 590 million barrels of crude that the government began stockpiling in response to the Arab oil embargo of 1973-1974. That's enough to offset all imports for 75 days at current import levels. Plans call for the reserve to reach 750 million barrels by the mid-1990s.

House and Senate conferees have been debating a proposal to increase the flow of oil into the reserve to bring it to one billion barrels, and the Mideast war gives the provision a boost. Until now, the Bush administration had opposed the higher level partly on the grounds that the likelihood of a supply disruption was minimal. At a news conference late yesterday afternoon, Deputy Energy Secretary Henson Moore said the government "has seen nothing to cause us to rethink that decision." But many people on Capitol Hill expect the higher level to be approved.

Mr. Moore told reporters that he expects neither the invasion nor the U.S. embargo of Iraqi oil to result in a "long-term, significant increase" in oil prices or a disruption in supplies to the U.S. Thus, he said, the Bush administration has no plans to tap the reserve. He cited the current oversupply of oil on world markets and high levels of domestic commercial stocks of crude oil and refined products.

The invasion of Kuwait sparked a debate on whether to use the reserve to ease the supply disruption that would ensue if the U.S. and several of its allies tried to impose a world quarantine on Iraqi oil. Philip K. Verleger, a visiting fellow at the Institute for International Economics, called for using the reserve oil to help ease the impact of sharply higher prices that would occur if such an embargo worked. With an effective embargo, he predicted, prices could reach $45 a barrel.

But Mr. Moore said no thought has been given to using the oil in that way. The reserve "isn't designed as a pricing mechanism," he said.

Currently all the oil in the reserve is owned by the U.S. But under pending legislation required to authorize the continued build-up of the strategic reserve, the U.S. would be allowed to begin leasing some of the crude as a way to reduce the cost of filling the reserve. Under this plan, an oil supplier would lease the oil to the U.S. for a fee that proponents say could be as low as 50 cents to $1 a barrel. Saudi Arabia and Venezuela have expressed interest in such an arrangement, according to congressional aides.

Under the leasing plan, the U.S. in case of an emergency would take possession of the oil and be free to use it. When and if any was sold, the supplying nation would get the proceeds.

The president can decide to draw down oil from the reserve after determining there is a serious disruption to the flow of imported oil or the potential for such an interruption. The Bush administration also would determine the rate at which the oil could be used, based on an estimate of how long the disruption is likely to last.

The 75-day supply of imported oil that the reserve could offset compares with more than 100 days in the early 1980s. The number of days has declined even as the reserve has grown because U.S. consumption of foreign oil has shot up in recent years.

Key Dates in the Crisis

July 17 -- Iraqi President Saddam Hussein denounces Persian Gulf countries that exceed their oil-production quotas, accusing them of stabbing Iraq in the back.

July 18 -- Iraqi Foreign Minister Tareq Aziz tells the Arab League Kuwait has stolen $2.4 billion worth of Iraqi oil and has built military posts on Iraqi land. The U.A.E. says it will cut oil output as promised. Kuwait's parliament denounces Iraq's tirade.

July 22 -- Iraqi foreign minister flies to Cairo; Egypt says Gulf crisis will soon be over.

July 23 -- Baghdad papers call Kuwait's foreign minister "a U.S. agent." Kuwait denies it seeks to draw foreign powers into the dispute and says it wants Arabs to settle the conflict.

July 24 -- Iraq reportedly deploys thousands of troops to the Kuwait border. U.S. warships in Gulf are said to be put on state of alert. Egypt's President Hosni Mubarak visits Iraq, Kuwait and Saudi Arabia. Iraq says oil prices should rise to $25 per barrel.

July 25 -- Iraq demands $2.4 billion in compensation from Kuwait. Egyptian President Mubarak says Kuwait and Iraq will meet in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia, to discuss the crisis. Arab diplomats say Iraq has given Egypt assurances it will not attack Kuwait.

July 27 -- OPEC agrees to raise the cartel's target price to $21 per barrel of oil. Iraq warns Kuwait it must meet Baghdad's "legitimate rights" at the planned Jeddah talks.

July 31 -- Senior Iraqi and Kuwaiti officials meet for two hours in Jeddah; reports say that Iraq has almost 100,000 troops at the Kuwaiti border.

Aug. 1 -- Jeddah talks collapse without further meetings planned.

Aug. 2 -- Iraqi tanks and troops cross the border into Kuwait.

Source: Associated Press
