Usinor Sacilor's Overseas Acquisitions Spark Criticism of Unfair Advantages

  • Author: David G. Pearson
  • Date: 08/03/90

PARIS -- Usinor Sacilor S.A.'s aggressive foreign expansion strategy is provoking criticism from abroad that the steel group's state-owned status has given it an unfair advantage over private companies. The French group has spun itself a web of cross-border alliances in recent years through a series of takeovers and partnerships that have made it the world's second-largest steel producer after Japan's Nippon Steel Corp.

Usinor's latest move came last week when it announced plans for a friendly takeover of New York-based Edgcomb Corp., one of the biggest steel-trading concerns in the U.S., for an undisclosed amount.

Edgcomb is the latest in a string of acquisitions by Usinor in the U.S., where it is now the biggest producer of stainless steel and has reached a critical mass in long steel products.

With a solid U.S. trading and distribution network now under its belt, analysts say, Usinor is now poised to carve itself a niche in flat products, the steel industry's mainstay.

LTV Corp., another large U.S. steelmaker, disclosed a few months ago that Usinor Sacilor had indicated it was interested in discussing the acquisition of a minority interest in LTV's steel unit, which produced 8.4 million tons of raw steel and had revenue of $4.1 billion in 1989. Officials at the French group say the talks remain at a very preliminary stage, however.

But Francis Mer, chairman of the steel group, rejects suggestions that Usinor has delusions of grandeur and says Usinor isn't expanding for the sake of size alone. "If one day we become the world's biggest steel producer it won't be because we set out to do so," he said in an interview.

Mr. Mer doesn't believe that Usinor will ever catch up with its Japanese competitor. Although Nippon Steel claims to have production capacity of 28 million tons, compared with Usinor's 23 million tons, the figure is nearer to 40 million tons if all Nippon's affiliated companies are included.

Usinor Sacilor's biggest foreign takeover to date was its acquisition last year of a controlling stake in West Germany's Saarstahl in a move that made the French group the second-biggest German producer after Thyssen AG.

Usinor's expansion plans got a boost this week, when the European Community Commission cleared the way for Usinor and British Steel PLC of the U.K. to expand further into specialist markets. Usinor's Sollac unit will be allowed to purchase two units from their parent company, Anglo-French packaging specialist CMB S.A.

"Usinor reflects the imperialistic style that appears to be in vogue in France at the present time," commented Peter Dupont, a steel-industry analyst with UBS-Philips & Drew Ltd. of London, referring to the wave of foreign takeovers by French state-controlled industrial concerns in recent months.

Usinor's chief executive says he would prefer to be in a position to issue common shares rather than nonvoting securities, as many nationalized companies have done in recent years.

Analysts say it's significant that during the difficult years, Usinor Sacilor was able to maintain its expenditure on improving productivity when private-owned concerns couldn't.

"Part of the reason may be that with the state's backing, Usinor could take out loans on more-favorable terms than its competitors. This raises the question of whether or not the playing field was level," comments Mr. Dupont of Philips & Drew.

Vincent Mordrel, an analyst with Parisian brokerage DLP James Capel, points out that unlike steel companies in the U.S., Usinor didn't have to worry about shareholder reaction to quarterly earnings reports.

Usinor Sacilor returned to profitability in 1988 after 12 straight years of losses, but Mr. Mer says it's unlikely that the 1990 earnings performance will match that of 1989, when consolidated net profit after payments to minority interests climbed to 7.592 billion francs ($1.41 billion) from 4.633 billion a year earlier.

He explained that steel demand was boosted artificially last year by stockbuilding linked to the inability of producers to meet rapidly rising demand. Right now there's a stock depletion trend that has pushed production levels below those of actual consumption, but this phenomenon is temporary, and production should start moving in line with demand next year.

Mr. Mer says steel demand in Europe is projected to rise by between 2% and 3% annually in volume terms over the next four or five years, provided that interest rates don't rise significantly.
