Politics & Policy: House Votes to Let Airports Charge Passengers Up to $3 Departure Tax

  • Author: Laurie McGinley
  • Date: 08/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The House voted to let airports slap departure taxes of up to $3 on passengers, but the Senate may ground the proposal. The House bill would let the nation's 71 largest airports impose the fees to pay for airport expansion and noise reduction. There would be a limit of two charges per one-way trip, but a passenger could end up paying as much as $12 on a round trip with intermediate stops.

After a contentious debate, the House, by a 252-171 vote, rebuffed a bid by Rep. Doug Bosco (D., Calif.) to delete the provision permitting the taxes.

"Airline passengers are once again on the verge of being taken for a ride by the White House," said Rep. Norman Mineta (D., Calif.), an opponent of the fees, officially dubbed passenger facilities charges. "The Congress shouldn't license this slap at the consumer." He said the government should first spend the $8 billion surplus in the aviation trust fund, which is financed by the 8% airline ticket tax.

But Glenn Anderson (D., Calif.), chairman of the House Public Works and Transportation Committee, argued that the new fees are needed to augment the spending.

Transportation Secretary Samuel Skinner has made approval of the fees a top priority, and that was reflected in the vote on the Bosco amendment. Republicans voted overwhelmingly against the amendment, while Democrats were split.

Still pending before Congress is a Bush administration proposal to increase the airline ticket tax to 10% from 8%. That proposal is expected to be adopted as part of the budget negotiations.

The House bill also authorizes $17.7 billion in spending by the Federal Aviation Administration on airport development and air-traffic control programs for the next two years. The final vote on the bill was 405-15.

In the Senate, prospects for the legislation are murky. Sen. Ernest Hollings (D., S.C.), chairman of the Commerce Committee, and Sen. Wendell Ford, (D., Ky.), chairman of the aviation subcommittee, oppose the passenger facilities charges. According to their aides, the senators don't plan to pass an FAA authorization bill this year because they don't want to provide a vehicle for the departure-tax provision. The agency would receive funding in any case.

Congress banned departure taxes in the early 1970s because some local governments were using the money for non-airport projects -- a practice prohibited under the new House bill. Airports levying departure taxes would be required to relinquish up to half of their regular federal funding. Most of the unused federal funds would go to a discretionary fund for smaller airports.
