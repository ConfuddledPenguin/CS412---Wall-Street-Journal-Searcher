Marketing & Media: Video Jukebox Sells Stake for $5 Million To Cable Concern

  • Author: Peter Waldman
  • Date: 08/03/90

Video Jukebox Network Inc. said Tele-Communications Inc. has agreed to invest $5 million in the company for a roughly 10% stake, lending credibility to the fledgling pay-per-view concern. Miami-based Video Jukebox operates a so-called interactive cable-TV network that lets viewers select music videos they want to watch by making a telephone call to a 900 number. Phone companies bill callers $2.50 per request, keep as much as 70 cents a call for themselves, and pay Video Jukebox the rest.

Video Jukebox, in turn, shares 20% to 25% of its "jukebox" revenue with cable operators that carry the network.

Denver-based Tele-Communications is the largest cable operator in the U.S., controlling about 12 million subscribers. Its cable system in Miami was the nation's first system to carry the Video Jukebox Network, which now has about 9.5 million subscribers. But since the network first aired in 1985, no other Tele-Communications-owned systems have added the channel to their offerings.

Hence, in addition to the equity investment, Tele-Communications is now in an excellent position to boost Video Jukebox's growth by distributing the channel to a wider audience.

"I love this interactive stuff," said Peter Barton, Tele-Communications' senior vice president of programming. "It enhances the audience's relationship with the TV set. Customers like it. We're very bullish on it."

In over-the-counter trading yesterday, Video Jukebox's shares rose $1.25 to close at $7 bid.

Though Video Jukebox is the most interactive of cable networks on the air today, its technology is still a long way from where media analysts and the company's own executives say the industry is headed. Its 9.5 million subscribers are divided among 109 separate "jukeboxes" nationally, meaning that viewers must wait while the requests of other subscribers connected to a given system are played before their own request comes on.

Eventually, with the spread of fiber-optic networks that can carry massive amounts of data, Video Jukebox and others will be able to sell customized TV programs to individual homes.

Currently, said Andrew Orgel, Video Jukebox's president and chief executive officer, the average wait per Jukebox Video request is about 25 minutes, depending on the market and time of day.

"We feel somebody should have to wait no more than 40 minutes to see a video," said Mr. Orgel. The channel, he said, wants to develop one "jukebox" system for every 30,000 to 40,000 subscribers. The ratio is currently one for every 87,000 viewers.

Mr. Orgel said the company also wants to sell between three minutes and six minutes per hour of advertising time, beginning later this year. And he said Video Jukebox is planning to test a non-music network, using the same technology, in the fourth quarter.

John Reddan, an analyst with John Moran & Associates, said Video Jukebox's revenue per "jukebox" system has grown 12% in the past 18 months. He estimates the company now collects $11,000 per system per month, compared with $9,800 in the beginning of 1989 -- without having advertised the channel.

"The future of TV is interactive, but that's really many years away," said Mr. Reddan. "This is interactive today."

For the first quarter, Video Jukebox posted a loss of $322,147, or three cents a share, compared with a loss of $680,804, or 10 cents a share, a year earlier. Its revenue climbed in the quarter to $2.6 million from $376,986.
