Marketing & Media: Maxwell Set to Sell Some Assets, Defers Tabloid Purchase

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 09/13/90

Maxwell Communications Corp. PLC is planning to sell some assets and may renegotiate the terms of its recent agreement to buy three U.S. supermarket tabloids. In London, Robert Maxwell, chairman, told Maxwell Communications' annual meeting that the company is in talks to sell some of its "non-strategic businesses." He said "negotiations are well advanced in a number of transactions which we expect to complete very shortly" but declined to be any more specific.

Meanwhile, executives of Globe International Publishing Inc. of Montreal confirmed that Maxwell's plans to buy three of its supermarket tabloids have been delayed. In May, Maxwell announced plans to acquire the Globe, National Examiner and Sun from Globe International for an estimated $100 million. The tabloids would make Mr. Maxwell an instant powerhouse at lucrative supermarket checkout stands.

Peter Rubenovitch, Globe's chief financial officer, wouldn't comment on negotiations, except to say they are continuing. "Yes, the timetable has changed," said Mr. Rubenovitch. "We planned to close within a month or two, but that was too ambitious." Although he declined to elaborate, Mr. Maxwell is said to have missed two closing deadlines. He is also attempting to renegotiate the purchase price to below $100 million, according to people familiar with the talks.

At the annual meeting in London, Mr. Maxwell said his company would repay about $400 million in short-term debt by Oct. 23. "We do not enjoy being a highly leveraged business," Mr. Maxwell said. The company is "determined" to bring down its level of debt as quickly as possible. Maxwell shares closed up a penny at 167 pence on the London Stock Exchange. Mr. Maxwell and other company officials couldn't be reached after the meeting.

Globe executives said Mr. Maxwell recently visited the Globe's publication headquarters in Boca Raton, Fla., where Globe executives expected to finalize the transaction. Instead, they said, Mr. Maxwell said he needed to knock down the price.

Mr. Maxwell, through his privately held Mirror Group PLC, publishes the London Daily Mirror, the second-largest newspaper in Britain, and recently launched the European, an English-language newspaper for Europe.

His biggest U.S. acquisition to date was the $2.6 billion purchase in 1988 of Macmillan Inc. Mr. Maxwell has hinted at more U.S. acquisitions without giving details. In June, at the annual meeting of one of his companies, Berlitz International Inc., in New York, Mr. Maxwell said he was negotiating to purchase a U.S. newspaper. He said that he was "interested" in Tribune Co.'s high-circulation but marginally profitable New York Daily News but that no negotiations had been held. The Chicago Sun-Times was also rumored as a possible target, but its owners denied negotiating with Mr. Maxwell.
