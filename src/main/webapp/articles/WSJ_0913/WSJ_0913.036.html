GAO Calls Goal to Reduce Federal Deficit Too Modest

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The budget negotiators' goal of reducing the federal deficit by $500 billion over the next five years is "considerably more modest" than is needed, the General Accounting Office said. The investigative arm of Congress said a $500 billion package would be only "a first step" and ought to be followed by an even larger $550 billion round of deficit reduction in fiscal 1996 and 1997.

In its 140-page report, the GAO said the government ought to run a surplus to ensure that the nation saves more and invests more for the future. By 1997, the GAO said, the surplus should equal 2% of the gross national product, the total output of the economy. "The average deficit as a percent of the GNP has doubled every 10 years for the past 40 years," the GAO said. It is now at about 4% of GNP.

As a result, interest payments are the fastest growing item in the budget. "Current projections indicate that debt service costs will replace defense as the largest item in the budget by 1992," the GAO said.

Separately, the director of the Congressional Budget Office, Robert Reischauer, said that the slowing U.S. economy and the effects of the Persian Gulf crisis could push the deficit as high as $267 billion in fiscal 1991 without some action by Congress and the White House. In July, the office put the deficit at $232 billion.
