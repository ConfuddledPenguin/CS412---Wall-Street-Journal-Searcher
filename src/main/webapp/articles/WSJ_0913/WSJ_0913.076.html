International: Four Allies of World War II Sign Treaty Clearing Way for Unified Germany Oct. 3 --- Restored State Will Be Part Of NATO, Won't Have Any Territorial Claims

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 09/13/90

MOSCOW -- The two Germanys and the four allies that won World War II signed a treaty giving their blessing to the united Germany scheduled to come into being Oct. 3. The treaty, which concludes seven months of bargaining, provides that the World War II allies -- the U.S., the Soviet Union, Britain and France -- will restore to Germany the full sovereignty it lost in 1945. The united Germany will be in the North Atlantic Treaty Organization, but no non-German NATO forces will be stationed permanently on the territory of the former East German state.

The treaty also declares that the united Germany "has no territorial claims whatsoever against other states" and "will never disturb the peaceful relations between nations."

The seven-page treaty was made possible by a side agreement between West Germany and the Soviet Union. Under it, Bonn will provide billions of dollars in aid to Moscow and help build housing in the U.S.S.R. for the nearly 400,000 Soviet troops that will leave German territory by the end of 1994 as a result of the German unity treaty.

Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev told Soviet reporters the German aid will total nearly $10 billion. The figure was worked out in the past few days, after Moscow unexpectedly raised its financial demands sharply.

Now that the unity treaty is signed, the two Germanys can proceed as planned with a separate internal pact and declare themselves a single state.

Two important issues related to the new treaty remain unresolved. Secretary of State James Baker will continue negotiating with Soviet officials today over a Soviet demand that the U.S. separately agree to limit its troop deployments in Europe to well below 100,000, compared with more than 300,000 now. Mr. Baker also is negotiating with Senate leaders about whether the unity pact will have to be ratified by the Senate.

Under the treaty, Germany will limit its armed forces to 370,000 troops, down from nearly 500,000 now, and it pledges never to acquire nuclear, chemical or biological weapons. United Germany also will sign a treaty with Poland guaranteeing their current border.

The treaty erases the military and political arrangements put in place after World War II, and it formally eliminates Soviet control established by Joseph Stalin over East Germany.

Mr. Gorbachev presided over the treaty signing at a hotel built for Communist Party leaders here. He was praised for encouraging reforms in East Germany and allowing the opening of the Berlin Wall. "We have drawn the line under World War II," declared Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze. "We are now dealing with a new Germany that has drawn its lessons from history."

West German Foreign Minister Hans-Dietrich Genscher said, "This is a day of joy and jubilation," but he also recalled the victims of Nazi Germany. "In this hour, we remember the victims of war and totalitarian domination," he said. "We are particularly thinking about the Jewish people. We would not want their agony to ever be repeated."

For his part, Secretary Baker hailed "the plain, ordinary people who breached the wall and tore asunder the Iron Curtain."

Last-minute disputes were still being resolved two hours before the signing. Mr. Baker was awakened in the middle of the night when Mr. Genscher appeared at his hotel to iron out language covering NATO troops on German soil.

In the pact, the Soviet Union agreed to let Germany decide when and how U.S. and other NATO troops based in the country's western half can "deploy" temporarily to the eastern half for maneuvers. Germany pledged to protect Soviet war monuments, to consult Moscow about honoring East German contracts and to bar Nazism.

The pact calls for total Soviet withdrawal from Germany over four years, but it allows the U.S. to keep as many troops on German soil as Washington and Bonn jointly want. An earlier, separate agreement between the U.S. and the Soviet Union, which permitted each side 195,000 troops in central Europe, has been junked as obsolete in light of the Soviet exit.

Moscow privately has been pressing Washington to declare publicly a new unilateral U.S. troop ceiling of well below 100,000. But the U.S. has resisted, wanting to keep a free hand and telling Moscow that Congress won't permit as many as 195,000 U.S. troops in Central Europe anyway.

Still, the Soviets want such a limit on U.S. troops to quell political concerns here over a separate conventional arms-control treaty, which would cut each side's arsenal of tanks, artillery pieces and other weapons. Mr. Baker will discuss the matter today with Mr. Shevardnadze and Mr. Gorbachev. The Bush administration is still debating what to do.

At Soviet insistence, the U.S. agreed to call the German unity pact a "treaty," rather than giving it the less august title of "agreement." But the administration still argues that the pact needn't be ratified by the Senate. Senate leaders disagree, and Mr. Baker spent time on the telephone to Capitol Hill trying to iron out the issue.

Because ratification in each nation takes time, the formal termination of the World War II victors' rights and full restoration of German sovereignty could be delayed past the Oct. 3 unification date.

To avoid that delay, the four allies agreed to sign a separate agreement in New York Oct. 1 suspending their legal rights over Germany. As a result, the nation will be fully sovereign when it unifies Oct. 3, even before the allied rights are terminated.
