Technology & Medicine: Brazil to Ease Curb on Imports Of Computers

  • Author: Thomas Kamm
  • Date: 09/13/90

RIO DE JANEIRO -- Brazilian President Fernando Collor de Mello, continuing his drive to liberalize the country's economy, unveiled plans to speed up the opening of the computer and electronics industry to foreign competition. In an announcement that will be welcomed by Brazil's major trading partners, the government said it will end its controversial "market reserve" for electronics by Oct. 29, 1992, two years ahead of schedule. The market reserve effectively prevented the import of foreign computers, software and electronics to Brazil.

At the same time, the government said the list of protected items is to be reduced to 30 immediately. Those items, to be specified later, will include microcomputers and minicomputers and software that cost no more than three times the price of foreign-made equivalents.

All other products can now be imported immediately, provided import duties are paid, or produced locally by multinational companies. Protection also will be lifted for computers, electronics and telecommunications equipment that is more than four years old.

In a bid to allow Brazil to overcome its technological lag, the government also said it will submit a bill to Congress that would reduce obligatory local content in electronics to 30% from 70% now. The government also said it would allow foreign companies to hold 49% of joint ventures, compared with a maximum 30% now.

The moves are part of Mr. Collor's efforts to boost the competitiveness of Brazil's economy and end its longstanding protectionist trade policies, which he feels have caused Brazil to fall behind in terms of technology and quality. "It will be a great challenge to the nation's industry to face foreign competition," Jose Goldemberg, Brazil's science and technology secretary, said.

The market reserve was created by the military governments that ruled from 1964 to 1985. Big multinational companies, such as International Business Machines Corp., were limited to the production of large computers.
