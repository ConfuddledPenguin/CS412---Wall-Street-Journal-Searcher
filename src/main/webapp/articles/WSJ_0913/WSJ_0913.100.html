Darling-Delaware, Despite Past Payouts To Owners, Will Omit Junk-Bond Interest

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 09/13/90

Darling-Delaware Co. lacks cash to pay interest on its high-yield junk bonds, just 20 months after it paid a $179 million dividend to the prominent Texas and Connecticut investors who own the company. Darling-Delaware, based in Dallas, is the largest U.S. rendering company, producing tallow and bone meal from animal parts. For the first six months of 1990, the company reported an $11.3 million loss on revenue of $177.8 million.

Darling-Delaware has stopped filing quarterly results with the Securities and Exchange Commission, asserting that it is a private company that doesn't need to make public filings. It still sends reports to bondholders, though, and in a footnote to its second-quarter report, the company said it "doesn't have sufficient cash" to make a $12.2 million interest payment that is due Saturday on its 14% senior subordinated notes. The company added that it "doesn't anticipate that it will be able to borrow funds" to make the payment.

Until this year, Darling-Delaware had been highly profitable for its owners. These include Fort Worth, Texas, investor Richard Rainwater; Edward W. Rose III, a co-owner of the Texas Rangers baseball team; and Airlie Group, a Greenwich, Conn., investment firm. Airlie's main owners and investors are former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. employee Dort Cameron, Equitable Life Assurance Society of the U.S. and the Bass family of Texas.

On Jan. 3, 1989, Darling-Delaware paid $179 million to its owners, including $25.8 million to Mr. Rose, $62.4 million to Airlie Group, then known as Investment L.P., and $9.5 million for Mr. Rainwater. The amounts were disclosed in an SEC filing for Darling-Delaware's junk bonds, which were issued two months later.

Analysts and bondholders voiced dismay at the downturn in Darling-Delaware's fortunes so soon after the company's big payout to the owners. "It's clear they took too much of a dividend," said Hannah Strasser, an analyst at Deltec Securities, which owns some Darling-Delaware bonds.

Executives at Airlie didn't return repeated phone calls seeking comment. Mr. Rainwater declined to comment, as did a spokesman for Mr. Rose.

Darling-Delaware's junk bonds have sunk steadily since being issued, traders said, and were quoted yesterday at just 22 cents on the dollar. The bonds, which were underwritten by the failed Drexel, were downgraded to a double-C rating last week by Standard & Poor's Corp.

Bondholders said Darling-Delaware and its financial advisers, Jefferies & Co., have called a meeting for today in New York at which they are expected to put forth a debt-restructuring plan. Deltec's Ms. Strasser said she expects bondholders will push for Darling-Delaware's owners to put more cash into the company and give some equity to bondholders in return for bondholders' willingness to exchange their bonds for less costly debt.

In March, Darling-Delaware's owners pumped $24 million into the company, helping the company meet its interest payment then.

In its second-quarter report, Darling-Delaware, which employs about 2,000 people, disclosed a variety of business setbacks. It said a slowdown in cattle slaughterings reduced its intake of raw material, while environmental regulators told the company it may be liable for the clean-up of waste dumps in Houston and Shelbyville, Ind., and a switch in U.S. eating habits away from beef and toward pork and chicken has left the company with less profitable raw materials.

Despite these difficulties, "Darling's sophisticated investor group must support this credit," said Jeremy Bloomer, managing director of Credit Research & Trading Corp., Greenwich, Conn. Otherwise, Mr. Bloomer said, the company's owners "might be forced to return part of their {$179 million} dividend" under various possible legal claims by bondholders.

Ms. Strasser said bondholders have hired a Chicago law firm, Chapman & Cutler, to advise them on their options but haven't made any decision yet on litigation.
