Pension Funds Crowd Real Estate Pools' Exits --- Pulling Investments Isn't Easy, Though

  • Author: James A. White
  • Date: 09/13/90

NEW YORK -- If you're having trouble selling a piece of real estate, you're in good company. Big pension funds that poured billions of dollars into real-estate investment pools now are finding it unexpectedly hard to get their money out.

Starting in the late 1970s, many pension funds decided to diversify their portfolios of stocks and bonds by investing in commercial real estate, such as office buildings, shopping malls and warehouses. Much of the estimated $120 billion they shelled out went to insurance companies and banks that pooled the money and bought scores of different properties.

Now, with the real estate market wilting, most of the two dozen so-called open-end real estate pools have been flooded with demands from pension funds wanting their money back. The withdrawal requests have fed on themselves and now are approaching $2 billion. But paying off waiting investors could take years, some pool operators say.

Pool operators say that selling properties in today's depressed market to meet withdrawal requests would force them to accept fire-sale prices. "We're not going to sell a property hastily to pay out one person to the detriment of all the other investors," says a spokesman for Aetna Life & Casualty Co. As a result, pool operators are asking investors to be patient; some, such as First Wachovia Corp., are imposing withdrawal moratoriums of a year or more. And at most pools, it is up to the operators to decide when to honor withdrawal requests.

The size of the waiting lists varies greatly. Investors accounting for almost half of Cigna Corp.'s $769 million real estate pool have asked for their money back. The insurer is asking investors to accept a two-year delay in payouts. In addition, it wants to make partial payouts, instead of doling out sales proceeds on a first come, first served basis.

At the biggest pool, Equitable Life Assurance Society of the U.S.'s $3.7 billion Prime Property Fund, 25 clients have asked to withdraw a total of $280 million. The fund began making partial payments to investors last May, and Equitable officials say they expect to catch up in nine months to a year.

That's a far cry from the promise of easy withdrawals that attracted pension funds to the real estate pools during the past 20 years. By the promise of letting investors move their money in and out on short notice, pools were intended to overcome investors' worries about real estate's lack of liquidity -- the ability to sell a property quickly without taking a financial bath.

But as investors learned in the 1987 stock market crash, liquidity can evaporate quickly when everyone rushes for the exit at once. Several years of deteriorating real estate prices and some pool operators' poor property selections have soured many pension funds on real estate pools. And as pools often pay withdrawals on a first come, first served basis, other investors have rushed to join the exit line for fear of being left behind.

"When things are going well, no one wants to get out {of pools}, and when things are going badly, everybody wants to get out -- and no one can," says SEI Corp. real estate consultant Nicholas Tannura. Richard Wollack, an Emeryville, Calif., broker who specializes in helping pension funds that want to get out of real estate pools, says, "There is no liquidity in an open-end fund unless people think there is liquidity."

With the real estate market slumping, one pool run by John Hancock Mutual Life Insurance Co. and others run by Bank of Boston Corp. and Travelers Corp. have decided to sell all their properties and return the proceeds to investors.

But even liquidation won't guarantee that clients will get their money back quickly. Bank of Boston, for example, has sold only one property after a year of trying to unload its $200 million pool portfolio. "We're going to be looking at an extended time for disposition," says Thomas Lunny, director of the Bank of Boston real-estate advisory arm.

The exodus from real estate pools doesn't necessarily signal that pension funds are souring on real estate as an investment. Specialists believe pension funds with long-term investment horizons will continue to use real estate to diversify their stock and bond holdings and to help hedge against inflation.

"The reasons that pension funds went into real estate -- diversification, lower volatility for the total portfolio and protection against inflation -- are still there," says Sylvia Ferrell-Jones, who handles real estate investing for Connecticut's public employee pension fund. "People should not panic, based on the past two or three years of declining performance."

But as pension executives become more experienced with real estate -- this was virtually unheard of in pension portfolios only 20 years ago -- many of the larger funds are starting to invest in closed-end pools, which don't promise quick withdrawals, or buy properties directly. "We would rather invest with a limited number of people or on our own where we have more control," says Edward T. Tokar Jr., head of Allied-Signal Inc.'s pension fund.

But the withdrawal bug has spread to some closed-end real estate funds. Unlike open-end funds, investors in closed-end funds agree to tie up their money for years, but they usually can withdraw if they find another investor to take their place. In the past 18 months, "we've seen a doubling or tripling" in the number of investors interested in such swaps, though the number isn't overwhelming, says Mr. Wollack, the California broker who helps arrange them.

Worsening the withdrawal problem at many open-end pools is the policy of making payouts on a first come, first served basis. This puts pressure on pension funds to ask for their money back as a protective measure, for fear that the properties in the pool might be overvalued. In that case, those pulling out first could get back more on their investments than those who stay in the pool.

"I am convinced that more than half those investors standing in line {to withdraw} are doing so simply because they saw the queue," says Paul Saylor of Atlanta-based Institutional Property Consultants. Several pools have switched to making partial payments, to ease withdrawal pressure. But such moves irk investors that are near the front of the withdrawal line and now will have to wait longer for the full return of their money.

Real estate veterans note that withdrawal lines have come and gone before with market cycles, and that most pension funds are slowing new investments but not abandoning the market.

Equitable, for one, says that its withdrawal requests now are running at about the average for the past five years; its withdrawal problem stems from a dearth of new investors putting money into real estate, providing cash to pay off those who want to leave, the insurer says.

In addition, some real estate pools, including Equitable's, actually have outpaced the stock and bond market indexes during the past 12 months. "All of a sudden, this year, when the stock and bond markets don't have dramatic returns, our returns don't look so shabby," says Harry Pierandri, portfolio manager for Equitable's Equitable Real Estate Investment Management unit.
