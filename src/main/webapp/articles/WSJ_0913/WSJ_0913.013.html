Technology & Medicine: Alzheimer's Is a Group of Disorders, Not a Single Disease, Researchers Say

  • Author: David Stipp
  • Date: 09/13/90

BOSTON -- Researchers here reported that different cases of Alzheimer's disease can spring from different causes, showing that it is a group of closely associated disorders rather than a single illness. The finding complicates the search for the causes of Alzheimer's disease. But by providing new clues in that search, it promises to accelerate efforts to understand how the disease develops, and, perhaps someday, how to ward it off.

Studies on the disease, which usually affects people over age 60, are increasingly urgent as the population grows older. It is the nation's fourth leading killer; an estimated four million Americans suffer from it, experiencing progressive memory loss and loss of other mental powers.

Scientists have long suspected that there are different forms of Alzheimer's disease, which develop in different ways, but which all lead to the same kind of brain deterioration. But strong evidence of that hypothesis was lacking before now, says Peter H. St. George-Hyslop, a Massachusetts General Hospital researcher who led a 60-person international team that made the latest discovery.

"This is the first {report of} pretty clear-cut evidence" that Alzheimer's disease represents "multiple disorders" rather than a single disease with one cause, he added.

In 1987, a group of researchers, including Dr. St. George-Hyslop, showed that a gene associated with a relatively rare, inherited form of the disease -- called familial Alzheimer's disease, or FAD -- resides on chromosome 21, one of the 23 pairs of coiled DNA that carry genetic material in human cells. Since then, scientists have been trying to pinpoint the gene.

The latest discovery, published in today's issue of the journal Nature, showed that "late-onset" cases of FAD, whose effects appear after age 65, stem from a cause, or causes, other than a gene on chromosome 21. That cause may be another genetic defect. Alternatively, it could involve a nongenetic factor. Scientists have theorized that viruses, or certain chemicals, could trigger the disease, perhaps in people with a genetic predisposition to it. In any case, researchers now are broadening their studies to look for different genes and non-genetic factors underlying Alzheimer's disease, Dr. St. George-Hyslop said.

One of the hottest new leads involves chromosome 19. In May, researchers at Duke University Medical Center reported they had found evidence that a gene on chromosome 19 is associated with common, late-onset Alzheimer's disease. The Duke team also reported that it had found evidence supporting the earlier discovery that a gene on chromosome 21 underlies the inherited "early onset" form of Alzheimer's disease, which strikes before age 65. The Duke scientists, however, haven't published details of their findings in a scientific journal, and the findings haven't yet been confirmed by other researchers.

Some scientists now believe that all cases of Alzheimer's disease are caused directly by genes, or by a genetic predisposition, that makes people vulnerable to suspected external triggers. According to one variation of the theory, everyone carries genes that cause Alzheimer's disease, and all people would get the illness if they lived long enough.
