Marketing & Media: Mont Saint-Sauveur Tries Placing Ads In Its Annual Report --- But Market Isn't Quite Ready To Pay the Price for Spice, Company Chairman Finds

  • Author: G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 09/13/90

ST. SAUVEUR, Quebec -- If Jacques Hebert's idea catches on, marketers will have a new way to reach the kind of people who read corporate annual reports -- ads in corporate annual reports. Mont Saint-Sauveur International Inc.'s 32-page report for fiscal 1990 features 22 ads -- not counting in-house blurbs. The ads include messages from Bell Canada, Chrysler Capital Corp. and two banks.

Unfortunately for Mont Saint-Sauveur, it had to give the ads away. That wasn't the original plan, says Mr. Hebert, chairman and president of the company, which operates ski resorts in Quebec and Vermont.

The company's first goal was to make the annual report more interesting. "I'm a chartered accountant, and even I always thought annual reports were boring," Mr. Hebert says.

The second goal was to cut costs. After deciding to put out Saint-Sauveur's report in the form of a tabloid-size newspaper, the inclusion of in-house ads was an obvious idea. From that point, it wasn't much of a leap to try and really cut costs by selling ads.

The market wasn't ready. "People resisted the idea," Mr. Hebert admits. Instead, Mont Saint-Sauveur offered its suppliers free advertising space. "It may generate more business," says Newport, Vt., attorney Robert Chimileski of his ad. But asked if he'd pay, Mr. Chimileski answers, "I wouldn't think so."

Mont Saint-Sauveur's annual report is probably a first in Canada, says Grace Lew, who watches annual-report trends for the Canadian Institute of Chartered Accountants. The only thing that came close in Canada is the annual report of a Toronto-based newspaper publisher; that report used a tabloid format, but didn't include outside ads.

As it turned out, Saint-Sauveur didn't save a dime, Mr. Hebert says. This year's effort cost just as much as last year's more conventional report, about five Canadian dollars ($4.30) a copy. And it was a lot more work. But Mr. Hebert isn't giving up. "Eventually we'll break even on our annual report," he says.
