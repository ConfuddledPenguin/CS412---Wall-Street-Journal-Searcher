Southeastern Peanut Crop Is Hurt by Drought, Heat

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/13/90

ATLANTA -- Drought and searing heat have dealt the Southeast peanut crop a double wallop that is expected to have an estimated $575 million economic impact on a three-state region and to drive up consumer prices for peanut products, especially peanut butter. The region -- which includes Georgia, Florida, and Alabama and produces 65% of U.S. peanuts -- is bracing for its worst crop in 10 years. According to U.S. Department of Agriculture statistics, the region will reap 2.04 billion pounds of peanuts, down 23% from last year and 24% below an Aug. 1 forecast.

The Peanut Advisory Board, a trade association, said peanut prices increased 12%, to $731 a ton, in the past week and may go higher, depending on weather and crop conditions through next month. The board said the direct loss to farmers will be about $150 million; the balance of the estimated $575 million loss will be felt by shellers and others in related businesses.

The two other areas that are big producers of peanuts -- the Virginia and North Carolina region and the region of New Mexico, Oklahoma and Texas -- are expected to enjoy increases in crop output of 15% and 10%, respectively over last year, but not enough to make up the loss from the Southeast. The USDA said this year's U.S. crop is expected to be 3.56 billion pounds, down 11%.

Prices of peanut butter are expected to rise the most, because it is made from the type of peanuts grown in the Southeast, the Peanut Advisory Board said. It estimates consumer peanut-butter prices will go up 10% by January.
