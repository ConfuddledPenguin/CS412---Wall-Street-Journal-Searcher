Marketing & Media: More Brand Names Gain Recognition Around the World --- Survey Finds Top 20 Products In Terms of Awareness, Esteem From Consumers

  • Author: Kathleen Deveny
  • Date: 09/13/90

Coca-Cola is no longer the only brand that can teach the world to sing, a new study says. Along with the ubiquitous soft drink, consumers now regard Sony, Mercedes-Benz, Kodak and Disney as truly "global brands." Those names -- along with Nestle, Toyota, McDonald's, IBM and Pepsi-Cola -- ranked high in a survey of brand awareness with consumers in the U.S., Japan and Western Europe. The study, conducted by Landor Associates, an image-consulting concern based in San Francisco, defines a global brand as one that is recognized well enough in the world's major markets for shoppers to evaluate it against other available brands.

"Ten years ago, we would have said Coca-Cola was the only truly global brand," said Donald Casey, president of Landor. The latest study identified up to 20 brands that were already internationally known, and noted a further 45 brands poised for global status.

To gauge the consumer attitudes, Landor questioned 5,000 consumers in the U.S., 3,000 consumers in eight European countries and 1,000 consumers in Japan. Consumers were asked how familiar they were with some 800 different trademarks. Respondents were then asked how highly they regarded those brands. Combining the familiarity and esteem scores, Landor then ranked the brands by "image power."

But some critics question the value of asking consumers to rate brands along such subjective criteria as esteem. "People are going to have a lot more esteem for a Mercedes than for a brand of toothpaste," said Alan Siegel, chairman of Siegel & Gale Inc., a corporate-identity unit of Saatchi & Saatchi Worldwide.

Indeed, brands made by some of the world's most powerful marketers, including Procter & Gamble Co. and Unilever Group, are noticeably absent from the survey's top 20 brands. Even Marlboro cigarettes, among the world's best-selling packaged consumer products, were absent from the global rankings. "It's hard to believe," says a spokesman for Philip Morris Cos., which makes Marlboro. "It points out a question about the validity of their idea of global brands."
