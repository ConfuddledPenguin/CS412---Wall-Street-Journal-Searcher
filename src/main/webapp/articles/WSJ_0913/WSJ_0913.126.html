House Demands Allies Pay More Toward Military --- Plan Includes Requirement Japan Pick Up All Costs Of the U.S. Forces There

  • Author: Andy Pasztor and Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 09/13/90

The House voted overwhelmingly to require heftier military spending by U.S. allies in Europe and the Pacific as part of any post-Cold War defense arrangements. The provisions, approved during debate on a $283 billion defense-authorization bill for fiscal 1991, include a requirement that Japan pay the full cost of U.S. forces in that nation.

The actions are the clearest signal yet that lawmakers are determined to make long-term, fundamental changes in how the Pentagon bases and pays for U.S. forces overseas. The short-term effect, however, depends on whether the Senate goes along with the House approach. The White House and Pentagon oppose the House language.

Meanwhile, lawmakers in the next few days are expected to receive details about a proposal to sell to Saudi Arabia an arms package substantially larger than the Pentagon or Saudi officials previously indicated. Some defense industry officials estimated that the proposed sales could total as much as $20 billion -- about twice as large as the packages disclosed previously -- if the White House doesn't pare the Saudi request for political reasons.

In yesterday's action on the defense bill, the House voted 370-to-53 to adopt an amendment requiring Tokyo to pay the entire $5 billion estimated annual cost of keeping 50,000 U.S. troops and their dependents in Japan. If Japanese officials balk, the amendment requires the U.S. to reduce its forces in that country by 5,000 soldiers each year.

Reflecting the frustration of many lawmakers, Rep. David Bonior (D., Mich.) said: "History has shown that we need to be tough to get action from Japan."

For the first time since World War II, the House also voted to require the Pentagon to draft plans for so-called dual basing of troops in Japan, West Germany and other nations. Under this concept, most of the Army and Air Force units stationed overseas, as well as their dependents, would be brought back to U.S. bases. Small contingents of troops would return without their dependents periodically for training and military exercises during six-month tours, but the cost of keeping them overseas would be far less than under the current system.

The Senate version of the bill doesn't contain such provisions, and senior members of the Senate Armed Services Committee have favored a more gradual approach to burden sharing.

Rep. Patricia Schroeder (D., Colo.), the main sponsor of the dual-basing provision, said the U.S. currently is paying to keep more than 300,000 troops to protect West Germany from East German forces, while "all of the East Germans are shopping in West Germany at the malls . . . . It doesn't make any sense."

In another section of the authorization bill, the House agreed to set up a bipartisan commission, similar to one created under the Reagan administration, to recommend which domestic bases should be closed as overall Pentagon spending declines. But the language effectively prohibits closing any bases for two years. Critics contend that would tie the hands of Pentagon officials and permit parochial interests to fight for jobs by keeping unnecessary bases in operation.

When the House resumes debate today on the bill, one of its most controversial provisions isn't expected to come to a vote. Proponents of the B-2 Stealth bomber, which the bill proposes to kill, have decided they don't want to risk a lopsided defeat on the House floor. Instead, they will rely on supporters in the Senate to try to keep the radar-eluding plane alive in a conference committee.

The B-2 will survive the conference "if we have a good strong commitment from the administration" for the program, predicted Sen. Malcolm Wallop (R., Wyo.) "But it's sometimes hard to know what that commitment is."

The White House has threatened to veto the defense bill if it kills the B-2, sharply restricts funding for Star Wars antimissile research and cuts too deeply into uniformed personnel.

Efforts to fashion a defense bill have been complicated by uncertainties over the outcome of the current budget talks between Congress and the White House. In addition, lawmakers haven't decided how to factor in the expected multibillion-dollar cost of military operations in the Middle East.

House Armed Services Committee Chairman Les Aspin (D., Wis.), for example, has been pushing to include the funds as part of a later supplemental appropriations measure. Other House leaders want to earmark at least $7 billion for the operation as part of the 1991 bill.

For the Saudi arms deal, U.S. defense industry officials said the Bush administration is planning to request expedited congressional approval to sell the Saudis about 24 F-15 fighters and 48 Apache tank-destructing helicopters, both made by McDonnell Douglas Corp. The package, according to these officials, also is slated to include Bradley Fighting Vehicles, built by FMC Corp.; Patriot air-defense missiles built by Raytheon Co.; as well as trucks and other support equipment.
