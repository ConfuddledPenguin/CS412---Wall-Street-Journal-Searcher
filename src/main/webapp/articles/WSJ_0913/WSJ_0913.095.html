Reynolds Metals Expects Earnings To Top Forecasts

  • Author: Dana Milbank
  • Date: 09/13/90

RICHMOND, Va. -- Reynolds Metals Co. Chairman William O. Bourke said he expects third-quarter earnings to exceed analysts' expectations over 20% because of increasing revenue from abroad. Mr. Bourke said the earnings will be near the second quarter's $1.75 a share, exceeding analysts' projections of about $1.45. Mr. Bourke, who is also chief executive officer, said the nation's No. 2 aluminum concern should surpass $7 a share in earnings for the year, against expectations of $6.67.

Even the higher-than-expected estimates, however, will fall short of last year's levels of $2.10 for the third quarter and $9.06 for the year. Mr. Bourke attributed the year-to-year decline to ongoing slumps in the transportation and construction industries and continued low market prices for aluminum ingot.

Mr. Bourke's bullish forecast apparently took Wall Street by surprise, as Reynolds's stock rose $2.375 to close at $68.375, a new 52-week high, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. On Tuesday, Mr. Bourke gave a sneak preview of the more bullish earnings outlook to analysts at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette's annual metals seminar.

In an interview at Reynolds's headquarters here, Mr. Bourke attributed the better-than-expected performance to foreign business -- growing at a rate of 20% to 30% -- which "more than offset" declining domestic demand. He also noted that aluminum producers had virtually no surplus inventory, contributing to a tight supply-demand situation that has firmed prices.

"There's just no surplus inventory anywhere, and we have no intention of letting them increase," the executive said.

Mr. Bourke also suggested that the earnings vindicated his ongoing restructuring of Reynolds's operations, in which he wrote off 45% of the company's assets and diversified into gold, plastics, and fabricated aluminum products. Mr. Bourke has tried to focus on such products in an effort to protect the company from aluminum's wide price swings.

Aluminum analyst Stewart Spector said Reynolds's strong performance was driven by higher-than-expected ingot prices. While off from last year's highs, the rise in raw aluminum prices -- 46% since January -- has exceeded Wall Street's expectations.

Reynolds's sluggish domestic growth, stemming largely from its reliance on depressed construction and transportation markets, won't improve before the middle of next year, Mr. Bourke said. The executive said the company's capital expenditure program of over $3 billion, begun in 1988, should add $150 million to profit in 1991 and as much as $200 million in 1992.

Separately, Reynolds Metals said its Mount Vernon Plastics Corp. unit opened a $15 million plant in Sparks, Nev., that makes plastic overcaps for canned coffee, margarine tubs and tennis ball cans, containers for margarine, yogurt and deli products, and microwave trays. By year end, the plant will have about 150 employees, the company said.
