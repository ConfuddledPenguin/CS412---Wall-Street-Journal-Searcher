Democrats in Capital Nominate Dixon In Mayoral Primary

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Sharon Pratt Dixon, a former Democratic National Committee treasurer, won the Democratic primary for mayor of the District of Columbia, topping a field of five contenders. Ms. Dixon is running to succeed Marion Barry, whose plans to seek a fourth term were cut short by his widely publicized trial and conviction on a misdemeanor cocaine-possession charge.

Ms. Dixon, 46 years old, faces former Police Chief Maurice Turner, a 55-year-old Republican, and a handful of other candidates in the November election. Ms. Dixon, who trailed her rivals throughout the primary, now enters the fall campaign as the favorite in a city where Democrats outnumber Republicans 8-1. Ms. Dixon surged in the final days of the campaign on the strength of a surprise endorsement by the Washington Post and a strong anti-incumbent message.

Former Carter administration civil rights official Eleanor Holmes Norton overcame last-minute disclosures that she hadn't filed local tax returns to win the Democratic nomination for the city's nonvoting delegate seat in Congress. She said her husband was responsible for family finances and neglected to tell her. She faces Harry Singleton, a former Reagan administration official. The winner will succeed Walter Fauntroy, a longtime civil rights activist who gave up a seat he had held for 19 years to run for mayor.

And the Rev. Jesse Jackson, in his first bid for local office, won one of two unpaid positions as a "shadow" senator to lobby for statehood for the district.

In Connecticut, Rep. Bruce Morrison won a close race for the Democratic nomination for governor. He will face former Republican Sen. Lowell Weicker, who is running as an independent, and the GOP nominee, Rep. John Rowland, who had no primary opposition.

In New Hampshire, Gov. Judd Gregg was nominated for a second term as governor. He will face state Democratic Party Chairman Joseph Grandmaison, who won a close race against state Senate Minority Leader Robert Preston.
