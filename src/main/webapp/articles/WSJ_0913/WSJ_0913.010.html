Education Agency Draws Criticism On Student Loans

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 09/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Government investigators accused the Education Department of significant shortcomings in its management of the guaranteed student-loan program, which is plagued by defaults of more than $2 billion annually. James Thomas, the department's inspector general, said the government's approach for policing schools that participate in the loan program "is all too often a paper chase." In testimony before the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations, he said that the department's "accrediting, eligibility and certification system is not effective in protecting federal funds from misuse or in protecting students."

In addition, a report by the subcommittee's staff accused the Education Department of "serious shortcomings in the current oversight system," particularly as it applies to proprietary schools, which train people for such jobs as hairdressers and truck drivers. Trade schools generally have the highest loan-default rates. The staff charged that the department certified schools "even when they have clear indicators of administrative deficiencies."

Last year, the department announced a new initiative to discipline or close schools with high loan-default rates. And earlier this week, Education Secretary Lauro Cavazos announced steps designed to force the agencies that accredit schools to do a better job.

Yesterday's hearing was the latest in a series of congressional inquiries following the July disclosure that the Higher Education Assistance Foundation, which has guaranteed some $9 billion in student loans, is on the brink of collapse because too many of its loans are in default. The administration currently is reviewing proposals from United Student Aid Funds, another guarantee agency, and the Student Loan Marketing Association to take over HEAF's portfolio. A decision is expected later this month.

Under the federal loan-guarantee program, students who attend accredited schools are eligible to apply for loans. The government doesn't accredit schools directly. Instead, it relies on about 100 accreditation agencies which, in turn, are subject to Education Department review. In addition, the department must certify that the schools are properly accredited. The investigators charged that the department was too lax in both procedures.

In an effort to tighten procedures, Mr. Cavazos said the department plans to review the activities of agencies that have accredited schools that have unusually high default rates. According to 1988 data released by the subcommittee staff, students at about 70 of some 8,400 accredited schools accounted for half of the default volume. And all of these 70 schools were accredited by only seven of the about 100 accrediting agencies.

One of the reasons HEAF got into trouble was that it guaranteed a large volume of loans to students at proprietary schools, many with high default rates. Tommy Downs, who left a prison where he was sentenced for defrauding the student-loan program to testify before the panel, called HEAF "a school owner's dream and a source of almost instant money. . . . Almost anyone could get a loan through HEAF if they could figure out how to fill in the blanks on its computerized forms and deal with its easy-to-satisfy, Kelly-girl-type administrative personnel."

However, Mr. Downs conceded, it was a secretary who discovered that a proprietary school he ran was illegally collecting money from the government by submitting loan applications for nonexistent students and triggered the investigation that sent him to prison.

Richard Hawk, the chairman of HEAF, said that Mr. Downs's criminal conviction "was in large part the result of the diligent efforts of HEAF personnel. . . . We regret that an unscrupulous trade school representative convicted of student loan fraud knowingly took advantage of HEAF's efficient and effective service in abusing the student loan program."
