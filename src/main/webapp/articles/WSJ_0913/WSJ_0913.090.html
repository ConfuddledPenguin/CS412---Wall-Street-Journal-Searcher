Who's News: Bertram Lee Named to Reebok's Board Amid Black Group's Attack on Industry

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 09/13/90

STOUGHTON, Mass. -- Reebok International Ltd. named Bertram Lee, a black businessman who is chairman of the Denver Nuggets basketball team, to its board. The action comes at a time when some black groups are charging that the sneaker industry has profited hugely from sales to black youths while having few blacks in upper management and spending little money in the black community. Operation Push, a Chicago-based civil rights group, has started a boycott of shoes made by Nike Inc., Beaverton, Ore., Reebok's archrival, demanding among other things that it name a black person to its board.

"The industry has been coming under a lot of criticism lately, and Reebok is trying to address some of the issues raised by Operation Push," said Heidi Steinberg, a shoe analyst with Salomon Bros.

Mr. Lee, who has served on other boards, said he always evaluates human rights when he considers whether to join a board. Reebok "came through with flying colors," he said.

He added that he hasn't had any contact with Operation Push about the shoe companies, but he said he expects Operation Push will evaluate Reebok at some point. Nike has charged that Operation Push decided to boycott Nike because Reebok has advertised in the group's magazine while Nike hasn't.

Nike has said the boycott hasn't had any effect on sales. Operation Push said officials were unavailable for comment.

Mr. Lee and Reebok said he was invited to consider joining the Reebok board before Operation Push started its boycott in August. He said he met Reebok Chairman Paul Fireman last spring when the two helped organize Nelson Mandela's visit to Boston.

Some critics have complained that shoe companies encourage poor inner-city youths to spend exorbitant funds buying sneakers, such as Reebok's Pump shoes that retail for $150. In some places police have reported people being mugged for their sneakers. Mr. Lee said that Reebok sells shoes at a variety of prices and that "Reebok has done no more and no less than I would do as a businessman." He added, "I'm not sure I've been able to see the ethical problem."

Mr. Lee said that as a director "my primary responsibility is a fiduciary duty to the shareholders." He said he believes he was added to the board because of "my assumed business acumen, my interest in sports and my enthusiasm for human rights and companies that have an interest in human rights." He said, "Reebok was thrown an added incentive because I happen to be an African American."

Mr. Lee, 51 years old, is chairman of BML Associates, a Boston holding company with interests in radio stations and the Nuggets. He is also chairman of Boston Bank of Commerce.

In the past he has been president of New England Television Corp., which owned the first minority-controlled CBS network affiliate. Until last year he was a director of Boston's Shawmut National Bank. He said he resigned that post because he was spending much of his time in the Midwest. He is also a director of numerous nonprofit institutions.

He is chairman of TransAfrica Forum, an anti-apartheid group, based in Washington, D.C., with which Reebok has worked since sponsoring the Human Rights Now Tour for Amnesty International, two years ago.
