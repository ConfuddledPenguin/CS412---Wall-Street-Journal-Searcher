Senate Approves Fish Inspection Plan Giving Lead to Agriculture Department

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 09/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate adopted a mandatory fish inspection plan that would put the nation's $30 billion seafood industry under a triad of regulatory agencies. The landmark food-safety measure was approved by voice vote after lawmakers voted, 59-39, to reject a rival inspection plan favored by the Bush administration. The measure, sponsored by Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell (D., Maine), would give the Agriculture Department the lead in inspecting 1,900 processors nationwide. Currently, only a fraction of four billion pounds of fish and shellfish consumed each year in the U.S. undergoes some form of product-grading or inspection.

"No longer will consumers have to guess whether or not the fish they eat is safe," said Sen. Patrick Leahy (D., Vt.), floor manager of the bill. "For the first time, they will be able to buy seafood with the USDA's seal of approval."

Defeated was the proposal by Sens. Ernest Hollings (D., S.C.), Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.) and Ted Stevens (R., Alaska) to give the inspection duties to the Food and Drug Administration and the Commerce Department. In floor debate, Sen. Mitchell successfully argued that the lack of a lead agency "would muddle lines of authority and accountability, making the {joint} program unworkable and incapable of protecting public health."

Under the Mitchell-Leahy bill, the Agriculture Department's Food Safety and Inspection Service would conduct the seafood inspections, the FDA would set limits for bacteria and other contaminants, while the Commerce Department's National Marine Fisheries Service would continue to oversee the nation's fisheries, including closing polluted waters to fishing and shellfish harvesting.

Awaiting floor action in the House are rival fish inspection bills pushed by Agriculture Committee Chairman Kika de la Garza (D., Texas) and by Commerce Committee Chairman John Dingell (D., Mich.). They are engaged in battle over turf, just as their Senate counterparts, Messrs. Leahy and Hollings, have been. Mr. de la Garza would give the lead to the Agriculture Department, which falls under his panel's jurisdiction, while Mr. Dingell favors the FDA, which his committee oversees.

Mr. Mitchell is expected to urge the House Democratic leaders to expedite a vote on seafood-inspection legislation. Backers of the Mitchell-Leahy bill have indicated a willingness to accept a compromise measure. By giving the FDA an expanded role, they hope to win over Mr. Dingell and avert a presidential veto, which has been threatened by the White House.

For the consumer movement, however, last night's vote, coming after more than 20 years of failed legislative initiatives, was a mixed victory. The safe-food coalition split over fish inspection, with Public Voice for Food and Health Policy supporting the Mitchell-Leahy measure and most other consumer groups favoring the Hollings-Kennedy-Stevens bill.

The bone of contention is whistle-blower protection. Under the Mitchell-Leahy bill, there wouldn't be continuous inspection of seafood processing plants. Instead, inspectors would make random visits at the plants and rely on plant workers for tips on improper or unsanitary pratices when the inspectors aren't there.

For this reason, most consumer groups supported the Hollings-Kennedy-Stevens measure because it would have protected whistle-blowers from reprisals by their employers. Aides to Sen. Leahy said that whistle-blower protections will be added to the Mitchell-Leahy bill when it goes to conference.
