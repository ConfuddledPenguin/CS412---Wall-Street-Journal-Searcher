Law -- Legal Beat: New York Judge Halts Publication Of Book About Israeli Espionage

  • Author: Amy Dockser Marcus and Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 09/13/90

A state judge in New York temporarily barred St. Martin's Press from selling a book about the Israeli intelligence service, the Mossad. State Supreme Court Justice Michael J. Dontzin signed the order early yesterday morning after lawyers for the Israeli government argued that publication of the book would endanger lives and disclose confidential in formation.

Justice Dontzin ordered the New York publishing house to recall approximately 18,000 copies of the book that have already been shipped. And St. Martin's said an additional 30,000 orders are being withheld from distribution as a result of the judge's order.

The book, "By Way of Deception: The Making and Unmaking of a Mossad Officer," was written by Victor Ostrovsky. Mr. Ostrovsky, who now lives in Canada, claims he spent two years as a Mossad agent. He wrote the book in collaboration with Claire Hoy, a Canadian journalist.

A Canadian court last week barred the book's Canadian publisher, Stoddart Publishing Co. of Toronto, from continuing production or releasing the book before Sept. 17, when a hearing is expected to be held, a spokeswoman for Stoddart said.

Roy Gainsburg, president of St. Martin's, said he believed this was the first time that a foreign government had successfully enjoined publication of a book in the U.S.

Jonathan J. Lerner, a lawyer representing the Israeli government, said the book's author had violated a confidentiality agreement not to divulge any secrets. "If a CIA agent were seeking to publish vital U.S. secrets in Israel, wouldn't we expect the Israeli courts to protect those secrets?" said Mr. Lerner, a lawyer with the New York firm Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom.

First Amendment lawyers said courts won't restrain the publication of a book except in extraordinary situations. These experts said the Israeli government's fears that the book might endanger lives may have persuaded the judge to issue the order.

But P. Cameron DeVore, a First Amendment lawyer with the Seattle firm Davis Wright Tremaine, said the U.S. Supreme Court has held that such orders are unconstitutional unless an immediate and direct threat to U.S. security -- not that of a foreign government -- exists.

"Even if this book is published, as I think it will be, it's a setback that a restraint order was entered at all," said Floyd Abrams of the New York firm Cahill, Gordon & Reindel, a First Amendment expert.

Israeli media reports indicated the book contains allegations that the Mossad knew in advance about the terrorist attack that killed 240 U.S. Marines in Beirut in 1983 and also includes information about Mossad espionage activity inside the U.S.

Justice Dontzin has scheduled a hearing for tomorrow to review evidence to determine whether the ban should be continued. But the appellate court is expected to consider today St. Martin's request that the order be overturned immediately.

CAMERAS IN FEDERAL COURTS are approved as a limited experiment.

The Judicial Conference, the policy-making arm of the federal courts, voted to allow a three-year experiment allowing the use of cameras and broadcasting equipment in civil trials in two federal appeals courts and six federal district courts. The broadcasting experiment won't involve criminal trials because rules of procedure prohibit it.

The experiment, which begins in July 1991, will mark the first time television or still cameras or radio broadcasting equipment have been allowed in the federal courts in at least 50 years. In the past decade, most state court systems have experimented with allowing cameras in courtrooms, and 44 states now allow broadcast of some trials.

But the trend in the states was never matched in the federal courts, due to the skepticism of many judges and the adamant opposition of Chief Justice Warren Burger, who retired in 1986. Chief Justice William Rehnquist, although firmly opposed to televising Supreme Court sessions, appointed a committee in 1988 to study the use of cameras in other federal courts.

Last month, the committee recommended the three-year experiment, and yesterday the 27-judge Judicial Conference gave its permission. A conference spokesman said the approval was on a voice vote, but that a few judges expressed objections at the closed-door session.

Once the participating courts are chosen, the experiment will be conducted under restrictions set by the Judicial Conference. News media wishing to televise a trial must notify the judge in advance; the request may be refused at the judge's discretion.

The experiment will be evaluated by the Federal Judicial Center, the judiciary's research arm, after it is concluded in June 1994. Any decision to open the federal courts further for broadcasting will depend on the evaluation.

The experiment doesn't include the Supreme Court, which isn't covered by the Judicial Conference's action. It is up to the nine justices to decide whether to allow television coverage, and they repeatedly have rejected requests by lopsided votes.

FTC INVESTIGATES recruiting by law firms.

The Federal Trade Commission is looking into whether major law firms are violating antitrust laws by cooperating with each other in their recruiting and hiring of law students.

The investigation, believed to be the first of its kind, focuses on agreements among big law firms to limit expenses related to recruiting, such as airline tickets, hotel accommodations and restaurant meals, according to people familiar with the inquiry.

The FTC has targeted law firms based in Cleveland. It isn't known how many firms are under scrutiny or whether firms from other cities also are involved.

In the annual competition for top graduating law students, large firms routinely coordinate and share expenses associated with interviewing and wooing potential associates. The FTC apparently believes that such coordination may include illegal collusion to limit costs and restrain competition for sought-after recruits.

Without "confirming or denying" any specific investigation, FTC official Michael McNeely said that "if the firms agree on the level of wining and dining people," they may be violating federal antitrust laws.

Employees with three Cleveland firms -- Arter & Hadden, Baker & Hostetler and Squire, Sanders & Dempsey -- confirmed that they were under investigation. Another firm under scrutiny is Jones, Day, Reavis & Pogue, according to people familiar with the FTC action. The FTC's investigation was first reported this week by Legal Times, a Washington trade paper.

If the commission determines that antitrust laws have been violated, it could seek to impose fines on the law firms and request a court order barring anti-competitive recruiting practices.

Arter & Hadden's managing partner, Robert Glaser, called the investigation "ludicrous" and denied any wrongdoing by his firm or the others under scrutiny.

Mr. Glaser acknowledged that law firms coordinate student interview trips, and that this cooperation saves costs for the firms. But he insisted that coordination takes place primarily for students' benefit and only when requested by students. "We aren't limiting our recruiting," he said. "We aren't colluding on who we invite. We hire independently."

Employees with Baker & Hostetler and Squire Sanders confirmed that the firms are being investigated but declined to elaborate. An employee with Jones Day said the firm's managing partner, Richard Pogue, wasn't available to comment.

INVESTORS SUING DREXEL seek relief from court stay.

The judge overseeing Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc.'s bankruptcy case has been asked to allow six lawsuits to go forward accusing Drexel, its former junk-bond chief Michael Milken and others of a massive conspiracy to rig the junk-bond market.

The federal and state civil lawsuits are class actions filed by investors who allege an intertwined scheme of securities-law violations, racketeering and other wrongdoing. In the alleged scheme, sometimes referred to as a daisy chain, Mr. Milken, Drexel and several corporate raiders are accused of creating a false market for the securities through sham transactions and price manipulation.

Some of the suits also blame the alleged scheme for exacerbating the crisis in the savings and loan industry. Federal regulators and Congress have begun their own investigations into possible criminal activity in the alleged scheme.

Although trial dates have already been set for some of the lawsuits, the cases can't go forward because of an automatic stay that goes into effect when a company files for bankruptcy-law protection. Drexel filed under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code on Feb. 13.

"The public interest demands that there be a full public airing and fair prosecution of all civil claims relating to Drexel's notorious operations, and that the bankruptcy laws not be used as an offensive weapon to shield the responsible wrongdoers," the plaintiffs said in a motion filed in federal bankruptcy court in Manhattan. The motion was filed by Milberg Weiss Bershad Specthrie & Lerach, which represents the securities class-action group.

The motion argues that it will be impossible for Drexel to come up with a feasible bankruptcy reorganization plan without getting a better handle on the damages it will face from the litigation. U.S. Bankruptcy Court Judge Howard Buschman III set a hearing for Oct. 2 on the request.

A spokesman for Drexel said the company will file its response to the motion later this month. He said, "The reason for a stay in bankruptcy proceedings is, among other things, to allow the debtor to develop a comprehensive plan in an organized way without the need to attend to a multitude of separate claims."

Under the plaintiffs' proposal, the court would allow the cases to continue until damages, if any, are determined, but it wouldn't allow the plaintiffs to try to collect the money.

FORMER HUGHES AIRCRAFT employee wins whistle-blower suit.

A state Superior Court jury in Los Angeles awarded $1.95 million to a former Hughes Aircraft Co. employee who contended he was fired after accusing the company of overcharging the government on defense contracts.

After five days of deliberations, the jury upheld claims made by Bobby Coffey, 55 years old, who had worked at Hughes for more than two decades. As a program-cost analyst at Hughes's missile division, Mr. Coffey pointed out allegedly fraudulent practices in which the company overcharged the government for labor and materials. Melanie Lomax, Mr. Coffey's attorney, alleged yesterday that the overcharging amounted to "hundreds of thousands, if not millions" of dollars.

Mr. Coffey also claimed age discrimination, but the jury didn't uphold that particular claim, said Hughes, a unit of General Motors Corp.

The company, which is planning to appeal, has denied that it ever overcharged the government and maintains that Mr. Coffey was dismissed because he had falsified a time card. "We feel the verdict was not consistent with the evidence that was presented," said a spokesman for Hughes.

FORMER TAKEOVER LAWYER at Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom pleaded guilty to three insider-trading counts. Steven Glauberman, a former Skadden Arps associate, pleaded guilty to two counts of disclosing confidential information that he learned at the firm and one count of trading on such information. Judge Miriam Goldman Cedarbaum in federal court in Manhattan set sentencing for Dec. 21. Mr. Glauberman is cooperating with prosecutors in the case, in which three other defendants have been named.
