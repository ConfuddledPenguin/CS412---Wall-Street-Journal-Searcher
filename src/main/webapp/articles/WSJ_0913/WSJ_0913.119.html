Accountants' Panel Drops Its Proposal On Frequent Fliers

  • Author: Katherine Graven
  • Date: 09/13/90

An accounting panel abandoned a proposal that would have required airlines to reflect much higher costs on their books for the millions of unused frequent-flier travel coupons the carriers have outstanding. Meeting in Seattle, the Accounting Standards Executive Committee of the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants deliberated over a proposal that would have required airlines to adopt two different methods of accounting for frequent-flier liabilities. But "we terminated the project," said John L. Kreischer, chairman of the 15-member committee.

A special task force of institute accountants spent two years studying frequent-flier accounting methods. The proposal abandoned yesterday called for airlines to use both incremental-cost and deferred-revenue accounting methods.

The first allows airlines to set aside money for liabilities based on its incremental cost -- that is, the cost of flying a frequent flier in an otherwise empty seat. The second requires that at a future time, airlines must account for frequent-flier miles based on the price of the revenue that would be lost from displacing a paying passenger.

Currently, each airline determines its own frequent-flier liabilities, based on incremental costs for each additional nonfare paying passenger. These costs include food, fuel and handling; the airlines also take into account the rates at which the frequent-flier coupons are redeemed.

The debate is extremely important to all airlines, which have opposed changing the accounting rules for frequent-flier programs.

The travel awards have become tremendously popular since they were created in 1981, but they've left airlines with significant liabilities. According to an internal study by the BusinessFlyer, a newsletter on frequent-travel awards, airlines have a total 34.6 billion frequent-flier miles outstanding.

John Holland, the newsletter's publisher, says that redemption rates are rising, but still only 26% of the mileage under the awards is redeemed. Forcing airlines to reserve for all mileage that might be redeemed could double the industry's liabilities outstanding to $4.7 billion and might even threaten some carriers' solvency.

Still, some accountants argue that as frequent-flier programs have skyrocketed, airlines are incurring huge potential future liabilities and aren't accurately reflecting such liabilities on their books.

Mr. Kreischer said the committee decided to abandon the dual-method proposal because it did not want to provoke a battle with the Financial Accounting Standards Board, the body that has final say over accounting standards for the airline and other industries.

Through prior meetings, Mr. Kreischer said, AICPA members determined that the FASB prefers only the deferred-accounting method of recording frequent-flier liabilities.

"We felt (the dual method) was a reasonable position, but we didn't want to go counter to the FASB," Mr. Kreischer said. The panel "agrees in principle" with the deferred-revenue method, but it wanted to go a step further to allow for travel awards that are not redeemed.

The FASB now must decide whether to battle the airlines over the issue, Mr. Kreischer said.

Dennis R. Beresford, chairman of the FASB, said he was somewhat surprised by the outcome of the AICPA meeting. After working with the AICPA task force, he anticipated that the group would go along with the FASB's strong preference for a single deferred-revenue method.

Mr. Beresford maintains that this method more-accurately reflects the economics of frequent-flier transactions. Mr. Beresford added that it is too early to say whether the FASB will take up the matter further. "It sounds like they (the AICPA) have passed the buck. We have to decide whether we want to accept it,"he said.
