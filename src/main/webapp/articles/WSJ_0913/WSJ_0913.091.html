Letters to the Editor: Nuclear Energy Vs. Foreign Oil

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/13/90

Your Aug. 17 page-one story "Energy Options" concentrates so much on short-term oil displacement that it ignores the great contributions that have already been made by nuclear energy in replacing foreign oil in the generation of electricity. You fail to recognize that U.S. nuclear-energy plants have reduced dependence on foreign oil since the 1973 oil embargo by 4.3 billion barrels and continue to cut our use of foreign oil by 740,000 barrels every day. This is just about the amount we had been importing from Iraq and Kuwait before Iraq's invasion of Kuwait. Construction of nuclear-energy plants to supply electricity was a major part of this nation's efforts to use domestic sources of energy to avoid its increasing dependence on foreign oil after the 1973 embargo. We've forgotten that lesson and, apparently, must relearn it.

Harold B. Finger

Washington

---

You dismiss renewable energy as "far-fetched alternatives" that aren't economically viable. One need only look at California's use of renewable energy resources last year to see that these statements are completely untrue. In 1989, 18.2% of all electrical energy used in California came from renewable energy resources. As one of those renewable technologies, we believe that solar electric generating systems (SEGS) are neither far-fetched nor uneconomical. As a matter of fact, our newest 80-megawatt SEGS plant is currently providing Southern California Edison electricity at a cost of eight cents a kilowatt hour. This cost is cheaper than nuclear and almost competitive with oil and gas.

James C. Bazor

Chief Operating Officer

Luz International Limited

Los Angeles
