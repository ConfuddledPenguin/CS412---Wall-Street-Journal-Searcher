Credit Markets: New York City $850 Million Bond Issue Sells Out As Small Investors Flock to Yields That Top 8%

  • Author: Kevin Donovan and Sharon King
  • Date: 09/13/90

NEW YORK -- Yields that topped 8% attracted a large number of individual buyers to New York City's $850 million general obligation bond issue. But the high yield, which translates into a taxable equivalent yield of more than 12% for many individuals, wasn't high enough to lure many big investors. Dealers said institutions considered the yields too low in light of New York City's financial troubles.

Still, officials at lead underwriter Bear Stearns & Co. said the issue was oversubscribed due to strong demand by individual investors.

"We had very strong retail demand throughout," said Dan Keating, senior managing director of municipal underwriting at Bear Stearns. "Institutional business was scattered in the shorter and longer maturities."

The New York City issue was priced to yield from 6.50% for two-year notes to 8.05% for 30-year bonds. Moody's Investors Service Inc. confirmed a single-A rating on the issue while Standard & Poor's Corp. confirmed a single-A-minus rating.

Investor reaction to the New York City sale spilled over into the rest of the municipal bond market, helping tax-exempts to outperform the broader bond market.

Municipal bonds ended unchanged to as much as 1/8 point higher, a gain of $1.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. But Treasury, corporate and mortgage-backed bonds ended unchanged to as much of 1/8 point lower in quiet trading. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 8.94% from 8.92% on Tuesday.

Mitchell Held, economist at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., said the Treasury market opened with a "nice little runup," which reflected optimism that congressional negotiators would soon forge a credible budget-reduction plan. That was considered good for bonds because many economist argue that a smaller budget deficit could lead to lower interest rates.

But later in the day, bond prices slid on news that Iran's spiritual leader, Ali Khameni, said that the U.S. military presence in the Middle East justifies a Moslem jihad, or holy war.

Mr. Held said the pronouncements raised the specter of still higher oil prices. On the New York Mercantile Exchange, crude oil futures contracts for October delivery settled at $30.97 a barrel, up 21 cents on the day.

Municipal Issues

Yields topping 8% attracted many individual buyers to New York City's $850 million general obligation bond issue.

But the high yield, which translates into a taxable equivalent yield of more than 12% for many individuals, wasn't high enough to lure many institutional investors. Dealers said the institutions considered the yields too low in light of New York City's financial troubles. Still, officials at lead underwriter Bear Stearns & Co. said the issue was oversubscribed.

The New York City issue was priced to yield from 6.50% for two-year notes to 8.05% for 30-year bonds. Moody's Investors Service Inc. confirmed a single-A rating on the issue while Standard & Poor's Corp. confirmed a Single-A-Minus rating.

In competitive action, an issue of $100 million Los Angeles Department of Water and Power electric plant revenue bonds sold out immediately after they were purchased by a BT Securities Corp. group, officials at the firm reported. BT Securities is a unit of Bankers Trust Corp.

The bonds, rated double-A by Moody's and S&P, were priced for reoffering to yield from 6.40% for six-year notes to 7.39% for 30-year bonds.

Meanwhile, Swiss Bank Corp. said it would not back $50 million of Philadelphia temporary loan notes. Philadelphia had been expected to sell about $375 million of the notes this week, with proceeds intended to keep the city afloat financially until tax collections begin. Without the note sale, Philadelphia officials have said the city will run out of cash later this month.

"The deal has definitely been postponed," said Alan Schankel, first vice president and manager of the municipal bond department at Janney Montgomery Scott Inc. in Philadelphia. "I think they'll go back to the drawing board and try everything," such as trying to obtain another letter of credit or getting the state more involved with the sale, he said.

Mr. Schankel quoted Philadelphia general obligation bonds 8 1/4% due in 2009 as trading late yesterday at 96 to yield about 8.60%. Trading halted for about one hour after the Swiss Bank news. After it resumed, bond yields rose slightly before returning to the 8.60% level.

Elsewhere yesterday, most municipal bonds were unchanged to 1/8 point higher.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond ended at a price of 97 28/32 to yield 8.94% compared with 98 2/32 to yield 8.92% on Tuesday. The latest two-year notes ended unchanged at 100 5/32 to yield 8.02%.

Short-term securities were lower yesterday. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.37% for a bond equivalent yield of 7.59% while the rate on six-month bills fell to 7.30% for a bond equivalent yield of 7.66%.

Junk, Corporate Bonds

The Resolution Trust Corp.'s junk-bond sales put a damper on the high-yield market, sending prices moderately lower.

Most junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, fell 1/4 to 1/2 point.

The declines came after the RTC sold $70 million of junk bonds Tuesday and was seeking to sell another $40 million of high-yield bonds yesterday.

A spokeswoman for the RTC, the federal agency set up to bail out the nation's troubled thrifts, said the RTC managed to sell between two-thirds and three-quarters of the $40 million in bonds. "We could have sold them all but we were not happy with the prices," she said.

Although the spokeswoman declined to say which junk bonds the RTC was trying to liquidate, dealers said the list included a number of cable TV companies, including Century Communications Co., American Cablesystem and Comcast Corp. On Monday, the House passed a bill that would regulate cable TV rates for basic service and assist emerging competitors.

Among the big losers in the high-yield market were junk bonds of AmBase Corp., which plunged a day after the company said its Home Insurance Co. unit has discontinued negotiations to sell US International Reinsurance Co.

AmBase's 14.875% senior subordinated notes, maturing in 1998, tumbled six points to 61.

In the investment-grade corporate debt market, high-grade corporate bonds ended unchanged to slightly lower.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Prices of mortgage securities ended with slim gains.

Government National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% issue for September delivery rose 2/32 to 98 7/8. The 9 1/2% securities of Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. and Federal National Mortgage Association each rose 3/32 to 98 14/32 and 99, respectively.

Lehman Brothers announced the pricing of a $300 million Fannie Mae real estate mortgage investment conduit, or Remic, issue. The 16-class deal, Series 1990-123, is backed by Fannie Mae's 9 1/2% securities. Details were not immediately available.

Foreign Bonds

The World Bank managed to sell its third and largest global bond issue yesterday but underwriters said it wasn't easy.

The $2 billion of 8 5/8% five-year notes were priced within expectations but the latest tremors from the Persian Gulf, coupled with tough market conditions, nearly derailed smooth placement.

"This was a deal which people had to work for," said Walter Henniges, head of syndication at Deutsche Bank Capital Markets Ltd., the joint lead underwriter with Salomon Brothers International Ltd. "The Treasury market was very nervous and this was a very difficult market to work in."

In addition to negative talk from Iran, underwriters had to contend with lukewarm demand from Japanese investors. The dollar's weakness and the prospect of lower rates in the U.S. while Japanese yields are increasing muted demand.

European investors, who have previously been more willing to buy World Bank bonds than U.S. or Japanese investors, picked up only 35% of the issue.

West German and British government bonds closed moderately lower after earlier gains were wiped out after an Iranian call for a "holy war" against the U.S. presence in the Middle East.

In Japan, government bonds rose.
