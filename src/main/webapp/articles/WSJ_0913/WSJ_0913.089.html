International: U.S.-Soviet Plan For Plane Venture Faces Obstacles

  • Author: Susan Carey
  • Date: 09/13/90

BRUSSELS -- Since the start of the year, negotiations have been going on about the possibility of a U.S.-Soviet passenger airplane venture. The little-known talks came to light when Mikhail Gorbachev was asked by Soviet television what else he and President Bush discussed, besides the Persian Gulf, at the Helsinki summit last Sunday. The Soviet president mentioned the airplane venture.

The planned project involves two Soviet aircraft design bureaus, Ilyushin and Tupolev, Israel Aircraft Industries Ltd., and the Pratt & Whitney unit of United Technologies Corp. The point man for this unlikely team is none other than the original Russia hand, Armand Hammer, the 92-year-old chairman and chief executive of Occidental Petroleum Corp.

The plan is to take four-engined Ilyushin Il-96s and twin-engined Tupolev Tu204s to Tel Aviv, where the Israeli aerospace concern would outfit them with Pratt & Whitney engines, U.S. avionic systems and comfortable cabin interiors. The planes then would be offered for sale world-wide as cheaper alternatives to Airbus Industrie and Boeing Co. aircraft.

In a telephone interview yesterday, Dr. Hammer said he would travel to Moscow next month and hopes to get a firm contract from President Gorbachev so that a new company he chairs, Aviation Venture International, can buy the Soviet airplanes for refitting in Israel and export to the West. "I have written Mr. Gorbachev about it and I have had encouraging replies," he said.

Dr. Hammer also said he met with President Bush three weeks ago and discussed the plan. The president's reaction was "very favorable," he said.

These refitted-for-export jetliners, which are intended to compete with the best that Airbus can build, would offer airlines an alternative to the three big Western manufacturers -- Airbus, Boeing and McDonnell Douglas Corp. -- which are nearly sold out for the next several years, Dr. Hammer said. They could be flying by 1995 and would cost less than competing Western models because of lower Soviet and Israeli labor costs, he said.

However, big problems could lie ahead. Aeroflot, the Soviet Union's mammoth airline, itself needs nearly all of the early production of both new models. Western technology-transfer authorities may balk at letting sensitive avionic systems go into Soviet-built airplanes. Aviation specialists say the refitted planes would have to win certification from exacting U.S. and European air-worthiness authorities, a potentially large stumbling block. Finally, they ask, who would supply after-sales support to airlines that bought the planes?

Israel Aircraft Industries is keeping mum on the subject. A spokesman in Tel Aviv said the state-owned defense and aerospace company won't be commenting until "there is a logical opportunity with a strong profit potential." Israel and the Soviet Union have no diplomatic relations. But Dr. Hammer said the company has made five trips to the Soviet aircraft factories this year and that, under orders from Mr. Gorbachev, the Israelis were "shown everything."

Aero-engine builder Pratt & Whitney confirmed that it has been in technical talks for the past several months with the Ilyushin and Tupolev bureaus about fastening its PW2037 engine on their newest models. "We're pursuing it as a very serious venture," says a spokesman. The PW2037 currently flies on Boeing's twin-engined 757.

Dr. Hammer said Aviation Venture International has raised $2.5 million in startup money for the project from the likes of British publisher Robert Maxwell, Canadian developer Albert Reichmann and Israeli businessmen Shaul Eisenberg and Al Schwimmer. He added that these investors hope to raise $50 million from banks or a public offering next April so that Aviation Venture International can start buying planes, engines and avionic systems. With firm orders from airlines, the venture would then be able to raise more capital from the public.

Aerospace specialists are enthusiastic about the design of the long-range Il-96 and the short- to medium-range Tu-204, both of which are now undergoing flight tests and Soviet certification. Both have the new "fly-by-wire" cockpit technology pioneered for civil airliners by Airbus. But the problem with the existing models, especially for export prospects, is the engines, a technology in which the Soviets trail.

Pratt & Whitney says there would be no problems fitting its PW2037 on the two Soviet jetliners. The company has gotten U.S. government clearance to talk to the Soviets about the project, the spokesman said, but it hasn't yet received export licenses for the engines.

As for prospective buyers apart from Aeroflot, GPA Group Ltd., the Irish airplane lessor, has said it likes the Tu-204 model and even has talked about buying some with Soviet engines. No doubt with Western engines and cockpit equipment, GPA would be even more interested. One GPA manager calls the project "feasible."

The concept of a joint airplane venture isn't entirely new. Earlier this year, Ilyushin reportedly had talks with Construcciones Aeronauticas S.A. of Spain and other Western aerospace companies about joint development of an export model of Ilyushin's new, 60-seat Il-114 turboprop commuter aircraft.

And there is a precedent of sorts. Gulfstream Aerospace Corp., a unit of Chrysler Corp., and the Sukhoi Design Bureau have teamed up on plans for a supersonic business jet. The two showed a model at the big Farnborough Air Show in Britain earlier this month.

The first international aerospace exhibition in the Soviet Union is scheduled to open Sept. 21 in Moscow and interest from the West is high. More than 200 exhibitors from 15 countries have signed up and 90 Soviet companies will be represented. The U.S.-Soviet-Israeli project is expected to be a hot topic at the show.
