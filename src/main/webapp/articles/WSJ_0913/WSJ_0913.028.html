Taxes at Issue As Budget Talks Yet Unresolved --- Democrats Want Increase For Rich if GOP Insists On Capital-Gains Cut

  • Author: Jackie Calmes and Jeffrey H. Birnbaum
  • Date: 09/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Budget negotiators labored to overcome differences on taxes and on how to enforce a deficit-reduction accord as the bipartisan talks continued. Among the crucial unresolved issues were the Republicans' demand for lower capital-gains taxes and the Democrats' insistence that any such tax cut be combined with an increase in income-tax rates for upper-income Americans, people close to the talks said.

However, there was some tangible progress as the administration accepted a new 10% tax on the purchase of luxury items, a proposal advanced by the Democrats.

Negotiators, who had hoped to reach an accord by this morning, said they see little chance of reaching agreement before tomorrow. House Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.) said he expects an agreement before week's end.

Rep. Foley said Democrats will find it "extremely difficult" to agree to a capital-gains cut unless it is accompanied by higher income-tax rates "or some comparable action." He didn't elaborate on what alternatives to higher rates might be acceptable.

"I don't sense any great hostility" to capital gains, said Republican negotiator Rep. Bill Frenzel of Minnesota. "The problem is the price." He and other Republicans said they won't go along with increased tax rates.

Although there had been speculation that the administration might back off its proposal to cut capital-gains tax rates, President Bush underscored his commitment to the idea in his speech to Congress.

Both the administration and congressional Republicans have emphasized the importance of changing the congressional budget process to assure that any agreement is carried out. Changes in taxes and benefit programs can be written into law, but some other mechanism is being sought to limit other domestic spending and defense spending, which are subject to annual appropriations bills.

Some Democrats, particularly Senate Appropriations Committee Chairman Robert Byrd (D., W.Va.), have resisted any attempt to tie their hands for more than one year. The initial Democratic offer made last week said the appropriations committee would honor any ceiling on overall spending, but would consider as "advisory only" any attempt to set totals for defense and non-defense spending beyond fiscal 1991, which begins Oct. 1.

To meet their goal of reducing the deficit $500 billion in five years, negotiators are converging on a plan that would result in a net tax increase of about $130 billion. People close to the talks said the tax package would probably contain higher energy taxes, including an increase in the gasoline tax, higher taxes on alcoholic beverages and an extension of the 1.45% Medicare payroll tax to cover wages above the current $51,300 ceiling. Tax breaks to encourage oil exploration probably will be a part of any accord, they added.

In one of its latest offers, the administration proposed four new taxes that would raise $2.5 billion in fiscal 1991 and more than $24 billion over the five-year period. The largest of these taxes is a one-cent-agallon tax on refined petroleum products that would raise about $1 billion in the first year and $8 billion over five years.

The GOP and White House also would impose a luxury tax that would raise $900 million in the first year and $7.8 billion over five years. This closely matches a Democratic proposal for a new tax on purchases of luxury items. The Republicans' 10% luxury tax would be imposed on automobiles that cost more than $30,000, boats that cost more than $30,000, electronics of more than $1,000, jewelry of more than $5,000, and furs of more than $500.

In addition, the Republican would index to inflation a variety of excise taxes and would accept a tax on ozone-depleting chemicals similar to one proposed by the Democrats.

Defense spending would be cut between $170 billion and $200 billion from projected levels over five years, based on offers made by the two sides. Domestic spending of all sorts would be trimmed between $103 billion and $126 billion, with both sides proposing to make the elderly pay more of the cost of Medicare.

The American Association of Retired Persons, recalling the uproar that led Congress to repeal a surtax on upper-income elderly that was to pay for an expansion of Medicare to cover catastrophic illness, objected to proposals to increase Medicare premiums for upper-income people. "It is catastrophic without the benefits," the group said.
