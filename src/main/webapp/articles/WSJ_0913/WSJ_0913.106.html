Fed Rules on Banks' Right to End Payments In Home-Equity Loans

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/13/90

WASHINGTON -- Banks can stop borrowers' ability to tap into home-equity lines of credit, the Federal Reserve said, if the fluctuating interest rate paid by the borrower hits the cap specified in the loan agreement. The amended regulation stems from a 1989 lawsuit filed by Consumers Union, which challenged the Fed regulation as a violation of the Home Equity Act.

The law prohibited lenders from changing the terms of a home-equity loan after the account had been opened. But the Fed added an exemption, which allows the banks to stop disbursing funds if an interest-rate cap is reached and those terms are spelled out in the lending agreement.

"Under this regulation, the consumer can't borrow money he expected to under his line of credit," said Michelle Meier, counsel for government affairs for Consumers Union. "He will have to go another lender at a higher interest rate."

The U.S. District Court here dismissed the suit in May, saying that the Fed has broad authority to rule on such matters. Consumers Union said it will appeal. As of 1988, about $75 billion in home-equity loans were outstanding, Ms. Meier said.
