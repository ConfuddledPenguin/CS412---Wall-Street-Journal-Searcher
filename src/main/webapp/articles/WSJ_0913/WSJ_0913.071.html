REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Foreign Policy Coherence

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/13/90

A Republican President, George Bush, and a U.S. Congress controlled by Democrats stood together Wednesday night. For those of us who have watched U.S. foreign policy degrade over 25 years into a recent condition of almost reflexive, corrosive partisanship -- from Vietnam through the trial of Oliver North -- Wednesday evening's display of solidarity was extraordinary. In replying to the President's speech, House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt said, "Here at home, a sense of unity and the absence of widespread opposition to this action in the Persian Gulf, testify to our powerful, instinctive feeling that this is a cause worth standing and fighting for." Similar sentiments suffused Mr. Gephardt's speech. It is no doubt possible to explain Mr. Gephardt's remarks in terms of normal political calculation. American troops are in the Gulf and at risk, and it is late in the day to start carping about the President's actions. Congressional elections are two months off. However, it is also possible that those of us who get paid to chronicle the daily mayhem of Washington's picaresque world sometimes are liable to miss the larger turns in the political temper.

Vermont Royster, this paper's former editor and its historical memory, called us from North Carolina yesterday to suggest that just such a turn may have taken place on America's television screens Wednesday night. Mr. Royster said the atmosphere of the evening drew to mind memories of December 8, 1941, the day after Pearl Harbor, when President Roosevelt went before Congress to seek a declaration of war against Japan. The Senate voted it unanimously and the House by 388 to 1, thereby putting to rest the deep political divisions of the previous decade over the question of American isolationism from the world's troubles.

As in our time, with the War Powers Resolution and the Nicaraguan-directed Boland amendments, Congress in the 1930s passed a series of Neutrality Acts to circumscribe the President's freedom to act in foreign affairs. Predictably, these legislative interventions proved to be clumsy and unwieldy instruments. Each made the formation and execution of American policy difficult. Some welcomed that result. But America's allies surely did not, instead seeing these seemingly endless tensions as a formula for uncertain and incoherent U.S. policy.

It of course remains to be seen whether the current entente on foreign policy is lasting. A new defense bill will soon be debated by the Congress, and even in the midst of this historic moment there is no assurance that the Democratic leadership can prevent defense from being irrationally pulled apart by the party's left wing or the usual pork barrelers. Yet there is reason to hope that a workable bipartisan coherence on foreign affairs may yet develop.

The Bush administration's recurring reference to the need to create a new, civilized world order in the wake of the Cold War found its echo in Mr. Gephardt's remarks: "We can see beyond the present shadows of war in the Middle East to a new world order where the strong work together to deter and stop aggression. This was precisely Franklin Roosevelt's and Winston Churchill's vision for peace for the postwar period."

If indeed Republicans and Democrats are ready to turn the page on more than two decades of acrimony in U.S. foreign policy, the world's movement toward Roosevelt's and Churchill's vision, interrupted at its creation for more than 40 years, may commence again. We shall see.
