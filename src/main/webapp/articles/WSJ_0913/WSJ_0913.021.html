International: Japanese Surplus In Trade Increased 3.7% Last Month

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 09/13/90

TOKYO -- Japan's trade surplus for August edged up 3.7%, as another sharp increase in the surplus with Southeast Asia offset continued narrowing of surpluses with the U.S. and European Community. The August figures, however, don't reflect the recent jump in crude-oil prices, the full effect of which isn't likely to show up before October, Finance Ministry officials said.

The Finance Ministry said the overall surplus, measured on a customs-cleared basis, rose to $3.49 billion from $3.37 billion a year earlier.

Exports rose 2.6%, to $22.39 billion from $21.82 billion, representing the third consecutive increase after eight consecutive monthly declines. Imports edged up 2.4%, to $18.9 billion from $18.46 billion, led by a 23% jump in imports from the EC.

Adjusted for seasonal factors, the August surplus was $4.74 billion, little changed from the $4.76 billion surplus in July.

While some economists had been predicting a renewed widening of Japan's surplus in the second half of this year, the recent surge in oil prices makes it increasingly unlikely that any sharp increase will materialize.

The rise in spot oil prices, to around $30 a barrel from less than $20 a barrel before Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, could eventually add several hundred million dollars a month to Japan's import bill. But the dollar value of oil imports actually slipped 0.5%, to $1.86 billion in August. Finance Ministry officials explained that it normally takes about two months for oil-price increases to show up in import statistics.

Higher oil prices also are likely to sap demand in Japan's major export markets, economists note.

The modest August increase "confirms there will be no major recovery in Japanese exports this year -- and you have the oil whammy" adding to the import bill, said Paul Summerville, senior economist at Jardine Fleming Japan Ltd. Japan's balance of payments surpluses are likely to run below many economists' predictions, he said.

Japan's surplus with the U.S. declined 6% from a year earlier, to $2.83 billion. Imports declined 1.3% to $4.27 billion. But that was outweighed by a 3.2% drop in exports, to $7.1 billion.

A softening U.S. economy contributed to shrinkage in exports of such items as video equipment and telecommunications equipment, Finance Ministry officials said. The main factor in the lower imports was a 71% drop in imports of aircraft, to $91.7 million.

Trade with the rest of Asia continued to boom, however. Japan's surplus with Southeast Asia and South Korea jumped 75%, to $1.57 billion, as exports of autos, video equipment, engines and other machinery remained strong. Imports from the region declined 3.4%, to $5.48 billion. But Mr. Summerville said imports from the region could pick up if higher oil prices eat into local demand. Such a development could cause Southeast Asian producers to redirect output from local markets to Japan, he said.

The surplus with the EC fell 16%, to $1.13 billion. The 23% jump in imports from the EC came as European producers of alcoholic beverages, autos and art works continued to find an increased appetite for their goods among Japanese consumers.
