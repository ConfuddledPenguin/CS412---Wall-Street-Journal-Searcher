OTC Focus: Market Loses Steam on Inflation Fears After Early Rally; Finnigan Jumps 4 1/2

  • Author: Anne Newman and Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 04/25/90

Shares of over-the-counter stocks slipped in the last hour of trading, losing their grip on a morning rally as concerns about rising interest rates and inflation fears continued to haunt investors. The Nasdaq Composite Index fell .89, or 0.2%, to 419.19. Trading remained light at 126.7 million shares, up slightly from 125.9 million Monday. Advancing shares closed the gap with declining stocks but still trailed, 870 to 1,040, while 191 stocks hit new lows and 57 struck new highs.

Traders said technology issues were pressured by a late afternoon news service report that Compaq's first-quarter growth for domestic personal computer sales would be flat. However, analysts at Goldman Sachs and Hambrecht & Quist told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report there was nothing new in the report and they aren't changing their earnings estimates for the computer maker.

As shares of Compaq fell, Apple closed off 1 at 38 3/4, Intel fell 1/2 to 39, Seagate Technology dropped 1 to 11 1/4 and Microsoft fell 1 to 58. The Nasdaq 100 index, which tracks larger stocks and includes many technology shares, fell 1.74 to 409.52.

But most of the day was marked by cautious trading, much of it among market dealers, traders said. E.E. "Buzzy" Geduld, president of Herzog Heine Geduld, said market makers trading among themselves accounted for more business than institutional investors.

In what trading institutions did conduct, "the stocks moved sideways," said Robert O'Toole Sr., head of institutional OTC trading at Shearson Lehman Hutton. "These stocks tried to bounce, but it was a standoff," he added.

William Rothe, head of OTC trading at Alex. Brown & Sons in Baltimore, suggested the market won't rally significantly until fears about higher interest rates and inflation ease. "You're going to have to see something positive happen on that front to get a meaningful rally," Mr. Rothe said. He added that investors continued to selectively buy "bargain stocks."

Finnigan was one of the day's biggest gainers, adding 4 1/2 to 18 3/4. The San Jose, Calif., maker of scientific instruments signed a memorandum of understanding to be acquired by Thermo Instrument Systems for $20 a share. Finnigan also reported a first-quarter loss of 20 cents a share yesterday, compared with year-earlier net income of six cents a share.

Weyenberg Shoe Manufacturing jumped 3 1/2 to 59 after reporting first-quarter profit of $1.33 a share, up from $1.23 a year before. The company, based in Milwaukee, also declared a two-for-one stock split and increased the dividend on a post-split basis to 15 cents a share from 12.5 cents. Shareholders approved changing the company's name to Weyco Group Inc.

Peerless Manufacturing surged 3 to 35 on its fiscal third-quarter earnings report. The Dallas maker of oil and gas control equipment earned $1.51 a share in the quarter ended March 31, up from six cents a year earlier.

Image Bank fell 2 3/4 to 5 1/4 after restating its first-quarter net income sharply downward. In February, the photo agency said it had earned 12 cents a share in the quarter ended Dec. 31, but yesterday restated that figure as seven cents a share. The company said the earlier earnings report failed to account for certain purchase invoices for books that were included in the Dec. 31 inventory of its recently acquired British unit, Book People Ltd.

The American depositary receipts of AB Volvo closed unchanged at 55 3/4. Pharmacia AB said its board unanimously recommended that its shareholders accept tender offers by Volvo and Procordia AB.

Poughkeepsie Savings Bank was a loser among bank stocks. It fell 1 to 5 1/8 after suspending its quarterly dividend and reported a $2.21-a-share loss for the first quarter, compared to net income of 53 cents a share a year earlier. The bank made $8 million in loan-loss provisions in the quarter.
