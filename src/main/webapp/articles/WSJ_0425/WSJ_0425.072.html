Two Steel Firms' 1st-Period Profit Fell Sharply

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 04/25/90

The nation's two biggest steelmakers, hurt by reduced volume and pricing, posted sharply lower first-quarter earnings. USX Corp. profits slid 41% to $176 million, or 66 cents a share, from $296 million, or $1.09 a share, a year ago. However, last year's results included $179 million in one-time special items as well as a pretax gain of $74 million from the sale of assets.

At Bethlehem Steel Corp., the nation's No. 2 steelmaker, earnings plunged 68% to $21 million, or 20 cents a share, from $65 million, or 78 cents a share, a year ago. On an operating basis, the fall was sharper because last year's first quarter included a $55 million restructuring charge.

Still, some industry analysts were pleasantly surprised at the earnings performance of both USX and Bethlehem and believe a recent uptick in orders portends second-quarter improvements.

"I'm surprised at how well they did," said John Jacobson, an analyst with AUS Consultants. "Five or six years ago, steelmakers' ability to adapt to lower pricing and somewhat weaker demand wasn't nearly as good."

A year ago, the industry was enjoying relatively strong demand and firm pricing. But business began dropping off in the second half of 1989 as auto makers cut back on production and world steel prices fell. Those trends continued into the first quarter. Recently, though, business appears to have strengthened.

In the recent quarter, USX's steel segment reported a 39% drop in operating profit to $60 million from $99 million a year ago. Sales dropped to $1.3 billion from $1.5 billion. Both drops reflect lower steel prices and the sale of the company's Lorain, Ohio, works to a joint venture with Kobe Steel of Japan last year.

USX earned about $23 a ton of steel shipped, higher than most analysts' projections of $15 to $18 a ton. Analyst John Tumazos of Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities believes stronger orders in the last part of the quarter boosted profit.

USX's performance was aided by Marathon Oil, which benefited from higher crude oil prices. Marathon's operating profit rose to $242 million from $241 million; the year-earlier quarter included a $60 million gain related to inventory valuation.

Texas Oil & Gas, which USX put on the block last year, continued its disappointing performance. It reported a loss of $5 million, compared with operating profit of $147 million a year ago. Last year's quarterly results at the natural gas unit included a $144 million gain from the settlement of lawsuits.

Bethlehem's biggest problem, meanwhile, was in its basic steel operations, where operating profit plummeted 79% to $29 million from $137 million a year ago. A key factor in the segment's disappointing showing was a 19% drop in steel shipments and lower operating rates. Bethlehem's operating rates fell to 70% from 82%, giving it fewer tons to absorb fixed-interest and labor costs.

At the same time, Bethlehem's operating costs were higher because of a labor pact signed last year with United Steelworkers of America, as well as outages at its blast furnaces.

Losses at Bethlehem's steel-related operations narrowed markedly to $4 million from $63 million a year ago, when the company took a $55 million charge to shrink its Baltimore Marine division.

Bethlehem said that as a result of recent order improvements, the industry may ship more than the originally expected 77 million tons this year. That would be lower, however, than the 84 million tons shipped last year.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, USX shares closed at $32.75, down 25 cents, and Bethlehem closed unchanged at $17.625.
