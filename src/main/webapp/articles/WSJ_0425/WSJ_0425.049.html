Economy: U.S. Car, Truck Sales Dropped 17.7% In Mid-April as Big Three Fared Poorly

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 04/25/90

DETROIT -- Sales of domestically built cars and trucks plunged 17.7% in mid-April as auto makers posted another disappointing performance during what should be the heart of the spring selling season. "There's only one word to describe it: tough," said Clay Lowenfield, general sales manager of a Ford and a Nissan dealership in El Paso, Texas. Both dealerships saw sales drop about 25% from year-earlier levels, he said.

Overall, the 10 companies that build vehicles in the U.S. sold 306,637 cars, trucks and vans in the April 11-20 period, down from 372,419 a year earlier. Sales of domestically made cars fell 19.1% from year-earlier levels, pulling the seasonally adjusted annual selling pace for the mid-April period down to 6.6 million cars from a robust 7.9 million selling rate in mid-April of 1989.

The Big Three U.S. auto makers all suffered sharp declines in car and truck sales. The worst numbers came from Chrysler Corp., which had a 38.8% drop in total vehicle sales. General Motors Corp.'s total car and truck sales fell 15.5% during the latest 10 days, while Ford Motor Co.'s car and light truck sales skidded 19.3%.

Among Japanese car makers, however, all but one boosted sales of domestically built vehicles over year-earlier levels, although that was in part because they are building more cars in the U.S. than a year earlier. The exception was Mazda Motor Corp., which saw sales of its two domestically built vehicles slump 55.7% after consumer incentives expired April 10.

Chrysler has been struggling since the end of January, when it halted a first-time $1,000 rebate offer on its best-selling minivans. That offer sparked record January sales for the auto maker. But Chrysler has suffered double-digit sales declines in almost every 10-day sales period since then. For mid-April, the No. 3 U.S. auto maker's domestic truck and van sales fell 33.9% from a year earlier. That compares with a 15.1% decline in overall domestic truck sales during the mid-April period. Sales of domestically made Chrysler cars tumbled 38.4% in the period.

To stop the slide, Chrysler has reintroduced the sort of cut-rate financing offers that helped to boost sales a year ago. Chrysler announced Saturday that it will offer interest-free loans for as much as two years on many of its vehicles.

"The last time that happened, we had people standing in line to buy cars and trucks," said Donald Warnock, who owns a Chrysler and a Dodge dealership in New Jersey.

Chrysler stock was one of the most active in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, with almost 1.1 million shares changing hands. It closed at $15.875, unchanged.

At Ford Motor, sales of domestically built cars were down 22.7%, while sales of domestic trucks fell 13.1%. Ford dealers said they expect the new Escort subcompact, which goes on sale this week, to help the No. 2 auto maker bolster its market share. "I'm looking for that to give us a real shot in the arm," said Fred Ricart, owner of Ricart Ford in Columbus, Ohio.

General Motors, meanwhile, outperformed Ford and the overall market. The No. 1 auto maker's domestic car sales dropped 18.9%, less than the overall drop in domestic car sales. GM's domestic truck sales fell 9.6%, but that was considerably better than the performance of the total domestic truck market.

The major Japanese car makers, however, did much better. Toyota Motor Corp., which has been boosting its U.S. output, posted a 55.3% increase in sales of its domestically produced cars. Nissan Motor Corp. boosted sales of its U.S.-built cars and compact trucks 31.5% in the period.

Honda Motor Co. posted a 6.7% gain in sales of its domestic car models. Sales of Mitsubishi Motor Corp.'s U.S.-made cars rose 63.8%. A year earlier, Mitsubishi was still boosting production at the Illinois factory it operates in a joint venture with Chrysler.
