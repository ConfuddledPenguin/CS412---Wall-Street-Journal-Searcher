Hunt for Black Gold At Green Canyon Ends in Red Ink --- `Disappointing' Production Blamed as Placid Oil Co. Abandons Drilling Project

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 04/25/90

DALLAS -- Blaming "disappointing" production results, the Hunt brothers' Placid Oil Co. said it was abandoning the risky Green Canyon drilling project that the fallen oil tycoons gambled would be their return ticket to prosperity. Located in the deep waters of the Gulf of Mexico, the controversial $340 million project involved using an experimental floating rig to drill to unprecedented depths in search of what brothers Nelson Bunker Hunt, William Herbert Hunt and Lamar Hunt contended was a 70-million barrel oil reserve.

According to a report in the Oil and Gas Journal, one of Placid's wells at Green Canyon -- drilled in 2,243 feet of water -- set a world water depth record for production. But equipment problems plagued the project from the start and the gusher anticipated by the high-rolling Hunts never appeared.

While a major blow to the Hunts' efforts to rebuild at least part of their oil empire, Placid officials said Green Canyon's failure did not mark the end of the company's exploration efforts. "We're involved in looking for oil in a lot of other places," said P.G. Clark, Placid's vice president of operations. "We're just not going in deep water."

Mr. Clark declined to discuss details regarding the Green Canyon project's production problems. But last November Dallas-based Enserch Corp., which owned a 25% working interest in the project, reported that the only two producing wells at the site were yielding a meager 200 barrels of oil and eight million cubic feet of gas per day.

"It apparently didn't get any better," said Prudential-Bache analyst Tom Driscoll.

"I think you could reuse a whole lot of the equipment at another location so long as you actually did find commercially producable quantities," said Tom Marsh, drilling editor for Houston-based Offshore Data Services Inc.

Mr. Marsh noted, however, that the hugely expensive Green Canyon project was conceived in the days when oil was selling for $30 a barrel.

Green Canyon's failure marks the end of another controversial chapter in the brothers' decade-long financial decline and comes just one month after the Hunts lost their remaining interest in Penrod Drilling Co. and its prized fleet of off-shore drilling rigs.

Like Placid, the family's exploration arm, Penrod had been owned by the trust estates of Hunt brothers.

Despite widespread industry skepticism about its prospects, the Hunts wagered that the Green Canyon project would help them dig out from under the $2 billion mountain of debt resulting from their disasterous speculation in the 1979-80 silver market.
