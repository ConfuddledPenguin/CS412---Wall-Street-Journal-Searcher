Technology: EPA Considers Proposals To Recycle, Reduce CFCs

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/25/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency is considering a range of recycling proposals designed to reduce emissions of air conditioning coolants and other chemicals that damage the stratospheric ozone layer. These chemicals -- chlorofluorocarbons, or CFCs -- are frequently released when auto and commercial air conditioning and refrigeration units are serviced. The EPA proposals, which include mandatory and voluntary recycling programs, are designed to ensure that the chemicals are reused, rather than released into the atmosphere.

The ozone layer helps shield the Earth from harmful ultraviolet radiation, which causes skin cancer.

President Bush has committed the U.S. to phase out the production and importation of CFCs by the year 2000, under an international treaty process. An EPA official explained that recycling could cut $2 billion from industry's estimated $4.7 billion cost of phasing out CFCs and halons, another ozone-depleting chemical, by 2000.

A notice inviting public comment on the proposals will appear in the Federal Register soon.

Initially, the program would focus on the motor-vehicle air conditioning industry, which accounts for 21% of CFC use in the U.S. The agency estimates that 63% of CFC use in the U.S. occurs in industries where recycling is feasible.
