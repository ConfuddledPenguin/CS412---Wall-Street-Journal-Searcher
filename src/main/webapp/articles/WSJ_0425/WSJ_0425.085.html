Politics & Policy: Bush Declines To Take Steps Against Soviets --- President Hopes Lithuania Can Start Negotiating, Cites Allies' Reluctance

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 04/25/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush said that for now he won't impose sanctions against the Soviet Union for its crackdown on Lithuania, a decision reflecting both his continued hopes for negotiations and allies' unwillingness to support sanctions. After days of debate within the administration about whether to cancel talks with the Soviets on economic issues, Mr. Bush said he still wants to avoid steps that might provoke a Kremlin backlash and scuttle chances for a negotiated end to the Lithuanian standoff. Mr. Bush noted that a Lithuanian delegation is visiting Moscow, and he said he didn't want to do anything that might reduce its chances of persuading the Kremlin to open talks.

He also indicated his worry that imposing sanctions now might imperil progress already made in pulling Eastern Europe out of the Soviet grip. "I'm concerned that we not inadvertently do something that compels the Soviet Union to take action that would set back the whole case of freedom around the world," Mr. Bush told reporters.

The president's decision means negotiations with the Soviets in Paris on a broad new trade treaty, as well as talks on a civil aviation agreement, will proceed as scheduled this week, the White House said.

Though the overriding reason for Mr. Bush's decision appears to be his fear of undermining negotiations over Lithuania, a widespread distaste for sanctions among U.S. allies was a factor as well. Mr. Bush himself has been hoping all along to avoid imposing sanctions for fear that might spoil the climate for closing big arms-reduction deals with the Soviets. European allies apparently have reinforced that feeling by voicing similar concerns in consultations with the White House over the last few days.

"Our allies aren't prepared to join in on any sanctions, and under those circumstances I am not sure that I would recommend unilateral sanctions," said Rep. Dante Fascell (D., Fla.), chairman of the House Foreign Affairs Committee, after a meeting with Mr. Bush yesterday.

Some allies are worried that the problems facing Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev at the moment are so acute that U.S. sanctions might be a serious blow to his regime, a senior administration aide said. U.S. analysts don't think Mr. Gorbachev is in such dire straits, the official said, but agree that sanctions might have an especially substantial impact on him right now.

Mr. Bush and his aides insist that the administration could reverse its position at any time and choose to impose sanctions. In particular, the senior official said, they would reconsider sanctions if the Kremlin continues to refuse to open negotiations or further tightens an economic blockade under which the Soviets already have cut oil, gas and other supplies to Lithuania.

Mr. Bush's inclination to avoid acting immediately was reinforced by a meeting he held yesterday with a delegation of leading congressional Democrats and Republicans, officials said. Though some congressional conservatives have been complaining about Mr. Bush's failure to lash out at the Soviet Union, both administration aides and lawmakers said after the meeting that the lawmakers offered general support for Mr. Bush's cautious approach.

Rep. Fascell, for instance, repeated a suggestion he's made before that Mr. Bush cancel a summit meeting schedule with Mr. Gorbachev for May 30 to June 4. And Sen. Jesse Helms (R., N.C.) urged Mr. Bush to send a U.S. ambassador to Lithuania in recognition of the breakaway republic's declaration of independence.

Overall, though, there was a "general feeling of support" for Mr. Bush, said Sen. Claiborne Pell (D., R.I.), chairman of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee.

Still, some signs of brewing unrest in Congress are emerging. Sen. Robert Byrd (D., W.Va.), chairman of the powerful Appropriations Committee, declared Sunday in a Senate speech that he opposes "extending any new economic benefits to the Soviets so long as they continue to frustrate the legitimate status of Lithuania as an independent nation."

In addition to the talks on trade and civil aviation agreements, the U.S. also has pledged to support observer status for the Soviet Union at the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, the West's primary trade-regulation body. Officials said withdrawing that support also could be a possible sanction.
