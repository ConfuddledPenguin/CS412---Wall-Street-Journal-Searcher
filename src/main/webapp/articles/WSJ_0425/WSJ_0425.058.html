Technology: Low-Priced IBM Home Computer To Be Introduced in Early Summer --- Move Will Mark New Effort To Succeed in Market Where Firm Has Lagged

  • Author: Laurence Hooper
  • Date: 04/25/90

International Business Machines Corp. will introduce several models of a low-cost home computer early this summer, signaling a splashy new assault on a market in which it has failed in the past, industry executives said. The executives said the new machines will avoid most of IBM's previous missteps: They will run popular software and will be reasonably powerful, easy to use and priced aggressively -- between $1,000 and $2,000.

Still, going after the home market is a gamble for the Armonk, N.Y.-based computer giant. As IBM discovered to its embarrassment in 1984 and 1985, when its PCjr home computer was met with widespread ridicule, home users are a demanding bunch. They're also very price-sensitive, making the formula for a success particularly hard to find.

IBM is taking another gamble, as well, the executives added. In an expansion of a trial program, IBM will sell the computers through high-profile department stores such as R.H. Macy & Co. and Sears, Roebuck & Co., they said. That could well help reach customers who don't regularly visit computer stores. At the same time, IBM needs to be careful not to alienate its existing network of dealers, whose good relations with IBM over the past year and a half have contributed to IBM's gains in the personal-computer market.

An IBM spokeswoman declined to comment on the reports, saying the company doesn't talk about products before they are announced. But IBM executives have hinted around such a move for months, and vice president James Cannavino issued a statement yesterday that emphasized IBM's current presence in the marketplace and said IBM is "working hard to satisfy (home computing) needs with highly competitive products."

While IBM has had some success with computers on the low end of its Personal System/2 line, the home market is dominated by makers of IBM "clones" such as Packard Bell Inc. and Epson America Inc., and by home-computer specialist Commodore International Ltd.

The size of the market is hard to quantify, because any personal computer can be used at home. International Data Corp., a market research firm in Framingham, Mass., estimates that U.S. sales of "homehobby" computers -- a definition that excludes machines used mostly for business -- will reach $975 million, or 1.1 million units, in 1990. That's a very small slice of the total $28 billion, 9.8 million-unit market for personal computers in the U.S.

Slicing the data another way, Personal Technology Research of Waltham, Mass., says $3.8 billion of computers, or 5.5 million units, will be sold during 1990 through electronics stores that don't specialize in computers. That's how most people buy home computers, whether they're for personal or business use, says PTR general manager Casey Dworkin.

In any case, analysts said, several trends in the market are evident. Machines are getting more powerful, and the ability to run heavy-duty business applications is key.

"We aren't looking at the toy most people were buying five or six years ago," said Mr. Dworkin. "People want to be able to bring work home." A $1,000 entry-level price also appears to be important, and ease-of-use has emerged as a major selling point.

IBM's new offerings will apparently address all these points. But company executives have indicated that they don't just want to grab a share of an existing marketplace: They want to create a whole new class of customers.

"New users -- people who've never seen computer outside of a video arcade -- make up most of our potential market," Mr. Cannavino, the IBM vice president, told analysts recently.

Mr. Cannavino, who heads IBM's personal systems business, is shaping up as a cheerleader for home computers. He has told many people that he sees the nation's 90 million households as a tempting target, and talks of "a computer in every kitchen."

This vision -- the computer as household appliance -- means that IBM will likely have to market its home computers a lot differently than its other personal computers, which are sold mostly through authorized computer stores. Industry executives said the company is lining up department stores that will sell the machines in much the same way they sell televisions or microwave ovens.

The executives said the new machines will be based on Intel Corp.'s 80286 microprocessor, which also serves as the heart of IBM's low-end Model 25 and 30 personal computers. They will also include a 30-megabyte hard-disk drive, a 3 1/2-inch floppy-disk drive, 640 kilobytes of internal memory, a mouse, and easy-to-use software that will allow users to start working as soon as the computer is turned on. Some models will offer an internal modem, allowing the computer to communicate across telephone lines.
