Law: Man Accused of Bid To Snarl Operation Of IRS Is Indicted

  • Author: Scott R. Schmedel
  • Date: 04/25/90

A federal grand jury in Fargo, N.D., has indicted a man accused of originating a scheme to botch up the Internal Revenue Service by filing false reports of income paid to others. The indictment issued last week in Fargo charges that Roger N. Elvick, 53 years old, of Moorhead, Minn., "concocted and promoted" the scheme to impede the IRS and harass other persons and conspired with other defendants to market the scheme by selling written material and audio and video tapes.

The Wall Street Journal reported in a front-page article last week that the IRS was pursuing 40 criminal investigations into such subtly disruptive activities in unidentified locales around the country.

In the only previous indictment, six New Mexico tax protesters were charged by a federal grand jury in Albuquerque in February with filing 49 fraudulent Form 1099s naming public officials and others as the recipients of millions of dollars of nonwage income.

A taxpayer's failure to declare income reported by third parties to the IRS on 1099s may lead to an audit. One apparent purpose of the falsified forms is to provoke needless audits that could be costly and time-consuming for the targets and for the IRS.

Assistant U.S. Attorney Gary Annear in Fargo said the IRS has uncovered over 2,600 phony 1099s filed by 193 people in 27 states purporting to have made payments totaling more than $11.1 billion. Mr. Annear says his office believes it can prove that Mr. Elvick originated the scheme.

Mr. Elvick was in custody in Austin, Texas, on other federal charges before the Fargo indictment, Mr. Annear adds.

IRS investigators found videocassettes explaining the scheme in the Farmington, N.M., office of two of the defendants under arrest in New Mexico, but the investigators haven't disclosed the source of those cassettes.

In Washington, the IRS director of public affairs, Ellen Murphy, said the indictment is "an important case." But while the 40 investigations around the country have "a common theme" involving fraudulent 1099s, she adds, the IRS so far doesn't have information that the incidents are otherwise related to each other.

Besides Mr. Elvick, the Fargo grand jury indicted Ronald W. Knutt, 47, of Rocklake, N.D.; Thomas D. Porter, 49, of Cando, N.D., and Natalie C. Telemaque, 33, of Glendale, Ariz. The 19 counts include conspiracy to defraud the U.S. and filing false 1099s and tax returns with the IRS.

The indictment alleges that the four had other purposes besides harassing the IRS and "any person whom defendants felt had wronged them." It charges that phony 1099s were one link in a chain of arcane devices meant to culminate in fraudulent tax refunds and other payments to the defendants by the IRS.

Three defendants even applied to the IRS for rewards for tipping it off to alleged tax violators, the indictment says. And, it continues, one possessed forged documents purporting to be drafts payable through IRS Commissioner Fred T. Goldberg Jr. to enable people to collect from the IRS.
