RJR Reports 1st-Period Loss Of $222 Million --- But Firm's Operating Profit And Cash Flow Gained As Cigarette Sales Grew

  • Author: Peter Waldman
  • Date: 04/25/90

RJR Nabisco Inc. reported a loss of $222 million for the first quarter because of massive debt expenses, but the food and tobacco company showed strong gains in operating profit and cash flow. RJR, taken private last year in a $25 billion leveraged buy-out by Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., reported brisk growth in cigarette revenue, mostly because of price increases and cuts in overhead costs. Most importantly, RJR's debt coverage -- its cash flow as a percentage of total interest obligations -- reached 1.14 in the quarter, up from 1.04 three months earlier.

"That's pretty good for a first-year LBO of that size," said Sam DeRosa-Farag, a bond analyst with First Boston Corp. "That's the beauty" of taking a tobacco company private through an LBO: "They're cash cows."

RJR revenue jumped 9.5% to $3.2 billion from $2.93 billion. The growth was paced by a 15% increase in tobacco revenue, to $1.86 billion from $1.62 billion. Revenue from food grew 3% to $1.34 billion from $1.31 billion, which includes several units that have been divested in the past year.

Louis V. Gerstner Jr., RJR's chairman and chief executive officer, said the New York company's results benefited from RJR's decision last year to end the practice called trade loading, whereby the company built up huge excess inventories among its cigarette distributors. In past years, RJR's heavy fourth-quarter trade loads had the effect of artificially depressing first-quarter tobacco shipments by swamping distributors shelves, thus making shipments in this year's first quarter seem unusually strong by comparison.

"Nevertheless," said Mr. Gerstner, "these results also reflect strong fundamentals, and there is a growing sense of momentum in the company."

That momentum may have been reflected lately in the steadily rising price of RJR's publicly traded bonds. RJR's two most widely held debentures, the 13.71% pay-in-kind debt due 2009 and the pay-in-kind debt due 2007, have rallied several points in the past month, on the strength of analysts' high expectations for the company's operations and persistent rumors that RJR may soon buy the bonds back, or offer some other type of exchange for the debt.

Covenants require RJR to reset the bonds' interest rates by a year from now so they trade at par, a requirement that many analysts agree would be prohibitively expensive at current prices.

Yesterday, the 13.71% pay-in-kind debentures due 2009 rose 5/8, or $6.25 for a bond with $1,000 face amount, to close at 81 5/8, on the New York Stock Exchange, while RJR's pay-in-kind debentures due 2007 rose 3/4 to 68 1/8.

"They've still got a long way to go," said Alan Snyder of San Francisco-based Snyder Capital Management, an investment-management firm that holds some RJR bonds. The bond prices "are still about 30% or so from where they've got to be to reset" interest rates.

Karl von der Heyden, RJR's chief financial officer, wouldn't comment on what RJR may do to address the reset requirement, except to say, "We're always looking at all the options."

RJR said operating profit in the first quarter jumped 50% to $602 million from $402 million. The company's operating profit from tobacco rose 40% to $637 million from $453 million. Operating profit from food rose 18% to $141 million from $119 million.

Much of RJR's first-quarter growth in operating profit came from slashing costs, analysts and RJR executives said. In the past year, for example, the company has cut its headquarters work force by more than half, said Mr. von der Heyden, to fewer than 300 employees from more than 650. In the first quarter, the cost of maintaining the head office fell to $24 million from $46 million a year earlier.

Meanwhile, Mr. von der Heyden said RJR's expenditures on advertising and marketing rose more than 10% in the period to more than $700 million. He added, however, that U.S. consumption of RJR cigarettes fell 3% in the quarter, continuing a worrisome trend for the No. 2 U.S. cigarette maker.
