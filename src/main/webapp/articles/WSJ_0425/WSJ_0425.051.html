Credit Ratings: CrossLand Savings Ratings on Debt Lowered by S&P

  • Author: Meg Cox
  • Date: 04/25/90

NEW YORK -- Standard & Poor's Corp. lowered the ratings on subordinated debt of CrossLand Savings and said that if the savings bank continues to suffer from a capital crunch, a further downgrade is possible. The ratings agency said the downgrade reflects "CrossLand's deteriorating asset quality, accentuated by a large loss in the first quarter ended March 31, as well as its ongoing capital deficiency." On Friday, CrossLand reported a first-quarter loss of $136.5 million, reflecting a $100 million provision for possible losses on problem loans.

Brooklyn-based CrossLand, the largest savings bank in New York and one of the largest in the country, has suffered from weakness in the regional real estate market. The bank's nonperforming assets have been soaring just as new, strict capital requirements were instituted in thrift-reform legislation passed by Congress.

CrossLand has been ordered to bolster its low tangible capital base, but a recapitalization plan collapsed last month when the Pritzker family of Chicago decided against an investment.

As an alternative, CrossLand recently filed a proposal with the Office of Thrift Supervision to convert preferred stock, which isn't counted as core capital because of its cumulative feature, into common stock. But shareholders have to approve the plan, and even if they do, said S&P, "it will leave the company shy of the risk-adjusted capital minimum."

Maurice Reissman, chairman, president and chief executive officer of CrossLand, said S&P is wrong in its assertion that the stock reclassification plan won't cover its capital requirement. "The total risk-adjusted capital requirement is for $621.8 million," said Mr. Reissman, "And if the reclassification is successful, we'll have $752.3 million."

In its statement, S&P added, "Partially mitigating the concerns is CrossLand's continued strong market position."

The agency downgraded CrossLand's subordinated debt to triple-C-plus from single-B, and its certificates of deposit to single-B/single-B from double-B-minus/single-B. These ratings were placed on CreditWatch in November. S&P said about $170 million of subordinated debt is affected, but it affirmed its single-C rating on CrossLand's $363 million of preferred stock.

Separately, the crowded CrossLand annual meeting lasted nearly two hours, as a variety of shareholders criticized the company for its judgment and performance.

In remarks about the future of the company, Mr. Reissman reiterated statements in the annual report about cutting back on construction loans, emphasizing residential real estate loans, and exploring the sale of branch operations in several states.

Mr. Reissman didn't offer new proposals for raising capital. He said he couldn't answer questions about how seriously the bank will be hurt if the stock reclassification doesn't get approved by the preferred and common shareholders or what alternatives the company has.
