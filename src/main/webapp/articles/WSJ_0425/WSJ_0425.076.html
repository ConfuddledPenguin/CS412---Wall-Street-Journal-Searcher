Ames Stores' Securities Slump as Firm Hires Skadden Arps to Explore Options

  • Author: Joseph Pereira
  • Date: 04/25/90

Ames Department Stores Inc.'s stock and bonds tumbled further yesterday, amid growing speculation that the troubled discount department store chain could soon seek shelter from its creditors under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code. Speculation was fueled by a report that the cash-strapped chain has hired the New York law firm of Skadden Arps Slate Meagher & Flom to prepare papers for a possible Chapter 11 filing.

A Skadden Arps spokesman confirmed the firm had been retained by Ames "to explore various restructuring alternatives." But he declined to say specifically if Ames was planning to file for bankruptcy-law protection. The hiring of Skadden Arps was initially reported in the Hartford (Conn.) Courant.

Ames shares closed at $1.375, down 62.5 cents, in heavy composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. The company's 7 1/2% subordinated convertible debentures, which had been trading over $50 for each $100 face value as recently as mid-March, fell to $10.75 from $14.25.

Ames officials didn't return phone calls seeking comment.

The future of Ames, based in Rocky Hill, Conn., has been in question since the company reported earlier this month that it expects to report a $228 million loss for the year ended Jan. 27 because of problems with the Zayre discount chain it acquired two years ago. The company said it plans to close 74 of its 680 stores because of the problems, and is negotiating with its banks for a new credit agreement.

Whether or not Ames decides to make a bankruptcy-court filing, hiring a bankruptcy specialist could help current negotiations with its bankers. "It's a way of saying, `We're going to pull the trigger unless you, the lenders, go along with us,'" said Howard Davidowitz, a retail and financial consultant in New York.

He added: "The big question stalling those negotiations is who gets the money from the disposition of those {74} stores. Naturally the banks want it," he added, noting that Ames already has violated certain conditions set by its lenders.

Last week, Ames announced that it had been granted one waiver and that it was requesting a further waiver from Citibank, the lead bank in a group of approximately 20 institutions.

Because of its financial problems, Ames hasn't paid a number of retailers for recent shipments of merchandise, retailers say. Unable to reach the company by telephone, several retailers have visited officials in person at the company headquarters. But they were told by the company that the banks have ordered a hold of payment to vendors.

Some, however, don't believe that Ames is about to throw in the towel. "I doubt if they're going to declare bankruptcy," noted Kurt Barnard, a New York publisher of a retail newsletter. "Over half the stores, the Ames stores, are doing well," he said. "Before filing {for bankruptcy protection} they could just get rid of the Zayre chain altogether."
