GM Likely to Move Camaro and Firebird Production to Canada

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 04/25/90

DETROIT -- General Motors Corp. is expected to announce today that it will move production of its Chevrolet Camaro and Pontiac Firebird muscle cars to a Canadian plant from one in Southern California. But GM and United Auto Workers union officials said yesterday there is still a chance that the 43-year-old Van Nuys, Calif., factory that currently builds the cars will not join the seven other GM assembly plants that have halted production or been targeted for shutdown since 1988.

GM scheduled a news conference at its Ste. Therese, Quebec, plant for this afternoon. A GM spokesman in Canada wouldn't elaborate on the reasons for the news conference. Officials of the Canadian Auto Workers union said they're expecting the official announcement that the Canadian factory will build the next generation of the Camaro-Firebird, beginning in 1992.

The Ste. Therese plant, which currently builds a line of midsized cars, recently got a new paint shop with the help of 220 million Canadian dollars (US$189.5 million) in interest-free Canadian government financing. Union officials there also have agreed to company demands for more flexible work rules.

GM and United Auto Workers union officials insisted yesterday the Van Nuys plant may survive, despite its age, its distance from GM's major parts operations and the poor quality marks accorded its current products. Company and union officials say they are working on plans to make the plant into a flexible factory capable of building several different models for the West Coast market.

"We're very optimistic that we'll be able to convert Van Nuys" into a flexible manufacturing plant, a GM spokeswoman said yesterday. "We don't expect it to close," added Henry Gonzalez, assistant director for the UAW region that covers California.

Moreover, by moving the Van Nuys plant's cars to Canada, GM could trigger provisions of the current UAW-GM agreement that prohibit layoffs except as a result of a sales downturn, UAW officials said. Under those provisions, GM would have to keep the 3,000 Van Nuys workers on the payroll even if they had no production work to do.
