Corporate Focus: Quaker Oats to Spin Off Fisher-Price Unit --- Move Allows Food Firm To Focus on Core Business

  • Author: Richard Gibson
  • Date: 04/25/90

CHICAGO -- Quaker Oats Co. decided to spin off its troubled Fisher-Price toy business to shareholders, allowing management to concentrate on other difficulties at what, for the first time in two decades, is a "pure" food company. The big packaged foods company boasts some sterling brand names and strong market positions. Overseas business is booming. But in recent months it has struggled with sluggish sales in its U.S. and Canadian grocery products businesses, particularly with pet foods and snacks. Meanwhile, competition across the table is becoming increasingly intense; soft-drink giants Coca-Cola and PepsiCo are organizing attacks on Quaker's biggest-selling product, Gatorade.

Thus, the decision to spin off Fisher-Price seems, as Quaker's oatmeal spokesman Wilford Brimley puts it, "the right thing to do."

"Hallelujah]" exclaimed Prudential-Bache Securities food analyst John McMillin, long an advocate of the spinoff.

Other analysts also applauded. "Management was spending a lot of time on Fisher-Price, so just being able to focus on their basic businesses has got to be a plus," said Roger W. Spencer of PaineWebber Inc.

Quaker stock initially jumped on the news, rising more than a dollar before settling at $49.25 a share, up 62.5 cents in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday. Quaker's 52-week high was $68.875.

If regulators approve the spinoff -- now planned for early next year -- Fisher-Price would become an independent, free-standing company ranking among the top six U.S. toy makers. In its most recent fiscal year, Fisher-Price reported sales of $844.8 million and $97 million in operating earnings. But after a disastrous Christmas season, the company will end this year substantially in the red. William D. Smithburg, chairman and chief executive of Quaker Oats, estimated Fisher-Price's total losses for the year could reach "somewhere around $20 million to $30 million."

Under the planned spinoff, Quaker shareholders would receive stock in the new company, but the number of shares won't be determined for some time, a spokesman said. That will depend on the number of Quaker shares outstanding and the value of the toy company. Quaker now has 75.5 million shares outstanding, but has been aggressively repurchasing its stock of late.

Quaker said it will transfer an unspecified amount of its debt to the new entity, but "we intend to structure the debt so Fisher-Price is in a position of strength and able to be self-sufficient," a spokesman said.

In the latest nine months, the toy division had sales of about $566 million, but inventory write-offs and trade markdowns following the Christmas fiasco, plus a subsequent plant closing, led to an operating loss of about $34.8 million for the period. Based in East Aurora, N.Y., Fisher-Price has about 7,400 employees world-wide and represents about 17% of its parent's identifiable assets.

Quaker had built Fisher-Price from a $30 million business in 1969 and profited handsomely from toys that became household staples for preschoolers. But last year's overly ambitious product line, which not only couldn't be delivered to stores in time but failed to wow consumers, left the parent company bloodied and angry. Fisher-Price's former president departed in January, to be temporarily replaced by executives coincidentally named Fisher and Price.

Paul E. Price, senior vice president of finance at the parent company, has served as Fisher-Price's temporary president, but will return to Quaker after the spinoff, the company said. It is now searching for a new chief executive officer to head the toymaker when it strikes out on its own.

Shareholders wondering how Fisher-Price will fare independent of Quaker might get some guidance from the past. In a move strikingly similar to Quaker's, in 1985 General Mills Inc. spun off its Kenner and Parker Brothers toy group as an independent concern. The spun-off company subsequently was acquired by Tonka Corp., which earlier this week reported a loss for the latest quarter.

"It is a risky business, totally product driven," said toy industry analyst Carol Palmer of Duff & Phelps, Chicago, commenting on an independent Fisher-Price's chances. "To the extent that Fisher-Price has toothpaste-type products, that makes them a little more stable."

Assuming the spinoff goes as planned, Quaker executives will be able to concentrate on fixing the bread-and-butter businesses. Noting that Fisher-Price's latest "trough" was its third under Quaker's ownership, Mr. Smithburg said that without the toy business, "We can clearly focus better on groceries."

Chief among his concerns is likely to be getting the Gaines dog food house in order. Although Quaker has a strong European franchise, it is fighting to hold a low-margin 20% of the U.S. dog food market -- a performance that Mr. Smithburg acknowledges "for the last few years has been below expectations." But, he added in an interview yesterday, "we think profitability on pet foods has bottomed out, and expect to see increases in profit next year and volume stability on key brands by the summer."

Volume is a tricky issue at Quaker. While overall volume in its U.S. and Canadian grocery products business fell 4% in the latest quarter, the company increasingly is concentrating its marketing dollars on higher-margin items. Despite "intense competition," Mr. Smithburg predicts "a good fourth quarter" in grocery products.

Analysts are circumspect. Nomi Ghez, an analyst at Goldman, Sachs & Co., yesterday cut her estimate for Quaker's 1990 operating earnings to $3.10 from $3.20. "I frankly could see some benefits in keeping the {toy} business and turning it around, since they have gone through most of the pain already," she said. "Now they're left with a lackluster food business that can maybe grow over time at 10% to 12%."

Quaker has been atop many of Wall Street's takeover target lists in recent months, as its stock slumped below food-industry rivals. Prudential-Bache's Mr. McMillin said: "You can argue they're doing a raider's dirty work" by spinning off Fisher-Price, but the move "creates shareholder values, which should boost the stock. Investors have been valuing Fisher-Price at zero."

Mr. McMillin said he recently advised clients that Nestle S.A., the world's biggest food conglomerate, "has always coveted Quaker's businesses and would be the most likely corporate buyer. Unilever and Philip Morris would also be candidates."

But Mr. Smithburg, who lately has stressed his company's desire to remain independent, yesterday sought to minimize such speculation. "I don't lose a lot of sleep over takeovers," he said. "My task is to run the company the best way possible."
