Western Union to Propose Swap Offer To Note Holders to Avoid Chapter 11

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 04/25/90

NEW YORK -- Western Union Corp., trying to avoid a Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law filing, said it will make a proposal to recapitalize with an exchange offer of new secured debt and common stock. The company said it will file "within the next few business days" a registration statement and proxy materials with the Securities and Exchange Commission, providing an outline of the exchange offer.

In March, Western Union told the SEC in its annual filing that it could be forced to seek protection under the U.S. Bankruptcy Code if it doesn't work out a suitable restructuring of its debt or raise sufficient funds through asset sales.

The 139-year-old company has been hit by a steep decline in its telex business amid an explosion in facsimile transmission. And Western is meeting stiff competition from American Express Co. and MCI Communications Corp. in the money-transfer and electronic mail businesses.

In its filing with the SEC, Western Union said it will request clearance from the commission to make the exchange offer to holders of its senior secured reset notes due Dec. 15, 1992, which currently carry an interest payment of 19.25%, as well as to holders of 16% notes due June 15, 1991.

Western is also seeking approval from its stockholders to reclassify its widely distributed preferred shares and the Class B common shares held by Brooke Partners L.P. into an as yet undisclosed number of regular common shares.

Brooke, which controls Western, has been wrestling with the company's senior reset bonds, which currently total $500 million. The terms of these bonds require Western to raise the interest rate on the bonds from 16.5% to a level that would allow them to trade at par. This year the company raised the rate to 19.25%, but the bonds nonetheless are trading at 37 cents on the dollar, or $370 for each $1,000 note. And the interest burden on this senior debt will hit $96 million this year. The total interest expense is $116 million, a Western Union spokesman said.

"The current level of debt payments is not sustainable by the business," he said. Cash flow available to pay off the company's debt in 1989 totaled $62 million, he said.

On April 6 Western met with a group of its senior note holders that had agreed in writing to keep the briefing confidential and not to trade on the company's securities until the proceedings were made public.

At that meeting Western told the holders that the company now operates as two units -- financial services and business services. Financial services include money transfer, a bill payment service (formerly National Payments Network, which Western acquired in 1989), Mailgram and Telegram. Business services include telex, the EasyLink electronic mail service, and Priority Mail service.

Western said the two units together had operating profit of $167 million last year.

Western also disclosed that it had cut annual expenses by more than $100 million in 1989 to $655 million, primarily because of a 50% reduction in its work force to 3,800 employees and reduced lease charges for transmission lines.
