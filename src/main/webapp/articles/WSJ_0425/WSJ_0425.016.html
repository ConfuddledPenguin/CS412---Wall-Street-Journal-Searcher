Yale and Barnard Quit Group as U.S. Probes Student Aid

  • Author: Gary Putka
  • Date: 04/25/90

Yale University and Barnard College dropped out of a college group whose financial-aid setting practices are being investigated by the U.S. Justice Department. The two schools' exit from the "overlap" group came after government antitrust attorneys declined to give explicit legal clearance to a meeting of the group last month. At the meeting, the group compared financial-aid awards to students admitted to more than one of the group's schools. Before Yale and Barnard dropped out, overlap included 23 private Northeastern colleges, including all eight Ivy League schools.

The overlap practice is under government scrutiny as a possible form of illegal price-fixing. In past years' meetings, common admittees' aid awards were adjusted so that students' tuition, net of financial aid, would be about the same no matter which school the student chose. The practice originally was started to keep schools from "bidding" for the best academic talent among students who needed aid.

Just why Yale and Barnard dropped out remains unclear, but their exit is the first tangible sign that colleges under scrutiny have legal doubts about overlap, which they have heretofore defended as a way to best serve students. The government inquiry includes about 55 private schools, and also focuses on tuition and salary-setting practices.

Yale, of New Haven, Conn., confirmed its decision to pull out of overlap and said it remained committed to meeting the full financial needs of students, irrespective of means. It declined to comment further.

Barnard showed up at the overlap meeting at Wellesley College in suburban Boston, according to officials at other schools represented there, but left before substantial discussions about aid took place. Barnard, located in New York city, declined to comment.

Robert Durkee, a spokesman for Princeton University, said Barnard left after a letter from the Justice Department arrived in Wellesley on March 26 while the meeting was in progress. The letter emphasized that the department hadn't given legal clearance for the meeting to take place, although 19 of the schools had asked for such a comfort letter ahead of time.

Mr. Durkee said attorneys from the Justice Department met with several of the schools prior to the meeting, and were noncommittal on the permissibility of the meeting. In his opinion, Mr. Durkee said, neither Yale nor Barnard believes that it has violated any laws but worried they could further complicate their position by participating in the meeting.

Recently, the government's investigators have asked several of the schools, including Princeton, to supply more data on tuition-setting practices. Mr. Durkee said that the new request for information, which arrived this month, gives the schools until May 1 to produce documents and other data on how tuition was set between 1979 and 1985. The government's prior requests covered 1985 to the present.
