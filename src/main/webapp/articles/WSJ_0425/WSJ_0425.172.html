Credit Markets: Bond Prices Stabilize Following Seven-Day Slide But Futures Prices Fall for 13th Consecutive Day

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 04/25/90

NEW YORK -- Following a seven-day losing streak, bond prices were little changed yesterday. But in the futures market, the rout continued with prices of bond futures falling for the 13th consecutive trading session. Traders termed yesterday's bond market as dizzy because prices meandered for much of the day. In early trading, Treasury bonds posted modest declines in reaction to two key economic indicators: the Commerce Department's durable goods report and the Labor Department's employment cost index.

Orders for durable goods, such as factory equipment, cars, appliances and other items intended to last at least three years, jumped 6.7% in March following a 2.3% advance in February. The report provided fresh evidence that some sectors of the manufacturing industry are rebounding.

While that may be good for the economy, the bond market reacts badly to such news because investors fear that a pickup in economic activity could lead to higher inflation and interest rates, two trends that reduce the value and appeal of fixed-income securities. In fact, a sharper-than-expected increase in the employment cost report demonstrated that inflation is accelerating.

The employment cost index for all civilian workers rose 5.5% for the 12 months ended in March, up from a 5% rise in 1989.

Prices of the benchmark 30-year bond fell more than 1/4 point, or $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount, after the reports were released.

But when yields on the benchmark bond hit 9%, an important psychological level, Wall Street dealers and professional traders rushed into the market to buy cheap securities to cover short-sale positions. In a short sale, dealers and professional traders sell borrowed securities and later seek to make a profit by replacing what was borrowed with cheaper securities. Essentially, these dealers are betting that prices will fall.

"It was a crazy day," said Kevin Flanagan, money market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. "Everybody was expecting the market to sell off sharply after the reports and stay that way," he said.

The bond market rally fizzled later in the day due to lack of interest by retail investors. "There was just no follow through by investors and with no retail support, the market could not sustain the gains," said Mr. Flanagan.

In the futures market, traders said the 13-day decline was the longest losing streak in recent memory. And some technicians say the selling spree isn't over yet.

"The trend is still toward higher interest rates," said Roger Froehlich, president of Technical Dimensions, Mount Kisco, N.Y.

John Slosberg, a senior analyst at Technical Data Global Markets Group, warned clients yesterday that the futures market remains "vulnerable to the downside."

Bond futures have fallen more than four points since the first week in April and now are at the lowest levels in nearly a year. June bond futures settled yesterday at a price of 89 4/32, down 1/8 point. Mr. Slosberg said if June bond futures contract prices break below 88 26/32, which corresponds to a 9% yield on the government's benchmark 30-year bond, "you will see frenzied selling," he said.

Junk Bonds

News that former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. junk-bond chief Michael Milken pleaded guilty to six felony counts had little effect on the junk-bond market.

Mr. Milken is widely credited with creating the market a decade ago, but his presence has diminished considerably during the last two years.

Most junk bonds ended unchanged yesterday. However, there were some notable exceptions.

Among the biggest losers was Ames Department Stores Inc. The retailer's junk bonds plunged on reports that it hired the New York law firm of Skadden Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom to prepare papers for a possible Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing. A Skadden Arps spokesman confirmed that it had been retained by Ames "to explore various restructuring alternatives." But the spokesman declined to say specifically if Ames was planning to file for bankruptcy-law protection.

Ames 13% senior subordinated reset notes fell five points in early trading, but rebounded by the afternoon to close one point lower at 21. The company's 7 1/2% subordinated convertible debentures fell three points to about $11.25.

Among winners: The junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. advanced despite news that the food and tobacco giant reported a loss of $222 million for the first quarter.

Analysts said some investors ignored the net loss and instead focused on operating income, which climbed to $602 million from $402 million in the first quarter a year ago.

"I think at minimum the market should be very pleased with the numbers," said Dirk Van Doren, a high-yield analyst at McCarthy Crisanti & Maffei Inc.

RJR's pay-in-kind bonds, which had been up more than a point, finished as much as 3/4 point higher. The 13.71% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, rose 5/8 to 81 5/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. The 13.71% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, ended 3/4 point higher at 68 1/8.

Uniroyal Goodrich Tire Co.'s junk bonds jumped four points on news that the Justice Department's antitrust division will allow Michelin Group's proposed acquisition of Uniroyal to go forward as scheduled.

Uniroyal's 14 1/2% subordinated debentures, maturing in 2000, finished four points higher at 109.

Treasury Securities

Bond prices ended narrowly mixed after a day of seesaw trading activity. The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 95 5/32, slightly higher than 95 4/32 on Monday. The yield was unchanged yield 8.96%.

The latest 10-year notes were slightly higher at 96 27/32 to yield 8.97% compared with 96 25/32 to yield 8.98%.

Short-term rates moved lower yesterday. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills was 7.76% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.00%, while the rate on six-month bills was 7.90% for a bond equivalent yield of 8.32%.

On Monday, the average discount rate of Treasury bills at the government's auction was 7.78% for a bond equivalent yield of 8.05%, while the average auction rate for six-month bills was 7.91% for a bond equivalent yield of 8.35%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Helped by demand for collateral for new derivative issues, mortgage securities fell 1/32 to 2/32 but held up better than the intermediate Treasury market.

The Federal National Mortgage Association announced two real estate mortgage investment conduit issues, or Remics, totaling $550 million, while the Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. offered a $300 million Remic.

With dealers seeking collateral for these deals, Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae securities fared well for the second straight session.

The benchmark Freddie Mac 9 1/2% issue eased only 1/32 at 96 6/32, while the Fannie Mae 9 1/2% issue dropped 1/32 to 96 23/32.

In the Government National Mortgage Association market, the 9% Ginnie Mae issue ended at 93 27/32, down 2/32; the 9 1/2% issue at 96 13/32, down 2/32; the 10% issue at 98 31/32, down 2/32; and the 11% issue at 103 1/4, also down 2/32.

In the Remic market, Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. underwrote a $300 million Fannie Mae deal backed by Fannie Mae's 10% issue, Shearson Lehman Hutton sold a $250 million Fannie Mae issue backed by 15-year 9 1/2% securities, and Morgan Stanley underwrote the $300 million Freddie Mac issue, which is backed by 9% securities.

Municipal Issues

Tax-exempt issues were 1/4 to 3/8 point lower in listless late trading.

"I think it's a general lack of enthusiasm, a general lack of optimism. The market's suffering from some kind of malaise," one municipal trader said.

Traders cited a lack of interest among investors in buying bonds. Some participants may be waiting for municipals to bottom out before they begin bargain hunting, a few traders said.

In one of the largest deals of the session, a Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. group set the preliminary pricing for a $283 million issue of Transmission Agency of Northern California revenue bonds. The bonds were tentatively priced to yield from 6.40% in 1994 to 7.35% in 2024.

In new-issue activity, a Smith Barney Harris Upham & Co. group repriced an issue of $82 million Ohio Water Development Authority revenue bonds. The bonds are now priced to yield from 6.10% in 1991 to 7.35% in 2009 and 2016.

Foreign Bonds

The rebound of the dollar against the yen put a damper on the Japanese government bond market yesterday.

The dollar ended at 158.15 yen, up from 157.55 yen in Tokyo late Monday and 157.55 yen at the Tokyo close Monday. In response, Japanese government bond prices fell 0.26 points to 85.96 to yield 7.375%.

British government bonds ended little changed while West German bonds were modestly lower. Meanwhile, the Canadian subsidiary of International Business Machines Corp. tapped the Eurobond market yesterday. IBM Canada Ltd. issued 150 million Canadian dollars (US$129 million) of five-year notes through lead manager Credit Suisse First Boston. The coupon rate was set at 13 1/2%.
