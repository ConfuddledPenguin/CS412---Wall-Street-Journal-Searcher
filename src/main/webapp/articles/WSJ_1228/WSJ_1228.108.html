Thursday's Markets: Stocks Fall On Signs Of Slump --- But a Sharp Plunge In Orders of Goods Gives Bonds a Lift

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 12/28/90

More evidence that the economy has slipped into recession put a damper on stock prices but nudged bond prices higher. The Dow Jones Industrial Average lost 11.63 points to close at 2625.50 after a session shortened by an electrical fire that delayed the opening of the New York Stock Exchange for 90 minutes.

Bond prices rose in light trading. The dollar was mixed, posting gains against the Japanese yen, but falling against the German mark.

A surprisingly big drop in November durable goods orders set the tone for the day, pushing bond prices higher. The orders plunged 10.5% last month, virtually confirming that the economy is mired in recession. Economists had estimated the order rate would be down a little more than 2.2%. Even excluding the volatile transportation sector, which swings widely from month to month because of huge aircraft orders, the November pace was down 3.5% to the lowest level in two years.

Economists said the dismal news almost certainly means more layoffs in the industrial sector of the economy early next year, adding to economic woes already afflicting large parts of the country. The news makes it more likely that the Federal Reserve will lower short-term interest rates further in hopes of stimulating the rapidly weakening economy.

Some analysts predict the Fed will again cut the federal funds rate -- which banks and other financial institutions charge one another for short-term loans -- as early as next week.

An explosion and fire outside a New York building housing the computer nerve center for the major stock exchanges prevented the New York Stock Exchange from opening until 11 a.m EST. But with trading activity stuck in low gear for the holidays, traders said the delay had little perceptible effect on either volume or prices.

Foreign currency trading was "extremely light" because traders were reluctant to take positions in a thin market without any compelling developments. While traders look to the Middle East crisis and the Soviet Union's deepening political problems to provide some support for the dollar, profit-taking after last week's big runup has eaten away at the dollar's recent gains against the mark. They said Japanese purchases of short-term dollar-denominated investments has strengthened the dollar against the yen modestly in the past few days.

In major market action:

Stock prices slumped in thin trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 103 million shares. Declining issues on the Big Board were ahead of gainers 892 to 598.

Bond prices climbed. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond gained nearly one-half of a point, or almost $5 for each $1,000 face amount. The yield on the issue slipped to 8.24%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading the currency was quoted at 1.5255 marks and 136.40 yen compared with 1.5368 marks and 136.25 yen Wednesday.
