World Markets: Tokyo Stocks End Little Changed as Investors Find Few Incentives to Buy Before the New Year

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 12/28/90

Tokyo stock prices finished little changed Thursday, as gains posted earlier in the day were chipped away by profittaking toward the end of the session, traders said. In London, share prices closed higher, and at the top of the day's trading range, in a market starved for activity.

The Nikkei index of 225 selected issues was up 53.14 points at 23940.70.

At the end of Friday's morning session, the Nikkei closed at 23,848.71, down 91.99.

Volume on the first section Thursday was estimated at 330 million shares, up from 223.5 million shares Wednesday. Gaining issues outnumbered declining issues 599 to 345, while 153 issues remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section was up 10.74 points, or 0.62%, at 1740.55.

The Second Section Index gained for the first time in nine trading days, climbing 3.99 points, or 0.15%, to close at 2722.06. Volume in the second section was estimated at four million shares, virtually unchanged from 3.99 million shares Wednesday.

Stock prices in general found support at their intrasession lows through a moderate amount of buying by newly launched investment trust funds, and by arbitrage buying, traders said.

Large-capital shares such as steel manufacturers and shipbuilding companies were bought, as well as big-name companies such as Hitachi and Mitsubishi Estate. Talk that Secretary of State James Baker may visit Iraq for discussions on Jan. 9 also provided some stability to the market, traders said.

But the market failed to sustain investors' buying interest. After advancing to the intraday high of 24263.63 points at midafternoon, the Nikkei index quickly turned downward, falling 322.93 points by the end of the day.

Most investors were sitting on the fence earlier in the day, but started taking profits on the perception that stock prices were unlikely to rise further, said Shigeru Akiba, head of equity at UBS Phillips & Drew International.

With only the Friday morning session left for this year, investors were wary of keeping positions high amid the ongoing Persian Gulf crisis and political uncertainty in the Soviet Union, participants said. The market will be closed from Dec. 29 to Jan. 3.

"There are no strong incentives to buy. But at the same time investors have no reason to hurry to sell as stock prices are unlikely to fall widely below the current level," said a senior trader at Yamaichi Securities.

Mr. Akiba of UBS Phillips & Drew International predicts stock prices will be supported Friday as arbitragers are likely to buy stocks to take advantage of a wide premium on futures compared with stocks at the end of Thursday's trading.

Nippon Steel was the most heavily traded issue for the day, ending at 448 yen ($3.29), up 10 yen. Among other steel makers, Sumitomo Metal gained six yen to 456 yen, while NKK ended one yen higher at 401 yen, pushed down from its intraday high on profit-taking.

Other gainers included Hitachi, which was up 10 yen to 1,120 yen, and Mitsubishi Estate, which advanced 50 yen to 1,390 yen.

Shipbuilding companies also attracted buying, with Mitsui Engineering & Shipbuilding up 12 yen to 542 yen, while Mitsubishi Heavy Industries gained three yen to 673 yen.

Also popular were shares of some pharmaceuticals. Mochida gained 190 yen to 2,540 yen, while Ono Pharmaceutical was up 350 yen to 4,500 yen.

Separately, the Tokyo Stock Exchange reported data on stock-futures arbitrage trading for the week ended Dec. 21. The number of short cash positions held at the end of the week totaled 5.7 million units, amounting to 6.13 billion yen. The number of long positions, meanwhile, totaled 182.9 million units, amounting to 209.37 billion yen.

As of Dec. 21, the number of short cash positions held against March futures contracts declined 37.4 million units to zero, marking a 42.59 billion yen loss. On the other hand, the number of long positions held against March contracts increased 142 million units to 485.6 million units. The value rose to 541.1 billion yen.

In London, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-Share Index closed 11.5 points higher at 2167.8, up from the day's worst level of 2155.7 recorded in the first hour of trading.

The narrower FT 30-Share Index closed 8.9 points higher at 1687.8. Volume was a meager 177.4 million shares.

The London exchange was closed Tuesday and Wednesday for holiday.

Brokers said trade is effectively on hold until the new year, with most operators absent from the market for the holiday season. This gave activity an overbearingly technical tone, apart from a few new year trading tips that generated volume in selected issues.

A late start to trading on the American and New York stock exchanges, caused by a fire in the Wall Street area, further deprived London of incentive during the afternoon session. By the close of trading in London, the Dow Jones Industrial Average was off to a poor start.

Among shares benefiting from the new year trading recommendations published by various newspapers, the property development group Trafalgar House rose seven pence (13 cents) to 203 pence a share while the telecommunications group Cable & Wireless ended two pence higher at 446 pence.

Food distribution and service company Albert Fisher was unchanged at 117 pence. Earlier, the company said it paid #15 million ($28.2 million) for the European frozen vegetable and seafood assets of Campbell Soup Co.

Shares in building products company RMC ended two pence lower at 650 pence. RMC has reported to the U.K. Office of Fair Trading that some of its units operated unlawful market-sharing practices.

In the pharmaceutical sector, shares in Wellcome ended five pence higher at 435 pence, despite the potential competition of a new treatment for AIDS developed through a U.S.-Swiss joint venture.

Glaxo closed 12 pence higher at 861 pence, and SmithKline Beecham finished five pence higher at 625 pence.

Meanwhile, share prices closed higher in Milan, lower in Stockholm, Paris, Frankfurt and Madrid and mixed in Brussels, Zurich and Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed firmer.

Share prices closed higher in Sydney, Singapore and Taipei, and lower in Manila, and were little changed in Wellington and mixed in Hong Kong. The Seoul stock market was closed for holiday.
