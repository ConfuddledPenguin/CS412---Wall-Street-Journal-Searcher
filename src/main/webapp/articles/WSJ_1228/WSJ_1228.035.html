Economy: November Durables Orders Sank 10.5%, Matching Record Drop Set Last January

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 12/28/90

WASHINGTON -- New orders for durable goods plummeted 10.5% in November, the Commerce Department reported, matching the record drop set last January and offering further confirmation that the economy took a steep downturn in the fourth quarter. Though the plunge was led by a big slide in the volatile transportation category, declines in orders were widespread. Excluding transportation, the decline was 3.5%. The report was doleful news for the beleaguered manufacturing sector, which cut payrolls by 200,000 workers in November, according to figures released earlier this month by the Labor Department.

"This clearly shows the economy to be in the free-fall stages of a recession," said Gordon Richards, an economist with the National Association of Manufacturers.

The November decline in orders -- to $115.94 billion -- put orders at about the same level as in early 1988. The drop followed an increase of 3.6% in October and a decline of 1.6% in September.

Orders in the transportation sector fell 27.4% in November after increasing 14.1% in the previous month. Aircraft orders, particularly big orders to Boeing Co., have made the transportation order figures erratic in the last few months. Aircraft orders in November dropped 38.8%, after a 20.4% increase that included a major Boeing order in October.

The record decline set last January also was led by decreases in orders for aircraft and autos. Orders subsequently moved slowly upward, increasing about 10% between January and October.

Orders for nondefense capital goods, which economists view as a sign of business investment plans, fell 16.4% in November after rising 8.7% in October. Excluding aircraft, nondefense capital goods orders dropped 6%, as electrical machinery orders were off 7.6% to their lowest level since March 1989. Orders for nonelectrical machinery were flat after a 1.4% decline in October, while orders for primary metals sank 9.8% in November, after a 5% increase the previous month. All figures are adjusted for seasonal variations.

"There's an unusually rapid decline in economic activity going on," said Bruce Steinberg, an economist with Merrill Lynch & Co. "Both production and orders are down. The question now is whether this will continue or whether the sharp declines now can pre-empt further declines later."

Many economists have been revising their estimates of fourth-quarter economic growth in response to the last few weeks' parade of bleak economic statistics. Many Wall Street economists are now predicting that real gross national product -- the inflation-adjusted output in goods and services -- could contract 3% or more in the fourth quarter. Merrill Lynch's estimate is that GNP will shrink 3.5%.

"That's sobering," Mr. Steinberg said, because in all recessions going back to the 1950s, the first quarter of decline was followed by other quarters in which the decline was even bigger. But some economists are coming to the view that managers have become more cautious and began slowing down orders and production in this business cycle earlier than usual. That could mean that a rebound from the sharp drop in the fourth quarter will come more quickly.

Shipments of durable goods in November were down 3.6% after a 1.5% increase in October. Unfilled orders slipped 1.1%, following a 0.7% rise the previous month.
