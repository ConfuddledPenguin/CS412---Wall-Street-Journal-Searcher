Letters to the Editor: Some Documents Rightly Withheld

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/28/90

Regarding your Dec. 10 article about the Department of Justice and the House Judiciary Committee concerning the Inslaw matter: The attorney general has directed that the department cooperate fully with the committee's investigation and, as a result, the department has provided hundreds of thousands of documents to the committee, including documents related to the pending litigation with Inslaw.

The department went so far as to obtain a court order to permit the committee access to certain Inslaw software, which culminated in the recent delivery of a truckload of material to the committee.

The small fraction of material the department has not turned over to the committee is protected by a fundamental principal of the American legal system that keeps documents revealing the thought processes of litigators confidential. The documents in dispute are the memos and notes of departmental attorneys assigned to defend the U.S. in the litigation brought by Inslaw and are not factual materials that would be evidence in the pending case.

By the same token, the government would be unable to obtain comparable documents from Inslaw. In fact, such documents, whether they belong to Inslaw or the U.S., cannot be obtained by the opposing party in litigation. The disclosure of the department's documents could be construed to permit access by Inslaw, which would be fundamentally unfair to the U.S. -- to the detriment of the American taxpayers, who stand to lose more than $9 million in this litigation.

W. Lee Rawls

Assistant Attorney General

Department of Justice

Washington
