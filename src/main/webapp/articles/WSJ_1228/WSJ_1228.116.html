Politics & Policy: Treasury Asked To Launch Study Of Insurance Firms

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 12/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The chairman of the Senate Banking Committee has asked the Treasury to launch a wide-ranging study of U.S. insurance companies, as congressional concern about the industry increases. Sen. Donald Riegle (D., Mich.) requested that as part of an eight-month study, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady consider the merits of abandoning state regulation of insurance companies in favor of federal oversight.

"I believe that the recent history of the savings and loan and commercial banking industries makes clear that the federal government cannot afford to ignore serious problems in any industry that lies close to the heart of the American economy," Sen. Riegle said in a letter dated Dec. 20 to Mr. Brady.

The request occurs as other lawmakers intensify their scrutiny of the industry. Both Sen. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio) and Rep. John Dingell (D., Mich.) intend to push for legislation to tighten insurance regulation, possibly moving it to the purview of a federal agency. Sen. Metzenbaum also favors repeal of the insurance industry's 45-year-old antitrust exemptions. Such a bill was approved by the House Judiciary Committee last year, and nearly made it to the House floor for a vote.

A Treasury spokesman said the department was considering the request, but had no reaction yet. Sen. Riegle requested that the study be completed by Sept. 1. Already, Messrs. Dingell and Metzenbaum have indicated that they plan to press ahead on insurance legislation.

Sen. Riegle asked the Treasury to assess the general financial health of the insurance industry, which suffered a record number of failures in 1989. He also requested an evaluation of the industry's exposure to risky assets, such as real estate and junk bonds, as well as the health of the state guaranty funds and the effect of a large insurance company failure on the economy. He also requested comment on whether insurance companies, increasingly marketed as investments, should be subject to more extensive disclosure requirements.

Insurance industry lobbyists say that Capitol Hill concern is overblown, but understandable, following the S&L industry bailout, which will cost taxpayers $200 billion plus hundreds of billions more in interest. "What is of grave concern is that the wrong image about the industry's financial strength is being promoted by unwarranted inquiries," said Henri Bersoux, a lobbyist for the American Council of Life Insurance.

He said that Congress believed the life insurance industry is healthy enough to incur an $8 billion increase in its tax burden, as part of the recent federal budget pact. The total industry has roughly $2 trillion in assets.
