Foreigners Close Wallets to U.S. Markets --- Investors Stage 3rd-Period Sell-Off

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 12/28/90

Anyone counting on wealthy foreigners and foreign institutional investors to give U.S. stock and bond markets a shot in the arm had better not hold their breath -- at least judging the latest statistics. Private foreign investors actually sold $1.5 billion more U.S. stocks and bonds than they bought in the third quarter. That compares with net purchases of $3.6 billion in the second quarter, according to U.S. Treasury data analyzed by the Securities Industry Association.

Stocks were hard hit. So far this year, foreign investors have unloaded a net $9.2 billion of U.S. stocks -- $2.4 billion of that in the third quarter. By comparison, they bought a net $10.8 billion in the same period last year.

If the trend of the first nine months continues, foreign individual and institutional investors will have bought a net $2.3 billion of U.S. securities in all of 1990. That is a mere 3.2% of the $71.2 billion they added to their portfolios in the preceding year.

"You've got fewer private foreigners stepping forward to absorb U.S. securities," said Susan Hering, an economist at Salomon Brothers Inc. "That shifts the burden to foreign official investors or domestic investors."

How much that affects U.S. markets and the financing of the U.S. budget deficit is uncertain. Foreign investors traditionally have influenced U.S. bond prices more than stocks, but British and Japanese investors have at times moved U.S. equity markets.

In the short term, foreigners' diminishing appetite for U.S. bonds means that U.S. interest rates would be higher and the dollar weaker than otherwise, if other things remain constant, said David G. Strongin, director of international finance at the Securities Industry Association.

He and others also pointed out, however, that while private foreign investors sold a net $100 million of U.S. Treasury securities in the third quarter, foreign governments and central banks bought a net $11.3 billion.

That's a big jump from late 1989 and early 1990, when they were net sellers of U.S. Treasurys. Some analysts attribute the huge shift to a flight to quality, as governments and central banks -- particularly in Europe and the Middle East -- sold offshore Eurodollar holdings to buy U.S. government paper after Iraq invaded Kuwait on Aug. 2.

No one knows whether the heavy foreign official purchases will continue. Regardless, Ms. Hering pointed out that in a recession, corporations borrow less to finance inventory and capital investment, and individuals borrow less for mortgages. In addition, "there's a flight to quality" from stocks and corporate bonds to U.S. Treasurys. All this makes it easier for the U.S. government to finance its budget deficit.

For foreign investors, the factors deterring investment in the U.S. have been the falling dollar, interest rates that are lower than those in most other major industrial countries and the huge federal budget deficit.

"The biggest problem I see in the U.S. has got to be the state of the economy in light of the budget deficit and the ability of the U.S. political leadership to bring it down," said Richard D. Muckart, director of sales and investing for Ivory & Sime PLC in Edinburgh, Scotland. "There's no political will to face up to it, and it could get worse."

Many analysts believe the dollar could strengthen next year. "But I'm still concerned with the budget deficit, the balance-of-payments deficit, and the {U.S. stock} market is expensive on its own. At 14 times earnings, it's the most expensive market with the exception of Japan and a few others," said Iain Watt, investment director at Edinburgh Fund Managers PLC in Edinburgh.

"We aren't looking for any earnings growth next year," he added. "There are more negatives in the U.S. market than positives."

All told, private foreign investors purchased only $900 million of U.S. bonds in the third quarter, down from $7.4 billion in the second quarter. Based on these trends, "we are headed for record lows in equities, record lows in Treasurys and a seven-year low in corporate bond purchases by foreigners in 1990," said the Securities Industry Association's Mr. Strongin.

He pointed out that, after being net sellers of U.S. stocks for eight consecutive months, foreign investors did buy $1.4 billion in July. But they quickly switched to selling again after the Mideast crisis erupted in early August.

If foreigners are taking money out of U.S. markets, so are Americans. In the first three quarters, U.S. investors purchased a net $7.3 billion of foreign stocks and $15.6 billion of overseas debt. While the stock purchases are running behind the $9.4 billion for the same period last year, foreign bond buying is double any previous full year. A big lure was the high interest rates and strong currencies available in several major overseas markets.

Most surprising, said Mr. Strongin, is that Americans' purchases abroad were unaffected by the Mideast crisis. Even though many foreign markets fell after Iraq's invasion, U.S. investors bought a net $90 million of foreign stocks in August. In the same month, American institutions sold a net $1.8 billion of U.S. stocks, according to the securities association.

"This trend reflects American institutions' continued commitment to diversifying their portfolios to include foreign securities," said Mr. Strongin. He noted that U.S. institutions have a smaller percentage of their total holdings invested in foreign securities than institutions in other major countries do.
