What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/28/90

NEW ORDERS for durable goods plummeted 10.5% in November, matching January's record fall and offering further evidence of a steep economic downturn in the fourth quarter. Excluding transportation orders, the decline was 3.5%. Orders for nondefense goods were down 16.4%.

Consumer confidence remained virtually unchanged this month from November but is much lower than a year ago, a survey reported. Separately, help-wanted advertising reached a five-year low in November.

---

Stock prices slipped on the weak durable-goods report, but bonds rose on the news. The Dow Jones Industrial Average declined 11.63 points to close at 2625.50. The 30-year Treasury bond gained almost half a point.

An electrical explosion shut down the Big Board for 1 1/2 hours, prompting the SEC to order a trading halt at all U.S. exchanges. But some brokers and institutions bypassed the exchanges and continued to trade.

---

Retail gasoline prices plunged an average of nearly nine cents a gallon in the past week, and could fall more. The decline at the pump follows a recent drop in wholesale prices.

---

First Executive agreed not to write new insurance policies in New Jersey rather than pay a $500 million security deposit the state demanded.

---

TWA owner Carl Icahn is preparing to make the ouster of Pan Am Chairman Thomas Plaskett a condition for a purchase of Pan Am by TWA, people close to their talks say.

---

AMR's American Airlines won a court order barring its pilots union from engaging in a sickout or other work slowdowns. The union denied it is conducting such a job action.

---

Contracts for new construction reached their lowest level in five years in November. Nonresidential building contracts declined 8%, while new-home start-ups were off 4%.

---

Most stock funds performed worse than the S&P 500 this year for the eighth straight year, despite the benefit of holding cash in a bear market. The average fund's total return is a negative 6.8% so far this year.

---

Private foreign investors sold $1.5 billion more U.S. stocks and bonds than they bought in the third quarter.

---

Georgia-Pacific and International Paper were named in a $2 billion law suit alleging the companies dumped dioxin into three rivers in Mississippi.

---

The Senate's banking chief asked the Treasury to launch a wide-ranging study of insurance companies amid growing congressional concern.

---

Procter & Gamble created an Eastern European division to oversee its sales operations in the region.

---

Taiwan's cabinet approved regulations opening the Taipei stock market to direct investment of as much as $2.5 billion by foreign institutions.

---

Tokyo stocks ended little changed Thursday after profit-taking late in the session. London shares rose.
