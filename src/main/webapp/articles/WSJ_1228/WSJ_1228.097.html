Credit Markets: Bond Prices Rise as Record Decline in Orders For Durable Goods Underscores Economy's Woes

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Lynn K. Adler
  • Date: 12/28/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices jumped yesterday after the government reported a record decline in orders for durable goods. The bond market's bellwether 30-year Treasury bond ended nearly 1/2 point higher, a gain of nearly $5 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, eased to 8.24% from 8.28% on Wednesday.

Prices of tax-exempt municipal bonds, which usually lag the Treasury market, posted strong gains with some general obligation bonds issued by Massachusetts climbing as much as one point. Traders attributed the rise in Massachusetts bonds to short-covering, where dealers scramble to buy securities to replace securities they borrowed at an earlier date.

There also was a flurry of buying in the investment-grade corporate bond market, which helped to push prices between 1/4 and 3/8 point higher. Mortgage-backed securities rose between 1/8 and 1/4 point.

The rally in the bond market was sparked by an extremely weak economic report. The Department of Commerce reported that orders for new durable goods plunged 10.5% last month, offering further evidence that the economy is eroding.

The decline matched a record drop set last January and was much deeper than the 2.2% decline that had been forecast by economists surveyed by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report.

Bond investors react bullishly to bearish economic news because slow economic growth often leads to lower interest rates and lower inflation -- two trends that increase the value of fixed-income securities.

The report also set a positive tone for the Treasury's $8.57 billion auction of four-year notes.

The average yield set on the notes at yesterday's auction was 7.66%, sharply lower than the 8.53% average yield at the previous four-year note auction in September. The average yield at yesterday's auction was lower than some analysts expected and was even below the peak when-issued yield of 7.75% set Monday.

Traders said that prices of notes and bonds were little changed after the Federal Reserve preannounced, unexpectedly, that it will execute two-day system repurchase agreements on Monday. That's a technical trading maneuver designed to add reserves to the banking system.

Traders said the Fed made the unusual preannouncement in an effort to relieve some of the pressures that have been building in the federal funds market. The federal funds rate, the rate at which banks charge each other for overnight loans, hit 100% late Wednesday. The target for the rate is 7%.

Although the rate often gyrates on the last day of a two-week bank settlement period, as was yesterday, Wednesday's spike to 100% was widely viewed in the market as the result of a serious miscalculation of bank funding needs by the Federal Reserve.

While the Fed's announcement may have offered some relief to the market, it still did not stop the Fed funds rate from rising far above its target. The rate exceeded 10% yesterday for some domestic transactions and was far higher on trades involving some foreign banks.

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 105 16/32, up from 105 1/32 on Wednesday. The issue's yield fell to 8.24% from 8.28%.

Treasury bill rates were slightly higher. The discount rate on three-month bills ended at 6.49% for a bond-equivalent yield of 6.67%, up slightly from 6.48% to yield 6.66% on Wednesday. The rate on six-month bills rose to 6.59% to yield 6.89% compared with 6.56% to yield 6.86% on Wednesday.

Corporate & Junk Bonds

A flurry of retail buying enlivened the investment-grade corporate bond market, injecting a dose of excitement into an otherwise stultifying week.

Corporate bond traders said much of the buying was concentrated in shorter-maturity corporates and high-quality issues.

The buying was unexpected since investors typically sell corporate bonds at year end to clean up their balance sheets and remove tainted credits.

"This is the first time in a very long time we've gotten year-end buying which is causing" yields to fall relative to yields on Treasury securities, said Andrew Tarica, a corporate bond trader at Kidder, Peabody & Co.

Mr. Tarica notes that Associates Corp. of North America's 9% five-year notes, which were trading at a yield of about 1.55 percentage points above the comparable Treasury issue earlier this month, now trade at a spread of about 1.27 percentage points.

While high-grade corporate bonds ended between 1/4 and 3/8 point higher, junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, finished only marginally higher.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds ended unchanged to 3/8 higher, with some issues up 1/2 to 1 point.

Among actively traded secondary issues, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 were 3/8 higher, at 99 for a return of 7.26%.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended higher.

Government National Mortgage Association 9% for January delivery were up 7/32 at 99 4/32. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. Gold 9% issues and Federal National Mortgage Association 9% issues were up 5/32 at 98 24/32 and 98 16/32, respectively.

Traders said retail participation has ground to a halt during the year-end holiday period, as accounts have long closed their books for the year.

In the primary market, no new issues were announced.
