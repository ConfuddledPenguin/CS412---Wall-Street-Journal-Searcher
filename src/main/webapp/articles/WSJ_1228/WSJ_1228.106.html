Economy: New Construction Hit a 5-Year Low During November

  • Author: Tania Bien-Lambert
  • Date: 12/28/90

NEW YORK -- Contracting for new construction in November reached its lowest level in five years, further evidence that the economy is slumping. F.W. Dodge, the forecasting unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., said its seasonally adjusted Dodge index fell to 144 in November from 145, a four-year low, in October. The index uses 1982 as a base year of 100.

The value of newly started construction of all kinds fell to an annualized $225.9 billion in November from $228.3 billion in October. Nonresidential building contracts and new-home start-ups fell 8% and 4%, respectively, to $77.6 billion and $91.8 billion. In contrast, values for nonbuilding construction, which includes public works and utilities, surged 16% to $56.5 billion, reflecting an $800 million program of pipeline repair and replacement in Alaska. This project helped to keep the index from falling more steeply.

Despite economic uncertainties, construction start-ups aren't unusual in the category of large nonbuilding projects requiring three to five years lead time. But conventional commercial and residential projects are hurting for financing. George A. Christie, vice president and chief economist for F.W. Dodge, said, "Lenders are properly wary of real estate investment after the S&L mess, but if lenders won't lend, developers can't build."

Construction activity declined in all five major regions in the nation, particularly in the Northeast and the South Atlantic, which recorded 21% and 16% declines, respectively. Smaller than average slumps, ranging from 3% to 6%, were reported in the South Central, the West and the North Central regions.

Through the first 11 months of 1990, total construction contract values declined 10% to $225.6 billion from $251.3 billion in the previous year. Nonbuilding construction remained flat.
