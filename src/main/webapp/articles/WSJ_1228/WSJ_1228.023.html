International: Chemical Banking Unit Picked by Japan For Building Project

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/28/90

TOKYO -- Chemical Trust & Banking Co. (Japan), a subsidiary of New York-based Chemical Banking Corp., was chosen over eight Japanese bidders to build and manage a 10-story building on a site here that is used as a postal warehouse. The contract was granted by Japan's Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications, in one of the first such projects arranged by the Japanese government.

Though the award involves less than 2,300 square feet of land, the choice of a U.S.-owned company rather than Japanese trust banks is likely to attract attention. U.S. officials have been watching Japanese government procurement practices in areas ranging from construction to computer systems. And the financial services area is among the industries in which the U.S. has been pushing hardest for fair access to the Japanese market.

The Japanese government in the mid-1980s proposed developing public property through trust arrangements, but only two such projects have been arranged so far, the Ministry of Finance said.

The Ministry of Posts said the winning bid was based on projections for total dividends and estimated value of the building when it is handed over to the government at the end of the 20-year contract. Total projected dividends for the ministry are 8.68 billion yen ($63.8 million) under Chemical's plan, which calls for the first eight floors to be rented as office space and the two top floors as residential units. Estimated value of the building at the end of the contract is 864.8 million yen ($6.4 milllion).

A spokesman for Chemical in Tokyo declined to estimate the value of the contract, saying details of the agreement are expected to be negotiated by April 1. The 20-year life of the project means there are many uncertainties in calculating its profitability, he said.

Under the arrangement, Chemical will borrow as much as two billion yen to finance construction, and manage the building for 18 years after its scheduled completion in 1993. Chemical is to extract costs, interest and its own fees from tenants' rents, and pay the remainder to the ministry as dividends.
