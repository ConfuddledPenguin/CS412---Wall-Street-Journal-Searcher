Executive Directors Of IMF, World Bank Disburse Billions

  • Author: Edward T. Pound
  • Date: 12/28/90

WASHINGTON -- Everybody has a boss, but who is Jacques de Groote's? Technically, he has none. As an executive director of both the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund, which pay his salary, he is meant to be an independent official who can vote as he chooses on approving aid and helping countries adopt sound monetary policies.

But since he is elected by six nations to look after their interests, he must pay attention to their needs if he wants to be re-elected every two years. Those countries are Belgium, Austria, Luxembourg, Turkey, Hungary and Czechoslovakia -- but not Zaire. It has its own executive directors.

Mr. de Groote says he also reports to officials at the National Bank of Belgium, which sent him to Washington in 1973, and at the Belgian finance ministry. Alfons Verplaetse, the Belgian National Bank governor, is satisfied that Mr. de Groote has kept him posted on matters essential to Belgium's interests in Zaire. But some others say they have been either unaware of or uneasy with his Zairean actions. A former national bank official who dealt directly with Mr. de Groote recalls warning him that "Zaire is not your business" and urging him to be prudent. And in the early 1980s, according to a former finance ministry official, Mr. de Groote told Belgian Finance Minister Willy de Clercq he had stopped advising Zaire.

Mr. de Groote, who plans to leave his posts in 1992, is the senior member of the World Bank board and second-ranking in seniority on the IMF board. He helps direct the affairs of the multibillion-dollar, taxpayer-financed institutions. This includes approving large loans, most of them to poorer nations; the World Bank alone has lent more than $160 billion since its founding in the 1940s.

Both the World Bank and the IMF have 22 directors, most elected by groups of countries. For seven nations -- the U.S., Germany, Japan, England, France, China and Saudi Arabia -- directors are appointed by their governments and report only to them. U.S. executive directors are appointed by the president, are subject to Senate confirmation and report to the Treasury Department.

Executive directors, whose board meetings are private, work in a pampered environment. They benefit from first-class air flights, luxury hotels, six-figure tax-free salaries, salary supplements for non-working wives and other perks. Unlike staffers, who are explicitly barred from leaking information or using their positions for personal gain, directors aren't subject to written restrictions. This "doesn't mean it is a totally lawless situation," says a senior World Bank lawyer. "If something egregious happens, I think the board has the general power to control what executive directors do," though there haven't been any test cases.
