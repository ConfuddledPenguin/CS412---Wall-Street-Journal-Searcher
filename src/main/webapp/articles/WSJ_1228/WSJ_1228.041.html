Japan's Steel Exports Fell in November Prices Rose to Record

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 12/28/90

TOKYO -- Japan's steel exports in November fell 5.7% to 1,356,000 metric tons from a year earlier, marking a continuing decline as manufacturers struggled to meet strong domestic demand, Japan's Iron and Steel Federation said. A metric ton is equal to about 2,204.62 pounds.

Export prices for the month soared to $818 a ton, climbing above the $800 level for the first time in history. A federation official said the surge indicates that a large part of exports consists of expensive items such as seamless pipes and galvanized iron products.

Aside from such profitable, high value-added items, "Makers have completely shifted to domestic demand-oriented production," the official said.

Reflecting continued expansion of the Japanese economy, orders from domestic auto makers, construction companies, industrial machinery makers and shipbuilding firms remained at "an extremely high level," he said.

While domestic steel production is expected to mark a record high in 1990, steel exports for the year are likely to drop to the lowest level in 20 years, the official said.

By market, exports to the U.S. posted their first year-to-year gain in six months, up 8.2% at 237,000 metric tons, helped by increases in exports of seamless pipe and other oil drilling-related products, the official said.

Exports to the Soviet Union continued to improve, though the total amount remained small. Exports rose 15% to 40,000 tons, as shipments of oil-drilling products resumed, after price negotiations between producers and the Soviet government were successfully concluded earlier this year.
