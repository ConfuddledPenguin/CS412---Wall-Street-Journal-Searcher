Economy: Consumer Level Of Confidence Holds Steady --- December Measure Is Flat From November, But Is Off Sharply From 1989

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 12/28/90

NEW YORK -- Consumer confidence in December remained virtually unchanged from November but still much lower than a year ago, as concerns about the economy and the Middle East persist, according to the Conference Board, an independent business research organization. Meanwhile, help-wanted advertising volume reached a five-year low in November, the board said, as its seasonally adjusted help-wanted ad index fell eight points.

The Conference Board's Consumer Confidence Index stood at 61.3 in December, slightly lower than a revised 61.7 for November. The results of a nationwide survey of consumers show that Americans are more worried today than they were a month ago about economic conditions. And confidence has plummeted compared with a year ago when the index registered above 113.

The biggest single monthly drop came in October during the U.S. budget debate, when the confidence index fell to 62.6 from 85.6 in September. Since then, the index has been hovering in the low 60s. The index uses 1985 as a base year of 100.

"Consumers' continuing disenchantment with prevailing business conditions and their low expectations for the immediate future reflect the increasingly soft economy," says Fabian Linden, executive director of the board's Consumer Research Center.

The employment situation clearly remains a big worry. About 33% of those surveyed complained that jobs are "hard to get," up from 29% in November. Looking ahead, 34% expect few jobs to be available over the next six months, while only 10% expect there to be more jobs. On the other hand, only 26% said they expect business conditions to worsen, down from about 30% last month.

Concern about jobs, along with expectations for little or no real personal income growth, is prompting many consumers to keep their hands on their wallets. In the December survey, only 5.9% said they intend to purchase a car, down from 6.5% last month. In December 1989, over 7% said they planned to buy a car. Home-buying interest is also weak at 2.6%. And many consumers are shelving plans for a vacation. Less than 47% of those surveyed said they plan to take a vacation in the next six months, down from more than 52% a year ago.

The index is based on a survey of 5,000 households across the U.S. conducted for the Conference Board by National Family Opinion Inc. of Toledo, Ohio. The decline in help-wanted advertising reflects the fact that "the labor market has been weakening for the last six months, and it may be another six months before it turns around," said Ken Goldstein, the Conference Board's economist. The nation's unemployment rose to 5.9% in November, from 5.7% in October. The rate stood at 5.3% a year ago.

But he said help-wanted volume is unlikely to fall another 20% over the next six months, as it has done over the previous six months. The index, which fell last month to 108 from 116 in October, is currently 37 points below the year-ago level of 145. The index uses 1967 as a base year of 100.

Help-wanted advertising volume declined in all nine major regions of the country, particularly in the Pacific, where it fell 10.8%. The East North Central and Middle Atlantic regions followed, recording drops of 8.1% and 7.4% respectively. The Conference Board said the best news is that labor conditions in the West South Central oil patch, held up better than anywhere else in the country.

Mr. Goldstein said the downward trend that began in the Northeast more than a year ago has spread to the South Atlantic, Pacific, and more recently into the Midwestern regions. Still, he expects signs of improvement in labor market conditions soon.

The survey measures help-wanted ad volume in 51 major newspapers in the nation.
