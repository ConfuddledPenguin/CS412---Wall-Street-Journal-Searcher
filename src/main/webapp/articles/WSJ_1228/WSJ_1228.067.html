Computer Firm Servicing Exchanges Expects Partial Backup by Next Year

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 12/28/90

NEW YORK -- When smoke forced 60 workers to evacuate two floors of a Manhattan office building early yesterday morning, it shut down the nerve center of the U.S. stock market. The Securities Industry Automation Corp. is the generally unseen computerized engine behind the trading of many millions of shares on the New York, American and regional stock exchanges.

Formed in 1972 through a merger of the data processing facilities of the New York and American exchanges, the company processes the stock quotations from all of the nation's major exchanges. It puts out the consolidated tape of prices of all exchange-listed stocks, among other services.

The stream of price and volume information from the exchanges on stock trades that are processed by the company give market makers, specialists and investors the same information, and therefore an equal chance at bargaining for stocks.

When business operates as usual, the company goes unnoticed. But disruptions such as yesterday's evacuation emphasize how dependent the nation's stock exchanges are on computers.

To prevent such disruptions, the company is completing a backup facility in downtown Brooklyn. Nearly 60,000 feet of office space have been developed there to house facilities similar to the two acres of office space in lower Manhattan where the company is housed.

By the middle of next year, enough computers should have been moved to the Brooklyn facility to provide full backup service to handle a normal day's trading on the New York Stock Exchange, said Richard Leyh, executive vice president and chief of operations. However, a complete backup system for services to all U.S. exchanges won't be available until 1992, Mr. Leyh said.

By next summer, he said, the Brooklyn facility should be able to provide backup for the Big Board's SuperDot system, which routes member firm's orders to appropriate specialists, or market makers, and back to member firms. SuperDot accounts for 80% to 90% of Big Board volume, Mr. Leyh said.

Some traders complain, however, that until the company can provide instant backup service, its owners will remain vulnerable to competition from offshore and other trading services.

None of the company's computers were damaged yesterday, and power wasn't cut off, Mr. Leyh said. Trading was delayed because New York City environmental officials sealed the building until tests showed there was no evidence of contamination from polychlorinated biphenyls, or PCBs. The chemical is often used in electrical transformers like the one that exploded early yesterday outside the company's Water Street offices.
