Marketing & Media: Oakland Tribune Set To Dismiss a Quarter Of Its Work Force

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/21/90

OAKLAND, Calif. -- The financially beleaguered Oakland Tribune said it was firing one-fourth of its 725-person work force -- including 40% of its executive staff -- in an effort to stay afloat. The announcement was made by Robert C. Maynard, the newspaper's owner and president. The declining fortunes of the former Gannett Corp. publication have been the subject of persistent speculation for months. The paper, acquired by Mr. Maynard in 1983, has been plagued by steadily slumping circulation and advertising, in part from stiffening competition, in part from Oakland's economic woes. The Tribune suffered yet another blow when severe damage from the Oct. 17 earthquake set back blossoming Oakland revitalization efforts, and subsequently sapped advertising revenues.

Mr. Maynard, in a statement, said he has asked the Tribune's various unions, representing 70% of its employees, for their "participation in the restructuring."

"It gives me no pleasure to think of the Tribune losing a quarter of its employees. We are, after all, family," he said.

Mr. Maynard is a nationally known journalist and syndicated columnist whose buy-out of the Tribune drew considerable attention when it made him the first black owner of a major metropolitan newspaper in the U.S. The paper, with a circulation of 130,000, earlier this year won a Pulitzer Prize for photography.
