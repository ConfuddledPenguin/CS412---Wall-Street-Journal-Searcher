Insurers Could Lose Antitrust Exemption Under Bill Cleared by House Committee

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Judiciary Committee narrowly passed legislation that would repeal major portions of the insurance industry's exemption from federal antitrust law. The committee's action, by a 19-17 vote, poses the biggest threat the insurance industry has ever faced to the antitrust exemption, passed by Congress as the McCarran-Ferguson Act in 1945.

"Congress can no longer ignore the widespread economic dislocation caused by premiums that are unreasonably high -- or policies that are unavailable at any price," said Rep. Jack Brooks, a Texas Democrat and Judiciary Committee chairman, who sponsored the new legislation.

Approval of the bill set the stage for a battle in the full House, possibly in late summer. But if the measure passes the House, over strong opposition, a Senate proposal also faces a tough fight. Consumer groups that support the legislation concede that the best they may be able to hope for is passage by the House this year, and a quick start in the new Congress next year.

The bill passed the committee despite opposition by all 14 of the Republicans on the 36-member panel. The measure was supported by 19 Democrats, and was opposed by three Democrats.

The legislation would repeal insurers' exemptions for the practices that are generally deemed by antitrust experts to be most anti-competitive. These include price-fixing, allocating geographic regions among competing companies, attempting to monopolize markets, and requiring consumers to buy insurance packages that include policies they don't really want.

The proposal would allow insurance companies to continue sharing some basic information about claims and economic trends, while phasing out the sharing of some other types of information. And the exemption from federal enforcement would remain intact in states that actively regulate the insurance industry.

A broad coalition of some 30 consumer and professional groups, including the American Bar Association, supports some type of legislation to amend the McCarran-Ferguson law. "I can't imagine losing on the {House} floor during an election year," said Robert Hunter, head of the National Insurance Consumers Organization in Arlington, Va. "We're going to frame this debate as `Who is for or against insurance price-fixing.'"

Earl Pomeroy, North Dakota insurance commissioner and president of the National Association of Insurance Commissioners, calls the bill "bad public policy." He said supporters of the legislation haven't demonstrated how "it will lower prices and improve service -- the two things consumers most want."

Mr. Pomeroy also said that, although the measure would allow for federal enforcement in insurance by enabling the Federal Trade Commission to investigate allegations, passage of the bill "doesn't mean we'll have federal regulation tomorrow."

Some large insurers, such as Allstate Insurance Co., a unit of Sears, Roebuck & Co., and State Farm Mutual Insurance Co., would be willing to live without the federal antitrust exemption if states abolished laws that regulate rates, which they said just adds another layer of regulation to what already exists. "Give us what other competitive industries have -- complete pricing freedom," said Robert Pike, Allstate's general counsel.
