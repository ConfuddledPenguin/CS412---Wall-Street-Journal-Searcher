Westpac Joins Primary-Dealer Dropouts

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit and Tom Herman
  • Date: 06/21/90

Another financial institution fell from the ranks of the exclusive but brutally competitive U.S. securities dealers that do business directly with the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. Australia's Westpac Banking Corp. said it was closing its Westpac Pollock Government Securities Inc. subsidiary immediately. The primary dealer had losses of about $29 million in the first half of its year that began Oct. 1.

Once one of the most coveted franchises in international finance, the group of firms that deal with the New York Fed when it buys and sells U.S. government securities as part of its activities to influence U.S. monetary policy -- known as "primary dealers" -- have had a tough time making money over the past two years.

The U.S. government-bond business "has become very difficult," said John R. Burrus, president and chief executive officer of CRT Government Securities Ltd., another primary dealer. "Trading volume is down, profit margins are down, too, and volatility is way down from prior years."

Westpac is Australia's biggest bank with about 118 billion Australian dollars (US$92.5 billion) in assets, of which roughly 10% is in the U.S. The bank said most of the closed unit's approximately 160 employees have been dismissed. Westpac paid $120 million for Wm. E. Pollock Government Securities Inc., an independent securities firm, in late 1986.

The securities subsidiary posted a deficit of $3.9 million in fiscal 1989, ended Sept. 30, mostly because of losses associated with a failed attempt to start up a mortgage-backed securities operation. But Anthony J. Walton, executive vice president and general manager at Westpac in New York, said that the government-bond dealing operations had remained profitable until this year.

People in the bond market said that Westpac tried selling the operations to France's Cie. Financiere de Paribas and a big Japanese bank, but was unsuccessful. Paribas didn't return a phone call for confirmation.

Some other firms are rumored to be either on the block or considering closing down their operations, several traders said.

Despite the closing, Mr. Walton said: "Westpac's other operations in the U.S. are very healthy and very profitable." He said the bank maintained relationships with about 250 major U.S. corporations.

"You have to be very, very clear on focus, and we felt that strategically the primary dealership no longer fitted as it did in 1986," said Mr. Walton, noting the intense competition in global securities markets. "It's a very tough decision to give up something that so many people prize such as this; but at the end of the day, it's the shareholder that counts."

Last year, two British banks -- National Westminster Bank PLC and Lloyds Bank PLC -- abandoned the business, along with L.F. Rothschild & Co. Two dealerships were also eliminated when Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. last year acquired the brokerage business of Thomson McKinnon Securities Inc. and Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc. filed for protection under the federal Bankruptcy Code.

Westpac's dropout lowers to 43 the number of primary dealers, from a peak of 46 at the end of 1988. Sixteen of the firms are controlled by foreign institutions: eight Japanese, three British, two Swiss and one each from Canada and Hong Kong. The Fed also lists First Boston Corp. as a foreign institution, because over 50% of it is owned by foreigners, but doesn't assign it a specific national designation. However, some people regard it as a Swiss institution, because it is 44.5% owned by CS Holding, which also owns Credit Suisse, a big bank.

Still, several firms, mostly foreign, have eagerly sought to be designated as primary dealers. A major reason is that numerous U.S. pension funds, insurance companies and other large institutional investors will do business only with primary dealers. Moreover, winning admission is widely considered a mark of prestige.

A subsidiary of Swiss Bank Corp. became a primary dealer in March. Last December, units of Union Bank of Switzerland and Britain's Barclays Bank PLC joined the elite club. And other foreign institutions, including West Germany's Deutsche Bank AG, are waiting in the wings.
