House Panel Adopts Measure to Let SEC Fine Violators of Law

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- A House subcommittee approved legislation that would expand the Securities and Exchange Commission's power to impose fines on individuals and firms that violate securities laws. The telecommunications and finance panel also passed a bill aimed at curbing penny-stock fraud, after substantially changing provisions opposed by the SEC and the securities industry. Both bills now move to the full Energy and Commerce Committee.

The civil-penalties bill, identical in most respects to legislation recently passed by the Senate Banking Committee, would give the SEC power to impose monetary penalties for offenses other than insider trading, for which the agency currently has fining power. The House bill also gives the SEC the power to issue cease-and-desist orders to enforce the securities laws. But compared with the Senate bill, the House bill includes additional protections for parties subject to such orders to assure the target's right to due process.

In the penny-stock bill, the House subcommittee softened a provision that would have required the creation of an electronic system to disseminate penny-stock quotes. The revised bill directs the SEC to adopt rules to improve dissemination of penny-stock quotes. The revised bill also directs the SEC to adopt rules providing for increased disclosure of information about companies that sell penny stocks to the public.

Moreover, a provision in the bill that would have banned so-called blind pools and blank-check offerings was softened to require the SEC only to increase disclosure requirements for such offerings. In both types of offerings, the issuers don't have a business plan or specific assets.
