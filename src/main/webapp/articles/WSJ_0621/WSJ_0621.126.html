World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Gain 47.38 Points to 32087.76 After Bouncing Up and Down in Moderate Range

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/21/90

Tokyo stocks settled higher after bouncing up and down within a moderate range. Traders said that rounds of arbitrage buying and scattered buying on incentive issues added strength to the indexes. But performance on currency and bond markets was disappointing, putting a cap on aggressive buying, traders said.

Meanwhile, London stocks finished a quiet session narrowly mixed and little changed, as traders said investors awaited the end of the quarter before opening fresh positions.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues gained 47.38 points to 32087.76, after falling 336.42 points Tuesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index added another 51.33 points to 32139.09.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 400 million shares, up from 335 million shares Tuesday. Advancers outpaced decliners 492 to 446, while 193 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section gained 12.87 points, or 0.55%, to 2366.80. The second section index rose 2.52 points, or 0.06%, to 4147.58 on volume estimated at 23 million shares, off from 24.5 million shares Tuesday.

Trading yesterday morning was lackluster, as softer yen and bond performances led to unenthusiastic activity, while scattered arbitrage buying lent some support.

But traders said that professional investors sifted through prospects for strong research-oriented companies, buying shares that also seemed underpriced.

The bargain hunting on incentive-backed issues remained strong all day, as dealers passed the shares on to their customers, who lured in the broader market, observers said.

One domestic trader mentioned that talk among dealers in the morning included theories that foreign investors, particularly from the U.S., will get out of long cash positions overseas and place their money in Japanese stocks. "Especially in New York, many feel the market is overheated and won't be improving for some time," he said.

The Japanese trader felt that some dealers acted on the speculation, resulting in the initial buying on incentive-backed issues.

On the other hand, financial conditions haven't really turned to favor yen investments, observers noted.

From an equity point of view, the dollar-yen rate has been trapped in an unattractive range for some time, with little sign of breaking out of its range, traders said.

"There's a very interesting tug-of-war going on" with the relationship between the markets, said Julian Jones, an equity salesman at W.I. Carr, Sons & Co. The battle increases investors' attention to bond-yield movements.

The market is unlikely to make any large movement until it receives a firm indication of the currency's direction, Mr. Jones said.

Arbitrage and other types of index-linked buying, meanwhile, is keeping the market from falling on weak fundamentals, traders said. Morgan Stanley, Salomon Brothers and Baring Securities were underlined as key players.

A chief trader at a midsized Japanese securities firm submitted that yesterday was the settlement date for pension fund assessment, so firms would naturally push for a higher result on the cash index, he said.

Notable gainers included NTT, which advanced 70000 yen to 1.15 million yen ($456 to $7,494). According to a local news service report, the company's chairman hinted that there is potential for a gratis issue.

Tokyo Steel rose 100 yen to 4950 yen, while Fuji Photo Film gained 100 yen to 4390 yen, both boosted by incentive-backed dealer buying, traders said.

Fujisawa Pharmaceutical, which Tuesday disclosed news on a new drug to be targeted for testing in the U.S., Soviet Union, Japan and Western Europe, rose 70 yen to 2100 yen.

In London, dealers said institutional investors generally have stated that they're content with the profits they have made so far during the second quarter and are awaiting the start of the next quarter for any new investment.

In addition, the running of the Royal Ascot horse race kept many participants out of the market, dealers said, as did a mediocre start to Wall Street and a dearth of major U.K. corporate or economic news.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index gained 1.5 points to 2371.2, off the midmorning best level of 2384.5, but well above the early morning minimum of 2369.6.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index fell 4 points to 1903.4. Volume reached a sketchy 436.8 million shares, compared with 408.3 million issues Tuesday.

Among the major losers on the day was Imperial Chemical Industries, Britain's largest chemical concern. The stock fell 30 pence to #11.82 (52 cents to $20.37) on news that the U.S. Commerce Department is suing eight corporations, including an ICI unit, for allegedly polluting the coastal waters off California.

Also falling was drug maker Wellcome, which shed 34 pence to 633 pence after an earnings downgrade by brokerage firm S.G. Warburg, and worries over emerging competition for the company's Retrovir drug used to combat the effects of the HIV virus.

Defense stocks continued to tailspin after the British government Monday said it is canceling an order for 33 Tornado aircraft. British Aerospace lost 26 pence to 520 pence, GEC shed 11 pence to 197 pence, Rolls-Royce slipped 2 pence to 215 pence, while Vickers dropped a penny to 232 pence.

Food and dairy group Unigate rose 7 pence to 312 pence, after reporting a 5% rise for fiscal 1990 to #105.5 million, exceeding market forecasts.

Hambros, a banking and financial services concern, increased 3 pence to 286 pence, after it reported a 10% increase in pretax profit for the fiscal year.

STC, a computers and electronics company, fell 19 pence to 239 pence, after issuing a half-year warning that profit won't match market expectations.

Elsewhere, stocks closed higher in Frankfurt, Amsterdam, Stockholm and Madrid; mixed in Paris, Brussels and Zurich, and lower in Milan. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Also, shares closed higher in Sydney, Wellington, Hong Kong, and Manila, were mixed in Singapore, and lower in Seoul and Taipei.
