Henry Nau, senior staffer at the National Security Council from 1981 to 1983, writing in the March/April issue of the International Economy:

The Bretton Woods standards did not subordinate domestic growth to international stability, as critics of U.S. dollar policy began to argue in the 1960s; nor did they manipulate international mechanisms such as exchange rates to accommodate domestic expansion or inflation, as was done in the 1970s. They recognized that domestic and international stability and growth were directly interrelated. If domestic prices began to diverge in the major countries, international prices or exchange rates would also have to diverge. If domestic economies became increasingly inflexible, countries increasingly would have to make their balance of payments adjustments through borrowing or through trade, exchange rate, and capital market restrictions. There was no way to have your cake and eat it, too. Domestic and international policies depended upon one another: Domestic policies couldn't succeed without international competition, and international cooperation could not compensate for undisciplined domestic policies.
