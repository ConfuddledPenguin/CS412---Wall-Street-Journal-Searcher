Politics & Policy: Product-Liability Bill Provides Opportunity For Long-Term Milking of PACs by Congress

  • Author: Jill Abramson
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- A titanic struggle is under way in Congress over legislation that would overhaul product-liability law. Just the kind of fight lobbyists and lawmakers love, it pits two giant special interests, Big Business and trial lawyers, against each other. But despite million-dollar lobbying campaigns by both sides, Congress is unlikely to act on the measure this year.

And that's good news for members of Congress and lobbyists who, for almost a decade, have been feeding off the contributions from political action committees and the fat retainers that product-liability legislation has generated. It means PAC contributions and lobbying fees will continue next year.

The bill is part of a breed of legislation known as a "cash cow." Rarely do these cash-generating pieces of legislation ever pass. But like perennial flowers, they sprout again every Congress, replenishing the coffers of lawmakers and lobbyists. Other cash cows have resulted from efforts to narrow the insurance industry's exemption from federal antitrust law and to curb the civil use of the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations law.

A business lobbying coalition, made up of chemical, auto and pharmaceutical manufacturers and insurance companies that want federal legislation to limit the rights of plaintiffs in lawsuits over allegedly defective products, has contributed more than $2 million since 1983 to members of the Senate Commerce Committee. A product-liability bill has been introduced in each new Congress in that period, and last month the panel approved another such bill by a 13-7 vote.

For Sen. Slade Gorton (R., Wash.), a co-sponsor of the current product-liability bill, this lobbying effort has meant more than $200,000 in PAC contributions for his Senate races in 1986 and 1988. For Sen. John Rockefeller (D., W.Va.), a more recent convert, it has meant $60,000 in PAC contributions for his 1990 re-election drive.

Trial lawyers, whose contingency fees in product-liability cases are endangered by the bill, have contributed, too. Their PAC money has been a much more modest $81,000, but hundreds of thousands of dollars more in contributions flow directly from individual lawyers.

Sen. Albert Gore (D., Tenn.), who voted against the bill last month, has received $20,000 from the PAC of the powerful Association of Trial Lawyers of America in recent years. Sen. Ernest Hollings, (D., S.C.), chairman of the panel and a foe of product-liability overhaul, received $5,000 from the trial-lawyers association's PAC and thousands more from individual trial lawyers.

"The bill is a PAC annuity for members of Congress," says Ralph Nader, one of several consumer activists allied with the trial lawyers. "It's like rubbing the golden lamp." A PAC analysis by Public Citizen, one of Mr. Nader's consumer-advocacy groups, shows that six members of the Senate Commerce Committee, all of whom voted for the product-liability bill, received more than 12% of their PAC money in their most recent re-election campaigns from companies or trade associations lobbying for the measure.

But Mr. Nader's own sources of funding have become an issue in the product-liability fight. Some proponents of the bill allege that he and other consumer activists lobbying against the bill depend on trial lawyers for financial support.

"I'm not saying he's in their pocket, but Ralph Nader is the trial lawyer's hero," says Victor Schwartz, a Washington lawyer who represents one of the business groups lobbying on product-liability legislation. His side has spent more than $1 million each year on a massive lobbying drive.

Mr. Nader concedes that some of his groups receive contributions from individual trial lawyers. "But if they didn't exist, this fight would go on," he says. Joan Claybrook, president of Public Citizen, says contributions from trial lawyers amount to a "minuscule" portion of Public Citizen's $4 million annual budget. Public Citizen doesn't accept corporate contributions, including any funds from the trial-lawyers association, she adds.

Some members of the Senate Commerce Committee have taken PAC money from both the coalition promoting the product-liability legislation and the trial lawyers opposing it. "The bill has been a boon to many legislators running for Congress because they get money from both sides," says Alan Parker, deputy executive director of public affairs for the trial-lawyers association.

Besides its contributions, the association also has a team of experienced lobbyists, led by Thomas Boggs Jr. of the politically well-connected Washington law firm of Patton, Boggs & Blow.

The politically touchy issue of money spilled into the open at a Commerce Committee hearing on the bill in May. A number of senators, including Mr. Rockefeller, expressed outrage over a letter Mr. Nader sent to their home-state newspapers. In the letter, Mr. Nader suggested that members of the panel were "huddled" with Washington lobbyists, also major contributors to their campaigns, who favor the product-liability bill. At the hearing, an angry Sen. Rockefeller asked the committee staff to investigate the funding of several consumer groups to see how much money they receive from trial lawyers.

Although the West Virginia Democrat quickly called off the investigation, he remains angry over Mr. Nader's lobbying tactics. "The one thing the people of West Virginia know is that I don't owe my soul to anybody," says Mr. Rockefeller, who is the great-grandson, namesake and heir of the late founder of Standard Oil, and who spent millions of his personal fortune to win his first Senate election in 1984. "I don't say that snottily," he adds, "that's just one of the advantages of having independent means."

The Nader forces, meanwhile, intend to make the large PAC contributions from companies and trade associations supporting the bill a big issue as the fight moves toward the Senate floor. "The issue would not exist in Congress were it not for the PAC contributions," says Pamela Gilbert, legislative director for Congress Watch, another arm of the Nader organization.

St. Clair Tweedie, director of government affairs for American Cyanamid Co., says such arguments are overblown and that the PAC money contributed to Commerce Committee members by companies and groups in the product-liability coalition isn't necessarily tied to the bill. "We have many issues," he says. "They {plaintiffs' lawyers} have only one: Don't screw with my yacht."

Although the Commerce Committee approved the bill, the measure is now in the hands of the Senate Judiciary Committee, which is less likely to give its approval, and the Senate floor outlook is even more uncertain. There hasn't been any House action this year on a product-liability bill, and there probably won't be.

And this keeps the lawmakers in the catbird's seat. The bitter lobbying stalemate means that product-liability legislation will remain in legislative limbo -- a cash cow with plenty of milk left. "This bill has the perfect tension to keep it alive," says Mr. Schwartz.

Corrections & Amplifications
