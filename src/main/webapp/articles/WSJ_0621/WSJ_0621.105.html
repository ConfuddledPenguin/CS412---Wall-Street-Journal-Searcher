Law: Lawyer in Vermont Is Selected by Bush For Federal Bench --- Decision Is Made Months After Recommendation, Week Afer Veto Support

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush has decided to name a Vermont lawyer to the federal bench -- nearly 16 months after Sen. James Jeffords recommended the nomination and a week after the liberal Republican backed Mr. Bush on a veto override. The lawyer, Fred Parker of Weybridge, Vt., was telephoned by Mr. Bush Tuesday night, according to Mr. Jeffords's office, which denied any connection between the timing of the appointment and the senator's support for a Bush veto of an Amtrak authorization bill seven days before.

The appointment, for the U.S. District Court in Burlington, Vt., is subject to Senate confirmation, and Mr. Parker is expected to win approval easily.

Labor unions backing the Amtrak legislation initially had counted Mr. Jeffords among their supporters in the fight, which centered on the Bush administration's objections to a provision requiring the Interstate Commerce Commission to review the purchase of major freight railroads by non-rail companies. Transportation Secretary Samuel Skinner, in partnership with Union Pacific Corp. Chairman Drew Lewis, painted the bill as a return to rail regulation, and a White House lobbying campaign prevailed by a margin of two votes in the Senate.

In an interview before the vote, Mr. Jeffords said he favored such legislation to deal with smaller freight lines like those in New England, but as the provision dealt only with Class I, or the nation's 16 biggest railroads, he felt free to back Mr. Bush on the regulation issue. "If it's not an issue that Jim feels affects the state or disadvantages his constituents, he is more apt to listen to the president," a Jeffords aide said.

"He was a hard vote to get," a person familiar with the administration's lobbying said of Sen. Jeffords. "What it took, I do not know."

Mr. Parker's nomination is an issue that the senator has taken personally, and he was infuriated last year over the long delays by the White House in nominating his friend to the federal court. In November, Mr. Jeffords held up action on eight other nominees to register his protest, but an aide to the senator said a reconciliation had been reached well before the veto vote and he was only awaiting final word from the president.
