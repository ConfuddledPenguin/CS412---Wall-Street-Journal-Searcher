Abreast of the Market: Stocks Finish Mixed on Late Rally After Fed Report Depresses Prices

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 06/21/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices closed mixed as a late round of buying offset a midday sell-off that was triggered by a report from the Federal Reserve that further damped hopes for lower interest rates. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rebounded from double-digit losses to finish with a gain of 1.74 at 2895.30, and broader indexes also ended slightly higher.

Despite the market's gyrations, trading activity remained lackluster as concerns with rates and corporate earnings continued to hang over investors. New York Stock Exchange volume amounted to 137,420,000 shares, compared with 134,930,000 on Tuesday.

"We really have a market that has no sustaining power up and no sustaining power down, as indicated by the lack of volume in either direction," said Jack Solomon, a technical analyst at Bear, Stearns.

After stocks failed to sustain opening gains, the market skidded to session lows at midday after the Fed issued its "beige book" summary of economic conditions. In the report, the central bank said there had been "continued slow growth in the economy with little change in the underlying inflation rate."

That assessment, arriving on the heels of some better-than-expected economic reports for May, triggered a sell-off in bonds that spilled into stocks. Long-term Treasury issues showed losses in late trading of around 1/2 point, or $5 per $1,000 face amount.

But stock prices rebounded near the close in a recovery fueled by program buying related to stock-index arbitrage, which went into effect after index futures contracts advanced to sharp premiums to the cash indexes.

Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index climbed 0.63 to 359.10, while the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 0.23 to 195.84 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 0.45 to 334.22. Declining issues outnumbered advancers on the Big Board, however, by 777 to 668.

Rate-sensitive issues, especially those in the banking and financial-services sectors, were hit with selling as the outlook for rates turned for the worse. Citicorp went down 3/8 to 22 1/2 in Big Board composite trading of 1.4 million shares, Federal National Mortgage Association declined 7/8 to 40 on 1.2 million shares, and American Express lost 5/8 to 30 1/8.

Among regional banks, Banc One eased 5/8 to 28 7/8 as 1.9 million shares changed hands. An offering of 10 million common shares of the bank holding company was made at $29 a share. First Interstate Bancorp dropped 1 1/2 to 40 1/8, NCNB lost 5/8 to 37 5/8, MNC Financial eased 3/8 to 14 1/4 and SunTrust Banks fell 5/8 to 21 1/8.

Chevron fell 3/4 to 70 7/8, but the market's late rebound swept oil stocks higher even though oil prices extended a decline in which the price of crude has reached 18-month lows. Exxon rose 1/2 to 47 7/8, Texaco added 3/4 to 58, Mobil gained 5/8 to 63, Amoco rose 1 3/4 to 53 1/2 and Unocal went up 3/8 to 28 5/8.

Upjohn rose 2 1/8 to 42 1/8 on 1.5 million shares amid renewed speculation that the drug company could follow the lead of others in the industry and seek a merger partner. Schering-Plough was mentioned as a potential suitor for the company, along with American Home Products, traders told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Upjohn declined to comment.

Diversified Energies rose 1 1/4 to 32 1/2. The stock's advance came after a block trade of 968,000 shares, amounting to 6% of its shares outstanding and accounting for the bulk of the stock's trading. The company said it believed Service Corp. International, which owned a 7.1% stake as of early April, sold the shares into "friendly hands."

Consolidated Freightways fell 1 7/8 to 13 7/8. The company said it would have to suspend dividend payments on its common stock under provisions of an agreement for a new $860 million line of credit, as well as amendments to existing bank arrangements.

Abbott Laboratories gained 1 to 40. A Food and Drug Administration advisory committee recommended approval of a test kit, produced by the company, to help physicians assess growth or shrinkage of tumors in breast-cancer patients.

Motorola went up 1 to 86 after a federal court granted a stay of a lower-court decision that would have required the company to stop selling a computer chip found to infringe on Hitachi's patents. Separately, the company is expected to disclose plans for a new satellite-communications system that may permit expanded coverage by cellular-phone systems within a few years.

A set of dividend-related trades early in the session swelled volume in Canadian Pacific, which added 1/8 to 18 on 2.1 million shares. The stock carries a dividend yield of 5.1% and goes ex-dividend today.

Hilton Hotels rose 1 3/8 to 57 3/8 amid speculation that the company may be interested in reacquiring the Trump Castle hotel-casino in Atlantic City, N.J., from Donald Trump, who has run into financial troubles. Hilton declined to comment.

RJR Nabisco Holdings warrants fell 1/8 to 6 1/2. As expected, the company and Kohlberg Kravis Roberts announced that they are working on a refinancing that will involve retiring "a significant amount" of the junk bonds issued in its leveraged buy-out.

AMP rose 3/8 to 51. Merrill Lynch named the issue, which carries its highest investment ratings, as "focus stock of the week." Among other things, the firm said it expects AMP's earnings to begin improving before year end.

Environmental Systems went up 3/8 to 14 after Brambles Industries, the company's largest shareholder, said it hasn't sold any part of its 23% interest and isn't planning to do so. However, Brambles acknowledged that it had considered reducing its stake.

Contel, which filed for the sale of 14.5 million common shares, declined 5/8 to 27 1/8. The company said it would use the proceeds to reduce debt resulting from its Contel Cellular unit's purchase of cellular properties in February.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 0.02 to 360.81. Volume totaled 13,890,000 shares.

Fruit of the Loom dropped 7/8 to 12 1/4 on 1.9 million shares. Chairman William Farley has been reducing his holdings in the company since March; company officials couldn't be reached for comment on the stock's latest round of activity.

Oneita Industries lost 1 1/4 to 8. Oppenheimer & Co., citing weakness in the T-shirt industry, lowered its earnings estimates for the fiscal year that ends in September and the following year.

Binks Manufacturing gained 2 1/4 to 42 although the company denied it had received a takeover offer, as disclosed by Kelso Management, a major shareholder. Kelso said the bid was higher than the $45-a-share offer made earlier this year by Illinois Tool Works.
