Chase Chairman Plans to Retire As of Oct. 31 --- Butcher Will Be Succeeded By Labrecque as Chief; Reorganization Is Slated

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease and Robert Guenther
  • Date: 06/21/90

NEW YORK -- Chase Manhattan Corp. Chairman Willard C. Butcher said he will retire Oct. 31, two months earlier than expected, to clear the way for a major reorganization of the nation's second-largest banking concern. Mr. Butcher said Thomas G. Labrecque, president, will succeed him as chairman and chief executive officer, as expected. Arthur F. Ryan, currently the vice chairman in charge of Chase's highly successful consumer bank, will succeed Mr. Labrecque.

"Tom Labrecque and I have been working on some of the responses to the challenges facing the banking business and we think those responses require some organizational changes," Mr. Butcher said in an interview. "There will be some changes in the next level of the corporation and we believe we couldn't implement those changes until the succession issue was resolved." He said his long-stated plan had been to retire on Dec. 31, but that the decision was advanced in order to give Mr. Labrecque a chance to put the new organizational structure into place before the end of the year.

Details of the reorganization will be released next week, but it is understood to include cutbacks in the bank's corporate finance department and top personnel changes in other areas of the company.

Mr. Butcher said that among the challenges facing Chase, as well as other banks, is the corporate financing business, where competition for the lower volume of business is vicious and profit margins are under pressure.

"Obviously, we're going to have to skinny down there," he said. "We also face challenges in how we configure Europe, we've got to grapple with the real estate challenge, and we have to continue to grow certain important businesses like consumer banking that has been so good to us."

Mr. Labrecque said he expects the reorganization to be fully in place by the end of the year. He wouldn't comment on how many employees will lose their jobs as a result of the reorganization, but he said the negative financial effects would be outweighed by the postive effects and that most write-offs would be taken this year.

Chase isn't alone in its cutbacks. Earlier this week, Bankers Trust New York Corp. announced that it was slicing 75 people from its corporate finance department.

Manufacturers Hanover Corp. executives say that they have seen memos targeting 25% reductions in personnel for some departments, and Bank of Boston Corp. recently eliminated its highly leveraged transaction unit.

In addition, for the first quarter Chase posted disappointing earnings and a big rise in non-performing loans. Chase reported earnings of $44 million, or 20 cents a share, compared with $132 million, or $1.27 a share a year earlier. Non-accrual loans rose to $4.1 billion in the first quarter from $3.9 billion at the end of last year.

Mr. Butcher, 63 years old, will become chairman of the executive committee of the board of directors on Oct. 31 for a one-year term. He has served as chairman of Chase since 1980 when he succeeded David Rockefeller. Mr. Labrecque, 51, was named vice chairman of the board and chief operating officer in June 1980 and was subsequently elected president in April 1981. Mr. Ryan, 47, was named vice chairman for individual banking in November 1984.
