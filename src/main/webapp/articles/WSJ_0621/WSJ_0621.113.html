Bill Giving U.S. Role In New Europe Bank Clears First Hurdle

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Legislation authorizing U.S. participation in the new European Bank for Reconstruction and Development, which is expected to start approving loans to Eastern European countries next year, cleared its first hurdle in Congress. A House Banking subcommittee approved a bill that would allow the White House and the Treasury to provide $1.2 billion of the $12 billion envisioned as the bank's initial capital. Congress would be expected to appropriate about $360 million of this amount over a five-year period. The remainder of the U.S. pledge would be in the form of a callable capital commitment.

Separately, the White House asked the House and Senate Appropriations Committees to approve the first $70 million installment of the proposed U.S. "paid-in" capital.

In other action, the House panel agreed to authorize about $3.18 billion to help finance loans by the International Development Association to poorer Third World nations. The U.S. and other countries have agreed to channel about $15 billion to the World Bank affiliate over three years. The U.S. share still would have to be approved by the House and Senate Appropriations committees.

The subcommittee also voted to allocate about $500 million in funds for the U.S. Export-Import Bank for "tied-aid" loans to help U.S. companies compete for business in Eastern Europe. Such loans could be approved when other western nations offer export credits on highly-concessional terms in an effort to win contracts for companies in their countries.
