LEISURE & ARTS: TV: Nazi Art, Popular Jocks, Democracy Update

  • Author: Dorothy Rabinowitz
  • Date: 06/21/90

Friday, June 22, 9-10 p.m. EDT, on PBS (PBS air dates and times vary, so check local listings): "Evening at Pops." Melissa Manchester sings Gershwin, along with an assortment of torch songs and blues, accompanied by saxophonist Benny Carter. The centerpiece of this production is their joint rendering of the immortal "Sophisticated Lady."

Sunday, June 24, 8-9 p.m. EDT, on Arts & Entertainment: "Art in the Third Reich." The first of a two-part series on Nazi painting, sculpture and architecture. A detailed look at how fascist culture and ideology found its expression in all forms of art in the Reich, including furniture design. Nazi art, the documentary notes, drew from the styles of the period, in a sinister wedding of ideology and fashion. Repeats at midnight. (Part two airs Sunday July 1, 8-9 p.m. and repeats at midnight.)

Monday, June 25, 8-9 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "The Struggle for Democracy -- An Update." Host Patrick Watson conducts interviews with citizens and government officials in Poland, the Soviet Union and Romania.

Monday, June 25, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on ABC: "All-Star Pro Sports Awards." This first annual ceremony will name the public's choices for athlete of the year in baseball, basketball, boxing, football, golf, ice hockey, horse racing, tennis and auto racing. Winners were chosen by ballots mailed or phoned in by fans. Stars of Hollywood and television -- Clint Eastwood, John Candy, Arnold Schwarzenegger and such -- will be making guest appearances at this ceremony, the producers of which hope to make it an annual event on the order of the Oscars. If only in the interest of social responsibility, they should consider putting this particular ambition aside. The prospect of yet another annual rite of congratulation, another parade of winners discoursing lengthily on the thanks owed to family, coaches, longtime companions and astrologers is almost as chilling as the possibility of Oscar-style political manifestoes delivered by jocks. This year, at least, intentional hilarity will be provided by the awards emcee, comic Richard Lewis.

Monday, June 25, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "Firing Line Special Debate." "A United Germany: Anything to Worry About?" Former Secretary of State Henry Kissinger discusses the possible rise of the Fourth Reich with former Treasury Secretary William Simon, British Member of Parliament Roy Hattersley, U.S. Ambassador to West Germany Vernon Walters, International Herald Tribune editor John Vinocur and, of course, William F. Buckley Jr.

Tuesday, June 26, 9-10 EDT, on PBS: "Giving Up the Canal." One participant in this documentary calls it "our moon shot of the early 1900s." The building of the Panama Canal was in fact a triumph of American achievement. Where the French had been defeated by the jungle and the mosquitoes, the Americans prevailed and built the canal. In 1977, the Carter administration agreed to treaties that will give the canal to Panama in the year 2000. This somewhat deceptively titled film, narrated by the always reassuring Edwin Newman, provides riveting material on the digging of the canal, and what it calls "the U.S. city-state" in Panama. But all too soon, history is cast aside in favor of talkiness, mostly talkiness of a familiar, if not absolutely mindnumbing, kind about the failures of our Latin American policy.

Wednesday, June 27, 9-10:30 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "American Playhouse" this time out offers Tom Kempinski's aptly named "Separation," the story of a romance begun under what are, you will admit, daunting circumstances. An emotionally crippled British playwright and an American actress afflicted with a rare neurological disease face up to the challenge of love. Even people without this duo's fundamental disabilities would find it challenging to conduct an amorous relationship entirely via transatlantic telephone.

Wednesday, July 4, 8:05-9:35 EDT, on TBS: "Festival at Ford Theater." There is but one reason to watch this: the remarkable performance of presidential impersonator Jim Morris as he captures George Bush's preppy persona perfectly, with an admirable touch of spleen. Encore Tuesday, July 10, 12:35-2:a.m.

Wednesday, July 4, 9-10 p.m. EDT, on A&E: The first installment of a new 17-part documentary series, "The War Years," to be continued on subsequent Wednesdays. The World War II footage is extraordinary and mostly unfamiliar, edited so as to concentrate in almost leisurely fashion on details. The result is a harrowing, suspenseful close-up of the war, and the preparation for it. The first chapter, dealing with the Nazi staging of the invasion of Poland, and then, the onslaught itself, provides a lingering last look at the Warsaw that was. It is history as it should be told. It is, above all, history that should not be missed.
