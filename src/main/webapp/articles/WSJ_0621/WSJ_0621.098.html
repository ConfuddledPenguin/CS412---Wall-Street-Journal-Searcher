RJR Nabisco's Junk Bonds Rise As Much As 6% --- Firm Says It Studies Ways To Retire Large Amount Of Troublesome Debt

  • Author: George Anders and Peter Waldman
  • Date: 06/21/90

RJR Nabisco Inc.'s junk bonds soared as much as 6% yesterday as the company confirmed it is considering ways to retire "a significant amount" of its most troublesome bonds. As reported in yesterday's Wall Street Journal, RJR has begun a vast refinancing effort that centers on retiring at least $4 billion of high-yield "junk" bonds. The program is being negotiated by RJR's main shareholder, Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. It involves bonds that are due to have their interest rates reset by next April; difficulties with that reset could hurt the food and tobacco company's financial health.

The biggest price run-up yesterday came in RJR's convertible reset bonds of 2009, which jumped $62.50 for each $1,200 face amount, to close at $971.25 each. Those bonds now are regarded as likely candidates to be exchanged for cash or a new convertible preferred stock. RJR's reset bonds of 2007, meanwhile, climbed $42.50 for each $1,000 face amount, to $806.25. As word of RJR's refinancing plans has gradually emerged the past two weeks, both series of bonds have risen nearly 15% in price.

Junk-bond specialists were pleased about RJR's plans, but wary of sounding too enthusiastic until it's clear that all the necessary financing is in place. "It's pretty good news," said Carolyn Katz, an analyst at Goldman, Sachs & Co., "but we have to see the precise terms first."

RJR said it expects to finish its deliberations and start its refinancing program by the end of July. Bankers expect RJR to raise $2.25 billion in fresh bank loans and as much as $1.7 billion from an equity infusion by limited partnerships that KKR controls. In addition, RJR may issue $1.5 billion of convertible preferred stock in exchange for some of the reset bonds outstanding.

If the RJR refinancing is carried out as currently planned, it will reduce the company's debt-to-equity ratio from a daredevil 28:1 to a more conservative, though still high 5:1, according to sources familiar with the plan. Although that would reduce the potential profits that the leveraged buy-out firm of KKR can earn on the buy-out, it would put RJR on a sounder financial footing.

Beyond its three-paragraph news release, RJR declined to comment on its refinancing. "We think that further elaboration at this point would complicate things," a spokesman said.

Even so, some money managers quickly issued bullish assessments of the likely refinancing package. "It's pretty attractive," said one West Coast arbitrager, who had bought RJR junk bonds at prices as low as 60 cents on the dollar. "I'm not a pig," he added. "If they can get me out at 85 cents on the dollar, I'm a happy departee."

Analysts cautioned that many junk-bond holders' selling price appears to be 7% to 10% above whatever the current market price is. That suggests that as the junk bonds appreciate, RJR may have a hard time buying them back at much less than face value.

Some investors said the reported terms of the preferred -- a 10% dividend and conversion into RJR equity at about a 50% premium to the indicated current market price -- seem appealing enough. Alan Snyder, president of San Francisco-based Snyder Capital Management, argued that the preferred issue will compare favorably with a similar issue of convertible preferred stock issued by Time Warner Inc.

But a more bearish view was expressed by traders at Goldman, Sachs & Co., who suggested that RJR would have to lower the conversion premium to make the preferred trade at face value. Terms are likely to be thrashed around for the next month or more.

Analysts say it's clear that KKR is choosing a more cautious, safer course. "People believe this deal is KKR's franchise," said Edward Mally, an analyst at Salomon Brothers Inc. "They want to believe that at the end of the day, they have to make it work. This isn't a situation where they can afford to hurt bondholders."

Executives close to RJR say an improving outlook at the company may be encouraging KKR to invest more equity in its buy-out. Although RJR posts net losses because of its big interest bill, operating profit in the first quarter of this year surged 50% from a year earlier. Its operating profit from tobacco jumped 40% in the first quarter, robust growth for any industry, let alone one in which domestic demand is shrinking more than 3% a year. RJR's operating profit from food grew 18% in the first period.

"They're getting a hell of a lot more cash flow than they ever expected," said a senior executive close to RJR.

RJR also may be starting to look for growth by acquisitions, after a flurry of divestitures the past 16 months that helped reduce debt. Since the buy-out, many food-industry analysts have expected RJR to shed its shredded-wheat breakfast cereal division because the unit holds just a small slice of its market. But RJR executives have apparently decided to hold the business for now, said an executive close to Nabisco, and are even considering an acquisition to increase market share.
