REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Learning from S&L History

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/21/90

Perhaps the only good news coming out of the S&L mess is that it has awakened some policy makers to the $863 billion in potential liabilities piled up by such entities as Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac and Sallie Mae. Last month, a Treasury study of their financial safety and soundness concluded that these off-budget lending institutions must adopt stricter capital standards, a credit rating not based on their taxpayer guarantees and full disclosure in the federal budget. It makes little sense to allow these entities to operate as private-sector companies and still be backed up by federal guarantees.

However, some of them already have started wheeling out their lobbying guns. Three House committee chairmen -- Henry Gonzalez and Kika de la Garza of Texas and Gus Hawkins of California -- have written Speaker Foley demanding that reform of these government-sponsored enterprises, or GSEs, be taken off the table at the budget summit. That way any reform proposals will have to be routed through their committees, where they can be "modified."

The sudden concern of these committee chairmen for the prerogatives of these agencies is striking. Save for when the Farm Credit System went bust in 1985, no one at their respective committees could recall the last time any of them held an oversight hearing on their financial condition. Given the lack of attention that GSEs have received to date, the budget summit negotiators would be remiss if they didn't put reforming them high up on the agenda.
