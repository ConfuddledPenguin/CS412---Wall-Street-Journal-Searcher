Grand Jury Indicts Husband and Wife In Thrift Scheme

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- A federal grand jury indicted a husband and wife, both former officers of Republic Bank for Savings of Jackson, Miss., on charges of falsifying bank reports and other charges. The grand jury in Jackson charged Philip Shunk and Linda Shunk with conspiring to misapply the bank's funds, with three counts of making false bank entries, and with one count of converting one share of Country Club of Jackson stock held by the bank to their own use. Mr. Strunk was charged with seven additional counts of making false entries in bank reports.

If convicted of all charges, Mr. Shunk faces a maximum penalty of 65 years in prison and a $3 million fine. Mrs. Shunk faces up to 30 years in prison and a $1.25 million fine.

Mr. Shunk was president of the federally chartered thrift from October 1985 until December 1987, when he resigned. He was succeeded by his wife, who had been executive vice president. She remained president until she was removed by federal regulators when they took control of Republic in July 1988.

News of the indictment came as the House overwhelmingly approved $75 million in fiscal 1991 appropriations for Justice Department prosecutions and civil proceedings related to financial institutions. That represents more than a 50% increase above the current spending. The money was added to a pending bill that would provide $10.5 billion for the Justice, State, and Commerce Departments for the new budget year, which begins Oct. 1.

Separately, the Justice Department said that the 93 U.S. attorneys have been called to Washington for a meeting tomorrow with Attorney General Dick Thornburgh concerning prosecution of savings and loan fraud cases. The department said President Bush will speak to the prosecutors.

Yesterday, a private government watchdog group accused the Bush administration of tardy and weak prosecution of S&L fraud. Federal prosecutions have recouped less than 1% of taxpayers' losses that are attributable to fraud, collecting less than $100 million in restitution and fines, according to Public Citizen, a Ralph Nader-backed group.

"Our study leads to an inescapable, disturbing conclusion: The Bush administration's efforts to pursue and punish S&L fraud have been anemic at best," said Sherry Ettleson of Public Citizen.

Such comments, and the lopsided 420-1 House vote to increase government funds for cracking down on financial wrongdoers, reflect the increasing political volatility of the S&L issue. But President Bush, speaking at a news conference during a speaking trip to Alabama, declined to repeat this week's White House assertions that Democrats are to blame for much of the S&L mess and subsequent bailout. Mr. Bush said he doesn't want to "be out there saying here's who's to blame and here's who's not to blame."

Mr. Bush, sounding a theme his administration intends to repeat more and more to deflect its handling of the crisis, promised "plenty more" prosecutions of thrift officials who violated laws. Mr. Bush said he met with Mr. Thornburgh this week to discuss the cases being brought and said his administration will "put those that are guilty into the dock where they belong and see that they are brought to responsibility for what they've done."
