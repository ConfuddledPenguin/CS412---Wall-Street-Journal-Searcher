House Backs Overriding Federal-Worker Bill's Veto

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- The House cast a solid vote for overriding President Bush's veto of a bill that would ease restrictions on political activity by federal workers, but the real test comes in the Senate today. The House voted 327-93 to override the veto, which was Mr. Bush's 12th since taking office. The Senate, which voted 67-30 for the bill last month, is scheduled to vote today on overriding the veto, and Democratic proponents of the measure and Republican opponents both say the outcome will be very close. As in the House, a veto override requires at least a two-thirds vote.

The Senate vote would have been 68-32 with all senators voting, based on later statements by the three senators who didn't vote. Those favoring the legislation included all 55 Democrats and 13 Republicans, while 32 Republicans opposed it. So two of the 13 Republicans will have to join their GOP colleagues in a vote to sustain the veto, assuming no other switches occur.

The bill vetoed by Mr. Bush was the more-modest Senate version, accepted by the House last week. It would let federal employees engage in more off-the-job political activity, including holding positions in political parties, attending party conventions as delegates and raising money from fellow union members. But running for elective office would still generally be banned. And on-the-job political activity, such as wearing a campaign button at work, wouldn't be permitted anymore.
