NASD Plans A One-Share, One-Vote Rule

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- The National Association of Securities Dealers, in a move cheered by shareholder groups, said it plans to introduce a "one-share, one-vote" rule similar to that recently struck down by a federal appeals court panel. In an interview, NASD President Joseph Hardiman said the group is eager to maintain the rule despite last week's appeals court decision that the Securities and Exchange Commission had overstepped its authority in adopting the rule. He said he expects to present "reasonably promptly" a proposal to the NASD advisory board and board of governors.

The move comes at a time when many on Wall Street expected the court ruling to reignite a battle among U.S. exchanges to lure companies to list shares. Exchanges could use relaxed listing requirements as incentives to companies concerned about hostile takeovers.

But Mr. Hardiman said the NASD "is not going to trigger a race to the bottom" in listing standards. "We will abide by 19c4 {the one-share, one-vote rule} as long as it is in effect, and if appeals fail we hope to adopt a similar rule," he said.

Mr. Hardiman said that part of the reason behind the move is an agreement that the NASD has with states that allows the over-the-counter market exemptions from state securities laws. If the NASD adopts rules of corporate governance that are more lenient than those of the exchanges, he said, NASD dealers will fall under tougher scrutiny from state regulators.

Mr. Hardiman quickly added, however, that his stance "has to be tempered by what the competition is doing." The New York Stock Exchange adopted its own version of one-share, one-vote after the SEC rule went into effect, but the American Stock Exchange and Nasdaq didn't.

Amex Chairman James Jones said the exchange is still studying the court's decision. He added, "But the fact that we haven't made a decision doesn't mean one thing either way."

A Big Board spokeswoman said: "We don't see things changing immediately. We don't see many companies that would want to do anything with their corporate structure that would fall under the 19c4 rule. If they do, we would review the rules as the situation arises."

An SEC spokeswoman said the agency is "pleased" with the NASD's action but declined to comment further. She said the agency hadn't yet decided whether to appeal the court's decision.

The SEC's rule prohibited already traded companies from issuing a class of common stock with lesser voting rights. That practice had become common in the mid-1980s as a takeover defense. But the SEC's rule was challenged by the Business Roundtable, a trade group of large companies, which argued that the federal rule overstepped the traditional rights of states in corporate governance. The appeals court agreed.

The SEC rule never precluded public offerings from having two classes, because shareholders buying into those offerings would know of the limited voting rights. The agency's argument for 19c4 was that holders shouldn't have rights taken away when they bought the stock under other conditions.

The NASD plan met with applause from shareholder groups. "That was what we had pressed for: That the exchanges would do this on their own without regulation," said Ralph Whitworth of United Shareholders Association. "We believe that dual-class voting is the ultimate management-entrenchment device."

He added that he expects the move to put pressure on the Amex to abide by a one-share, one-vote rule, especially if the Big Board holds the line on its 19c4-type rule.
