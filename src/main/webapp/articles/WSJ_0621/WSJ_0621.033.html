International: SCA of Sweden Agrees to Buy Reedpack Ltd. --- By Stephen D. Moore Special to The Wall Street Journal

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/21/90

STOCKHOLM -- Sweden's Svenska Cellulosa AB agreed to acquire British forest products concern Reedpack Ltd., continuing the spate of recent takeovers sweeping Europe's pulp and paper industry. SCA agreed to pay #543.9 million ($937.4 million) for Reedpack's fully diluted share capital and will assume #500 million in debt. Reedpack was formed two years ago in a #610 million management buy-out of Reed International PLC's European paper, packaging and office supplies operations.

SCA's offer is nearly triple the #210 million in equity and preference capital injected by management and institutional investors under the buy-out -- Britain's biggest management buy-out to date. Reedpack directors unanimously recommended the offer and irrevocable acceptances have been received from investors representing 75% of Reedpack shares outstanding, the companies said.

The transaction remains subject to shareholder approval, as well as an antitrust review by U.K. authorities. British officials raised no objections during preliminary contacts, SCA said. To facilitate the antitrust review, the company agreed to sell its minority stakes in two U.K.-based packaging ventures to their controlling owner, Jefferson Smurfit Group PLC.

SCA Chief Executive Sverker Martin-Loef said the big Swedish forestry group had been a suitor when the Reed operations were spun off in 1988 but was outbid by the buy-out group.

The acquisition would strengthen SCA's position as one of Europe's biggest producers of corrugated packaging products -- and make the Swedish concern a major producer of newsprint using recycled waste paper as raw material.

SCA said interest costs for the debt-financed acquisition will dilute per-share earnings this year, but Mr. Martin-Loef predicted that gradual synergy gains -- plus effects of substantial capital investments made by Reedpack during the past two years -- would add about 10% to SCA's previous projection of per-share earnings in 1992.

Swedish investors seemed to approve of the marriage. SCA's B-free shares, the series most traded internationally, climbed one krona to 115 kronor in moderately heavy trading on the Stockholm Stock Exchange yesterday.

But some analysts questioned the acquisition's high price -- a multiple of 20 times Reedpack's fiscal 1990 earnings -- and warned that SCA's 1990 pretax profit could plunge by as much as 20% as a result.

"This shows that companies are prepared to pay exceptional prices to achieve market positions, size and long-term growth in European pulp and paper, though markets probably will soften for the next two or three years," said forestry analyst Stephen Weller at London securities firm UBS/Phillips & Drew.

Swallowing Reedpack would increase SCA's annual sales by about 25% to an estimated 33 billion Swedish kronor ($5.44 billion).
