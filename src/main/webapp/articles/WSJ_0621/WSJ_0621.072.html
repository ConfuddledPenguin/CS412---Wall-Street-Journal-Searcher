MCI Unveils Plan to Give Small Business Long-Distance Service Used by Big Firms

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 06/21/90

WASHINGTON -- MCI Communications Corp. rolled out a long-distance service designed to give small- and medium-sized business customers features that generally had been available only to big companies. The new service, called MCI Vision, is based on a flat per-minute charge and is targeted at companies that spend between $500 and $50,000 a month on long-distance service.

Timothy Price, MCI's vice president for general business, said the package includes flat-rate pricing, volume discounts, virtual private-line services, and a comprehensive bill that lists credit-card calls as well as domestic and international long-distance charges for all of a company's branch offices. Calls can be tracked by employee, departments or calling cards.

The $18 billion smaller-business segment of the long-distance industry is getting more attention as competition has squeezed margins in the residential and big-business segments. "MCI is focusing its sales force on the middle market because that's where the richest margins are," said Edward Greenberg of Morgan Stanley. "Frankly, the margins are drying up in the high end."

Jack Grubman, an analyst with PaineWebber, said the MCI move shows that competition is shifting away from price cutting. "This isn't some deep discount," he said. "It's an attempt to bring down functionality to a lower market segment." MCI, he said, is "clearly reacting to AT&T's success in defending its share of the middle market."

While American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s share of the middle market is about 50%, MCI figures it has 14% to 15%. To increase its share, MCI has beefed up its sales force by about 500 since the beginning of the year, Mr. Price said.

At AT&T, Joseph Nacchio, vice president-marketing for business communications services, said AT&T could replicate the MCI package in a couple of days, but wants to watch MCI's performance first. "We were waiting for some killer product," he said. "But I didn't find anyone here lying down because bullets were flying."

Investors apparently expected a more significant announcement as well. MCI's shares fell 87.5 cents to $41.625 in national over-the-counter trading. AT&T's shares rose 37.5 cents to $43.125 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

To promote the new service, MCI launched a multimillion-dollar advertising campaign developed by Wells, Rich, Greene Inc. The campaign, which uses the tag line "Big-business long distance for your business," includes 15- and 30-second television spots. The spots are being aired during network morning and evening news shows and sports programs, as well as CNN business shows and ESPN baseball games. The company also has a print campaign that will be run in newspapers and local business journals.

Among the MCI Vision features is a "hotline" service that allows a caller to connect automatically to a telephone in another city. Another is a "remote" service that allows a company to maintain a local number that actually rings at a distant location. If a company does a lot of business in one city, but doesn't want to maintain an office there, it can set up a number that would be answered at the company's main office.

Basic rates for domestic service are 20 cents a minute between 8 a.m. and 5 p.m. for direct dialed calls into the MCI network and 15 cents for calls placed through a direct connection between the customer and the MCI network. Off-peak calls are 20% less. Rates for international calls are based on the destination, time of day and type of access to the MCI network. MCI also is offering customers the option of delivering bills on paper or magnetic tape.

"This is a one-size fits-all product," Mr. Price said. "It can grow as the company grows. Some companies that end up buying this product may not use every feature -- yet."

Earlier this year, in another major attempt to gain market share in the general business market, MCI announced plans to acquire Telecom USA for $1.25 billion.
