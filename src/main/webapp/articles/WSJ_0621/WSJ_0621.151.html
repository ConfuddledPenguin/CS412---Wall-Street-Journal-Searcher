Marketing & Media: Pathe Confident On Bid for MGM, Despite Reports

  • Author: Kathleen A. Hughes and Dennis Kneale
  • Date: 06/21/90

Pathe Communications Corp., amid a flurry of reports that its $1.2 billion bid for MGM/UA Communications Co. is on the brink of collapse, said it remains confident it will complete the acquisition of the movie studio. However, yesterday, Pathe's negotiations with its chief financial backer in the bid, Time Warner Inc., appeared to be deadlocked. So far, Time Warner has refused to alter the terms under which it agreed to finance about half of Pathe's $20-a-share offer.

Edmund Hamburger, general counsel to Pathe, said Pathe has asked MGM/UA to extend the original deadline of June 23 and the request is "under discussion."

Officials of Time Warner say they continue to hope the Pathe-MGM/UA deal goes through and believe that it would be a good one for Time Warner. "We haven't changed our position," one company executive said.

Yesterday, MGM/UA shares closed at $16.625, off 50 cents, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. A spokesman for MGM/UA said: "The negotiations are proceeding. Our position is {that} nothing has changed."

Time Warner had said that Pathe itself must come up with $450 million in equity as part of the offer. Pathe also must come up with another $150 million in equity, rather than debt, from outside parties. Time Warner officials say they aren't prepared to put up any equity themselves or provide any other kind of financing beyond the existing agreement.

But Pathe, headed by Italian financier Giancarlo Parretti, has had trouble raising the equity and has asked Time Warner to ease the equity requirements, a request that Time Warner has rejected.

Mr. Hamburger said Pathe is now arguing that a loan to Pathe from Comfinance S.A., a Luxembourg-based affiliate of Mr. Parretti, should be considered equity because the terms allow it to be repaid in Pathe stock. Comfinance has agreed to provide Pathe with $368 million it raised through a combination of bank loans and asset sales. "We are arguing that we meet the original terms of Time Warner," said Mr. Hamburger.

Pathe already has deposited $200 million in cash toward the acquisition. In the event that the deal fell through, that amount would be converted into 10 million shares, at $20 each, and held in an escrow account until any litigation was resolved.

A second issue still under negotiation between Time Warner and Pathe is additional financing for MGM/UA movie production. Warner had asked Pathe to provide $200 million for that purpose. A Pathe spokesman, Craig Parsons, said Pathe has secured a bank credit line of $125 million toward movie production. "Pathe is saying we think that is sufficient," Mr. Parsons said.

Privately, some Time Warner officials acknowledge that the odds of Pathe completing the transaction on acceptable terms appear to be diminishing. The snags also leave some analysts on Wall Street doubting that the MGM/UA acquisition will ever be completed.

"I've always thought it was an iffy deal. That's going to be my attitude until the ink is dry," said Alan Kassen of Morgan Stanley & Co.

"From the start," added analyst John Reidy of Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., "the only thing that gave this deal credibility was the entrance of Time Warner onto the scene, and {now} even they have some constraints as to what they're willing to do."

The question, Mr. Reidy said, is what other deep-pockets buyer could emerge should the Pathe bid falter. Because an earlier offer last year from Rupert Murdoch's News Corp. didn't work out, that company is an unlikely bidder, he added.

Last October, MGM/UA terminated a $1.5 billion agreement to be acquired by Qintex Australia Ltd. after Qintex failed to deliver a $50 million letter of credit.
