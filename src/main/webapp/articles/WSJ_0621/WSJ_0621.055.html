GERMANY'S FUTURE CAPITAL

As German unity nears, the argument over a future capital continues to grow.

Berlin, capital of the old Germany, is working hard to prepare to resume its role as the power center of the new nation. But Bonn, West Germany's "temporary" capital since 1949, has launched a campaign to hold on to parliament, ministries and foreign embassies.

What's likely to emerge is a compromise, with the head of state, whose role is strictly ceremonial, moving to Berlin and the city being made the official capital, while the real seats of power -- the chancellor's office, the parliament and government ministries -- stay in Bonn.

A full move to Berlin by all of Bonn's politicians, bureaucrats, lobbyists, diplomats and journalists could cost, pro-Bonn campaigners say, at least 80 billion marks ($48 billion).

APPEAL FROM AFRICA

At a summit of African leaders with French President Francois Mitterrand, the Africans appealed for Western help to salvage their shattered economies before they can embrace multiparty democracy. "Africa is suffering more from its economy than from its politics," Togo's President Gnassingbe Eyadema told Mr. Mitterrand. Evidently in agreement with that view, Mr. Mitterrand told the French daily Le Monde that "it is for the Africans to decide who rules them and how."

LATIN AMERICAN HEALTH CRISIS

Some 170 million people in Latin America are denied basic nutrition and health care, and more than 40 million live in critical poverty, a World Health Organization official told an international conference on "Democracy and Health" in Caracas. The official, Carlyle Guerra de Macedo, said health conditions in the region had worsened in the 1980s. Although the region spent $45 billion on medical care, an estimated 30% of the money was wasted or wrongly spent, he said.

CORRUPTION IN HONG KONG

In a Hong Kong magistrate's court yesterday, Charles Warwick Reid, former head of the commercial-crime unit of the Hong Kong Legal Department, pleaded guilty to corruption. Mr. Reid was charged under the British colony's Prevention of Bribery Ordinance with possession of assets disproportionate to his government pay. The assets cited include 2.4 million New Zealand dollars (US$1.4 million) in bank balances and three tracts of property in his native New Zealand. The offense to which Mr. Reid pleaded guilty carries a maximum penalty of 10 years in prison and a fine of 100,000 Hong Kong dollars (US$12,840).

FRENCH LANGUAGE CHANGES

France's Premier Michel Rocard accepted a report by the High Council of the French Language incorporating 15 pages of recommended spelling changes. The premier said he will ask National Education Minister Lionel Jospin to assure the changes are taught in all schools as of next year. The recommendations indicate the hyphen, much used in French, is all but being discarded. Thus, "cow-boy" and "week-end" -- two words borrowed from English -- will under the new rules become "cowboy" and "weekend."

TOBACCO RESTRICTIONS IN CHINA

China's legislature began deliberating a proposed law that would ensure state revenue from tobacco sales and address the risks of smoking. The draft bill would restrict cigarette imports, ban the sale of cigarettes to teenagers and require China's tobacco industry, the largest in the world, to warn that "smoking is harmful to human health." Under the proposed law, the state would control all tobacco imports and exports. China has an estimated 300 million smokers among its 1.1 billion population.

SWISS REFUGEE LAW

The Swiss parliament gave its final approval to more restrictive immigration legislation.

The new law, effective tomorrow, speeds up screening procedures to separate genuine refugees fleeing political persecution from people seeking better living conditions. In a bid to make Switzerland less attractive to economic migrants, the law also prohibits asylum seekers from working for the first three months of their stay.

Last year, nearly 25,000 people asked Switzerland for refugee status, while a further 40,000 requests remained outstanding from previous years. More than 95% of applications processed in 1989 were rejected. Turks constituted the largest group of asylum seekers, followed by Sri Lankans.

Authorities have anticipated there will be at least 32,000 applications for refugee status this year. In a country where 16% of the 6.7 million inhabitants are foreigners, the influx of immigrants has become a contentious political issue.
