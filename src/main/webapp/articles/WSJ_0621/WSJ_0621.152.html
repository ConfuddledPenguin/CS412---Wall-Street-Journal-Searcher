Giving a Hand: Young, Urban Professionals Work to Show New York Cares

  • Author: John A. Conway
  • Date: 06/21/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

YUPPIE, THE 1980'S ACRONYM for "young urban professional," became a synonym for greedy strivers. Like most blanket condemnations, it was wrong.

A few typical young New York professionals banded together in 1985 to figure out how busy people with little free time could do volunteer work. Today, New York Cares, the organization they formed, sends hundreds of volunteers to staff soup kitchens, visit the elderly, haul debris at low-cost housing sites, look after AIDS sufferers and crack babies, tutor children, and pitch in on the myriad thankless tasks imposed by inner-city problems.

Real estate analyst Andrea Gittings was one of the original four, along with Andrew Luger, now an assistant U.S. attorney, and Jeanne Straus, who works in her family's broadcasting business. Ms. Gittings says the idea sprang from seeing the "people we had to step over on the way to work." By 1988, the four had an organization, a full-time director and a mailing address.

The nerve center of New York Cares is a four-room walk-up a block from Bloomingdale's, which executive director Kenneth Adams rents for only $6,000 a year. ("The landlady like volunteers," he says.) Besides Mr. Adams, the overhead is his staff of three. Everything else, from paint and furniture to computers and legal services, is donated. Working with a 1990 budget of less than $200,000, Mr. Adams runs an army of 3,000 volunteers, about 700 of them working at any one time on one of his 60-odd "team projects." Fifty to 70 of his young volunteers keep track of the rest, others run orientation sessions for the approximately 130 new volunteers that show up each month.

The disparity between the size of Mr. Adams's army and the number at work is dictated by the nature of New York Cares. All are young, 25 to 35, most are single, and all have full-time jobs. The time they give comes after (or before) regular jobs. For some, this is once a week, for others once a month, and some at even longer intervals.

The wide variety of chores is an advantage. Volunteers stand ready to give time but are not sure what jobs they can handle. One young woman, for instance, explained that she started taking city children on outings but discovered "I couldn't stand kids." Now she hauls trash from housing-rehab sites. "You'd be surprised," she says, "at the satisfaction you can get when you see a dumpster finally fill up."

The recruiting, all word of mouth, produces remarkably durable connections. Founder Gittings, for example, still puts in 15 hours a week as president and treasurer of New York Cares and finds time to visit a disabled man each week. Ms. Straus, a director, does the same for a house-bound woman. Mr. Luger gave up the New York Cares presidency when he went into public service but is still on the board and manages one shut-in visit a week.

Mr. Adams, a 30-year-old, Manhattan-born graduate of Vermont's Middlebury College, passed up a shot at Yale's management school to take on his job at New York Cares. ("My mother still wonders.") A slender, intense embodiment of a Central Casting yuppie, he sees New York Cares developing into a Peace Corps for the Big Apple. He thinks it could serve as a model for a national network of volunteer bands of young professionals. (Two copies have been created in Atlanta and Washington, D.C.) Now he is helping New York corporations organize in-house volunteer organizations.

Mr. Adams's only concern is the same one Ms. Gittings remembers. "The volunteers are out there. Our nightmare is we could be overwhelmed by their numbers."
