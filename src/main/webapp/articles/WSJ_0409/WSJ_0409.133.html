Foreign Exchange: Dollar's Climb Against Yen Is Expected To Continue Despite Efforts to Slow Rise

  • Author: Candace Cumberbatch Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/09/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar is expected to continue its climb against the yen, despite any turbulent trading that may be ahead. Any profit-taking and central-bank intervention that may occur probably wouldn't be enough to knock the dollar off course, currency analysts say. They cite skepticism about the new trade accord between the U.S. and Japan and low confidence in Japan's financial markets as influences working on the dollar's behalf.

"To stay cautiously long dollars" is how many traders may try to play the market, says David Lapin, national foreign-exchange sales manager at Bank of America, Los Angeles. "We're still looking for a stronger dollar."

In late afternoon New York trading Friday, the dollar was at 157.65 yen, up from 157.40 late Thursday. But the dollar was below the 39-month high of 160.30 yen it reached Monday before retreating on profit-taking before this weekend's Group of Seven meeting of major industrial nations in Paris. G-7 nations include the U.S., Japan, Britain, West Germany, France, Italy and Canada.

In early trading Monday, the dollar briefly sank below 156 yen, responding to optimistic Japanese news coverage about the G-7 meeting. The dollar later regained some ground and closed Tokyo's morning session at 156.70.

The dollar traded at 1.6950 marks late Friday, off from 1.6995 marks. Sterling was at $1.6425, slightly higher than $1.6422 late Thursday.

Because of the yen's incessant weakness during the past 15 months, the dollar's relationship to the Japanese currency has become the primary concern of the foreign-exchange market and global-monetary officials. While the dollar has soared against the yen recently, it has barely budged against the mark and other major foreign currencies.

Since February 1987, market participants have believed that the G-7 is content when the dollar is in a trading range of 120 to 140 yen, a band that would foster stable exchange rates and help diminish inflationary pressures and trade imbalances in the U.S. and Japan.

Another round of profit-taking could hit the dollar if it surges to 160 yen, and central banks may fight to keep it from soaring above that level with more concerted sales, some traders say.

Even so, the dollar's uptrend isn't seen crumbling anytime soon.

Traders are still on the lookout for "the best place to buy" whenever the dollar slips, says Takaharu Fukawa, a treasury officer at Sumitomo Trust & Banking Co.

There were some suspicions that the dollar would come under pressure as soon as the U.S. and Japan hammered out an agreement to solve their sticky trade problems. But that probably won't be the case, even though U.S. criticism of Japan is abating.

U.S. and Japanese officals agreed on a tentative plan Thursday, the so-called Structural Impediments Initiative, that aims to topple trade barriers in Japan and help the U.S. compete more in that nation's markets. But until the agreement is completed and Japan is out of political hot water with the U.S., the trade accord probably won't be used as an excuse to sell dollars, some currency analysts say.

External and internal pressures on Japan to fix its trade imbalance contributed to the lessening of confidence in that nation and the battering of the yen.

"I don't see any reason for a sea change in sentiment" because trade relations between the U.S. and Japan are warming, says Carl Weinberg, chief economist at High Frequency Economics Ltd., an international economic-research firm in New York.

The U.S. trade deficit with Japan, the largest with any single country, contracted to $2.86 billion in January from $3.49 billion in December. Last year, the total shortfall was $49 billion.

Meanwhile, the battery of bearish yen fundamentals is expected to keep the stronger dollar's motor running.

Capital continues to leak out of Japan, where the stock and bond markets have been in turmoil for months; interest rates remain relatively low, and land prices are astronomically high. "There's no real good place to put money in Japan right now," says Mr. Weinberg.

Despite the growing belief that Japanese investors will have a big appetite for European investments this year, any diversification of portfolios probably won't be very detrimental to the dollar, Mr. Weinberg contends.

There is a "growing pie" of Japanese capital, he says, adding that the slice for the U.S. should be enough to keep the dollar "comfortable" at high levels.

But despite the dollar's impressive 11% rise against the yen so far this year, there remains an undercurrent of doubt among some traders about the potential of the currency to climb in the near term.

"I think we're going to have temporary dollar weakness," says Christiane Mandell, a trader at Security Pacific Merchant Bank in Los Angeles. The dollar, she says, may head toward 150 yen soon.

On New York's Commodity Exchange Friday, current-delivery gold settled at $380.70 an ounce, up $5.90. Estimated volume was a moderate 2.6 million ounces.

In Hong Kong, gold opened Monday at $380.10 an ounce.
