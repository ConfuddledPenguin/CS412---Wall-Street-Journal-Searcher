Computer Horizons Says 1st-Quarter Net Slumped but Operating Results Climbed

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 04/09/90

Computer Horizons Corp. officials estimated that first-quarter net income fell 57% from the year before, but excluding a year-earlier gain from an accounting change, earnings advanced 50%. The computer-systems integrator cited new contracts and the benefits of a fourth-quarter 1989 restructuring for the improved operating results. The company expects second-quarter results to exceed the first quarter's and it projects, without being specific, a "very strong" year.

Computer Horizons also rejected a major shareholder's request for board representation and for a standstill agreement.

Computer Horizons, based in Mountain Lakes, N.J., expects to report net of "better than 25 cents a share," or $600,000, compared with $1.4 million, or 59 cents a share, John J. Cassese, president and chief executive officer, said in an interview. But year-earlier earnings before the accounting change, which was related to deferred taxes, were $401,000, or 16 cents a share.

Revenue exceeded $23 million, up at least 17% from $19.7 million, Mr. Cassese estimated.

In 1989's fourth quarter, Computer Horizons charged $3.3 million to restructure and $600,000 to cover bad debts and for other reserves. The revamping "opened us up in 1990 with reduced costs," said Bernhard Hubert, senior vice president and chief financial officer.

"We closed or downsized seven offices," cutting selling, general and administrative staff 15%, Mr. Hubert said. Computer Horizons wrote off a few small balance-sheet items and "downsized some compensation plans," Mr. Hubert said.

Full-year 1989 "is blurred by the restructuring charge. But for that {charge}, we'd have made 40 cents" a share, Mr. Hubert said. For 1989, Computer Horizons posted a loss of $291,000, or 55 cents a share.

The business of integrating computer systems has plenty of players, big and small. One of the company's strategies is to work in partnership with the majors, Mr. Cassese said, noting that the company is working with International Business Machines Corp. to put together a system for an IBM client.

And contracts Computer Horizons itself has signed recently include systems work for Exxon Corp. and the A.C. Nielsen unit of Dun & Bradstreet Corp. The company also continues to do significant work with General Electric Co., Mr. Cassese said.

Recently, Compagnie Generale d'Informatique, a Paris company with systems-integration interests, boosted its stake in Computer Horizons to 16.8%, bumping up against the level at which Computer Horizons' poison-pill anti-takeover rights would kick in. Such rights are designed to make a hostile takeover prohibitively expensive.

In a March 22 letter to Mr. Cassese, CGI President Bernard Chapot said CGI regrets Computer Horizons managers' "persistent refusal . . . to discuss areas of common interest. . . ." Mr. Chapot calls the poison pill "unwarranted."

CGI then proposed a six-month standstill accord. CGI said it wouldn't make a tender offer or wage a proxy fight for Computer Horizons. In return, Computer Horizons would add one CGI representative to the board and name an additional director independent of both companies.

But in a letter dated April 5, Mr. Cassese told Mr. Chapot, "If, as you have been holding out, you have any suggestions that will benefit" Computer Horizons and its shareholders -- "not just CGI -- we want to hear them. We keep meeting with you to hear them, but we've heard nothing yet."
