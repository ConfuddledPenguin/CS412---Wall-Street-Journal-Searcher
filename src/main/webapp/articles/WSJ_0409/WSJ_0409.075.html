Marketing & Media: Heinz to Unveil Recyclable Bottle For Its Ketchup --- Squeezable Plastic Container Designed to Meet Concern On Solid-Waste Disposal

  • Author: James S. Hirsch
  • Date: 04/09/90

PITTSBURGH -- Add ketchup bottles to the growing list of environmentally friendly containers. H.J. Heinz Co., the world's largest ketchup maker, said it will introduce a recyclable plastic ketchup bottle next year. Although the package material, called polyethylene terephthalate, or PET, has been used for soft-drink bottles since the 1970s, the food company spent three years and $8 million developing the material for its own bottles. It worked on the project with Continental PET Technologies, a division of Continental Can Co. that also makes PET containers for soft drinks.

The move is the latest response to increased concerns over environmental problems created by nonrecyclable plastics. Last month, Cincinnati-based Procter & Gamble Co. said it would begin to sell some of its detergents and cleaners in bottles made from recycled plastic.

Heinz said the recyclable package will replace its opaque, squeezable plastic container, which was introduced in 1983 and accounts for 60% of the 190 million ketchup bottles Heinz sells in the U.S. each year. The rest are glass. The new recyclable package also is squeezable but is clear like a glass jar, which the company believes consumers will prefer.

The new container "meets consumer demands on two levels: the recycling issue as well as the functionality of the package," said Brian Ruder, vice president of the consumer-products division for Heinz U.S.A.

Heinz said that the change will require some retooling of its factories but that the new package will not increase ketchup prices.

Consumers will be asked to put the recyclable ketchup container in a bag for curb-side pick-up. But only homes that are in a region served by special reclamation facilities, where the containers are recycled into consumer goods, can participate. There are currently about 30 such facilities, but Heinz says that number will increase as the special containers become more widespread.
