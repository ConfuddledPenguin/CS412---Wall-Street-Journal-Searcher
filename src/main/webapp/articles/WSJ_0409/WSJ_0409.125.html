Abreast of the Market: Stock Market Shrugs Off Takeover Slump --- Share Buy-Backs Offer Some Help

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 04/09/90

NEW YORK -- The takeover business may be in the doldrums, but the stock market doesn't seem to care. Six months ago, the collapse of a proposed deal to take over UAL Corp. battered the already shaky merger-and-acquisition business. It also sent the Dow Jones Industrial Average plunging more than 190 points to 2569.26 on Oct. 13, 1989, effectively eliminating what many analysts termed "the merger premium" built into stock prices.

Since then the deal business has remained depressed. Data compiled by Birinyi Associates, a stock-market strategy firm, show that the value of mergers and acquisitions announced in the first quarter of 1990 totaled $12.5 billion, down from $23.3 billion a year ago, and $68.5 billion in the first quarter of 1988.

Yet in the six months since the UAL debacle, the Dow hit a new high, slipped again, and now stands at 2717.12; that is within 93 points of its record high of 2810.15, an overall decline of less than 3%.

No one is claiming this is a roaring bull market. But the fact that stock prices are holding their own while takeover deals have seemingly disappeared does give some analysts a bit more confidence in the U.S. stock market.

"Deals were one of the two principal sources of capital for the financial markets in 1988 and 1989," says Hugh Johnson, chief investment officer at First Albany Corp. "For us to be able to hold together as well as we have is pretty impressive."

One reason stock prices haven't collapsed, he points out, is that while the high-profile leveraged buy-outs and acquisitions that drew so much attention in 1988 and 1989 have virtually disappeared, corporations still are buying up stock through stock buy-backs. He cites Federal Reserve data that show corporations purchased shares at the annual rate of $143.33 billion for the first three quarters of 1989, and that those purchases continued at a $93 billion annual rate in the fourth quarter of 1989, the latest period for which the Federal Reserve provides data.

"The point is, while corporations are certainly buying a lot less stock, they're still buying," Mr. Johnson says. "Corporate stock purchases didn't just disappear."

Beyond that, Mr. Johnson points out that foreign investors, the other big source of capital in 1988 and 1989, are still putting their money into U.S. financial markets. Indeed, he contends that the troubles afflicting the Japanese stock market since the beginning of this year are giving U.S. stocks and bonds a boost as international portfolio managers divert money away from Japan and into the U.S. and West Germany.

Although there isn't yet solid data to support this contention, Mr. Johnson cites the dollar's increasing strength against the Japanese yen as evidence that funds are flowing this way. And while much of that money is aimed at the U.S. bond market, it indirectly bolsters the stock market by producing lower interest rates than would otherwise be the case.

"If you help the bond market, you help the stock market," he says.

New money from individual investors, much of it funneled into the stock market through mutual funds, also has helped offset the decline of corporate stock purchases in the past six months. For the first nine months of 1989, mutual funds were net sellers of stock at an annual rate of $5.8 billion. But in the fourth quarter of 1989, just as corporate stock purchases were slackening, mutual funds were net purchasers of stock to the tune of $22.4 billion on an annualized basis.

Finally, the first quarter of 1990 brought a modest upturn in the economy, effectively allaying fears among many investors that the U.S. was headed for a recession. The prospect that corporate profits later this year will reflect the modest rebound in the economy is bolstering stock prices, especially in the so-called cyclical stocks that move with the economic cycle.

Yet while Mr. Johnson is encouraged that those various props are supporting stock prices, he cautions that if one or more of the props falls away, prices could fall, too.

"If for some reason or another mutual funds don't like the outlook or the Japanese market stabilizes, we might not get the kind of nourishment we need from that money flowing into the market," he says. "That makes it kind of a risky period."

And some analysts argue that the Dow industrials don't really reflect what has happened to the broader stock market following the slump in takeover deals. The broader market is where the loss of the "merger premium" has been felt the most, they say.

"The Dow may be at 2700, but to conclude that the rest of the market has come back that far is an illusion," says Suresh Bhirud, chief portfolio strategist at Oppenheimer. "You can look at the Dow and say it hasn't been a bad market since last October, but if you're running a broad portfolio of stocks, you've been in more or less a bear market." In particular, he points to the Nasdaq over-the-counter index, which has lost some 15% since last October.

Mr. Bhirud says the big problem with the merger boom in 1988 and 1989 is that it persuaded many investors to abandon conservative forms of stock valuation based on earnings in favor of such methods as "breakup value" and "price to cash flow ratios."

"If you look at the market prior to Oct. 13, you'll find at least a couple of dozen stocks that were rumored to be takeover targets selling at 20 to 30 times earnings," he says. "Those kinds of values were not really justified except under some immensely rosy circumstances."

And investors who chased some of the stocks that were supposedly targets of takeover attempts last year have been burned this year. Birinyi Associates compiled an index of speculative stocks, including such names as USX, Hilton, Mead and Unisys, that were the targets of takeovers that were never consummated. So far this year, the index is down 11.9%.

"That tells us that investors have to be a little more disciplined," says Mr. Birinyi. "It isn't going to pay to be an ambulance chaser."

Joseph A. Feshbach, Prudential-Bache's chief technical stock-market analyst for six years, has been hired by Shearson Lehman Hutton. He is related to, but unconnected with, the Feshbach brothers of Palo Alto, Calif., well-known shortsellers who aim to profit from stock price declines. His Prudential-Bache market comment Wednesday said the "next test" for the industrial average will be 2600. "All the trend measures are extremely negative," he said, citing a "breakdown in the Dow Jones Utility Index" and "extremely high over-the-counter volume" compared with Big Board volume. But he expects "good support" when the industrial average hits 2580 to 2600.

Friday's Market Activity

Stock prices drifted lower in slow trading as investors continued to dump regional bank stocks and other issues considered vulnerable to earnings problems.

The latest bout of selling came after the March employment report provided an indication of possible weakness in the economy -- a rise in nonfarm payrolls that was considerably smaller than expected.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped 4.05 to 2717.12, but managed to record a gain for the week of 9.91 points. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index dropped 0.65 to 340.08 while the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index fell 0.37 to 186.78 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index fell 0.78 to 316.62.

Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 137,490,000 shares. Declining issues on the Big Board were ahead of advancers 860 to 622.

The employment data, released before the opening, showed that payrolls rose just 26,000 last month -- well below the consensus forecast of a Dow Jones Capital Markets survey of economists, which projected an increase of 178,000. The jobless rate fell 0.1 point, to 5.2%.

Although economists said the report eased concerns that the Federal Reserve might tighten monetary policy in order to keep inflation under control, a round of sell programs resulting from stock-index arbitrage sent prices lower in early trading. The selloff in regional banks' shares then regained momentum, keeping pressure on the broad market.

The sector's downturn began Thursday, when analysts turned negative on the group after meeting with federal banking regulators who indicated that the companies face extensive reviews of their loan portfolios.

Banks are expected to begin reporting first-quarter results this week, and the tough talk on loans has spurred expectations that the banks' lending problems will translate into weak earnings.

PNC Financial, which estimated that its nonperforming assets rose about 43% during the first quarter, plunged 3 7/8 to 34. Because of an increase in its provision for loan losses, the concern said it expects to report net income for the quarter fell 35% to 40% from the year-ago quarter.

Bank of New York fell 1 1/4 to 30 as 1.4 million shares changed hands. Other regional bank stocks that were hit with selling included NCNB, which dropped 3/4 to 35 3/8; National City, which fell 1 1/4 to 33 3/8; Mellon Bank, down 1 1/8 to 24 1/8; Citizens & Southern, down 5/8 to 24 5/8; Sovran Financial, down 5/8 to 32, and Signet Banking, down 1 1/4 to 23.

Fears of shaky loans also sent Federal National Mortgage Association, the most active Big Board issue, retreating 2 5/8 to 32 on 3.7 million shares. First Boston cut its estimate of 1990 earnings slightly, citing its belief that a widening of the difference between its loan rates and its borrowing costs has peaked. Meanwhile, Federal Home Loan Mortgage fell 3 1/2 to 65 1/4.

Shares of big commercial banks closed mixed. Citicorp rose 3/8 to 22 7/8, Chase Manhattan slid 1/4 to 27 5/8, Manufacturers Hanover added 3/8 to 31 1/8, BankAmerica fell 7/8 to 25 1/2, Chemical Banking slid 1/8 to 22 7/8 and J.P. Morgan lost 3/8 to 34 5/8.

Aluminum Co. of America dropped 1 3/8 to 62 on 1.4 million shares, contributing to the decline in the Dow industrials, as analysts at Prudential-Bache Securities and First Boston made unfavorable comments on aluminum stocks. Reynolds Metals dropped 5/8 to 53 1/2 after Prudential-Bache cut its estimate of the company's first-quarter earnings.

The technology sector, which has already delivered some disappointing earnings estimates for the first quarter, came in for some selling as well. International Business Machines eased 1/4 to 105 7/8, Digital Equipment fell 1 1/4 to 78 1/2, Compaq Computer declined 7/8 to 96 1/8, Hewlett-Packard declined 1 1/8 to 43 3/4 and Motorola fell 3/4 to 65 1/8.

UAL dropped 2 to 163 1/2 even though its board agreed in principle to a buy-out bid, valued at $201 a share, from a group of unions at its United Airlines subsidiary. The unions said they are contributing $2 billion of labor concessions to the offer -- but no cash -- and haven't yet obtained any outside financing.

Browning-Ferris Industries, which put its hazardous waste-disposal business up for sale, climbed 1 1/2 to 36 7/8 as 1.8 million shares changed hands. The company said its results for the March quarter would include a charge of $295 million related to the pullout.
