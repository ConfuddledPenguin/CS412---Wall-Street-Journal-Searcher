SEC Task Force to Conduct Wide Study Into Regulation of Investment Firms

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 04/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission's investment management division has set up a task force to conduct a sweeping study into the regulation of mutual funds and other investment companies, the division's head said. Kathryn McGrath, the SEC's director of investment management, said in an interview that the 12-member group will work for at least a year to "study what should be done legislatively or by the commission to bring the U.S. system of regulation" up to date.

The study will be the first in almost 25 years to take a comprehensive look at how mutual funds should be registered and managed and whether the current disclosure process still works, she said. "We're looking at anything and everything about investment companies," Ms. McGrath said. "We want to know anything that's too onerous so that it can be removed. Anything that's missing we might recommend be added."

She said the division will issue a release in the next month or so asking for input from the public and investment companies on directions the study should take.

Separately, Ms. McGrath said her division is working on proposals to tighten the flexibility of money-market funds. The proposals would focus on making sure that funds aren't too reliant on a single issuer of short-term paper, she said, "so that if they hit a bad issuer it won't hurt them as badly."

She said the measures were triggered in part by last year's problems in the bond market and by the difficulties this year with some issuers of commercial paper. "This will help make money-market funds a tad safer," Ms. McGrath said.

Ms. McGrath added that the SEC received a number of comment letters about a three-pronged proposal to beef up disclosure of mutual funds. The most controverial measure would force funds to tell shareholders or prospective investors who manages the fund and details about that manager's business experience.

Ms. McGrath said industry comments generally opposed the proposal, as had been expected. Industry officials have argued that such disclosure would create a star system in the industry and that many funds are run by teams of managers, rather than a single individual.

The SEC said at the time it issued the proposal, however, that it received more than 300 letters from investors supporting management disclosure after it proposed a similar requirement for closed-end mutual funds.

Ms. McGrath also said industry reaction was cool to a second proposal that would require funds to state in prospectuses how well they met investment goals for the prior year.

But the industry and others backed the third measure, she said, which would add a line to the prospectus to show a fund's 10-year total return, or performance plus dividends. "That's really the bottom line of what an investor would make from making an investment," she said.
