International: Soviets Report First Trade Gap In Last 14 Years --- Deficit Increases Pressures On Gorbachev's Plans To Overhaul Economy

  • Author: Peter Gumbel
  • Date: 04/09/90

MOSCOW -- The Soviet Union announced its first trade deficit in 14 years amid growing signs President Mikhail Gorbachev is considering big purchases of Western consumer goods to cushion the impact of his economic overhaul program. According to official statistics published over the weekend, the Soviet trade balance swung to a deficit of 3.3 billion rubles ($5.4 billion) in 1989 from a surplus of 2.1 billion rubles in 1988. A drop in exports of oil and other raw materials and increased imports of Western goods were the main causes.

The government statistics office also issued preliminary figures for the first three months of this year that showed continued deterioration of the economy. Industrial production dropped 1.2% before adjustment for inflation in the first quarter, largely because of strikes and ethnic unrest in parts of the country. At the same time, inflation showed no sign of slowing, with average wages rising by 9%, according to the Izvestia newspaper.

Faced with such economic problems and widespread shortages, Mr. Gorbachev is expected to unveil a package of economic reform measures soon, perhaps this week. He has called for a "radicalization" of economic overhaul involving a push toward setting up a market economy, but few details of his plans have leaked out.

But a senior West German banker who met leading Soviet officials last week said the Kremlin is clearly looking for new credits to finance a big import program. The banker, who asked not to be identified, said Moscow is hoping for substantial assistance from Western Europe and the U.S., particularly following next month's superpower summit.

The Soviet government so far has been reluctant to embark on a crash import program, which Prime Minister Nikolai Ryzhkov has warned would further weaken the economy. But some of Mr. Gorbachev's advisers say the planned reforms, which are expected to include big price increases for many goods, must be accompanied by measures to win public confidence and curb potential unrest.

"The president and his cabinet must be ready to take several painful and unpopular decisions, and at the same time they must keep the trust of the people," Nikolai Petrakov, an economist who has become a personal adviser to Mr. Gorbachev, told a Soviet newspaper last week. He advocated taking out large credits to buy consumer goods, and even raised the prospect of seeking large-scale assistance from the West along the lines of a Marshall plan.

If Mr. Gorbachev follows such advice, he will be picking the worst time in years to embark on a credit-financed spending spree. The cost of Soviet borrowing on Western financial markets has soared in recent weeks, largely because of political upheaval in the U.S.S.R. and long delays on payments by Soviet organizations to Western companies.

Mr. Petrakov and several other leading Soviet economists spent the weekend in a dacha outside Moscow, working out details of the program that will be submitted to Mr. Gorbachev and his council of presidential advisers this week. Soviet officials say privately that several versions could be submitted, enabling the president to choose between drastic Polish-style measures to free prices and sell off state industries and more cautious moves involving a gradual transition to a market economy.

Leonid Abalkin, a deputy prime minister in charge of economic overhaul, said last week that the Soviet Union won't copy the Polish model because of substantial differences between the economies of the two nations. But he and other officials concede that speedy moves are needed to shake off the old system of bureaucratic economic management, put more goods into Soviet stores and curb a runaway money supply that could spark serious inflation. Soviet economists estimate people are sitting on about 165 billion rubles that they would spend if they could find something to buy.

Soviet officials say opposition to a rapid move toward a full-fledged market system is strongest among members of the Communist Party Central Committee and the labor unions. If Mr. Gorbachev goes ahead with a radical option, he would also be undermining the position of his prime minister, Mr. Ryzhkov, who only last December presented a more cautious economic program to parliament.

But the gloomy trade figures paint a picture of an economy in need of an urgent fix. The Soviet Union last year was forced to import 360 million rubles worth of soap and detergent, more than six times the amount of the previous year, after panic-buying broke out among consumers. Moscow tripled purchases of coffee and vegetable oil to make up for domestic shortages, while grain imports jumped almost 6% to 37 million metric tons. The major beneficiaries of the boost in imports were West Germany, the U.S., Italy, Britain and other West European nations.

By contrast, oil exports, the Soviet Union's largest foreign-currency earner, dropped almost 12% to 127.3 million metric tons, and coal and steel sales were also far below levels of the previous year. Although Moscow increased its exports to Western industrialized nations, it cut back on deliveries to Eastern Europe.

Overall, imports soared to 72.1 billion rubles from 65 billion rubles in 1989, while exports rose only slightly to 68.8 billion rubles from 67.1 billion rubles.
