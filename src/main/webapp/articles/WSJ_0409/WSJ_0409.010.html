This State May Be Penny-Wise, But It Sure Looks Pound-Foolish

  • Author: Diane Tracy
  • Date: 04/09/90

BOSTON -- At last count, Massachusetts is facing a budget deficit this year of $800 million. Make that $799,999,999.99, thanks to a punctilious computer that figures a penny taxed is a penny earned.

Part of the credit for reducing the state's megadeficit also goes to Anne Eustis, the exceedingly scrupulous town treasurer of Princeton, Mass. In February, during a year-end review of the town's books, Mrs. Eustis found she'd shortchanged the state 10 cents on town payroll withholding last July. "So I called {the state revenue department} up and asked them if they wanted it paid," Mrs. Eustis notes.

She needn't have asked. "They said yes, they did." Off went a letter explaining the error and a check for the 10 cents.

Her fiscal good nature was soon to be taxed, however. Two weeks later, the state responded with a bill seeking an additional penny for interest due on the 10 cents. "I literally got a bill for a penny," she says, noting that it seems rather ridiculous that the state would spend a quarter on postage to collect a penny.

The state blames the dunning notice on a computer. Once an adjustment has been made to a payment, explains spokeswoman Betsy Houghteling, it automatically goes into a computer system, and the state issues a notice of assessment. The computer, in kicking out a notice of assessment for the 10 cents, even though it had already been paid, tacked on a bill for the penny interest.

The taxpayer-assistance person who originally advised Mrs. Eustis to pay the dime was probably just trying to help, and had Mrs. Eustis talked to a different person she might have been told to forget about it, Ms. Houghteling says. She adds: "I'm sure it was a sincere attempt to keep her on the right side of everything, but it ended up generating a lot of paper around it."

Ms. Houghteling says the Princeton case is a "once in a blue moon" situation and Mrs. Eustis can rest assured the state won't pinch Princeton for the penny. "It's good that she's so careful with her accounting, but she needn't worry that anything will happen subsequent to this," Ms. Houghteling adds.

Mrs. Eustis says she won't write a check for the one-penny interest, which is due on April 15, but will probably tape a penny to the bill and send it back.
