The Outlook: U.S. Firms Increase Overseas Investments

  • Author: Bernard Wysocki Jr.
  • Date: 04/09/90

NEW YORK -- Given foreigners' high-profile purchases of U.S. real estate and U.S. companies, foreign investment sometimes seems a one-way street. Foreigners, especially the Japanese, are accused of buying up America. This is a half-truth at best. Foreign investment is flowing into the U.S., but capital also is flowing out rapidly.

American companies are spending more and more on acquisitions overseas, especially in Europe. Moreover, they are raising their foreign spending on plant and equipment at a record pace. One recent example: General Motors' Opel unit plans to spend more than $500 million on a car-building joint venture in East Germany.

The larger issue is whether U.S. companies are likely to be leaders or laggards in the trend toward the globalization of markets and industries. Companies operating in the major markets of North America, Europe and Asia are far more likely to prosper in the 1990s than those that don't, experts say. So, U.S. companies' stepped-up activity abroad is an encouraging sign.

For many U.S. companies, the spending increases are a matter of building on their past success. Large American companies have long been important in Europe; some are even more competitive than their European and Japanese rivals.

"IBM and Digital Equipment are very strong. They made most of their money in Europe last year," notes Todd Hixon at Boston Consulting Group.

Much of such profits is being plowed back into operations overseas. Last week, the Commerce Department estimated that U.S. plant-and-equipment spending overseas would rise 13% this year to $54.9 billion.

Western Europe and the newly industrialized countries of Asia are the big investment targets. "Increases in Europe have been in double digits since 1987," says Raymond J. Mataloni at the department's Bureau of Economic Analysis. "And spending in the NICs during the last three years has been up 37%, up 31% and up 24%."

Another form of investment into Europe is acquisitions of European companies. Translink's European Deal Review notes a big upturn in intra-European takeovers. And smaller U.S. companies are rushing to buy companies, both in Britain and on the Continent, with 185 acquisitions valued at $15.2 billion last year, about double the 1988 level.

Such figures are only partial measures of foreign investment, however. Direct foreign investment in the U.S. is increasing faster than Americans' direct investment overseas. And in 1988, for the first time, the total book value of foreign holdings in the U.S. in 1988 exceeded U.S. holdings overseas. At the end of 1989, the foreign holdings totaled $390 billion, compared with U.S. overseas holdings of $368.1 billion. However, since inflation leaves book values far below market values, the true worth of the U.S. holdings, which are far older on the average, is greatly understated.

Japanese investors have emerged as some of the most aggressive investors in American real estate and American companies, and their purchases of "trophy" assets -- such as Sony's acquisition of Columbia Pictures Entertainment and Mitsubishi Estate's purchase of a controlling interest in Rockefeller Center -- has sparked a political outcry on Capitol Hill.

---

However, another view suggests that foreign investment flows to the economies with the strongest growth prospects. During much of the 1980s, the economy in the United States expanded rapidly and thus sucked in foreign capital. But what about the 1990s? "The U.S. will attract investment, although more slowly," says John Rutter, a senior economist at the Commerce Department.

The rate of growth in foreign investment in the U.S., including that by the British and Japanese, slowed a bit last year. "European and Japanese buyers are choosing to focus more heavily on Europe," says Ilan Kaufthal, managing director at Wertheim Schroder & Co.

The one area where investment flows remain lopsided is Japan, which historically has been inhospitable to foreign investment. Acquisitions of Japanese companies are still rare, partly because selling a company is repugnant to many Japanese. Less than 1% of Japanese corporate assets were in foreign hands in 1986, according to a study by the London-based Royal Institute for International Studies. The U.S. figure was 5%, and Britain and West Germany had in excess of 10% in foreign hands.

Last week, U.S. and Japanese trade negotiators reached an agreement for Japan to relax its investment rules. If the investment inflow does expand, it could increase trade and reduce tensions across the Pacific. But if investment remains largely one way, protectionists may well try to close the investment street or at least limit the traffic from Japan.
