A RECORD LOW FOR THATCHER

Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher is the most unpopular British leader since the Gallup Organization began polling more than 50 years ago, according to a new Gallup survey.

The opposition Labor Party, with a massive 52.5% support, now holds a record 24.5 point lead over Mrs. Thatcher's Conservative Party, the survey indicated.

Battered by economic troubles and a backlash against an unpopular new local government tax levied on almost all voters, Mrs. Thatcher's ratings have been declining for nearly a year. The Gallup poll, published in London's pro-Conservative Daily Telegraph, showed only 24% of voters questioned approve of the prime minister's performance.

Mrs. Thatcher's rating outstrips the Gallup unpopularity ratings of other British leaders in their darkest times. In May 1940, Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain, his policy of appeasing Hitler a failure and Britain in danger of a Nazi invasion, recorded 32% support.

EAST EUROPE'S PRICE CONTROLS

The preferable path to economic reform in Eastern European countries would be through removal of price controls in one quick stroke, said Gyoergy Szapary, the International Monetary Fund's chief representative in Hungary. "The problems associated with the transition are daunting," he said, adding that "people's expectations for dramatic improvements in availability of goods have increased sharply." He warned, however, that immediate removal of all price controls could trigger dangerous inflation.

A CAPITAL FOR UNITED GERMANY

Most West Germans would like to see Berlin, not Bonn, as capital of a united Germany, according to a survey conducted for the Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung newspaper. Nearly 52% of 1,000 West Germans questioned last month favored Berlin as the capital while 26% preferred Bonn. The survey said the Bonn supporters were mostly among the young generation while older people generally favored Berlin, capital of the former German Reich. About 20% of those questioned were undecided.

EC STUDIES FOOD POISONING

Food poisoning is massively underreported throughout the European Community, according to a study by EC researchers. Looking into occurrences of poisoning from salmonella and botulism, the researchers found that illnesses fre quently stemmed from dirty kitchens. "Dishcloths are teeming with salmonella," said Paul Gray, senior EC official in charge of food policy. "My grandmother used to boil them every day."

CENTRAL AMERICAN PATROL BOATS

The United Nations accepted an Argentine offer to provide four patrol boats and their crews to help monitor irregular cross-border movements in Central America. The boats will serve in the Gulf of Fonseca, between Nicaragua, Honduras and El Salvador, as part of the U.N. Observer Group in Central America. The group was set up last November to check on the cross-border movement of arms and men to irregular forces operating in the region.

WHAT ROMANIA NEEDS

Romania needs $1.5 billion to $2 billion a year in foreign aid, investment and export credits to modernize its infrastructure and rebuild its economy, a top economist said. Eugen Dijmarescu, the head of a commission formed to set priorities for foreign aid and investment, said emergency aid in the form of medical supplies, food, clothing and fodder is still essential almost four months after the revolution that toppled dictator Nicolae Ceaucescu. He said Romania hopes to move swiftly from emergency aid to investment and credits that would rebuild the economy.

LIFE AND DEATH

Japan is at the top of rankings of life expectancy in 33 developed nations, according to new figures from the Centers for Disease Control in Atlanta.

The average life expectancy in Japan is 79.1 years, 4.1 years ahead of the U.S. figure of 75.0 years; the lowest of the 33 was Hungary, where the average citizen lives 69.7 years. Japan was also best among the 33 nations in death rates from all causes, with 628.8 deaths for each 100,000 people each year. The U.S. ranked near the middle, with 828.4 deaths, and Romania was worst, at 1,242.

On average in the 33 countries, heart disease accounted for 30% of deaths, with cancer causing 21% and stroke causing 14%. Fourteen percent of deaths in the 33 countries -- about 1.5 million a year -- are attributed to smoking, the CDC said.

Fewer Americans smoke, compared with the other countries; 31% of U.S. males and 26% of American females smoke, compared with 41% of men and 29% of women in the other countries.
