Major Oil Concerns Expected to Report Flat to Lower First-Quarter Earnings

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 04/09/90

HOUSTON -- Despite higher crude oil prices, most major oil companies are expected to report flat to lower first-quarter earnings. And the profit outlook for the current quarter isn't any better. "For the industry, last year's first quarter is going to be tough to match," C.J. Silas, chairman of Phillips Petroleum Co., said in a recent interview.

Specifically, the 18 largest oil companies are expected to post first-quarter operating earnings totaling about $5.56 billion, down 2.2% from a year earlier, according to Bernard Picchi, a securities analyst with Salomon Brothers Inc.

"The big negative factor is chemicals," Mr. Picchi said.

Petrochemical profits were exceptionally high in the 1989 first half but have plummeted since that time. Weakened demand and rapid overbuilding of capacity have created a glut of many chemicals. Ethylene, for instance, a commodity petrochemical used to make more sophisticated materials, now sells for around 24 cents a pound, 22% below the year-ago price.

The rapid decline in chemical prices had even sparked some industry concern that a long-term drop might be in the works. For the moment, it appears that isn't happening. "Prices have leveled off and come back up some," Sir Peter Holmes, chairman of Shell Transport & Trading, said. "We don't expect to see the early '80s happening again" when a glut drove the industry into a depression.

The chief beneficial factor for oil company results in the just-ended period was the price of crude, which averaged around $19.50 a barrel, up $3 from a year ago.

Natural gas prices rose only slighty, disappointing many companies that were hoping last winter that gas would break out of its long price depression. Gas supplies were extremely tight during the freeze in December and prices shot up, but then the weather suddenly became warmer-than-normal, and prices tumbled back down even faster.

Gasoline refining and marketing margins in the first quarter were anemic. "They were very weak early in the quarter and didn't come back much," Frederick Leuffer, a securities analyst at C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc., said. Margins were also weak in the 1989 first quarter, so refining results will be flat to slightly lower.

For the current quarter, refining and marketing is the industry's big profit question mark. "Right now, margins are low," said a spokesman for USX Corp. "But I would expect margins this summer to improve."

That feeling isn't universal, however. Crude prices have been falling lately, which generally helps margins because oil is the feedstock for gasoline refining. But many industry economists think gasoline demand for the critical summer driving season could be weaker than it was last year, countering that possible margin improvement.

Meanwhile, most oil company exploration and production earnings probably won't compare well with last year's second quarter. "We're in that weak demand part of the season and {crude oil} prices could drop another $1 or $2," from current levels, said Eugene Nowak, a securities analyst with Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

On the other hand, comparable year-ago prices were unusually high for the period, breaking the seasonal pattern in part because of the Alaskan oil spill and disruptions in the British North Sea. Those events temporarily reduced the supply of certain desirable crudes, driving prices up during much of the quarter. In last year's second quarter, in fact, some crude prices eclipsed $24 a barrel. Currently, they are around $19 a barrel.
