International -- Washington Insight: U.S. Toughens Stance Toward Iraq To Pressure It to Halt Arms Programs

  • Author: Robert S. Greenberger
  • Date: 04/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. appears headed for a tougher policy toward the brutal regime of Iraqi President Saddam Hussein. Some administration officials argue privately that it's time to abandon Washington's soft-glove approach to Iraq. They want to put Baghdad on notice that the U.S. will tighten the economic screws unless Iraq radically alters its behavior, specifically by curtailing its efforts to develop nuclear and chemical weapons.

The tougher stance is most evident in Congress, especially the House, where a drive to impose economic sanctions already is under way. The ever-cautious Bush administration will oppose these efforts, White House officials say, because it believes quiet diplomacy is more effective than legislation. But even some administration Mideast analysts concede that the U.S. has little to show for its attempts to prod Baghdad toward moderation.

Arguments for a tougher policy were fueled by the recent reports that Iraq tried to smuggle nuclear-bomb trigger devices out of the U.S., and even more so by President Hussein's threat to use chemical weapons against Israel if his country is attacked.

In addition, an Iraqi-American was indicted Friday by a federal grand jury in California for allegedly participating in an Iraqi plot to kill two foes of the Iraqi government in the U.S.

Recent concerns about Iraq were first aroused by a speech Mr. Hussein delivered in Jordan in February. For the first time since the Iran-Iraq war ended in 1988, the Iraqi president questioned the need for the continuing U.S. naval presence in the Persian Gulf. Moderate Arab states told Washington privately that they disagreed with that view, and they voiced worries that the Iraqi leader was trying to give himself a freer hand to dominate the region.

Before the Iran-Iraq war, Iraq regularly threatened its neighbors. Kuwait faced Iraqi claims to part of its territory; Jordan encountered pressure from Baghdad whenever it became involved in efforts to achieve an Israeli-Palestinian peace; and Saudi Arabia worried about the vulnerability of its oil fields to the huge Iraqi military machine to its north.

Iraq moderated its behavior and improved its relations with the U.S. when it was bogged down in its war with Iran. The U.S., in turn, softened its stance, and American policy since then has been designed to keep Baghdad in the moderate fold. But some analysts say the February speech signaled that Mr. Hussein really hasn't altered his long-term intention to dominate the region. These officials suggest that the mild U.S. policy now may be jeopardizing Washington's Arab friends.

While the accommodative U.S. policy appears to have failed, there are reasons to believe that Saddam Hussein could be vulnerable to increased U.S. pressure.

Iraq is trying to rebuild its war-torn economy and badly needs Western help. Sanctions legislation proposed by Rep. Howard Berman (D., Calif.) would deny the Iraq credit from the U.S. Export-Import Bank, ban exports of items that could have a military use and commit the U.S. to oppose any loan or other assistance to it from international financial institutions.

But the sanctions bill wouldn't ban agricultural credit guarantees, even though farm sales are the bulk of U.S. exports to Iraq. An aide to Rep. Berman says such a restriction would make the bill unacceptable to grain-state legislators. The aide argues that the bill, nevertheless, would impede Iraq's rebuilding efforts.

Iraq also may be susceptible to pressure because it soon could be involved in the kind of international dispute that pushed it toward moderation in the past. Turkey is building a dam on the Euphrates River, which supplies water further downstream to Syria and Iraq. Turkey refuses to commit itself to a water-sharing agreement, and Syria is a bitter enemy of Baghdad. So water, the Middle East's most precious commodity, could produce new tensions and put Iraq, once again, in need of friends.

The U.S., meanwhile, may feel compelled to get tough with Iraq to further its claim to a leadership role in a world of resurgent democracies. U.S. allies who want to see that happen could cite the State Department's own words. In its report this year on human rights, the department calls Iraq's record "abysmal." The report says that the country uses torture and summary execution to deal with dissidents and that freedoms of press, speech, assembly and association are "virtually nonexistent" under Mr. Hussein's regime.
