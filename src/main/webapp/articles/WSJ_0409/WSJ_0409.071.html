Politics & Policy: Bell Atlantic Is Barred From U.S. Contracts

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 04/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The Treasury Department suspended Bell Atlantic Corp. from receiving future business from the federal government because the telecommunications company allegedly misled the agency on a telephone bid. The Treasury, in a letter dated last Tuesday, informed Philadelphia-based Bell Atlantic that it believes a Bell Atlantic subsidiary, Chesapeake & Potomac Telephone Co., made "material misrepresentations" in a bid for a $100 million contract to modernize the Treasury's telephone system in the Washington area. American Telephone & Telegraph Co. also is bidding for the work, in the latest of a series of bitter contests to update the federal government's phone system after the breakup of the old Bell system.

A Treasury spokesman said the allegations of misrepresentation had been forwarded to the agency's inspector general for an investigation. Until the investigation is completed, Bell Atlantic is barred from receiving future contracts from any federal agency.

It's unclear how the ban will affect Bell Atlantic, which estimates its revenue from the federal government at more than $250 million annually. Bell Atlantic is set to begin service this week on a telephone contract, valued at tens of millions of dollars annually, to hook Washington-area federal agencies together. The telecommunications company also has won a big Pentagon telephone contract, but that award is being appealed.

Bell Atlantic denounced the Treasury decision as showing "clear bias" in favor of AT&T. It also accused the agency of trying to "eliminate C&P {Chesapeake & Potomac} from the bidding." The Treasury denied any bias; AT&T declined comment.

The allegations of misrepresentation focus on whether Bell Atlantic based its bid on telecommunications equipment that wasn't "commercially available," as required by the Treasury. Bell Atlantic said it had successfully challenged two attempts by the Treasury to award the contract to AT&T by making the same allegations -- that AT&T hadn't used commercially available equipment.

The Treasury telephone contract was supposed to have been awarded in 1987, but is now three years behind schedule because of the wrangling between Bell Atlantic and its former parent, AT&T. The latest Treasury decision will delay the award at least until the end of the inspector general's investigation. If history is any guide, the final contract award will probably be challenged by the loser.
