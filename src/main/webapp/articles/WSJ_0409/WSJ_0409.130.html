REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): After Poindexter

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/09/90

He is a victim of a concerted action by Congress, working hand-in-glove with the independent counsel's office to try to do something with this political battle. -- Richard Beckler, lawyer for John Poindexter, to the jury.

{Do not consider} who was right in political disputes between Congress and the president, between Democrats and Republicans or between liberals and conservatives.

-- Judge Greene, instructing the jury.

{There's no question that even being charitable to Admiral Poindexter, he did it because he wanted to protect the political viability of Ronald Reagan. And I consider that to be a selfish motive.}

-- Independent counsel prosecutor Dan Webb after the verdict.

What has been resolved by the Iran-Contra trials, what has been established? Congress and the Democratic Party have achieved a victory of sorts. A jury has declared that John Poindexter violated congressional law by not being frank enough in his dealings with Congress. Politics goes on, though, and it's a little ironic how far away politics has moved from the original substance of this dispute.

For example, is the Poindexter verdict a repudiation of President Reagan's Nicaragua policy? Judge Greene's directive notwithstanding, that of course is a political question. On the evidence, the policy is a success. Congressional policy was to stop the Contras from pressuring the Soviet-supported Sandinista government. Despite the five anti-Contra Boland amendments, the White House did keep the Contras alive body and soul, and they made Sandinista consolidation impossible. When the Nicaraguans got to vote, they removed the Sandinistas from power.

On the legal side, if the Iran-Contra convictions are upheld on appeal we will have some very curious precedents. President Reagan was forced to testify, but Judge Greene refused to subpoena 65 congressmen. Mr. Poindexter's lawyers wanted to show that Members were not deceived because they knew before Mr. Poindexter's testimony about aid to the Contras and the missile shipment to Iran. In other words, we may never know what congressmen knew and when they knew it. Those who say the Poindexter case shows that the President isn't above the law profess no concern that Congress is far above the law.

Treating criminal trials as an extension of partisan politics leaves the President's political status vis a vis Congress pretty much where it was. Congress will keep trying to commandeer presidential power by any means so long as the President fails to guard his office. One likely change is that on advice of counsel, executive-branch officials will almost certainly be less frank in their congressional testimony, lest in difficult matters they become the target of a congressman's demand for a special prosecutor.

This congressional criminalizing of policy differences with the executive branch will probably only end when the Democrats think they have some prospect of regaining the presidency. They now pay no price for substituting litigation for politics. They are happy for judges and juries to become a proxy for the electorate -- except that in the Ollie North and Poindexter trials the juror-voters had to convince the court that they knew nothing whatsoever about the political life of the country. The judiciary might ask itself if it really wants to play the top role in this brutal political game.

For the larger electorate, Iran-Contra will be remembered for the televised hearings which gave ample opportunity for the public to form its opinion of the matter. For President Reagan and his treatment by history, Iran-Contra will likely be a footnote against the fall of communism -- including in Nicaragua.

President Bush now has the opportunity of the post-Cold War era to find a modus vivendi with Congress that doesn't drive politics into courtrooms. He should seriously consider a pardon for the Iran-Contra defendants, veto any more special prosecutor laws and tell Congress that foreign policy will again be set the old-fashioned way, by give and take between the two political branches of government.
