GE Sues Daimler-Benz for $1.15 Billion, Alleging Breach of Jet-Engine Contract

  • Author: Amal Kumar Naj
  • Date: 04/09/90

NEW YORK -- General Electric Co. filed a $1.15 billion suit against Daimler-Benz AG, charging the West German industrial group with breaking an agreement to develop high-thrust commercial jet engines. The suit stems from Daimler-Benz entering "a memorandum of understanding" with United Technologies Corp. on a wide range of commercial engine programs between their jet engine units.

GE charged in the suit, filed in federal court here, that Daimler-Benz broke the exclusive and binding relationship in this area with the company. GE, a Fairfield, Conn., manufacturer, claims that proprietary and other sensitive information it shared with Daimler will find its way into the hands of its competitor, United Technologies.

Daimler-Benz said it is studying the suit but as yet sees no basis for it.

In the suit, GE said that for more than 25 years its aircraft engine unit and Daimler's Motoren-und Turbinen-Union unit, known as MTU, have been "parties to a series of contracts" in which they have shared "highly confidential technological information, revenues and costs in the development and sale of commercial jet engines."

According to the court papers, in 1981 GE entered into a contract with MTU under which GE shared a variety of information in connection with the development of a subsonic jet engine for commercial aircraft. That engine is now a major source of revenue for GE's aircraft engine business. Then in 1988, GE and MTU signed a letter of agreement to cooperate in a program to develop the next generation of high-thrust commercial jet engines.

The latest agreement on high-thrust engines also stated that "for the life of this program, MTU will not participate" with competitors of GE in programs involving engines in the thrust range over 50,000 pounds, GE said. GE's cooperative arrangement with MTU on the new generation of jet engines, known as GE90, intended to ultimately have thrust of 95,000 pounds or greater. These engines are aimed at powering next-generation widebody planes.

GE said it has shared detailed design specifications, thrust ratios, parts configurations and fuel efficiency and materials data with MTU. "The extent of the proprietary information provided to MTU enables MTU or a competitor of General Electric to plan and eventually develop and market the new high-thrust jet engine being developed by General Electric," the company said in its complaint.

GE said that on March 20, MTU told GE about its plan to develop with Pratt & Whitney engines that would directly compete with GE's.

A spokesman for United Technologies declined to comment on the suit, noting that the company wasn't named in the litigation. But he said the company's Pratt & Whitney unit has had "a close partnership with MTU since the early 1970s on several engines" and that its alliance with MTU was a "logical outgrowth of that."

As for the issue of GE's proprietary information in the hands of MTU, he said Pratt & Whitney doesn't "need or want any GE confidential information."
