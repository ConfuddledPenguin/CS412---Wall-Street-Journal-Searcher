Tucson Electric Auditors Note Firm's Problems

  • Author: Frederick Rose
  • Date: 04/09/90

TUCSON, Ariz. -- Tucson Electric Power Co.'s auditors cited a host of contingent liabilities and uncertainties. In a letter to shareholders, the auditors, Deloitte & Touche, noted that the company faces various regulatory and legal decisions as well as contractual matters.

In 1989, Tucson Electric had a loss of $89.9 million, or $3.55 a share, on $593.7 million in revenue.

Tucson Electric said it decided not to take charges that last year would have resulted in a $74 million widening of the loss. The utility said that it may decide to record a liability for the potential charge, which involves a gain posted years ago, a portion of which regulators now are saying belongs to rate-payers. Tucson Electric is challenging that ruling by the Arizona Corporation Commission.

Also troubling the company is the $722 million investment in the Springerville 2 coal-fired generating station. The unit is nearing completion. However, Arizona regulators have questioned the need for the capacity, which could result in Tucson Electric's being barred from recovering some or all of the unit's costs from rate-payers.

Tucson Electric warned that "either substantial retail rate increases or additional wholesale sales {or a combination thereof} will be required to recover such costs" of Springerville 2. Without recovery, "the company will suffer increasingly deteriorating financial results, will be unable to earn a reasonable return on its utility investment, and will continue to have negative cash flow."

Tucson Electric eliminated dividend payments on its common stock earlier this year. The company has said it expects to post losses and sees no chance of restoring the dividend in the foreseeable future.

Based on certain coverage tests, the company is barred from issuing additional preferred stock. A net earnings test also limits to about $70 million the amount of additional first mortgage bonds that can be sold by the company, which added that the amount of bonds it can issue under the earnings test is expected to dwindle through this year, as anticipated operating losses are reported.

However, Tucson Electric said it can issue as much as $118 million of bonds by current calculation without meeting the net earnings test, based on bonds retired after the most recent filing of a net earnings certificate with the bond indenture trustee.

The company currently is trying to negotiate a new master credit agreement that would provide as much as $350 million of borrowing capability and would supplant all existing short-term credit facilities.

Current company short-term financial facilities include a $150 million revolving bank credit agreement that expires in May 1991, and $100 million in various bank lines expiring from March 30 to July 31 of this year. Tucson Electric said it is seeking a renewal of these lines until a master credit agreement can be negotiated. Four banks holding $40 million of debt refused to extend and were paid in December and January.
