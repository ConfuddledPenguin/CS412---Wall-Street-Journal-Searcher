Letters to the Editor: Bombardment of Noise From the Crowded Sky

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/09/90

Your March 2 story on the nation's air-capacity problem ("FAA Predicts Big Jump in Air Traffic . . .") made an oblique reference to the airlines blaming part of the problem on the government's refusal "to get tough with noise restrictions at individual airports." Individual airports have had to implement noise restrictions because neither the airlines nor the government has been sensitive to the environmental consequences of stuffing more routes into the nation's already-saturated skies. As a result of the airline industry's awesome political clout and overflowing political-action coffers, it has been allowed to spread noise and air pollution with virtually no legislative constraints. Its incestuous relationship with the FAA is legend, even to the extent that the last FAA head came from Federal Express.

And the government's typical lack of long-range planning and political courage is as much to blame. Why, for example, has the government not invested in a bullet-train system, as in Japan and Europe? Why isn't the government considering tougher restrictions on noisier, older, less-safe Stage 2 aircraft, as is being done by the European Economic Community?

It is estimated that nine million Americans suffer intolerable levels of jet noise, and the number is increasing dramatically each day. In New Jersey alone, where 50% more routes were added three years ago, more than 4,000 square miles of land and three million people are being bombarded by the constant roar of jet aircraft, night after night, day after day.

As a business executive who has learned that concern for the environment is good business, I hope the airline industry wakes up before it's too late. Deregulation has given the industry opportunities for growth. With that opportunity comes social responsibility.

Craig J. Cantoni

President

New Jersey Coalition Against Aircraft Noise

Basking Ridge, N.J.
