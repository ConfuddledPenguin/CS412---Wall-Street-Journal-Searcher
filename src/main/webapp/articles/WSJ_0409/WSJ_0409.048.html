Letters to the Editor: Creditors Closing In? Turn to Chapter 7

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/09/90

In your Feb. 21 Law-column item "Declaring Insolvency Called a Bad Solution," St. Louis lawyer Donald B. Kramer asserts that filing a bankruptcy petition can be a terrible error. With proper counsel, bankruptcy is a sound and effective strategy and can be beneficial, improving one's credit-worthiness and reputation.

Mr. Kramer's assertion suffers basic flaws:

A Chapter 7 bankruptcy petition can be filed only once every seven years. In most bankruptcies, the debtor gets a "clean slate" and thus is a more attractive candidate for credit. While filing a bankruptcy petition may result in a temporary "black mark," the benefits outweigh the negatives.

Mr. Kramer implies that all problems can be worked out amicably. However, many times it is either impossible or far too costly in terms of legal fees to negotiate with credit-card companies. If creditors established "workout departments" specifically for consumer debts -- similar to the departments that exist for commercial loans -- the likelihood of amicable resolutions might be increased.

Kenneth A. Rosen

Roseland, N.J.
