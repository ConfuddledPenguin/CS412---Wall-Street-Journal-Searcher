Economy: Jobless Data Point to Weak Economy --- Unemployment Rate Fell To 5.2% in March; Gain In Payrolls Was Meager

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 04/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The latest employment statistics cast some doubt on the growing sentiment that the economy's weakest point is past. Although the Labor Department's monthly survey of households found that the nation's civilian unemployment rate declined slightly in March, the department's closely watched survey of businesses found that employers' payrolls grew by only 26,000 during the month, a tiny gain by economists' standards. The unemployment rate dropped to 5.2% of the civilian work force, from 5.3% in February.

The meager job growth -- which came despite the hiring of some 85,000 Census workers during the month -- disappointed many economists. "This shows widespread weakness," said Gary Ciminero, chief economist at Fleet/Norstar Financial Services Group in Providence, R.I. "I'm at a loss to explain the March weakness without making reference to the fact that the economy may be a little weaker than the consensus thinks."

But concern over the numbers was tempered by strong employment gains in the first two months of the year, when unseasonably warm weather brought on a spurt of hiring in the construction business, for example. In both January and February, employers hired more than 300,000 new workers.

Paul Anton, president of Anton Financial Economics, a consulting company in Minneapolis, said the figures don't cause great concern "because we had such strong numbers in the past two months. But I certainly would be concerned if we had another month at this level."

One of the most disturbing figures in the report was a 30,000 drop in manufacturing jobs. Over the past year, the report said, the U.S. has lost 250,000 manufacturing jobs.

Elliott Platt, director of economic research at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette, said the decline in manufacturing employment wasn't confined to certain industries. "It's all over," he said. "It's widespread weakness. That's what's surprising." He cited recent reports from the Federal Reserve and the National Association of Purchasing Managers that "seemed to suggest manufacturing was stabilizing."

After severely slowing in the fourth quarter, the economy has seemed to rebound a bit in the first quarter of 1990, buoyed partly by warm weather.

But Kathleen Cooper, chief economist at Security Pacific National Bank in Los Angeles, said, "I still believe, based on these and the last couple of months' numbers on employment that we will be settling down in the second quarter to much lower employment growth and lower economic growth."

Another industry that appears to be weakening is retail trade, analysts said. Retail employment declined in March for the second month in a row following a large increase in January. "These up and down movements make it somewhat difficult to detect underlying trends, but net job growth for this industry in recent months is clearly much slower than in 1989," said Janet Norwood, the Labor Department's commissioner of labor statistics, in congressional testimony Friday.

The service industries continued to offset weakness in other areas of the economy as they have for several months. Overall, service-producing jobs grew by 122,000 in March, the department said.
