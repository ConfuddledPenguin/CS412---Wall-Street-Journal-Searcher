Politics & Policy: GAO Head Says Tax Increase Is Needed To Pay for Cost of the Thrift Bailout

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 04/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The head of the General Accounting Office said a tax increase is needed to pay for the savings-and-loan bailout, which the GAO says will cost far more than originally forecast. The investigative arm of Congress said the cost over 30 years should reach $325 billion rather than the $257 billion it previously estimated. The added costs include additional S&L insolvencies, a higher estimate of what previous bailout deals actually cost, additional interest costs, and larger administrative expenses. Under the current plan, most of the costs will be borne by the taxpayer.

The often-cited 10-year cost, previously estimated by the Bush administration at $166 billion, is expected by the GAO to be roughly $243 billion.

The GAO report, released Friday, called for Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady to consider legislation to determine the source of new funds and also to report current S&L restructuring costs to Congress semiannually in order to avoid big surprises in the future.

Yesterday on NBC's "Meet the Press," Charles Bowsher, comptroller general, said the administration's early estimates of the plan's costs were artificially suppressed so that the government could appear to meet its deficit-reduction targets.

The higher new S&L estimates could make more likely an overall congressional budget deal to reduce the deficit, including a tax increase. "You are still going to have to look at the revenue side of the budget," said Mr. Bowsher, adding that it would take more than congressional consensus. "You have to have White House leadership," he said.

It's impossible to know yet the final cost of the savings-and-loan bailout, which was devised to protect depositors in S&Ls that made bad investments. The cost -- bigger than all previous U.S. bailouts of private industry or municipal governments combined -- won't be known until the bad investments that the S&Ls made have been liquidated.

Economists have been skeptical about the Bush administration estimates, which projected 7% deposit growth at a time when thrift deposits are shrinking rapidly, almost no inflation, and no recession for 10 years. Economic projections that are less sanguine push cost estimates even higher.

"If we had a strong economy working against us, interest rates going up much higher, some people are making estimates of half a trillion dollars" over 40 years, said Mr. Bowsher at a Senate hearing Friday.

The GAO, moreover, sounded an ominous warning about the deposit insurance fund that protects banks, at a time when banks are reporting higher losses associated with sliding real-estate markets nationwide. The Federal Deposit Insurance Fund reported its first loss ever in 1988 and is expected to report another loss for 1989.

The GAO said it is considering recommendations of extraordinary auditing requirements, particularly for large banks. Those would include more frequent on-site examinations by banking regulators as well as double teams of outside auditors. The GAO has cited past, superficial audits of S&Ls as a major factor in allowing thrift insolvencies to grow to the present size.

"I don't think we can afford to let much time go by before we deal with these issues," said Donald Chapin, assistant comptroller general.

The higher estimates occur at a time when the administration has been scrambling to salvage the S&L restructuring plan. So far, it has been bogged down, finding far fewer buyers of insolvent thrifts than anticipated, as well as more S&L insolvencies with greater losses than expected.

Resolution Trust Corp. is gearing up to sell or liquidate 140 thrifts by July 1 -- a blistering pace compared with the 52 S&Ls it has sold since last August -- by using more flexible terms. The sales are likely to leave the government with the bulk of bad assets for later sale. The government, however, is likely to receive better bids from buyers for the thrift deposits if they are sold before their value diminishes.

After several weeks of inactivity, several thrift sales are expected to be announced this week, said an RTC spokesman.
