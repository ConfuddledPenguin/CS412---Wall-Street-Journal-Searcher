Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Retail Sales Report Expected to Reflect Dearth of Discounting on Apparel Lines

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 04/09/90

NEW YORK -- Will your Easter bonnet have a discount on it? Early reports on March retail sales indicate that important spring-apparel lines are selling with minimal markdowns-so far. That's a far cry from the massive discounting that afflicted inventory-laden retailers at Christmas and from last year's soft Easter results.

"It looks like, at least through March, most retailers were able to push new merchandise out with normal prices," said Brian J. Fabbri, chief economist at Midland Montagu Economics, New York. Still, he and others concede that the book on spring merchandise won't be closed until April results are known, as Easter arrives late this year.

March retail sales, due out Thursday, are expected to show a small gain, buoyed by strength in apparel, a high-profit line for stores, particularly if they maintain full price markups. A sharp, 3.3% jump in February clothing prices pushed up the consumer price index that month in a move at least partly attributable to firmer pricing. Automobile sales lately have been stirred only by incentives and other big-ticket goods, such as furniture, have been lifeless. The week's other indicators include March producer prices and February business inventories.

Discounting on Easter goods is still possible. "If they have a really lousy weekend, retailers may choose to have a one-day or two-day sale," said Stacy Dutton, retail analyst at Morgan Stanley & Co., New York. She noted Friday that cold, rainy weather was in the offing for much of the Midwest and East. "But I don't sense there's a huge panic out there," she added.

Others aren't so sure. "This is a very competitive retail market with pressures on margins and bargain-conscious consumers in the stores," said Allen Sinai, chief economist of Boston Co. "Americans are great mall-walkers, not necessarily shoppers," he added.

"Inventories may be lean, but with costs rising on the wage side, that means profit pressure," Mr. Sinai added, alluding to Friday's March employment data, which showed another increase in average hourly earnings.

Overall March retail sales are expected to rise 0.2%, according to MMS International Inc., a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York. Excluding the weak auto component, retail sales show a larger, 0.4% rise, the survey indicates.

Discounting may be on the way out as a viable marketing strategy anyway, said Daniel D. Barry, retail analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co. "There is a secular trend toward less promotion and less discounting and more every-day low pricing," he said. Sears, Roebuck & Co. recently made a major thrust in that direction and Mr. Barry noted that others, including Dayton-Hudson Corp., are trying the policy. He said new technology, such as computer-aided inventory control, is making cost-management easier to achieve at the front of the selling process, helping retailers avoid disaster sales at the rack.

Retailers save by stabilizing their cost-of-carrying goods and eliminating the often-panicky closeout expenses of a sale. And the consumer "has the feeling coming into the store where you can save money on a regular basis rather than coming in just to buy on sale. There's more good will," said Mr. Barry.

Friday's report on February business inventories is expected to register a flat reading, according to MMS, corroborating the view that retailer stocks are trim.

As for consumer pocketbooks, economists said not to expect much of a clue from Friday's release of March producer prices. The producer price index is expected to rise 0.1%, the MMS survey said. Excluding volatile food and energy components, it's expected to jump 0.3%.

Economists said the consumer price index, which isn't reported until next week, could benefit in a backlash from February's sharp jump in apparel prices as well as from lower energy prices that weren't fully passed through the previous month. The early consensus view from MMS pegs the CPI with a rise of 0.2%. But Midland Montagu's Mr. Fabbri notes that if the mitigating apparel and energy factors are present, "there's a real good chance the CPI could be a negative number."

Merrill Lynch says in the firm's weekly commentary that economic activity in the first half now "appears to be stronger than we originally projected." The firm now expects first-half real gross national product to grow by about 2.5%. Treasury bond yields could rise by more than one-quarter of one percentage point to 9% by the end of the quarter, the report says. Still, Merrill notes that much of the first-half growth was due to quirks of nature, statistics and auto makers. It adds, "The underlying state of the economy remains soft" and real GNP may rise by only 1.5% by the fourth quarter.
