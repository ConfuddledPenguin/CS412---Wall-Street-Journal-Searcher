Federal Express Posts 1.3% Rise In 2nd-Period Net

  • Author: Daniel Pearl
  • Date: 12/21/90

Federal Express Corp. reported a slight increase in earnings for the second quarter ended Nov. 30, surprising analysts who had expected a drop of as much as 25%. The Memphis, Tenn., express-delivery company reported net income edged up 1.3% to $37.5 million, or 71 cents a share, from $37 million, or 70 cents a share, in the year-earlier period. Revenue rose 16% to $1.99 billion from $1.72 billion.

Earnings rose despite slower volume growth, continued losses from its overseas operations, and a $73 million, or 54%, rise in fuel costs from last year.

"The quarter was better than expected, but given the economy and the very muddled outlook for petroleum prices, they're not out of the woods yet," said David M. Guthrie, analyst with Morgan, Keegan Inc. in Memphis.

Indeed, after releasing earnings late yesterday afternoon, FedEx reported that its average daily volume during November was up a scant 3.8% from the year before. That growth rate compares with 9% in October and 16% to 20% earlier this year.

The company said cost cutting, including reduced overtime and fewer part-time workers' hours, helped offset the overseas losses and slower revenue growth. Salaries and benefits dropped to $846 million from $861 million in the first quarter.

The results "reflect the concerted efforts of all of our employees to improve productivity and reduce expenses in a very difficult environment," said Frederick W. Smith, chief executive officer.

Federal Express also was helped by its participation in the Operation Desert Shield buildup of military forces in the Middle East, though the company wouldn't specify how much that contributed to earnings. A spokesman said the charter business added "at least" $10 million more to revenue than it did last year.

George V. Robertson, analyst with Alex. Brown & Sons Inc. in Baltimore, said that by diverting four 747s to Desert Shield, Federal Express likely gained efficiency in the rest of its fleet, since the company had extra capacity.

"If you get extra hours out of each plane, that's all to the good, especially if the planes are flying full," he said.

Also helping FedEx was a lower tax rate, which the spokesman said resulted from the reorganization of the company's international corporate structure. Second-quarter profit before tax dropped to $76.6 million from $80.8 million a year earlier.

The company didn't quantify its international losses, but Morgan Keegan's Mr. Guthrie said FedEx officials told analysts that results didn't improve from a year ago.
