Chevron to Take A $220 Million Charge for Quarter

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 12/21/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- Chevron Corp. said it expects to take a $220 million special after-tax charge related to the planned disposal of its fertilizer operations against fourth-quarter profit. The oil giant said last month that it began actively seeking offers for the operations, which had 1989 revenue of $287 million. Chevron wants to sell the operations because they are only "marginally profitable" and growth is likely to be "low to zero," spokesmen said.

Chevron hasn't disclosed any other charges or profit estimates for the quarter. The $220 million charge is equal to about 62 cents a share, based on the company's 353 million shares outstanding at Sept. 30.

In the year-earlier fourth quarter, the company had a $1.2 billion charge, resulting in a net loss of $883 million, or $2.58 a share. The largest part of the charge was a write-down of producing and nonproducing oil and gas properties.

Analysts said they were mildly surprised by the size of the anticipated charge for the fertilizer operations, especially in view of their revenue. "It isn't an insignificant number, but overall it's another positive step in the company's restructuring effort," said Eugene Nowak, an oil analyst at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

Several analysts said they don't expect any other large unusual items in the quarter. Analysts' estimates for fourth-quarter operating profits cover a huge range from as little as $1 a share to about $2.55 a share, mainly because of recent large swings in crude oil prices.

Chevron shares rose $1 to $72 yesterday in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Chevron's fertilizer operations include a phosphate mine near Vernal, Utah, connected by a 90-mile pipeline to a plant in Rock Springs, Wyo., that opened in 1986. The company also owns three nitrogen fertilizer plants and a half interest in a fourth plant. The division employs about 600 people.
