Letters to the Editor: Capital-Gains Taxes Can Eat Up a Nest Egg

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/21/90

I hope those who prattle about proposed capital-gains tax reductions being a "break for the rich" read your story about John Kling and his wife ("Autumn Years," page one, Nov. 13) and have some insight about what those levies really do. When Mr. Kling had to surrender his Gulf stock, (the backbone of his life savings) he saw capital-gains taxes chew away the nest egg he was counting on for retirement. The upshot of making capital-gains taxes oppressive is to penalize those frugal enough to make even a modest investment or two during their lifetime. Not just the "rich." (And I'm amazed at what some people call rich.) But considering the credit-card-junkie mentality of this country, our illustrious leaders included, I guess penalizing the prudent among us is right in line with reality.

Forrest Preece Jr.

Austin, Texas

---

Your article consisted primarily of anecdotes, including a bleak portrayal of elderly people who might be considered "poor." There was no mention of just how rare such people are. The official poverty rate for the elderly is only 6%, yet the rate is 20% for American children. Ours is the only developed country in the world with such a generational reversal.

People over age 65 constitute the wealthiest social class in the country, with more than $8 trillion in net worth, not counting pensions and Social Security. Today's retirees who receive Social Security have higher incomes than those paying the tax. They're receiving two to seven times what they put in. Medicare recipients are receiving five to 20 times the actuarial value of prior contributions. Significant cuts are prevented by the most powerful lobby in the country, the American Association of Retired Persons.

As this politically powerful and self-interested portion of our population continues to swell, their benefits are paid for by young workers who will never see the same benefits.

Tom Bohr

Redlands, Calif.

---

As an Elderlaw practitioner, I am reminded constantly that the difference between the (well-off) Brethauers and the (downtrodden) Klings and Macons is, basically, luck -- the former are apparently healthy, and the latter are not. We don't have a national health-care system in this country, a fact that doesn't really confront many people until retirement, when their incomes are fixed and their bodies are more likely to be deteriorating. When they come to see me about a potential nursinghome placement they are astonished to find out that they have lost in the draw if they have an illness for which neither insurance nor Medicare will pay, and they must exhaust their assets to receive assistance from Medicaid, often leaving their spouses impoverished.

When there is no national leadership in a problem area, no rational planning is done, and whatever emerges is a result of many political compromises and has almost nothing whatsoever to do with what the real needs are.

Priscilla Camp

Oakland, Calif.
