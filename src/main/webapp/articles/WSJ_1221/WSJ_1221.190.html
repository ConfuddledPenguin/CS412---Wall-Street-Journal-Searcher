New York Area Lost 100,000 Jobs In 1990, Data Say

  • Author: Kathryn Graven
  • Date: 12/21/90

NEW YORK -- The New York-northeastern New Jersey area lost some 100,000 private sector jobs in 1990, and the employment outlook is expected to remain bleak in 1991, the U.S. Department of Labor's Middle Atlantic Regional office said. Samuel M. Ehrenhalt, regional commissioner of labor statistics for the U.S. Department of Labor, said "1990 is the year the slowdown turned into a substantial downturn." He added, "We're not seeing any let up in 1991."

In a year-end review of labor market conditions in the 23-county area, labor officials said that job losses in key industries such as manufacturing, construction, finance and insurance accelerated in 1990, with New York City, particularly Manhattan, suffering the worst blows.

Once a major source of employment for the area, manufacturing jobs are now quickly disappearing. Some 50,000 manufacturing jobs were cut in the first 10 months of this year, a faster rate than the average decline of 30,000 a year in the 1980s. Since 1979, the region has lost 360,000 manufacturing jobs.

The construction industry also took a serious hit as jobs declined by 37,000 during the first 10 months of 1990, after dropping by 8,000 in 1989 and 4,000 in 1988. The 50,000-plus jobs lost in the last three years reversed a substantial rise of 130,000 construction jobs from 1979 to 1987.

Mr. Ehrenhalt traced the beginning of the decline to the October 1987 stock market crash. Since then, the financial services industry has been forced to undergo a major restructuring. According to the bureau of labor's statistics, the securities industry declined by 10,000 jobs in 1990. Since the peak in late 1987, Wall Street alone has lost some 29,000 jobs. That was after it added 93,000 jobs in the prior 10 years. Another 10,000 jobs were cut this year in banking and nondeposit credit institutions.

While much of the job growth during the 1980s took place in the service sector, the rate of growth of those jobs slowed significantly in the past year, Mr. Ehrenhalt said. Service jobs rose by 55,000 in 1990, but the increase falls short of the 60,000 to 75,000 yearly gains posted during the 1980s.

Mr. Ehrenhalt noted, however, that the loss of jobs hasn't resulted in significantly higher unemployment rates. The region's unemployment rate averaged 5.3% in the first 10 months of this year, close to the national average of 5.4%. In part, that's because there has been a reduction in the growth of the labor force, or simply, there are fewer people in the area looking for work. Mr. Ehrenhalt said another factor might be that in difficult economic times, women and other minorities are discouraged from applying for jobs.

For those who do work in the area, wages aren't rising as fast as they used to. Payroll earnings in the region rose 3.4% in 1989, lower than the national increase of 5.7% and far below the 9.3% rise the previous year.

While the report mostly contained dismal news on the job situation, the one mildly bright spot was the slowing of inflation. Consumer prices in the region rose 6.2% for the 10-month period ending in October, only slightly higher than the 5.8% rate in 1989. A big jump in energy prices accounted for most of the increases. Excluding energy, consumer prices actually declined 0.3% to 5.4% this year, reflecting lower housing costs.

Even so, the prospects for 1991 don't look good. Given the weakness in the national economy and recently announced job slashing at major New York banks, the region's job market isn't likely to come back anytime soon. "The growth engines have run out of steam," says Mr. Ehrenhalt. "This is going to be a tough year for the regional economy."
