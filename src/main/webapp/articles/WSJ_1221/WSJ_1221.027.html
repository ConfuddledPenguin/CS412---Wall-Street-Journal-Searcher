Spanish Fashion Designers Finding Bankers Are Less Kind Than Critics

  • Author: Carlta Vitzthum
  • Date: 12/21/90

MADRID -- When Mexican-born designer Rene Zamudio arrived here four years ago, the first thing he did was ask directions to the garment district. "They sent me to a zipper store down the street," says 32-year-old Mr. Zamudio, who came to Madrid after free-lancing on New York's Seventh Avenue. "That's the way things were."

Although Madrid is a far cry from Milan and Paris, Spanish fashion has gained growing international recognition in recent years. Designers such as Mr. Zamudio have become regulars at fashion shows in Europe and Japan, and their collections have won world-wide praise. This sudden flurry of interest has raised government hopes that Spain is on its way to becoming an important force in European design.

As the experiences of many Spanish designers illustrate, however, critical acclaim doesn't always attract the financial and industrial backing that designers need to survive in a competitive market. Nor does it necessarily translate into growing sales, particularly at home where fashion has traditionally meant French, Italian or German design.

"Spain has a strong cultural force that has been a source of inspiration for many foreign designers," says Maria Jesus Escribano, director of the government's Center for the Promotion of Design and Fashion. "But Spain is still in the process of creating its own fashion culture."

Glossy advertising campaigns, scholarships abroad and two annual fashion shows, one in Madrid and the other in Barcelona, are the mainstay of an eight billion pesetas ($84 million) government plan designed to promote Spanish fashion at home and abroad. Last year the apparel industry, including footwear, jewelry, leather goods, textiles, clothing and accessories, employed 430,000 people and posted sales of three trillion pesetas, equivalent to 7% of Spain's gross domestic product. The government hopes quality and design will ensure a place for Spanish apparel in foreign markets, just the way low prices have in the past.

The mixed success of the plan's first five years underscores many of the problems facing Spanish industry as it gears up for Europe's single market after 1992. The government's program has stirred international interest in Spanish fashion, and some designers have flourished. But it has failed to smooth out many problems that keep the industry at a disadvantage with other European countries.

A shortage of financing, poor quality and a lack of professionalism on the part of retailers, manufacturers and some designers have brought several struggling clothes businesses to their knees.

"The general rule in Spain is that industry isn't prepared to sell or produce a quality product," says Jesus del Pozo, 44, one of Spain's veteran designers.

Antonio Alvarado, wardrobe designer for several movies by Spanish film director Pedro Almodovar, says debts forced him to close shop temporarily last year until one of a few industry backers bailed him out. Now that he has secured financial and industrial support, Mr. Alvarado hopes he can produce more at lower prices.

Mr. Alvarado was one of the lucky ones. In October, designer Manuel Pina decided to give up the fashion business due to mounting debts. The move came after a financial battle with his manufacturer, who prevented him from meeting a 1.5 billion peseta order from a Japanese client. "I'm the first one to fall," he said. "But I don't think I'll be the last." Now he is designing uniforms for Spain's postal service.

In the case of Enrique Loewe, the chairman of Spain's 144-year-old leather goods and clothing store Loewe, it's often easier to fly to Milan to do business than to deal directly with Spanish industry.

But Mr. Loewe, who has worked with top European designers such as Karl Lagerfeld and Giorgio Armani, doesn't limit his criticism to industrialists. Many Spanish designers, he says, don't have sufficient training or discipline to survive in a competitive market. Others, he says, have lost touch with their clients. "In Spain many {designers} experiment too much, they bet too heavily on clothes that are difficult to wear," he says. "There's a lack of interchange" with clients.

Adds Mr. Zamudio: "We have a really bad reputation in the U.S. I know what it's like to work in a store; when you don't get a delivery it's a disaster, and here they don't understand the damage it does."

To avoid problems of quality and late delivery, several Japanese buyers have expressed interest in acquiring the rights to produce Spanish designer clothes in Japan, where Spanish fashion is enjoying an unprecedented boom. Many designers feel their salvation lies in similar arrangements abroad, particularly in promising markets such as Germany and some Asian countries, where they feel appreciated by both consumers and industrialists.

For their part, textile manufacturers claim they can't accept the risks of financing the fashion industry. Not only are designers often considered inexperienced and unprofessional, but a strong peseta, high interest rates and onerous social security payments are said to stack the cards against them in an increasingly competitive world. Analysts expect hundreds of Spain's 15,000 textile and clothing manufacturers to fold in coming years because of growing costs and slumping sales.

Niki Bosch of the Barcelona textile company Niki Bosch Mir says: "The environment is this: a strong peseta and high interest rates. But we have to make a product that enables us to overcome these problems."

Thanks to Spain's import barriers and cheap labor, many manufacturers expanded rapidly in the past, but have failed to adapt to new market conditions and changing consumer tastes.

Since joining the European Community in 1986, Spain's economy began to prosper. In that period, its tastes have evolved rapidly, a fact many manufacturers don't seem to realize. Spaniards, for example, are once again demanding natural fabrics, which fell out of use during Spain's economic boom in the 1960s. At that time, synthetics formed part of Spain's entry into the modern world of mass production.

The weak link between design and industry also has resulted in a dearth of funding for struggling designers. Though the government provides scholarships abroad and other financial help, the fashion industry is still short of funds.

But designers say they don't expect the government to clear the way for them. Instead, they say, the key to the future lies with cementing solid relations with retailers and industry, and seeking new markets abroad. Although the future looks difficult, the worst appears to be in the past.

"We had to work in circumstances that almost nobody else could have survived," says Roberto Verinno, a designer from Galicia who is well-known internationally. "Our {experience} is almost a miracle."
