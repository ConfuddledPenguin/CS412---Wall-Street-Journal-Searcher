Mutual Funds: Does Wave of Muni Mania Mean Now Is Time to Sell?

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 12/21/90

NEW YORK -- Mutual fund investors, shrugging off the recent spate of bad news about state and city budgets, are throwing money at municipal bond funds at a feverish pace. Investors are eager to minimize taxes and they "sense that this is the last game in town," says Dale Rabiner, manager of the $160 million Bartlett Fixed Income Fund, a no-load mutual fund based in Cincinnati.

Mutual fund investors are notoriously bad at guessing market swings. So the municipal-bond buying binge is prompting some nervousness. "Time and time again, contrarian thinking pays off," Mr. Rabiner notes.

Single-state municipal bond funds were the best-selling category of mutual funds in 1989. And they are on track for another chart-topping year.

According to the Investment Company Institute, the trade group for the mutual fund industry, $5.3 billion flooded into single-state funds in the first 10 months of this year. That inflow outstripped any other mutual fund category except money market funds. General municipal bond funds also did well, pulling in $3.2 billion. These figures represent sales minus redemptions, plus the net effect of switches involving other funds in the same family; reinvested dividends are excluded.

Some fund managers believe the current muni mania is entirely justified. "The municipals haven't rallied as much as Treasurys," Ian MacKinnon, Vanguard Group's head of fixed income, says. "Clearly, munis are at the cheap end of their trading range, compared to Treasurys. We think it's a smart idea to buy munis right now."

But if fund investors' current enthusiasm for municipal bonds does foretell a coming decline, it wouldn't be the first time.

Fund investors bought stock funds heavily prior to both the 1987 and 1990 bear markets. They also poured money into U.S. government and tax-free bond funds right up until the April 1987 bond market slump. In 1988, high-yield "junk" bond funds pulled in more money than any other mutual fund category. The following year, the junk bond market started unraveling.

Why are fund investors such poor market-timers? Mr. Rabiner says that when it comes to bonds, the problem arises because many fund investors buy based on yield. What they should really look at, he says, is total return, which includes both the share-price change and the yield. This is because prices for older bonds will slump whenever yields are rising.

Lately, few investments have matched the yield offered by municipal bond funds. According to Lipper Analytical Services, general municipal bond funds have yielded 6.7% over the past 12 months. Figure in a top 33% tax rate, and this yield is worth 10% on a pretax basis. Among the major fund categories tracked by Lipper, only two groups have offered a higher yield, junk bond funds and short-term world income funds.

So far, municipal bond investors have done pretty well. Over the past 12 months, there hasn't been too much disparity between the yields on municipal funds and the total return.

"Compared to stocks, owning municipals has been brilliant," Guy Wickwire, manager of Fidelity High Yield Tax-Free Portfolio, says. "There have been isolated, small defaults over the last few years, and if the economy continues to be bad, we will probably have more. But I don't think the defaults will pick up any."

Bartlett's Mr. Rabiner, however, reckons problems may lie ahead. He notes that many state and local governments are struggling with budget deficits, and yet the yield spread between the highest-rated and lowest-rated municipal bonds is still relatively narrow.

Mr. Rabiner says investors should make sure it really makes sense for them to be in a municipal bond fund, given their income levels. He suggests fund investors might want to switch out of high-yield municipal bond funds and state-specific funds and into a high-quality general municipal bond fund. Even people in high-quality funds should be prepared to sit tight in the face of short-term losses caused as credit problems hurt the price of all municipal bonds, he believes.

But others see little reason to worry. "I think it's a myth that fund investors are generally wrong," Avi Nachmany, a fund analyst with Jesup, Josephthal & Co., says. He points out that the recent returns of municipal bonds haven't been lavish enough to prompt speculative buying. Mr. Nachmany reckons that investors have been buying municipal bond funds simply to shelter income.

For First Investors Consolidated Corp., the bad news keeps on coming.

The New York-based mutual fund company, which is already battling charges of sales abuse brought against it by New York and Massachusetts, has just lost its top fund manager. Daniel Duane, who used to run the top-performing First Investors Global Fund, has left to run three funds managed by Prudential Equity Management Associates, a subsidiary of Prudential Insurance Co. of America, Newark, N.J.

"We don't like to lose anybody," says David Grayson, First Investors president. "But we do have his {Mr. Duane's} righthand person, who worked with him, taking over the fund."
