Coke Enterprises Lifts Its Estimate on Cost Of Fuel-Tank Work

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/21/90

ATLANTA -- Coca-Cola Enterprises Inc. said the cost of inspecting and replacing underground fuel storage tanks to meet federal environmental guidelines will total $71 million, far higher than an earlier estimate of $12 million. The company, a major Coca-Cola bottler that is 49%-owned by Coca-Cola Co., made the disclosure yesterday in conjunction with a new common-share buy-back program, covering as many as 15 million shares.

Coca-Cola Enterprises said the $71 million cost to replace tanks and clean up the surroundings will be spread out between 1990 and 1993. It said an earlier $12 million estimate, covering only 1990 and 1991, was made in its annual report in March, before the company had completed soil testing and tank extraction.

The bottler has a large number of underground tanks around the nation at various distribution centers for storing gasoline and diesel fuel for its truck fleet. Under a 1988 federal law, companies with underground fuel storage tanks must inspect them, replace them as needed, and clean up leakage.

Margaret Carton, a spokeswoman, declined to say how many tanks the company has, how many have been or will be replaced, or how many were discovered to be leaking fuel. However, she said all those that had been leaking fuel have already been replaced and the company is "completely in compliance with all federal, state and local laws." Company spokesmen said the concern had discovered some soil and water contamination, but didn't know the extent.

Separately, the company said it will buy back shares with funds from excess cash flow. The period of time for the buy-back wasn't specified.
