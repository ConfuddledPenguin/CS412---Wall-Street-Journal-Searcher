Canadian Banks Lower Prime Rate One-Half Point

  • Author: Rose Tamburri
  • Date: 12/21/90

OTTAWA -- Key Canadian interest rates fell yesterday to their lowest levels in almost two years amid warnings of a deepening Canadian economic recession. Major commercial banks cut their prime lending rates one-half percentage point to 12.75%, the lowest level since February 1989. The prime rate is a benchmark for various lending rates.

The Canadian Manufacturers' Association, an industry group, said Canada's manufacturing sector "will likely remain in a recession until at least the third quarter of 1991."

Royal Bank of Canada economist Michael Gregory, warned that the current economic recession "is going to get worse." Canada's gross domestic product declined 0.3% in the second and third quarters.

Aron Gampel, senior economist at Bank of Nova Scotia, said a cut in U.S. short-term rates this week gave the Bank of Canada room to lower Canadian rates without weakening the value of the Canadian dollar. The Canadian currency has been buoyed by wide spreads between comparable Canadian and U.S. short-term interest rates.

Despite yesterday's decline, Canadian short-term interest rates remain more than 4.5 percentage points above U.S. rates. "That's still a very tough monetary policy," Mr. Gampel said.

The Canadian central bank has maintained a restrictive economic policy because of inflation concerns.

Some provincial politicians who met with Canadian Finance Minister Michael Wilson yesterday warned that failure to cut interest rates further will prolong the current recession. "I think interest rates have to come down even further," said Alberta Treasurer Dick Johnston.

Mr. Wilson said there are still some lingering inflation pressures in the Canadian economy, particularly high wage gains. Interest rates are moving lower, Mr. Wilson said. "How quickly we can achieve that depends on the inflationary pressures."

The Canadian Chamber of Commerce urged the government to freeze spending over the next two years. The group said a spending freeze was the "only viable solution" for achieving "lasting reductions" in interest rates.

Yesterday's prime rate cut followed a drop in the Bank of Canada's influential bank rate, which fell to 11.78% yesterday from 12.01%. The bank rate is the levy charged by the central bank on loans to financial institutions.

Royal Bank's Mr. Gregory said Canada's short-term interest rates will likely continue declining until February and then level off to offset the inflationary impact of Canada's new 7% value-added tax. The new tax, which received final approval this week, is expected to boost the consumer price index by a one-time increase of 1.25%. The tax will take effect Jan. 1.
