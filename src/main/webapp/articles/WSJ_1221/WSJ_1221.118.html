Grumman Official Quits Amid Reports Of Pentagon Fraud

  • Author: David J. Jefferson
  • Date: 12/21/90

BETHPAGE, N.Y. -- The president of Grumman Corp.'s military electronics group, Thomas A. Guarino, resigned amid news reports of defense-procurement fraud allegedly involving him, a now deceased Long Island businessman, and former Grumman Chairman John O'Brien, who resigned in July. Mr. Guarino's resignation came one day after a Newsday cover story titled "The Favored" detailed Messrs. Gaurino's and O'Brien's alleged involvement with the late James T. Kane, a Long Island businessman who helped run a now-disgraced political lobbying group representing area defense firms. Among other things, the article alleged that Mr. Kane "showered gifts and favors worth hundreds of thousands of dollars" on Messrs. Guarino and O'Brien while he did business with Grumman.

"Although I am innocent of any wrongdoing, suggestions created by Newsday articles may undermine the confidence people must feel in me as president of the systems group," Mr. Guarino, 52 years old, said in a company-issued statement. "I reached this painful decision out of the respect that I feel for the people of Grumman and others, in government and industry, with whom I have worked. I have always sought the constructive solution; in this case, I believe that requires my resignation."

An attorney said to be representing Mr. O'Brien declined to comment.

Grumman systems group, established in April, 1989, comprises the electronics systems division in Great River, N.Y, Melbourne systems in Melbourne, Fla., and space systems in Bethpage. Presidents of those divisions will now report to Grumman Chairman and Chief Executive Renso L.Caporali, who succeeded Mr. O'Brien in July.

Mr. Guarino's stated reason for resigning was in contrast to that of Mr. O'Brien's, who said only that he was leaving for personal reasons. As previously reported, before his resignation, Mr. O'Brien became embroiled in the federal criminal investigation of Pentagon procurement fraud and corruption involving Long Island companies, as investigators stepped up their interest in his ties to Mr. Kane, who died in February. Federal investigators are trying to determine whether Grumman officials or their families received loans or other benefits from Mr. Kane or his firms, according to law enforcement officials.

In May, a former shipping manager at Grumman's aerospace unit pleaded guilty to charges of conspiring to defraud the government by submitting to the Pentagon $300,000 of bogus bills submitted by a subcontractor controlled by Mr. Kane. The former Grumman manager is cooperating with prosecutors.
