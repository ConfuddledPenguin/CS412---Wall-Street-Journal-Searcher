Marketing & Media: NBC Cuts Schedule For Daytime, Gives Time to Affiliates

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 12/21/90

NEW YORK -- The NBC network, running a distant third in daytime programming, will give one hour back to affiliates to let them fill the time themselves, beginning in September 1991. The move means many of NBC's 209 affiliates will have to scramble to buy syndicated programs to fill the suddenly vacant hour. Affiliates generally applauded the move. "It's a practical solution to a practical problem," given NBC's lack of success in daytime programming, said Bill Bolster, president of Multimedia Broadcasting Co., which owns three NBC affiliates.

The decision by NBC, a General Electric Co. unit, reduces the network's daytime schedule to four-and-a-half hours, the lowest of any broadcast network. CBS Inc.'s network, rated No. 1 in the period, programs five-and-a-half hours; and ABC, a unit of Capital Cities/ABC Inc., programs five hours in daytime, which runs from 10 a.m. to 4 p.m.

NBC said it is likely to cancel its 10 a.m. block of game shows -- "Let's Make a Deal" and "Classic Concentration" -- which is cleared by only two-thirds of its affiliates.

"It's a (part of the day) that's had serious erosion in ratings," said John Damiano, NBC's senior vice president of affiliate relations. For the first 13 weeks of the 1990-91 TV season, NBC's daytime schedule was viewed by 3.1% of the U.S. homes with television sets. CBS had 5.5%; ABC had 4.6%.

NBC's move, which had been under consideration for months, comes as all of broadcasting is suffering from a slump in national advertising. Kenneth H. MacQueen, vice president at WHO in Des Moines, Iowa, said he was "less than excited" by NBC's reduction in daytime. "I wouldn't consider it a positive move," he said. "You don't see the other networks throwing back (advertising time) that's hard to sell, do you?"

It is the second shake-up in daytime programming at NBC, which recently canceled the half-hour soap opera "Generations." NBC News is preparing a half-hour midday information show, "A Closer Look," anchored by Faith Daniels, to premiere Jan. 8. NBC is also planning a reality-based show, "Trial Watch," which will feature videotaped footage of actual trials; it will probably replace another daytime program, "The Marsha Warfield Show."

NBC also has ordered pilots of two female-oriented talk shows, "Women's Diaries" with Linda Ellerbee and "AM Assignment," as well as two NBC News productions, "Today's Women" and "Today's People."
