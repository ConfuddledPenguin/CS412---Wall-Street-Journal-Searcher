Commodities: Grain, Soybean Prices Decline Amid Concern About Effects on Trade of Shevardnadze Move

  • Author: Scott Kilman
  • Date: 12/21/90

CHICAGO -- Grain and soybean prices sank on worries that the surprise resignation of Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze might slow trade between Moscow and the U.S. The resignation might complicate top-level discussions about how Moscow will use the $1 billion in food credit guarantees granted by the Bush administration earlier this month, traders said.

In trading at the Chicago Board of Trade, the soybean contract for January delivery sank 5.5 cents a bushel to settle at $5.7075 a bushel. The price of the corn contract for December delivery slipped 1.25 cents a bushel to settle at $2.4975 a bushel.

U.S. grain officials had expected the Soviet Union to quickly use the guarantees to arrange bank credit to purchase as much as 1.2 million metric tons of U.S. wheat, as well as quantities of soybeans and corn. However, snags were developing before Mr. Shevardnadze's resignation.

Earlier this week, the U.S. government reportedly balked at a request by Moscow to use part of the U.S.-backed credit to pay for the expense of shipping the U.S. foodstuffs to Soviet ports. The U.S. government apparently wants Moscow to use the credit solely to buy U.S. goods.

"The resignation casts a further pall on the outlook for any quick shipments to the Soviet Union," said Robert Lekberg, an analyst at Farmers Grain & Livestock Corp., Chicago. "We don't know who is in charge."

Soybean prices also were depressed by reports that the Soviet Union bought a large amount of soybean meal from South American sources instead of the U.S.

The U.S. credit guarantees were granted in part because credit problems had prompted the Soviet Union to slash its purchases of U.S. crops, which in turn had depressed the prices received by U.S. farmers for corn, wheat and soybeans.

Some U.S. traders are speculating that prices of corn and soybean futures contracts will approach their life-of-contract lows unless U.S. exports increase soon.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

ENERGY: Petroleum futures trading on the New York Mercantile Exchange was extremely thin in a session traders termed "dead." Market players seemed more focused on holiday parties than on the Persian Gulf crisis that has dominated oil market news in recent months. Generally, prices fell with the only exception being the two nearest months of heating oil futures contracts. Spurred by forecasts of harsh weather encroaching upon the northeastern U.S., January heating oil prices rose 1.04 cents a gallon to settle at 78.88 cents. The Northeast, the nation's principal heating oil market, has seen unseasonably warm weather so far this winter. And some traders doubt that the Northeast will see quite as severe a cold wave as some of the rest of the country has. Still, dropping temperatures forced analysts and others to take note of the supply picture for heating oil, which reflects the halting of certain exports from Saudi Arabia. In crude, February contracts lost 54 cents a barrel to end the day at $26.39.

COPPER: Futures prices were strong, with the March contract trading as high as $1.1130 a pound. It ended at $1.1005, up 1.15 cents a pound. The March contract traded at a low of $1.0550 on Dec. 7, but has since been locked in a range between $1.06 and $1.09, noted Richard Hirsch, metals trader for Sharps Pixley Inc., New York metals brokers. The fact that the contract rose above $1.10, a key resistance level, and retained the gain yesterday indicates that the copper market is strong, despite the continued buildup of copper supplies, Mr. Hirsch said. One factor that enabled the market to rise sharply was that traders who had previously sold short had to buy contracts to cover their positions as prices rose, he said.

PRECIOUS METALS: Futures prices were mixed, with silver strong, gold easier and platinum slightly higher. March delivery silver ended with a gain of $10.30 cents an ounce at $4.2630, after rising as high as $4.40 (an intraday gain of 24 cents) in early trading. February gold was off $1.30 an ounce at $386.70. April platinum ended with a gain of $2 an ounce at $423.90, but was well off its high for the day of $429. The thinness of the pre-holiday market contributed to silver's strong advance, analysts said. The advance also was accelerated as the March contract rose through several key resistance levels until it reached its high of the day, they said. The fact that it fell back so sharply from its best level indicated that it was mostly a technical market, one analyst said. Continued uncertainty in the Persian Gulf was also a price-firming factor, according to analysts. The resignation of Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze should have had more impact on platinum than on silver because the Soviet Union is the world's second-largest producer of platinum, and any internal unrest could affect that nation's output, one analyst said.

ORANGE JUICE: Futures prices shot up on concern about a forecast of a cold front moving into California's citrus producing areas. The January contract ended with a gain of 9.95 cents a pound at $1.20, just under its intraday high of $1.2005. Wayne Barnes, a meteorologist at Weather Services Corp., in Bedford, Mass., said an Arctic cold wave that brought heavy snow to Seattle late Tuesday was moving down into California. Frost advisories have been issued for Southern California, including the citrus-growing areas, and temperatures are expected to drop into the 20s tonight, tomorrow night and Sunday night. The coldest readings, in the mid-20s, are expected tomorrow night and Sunday night, Mr. Barnes said. The anticipated cold wave is apparently evoking memories of the devastating freeze that hit Florida around this time last year and severely damaged the citrus crop there, analysts said. But the California orange crop is minor compared with Florida's, and the amount of oranges processed for juice is even significantly smaller, said Merrill Lynch analyst Judith Ganes. California is expected to produce only about 20 million gallons of juice, while Florida's output is expected to amount to 236 million gallons, Ms. Ganes said. She also noted that minor damage to the California crop could be more significant for the market than major damage that made some oranges unsuitable for picking and hauling. If only cosmetic damage occurred, she said, it could result in more oranges than normal going into processing for juice, adding an unexpected increase in the total orange juice supply.
