Eastern Airlines Loses Court Round On Striking Pilots

  • Author: Bridget O'Brian
  • Date: 12/21/90

Eastern Airlines should have reinstated striking pilots who returned to work before it put some replacement pilots in its cockpits, a federal appeals court ruled. A three-judge panel of the 11th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals upheld a lower court ruling that said Eastern, a Miami-based unit of Continental Airlines Holdings Inc., shouldn't have favored pilots in training in August 1989 when returning strikers were available.

A lawyer for the pilots union said Eastern might have to pay tens of millions of dollars in back pay. But Eastern said the ruling won't have any immediate impact, according to a spokeswoman, because the airline plans to appeal for a rehearing before the entire 11th circuit. Only about 200 pilots could be affected, she said.

Eastern was nearly grounded in March 1989 after most of its 3,600 unionized pilots walked out in support of striking machinists. But many members of the Air Line Pilots Association started returning to work several months later after Eastern began hiring replacement pilots and it became clear the strike was no longer effective.

Eastern, however, said it had no jobs for returning strikers. Instead, it put them on a recall list for pilot jobs that could become available later. Then, the airline went to court to have the trainees declared permanent employees. For a year, Eastern filled vacant pilot jobs by giving preference to the new-hire list, rather than the returning striker list.

The ALPA contended the trainees can't be permanent replacements under the federal laws that govern airline labor relations. The 11th Circuit, upholding the district court in Miami, agreed, saying the trainees hadn't completed the Federal Aviation Administration-mandated examinations and hadn't operated any regular revenue flights. "Eastern's trainees were not actually performing the work ordinarily discharged by the former strikers," the court said in its opinion.

Separately, BHA Aviation Inc., Miami, said it agreed to acquire an Eastern unit for more than $15 million in cash plus assumption of debt. The unit, Eastern Express, a small commuter carrier that flies only in Florida and the Bahamas, is expected to change hands in January 1991, if the bankruptcy court overseeing Eastern's Chapter 11 case approves.

BHA Aviation is an investor group led by Jack E. Robinson, who was briefly president of Eastern Express earlier this year. Eastern Express was formerly Bar Harbor Airways until August.
