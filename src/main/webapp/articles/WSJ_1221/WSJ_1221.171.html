Pan Am's Talks With TWA Seek Special Financing

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani
  • Date: 12/21/90

Pan Am Corp. appears to be intensifying its efforts to receive needed financing. It is talking to Trans World Airlines about a special type of financing that is usually arranged only for bankruptcy court proceedings, people familiar with the talks said. The special terms are said to provide for so-called "debtor-in-possession" backing by TWA.

This type of financing is arranged for a company that is seeking reorganization. It normally becomes effective after the filing of a petition for bankruptcy, subject to bankruptcy court approval. The lender gets priority liens on unencumbered assets of the company and typically gets paid back before other creditors.

Asked whether the company is seeking such a type of financing, a Pan Am spokesman said, "we are not commenting on any such speculation." Mark Buckstein, general counsel at TWA, said, "While we've had discussions with Pan Am, it's premature to comment further on those discussions."

Pan Am's board is said to have discussed filing for Chapter 11 bankruptcy court protection during a morning meeting this past Wednesday. Thus far, Pan Am has confirmed only that its board met and that it discussed the TWA proposal, as well as developments in the $400 million sale of Pan Am's London operations and other assets to UAL Corp.'s United Airlines.

Pan Am is said also to be asking United to extend it some financing, but the larger carrier is skittish about giving the struggling line any money for fear it will be at risk if Pan Am ceases operations.

People familiar with Pan Am agreed the airline desperately needs cash to cover daily costs and other debts. Like the rest of the industry, Pan Am is being pinched by high jet fuel costs, the company's second-largest expense behind labor.

It has outstanding bills to Airbus Industrie, the European aircraft consortium; United Technologies Corp.'s Pratt & Whitney engine-making unit and the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp., a government agency that insures pension plans.

The TWA talks pertain to a merger proposal TWA owner Carl Icahn sent Pan Am Chairman Thomas Plaskett this past Sunday. Mr. Icahn proposed buying Pan Am's 150 million shares outstanding for $1.50 a share in cash and $1 a share in preferred stock or notes. That amounts to $225 million in cash and $100 million, at the most, in subordinated notes. The offer is slated to expire today, though Mr. Icahn may extend it.

Pan Am executives are said to have told Mr. Icahn that Pan Am needed some sort of bridge loan as part of any merger proposal. A bridge loan is a type of short-term financing that borrowers repay after they get a longer-term loan or cash from the sale of assets.
