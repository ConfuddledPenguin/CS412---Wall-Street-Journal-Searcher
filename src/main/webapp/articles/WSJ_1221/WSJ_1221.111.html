Schumer Seeks Tighter Conflict Of Interest Rule

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 12/21/90

WASHINGTON -- An influential member of the House Banking Committee said he will seek tougher conflict-of-interest rules in the financial system, following an investigation of a director of the quasi-government Topeka Home Loan Bank, who borrowed $6 million from thrifts he regulated. Rep. Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.) released a report by a House task force that outlined how Richard H. Hughes, the director, defaulted on $2.8 million of $6 million in loans. Mr. Hughes, who served on the Topeka, Kan., bank's board from 1979 until a week ago, also unsuccessfully sought to acquire two thrifts under the management of the Topeka bank.

Mr. Hughes's failure to disclose that he moved from Topeka to Dallas while he was still a director of the Topeka bank apparently was his only violation of government regulations. Rep. Schumer asserted that because Mr. Hughes didn't specifically violate thrift regulations, his actions clearly make tougher conflict-of-interest rules necessary.

"Even the possibility that a government official could own a thrift for which he might also oversee credit decisions is extremely troubling," said Rep. Schumer. He added that Mr. Hughes "epitomizes the inherent potential for conflict-of-interest that fueled the ongoing thrift crisis."

Mr. Hughes, also the president of Hinderliter Industries Inc., Dallas, was removed from his position at the Topeka bank a week ago, following the conclusion of the investigation. He was out of his office yesterday and couldn't be reached for comment.

Under previous S&L laws, the 12 regional home loan banks were both a source of housing funds for the S&L industry and served as thrift examiners and regulators. Last year's savings-and-loan cleanup law severed that link.

The home loan banks are currently under the direction of the Federal Housing Finance Board, an arm of the Department of Housing and Urban Development. A spokesman for the Finance Board said that after learning of the House investigation, HUD Secretary Jack Kemp delayed roughly 25 appointments to the regional home loan banks, pending review. The Finance Board will also require more extensive financial disclosure of the regional bank directors, a spokesman said.

The actions of Mr. Hughes, first reported in regional and trade publications, had been challenged by several Topeka bank employees, the report said. Those concerns particularly stemmed from his attempt to acquire two S&Ls that were regulated by the Topeka bank, Fidelity Federal Savings & Loan Association of Claremore, Okla., and Valley Federal Savings & Loan Association of Hutchinson, Kan. Mr. Hughes had borrowed $1.1 million from Valley Federal that became delinquent. The thrift was later seized by the government.

According to the report, Mr. Hughes was asked to resign in 1988 and 1989, and refused. The Federal Housing Finance Board fired Mr. Hughes the day after receiving a copy of the House task force report. He was succeeded by Sister Lillian Murphy of Mercy Housing Inc. in Denver.
