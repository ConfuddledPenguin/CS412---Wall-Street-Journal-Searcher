Thursday's Markets: Soviet Shift Boosts Dollar As Europe's Markets Drop --- Currency Surges as Anxiety Rattles Foreign Traders; Prices of U.S. Bonds Fall

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit and Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 12/21/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar rocketed higher and Europe's financial markets reeled as a growing threat of political instability in the Soviet Union stunned international investors. The U.S. currency gained as international investors sought safety in the face of probable political turmoil. At one point, the dollar was up 2.5% on the day against the mark, and it remained nearly that high late in the New York trading session. The rush to dollars was triggered by the sudden resignation of Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze, a move that called into question the stability of the Soviet Union.

European financial markets were shaken by the news. The German stock market plummeted 3.3%. Prices of German 10-year government bonds, or bunds, fell nearly 1%. Italian stocks were off 3%, Norway's market lost 2.8%, and both the French and Austrian stock markets were down 2.3%. The London stock market, Europe's largest and most liquid, fared better than most others. It was off slightly less than 1%.

U.S. financial markets tended to shrug off the Soviet Union's problems. Treasury bond prices were off for the day, but the Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 2.73 points to close at 2629.46 in active trading.

Some analysts suggested that the lingering Persian Gulf crisis is taking a back seat, at least in European investors' minds, to the Soviet Union's internal problems.

"The Soviet risk is the real political risk," said Anne Parker Mills, a currency strategist at Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc.

Yesterday's rush to the dollar went a long way toward restoring the U.S. currency's tarnished reputation as a haven. The dollar's failure to gain significantly after Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait led many analysts to question that traditional role for the currency. But its sharp rise yesterday, coupled with drops in both the Swiss franc and gold, indicated that the dollar still commands the respect of international investors despite a weak U.S. economy and falling U.S. interest rates. If the Soviet Union's political situation deteriorates further, analysts predict, the dollar will continue to climb.

"At some point," Ms. Mills said, "the European investor will say, `I don't care whether I get a lower rate of return on dollar assets, and I don't care if the U.S. is in a recession. I want to increase my holdings in the U.S. because the dollar is the only real safe haven currency.' That move may have begun in earnest."

Mr. Shevardnadze's resignation is important because of his close association with Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev and with Mr. Gorbachev's program to liberalize the Soviet Union internally and conduct a more accommodating foreign policy. Mr. Shevardnadze also is well known and respected outside the Soviet Union. Analysts said his resignation raises the possibility of two dangerous scenarios: either a return to a hard-line dictatorship in Moscow or the ultimate breakup of the Soviet Union.

Turmoil in the Soviet Union has a direct and pronounced effect on European markets, particularly Germany's, because of Germany's proximity to Eastern Europe and the fact that Germany is Moscow's largest trading partner in the West.

"Germany was the market that benefited the most" from the political and economic changes in Eastern Europe, said Steven Keddell, a first vice president at Deutsche Bank Capital Corp. in New York. Therefore, German markets stand to suffer most from a reversal of those changes, he said.

In major market action:

U.S. stock prices were mixed. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 174.7 million shares. Declining issues on the Big Board were ahead of gainers, 813726.

Bond prices fell. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond lost 5/8 of a point, or $6.25 for each $1,000 face amount. The yield on the issue rose to 8.23%.

The dollar rose sharply. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5115 marks and 135.85 yen, compared with 1.4785 marks and 134.05 yen late Wednesday.
