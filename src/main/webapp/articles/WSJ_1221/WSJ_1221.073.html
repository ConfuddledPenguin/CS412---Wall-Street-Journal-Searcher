Economy: Businesses, Seeing Recession Deepening, Scale Back '91 Capital Spending Plans

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 12/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Amid signs of a deepening recession, businesses are scaling back their capital investment plans, the Commerce Department reported. In the government's first survey of capital spending plans for next year, businesses told the department that they planned to increase their spending for new plant and equipment a cautious 2.4% in 1991 over 1990.

According to the survey taken in October and November, businesses expected to increase capital spending 5.2% in 1990 over 1989. That estimate has headed steadily downward this year along with the outlook for the economy, according to the quarterly surveys.

In the January-to-March survey, capital spending in 1990 was expected to increase 8.1%. In the second quarter, businesses told the department they expected a 6.8% increase in investment. By the third quarter, a 5.4% gain was predicted.

The 5.2% increase now expected would put plant and equipment spending for the year at $533.91 billion. In 1989, spending rose 11.4%, following an 11% jump in 1988.

After adjustment for inflation, the spending figures evince even less confidence on the part of businesses: The increase in 1990 would be 4.1% and the increase in 1991 would be a tiny 0.4%.

"These are all slumpy, recessionary numbers," said Lawrence Kudlow, chief economist with Bear Stearns & Co. in New York.

In fact, some economists said actual spending in 1990 and plans for 1991 may well come down from the October and November survey estimates. A spate of economic reports in recent weeks have suggested that the economy took a serious tumble in the fourth quarter. Manufacturing slowdowns have pushed up unemployment, the trade deficit has worsened and consumer spending has risen only slightly.

The October-to-November survey is "still an optimistic reading," said Allen Sinai, chief economist at Boston Co. He said the downturn in the economy has been so sharp in the fourth quarter that inflation-adjusted capital spending plans for 1991 likely would turn down in the next quarterly survey.

The 2.4% increase anticipated next year would be the smallest capital investment increase since a 2.6% decline in 1986.

Manufacturers, in particular, were planning to restrict their capital spending sharply from previous years. They said they planned a 0.7% increase in 1991, following an expected 4.6% increase this year and gains of 12.5% in 1989 and 15.9% in 1988. Within the manufacturing category, makers of durable goods -- more expensive items intended to last at least three years -- expected to decrease spending 0.8% next year after an increase this year of 1.4%. Nondurable goods makers were planning a 1.8% increase next year, following a 7.3% rise this year.

Nonmanufacturing businesses said they expected to boost spending 3.4% next year, compared with an expected 5.6% increase in 1990 and increases of 10.8% in 1989 and 8.4% in 1988.

The figures are adjusted for seasonal variations.
