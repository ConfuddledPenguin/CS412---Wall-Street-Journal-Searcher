Economy: Consumer Spending Rose Scant 0.1%, Income Edged Up 0.3% in November

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 12/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Consumer spending barely climbed in November as personal incomes increased only slightly, suggesting that consumers have hunkered down and won't contribute to economic strength in the next few months. The Commerce Department reported that consumer spending increased only 0.1% in November to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $3.727 trillion, after remaining flat in October. Personal income rose a scant 0.3% last month, to a rate of $4.711 trillion, also after no gain in October. The department said that almost all of the November increase in personal income came from higher federal subsidy payments to farmers.

"We are seeing a real retrenchment of the consumer," said John Silvia, an economist with Kemper Financial Services in Chicago. Many economists have pointed in recent weeks to growing consumer fears about the economy as a key factor in the slowdown in consumer spending. But the report for November suggested that laggard growth in incomes also is holding back spending.

Even more striking, consumer spending after adjustment for inflation fell 0.2% in November after dropping 0.8% in October. Consumer spending accounts for about two-thirds of gross national product -- the nation's output of goods and services. The recent declines in spending could subtract more than two percentage points from the inflation-adjusted GNP growth rate in the fourth quarter, said Michael Penzer, an economist for San Francisco-based Bank of America.

The vast majority of economists agree that GNP likely will shrink in the fourth quarter; estimates of the contraction range from about 1% to 4% at an annual rate.

Moreover, the report suggested spending probably won't climb for at least a few months because real disposable income -- after-tax income adjusted for inflation -- has stagnated. This measure of income was flat in November, following decreases of 0.8% in October and 0.3% in September. So far, disposable income is falling at a 4.6% annual rate in the fourth quarter, Mr. Penzer said.

"There just isn't the income there to support spending," he said. "Real disposable income is falling off a cliff. It's likely to hit first-quarter spending as well."

The savings rate -- savings as a ratio of disposable personal income -- totaled 4% in November, compared with 3.7% in October and 3.8% in September. The rate is still at relatively low levels compared with the last decade. In the early years of the 1980s, it hovered around 7%. Last year, the savings rate was 4.6%.

Consumer spending on durable goods -- goods intended to last more than three years -- fell 1.9% after a 2.6% drop in October. Spending on nondurables was up 0.5% in November, following a 0.2% rise the previous month. Spending on services increased 0.3% after a 0.5% gain.

Wage and salary income decreased a slight 0.1% in November to a $2.728 trillion annual rate. Manufacturing wages fell 1.7% to a $540.3 billion rate, and wages in commodity-producing industries dropped 1.5% to a $719.6 billion rate.
