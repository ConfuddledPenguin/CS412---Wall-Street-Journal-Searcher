Bank of Boston Projects Sizable 4th-Period Loss --- Weak Economy and Market For Real Estate Blamed; Loss for Year Is Certain

  • Author: Ron Suskind
  • Date: 12/21/90

BOSTON -- Bank of Boston Corp., blaming the slumping New England economy, said it will show a "substantial loss" for the fourth quarter, ensuring a loss for the year. Although the bank didn't give an estimate of the loss, a spokesman said it would be smaller than the bank's third-quarter loss of $255 million.

"I expect this quarter's loss to be somewhere between $150 and $200 million and for the losses to continue in 1991," said Gerard Cassidy, a bank analyst with Tucker Anthony and R.L. Day in Portland, Maine. "The bank's dividend will almost surely disappear next year." The problems at Bank of Boston, the region's largest bank, underscores the continuing slide of results at banks throughout New England, many of which have reported losses in the past year.

With a weakened real estate market worsened by a sluggish economy, Bank of Boston was struggling under $1.86 billion of nonperforming assets, mostly soured commercial real estate loans, as of Sept. 30.

"The prolonged deterioration in the commerical real estate markets and the ongoing downturn in the regional economy continue to adversely affect our operating results," said Ira Stepanian, chairman and chief executive officer. "However, we are unwavering in our commitment in this environment to deal aggressively with credit issues and to maintain what we believe to be strong loan-loss reserves."

The projected fourth-quarter loss means that the Bank of Boston, which had a loss of $207.8 million for the first nine months, will post a sizable loss for all of 1990. Last year it had net income of $70.4 million, or 80 cents a share, after a one-time pretax gain of $147 million from the sale of its credit-card business.

In declaring its third-quarter loss, the bank slashed its dividend from 31 cents to 10 cents a share. Bank officials said yesterday that the dividend would be maintained at 10 cents for the payment due in January.

The company's stock closed at $7 a share in New York Stock Exchange composite trading, down 37.5 cents.

As with many banks in the region, Bank of Boston is slashing expenses as earnings fall. The bank announced in September that it planned to reduce its work force by 8%, or about 1,500 employees, by offering voluntary severance packages. So far, only about 800 employees have accepted. The bank said it will now begin a program to lay off at least 700 more employees by the end of next year's first quarter. As well, the bank announced a deferral of salary increases affecting at least 5,000 bank officers.
