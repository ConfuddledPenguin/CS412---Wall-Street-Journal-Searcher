College Bowl Sponsors Changing Games' Names

IN COLLEGE bowl games, sponsors grapple with how to score in the name game.

Over the past four years, corporate sponsors have rushed to put their names on 12 of 19 college football bowl games. Despite spending millions of dollars, companies find their names often slip through the cracks because of inconsistent media treatment.

For example, Chrysler calls the game to be played on Christmas Day the Eagle Aloha Bowl, after its Jeep/Eagle division. That's also how USA Today and the Gannett News Service refer to it. But in Associated Press stories and this week's TV Guide, it's simply the Aloha Bowl.

This year, marketers will be closely watching the results of two so-called sole-title sponsorships. Blockbuster Entertainment, the video-rental chain, is spending $2.5 million to create the first Blockbuster Bowl. And for the second year, the former Sun Bowl will be called strictly the John Hancock Bowl. "We're in this for one thing: visibility," says Jack Mahoney, who directs sports marketing for John Hancock Financial Services.

"This kind of exclusivity may be the focus of the future," says Frank Potter, director of marketing for Joyce Julius & Associates, publisher of Sponsors Report, which monitors sponsor exposure in televised sports events.

Scrambling to Reach

Yanks Working Abroad

EXPATRIATE executives have to be one of the most attractive yet elusive targets in marketing. Now, a handful of companies are developing new ways to reach this mostly scattered audience.

It's no easy task. Ads, for example, won't help much -- no single periodical or television or radio station effectively reaches the estimated three million Americans working abroad.

But for some companies, it's worth the effort. Executives abroad represent a demographic gold mine. According to Citibank, the typical U.S. executive working overseas is a 41-year-old married man with children and an annual income of at least $100,000.

Enter Living Abroad Publishing, a company based in New York that's preparing to launch two guidebooks for executives living and working in Europe and Asia. The $45 guides include information on tax and financial services, travel, children's education and medical services, such as lists of hospitals abroad that accept U.S. insurance policies.

Citibank, one of the guide's sponsors, hopes to get a boost for its own program, Personal Banking for Overseas Executives. The service, which evolved out of the bank's in-house program for international staffers, handles transactions such as paying mortgages for U.S. homes and insurance premiums. Citibank found that people about to ship out rarely give much thought to banking. So the bank has been working with providers of more obvious expatriate services, such as relocation consultants and language schools, to boost its program's visibility.

Says Jon B. Clements, president of Clements & Co., a Washington, D.C., firm that specializes in insurance for foreign service workers and is increasingly targeting executives abroad: "The market is endless."

Coke Wins a Round

In Fight With FTC

THE SOFT-DRINK industry has emerged from under a cloud, at least for now.

In a case stemming from its aborted attempt to acquire Dr Pepper in 1986, Coca-Cola last week was handed a victory in an antitrust battle with the Federal Trade Commission. Administrative Law Judge Lewis Parker could have ordered Coca-Cola, the nation's No. 1 soft-drink company, to seek FTC clearance to acquire any soft-drink or bottling operation for 10 years.

Such a ruling, which the FTC sought, could have profoundly reshaped the nature of competition in the marketing-intensive, $44 billion soft-drink business. An unfettered PepsiCo, for instance, could have scanned the beverage landscape and cherry-picked bottlers or smaller root-beer or specialty soft-drink companies, while Coca-Cola would have to wade through the federal bureaucracy to pull off any deals.

Coca-Cola won't comment on the ruling, which the FTC says it may appeal.

The ruling "could have handcuffed Coke's abilities to deal in a hotly competitive marketplace," says Jesse Meyers, publisher of Beverage Digest newsletter. Also, industry executives fretted that the case could have led to governmental interference with other soda companies.
