Acquisition of PS Of New Hampshire Is Cleared by SEC

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 12/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Public Service Co. of New Hampshire cleared a major regulatory hurdle in its plan to be acquired by Northeast Utilities and emerge from Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection. The embattled utility's plan was unanimously approved by the Securities and Exchange Commission, which has partial control over the accord because Northeast is a holding company for utilities and must register with the agency.

The transaction, which required nine federal and state regulatory clearances, also received the conditional blessing yesterday of an administrative law judge at the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission. The acquisition now requires the energy commission's approval.

The U.S. Bankruptcy Court in Manchester, N.H., has approved the accord, but that decision is being appealed. The Nuclear Regulatory Commission also needs to allow the transfer of the license for the Seabrook Nuclear Power Project to Northeast, Hartford, Conn. Still standing in the way of the deal is the final permission of several states, including New Hampshire, a state in which a rate increase recently was approved and is being appealed.

Despite those remaining hurdles, yesterday's actions are a boost for PS New Hampshire, which filed for bankruptcy-court protection in January 1988 after drowning in debt related to its 35.6% holding in the controversial Seabrook plant. Northeast then proposed to buy the Manchester utility for $2.3 billion, setting the regulatory steps in motion.

In examining the proposed acquisition, the SEC staff looked at the financing of the accord, the schedule of rate increases, the proposed savings to the utilities of combining their overhead, and how the move would change the concentration of electric-power generation in New England. The commission followed the staff recommendation to approve the deal.

In doing so, the agency rejected a proposal by several intervenors to hold a hearing on the plan at the SEC, said William Weeden, the SEC official most closely involved in the proposal. He said their objections centered on whether the acquisition by Northeast would create a near monopoly for electric power in the region.

Separately, the SEC voted to put out for public comment a proposal designed to help overseas underwriters make offerings in the U.S. The plan, already in place for U.S. underwriters, would allow the overseas underwriting firm making an offering in the U.S. to support the stock price.

"The commission has always recognized that as a form of manipulation, but a form of manipulation that is needed," said Larry Bergmann, the agency's associate director for market regulation. He said that the SEC has been giving exemptions for these "stabilizations" to foreign underwriters but that the agency is looking to "codify" those exemptions.
