GLOOMIER OECD PREDICTIONS

Worse-than-expected U.S. economic data have caused the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development to revise downward already gloomy predictions about the world economy.

Scrapping forecasts compiled just a month ago, the OECD now says the U.S. economy probably didn't grow at all in the second half of this year, and the total output of growth and services may decline during the first half of next year. Previous projections were for 0.6% growth in each of those periods.

The only good news in the 24-nation organization's prognosis is that the stagnant U.S. economy won't drag down other industrial economies as much as in past recessions. "We don't see an OECD-wide recession coming," said Stephen Potter, an OECD economist. Continued growth in Germany and Japan is the main factor keeping the broader group of nations from sinking further economically.

Factoring in the worsening outlook in the U.S., OECD chief economist David Henderson estimated that growth in 1990 for the 24 nations would come to 2.7%.

AUSTRALIA VS. INSIDER TRADING

Penalties for insider trading in Australia will be increased sharply under draft legislation made public yesterday. Attorney General Michael Duffy said the legislation would see the maximum penalty for individuals convicted of insider trading lifted to 200,000 Australian dollars (US$154,340) from A$20,000 currently. The maximum penalty for corporations would increase to A$1 million from A$50,000 currently. Mr. Duffy said the higher penalties "signaled in no uncertain terms . . . that insider trading won't be tolerated."

PRIVATIZING PORTUGAL'S PRESS

Portugal announced plans to complete its privatization of the press, with the sale of the prestigious daily newspaper Diario de Noticias. The sale of two million of the paper's shares is expected to bring the state about 10 billion escudos ($75 million). The real-estate value of the paper's offices in downtown Lisbon accounts for almost half the price.

ISRAEL-CHINA TIES

Israel may send three diplomats to Beijing to upgrade the status of an academicliaison office in the Chinese capital. The three would be the first Israeli diplomats sent to China, the Jerusalem Post reported. The Foreign Ministry declined comment on the report. But a government source said the dispatch of diplomats to China was "a major possibility" that required only final approval.

GROWING THREAT TO WORLD HEALTH

An estimated 250 million or more sexually transmitted infections -- including syphilis, genital herpes, gonorrhea and the human immunodeficiency virus, or HIV -- occur annually around the world, and the number is increasing, according to the World Health Organization. "Sexually transmitted infections are not under control, either in the developed or developing world," said Andre Meheus, chief medical officer of the WHO Program of Sexually Transmitted Diseases. WHO said, "Prevention of sexually transmitted diseases and infections is rendered difficult by a variety of factors, including the relative ease of transmission, the difficulties of changing sexual behavior {and} the lack of vaccines or, in some cases, curative treatments."

REPEAL OF APARTHEID LAWS?

A leading South African newspaper reported that the South African government would announce plans to repeal major apartheid laws when Parliament opens Feb. 1. Business Day said President F.W. de Klerk is expected to call for the scrapping of the Group Areas Act, which segregates neighborhoods by race, and the Land Acts, which reserve 87% of the country's land for the white minority.

LARGEST OVERSEAS INVESTOR

JAPAN became the world's largest investor in overseas industry and property in 1989, surpassing Britain, the Japan External Trade Organization said in its annual report on direct investment.

The U.S. remained the largest recipient of direct-investment funds in the year. But the report shows increasing flows of funds to Europe, and large cross-border investment within both that region and Southeast Asia.

A total of $197.878 billion was spent in direct investment in 1989, an increase of 21.5% from the previous year, the organization said. It estimated that the total amount of outstanding cross-border investment was $1.263 trillion at the end of the year. Direct investment includes investment in companies, factories and property, but not securities purchases or similar purely financial transactions.

Japan invested $44.13 billion overseas in 1989, up 29% from the previous year, the organization said, bringing its total direct investment overseas to $154.4 billion. Behind Japan were Britain, in 1988 the largest investing country, with $31.753 billion, down 13.8%, for a total of 191.9 billion; and the U.S., with $31.722 billion, up 80.9%, for a total of 373.4 billion.
