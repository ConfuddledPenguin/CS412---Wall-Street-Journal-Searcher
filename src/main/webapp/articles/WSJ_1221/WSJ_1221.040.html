Homeownership Rate Fell During the 1980s, to 63.9%

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/21/90

WASHINGTON -- The rate of homeownership in the U.S. fell during the 1980s, the first decade-long decline since the 1930s, the government reported. The Census Bureau said 63.9% of all households owned their homes in 1989, down from 65.6% in 1980. Nearly all the rest rented. Homeownership, however, is still much more common than it was in 1950, when 55% of households owned their homes.

The decline in homeownership during the 1980s was particularly noticeable among younger Americans, who saw housing prices in some regions soar beyond reach.

Some 39.1% of households headed by a person under 35 years old owned homes in 1989, down from 41.2% in 1982, the first year for which such data are available. Among households headed by someone over 50, the figure remained constant at 77.2%.

Although homeownership rates in the Northeast and West continue to be consistently lower than the national average, the Northeast was the only region where a higher fraction of households owned houses at the end of the decade than at the beginning.

Patterns of homeownership vary widely; renting is more common in urban areas. In the District of Columbia, for instance, only 38.7% of the households own their own homes. In New York state, the fraction is 52.3% and in California, it's 53.6%. But in West Virginia, 74.8% of the households own their homes; in Maine, 73.6% do.
