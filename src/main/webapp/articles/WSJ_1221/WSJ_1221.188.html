Technology: Intel Charges Cyrix With Violating Patent On Computer Chip

  • Author: null
  • Date: 12/21/90

SANTA CLARA, Calif. -- Intel Corp. said it sued Cyrix Corp. in a Texas federal court, charging that the small Texas chip maker violated an Intel patent in a chip that mimics a popular Intel computer chip. Intel said its suit, filed in U.S. District Court in Dallas, charges that a Cyrix "co-processor" chip duplicates the function of a patent that Intel has held since 1982. Co-processor chips are used in personal computers to speed up certain calculations, and work together with the computer's main microprocessor, or "brain," chip.

Cyrix's chip mimics Intel's 80387 co-processor, which is designed to work alongside Intel's popular 80386 microprocessor. Intel said it filed the suit after it failed to reach agreement with Cyrix after several months of negotiations over the disputed chip. Intel said its suit requests unspecified damages and an injunction to prevent Cyrix from continued infringement.

Cyrix President Jerry Rogers said that Cyrix is "absolutely not" infringing on the Intel patent and that the Intel suit is probably a reaction to suits filed by Cyrix. Cyrix, based in Richardson, Texas, last week filed antitrust lawsuits against Intel in federal and state courts in Texas, Mr. Rogers said, charging that Intel "has been trying to keep us out of the market and hinder our business" by intimidating Cyrix's suppliers and customers, among other things.

Intel's suit illustrates the tough time chip makers have in developing components that clone popular established chips without sparking legal challenges. Intel has a separate suit pending against Advanced Micro Devices Inc., whose co-processor chip Intel claims violates Intel copyrights. Intel has a history of suing companies that try to clone its chips.
