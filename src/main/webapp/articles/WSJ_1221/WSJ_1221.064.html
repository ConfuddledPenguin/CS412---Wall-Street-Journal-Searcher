Temple-Inland Expects Increase In Quarter's Net --- Earnings Are Seen Reaching $46 Million in 4th Period, Lifted by Thrift Results

  • Author: Karen Blumenthal
  • Date: 12/21/90

DIBOLL, Texas -- Temple-Inland Inc., bolstered by its thrift operations, expects to report net income of $46 million to $50 million, for the fourth quarter, up from $44 million last year, said Clifford J. Grum, president and chief executive officer. Mr. Grum said in an interview that the estimated earnings gain reflects better performance in its thrift business and the inclusion, for the first time, of its Guaranty Federal Savings Bank unit in its results. Temple-Inland's financial services sector is expected to report a $10 million to $12 million operating profit, compared with a year-ago operating loss, while the company's manufacturing unit's operating earnings are expected to decline 30% to 40% from a year ago, to $44 million to $52 million.

In addition to better performance from its thrift businesses, Temple-Inland also received tax benefits from its thrift purchases that reduced its 1990 tax rate to about 13% from 34% last year, Mr. Grum said.

Temple-Inland is estimating that per-share earnings will be 83 cents to 90 cents for the quarter, compared with 80 cents last year. The year-ago results have been restated to exclude two insurance companies that were sold.

Fourth-quarter revenue is expected to rise about 21%, to $593 million from $489 million. Manufacturing operations account for about $500 million, while the inclusion of Guaranty makes up most of the remainder of the increase, Mr. Grum said.

Mr. Grum said that volume has been strong in its container, containerboard, pulp and paperboard businesses, but costs have been rising. A $30 price increase early in the quarter to $410 per ton of linerboard failed to stick, eroding margins, he said.

The company's building products division, which includes lumber and particleboard products, however, has been hurt by the decline in home building and by a decline in residential repairs and remodeling. The "building materials {business} is not just in a recession. It may be in a depression," he said.

In contrast, Wayne McDonald, Temple-Inland's chief financial officer, said the company's two thrifts are growing, and benefiting from a sharp decline in foreclosures and an increase in loan closings. Mr. McDonald said that costs related to foreclosures will drop to about $5 million this year from $11 million last year. At the same time, the company's thrifts made about $560 million in loans, compared with less than $300 million in 1989. The thrifts also have reduced brokered deposits, lowering their cost of funds.

Mr. Grum said that he can't currently predict that the company will post another earnings gain in 1991 given the current state of the economy.

For 1990, the company is expected to report net income of $230 million to $234 million, or $4.15 to $4.22 a share, compared with 1989 net income of $207.4 million, or $3.75 a share. Financial services operating earnings are expected to be about $50 million to $52 million, including about $34 million from Guaranty Federal, while manufacturing operating profit is expected to be $250 million to $258 million.

Revenue is expected to rise to $2.4 billion from $1.9 billion, largely because of the inclusion of Guaranty Federal's results.

Mr. Grum said he believes the financial services group will have a record year in 1991 that "should allow the company's overall results to be very favorable compared to most of our competition." But he said, "if you wired me to the wall, I think both building products and paper would be down next year." In addition, because some tax credits were unique to this year, the company's tax rate will increase to about 20% next year, he said.

After next year, the future of those tax credits isn't clear. Because of criticism that some buyers of failed Texas thrifts like Temple-Inland got bargains under the so-called Southwest Plan, Mr. Grum said that he expects "sometime next year we will sit down with regulators" to discuss the agreement. However, he said he doesn't know whether anything will come out of any discussions.

In 1991, Temple-Inland is expected to spend about $350 million in capital expenditures, up from $325 million this year. The company will start up its new linerboard and bleached board product, called White Top, late next year. In addition, it is expanding its bleached paperboard mill in Evadale, Texas, scheduled for completion in 1992, and is building a recycled paper mill in Maysville, Ky., that is expected to open in 1993.

Partly because of capital spending and partly because of thrift acquisitions, Temple-Inland's long-term debt will increase to about $560 at year-end from about $390 million last year. Mr. Grum said the company probably will add to its debt next year, possibly by selling senior debt through private placements to institutions.
