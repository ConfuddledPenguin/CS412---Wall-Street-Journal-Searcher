Bank of New England Tentatively Sets Debt-for-Equity Swap With Bondholders

  • Author: Ron Suskind
  • Date: 12/21/90

BOSTON -- Bondholders of Bank of New England Corp. tentatively agreed to a debt-for-equity swap that would give them 92% of the company's common stock and give the bank breathing room in its effort to survive. By reducing bank's debt payments, the swap of $706 million in debentures for almost all newly issued stock would ease a squeeze on the bank's cash while it struggles to recover from bad real estate loans that have resulted in steep losses.

"The swap is a necessary but not sufficient condition for survival," said Don Kauth, an analyst with First Albany Corp. "The added capital would certainly buy them some time, maybe three or four quarters of time."

Even with a successful swap, "the bank needs economic growth in the region so it can begin booking good loans and grow out of its problems, just like Bank of America did a few years ago," says Gerard Cassidy, an analyst with Tucker, Anthony and R.L. Day in Portland, Maine. "Of course, the New England economy might not turn up for another year. The bank's destiny is, at this point, not within their control."

BNE has some 69,545,000 common shares outstanding now. In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, BNE common closed at $1.125, up 12.5 cents.

The tentative agreement, following months of negotiations, was reached by committees representing bondholders and must still be approved by the Bank of New England's directors, according to people familiar with the negotiations. The bank's shareholders -- whose stake would be severely diluted -- also must approve the swap. In addition, at least 90% of the bonds must be tendered for the swap to proceed.

Bondholders had initially hoped to get a combination of cash and stock for their bonds, but the bank is cash-starved and according to bank officials, regulators have directed the bank to apply cash gleaned from recent asset sales to other needs.

Bank of New England's current capital cushion is a thin $255 million, or 1.1% of its total assets of $23 billion. The swap, if it moves forward, will save the bank about $60 million a year in interest payments and boost its capital to nearly 3%, a level regulators mandated the bank reach by last spring. Healthy banks have capital levels of 6% or more.

The arrangement would split the bank's 92% ownership almost evenly between holders of $181 million of senior debt and holders of $525 million of subordinated debt. Under the plan, bondholders also would receive another $100 million in newly issued debt; the new bonds won't pay interest but are eventually redeemable for cash or stock in five years. Committees representing bondholders want new bonds issued so that they will have some contractual claims on assets if the bank fails or is purchased; debt holders' claims take priority to stockholders in cases of bankruptcies or failures. Bank officials declined to comment.

The bank is currently burdened by $2.84 billion in nonperforming assets -- mostly commercial real estate loans -- that have been rising steadily through 1990 as the New England economy continues to slump. The carrying costs of such bad assets, estimated at $250 million annually, are largely responsible for the bank losing $203 million in the first nine months of this year.

If the swap were to fail because not enough bondholders participate, most analysts agree that the bank would be unable to survive for long.
