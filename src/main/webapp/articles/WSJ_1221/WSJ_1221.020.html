Credit Ratings: Revlon's Junk Debt Of $828 Million Is Downgraded

  • Author: Kathleen Deveny
  • Date: 12/21/90

NEW YORK -- Ronald O. Perelman's debt-laden holdings sustained a blow yesterday when Moody's Investor Service Inc. downgraded the ratings of $828 million of junk bonds at two of his companies. Affected was the subordinated debt of holding company Revlon Group Inc., which was downgraded to Caa from single-B-3. Moody's also downgraded Revlon Inc.'s senior debt to single-B-2 from single-B-1.

In part, the move was prompted by Revlon's lack of financial flexibility at a time when the U.S. economy is weakening, and competition is increasing in global beauty care and cosmetics, according to the rating service. But Moody's also attributed the action to the probability that the company's debt will need to be refinanced beginning in 1992, and further access to the credit markets will be restricted by the covenants of the company's bank loans.

Mr. Perelman has operated Revlon with a heavy debt load since he acquired the cosmetics maker in a hostile takeover in 1985. A series of asset sales have reduced that debt somewhat, although the closely held company isn't required to report the debt of Revlon Group. Moody's said it believed additional asset sales could result in lower margins and cut into cash flow.

A Revlon Group spokesman said an agreement that it made earlier this year to restructure a $1.8 billion bank loan required certain asset sales. He added that the company has identified several assets, including a flavorings company and real-estate holdings, that could be sold without reducing the company's cash flow.

Junk bond analysts said they weren't surprised by Moody's action, and the prices of Revlon's bonds weren't affected by the announcement. "Nobody is happy to see it, but it wasn't a shock," says one high-yield analyst.

Moody's confirmed the single-B-3 rating for the subordinated debt of Revlon Inc., as well as the not-prime rating for its commercial paper.
