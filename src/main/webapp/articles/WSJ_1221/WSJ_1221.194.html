Law: Dixon Is Convicted, McBirney Agrees To Plead Guilty in Separate S&L Cases

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 12/21/90

DALLAS -- Edwin T. McBirney III, former owner of Sunbelt Savings Association of Texas, has agreed to plead guilty to federal criminal charges and the agreement will be filed today, his defense attorney said. The filing would come less than 24 hours after a jury convicted Don R. Dixon, ex-owner of Vernon Savings & Loan Association. Messrs. McBirney and Dixon have been among the highest-priority targets of the U.S. Justice Department's war on thrift fraud because of their gaudy life styles and the millions of dollars in losses racked up by their thrifts. Winning the cases against two such high-profile defendants is a major coup for the Justice Department's oft-criticized Dallas Bank Fraud Task Force.

Mr. Dixon was convicted yesterday of 23 federal criminal charges, most of them related to the use of Vernon funds to lease a California beach house for the thrift owner. Three of the charges related to thrift funds that paid for prostitutes for Vernon board members. The charges carry a maximum possible prison term of 120 years.

Mr. Dixon was acquitted of 15 other charges relating to the use of thrift funds to reimburse Vernon employees for political campaign contributions and out-of-state trips with the former commissioner of the Texas Savings and Loan Department.

None of the charges against Mr. Dixon dealt with any of the questionable loans and real estate transactions that led to Vernon's losses of almost $2 billion and its collapse in 1987. Because the charges against Mr. Dixon dealt with relatively low-dollar expenditures, his attorney, William M. Ravkind, predicted throughout the trial and almost nine days of jury deliberations that Mr. Dixon would be acquitted.

After the verdict yesterday, Mr. Dixon was ordered jailed without bond until his sentencing in February, and an embittered Mr. Ravkind said, "I should have pleaded him guilty."

The possibility of a substantial prison term following a guilty verdict may have prompted Mr. McBirney's expected plea agreement. The former Sunbelt Savings owner faced a maximum possible sentence of 85 years in prison if convicted of all 17 charges filed against him in his July 11 indictment.

Mr. McBirney's attorney, Paul E. Coggins of Dallas, said the plea agreement will be filed in federal court in Dallas today. A Justice Department spokesman declined to comment. It wasn't known what specific charges Mr. McBirney was expected to admit or what the terms of his plea would be.

But in previous plea agreements with thrift executives, the Justice Department has required defendants to cooperate in other investigations. Mr. McBirney's cooperation, if required, could be extremely valuable to prosecutors because of his extensive dealings with other thrifts and their executives.

Among the now-failed thrifts that were involved in real estate and loan transactions with Sunbelt and Mr. McBirney were San Jacinto Savings Association of Houston, a unit of Southmark Corp. until its recent seizure by thrift regulators; Independent American Savings Assocation, which was owned by Thomas R. Gaubert of Dallas; and Western Savings Association of Dallas, formerly owned by Jarrett E. Woods Jr.

The Federal Bureau of Investigation has acknowledged an ongoing criminal investigation of San Jacinto, and Mr. Gaubert has remained a target of federal prosecutors despite his acquittal in October 1988 on charges related to loans from an Iowa thrift. Mr. Woods is scheduled for trial in January on 37 charges related to the failure of Western Savings. If convicted, he would face a maximum possible sentence of 185 years in prison.
