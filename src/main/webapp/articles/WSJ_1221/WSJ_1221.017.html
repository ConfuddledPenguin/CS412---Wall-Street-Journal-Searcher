La-Z-Boy Expects Period Net to Meet Year-Ago Levels

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 12/21/90

MONROE, Mich. -- Despite a recent drop in orders, La-Z-Boy Chair Co. expects to match last year's profit of $6.1 million, or 34 cents a share, for its third quarter ending in January, said Treasurer Gene M. Hardy. However, Mr. Hardy is expecting a profit "slippage" in the fourth quarter and Chairman Charles T. Knabusch said it is unlikely the dividend will be increased this fiscal year.

The order rate for the company's products dropped about four weeks ago but company officials are expecting it to bounce back in January. If that doesn't happen, the company's earnings could fall 10% or 15% below analysts' predictions of a profit of $25 million, or $1.40 a share, for the current fiscal year ending in April, Mr. Knabusch said. If orders do bounce back, he expects to meet analysts' expectations.

In the last fiscal year, La-Z-Boy earned $28.3 million, or $1.58 a share. Mr. Hardy said it was now "out of the question" that the company would match these profits. This would be the first time the company hadn't increased its annual profits since 1985.

La-Z-Boy posted a profit of $8.8 million, or 49 cents a share, in the six months ending Oct. 27. That was down from year-earlier profit of $11.1 million, or 62 cents a share. The big drop stemmed largely from one-time costs in the first quarter to expand and improve several plants, company officials said.

The company, which last boosted its dividend in September 1989 to 14 cents a quarter, has traditionally raised its dividend every "four or five quarters," Mr. Knabusch said. But he said a dividend increase this year was unlikely if the company's profit falls short of last year's, as expected.

La-Z-Boy, which has a 30% share of the reclining chair market, expects revenue of roughly $620 million in the current fiscal year, up from $592 million last year. In recent years, La-Z-Boy has branched out into bedroom sets and other hardwood furniture through acquisitions.

However, these new lines of furniture have been harder hit by the economic slowdown than reclining chairs. In addition, La-Z-Boy had to take on nearly $50 million in debt to finance its 1988 purchase of Kincaid Furniture Co. in Hudson, N.C. The company has since paid down that debt to about $40 million.

La-Z-Boy's capital spending is expected to drop to between $13 million and $15 million in the next fiscal year from about $22 million in the current year, Mr. Hardy said.

The company expects hardwood furniture sales will rise faster than its reclining chair sales in an improving economy.
