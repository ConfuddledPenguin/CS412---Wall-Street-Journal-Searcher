Credit Markets: World Bond Prices Fall on News of Resignation Of Shevardnadze, Hint of More Soviet Turmoil

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Silvia Ascarelli
  • Date: 12/21/90

NEW YORK -- Perceptions of growing political turmoil in the Soviet Union roiled international bond markets yesterday, hammering prices and boosting yields. Early yesterday, Eduard Shevardnadze resigned his position as Soviet Foreign Minister and warned that "dictatorship was coming" to the Soviet Union. His departure is seen creating more problems for Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev.

"The hint of anarchy is very unnerving to the bond market," said David McClean, a proprietary trader at Credit Lyonnais Securities USA, a unit of the big French bank.

Erick Muller, bond market economist at Credit Commercial de France, said the Shevardnadze resignation brought the Soviet Union's problems to the forefront and "showed {investors} what a big problem the U.S.S.R. can become."

Analysts worry that should the Soviet Union disintegrate into chaos, others countries in Europe -- especially Germany -- could be faced with a flood of refugees. To finance the potentially enormous cost of absorbing the refugees, along with other costs associated with helping to stabilize the Soviet Union, European governments will be forced to borrow enormous amounts of money in the credit markets. Such borrowing, in turn, would put upward pressure on European interest rates.

Even before the Shevardnadze resignation hit the news wires, bond market sentiment in Europe was bearish due to speculation that the Bundesbank, Germany's central bank, may soon raise interest rates another notch in an effort to wring out inflation. In turn, that could force other European countries, such as France, to raise rates also.

Klaus Baader, a bond market analyst with UBS Phillips & Drew in London, said that while political uncertainty in the Soviet Union "is bad news for bonds all around" the world, it's particularly bad for Germany because German banks have lent the Soviet Union billions of marks, all of which are guaranteed by the Bonn government. The latest loan, for 1.2 billion marks ($811.9 million), was signed Tuesday.

In addition, trade with the Soviet Union remains important for industries in eastern Germany. "If you take away that last bit of demand for eastern German industry, it's going to make the adjustment process that much more difficult."

In trading yesterday, bonds issued by Germany and France were hardest hit of the major government bond markets, followed by Britain and the U.S.

Germany's benchmark 10-year government bond, or bund, fell nearly one point to a price of 100.3 to yield 8.83%. France's 8.5% 10-year bond also fell nearly a point to 91.32 to yield 9.99% while the 8.5% 30-year bond fell 1 7/8 points to 85.65 to yield 10.04%.

In Britain, the benchmark 11.75% gilt due 2003/2007 was quoted at 106 27/32, down 26/32 from late Wednesday to yield 10.72%.

Meanwhile, First Chicago Corp. said its First National Bank of Chicago subsidiary lowered its prime, or base lending rate, to 9.5% from 10%. American National Bank & Trust Co. of Chicago, also a First Chicago unit, was the only other bank to follow suit. On Wednesday, several regional banks lowered the prime rate, although no major money center bank has yet done so.

Treasury Securities

Prices of U.S. Treasury securities followed the European markets yesterday, posting modest losses on news of Mr. Shevardnadze's resignation.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended 5/8 point lower, a decline of $6.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, rose to 8.23% from 8.17% on Wednesday.

Rates on Treasury bills were modestly lower. The discount rate on three-month bills ended at 6.55% for a bond-equivalent yield of 6.73%, down from Wednesday when the discount rate ended at 6.59% to yield 6.77%.

Yesterday's loss followed even sharper declines on Wednesday, when investors sold bonds in an effort to capture profits as the year end approaches.

But trading activity has been light because many investors have moved to the sidelines, where they will remain until the new year begins.

Market players "want no risk, and any political turmoil that may take place outside or even in the domestic market will have a magnified impact," said Maria Ramirez, who heads Maria Ramirez Capital Consultants Inc.

Recent declines in interest rates and news of political strife in the Middle East and Soviet Union has prompted investors to "assume the opportunity to take profits, to lock in profits before they totally evaporate," said Ms. Ramirez. At this juncture, "there's no reason to take risk."

Corporate Bonds

Most investment-grade corporate bonds ended 1/4 point lower. In the junk bond market, prices of speculative-grade bonds, or securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, finished little changed.

There were no new issues.

Municipal Securities

Municipal bonds ended steady to 1/4 point lower.

In ratings action, Standard & Poor's Corp. placed the triple-B rating of the Philadelphia International Airport's airport revenue bonds on its Credit-Watch surveillance list with negative implications.

According to S&P, the action reflects "the city's inability to access the note market," and possible repercussions relating to the airport's capital funds.

The bonds are rated B-a by Moody's Investors Service Inc.

A total of $154 million of outstanding debt is affected by the action. Philadelphia traders report that the bonds were unaffected by the news and continue to trade at yields in the 9% range, similar to those of the city's general obligation bonds.

In secondary trading, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 were 1/4 lower at 98 7/8 to yield 7.27%.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended with losses of 6/32 to 8/32.

Government National Mortgage Association 9% securities for January delivery were down 8/32 at 99 5/32. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. Mac Gold 9% securities and Federal National Mortgage Association 9% securities were down 6/32 at 98 29/32 and 98 21/32, respectively.

The Resolution Trust Corp., the government-sponsored thrift bailout organization, sold approximately $750 million of mortgage securities.

According to one Freddie Mac trader, the issues were very well received.

In the primary market, no new issues were announced.
