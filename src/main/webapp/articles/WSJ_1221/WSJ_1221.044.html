World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Fall on Profit-Taking as Investors See No Sign of Credit Easing; London Closes Down

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 12/21/90

Tokyo stock prices fell back Thursday, finishing just above their intraday lows under pressure of profit-taking. The impact of Tuesday's discount-rate reduction by the U.S. Federal Reserve Board, which spawned active buying Wednesday, ebbed as investors were unable to see a clear sign that the Bank of Japan would ease credit, traders said.

In London, share prices finished weaker as the resignation of Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze injected a note of uncertainty into world financial markets.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 selected issues was down 351.84 points at 24524.94. In trading early Friday, the Nikkei Index was down 416.49 to 24108.45.

Volume on the first section Thursday was estimated at 360 million shares, down from 639 million shares Wednesday.

Declining issues outnumbered advancing issues 856 to 157, while 125 issues remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section was down 26.04 points, or 1.4%, at 1795.37.

The second section index was down 23.51 points, or 0.8%, to close at 2823.27.

Volume in the second section was estimated at five million shares, down from 5.6 million shares Wednesday.

As the yen was weaker against the dollar and the Japanese bond market softened Thursday despite the U.S. discount-rate cut Tuesday, stock market participants shared a feeling that they've already heard the extent of the good news.

The dollar finished Thursday at 134.58 yen, up from 134.05 yen late Wednesday in New York and above 133.27 yen late Wednesday in Tokyo.

Traders said both bond and stock markets had discounted an expected reduction of the U.S. rate.

"Participants are already looking for the next news on interest rates, especially a decline in short-term domestic interest rates," said Atsushi Koishihara, general manager of stock trading at Taiheiyo Securities.

Supporting stock prices at the bottom range Thursday were investment trust funds, "eager to push up and keep the Nikkei Index above 25000 until the year end," said a trader at Yamaichi Securities.

Strong resistance at 25000 on the Nikkei is discouraging investors from buying stocks at higher prices, however, traders said, prompting more investors, including dealers, to begin reducing positions ahead of the long weekend. The market will be closed Monday for a national holiday.

Trading by overseas investors slowed down as many of them have already left for year-end holidays, traders said. Foreign participation was also discouraged by continued reports about the involvement of a ruling party politician, Toshiyuki Inamura, in the ongoing Koshin stock scandal.

"Foreigners are more sensitive to such a political scandal" than the Japanese, as reflected on the yen's weakness on the foreign-exchange market, a trader at Daiwa Securities said.

But overall, the impact remained minimal as it was the previous day.

Large-capital shares were sold in a broad range, with steel manufacturers, shipbuilding companies and electric power companies ending lower. Their prices, however, rebounded from intraday lows on buying by investment trust funds and dealers in the afternoon session, traders said.

Nippon Steel was the most heavily traded stock for the day, finishing at 444 yen ($3.31), down 1 yen.

Mitsubishi Heavy Industries lost 15 yen to 700 yen, while IHI fell 16 yen to 700 yen.

High-priced, high-technology shares fell from their midday highs to end mixed.

Sony was down 130 yen to 6,100 yen and Pioneer lost 70 yen to 4,050 yen. TDK was up 50 yen to 4,540 yen, while Kyocera edged up 10 yen to 5,850 yen.

Investors took profits from construction shares as Kajima declined 60 yen to 1,620 yen, Shimizu plummeted 20 yen to 1,580 yen and Taisei dropped 30 yen to 1,000 yen.

London traders said the impact of Mr. Shevardnadze's decision to leave the Soviet government was felt extensively across European equity markets.

"The fears are widespread," said John Reynolds, equity strategist at brokerage County NatWest WoodMac in London. "Europe wants a stable Russia without Soviet people piling over their borders. (The resignation) does add to the general economic uncertainty which makes markets nervous."

Wall Street was also having its share of jitters over the news. Throughout most of the European afternoon, the Dow Jones Industrial Average was under pressure, although by the close of the session in London, Wall Street appeared to be reversing its negative direction.

At the closing bell, the broad-based Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-Share Index was off 19.9 points at 2158.8.

In the futures market, the December FT-SE 100-Share Index contract settled at 2163.0 on the London International Financial Futures Exchange, compared with its Wednesday closing of 2193.0.

At its final calculation, the narrower FT 30-Share Index was off 19.9 points at 1687.2. Volume reached a moderate 590.2 million shares compared with the 509.3 million issues traded Wednesday.

Shares in Imperial Chemical Industries, Britain's largest chemical concern, slid 24 pence (46 cents) to 886 after several brokerages cut their full-year pretax profit forecasts to under #1 billion.

Glaxo shares continued their week-long slide on competition worries from Astra AB's anti-ulcer drug Losec. The stock lost 11 to 823.

Pharmaceutical maker Wellcome lost 22 to 441, after gaining 20 pence a day ago, on mixed reaction to an analysts meeting. The company also announced that it sold its Calmic hygiene service business to Rentokil Group, an environmental services and property care concern for #26.2 million.

Unilever also retreated, dropping 10 to 687 after a downgrading from brokerage UBS-Phillips & Drew.

Among featured stocks in the broadly lower market, retailer Marks & Spencer hardened 2.5 to 226 in a flight to quality amid recessionary times, dealers said. A robust 4.8 million shares changed hands.

Composite insurer General Accident advanced 3 to 496. The company is expected to make an announcement Friday, which market professionals say will be bullish for the company.

Elsewhere, share prices closed lower in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Brussels, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Milan and Madrid. South African gold stocks closed higher.

Stocks closed lower in Sydney and Hong Kong, and higher in Singapore, Taipei and Manila, while they closed little changed in Wellington and mixed in Seoul.
