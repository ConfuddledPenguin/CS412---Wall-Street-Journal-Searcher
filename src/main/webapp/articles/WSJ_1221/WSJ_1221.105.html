Law -- Legal Beat: California Rulings May Limit Worker Suits

  • Author: Stephen Kreider Yoder and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 12/21/90

In three closely watched labor-law cases, the California Supreme Court delivered two rulings that favor employers and one that aids employees. The court issued two rulings that could limit employees' ability to seek damages for emotional distress stemming from wrongful discharge. But in a third case, the court ruled that an employee can bypass the state civil-rights agency to sue in the courts for wrongful-discharge damages in sexual harassment cases.

The cases were important to businesses because they speak to an expensive problem: employees seeking increasingly large damages for "pain and suffering" caused by wrongful termination.

The court's rulings were "a mixed bag," said Paul Grossman, general counsel for the California Employment Law Council, an association of major businesses. But they "indicate that California isn't going to be way out in crazy land by itself with respect to compensatory and punitive damages in routine discharge disputes."

In one case, Shoemaker vs. Myers, the court ruled that, if an employee sues for damages stemming from wrongful termination, any disabling injuries -- mental or physical -- fall under the coverage of workers' compensation, which strictly limits the amount of money that can be awarded. But the court ruled that an employee can sue for damages outside workers' compensation if there are statutory exceptions or if the discharge resulted from risks that aren't within the compensation bargain.

Jack Shoemaker, an investigator with the state health department, was fired in 1981 after he reported a superior's illegal activities. Reinstated the next year, Mr. Shoemaker sued for damages for emotional distress and humiliation. The court ruled that, under the state's statutes to protect "whistle blowers," Mr. Shoemaker is allowed to sue for damages outside the provisions of workers' compensation.

Although it is a victory for whistleblowers, the ruling was seen as a confirmation of the increasingly conservative tone taken by California's high court, which has eviscerated thousands of lawsuits by employees seeking punitive damages for wrongful discharge. Until now, though, the use of compensatory damages for "pain and suffering" remained "the one area of possible tort recovery that could really sting employers," said Stanford University labor-law Prof. William Gould. With the Shoemaker ruling, "they've deprived plaintiffs of one of their most important weapons."

In a second case, the Court ruled that the state civil rights commission doesn't have the right to award damages for emotional distress for employment discrimination. In the case, a temporary secretary, Rose Brown, charged that she was sexually harassed by a superior at Peralta Community College where she worked in 1981 and 1982. The Fair Employment and Housing commission awarded Ms. Brown $20,000 in damages, an award that was overruled by a trial court, which argued that the commission couldn't award damages for trauma and humiliation. Overturned in an appeal, the ruling was reinstated by the Supreme Court.

The third case, Rojo vs. Kliger, involved an employee, Emma Rojo, who charged that she refused her employer's demands for sexual favors, which ultimately forced her to quit the job. The Court ruled that the sexual harassment involved violated Ms. Rojo's common law rights and therefore that Ms. Rojo can bypass the provisions of the state civil rights act and sue for damages in court.

PHILIPPINES SETTLES litigation over Marcos properties in Manhattan.

The Philippine government sued Ferdinand Marcos and his wife, Imelda, in 1986, accusing them of buying four Manhattan buildings with funds stolen from the Philippines. Mr. Marcos was ousted as president of the Philippines in 1986, and he and his family fled to the U.S. Mr. Marcos died in 1989.

The settlement also ends other related litigation, including a lawsuit brought by Joseph and Ralph Bernstein seeking money from the Marcos buildings. The Bernsteins are real estate developers who managed some of the Marcos properties.

Under the terms of the settlement, the Philippines apparently will get far less than the $160 million of allegedly stolen money that it claims the Marcoses spent to acquire the buildings. Two of the buildings have already been sold, but delays resulted in a much lower price than the Philippine government had expected.

The settlement states that if the money from the sale of the buildings totals $10.8 million or less, the Philippines will receive 47% of the total, and the Bernstein interests will receive 20%. The rest will go to Karl Peterson, an attorney who works for Saudi businessman Adnan Khashoggi, who was acquitted last year of illegally helping Mrs. Marcos conceal her ownership of the buildings. Mr. Peterson's company also claimed a stake in the buildings. At trial in federal court in Manhattan, Mrs. Marcos also was cleared of any wrongdoing in the transactions.

If the buildings bring more than $10.8 million, the additional money will be divided with 55% going to the Philippines, 40% to the Bernstein interests, and 5% to Mr. Peterson.

The settlement doesn't state how much money actually will be available from the sale of the buildings, after maintenance costs and other expenses. The total amount, however, is expected to be between $10 million and $11 million.

The settlement says that the money turned over to the Philippines "will be paid in recognition of its claim . . . for monies stolen from the Philippines by Ferdinand Marcos, Imelda Marcos and certain other non-settling parties."

Jeffrey Greenbaum, an attorney for the Philippines, said the settlement was a "moral vindication" of the Philippine government's position.

CASINO UNION SAYS it is outraged by government charges of mob domination.

"Any allegations that individuals other than {the union's} membership have influence on our activities are totally false," said Roy Silbert, the president of New Jersey Local 54 of the Hotel and Restaurant Employees International Union, at a news conference yesterday.

The Justice Department filed a civil racketeering suit on Wednesday against the local, union officers and organized crime figures. Filed in federal court in Trenton, N.J., the suit alleges that for the last 20 years the 22,000-member local has been controlled by a Philadelphia-based organized crime family and that the family's head, Nicodemo Scarfo, is running the local now from his federal prison cell.

The suit calls for a court-appointed trustee to take control of union affairs. Local 54 represents bartenders, hotel employees and casino workers in Atlantic City and other New Jersey towns.

Mr. Silbert, the local's president for six years, called the suit "an attempt at government takeover, reflecting the worst in totalitarianism."

The union, he said, has told its lawyers at the Philadelphia law firm Meranze & Katz "to do whatever is necessary to stop this inexplicable government attempt to create a media circus."

MICHAEL MILKEN APPEALS 10-year prison sentence.

As expected, attorneys for Mr. Milken asked the U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals to review the sentence, handed down last month by U.S. District Judge Kimba Wood. Mr. Milken, formerly head of the junk-bond operation at Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., pleaded guilty in April to six felonies related to securities transactions.

Judge Wood said she would consider reducing the sentence if Mr. Milken cooperated. At the time of his guilty plea, Mr. Milken also agreed to pay $600 million in fines and restitution.

Oral arguments on the appeal are scheduled for the week of March 18.

PICTURE-TAKING in federal court in Manhattan will begin in July.

The U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals and the U.S. district court in Manhattan have been selected as test courts for plans to allow still and video cameras in the federal courts. Although some state courts allow cameras in courtrooms, they currently aren't permitted in federal court.

Another federal appeals court and five other district courts are expected to take part in the three-year program, which will apply only to civil cases.
