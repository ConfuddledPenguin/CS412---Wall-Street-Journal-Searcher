Letters to the Editor: Voters' Lament: But You Promised...

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/26/90

You make a critically important point in your July 2 editorial "Mr. Bush's Leadership." Democracy cannot work if candidates for office do not fulfill their campaign promises at least as often as they break them. The increasingly low voter turnout in national elections reflects cynicism about the process. President Carter contributed significantly to this disaffection after his election in 1976, in which he pledged to end control of energy, end the double taxing of dividends, and vowed never to give up the Panama Canal. All three pledges were abandoned soon after his inauguration.

Those who continue to vote are those who still think they can tell an honest promise from a cynical one. President Bush was so emphatic in his pledge not to raise taxes that he seemed a good bet at the time. He still has not broken his pledge, although it is certainly bent. If the package he negotiates with the Democratic Congress is seen to be in the spirit of his pledge, which has economic growth as the objective, the electorate will understand the bending of his promise.

The worst recent example of political cynicism is New Jersey Gov. James Florio's decision to push mammoth tax increases through the Legislature his party controls. He had indicated he would raise taxes only as a last resort. Had he presented anything like the plan that now makes the New Jersey income tax effectively the highest in the nation, it is impossible to imagine him being elected.

It is also difficult to imagine Gov. Florio getting his package through the Legislature if he had permitted time for public hearings. But he did not give the electorate a chance to express itself in this manner either, leaving the impression that he simply does not trust his own constituents to have valid opinions -- a common trait in authoritarian regimes. Californians, on the other hand, protect themselves against this kind of political arrogance, through the initiative and referendum process. Their recent decision to permit tax increases through this process has to be considered a correct one, as it derives from the collective wisdom of the electorate.

The Florio package already is leading to movements in New Jersey to acquire the protection of the initiative and referendum. If politicians at the national level cannot manage to keep their most ardent campaign pledges, there surely will be movements at that level for national referendum and initiative.

Jude Wanniski

President

Polyconomics Inc.

Morristown, N.J.
