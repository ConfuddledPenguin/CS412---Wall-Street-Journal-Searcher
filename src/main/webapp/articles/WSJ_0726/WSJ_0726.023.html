Avon Products Net In 2nd Quarter Increased by 20%

  • Author: Jeffrey A. Trachtenberg
  • Date: 07/26/90

NEW YORK -- A successful cost-cutting program enabled Avon Products Inc. to report a 20% gain in second-quarter earnings on a revenue improvement of only 2%. Avon, a door-to-door seller of gifts, cosmetics and accessories, earned $47.5 million, or 68 cents a share, compared with $39.5 million, or 56 cents a share, a year earlier. Sales were $793.3 million, compared with $777.7 million. Excluding 1989 revenue from the Parfums Stern division, which Avon sold in January, sales increased 6%.

Although its U.S. revenue was up only 2%, domestic pretax operating earnings increased 29%. Avon also said revenue increased 10% in its international division, which includes Europe, Latin America, Canada and the Pacific, but operating profit was down 5%. The company said the division's pretax earnings last year were inflated by a tax refund in Britain. Excluding the 1989 refund, it said, Avon International pretax earnings rose 5%.

In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, Avon shares closed at $36.875, up 37.5 cents.

James Preston, Avon's chairman and chief executive officer, said he expects U.S. revenue will show gains for the rest of the year. U.S. sales softened in the second half of 1989.

"Avon's business is gaining momentum," said Andrew Shore, an analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "They've succeeded in lowering their cost structure. It's fair to say that the company is doing better than it has in some time."

Mr. Shore said that one of Avon's key challenges involves increasing U.S. revenue. "You only have so many rabbits to pull out of the hat," he said, referring to Avon's cost-cutting program. "At some point they will have to prove they can grow their business here."

The cost-cutting was evident in Avon's income statement. Interest expenses dropped in the quarter to $24.1 million from $28 million, reflecting a cut in Avon's debt to $645 million as of June 30 from $1.09 billion at the end of the 1989 second quarter. Marketing, distribution and administrative expenses were reduced to $379.9 million from $398 million.

"We like the stock because we expect to see further cost-cutting and debt reduction," said Diana Temple, an analyst with Salomon Brothers. "We're estimating that the company will earn $2.65 {a share} this year and $3.15 next year."

Robert Renck Jr., managing partner of R.L. Renck & Co., a brokerage firm that owns Avon stock for its money-management clients, also expressed confidence despite the current gloomy outlook for most retailers.

"Avon is basically selling low-priced products which people can afford even during a recession," he said. "People can afford them. The economy isn't a major worry for me."

In the six months, Avon's net income rose 37% to $62.7 million, or 79 cents a share, from $45.7 million, or 51 cents a share. Sales climbed 1% to $1.5 billion from $1.48 billion.
