Letters to the Editor: Brokers Quaking? I Certainly Doubt It

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/26/90

Your June 11 page-one article "Blow to Brokers" presents a skewed view of the status of customer-broker arbitrations. The notion that securities firms suddenly have become concerned with the prospect that the arbitration system may threaten their financial well-being is as absurd as it is ironic. After pursuing a single-minded effort to deny customers access to the courts, the securities industry apparently has undertaken to establish that customers now have the upper hand. To emphasize this, you cite "a surge in punitive-damage awards," noting that punitive-damage awards were handed down in 21 instances since May 1989. You fail, however, to mention that this represents about one-third of 1% of the 6,101 arbitration cases that the article notes were filed in 1988. Somehow it is difficult to believe that these results have caused Wall Street brokers to quake before their computers.

The fact is that the awarding of punitive damages is still unusual. But customers in some states (such as Georgia, Florida and Arizona) are far more likely to recover punitive damages than those in other states (such as New York, where the largest number of claims are filed).

As long as customers do not receive uniform treatment regardless of their state of residence, the system has failed.

Even where customers are successful, most arbitration awards do not cover the full amount of their losses.

Hartley T. Bernstein

New York
