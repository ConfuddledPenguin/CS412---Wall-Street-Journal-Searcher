Stotler to Stop Brokering Futures After Big Default

  • Author: Scott McMurray
  • Date: 07/26/90

CHICAGO -- Financially crippled Stotler Group Inc. said it was pulling out of the futures brokerage business after defaulting on $750,000 in short-term commercial paper obligations. Stotler's announcement highlighted the precarious financial underpinnings of many local futures trading firms, whose capital accounts haven't kept pace with the exponential growth in futures trading during the past decade. The company's slide to the brink of insolvency also raises questions about the ability and willingness of futures industry regulators to rein in the activities of well-established brokerage firms, said senior officials at other futures brokerage firms here.

"We still don't have any idea how this could have happened so quickly," said the head of a rival futures brokerage house, who asked to remain anonymous.

Stotler Group, whose chairman, Karsten "Cash" Mahlmann, is also chairman of the Chicago Board of Trade, said it faces an additional $7.6 million in debt payments in the next 30 days. Stotler said it is weighing "all available options" to try to shore up its finances, including restructuring or refinancing its debt and selling subsidiaries and assets.

Stotler's rapid decline over the past two weeks has been a blow to Mr. Mahlmann's prestige and his wallet. The taciturn executive, who prides himself on his organizational acumen, is serving his third consecutive one-year term as the top elected official of the world's largest futures exchange.

He is also believed to have a substantial portion of his net worth tied up in Stotler Group stock, which plunged to $2.50 a share Tuesday on the American Stock Exchange, after trading at about $8 a share two weeks ago. The stock didn't open for trading yesterday. Mr. Mahlmann owns about 10% of the outstanding shares of Stotler Group, according to regulatory filings.

Stotler said it would wind down its futures brokerage business "in an orderly manner over the coming days." Stotler officials didn't respond to calls seeking elaboration.

Stotler Group officials have scrambled the past two weeks to hold the company together after the Board of Trade said its principal subsidiary, Stotler & Co., had failed to meet capital requirements. Futures industry regulations require firms that clear and guarantee customer trades to maintain a capital cushion equal to at least 4% of customer-segregated funds.

The default apparently was triggered when the company failed on Monday to secure a capital infusion of about $10 million from a Taiwanese investor. To reduce its regulatory capital requirements, Stotler & Co. on Tuesday gave up its clearing memberships on all major futures exchanges. The firm said it would clear its futures trades through another futures firm, Brody, White & Co.

During much of the past two weeks, the firm was furiously shipping customer accounts to other futures brokers to reduce the amount of capital it was required to carry on its books. The company said it reduced its customer funds to $120 million from $290 million.

Neither Stotler Group nor the Board of Trade have said what caused the futures brokerage firm's failure to meet capital requirements. Board of Trade members said the problem appeared to stem from a dispute with regulators regarding what assets could be counted as regulatory capital and how that capital should be apportioned among the Stotler Group holding company, the Stotler & Co. unit and other subsidiaries.

Futures industry officials said it was unclear how Stotler Group's Des Moines, Iowa, securities subsidiary, R.G. Dickinson & Co., has been affected by its parent company's woes. Stotler Group bought Dickinson after the small broker-dealer underwrote Stotler Group's initial public stock offering in 1988. Dickinson officials didn't return calls seeking comment.
