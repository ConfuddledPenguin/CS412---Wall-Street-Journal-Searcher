International: Japanese Visas Elude Students Who Fled China

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 07/26/90

TOKYO -- Some Chinese students in Japan aren't being granted visa extensions that would allow them to remain in the country, despite fears they face retaliation in China for their pro-democracy activities, students and Japanese human-rights advocates say. Meeting with foreign reporters, three students and their Japanese supporters said four leaders of a pro-democracy group have been told by immigration authorities that they must return to China in the next month. About 20 Chinese students active in the group have been told their applications would be taken under consideration. But none have actually received extensions, the students and lawyers said. Some have been kept for months in an uncertain legal status, their visas having expired and their renewal applications classified merely as "pending."

The Japanese government, along with other developed nations, "said it would protect students after Tiananmen," said Katsutoshi Fujimori, an attorney representing Chinese students in Japan. "But it doesn't look like they plan to really honor that promise." Chinese soldiers who cleared pro-democracy protesters from Tiananmen Square in Beijing in early June 1989 killed hundreds of students, workers and others there and in other parts of the Chinese capital.

Japanese officials say they are standing by a pledge, made after last year's summit of industrial nations in France, to give pro-democracy Chinese students favorable treatment within the framework of Japanese immigration law. A section chief in charge of residence issues in the Justice Ministry's immigration agency said most extension applications are being processed within three months. And he said applicants with documentation of continued student status have received extensions, though he couldn't say how many.

More than 11,000 Chinese entered Japan in 1989 to study, according to the immigration official. About 65 students last year asked for visa extensions for political reasons, a Foreign Ministry official said.

Unlike the U.S. and some other countries, Japan hasn't granted a general right for Chinese students to stay. Instead, authorities here have insisted on treating Chinese applicants' cases individually. The immigration official wasn't available to comment on the cases of students at the news conference.

Japan has been criticized by humanrights advocates for its inhospitality toward political refugees. Many Japanese fear that open immigration could disrupt the country's social harmony.
