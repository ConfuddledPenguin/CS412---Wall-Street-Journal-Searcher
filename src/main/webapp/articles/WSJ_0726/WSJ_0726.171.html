Law: Store Is Kept As Defendant In Tobacco Suit

  • Author: Suzanne Alexander and David Stipp
  • Date: 07/26/90

BOSTON -- The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court threw out part of a tobacco-liability lawsuit against Philip Morris Cos. But in a potentially precedent-setting move, the court allowed plaintiffs to name as a defendant the retail store that allegedly sold the company's cigarettes to minors. The plaintiffs claimed Philip Morris should be liable for injuries sustained by two minors who bought cigarettes at a chain of convenience stores. The high court, in granting summary judgment in favor of Philip Morris, dismissed claims that the tobacco company should be held liable for the selling of its cigarettes to minors by convenience stores.

But the court allowed the plaintiffs to pursue their claims that the product caused injury -- in this case nicotine addiction -- because it was "defective" and that Philip Morris breached an implied warranty that its products were safe.

The court didn't raise issue with the naming of Store 24 Inc., the convenience-store chain, as a co-defendant in the case, although no retailer has ever been held liable in such a case before, according to legal experts.

"I think the effect of this is there will be cases brought against retailers all over. They will now be held accountable and I think they're going to be in big trouble," said Edward Greer, attorney for the plaintiffs, Theresa Kyte and Sean Cann. The suit says the plaintiffs began buying cigarettes at 12 and 15 years old, respectively.

Mr. Greer said the plaintiffs plan to pursue the remaining claims against Philip Morris.

He added that the charge of defect in the cigarettes is based on, among other things, their high level of nicotine.

Hadrian R. Katz, an attorney for New York-based Philip Morris, said he was "very pleased" with the ruling. He maintained that the court's decision effectively eliminated the plaintiffs' case against Philip Morris.

Several previous liability cases against tobacco companies have been defeated or overturned on appeal. This case is an important precedent because it is "putting both retailers and manufacturers on notice that it is not permissible to sell a lethal drug illegally," asserted Brad Krevor, executive director of the Group Against Smoking Pollution, or GASP, a Boston-based advocacy group.

Mr. Greer said "no one had ever sued a retailer before" in a tobacco-liability case. He said the ruling didn't exempt Store 24 from liability, so "when we go to trial, Store 24" will be a defendant.

The Massachusetts' ruling may have wider impact as courts in other states look outside their own borders for precedents set elsewhere.

Officials of Store 24, based in Waltham, Mass., couldn't be reached for comment.

In its ruling, the Massachusetts court stated that because "Philip Morris has no control over Store 24 or other retailers," among other reasons, the cigarette maker hadn't conspired with the retailer to sell its products illegally to minors, as the plaintiffs charged.

In their suit, the plaintiffs' families allege that one of their children, Theresa Kyte, hasn't been able to stop smoking Philip Morris cigarettes after six years despite many attempts to quit. The families charge that Sean Cann, now 20, also hasn't been able to kick the habit. They further allege that the minors repeatedly purchased Philip Morris's Marlboro and Parliament brands at Store 24 convenience stores in violation of a statute prohibiting sale of tobacco to minors.

Said Mr. Krevor of GASP: "There are 100 million teen-agers who become smokers everyday. Invariably, cigarettes are obtained illegally. The laws against it are not enforced." This case, he said, could "put teeth in the laws that protect teens."
