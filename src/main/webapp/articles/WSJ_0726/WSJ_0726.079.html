Dividend News: Panhandle Eastern Cuts Payout 60%, Cites Cash Woes

  • Author: Caleb Solomon
  • Date: 07/26/90

HOUSTON -- Panhandle Eastern Corp., citing current and potential cash problems, slashed its dividend 60% despite its repeated insistence that the payout would remain intact. "Today's dividend action recognizes that financial and operating improvements expected when we acquired {Texas Eastern} are developing more slowly and less dramatically than anticipated," Panhandle's new chairman, Philip Burguieres, said in a letter to shareholders.

The natural gas pipeline company's debt soared after its $3.22 billion acquisition of Texas Eastern Corp. in early 1989, with its debt-to-equity ratio moving above 70%. At the time, many critics were skeptical that Panhandle would have strong enough cash flow to maintain the dividend, with some viewing the purchase price as too high. A spokesman said the current debt ratio of about 65% is still well above the goal of 50%.

While layoffs or other cutbacks haven't been announced, the company said it would consider them. The board "directed management to examine carefully all areas in which cash can be saved, including capital expenditures and operating expenses," Mr. Burguieres wrote.

The dividend cut, to 80 cents a share annually from $2 a share, will save Panhandle about $105 million a year. Rumors of the cut, which the company has denied ever since the Texas Eastern acquisition and as recently as its annual meeting in April, had been circulating on Wall Street for several weeks. Panhandle's stock ended yesterday at $17.875, down 75 cents in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Panhandle's difficulties were reflected in the second-quarter earnings it announced yesterday. The company said its loss widened to $13.6 million, or 15 cents a share, from $5 million, or nine cents a share, a year earlier, which included an $11.1 million one-time gain. Revenue declined 6.7% to $494 million from $529.4 million.

Among the problems cited, the company said its cleanup of polychlorinated biphenyls, or PCBs, at Texas Eastern pipeline stations would exceed its earlier $400 million estimate. "We don't know how much higher" it will be, the spokesman said.

Unexpectedly low oil and natural gas prices in the first half hurt pipeline volumes, particularly in the Northeast and New England, because many customers switched to industrial fuel oil from natural gas.

"Competitive pressures may become even more severe over the short term," Mr. Burguieres said. The spokesman explained that certain Panhandle pipeline rates to the Northeast will go up in December, which could further hurt volume.

Also, a number of expansion projects have been delayed or curbed, reducing expected gas volume for the company.

Panhandle said court rulings on regulatory matters could force refunds that would strain cash holdings in the short run, although the company said ultimately it will reap positive cash in these cases. Panhandle said it may have an unexpected $100 million tax liability because of Internal Revenue Service interpretations affecting the sale of some Texas Eastern assets.
