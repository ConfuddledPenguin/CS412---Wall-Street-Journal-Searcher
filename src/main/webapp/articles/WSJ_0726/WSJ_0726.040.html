Brokerage Firms Urged to Speed Arbitration Awards to Investors

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 07/26/90

NEW YORK -- Brokerage firms soon will have to put the check in the mail faster when they lose an arbitration case brought by an investor. This is because an arbitration rule-making group, spurred by a rise in complaints from investors who say they have waited as long as 90 days to collect, is prodding the firms to accelerate payment.

Take Edward Strehlau. He won $6,000 in an arbitration case from Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. in April. Weeks rolled by with no sign of the money. Early this week, he finally received the check -- mistakenly made out to his lawyer.

"You wait and wait and wait," Mr. Strehlau, an unemployed salesman, says, "and then you end up losing all the interest."

Under new rules approved by the Securities Industry Conference on Arbitration, most major arbitration forums will soon require securities firms to pay investors within 30 days of an arbitration decision. Until now, there hadn't been any strict guidelines on how quickly firms had to pay.

Most of them typically pay within 90 days, which is their time limit to move to have decisions set aside. This period won't be changed.

Firms still could delay payment for as long as 90 days, but only if they filed a challenge within a month after the award was ordered. As a result, industry self-regulatory groups believe they will be in a better position to take enforcement action against any foot-dragging firms.

"It's really the coercive value of having regulators hopping on delinquent payments that's most effective," says Richard P. Ryder, editor of the Securities Arbitration Commentator, a Maplewood, N.J., newsletter.

Payments have been delayed in part because brokerage firms have been swamped with a heavy arbitration caseload in recent years. But they also have benefited from paying late, because they weren't required to tack on interest when mailing these checks to investors.

The newly issued directive from SICA, an ad-hoc group that proposes arbitration rules, will prod most arbitration forums to require securities firms to pay interest on awards from the date they are issued. This will give the firms added incentive to make the payments to investors quickly.

The upshot: Customers will see their money faster. And this, legal specialists say, will help build confidence in a system that is supposed to be far speedier than going to court.

Some arbitration forums already have tough payment guidelines. The Municipal Securities Rulemaking Board, which handles municipal-bond cases, last year began requiring brokerage firms to pay investors within 20 days of an award. Even if a firm disputes an award, it still must put the amount in an escrow account.

Board officials say they decided to act, after a few small firms delayed paying investors because they knew they were soon going to be out of business.

"It appears that a number of dealers don't pay arbitration awards until the very end of the appeal period, even when they have no intention of bringing an appeal," the board stated before adopting the rules.

The SICA also has approved other rules that most arbitration forums will soon adopt. Some fees beyond the award, such as forum fees, must be paid immediately following the arbitrators' decision.

And investors will be able to join in filing arbitration cases with others who have similar complaints, such as disputes arising out of similar transactions involving a securities firm or broker. To file such a joint case until now, a direct relationship has had to be shown among the claimants, such as being members of the same investment club.

The National Association of Securities Dealers and the New York Stock Exchange, which together handle more than 90% of all arbitration cases, say investors won't have to wait long to feel the effect of the new rules. The NASD says it will approve them by autumn. The Big Board hopes to approve them in the next several weeks.
