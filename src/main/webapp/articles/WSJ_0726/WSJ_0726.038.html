Equimark Fees For Texas Bank Are Challenged --- U.S. Seeks Reimbursement For Management Costs Of National Bancshares

  • Author: James S. Hirsch
  • Date: 07/26/90

PITTSBURGH -- Federal regulators told Equimark Corp. they will request reimbursement for expenses the bank holding company charged the federal government while managing an insolvent Texas bank. Equimark charged the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. more than $10 million for its nine-month management of National Bancshares Corp. of Texas.

Among the alleged expenses is a charge of about $1,400, apparently for transporting from Pittsburgh to Texas a 1962 black Rolls-Royce owned by Equimark Chairman Alan S. Fellheimer.

FDIC Chairman William Seidman said that until the agency reviews Equimark's records, he isn't sure how much Equimark will be asked to reimburse. "But it may run into the millions," he said. "There are a lot of questions about the kinds of things that were charged when then were running {National Bancshares} on our behalf."

Mr. Fellheimer vigorously denied that Equimark charged the government with any improper expenses, and he said he personally paid for transporting his Rolls-Royce. The bill was less than $1,000, he said.

"We're prepared to back with documentation every nickel we charged them," Mr. Fellheimer said.

The FDIC, however, said Equimark incurred "questionable expenses" for lodging, travel and a data processing system it installed at National Bancshares. Alan J. Whitney, an FDIC spokesman, said the agency sent Mr. Fellheimer a letter July 17 requesting documentation for expenses incurred in San Antonio, Texas.

Regulators first informed Mr. Fellheimer in April that they planned to request documentation about expenses, Mr. Whitney said.

Among other things, the inquiry seems to center on bills run up by Equimark employees who shuttled between Pittsburgh and San Antonio. Most stayed in hotels.

Mr. Fellheimer contended that he hadn't yet received the FDIC letter and said regulators make frequent requests for increased documentation.

The inquiry is the latest problem in Equimark's failed attempt to acquire National Bancshares in a $116 million federally assisted buy-out. The acquisition was to be the centerpiece of Mr. Fellheimer's driving ambition to expand the bank beyond Pennsylvania.

The attempt proved costly. The deal collapsed in April when National Bancshares' bondholders rejected the offer and regulators refused to allow Equimark to sweeten the deal. Consequently Equimark took a $3.7 million charge for employee severance packages and other items.

The FDIC request comes at a time when Mr. Fellheimer is under increasing criticism from Equimark directors. In May, the board deliberated for six hours over whether he would keep his job. While they voted to retain Mr. Fellheimer, he must stem losses from bad real-estate and consumer loans and ill-fated acquisitions in Idaho and Philadelphia.

In the second quarter Equimark had a loss of $29.8 million before one-time gains from the sale of a credit-card unit. In the past 12 months, its share price has fallen more than 60%.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Equimark shares closed at $5.125, unchanged.

Regulators aren't the only ones to raise eyebrows over the $10 million payment. At a U.S. bankruptcy court hearing in San Antonio earlier this month, National Bancshares creditors gasped when they learned the bank had paid that much money, and banking experts have said the amount seems extravagant.

National Bancshares executives have said the fees were determined in a contract between the FDIC and Equimark. While National Bancshares technically paid Equimark, in effect it was FDIC money because the Texas bank was insolvent.

While Equimark ran National Bancshares, it instituted an aggressive marketing campaign, set up a telephone loan line and threw customer parties at bank branches and country clubs.
