Brokerage Firms In Study Are Judged On Various Portfolios

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/26/90

NEW YORK -- The quarterly study of asset-allocation by The Wall Street Journal and Wilshire Associates of Santa Monica, Calif., includes the 10 brokerage houses that had the largest number of registered representatives at the time they were selected. Some firms publish more than one set of asset-allocation recommendations. Within reason, they may pick which set they want to be judged by, provided they specify in advance.

A.G. Edwards is judged on a "growth and income" portfolio, Kidder Peabody on an "individual balanced" portfolio, and PaineWebber on a "typical balanced portfolio."

Raymond James is judged on its advice to "young urban professionals," Goldman Sachs on advice for a "smaller, aggressive pension fund," and Prudential-Bache on its recommendations for a "small, aggressive fund."

The four other firms -- Dean Witter, Merrill Lynch, Shearson and Smith Barney -- publish only one set of asset-allocation advice, which can be modified to fit individual clients' needs.

Each house's advice is mirrored in a theoretical portfolio. Holdings are adjusted whenever the advice changes, and also on the last day of each month, to keep assets in the recommended ratio.

Investments are assumed to do as well as the following indexes: Standard & Poor's 500-stock index for stocks; Merrill Lynch corporate and government master bond index for bonds; 90-day Treasury bills for cash; the National Council of Real Estate Investment Fiduciaries/Frank Russell Co. index for real estate; and the average monthly price of gold for tangible assets.

The study disregards the cost of trading, mainly to make the results comparable to market averages. In practice, people who often adjust their investment blends may incur high commissions.
