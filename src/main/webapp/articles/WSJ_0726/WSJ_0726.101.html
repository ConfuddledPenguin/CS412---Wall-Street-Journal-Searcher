Brady Supports Lifting Curbs On Bank Lines

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 07/26/90

WASHINGTON -- Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady called for broadening the businesses that banks can engage in, but added that they should have to meet strict capital standards so that deregulation doesn't foster the reckless practices that created the thrift debacle. Mr. Brady, in testimony before the Senate Banking Committee, said the Treasury is worried that banks' profitability is being hurt by restrictions that prevent them from fully entering the securities and insurance business, and from opening branches throughout the U.S. Many of the banks' best customers, "including blue-chip corporations, now borrow directly from investors" through short-term debt securities, Mr. Brady said.

"The result has been concentration in the riskiest lines of permitted business, such as commercial real estate lending, highly leveraged transactions, and loans to lesser developed countries," the Treasury secretary said. He added that the limitations are creating "greater risk to the system and the taxpayer, not less."

Mr. Brady's comments were the opening salvo in a controversial attempt to expand bank powers. The Treasury is preparing proposals to restructure the banking industry and the deposit-insurance system and expects to announce them by early next year. But amid the savings-and-loan bailout, expected to cost the government up to $500 billion with interest, many members of Congress are wary of giving banks more powers.

But Mr. Brady, echoing the view of many banking experts, said lack of capital was the culprit in the S&L mess, not the deregulation of the industry in the early 1980s. Capital represents the investment of a bank's or thrift's owners that is drained off before federal deposit insurance is called in. Though he didn't specifically call for higher capital requirements, Mr. Brady suggested that broader powers should be given only to well-capitalized institutions.

"I can't imagine recommending any changes in the banking law that wouldn't put a very high premium on capital," he said.

Mr. Brady was less specific about the Treasury's proposals to change deposit insurance, but appeared to be leaning toward recommending that the $100,000 insurance limit on deposits remain in place. "We've got to take a very hard look at that problem," he said. "But frankly, I can't see at this particular point in time a rush to lower that amount of insurance when we have a question of confidence not only in our banks, but obviously in our thrifts."

Asked by Sen. Alan Dixon, (D., Ill.) about limiting deposit insurance to one account per person, a very popular idea on Capitol Hill these days, Mr. Brady said, "Well, if you were going to start anyplace, that would be a place to start."

Commenting on recent statements by William Seidman, head of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., that the FDIC's bank-insurance fund is "under stress," Mr. Brady emphasized "the qualification" Mr. Seidman made: that it would take a severe recession before taxpayer dollars would be required to back up the bank fund. "We can't make all our economic judgments" as if a big recession is in the offing, the Treasury secretary said.

"The point is to stay out of a severe recession, which I think we will do," he added. Mr. Brady also rejected suggestions by some private analysts that the banking industry is currently harboring problems that could grow to the size of the S&L crisis in a few years. "To compare the banking system to the S&Ls in 1988 is a comparison I don't think should be made," he said.
