National Intergroup Seems to Have Lost Three Board Seats to Dissident Holder

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 07/26/90

PITTSBURGH -- National Intergroup Inc. apparently lost the chairman's board seat and two others to dissident shareholder Centaur Partners Group, which claimed it won a mandate for its plan to liquidate the company. But Centaur would have only a minority voice on the 10-person board, and must convince directors, particularly independent members, to go along. Although the final vote won't be known until Aug. 9, Centaur is believed to have received at least two-thirds of the votes.

Centaur, which owns 16.5% of National Intergroup, said it wouldn't rush into a fire sale of FoxMeyer Drug, the wholesale-drug distribution operation and essentially National Intergroup's only ongoing business. Still, "we think it's in the best interest to pursue a potential sale of FoxMeyer in a responsible way," said Melvyn J. Estrin, a principal of Centaur, who was elected to the board. "We think the shareholder vote is a mandate."

Management has argued that an immediate sale wouldn't capture the potential value for FoxMeyer, which has lagged its competitors but is improving. Indeed, FoxMeyer's sales rose 23% and operating earnings surged 40% in the first quarter ended June 30.

Still, Mr. Estrin estimated that FoxMeyer, with its 11 distribution centers, could fetch between $475 million and $525 million, or at least $21 a share.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, National Intergroup common stock closed unchanged at $18 a share.

Centaur's apparent win likely marks the end of an embattled tenure for Howard M. Love, the company's chairman and chief executive officer, who helped steer the company from steel and aluminum into retail, oil, financial services and ultimately wholesale-drug distribution. Up until now, Mr. Love has managed to fend off dissatisfied shareholders, frustrated with seven consecutive years of operating losses; he even won an earlier proxy fight in the mid-1980s.

Still, he conceded the grand plan of becoming a drug wholesaler has taken "far longer than I had anticipated." Although disappointed in the vote, he said he hopes the company continues to operate FoxMeyer "without a lot of bickering on the board."

He wouldn't say whether he would step down as chief executive before his planned retirement in March. However, he will likely have to relinquish his title as chairman.

Yesterday's shareholder meeting culminates a proxy fight that included several lawsuits and seven months of litigation challenging the company's anti-takeover "poison pill" plan and bylaws regarding board size. In that time, the company announced a major restructuring that called for the sale of its Ben Franklin general merchandise franchise chain and Permian Partners L.P. oil operation, with proceeds going towards a special dividend and share buy-back program. The plan, which the company had hoped would console impatient investors, also called for Mr. Love's retirement next spring.

But the plan failed to impress shareholders, who noted that they haven't been paid a dividend since the fourth quarter of 1988. Meanwhile Mr. Love received a $300,000 bonus in 1990 and options to buy 325,000 shares at $16.80 each.

"You ask for support against Centaur. Do you deserve it?" asked Eugene Cohen at the meeting; Mr. Cohen owns more stock than any director besides Mr. Love. National Intergroup directors own less than 1% of the company's stock outstanding.

The two other Centaur-nominated directors are Robert J. Slater, former vice chairman of Crane Co. and William G. Tull, former president of American Security Bank.

Separately, National Intergroup said that as a result of the closing of its Pittsburgh headquarters and the sale of its Ben Franklin and Permian units, it would "incur significant losses which will negatively impact first-quarter results." The company declined to say whether it would post a net loss. In last year's first quarter ended June 30, National Intergroup earned $13 million, or 53 cents a share, on revenue of $853.5 million.
