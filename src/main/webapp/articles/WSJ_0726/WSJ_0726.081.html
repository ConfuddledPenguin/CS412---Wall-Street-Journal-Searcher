Alcan Aluminum's Striking Employees Reject Labor Pact

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/26/90

KITIMAT, British Columbia -- Striking workers at Alcan Aluminium Ltd.'s smelter here soundly rejected a tentative three-year contract reached Tuesday, setting the stage for a possible shutdown of the plant. "We're a little surprised and a little disappointed" by the workers' rejection of the pact, said Alcan spokesman Allan Hewitson. The proposed accord, which was rejected by 57.4% of the workers, called for annual wage increases of 6% in each year of the pact, plus other benefits, including an immediate signing bonus of 1,500 Canadian dollars (US$1,297) per worker.

Management personnel had managed to keep the smelter operating since midnight Monday, when about 1,600 members of the Canadian Association of Smelter and Allied Workers union walked off their jobs as the previous two-year contract expired. Most of the workers are employed at the smelter, though a few operate Alcan's neighboring hyrdo-electric power generating plant.

Mr. Hewitson said Tuesday that management workers could keep the 260,000-metric-ton per year aluminum smelter running for about 48 hours, pending ratification of the proposed contract. He wouldn't immediately confirm yesterday whether the smelter would be shut down, in view of the vote results.

"We're reviewing the options that we have and we'll be making a fuller statement later," he said.

It wasn't immediately clear why the workers rejected the pact, though some workers said before the vote they viewed the proposed 6% annual wage increases as inadequate, once inflation is taken into account. The pact provided for cost-of-living-allowance payments in the contract's third year, but no such payments in years one and two.

It wasn't apparent late yesterday afternoon whether any new talks were planned between the sides.

Montreal-based Alcan, North America's biggest producer of primary aluminum, with large operations in Quebec, depends on the Kitimat smelter for about 18% of its aluminum ingot production, a spokesman said. The smelter exports about 85% of its output to the Far East, with the balance destined for the U.S. West Coast, Mr. Hewitson said.

A shutdown of the smelter would likely spark an increase in aluminum prices, which have fluctuated in recent days in response to developments in the contract talks.

In London Metal Exchange trading yesterday, aluminum closed at about 72 U.S. cents per pound on a spot basis, up from about 71 cents Tuesday. The three-month aluminum contract price closed slightly above 73 cents a pound, up half a cent from Tuesday.
