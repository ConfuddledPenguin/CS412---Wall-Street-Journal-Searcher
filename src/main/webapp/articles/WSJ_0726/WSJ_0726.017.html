SPANISH CREDIT LINE FOR ART

Spain's Banco Espanol de Credito said it had set up a special line of credit to finance the purchase of paintings, sculptures and antiques.

The credit line is the first of its kind in Spain and reflects a growing interest in art as an investment, said Fernando Falero, a Banesto manager. He said the credit line was open-ended, but added that clients would be charged interest of around 16%. Average bank-loan rates in Spain stand at around 15%.

"There's no limit to how much we'll lend, within reason," Mr. Falero said. "If a client wants to purchase a Goya, we're certainly willing to help fund the acquisition."

Mr. Falero said the fund would underwrite purchases of both old masters' art and works created by new artists. "We're primarily interested in paintings and sculpture," he said.

JAPAN: WORLD'S LEADING INVESTOR

Japan has overtaken the U.S. to become the world's biggest spender on new factories and industrial equipment, according to a new study by the Washington-based Council of Competitiveness, a group of 150 academics, union officials and corporate executives. Despite an economy less than half the size of the U.S., Japan spent $36 billion more on capital investment in 1989 than the U.S.: $549 billion vs. $513 billion. "This marks the first time that any country has out-invested the United States in plant and equipment in absolute terms since World War II," the study said.

NEW ZEALAND IMMIGRANT FEE

New Zealand plans a levy on immigrants to pay for a fund to help them settle into the country, Immigration Minister Annette King said. The money, to be charged when an immigrant's application for permament residence is accepted, would pay for help including English language classes. The fee has yet to be set but will be imposed soon. Refugees and people entering on humanitarian grounds or to reunite with relatives will be exempt.

PHILIPPINES WANTS DEBT RELIEF

The Philippines will seek debt relief from foreign creditors to help recover from last week's earthquake, President Corazon Aquino's spokesman said. The announcement came amid growing pressure from Congress to suspend payments of the $26 billion foreign debt to free funds for reconstruction. The preliminary estimate of the damage totaled 15.97 billion pesos ($697.4 million). "There are various forms (of debt relief) that may be set . . . ranging from the suspension of debt service to outright condonation," the spokesman said.

NEW SOVIET CURRENCY RULES

Soviet citizens will be allowed to spend foreign currency at home beginning Aug. 1 without telling the government where they got the money, the news agency Tass reported. Deputy Prime Minister Stepan Sitarian said individual Soviets had $324 million in foreign currency that they had legally acquired but were unable to spend in the Soviet Union, except at special shops for foreigners. Tass said under the new rules, a network of stores and trade houses would be set up, and Soviets would be able to buy "imported and high-quality domestic goods."

BRITISH ROYALTY'S INCOME

Britain's royal family has been put on a fixed income until the year 2000. Queen Elizabeth II will receive 7.9 million ($14.34 million) a year when the new system starts next Jan. 1. Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher told Parliament the fixed salary would replace the annual review in the spring budget. Among other royals receiving money from the state, the queen's husband, Prince Philip, will get 360,000 a year, and the queen's sister, Princess Margaret, will get 220,000. The Prince of Wales, heir to the throne, doesn't figure on the list because he draws his income from his personal estate, the Duchy of Cornwall.

EC'S WORKING-HOUR PROPOSAL

Working-hour rules proposed yesterday by the European Community Commission could drive up costs in many businesses, industry officials said.

Because of the restrictions proposed on night-time work, the proposals would hit small enterprises particularly hard, critics said. For example, French and Italian consumers might have to pay more for freshly baked bread, or British households might have to pay a higher price for the milk they have delivered at their doorstep every morning.

Among other things, the proposed working-hour rules would ensure that workers get a minimum of 11 hours of continuous rest per 24 hours.

Both the EC employers' federation Unice and the British government reiterated their opposition to labor legislation at the EC level. "It is not necessary and it really doesn't help" bargaining between labor and industry, a Unice official said.
