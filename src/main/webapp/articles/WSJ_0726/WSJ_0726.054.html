Work on Iroquois Gas Line to Northeast Delayed by FERC Plan to Hold Hearing

  • Author: Suzanne McGee and Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 07/26/90

A surprise decision by the U.S. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission will delay construction of the Iroquois Gas Transmission System's pipeline from Canada to the U.S. Northeast and give the natural gas project's opponents another chance to state their case. Iroquois officials said gas probably won't start flowing to the energy-hungry Northeast by the scheduled start-up date of November 1991, but they said the delay likely wouldn't be significant.

FERC commissioners, who had been expected to issue a certificate allowing construction on the 369-mile, $582.6 million project to begin, instead stunned the project's supporters by ordering a hearing for "public policy" reasons. The hearing is intended to review the need for the gas and the project's proposed rate structure.

The order calling for the hearing means that the fate of the project won't be decided until late November at the earliest, commissioners said. The project calls for shipping about 575.9 million cubic feet of gas daily to the Northeast.

Edward Sondey, vice president of gas supply, Brooklyn Union Gas Co., said he was "disappointed and shocked" by the decision to call a hearing rather than issue a certificate for the pipeline, adding that the issues surrounding Iroquois have been "hashed and rehashed" in many different forums in the last three years.

"We had counted on beginning service in 1991," Mr. Sondey said. The New York-based utility, which has an 11.1% stake in the Iroquois project, is one of the largest customers for the Canadian natural gas.

Robert Reid, president of the Iroquois Gas Transmission System consortium, said that the order will delay construction of the pipeline, which had been scheduled to begin in early 1991. Iroquois officials said they haven't yet determined whether it will affect the pipeline's proposed start-up date.

A group of U.S. natural gas producers welcomed the FERC decision, saying that "approval of the project would not have been in the public interest." The Independent Petroleum Association of America, along with other Iroquois opponents, argues that the need for the project hasn't been demonstrated and that the proposed rate structure discriminates against U.S. domestic producers.

Brooklyn Union's Mr. Sondey said that he met with IPAA members on four occasions to negotiate a gas-supply contract before the utility decided to buy Canadian gas. "They weren't willing to meet our conditions," he said. "The Canadians were. If (domestic producers) aren't willing to compete, they should just pack their bags and go home."

FERC Commissioner Elizabeth Moler, who voted against holding the hearing, argued not only that there is a demonstrated need for the project, but that the FERC is treating Iroquois differently from the way it approaches comparable U.S. projects.

While its sponsors remain optimistic that Iroquois eventually will get FERC approval, other observers, noting the highly charged atmosphere surrounding the project, are less bullish.

"Now we have a new procedure, it creates the potential for a different outcome than we all originally expected to see: approval of Iroquois," said Roger Berliner, a Washington natural-gas market analyst.

A $2.2 billion expansion of Trans-Canada PipeLines Ltd.'s main line, needed to transport the gas to the U.S. border, is also bogged down by separate regulatory issues in Ottawa. Canada's National Energy Board is expected to rule on the Trans-Canada project by year end.
