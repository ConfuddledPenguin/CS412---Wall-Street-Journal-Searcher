Abreast of the Market: Industrials Advance 8.42 Points But Oil Stocks Show Weakness

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 07/26/90

NEW YORK -- Stocks rebounded as investors started to scoop up beaten-down shares, switching into previously neglected industry groups. The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 8.42 to 2930.94. New York Stock Exchange volume of 163,530,300 shares was the lightest in four days. Advancing issues on the Big Board outpaced decliners, 895 to 583.

Oil stocks tanked. Mary Quinn, oil stock analyst at S.G. Warburg, said investors were taking profits on the group, especially with meetings of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries under way. Chevron fell 1 to 77 3/4, and Exxon dropped 3/8 to 49 5/8. Ms. Quinn issued a sell recommendation on Mobil, which fell 1 to 66. Mobil "is a little expensive relative to the group and its prospects," she said. Ms. Quinn restated a buy recommendation on Atlantic Richfield because she thinks the value of certain assets haven't been appreciated. Atlantic gained 1/4 to 125 3/4.

Despite the sell-off in major energy issues, a 1 1/2-point rise in Alcoa to 69 1/8 and a 1 3/8-point gain in Boeing to 58 1/4 helped lift the industrial average to a second consecutive gain.

Dozens of other metals stocks climbed yesterday, and some investors seem to be making a bet that inflation will surge again, said Robert Devine, head of equity trading at UBS Securities Inc. Phelps Dodge gained 1 3/4 to 69 1/8, for example.

Stocks coming back to life yesterday after previous earnings-related routs included Capital Cities/ABC, which gained 12 1/8 to 545 1/8; Storage Technology bounced 1 1/8 to 27 1/8.

However, the earnings surprise blood-letting started again in other shares. L.A. Gear tumbled 2 1/8 to 23 on fears of weaker-than-expected earnings.

Sellers circled around Digital Equipment yesterday morning after the company reported a fourth-quarter loss of $2.11 a share, which included a $400 million charge related to layoffs and other cost-reduction measures.

The Big Board flashed a pre-opening indication that the stock might open 7 1/2 points lower than Tuesday's close. But buyers flocked to the stock and Digital opened 1/8 higher at 75 5/8 at 9:48 a.m. EDT and traded as high as 78. During the order imbalance, news services reported that Digital planned to cut as many as 6,000 jobs, which also helped draw in buyers, traders said. However, sellers had the upper hand at the close, and pushed the stock down 5/8 to 74 7/8.

Other technology issues regained favor: International Business Machines was unchanged at 114 3/4; Compaq Computer advanced 2 to 59 3/8; Hewlett-Packard added 7/8 to 45 1/8; NCR rose 1/2 to 69 and Motorola gained 2 1/8 to 79 3/4.

Cray Research fell 2 to 45 in the wake of its second-quarter earnings report. While earnings and revenue for the quarter were sharply higher than a year earlier, the company said it expects "flat" revenue for the full year.

Meanwhile, latest results from Investors Intelligence showed that 49.6% of participants in their "Sentiment Index" survey were bullish -- matching the highest reading of the year. The survey said 34.1% were bearish and 16.3% expected a correction in the stock market. Many of the forecasts were made before Monday's market drop.

Du Pont rose 1/2 to 39 3/8 after issuing its report for the quarter, which was stronger than generally expected despite a 2.8% decline in net income. Other chemical stocks to attract buyers included Union Carbide, which rose 3/4 to 20 as two million shares changed hands, and Monsanto, which added 3/4 to 51.

On the other hand, investors continued to dump shares of Dow Chemical, which lost 3/8 to 52 on 1.8 million shares. The stock plummeted 5 3/4 Tuesday after the company reported that second-quarter net fell 50% and that earnings may be softer in the second half, compared with the first.

Gerber Scientific plunged 2 7/8 to 10 3/8 after saying it expects earnings for the six months ending in October to be significantly lower than in the year-ago period, when it posted net of 66 cents a share.

Network Equipment Technologies, which posted a loss for the fiscal quarter ended last month and announced changes in its management, dropped 3/8 to 7 3/4.

Upjohn rose 2 1/8 to 42 7/8 on 1.6 million shares amid a revival of takeover speculation. The list of rumored suitors includes American Home Products, Schering-Plough and Britain's Glaxo Holdings. Also, West Germany's Hoechst was cited in the latest bout of speculation; a spokesman for its U.S. unit, Hoechst Celanese, declined to comment, as did Upjohn.

General Instrument declined 7/8 to 44 3/4 on 1.2 million shares after a potential rival bidder to Forstmann, Little & Co., which has agreed to acquire the company in a leveraged buy-out at $44.50 a share, disclosed it wasn't interested in a takeover. Shortly before the close, trading was halted as the company said neither that party nor another entity to which it had provided confidential information would mount a bid.

Dividend-related trading boosted volume in Chase Manhattan, which gained 5/8 to 20 3/8 and topped the list of most active Big Board issues on 4.1 million shares, and Citicorp, which rose 3/8 to 21 on 3.6 million shares. Both bank holding company issues both carry hefty dividend yields and go exdividend this week.

Two new mutual funds investing overseas, Singapore Fund and Latin American Investment Fund, closed below the price of their initial public offerings in active trading. Singapore Fund fell 1/4 from its offering price to 11 3/4 on 1.3 million shares, or more than one-quarter of the five million shares sold in the offering. Latin American closed at 14 3/4 on 1.4 million shares; the initial offering of four million shares was made at $15 each.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 1.44 to 356.95. Volume totaled 11,200,000 shares.
