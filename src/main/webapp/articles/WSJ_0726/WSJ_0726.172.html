Rohm & Haas Study Shows Tie From DDT To Pancreatic Cancer

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/26/90

PHILADELPHIA -- Rohm & Haas Co. disclosed that a study at its chemicals plant here showed a statistical correlation between pancreas cancer in workers and exposure to the pesticide DDT. The chemicals company emphasized, however, that the finding was preliminary and didn't by itself establish a cause-and-effect relationship between the pesticide and the cancer.

The study is part of a years-long effort by Rohm & Haas to track the health of some 6,000 workers at the plant. In 1987, the company noted the incidence of pancreatic cancer at the plant was somewhat higher than what would be expected in the general population. At the time, however, the company couldn't link the observation with any products or production processes.

"There are no previous studies that indicate DDT is associated with pancreas cancer in humans," Phillip Lewis, the company's medical director said in a statement yesterday.

The study looked at the 28 cases of pancreas cancer among workers at the plant between 1948 and 1971, all of whom have since died. Eleven of those workers had handled DDT and related products.

"We see clear association between pancreas cancer and DDT exposure," David Garabrant, director of occupational medicine at the University of Michigan, said in an interview.

Dr. Garabrant, who conducted the study, said there is less than a 3% possibility that the statistical significance of the association was due entirely to chance, even after controlling for such factors as which of the workers were smokers.

Rohm & Haas stopped making DDT-related products years ago, and DDT is no longer made anywhere in the U.S., according to a spokesman at the Environmental Protection Agency.
