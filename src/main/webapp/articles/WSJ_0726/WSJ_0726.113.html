SEC Votes to Limit Money Managers' Soft-Dollar Deals

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen and James A. White
  • Date: 07/26/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission, in a move likely to change the way pension funds pay some Wall Street bond commissions, voted to restrict some trading arrangements used by money managers to buy securities. The SEC ruling could alter the business climate on Wall Street by giving advantages to old-line firms. But many firms think the ruling is a narrow decision that won't have much effect on bond trading.

The agency's interpretation focuses on so-called soft-dollar deals, in which pension funds and others receive credits from brokerage firms in exchange for trading commissions. Much like frequent-flier miles, the credits can be used to pay for research, computer services and client referrals.

The SEC said pension-fund trades done through principals, or firms that trade from their own positions, or trades using futures aren't covered by a securities-law exemption that allows broad use of soft dollars.

If a principal transaction uses soft dollars, the fund manager could be in violation of Employee Retirement Income Security Act laws administered by the Labor Department, which had sought the SEC interpretation.

Still, the SEC left unanswered the broader questions of soft-dollar use, which has been criticized by many Wall Street firms as an area rife with abuses. The SEC said it will try to tackle those issues later in the year.

In voting for the restriction, SEC Chairman Richard Breeden stressed that the decision was a narrow clarification of rules dealing with specific trades. He contended that it wouldn't have a broad-based effect on soft-dollar business.

Frances Bermanzohn, general counsel at the Public Securities Association and a staunch critic of soft dollars, applauded the ruling as "promoting fiduciary duties to protect investors and enhance market liquidity."

The Labor Department called the SEC move "a wonderful result for pensioners and retirees." The agency had grown concerned about the growing use of soft dollars under the exemption, said David Ball, assistant secretary for the department's Pension and Welfare Benefits Administration. Some of the costs that should be absorbed by the pension manager are being passed along to the plan beneficiaries, he added.

But several brokerage firms and lawyers objected to the SEC move. New York-based Lynch, Jones & Ryan, a soft-dollar firm, called the SEC action a "naive" response in favor of soft-dollar critics. The critics "are trying to leave it to the regulators to limit their competition," said Stan Chamberlin, the firm's chairman.

Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. wrote to the SEC urging that principal transactions, trading in Nasdaq stocks and the use of debt-securities trading to pay for debt-securities research be allowed to continue with protection. Removing the protection "will significantly impact the flexibility of the investment manager in obtaining the information necessary to make informed investment decisions," senior Shearson officials wrote.

Moreover, Lee Pickard, a Washington lawyer who represents soft-dollar clients, argued that the SEC ruling is "likely to benefit the traditional investment banking firms and may well reduce competition in the dealer area." Several large investment banking firms don't engage in soft-dollar transactions. He added, "It seems to me that the actions of the commission are likely to have a broader impact than is assumed. It may well shift business from one segment of the securities industry to another segment."
