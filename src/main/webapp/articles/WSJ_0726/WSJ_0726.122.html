MNC Is Said to Tentatively Line Up Capital Infusion of at Least $200 Million

  • Author: Robert Guenther and Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 07/26/90

Battered by the slumping commercial real estate market, MNC Financial Corp., a big Baltimore-based regional banking company, has tentatively lined up a capital infusion of at least $200 million from an investor group, according to people familiar with the bank. Meanwhile, J.P. Morgan & Co., the big New York bank, is attempting to assemble a $1 billion line of credit for MNC, the people said.

One of the nation's most aggressive real estate lenders, MNC posted a second-quarter loss of $74.7 million Tuesday as problem loans more than doubled. The banking company said then that it is considering the sale of some non-banking units to reduce its need for short-term debt.

With $28 billion of assets, MNC is the largest banking company in the Baltimore-Washington, D.C., area, the latest region to fall victim to the real estate slump. MNC is the parent of Maryland National Bank, American Security Bank and Equitable Bank.

MNC is under pressure to reduce its borrowings following the recent downgrading of its debt to non-investment grade, or junk, status by Moody's Investors Service Inc.

According to an individual close to the bank, MNC's board has agreed, subject to final terms, to accept a capital infusion of at least $200 million from an investor group led by Alfred Lerner.

The Cleveland investor, who was chairman of Equitable Bancorp. before its merger with MNC early this year, owns a 8.9% stake in MNC and is a director of the banking company.

MNC's directors have approved the proposed capital infusion by Mr. Lerner's group, the individual said.

Faced with the ratings downgrade, MNC, which relies on the capital markets for funds to a greater degree than most regional banks, has quit selling its commercial paper to raise money.

As recently as March 31, MNC had $2 billion of its commercial paper, or short-term IOUs, outstanding. But that had shrunk to about $126 million as of June 30, a bank spokesman said. An additional $823 million in other MNC debt comes due over the next year, he said.

The MNC spokesman declined to say whether the bank has arranged alternative credit lines. However, he said MNC hasn't had to resort to borrowing from the Federal Reserve System's discount window.

People familiar with the company said the current cash shortage exists at the parent holding company. They said MNC's banking units have been building their cash levels in recent months and don't presently face a cash problem.

Top federal banking regulators declined to comment.

According to Thomson BankWatch, a ratings agency, MNC has the second-largest exposure to commercial real estate of any major bank -- 30.9% of all loans -- and posted the second-highest growth rate in its commercial real estate portfolio last year, 46.5%.

Bank analysts say a rapid rise in real estate loans, which are very profitable in good times but are subject to cyclical downturns, often is a warning light of possible future problems.

With MNC's January merger with Equitable Bancorp., the banking company's real estate portfolio totals $5.8 billion. Its nonperforming assets stand at $757 million, or 4.7% of its $16.4 billion in loans.

In comments to investors earlier this month, Standard & Poor's Corp. said MNC's real estate growth "was not accompanied by sufficiently strong credit standards. The combination of aggressive growth and weak underwriting magnifies the company's asset quality exposure to continued adjustments in the real estate markets."

The current quarter could well be a crossroads for MNC. The federal Comptroller of the Currency is conducting a targeted exam of MNC's loans, and analysts said a 15% or more increase in nonperforming loans could imperil its $1.16-a-share annual dividend.

Frank Barkocy, an analyst with Merrill Lynch & Co., however, said he expects only modest increases in nonperforming assets, if any, in the third quarter. "The third quarter could reflect a return to profitability, albeit modest," he said.

One reason MNC is in better shape to ride out its woes than many banks is that it has a cash cow in the form of its Delaware credit card bank.
