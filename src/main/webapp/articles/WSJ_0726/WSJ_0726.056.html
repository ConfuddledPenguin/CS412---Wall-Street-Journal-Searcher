Digital Reports First Net Loss In Its History --- Computer Maker's Deficit Tied to Severence Costs; 4th-Period Revenue Fell

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 07/26/90

Digital Equipment Corp. reported the first net loss in its 32-year history after a $400 million charge for severance and other costs in its fourth quarter, ended June 30. The No. 2 computer maker also reported a 3.7% revenue decline as business in previously robust European markets slowed unexpectedly. Digital, like many other established computer makers, has suffered in the past year as customers held down spending and switched some of their purchases to personal computers.

Although Maynard, Mass.-based Digital's results were worse than most analysts had forecast, Digital's stock dropped only slightly. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, Digital was down 62.5 cents at $74.875 on volume of more than 1.1 million shares.

Analysts said investors approved of Digital's plan to cut an additional 5,000 to 6,000 jobs and that they look for an upturn in future quarters.

Looking to fiscal 1991, "I think we'll see steadily improving results, with the biggest improvement in the second half," said Marc Schulman, an analyst with UBS Securities.

John Smith, Digital's senior vice president, said, "We're fairly optimistic about the future. It definitely will come back. The question is how fast."

Digital reported a net loss for the quarter of $256.7 million, or $2.11 a share, after taking the $400 million pretax charge. Without the charge, Digital said, net income would have been $85.3 million, or 68 cents a share. Many analysts had predicted earnings of about $1 a share before any restructuring charge. A year earlier, Digital had net of $313.2 million, or $2.51 a share.

Digital's results reflected an unexpectedly large revenue decline to $3.37 billion from $3.49 billion. Sales of computers and other products dropped 8.4% to $2.06 billion, while service revenue rose narrowly.

The $400 million charge will pay for retraining and relocating workers for new jobs and providing severance pay for 5,000 to 6,000 who choose a severance option. Digital announced a $150 million charge in April for voluntary severance programs, and the programs have resulted in the departure of about 3,000 workers, Digital said. Currently Digital has about 124,000 workers. Mr. Smith said the firm plans to reduce the work force to about 119,000.

Mr. Smith said the new severance programs are likely to be less generous than the past one, which provided as much as two years' pay for senior employees as well as health and stock benefits. However, he said, the company will maintain its practice of avoiding layoffs. He said, "We don't contemplate any further restructuring costs."

Mr. Smith warned that the staff reduction won't have its full cost-cutting impact until the end of fiscal 1991.

For fiscal 1990, Digital's net fell 93% to $74.4 million, or 59 cents a share, after $550 million in restructuring charges. In fiscal 1989, Digital had net of $1.07 billion, or $8.45 a share. Revenue rose 1.6% to $12.94 billion from $12.74 billion.

Looking at the weak sales, stock analysts promptly cut their earnings estimates for fiscal 1991. Jay P. Stevens of Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., who recommends Digital stock, cut his estimate to $7.25 a share from $8. Steven Milunovich of First Boston Corp., who doesn't recommend the stock, cut his estimate to $5.50 a share from $6.80.

Analysts were especially worried by Digital's statement that sales in continental Europe were weaker than expected in the fourth quarter. While its U.K. business had been depressed by high interest rates, other European countries were a bright spot for Digital and most other computer makers until now. Digital got 56% of its revenue outside the U.S. in fiscal 1990.

Bulls on Digital say the company is entering a product cycle that will boost earnings. The company said production problems with the VAX 9000, a mainframe that was sold to a few customers in June, have been solved. Mr. Smith said Digital expects to ship 100 of the new machines this quarter and more in future quarters. The early models have an average selling price of about $1.2 million, although when more expensive models are on sale by the end of December, Digital has said, the average selling price will be about $1.6 million.

Digital could use the boost. Its revenue from high-end products has dwindled as customers waited for the new machines.

Digital said its VAX 4000 series, announced this month, is selling strongly. Mr. Smith said shipments of engineering workstations "are doing better than we anticipated" and that midrange systems "seem to be hanging in there."

Separately, Moody's Investors Service Inc. downgraded Digital's long-term debt rating to double-A-2 from triple-A. The downgrade, which covers about $100 million of 12 5/8% senior long-term notes due in 1994, reflects a Moody's opinion that "Digital's earnings will be under stress over the intermediate term as it reorganizes its operations in line with slowed revenue growth, thinner margins and a change in the product mix."

Moody's said Digital's balance sheet "remains exceptionally strong." Digital has $2 billion in cash, and its current assets are more than twice current liabilities. A Digital spokesman said the Moody's move "doesn't surprise us," as the rating concern "had held conversations with us."
