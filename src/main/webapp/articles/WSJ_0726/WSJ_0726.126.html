Politics & Policy: Senate Denounces Sen. Durenberger For His Conduct

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 07/26/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate denounced Sen. David Durenberger for "reprehensible" and "unequivocally unethical" conduct. As the Minnesota Republican sat with his arms crossed in his usual seat in the front row of the chamber, the Senate upheld the unanimous findings of its six-member, bipartisan Ethics Committee. Ninety-six senators rose from their seats to support the committee. Sen. Durenberger, and his Minnesota GOP colleague, Sen. Rudy Boschwitz, voted present. Two senators were absent for the vote.

The committee concluded that Sen. Durenberger "knowingly and willingly" violated Senate limits on speaking fees and gift limits by funneling them through his publisher, Piranha Press; misled the Senate about his ownership of a Minneapolis condominium in order to claim $65 a day in reimbursement when he stayed there; and improperly allowed businesses and organizations to provide him limousine services while he was on personal business.

"I wish there were ambiguity here, but there is none," Sen. Howell Heflin (D., Ala.), chairman of the Senate Ethics Committee, said. "I wish I could find innocence or, at most, inadvertence or inattention or simple ignorance . . . but I cannot."

Sen. Durenberger didn't contest the charges. He told his colleagues, "It has been a costly education. I have reordered my personal and professional priorities to be, first, a better man and, then, a better senator."

The Senate directed Sen. Durenberger to repay the Senate $29,050 plus interest and to donate to charity $93,730, minus taxes he has already paid, in excess honorariums. It also referred the matter to the Senate Republican caucus, which could strip the senator of his seniority; Senate Minority Leader Robert Dole (R., Kan.) has said he doesn't expect the caucus to take any action.

Seven members of the Senate have been censured since the first recorded case in 1811. One, Democratic Sen. Herman E. Talmadge of Georgia, was denounced in 1979. Sen. Talmadge lost his bid for re-election in 1980. Sen. Durenberger, who has served in the Senate for 12 years, doesn't face re-election until 1994.

The last major ethics case in the Senate involved New Jersey Democrat Harrison A. Williams Jr., who resigned in 1982 as his colleagues were about to oust him for submitting false expense vouchers and failing to report campaign contributions.
