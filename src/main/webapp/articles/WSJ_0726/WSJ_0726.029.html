World Markets: Tokyo Stocks on First Section End About Flat As Buying Incentives Dry Up in the Afternoon

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/26/90

Tokyo stocks on the first section ended essentially flat, with most early incentives drying up in the afternoon. London shares finished marginally higher as investors were circumspect about the outlook for U.K. corporate earnings, Wall Street, interest rates and the timetable for sterling to enter the exchange-rate mechanism of the European Monetary System.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues edged down 1.19 points to 31701.27. In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index rose 86.84 points to 31788.11.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 350 million shares, down slightly from 355.5 million shares Tuesday. Advancers outpaced decliners 541 to 388, while 194 issues were unchanged.

As a result, the broader-based Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section reflected a somewhat firmer tone than the benchmark indicator. The Topix rose 2.72 points, or 0.12%, to 2307.31.

Investor interest was clearly on the second section, where buying on high-priced shares of value rebounded from recent softness.

The second section index gained 51.53 points, or 1.19%, to 4380.76. The section's volume was estimated at 21.2 million shares, up from 15.6 million shares.

"The second section had a fantastic day," one U.K. broker said. "The high-priced second section shares are where investors are seeing value short term," he added.

As investors have been increasingly selective about companies, he noted, action will likely continue to be drawn to the second section.

Trading on the first section in the morning was active, though movement on the indexes was limited.

The broad market firmed more than 100 points at the opening as investors welcomed the New York market's recovery overnight, which was driven mostly by buying on oil stocks.

Linked to increased political tension in the Persian Gulf and anxiety amid this week's meeting of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, higher oil prices have also encouraged buying of Tokyo oil shares, traders said.

By the midmorning, overall stock prices were softer, as investors who weren't able to unload positions over the previous three days of losses on the Nikkei Index sold down some high-priced shares, erasing early gains.

Also midmorning, the yen began to weaken further against the dollar. But although the currency's softening often ignites some buying on export-oriented shares, too much uncertainty about the direction of interest rates left investors reluctant to purchase, traders said.

Participants yielded to wait-and-see sentiments by afternoon, during which the Nikkei Index drifted with little direction.

In the afternoon session, some professional buying gave support to speculative and high-technology shares, though traders said the atmosphere remained timid.

Traders also said that volume will likely be slow today, ahead of the first day of trading for accounts for August settlement. General selling is expected to be thin during the transition period, although the Big Four Japanese brokerage firms are expected to be active, one Japanese trader said.

Sony rose 210 yen to 8930 yen ($1.41 to $60.07), TDK gained 120 yen to 7290 yen, Fanuc advanced 180 yen to 7390 yen, and Kyocera added 180 yen to 9040 yen.

Also higher were CSK, up 180 yen to 6290 yen, and Hirose Electric, up 180 yen to 7310 yen.

Speculators' darling Jujo Paper rose 24 yen to 945 yen, while Sanrio gained 120 yen to 8450 yen.

But a broad range of shares were lower, including steel, real estate and some financial issues.

Kawasaki Steel fell 20 yen to 525 yen, Kobe Steel fell 18 yen to 520 yen, and NKK dropped 13 yen to 542 yen.

London's day began on a positive note, with the broad-based index charging to intraday peaks. But the advance couldn't be sustained, analysts said, on the perception that more corporate downgradings are likely to be in the pipeline.

Moreover, dealers said investors were agitated by sterling's slump, which came after Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major failed Tuesday to mention plans for the pound's entry into the exchange-rate mechanism in an economic debate in the House of Commons.

"The market is realizing that {ERM membership} will take longer than anticipated," one market maker said. "Some of the euphoria is evaporating."

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index was up 3.8 points at 2364.7. The Financial Times 30 Share Index fell 1 point to 1865.7. Volume was 427.5 million issues compared with 385.7 million shares Tuesday.

Reuters continued to attract attention after collapsing 184 pence ($3.34) Tuesday. Market watchers said they were awaiting news from an analysts' meeting in the U.S., where 47.9% of the shares are held. "The jury's still out on Reuters for the short term," one trader said. The stock gained 18 pence to #10.63 on volume of 5.8 million shares.

Elsewhere, oil stocks "held the market together," one trader said, as the sector gained ahead of the start of the full ministerial OPEC meeting. News that Iraqi troops were massing at the Kuwait border also gave the sector a fillip, as did a sharp gain in August Brent crude prices.

British Petroleum rose 3 pence to 343 pence, Enterprise increased 11 pence to 687 pence, and Shell Transport & Trading lost a pence to 488 pence.

Another oil stock, Lasmo, fell 3 pence to 445 pence after the company failed to beat market forecasts with an interim pretax profit increase of 22%.

Elsewhere, shares closed higher in Paris, Brussels, Madrid, Amsterdam, Stockholm and Zurich. Shares ended lower in Milan, and mixed in Frankfurt. South African gold stocks closed higher.

Stocks also ended higher in Hong Kong, Sydney, Singapore, Taipei and Seoul, mixed in Manila, and lower in Wellington.
