Economy: Durables Orders Fell 3.2% in June After 4.2% Gain --- Last Month's Data Show Continuing Weakness In Manufacturing Sector

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 07/26/90

WASHINGTON -- New government statistics show lingering weakness in the manufacturing sector. Orders to factories for durable goods dropped 3.2% in June after a 4.2% rise in May, the Commerce Department reported. Nearly every category of the big-ticket items suffered declines: transportation equipment, primary metals, machinery and military equipment.

Orders for durable goods -- items expected to last at least three years, such as cars, home appliances and industrial machinery -- often fluctuate, but this year they seem to have taken wider swings than usual, moving up substantially one month and down the next.

Their latest reversal suggests that U.S. manufacturing, which appeared to have been scrambling out of the doldrums a bit this spring, still has very little spark, a number of economists said.

"I think these numbers say the improvement has gone as far as it can go," said Robert McGee, an economist at Tokai Bank in New York.

Manufacturing has been hurt by the Federal Reserve's efforts to fight inflation over the past few years by holding interest rates high. That has increased the cost of financing major purchases and consequently has slowed demand.

Almost two weeks ago the Fed nudged down a key interest rate for the first time in six months, but Chairman Alan Greenspan has stressed that the action was only an effort to "offset" banks' tight lending policy. He has indicated that no further moves are planned.

That could leave the manufacturing sector flailing for a while, economists said.

Before there are substantial improvements in manufacturing, Mr. McGee said, "I think it's basically going to take some reason for consumer spending to pick up. . . . And I just don't see where that is on the horizon."

Some economists were also disturbed by a 0.5% drop in the backlog of orders. "That doesn't augur too well for industry going forward," said Peter Radford, chief economist at National Westminster Bancorp in New York. "If backlogs start to get worked off, that points to slower activity as we go into the fall, and that's not so encouraging."

Last month's decline brought new orders to $124.69 billion in June. For the first six months of the year, new orders came to $744.7 billion, 1.5% below a year earlier.

A 20.7% plunge in orders for defense capital goods accounted for much of June's drop. But even excluding military equipment, orders would have been down 1.7%, the department said.

Analysts said they don't believe the slide in defense orders reflects cutbacks in military spending but rather the timing of government contracts.

"I think it would be a little too early to see movements of that kind from cutbacks in defense spending," Mr. Radford said.

Orders for transportation equipment fell 4.8%, largely because of a decline in aircraft bookings. The only major category to post a rise in orders was capital goods outside the defense sector, an important indicator of business's investment plans. But the 0.3% rise wasn't enough to offset declines in April and May.

All the figures were adjusted for normal seasonal fluctuations but not for inflation.
