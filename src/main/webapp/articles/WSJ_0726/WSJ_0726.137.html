SEC Approves Rule Curtailing Program Trades

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 07/26/90

WASHINGTON -- Just two days after Monday's stock-market plunge, the Securities and Exchange Commission reluctantly approved a rule that would sharply curtail program trading on volatile days. The rule, proposed by the New York Stock Exchange, probably would have slowed Monday's drop if it had been in place, said Richard Ketchum, the agency's chief market watcher. "The slope of the decline picked up" Monday at the point where the Big Board's restriction would have kicked in, he said.

Still, all four SEC commissioners expressed reservations about the rule. Moreover, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission worried in a letter to the SEC that the rule "will decrease the linkage between markets." Nonetheless, the SEC unanimously voted to allow the Big Board to adopt the pilot plan for one year.

The SEC also voted to allow the introduction of a new product that will enable investors to protect themselves from market drops. The product, called SuperShares, was designed by Los Angeles investment firm Leland O'Brien Rubinstein Inc. to improve on the concept of portfolio insurance -- the now almost-defunct strategy that Leland O'Brien designed to protect institutional investors in market declines. Regulators and others who analyzed the crash concluded that it helped fuel the 508-point drop by the Dow Jones Industrial Average in the October 1987 crash.

SuperShares uses a complicated variation on mutual funds to enable investors to bet on stock movements. One form of SuperShares is tied to the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, to allow investors to benefit from big rises in the stock market. Another kind uses money-market funds to let investors benefit from big drops. "We don't know whether this product is going to be successful or not," said SEC Chairman Richard Breeden. But the commission decided to offer the choice to the marketplace.

The Big Board rule will place severe restrictions on program trading whenever the Dow Jones Industrial Average moves up or down 50 or more points in a day. Specifically, the plan targets index arbitrage, a computerized program-trading strategy that allows market players to take advantage of small price differences between a basket of stocks and a stock-index futures contract. That contract, traded on a commodity exchange, allows investors to bet on the movement of a stock basket without owning the stocks.

Index arbitrage has been blamed for exacerbating sharp market moves. But the new rule will force traders to wait for "upticks" in stock prices before completing the stock side of their transaction in a market falling by more than 50 points. That means firms would be taking market risk while they are waiting for stocks to move up. It is expected that many firms won't do index-arbitrage trades if they can't be assured of completing the transaction with a rapid-fire trade.

SEC officials emphasized that the rule wouldn't prohibit index arbitrage. "Index arbitrage becomes riskier, but it can be done," Mr. Ketchum said. He suggested that it would induce program traders to reduce the size of their trades, so they could take less market risk.

The Big Board rule would be triggered an average of once a month, the SEC said. The restriction would be unwound if the Dow industrials move to within 25 points of the prior day's closing level. The Big Board first proposed the rule to kick in after a 35-point move, but revised it after SEC complaints that it was too restrictive.

Still, each SEC commissioner in turn expressed concern that the 50-point level would be reached too frequently and that placing roadblocks to index arbitrage could add risk to the markets. The agency's chief economist, Kenneth Lehn, told the commission that the impact on the futures and options markets could be dangerous, and recommended the SEC "send the exchange back to the drawing board" for more economic analysis.

Even Mr. Ketchum, the SEC market-regulation director who advocated approving the pilot program, suggested that he was concerned about "de-linking the markets."

But Commissioner Edward Fleischman said the exchange "should be allowed to make its own mistakes" and learn from them.
