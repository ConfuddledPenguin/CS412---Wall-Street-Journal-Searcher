Foreign Exchange: Belief That Japan Won't Raise Rates In Near Future Helps Push Yen Down

  • Author: Charles Tenny Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/26/90

NEW YORK -- A growing belief that Japan's central bank won't raise interest rates in the near future helped push the yen down against the dollar and the mark. The dollar flitted above 150 yen several times around midafternoon in New York and was quoted late in the day at 149.89 yen, up from 148.78 yen the day before. The dollar also was trading at 1.6250 marks, up from 1.6215 marks late Tuesday. Sterling stood at $1.8117, down from $1.8133 late Tuesday.

In Tokyo Thursday, the dollar was trading at 150 yen soon after the opening; in Tokyo Wednesday it closed at 149.13 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6263 marks, up from the Tokyo close of 1.6241 marks on Wednesday.

The dollar hasn't closed above 150 yen since July 5.

The yen's slide also sent the mark up past the 92-yen level, a threshold that triggered customers' orders to sell yen to avoid further losses, traders said. The yen was quoted late in the day at 92.24 yen, compared with 91.76 yen late Tuesday in New York.

Two waves of activity dominated New York trading, dealers said. One centered on the dollar, as markets reacted quickly to comments by Federal Reserve Board Governor Wayne Angell in a Boston speech that he opposes any easing of U.S. monetary policy at this time. The dollar -- which has been drifting amid conflicting signals on U.S. rates since Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan referred to a marginal easing of rates two weeks ago in congressional testimony -- was pushed upward as traders jumped to buy the currency.

The Angell comment came as a surprise, said Alexis Egan, corporate foreign exchange trader at Banque Indosuez in New York. It was a "comment no one expected from a place no one expected," she said, and it caused a surge that helped trigger sell orders against the Japanese currency as the dollar approached 150 yen.

But the dollar was on the sidelines for much of the day as attention was focused on the yen's slide against other currencies, especially the mark. "Basically, today is a cross (or non-dollar rates) day and not a dollar day," Kenny Kimura, an assistant vice president and trader at Sumitomo Bank in New York, said.

Mr. Kimura said the yen's slide against the mark in Asian and European trading was unexpected and triggered sell orders. He and others cited a growing view among traders that Japan's central bank won't boost rates soon. Speculation that Japanese rates would rise had helped boost the yen in recent weeks.

Shigeito Yanase, a foreign exchange manager with Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank in Tokyo, echoed other traders when he said the Bank of Japan may wait until late August or early September to raise rates.

Ronald Holzer, vice president and chief dealer at Harris Trust & Savings Bank in Chicago, said attention in the market now will focus on the first estimate of U.S. second-quarter gross national product, due for release tomorrow.

"I think the market is poised fundamentally to lower the dollar," he said.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery jumped $4.10 to settle at $372 an ounce. Estimated volume was a heavy seven million ounces.

In Hong Kong Thursday, gold opened for trading at $371.35 an ounce.
