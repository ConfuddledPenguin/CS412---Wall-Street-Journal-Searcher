Cocoa Futures Trades Probed By Exchange

  • Author: Stanley W. Angrist
  • Date: 07/26/90

NEW YORK -- The Coffee, Sugar & Cocoa Exchange said it was investigating what it termed fraudulent trades in cocoa futures Tuesday. Orders to sell as many as 2,000 futures contracts caused the cocoa market to drop sharply for more than an hour Tuesday morning. When the orders were found to be of dubious origin, prices rebounded as the futures contracts were bought back.

But any traders who lost money as the market whipsawed stand little chance of recouping their losses, the exchange said. Exchange officers said these trades were legitimate.

The September cocoa contract opened Tuesday near Monday's closing price of $1,259 a ton, then quickly dropped $57 as big sell orders hit the floor. A cocoa analyst familiar with the incident said orders to sell 1,500 to 2,000 contracts were executed in lots of 300 contracts each.

At 11:15 a.m. EDT, buying started and the market regained all of its losses. The September contract even rose to a new daily high of $1,290 a ton before settling at $1,269, up $9.

James Bowe, senior vice president of the exchange, said the trades were placed by someone who fraudulently misrepresented himself in a phone call to the clerk of an independent floor broker. Mr. Bowe said the exchange immediately offered to guarantee that the floor broker who executed the trades would have sufficient funds to cover his losses.

"We did that to guarantee the integrity of the market," Mr. Bowe said. He declined to give other details, including the name of the trader or the amount of money involved. Figuring an average loss of $30 a contract -- about half of the total price drop, since the trades were executed at various prices -- the floor broker may have lost about $600,000.

Andrea Corcoran, director of the division of trading and markets for the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, said she understands the floor broker "has now accepted responsibility for the trades, which is entirely appropriate for a situation like this." She wouldn't comment on whether the CFTC was conducting its own investigation of the trades.

Floor brokers said they believe the clerk who accepted the order was provided with a legitimate account number or that he recognized the voice of the caller. The phone number given to the clerk was supposed to be at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. in New York, but when the clerk later called the number it turned out to be a phone booth in the World Trade Center.

A Prudential-Bache spokeswoman said that the trades in question weren't entered by Prudential-Bache to the independent floor broker. She added, "We are fully cooperating with the Coffee, Sugar & Cocoa Exchange so that they can expeditiously complete their investigation." Prudential-Bache is a unit of Prudential Insurance Co. of America.

The exchange said that in addition to conducting its own inquiry and notifying the CFTC, it also notified the U.S. Attorney in New York.
