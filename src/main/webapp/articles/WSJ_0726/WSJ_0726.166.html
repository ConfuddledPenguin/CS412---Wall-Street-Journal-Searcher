Enterprise -- The Part-Time Entrepreneur: In Summer, Teachers Test Their Entrepreneurial Skills --- Some Learn Lessons of Their Own During Break From the Classroom

  • Author: Barbara Marsh
  • Date: 07/26/90

A summertime small-business phenomenon is now in full swing: the teacher as part-time entrepreneur. Few professions produce as many part-time entrepreneurs as teaching. And the long summer vacation is the time when thousands put down their chalk and start calculating their profit margins -- often so they can afford to pick up the chalk in September.

Johnny Hamilton, a 42-year-old junior high school teacher in Monroe, La., loves teaching industrial arts, but his salary is only $23,000 a year. He says he and his wife, also a teacher, and their four children could "survive" on their teaching income alone. But the family would have to forgo a lot of extras, and the kids wouldn't get to attend dance class or football camp.

So, he runs a summertime lawn service. It boosts his annual income by as much as 25%. And though friends ask why he doesn't just relax when summer comes, he tells them, "I'm working to be able to teach next year."

With three months of vacation, other teachers just crave something different to do after months in the classroom. Others just hate being idle.

Susy Philhours and Virginia Cooper, middle-aged elementary school teachers in Bowling Green, Ky., wanted something to fill their summers after their grown children left home. Last year, they started a part-time, hand-painted apparel business, called the Two T's Inc. ("T" is for teacher). They're learning some lessons of their own in real-world economics.

At a recent teachers' convention where they exhibited their wares, they priced their T-shirts at $12 only to find a competitor's moving faster at $10. They felt their stuff is "prettier," and worth the higher price. But they met the rival's price anyway, and sales jumped. "It's a lot different than teaching your vowels, rules and numbers," allows Ms. Philhours, who was named Kentucky Elementary Teacher of the Year for 1990.

Nobody knows how many teachers transform themselves into entrepreneurs each year, but educators clearly moonlight more often than other professionals. Last year, 477,000 elementary, secondary and vocational school teachers ran their own businesses or worked a second job, say government surveys. This group accounts for 11.8% of all teachers, a level nearly twice that of multiple jobholders in the general working population.

Many are young, inexperienced teachers with low base salaries, says another government survey. They make enough in summer to close the income gap with more experienced peers, says Sharon Bobbitt, a government statistician.

For some teachers, a summertime business is a welcome break from the school grind. Burr Rhodes, a high school counselor in Hummelstown, Pa., runs a painting business with two teaching pals. After some school days, he wonders whether he did anybody any good. But the results of painting, he says, are "so visible."

For others, a summer business means security. Susie Mangus, a 25-year-old teacher in Evergreen, Colo., has worked as a waitress during the summer since she was a teen-ager. She's sick of hearing diners complain, but she doesn't know where to find better employment. "It's real hard to get a three-month job," she says. Next summer, once she has completed her masters' degree in teaching, she plans to start making money at something she has done mostly for fun until now: running a business selling T-shirts and sweat outfits that she decorates.

But teachers find that some businesses don't lend themselves to being a part-time operation. Jan Bohlin and Gerald Muto, two mathematics teachers in Topsham, Maine, started a summertime carpentry business so they could afford luxuries their teaching salaries wouldn't allow, such as their own custom-built homes. However, they found the work wasn't very profitable. They couldn't handle enough volume in just three months to recoup their insurance, workers' compensation, equipment and advertising costs.

Therefore, they decided to run the company year-round. They hired a supervisor to oversee their projects during school hours, check on the work after 3 p.m., and sometimes work until nightfall, while still trying to give their schoolwork top billing. "Sometimes it's very busy and sometimes we're very tired," says Mr. Bohlin.

Not all teachers find owning a business, even part time, to their liking. Charles Brads, a mathematics teacher in Clayton, Ohio, used to enjoy the roofing business he started 16 years ago. It helped support his children. He got to work outside after being cooped up in a classroom. And the neat look of well-laid rows of shingles appealed to his mathematical mind.

Oddly enough, he grew to resent the arithmetic required to keep tabs on things. He had to squander a day a week on finances, taxes and other paper work. "That's not my kind of math," he says. "I enjoy finding the area under a curve, but I hate adding a long column of figures."

What's more, now that he is 45, he finds it harder to run up the ladder. So, he retired from roofing this year; in his spare time, he is selling mutual funds.

For some, however, a part-time business becomes a life raft out of a dead-end career. Dale Eby, an Englewood, Ohio, resident, says he burned out after running the same woodworking class, seven days a week, for nine years. He found, meanwhile, that he liked being his own boss, running a home-remodeling business during the summer.

He quit teaching last fall. After a brief stint working for a construction contractor, he started his own remodeling company this spring. So far, he's completed $15,000 of work. And he says the only thing he misses from school is cutting out at 3.
