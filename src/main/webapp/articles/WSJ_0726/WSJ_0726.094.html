Trouble Is Brewing Over Folgers' Use Of Salvadoran Beans --- Descendants of P&G Founder Urge Changing Suppliers Until Unrest Ends in Area

  • Author: Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 07/26/90

Descendants of a founding father of Procter & Gamble Co. have launched a proxy fight to force the Cincinnati-based consumer-products giant to stop buying Salvadoran beans for its Folgers coffee. James Gamble, great-great grandson and namesake of the soap maker who founded P&G with candle maker William Procter in 1837, has submitted the proposal for consideration at P&G's annual meeting in October.

The proposal is backed by Mr. Gamble's brother Robert and their father, Walter, along with P&G shareholder Pat Close, according to Steven Koenig, a vice president of the money-management firm Franklin Research & Development Corp. Mr. Koenig said the resolution asks P&G to stop buying from suppliers in El Salvador "until parties to the Salvadoran conflict fully negotiate a peaceful settlement of the war." The U.S.-supported Salvadoran government has been fighting leftist guerrillas in a civil war for about a decade.

Mr. Koenig wouldn't disclose details about the shareholders involved or the size of their P&G holdings, describing them only as "substantial shareholders." None of them could be located for comment.

P&G has 102,953 shareholders, with no individual holder owning more than 0.5% of its 346 million shares outstanding, according to P&G officials.

Mr. Gamble has talked with P&G officials, but recent talks weren't fruitful: "We're just too far apart," Mr. Koenig said.

P&G's board will oppose the proposal, but a spokesman declined to comment further on Mr. Gamble's actions until next month, when P&G's proxy statement is to be mailed to holders.

Folgers coffee, the target of a boycott by other activists, contains less than 2% Salvadoran beans, the spokesman said. "Folgers has been unfairly singled out," the spokesman said. "We're doing the right thing by abiding by current U.S. policy" toward El Salvador, he said.

Mr. Koenig said the group plans to send proxy solicitation statements to all holders in the next few weeks. At the least, the group wants enough votes to ensure that the proposal can return to the P&G ballot next year if it's defeated in October. That would require support from holders of 3% of the shares. "But it's not unheard of to think we could get at least 20% to 30%," Mr. Koenig said.
