Politics & Policy: Deficit-Reduction Agreement Appears Unlikely Before Congress Starts Recess

  • Author: Jeffrey H. Birnbaum and David Wessel
  • Date: 07/26/90

WASHINGTON -- Budget negotiators appear unlikely to meet their goal of reaching a deficit-reduction agreement before Congress begins its August recess. Frustrated with the progress, President Bush in private meetings raised the possibility of asking Congress to delay its vacation. But leaders in Congress have asked him not to take that action, and instead may convene summit talks during the break.

The budget summit negotiators had hoped to reach an agreement before the Aug. 3 beginning of the recess. But congressional and administration officials now say that is extremely unlikely. "It would take a miracle," said Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) of the House Budget Committee.

Congressional leaders advised the president not to take the dramatic step of asking Congress to stay at work because it would probably antagonize lawmakers more than foster compromise. Instead, they discussed the possibility of having the administration and congressional leaders continue talking while Congress is away.

The betting on Capitol Hill is that Congress will begin its month-long vacation on time. Budget Director Richard Darman declined to comment, but another White House official said the president was "very serious" about his suggestion of keeping Congress in session.

House Minority Leader Robert Michel of Illinois quoted Mr. Bush as asking, "Does it help any if I say you ought to stay here if you don't get this agreement?" Mr. Michel also quoted the president saying: "I want to make progress, I want to get something going."

"We told him the August thing is sacrosanct. He understands that," Mr. Michel said. He also noted that it might not make sense to keep the whole Congress in session before an agreement is reached among the leaders.

"It could be that we are hung up on three or four major clumps of disagreement . . . and that we've got to have the summit come back during August to try to get it completed," House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.) said after meeting with the president.

Early in the day, Rep. Gephardt and White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater confirmed that administration and congressional negotiators this week hope to trade deficit-reduction packages aimed at paring the fiscal 1991 deficit by $50 billion. Mr. Fitzwater said the two sides planned to introduce their proposals simultaneously, but Democrats said they hadn't agreed to that.

Staffers later were saying that such an exchange might not occur soon, partly because Democrats and, to a lesser extent Republicans, are having trouble reaching agreement among themselves. If proposals are made soon, participants in the talks said they are more likely to be refinements of opening bargaining positions than major moves toward compromise.

Meanwhile, the House Ways and Means Committee voted to extend the federal borrowing limit. Congress will need to raise the borrowing limit before it goes on recess. The panel approved two versions of the debt-ceiling bill. One would extend the limit until Oct. 15, and the other would extend the ceiling through fiscal 1991. The short-term extension is the more likely outcome, raising the limit to $3.195 trillion from $3.122 trillion.

The committee also voted, at the urging of Rep. Byron Dorgan (D., N.D.), to ask the House Rules Committee to permit a vote on the House floor to remove the Social Security Trust Fund from the calculations that go into the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law. That amendment would be placed on the long-term extension.

Committee Chairman Dan Rostenkowski (D., Ill.) said he hoped any amendments that are added to the legislation be attached to the long-term extension and that the short-term extension be passed amendment free.
