TRACKING TRAVEL

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani

Ground Crew Sometimes Grounds a Flight

MODERN jetliners fly through thunderstorms, blizzards and hail, but often it's the most mundane problems that ground flights.

Mishaps on the runway are often the most troublesome. They range from a driver ramming a baggage cart into the side of a plane to a handler thrusting baggage containers so hard that they dent the cargo bin. Because planes aren't supposed to fly with even the smallest damage, they must be repaired immediately.

"The catering truck might slam into the catering door. The wing tip can catch on the gate area. Anything that can happen will happen," says William Reed, acting assistant manager of the Federal Aviation Administration's aircraft maintenance division. "It could be a delay from several minutes to a cancellation."

The problem had become such a big one at Pan American World Airways that the carrier started giving its operations employees bonuses if damage incidents decrease on a monthly basis.

In an internal memo in December, Robert L. Gould, senior vice president of operations at the Pan Am Corp. unit, said: "Ground damage is killing us."

Systemwide incidents ballooned 2 1/2 times to 295 in 1989 from 120 in 1986, according to the memo. Repair bills were "unbelievable," it said. Another repercussion: loss of customer good will. Fleet-service employees were warned to slow down and to stop driving underneath planes.

From Jan. 1 to May 31, Pan Am says, ground-damage incidents have dropped 30% from a year earlier.

Fliers are mostly kept in the dark about such incidents; the Transportation Department's monthly reports on cancellations and delays don't include schedule glitches due to "mechanical problems." In fact, a carrier with such delays could still appear among the top carriers on the department's on-time list.

Hints on Tipping

Doormen at Curbs

AT THE FOUR Seasons Hotel in Chicago, Horand M. Vogel whisked by a doorman holding open a cab door. Did the well-heeled consultant misstep by not tipping the doorman?

"Absolutely not," says Mr. Vogel. "Going in and out of taxis isn't a tip-worthy affair." Or is it?

There are etiquette rules that dictate how to tip waiters, cabbies, bartenders and bellmen but none for the men -- and a few women -- working at curbside. (They aren't the ones carrying luggage to rooms.) Most depend on tips. In one union, their salary is just $29 a day.

Here are suggestions from Elizabeth L. Post, granddaughter-in-law of Emily Post and author of etiquette books.

Her tips: If doormen hail a waiting cab in good weather, no tip; in bad weather and they get wet, $1. If they have to scare up a cab in good weather, $1. If they carry a couple of bags to the lobby, $1; to the curb, no tip. In big cities such as New York or Chicago, $1 is the norm; in smaller cities, 50 cents will do.

Never tip more than $1 for anything from keeping an eye on a bag to holding an umbrella for you in rainy weather. The exception is a $2 tip at the end of a visit in which you've tipped zilch. "Is it confusing?" says Mrs. Post. "Yes, oh, certainly."

As for Mr. Vogel, he didn't err, says Mrs. Post.

Kids-Fly-Free Gimmick

Is Unlikely This Summer

DON'T EXPECT an encore of last year's kids-fly-free promotion.

Last summer, a kid could fly free or for a nominal $1 when traveling with an adult. Families saved hundreds of dollars on vacation costs. The gimmick was so hot that airlines blamed it for trashing third-quarter revenue.

It's probably too late now for similar hoopla to start this year.

Texas Air Corp.'s Continental Airlines unit was the first to make the pitch last year, announcing its promotion in April. Most of the industry had matched it begrudgingly by the first of May.

Continental says it's not going to give away seats this year. "We don't feel the need to do the kids-fly-free," says John Nelson, executive vice president of marketing at Continental. The big reasons, he says: Continental's market share nationally has improved "dramatically" since a year ago, and vacation fares are cheaper than last year. "New York to Florida fares were $129 last year. This year, they're $89. When you look at $129 vs. $89, the $89" oneway fare generates enough traffic, he says.

One of the few deals for kids this summer is the restricted $29 and $39 one-way fares being offered by some airlines on flights from the Northeast to Florida.

But airlines cringe at the idea of introducing a children's freebie. Says Randall Mailen, USAir Group Inc.'s executive vice president of marketing: "We will not initiate the program."

Odds and Ends

CONSIDER the query Northwest Airlines Chairman Alfred Checchi faced: Would he fire an employee who posed nude in Playboy? In a letter to a flight attendant considering such a move, Mr. Checchi said the woman wouldn't be fired so long as Northwest wasn't named and she kept "company standards" on the job. He added: "A company should stand for something -- have values. . . . You owe it to all of us to consider the possible effect on our efforts if one of our people poses in Playboy." The flight attendant isn't posing, for now. . . . People registering travel agents at the International Travel Industry Expo earlier this spring got a memo from organizers warning that "although 99% of these people are educated adults, they will probably act like three-year-olds."
