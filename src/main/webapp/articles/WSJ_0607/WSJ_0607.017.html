Letters to the Editor: Forget Marketing, Build Better Cars

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/07/90

I was amused and dismayed by your May 4 article "GM Begins Quest to Win Back Consumer Confidence." I was amused because GM management has taken 10 years to admit publicly what owners of 1980s vintage GM cars know by heart: The cars were poorly designed, shabbily constructed and erratically supported. GM's continuing drop in market share shows the buying public is not as naive as the marketing professionals believe. I was dismayed that some forces in GM management still have not learned the lessons of their 1980s failures. The belief that better marketing will improve their car sales is a delusion. Consumers desire quality products offered at a reasonable price. Engineering and production quality are necessary to produce a competitive product. The auto maker should not expect improved sales until its cars match the performance of competitive models.

Timothy James Daniel

Irving, Texas

---

GM's cold relations in my case was topped only by its computer calling me at 3 a.m. to ask if I was happy with my '86 Buick in the midst of its 71 days in the shop the first year.

It's too late, GM.

Gary L. Hawkins

Torrance, Calif.

---

Despite the $40 million ad campaign, and the hundreds of millions spent to buy out H. Ross Perot, GM doesn't have 25 cents to send me a letter explaining why it won't stand behind the pile of junk it sold me labeled "Chevrolet."

From all the GM people I interacted with, I encountered the attitude that it's foolish to expect much when you purchase a GM product. I've learned my lesson.

Cheryl Klein

Ridgefield Park, N.J.

---

Perhaps GM would be better served by taking the $40 million it's going to spend on advertising and putting it toward a money-back-guarantee program. Any average guy on the street knows from experience that what any company tells you about its quality and service really doesn't mean anything unless the company puts it in writing with a money-back guarantee.

I haven't forgotten the new 1981 Oldsmobile that was in the shop 21 days in the five months I owned it. I haven't forgotten the burned-out transmission hundreds of miles from home and the consequential damages that weren't covered.

By the way, the Toyota's going strong after nine years and 116,000 miles. It has required little besides ordinary maintenance.

H.L. Pierson

Newark, Del.
