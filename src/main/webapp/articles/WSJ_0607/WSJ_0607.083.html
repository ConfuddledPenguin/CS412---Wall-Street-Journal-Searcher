Wednesday's Markets: Stock Prices Slip, Bonds Edge Higher --- Investors Take Profits, Shift to Other Issues; Dollar Rises Slightly

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/07/90

NEW YORK -- Investors, taking profits in some stocks and shifting into others, pushed stock prices slowly but steadily lower for the second day running. Bond prices moved slightly higher in extremely light activity after opening lower in the morning on news reports that the Federal Reserve Board isn't likely to ease interest rates in the near future. The dollar, meanwhile, rose slightly against the West German mark and Japanese yen, with traders generally focusing their attention on non-dollar currency trades.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 13.35 points to close at 2911.65 in moderately active trading. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices rose 1/8 point, or $1.25 for each $1,000 of face amount.

As an example of the shift in emphasis in the stock market, Argus Research said it is dropping such quality growth stocks as PepsiCo Inc. and Johnson & Johnson from its list of "sacred cows" -- or candidates for inclusion in new portfolios -- and replacing them with interest-rate sensitive issues such as utilities and financial institutions.

"Many of the financial-services companies should benefit from both lower interest rates and an underlying resilience in the economy," said A.C. Moore, research director at Argus. The economy is sluggish, he said, but it isn't facing a recession. "We're looking for a decline in interest rates based on slowing economic growth and falling food and oil prices."

Jon Groveman, president of Ladenburg, Thalmann & Co., attributed yesterday's sell-off partly to queasiness about indications that many bears have already been converted to bulls, suggesting ebbing buying pressure.

"We've been able to take some profits," said Ben Capaldi, partner at Brandywine Asset Management, Wilmington, Del. "People have been tending to throw money at the market," he said, referring to institutions and index funds scrambling to get into the market as it rallied through Monday.

In the bond market, activity was minimal, said Roger Craig, director of fixed-income investing at Banc One Asset Management, Columbus, Ohio. He said there wasn't much fallout from news reports about the Fed's inclination not to ease rates. "If investors were alarmed, we'd be at an 8.75% yield" on the Treasury's 30-year bond, he said.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell as 164 million shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues outnumbered advancers, 872 to 656.

Bond prices edged higher. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 8.44%.

The dollar rose. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6885 marks and 152.65 yen, compared with 1.6850 marks and 152.45 yen Tuesday.
