Moscow Offers Assurances It Won't Slow Emigration of Jews, U.S. Official Says

  • Author: Robert S. Greenberger
  • Date: 06/07/90

TURNBERRY, Scotland -- The Soviet Union assured the U.S. that it won't impede the emigration of Soviet Jews despite its public threat last week to do so, according to a senior U.S. official. During a private meeting Tuesday night in Denmark, Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze told Secretary of State James Baker that Moscow has "no plans to change {its} approach to emigration," the official said. The Soviets have allowed virtually unrestricted emigration during the past 18 months, and Mr. Shevardnadze emphasized that Moscow is "committed" to continuing this policy, the official added.

The clarification helps avoid what could have become a contentious issue between Washington and Moscow. Messrs. Baker and Shevardnadze were in Denmark to attend an international human-rights meeting. The U.S. official briefed reporters aboard Mr. Baker's plane as it departed for a meeting here of foreign ministers from members of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization.

Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev, during a news conference in Washington last Sunday after his summit meeting with President Bush, threatened to slow or halt the issuance of exit permits unless Israel promises to stop settling the Soviet-Jewish refugees in occupied Arab territory. Mr. Gorbachev said he has been "bombarded" with complaints from Arab governments about resettlement in the occupied territories. Mr. Gorbachev's remark produced sharp criticism in the U.S. and puzzled American officials because he hadn't said it privately.

Mr. Shevardnadze explained to Mr. Baker during their meeting Tuesday that the Soviet president's statement was meant "to demonstrate that we {the Soviets} would like to have certain understandings from the Israelis because there's a problem here as far as we're concerned," according to the U.S. official's account.

The Bush administration strongly supports the unconditional emigration of Soviet Jews. But it, too, has pressed Israel to bar the refugees from settling in the occupied territories, saying that their settlement there would be an obstacle to peace efforts. As the flow of Soviet Jews to Israel has reached 10,000 a month, Arab governments have grown increasingly alarmed that the refugees will displace Palestinians.

Israel claims that fewer than 1% of Soviet-Jewish refugees settle in the occupied territories. President Bush disagrees with this figure, however, because it doesn't include Soviet Jews who have settled in the outskirts of East Jerusalem, which Israel occupied after the 1967 Six-Day War. Israel considers united Jerusalem its capital. Mr. Bush provoked a storm of protest in Israel earlier this year when he urged Israel to stop settling refugees in the occupied West Bank and East Jerusalem.

The senior American official also said that Mr. Shevardnadze wasn't certain when the Soviet Union's parliament, the Supreme Soviet, would approve a pending law codifying Mr. Gorbachev's open-emigration policy. Earlier this year, the Soviets had predicted the law would be passed before the Washington summit. But Mr. Shevardnadze told Mr. Baker that with the new political freedom in the Soviet Union, Mr. Gorbachev doesn't control the Supreme Soviet's legislative calendar. Right now, he said, the parliament's "whole preoccupation" is with economic reform.

President Bush signed a trade pact with Mr. Gorbachev at the summit, but he won't submit it to Congress or grant Moscow most-favored-nation trade status until the Soviet emigration law is passed. The Soviet delay in passing the law takes some pressure off Mr. Bush. Congress is expected to resist approving a trade agreement until the Soviets lift their economic embargo of Lithuania and begin talks with its government.
