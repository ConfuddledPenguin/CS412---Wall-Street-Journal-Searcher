What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/07/90

SHEARSON SPLIT itself into two divisions in the brokerage firm's latest attempt to revive its sagging fortunes. One unit, responsible for investment banking and trading, will resurrect the Lehman Brothers name. The other half will handle individual investors and money management. The regrouping in effect recognizes that Shearson's two big takeovers in the 1980s never really worked. ---

The credit squeeze facing some businesses isn't affecting consumers so far. Any slowdown in borrowing by consumers seems to reflect their caution more than tighter bank lending policies. Some banks are even soliciting new consumer-loan business.

---

The federal fund that insures single-family home mortgages with low down payments will be broke by the end of the decade, Housing Secretary Kemp said. He called for higher fees on such mortgages, a prospect bankers said would make it harder for low-income people to buy homes.

---

Deficit-reduction talks between Congress and the White House resumed, with both sides jockeying over how to proceed with fiscal 1991 appropriations in the absence of a final budget agreement for the year.

---

The Bell companies received a potential setback in their efforts to combine unregulated operations with their basic phone businesses. A federal appeals panel overturned an FCC decision allowing such combinations.

---

Real estate woes could hurt banks in certain areas outside New England, but the newly identified problems aren't as great as those in that part of the country, the U.S. comptroller of the currency said. He refused to disclose the additional regions.

---

Stock prices declined in a second day of profit-taking. The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at 2911.65, down 13.35 points, in moderately active trading. The dollar rose slightly against the mark and the yen.

Junk bonds rallied, even as the Resolution Trust Corp. put up for sale more than $50 million of bonds held by failed S&Ls. The price stability may indicate the market is firming.

---

Oil prices rose despite uncertainties about OPEC output and bearish figures on U.S. petroleum inventories. Some traders saw a turnaround in the making for the world oil markets.

---

Moody's lowered its rating on New York state's general obligation debt, citing the state's financial condition and the region's economic uncertainty.

---

Salomon beat out nine other investment banks to be adviser to the U.S. Overseas Private Investment Corp.'s Eastern European Growth Fund.

---

Small-business earnings rose 6.2% last year, less than the 6.7% and 9.2% increases in the two preceding years.

---

Lands' End's top merchandiser resigned amid criticism of the catalog firm's product selection. He hinted at differences with senior executives over product introductions.

---

Life insurers are increasingly drawing a distinction between people who are basically healthy and those they consider "preferred" risks.

---

West Germany's economy grew an inflation-adjusted 2.5% in the first quarter from the preceding period, and economists predict the pace will continue through 1990 as firms expand to rebuild East German industry.
