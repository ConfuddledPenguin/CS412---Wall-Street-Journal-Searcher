Letters to the Editor: Those Vietnam Dominoes Keep Falling

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/07/90

In regard to "Setting the Vietnam Record Straight," editorial page, May 4: Without questioning the honest intent of Arthur Kobler, W. Gage McAfee and Warren Williams, their attempt to justify the U.S. invasion of Vietnam by proposing that the legacy "is profoundly constructive" is redolent of the immortal phrase, "the patient died, but the operation was successful." The record can never be set straight until participants and historians forbear making such statements as "it was devastating . . . but . . . " The inclusion of that awful word "but" is the standard political justification for keeping open options for the next conflagration. The honor and dignity of the 55,000 American servicemen who died in Vietnam cannot be sustained (or vindicated) by economic successes. Their lives were much more important.

Perhaps the three gentlemen have overlooked the recent events in Europe where, without bloodshed, an economically free society has emerged from 45 years of communist aggression. And we didn't use one napalm bomb.

Patrick A. Dunn

Decatur, Ga.

---

I quote Messrs. Kobler, McAfee and Williams, " . . . the transcending legacy of the U.S. war effort is profoundly constructive: It is the growing prosperity of the 300 million people who live in Southeast Asia outside of Indochina and Burma." Apparently, the domino theory will never die. I suggest they read Barbara Tuchman's "The March of Folly," especially the chapter titled "America Betrays Herself in Vietnam," and Michael Sheehan's "A Bright and Shining Lie." They should address themselves to Ms. Tuchman's and Mr. Sheehan's profoundly disturbing analyses of the domino theory as the rationale for America's war in Vietnam. As for the transcending legacy of Vietnam: Why not include the loss -- perhaps forever -- of America's high moral reputation, the disintegration of authority, widespread drug use and the crippling of presidential power. This legacy from Vietnam has had an undoubted and profoundly transcending effect on America, destructive not constructive.

Sidney Marks

Knox, Ind.

---

The U.S. committed three gigantic errors in the Vietnam War. If we citizens forget them, our leaders may err again.

1. We had no overall goal or purpose.

2. Our leaders didn't know, or didn't want to know, how badly the war was going.

3. Our troops had so many restrictions placed on them there was no way they could win.

Elected and appointed officials who made a mess of things can never be brought to trial. However, we should remember our Vietnam debacle -- that we may never repeat it.

Tony Weissgarber

Lt. Col., USAF, Retired

San Antonio, Texas
