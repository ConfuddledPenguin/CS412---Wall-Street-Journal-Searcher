Law: Drug-Test Results Of New York City's Transit Unit Barred

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/07/90

NEW YORK -- Citing inaccurate testing procedures, a federal judge quashed thousands of marijuana tests performed on New York City Transit Authority employees in the mid-1980s. U.S. District Judge Robert P. Patterson, in a 100-page opinion, also invalidated the Transit Authority's policy of having employees who are neither suspected drug users nor in safety-sensitive jobs take urine tests.

"We believe this is the first case in which an entire drug-testing program has been invalidated for violating the due process rights of public employees," said Edward J. Davis, staff attorney for the Legal Action Center, which brought the 1985 class action lawsuit against the Transit Authority. "Public employers now have to be very careful to use accurate drug-testing methods, and to provide employees with adequate opportunity to challenge the results."

A Transit Authority spokesman said the agency had no comment on the decision other than to say, "We do plan to appeal it, and feel it will be overturned in the appeals process."

In his decision, Judge Patterson said urine samples were frequently contaminated at collection sites, and that laboratory methods were innaccurate. Moreover, he noted that employees weren't given adequate notice of test results, or access to some of their samples, so that they could have them independently tested to challenge the results.

The decision came after a partial settlement last February in the same suit. At that time, the Transit Authority agreed to compensate about 100 employees, including rehiring many who had been fired after undergoing testing. It paid other employees compensation of up to $25,000. Yesterday's decision covered the remaining employees who joined the suit.

Judge Patterson has set a conference for June 19 to begin defining compensation and to determine whether an injunction is needed to cover current testing policies.
