Politics & Policy: Senate Panel Targets `Circle of Poison' As It Votes to Ban Some Pesticide Exports

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 06/07/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Agriculture Committee voted to ban the export of unsafe pesticides. The move was an effort to protect U.S. consumers who eat imported foods from the so-called circle of poison. A loophole in current law allows U.S. agrichemical companies to export pesticides even if they aren't registered for farm use or aren't tolerated on foods in this country. Illegal residues of these pesticides, including some suspected carcinogens, often are found in imported fruits and vegetables as well as some beef and dairy products.

"If the Environmental Protection Agency says a pesticide is too unsafe to be used on American-grown food, then it is too unsafe to be used on foreign-grown food," asserted Sen. Patrick Leahy (D., Vt.), the committee chairman.

The panel adopted the legislation, part of the 1990 farm bill, by voice vote despite the objections of the National Agricultural Chemicals Association, a trade group comprising pesticide manufacturers whose export sales totaled $2.2 billion last year. The group warned that the bill, among other things, could impede trade in agricultural products and force companies to move plants overseas, eliminating jobs in the U.S.

The Senate committee's vote was expected to spur House action on an identical measure sponsored by Reps. Leon Panetta (D., Calif.), Dan Glickman (D., Kan.) and Mike Synar (D., Okla.). Rep. Synar already has held hearings on the circle-of-poison issue.

The U.S. imports 33 billion pounds of fruits and vegetables a year, roughly one-quarter of its total consumption. The Food and Drug Administration inspects only a tiny fraction of the imports, but government inspectors have found about 5% to be tainted with pesticides. Recent FDA sampling showed 10% of imported peas and 8% of imported pears contained illegal residues, while U.S.-grown peas and pears contained none.

Besides barring exports of unsafe pesticides, the Senate legislation would prohibit imports of any food tainted with pesticides that are banned in the U.S. It also would revoke residue tolerances that remain on the books for such pesticides. This provision would put U.S. farmers on the same competitive footing as foreign growers, who currently can use certain effective pesticides that aren't registered for use on U.S. farms.

"The point of this legislation is to eliminate the double standard in pesticide policy," said William McNichol, a director of a consumer group fighting what it considers pesticide misuse. The bill, if enacted, would be first substantial change in pesticide law since 1978.

Among other things, the bill would require U.S. manufacturers to obtain prior consent from foreign countries before shipping potentially hazardous pesticides across their borders. And it would require safety instructions on pesticide labels to be written in the language of the importing country.

To help the FDA screen imports, the measure would require U.S. importers to list the pesticides generally used on imported foods. The bill has 25 co-sponsors in the Senate.
