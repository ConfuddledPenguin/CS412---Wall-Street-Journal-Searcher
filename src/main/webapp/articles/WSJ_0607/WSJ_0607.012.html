Blood Tests Used More Extensively For Life Insurance

  • Author: Karen Slater
  • Date: 06/07/90

Life-insurance companies are requiring blood samples from more and more applicants mostly because of the AIDS epidemic. But the practice has had other effects besides helping insurers turn away people exposed to the deadly virus. The ability to analyze blood for cholesterol levels and other indicators is one factor that has prompted many companies to offer lower rates to people judged "preferred" risks.

At the same time, the blood analyses are also leading some insurers to "rate" -- that is, demand higher premiums from -- a small number of people who wouldn't previously have been identified as "substandard" risks.

Around 6% to 8% of applicants are being rated at Lincoln National Life Insurance Co., up from 5% to 7% before blood testing was so extensive, says Donald Chambers, chief medical director at the Fort Wayne, Ind., company. He says the added ratings mostly reflect findings of impaired liver function, high cholesterol and previously undetected diabetes.

Insurers are also postponing action on some applications until the individuals' doctors can sort out what the abnormal test readings mean. "We are not going to write the insurance until the situation is resolved, or at least defined," says John Snore, vice president for underwriting and claims at Prudential Insurance Co. of America, Newark, N.J.

Most insurers are requiring blood samples from people who seek life-insurance coverage of $100,000 or more. But some companies have gone down to $50,000, at least in jurisdictions where the risk of acquired immune deficiency syndrome looms largest. For instance, Massachusetts Mutual Life Insurance Co., Springfield, this month dropped to a $50,000 threshold in California, while holding at $100,000 elsewhere.

Insurers have also expanded their scrutiny of urine specimens over the past few years to include tests for cocaine use. A "surprisingly high" 1% of people tested by Prudential are showing up as cocaine users and thus are being turned down for insurance, Mr. Snore says.
