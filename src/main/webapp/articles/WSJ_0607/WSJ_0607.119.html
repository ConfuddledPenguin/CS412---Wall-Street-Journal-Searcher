Machine Tool Exports Rose 26% in 1st Period From a Year Earlier

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/07/90

Exports of machine tools in the first quarter jumped 26% from a year earlier, and producers say foreign demand continues strong. The nation's machine tool builders exported $289.5 million of machines in the first quarter, up from $230.6 million a year earlier, according to the NMTBA -- The Association for Manufacturing Technology, a McLean, Va., trade group.

Manufacturing companies in Canada, Western Europe, Mexico and the Pacific Rim all have been buying equipment at a lively pace in recent months, U.S. machine tool producers say. Shipments to Canada more than doubled in the first quarter, while exports to West Germany jumped 55% and sales to Mexico rose 18%.

Machine tools are complex, often computer-controlled, machines, ranging from lathes to metal-forming presses, that shape most metal parts. Rising foreign demand indicates aggressive efforts by overseas durable goods producers to increase their productivity, machine tool producers say. That could spell trouble for U.S. auto makers and other U.S. companies competing in world-wide markets.

U.S. machine tool imports declined 8.2% during the first quarter, NMTBA said. But the $538.5 million of imports still were nearly twice U.S. exports. Imports have accounted for about half the new machine tools installed in U.S. factories in recent years.

However, U.S. producers are faring better in world machine tool trade so far in 1990 than they have during any year since 1981. Imports last year were 2.5 times exports, and back in 1986, U.S. machine tool imports were 3.8 times larger than exports.

Machine tool imports from Japan dropped 23% in the first quarter, the major factor in the import decline. Japanese producers, nonetheless, supplied nearly half the machine tools imported into the U.S. during the latest quarter, according to NMTBA figures. Imports from West Germany, the second largest supplier with 14% of the first-quarter total, were up a slight 1.6%.
