Credit Ratings: Moody's Cuts New York State Debt Rating --- Agency Expresses Concern On Financial Condition, Economic Uncertainties

  • Author: James C. Hyatt
  • Date: 06/07/90

NEW YORK -- Moody's Investors Service Inc. lowered its rating on New York state general obligation debt, expressing concern about the state's financial condition, its heavy debt load, and economic uncertainties in the region. The ratings agency lowered to single-A from single-A-1 its rating on $4.27 billion of general obligation and fully guaranteed bonds. Only two states, Louisiana and Massachusetts, have a lower rating.

New York has seen its short and long-term credit ratings shrink this year amid efforts to balance the budget against spending pressures, regional economic slowdown, and resistance to higher taxes. State legislators in late May agreed on a $49.8 billion budget and tax package.

Standard & Poor's Corp. jolted the bond market in March when it cut its New York state rating two notches, to single-A from double-A-minus. At that time, Moody's rated the state's long-term debt single-A-1, a medium-grade ranking it had granted in 1986. During the previous decade, Moody's had rated New York state single-A.

Like S&P, Moody's in March had lowered its rating on short-term notes, but held up issuing a new long-term rating while awaiting results of the budget negotiations. The latest announcement by Moody's puts the two major ratings agencies in agreement for long-term debt ratings as well.

In response to the latest ratings, Dall W. Forsythe, state budget director, said the decision by Moody's "was not unexpected, particularly after a six-week delay in enacting a budget." He said the state's new budget includes a number of fiscal reforms, including eliminating spring borrowing and retiring an accumulated deficit, as well as imposing a spending cap. Thus, he added, "we are confident that as the fiscal reforms achieve their intended effects, the rating agencies will re-evaluate and restore the state's credit rating."

Moody's assigned a MIG-2 rating to a $4.1 billion issue of tax anticipation notes expected to be sold June 8. It affirmed at single-A its rating on limited-liability lease and contractual obligation issues. The state's gross debt totals about $26.2 billion.

In assigning an SP-1 rating to the new notes issue, S&P affirmed its single-A general obligation debt rating and other ratings on about $17.4 billion of New York state obligations.

Both ratings agencies expressed reservations about the state's ability to make good on its promised intentions of improving its budget situation. "Unfortunately, the state's track record in deviating from prudent fiscal paramaters" isn't so great, said Hyman Grossman, a managing director at S&P.

The lower rating presumably will make it more costly for the state to borrow in the future. "The lower rating would indicate a higher degree of risk," said George Leung, vice president and managing director of state ratings at Moody's.

To boost its credit ratings, he said, the state would have to show "improvement in its financial condition," including avoidance of one-time measures that contribute to problems in future years.

Moody's noted that the state's budget balance "remains narrow, measures intended to add revenues are reliant on taxes that have proven difficult to predict, and internal sources of liquidity have eroded."

And, it added, "spending pressures pose a risk, particularly in the areas of Medicaid, social services and corrections. With the election of all major state office-holders in November, the recent budget process did not resort to spending cuts in a substantial way."
