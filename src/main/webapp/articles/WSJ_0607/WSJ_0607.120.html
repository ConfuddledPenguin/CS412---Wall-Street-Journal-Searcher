Credit Markets: Junk Bonds Continue to Rally, Despite Move By RTC to Sell Off $50 Million of Securities

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 06/07/90

NEW YORK -- Junk bonds continued to rally yesterday, even as the Resolution Trust Corp. put more than $50 million of junk bonds up for sale. The RTC is one of the nation's largest holders of junk bonds, which the federal agency inherited when it seized a number of failing savings and loan associations.

Traders on Wall Street said the agency sent a so-called "bid list" to brokers and institutional investors and successfully sold the bonds at what were termed "attractive" prices. Securities analysts estimate that the RTC holds between $4 billion and $6 billion of junk bonds, which it plans to liquidate slowly over time.

Officials at RTC said it sells junk bonds frequently in the open market, however they wouldn't elaborate about yesterday's transaction.

But traders said the bid list, which represented bonds held by just one savings and loan under RTC conservatorship, included names such as Kroger Co. and Coltec Industries. These are some of the so-called "good junk" bonds that trade at prices that are close to par, or full face value. Also on the lists were some mid-quality bonds, including certain securities issued by RJR Nabisco Inc.

Analysts said RTC's ability to sell junk bonds without disrupting the entire market is a sign that the market is beginning to recover -- although modestly -- after a long depression. Junk-bond sentiment is more positive now than it has been in several months and prices of upper-tier and middle-tier issues have been rising.

Meanwhile, the number of new issues has dropped sharply, leading to complaints of supply shortages.

Thomas Madden, a high-yield portfolio manager at Federated Investors Corp., said RTC tapped the market at just the right time.

"In the last several days, you've almost had a buying panic in some names. Now you have one of the largest holders of junk bonds putting some bonds up for sale." He said the timing nearly ensured that RTC would get good prices for its bonds.

Peter Karches, head of the high-yield sales and trading at Morgan Stanley & Co., termed the RTC sale a "non-event" for the junk bond market. He said traders and investors took the news in stride and "prices trended higher," anyway.

However, prices of the individual issues sold by RTC did come under modest pressure yesterday and ended between 1/8 and 1/2 point lower.

Most junk bond prices rose between 1/4 and 1/2 point higher yesterday. That was a respectable advance considering Treasury bonds ended unchanged to just 1/8 point higher.

Treasury Securities

Treasury securities ended unchanged to modestly higher in sluggish trading.

"The bond market is hungry for some new data, but unfortunately it's not going to get anything until next week," said Robert Chandross, a vice president and economist at Lloyds Bank PLC.

Bond dealers said there was a brief flurry of selling in early trading as investors reacted to a story in this newspaper. The article, which cited several Federal Reserve officials, said the policy makers appear to have no intention of cutting short-term interest rates at this time.

Bond prices firmed later as investors looked beyond the present and began to speculate about possible Fed easing in the future.

"Market sentiment is still bullish," said Michael T. Moran, manager of government trading at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. "The Fed is deemed to be on hold, but if next week's data show inflation is behaving, then a Fed easing would come under consideration once again," he said. Key next week will be statistics on last month's producer prices, consumer prices and retail sales.

In trading, the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose 1/8 point, or about $1.25 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, eased to 8.44%.

The benchmark bond was quoted late yesterday at a price of 103 9/32, up from 103 5/32 on Tuesday. Shorter-term securities were little changed.

Corporate Issues

Prices of investment-grade bonds ended unchanged to 1/8 point higher.

Among the corporate borrowers that tapped the credit markets Wednesday was Du Pont Co., which offered $1.85 billion (redemption amount) of zero-coupon convertible subordinated notes.

The notes, which mature in 20 years, were priced at $218.55 per $1,000 note for a yield to maturity of 7.75%.

Strong investor demand prompted underwriters at First Boston Corp. to boost the size of the issue from an originally planned $1.1 billion. The offering will result in proceeds to the company of $404,317,500.

Georgia-Pacific Corp. offered $600 million of credit-sensitive notes through underwriters led by Bear, Stearns & Co. The issue consisted of $300 million of seven-year notes priced at par to yield 10% and $300 million of 12-year notes priced to yield 10.109%.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds ended unchanged to 1/8 point lower after listless trading.

"We're just sort of status quo; the market's just pausing here," one municipal trader said.

In municipal market activity, Standard & Poor's Corp. has assigned a SP-1 rating to an issue of $4.1 billion of New York State tax and revenue anticipation notes.

The notes, which constitute the state's spring borrowing, are expected to be priced today by a Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. group.

The SP-1 rating on the tax and revenue anticipation notes is below the SP-1-Plus rating that S&P assigned to last year's $3.9 billion spring borrowing. The rating equals that given to the state's sale of $775 million tax and revenue anticipation deficit notes in March.

Late yesterday, Moody's Investors Service Inc. assigned a MIG-2 rating to New York State's tax and revenue anticipation notes. In addition, Moody's downgraded the rating on New York State's general obligation debt to single-A from single A-1.

Market participants had expected the rating changes, due to the state's financial condition, its heavy debt load and lengthy delay in adopting a budget.

In secondary market dealings, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 were unchanged, at 98 5/8 to yield 7.32%.

Foreign Bonds

British government bonds, called gilts, ended sharply lower yesterday on speculation that several economic reports due out soon will provide unfavorable news for the bond market.

Britain's benchmark 11.75% bond due 2003/2007 fell 3/4 point to 100 6/32 to yield 11.72% while the 12% issue due in 1995 was off 1/2 point at 98 27/32 to yield 12.34%.

Prices began to fall during early trading as overseas investors began selling gilts. British bonds also were hurt by declines in other major bond markets and weakness in long-term bond futures.

Analysts said that investors anxiously await Monday's report on May retail sales volume.

Bond prices were lower in Japan and West Germany.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities prices ended with slight advances in sympathy with U.S. Treasurys.

Prices in both markets were stagnant all session in the absence of any new major economic reports.

Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities for June delivery were up 3/32 at 99 10/32.

In the new-issue arena, two Federal National Mortgage Association issues were offered. Both were Remics, or real estate mortgage investment conduits, each totaling $300.1 million.

One was backed by Fannie Mae 9.5% securities and priced in 13 classes by Shearson Lehman Hutton.

The other issue was priced in 11 classes by Goldman, Sachs & Co. and backed by new stripped mortgage securities formed from Fannie Mae 10.5% securities. A stripped mortgage security is formed by separating the principal portion interest portion of a security and selling them separately.

With these offerings, a Fannie Mae spokeswoman said there have been $27.402 billion of Fannie Mae Remics priced so far in 1990. Since the inception of the securities in August 1987, Fannie Mae has issued $80.99 billion of Remics, she said.

As for Fannie Mae strips, the spokeswoman said $2.97 billion have been issued so far in 1990 and $22.03 billion have been issued since their inception in February 1987.
