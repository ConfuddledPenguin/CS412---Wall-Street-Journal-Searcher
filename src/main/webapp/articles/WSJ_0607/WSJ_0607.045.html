CHANGES AT AUSCHWITZ

Poland is removing communist propaganda from Auschwitz, the Nazi death camp preserved as a Holocaust museum.

Removing the propaganda will amount to giving Auschwitz back to the Jews, said Stefan Wilkanowicz, secretary of the government's International Commission on the Future of Auschwitz. Although more Jews were gassed at Auschwitz than anywhere else, existing exhibits "are done in such a way that the fate of the Jews is marginalized," he added.

Most Holocaust historians estimate that more than 90% of the victims at Auschwitz were Jews. But this is stated nowhere at the museum, which portrays the Jews as just one of the 19 ethnic groups or nationalities killed at the camp.

Poland's new Solidarity-led government, embarrassed by a recent Roman Catholic-Jewish dispute over a Catholic convent at Auschwitz, has tripled its subsidy for the museum to $315,000.

PROTECTIONISM IN SWEDEN

Sweden's agricultural sector is among the most protected in the world, costing consumers an extra 18 billion kronor ($3 billion) a year for basic agricultural commodities such as cereals, dairy products, meats and sugar, according to a report by the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade. According to GATT, the average Swedish household could save 8,500 kronor a year if current protectionist measures were eliminated. Parliament is scheduled to vote Saturday on a plan to end some of the protectionist measures.

BRAZIL GOES AFTER SMOKERS

The Brazilian government has instituted new anti-smoking measures. The measures, which went into effect at the beginning of June, include a ban on cigarette sales to those under 18, a ban on TV advertising for cigarettes between 6 a.m. and 9 p.m. and a ban on free samples. Current laws prohibiting smoking in elevators, on buses and in other public areas are routinely ignored in Brazil, where an estimated 54.6 million people -- or about a third of the population -- smoke.

AFRICA'S TRAGEDIES

One child dies every minute in Ethiopia of malnutrition or preventable disease, Unicef officials told a news conference. In Mozambique, they said, one child in three dies before reaching the age of five. The officials said that war, drought and donor fatigue combined to produce such grim statistics in parts of Africa. Unicef has appealed for $15.3 million for emergency work in Ethiopia and $11.7 million for Mozambique.

NEW COINS FOR TAIWAN

Taiwan's central bank said it would issue gold-bullion coins early next year to commemorate the 80th anniversary of the founding of the Republic of China. The coins will be denominated at 1,000 New Taiwan dollars (US$37) each, the Central Bank officials said. The new coins will be sold principally to local consumers, officials said, although they didn't rule out the possibility that they would be exported in the future.

PROPOSED FILM BAN IN BRITAIN

Britain's opposition Labor Party called for a ban on a Pakistani film that depicts the death by lightning of British author Salman Rushdie. The film "International Guerrillas," which portrays Mr. Rushdie as a decadent playboy dedicated to the overthrow of Islam, has been playing to packed houses in Pakistan. The real Mr. Rushdie has been in hiding since Iran's late Ayatollah Khomeini told Moslems to kill him, charging that his novel "The Satanic Verses" blasphemed Islam. Labor Party spokesman Donald Anderson said he opposed censorship but the film could hurt race relations in Britain.

CHINA'S GENDER IMBALANCE

Millions of Chinese men have failed to find a wife, and eligible women will be in even shorter supply in coming years, according to a survey published by Beijing's China News Service.

The service said the serious imbalance in the ratio of men to women in China would worsen over the next 20 years. A random check on 100 unmarried Chinese adults showed 93 were men, it said.

Bachelors were found to outnumber single women by 10 million within the 29- to 49-year-old age group, the news service said. It said rural men faced the bleakest prospects as more and more city dwellers turned to rural areas to find brides.

The report didn't give any reason for the sex-ratio imbalance. But last February, the service attributed the disparity to such factors as the drowning of baby girls in rural areas, modern techniques allowing doctors to identify fetal gender and the availability of abortion. Many Chinese still believe it is imperative to bear a son to carry on the family name.
