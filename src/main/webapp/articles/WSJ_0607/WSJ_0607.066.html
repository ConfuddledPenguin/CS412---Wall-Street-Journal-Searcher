Kemp Warns That Biggest FHA Fund Risks Insolvency Unless Premiums Rise

  • Author: Joe Davidson
  • Date: 06/07/90

WASHINGTON -- The largest Federal Housing Administration fund will be broke by the end of the decade, predicted Housing Secretary Jack Kemp, who urged higher FHA fees on mortgages with low down payments. Mr. Kemp told the Senate subcommittee on housing and urban affairs of a five-part strategy the Bush administration is proposing to Congress "to ensure that the FHA continues to meet its historic mission to provide affordable home-ownership opportunities for low- and moderate-income American families."

The FHA's mutual mortgage insurance fund, which insures low down-payment mortgages on single-family homes, currently is solvent, Secretary Kemp said, but posting losses of $350 million yearly. The losses result mainly from defaults on insured mortgages. The fund's net worth shrank to $2.6 billion at the end of fiscal 1989 from $8 billion in 1979.

"Eventually, therefore, FHA will have a negative net worth unless something is done," he added in a prepared statement. "This is the clearest possible evidence of the urgent need to reform FHA and manage it properly." The FHA is an agency of the Department of Housing and Urban Development.

The "reform" strategy would impose an additional 0.5% annual premium onto loans with lower down payments, which are considered higher risks. The term of the added premium would vary with the size of the down payment. The current premium is 3.8% of the loan amount.

Closing costs would no longer be financed in the mortgage, under Mr. Kemp's plan. "Home buyers would have to pay two-thirds of the closing costs, in cash, when they buy the house," he said. "With this change, every home buyer would have at least 2% real equity in their home, which will reduce defaults."

The additional premium and the closing-cost proposal would make it more difficult for low-income families to buy homes, according to the Mortgage Bankers Association of America. "In addition to increasing premium fees on those least able to afford higher fees, the proposals double the amount of cash the FHA home buyer will need up front to buy a home, thereby raising the cost of housing and negating the very benefit that the program has provided to homeowners for the last 50 years," complained Warren Lasko, the association's executive vice president.

Suzanne Hutchinson, executive vice president of the Mortgage Insurance Companies of America, said her group supports greater flexibility in the FHA premiums so they "more accurately reflect the risk." She suggested, however, that low-income, first-time home buyers be given special consideration.

Mr. Kemp told the senators he will announce within the month a plan to streamline procedures to reduce the FHA's inventory of properties under foreclosure. The 80,000 houses in the inventory cost the government $500 million a year to maintain. "Foreclosed and boarded-up homes do not improve with age . . . ," Mr. Kemp said. "FHA can achieve substantial savings if it can cut its losses on foreclosures."

In testimony to the subcommittee, a General Accounting Office housing expert said that allowing FHA to raise its loan limit "would have the greatest effect" on increasing the fund's cash balance. But John Ols, the GAO's director of housing and community development issues, added that the loan ceiling, which is currently $124,875, shouldn't be raised until the fund is sound, which it now isn't.

Mr. Kemp cited a Price Waterhouse accounting firm study of the FHA that said raising the loan limit wouldn't significantly affect the status of the fund. "The resources of FHA should not be committed to allowing relatively high-income borrowers to buy homes with very low down payments," he said.

He added that the FHA should stop insuring second homes. "FHA's job is to help low- and moderate-income families become first-time home buyers," the secretary said, "not first-time second home buyers."
