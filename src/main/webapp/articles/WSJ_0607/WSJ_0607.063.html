Who's News: Land's End Says Gaitskill Left Job In Merchandising

  • Author: Francine Schwadel
  • Date: 06/07/90

DODGEVILLE, Wis. -- Lands' End Inc.'s top merchandising executive resigned at a time when analysts believe the catalog company isn't doing enough to freshen its selection. Russell C. Gaitskill, a department-store veteran who joined Lands' End less than two years ago as its executive vice president of merchandising, said he left to pursue "other things" including the possibility of starting his own manufacturing business.

Lands' End named David Dyer as his successor. Mr. Dyer, 40 years old, joined Lands' End in July as managing director in charge of launching a separate catalog of bed and bath items and other home accessories. He had been a merchandising executive at the Burdines unit of Federated Department Stores Inc.

A spokesman said Mr. Dyer's appointment doesn't signal any change in direction at Lands' End. "We're going to continue to focus on freshening our product line," he said.

The 41-year-old Mr. Gaitskill nevertheless hinted in an interview that he may have had differences with senior Lands' End executives over product introductions. He said he "absolutely" wanted to bring in new merchandise, but wouldn't comment on the reaction of his superiors.

"I don't think it's a matter of being unhappy," he said of his decision to leave Lands' End. "It's a matter of being mismatched."

Mr. Gaitskill drew a distinction between his background in merchandising at Federated's Lazarus unit and Lands' End, which he described as "more of a marketing" company. "One is the medium and one is the product," he said.

Analysts attribute Lands' End's current troubles to its failure to introduce enough fresh merchandise to capture the imagination of its customers. Just 11.5% of the company's catalog items this past year and 8% the year before were new. Lands' End plans to increase that percentage this year to 15% to 18%. A separate children's clothing catalog is planned for August and the bed and bath catalog is due next year.

But the company, which sells mostly traditional casual clothes like khaki pants and rugby shirts, has already hit some serious snags. After sailing smoothly through the 1980s, ringing up annual sales and profit gains of more than 40%, net income for the year ended Jan. 31 fell 10% to $29.1 million, or $1.45 a share, on sales of $545.2 million. Earnings for the first quarter ended May 4 plunged 64%. And analysts say earnings for the current fiscal year could decline more than 10%.
