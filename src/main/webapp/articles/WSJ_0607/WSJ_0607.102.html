Law -- Legal Beat: Judge Chides Lawyers Facing Suit For Thousands of Asbestos Claims

  • Author: Milo Geyelin and Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 06/07/90

A federal judge excoriated two lawyers who filed thousands of asbestos-related claims, saying greed motivated them to solicit and pursue many cases that apparently were groundless. The two California lawyers, Gordon A. Stemple and Richard F. Gerry, represented 6,000 asbestos claimants against Raymark Industries Inc., an asbestos maker that paid $16 million to tire workers nationwide for injuries claimed by Messrs. Stemple and Gerry. The company then sued the two lawyers in 1987 challenging the validity of the claims and alleging fraud and racketeering.

In response to a pretrial motion seeking dismissal of the suit, Judge Patrick F. Kelly, of Wichita, Kan., answered with a scalding 63-page opinion. He accused the two lawyers -- and three doctors they employed to evaluate the claims -- of fraud, deceptive solicitation and "reckless disregard of the truth."

"It appears that this unusual, distasteful and disappointing case emanates from the attorneys' greed, which has clouded their professional judgment; i.e., their indifference as to whether any of the 6,000 claims meet professional standards or not," Judge Kelly wrote.

Messrs. Stemple and Gerry formed the National Tire Workers Litigation Project in 1986, equipped vans with X-ray machines and traveled to Raymark tire plants across the country in the "examobiles" to conduct massive employee examinations. In his order, the judge called the technique an "unethical nationwide solicitation process" and charged that the three doctors involved compromised their professional responsibility by using flawed screening procedures to churn groundless claims.

"There is evidence in this case which indicates that {Messrs.} Stemple and Gerry violated ethical obligations by conducting a contrived and deceptive solicitation program," the judge wrote.

Reached at his office in Century City, Calif., Mr. Stemple said he disagreed with the opinion and predicted that he, Mr. Gerry and the three doctors will prevail when the case goes before a jury. Mr. Gerry, of San Diego, was out of town and couldn't be reached for comment.

Raymark was a unit of Raytech Corp., a Bridgeport, Conn., maker of fastners and brake and clutch materials, that stripped the company of its non-asbestos-related assets and spun it off in 1988 to distance itself from asbestos litigation. Raymark has been forced into bankruptcy-court proceedings under the weight of more than 68,000 asbestos-related claims. Last year, Raytech sought bankruptcy-court protection.

The company has been known for particularly aggressive litigation tactics, said Mr. Stemple, pointing to the suit against him as the most recent example. But Raymark lawyer William L. Myers Jr., of the Philadelphia firm of Ballard, Spahr, Andrews & Ingersoll, said Judge Kelly's opinion was a "timely expression of judicial disapproval of mass-produced lawsuits."

STUDENT-LOAN INQUIRY leads to indictment of ex-official at Florida thrift.

A federal grand jury in Tampa, Fla., returned a 25-count indictment against Jeffrey A. Flatten, charging that he conspired to defraud the U.S. Department of Education in connection with its Guaranteed Student Loan Program. The program makes low-interest loans available to students for college expenses.

Mr. Flatten, who headed Florida Federal Savings Bank's student-loan division, was also charged with committing mail fraud and stealing government funds.

The indictment follows Florida Federal's conviction by the federal jury for falsifying statements related to government-backed student loans. Last month, in what is believed to be the largest student-loan case ever prosecuted, the federal jury found the company guilty of 43 counts of falsifying statements between November 1986 and July 1987. Two former officers were convicted of falsifying statements.

In 1988, Florida Federal came under scrutiny for allegedly filing as many as 17,000 fraudulent insurance claims on $35 million in student loans. The investigation helped spark a management shake-up that resulted in the resignation of the thrift's chairman.

The indictment against Mr. Flatten alleges that from November 1986 to September 1987 he conspired with other officers and employees of Florida Federal to submit the 17,000 fraudulent claims. Those claims, the indictment says, were supported by documents falsely reflecting that Florida Federal performed required collection activity on the loans.

In St. Petersburg, Fla., Florida Federal officials hadn't any comment. Mr. Flatten couldn't be reached.

METROMEDIA WINS damages of $46 million in fraud suit.

Metromedia Co. sued William D. Fugazy in 1987 for allegedly fraudulently overstating the value of Fugazy Express Inc., a limousine service that Metromedia acquired in 1985. Fugazy Express, which Metromedia owned for two years, is in bankruptcy-law proceedings.

In the civil-racketeering suit, Metromedia claimed Mr. Fugazy overstated the value of the company by $7 million, said Martin Shelton, an attorney for the Secaucus, N.J., communications company. Mr. Shelton said Metromedia, controlled by billionaire John Kluge, had to invest an additional $30 million in the company because of the alleged misrepresentations.

The jury hearing the case in federal court in Manhattan, N.Y., awarded damages of $15 million, which Judge Robert Carter trebled under racketeering law.

Anthony Princi, an attorney for Mr. Fugazy, plans to appeal the decision.

FEES MOUNT in Southmark Corp. bankruptcy proceedings.

Lawyers alone are seeking more than $8.7 million in fees in the reorganization of the real-estate and thrift concern. Investment bankers and accountants aren't far behind with fee requests totaling $7.7 million, bringing the total professional fees to $16.5 million up to March 31.

"That is a big chunk of assets out of this estate that would otherwise be available to pay creditors," U.S. Trustee George McElreath said during a hearing on the fees before Dallas Bankruptcy Judge Steven Felsenthal.

Southmark has proposed paying its creditors about $300 million in cash, notes and stock to settle debts of $2 billion. The company's counsel alone are seeking almost $6.5 million in fees, with the largest single amount, $3.4 million, to go to the New York law firm of Shearman & Sterling. Judge Felsenthal took the fee applications under consideration.

In his comments to Judge Felsenthal, Mr. McElreath cited another Dallas bankruptcy judge who frequently criticizes lawyers' high fees by saying they're "buying a Cadillac when they could buy a Ford."

"In this case," Mr. McElreath said, "we're paying for an Indy race car."

ARNOLD & PORTER will market services with defense consultant.

The Washington, D.C., law firm entered a "teaming agreement" with Interpar, a new defense-consulting firm that will specialize in accords between foreign governments and companies to market arms to more than one country. The venture is aimed at helping the defense and aerospace industries adjust to the decline in defense budgets here and abroad as the Cold War winds down.

In recent years, Arnold & Porter has launched several subsidiaries that perform non-legal work, including the consulting firm Apco Associates. But according to Arnold & Porter partner Jeffrey H. Smith, a former general counsel to the Senate Armed Services Committee, Interpar isn't an Arnold & Porter subsidiary, and has no financial links to the law firm. Interpar Chairman Milton Lohr and President Vitalij Garber are former high-ranking defense officials in the Reagan and Carter administrations.

Under the "teaming agreement," lawyers for Arnold & Porter will make joint presentations with Interpar to potential clients. The two firms will also notify their own clients that the firms are available to work together, although clients will be required to hire the concerns separately. According to Mr. Smith, the company determined that separate transactions are required by the legal canons of ethics.

Separately, a top Arnold & Porter trade litigator, Patrick Macrory, will leave the firm for the Washington office of Akin, Gump, Strauss, Hauer & Feld.

According to Akin Gump Managing Partner Bruce McLean, Mr. Macrory will be working on trade matters in the firm's Washington office and also in a new 25-lawyer Brussels office that the firm opened last year.

FDIC SUES LAWYERS: The Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. is seeking $30 million in damages from three Texas lawyers who advised the defunct Western Savings Association of Dallas to make a series of loans that allegedly violated federal regulations. Named in the suit, filed in Dallas federal court, are Waco lawyers Michael J. Vaughn and Donald B. Lynn and Austin lawyer A. Rick Hightower. Mr. Lynn said, "It's our position that the whole case is without merit, and we are going to litigate it."
