World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Gain Slightly as Traders Await Expiration of June Options, Futures Contracts

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/07/90

Tokyo's Nikkei Index rose softly in light trading with many participants cautious ahead of today's expiration of June options and futures contracts. Meanwhile, prices in London ended moderate trading near their intraday lows, as the market failed to muster any strength for a late-day rally.

The Nikkei Index of 225 issues gained 31.88 points to 32953.50. The index slipped 3.75 points Tuesday. But decliners yesterday outnumbered advancers 498 to 438, while 180 issues were unchanged.

In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index was up 91.32 points to 33044.88.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 600 million shares, up a bit from 567.9 million on Tuesday.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues in the first section, which lost 2.31 points Tuesday, slipped 1.86 points, or 0.08%, to 2423.07.

The second section index rose 26.20 points, or 0.65%, to 4039.37. The index added 20.23 points Tuesday. Volume in the section was estimated at 30.7 million shares, up from 29 million shares Tuesday.

Because many participants are anticipating a potential overhang of positions outstanding amid the June options and futures contract expiration today, market atmosphere was fairly cautious throughout yesterday, traders said.

Limited buying in blue chips in the morning was met by arbitrage selling and profit-taking, while early sentiment was weak on softer bond and currency markets, traders said.

But in the afternoon, at least one of the Big Four Japanese securities firms was seen buying approximately 10 million shares of Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, resulting in a minor rally in shipbuilding shares, according to Masahiko Tsuyuzaki, chief trader at Tachibana Securities Co.

Other financial institutions later pursued large-capital issues -- particularly steel stocks -- although rounds of profit-taking chipped away at gains, the trader said.

At the same time, major foreign brokerage houses bought lightly on programs linked to the Nikkei 225 issues, observers said.

Speculative buying also drove up prices on selected shares, the traders said.

They added that a late rumor, which began after the close of cash trading but before the futures closing, boosted activity somewhat in futures trading.

An announcement by Narita Airport officials that flights from Japan to and over the Soviet Union were redirected by flight-control authorities in Moscow sparked speculation of a coup in the region. But Narita officials reportedly gave no explanations, outside of citing flight-traffic problems, to warrant such a rumor, traders said.

Mitsubishi Heavy Industries rose 30 yen to 1100 yen (20 cents to $7.21) on trading house buying, while the rest of the sector also posted healthy gains.

Mitsui Engineering & Shipbuilding rose 30 yen to 1030 yen, Hitachi Zosen gained 10 yen to 885 yen, Kawasaki Heavy rose 12 yen to 997 yen, and Ishikawajima-Harima Heavy Industries rose 30 yen to 1200 yen.

Auto stocks were also firmer, with Isuzu Motor rising 20 yen to 1090 yen, and Toyota adding 70 yen to 2610 yen.

Other large-capital issues, including Kawasaki Steel, NKK and Nippon Steel, advanced.

On the other hand, high-technology electrical shares ended lower after battling profit-taking.

Sony fell 90 yen to 8560 yen, Pioneer slipped 40 yen to 6160 yen, and TDK fell 90 yen to 6610 yen.

Meanwhile, London opened lower, and continued its downward slide as profittakers moved in and market makers marked prices lower. In addition, a poor showing by stock futures reinforced stocks' downward momentum.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index fell 21.6 points to 2358.5, after falling as many as 25.6 points. The day's high was when the index was down 12.7 points.

Other indexes reflected the trend. The Financial Times 30 Share Index dropped 12.9 points to 1881.

Volume was a moderate 429 million shares, compared with 618.2 million issues Tuesday.

In the futures markets, the June FT-SE 100 index contract closed at 2399, down from Tuesday's close of 2423.

Share losses were spread across most sectors, with the largest declines recorded by Bass, down 30 pence to #10.70 (51 cents to $18.05), Lloyds, down 12 pence to 297 pence, Pearson, down 16 pence to 739 pence, and RMC, down 14 pence to 691 pence.

The few companies posting gains included Asda, up 5 pence to 113.5 pence on unconfirmed rumors of a takeover bid. This gain helped the sector, with Associated British Foods, up 4 pence at 409 pence, and Grand Metropolitan, up 5 pence to 642 pence.

Rank rose 5 pence to 836 pence, while Hillsdown was up 6 pence to 265 pence, and STC gained 9 pence to 275 pence.

Elsewhere, stocks ended lower in Amsterdam, Madrid, Frankfurt, Milan, Zurich, Stockholm and Paris, and mixed in Brussels. South African gold stocks closed marginally higher.

In the Asian and Pacific region, shares closed higher in Hong Kong and Singapore, and lower in Sydney, Manila, Wellington and Taipei. Seoul was closed.
