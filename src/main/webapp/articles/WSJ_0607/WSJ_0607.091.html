Abreast of the Market: Industrial Stocks Fall 13.35 Points As Profit-Taking Hits Blue Chips

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 06/07/90

NEW YORK -- Profit-taking in some high-flying blue chips and growth stocks knocked the stock market down for the second consecutive day. The Dow Jones Industrial Average slipped 13.35 to 2911.65. Among broader averages, Standard & Poor's 500-stock index fell 1.68 to 364.96, and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index dropped 1.32 to 339.98. Declining issues topped advancers by 872 to 656 on the New York Stock Exchange, where volume totaled 164,020,000 shares.

Meanwhile, some small stocks gained. The Nasdaq composite index rose 0.35 to 464.96.

Though large institutions are still looking for cheap stocks to buy, yesterday they sold many of the key stocks that have led the rally for the past few weeks. Home Depot, which is trading at nearly 40 times expected 1990 earnings, lost 1 1/2 to 58 1/2. Among other growth stocks with prices at about 20 times expected 1990 earnings: Merck fell 1 to 82 3/4; PepsiCo dropped 7/8 to 74 7/8 and Limited fell 1 to 49 1/4.

Oil shares also fell prey to profit-taking, despite a rebound in crude oil prices, which came amid unsubstantiated rumors of a nuclear accident in the Soviet Union. July futures on West Texas Intermediate crude rose 24 cents a barrel, to $16.92, in New York trading.

Eugene L. Nowak, an oil analyst at Dean Witter Reynolds, said Saudi Arabia's discounts on crude oil purchase contracts for July should hold down oil prices, causing "downward trading pressure" on energy stocks.

"Right now, it's looking like (the price of oil) might get a little bit weaker than we've thought," said Mr. Nowak, although he added that he is maintaining his estimate of $19 to $19.50 a barrel as an average price this year for West Texas Intermediate, the benchmark U.S. crude.

Oil stocks ranking among the most active Big Board issues included Occidental Petroleum, which dropped 5/8 to 26 1/2 on composite volume of 1.6 million shares; Exxon, which fell 3/8 to 47 7/8, and Texaco, which lost 1 1/2 to 57 7/8.

Among other oil issues, Chevron fell 7/8 to 69 1/2, Mobil lost 1 to 62 1/8, Amoco slid 3/4 to 52, Royal Dutch Petroleum fell 1 to 75 1/4 and Atlantic Richfiel lost 2 to 116 1/4.

Amerada Hess dropped 3/4 to 44 3/4; Dean Witter lowered its estimate of second-quarter earnings from 45 cents a share to a range of zero to 25 cents.

Oil field-services stocks were active for the second consecutive session, but prices in the sector stabilized after dropping sharply Tuesday. Schlumberger gained 1/2 to 56 1/4, Halliburton lost 1/4 to 46, Baker Hughes dropped 1/4 to 27 3/8 and Smith International slid 3/8 to 14 3/8.

Meanwhile, shares of oil refining and marketing companies bucked the group's decline. This newspaper reported in its "Heard on the Street" column that Alan Gaines, a money manager specializing in energy issues, is making a long-term case for buying the stocks based on the potential for the companies' properties to increase in value.

Diamond Shamrock, one of several issues Mr. Gaines favors, gained 1/2 to 25 1/2. Others include Ashland Oil, which slipped 1/4 to 36; Horsham, which closed unchanged at 8 1/2, and Crown Central Petroleum, whose Class B shares jumped 1 3/8 to 33 5/8 on the American Stock Exchange.

Eastman Kodak climbed 1 7/8 to 41 7/8 on two million shares in trading fueled by speculation that Hoffmann-La Roche may be interested in the company's Sterling Drug unit and that the judge in Polaroid's patent-infringement case against the company, won in 1985, would delay awarding damages until next year. Kodak declined comment on the activity in its stock; Hoffmann, the U.S. unit of Switzerland's Roche Holding, denied the Sterling rumor.

Upjohn dropped 1 1/4 to 42 on 1.8 million shares as merger speculation cooled. The stock had advanced 4 5/8 points over the three previous sessions after Business Week reported that the company may become the next pharmaceutical maker to seek a merger partner.

Lowe's fell 1 1/8 to 46 3/4. The company reported that sales at stores open at least one year rose just 1% from the year-earlier level.

Construction stocks rallied following the passage of a California referendum that would double the state's gasoline tax, to 18 cents a gallon, to provide funds for an $18.5 billion freeway-building program. Beazer rose 5/8 to 10 1/2, CalMat rose 1 1/4 to 34 3/4 and Perini jumped 3 to 25 1/2.

Several bank issues, which recently rebounded as interest rates moved lower, fell on profit-taking yesterday. Bankers Trust fell 1 1/8 to 45; BankAmerica slipped 1/8 to 33 and Citicorp fell 5/8 to 24 3/8.

Union Carbide, a target of restructuring speculation in the past, topped the Big Board's list of most active issues. The stock went up 1 to 21 as nearly 2.1 million shares changed hands.

Norfolk Southern gained 1 3/8 to 43 5/8. Merrill Lynch recommended the stock and said it believes the company's earnings will grow at a significantly faster pace than those of other railroads.

NCH plummeted 4 1/2 to 58 3/4, reflecting disappointment with its earnings report for the fiscal quarter ended April 30. Net income for the period fell 3.7% from a year earlier.

TransTechnology skidded 1 7/8 to 7 7/8 after saying results for the fiscal year ended March 31 were worse than it had expected. The company said it expects to report losses for both the fiscal fourth quarter and the year.

General Host gained 5/8 to 7 1/4. The company reported that net for its fiscal first quarter, a 16-week period ended May 20, jumped 261% from the comparable year-ago period.

Armco added 3/8 to 9 3/8. The company said a Texas judge set aside $26 million of the $48.8 million in damages assessed against the company in a breach-of-contract dispute over an office lease.

Eagle-Picher Industries rose 1/2 to 4 3/8. The company said it is putting three units up for sale as part of a plan to raise cash for payment of asbestos-litigation climbs.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.36 to 364.19. Volume totaled 14,490,000 shares.
