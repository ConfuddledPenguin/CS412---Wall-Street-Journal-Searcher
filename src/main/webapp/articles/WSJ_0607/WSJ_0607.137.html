House Measure to Speed the Transfer Of Western Technology to Soviet Union

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 06/07/90

WASHINGTON -- The House passed a bill that would speed up the transfer of Western technology to the Soviet Union and the emerging democracies of Eastern Europe. The measure, approved last night by a 312-86 vote, was timed to prod the Bush administration into expanding its offer to relax export restrictions on computers, telecommunications equipment and machine tools that the West can sell to these countries. The U.S. and 16 of its European and Asian allies are currently meeting in Paris to discuss how they can safely meet the demands of the Soviet Union and its former satellites for Western products and know-how.

The House bill would allow the U.S. to freely sell the Soviet Union and East European countries computers with a capacity of 550 megabits of information per second. Current rules set a limit of 78 mps. The administration has offered to raise it to 275 mps, but would allow shipments of more powerful computers if the purchasing governments promise to guard against unauthorized military use. The legislation would require the administration to seek the 550 mps level in negotiations with its allies, who would have to ratify any change.

The higher standard would permit East European nations -- and the Soviet Union under some conditions -- to buy some U.S. mainframes as well as practically all personal computers available in the West.

While the Bush administration offer stops short of giving East Europe access to telecommunications systems powered by digital switching, the House bill would give the West the discretion of selling even the top-of-the-line systems.

The Bush administration has expressed opposition to the bill, saying that it pre-empts the administration's authority. In the Senate, the Banking Committee plans to begin work soon on its version of the bill.

Before passing the bill last night, the House attached amendments to withhold certain trade benefits from the Soviet Union and China because of continuing concerns over human rights in those countries.

Reacting strongly to Soviet attempts to squash Lithuania's moves towards independence, the House approved an amendment to deny the Soviets the higher levels of technology until President Bush certifies to Congress that the Soviets have entered into "serious negotiations" with Lithuania's duly-elected government and are conducting these talks without coercion. The amendment, introduced by Rep. Richard Durbin (D., Ill.) and passed on a 390-24 vote, would effectively freeze current restrictions on the Soviet Union.

Mr. Durbin vowed that the U.S. won't "abandon Lithuania in its efforts for self-determination."

Signaling its distaste for China's human-rights record, the House approved an amendment that would strip Mr. Bush of his discretionary power to allow U.S. owners of communications satellites to buy launch services from China. Rep. Gerald Solomon (R., N.Y.), the amendment's sponsor, took Beijing to task for failing to make amends for the massacre of democracy marchers in Tiananmen Square a year ago.

Last December, Mr. Bush, invoking a national-security waiver from existing sanctions, authorized the shipment of three U.S. satellites to be put in orbit by China. However, he would lose such leeway if this provision becomes law. The Solomon amendment was approved 395-15.

The House bill, a bipartisan effort steered by Rep. Sam Gejdenson (D., Conn.) and Rep. Toby Roth (R., Wis.), would also create a "license-free zone" for exports among the U.S. and 16 of its cooperating allies. Such a step eliminate licensing requirements for exports of all but the most sophisticated equipment, and would rid the U.S. licensing bureaucracy and exporters of one-third of their normal paperwork.

The bill's "sunset" provisions would automatically eliminate export controls on whole categories of commodities after a prescribed period, unless the authorities can justify reinstating controls on individual items.
