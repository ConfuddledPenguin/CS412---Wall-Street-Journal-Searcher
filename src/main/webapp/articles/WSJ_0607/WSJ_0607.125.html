California Adopts New Rules to Guide Cellular Industry

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 06/07/90

The California Public Utilities Commission adopted a set of rules for the mushrooming cellular-telephone business designed to encourage price cuts and discourage rate increases. The new regulations in California, the country's largest cellular market, also protect resellers against anti-competitive behavior by cellular companies. Resellers, which are non-utility companies that buy cellular service at wholesale prices and resell them to retail customers, have expressed concerns that cellular companies undercut their prices.

The commission decided that applications for rate reductions -- as much as 10% at a time -- would be effective as soon as they are filed. Increases, however, require formal applications and a 30-day comment period before they go into effect.

"The cellular-telephone market is an exciting and dynamic new industry, but if we are to realize its full potential, prices have to come down," said PUC President G. Mitchell Wilk. The commission, however, defined cellular service as "discretionary," and declined against a more rigid approach.

A spokeswoman for Pacific Telesis Group, the San Francisco-based regional Bell company that provides cellular service in much of California, said the company is "generally satisfied with the PUC's decision." However, the company would have liked more flexibility to raise prices, especially since cellular systems will need to upgrade in the future to meet growing capacity and convert to digital transmission, she said.

Like California, many states have been adopting rules designed to encourage competition in the cellular industry and hold prices in check.

Separately, in a decision that could serve as a model for other states, the commission adopted safeguards for consumers using pay phones in most of the state. The rules were sparked by numerous consumer complaints about difficulties using pay phones or exorbitant costs of making long-distance and local calls.

Within 60 days, the commission said, pay phones must allow customers without a coin to dial emergency assistance, directory assistance and repair service, as well as long-distance access codes, operator assistance, and 800 numbers.

In addition, the commission ruled that the phones must allow customers to make local or long-distance calls with coins or calling cards. And the phones must have signs that give information on long-distance charges, dialing instructions and the name and number of the pay-phone provider.
