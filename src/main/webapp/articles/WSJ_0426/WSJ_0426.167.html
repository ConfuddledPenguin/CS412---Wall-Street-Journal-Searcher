Letters to the Editor: The Lone Analyst vs. Taj Mahal

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/26/90

Your March 26 article on Marvin B. Roffman, the former Janney Montgomery Scott analyst who, as you reported, was fired because of his refusal to apologize for his negative comments on the Taj Mahal casino project, should send a real chill throughout the investment community. An analyst's role is to develop and provide an informed, objective opinion regarding the future of a company or an industry and make some determination about the value of the securities of those entities. The analyst is an arbiter of values between the issuers and the investors. He is required to gather and sift through all types of information from sources each with their own biases. Out of that mosaic, looking into the future, the analyst then must put forth predictions. Ultimately, job security, or at least compensation, is determined by the accuracy of those predictions.

Apparently, Mr. Roffman sustained what can be a rather demanding career for 16 years, only to be ousted for doing his job -- offering an opinion. I could understand firing Mr. Roffman next February, after the cold winds had blown and he proved to be wrong. That is a risk all analysts run. But if, as you reported, he was fired for putting forth an opinion, that decision brings into question the opinions of any analyst at Janney Montgomery and, unfortunately, the opinions of all analysts. If an analyst's job is at risk for something other than fraud, faulty analysis or uninformed opinion then there is limited credibility and value to the role. Once the partisans can influence the process because of their own fears and greed, investors -- big and small -- will suffer.

Jack L. Rivkin

Senior Executive Vice President

Director of Equity Research

Shearson Lehman Hutton

New York

---

Mr. Roffman's statement about Donald Trump's Taj Mahal casino won't make or break that casino. I imagine Mr. Trump has heard negative remarks worse than this before and laughed. It now appears he can't take the heat. Does one man, because of his money, have the right to dictate what should be said about him?

Mr. Trump should believe in what he is creating and not let Mr. Roffman's statement bother him. Or does Mr. Roffman's statement make sense? Does he know the market better than Mr. Trump?

It was a low cut on Mr. Trump's part, to have this man fired. Come on, Mr. Trump, grow up.

Carleen Gollahon

Broken Arrow, Okla.

---

Janney Montgomery Scott did not hire Mr. Roffman as a public servant, to act as an outspoken watchdog of (in this case) the casino industry, but rather to provide objective analysis, positive and negative, solely for the firm and its clients. Nor did it hire Mr. Roffman in order to provide an independent public forum for his views. He was fired for writing a direct, unauthorized letter to Mr. Trump, for publicly denigrating a potential client's business, and for disregarding the explicit obligations to his employer. Janney Montgomery asked for and received this analyst's views, and paid for these opinions. As such, Mr. Roffman's analyses were proprietary, and his employer was under no obligation to release them publicly.

Mr. Roffman may believe that he can find a similar job in Philadelphia or elsewhere and use that position to air his views publicly and at will, but any brokerage or research firm would be hard pressed to share that belief at the expense of its reputation and its clients.

Robert Rimm

Managing Partner

Chronos Capital Management

Philadelphia

---

I congratulate securities analyst Roffman for refusing to compromise his professional ethics at the demand of Donald Trump.

Janney Montgomery certainly won't be staggered by my personal censure for its firing of Mr. Roffman. It might, however, become concerned if a few thousand other investors boycotted the firm in protest. It might wish it had stood firm in the face of the mighty wrath of Mr. Trump.

Frank D. Hammer

Groveland, Calif.

---

The morality play of Janney Montgomery vs. Donald Trump offers, in addition to the usual lessons of the arrogance of power on one side and moral cowardice on the other, two prosaic ones. One is that any analyst considering issuing a negative report on a company also had better update his resume. The other is that investors can expect stock analyses to be worth even less than they are now.

Bob Beckett

Fountain Valley, Calif.
