Foreigners Keep Gobbling Up U.S. Firms As Americans Have Financing Troubles

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 04/26/90

With many U.S. buyers hobbled by problems getting bank debt or junk-bond financing, a steady parade of foreign buyers continues to gobble up U.S. corporations. Ever since Japanese purchases of Rockefeller Center and Columbia Pictures jolted Americans last year, foreign buyers have kept up their acquisitions even as many U.S. concerns have held back.

The latest examples came within hours of each other yesterday morning when Norton Co. agreed to be sold for $1.9 billion to Cie. de Saint-Gobain of France, and a Mideast investment concern won an auction for Saks Fifth Avenue with a $1.5 billion bid. Both bids were blowouts that far exceeded expectations.

Mike Overlock, head of mergers and acquisitions at Goldman, Sachs & Co., said he believes foreign buyers are gaining a greater share of the $1 billion-plus acquisitions of U.S. concerns than in the past several years. He said that's partly because U.S. leveraged buy-out firms have faded from the scene.

Stephen Waters, co-head of mergers and acquisitions at Morgan Stanley & Co., which advised the winning bidder for Saks, agreed. "The collapse of the junk-bond market and the strictures that the Federal Reserve is putting on U.S. banks have allowed foreign buyers who aren't operating under the same strictures to be more aggressive on pricing," Mr. Waters said.

Felix Rohatyn, senior partner at Lazard Freres & Co., which advised Saint-Gobain, said foreign purchases of U.S. concerns have risen "dramatically," reflecting both the growth of foreign economies and the fact that many U.S. companies have taken on too much debt to make purchases.

"It may be that foreign companies leveraged themselves less over the last 10 years than American companies, and are in a stronger position now to make acquisitions," said Mr. Rohatyn. "The Europeans and Japanese have invested heavily in their own businesses, and have grown to have the critical mass to do these sort of things."

According to Securities Data Corp., five of the 10 biggest announced bids for U.S. concerns this year have come from foreign bidders. And foreign companies accounted for 31% of the dollar value of all announced bids for U.S. targets.

Without a healthy market for junk bonds, U.S. leveraged buy-out firms haven't been able to compete for many of the companies that came up for grabs. This year's only big announced LBO, a $780 million purchase of Chrysler's Gulfstream business aircraft unit, is being done by Forstmann, Little & Co., which has its own subordinated debt fund and so doesn't need to sell junk bonds.

In the Saks auction, Bahrain-based Investcorp International Inc. was helped by its ability to tap its own big pools of both equity and subordinated debt, which eliminated any need for selling junk bonds. According to people familiar with the Saks auction, Investcorp plans to put up about $600 million in equity and $300 million from its own debt fund.

The hefty equity and debt contributions reduced the need for bank debt to less than half of the purchase price, making it easier for Investcorp to line up $700 million in bank debt from a group led by Manufacturers Hanover Trust Co. Only one buy-out fund, Merrill Lynch Capital Partners, bid for Saks, in a joint venture with another retailer. Another, Wasserstein, Perella & Co., expressed interest, but didn't make a formal bid.

The runners-up in both contests also were foreign. The No. 2 bidder for Saks was a team of management and a Japanese concern, which is said to have bid $1.3 billion -- itself at the top of the range of expected bids. And the company Saint-Gobain outbid for Norton was a British conglomerate, BTR PLC.

J. Tomilson Hill, co-head of investment banking at Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc., which conducted the Saks auction, said that foreign acquirers such as Hanson PLC and Grand Metropolitan PLC have generally made the kind of drastic operating changes that eventually justified what at first appeared to be high prices for their U.S. purchases. He called both the Saks and Norton price tags "very full." Saint-Gobain, however, has promised to avoid certain drastic cost-cutting and plant-closing measures.

William Strong, co-head of world-wide mergers and acquisitions at Salomon Brothers Inc., noted that foreign companies continue to be helped in buying U.S. assets by the weak dollar. Although the dollar has rebounded recently, it remains nearly 40% below its 1985 peak against the yen, for example.

To be sure, many takeover experts believe the appetite of Japanese concerns for foreign acquisitions will inevitably be curbed by this year's big 30% sell-off in the Japanese stock market. Also, Saks was already owned by a British concern, B.A.T Industries PLC.

And takeover experts said U.S. corporations that have big cash hoards or don't already have big debt loads will continue to be able to bid for other U.S. companies, as Georgia-Pacific Corp. did successfully in winning its $3 billion battle for Great Northern Nekoosa earlier this year. This past week, Dayton-Hudson Corp. won Marshall Field for $1.04 billion.

One symbolic test of U.S. buyers' ability to complete acquisitions will come when a group of unions attempts to raise financing this summer for a $4.38 billion leveraged buy-out of United Airlines parent UAL Corp. With UAL stock trading far below the $201-a-share price tag, traders aren't betting on the group's success.

Although foreign acquisitions sometimes ignite political outcries in the U.S., there isn't any consensus among economists or others on Wall Street that the purchases harm the U.S. economy or damage the national interest. "Capital comes into the U.S. and the jobs stay here," said one takeover lawyer.

But at the moment, the same lawyer noted, "what you're seeing now is an almost exclusively foreign market."
