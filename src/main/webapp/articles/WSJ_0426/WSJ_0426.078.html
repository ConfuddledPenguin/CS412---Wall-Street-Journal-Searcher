Politics & Policy: Bush Offers Steps To Expand Trade With Nicaragua

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 04/26/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush, in an effort to bolster the new government of Violeta Chamorro, made Nicaragua eligible again for Export-Import Bank financing and restored its full authority to export sugar to the U.S. But the steps will be of only minimal help to Nicaragua's new president, who was inaugurated yesterday to succeed Sandinista leader Daniel Ortega. The biggest and most useful chunk of likely U.S. assistance, a $300 million dose of foreign aid Mr. Bush requested last month, remains bogged down in wrangling between the White House and Congress.

Mr. Bush tried to offer at least a token of direct aid to Nicaragua yesterday by authorizing up to $2.5 million in emergency medical assistance. The U.S. moves were timed to coincide with Mrs. Chamorro's inauguration, at which Vice President Dan Quayle represented the Bush administration. But White House officials couldn't predict when the larger aid package might be sent.

To make Nicaragua eligible for Export-Import Bank credits and loan guarantees, the president signed a proclamation that Nicaragua isn't ruled any longer by a Marxist-Leninist government. The proclamation removes Nicaragua from a list of nations barred from financing under a law blocking such aid to countries with Marxist-Leninist governments.

White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said, however, that any financing for exports to Nicaragua still is contingent on a ruling by the Export-Import Bank that the Central American nation is creditworthy.

Mr. Bush's decision to restore Nicaragua's full sugar import quota reverses decisions the Reagan administration took in its efforts to turn up economic pressure on Nicaragua's former Sandinista rulers. Mr. Reagan signed an order in 1983 reducing Nicaragua's quota of sugar exports to the U.S. to just 6,000 tons. Then, in 1985, Mr. Reagan imposed an embargo on virtually all trade with Nicaragua.

Mr. Bush ended the broad trade embargo last month, but he didn't move to restore Nicaragua's full sugar import quota. The quota represents Nicaragua's share of the total amount of sugar imports the U.S. allows. Mr. Bush's move yesterday means that Nicaragua now will be able to export about 54,000 tons of sugar to the U.S., officials said.
