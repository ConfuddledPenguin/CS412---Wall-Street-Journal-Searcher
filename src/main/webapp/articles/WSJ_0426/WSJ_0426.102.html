Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Media Sector in Recession; Few Admit It

  • Author: Joanne Lipman
  • Date: 04/26/90

In media circles, everyone's thinking about it, but almost no one wants to say it: The business is in a recession. Newspaper advertising is in the deepest slump in recent memory. Magazine advertising is faring no better. The outlook for broadcast advertising is unclear. Yet no one dares use the dreaded "R" word.

"It's like the old saying: If it walks like a duck, talks like a duck and looks like a duck, maybe we should start calling it a duck," says Spencer Plavoukos, chairman and chief executive officer of the New York office of Interpublic Group's Lintas ad agency. "We may not have reached the criteria for what an official recession is, but everyone has hit the bunkers. I don't see a major trending away from where we ended up in 1989. I don't see {a turnaround} in 1990."

According to the indicators filtering out from Washington, the economy as a whole isn't in a recession -- usually considered two consecutive quarters of declining inflation -- adjusted gross national product. But media advertising, which closely mirrors the economy, suggests that the indicators aren't telling the full story. "The question is, have we, despite what all the indicators say, already slipped into a recession?" asks John Morton, a media analyst with Lynch, Jones & Ryan. "Certainly the media business is having one."

Clearly, the outlook for most major media is bleak. Newspaper publishers, after a dismal 1989, have been caught by surprise by an even worse 1990 so far. In March, many were alarmed by a falloff in advertising even steeper than the previous two months. Publishers now are expecting at best flat ad revenue for 1990, and perhaps a small uptick in 1991 -- although some are skeptical even of that.

At the just-concluded meeting of the American Newspaper Publishers Association in Los Angeles, the mood was downbeat. Newspaper advertising has been battered primarily by a drop-off in retail advertising, which typically accounts for half of all newspaper ads. Many publishers at the meeting questioned whether retail advertising will ever return to former levels, to the heady days before the bankruptcy-law filings of L.J. Hooker Corp., Campeau Corp.'s U.S. unit and other retailers.

Classified advertising, meanwhile, accounting for about 40% of most newspaper advertising, has shown virtually no growth in most major markets. That weakness is particularly troubling to publishers, because classified ads are the most profitable type of newspaper advertising. "We are in a recession," says Valerie Salembier, president of the New York Post. "But no one wants to use that word."

The situation is similar in the magazine business, where first-quarter ad pages dropped an average 3.5%, according to the Magazine Publishers of America. Individual declines were staggering, ranging from a 24.3% drop at Family Media's Savvy Woman to an 18.4% plunge at Time Warner's People. Ad revenue at Time Warner's magazine group, which also includes Sports Illustrated, Time and Fortune magazines, was off about 6% to 7% in the first quarter, the company says. Magazines' major problem: weak automotive and tobacco advertising.

Reginald K. Brack, who heads up Time Warner's magazine company, concedes that the first quarter was "terrible," and says that while the second and third quarters look "a little stronger, I'm still not looking at a big turn upward in ad spending." But he contends that the word recession "is probably too strong," since previous quarters had shown increases. "If you went two or three quarters like the first quarter, that would be a recession," he says.

Still, Mr. Brack says, "it's going to be a real tough year no matter where you are in advertising, whether it's an ad agency or newspapers or TV."

The broadcast business currently is something of a wild card. Robert Coen, director of forecasting for Interpublic's McCann-Erickson ad agency, believes that network TV, and in fact all mass media, have "been in a recession since 1985." In inflation-adjusted dollars, network TV ad revenue actually declined by 5% to 6% in 1989, he says. The networks have been battered by an explosion of other options, like cable TV and videocassettes, as well as by a decline in audience levels.

The big question now is what will happen to demand and prices in the 1990 fall season; the "upfront" buying season for fall begins around June. Advertising executives are reluctant to speculate about where network advertising will head in a year already notable for its miserable ad environment.

The networks "are in a critical situation. It all depends on how the business climate and economy is by midyear when the upfront is negotiated," says Mr. Coen. "I think they'll probably just about hold their head above water."

Mr. Coen believes the problem with the networks, along with other mass media, is that in previous years, they responded to strong demand by overinflating their prices, and now are suffering through an inevitable correction. So why won't anyone come out and call a spade a spade, if this is indeed a recession? Says Mr. Coen, "I guess it's kindness."
