P&G Net Rises 30% While Sales Increase by 13%

  • Author: Alecia Swasy
  • Date: 04/26/90

Procter & Gamble Co. reported fiscal third-quarter net income soared a higher-than-expected 30% as sales rose 13%. For the third quarter, ended March 31, net income climbed to $404 million, or $1.14 a share, from $310 million, or 92 cents a share, a year earlier. The company had more shares outstanding this year because of an acquisition, and the year-earlier per-share figure is adjusted for a 2-for-1 stock split last November.

Sales rose to $6.12 billion from $5.43 billion.

Analysts had predicted a strong quarter, but the results beat even the rosiest forecasts. "It's like a broken record," said Joseph Kozloff, analyst with Smith Barney in New York, pinning the higher results on a variety of P&G's operations, especially overseas.

Based on the higher earnings report, some analysts boosted their forecasts for P&G's fiscal 1990 and 1991 results. Mr. Kozloff, for instance, raised his 1990 estimate to $4.50 a share, up from his earlier estimate of $4.40. For 1991, he expects P&G to earn $5.20 a share, up from his earlier $5.10 forecast.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, P&G shares rose $1.125, to $69.50.

One factor that helped the Cincinnati-based maker of consumer products was a lower tax rate of 32.1%, compared with 38.5% the year before.

More important, though, was P&G's continued growth in Japan and other international markets, analysts said. In the U.S., P&G's market shares in laundry and cleaning products improved.

Cost-cutting measures continue to be a priority at the company. For instance, P&G is expected to announce some plant closings, because in the second quarter the company took a one-time charge of $174 million to cover the consolidation.

For the fiscal first nine months, P&G posted net income of $1.32 billion, or $3.71 a share, up 27% from $1.04 billion, or an adjusted $3.06 a share. Sales rose 12% to $17.87 billion from $15.97 billion.

The year-earlier figures included a gain from P&G's switch to a hyperinflationary accounting system for certain operations in Latin America. P&G didn't quantify the gain at the time, but analysts say it was worth about seven cents to nine cents a share.
