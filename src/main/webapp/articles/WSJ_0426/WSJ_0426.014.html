SEC Chief Warns House On Penny-Stock Measure

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/26/90

WASHINGTON -- Securities and Exchange Commission Chairman Richard Breeden said several provisions in a House bill aimed at curbing penny-stock fraud "would have costly and undesirable side effects." Speaking before the House finance subcommittee, Mr. Breeden said the SEC doesn't support a provision that would ban certain "blind pool" stock offerings. Blind pools are offerings in which the business plan of the issuer is to seek mergers or acquisitions in a specific line of business. They have long been used by issuers in venture capital, real estate and oil- and gas-exploration programs.

"Thus, substantial costs and burdens could be imposed on this kind of financing technique for which disclosure regulation has been adequate in the past, and which has been an important source of producing capital," the SEC chairman said.

Mr. Breeden also raised questions about a provision in the bill that would require all penny stocks to be listed on an automated quotation system. The measure might impose costs that would discourage market-making in inactively traded stocks of reputable companies, he said.

Despite Mr. Breeden's opposition, the bill has strong bipartisan support in the subcommittee. The bill was introduced this month by Rep. Edward Markey (D, Mass.), chairman of the subcommittee.
