Who's News: IBM Asks Schwartz To Take New Post As Trouble-Shooter

  • Author: Paul B. Carroll
  • Date: 04/26/90

International Business Machines Corp. appointed a senior executive to a sort of trouble-shooter position in which he'll try to cut through IBM's formidable bureaucracy. The computer giant didn't announce the move, but a spokesman in Armonk, N.Y., confirmed that Stephen B. Schwartz, a vice president, will be put in charge of a companywide attempt to cut across organizational lines to improve customer satisfaction and reduce product defects.

Mr. Schwartz has a reputation as an effective no-nonsense manager. The 55-year-old Mr. Schwartz is also one of the 15 or so most senior executives at IBM and has worked closely over the years with Chairman John F. Akers, to whom he will report directly. While IBM has had lower-level executives take cracks over the years at cutting bureaucracy, Mr. Schwartz's appointment seems to indicate a plan to escalate that effort.

"I look upon Schwartz as a sort of shadow government," said Sam Albert, a former IBM executive who is now a consultant in Scarsdale, N.Y. "You have to have somebody who can cut through things, who knows fact from fiction. I think he's an ideal candidate for that."

Most recently, Mr. Schwartz ran IBM's AS/400 minicomputer line, which was perhaps the brightest spot in IBM's product lineup last year. He also has extensive marketing experience, having moved up that side of IBM's operations, and spent a few years overseas, in Japan.

IBM has already won credit from many customers for improving communications with them. Yet many customers and companies that work with IBM still complain that it's hard to get a decision made quickly. In addition, IBM still can be much slower on the uptake than competitors when it comes to new technologies -- such as those that let others dominate the laptop computer market. IBM also has had some troubling product defects lately, such as those that limited mainframe shipments early last year and that delayed by months the introduction of IBM's high-end disk drives in the 1989 second half.

Succeeding Mr. Schwartz as general manager of the AS/400 business is Robert J. LaBant, 45, a vice president who had been responsible for IBM's direct sales force in the U.S.

Mr. LaBant will face the difficult job of maintaining the line's momentum. The 1 1/2-year-old system has already collected most of the easy sales, from customers who ran out of power in a predecessor line, the System 38. Now, IBM must win more difficult business, from users of the other predecessor, the System 36. Many System 36 users have found it painfully hard to move their existing software to the AS/400.

IBM also named to its corporate management board Ned Lautenbach, which may mark the first time that two brothers have been on the 20-or-so-member board at the same time. Mr. Lautenbach, who runs IBM's application solutions division, is the younger brother of Terry Lautenbach, who is on the board because he is responsible for all of IBM's U.S. operations.
