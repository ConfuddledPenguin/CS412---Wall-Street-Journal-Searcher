Politics & Policy: Bill Seeks to End Count of RTC Fund Under Deficit Law

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 04/26/90

WASHINGTON -- A little-noticed provision in a fiscal 1990 spending bill before Congress serves to paper over the short-term deficit impact of billions of dollars in working capital needed by the Resolution Trust Corp. Budget analysts have estimated that as much as $28 billion in capital could be needed by the RTC in the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1, and the Congressional Budget Office has counted this borrowing as part of its projections for the fiscal 1991 deficit under the Gramm-Rudman law.

The proposed amendment, which was approved by the Senate Appropriations Committee without public discussion Tuesday night, would sidestep the problem by changing Gramm-Rudman's score-keeping conventions to exclude the ebb and flow of RTC working capital from calculations of the budget shortage. The RTC, created by last year's big thrift-industry law, needs working capital to manage the assets of failed savings and loans before they are sold or merged.

Politically, the change would help the White House and Congress past an election-year headache. But the change is defended too as a serious attempt to avoid what could be a scoring nightmare beyond fiscal 1991, when revenues will presumably begin returning to the government as assets are sold and the White House Office of Management and Budget would have immense leverage in saying how much the deficit could be reduced.

As attached to the spending bill, the measure is subject to parliamentary challenge on the floor, but the Bush administration and the Congressional Budget Office apparently have joined with the bipartisan Senate budget leadership in backing the move.

Within the current fiscal year, the underlying bill provides for nearly $3.3 billion in new spending, which would be largely offset by $2.86 billion in reductions or transfers from various defense accounts.

The administration's top priority has been securing increased aid for Central America, but as the bill has moved through Congress, the measure has grown to where nearly three-quarters of the new spending would go to a variety of domestic accounts. Among these is $185 million for a new fingerprint-identification facility for the Federal Bureau of Investigation, expected now to be located in West Virginia, the home state of Appropriations Committee Chairman Robert Byrd.
