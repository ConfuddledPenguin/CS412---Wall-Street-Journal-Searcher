Tokyo Exchange Pushes to Halt U.S. Sales of Nikkei Warrants

  • Author: Craig S. Smith and Kevin L. Dunn
  • Date: 04/26/90

NEW YORK -- The Tokyo Stock Exchange is applying pressure to halt, at least for now, the further sale of warrants allowing investors to bet on the direction of the Japanese stock market. Reacting to these concerns from the Tokyo exchange, U.S. underwriters and the American Stock Exchange, where previously issued warrants are being traded, are expected to voluntarily refrain from offering more of these instruments to the public. The warrants have been blamed for adding to the volatility in the Japanese stock markets.

Any pullback of this kind requested by Japanese financial officials could exacerbate the debate in the U.S. and Europe about how much Japan is willing to let market forces dictate price movements in the Tokyo securities market.

An exchange official in Tokyo said the exchange has had discussions about the warrants with the Amex. The official, who asked not to be identified, said that the decision to withhold future issues includes warrants that profit from either a rise or fall in the Nikkei index of 225 leading stocks that trade in Tokyo.

While the Tokyo exchange hasn't any regulatory authority over securities issued in the U.S., it maintains an information-sharing agreement with the Amex, through which matters relating to the two markets are discussed.

Ivers Riley, Amex senior executive vice president, says his exchange "would proceed cautiously" when and if another application for a Nikkei warrant comes before the exchange. "We value our relationship with the Japanese markets and want to maintain a liaison with them, and would want to be sensitive to their marketplace."

Currently, Goldman Sachs & Co. is planning to underwrite an issue of 7.5 million Nikkei put warrants -- which rise in value with a fall in the Nikkei index -- to be sold by Norway's export-finance agency.

Goldman Sachs said it couldn't comment because the offering was in registration, but Amex officials said there wasn't any reason to think the issue wouldn't come to market. However, it might be the last for a while, according to people in the U.S. underwriting community.

The New York Stock Exchange and the Pacific Stock Exchange have applied to the Securities and Exchange Commission to trade Nikkei warrants. A spokeswoman for the New York exchange said she was unable to determine late yesterday the status of the application. The Pacific exchange said it is awaiting SEC action.

The Tokyo exchange has also asked individual U.S. investment banks in the U.S. to refrain from underwriting more warrant issues for the time being.

"At this point in time we are complying with the TSE request not to issue any more Nikkei warrants," said a spokesman at Salomon Inc., which has issued two Nikkei put-warrant issues and one call warrant issue. Call warrants increase in value with a rise in the Nikkei stock index. The Salomon spokesman said the subject "will be revisited" with the Japanese soon.

Like an option, a warrant gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to sell or purchase a security, currency or, in this case, the cash equivalent of the Nikkei index at a specific price on or before a specific date.

There is a heated debate in financial and academic circles over the effect of the Nikkei warrants on the prices of securities traded on the Tokyo stock market. The first warrants to be issued were puts, which rose sharply in value as the Japanese stock market tumbled more than 25% this past winter. It has since recovered a bit.

There is a feeling among some U.S. securities firms that the Nikkei warrants are being fingered as a scapegoat for the volatility in Japan. One U.S. investment banker said that stocks are sold in the Tokyo market to hedge Nikkei put warrants when they are first created, but that Japanese stocks are bought as a hedge when the warrants are exercised.

"Lately, there have been a lot more warrants exercised than have been created, so the net effect of the warrants over the last couple of months has been to stabilize a weak market," the investment banker contended.

The Tokyo exchange official said, "We are in the middle of investigating whether or not {Nikkei warrants} have had an influence" on Tokyo stock prices. Japan's Ministry of Finance is also studying the matter.

While only about a half-dozen Nikkei warrant issues have been sold to American investors, as many as 25 issues have been sold during the past two years in Europe, where they trade on an over-the-counter basis.

Underwriters in London say the question of a "Nikkei warrant moratorium" hasn't been raised there because there is not much demand at present for the warrants in the Euromarket. But an official at one of the leading securities houses in Europe said each warrant issue is discussed with the Japanese Ministry of Finance before being brought to market.

"If it's felt that such an issue wouldn't be looked favorably upon by the Japanese parties concerned, we would hold back," the official said.

In addition, many Nikkei put warrants have been sold privately to institutional investors in the U.S. and abroad.
