Letters to the Editor: Government Neglect Hampers AIDS Care

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/26/90

Regarding your March 12 page-one article "Noble Experiment: Volunteers' Distress Cripples Huge Effort to Provide AIDS Care," AIDS volunteerism can certainly be distressing. Some volunteers do burn out and drop out. However, problems of overextension can be addressed with appropriate training, close supervision, continuing support, sabbaticals, job rotation and the setting of limits. The real problem with AIDS care in this country is not "volunteer distress" or how to manage it, but the absence of government support. As remarkable as the volunteer AIDS effort has been all around the country, volunteerism can never take the place of a meaningful government commitment to addressing the epidemic, nor was it ever intended to. Volunteers cannot build hospitals, do research or provide medical care. Nor can they provide the political leadership that we need at the highest levels of government.

Congress can demonstrate its commitment by expeditiously passing the Americans With Disabilities Act, which will provide equal treatment in employment and public accommodations to all people with disabilities; and the Comprehensive AIDS Resources Emergency Act of 1990, which will provide emergency relief funds to the 12 urban areas hardest hit by the AIDS epidemic.

Jeffrey Braff

Executive Director

Gay Men's Health Crisis

New York

At Kairos House (meaning " this is the moment of life"), a resource center for the "caregivers" of AIDS patients, we provide support groups, workshops, counseling, resource materials and a peaceful haven away from the workplace. We encourage caregivers to deal with their grief and stress issues, and we emphasize healthy caring for oneself to prevent the empty feelings and stress that precede and accompany burnout. We also try to learn the "life lessons" those we are caring for are offering us.

John McGrann

Executive Director

Kairos House

San Francisco
