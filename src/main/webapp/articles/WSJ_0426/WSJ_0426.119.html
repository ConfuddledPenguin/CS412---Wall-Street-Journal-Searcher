Thai Airline Gives Orders to United Technologies, GE

  • Author: Charles W. Stevens
  • Date: 04/26/90

General Electric Co. and United Technologies Corp. said they received major aircraft engine orders from Thai Airways International. GE said its Aircraft Engines division received a new order from the state-owned airline valued at $300 million.

At the same time, Thai Airways said it had placed an order with United Technologies' Pratt & Whitney division, which could total $240 million if options for $80 million in extra engines are executed.

The order to Pratt & Whitney is significant because it increases the company's penetration at an airline that until last July bought GE engines exclusively for 14 years. The order comes in the wake of a $500,000 gift by United Technologies to a charity run by the Thai royal family.

In East Hartford, Conn., a Pratt & Whitney spokesman reiterated the company's position that the donation to a typhoon-relief fund, which was reported last December, wasn't conditioned on receiving new engine orders.

It's natural for the company to engage in philanthropy "where we want to establish business relationships and become involved," the spokesman said, adding: "That money was used to help people {and} it may have been a factor or may not have" in the engine order decision. "If that helped us in our business that's fine."

United Technologies is based in Hartford, Conn., and is also involved in building systems and automotive products. GE, based in Fairfield, Conn., has interests in broadcasting, financial services, and industrial products.

The competition between the two companies to supply Thai Airways has intensified during the past year. Last summer, officials at the airline said the Thai government asked the carrier to order Pratt & Whitney engines instead of some GE equipment on order because the government believed Pratt & Whitney had closer ties to the Bush administration and could improve Thailand's relations with the U.S. Meanwhile, both engine makers were said to be offering significant concessions to the carrier in return for business.

These latest orders have importance beyond the issue of which company gets to call Thai Airways its customer. Thailand is just one prize in a larger sweepstakes for aerospace concerns as they fight for pieces of Asian business, the world's fastest-growing commercial aircraft market.

According to industry estimates, air passenger traffic to, from and within Asia will grow an average 9% annually during the next 10 years, almost twice the projected growth for the U.S.

The significance of the Thai Airways business was underscored yesterday as John F. Welch Jr., GE chairman, announced at the company's annual meeting in Erie, Pa., the latest award to GE. The order covers the company's highest thrust engines, the CF6-80C2 and 80E1, to power five Boeing Co. 747-400s and four A330s built by Airbus Industrie, the European aircraft consortium. Both planes are widebody aircraft.

The Thai airline will take initial delivery of the 747-400s in late 1991; the A330s are scheduled for delivery in 1993.

The order to Pratt & Whitney is for PW4000 engines to power Airbus A300s, A310s and A330s.

The order includes the conversion of existing options for five PW4000-powered A300-600R twinjets into firm orders, and two PW4000s to power a new Airbus A310 for the Royal Thai Air Force. Deliveries will begin in 1991. The rest of the order covers engines for four A330s and spares.
