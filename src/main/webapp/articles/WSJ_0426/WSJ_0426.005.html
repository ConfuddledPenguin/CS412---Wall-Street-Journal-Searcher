Who's News: Merrill's Private Placement Group Hit by Departures

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 04/26/90

NEW YORK -- Merrill Lynch & Co.'s private placement group has been racked by key departures, just as the $170 billion market for privately placed securities is set to explode. Jerome P. Greene, who headed Merrill Lynch's private placement group for nine years, and Benjamin J. Sullivan Jr., a director, were hired by Benedetto, Gartland & Greene Inc., a small investment banking boutique formed by two former Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. executives. Philip B. Pool Jr., a Merrill Lynch managing director, had informally agreed to move to the boutique, as well, but switched gears at the last moment and will remain at Merrill Lynch.

The moves, which follow two other departures from Merrill Lynch's 15-member private placement group in recent months, are a setback for the nation's biggest securities firm. Merrill Lynch said it won't name a successor to Mr. Greene. Just last week, the Securities and Exchange Commission ushered in a new era of selling bonds and stocks in the U.S. by approving a rule to make it easier to sell and trade privately placed securities.

Wall Street securities firms are jockeying for position to take advantage of the new rule. They see the expected bulge in the private market as a way to offset sagging investment banking business. Last year, Merrill Lynch ranked No. 7 in private placements, underwriting $9.3 billion in securities for a 5.6% market share, according to IDD Information Services.

"The way of the future is the smaller boutique-type firms," said Mr. Greene, who became a partner in Benedetto Gartland and was added to its masthead. "The larger firms are more like battleships and take a while to turn around," he said. "Smaller firms get focused almost immediately on specific opportunities or areas."

Mr. Greene, who ran the private placement group at Salomon Inc. before joining Merrill Lynch, said Benedetto Gartland plans to focus on specialty areas in the private market "that don't fit in with the mainstream on Wall Street." These include equity-related issues, such as limited partnership interests; special-purpose funds, including offshore investment funds; venture capital funds; leveraged buy-out funds, and blind pools.

The hirings are a coup for Benedetto Gartland, which was launched in 1988 by M. William Benedetto, a former executive vice president and manager of Dean Witter's investment banking division, and Arthur Gartland, a veteran of Dean Witter and Kidder, Peabody & Co. In its first year, the boutique helped complete deals valued at more than $1 billion; with the new hirings, the firm has five partners.

"What is going to drive the '90s is not how much capital you can throw at a problem, but how much brainpower," Mr. Benedetto said. "That's what we want to do."

Mr. Greene, 48 years old, and Mr. Benedetto have been friends for 20 years. They met while at Salomon Brothers in the 1970s. Mr. Sullivan, 36, joined Merrill Lynch in 1986 after a stint as vice president in Dean Witter's investment banking department.

Benedetto Gartland said in a statement it had already hired Mr. Pool, too. But Merrill Lynch yesterday managed to persuade Mr. Pool to stay on board, according to people familiar with the matter.

Merrill Lynch nevertheless has lost two other members of its private placement staff recently. Luke Bowes moved to Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. and Jay Applegate departed to Dean Witter. At the same time, Merrill Lynch has hired two former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. employees, Lawrence Alletto and David Weiss, for its private placement sales desk.

Merrill Lynch, for its part, said it sees financing opportunities in the burgeoning private placement market related to small leveraged buy-outs and private placement funds. "We're very committed to this area and do a very substantial business in it," a Merrill Lynch spokesman said.
