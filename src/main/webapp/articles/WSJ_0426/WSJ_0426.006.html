What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/26/90

CHAMORRO ASSUMED Nicaragua's presidency, ending Sandinista rule. After a decade of U.S. hostility toward the nation's leftist leaders, the 60-year-old publisher took the oath of office in Managua before about 20,000 people. In her inaugural speech, Chamorro, a conservative elected in February, declared a political amnesty and ended the military draft. She also said that Gen. Humberto Ortega, the ex-president's brother, would head the armed forces, but that she would be the defense minister. In Washington, Bush made Nicaragua eligible again for Export-Import Bank financing and restored its full authority to export sugar to the U.S.

Chamorro's decision to retain Gen. Ortega is expected to rile the anti-Sandinista Contra rebels, who have signed an accord to disarm by June 10.

---

The shuttle Discovery's astronauts sent the Hubble Space Telescope on a 15-year mission to explore the far reaches of the universe. After the crew overcame some balky gear, the telescope was placed into orbit and began circling the Earth at an altitude of 380 miles -- high above the atmosphere that has limited the view of ground-based devices.

---

A woman stabbed West Germany's leading opposition candidate, Oskar Lafontaine, during a campaign rally in Cologne. Officials said the 46-year-old politician, chosen by the Social Democratic Party to challenge Kohl in December's national elections, was in serious condition after being wounded in the neck. The assailant was arrested.

---

Lithuania is urgently seeking direct trade links with Soviet regions as a way of skirting the Kremlin's cutoff of energy supplies to the republic, which proclaimed its independence March 11. Gorbachev, meanwhile, traveled to a Ural Mountain industrial center in an effort to ease worker concern over the transition to a market economy.

Bush rejected criticism of his refusal to act against the Soviet Union for its crackdown on Lithuania, saying he has "strong support from the American people." The president's decision was denounced in Lithuania as a gesture of appeasement toward Moscow. A spokesman said the U.S. sympathized with Vilnius's "frustration."

---

Shiite Moslem leader Musawi urged pro-Iranian kidnappers to continue holding their Western hostages, saying the U.S. had sabotaged efforts to win the release of other captives. Musawi, believed to have played a role in Sunday's release of Polhill, cited the House's passage Tuesday of a resolution endorsing Jerusalem as Israel's capital.

---

Chinese Premier Li sought to minimize suggestions of an ideological rift between Beijing and Moscow, but made clear that the Communist powers remained divided over how to "build socialism." Li, on a visit to the Soviet Union, also said there won't be a repeat of last spring's protests in China.

---

India and Pakistan discussed separatist violence in Kashmir, and agreed that tensions should be reduced and confrontation avoided. The foreign ministers of the two nations, who met in New York, issued a communique urging continuing contacts.

---

A car bomb exploded near a truck carrying anti-terrorist police in the Colombian city of Medellin, killing at least six people. Responsibility for the attack wasn't immediately claimed, but cocaine traffickers have pledged to kill police officers in a campaign against Bogota's crackdown on drugs.

---

The House approved a bill giving federal regulators the power to revoke a bank's charter or withdraw its deposit insurance if it and its directors or officers are convicted of laundering money.

---

Israel's Peres acknowledged defeat after five weeks of attempting to form a government following the collapse of the coalition led by Prime Minister Shamir, which had refused to enter peace talks with Palestinians. The Labor Party leader said he would formally abandon his effort today.

---

Rival Christian forces battled in residential areas of eastern Beirut and in outlying towns, killing at least 15 people. It was considered the worst fighting in more than a month as the Lebanese Forces militia and Gen. Aoun's troops pressed the struggle for control of Lebanon's Christian enclave.

---

The Soviet legislature adopted a program to resettle another 200,000 people from areas irradiated in the Chernobyl nuclear power plant explosion. The move came on the eve of today's fourth anniversary of the accident. Official reports have said 31 people were killed, and more than 100,000 people have been evacuated from the area.

---

Died: Dexter Gordon, 67, tenor saxophonist who helped define the be-bop style of jazz and was nominated for an Oscar for his starring role in the 1986 film "Round Midnight," in Philadelphia, of kidney failure.
