Letters to the Editor: `Silent Rationing' Of Medical Care

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/26/90

In your March 27 article "Debate Grows Over Rationing Medical Care," the case of Tishna Rollo, an eight-year-old girl with kidney cancer, had nothing to do with rationing. It concerned limiting the use of a medical technology until it has been proven safe and effective for a given medical condition. The proposed treatment of kidney cancer with a bone marrow transfusion, a very expensive procedure often associated with harmful side effects, has no scientific basis as a treatment in this instance. Such inappropriate use of medical technology has been estimated to contribute as much as 50% of the increase in our nation's annual health-care costs.

Implicit rationing or "silent rationing" has been with us for many years as you indicate, but while rationing has been advocated as a means to control health-care costs, it has not been adopted as official national policy. As you point out, the fact that 30 million (actually, more than 35 million) Americans have no health insurance constitutes a form of rationing. In my view, the large number of our citizens who have little or no access to health care is a national disgrace. There are many steps we could take to make our system more efficient; one of these steps could be to reduce the inappropriate and wasteful use of expensive medical technologies.

Seymour Perry, M.D.

Director

Program on Technology & Health Care

Barbara Pilar, R.N.

Research Associate

School of Medicine

Georgetown University

Washington
