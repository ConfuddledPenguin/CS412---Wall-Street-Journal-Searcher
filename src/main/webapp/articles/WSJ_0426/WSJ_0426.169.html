EC Proposes Rules On Brokerage, Investment Firms

  • Author: Martin du Bois and Mark M. Nelson
  • Date: 04/26/90

BRUSSELS -- The European Community Commission unveiled a major proposal aimed at establishing a single market for brokerage and investment services. The proposal, which would set minimum capital-adequacy rules for securities firms to apply across the EC, is considered a major step in the EC's plan to create a single market by the end of 1992. In combination with other pending legislation, it will allow any investment-services firm established anywhere in the EC to do business throughout the trading bloc.

The plan still has to be approved by the member states and could face amendments and tough bargaining. But the proposal is likely to form the basis of a final agreement.

Early drafts of the proposal drew complaints from firms in the U.K., including U.S. firms established there, because the rules would force them to substantially boost capital. The revised plan seeks to answer this complaint by allowing more flexibilty in covering investment risk.

German banks, meanwhile, had protested that securities firms would have an unfair advantage because the banks would remain subject to more rigid -- and potentially more costly -- minimum solvency requirements the EC is imposing on banks. West German markets are dominated by so-called universal banks, which offer a full range of financial services from commercial banking to insurance and brokering.

The compromise proposal unveiled yesterday allows EC states to make an exception for universal banks, whose securities operations may be covered by the capital-adequacy rules rather than by the banking solvency rules. It also allows the banks more flexibility in raising capital to cover securities operations.
