Ruling May Blunt New Regulation On S&L Lending

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 04/26/90

WASHINGTON -- A federal judge in Tucson, Ariz., issued a ruling that could blunt a savings-and-loan regulation that limits how much a thrift may lend to any one borrower. U.S. district Judge William Browning ruled that Mississippi Savings Bank, Batesville, Miss., must return $749,775 to a borrower that had put up that amount as security in a line-of-credit contract. The $1.7 million line of credit exceeds the amount Mississippi Savings may lend to a single borrower under the new regulation, which stems from last year's S&L restructuring law.

Judge Browning issued a preliminary injunction against Mississippi Savings, ordering the return of funds to the borrower, All American Finance Co., Tucson.

The judge, however, stopped short of ordering Mississippi Savings to fund the full $1.7 million contract. But he said that on the strength of its contract, All American is likely to prevail on the merits in a trial. The judge further blocked federal regulators from taking action against the thrift's officers for seeking to honor the lending agreement.

Although Judge Browning didn't deal directly with the so-called loan-to-one-borrower regulation, this was the second time a federal judge has undercut strict new provisions of the S&L bailout law. An earlier ruling held up the industry restructuring when it was determined that the director of the Office of Thrift Supervision wasn't constitutionally appointed. That ruling was rendered moot when the Senate confirmed a new OTS director early this month.

Yesterday's ruling could lead to further litigation, or even a change in the regulation, which was issued on an interim basis one month ago. The loan-to-one-borrower regulation limits the amount a thrift may lend to any borrower to 15% of its capital, or $500,000, whichever is greater. A final regulation is expected next month.

An attorney for the thrift office said he didn't believe this decision will affect the loan-to-one-borrower regulation, because it simply returns the security to the borrower that the thrift held under the contract. "The decision doesn't say whether the judge intends to abrogate the contract or not," the thrift office attorney said.

A spokesman for Mississippi Savings declined to comment immediately after the ruling.

The case illustrates how the regulation places many thrifts in a legal bind. When Mississippi Savings tried to fund its line-of-credit contract to All American, the Office of Thrift Supervision blocked it by issuing a cease-and-desist order. And the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. said that because Mississippi Savings was violating the new regulation, it could no longer raise funds through brokered deposits, a volatile source of funding that requires regulatory approval.

All American, which services and sells auto and consumer loans, felt it had a simple contract case. "We are an innocent third party being damaged by an action taken by the Office of Thrift Supervision," said Helen Davis Chaitman, an attorney for All American.

The judge agreed. "To unilaterally destroy otherwise solvent enterprises who deal with government-regulated businesses with impunity and without judicial scrutiny seriously undermines public confidence," his ruling said.
