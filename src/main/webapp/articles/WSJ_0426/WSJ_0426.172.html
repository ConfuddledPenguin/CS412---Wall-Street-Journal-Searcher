Investor Group Appears to Win Fight for Penrod --- Energy Service, R.D. Smith Gain 68.8% Total Stake; Rainwater Is Involved

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 04/26/90

An investment group including Fort Worth, Texas, investor Richard Rainwater apparently won the bidding war for control of Penrod Drilling Co., owner and operator of one of the world's largest and most modern fleets of offshore drilling rigs. Mr. Rainwater, former investment adviser to the Bass family of Texas, owns a 24% interest in Houston-based Energy Service Co., which said it had joined forces with R.D. Smith & Co. to buy an additional 17.3% of Penrod's senior debt and equity from two banking concerns for $82.7 million. The move boosts the groups' total stake to about 68.8% of Penrod's senior debt and equity.

Ensco, a drilling and energy services concern, and R.D. Smith, a New York brokerage and investment concern, also said they planned to cooperate in efforts to restructure the huge drilling company once owned by the Hunt brothers of Dallas.

Ensco finance manager Earl McNiel said the Ensco-R.D. Smith group planned to continue operating Penrod as an "ongoing franchise" and that any restructuring probably wouldn't involve substantial changes in Penrod management. "Penrod has a very fine operating management," Mr. McNiel said. "They're well-respected in the industry, and that's probably one of their strongest assets."

Mr. McNiel also said the Ensco-R.D. Smith group planned to continue negotiations toward the purchase of 31.2% of Penrod debt and equity still owned by a group of Hunt creditors led by Manufacturers Hanover Trust Co., a unit of Manufacturers Hanover Corp.

The debt and equity interests involved in the most recent transaction included a 6.9% stake formerly held by Bankers Trust Co., a unit of Bankers Trust New York Corp., which an Ensco-affiliated limited partnership purchased for $33.1 million. Other Ensco partnerships and an R.D. Smith-affiliated trust jointly acquired the 10.4% debt and equity interest of Citibank, a unit of Citicorp, for $49.6 million.

"It just seemed to make a lot of sense to join forces and pick up these two pieces that were available," Mr. McNiel said of the move.

Previously, Ensco and R.D. Smith had separately been among a large group of bidders eager to buy Penrod from the Hunt creditors. Among the other bidders were Houston-based Rowan Cos., an oil services concern said to have made a $325 million offer, and New York's Tisch family, reported to have made a $450 million bid through its Loews Corp.

According to persons close to the Penrod talks, most of those other parties have now dropped out of the bidding. "We sort of left it in the dust long ago," said a person involved with one of the bids. "These prices are much too high for us."

But with offshore rig counts up sharply in recent months, industry analysts seemed to agree that the additional investment was a good move for the Ensco-R.D. Smith group.

"This is one of the true quality fleets in the offshore industry," said Sam Albright, an analyst with Howard Weil. "If this cycle continues to gain momentum, the fact that they may have paid full price won't make any difference two years from now."

Penrod's 89-rig fleet includes 39 offshore rigs, many of them relatively new. During the 1980s, the Hunt brothers borrowed heavily to modernize and enlarge the Penrod fleet, amassing more than $760 million in debt. Coupled with other losses, those debts forced the family trusts owning Penrod to seek Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law protection.

The Hunts lost operating control of Penrod in a 1988 settlement with creditors and turned their remaining 50% interest in Penrod over to creditors last month.
