Harcourt Brace Considers Sale Of Some Assets --- Chief Says Firm Discussing Potential Transactions; Thomson May Play Role

  • Author: This article was prepared by Huiyong Yu in New York, Suzanne McGee in Toronto And Peter Waldman in Atlanta
  • Date: 04/26/90

Harcourt Brace Jovanovich Inc., still struggling under a mountain of debt incurred in a 1987 recapitalization, may sell some of its publishing operations to Thomson Corp. Eugene McCarthy, a Harcourt director, said yesterday that Harcourt's board met Monday to discuss the possible sale of some of its assets to Thomson, the Toronto-based publishing and travel concern.

"Thomson hasn't asked to buy the whole company," Mr. McCarthy said. "{It} has only been offered part of the company."

Mr. McCarthy, the former U.S. senator, said the two companies have been in talks for the past month, although he said he didn't know who initiated the discussions. Harcourt late yesterday evening confirmed that it has "had discussion with certain parties with respect to possible transactions." Peter Jovanovich, Harcourt's president and chief executive officer, said the possible transactions involve, among other things, offers for some or all of the publishing and insurance company's existing public debt at a discount from its aggregate book value, as well as the acquisition of certain assets of Harcourt by a third party.

But Mr. Jovanovich declined to identify any third party or what assets might be sold. He said that the discussions have been preliminary and that no agreements have been reached. He added that there is no assurance that negotiations will progress or that a transaction will be completed.

He also said in a prepared statement that Orlando, Fla.-based Harcourt isn't for sale. Meanwhile, executives at Thomson couldn't be reached for comment.

For Harcourt, the sale of one of its publishing units would mark another step in the unraveling of the company that began in 1987. That was the year William Jovanovich, Harcourt's chairman, engineered a $2.9 billion recapitalization to evade a takeover by British publishing mogul Robert Maxwell, plunging Harcourt into more than $2 billion of debt. The interest costs proved so onerous that last summer -- despite previous denials that it would need to shed assets -- the company sold its prized Sea World theme parks for a $1.1 billion, a price below what analysts had thought the parks were worth.

In the past several months, as Harcourt's stock has languished, many publishing-industry executives and analysts have expected the company to sell more assets to help pay down debt. But the company's apparent talks with Thomson baffled some observers, who had identified Harcourt's insurance units as the most likely candidates for divestiture.

"They have no business being in insurance," said one publishing executive close to the company, "because nobody on the board knows {anything} about it."

Charles Elbaum, president of Publishing Economics Inc., a consulting firm in New York, said Thomson is probably after one or both of Harcourt's medical and scientific publishing companies, Academic Press and W.B. Saunders. Both units garner about $140 million in revenue per year and are Harcourt's "crown jewels," Mr. Elbaum said, earning "very significant" pre-tax profit margins.

Another possible target is Harcourt's legal publishing unit, Harcourt Brace Jovanovich Legal & Professional Publishers Inc. While the unit is small, Thomson has a demonstrated interest in acquiring similar specialty publications. Last May it paid $810 million to buy Lawyers Co-operative Publishing Co. in Rochester, N.Y., making Thomson one of the top three legal publishers in the U.S.

Harcourt's other major publishing arm, its elementary and high school textbook division, has done poorly in the past 18 months after failing to meet changing demands by educators. The unit became saddled with so many unwanted books that Harcourt had to take an $88 million writeoff last year to cover obsolete inventory.

The apparent interest by Thomson in Harcourt's assets also puzzled analysts, since Thomson has repeatedly said it wants time to digest its 1989 acquisitions. In its annual report, the company said that only outstanding opportunities will be pursued.

The Canadian concern would be able to finance such a transaction with considerable ease, however. It is on record as saying it doesn't want to move beyond a debt-to-equity ratio of 1-to-1, which would still leave the company with resources of about $1 billion to draw upon if acquisition opportunities presented themselves.

"I don't think Thomson would be interested in . . . all of Harcourt," said Ella Brown, an analyst with Richardson Greenshields in Toronto. "But they're likely to take a serious look at some of the parts."

In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, Harcourt closed at $4.125, up 12.5 cents.

Separately, a unit of Thomson, American Banker-Bond Buyer, said it acquired the assets of Rand McNally & Co.'s Financial Publishing division.

Terms weren't disclosed.

American Banker-Bond Buyer is a New York information company with specialty-publishing activities in banking, securities and financial information services. Among its publications are the national daily newspapers American Banker and the Bond Buyer.

With Rand McNally's Financial Publishing division, the Thomson unit is getting a number of national and international banking directories. Also, the division, with about 75 employees, administers the routing numbers by which checks are sorted and cleared through banks around the country, under a contract with the American Bankers Association.

Rand McNally is a closely held publisher based in Skokie, Ill.
