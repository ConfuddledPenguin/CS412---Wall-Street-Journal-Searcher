International: Svenska Cellulosa Abandons Pursuit Of West Germany's Feldmuehle Nobel

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 04/26/90

STOCKHOLM -- Svenska Cellulosa AB called off its pursuit of West Germany's Feldmuehle Nobel AG -- but another big Swedish pulp and paper maker, Stora Kopparbergs Bergslags AB, quickly took up the chase. Separately, Veba AG said it's in negotiations with Stora and other parties to break the deadlock over control of Feldmuehle, and hinted that it may sell its 50.01% stake if it can't consolidate its control at a price it considers reasonable. Veba is "open to . . . solutions," a company spokesman said, "as long as they are in the interest of {Feldmuehle}."

SCA yesterday said it sold its 5% Feldmuehle stake, acquired last month, for 500 million Swedish kronor ($81.5 million) to 600 million kronor. The Swedish forestry giant bought the stake to increase its bargaining power with the West German paper, chemicals and engineering group, and claimed its proposals for cooperation -- and perhaps even a merger or acquisition -- had the support of shareholders owning another 20% of Feldmuehle.

But the Swedish concern said yesterday that its proposals were rebuffed.

Stora, Sweden's biggest pulp and paper group, disclosed a few hours later that it is in talks with Feldmuehle. The company declined further comment, but it and Feldmuehle have a track record of cooperation, and currently operate joint newsprint and pulp-making ventures.

Veba's comments indicate the West German chemicals and energy concern may be willing to abandon its quest for Feldmuehle, ending a two-year board-room drama that marked West Germany's first big hostile takeover bid.

Despite its majority stake, Veba hasn't been able to gain control of Feldmuehle because of that company's 5% voting restriction on shareholders, regardless of the size of their stake. This has led to a deadlock over control between Veba and Feldmuehle's second-biggest shareholder, an investment group led by U.S. brokerage firm Merrill Lynch & Co., which is thought to hold more than a fifth of Feldmuehle's shares.

The Merrill Lynch group repeatedly has offered to sell its shares to Veba for around 600 West German marks apiece ($353.22), a price Veba has rebuffed as too expensive. Veba acquired its Feldmuehle shares for around 400 marks apiece.

If Veba does abandon its quest, it could open the way for Stora, which, like SCA, is scrambling to expand in the European Community ahead of the EC's 1992 single-market plan. Both companies have snapped up European acquisitions to bolster market shares and strengthen distribution networks.
