Credit Ratings: S&P Puts Sotheby's On Its CreditWatch On Spending Plan

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 04/26/90

NEW YORK -- Standard & Poor's Corp. placed the commercial paper of Sotheby's Inc. on its CreditWatch with negative implications, after the giant auction house announced plans for a joint venture to purchase the Pierre Matisse Gallery's extensive art inventory for $142.8 million. Sotheby's is a unit of Sotheby's Holdings Inc., New York. The unit's commercial paper carries a top single-A-1 rating from S&P and is guaranteed by Sotheby's Holdings. Moody's Investors Service Inc., the other major rating agency, hasn't rated the company's commercial paper.

Sotheby's didn't return phone calls seeking comment on the S&P move.

Carla Kennedy Newman, an S&P analyst, said that Sotheby's is taking on "a new risk, inventory risk, that wasn't there before" by purchasing the gallery inventory. "The company hasn't played as a principal in the art market before, except in very limited ways, but it's doing it in a big way this time," she said.

With the purchase, Ms. Newman said, Sotheby's is essentially "working as a merchant banker, a broker," in the art market.

Sotheby's said it had arranged to purchase the Matisse Gallery's 2,300-painting inventory, in a joint venture with Acquavella Contemporary Art Inc., owned by New York art dealer William Acquavella. Mr. Acquavella will serve as the venture's managing director, with profits from the sale of paintings to be split equally between himself and Sotheby's.

The auction house will raise the purchase price for the inventory through the commercial paper sale, then lend the money to the joint venture, which will pay an undisclosed rate of interest.

Mr. Acquavella said that "the financing could have been obtained by other sources" but that he was seeking "a partner who could help me market this is in best fashion possible." Mr. Acquavella will decide which works will be sold privately or at auction.

The works owned by the New York-based Matisse Gallery include works by Joan Miro, Jean Dubuffet and Marc Chagall, almost all acquired directly from the artists themselves. Several of the works have been off the market and in warehouses for years, Mr. Acquavella said. Pierre Matisse, who died in August 1989, was the son of painter Henri Matisse and represented many of those artists.

"Wall Street just picks on the negatives," Mr. Acquavella said of the S&P move. "Here's a deal where Sotheby's has something -- not just air and a franchise -- with tremendous upside potential."

Separately, Sotheby's held a meeting of Wall Street analysts who follow the company. The auction house's stock recently has fallen sharply in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. Yesterday it closed up 75 cents a share to $18.50.

Salomon Brothers analyst Harvey Katz said he views the venture with Mr. Acquavella as "a positive" for the company. "Sotheby's, because of its flexibility and liquidity, was able to step up to the plate and gain a substantial consignment on favorable terms," he said.
