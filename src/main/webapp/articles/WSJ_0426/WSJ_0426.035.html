Goodyear Profit Fell 78% in First Quarter; Higher Costs Cited

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/26/90

AKRON, Ohio -- Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co.'s first-quarter net income plunged 78%. The earnings drop stemmed from price competition, higher costs from increased advertising and compensation, and lower tire demand from auto manufacturers. That lower demand resulted in temporary Goodyear plant closings.

The Akron, Ohio-based tire maker announced earlier this month that it would report sharply reduced earnings; the decline was in line with analysts' projections. Net was $20.9 million, or 36 cents a share, compared with the year-ago $94.5 million, or $1.64 a share. Sales were $2.69 billion, up slightly from $2.64 billion a year earlier.

Harry W. Millis, an analyst with McDonald & Co., Cleveland, said he expected first-quarter net to be between 25 cents and 50 cents a share. "Profit was in line with what I expected," he said.

Goodyear said previously that its oil pipeline would decrease earnings by 50 cents a share, and the pipeline's losses weren't reflected in the 1989 first-quarter report. Profits were also squeezed by Brazilian government efforts to brake inflation in that country, efforts which hurt the company's large subsidiary there. Goodyear is also incurring start-up costs on a tire plant in Canada.
