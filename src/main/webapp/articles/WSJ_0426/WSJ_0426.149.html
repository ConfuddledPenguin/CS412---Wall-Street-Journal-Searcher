What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/26/90

NORTON AGREED to be acquired for $90 a share, or about $1.9 billion, by French glassmaker Saint-Gobain, a surprising rescue of the Massachusetts abrasives maker from hostile suitor BTR of Britain. BTR, which offered $75 a share, ended its proxy fight against Norton and said it wouldn't top Saint-Gobain's bid. Norton's stock surged $13.50, to $88.75.

More foreigners are buying up U.S. companies, in part because American buyers are having problems getting bank loans or junk-bond financing.

---

Bush won't designate Japan as an unfair trading partner, aides said, a shift from the tough U.S. stance of a year ago. Trade Representative Carla Hills said that "Japan has moved further this year than any other country" in lowering trade barriers.

---

B.A.T agreed to sell its Saks Fifth Avenue chain for about $1.5 billion to Investcorp, a low-profile partnership of Arab investors. The group is taking a big gamble on an industry that has bankrupted other foreign investors.

---

Environmentalists claimed a symbolic victory against Exxon at its annual meeting. Six shareholder proposals on the environment failed but won more votes than expected.

---

Safeway underwriters slashed the price of stock offering that is due to begin trading today. The supermarket chain went private in 1986 and is one of the crown jewels of Kohlberg Kravis.

---

Michael Milken may be vulnerable to the many shareholder suits filed against him, some attorneys say. But lawyers for Drexel's ex-junk bond chief, who pleaded guilty to six felony counts Tuesday, dismiss the idea.

---

The SEC is cracking down on program traders who have exploited a regulatory loophole for as long as four years. The move may curb index arbitrage, a type of program trading.

---

A consumer-protection bill that requires nutrition labeling on all packaged foods was approved by a Senate panel, which brushed aside objections from the food industry.

---

The Tokyo Stock Exchange is trying to halt the further sale of warrants that let investors bet on the market's direction. U.S. underwriters and the Amex are expected to comply.

---

Ralph Ingersoll closed the St. Louis Sun, the ambitious daily newspaper he launched in September.

Gannett Foundation said it may sell its 10% stake in the Gannett newspaper chain, citing an IRS rule.

---

Harcourt Brace may sell some of its publishing operations to Thomson to help pay off its massive debt from a 1987 recapitalization.

---

Procter & Gamble's profit soared a higher-than-expected 30% in the latest quarter, as sales rose 13%.

---

Control of Penrod Drilling has apparently been won by a group that includes the influential Texas investor Richard Rainwater.

---

Merrill Lynch's private placement group has been hit by key departures just as the market for privately placed securities is set to explode.

---

Britain's trade deficit worsened surprisingly in March, rattling London markets and stirring concern about Thatcher's inflation policy.
