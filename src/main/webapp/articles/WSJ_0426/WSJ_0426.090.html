Politics & Policy: Senate Committee Clears Measure On Food Labels

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 04/26/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Labor and Human Resources Committee brushed aside the objections of the food industry and approved a sweeping consumer-protection bill that requires nutrition labeling on all packaged foods. The measure also would require supermarkets and other retail outlets to post nutrition information regarding 30 fruits and vegetables as well as raw fish and shellfish. In addition, the bill, which passed in a 10-5 vote yesterday, would empower the Food and Drug Administration to regulate health claims and would authorize state agencies to enforce federal labeling requirements.

"Consumers are fed up with confusing, misleading food labels," said Sen. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio), chief sponsor of the legislation. But lobbyists for the nation's major food processors complained that the bill doesn't go far enough in preempting state food-labeling laws.

"This is a recipe for consumer confusion and distribution chaos," said C. Manly Molpus, president of the Grocery Manufacturers of America.

John Cady, president of the National Food Processors Association, said the bill is likely to provoke a floor fight in the Senate and, if it clears Congress, to become "veto bait" at the White House. Agriculture Secretary Clayton Yeutter and other administration officials advocate food labeling uniformity throughout the U.S. and federal preemption of state laws if necessary.

"This bill could truly result in a Tower of Babel, with food manufacturers required to label products differently depending on the state to which they were shipped," Mr. Cady said.

The food processors "want to stop the states from doing things on their own where federal law falls short," said Joel Johnson, Mr. Metzenbaum's legislative director. He said they fear a proliferation of state laws patterned after California's Proposition 65. Passed in November 1986, Proposition 65 requires processors to put health warnings on any food containing a chemical believed to pose a risk of cancer or birth defects.

While the Metzenbaum bill would regulate nutrition labeling and health claims on packaged foods, it wouldn't cover health warnings or preempt state health-warning laws. "It doesn't overrule Proposition 65," said Jeffrey Nedelman, a spokesman for the Grocery Manufacturers of America.

Food processors also complain that the bill's health-claims provision would be so stringent that claims could be made for "only a handful" of food products, Mr. Nedelman said.

The bill, among other things, would require disclosure of serving size, the number of servings per container, the number of calories per serving and the number of calories derived from total fat and saturated fat in a serving. It also would require processors to list the total amount of fat, saturated fat, unsaturated fat, cholesterol, sodium, sugar, fiber, protein and carbohydrates as well as the total amount of important vitamins and minerals.

In addition, the percentage of fruit or vegetable juice in beverages would have to be listed. Currently, nutrition information is required only on products for which health claims are made.

The bill wouldn't cover meat and poultry, which are regulated by the Agriculture Department. It would exempt restaurant foods, prepared foods sold in supermarkets and delicatessens, food sold in bulk, medical foods, infant formula, and food with insignificant nutrient content. Small packages of food would have a partial exemption, while food sold by specialty-food processors with less than $1 million in sales would be exempted.

A similar bill, written by Rep. Henry Waxman (D., Calif.), is pending in the House Energy and Commerce Committee.

Some critics see no need for the legislative initiatives. Last month, Health and Human Services Secretary Louis Sullivan pledged swift action on a comprehensive food-labeling package. New health-message regulations have been proposed by the FDA for public comment, and a broad nutrition labeling package is expected by early 1991.
