Letters to the Editor: Pacific Rim Trade Dominated by Japan

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/26/90

Brink Lindsey's March 7 editorial-page piece "Trade Crisis? What Trade Crisis?" argues that, contrary to the conventional wisdom, there is a world trade boom driven by the relaxation of import barriers and characterized by the expansion of trade between Pacific Rim countries. What is happening in the Pacific Rim is not, as Mr. Lindsey contends, an illustration of the internationalization of world trade but a clear indication that the area is evolving into a distinct trading bloc, with Japan accounting for about 80% of the entire East Asian gross national product. Much of the "import surge" in Japan is owed to exports from Japanese subsidiaries in East Asian countries.

While the once "Newly Industrializing Countries" of East Asia may be increasing their levels of imports from the U.S., the fact remains that Japan, South Korea and Taiwan now account for nearly two-thirds of our trade deficit.

Finally, Mr. Lindsey's piece is laced with all sorts of impressive figures and percentages backing up his claim that the world trading system is being liberalized. The emphasis, however, should not be on the numbers, but on the composition of trade between countries. There are those of us who believe that there is a difference between Japan's imports of raw materials or products manufactured by its offshore facilities and its relative lack of imports involving value-added goods produced by foreign firms.

John P. Cregan

Vice President

U.S. Business and Industrial Council

Washington
