Safeway Underwriters Slash Price Of 10% Stake Being Sold to Public

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 04/26/90

NEW YORK -- Underwriters for Safeway Inc., one of the crown jewels in the Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. empire, slashed the price of a 10% stake that the supermarket giant is offering to the public. Goldman, Sachs & Co. and Morgan Stanley & Co., the underwriters, late yesterday priced the offering at $11.25 a share, 22% below the midpoint of the recently estimated price range.

The underwriters previously were forecasting that the Safeway shares would fetch $13 to $16 apiece. The offering totals 10 million shares.

Trading in the shares is expected to begin today on the New York Stock Exchange under the symbol SWY. A spokesman for one of the underwriters wouldn't comment on the price reduction.

Safeway, an Oakland, Calif., supermarket chain, was taken private by a KKR-sponsored corporation in 1986. Since then, Safeway has reduced debt and considerably improved its operating profit margins.

Underwriters had pitched Safeway on the basis of its rising cash flow. But potential investors apparently have balked at valuing Safeway only on the basis of cash flow, as distinct from earnings.

Several said the initial price range was steep, given that Safeway's 1990 net income has been forecast at 40 cents a share. For 1991, net has been forecast at 85 cents a share. (As of February, Safeway's holdings included 13.6 million shares of Vons Cos. common stock.)

Investors also cited the fact that, over the next five years, cash from operations will be plowed into building stores and remodeling old ones -- and not into reducing Safeway's $3.1 billion in debt.

This past summer, Safeway had envisioned a bigger offering priced at perhaps $20 a share. However, the crash in junk-bond prices in September and investors' disenchantment with leveraged stocks reduced the market's appetite.

Neither KKR nor any members of Safeway's management are selling stock in the offering. KKR is an investment firm with offices in New York and San Francisco.

Since the buy-out, Safeway has sold inefficient operations and remodeled or built from scratch more than 100 stores. Labor costs have been cut, and warehouse and buying practices have been computerized.

Almost half of Safeway's 1,100 stores are so-called superstores, with 45,000 items ranging from frozen yogurt to pizza. Profit margins on specialty items such as Chinese foods -- an increasingly important part of the business -- are especially high.

Safeway's operating profit as a percentage of sales climbed to 3.2% in 1989 from 2.3% in 1986. Sales last year were $14.32 billion.

Analysts generally think that more stock in Safeway may be offered in future years, perhaps to finance acquisitions, and to enable KKR and affiliated investors to reduce their stake.

KKR was thought to be eager to have the current offering come to market in order to establish a track record in the public market for Safeway shares.
