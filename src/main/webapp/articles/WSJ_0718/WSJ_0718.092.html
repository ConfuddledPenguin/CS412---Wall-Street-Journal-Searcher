Technology: Intel's Profit Rose By 72% to Record In Second Quarter

  • Author: Stephen Kreider Yoder
  • Date: 07/18/90

Intel Corp. said net income rose 72% in its second quarter as computer makers continued to snap up the company's popular microprocessor chips to build into personal computers. Net rose to a record $170.7 million, or 84 cents a share, from $99.3 million, or 53 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 30% to a record $968.3 million from $747.3 million.

The latest period adds to a series of winning quarters for the Santa Clara, Calif., chip maker, which saw earnings increases of 43% and 48% in the previous two quarters. "They're clearly riding pretty high right now," said Millard Phelps, an analyst with Hambrecht & Quist. Intel's net was several cents a share greater than most analysts had predicted, he said. Analysts expect revenue to surpass $1 billion in the current quarter.

The source of Intel's booming growth is its 80386 and 80486 microprocessors, the "brain" chips that computer makers are building into some of today's most powerful, and most popular, personal computers. Intel is the only company that makes the chips, so it doesn't compete with Japanese or U.S. companies in an otherwise fierce chip market.

What's more, demand for those chips, which carry fat profit margins, is so strong that Intel has had to allocate them among customers, and doesn't expect to be able to catch up with demand until year end. Earlier this year Intel expected to meet the demand by the summer. Intel has delayed closing an old chip plant and is increasing production in other facilities.

Chip profits overshadowed problems in Intel's computer business in the quarter. Factories making personal computers had quality problems early in the period, but the problems were solved, Intel said. And analysts said they were surprised by Intel's huge interest income in the quarter, $44.8 million vs. last year's $15.7 million.

Fat profits are almost run-of-the mill for Intel now. "Expectations were generally very high," said analyst William Tai at Alex. Brown & Sons Inc. "Had they not shown very strong growth it would have been extremely disappointing." Despite the better-than-expected earnings, which included a $17 million pretax gain on sold assets, Intel's stock fell 75 cents a share in national over-the-counter trading yesterday to $50.50 a share.

Mr. Tai said he expects Intel to see several more healthy quarters. Still, he said, shortages can quickly turn to ruinous oversupplies. In times of shortage, users tend to hoard chips, but sometimes quickly unload their inventory and cause a glut.
