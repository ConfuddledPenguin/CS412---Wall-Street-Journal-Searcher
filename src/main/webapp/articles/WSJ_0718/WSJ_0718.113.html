Technology: Japan Firms Face Charge of Dumping High-Tech Screens

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 07/18/90

WASHINGTON -- U.S. manufacturers of flat-panel electronic display screens, in what they described as a last-gasp bid for the U.S. industry's survival, filed an anti-dumping complaint against a number of Japanese competitors. The petition charges that Toshiba Corp., Hitachi Ltd., Matsushita Electric Industrial Co., Seiko Epson Corp., Sharp Corp. and other Japanese companies are selling the advanced, electronic display screens in the U.S. at far less than their production costs or home-market prices in violation of the U.S. anti-dumping trade law. If the complaint is upheld, the Japanese products could be subject to penalty duties ranging from 71% to 318% of their current U.S. prices.

"The industry is at a critical juncture," said James Hurd, president of Planar Systems Inc., a Beaverton, Ore., manufacturer. "Unless the dumping issue is addressed now, its future will be threatened." Mr. Hurd is one of the organizers of the Advanced Display Manufacturers of America, a trade group that filed the petition with the Commerce Department and the International Trade Commission.

Flat-panel displays gradually are replacing the bulkier cathode ray tubes, or CRTs, in televisions, laptop computers and other devices. The technology involved also is basic to establishing a high-definition television industry. The U.S. demand for non-CRT displays is estimated to be close to $700 million a year and growing rapidly.

The technology, which has been commercially available for about a decade, is of U.S. origin. However, Japanese producers have captured 90% of the U.S. commercial flat-panel market and are challenging U.S. producers for defense industry sales, the complaint states.

"The Japanese are investing hundreds of millions of dollars a year in order to create market dominance," Mr. Hurd claims. "They've publicly acknowledged that they are prepared to lose money for five to six years to get there."

The petition identifies the other respondents as Fujitsu Ltd., Hosiden Electronics Co., Kyocera Corp., NEC Corp., Optrex Corp., Seiko Instruments & Electronics Ltd. and Matsushita Electronics Corp., a unit of Matsushita Electric Industrial. Both Seiko Epson and Seiko Instruments & Electronics are units of Hattori Seiko Co. The petition claims that Sharp, Matsushita Electric Industrial and Hitachi are the dominant suppliers, with market shares in recent years of 13.5%, 13% and 12%, respectively.

The Japanese respondents are likely to base their defense on the principle of forward-pricing, which justifies some initial below-cost sales if producers intend to recover all their costs over the product's life. The Commerce Department insists that producers recover all their costs within a year.

It's unlikely that the claim will garner wide support from U.S. buyers of flat panels. Computer makers have been relying for years on Japanese suppliers. Some major computer makers such as Apple Computer Inc. and Tandy Corp. are even encouraging the Japanese to invest heavily in a new generation of color displays.
