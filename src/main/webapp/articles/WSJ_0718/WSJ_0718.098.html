Politics & Policy: Senate Panel Backs Bush Plan on Soviet Technology Access

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 07/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Finance Committee voted to endorse the Bush administration's recent initiatives to give the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe greater access to Western technology. But at the same time, the panel acted to restrict China's access to technology that the country could use to construct missiles and stockpile chemical weapons. And the committee voted to deny Iraq, an alleged human-rights violator, the right to buy U.S. weapons and munitions or to seek credits from the U.S. Export-Import Bank and Commodity Credit Corp.

These proposed measures are part of a bill to renew the Export Administration Act for two more years. The bill was sent to the Senate floor by a voice vote. The House already has approved its own agenda for revising export controls.

The Senate version supports trade liberalization for the East generally at the pace urged by the administration and endorsed by the U.S. and its allies at a high-level meeting in Paris in the spring. Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher said in a letter to the committee that the administration supports the bill's basic provisions on Eastern Europe.

The bill, however, departs from stated administration policy in taking a tougher stance on China and in expanding controls on exports of missile technology to countries suspected of having warlike intentions.

The committee also voted 15-6 to adopt an amendment that would authorize the Ex-Im Bank to help finance the sale of U.S. weapons and defense services to members of the North Atlantic Treaty Organization and Japan.

John Macomber, the bank's chairman, warned the committee against hastily authorizing such a "fundamental reorientation" of the bank's programs, which have focused exclusively on financing sales of non-defense products. However, the panel was swayed by pleas by the amendment's sponsors, Sens. Christopher Dodd (D., Conn.) and Christopher Bond (R., Mo.), that the U.S. defense industry needs such help to avert further layoffs.

The Export Administration Act, which expires Sept. 30, is the legal basis for the administration to establish or modify its policies for controlling the export of so-called dual-use technology, or products with both civilian and military applications. While the administration has already taken most of the steps in the Senate bill, the export act needs to be revised to sanction the changes.

The bill urges the administration to give "most favorable treatment" to proposed sales of telecommunications equipment to Poland, Czechoslovakia and Hungary. This provision is intended to avert unreasonable delays in the granting of licenses for such transactions. The bill also urges speedier action on those countries' requests to purchase software for computer networks.

The bill would require the U.S. to recognize by Dec. 31, 1991, a "license-free zone" encompassing the U.S., its allies and some neutral but cooperating nations. The proposal would effectively free U.S. exporters from the need to apply for a license to ship any dual-use product to any of those other countries. The Commerce Department already has lifted most licensing requirements for trade with NATO countries, Japan and Australia.
