Politics & Policy: Bush Summons Congressional Leaders In Apparent Bid for Budget Compromise

  • Author: David Wessel and Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 07/18/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush invited top congressional leaders from both parties to come to the White House this afternoon in what appeared to be another attempt to prod the bipartisan budget talks toward a compromise. The invitation, which White House Chief of Staff John Sununu confirmed late yesterday, comes as congressional Democrats attempted to fashion a tax package that staffers said is likely to include proposals to increase taxes on upper-income Americans in addition to increasing the marginal tax rate. Democratic leaders met yesterday to pare their list of tax options, but they said they hadn't finished the task. "The purpose is to see where our bottom line is," said House Budget Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.)

Separately, the House failed to approve a constitutional amendment, backed by Mr. Bush, that would have required a balanced federal budget. The House vote was 279-150, seven votes short of the two-thirds vote needed to approve an amendment. The action appears to kill, at least for this year, any effort to alter the Constitution to force balanced budgets.

The amendment, authored by Rep. Charles Stenholm (D., Texas), would have allowed federal outlays to exceed receipts only during wartime or if three-fifths of the House and Senate approved a specific deficit.

Mr. Bush last met with the bipartisan congressional leadership June 26. That meeting produced a brief White House statement in which the president acknowledged that tax increases are needed to reduce the deficit, and it gave the talks momentum.

Mr. Bush yesterday warned of unrest in the financial markets unless budget negotiations get moving soon. "The time for game-playing is over, and we have to get something done that is not only a sound budget agreement but is seen by the American people to be a sound budget agreement," he told a group of magazine publishers. "And I worry, if we don't get one, about the confidence in the marketplace."

The president acknowledged that he may have to do more to explain the urgency of the budget problem to the American people -- something Democrats have been asking him to do for weeks. "I still feel that there isn't quite the acute awareness on the part of the average American as deserves to be there," he said.

Today's planned meeting at the White House comes at a time of heightened tensions, both between parties and within them. Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell (D., Maine) and Minority Leader Bob Dole (R., Kan.), both of whom were invited to the White House meeting, spent much of yesterday in a bitter dispute on the Senate floor over the Democrats' tactics in handling a pending civil rights bill.

Meanwhile, Republican congressional budget negotiators continue to fret privately about contacts between Budget Director Richard Darman and the chairmen of the tax-writing panels, Sen. Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas) and Rep. Dan Rostenkowski (D., Ill.), and their staffs -- despite Mr. Darman's assurances that he isn't cutting a deal behind the backs of congressional Republicans.

"I don't know of anything concrete going on in the so-called behind-the-scenes meetings," said Sen. Pete Domenici (R., N.M.), senior Republican on the Senate Budget Committee. "We're aren't going to agree in little groups. We're going to agree in a big group."

Mr. Bush discussed the budget talks with Republican congressional leaders yesterday. One leading Republican hard-liner emerged sounding slightly more open to tax increases than he has in the past. `We're looking at a wide range of possible taxes," said Rep. Newt Gingrich (R., Ga.), the Republican House whip. "The position I've taken publicly is that we're not going to change the top {income tax} rate. We're not going to go for an increased income tax. But within that framework, I'm very open to looking at a wide range of how do you deal with it."

Republican leaders in the House are trying to head off a confrontation with their own rank and file over the wisdom of raising taxes. Rep. Richard Armey (R., Texas) today will ask other House Republicans to endorse his call for the budget summit to reject "new taxes and all tax-rate increases." Democrats have said repeatedly that they will back a compromise with the White House only if half the Republicans in the Senate and House support it.

Invited to the White House this afternoon, besides Sen. Mitchell and Sen. Dole, are House Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.), House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.) and House Minority Leader Bob Michel (R., Ill.).
