Economy: Industry Output Increased 0.4% During June --- Utilities' Production Led Rise Due to Heat Wave; Capacity Use Up a Bit

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 07/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The nation's industrial production rose modestly last month, bolstered by a jump in utility output during a torrid late-June heat wave, the Federal Reserve reported. Industrial output grew 0.4% in June following a 0.6% increase in May. With the rise in production, the country's factories, utilities and mines used more of their capacity in June: 83.5%, up from 83.3% in May.

The modest growth in output was higher than many analysts expected, but it still left economists disagreeing over the state of U.S. industrial health.

The 2.1% leap in utility output, largely due to a surge in air conditioner use, was obviously temporary. And another big factor, a substantial increase in automobile production, probably won't continue either, several analysts predicted. Car companies have been fattening inventories in anticipation of contract talks with the United Auto Workers union, which get under way today.

"This is actually confirmation, if we needed any at this juncture, that the economy is running low and slow and probably will continue to do so because the increases in industrial production and capacity utilization are related to factors that are temporary, not fundamental," said David Littmann, senior economist at Manufacturers National Bank of Detroit.

But while auto and utility production accounted for three-fourths of the total increase in output, according to the Fed, a number of other categories also posted small gains. Production of paper, chemicals, appliances, television sets, fuels, furniture and business equipment all rose.

"I'm encouraged," said Stephen Roach, senior economist at Morgan Stanley in New York. "I think these data do reflect a gathering of momentum in the manufacturing sector. I think that could be an important swing factor to an improved tone in the U.S. economy."

U.S. manufacturing, hurt by the Fed's efforts to fight inflation by keeping interest rates high, has been ailing for more than a year. Just a few weeks ago, the Labor Department reported that factory employment continued to decline in June, the 14th month of the past 15 in which it has dropped.

But Mr. Roach said the decrease doesn't entirely reflect weakness. "I think the job cuts are largely restructuring driven by a manufacturing sector that wants to compete in the global economy." He cited recent strong productivity gains in manufacturing as one reason behind continued improvement in output.

"We're still looking at a manufacturing sector that overall is in transition to leaner, meaner stuff," Mr. Littmann said.

Recent government reports have suggested to some analysts that the worst may be past for many manufacturing industries. Factory orders grew substantially in May. Exports continue to improve, the Commerce Department reported yesterday. Moreover, the Fed last week eased its grip on credit for the first time since December, allowing a pivotal interest rate to fall slightly.

Still, most analysts expect continued improvement in manufacturing to be gradual.

Figures in the industrial production and capacity reports are seasonally adjusted.
