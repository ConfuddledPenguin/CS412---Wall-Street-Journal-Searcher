Letters to the Editor: Health Care Needs Good Business Sense

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/18/90

High hospital costs in Kalamazoo are attributed by your reporter ("Rival Operations -- Competitive Anomaly: Consumers Pay More in 2-Hospital Towns," page one, June 6) to the existence of competition between two hospitals, the implication being that a monopoly would be better. The story, however, also supports an even more ironic hypothesis -- that Kalamazoo's large corporate purchasers of hospital services are responsible for the high costs and their own plight. After years of trying to encourage the hospitals to cooperate rather than compete (probably the cooperation envisioned would have been unlawful under the antitrust laws in any event), Kalamazoo's employers are apparently now considering for the first time purchasing health care just as they purchase other goods and services -- with price as a central consideration. Theodore Cooper, M.D., who was quoted as chairman of Upjohn Co. but who still sounds like the U.S. assistant secretary of health he once was, admits, "We've never tried to bid one {hospital} against the other." If employers had negotiated with the hospitals for particular services, demanding good quality and a competitive price in return for their employees' business, the duplication of services and high prices they complain of would never have occurred.

Clark C. Havighurst

Durham, N.C.
