Economy: Merchandise Trade Deficit Rose Slightly In May, but Analysts Aren't Concerned

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 07/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. merchandise trade deficit grew slightly in May, the Commerce Department said, but analysts showed little concern that the trend toward smaller deficits may be in jeopardy. The May deficit of $7.73 billion, which followed a revised $7.31 billion deficit in April, was fueled by a 2.9% increase in imports, to $40.52 billion from $39.36 billion in April. Exports also rose, climbing 2.2% to $32.8 billion from April's $32.1 billion.

A 15.2% increase in imports of petroleum products accounted for a large portion of the deficit. These imports averaged 1.4 million barrels more a day in May than in April.

Economists expressed little concern with the larger deficit figures, focusing instead on the increase in exports to Europe and the narrowing deficit with Japan. "Basically, we're still on the path to a lower trade deficit than last year," commented Brian Wesbury, vice president of Stotler Economics Inc. "Both imports and exports were up, and the low dollar continues to boost exports. Given the volatility of these numbers, I don't see this as a reversal of the trend."

The $811.6 million May trade surplus with Western Europe, while down from April's $1.36 billion, is still well ahead of last year's figure, which showed the U.S. running a $40.3 million deficit for the month of May. The U.S. is running a cumulative surplus of $3.53 billion so far this year with Western Europe, compared with a cumulative surplus of $281 million at this time last year.

The deficit with Japan narrowed to $2.97 billion from $3.95 billion in April.

"I'm looking at Eastern Europe and East Germany as a source for increased demand for trade," said Charles Renfro, chief economist for Alphametrics Corp. The opening up of the East German economy and the need to rebuild the country's infrastructure "are obviously going to increase our capability of exporting to Germany," he added.

Other economists saw the lower value of the dollar as an important component of the increasing exports. "We're seeing the tail-end effect of the low exchange value of the dollar," said Joan Schneider, a money-market economist with Continental Bank, Chicago.

The monetary union of Germany has other benefits for the U.S, according to Stotler's Mr. Wesbury: "Against the German mark, we should see a strengthening of the dollar," he said.

The cumulative trade deficit for January through May is $40.7 billion. If projected over the rest of the year, the deficit would total $97.7 billion for 1990. The deficit for 1989 was $109.4 billion.

"It strikes me that we've got a basis for a pretty good situation given where we are in the recovery," Mr. Renfro said.
