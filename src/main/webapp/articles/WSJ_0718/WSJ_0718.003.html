Mutual Funds: Where Does the Real Danger Lie for Fund Investors?

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 07/18/90

Many investors consider a mutual fund risky if its share price zigzags wildly. This is why so many investors avoid stock funds and instead leave their money in money market funds and bond funds. But specialists warn that the really worrisome risk isn't volatility, but inflation. "If you're 40 years old and investing for your retirement, then investing in fixed-income funds is a much riskier approach," Kenneth Gregory, who runs a San Francisco investment advisory firm, Litman/Gregory & Co., says. "By the time inflation and taxes are taken into account, the chances of beating inflation are much lower" than with stock funds.

At first blush, Vanguard Money Market Reserve -- Prime Portfolio seems like a safe investment. The fund has never had a down quarter. In the past 15 years, the fund's worst return for any of the 60 quarters was 1%.

Twentieth Century Select, on the other hand, is the sort of fund that can leave investors lying awake at night. This growth-stock fund posted losses in 17 of the past 60 quarterly periods. In the 1987 fourth quarter, which included the Oct. 19 stock market crash, it got pounded for a 23% loss.

Compared with Vanguard Prime Portfolio, Twentieth Century Select is the riskier investment, right? If risk is equated with wild price fluctuations, this certainly is true.

But if risk is defined instead as preserving capital against the ravages of inflation and Uncle Sam, then Vanguard Prime Portfolio turns out to be a real loser. In the past 15 years, the Vanguard Prime Portfolio money market fund posted an average annualized return of 9.1%, barely ahead of the 6.1% annualized inflation rate. Deduct taxes, and the money-fund investor made hardly any money at all. By contrast, Twentieth Century Select posted an annual return of 23.1% over the past 15 years, easily outpacing both inflation and the tax man.

Though most stock funds have posted some atrocious quarterly and 12-month returns in the past 15 years, the results look far more respectable if an investor stuck it out for at least three years. "The longer the period, the more the odds are in your favor," say Mr. Gregory, who, in addition to his investment counseling business, writes a mutual fund newsletter, the L/G No-Load Fund Analyst.

Investors have known for years that some investments are more volatile than others. But the debate about risk took on a sharper focus in the 1970s, when the so-called betas became a favorite Wall Street investment concept.

A beta measures a stock or stock fund's volatility relative to the stock market, as measured by the Standard & Poor's 500 stock index. The S&P 500 has a beta of 1. Twentieth Century Select, which is more volatile than the S&P 500, has a beta of 1.3. A low-risk fund such as Vanguard's Wellington Fund, which holds both stocks and bonds, has a beta of 0.74.

"People look at our funds and say they're too volatile, based on our betas," Robert Puff, a member of Twentieth Century's five-man stock-picking team, says. "But common stocks are not meant to be short-term investment vehicles. People should think about stocks the same way they think about their home."

Part of the problem, Mr. Puff says, is that many investors buy stock funds near market peaks, and then don't have the patience to hang on through a market slump. "One of the major determinants of a mutual fund investor's success is totally outside the control of the mutual fund company, and that's the decision of when to invest," he says. "The biggest risk is putting all of their money to work at the peak of the market."

One way to avoid this problem, Mr. Puff says, is to use dollar cost averaging, investing fixed amounts at regular intervals, regardless of what is happening in Neff's Windsor Fund, both of which have good long-term records but poor recent performance. The two funds are closed to new investors. Investors who want to invest with Michael Price can buy Mutual Beacon, which has been managed by Mr. Price since 1985. One drawback: It has a steep $50,000 minimum.

John Neff's talents are available through a publicly traded dual-purpose fund called Gemini II. Gemini II has two classes of shares outstanding, income shares and capital shares, which are listed on the New York Stock Exchange and can be bought and sold just as any other stock. The income shares get all the dividends kicked off by the fund, while the capital shares pick up all the capital gains. Taken together, the two classes of shares are trading at a minute discount to the value of the fund's portfolio.
