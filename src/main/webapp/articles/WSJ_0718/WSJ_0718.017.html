Technology: Kubota Says Heads Of Stardent Sought Money for Silence

  • Author: Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 07/18/90

OSAKA, Japan -- A top Kubota Corp. executive, responding to allegations that the Japanese company fraudulently obtained U.S. computer technology, said two Silicon Valley entrepreneurs brought the charges only after their private demand for a $7 million payment was refused. Last Thursday, the founders of a supercomputer graphics company filed a lawsuit in Superior Court in Santa Clara, Calif., alleging that Kubota forced the merger of their company with another -- a combination that became Stardent Computer Inc. They alleged that Kubota then began to transfer the technology to one of its subsidiaries.

But Masahiro Yoshida, a Kubota managing director, cast events in a different light in an interview at the company's headquarters here.

He characterized the allegations as "bold-faced lies" and portrayed Kubota as the savior of a struggling U.S. technology company that couldn't find U.S. capital. Kubota's only mistake, Mr. Yoshida indicated, was perhaps naively stepping into a high-technology market characterized by rapid ups and downs and heated personality clashes -- much different than the staid Japanese market.

Mr. Yoshida displayed a copy of a letter dated June 1, 1990, and signed by Joseph Alioto, attorney for Allen Michels and Matthew Sanders, the co-chairmen of Stardent who filed the suit. In the letter, various grievances the pair had against Kubota were outlined, and a request was made for a payment of $3.5 million apiece, to be paid in the form of a five-year consulting fee. The letter indicated the two men would quietly leave the company without bringing charges if they received the money.

"If we had paid the money they had demanded at the beginning, does that mean we can pay money to steal American technology?" Mr. Yoshida asked.

Mr. Michels acknowledged that Mr. Alioto sent a letter to Kubota proposing a $7 million settlement, which the two considered "reasonable." But other evidence of Kubota's alleged wrongdoing emerged after the letter was sent, he said.

As to accusations that the pair is presenting "bold-faced lies," Mr. Michels said, "That's why we have courts. We have a significant amount of evidence that we feel will back up our complaints."

Mr. Alioto said the pre-suit request for a settlement that Mr. Yoshida referred to was disclosed in the suit and didn't constitute a big disclosure.

Mr. Yoshida's remarks were the company's first detailed response to the allegations, although Kubota apparently plans a strong counteroffensive. The company has scheduled a news conference in Tokyo Friday to discuss the matter. Mr. Yoshida said Kubota hadn't yet formulated a legal strategy because the company hasn't yet seen the lawsuit.
