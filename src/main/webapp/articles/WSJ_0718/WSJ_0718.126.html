Politics & Policy: Civil-Rights Measure Is Facing a Veto, Sununu Says After Senate Votes Cloture

  • Author: Joe Davidson and Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 07/18/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush would veto civil rights legislation pending in the Senate, Chief of Staff John Sununu said soon after senators voted to limit debate on the measure. The 62-38 vote, which sets up a vote on the bill as early as tonight, was followed by accusations by Republican senators that the Democratic majority used a strong-arm procedural move when the White House and GOP senators were negotiating in good faith.

Mr. Sununu accused Sen. Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.), a leader of the proponents of the bill, of reneging on an agreement to use substitute language that would have made it clear that the measure wasn't intended to result in hiring quotas. The issue of quotas has been the main problem Republicans have had with the legislation.

"The bill as crafted right now is a quota bill, and the president has said he will veto that quota bill," Mr. Sununu told reporters.

But Mr. Sununu, mindful of the political damage the president would suffer if he is perceived as a foe of civil rights protections, emphasized the White House's willingness to accept a bill if the Senate returns to the compromise language. "The president is eager to sign" an acceptable bill, Mr. Sununu said.

At about 9:00 EDT last night, Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell (D., Maine) said negotiations on the bill would continue and that both parties agreed to 12 hours of debate beginning at 10:15 this morning.

Sen. Robert Dole (R., Kan.), who earlier suggested with some sarcasm that perhaps he should be replaced as the Senate GOP leader because of the eight Republicans who voted with the Democrats in cutting off debate, said last night: "It's worth one last shot, one last effort."

The cloture vote, in which the eight Republicans and one Democrat strayed from party lines, indicated that the civil rights bill would pass easily. But the vote also fell five short of the two-thirds vote needed to override a presidential veto.

Mr. Kennedy denied that he reneged on an agreement with the administration, saying neither he nor the president had ever said a deal had been "locked up." Paul Donovan, the senator's press secretary, said, "There was never a meeting of the minds on this, and there was never a final agreement."

The Civil Rights Act of 1990 is designed to nullify five Supreme Court decisions last year that the bill's advocates say severely hurt efforts to achieve equality in the workplace. It is the top agenda item for the civil rights community and the target of strong opposition by business groups.

Immediately after the cloture vote, Mr. Dole said that the Democrats' decision to "shove it down {GOP} throats" threatened bipartisan cooperation. He warned that there wouldn't be any action in the Senate "until we have some agreement," adding, "we're going to run this place in a civil manner."

Mr. Mitchell, trying to show that the bill wasn't being railroaded, outlined efforts by Democrats to work with Republicans on the measure.
