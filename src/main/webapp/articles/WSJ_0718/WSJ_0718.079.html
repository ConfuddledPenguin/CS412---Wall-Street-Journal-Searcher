Midland Bank Exits U.S. Group Of Primary Dealers

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 07/18/90

In another indication of the hard times in the U.S. government bond market, Midland Bank PLC said it was exiting the elite group of bond firms in the U.S. that do business directly with the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. The cutbacks are part of a general retrenchment that could include layoffs of as many as 4,000 people in London over the next few weeks, according to current and former Midland employees. "It isn't our policy to comment on rumors or speculation," said a bank spokesman in London.

Only a few years ago, many foreign banks and securities firms were eager to become U.S. primary dealers, either by building operations from scratch or paying top dollar for existing ones. The logic behind these decisions was that to be considered a major player in global finance, you had to be big in the U.S. Treasury market, the world's biggest securities market. But lately, the business has been a disaster, suffering from overcapacity, low customer volume, low volatility and razor-thin spreads between buy and sell quotes.

Allan Rosenberg, president of Midland Montagu Securities Inc., Midland's primary dealer and also the entity that houses most of Midland's U.S. banking operations, said about 140 people would be laid off in New York. That would shrink the British bank's U.S. presence to about 190 employees.

Besides the dealership, Midland is also closing its U.S. private banking unit and letting go operations and support staff. Midland inherited the dealership when it bought Crocker National Bank in 1980.

Concerning the dealership, Mr. Rosenberg said: "The biggest problem is that {the market} is oversaturated. There are too many dealers chasing too little business." He added, "Profit margins with institutional customer business are at a bare minimum, and risk appetites are certainly in general less today than they have been over the last 10 to 15 years."

Mr. Rosenberg said Midland's dealership was making money, about $1 million to $2 million for the first six months of 1990 on a market share of 1.5% to 2%. The bank's problem was insufficient return on capital: Maintaining the dealership with such low profits didn't justify devoting $100 million of capital to the business.

Midland officials said that instead, the bank had chosen to concentrate on its strength in Britain and continental Europe. Through subsidiaries and affiliates, the bank is a major player in the British and German government bond markets.

Midland's retreat reduces the number of primary dealers to 42 from a peak of 46 at the end of 1988. Fifteen of the firms are controlled by foreign institutions.
