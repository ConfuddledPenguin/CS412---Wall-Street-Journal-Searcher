OTC Focus: Market Is Lower on Increased Volume; MCI Falls on United Telecom Earnings

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 07/18/90

Over-the-counter stocks bore the brunt of investors' fears about earnings after exchange-listed United Telecommunications startled investors with lower-than-expected earnings. The Nasdaq Composite Index tumbled 5.12 to 464.48, a 1.1% decline, in heavy trading of 167.8 million shares, up from 138.1 million Monday. The Nasdaq Industrial Index tumbled from its recent highs in a 5.95 drop to 504.66 as investors took profits in better-performing OTC stocks.

The Nasdaq Utility Index plunged 2.1%, or 13.21 to 642.10, as the United Telecommunications news made investors skittish about other telecommunications issues.

MCI Communications, which comprises a 22% weighting of the Utility Index, fell 1 5/8 to 38 3/8 on nearly 8.6 million shares and traded as low as 37 1/4 and as high as 40 1/8 during the day.

OTC stocks "see-sawed all day," said William A. Rothe, head of OTC trading at Alex. Brown & Sons, Baltimore. "They'd take them down and then turn them on a dime and take them back up just as fast," he said. Mr. Rothe added that he thinks the failure of the Dow Jones Industrial Average to sustain a push above the 3000 level prompted investors to take profits in some of the better-quality OTC names included in the Nasdaq Industrial Index.

Intel fell 3/4 to 50 1/2, but swung between a high of 52 and a low of 50 1/4. The Santa Clara, Calif., semiconductor maker reported second quarter net income of 84 cents a share, up from 53 cents a year earlier.

Also under pressure were shares of Apple Computer, which fell 1 3/8 to 44 1/4 on 1.2 million shares. Cowen & Co. analyst Michele S. Preston said in an interview that investors are skittish about comments due late today by the Cupertino, Calif., computer maker after it reports earnings for the third quarter ended in June.

Ms. Preston said she expects Apple to earn about 90 cents a share, up from 74 cents a year earlier, and doesn't expect any surprises. Apple officials have been cautious about the outlook for the remainder of the year as the company plans to introduce a new, low-priced Macintosh computer, she explained. However, she noted that previously the company has surprised investors by reporting earnings in line with expectations but then warning of an uncertain outlook. A conference call among analysts and Apple officials is scheduled for 5 p.m. Eastern time today, she said.

Investors continued to punish stocks of companies with poor earnings outlooks.

Weitek skidded 4 to 15 1/2 on 1.9 million shares, or more than 15 times average volume, and was one of the day's most active stocks. Analysts told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report that officials of the Sunnyvale, Calif., maker of high-performance integrated circuits indicated Monday they're pessimistic about winning an important new contract from Sun Microsystems.

Robertson Stephens & Co. analyst Michael J. Stark told DJPIR that statement prompted him to lower his earnings estimates for Weitek to $1.05 a share in 1990, from $1.10 previously, and $1.25 a share in 1991, from $1.42 before; the company earned 81 cents a share in 1989. Dillon Read & Co. analyst Lawrence W. Borgman said his 1990 estimate has been $1.10 a share and that he expects to lower it to about $1.05 a share. But neither analyst changed his investment opinion on the stock; Mr. Stark has a near-term "hold" and long-term "buy" advisory, and Mr. Borgman says he will continue to rate the stock "attractive."

Advanced Logic fell 2 1/4 to a bid of 11 3/4 after the Irvine, Calif., maker of microcomputer systems said it expects revenue for the third quarter ended June 30 of $2.5 million to $2.8 million, below second quarter net income of $3.2 million but higher than year-earlier income of $700,000.

SofTech lost 1/4 to 2 7/8. Late Monday, the Waltham, Mass., developer of software services and products revised its earnings estimate for the fiscal year ended May 31 to a loss of about 16 cents a share from a previously estimated profit of seven to 11 cents a share, citing increased software investments and delays in two military projects. SofTech had 1989 net income of 46 cents a share.

Graphic Scanning fell 1 1/2 to 8 before the National Association of Securities Dealers halted trading in the stock pending an announcement early this morning. Investment firms controlled by Mario Gabelli said in May they hold nearly a 44% position in the Englewood, N.J., communications-services company.

Among the day's gainers, Class B shares of Adolph Coors climbed 3/8 to 26 1/4 after the Golden, Colo., brewer reported net income for the second quarter ended June 17 of 68 cents a share, up from 46 cents a year earlier.

Guy F. Atkinson rose 1 1/4 to 16 1/2 after the South San Francisco, Calif. construction company said it expects to receive $100 million in payments after settling five construction contract disputes.

PROPOSED LISTING STANDARDS REVISED: The National Association of Securities Dealers, which has proposed higher standards for the continued listing of stocks on Nasdaq, its automated quotation system, said yesterday its board changed several of the proposed requirements after reviewing more than 80 responses to the plan.

Under the latest proposal, stocks can stay on Nasdaq if they fall below $1 a share provided that the market value of shares in public hands -- known as the "float" -- is at least $1 million and the company has $2 million in equity. Under the original plan, companies that failed to maintain a public float of $200,000 or a minimum bid price of $1 for 10 consecutive days would be delisted and have to requalify with a minimum $1 million public float and minimum bid of $3.

If approved by the Securities and Exchange Commission, the latest proposals aren't expected to be effective until July 1, 1991, at the earliest-six months later than the originally proposed effective date of Jan. 1. The delay is designed to give companies time to comply with the latest version of the standards, NASD said.
