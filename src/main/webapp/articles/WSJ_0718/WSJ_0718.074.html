Credit Markets: RJR Nabisco's Junk Surges Again, but Activity Is Thin Ahead of Today's June Inflation Data

  • Author: Tom Herman and Robert Sherman
  • Date: 07/18/90

NEW YORK -- Prices surged on RJR Nabisco Inc. junk bonds for the second day in a row, but most other bond prices and interest rates were little changed. RJR Nabisco's bonds are surging in popularity because of a huge $6.9 billion refinancing plan announced Sunday by company officials. The plan is designed to ease the food and tobacco company's enormous debt burdens and strengthen its financial backbone.

RJR's largest issue, which soared more than eight points Monday, added another 2 3/8 points yesterday to close at 94 1/8. Investor enthusiasm about the RJR news spilled over into other sectors of the junk bond market, helping to lift prices of some issues by about half a point, or $5 for each $1,000 face amount.

But while trading volume in RJR bonds was heavy, volume elsewhere in the junk-bond market generally was "very light," said Joseph Bencivenga, head of high-yield bond research at Salomon Brothers Inc. Trading generally "was so thin that I find it hard to describe what happened to the market as a whole as much of a rally, aside from RJR where we saw a lot of activity," he said."

Elsewhere in the credit markets, Treasury bond prices held steady for the second day in a row. Yields on 30-year Treasury bonds continued to hover about 8.45%. Rates on short-term Treasury bills also were flat.

Treasury-bond trading was sluggish all day as investors awaited a government report this morning on consumer prices in June. Also this morning, Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan will present the central bank's semiannual economic and monetary report at a Senate Banking Committee hearing.

In addition, traders are waiting for signs that the government will take steps to slash its huge budget deficit. If the government shrinks the deficit, that would reduce borrowing pressures in the credit markets. And a large deficit-cutting package might persuade the Fed to reduce short-term interest rates further.

"My impression is that people are expecting at least a $50 billion to $55 billion cut in the budget deficit," said Morgan Stark, president and chief executive of Chemical Securities Inc. "The market will be very disappointed if this isn't done, and if it isn't done in a timely, credible fashion."

This morning's inflation report is expected to show a moderate rise in consumer prices of 0.3% for June, according to a survey of 21 economists by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report. That would follow increases of 0.2% in both May and April. Excluding the food and energy components, an increase of 0.4% is expected, analysts say.

Inflation fears that haunted the markets early this year now appear to be fading rapidly, with investors focusing increasingly on growing signs of economic weakness. "We definitely prefer bonds over stocks," says Arthur A. Micheletti, vice president at Bailard Biehl & Kaiser, a San Mateo, Calif., firm that manages about $1.3 billion for both individuals and institutions.

"It's a coin's toss whether the economy already is in a recession or merely heading for slow growth," Mr. Micheletti said. Either scenario "would mean strong gains" for holders of U.S. Treasury bonds, while the stock market is "an accident waiting to happen."

Bailard Biehl has about half its individual clients' funds in bonds, nearly all of which are in U.S. Treasury issues. "This isn't the time to reach for yield" and buy lower-rated bonds, he said. "This is a time to go for safety and liquidity."

Junk, Corporate Issues

RJR Nabisco's surging junk bonds dominated activity in the corporate-bond arena again yesterday.

RJR's pay-in-kind bonds posted especially large gains. The company's 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures maturing in 2007 climbed 2 3/8 points to 94 1/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. And its 17 3/8% convertible pay-in-kind debentures due in 2009 rose 1 1/2 points to 115 7/8. Pay-in-kind bonds pay interest in the form of additional securities.

Trading volume was very heavy. Southland Corp.'s junk bonds also posted solid gains as investors reacted with relief to the company's announcement Monday that it had agreed in principle on a restructuring proposal with a bondholders' steering committee and a group of Japanese investors.

That agreement averted a possible bankruptcy-law filing and drove up prices yesterday of some Southland issues by 1/2 point to one point. The agreement differs significantly from a previous debt-exchange offer from the company.

Southland, the troubled parent company of the 7-Eleven retail convenience stores, previously had said it didn't intend to make a June 15 interest payment on its senior subordinated debt. That payment officially went into default Sunday. Without an agreement from senior debtholders, the company could have been driven into bankruptcy proceedings.

Separately, prices of investment-grade corporate bonds were little changed on the day. Activity in the new-issue investment-grade market has perked up lately, and several bond specialists expect that to continue. "I think you're going to continue to see more {investment-grade} corporate supply" soon, said Thomas Pura, a partner at Goldman, Sachs & Co.

Foreign Bonds

British government bond prices tumbled, erasing all of Monday's gains, after the government reported an unexpectedly large budget deficit for June.

Among actively traded issues, Britain's 11.75% bonds due 2003/2007 fell 3/4 point to 102 7/32 to yield 11.41%. Britain's 12% bonds due 1995 declined 7/32 point to 99 3/4 to yield 12.08%.

U.K. bonds, known as gilts, have been star performers in the international bond markets during the past few months. Several analysts expect the rally to continue this summer because of growing signs of weakness in the U.K.'s economy that could drive down interest rates, which move in the opposite direction from bond prices. But yesterday's budget deficit news brought the recent rally to an abrupt halt.

Britain's budget deficit totaled #2.6 billion ($4.69 billion) in June. That was sharply higher than analysts' estimates of #1 billion to #1.5 billion. The bulging deficit raises the possibility that the government will have to resume selling sizable amounts of new gilts later this fiscal year to meet its funding needs, economists said.

Separately, West German government bond prices slumped, following the lead of futures prices. Several traders said they had expected a temporary setback following the market's week-long surge. Bonn's 8.75% bonds, due 2000, fell 0.35 point to 101.60 to yield 8.65%. Japanese bond prices also fell, hurt by high short-term interest rates.

A Salomon Brothers Inc. report released in New York yesterday predicted that inflation-adjusted yields on U.S. government bonds will remain considerably lower than those of major European countries through 1991.

Inflation-adjusted yields on U.S. government bonds now are about 4%, said Nicholas Sargen, a director of bond market research at Salomon. That, he said, is more than two percentage points less than those on bonds of Germany, France, Netherlands, and Canada. U.K. bonds have a real yield of about 5% to 5.5%, according to the report by Messrs. Sargen and John Lipsky of Salomon.

The report cited "heightened competition for funds in Europe and increased uncertainty about financing requirements for Germany and Eastern Europe" as the most important considerations in keeping real European bond yields high.

Municipal Bonds

A large volume of new municipal bond issues hit the market yesterday.

For example, New York sold $225 million of bonds through competitive bidding. The winning bid came from a group of underwriters led by First Boston Corp. The underwriters then reoffered the bonds to investors at prices to yield from 6% in 1992 to 7.15% in 2009-2020.

First Boston officials said the unsold balance among underwriters was about $72 million. The bonds are rated single-A by Moody's and S&P.

Investor demand for New York tax-free bonds typically is strong among New Yorkers because of the state's very high income tax rate. Moreover, New York City imposes a steep income tax, too, and many voters fear Gotham taxes have nowhere to go but up.

Connecticut offered $325 million of general obligation bonds. The issue was tentatively priced by underwriters, led by Merrill Lynch Capital Markets, to yield from 5.80% on bonds due in 1991 to 6.95% in 20072010. The bonds are rated double-A by both Moody's Investors Service Inc. and Standard & Poor's Inc.

Treasury Securities

Treasury-bond prices were little changed again yesterday as traders marked time ahead of today's economic news.

Among active issues, the Treasury's 30-year bonds were quoted late in the day at 103 1/8, to yield 8.45%, unchanged from the previous day.

"It was a very quiet, low volume session with minimal price change," said William Sullivan, director of money market research at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

Mortgage, Asset-backed Securities

Mortgage securities prices ended unchanged to slightly firmer, after spending the session in stifling trading ranges.

Only one major offering has been priced so far this week -- a $122 million Fleetwood Credit Corp. issue backed by loans for recreational vehicles that was priced yesterday by First Boston Corp. as 9% securities at 99 18/32 to yield 9.204%.

Fleetwood Credit is a unit of Fleetwood Enterprises Inc., Riverside, Calif., whose products include travel trailers and motor homes.

Among active secondary issues for August delivery, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were up 3/ 32 at 99 26/32, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% issues were up 2/32 at 99 1/8 and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were up 2/32 at 99 5/8.
