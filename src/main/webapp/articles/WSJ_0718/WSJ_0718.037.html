Sale of IBM's Low-Tech Lines Being Weighed --- Talks on $3 Billion Buy-out Said to Be Under Way With Potential Suitor

  • Author: Randall Smith and Paul B. Carroll
  • Date: 07/18/90

International Business Machines Corp. is considering the sale of low-technology businesses, such as electric typewriters and keyboards, in a buy-out that could total about $3 billion, Wall Street and industry executives said. The possible sale of the businesses is the latest sign of a new urgency among IBM executives to make each business stand on its own to strengthen the company's financial performance.

As part of a cost-cutting campaign, the computer giant has been looking for ways to get out of marginal businesses and has simply decided that it can't make some of these low-profit-margin items as inexpensively as its competitors can, the executives said. In addition, these businesses are no longer central to IBM. While electric typewriters, for example, were once a mainstay of IBM's office equipment business, they were supplanted in the 1980s by the proliferation of desktop computers and other word processing systems.

In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, IBM closed at $120.75, down $1.625.

In Armonk, N.Y., an IBM spokesman declined to comment. The industry executives cautioned that many details remain to be worked out before a sale can be completed. Still, they said that IBM is talking with a potential buyer, and that an agreement may be announced by the end of this month. The possible buyer's identity couldn't be confirmed.

The current plan provides for a leveraged buy-out, with some employees getting equity -- an arrangement that, while commonplace in the 1980s, would be unusual for a business owned by IBM.

A leveraged buy-out also would raise some troubling issues for IBM, because such transactions are sometimes followed by layoffs to cut costs and help enable the acquirer to repay the debt used in the purchase. Yet a key tenet of IBM's culture is a tradition of no layoffs. When IBM has sold businesses in the past, it has tried to allay employee fears on that subject, and IBM's concerns about employee morale are apparently part of what remains to be worked out.

Besides typewriters and keyboards, the computer industry executives said, the sale also could involve low-end printers. All three products are made at IBM's plant in Lexington, Ky. The number of employees there wasn't available. IBM has already turned over much of its typewriter marketing and service to other companies. It also backed away from a similar business, copiers, by selling most of the operations to Eastman Kodak Co. two years ago.

The move would be embarrassing for IBM, because it spent several hundred million dollars a few years ago to modernize and automate the Lexington facility, and touted it as an example of how a U.S. manufacturing facility could compete with the Japanese.

Still, IBM executives have indicated they will bite the bullet, if they must, to cut costs. In the fourth quarter, IBM took a $2.4 billion charge to cover a reduction of 10,000 jobs and some plant consolidations. It also has been pruning operations where possible; just last week, it said it was selling its automated teller machine operations into a joint venture with Diebold Inc., after capturing only about 5% of the worldwide market.

A Wall Street source said IBM is being advised on the sale by Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc., the securities unit of American Express Co. Advisers for the potential buyer are believed to include the investment banking unit of J.P. Morgan & Co., as well as the Kidder, Peabody & Co. unit of General Electric Co.

Executives of Shearson, Morgan and Kidder all declined to comment.

Any sale of these typewriter, keyboard or printer businesses would underscore IBM's intention to get out of marginal businesses, because, while IBM has sold assets it built up in ventures with other companies, it rarely parts with home-grown businesses such as these.

IBM's business as a whole has performed better than expected this year, with earnings up 6.8% in the first half to $2.45 billion, or $4.26 a share, on a revenue rise of 9.8% to $30.68 billion. But IBM isn't out of the woods yet.

Sales of mainframes declined in the second quarter, and IBM must manage a ticklish transition late this year and next year, as it moves to a new generation of mainframes that the company is expected to announce this fall. Customers often stop buying products while they pause to evaluate new ones, creating an awkward slowdown in sales.

IBM's mainframe competitors also have been taking market share away from the company in the past couple of years, and some industry executives worry that the new generation of machines expected from Amdahl Corp. and Hitachi Ltd. may accelerate that erosion of market share. Mainframes account for roughly half of IBM's sales and nearly two-thirds of its earnings, so any problems in that area would ripple through IBM's business.

Even while announcing solid earnings on Monday, IBM Chairman John Akers cautioned in a statement that "much remains to be done this year."
