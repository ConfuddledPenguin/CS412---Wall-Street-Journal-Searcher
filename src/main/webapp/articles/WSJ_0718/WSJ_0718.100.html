Abreast of the Market: Industrial Stocks Finish Unchanged After a Late Rally Erases Decline

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 07/18/90

NEW YORK -- In a wild final hour of trading, the Dow Jones Industrial Average surged from a 21-point loss to close unchanged at 2999.75 -- a fraction shy of the 3000 mark. It was the second consecutive day the industrial average failed to cross 3000.

At around 3 p.m. EST profit-taking in several leading growth stocks combined with futures-related selling to drive the industrial average lower, and what seemed decisively away from the 3000 mark for the day. By 3:30 p.m., the average was off about 20 points. Then one of the most powerful influences on the average -- the Chicago Board of Trade's Major Market stock-index futures -- came into play.

Donald Selkin, futures analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities, said stock-index arbitrage programs in the MMI shot the Dow industrials back up to the 3000 level in the final half-hour of trading. That view was confirmed by traders in Chicago who make markets in MMI futures.

Said one: "There was a lot of activity out here, and a lot of liquidity." The MMI, when active, has a powerful impact over the Dow industrial average because it contains 17 of the 30 Dow industrial stocks.

Mr. Selkin said MMI buy programs were set off by a number of large futures traders who are trying to buy back contracts they had sold "short" earlier. This buying pushed the futures to a large premium over the underlying stocks. Arbitrage traders, then, started selling the more expensive futures and buying the stocks underlying the MMI, vaulting the Dow average 20 points higher in minutes.

Mr. Selkin also noted that the number of outstanding futures positions in the July contract, which expires this Friday, has steadily increased over the past few weeks, to 9,544 after Monday's close. Normally, the number of outstanding positions decreases before expiration. The rise in open MMI positions shows that investors are starting to use the futures as a hedging vehicle again. That's not surprising because many money managers are holding a small group of growth stocks, some of which are components of the MMI, such as Johnson & Johnson, Procter & Gamble and Merck.

Mr. Selkin also noted that the large amount of open positions points to the possibility of a volatile expiration in the July futures this Friday.

Before the final-hour fireworks, investors focused on earnings. There was good news for some stocks in the Dow industrial average. Coca-Cola Co. reported that second-quarter profit was up 19% from a year earlier; the stock jumped 1 7/8 to 47 1/4.

Among other Dow industrial issues, Chevron advanced 1 to 75 7/8 in response to Pennzoil's disclosure that it will purchase 3.9 million additional common shares, boosting its 8.8% stake in the company to just below 10%. United Technologies continued to attract buyers, rising 1 5/8 to 62; American Express gained 1/2 to 31 3/8; Sears Roebuck added 3/4 to 34 1/2 and Goodyear Tire & Rubber rose 1/2 to 30 1/8.

But outside of the Dow industrials, the scene was grim. Declining issues on the New York Stock Exchange outnumbered advancers, 877 to 643. Broader market indexes spent most of the day lower, and closed that way: Standard & Poor's 500-stock index fell 1.43 to 367.52 and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index dropped 0.64 to 200.49.

Sellers pounced on United Telecommunications after it disappointed investors with second-quarter earnings. The stock fell 8 3/4 to 29 1/2 on nearly 12 million shares, making it the most active Big Board issue.

GTE, whose second-quarter earnings were a milder disappointment, dropped 3/4 to 28 1/4 on 3.6 million shares. While the company reported an 11% increase in net income, its earnings of 54 cents a share were slightly below the range of analysts' estimates.

Another issue hit with forceful selling was Lowe's, which retreated 6 to 41 3/8 on 1.9 million shares. Oppenheimer & Co. removed the stock from its list of recommended issues; also, Dean Witter Reynolds lowered its earnings estimate for the fiscal second quarter, citing pressure on the company's gross margins.

The plunge in Lowe's -- a home improvement retailer -- broke investors' confidence in several key retail-related growth stocks.

Home Depot tumbled 1 3/8 to 41 5/8; Wal-Mart Stores eased 3/4 to 35 1/2, and Woolworth slipped 3/8 to 35 3/4.

International Business Machines fell back 1 5/8 to 120 3/4 as 1.7 million shares changed hands. It had risen 1 3/8 Monday following a well-received earnings report for the second quarter.

Other technology issues also lost ground, including Unisys, which dropped 3/4 to 12 1/4 on 1.5 million shares. Prudential-Bache Securities analyst Rick J. Martin attributed the sell-off to "false rumors" that Unisys was having trouble placing a debt offering and that an analyst was predicting the company might cut its dividend. Company officials couldn't be reached for comment on the speculation.

But Honeywell, whose second-quarter earnings from continuing operations climbed 54% from a year ago and were better than expected, rallied 3 5/8 to 110 3/4.

Manufacturers Hanover gained 3/4 to 31 5/8 despite reporting that second-quarter operating earnings dropped 12%. Separately, the Federal Reserve Board permitted the bank holding company to handle futures and options brokerage.

Other bank stocks to move higher in the wake of quarterly reports included NCNB, which gained 1 3/8 to 37 7/8; Wells Fargo, which went up 1 1/2 to 80 1/8, and Dime Savings Bank of New York, which added 3/8 to 6 3/8. Bank of New York, whose second-quarter earnings were off 39% from a year ago, fell 1 1/8 to 27 1/2.

Chase Manhattan rose 3/8 to 21 1/8 on 5.4 million shares, with dividend-related trades by institutions apparently responsible for the bulk of the activity. While the stock now carries a dividend yield of 11.6%, the company hasn't yet declared its next quarterly dividend, and there is speculation that Chase may reduce the payout because its operating earnings for the first and second quarters have fallen short of its dividend expense.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.19 to 364.39. Volume totaled 13,339,000 shares.

Divi Hotels advanced 5/8 to 2 3/4. The company is in discussions for the possible sale of its Caribbean resort-hotel business to Accor of France.
