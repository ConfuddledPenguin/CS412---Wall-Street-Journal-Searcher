Marketing & Media: NBC Entertainment Appoints Tartikoff As Chairman, Littlefield as President

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 07/18/90

Brandon Tartikoff, the programming wizard who led National Broadcasting Co. to ratings supremacy through the 1980s, was named to the new position of chairman of NBC Entertainment Group. As part of a general restructuring at the network's Burbank, Calif.-based entertainment operations, Mr. Tartikoff's longtime No. 2 executive, Warren Littlefield, was named president of NBC Entertainment, succeeding Mr. Tartikoff. Mr. Littlefield will have day-to-day responsibility for major programming decisions in the network's daytime, prime-time, late-night and children's television operations.

NBC said Mr. Tartikoff, 41 years old, will focus on the expansion of NBC Productions, the in-house unit that produces prime-time series, miniseries and made-for-television movies. NBC Productions has yet to produce a successful prime-time series. Mr. Tartikoff will also concentrate on "the development of other alliances and outlets for the creation and distribution of NBC programming," according to a network statement. NBC aims ultimately to produce and sell shows to other networks including overseas TV networks.

In a telephone interview, Mr. Tartikoff said that after 10 years at the helm of NBC Entertainment he had "diminished interest" in the job. "We're all human and we all need new things," he said, adding he began discussions for a new position in January with Robert C. Wright, NBC's president and chief executive officer.

"I also thought there was a parade of other opportunities going by while I was stuck in meetings with producers," he said. "Being head of a network entertainment division is not a gold watch job. I kept hearing the same show ideas over and over, and I kept feeling deja vu."

The executive changes come as NBC continues its prime-time leadership for the fifth consecutive year, although its winning margin has narrowed. NBC's audience for the 1989-90 season fell 9% from a year ago, and it hasn't produced a breakthrough hit for several years. More disturbing to NBC is that the network's audience slipped by 13% in demographic groups that advertisers want to attract. The network has a projected $200 million shortfall in advertising revenue because of the declining ratings.

"I'm aware we were slipping," Mr. Littlefield, 38, said in an interview. Referring to the network's trademark, Mr. Littlefield said: "The peacock has ruffled feathers. But there's a great challenge and that excites me."

Mr. Tartikoff's withdrawal from day-to-day operations at NBC Entertainment is a radical departure for the boyish-looking, tough-talking executive. He is known to carry around the prime-time schedule at all times, constantly fiddling with it. But Mr. Littlefield said he, not Mr. Tartikoff, will make the decisions. "I wouldn't have taken the job unless I had the authority to run the division," he said. "I made that clear in all my discussions."

Mr. Littlefield's contract expires in August, and Mr. Tartikoff and Mr. Wright, the NBC president, assured Mr. Littlefield that he shouldn't look outside the General Electric Co. unit for a job. "They said they would make it work," Mr. Littlefield said.

Mr. Littlefield said he will consult with Mr. Tartikoff on programming matters, much in the same way Mr. Tartikoff solicited opinions from Grant A. Tinker when Mr. Tinker was chairman and president of NBC.

As part of the NBC Entertainment restructuring, Perry Simon, who headed NBC's comedy series development, succeeds Mr. Littlefield as executive vice president of prime-time programs. And John Agoglia was named president of NBC Enterprises while continuing as executive vice president of NBC Productions.
