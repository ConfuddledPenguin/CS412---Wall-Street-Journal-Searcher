Bond Auction by Computer Satisfied Major Traders

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/18/90

NEW YORK -- The first computerized auction of high-yield junk bonds produced a trickle of trades yesterday, enough to satisfy some of the giant institutions that participated in the sale. The auction was organized by Joel Greenblatt, a 32-year-old money manager who founded New York Securities Auction Corp. this year. Mr. Greenblatt had recruited institutions such as Fidelity Investments, Mutual Shares Corp. and Trust Co. of the West to list bonds for sale.

People familiar with the auction said about $80 million face value of bonds were offered for sale yesterday. Many were distressed-company bonds that traded at big discounts to their face value. Estimates were that about 40% of the bonds attracted purchase bids, with about one-quarter of the bids leading to actual sales.

"I think we did pretty well," Mr. Greenblatt said. "We traded enough to cover our costs." He added that some Wall Street firms showed a willingness to use his system, even though it competes with their bond-trading desks.

"This is another tool for us" to use in bond trading, said Howard Marks, a managing director at Trust Co. of the West. Future weekly auctions may produce brisker volume, Mr. Marks predicted.
