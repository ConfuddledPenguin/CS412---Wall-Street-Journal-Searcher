Newmont Gold Cuts Output Costs, Pays Bank Debts

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 07/18/90

DENVER -- Newmont Gold Co. said it reduced its production costs at some of its operations and mines in Nevada, countering an industry trend of rising costs. It also said it will increase production of gold to 1.7 million ounces in 1990, up from a projected 1.54 million ounces. The company also said it repaid all its debt to its banks and its 90% owner, Newmont Mining Co., in the second quarter. Newmont Gold had about $83.7 million in short-term debt on March 31.

The company declined to estimate second-quarter earnings, but a spokesman said results will "probably be better than most estimates." Net income was $27.8 million, or 27 cents a share, on sales of about $139 million, a year earlier.

The nation's largest gold producer said it has increased productivity, lowered supply costs and improved recovery rates in its northern area mines and mills of the Carlin Trend, a rich gold-bearing formation in north-central Nevada. The company said its cash costs of producing an ounce of gold fell below the $224 reported for the first quarter.

For the second half, the company projects its cash costs per ounce of gold will average about $200, and the average cost for the year will be about $210. The company had projected its cash costs would be about $240 an ounce for the year.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading, Newmont Gold shares rose 75 cents to $45.25. Newmont Mining shares rose 62.5 cents to $46.75.

The reduction in mining cost "is very unique," said John Tumazos, analyst at Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette. He figures the average pretax cost of mining an ounce of gold (including cash costs and non-cash expenses) has increased steadily over the past three years to about $330 an ounce from about $250 an ounce. Gold mining costs have increased because of higher prices for cyanide and other reagents, higher taxes in Nevada, stricter environmental controls and higher exploration costs, he said.
