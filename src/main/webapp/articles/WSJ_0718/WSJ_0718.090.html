Wells Fargo to Buy Branches of Great American Bank

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 07/18/90

Wells Fargo & Co. agreed to acquire the California branches of Great American Bank for $492 million in a move that analysts said was a strategic coup for Wells. The transaction will also infuse desperately needed capital into Great American. The thrift said yesterday its remaining tangible capital will be nearly wiped out by a large second-quarter loss that it attributed to new regulatory standards for reserves, and Great American could still collapse. Under terms of the transaction, it will still be left with a huge amount of nonperforming loans that could eat up the money provided by Wells.

The sale, which is to occur in two stages and may not be complete until the middle of next year, covers as many as 130 branches -- considered the crown jewels of Great American's operations -- plus $6.4 billion in deposits and $5.9 billion in assets. The price amounts to a premium on deposits of about 6%, which is at the high end of what banks have been paying for deposits of troubled thrifts lately.

But the acquisition would give San Francisco-based Wells the leading market share among banks and S&Ls in retail deposits in the San Diego area, where Great American is based, a position that Wells and other major California banks have long coveted.

"It is a very opportunistic move," said Dick Fredericks, bank analyst at Montgomery Securities. "They get an attractive market share in an attractive market." Great American said it received three bids for the franchise and that Wells's was "clearly the best."

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange yesterday, Wells Fargo shares closed at $80.125, up $1.50. Great American shares, meanwhile, closed at $2.125, down $1.25.

For Great American, the sale of the California franchise was a last resort in response to federal regulatory pressure to raise capital. "We have shown there is capital-raising ability in this institution, and very hopefully we will be able to demonstrate that what is left is a viable institution," said Robert Kemper, chairman and chief executive officer of Great American.

But analysts consider the sale part of a process that will eventually lead Great American into federal hands.

Wells is basically taking the best parts of Great American and leaving its holders with about $2 billion in bad loans and real estate that would equal roughly 20% of Great American's total assets. The California sale to Wells, which is still subject to regulatory approval, would leave Great American with 81 retail offices in Arizona, Washington and Colorado and mortgage-loan-origination offices in California.
