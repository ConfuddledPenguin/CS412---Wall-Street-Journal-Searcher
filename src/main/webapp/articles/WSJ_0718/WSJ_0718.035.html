Industry Focus: Earnings of Big Three Auto Firms Fell Up to 54% in Period, Analysts Estimate

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 07/18/90

DETROIT -- The Big Three U.S. auto makers had their worst second quarter since the recession years of the early 1980s as their combined earnings dropped as much as 54%, analysts estimated. General Motors Corp., Ford Motor Co. and Chrysler Corp. were hurt as competition from Japanese auto makers forced them to squeeze profit margins in their core North American vehicle operations, analysts said. In addition, GM and Ford probably broke even or were unprofitable on their Brazilian operations, which have been hobbled by economic turmoil there.

Combined second-quarter profit at GM, Ford and Chrysler are expected to be $1.47 billion to $1.84 billion -- about half of the $3.2 billion combined profit a year earlier. That's the lowest the Big Three's combined profit has been for the April to June period since 1982. That year, combined profit was $869.2 million.

"It was a lousy quarter given that spring is usually very strong," said John A. Casesa, auto analyst at Wertheim Schroder & Co. in New York. The second quarter is often the strongest period of the year for auto industry earnings.

Weak sales are only part of the problem. So far this year, total U.S. car and truck sales are just 3.4% behind the year-ago period. But Japanese auto makers are winning a greater share of the shrinking market, forcing Detroit to continue profit-gobbling discount deals despite relatively tight inventories. Analysts estimate the Big Three still are spending about $400 more per vehicle on incentives than a year ago, even though rebate costs have eased somewhat since earlier this year.

"Industry sales aren't at recessionary levels but their earnings are close to it," said Ann Knight, auto analyst at PaineWebber Inc.

Just last month, the Big Three took a step to offset the expense of rebates by announcing broad, midyear price increases. Ford led the pack, and GM and Chrysler followed shortly thereafter.

Analysts say higher prices, coupled with tight inventories, could boost profits in the second half. "With inventories the best they've been in five years, rebates coming down and prices going up, this sets the stage for an upside surprise in the second half," said Scott Merlis, auto analyst with Morgan Stanley & Co.

But in the just-ended quarter, Ford and GM derived the bulk of their automotive profits from Europe, where the market is still relatively strong, analysts said.

At GM, analysts estimate second-quarter earnings tumbled as much as 48% to between $756 million, or $1.20 a share, and $900 million, or $1.30 a share. GM earned $1.45 billion, or $2.23 a share, a year ago.

GM may have done relatively better than its rivals because it managed to hold its share of the North American car market at 35.7%, virtually the same level as a year ago, and increase slightly its share of the European market, analysts said.

GM probably earned money on its North American vehicle operations in the quarter, but "from a profitability standard, GM is a European company," said Morgan Stanley's Mr. Merlis.

At Ford, second-quarter earnings fell as much as 60% to between $571 million, or $1.25 a share, and $752 million, or $1.65 a share, analysts said. The No. 2 auto maker reported earnings of $1.41 billion, or $3 a share, a year earlier.

Ford's biggest problem in the quarter was its U.S. passenger car business. After three years in a row of gains, Ford saw its share of the car market slide two percentage points to 20.8% in the first half. The redesigned Ford Escort, introduced in the second quarter, is off to a slow start and one-time gold mines, like the Ford Mustang, are losing luster as they age.

"They are feeling the heat from GM's aggressive pricing and Japanese competitors," said Mr. Casesa of Wertheim Schroder.

Ford also saw its share of the European market slip by a tenth of a percentage point, anaylsts said. But Europe remains a strong market for the auto maker, and the company is doing well in the U.S. truck market, analysts said. Its profitable new Explorer off-road vehicle, launched in March, is selling briskly.

Chrysler, which has no significant overseas operations, was hit hard from two directions in the second quarter, analysts said. Its share of the U.S. car market slumped to 8.9% in the first half from 10.6% a year ago. And competitors are introducing new minivans and off-road vehicles, such as Ford's Explorer, which threaten Chrysler's dominance in both those lucrative light truck markets.

The result: Chrysler's profit fell as much as 60% to as low as $138 million, or 60 cents a share, some analysts said. Others said Chrysler's profit could be as high as $190 million, or 85 cents a share. But that's still down sharply from $341 million, or $1.46 a share, a year earlier.

"What has been paying the bills at Chrysler has been strong sales of minivans and Jeeps, but it's difficult to continue to maintain {profit} margins in this competitive environment," said John V. Kirnan, auto analyst at Kidder, Peabody & Co.
