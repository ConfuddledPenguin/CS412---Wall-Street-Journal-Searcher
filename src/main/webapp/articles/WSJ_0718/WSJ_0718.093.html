Spending Plan For '91 Cleared By Senate Panel

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 07/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Appropriations Committee approved a fiscal 1991 budget plan that would wipe out new funds for foreign aid sought by the Bush administration and cut more than $2 billion from House-passed domestic-spending bills. The measure, adopted 24-4, bears the strong stamp of Chairman Robert Byrd (D., W.Va.). In the absence of a formal budget resolution, it will set the limits for spending bills covering almost half of the federal budget for the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1.

Total defense appropriations would be cut to an estimated $283.6 billion, or more than 6.3% below current levels. Though foreign aid would grow with inflation, the new limits are significantly more severe than in the House. Direct outlays for foreign aid in fiscal 1991 would be held to $12.6 billion, or $574 million below a House-passed foreign-operations bill, and the tighter budget gives the administration little or no leeway to restore funds it wants for its military-aid program.

Among domestic programs, Mr. Byrd had less freedom than his House counterparts in allocating increases among the major subcommittees that will begin drafting bills as early as today. Almost $800 million in new discretionary spending would be provided for labor, health, and education programs, for example. But senators still face difficult choices when matched against comparable House figures, which are $800 million higher in fiscal 1991 outlays.

Subcommittees responsible for environmental, housing, and law-enforcement programs will face the same political difficulty when compared with the House, and the tighter limits again hurt the Bush administration as it seeks to restore funds for its troubled space program and its new initiative to explore Mars.

The House cut practically all the money for the Mars project from its bill covering the space program. Senators will begin drafting their own version from a starting point $630 million below the House for not only space but environmental, housing and veterans' programs in fiscal 1991.

Republicans were most conspicuous in complaining about the cuts, while the Democratic majority, out of respect for or fear of their chairman, said little. Sen. Byrd, in turn, quietly helped allies on both sides -- and himself. The three subcommittees that fare best in comparison to the House include Sen. Byrd's own Interior panel, as well as those covering energy and water programs and the Treasury. On the latter two subcommittees, the senior Republicans are Oregon Sen. Mark Hatfield, the ranking GOP member on Appropriations, and New Mexico Sen. Pete Domenici, an important ally on the Senate Budget Committee in shaping the plan.

While the cuts from defense are comparable to those in the House, the resolution builds in some modest leeway by making $500 million more available for weapons and personnel accounts while cutting more deeply from military construction for foreign bases.
