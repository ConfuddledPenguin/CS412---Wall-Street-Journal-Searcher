United Telecommunications Shares Fall 24% on a 55% Drop in 2nd-Period Net

  • Author: Robert Daniels and John J. Keller
  • Date: 07/18/90

NEW YORK -- United Telecommunications Inc.'s stock plunged 24% after the company reported a 55% drop in second-quarter net income and a $42 million operating loss at its long-distance communications unit, US Sprint Communications Co. The poor showing by Sprint, the nation's third-largest long-distance service provider, stunned Wall Street analysts, who had been expecting a much smaller decline and had cut their estimates for the company last month.

United took a charge of $72 million, or 18 cents a share, to cover a 5% cut in Sprint's work force, a write-off of outdated software used in the company's computers and accruals for certain contract disputes.

"Obviously the Street got blindsided," said PaineWebber Inc. analyst Jack B. Grubman, who along with numerous other analysts had cut his estimates in June. "This is a material change in the company's earnings situation."

"More important than the operating loss," said analyst Robert B. Morris of Goldman, Sachs & Co., is that without the charge, Sprint's operating profit would have been $30 million, which is "at a minimum $60 million away from what the market was expecting."

Profit at United Telecom, based in Kansas City, Mo., fell to $40.6 million, or 18 cents a share, from $90.5 million, or 43 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue rose 12% to $2.07 billion from $1.85 billion.

The company's stock dropped $8.75 to $29.50 on heavy New York Stock Exchange composite volume of more than 11.8 million shares.

United Telecom had planned to buy the rest of Sprint from GTE Corp. by September. Now, a spokesman for United Telecom said that while the company would like to buy GTE's 19.9% stake this year, the purchase could be put off until next year or until Sprint's profit improves.

William T. Esrey, United Telecom's chairman and chief executive officer, pegged Sprint's showing to severe competition in the lower end of the long-distance market: residential and small-business customers. Results from service provided to large corporate users are meeting expectations, he said.

Sprint will be "shifting considerable additional people into sales and marketing," Mr. Esrey said, and will pare about 1,000 support and other staffers from its 20,000-member work force.

Analysts said Sprint is losing market share -- revenue grew just 2% in the second quarter from the first period -- to American Telephone & Telegraph Co. and the nation's No. 2 carrier, MCI Communications Corp. "Starting in the fourth quarter, MCI literally attacked the market," hiring some 500 sales and marketing people, said Mr. Morris, adding that AT&T "was right there, toe to toe" with MCI.

Even more worrisome was Sprint's lack of control over its costs, noted Mr. Grubman of PaineWebber. "Sprint spent $80 million more in the quarter to get about $25 million more in revenue," he said. That's not going to change. "With the loss in revenue momentum, they're going to have to spend even more on sales and marketing to win customers back."

Mr. Esrey said "we haven't reacted as fast as we should have to competitive pressures." He added, "I look at this as a serious setback" but one that the company is addressing aggressively. All areas will be targeted for cost cuts, he said, declining to elaborate.

The company's local phone operations were comparatively robust, posting 10% higher operating profit on a 4% revenue rise; the business is "performing quite well," Mr. Esrey said. The parent's complementary businesses, including a directory publisher and an equipment unit, saw 8.3% higher profit on 2% more revenue.
