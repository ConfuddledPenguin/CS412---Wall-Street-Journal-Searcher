Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Lesser Quits Ogilvy's New York Office

  • Author: Joanne Lipman
  • Date: 04/24/90

Michael Lesser, who joined Ogilvy & Mather barely a year ago to turn around its troubled New York office, resigned as the office's president after his yearlong turnaround effort failed. Mr. Lesser will be replaced by Julian Clopet, 49 years old, the highly regarded president and chief executive officer of Ogilvy's Canadian operations. Mr. Clopet will take on the larger title of president of North American operations, but will concentrate at first on fixing the New York office. He is a close associate of Ogilvy's world-wide chairman and chief executive, Graham Phillips, who himself had previously headed North American operations.

Mr. Lesser's resignation, which was widely expected and will take effect June 30, followed a disappointing year. When he joined Ogilvy from Lowe Marschalk, where he had been chief executive, Ogilvy's New York office was in poor shape. It hadn't won any sizable accounts in many months, and morale was sagging. One of its largest accounts, Kraft General Foods' $50 million Maxwell House account, was up for review.

In the year since Mr. Lesser joined, the office hasn't improved. Ogilvy, which was subsequently acquired by WPP Group PLC, went on to lose the Maxwell House business. Last month, the New York office lost the Seagram Wine Cooler business, which had billed $15 million in 1989. It hasn't had any significant account gains.

Lesser, 47, made several management changes which weren't uniformly popular. For example, he brought in creative heavyweight Bill Hamilton from Chiat/Day/Mojo. Mr. Hamilton, a strong-willed executive who has been attempting to spruce up Ogilvy's creative product, was seen by some staffers as arrogant. Mr. Hamilton declined comment, while Mr. Lesser couldn't be reached for comment.

"The biggest challenge Ogilvy faces is to turn around the New York office, and to date there's no visible evidence of a turnaround," notes Emma Hill, an analyst with Wertheim & Co. Since the New York office accounts for a large majority of Ogilvy's U.S. business, "it's an important operation to turn around," she says. Ogilvy's New York office has billings of $800 million, making it larger than most entire agencies.

Still, analysts and industry executives said Mr. Lesser may have faced an almost impossible job in his turnaround attempt. The New York office's slide happened over several years, and may well take more than a year to reverse, they said.

"It clearly couldn't be mended in a year," said Alan Gottesman, an analyst with PaineWebber. "But in less than a year, you can probably tell if you're heading in the right direction or not. I haven't seen any sign that they were."

Mr. Clopet, a 23-year Ogilvy veteran, has worked closely with Ogilvy's chairman, Mr. Phillips, for a number of years. The two were part of a turnaround team that reversed the fading fortunes of Ogilvy's Canadian operations in the 1970s.

Mr. Clopet and Mr. Phillips are "a team that's worked together before, that's had successes before. They have had a good business as well as personal relationship," said Kenneth Roman, former chairman and chief executive of Ogilvy, who currently is a senior executive at Ogilvy client American Express. Mr. Clopet "is a strong leader who's run a big operation. I think he's an excellent choice."

In a statement, Mr. Phillips commended Mr. Lesser on his work at Ogilvy and said he was "sad" to see him leave. He said Mr. Lesser was leaving because "Mike would, frankly, prefer to do something more entrepreneurial."
