Coca-Cola Sets Invasion of East Germany, Plans $140 Million in Investments There

  • Author: Glenn Ruffenach
  • Date: 04/24/90

ATLANTA -- Coca-Cola Co., hoping to match its huge success in West Germany, is starting a push into East Germany with $140 million in capital investments. The soft-drink giant said negotiations are under way with beverage organizations in East Germany to bottle and distribute Coca-Cola throughout the country. The $140 million would be used to upgrade machinery, trucks, vending machines and other sales equipment connected with as many as six bottling plants.

A Coca-Cola spokesman said negotiations are expected to be completed before the summer.

East Germany could represent a significant addition to Coke's highly profitable overseas operations. International sales today account for 80% of the company's soft-drink operating profit and 65% of soft-drink revenue. West Germany alone accounts for about 12% of Coke's earnings, said Emanuel Goldman, an analyst with PaineWebber Research in San Francisco.

By contrast, Coke's domestic sales have been lackluster. While the company's international volume grew by 9% in the first quarter, retail case sales in the U.S. increased 3%, with fountain volume rising a slim 1%.

Coke first moved to take advantage of the East German market last fall when the Berlin Wall began to crumble, offering free soft drinks to people crossing the border. Since mid-February, Coke has sold more than two million cases of soft drinks in East Germany, shipped into the country from bottling plants in West Germany and France. By comparison, the company sold about four million cases in Poland in all of 1989.

The fact that Coke is already a known commodity in East Germany -- its West German television ads have been seen by East German viewers for 30 years-and the existence of bottling facilities in the country mean that Coke should be able to break into the market more easily and more quickly than it would normally in a foreign country.

"The demand is there, and the awareness is there," said a Coke spokesman. "But up until now, there's just been an inability to buy the product."

Added Mr. Goldman at PaineWebber: "The key is availability. If there's one thing Coca-Cola has shown it can do, it's making the product available and affordable. If they can meld the two operations (in East and West Germany), there's a potential for substantial profits."

The company's move into East Germany comes on the heels of PepsiCo Inc.'s new trade agreement with the Soviet Union. The Purchase, N.Y., company plans to spend more than $1 billion to upgrade and expand bottling plants and to provide concentrate, among other things. The accord could double Pepsi's sales in the Soviet Union during the next 10 years. The company sold about 40 million cases there in 1989. Coke sold about 20 million cases of all its products, including Fanta orange drink, in the Soviet Union.

While Pepsi claims to have a substantial lead over Coke in most of Eastern Europe, Pepsi acknowledges that East Germany is the one country in the bloc where Coke is dominant. A Pepsi spokesman said yesterday, however, that the company is "aggressively pursuing" franchise operations in East Germany and expects to reach agreements with some East German bottlers in the "next few weeks." The spokesman declined to elaborate.
