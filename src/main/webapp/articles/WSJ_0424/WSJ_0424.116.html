Groups of Disabled, Builders Oppose Apartments Plan

  • Author: Joe Davidson
  • Date: 04/24/90

WASHINGTON -- A group of disabled people has joined forces with home builders to oppose proposed federal guidelines that would make apartment buildings more accessible to the handicapped. The National Coordinating Council on Spinal Cord Injury and the National Association of Home Builders complained that the guidelines, which were written by the Department of Housing and Urban Development and are still being reviewed by the White House, are "impractical, inflexible and too costly."

But there is division among the disabled. Bonnie Milstein, co-head of the housing task force of the Consortium of Citizens with Disabilities, said, "Most groups that represent people who use {wheel}chairs support HUD's guidelines."

Jack Powell, president of the spinal injury council and executive director of the Paralyzed Veterans of America, said he opposes the guidelines because they would make housing too expensive, thus denying accessibility through high cost.

"Affordability is a central part of the accessibility equation," Mr. Powell said in a statement released yesterday. "Persons with disabilities -- like most other people -- have limited budgets to meet their housing expenses. If HUD's design guidelines result in significant increases in the cost of housing, then we have really defeated the purpose of the Fair Housing Amendments Act of 1988 and created another barrier to accessibility."

The guidelines were prepared to carry out the 1988 legislation, which prohibited housing discrimination against the disabled. New multifamily buildings with four or more units and occupied after March 13, 1991, would be covered.

Gordon Mansfield, HUD's assistant secretary for fair housing and equal opportunity, said the opponents of the guidelines base their opposition on "misunderstandings on their part as to what we're doing."

Mr. Powell estimated the HUD proposals would add $1,300 to $3,700 to the cost of each walk-up unit and $3,200 to $4,250 to the cost of each mid-rise to high-rise unit. "During the congressional debate in 1988, it was estimated by leaders in the House and Senate that the costs for complying with this legislation would run less than $500 per unit," Mr. Powell said.

Mr. Mansfield, a former official of the Paralyzed Veterans of America, said the cost complaint is a "red herring." An analysis of the HUD guidelines indicates they would add 1% to the cost of housing, he said. "That is not an undue burden to the building industry or people with disabilities," he added.

The home builders and the spinal cord council have their own set of guidelines that they say would be less expensive, while in some cases providing greater accessibility than HUD's. HUD's guidelines would require every bathroom in a unit to be larger than currently required, but not necessarily fully accessible to wheelchairs.

The protesting groups say at least one bathroom should be fully accessible, but each one doesn't have to be as large as HUD wants. "The issue here is whether you need the extra maneuvering space in the second bathroom or powder room," said Thomas D. Davies Jr., an architect for the Paralyzed Veterans of America.
