BONN PROPOSED a generous plan for monetary union with East Germany. In a bid to calm East German fears, the West German government unveiled the outlines of a plan for merging the two nations' currencies. Under the proposal, individual East German wages and savings of as much as $2,373 would be changed into West German marks at a favorable one-to-one rate. The offer is considered a major concession to East Berlin, which argued that workers need to be cushioned during a transition to a market economy.

East Germany said it would give more than $4 million to aid survivors of the World War II Holocaust, in which the Nazis killed six million Jews.

THE U.S. IS PRESSING its efforts to win freedom for all hostages in the Mideast.

While reiterating its refusal to make any deals, the White House said it was continuing "to probe all sources of information" about seven missing Americans, and Bush renewed his call for their "unconditional" release. In West Germany, Polhill, who was freed Sunday by pro-Iranian extremists, underwent a physical examinaton and doctors said he was malnourished and mildly dehydrated. The 55-year-old New Yorker had been held for more than three years.

The foreign minister of Iran, which joined Syria in helping arrange Polhill's release, reportedly called for Israel to free a Shiite cleric abducted in July.

NEPAL'S POLICE BATTLED mobs, threatening a transition to democracy.

In the first major violence since the Himalayan kingdom lifted a ban on political parties two weeks ago, police opened fire on demonstrators after crowds rampaged in Katmandu. At least 11 people were reported killed, including six police officers beaten to death by protesters. The mobs accused security forces of condoning a monarchist group blamed for a wave of crime aimed at undermining Nepal's four-day-old government. A curfew was imposed on the capital.

Prime Minister K.P. Bhattarai, who has said Nepal may be headed for a constitutional monarchy, met with King Birendra to review the situation.

Lithuania closed the Baltic republic's only oil refinery because of the Kremlin's cutoff of energy supplies. The prime minister said the republic, which last month declared its independence from Moscow, may try to sell gold to import fuel, and the president said Gorbachev should renounce the use of Soviet troops in Lithuania.

Bush plans to meet today with congressional leaders to discuss a U.S. response to the Soviet Union's standoff with Lithuania. The White House also welcomed what it called signs of flexibility from Moscow, which said Soviet leaders were willing to open talks with the republic if it froze for two years its move toward secession.

---

The Supreme Court rejected an appeal by an Oregon woman who doesn't want to share parental rights with a California man who donated his sperm for artificial insemination. The justices refused to block a trial in which the man would seek to prove he acted only after the woman said she would let him help raise the child, a girl born in 1986.

---

NASA prepared for today's scheduled liftoff of the space shuttle Discovery, the second attempt at launching the spacecraft, which is to place the Hubble Space Telescope into orbit. The space agency said the mechanical problems that grounded the shuttle April 10 have been repaired, and forecasters predicted favorable weather.

---

China's Premier Li arrived in the Soviet Union and met with Premier Ryzhkov, who said the two nations would cooperate on nuclear energy and space research. The trip by Li, the first Chinese head of government to visit Moscow in 26 years, is aimed at promoting ties and easing border tensions.

---

Sudan's military leader said loyalist forces crushed a rebellion by junior military officers in the African nation, the second such attempt in a month against Khartoum's ruling junta. Two retired army officers were accused of plotting the revolt and arrested. No casualties were reported.

---

A center-right coalition claimed victory in Sunday's multiparty parliamentary elections in Yugoslavia's republic of Croatia. In neighboring Slovenia, a progressive Communist won a presidential runoff election.

---

Post-Earth Day demonstrators blocked traffic and overturned trash bins along Wall Street to protest corporate involvement in pollution. About 200 people were arrested. Trading began on schedule. At the Pacific Stock Exchange in San Francisco, 49 people were arrested during a similar protest.
