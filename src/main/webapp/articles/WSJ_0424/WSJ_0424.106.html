Foreign Exchange: Dollar Moves Higher Against the Mark On Rates, Fears About German Union

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/24/90

NEW YORK -- U.S. traders followed the lead of Japanese and European foreign exchange markets in bidding the dollar higher yesterday on rising U.S. interest rates and new worries about the effects of the monetary union of the Germanys. The dollar briefly tested the 1.70-mark level three times during the New York day before falling back on a lack of follow-through buying.

But trading was termed thin, with participants hesitant to push the dollar aggressively higher after seeing it repeatedly fail to hold gains against the mark and yen in recent sessions. The market used the scheduled release of U.S. economic data today and Friday as an excuse to avoid taking new positions, said Ty Tan, a dealer at Deutsche Genossenschaftsbank in New York.

Long-term Treasury bond yields continued to inch toward the psychologically important 9% level. Late in the New York business day, the yield had risen to 8.96% from Friday's 8.94%.

Durable goods orders for March, due for release today, are seen up 1.7% from February, and the preliminary report on first-quarter gross national product, estimated to show a 2.3% annual growth rate, is scheduled for release Friday.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6995 marks, up from 1.6875 marks late Friday, and at 157.60 yen, up from 157.45 yen late Friday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6356, down from $1.6388 Friday.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the dollar was quoted at 158.05 yen, up from Monday's Tokyo close of 157.55 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.7025 marks.

On Monday, the U.S. unit's strength against the mark derived from news that the West German government had outlined plans to convert East German mark-denominated wages and pensions at a one-toone rate with the West German mark, participants said. Over the weekend, the conversion rate had been brought into the spotlight again by a report in Der Spiegel magazine forecasting a broader application of the one-to-one rate than that proposed by the West German central bank.

The mark also was hurt by a 2.5% plunge in prices on the Frankfurt stock market.

That the dollar is testing 1.70 marks is of more psychological importance than anything else, said Thomas Palladino, a vice president at Amsterdam-Rotterdam Bank N.V. in New York.

The market has switched its focus from the dollar-yen rate to dollar-mark dealings as monetary union creeps closer and the troubles in Lithuania highlight the dollar's role as a safe-haven currency, Mr. Palladino said.

Concern about the inflation that might result in West Germany from the conversion of so many East German marks to West German marks at a one-to-one rate also prompted selling of the mark against other currencies, particularly the yen, participants said.

After falling from 93.35 yen late Friday in New York to 92.99 yen at the close of Tokyo trading yesterday, the mark slipped further to 92.89 yen at the London close yesterday, finishing the global day in New York at 92.70 yen.

With activity firmly fixed on the dollarmark rate, dealings in the dollar-yen area remained static, traders said.

The U.S. currency is seen remaining stuck in a narrow band against the yen, with no large moves likely until the level of Japanese participation in the Treasury's refunding is revealed next month.

There is some fear in the market that Japanese investors might scale back their participation in the refunding from previous auctions. This anxiety stems from rumors that the Japanese Ministry of Finance requested insurance companies to restrain their purchases of dollar-denominated assets.

Tokyo participants said yesterday they expect Japanese participation at the U.S. auction to be lower than previously, but not markedly so.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $378.90 an ounce, up $2.30. Estimated volume was a moderate 2.6 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $378.25 an ounce.
