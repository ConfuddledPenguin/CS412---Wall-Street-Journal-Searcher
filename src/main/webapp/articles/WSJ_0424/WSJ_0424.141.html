World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Finish Lower on Profit-Taking; Frankfurt Shares Plunge; London Also Is Down

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 04/24/90

Tokyo stocks finished lower in lackluster trading Monday as the Nikkei index fluctuated in a fairly narrow range, pushed lower in the afternoon by some profit-taking on domestic-demand-related issues, traders said. London stocks were notably lower and at Frankfurt share prices plunged 2.5% in moderate but nervous trading in the market's steepest one-day drop since a 2.9% decline January. The Frankfurt market declined on worries about growing militancy on the part of the country's largest union, IG Metall, and concerns about a new proposal by the West German government to convert more East German marks into Deutsche marks at a one-to-one exchange rate. Traders also cited a fall in bond market prices and prospects for higher interest rates in West Germany and abroad as contributing factors to the market's steep descent.

Most other major world markets also finished with losses.

At Tokyo, the Nikkei index of 225 selected issues retreated 156.37 points to close at 29679.07. The index lost 109.97 points Friday.

In Tokyo Tuesday, the Nikkei index was down 333.66 points to 29345.41 at the end of the morning session.

Monday's volume on the First Section was estimated at only 330 million shares, down sharply from 687 million Friday.

Losing issues outnumbered winners, 583 to 357, while 169 remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index (Topix) of all issues listed in the First Section, which rose 0.67 point Friday, lost 12.96 points, or 0.59%, to 2201.20.

The Second Section index, which added 10.77 points Friday, lost 12.35 points, or 0.35%, to close at 3549.85.

Volume in the Second Section was estimated at 15 million shares, up from 10 million Friday.

Observers said there's strong resistance for the Tokyo Stock Price Index at around 2250 points, at which level heavy broad-based selling can be expected.

But "the resistance in the short term is less severe on the Nikkei," whose near-term ceiling is seen at 30000 points, because arbitrage activity is likely to bid blue-chip issues higher and keep the index afloat, one trader at a foreign securities firm said.

Traders said that there was little incentive to buy or to sell Monday, despite the relative stability of macroeconomic factors such as the yen and bond prices. The market's lackluster and somewhat wary sentiment was reflected in the day's scant activity.

"I was expecting more volume today," said Yusuke Fujiwara, head of trading at Baring Securities Ltd. Although the dollar-yen rate "wasn't moving much, I guess people are kind of cautious," he said.

But some investors were taking the opportunity to take profits whenever there were small price gains in selected sectors, traders said. Because the market will be closed Monday, Thursday and Friday next week for Golden Week, investors are likely to want to close long positions ahead of this weekend, they said.

Moreover, "futures slid down consistently most of the day, indicating that the market is probably in a profit-taking mode for the time being" rather than being manipulated by index-related trading, said one trader at Nomura Securities Co.

Activity should remain limited to such small-lot profit-taking in the short term, traders said.

"No one's going to do any real aggressive buying" ahead of Golden Week, the trader at Nomura added.

Large, domestic-demand-related stocks, such as steel, electric power and trading companies, were softer on profit-taking Monday.

Traders said that the "Big Four" Japanese securities firms were supporting high-tech electricals from falling too low Monday. Hitachi slipped 20 yen (13 cents) to 1,560 yen ($9.91), Kyocera lost 50 to 7,220, Pioneer was down 80 to 5,970, Sony fell 30 to 8,230 and TDK fell 90 to 6,810.

Kawasaki Steel slipped 2 to 600, Kobe Steel fell 8 to 593, Nippon Steel retrenched 8 to 590 and Daido Steel fell 18 to 799.

Tokyo Electric Power lost 80 to 4,110, while Kansai Electric fell 50 to 3,380.

Chubu Electric was down 30 to 3,180.

London share prices were lower, led down on bearish sentiment, a weak start on Wall Street and a handful of special situations.

Stocks were depressed from the outset in the wake of a savage attack on the banking sector in local press reports, dealers said.

Additionally, late-morning money supply figures added to the market's misfortunes; the figures showed a #9.2 billion ($15.09 billion) rise in bank lending for the latest month, much higher than the #7.5 billion expected.

These new money supply data for March showed that while domestic demand remains relatively buoyant, the country's corporate sector is coming under increasing pressure from the high level of base rates. The figures "took the market from being just slightly down to being really down," said one director of equities.

And although weak from the start, London shares descended more energetically on a disappointing start to Wall Street, where the Dow Jones Industrial Average was off 33.34 points at the close of the European day.

At the close, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index was down 27.9 points at 2159.2, just above the intraday minimum of 2159.1 and well below the opening best of 2185.1.

At its final tally, the narrower FT 30 share index was down 19.9 points at 1692.7. Volume amounted to a sparse 382.6 million shares, down from 410.7 million Friday.

Traders also suggested that activity remained thin as the market awaited Wednesday's release of U.K. balance of payments data for March.

In corporate developments, Hammerson shares were down on news of its results showing a 0.3% increase in pretax profit. The shares shed 23 pence (38 cents) to 720 pence ($11.80).

Clearing banks were among the hardest hit, as some suggested that the money supply data combined with poor results from banks in the U.S. and negative local press comment to knock banks' share prices.

Barclays dropped 18 pence to 519 in heavy dealing of six million shares. National Westminster shed 16 to 311, Midland Bank was off 6 to 310 and Lloyds lost 9 to 255. Dealers said program trades contributed to the weakness in the sector's prices.

Eurotunnel, which reported a long-expected increase in cost estimates for the channel tunnel project that will force a rights issue and additional bank lending, saw its shares drop as much as 10 pence before finishing 5 higher at 578.

Elsewhere, stock prices closed lower in Paris, Brussels, Amsterdam and Milan and mixed in Stockholm. Zurich was closed for a local holiday. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Share prices closed lower in Hong Kong, Seoul, Sydney, Singapore and Manila, were mixed in Wellington and higher in Taipei.
