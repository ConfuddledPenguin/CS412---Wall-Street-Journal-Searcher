Goldsmith Ends Attempt to Buy B.A.T Amid Public Worries on Leveraged Bids

  • Author: Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 04/24/90

LONDON -- Sir James Goldsmith threw in the towel in his quest for B.A.T Industries PLC, in what would have been the second-largest takeover in history. Sir James's Hoylake Investments Ltd. said it wouldn't renew its bid for the London-based tobacco, financial services, retailing and paper company. Hoylake's original #13 billion ($21.31 billion) bid, launched last July, lapsed last September under British takeover law. Hoylake gave no reason for its decision.

But it is clear that the Anglo-French Sir James, one of the most flamboyant corporate raiders of the past decade, again has fallen victim to a shift in official and public attitudes toward hostile bids, particularly highly leveraged proposals. And, as part of the change, individual U.S. states are increasingly flexing their muscles as anti-takeover champions.

Many in the international investment community said the fatal blow against Hoylake was struck in California on April 10 when the state insurance commissioner barred a change in control of B.A.T's Farmers Group Inc. insurance unit. Hoylake would have sold Farmers to a French insurance concern, Axa-Midi Assurances, for $4.5 billion if it had won control of B.A.T. But California insurance commissioner Roxani Gillespie said that the leverage of the deal would have endangered Farmers.

Taking stock of the changing mood toward hostile takeovers, Ron Freeman, head of international investment banking at Salomon Brothers Inc., said: "In the 1980s, all mergers and acquisitions were good. Now, in some people's minds, no M&A is good. Neither position is correct."

Mr. Freeman, whose firm wasn't involved in the bitter 10-month Hoylake-B.A.T fight, added: "The federal court system in the U.S. went too far in one direction, when it said that the only relevant constituency was the shareholders. But now states are becoming much more dominant, and are moving in the other direction," toward protecting corporate boards and employees.

Moreover, for foreign companies, a hurdle encountered more often is the flag-waving sometimes associated with such a takeover attempt. For example, in the $1.64 billion hostile bid by London-based BTR PLC for Worcester, Mass.-based Norton Co., the Union Jack was burned on the steps of Worcester's city hall last month by Norton workers fearing they would lose their jobs if the takeover goes through.

Ironically, it was to escape this wave that Sir James cashed in his U.S. chips in 1987 and moved back across the Atlantic to concentrate on European targets. He had been doused by the first groundswell in 1986 when he abandoned a bid for Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co. after workers of the Akron, Ohio, company demonstrated on the U.S. Capitol steps against foreign takeovers.

But the Atlantic has proven no shield for Sir James. Axa-Midi said it would withdraw its change-of-control applications in all nine states where Farmers operates. "We are naturally disappointed," said Claude Bebear, Axa chairman, who added that the French company remains "steadfastly committed to gaining a position in the U.S. insurance market."

Sir James couldn't be reached for comment.

However, in a conversation last September, he said that his strategy had gone awry early. The strategy was to launch an all-paper bid, thereby initially avoiding finance fees; get regulatory clearance in the U.S.; and then inject cash into the bid.

But he said he was surprised at the resistance of state insurance regulators. Sir James believed that the insurance commissioners would be glad to see Farmers divested from a tobacco company, and thought that B.A.T would be persona non grata because of the long fight it had with regulators when it took over Farmers in 1988.

Sir James conceded that a loss would damage his prestige. "If I lose," he said, "obviously it's because I made a mistake. If you get caught between two cars, it's your fault."

At 850 pence a share, the Hoylake bid would have made a B.A.T takeover second in size only to the $25 billion acquisition of RJR Nabisco Inc. by Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co. in 1988. Made last July 11 when B.A.T shares were at 694 pence, the bid caused the stock to soar as high as 936 pence last year.

But lately the stock has been receding as Hoylake's hopes grew dimmer. B.A.T shares closed in London yesterday at 724 pence, down 4 pence. The Hoylake announcement was made after the market closed.

With 1% of B.A.T, Hoylake has made enough to more than offset about #30 million in legal and other costs. But its paper profit apparently won't approach the $93 million Sir James made when he sold his Goodyear shares back to the company in 1986.

The Hoylake bid galvanized B.A.T into making major changes that the company said it was planning anyway. Reducing itself to a tobacco/financial-services company, B.A.T among other things last Friday sold Marshall Field's, the major Chicago retailer, to Dayton Hudson Corp. for $1.04 billion, and soon will sell its Saks Fifth Avenue retail chain.

In typical Sir James style, Hoylake's brief statement concluded: "The directors of Hoylake wish B.A.T and its unbundled offspring great success within their new structures."

But the changes, a B.A.T spokesman claimed, were "the turning point" in the company's battle against Hoylake.

"I am pleased that Hoylake and Axa-Midi have finally abandoned their bid," Patrick Sheehy, B.A.T chairman, said yesterday. "We can now devote our full attention to running the continuing group based on our financial-services and tobacco businesses."
