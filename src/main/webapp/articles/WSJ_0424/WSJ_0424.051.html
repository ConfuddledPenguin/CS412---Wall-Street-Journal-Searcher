International: Eurotunnel PLC Again Increases Cost Estimates

  • Author: Tim Carrington
  • Date: 04/24/90

LONDON -- Eurotunnel PLC once again raised the cost estimates for the cross-channel link and unveiled the outlines of a new financing plan. But in raising the estimated cost of the project to as much as #7.6 billion ($12.46 billion), Eurotunnel's chief executive officer, Alastair Morton, promised that the bad news should be coming to an end. "We don't intend to go through this process again," he said.

Investors will likely remain skeptical, because of the tunnel's history of revised cost estimates and funding arrangements. In 1987, managers of the Anglo-French project set the total cost at about #4.9 billion. More recently, Transmanche-Link, or TML, a consortium of 10 contractors, came in with hefty cost overruns that pushed the estimated cost to #7.2 billion.

The current plan for shoring up the financing involves an equity rights offering of as much as #500 million. Before that, however, Eurotunnel will have to arrange new loans of as much as #2 billion with a consortium of 208 banks.

Eurotunnel officials said that under the new plan, funds available for the project, including lines of credit, should amount to #8 billion to #8.5 billion. Mr. Morton stressed that this funding should provide an adequate cushion against future cost overruns.

Analysts said they believe the project is firming up its finances, but they also think uncertainties will plague the tunnel, and its shareholders, through the summer. "What are the banks going to demand in the talks in June?" asked one analyst in the City. "It's not a done deal."

Another uncertainty, noted William Beazington, an analyst with Shearson Lehman Hutton Securities in London, is whether the Thatcher government will resolve questions over the rail links between London and Folkestone, the English terminus for the channel tunnel. "Unfortunately, it's seen as a blot on the landscape," he said. These concerns have created snarls in deciding on how the connecting rail lines would be routed.

The success of the bank agreement, along with the Thatcher government's plans for the connecting rail links, will have a major impact on the success of the equity rights offering.

Meanwhile, actual construction of the tunnel is apparently proceeding more smoothly than the financing. The tunnel is half completed, having started the year only one-third complete.
