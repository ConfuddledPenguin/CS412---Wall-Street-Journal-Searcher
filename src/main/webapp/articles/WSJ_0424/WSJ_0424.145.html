THIRD WORLD'S CHILD LABOR

Millions of children are working the streets of Third World cities, shining shoes, washing cars or prostituting themselves, the International Labor Organization reported.

"Youngsters working as household domestic servants may be the most vulnerable and exploited children of all and the most difficult to protect," the ILO said.

The 60-page ILO report said improvements in industrialized nations had yet to penetrate the Third World, where areas of child exploitation remained hidden from public scrutiny. "Their existence is often kept under a tight veil of secrecy and isolation," it said.

Among positive developments, the ILO reported a decline of child labor in mills and factories of mainstream industries, chiefly because modern technology required skilled or semiskilled manpower.

CHINA'S LISTLESS ECONOMY

China's listless economy won't begin to recover until the second half of the year, jeopardizing the nation's industrial-growth target of 5% to 6% this year, said Zhang Zhongji of the State Statistical Bureau. He said that the economy had failed to respond to pump-priming measures during the first quarter and that loans intended to bolster slumping industries had contributed mainly to further overstocking of unsold products. "There has been no fundamental change in . . . stagnation in industrial production and poor economic efficiency," Mr. Zhang said.

EUROPE'S LARGEST GAS STATION

Deutsche BP AG will build a gas station in Dresden, East Germany, that will be the largest gas station in Europe, according to the East German news agency ADN. The West German unit of British Petroleum PLC will also be the first West German company to build a gas station in East Germany when it constructs a 48-pump station near the Dresden-North Autobahn. The station will be under a 5,000-square-foot roof and will offer eight different types of fuel, ADN said.

SONY TO INCREASE IMPORTS

Sony Corp. announced a plan to increase imports and overseas production over the next four years. Joining other Japanese companies that recently announced similar programs, Sony said it intends to increase its imports 3.5 times by the end of fiscal 1993, to 120 billion yen ($762.6 million) from their fiscal 1989 level of 34.3 billion yen. Imports would include Sony color televisions from the U.S., audio equipment from Asia and audio and video software from the company's CBS Records Inc. and Columbia Pictures Entertainment Inc. subsidiaries.

CANADA'S GREEN INDICATORS

The Canadian government has established a task force to develop a set of 20 to 30 "green" indicators that will give the public a regular report on the state of the environment. Canadian officials said the indicators could ultimately be linked with traditional economic statistics to help illustrate whether an economic upturn is being made at the expense of the environment. Anne Kerr, heading the task force for Environment Canada, said it's doubtful the group will come up with an overall "green index" but instead plans to devise several indicators to track such basics as the quality of water and air.

`ECONOMIC TERRORISTS' IN IRAN

To combat consumer shortages, Iran's parliament approved the death penalty for people convicted of hoarding, profiteering and black-market dealings, Tehran Radio reported. The broadcast said that "if profiteering or hoarding is meant to counter the Islamic Republic's establishment, the profiteer and hoarder will be executed." Iranian officials call such individuals "economic terrorists."

DOING BUSINESS WITH VIETNAM

The Asia-Pacific Council of American Chambers of Commerce adopted a resolution urging the Bush administration to lift restrictions preventing Americans from doing business with Vietnam.

The council, which represents 19 chamber chapters in the Asia-Pacific area, said it supported "immediate removal of restrictions on U.S. trade and investment with Vietnam." The economic sanctions, it said, "severely impact American competitiveness in the region."

There has been only one known exception to the U.S. embargo, which has blocked trade since the fall of South Vietnam's U.S.-supported government in 1975. In January, the Atlanta-based Cable News Network, a unit of Turner Broadcasting System Inc., was allowed to provide ground receivers that will enable Vietnam to pick up CNN's satellite signals.

The Bush administration is adamant that ties with Vietnam stay severed until, among other steps, Hanoi agrees to on-site verification that Vietnamese soldiers have left Cambodia.
