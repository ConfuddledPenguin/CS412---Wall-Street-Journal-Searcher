Dividend News: Philadelphia Electric Reduces Dividend by 45%

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 04/24/90

PHILADELPHIA -- Philadelphia Electric Co. slashed its quarterly common stock dividend 45% and announced an aggressive $100 million cost-cutting program to offset a rate increase that was disallowed by state regulators last week. The company also reported a first-quarter net loss of $60 million, or 40 cents a share. The loss was caused primarily by a one-time charge of $250 million, or $1.18 a share. The charge stems from various investments disallowed by the Pennsylvania Public Utilities Commission.

The quarterly dividend was cut to 30 cents a share from 55 cents. The utility's management had been warning investors for more than a year that a dividend cut was likely if the company didn't receive the $549 million rate increase it had requested to recoup its investment in building and operating its Limerick No. 2 nuclear power station.

Yet Philadelphia Electric shares fell 75 cents to $15.75 as more than 1.1 million shares changed hands in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday.

Doris Kelley Aloston at Merrill Lynch & Co. said the new lower dividend "is secure. It {Philadelphia Electric} can manage to pay it long term."

Daniel Scotto, a bond analyst with Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp., said the dividend cut should save the company about $208 million annually. He estimated that such savings should pump up the company's cash flow, which is good news for Philadelphia Electric's bondholders. Mr. Scotto upgraded his bond rating on the utility after the dividend cut and cost-reduction program were announced.

Joseph F. Paquette Jr., Philadelphia Electric's chairman and chief executive officer, said the company believes it can achieve the proposed $100 million in expense cuts without having to lay off non-management employees or cut their salaries. However, the company's top 70 to 80 executives won't be spared. Their salaries will be cut between 2% to 10%, including a 10% pay cut for Mr. Paquette. The retainer fee for directors also will be reduced by 10%.

Philadelphia Electric plans to reduce staff through an early-retirement plan and by cutting back on contract workers. Mr. Paquette said some $70 million to $80 million of its cost-reduction plan will come from labor savings. The rest should be achieved through more efficient purchasing practices. The company also plans to reduce its capital spending, but it hasn't targeted specific areas yet.

Mr. Paquette said a second phase of the cost-cutting program, which the company will undertake probably in early 1992, will entail an analysis of its work activity to find various tasks that can be reorganized or consolidated, allowing the utility to reduce workers without sacrificing productivity or service. Philadelphia Electric doesn't have an estimate on the savings it'll achieve under the second phase.
