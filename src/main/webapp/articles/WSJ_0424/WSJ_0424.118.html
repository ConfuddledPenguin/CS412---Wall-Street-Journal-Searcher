Heard on the Street: GM Class E Stock Has Taken Some Lumps, But Dip Considered as Buying Opportunity

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 04/24/90

NEW YORK -- General Motors Class E shares, which give investors a secondhand interest in GM's huge Electronic Data Systems subsidiary, have been roughed up a bit in the past week and a half. Just 12 days ago, the Class E shares hit a record high of 31 1/4. They since have dropped 8%, closing yesterday at 28 3/4, down 1/4. Besides general stock-market weakness, the decline reflects new uncertainty about a big Texas Air contract that investors had previously thought was in the bag.

But money managers who like EDS's long-term prospects say the dip may be a buying opportunity. They point out that EDS is the largest company in the fast-growing business of running other companies' computer operations.

"There's a tremendous move on the part of corporations in the past year and a half to outsource their MIS {management information systems} departments," says James Craig, portfolio manager of Janus Fund. "You turn over a nightmare to somebody who does it for you. It's such a huge fixed capital investment, and these guys can do it so well."

The outsourcing ball really got rolling when Eastman Kodak decided to do it, says Stephen T. McClellan, analyst with Merrill Lynch. "There used to be a real stigma to farming out your data processing," he says. "Since Eastman Kodak did it last fall, there's no longer a stigma. It's a snowball rolling downhill."

Accordingly, Mr. McClellan rates EDS a strong buy, both for the short term and long term. He expects EDS to earn $2.07 a share this year, up from $1.81 last year. For 1991 he predicts earnings of $2.37 a share.

EDS is also popular with other Wall Street analysts. Of 13 who have a formal opinion, 11 rate it a buy, according to Zacks Investment Research, Chicago. That's a mixed blessing since stocks rated as buys by the largest number of analysts tend to underperform the market, some research shows.

EDS, however, looks as if it's going from strength to strength. The percentage of its revenue derived from work for General Motors should fall below 50% this year for the first time since GM acquired EDS in 1984. EDS is the biggest factor in the corporate data processing market, ahead of such competitors as International Business Machines and the consulting arm of the accounting firm Arthur Andersen.

It has signed big long-term contracts -- ranging from $232 million to $600 million -- with such customers as Enron, First City Bancorp. of Texas, Great Western Bank and Freeport-McMoRan. The Texas Air contract, however, would dwarf those. As envisioned in an EDS announcement in February, EDS would run the computers for Texas Air's System One reservation system. The contract would run 10 years and bring in $4 billion.

Investors are worried about whether the woes of Texas Air subsidiary Eastern Airlines -- it's in bankruptcy-court proceedings and a trustee has been appointed to run it -- will derail that fat contract or at least force a reduction in its amount.

Yesterday an EDS spokesman said that the negotiations with Texas Air are still on track and that "nothing's really changed in light of recent revelations about Eastern."

Asked whether the $4 billion figure might have to be modified, the spokesman declined to comment specifically. "That gets into the realm of speculation," he said. The draft contract "doesn't lend itself to easy breakouts."

Eastern has "real problems. That's caused {EDS} stock to get hit," says Mr. Craig of Janus Fund. "But that's just one of multitudes of opportunities for them." Mr. Craig has bought 730,000 shares of the stock since the beginning of December.

Two drawbacks of the Class E shares are that they aren't cheap (selling for about 15 times the past 12 months' earnings) and that they aren't a true common stock. General Motors owns EDS. What Class E shareholders get is an earmarked flow of dividends, based on a specified percentage (currently 30%) of EDS's profits. The dividend yield is 2%.

After 1994, General Motors has the right to exchange its regular common shares for the special Class E shares. But to do so, it must pay 20% above the average market price for the previous 15 trading days.

Still, several big money managers are betting that the shares' virtues outweigh the drawbacks. Large money managers added 1.3 million shares to their holdings in the fourth quarter, according to CDA Investment Technologies. Among the buyers were RCM Capital Management (which has a crackerjack stock-picking record but won't comment on individual stocks it buys), Sit Investment Associates and Waddell & Reed.

Eugene Sit, president of Sit Investment Associates, says his holding now is over a million shares. He expects EDS earnings to grow 15% a year, beating out most other companies in a "slow-growth environment." As a result, he predicts the shares will provide an investment return of 15% to 20% a year, "not a home run, but a two-base hit."
