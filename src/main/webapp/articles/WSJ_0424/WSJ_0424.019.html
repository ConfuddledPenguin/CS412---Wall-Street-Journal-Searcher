Labor Letter: A Special News Report on People And Their Jobs in Offices, Fields and Factories

  • Author: Selwyn Feinstein
  • Date: 04/24/90

MOMMY TRACKING doesn't have to hurt careers, a long-term look suggests. Taking time off from a job to raise a family "doesn't appear to have had a major impact on final earnings" of women who graduated from college 45 years ago, says Edith Fierst, a Washington lawyer who surveyed the experiences of classmates from the Barnard College Class of 1945. "Those within a given occupation tended to end up in the same bracket whether or not they had taken time off for children," she declares.

Of 75 class members who responded -- a third of the class -- almost all had married and borne children, and most had spent 10 to 15 years at home or in part-time jobs while their families were young. Yet, Ms. Fierst says, of 11 graduates earning more than $50,000 a year, seven had taken substantial career breaks, as had most of those earning $25,000 to $50,000 a year.

Only 5% of the mommy trackers wished they had returned to their careers earlier. One regretted a break.

SUMMER INTERNSHIPS flourish, with emphasis on minorities.

Bausch & Lomb Inc., which started bringing students aboard for a summer's exposure only three years ago, expects to hire 25 this year, up from seven in 1989. It taps minority M.B.A. candidates, undergrads and talented high schoolers. Chevron Corp.'s hiring will increase to 350 from 320, with most going into engineering, computer science and accounting. Special scholarships are set aside to lure minorities. Texaco Inc., among its interns, looks to hire four African students attending U.S. universities.

Corning Inc., which will be hiring 90 interns this summer, focuses on women and blacks because they "have been leaving the company twice as fast as white males." Aluminum Co. of America seeks women and minorities with engineering and technical training, explaining that women and minorities "don't gravitate" to these disciplines and those that do should get "the encouragement internships can provide."

FIRING BAD WORKERS can drag on, managers say. Mercy or fear of suits?

Half the workers surveyed by consultant Wyatt Co. complained that "poor performance is tolerated too long in my company." Now managers say they agree. Observes Henry Nozko, president of Acmat Corp.: that's "1,000%" on target. A shortage of workers and fear of litigation make management "more tolerant," he declares. Last year, he recalls, it took three months to discharge a "boozing" worker who years back "would have been fired on the spot."

But Coastal Corp. argues that "letting someone go is easy; turning someone around is more of a challenge." Managers tend to give workers extra ground out of compassion and a reluctance to get involved in a hostile confrontation, a spokesman explains. Tasty Baking Co. says it follows a four-step procedure before firing anyone, which it equates to a "death penalty."

"Managers are more sensitive to human issues, and that's great," one retail executive asserts.

STOCK OWNERSHIP revisited. A survey of 774 publicly traded firms turns up only 9% that encourage managers to own the company's stock and a bare 1% that require it, consultant William M. Mercer Inc. finds.

MIDDLE-MANAGER rebound? Fired managers took 3.1 months to find a job in the first quarter, down from 3.3 months in 1989's final quarter, Challenger, Gray & Christmas says.

SECRETARIES AND CLERKS represented by unions earn $56 a week more than their nonunion counterparts, says a study for the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees.

MULTIEMPLOYER pension plans remain secure. Consultant Martin E. Segal Co. says the plans had assets last year to cover on average 96% of vested benefits. Three-quarters of them were fully funded.

BUT FOR THREE DAYS . . . A widow loses $100,000 in benefits.

The U.S. Court of Appeals in Chicago, reversing a lower court, says Delvina Lorenzen is entitled to only $89,000 in death benefits from Sperry & Hutchinson Co., rather than the $192,000 lump sum her husband, Warren, had elected to receive had he lived until his retirement, in three more days. Or retired five months earlier, at age 65, as he had been planning until the company asked him to stay to complete a project. Or if Mrs. Lorenzen hadn't agreed, three days short of his retirement, to remove him from life-support equipment after he suffered his second cardiac arrest in two weeks.

"The equities are not all on one side, however," the court held, adding, "they rarely are; the tension between formal justice and substantive justice is often, and perhaps here, illusory." Life-support equipment "should not be used to secure retirement benefits," and the cost could have been considerable, it stated. Moreover, by postponing his retirement, Mr. Lorenzen continued to draw full salary and slightly increased his expected retirement benefit.

"A gamble is not unfair merely because the gambler loses," the appeals court said in a 3-to-1 decision.
