Letters to the Editor: Grammar Ostentatious Is Not Perspicacious

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/24/90

I had never thought to cry "Et tu, Brute]" to the editorial writers of the Journal. Your arguments, whether or not one agrees with them, are generally cogent, and at times elegantly expressed. Always, in my memory, they have been models of diction. Yet on March 20 you told us that Congress continues to "flaunt" (a word meaning "to display ostentatiously") the revised minimum-wage law. You meant "flout" ("to mock or insult; to treat with contempt"). No Caesar I, but you have plunged a dagger deep into the heart of this old lover of the English language.

Willard R. Espy

New York

---

Your message is well worth an editorial, if not a front-page article, but the first sentence probably confused many readers. "Flaunt" generally means to show off ostentatiously, as in "flaunting his superiority." Instead, Congress is flouting the minimum-wage law, treating it with scornful disregard. While "flaunt" does carry this as a secondary meaning, its use in this context was confusing, since Congress often trumpets its newest legislation.

Jonathan V. Snyder

Philadelphia

---

Tsk. To flout flout you flaunt flaunt.

Instead, you should flaunt flout and so flout flaunt.

William W. Hall

Richland Center, Wis.

---

You say Congress is going to "flaunt a major piece of recently enacted federal legislation." No doubt this is the result of a misquotation on congressional defiance of minimum-wage legislation. No member of Congress said, "I flaunt to be alone." There was, however a clear chorus of "Flout, flout, damned spot]"

This modest adjustment to reality is offered in a spirit of constructive good will and is not intended to flout erudition or flaunt convention.

Bill Wild

Associate Editor

Dayton Daily News

Dayton, Ohio
