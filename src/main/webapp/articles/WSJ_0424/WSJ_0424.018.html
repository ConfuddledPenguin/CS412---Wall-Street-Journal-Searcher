International: Barriers Dividing North, South Koreans Melt a Bit at Unique Moscow Restaurant

  • Author: Joseph P. Manguno
  • Date: 04/24/90

MOSCOW -- It's a typical Sunday afternoon at the Pyongyang Restaurant in downtown Moscow. North Korean diplomats and South Korean businessmen rub elbows and exchange pleasantries. The founder of the martial art tae kwon do, Choi Hong Hi, a former South Korean army general accused by Seoul of defecting to the North 14 years ago, trades friendly chatter with both sides. Russian waiters in tuxedos serve students from the communist North and visiting tycoons from the anticommunist South.

While official negotiations aimed at ending four decades of hostility between the two countries routinely explode in accusations and threats, Koreans from both sides manage to meet and eat together civilly in the land of glasnost. For both northerners and southerners, who haven't been allowed since the early 1950s to so much as exchange letters across the demilitarized zone that divides the Korean peninsula, the attractions are kimchi (pickled cabbage), noodles and rice wine.

The restaurant is the brainchild of the state-run Moscow Restaurant Service, which also has brought Baskin-Robbins and McDonald's to the Soviet capital. "Our customers are foreign tourists, Russians, South Korean businessmen and North Korean Embassy people," says Gyenadi Krasnikov, who co-manages the Pyongyang with a North Korean official. "There is no problem with the Koreans. Here they are like friends."

The restaurant opened in September as an equally owned joint venture between the Soviet company and a North Korean concern called Rina. Mr. Krasinov says the initial investment figure is a "business secret." But Ri Chae Ho, one of 10 Koreans sent from the North to staff the restaurant, says Pyongyang invested about 400,000 rubles ($659,000) in the operation.

The drab exterior, typical of shops and restaurants in the Soviet Union, hides a nicely appointed interior with carved-wood furniture in one room and long communal tables in another. To the jarring mix of Russian rock music and revolutionary North Korean songs, diners wash down a typical feast of beef ribs, tofu, chicken and nuts, bean sprouts and the essential varieties of kimchi with Pepsi-Cola and North Korean vodka for 70 rubles a head.

Gong Ro Myung, head of the South Korean consular office in Moscow and the highest-ranking Seoul official in the Soviet Union, doesn't eat at the Pyongyang, "because the food isn't very good," he says. "It's very crude."

There is "no diplomatic reason" for staying away, he insists. "I'm telling many people that the fact that South-North relations {are} taking place like this in Moscow is very positive," he says. "If Mr. Soh {Soh Song Pil, the new North Korean ambassador to the Soviet Union} invites me, I'd be glad to go there myself."

Sung Jung Hyun, director-general of the Moscow office of the South's Korea Trade Promotion Corp., says he eats at the Pyongyang regularly but reluctantly. "The quality of the food and service is very bad," he says. "But if I want Korean food -- and all Koreans want Korean food -- I have no choice."

South Korean businessmen apparently are less fussy. "In the seven months since we opened, around 1,200 South Korean businessmen have visited," says Mr. Ri.

This past Sunday found Hyundai business group founder Chung Ju Yung as the host to a luncheon for company executives in a private room. The tycoon flew in earlier this month to seal a $6 billion petrochemical deal and a Siberian lumber project.

In an adjacent room, a group of North Korean Embassy officials, all sporting lapel badges of their country's "Great Leader" Kim Il Sung, lunched with their families. At a nearby table, Mr. Choi, president of the World Tae Kwon Do Federation, entertained Russian and Korean martial-arts officials. A founding general of the South Korean army and former ambassador to Malaysia, Mr. Choi fell afoul of President Park Chung Hee in the 1970s, fled to Canada and was branded a defector by successive Seoul governments.

"I did not defect. I am a nationalist. We Koreans want unification, and here in the restaurant," he said, surveying the room, "you can see unification."

On his way out, Hyundai's Mr. Chung stopped to chat briefly with Mr. Choi. Their conversation immediately turned to hopes for unification. Mr. Choi praised the Hyundai executive's unprecedented visit to North Korea last year to try to engineer a business breakthrough.

"I'm glad you're working for the benefit of unification," Mr. Choi said. "Our generation should accomplish unification."

Mr. Chung agreed, but the North Korean-born self-made man, whose conglomerate has annual sales rivaling the North's gross national product, parted with a more down-to-earth request of the Toronto-based Mr. Choi: "Please help us sell more Hyundai cars in Canada."
