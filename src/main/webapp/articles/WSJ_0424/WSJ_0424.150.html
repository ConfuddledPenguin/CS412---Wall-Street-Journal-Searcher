Southern Co. Probe Witness Pleads Guilty --- Ex-Vendor Is to Cooperate In Federal Investigation Of Political Donations

  • Author: Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 04/24/90

ATLANTA -- Ray Howell, a key witness in a federal probe of political contributions by a Southern Co. unit, pleaded guilty to contempt and tax charges. And he agreed to cooperate in a continuing investigation related to the giant electric utility. Mr. Howell, who was a vendor for Southern's Pensacola, Fla., Gulf Power Co. unit, vanished from an Atlanta hotel room in December 1988 on the eve of his scheduled appearance before a federal grand jury. He went into hiding for a year but surrendered to authorities in December, saying he had run out of money.

Federal investigators were expected to question Mr. Howell yesterday and today, his attorney said. Individuals familiar with the investigation say he can give details of the roles played by others in the political-contributions scheme.

In October, Gulf Power pleaded guilty to two felony charges of conspiracy to make illegal political contributions and tax evasion and paid a $500,000 fine. Papers filed in a federal court in Atlanta in that case alleged that Gulf Power used Mr. Howell's Design Associates Inc. to channel money to the 1988 campaign of Florida state Sen. W.D. Childers. Federal law prohibits utility holding companies from making political contributions.

Gulf Power's plea ended the case against it, but it doesn't bar prosecutors from pursuing charges against individuals, such as employees or politicians. Neither Sen. Childers nor any other elected official or employee has been charged with any wrongdoing.

In an interview, Sen. Childers said: "I don't have any knowledge, and I haven't seen any proof that Gulf Power funneled any money to my campaign."

John Hutchinson, a spokesman for Gulf Power, said Mr. Howell's plea "closes another chapter."

Mr. Howell's contempt charge relates to his failure to appear before the grand jury; the tax charge involves filing a false corporate tax return in 1987. He faces as long as five years in prison on the tax charge; his sentence for the contempt count is largely up to the court's discretion. He is scheduled to be sentenced June 27.

Suzanne Hashimi, Mr. Howell's public defender, declined to discuss what Mr. Howell might tell investigators.

Mr. Howell wants to go into the federal witness protection program, his attorney said. As part of the plea bargain agreement, federal prosecutors said they will help him apply for the program "should the circumstances warrant."

Ms. Hashimi declined to say whether Mr. Howell, who is jailed in the federal prison in Atlanta, has been threatened, but she said she is worried about his safety. "Only a fool wouldn't be, with Jake Horton dead," Ms. Hashimi said. "Part of the reason he is not on bond is for his own protection."

Jacob F. "Jake" Horton, a Gulf Power executive who oversaw the contributions scheme, was killed in a fiery crash of a corporate plane last April. The National Transportation Safety Board is expected to release a final report on the crash soon.

In a counterclaim to a civil suit filed in a federal court in Pensacola by Mr. Horton's estate, Southern Co. Services Inc. asserts that Mr. Horton deliberately caused the crash himself. Just before the crash, Mr. Horton had been advised that a Gulf Power audit committee had recommended his ouster because of invoice irregularities related to the political contributions scheme.

A federal grand jury is continuing other aspects of its Southern Co. investigation, including an examination of whether a method of accounting for spare parts resulted in a deliberate underpayment of federal income taxes and a probe into certain pension-fund transactions.
