REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): The Polhill Release

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 04/24/90

The weekend release of hostage Robert Polhill may signal that the Western allies can begin discussing in public again their long-term strategic no longer of much benefit to the Syrians and Iranians, and may in fact be a liability. With diminishing Soviet support, and with the pullback of the East Germans and the Romanians, Syria is now more vulnerable than it was. Within the context of a post-Khomeini Iran, moderates do exist. The Ayatollah took hostages in part to empty the Arab world of Westerners. He was uninterested in economics, or in economic benefits that come with Western activity. But his successor, President Hashemi Rafsanjani, knows that domestic turmoil is probable if he can't reverse Iran's economic slide.

With inflation about 60% and a 25% unemployment level, Iran is engaged in a process of economic reform. Mr. Rafsanjani has imposed domestic austerity, trying to restore monetary discipline that was lost during the Iran-Iraq war. Last October, Mr. Rafsanjani announced that Iran would encourage foreign trade, and would embark on at least a limited privatization campaign. Efforts to sell state-owned mines have faltered, mostly because buyers can't be sure of reliable electricity supplies. But private Iranian firms now have permission to strike deals with Western counterparts.

Iran's interest is foreign exchange, without which a nation today simply disintegrates. Mr. Rafsanjani has said that the country will likely borrow $27 billion as part of an effort to boost non-oil sectors. The success of that effort depends on Western predilections. Western nations need not betray the bedrock no-deal-with-terrorists policy to advance whatever inclinations exist inside Iran to establish a modus vivendi with the West. If Iran's moderation is genuine, the hostages will be released, though it could take time.

Any progress with Iran also might have a bracing effect on Iraq, which has emerged as one of the world's more dangerous nations. This is a country willing to use poison gas on its opponents, and as reports in recent days indicate, Iraq has been trying to develop a "super gun" capable of throwing chemical or nuclear warheads at its neighbors.

These seem to be days when decades of steadfastness are paying off. Western anti-communism has paid dividends in Eastern Europe, but also in the Middle East, where the collapse of bankrolling Communist governments may force the Syrians to moderate. A steadfast unwillingness to do business with terrorists means that nations that practice hostage taking will have to change if they expect Western economic cooperation.

As always, politics are treacherous in the Middle East, and progress may be quickly reversed. But Mr. Polhill's release is reason to hope that the politics of Iran may yet be headed in the right direction.
