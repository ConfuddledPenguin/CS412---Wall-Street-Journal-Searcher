Jefferies Is Hoping `Junk' Staffers Start California Dreamin' --- Move by New Yorkers Urged As Beverly Hills Group Of Drexel Comes Abroad

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 04/24/90

NEW YORK -- Jefferies Group Inc., after hiring a flock of Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc.'s junk-bond traders in California, is urging the rest of its "junk" staff here to move to the city that made Drexel famous: Beverly Hills. The Los Angeles brokerage firm is quietly urging its junk-bond professionals to leave New York for the California city, where a junk-bond universe was built by Drexel's former junk-bond chief Michael Milken.

The move will help ensure that the former Drexel traders will feel at home and more easily retain their clients, traders said. That's especially important at a time when the junk market has contracted sharply and Drexel's junk professionals are filtering throughout Wall Street and elsewhere.

Jefferies officials, for their part, sought to play down the shift. "We have an open speaker system" throughout Jefferies offices nationwide, says Frank Baxter, chief executive officer. "Where someone is located isn't really that important." Jefferies's equity operations in New York won't be affected by the shift.

Although some of Jefferies's junk-bond professionals "are mutually considering whether they'd like to move West," Mr. Baxter adds, he says there are no "present plans" to purge their entire New York-based junk operations.

The shift began several weeks ago, when Jefferies hired seven former Drexel junk professionals -- nearly doubling the size of its junk-bond staff, previously centered in New York, in one fell swoop. As part of a negotiated deal, some traders said, Jefferies promised the former Drexel executives that they could stay in California.

Since then, the firm's junk-bond professionals have been reporting to David Sydorick, one of the former Drexel salesmen who is based in Beverly Hills. And Ronald Davis, former head of the firm's New York junk-bond group, already has moved to the firm's Dallas office.

Jefferies isn't the first brokerage firm to urge some of its junk-bond staff to look West. Last year, Merrill Lynch & Co. had sought new offices for its growing junk operations in Beverly Hills but has since decided to keep its junk-bond trading desk in New York. Jefferies says it plans to eventually shift its staff to the Westwood section of Los Angeles.

Meantime, a former Drexel junk-bond trader who lives in California and recently moved to Jefferies concedes, "There's no good business reason to be in California and not be part of the Wall Street community." But, he adds, the former Drexel employees all live in California and will easily fit in at Jefferies.

Besides, he says, "All we need is a telephone."
