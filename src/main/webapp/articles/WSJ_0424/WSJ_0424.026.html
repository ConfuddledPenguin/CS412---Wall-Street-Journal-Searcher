IRS Reluctant To Give Its Nod For New Bond

  • Author: Hilary Stout and Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 04/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Not so fast. The Securities and Exchange Commission last week took a major step to open up the capital markets. But the Internal Revenue Service may be reluctant to loosen a tax regulation that some investors say still thwarts free global trade in debt securities.

This week the IRS will decide whether to permit a newfangled bond that proponents say would complement the spirit of the SEC's new Rule 144a by boosting securities-market liquidity.

In conversations with Wall Street tax lawyers, IRS and Treasury officials have indicated they are reluctant to allow the new bond, which would bridge the gap between the different species of bonds sold in the U.S. and Europe.

In 1982 Congress effectively eliminated the sale of bearer bonds -- bonds that aren't registered in the name of the holder -- to U.S. institutions or citizens. The worry was that if securities couldn't be linked to their holder, the holder might not pay taxes on the interest income.

But the European market deals primarily in bearer debt. So last summer the IRS asked for public comment on the idea of allowing bonds that can be converted from non-bearer to bearer form, so U.S. holders could easily trade securities with Europeans and vice versa.

The IRS was anticipating SEC enactment of Rule 144a, which allows big investors to trade certain securities without complying with U.S. reporting requirements. The new rule is aimed at increasing liquidity in U.S. capital markets and opening those markets to foreign companies, which have shied away because of U.S. reporting requirements.

If the IRS doesn't allow the convertible bond, "it will tend to make the markets less efficient for foreign issuers who are trying to use 144a," said Leslie Samuels, a partner at the law firm Cleary, Gottlieb, Steen & Hamilton in New York. For example, he said, a foreign company would have to pay a higher rate on its debt to attract a U.S. buyer because the U.S. buyer would have trouble reselling the debt in the European-bearer market.

The new bond would "facilitate an easier trade," said Jon Moldafsky, vice president of Meritus, a Los Angeles firm that provides credit ratings and analysis for privately sold securities.

Some investors say the new SEC rule is so attractive to foreigners that the remaining tax restrictions shouldn't matter much.

"The Europeans really want to tap the U.S. market," said Heiko Thieme, a New York-based consultant to Deutsche Bank of West Germany.

And others say that foreign investors have become more accustomed to holding non-bearer debt in recent years -- especially as the U.S. has turned overseas to fund its budget deficit with non-bearer Treasury securities. "I don't believe a favorable {IRS} ruling will have a big effect on the liquidity of 144a issues," said Kenneth Lay, senior manager in financial operations at the World Bank. Mr. Lay said about two-thirds of a $1.5 billion World Bank offering of non-bearer debt last September was purchased by foreigners.

IRS and Treasury officials won't disclose the position they plan to take. But in response to claims that the ability to take advantage of the new SEC rule would be limited if the IRS doesn't allow the convertible bond, a Treasury tax official said, "You can argue against that -- the fact that U.S. Treasury securities are issued in registered {non-bearer} form in the overseas markets without much trouble. . . . If we are talking about people who aren't into tax avoidance, the anonymity the bearer debt permits isn't an important factor."
