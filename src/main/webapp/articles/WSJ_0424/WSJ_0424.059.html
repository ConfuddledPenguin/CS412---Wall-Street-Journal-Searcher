At Walden Pond, Two Liberal Causes Seem One Too Many --- Rock Star Defends the Site From Condos but It Turns Out the Poor Need Them

  • Author: Lawrence Ingrassia
  • Date: 04/24/90

"If I knew for a certainty that a man was coming to my house with the conscious design of doing me good, I should run for my life. . . . I never heard of a philanthropic meeting in which it was sincerely proposed to do any good to me, or the like of me." -- from "Walden"

CONCORD, Mass. -- Henry David Thoreau, brace yourself. The philanthropists are coming.

Rock star Don Henley is leading the charge, bringing with him actors Don Johnson and Carrie Fisher and singers Bonnie Raitt, Arlo Guthrie and Bob Seger. Their mission is to save Walden Pond from developers, to keep it free from the blot of civilization.

They will do this in the way they know best, by charging locals $22 a ticket to hear them talk about the Pond and sing some classic rock 'n' roll. Two concerts are planned -- tonight and tomorrow night -- and it's all for a good cause.

Or is it?

Mr. Henley -- formerly of the Eagles (hit song: "Hotel California") and the host of the party where Gary Hart met Donna Rice -- thought he was going to save Walden Pond from an office building and condominiums. But it turns out that the condo project includes some affordable housing -- of which there is precious little in this exclusive community.

Suddenly, solid liberal ground became a little shaky. One state official, implying that Mr. Henley had been hoodwinked, wrote him a letter cautioning that "the environmental objections being raised . . . are a thinly veiled attempt" by hoity-toity Concordians to keep out hoi polloi.

Besides, others chime in, Walden isn't exactly the pristine retreat it was when Henry David (as the philosopher/naturalist is called here) was trekking around the woods in the 19th century. The office building, proposed by developer and magazine publisher Mortimer Zuckerman, wouldn't really mar the scenic shores of Walden. The site is separated from the pond by a heavily traveled four-lane highway, the town dump and a trailer park.

More than a few locals think it's a bit ironic that jet-setting stars are claiming kinship with the frugal Mr. Thoreau. But, Mr. Henley told a Boston newspaper, "Thoreau and Emerson had a great deal of influence in my life. They made me more aware of nature and the environment." He noted that he read "Walden" while studying English at North Texas State University about 20 years ago.

In a letter to the state, he wrote: "Pieces of our heritage disappear one by one without people even noticing. It always comes down to Nature vs. Economics. Nature vs. Jobs. Nature vs. Profit. Nature vs. this thing we call `progress' -- and Nature always loses."

When Mr. Henley found out about the proposed developments, he offered help to Thomas Blanding, a Thoreau scholar and president of the Thoreau Country Conservation Alliance.

No one really doubts the rock star's sincerity. He just happens to have stepped into a battle that's been going on for several years, with Mr. Blanding's forces on one side and the development and housing forces on the other.

The debate in part reflects Concord's own schizophrenia. It is trying to maintain its rich past: It was at the Old North Bridge in Concord on April 19, 1775, that the British were turned back by the Minutemen. Ralph Waldo Emerson, Mr. Thoreau's mentor, lived here. So did Nathaniel Hawthorne and Louisa May Alcott.

But it is also trying to maintain its rich present: In recent years Concord has become an expensive suburb, a home for wealthy Boston commuters. Mr. Thoreau built his humble one-room home on Walden Pond for $28.12 1/2 cents. Today, Concord homes cost an average of $300,000; some fetch more than $1 million.

Streets are lined with fashionable shops and real-estate agencies. The entrees at Walden Station Restaurant include Chicken Thoreau -- "excellent mesquite flavored boneless breast, with shiitake mushrooms and a boursin cream sauce" -- a dish Mr. Thoreau never mentions cooking on his wood stove at Walden Pond.

The pond -- actually more the size of a lake -- lies a mile-and-a-half south of the town center. Here, Mr. Thoreau lived a life of contemplation from July 1845 to September 1847. It was an almost idyllic spot, judging by but one description in his book: "This small lake was of most value as a neighbor in the intervals of a gentle rainstorm in August, when, both air and water being perfectly still, but the sky overcast, mid-afternoon had all the serenity of evening, and the wood thrush sang around, and was heard from shore to shore."

The pond is still beautiful, its water clear and smooth as glass on a windless day. And, with 411 acres surrounding the pond given to the state by the Emerson family and others in 1922, the immediate area hasn't been developed.

But if a wood thrush sings nowadays, you can't hear it, much less from the other shore.

At one end of the pond is a man-made beach that draws thousands of swimmers on hot summer weekends. Anglers line the shore hoping to catch the trout that's stocked in the pond by the state. A concrete boat launch sits at one corner (though only canoes and rowboats are allowed). Constant traffic on nearby roads creates incessant noise.

The undulating hills in the surrounding area, where Mr. Thoreau walked and meditated, are still wooded and, in places, relatively tranquil. Here -- well out of sight of the pond -- the new condominiums are planned.

While Thoreauvians concede that the woods are already dotted with high-priced homes, they argue that the condos and office building, though at two separate sites, take development one step too far. "It's a domino effect. They talk about the dump across the road," says Ann Chapman, a Thoreauvian. "If you put this {office building} in, it would justify another development. What's enough?"

Planning officials defend the projects. The condos would be about three-fourths of a mile away from Walden Pond, not on its shores, notes Judith Chanoux Cutler, Concord's town planner. Also, the 139-unit condo project, a venture between two Boston developers, would include 42 units of low- or moderate-income housing subsidized by the state. The state approved it because less than 3% of Concord's housing is classed as affordable.

Concord policeman D.A. Goranson, who grew up in town, favors the projects. "I know Walden Pond, and this isn't the Walden Pond I knew as a child," he says. Anyway, he adds, the area needs affordable housing. "Many town employees can't live here because they can't afford it," he notes.

Had it not been for Mr. Henley, the matter probably would have been merely debated over coffee, talked about after church. But then he stepped in. Radio stations began promoting the concerts and the issue became more public.

Mr. Henley and the Thoreauvians now promise to help find another spot in Concord for affordable housing -- and pitch in money to buy the land. But that will be easier said than done. Notes Ms. Cutler, "Nobody supports affordable housing unless it's someplace else. It's not easy to find another site."

If no other site can be found, Mr. Henley wrote that he would "explore the possibility" of building the affordable units at the existing site, but not the higher-priced condos. Mr. Henley is no longer giving interviews.

But first his group will have to raise the money to buy that site and the office site, a total that could exceed $10 million. The two concerts this week might net $500,000, supporters estimate, so more celebrity bashes may be necessary.

The Thoreauvians do have one well-heeled convert already: Mr. Zuckerman. While insisting there is nothing wrong with his proposed office building, he says he would happily sell it for no profit -- and will even contribute $100,000 to their cause.
