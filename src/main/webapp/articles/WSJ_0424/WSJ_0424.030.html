Law: Supreme Court Declines to Hear Appeal By GM to Kill Suit Tied to Warranties

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 04/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court refused to hear an appeal by General Motors Corp., which was seeking to kill a warranty-related lawsuit that may become a class action for nearly 600,000 purchasers of 1981-1985 cars and trucks with 5.7-liter diesel engines. The suit was filed by 183 purchasers of diesel-powered vehicles for alleged violations of a federal law that regulates warranties on consumer products. The purchasers, who experienced problems with the engines, sued over the company's failure to repair defects and challenged GM's limits imposed on "implied" warranties.

The lawsuit hasn't been certified as a class action, yet. The purchasers say that if a federal court recognizes the case as a class action, there may be 596,364 persons making claims against GM.

"We're dissapointed that the Supreme Court declined to review the court of appeals decision," a GM spokesman said. "The Supreme Court decision is not a decision on the merits of the case or that it can proceed with a class action. GM intends to vigorously defend itself in this case."

The dispute is over the so-called implied warranty of merchantability. Motor vehicle makers provide buyers some express, written warranties on parts. But most states also have laws regulating sales that add an additional, implied promise in consumer transactions that the merchandise is fit for consumer use.

Manufacturers may place limits on how long express warranties last, such as 24 months or 24,000 miles. But the GM purchasers sued over the company's effort to impose the same limit on the duration of implied warranties.

Before trial, a federal court dismissed the claims of 107 purchasers who said the problems with their vehicles began after express warranties had expired.

But a federal appeals court in Richmond, Va., reinstated the claims, ruling that the purchasers should be allowed to offer evidence that applying the time limits of express warranties to implied warranties is unfair to consumers.

The high court, without comment, refused to hear GM's appeal. (General Motors Corp. vs. Carlson)
