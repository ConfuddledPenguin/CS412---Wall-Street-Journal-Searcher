Marketing & Media: Ad Revenue at Newspapers Expected to Be Flat in 1990

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/24/90

LOS ANGELES -- Newspaper publishers will get more bad news today about advertising spending in 1990. Craig Standen, president of the Newspaper Advertising Bureau (NAB), is expected to report today at the American Newspaper Publishers Association's annual meeting that the rate of growth in advertising spending in the nation's newspapers will remain flat in 1990.

Mr. Standen, in the NAB's "state of the business" address, blames the industry stall on lowered retail and classified ad spending. Retail advertising, which is more than half of most newspapers' revenues, has dropped as retail sales have fallen. A slowed economy in most parts of the country has also resulted in large drops in the level of classified employment, real estate and automotive advertising. Classified ads make up about 40% of newspaper ad revenues.

Publishers were hoping for some good news after several years of a declining growth rate and recent severe falloffs in classified and retail ad spending. Mr. Standen is expected to report, however, that should the economy strengthen, the growth rate will increase to between 5% and 6% in 1991.

Newspapers continue to be the largest advertising medium in the U.S. -- larger than TV and magazines combined -- but their growth has declined steadily from 9% in 1987, to 6% in 1988.
