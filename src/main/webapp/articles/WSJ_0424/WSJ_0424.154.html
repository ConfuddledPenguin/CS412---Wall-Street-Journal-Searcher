ITC to Investigate Firm's Complaint On Chips From Korea

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. International Trade Commission agreed to investigate a complaint by SGS-Thomson Microelectronics Inc. that its patents are being infringed by imports of computer memory chips made by a South Korean company. SGS-Thomson, based in Carrollton, Texas, said Hyundai Electronics Industries Co. and its California-based subsidiary, Hyundai Electronics America, infringed on its patents. The U.S. company is asking the ITC to ban the offending imports, which include several types of dynamic random access memories, or DRAMs, and static random access memories, or SRAMs.

SGS-Thomson, which is jointly owned by Thomson SA of France and Istituto per la Recostruzione Industriale of Italy, is the successor to Mostek Corp., which registered the U.S. patents at the center of the controversy in the 1970s and 1980s.

The complaint is the first trade action taken by any U.S. semiconductor producer against imports from South Korea. The U.S. industry has been apprehensive there would be a surge of computer-chip imports from South Korea, which recently has increased its semiconductor production capacity.

SGS-Thomson also has filed a lawsuit in federal district court in Texas against Hyundai. In the suit, filed Feb. 27, SGS-Thomson is seeking money damages and a permanent injunction to halt alleged patent infringements.

Separately, acting on another trade complaint, the ITC decided to investigate charges by Ingersoll-Rand Co., a New Jersey maker of power-tools, that its U.S. trademarks covering "air-impact" wrenches have been infringed by manufacturers in Taiwan and by Astro Pneumatic Tool Co., Los Angeles, which distributes the Taiwan-made wrenches in the U.S.
