EECO Might Seek Chapter 11 if Lender Puts Pact in Default

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/24/90

SANTA ANA, Calif. -- EECO Inc. said it might seek bankruptcy-law protection from creditors if Sanwa Bank of California declares the high-technology electronics products maker's $10 million credit agreement in default. The accord expired March 31.

EECO's stock didn't open for trading yesterday on the American Stock Exchange because of the news. Friday, the shares closed at $1.75, unchanged.

Late yesterday, EECO said it had recommended that the exchange suspend trading in its stock until "its financial condition is clarified." The company said it didn't know when that might be.

EECO said that if Sanwa calls the loan, the company could have trouble getting other funding because of its poor financial condition. The company then would have to consider other options, it said, "including a restructuring under the protection of Chapter 11" of the federal Bankruptcy Code.

Robert Bonney, EECO chairman and chief executive officer, said in an interview that Sanwa has held off declaring the agreement in default while the company pursued the sale of some or all of its businesses. But he added that Sanwa "could call the loan at any time."

Sanwa declined to comment.

EECO has said it expects to report a $10.3 million net loss for 1989. Posting of its financial results had been delayed by a proposed sale of its Maxi-Switch computer keyboard unit. In 1988, EECO had a loss of $13.2 million, on sales of $13.9 million.

The company also said that negotiations with a potential purchaser of Maxi-Switch had broken off. Terms of the proposed sale, EECO said, weren't satisfactory to Sanwa, which holds liens on the unit's assets. The company said it is in preliminary discussions with two new possible buyers of the unit.
