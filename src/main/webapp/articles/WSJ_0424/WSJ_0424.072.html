Monday's Markets: Stocks Fall, Bonds Drop Moderately --- Worry About Rates Hurts Shares; Dollar Surges Against Mark

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 04/24/90

Interest rate fears continued to batter stock prices, though bond prices declined only modestly. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 29.28 points to 2666.67 in light trading. Long-term bond prices lost about a quarter of a point, or about $2.50 for each $1,000 of face amount. Though small, the decline was the seventh consecutive drop. The dollar rose sharply against the West German mark, but much less markedly against the Japanese yen.

Analysts said the decline in stocks was a continuation of the inflation and interest rate worries that pushed the Dow Jones Industrial Average down nearly 56 points last week to its lowest level in more than a month.

Last week's market activity was led by the bond market, where investors bid prices down sharply. As bond prices fell, the interest rates on those securities rose, posing a threat to already sluggish economic growth and giving investors an attractive alternative to stocks. Although the bond price decline moderated considerably yesterday, analysts said the likelihood of further rate increases, including the possibility that the Federal Reserve might soon push short-term rates higher, continued to frighten investors away from stocks.

There also are worries that Japanese investors will be assuming a much lower profile in the U.S. bond market in coming months, a development that also could drive interest rates higher. James Solloway, chief economist at Argus Research in New York, noted that while Japanese investors aren't the largest block of bondholders in the market, they are influential. And with interest rates in Japan approaching 7.25%, the real returns available to them in Japan are about as good as they are here without the currency risk of investing in dollar-denominated securities.

Mr. Solloway, contending stocks already are overvalued at current interest rates, said there is likely to be a psychological reaction among investors if the yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rises to 9%. The yield is at 8.96%.

"It's very difficult for stocks to beat a 9% yield on a long-term basis," said Mr. Solloway. He suggests that stock prices could tumble 10% to 15% if interest rates rise much further.

The dollar's rise against the West German mark followed published reports that German monetary union will include a one-to-one exchange rate for the bulk of East German savings as well as wages and pensions. Currency traders said a broad one-to-one exchange rate between the two Germanies' currencies would be inflationary. The West German Bundesbank has said it opposes a one-to-one exchange rate and central bank officials have threatened to drive interest rates higher if such an exchange rate is approved.

In major market action:

Stock prices sank in light trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 136.2 million shares. Declining issues on the Big Board were ahead of gainers 1229 to 328.

Bond prices eased. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark issue rose to 8.96%.

The dollar strengthened against most major currencies. In late New York trading the dollar was quoted at 1.6996 marks and 157.60 yen compared with 1.6995 marks and 157.45 yen Friday.
