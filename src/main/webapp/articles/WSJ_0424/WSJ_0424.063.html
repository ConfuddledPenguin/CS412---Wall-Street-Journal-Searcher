Media: Radio Stations Luring Boomers With Old Songs

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 04/24/90

NEW YORK -- Forget Madonna, Motley Crue, Janet Jackson and the other current staples of Top 40 radio. The hottest format across the radio dial these days showcases performers who haven't had hits in decades, such as Dion and the Belmonts, Elvis Presley, The Supremes and Lesley Gore.

Since last year, the number of stations that program the solid gold format has ballooned about 20%, making it one of the fastest-growing formats, according to Broadcasting magazine. "I've been following radio for about 20 years, and I've never seen anything like this," says James Duncan Jr., publisher of American Radio, a quarterly analysis of radio ratings, programming trends and financial data.

By attracting baby boomers in the key 25-54 age group that advertisers covet most, the oldies but goodies are turning out to be pure gold for stations such as New York's all-oldies WCBS-FM. "A lot of stations are looking for the quick fix, and solid gold is it," says Joe McCoy, program director at WCBS-FM, which switched from album-oriented rock to oldies in 1972. "The aging of America is looking for consistency and familiarity," he says.

WCBS-FM, which has become steadily more popular since the early 1970s, reached a milestone last week. For the first time in its history as an oldies station, it finished as the top-ranked radio station in New York in ratings compiled by Arbitron Co., a unit of Control Data Corp. The station, owned by CBS Inc., also finished No. 1 among 25-to-54-year-olds. Mr. Duncan of American Radio predicts that WCBS-FM will be the third-largest money-making station in the country, with ad revenues of about $30 million. It trails only WGN-AM in Chicago, with projected billings of $35 million, and KABC-AM in Los Angeles, with $32 million.

Even in markets where the oldies station has average or below-average ratings overall, its popularity with affluent listeners in the key demographic group is strong. For example, in Boston, even though oldies station WODS-FM finished a mediocre No. 11 in overall ratings, it scored its fourth consecutive No. 1 victory in the 25-to-54 age group.

"No question that the most attractive demographic likes to listen to oldies," says Richard Cotter, vice president and regional broadcast manager in the New York office of J. Walter Thompson Co., a unit of WPP Group PLC. Mr. Cotter adds that the age group also gravitates to adult contemporary (Neil Diamond, Fleetwood Mac, Kenny Rogers) and all-news outlets.

The success of the oldies format is prompting a number of stations with mediocre ratings in other formats to make the switch. "Stations are searching for a format that would attract the baby boomers, and in a lot of markets, the format did not exist," says Gerry Boehme, vice president and director of research at Katz Radio Group, a subsidiary of Katz Communications Inc.

Earlier this month, for example, Infinity Broadcasting Corp. switched its lackluster adult contemporary station in Detroit to oldies. "It's a big moneymaker, and you don't have to depend on high-visibility morning shows," says Mel Karmazin, president and chief executive officer of Infinity, which also operates album-oriented rock and so-called classic rock stations in cities such as New York, Los Angeles and Boston. "As an oldies station, you may not be everybody's No. 1 choice, but you are everyone's No. 2 choice," Mr. Karmazin adds.

Not everyone is thrilled about the oldies boom. Despite the surge in music video as a marketing tool for new recording artists, record companies still rely in part on radio to move new material. "The record companies do not like this format," says Nancy C. Widmann, president of the CBS radio division, which owns 20 stations, including 12 FM outlets, five with the oldies format. "We don't move their product."

An official at CBS Records, which is now a subsidiary of Sony Corp., says that it would be in the best interests of the record industry if each radio station would broadcast both current and past hits. However, he acknowledges that may be an unrealistic expectation, given the trend toward identifiable formats.

Some stations were initially wary of the oldies format, which includes a heavy diet of The Beatles, The Rolling Stones and other mainstays of the 1960s. Their executives were concerned that people would naturally tire of songs they were so familiar with. After all, how many times could you hear The Dave Clark Five belt out "Over and Over"?

"Some stations were afraid of the format because they thought it would burn out," says Bill Clark, president of Shamrock Broadcasting Co., which owns four oldies stations in cities such as Denver and Atlanta. "But people identify with an era of music and love to hear it again and again," he says, adding that "for people who are 35 to 45 years old, the oldies stations will be their equivalent of the big band stations their parents listen to."
