Who's News: Bank of New England Names McNamar, Ex-Treasury Aide, to Be Vice Chairman

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 04/24/90

BOSTON -- Bank of New England Corp. named R.T. McNamar, a former U.S. deputy Treasury secretary, as vice chairman. Mr. McNamar, 51 years old, will be responsible for regulatory compliance, investor relations and corporate communications at the financially troubled bank holding company. The vice chairman's post had been vacant since the end of January, when Richard Driscoll was forced to retire.

Mr. McNamar will play a sensitive role. Bank of New England is under strict orders from federal regulators to improve its financial condition and its operations. And the company's securities holders are watching anxiously to see whether it can work its way out from under its massive loan problems without government assistance. Any financial aid from the government would probably wipe out any remaining value in the company's shares and could mean big losses for debt holders, as well.

Mr. McNamar said in an interview that he sees "a reasonable possibility that we can avoid" government assistance. But he said it "will take another quarter or two" of monitoring the Bank of New England loan portfolio before the question of the company's viability can be answered with greater assurance. Mr. McNamar said that he is "very impressed" with the new loan-review process at Bank of New England, but that the system will have to be checked against factors such as rates of recovery on foreclosed real estate.

First-quarter results are to be posted in the next couple of weeks, and Lawrence Fish, the company's new chairman, has warned that nonperforming assets will rise from the $2.3 billion reported as of Dec. 31, partly resulting from deterioration in the New England real estate market and partly reflecting the revised loan-review process. Nonperforming assets include foreclosed loans and loans for which payments are past due or considered to become delinquent. Mr. Fish last week outlined a strategy to return the company to profitability, and said the bank will seek to place some of its problem assets in a separate entity -- often known as a "bad bank."

Mr. McNamar and Mr. Fish became acquainted after Mr. Fish left Bank of Boston Corp. in 1988 to become president and chief operating officer of Columbia Savings & Loan Association in Beverly Hills, Calif. The two men met at the suggestion of an executive recruiter they both knew in San Francisco, and have kept in touch since, Mr. McNamar said.

Mr. McNamar held the No. 2 job at the Treasury Department from 1981 to 1985, and was responsible for domestic and international capital markets, banking and tax policy. He was in charge of the department's handling of the Third World debt problem and served as principal negotiator for some assistance packages from 1982 to 1984.

Since leaving the government, he has run Conover & McNamar, a Los Angeles-based consulting and merchant banking firm that did work for the World Bank and several private banking companies. Co-founder C. Todd Conover, formerly U.S. comptroller of the currency, has since left the firm. Mr. McNamar, who started work at Bank of New England yesterday, said Conover & McNamar will be closed.

Mr. McNamar, who joins the boards of Bank of New England Corp. and its main banking affiliates in Massachusetts and Connecticut, will have a personal financial stake in the company's efforts to turn itself around: His compensation package at Bank of New England, like Mr. Fish's, includes a heavy weighting of stock options.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Bank of New England shares closed at $3.625, unchanged.
