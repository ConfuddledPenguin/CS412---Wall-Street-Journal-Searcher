Law -- Legal Beat: Court Allows Pricing Suit Against Conrail

  • Author: Wade Lambert and Wayne E. Green
  • Date: 04/24/90

A federal appeals court in New York has allowed an ailing railroad to sue Conrail for allegedly setting monopolistic freight rates. The U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals overturned a lower court decision dismissing the case. Delaware & Hudson Railway Co., which is in Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings, sued Conrail in 1986 over alleged antitrust violations in Conrail's rate-setting policy.

Delaware & Hudson had to rely on part of the Conrail system when it shipped newsprint from Canada to the middle Atlantic states. In its suit, the company alleges that a change in Conrail's rates in the early 1980s forced it out of that market.

The appeals court in Manhattan ruled that the lower-court judge failed to adequately consider Conrail's monopolistic power. The court said Delaware & Hudson either had to give up its business in the market or pay a sharp increase to Conrail that would wipe out its profits on the middle Atlantic route.

Delaware & Hudson, one of Conrail's few rail-freight competitors in the Northeast, filed for Chapter 11 protection in 1988. James K. Manning, an attorney for Delaware & Hudson, said the change in Conrail's rate-setting policy contributed to the railroad's financial troubles. "In our view, this has been part of the problem," said Mr. Manning. "We were denied any economic access in this market." For Delaware & Hudson, the newsprint market was "quite important because it happens to be a market that was well-suited to rail traffic," Mr. Manning said.

Attorneys for Conrail, a private rail freight company set up by Congress in 1976, couldn't be reached for comment.

Conrail, based in Philadelphia, had claimed in the district court that its practices weren't anti-competitive because it was merely trying to maximize its profits. The appeals court, however, said that considering Conrail's control over the market, there are grounds for a trial to determine whether the rate policy was monopolistic. Delaware & Hudson is seeking damages of more than $5 million, which would be trebled under antitrust law.

HEART SURGEON wins $29 million libel verdict in Texas.

A state court jury in San Antonio awarded the damages to Sudhir Srivastava, a heart surgeon who alleged that his practice was destroyed by stories broadcast by a local television station owned by Harte-Hankes Communications Inc. The award is believed to be the largest libel verdict ever issued in Texas.

The station, KENS-TV in San Antonio, carried a series of reports in 1985 stating that a group of physicians, including Dr. Srivastava, had performed unnecessary surgery on elderly patients for profit. Dr. Srivastava sued for libel, contending the stories were false, and that they had ruined his reputation.

James Branton, a San Antonio attorney with Branton & Hall, which represented the plaintiff, said his client didn't get another patient for more than three years, was hit with a "flood" of malpractice suits and eventually had to relocate to Midland, Texas, where he now practices.

The jury's award, issued April 10, included $11.5 million in actual damages and $17.5 million in punitive damages. The verdict still must be reviewed by State District Court Judge Carolyn Spears, who could uphold it, reduce it or set it aside.

Mark Cannan, a San Antonio attorney with Lang, Ladon, Green, Coghlan & Fisher, counsel for Harte-Hanks, said he will appeal if the verdict is approved.

"I feel there are various and substantial grounds for appeal," he said, declining to elaborate.

SOVIET SANCTIONS in Lithuania jeopardize opportunities for U.S. lawyers.

American law firms promoting joint ventures in the Soviet Union are concluding that now is not the time to move into the Baltic republics. James Dobkin, who leads Arnold & Porter's international joint venture practice, says that his clients who had considered such projects have decided that they are unthinkable now, "at least for the immediate future."

Ed Lieberman, a partner at the Washington-based firm of Cole Corette & Abrutyn, agrees that American law firms and their clients are staying away from the Baltics "as a result of concerns about Lithuania."

Tensions in Lithuania are having less effect on American legal entrepreneurship elsewhere in the Soviet Union. But Mr. Lieberman worries that rising tensions in Lithuania could scuttle trade and tax treaties being readied for discussion at the May summit, which in turn could slow the efforts of American law firms trying to develop a Soviet market for their clients.

Assistant U.S. trade representative Peter Allegeier told lawyers and businessmen last week that the U.S. is assuming that events in Lithuania won't disrupt trade.

ATTORNEY GENERAL pessimistic about recouping losses from S&L fraud.

"I don't think a lot of this money is available," said U.S. Attorney General Dick Thornburgh, after a speech yesterday in Dallas. Recovery is complicated, he said, because "some of those assets have been dissipated," and some of the top suspects have sought bankruptcy-court protection.

But the U.S. Justice Department is making an effort to obtain restitution in certain instances. In Dallas last week, Justice Department prosecutor Robert Hauberg urged a federal judge to order Woody F. Lemons, former chief executive officer of defunct Vernon Savings & Loan Association of Dallas, to turn over $9.7 million to the government. U.S. District Judge Robert B. Maloney hasn't ruled on the restitution request but he isn't expected to treat the prosecutor's motion lightly.

Earlier this month, the judge sentenced Mr. Lemons to 30 years in federal prison, one of the stiffest terms to date in a thrift-related fraud case.

PATENT RAID: Baker & Botts, a Houston law firm, raided the Dallas law firm of Baker, Mills & Glast for lawyers specializing in patent law. Baker & Botts recruited name partner Jerry W. Mills and as many as 11 associates, according to E. William Barnett, managing partner of Baker & Botts. Mr. Mills, 49 years old, will head Baker & Botts's patent section. Robert A. Baker, managing director of 90-lawyer Baker, Mills, said his law firm has not yet decided on a new name.
