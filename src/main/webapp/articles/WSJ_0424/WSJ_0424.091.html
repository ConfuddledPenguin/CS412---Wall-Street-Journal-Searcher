It's Rumored They Might Enlist The Blob to Frighten Litterbugs

  • Author: Ken Wells
  • Date: 04/24/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- Next time you attend a meeting of the Sierra Club, look for the big guy with the green scales. Godzilla has joined the environmental movement.

The fabled Japanese low-budget-movie monster has been adopted as the mascot of two San Francisco-based environmental groups that are trying to pressure Japan into halting lumber imports from endangered tropical rain forests.

The groups, the Rainforest Action Network and the International Rivers Network, point out that Godzilla historically has been "Japan's leading environmentalist." He merits this title because, in all of the old Godzilla movies, he is motivated to attack and destroy cities only after humans have messed with his habitat. He was also created by an environmental disaster -- a nuclear explosion -- and has to go around gobbling up nuclear power plants just to maintain a well-balanced diet.

This, according to Rainforest Action's Pamela Wellner, makes him an appropriate spokesthing for conservationists worried about what they contend is Japanese corporate despoliation of Third World environments through logging of tropical hardwood forests.

"Japan's monstrous destructive capacity requires monstrous solutions," adds Juliette Majot of the International Rivers group. Ms. Majot and Ms. Wellner not long ago joined Godzilla -- who looked trim and petite at a mere nine-feet tall -- in a picket of the U.S. headquarters of Mitsubishi Corp. here. A half-dozen large, helium-filled Godzilla look-alikes floated menacingly above the picket lines.

Godzilla and the human pickets accuse Mitsubishi of conducting, through subsidiaries, 24-hour logging operations that are destroying forests, and uprooting indigenous people, in remote areas of Borneo.

A Mitsubishi spokesman confirms that Godzilla made a brief "20-minute appearance" outside the company's San Francisco office. He declines to comment further on the monster, except to say the issues raised by Godzilla were being addressed by the company's environmental-affairs division in Tokyo.

To some people, the whole affair might look like monstrous Japan bashing. But Godzilla, in a news release, spurns such criticism; he notes that he is Japanese himself, and in solidarity with Japanese environmental groups, adding: "We're not just a bunch of whining creeps out to bash Japan. It's time to take them on."
