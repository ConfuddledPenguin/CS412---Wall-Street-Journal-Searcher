Letters to the Editor: Mexico's Phobia: Fear of Open Market

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/24/90

In regard to David Asman's interview with Mexican President Carlos Salinas de Gortari, on your April 4 editorial page: The Mexico-United States Institute is happy that Mr. Salinas now seems to favor a closer trade relationship with the U.S., and perhaps even a North American Common Market. We have advocated both all along. Of course, Mr. Salinas is right when he complains that he has opened Mexico's economy wide to foreign products, and that the U.S. and other foreign countries have not reciprocated. But when he claims that the opening "has meant lower prices for Mexicans" and has reduced inflation, he is on uncertain ground.

If Mexicans are paying less for some items, and inflation is under 20% -- still a lot higher than the U.S. rate -- that is because of the economic pact he and his predecessor have imposed on labor and management that freezes wages and manufacturing prices. Only when prices are permitted to find their own level in the marketplace will we learn how low, or high, they really are, and consequently the true rate of inflation. But after more than two years, there is no sign that the Mexican government is ready to trust the open market -- a different animal from Mr. Salinas's "open economy."

Further, he boasts that he has "forced domestic producers" to cut their prices to international levels but does not mention that move has, in turn, forced them to cut back on production, as their profit margins have correspondingly fallen. In the end the fall guy is the worker, whose services no shrunken enterprise needs or can afford.

Though one can only praise Mr. Salinas's advocacy of privatization, publicity has outstripped performance. The privatizations he has effectuated still leave intact the state-owned "strategic" enterprise -- soil, petrochemicals, banking, electric power, satellite communications, railroads, and so on -- that represent an estimated 75% to 80% of the state's assets in the economy and constitute the industrial infrastructure. Until most of them, at least, are privatized, Mexico's economy will not be free.

We wonder why Mr. Asman's interview was confined to economic affairs. After all, without political freedom and democracy, economic freedom will be found wanting. As the democratic revolutions in Eastern Europe and Nicaragua dramatically underscore, unless a government is accountable to the people it will commit excesses that inevitably drive national economies into the ground. Mr. Salinas's predecessors did just that in Mexico.

Unfortunately, as the heir to the world's second-oldest one-party state, after the Soviet Union, Mr. Salinas also is not accountable; hence he takes only half-measures in trying to modernize Mexico. There is still time, but not much, for him to do what the Poles and Hungarians are doing, and presumably the new Nicaraguan government will do: Go all-out for a free economy and free political system.

Daniel James

President

Mexico-United States Institute

Washington
