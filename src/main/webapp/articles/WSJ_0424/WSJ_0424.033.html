Your Money Matters: Bargain Hunters Lift Some Prices In the Resale Partnership Market

  • Author: Jill Bettner
  • Date: 04/24/90

Investors hunting for bargains are helping to boost prices of some limited partnerships in the informal resale, or "secondary" market. While prices of partnerships sponsored by troubled syndicators continued to fall during the first quarter, investors selling interests in some other partnerships got prices that were a bit higher than in the last three months of 1989.

These are some of the findings of the latest Wall Street Journal survey of trading at a number of small investment firms that buy partnership interests from investors, either for their own accounts or for resale to new investors. The survey covers resale-market trading in 20 widely held partnerships.

The survey found that the best prices investors got when selling real estate partnership units in 1990's first quarter averaged about 35% below their original prices. The average discount was about 41% in the previous quarter. Secondary-market firms reported that investors in 10 of the 11 real estate partnerships in the survey sold partnership units during the quarter; sales were reported in all 11 in the last three months of 1989.

All together, sales by investors were reported for 17 of the 20 partnerships in the survey, compared with 15 in the previous quarter. The best prices sellers got averaged 34% below the original offering prices, about the same as in the previous quarter.

The partnerships in the survey are a small sample of the nearly $90 billion of such investments sold publicly by major Wall Street brokerage firms and other syndicators since the late 1970s. About half invested in real estate or mortgages, while the rest undertook everything from leasing jetliners and computers to buying oil wells and cable-television systems.

Designed as long-term investments, partnerships typically have lives of about 10 years before their assets are sold and investors are paid off. But at least 20% of older partnerships are foundering, according to brokerage-industry estimates. The informal resale market is about the only outlet for investors who want to get out before a partnership runs its normal course.

Disappointment over performance isn't the only reason some investors sell. Some investors who got out in the first quarter, just prior to the annual tax-filing deadline, did so to make this the last year they had to face the chore of reporting on their partnership investments to the Internal Revenue Service, secondary-market brokers say.

Every year, they must decipher a sponsor-supplied "Schedule K-1 Partners' Share of Income, Credits, Deductions, Etc." for their partnership. Deciding where and how to report that information on their tax returns is often a time-consuming process. Hiring a professional to do it can easily add $400 a partnership to an investor's tax-preparation expenses.

Earlier this month, the Treasury Department and the IRS asked Congress to simplify the chore by replacing the complex K-1 with a streamlined Form 1099-K, similar to the form banks and others use to report investors' dividends and interest. The Investment Partnership Association, a Washington, D.C., trade group, helped develop the proposed form, which could be used by partnerships with more than 100 partners.

The secondary market also presents opportunities for investors to get into many partnerships for substantially less than the original offering price. Buying in the resale market can be treacherous, however, especially for investors looking for high current income.

While many partnerships make periodic cash distributions to investors, those payments typically aren't guaranteed. Thus, yield estimates, which like those in the Journal survey usually compare recent distributions and current resale prices, can be illusory. There isn't any certainty that those payouts will continue at the same rate. They could even stop altogether.

Sponsors of two partnerships in the Journal survey are among several troubled syndicators considering possible "roll-ups." In a roll-up, several limited partnerships are combined into a new, publicly traded investment. The survey partnerships that would be affected are Equitec Real Estate Investors Fund XV and Prudential-Bache Energy Income Series III 14.
