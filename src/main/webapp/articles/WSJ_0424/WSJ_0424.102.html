Official at HUD Is Expected to Get Thrift Bailout Post

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 04/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Peter Monroe, an official with the Department of Housing and Urban Development, is expected to be named to a top position in the savings and loan bailout operation, industry and administration sources said. Mr. Monroe, a deputy assistant HUD secretary for housing, would serve as the president and chief executive officer of the Oversight Board of the Resolution Trust Corp., a role that was initially the subject of some dispute. The Oversight Board sets policy for the RTC, which sells or liquidates insolvent thrifts and their assets.

Mr. Monroe would succeed William Taylor, a Federal Reserve Board official who has served in the post on a temporary basis since February. Mr. Taylor followed Daniel Kearney, a former Salomon Brothers Inc. executive, who resigned in February after just four months on the job. In resigning, Mr. Kearney said unanticipated limitations had been placed on his authority.

Some in Congress have called for abolishing the Oversight Board or streamlining the bailout operation in some way. The board is perched at the top of the multilayered S&L bailout bureaucracy. It is made up of Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady, Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan and HUD Secretary Jack Kemp.

Mr. Monroe would head the board's staff of several dozen people. After the earlier confusion, it seems certain that the responsibilities of the post will be clarified before it is filled.

The RTC, which has a board that's separate from the Oversight Board, carries out the sales of the thrifts, using mostly personnel from the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.

Mr. Monroe didn't return telephone calls yesterday, and a HUD spokesman declined to comment. Mr. Monroe has met with several administration officials who have a role in making the selection. The Oversight Board held a closed-door meeting yesterday on the post.

Mr. Taylor, who is director of the Fed's division of banking supervision and regulation, wasn't a candidate for the post. Under his direction, however, the Oversight Board made headway on setting sales policies, including sweetening some terms to attract more bidders.
