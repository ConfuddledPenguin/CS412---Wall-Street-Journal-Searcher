Ingersoll Extends Offer to Buy Back Affiliate's Bonds

  • Author: Meg Cox
  • Date: 04/24/90

NEW YORK -- Ingersoll Newspapers Inc. has extended its cash tender offer to holders of an affiliate's junk bonds, but it probably will have to sweeten the offer because it hasn't attracted a single bondholder. The financially strapped newspaper holding company, which is controlled by Ralph Ingersoll, made an offer last month to buy back about $240 million of junk bonds at a deep discount to their face value. The newspaper company is struggling to avoid default on its heavy debt load at a time of slumping newspaper advertising sales. Community Newspapers Inc., the issuer of the bonds, publishes daily and weekly newspapers in Ohio and New York.

But bondholders who objected both to the price and the structure of the deal organized an unusual, total boycott of the offer. Since no bondholder accepted the offer for 13% "reset" bonds and discount debentures by midnight EDT last Friday, Ingersoll said it will extend the offer until midnight May 4.

James Rubin of Sass Lamle Rubin & Co., which is a holder of the bonds and helped organize the group of disgruntled bondholders, said the holders won't tender their bonds until the offer is improved, but he expects that to happen. "They have solicited our assistance in formulating a more acceptable recapitalization," Mr. Rubin said. "We're optimistic that a successful solution can be achieved.

Ingersoll executives couldn't be reached for comment, but the company is under serious time pressure to resolve the matter. That's because of a provision covering about $125 million of the bonds, whose interest payments must be reset by July 7 to make up for a drop in the bonds' price, an event Ingersoll can't afford to let happen considering its financially weakened state.

Mr. Rubin said the bondholders' biggest objection is to the structure of the offer. Only a portion of the holders' bonds are to be tendered and the outstanding ones "are going to have severely reduced protective covenants that could render them worthless or near-worthless in certain scenarios," said Mr. Rubin.

Then there's the question of price. Analysts have said the junk bonds are worth about 80% of their value, though they've traded recently at 40%. Ingersoll offered 55% for the senior bonds and 25% for the junior bonds, said Mr. Rubin. "We think . . . bondholders are probably willing to accept somewhere around 80% and 40%, respectively, if it's resolved quickly," he said.

Mr. Rubin said that if Ingersoll doesn't reset its bonds, "it's a monetary default and we could accelerate the indebtedness, and I sincerely doubt they could pay off the entire principal amount in response." Such a crisis could tip Ingersoll into bankruptcy proceedings, said Mr. Rubin. But people close to Ingersoll say bondholders would also be hurt by losses and delays in payment that would result from a bankrupcty filing.
