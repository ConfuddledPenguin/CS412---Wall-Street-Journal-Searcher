Oversight Panel Of FASB Changes Rules Procedures

  • Author: Daniel Machalaba
  • Date: 05/24/90

NEW YORK -- An oversight body of the Financial Accounting Standards Board made it more difficult for the FASB to issue accounting-rule pronouncements. Heeding criticism from businesses that some FASB rule-makings were overly complex and costly, the oversight group boosted voting approval requirements for the seven-member rule-making body to 5-to-2. Currently, the FASB, the chief rule-making body for accountants, requires that a simple majority approve its pronouncements.

Some businesses applauded the new voting requirements, which "give the standard-setting process more credibility," said Tom Jones, executive vice president of Citicorp. "It was hard for me to say accounting principles were generally accepted if they were passed by a bare 4-to-3 majority."

But it isn't clear that the changes will make some FASB decisions more acceptable. The new voting requirements are scheduled to go into effect Jan. 1, after the FASB is expected to finish work on a controversial proposal that would force companies to accrue or set up a reserve for future medical benefits of retirees.

The FASB also plans to reconsider controversial standards it set several years ago that account for corporate-income-tax expenses. "We have four people in favor of the changes," said Dennis R. Beresford, chairman of the FASB, who opposed the tighter voting approval requirements. "Now that we will have to get at least five favorable votes to make changes, it's not clear we'll have the votes."

He said the new FASB voting requirements "may actually work against" the business community on some issues.

William Gladstone, co-chief executive officer of Ernst & Young, said the voting-requirement change was made "with a long-term view." Mr. Gladstone, a trustee of the Financial Accounting Foundation, the board's oversight body, added that "there wasn't even a hint of anyone trying to get this voting rule in time to stop or affect anything the FASB board is doing." The Financial Accounting Foundation's trustees approved the new voting standards by a vote of 11 to 5.

About 15% of the pronouncements made by the FASB since it was founded in 1973 were passed by a 4-to-3 vote. But such close votes have centered on some of the most controversial issues.
