Foreign Exchange: Dollar Mostly Rises in Quiet Trading; Market Has Little Reaction to U.S. News

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/24/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar rose modestly against most currencies in quiet dealings as participants found ways to discount several developments with market-moving potential. Statements by top U.S. monetary officials on interest rates and the release of figures on U.S. durable-goods orders for April caused little change in dollar rates, traders said.

The U.S. unit likewise remained little changed against its Canadian and British counterparts in spite of the continuing constitutional impasse in Canada and the release of statistics showing a wider-than-expected U.K. merchandise deficit for April.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6765 marks, up from 1.6736 marks late Tuesday, and at 151.45 yen, up from 151.20 yen late Tuesday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6940, up from $1.6928 late Tuesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the U.S. currency was trading at 151.18 yen, down a bit from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 151.35 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading in Tokyo at 1.6673 marks.

Comments by Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady that substantial Treasury borrowing will be necessary to pay the debt of insolvent savings-and-loan institutions had little effect on the dollar.

There was little reaction to the statement, said Martin McNulty, a vice president at Lloyd's Bank in New York. He said participants reasoned that any positive impact on the dollar caused by a rise in U.S. interest rates stemming from increased Treasury borrowing would be offset by the potentially bearish impact of a wider U.S. federal budget deficit.

The impact of Mr. Brady's comments, in which he said the S&L bailout could cost as much as $132 billion, was further diluted because it has been widely assumed in the market that the cost of rescuing insolvent S&Ls would be far higher than the $30 billion currently authorized, Mr. McNulty said.

A statement later by Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan that the resolution of the S&L crisis will have only minimal effect on interest rates served to confirm the market's initial assessment of the situation, said Bill Chepolis, a trader at Norwest Corp. in Minneapolis.

The release early in the New York day of a larger-than-expected 4.1% fall in U.S. April durable-goods orders from March didn't affect dollar rates to a great degree because the market is focused on budget-deficit talks currently under way in Washington between the Bush administration and Congress, dealers said. A fall of 2.2% had been expected.

While the fall in orders was seen as another sign of a slowing U.S. economy, the market is more interested in today's revision of first-quarter gross national product and its attendant price deflator because of its implications for interest rates, participants said.

Revised first-quarter GNP is expected to show a downward adjustment to 1.8% from an originally announced 2.1%. The price deflator is expected to remain at 5.7%.

Meanwhile, the Canadian dollar stabilized at slightly higher levels against the U.S. dollar as participants settled back following Tuesday's wild swings to assess the political situation there, said Diego Giurleo, corporate foreign-exchange manager at Royal Bank of Canada in New York.

"The political situation there is not out of control," Mr. Giurleo said, noting that the June 23 deadline for ratification of the controversial constitutional amendment on Quebec is still a month away.

In the meantime, the Canadian unit will be volatile but the currency will be prevented from falling too far by Canada's high interest rates, he said. As was the case yesterday, the high rates and occasional dips in the Canadian currency will prove very attractive to bargain hunters, he added.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery plummeted $11.50 to $364.10 an ounce. The close was the lowest since Oct. 13. Estimated volume was a very heavy seven million ounces.

In Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $366.65 an ounce.
