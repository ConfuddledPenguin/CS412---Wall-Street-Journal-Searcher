Southern's Addison Terms Awareness Of Son's Ties Small

  • Author: Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 05/24/90

PANAMA CITY, Fla. -- Southern Co.'s beleaguered chief executive officer, Edward L. Addison, said he had little knowledge of his son's lucrative ties to the company's pension fund. Mr. Addison, addressing the issue for the first time at the Atlanta-based utility's annual meeting here, said he is "truly sorry" about the affair, but insisted he had no role in steering business to his 30-year-old son.

Speaking to an overflow crowd of more than 800 shareholders, Mr. Addison expressed regret that he couldn't discuss the pension-fund controversy in detail. "It would certainly help to express my side of the story . . . and get rid of some of the pain and suffering to my family, but it is not the responsible thing to do at this time."

Mr. Addison similarly refused to discuss in detail the other aspects of a two-year federal grand jury investigation that is looking into a range of things, from the way in which the company accounts for spare parts to illegal political contributions. He cited "respect {for} the privacy of the grand jury process" as his reason for not commenting on the inquiry.

Mr. Addison acknowledged that his son, Scott, received commissions for pension-fund trading activity involving Southern Co. while a broker at Kidder, Peabody & Co. in Atlanta, but hastened to distance himself from that arrangement, insisting, "I know very little of the factual issues." He added that the "acts were not taken at my direction."

However, those familiar with the matter have said that the elder Mr. Addison was told of the proposed arrangement beforehand and said he would "like to see it done." Southern Co. has added that the chief executive insisted that the arrangement be done legally and at competitive costs.

Federal investigators are examining, among other things, the circumstances under which the younger Mr. Addison received stock trading commissions from the pension fund, individuals familiar with the investigation say.

A federal grand jury in Atlanta has subpoenaed records from a variety of Southern Co.'s pension-fund managers and has called a number of witnesses to give testimony tied to the pension-fund relationship. In addition, the Federal Bureau of Investigation recently joined an investigation by the U.S. Labor Department's labor-racketeering office of certain pension-fund transactions, according to the individuals familiar with the inquiry.

They say federal investigators are also looking at several of Southern Co.'s vendors that currently conduct some of their stock trading through the younger Mr. Addison.

Also at the meeting, Mr. Addison said clean-air legislation pending in Congress would cost Southern $2.5 billion in capital expenditures. He said that cost would require a roughly 8% rate increase to consumers in the year 2000, the peak year of expenditures.
