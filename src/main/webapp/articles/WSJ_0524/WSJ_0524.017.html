LEISURE & ARTS: TV: Bach, Stalin, the Summit and the Tonys

  • Author: Martha Bayles
  • Date: 05/24/90

Tonight, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on CBS: "Sisters." This drama about sibling rivalry in Manhattan may bring Woody Allen's films to mind. But really, it's more like "thirtysomething."

Friday, May 25, 9-10:30 p.m. EDT, on PBS (PBS dates and times vary, so check local listings): "The Stations of Bach."

This lushly scenic tour through the German cities that were home to Bach includes fine performances by German ensembles, and the deft musical insights of the host, violinist James Buswell.

Sunday, May 27, 8-9:30 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "National Memorial Day Concert."

Never before televised, this live concert from the Capitol in Washington, D.C., will honor America's war dead and veterans with patriotic standards and non-standards. Among the performers are Rosemary Clooney and the U.S. Army Fife and Drum Corps.

Sunday, May 27, 8-10 p.m. EDT, on A&E (repeated at midnight and June 2): "Tumbledown." For those who take a gloomier view of Memorial Day, this BBC film dramatizes the story of Robert Lawrence, a Scots Guard lieutenant who was physically disabled by wounds suffered during the Falklands War, then emotionally devastated by the treatment he received back in Britain.

Mondays, May 28-June 11, 9-10 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "Stalin."

Was he a lunatic who derailed a noble revolution, or was he the inevitable product of totalitarian ideology? This series, co-produced by WGBH Boston and Thames Television, gives no conclusive answer. But it does offer a frightening portrait of the tyrant described in an early police report as "active and serious . . . extremely conspiratorial."

Beginning Thursday, May 31, on various outlets: Coverage of the summit meeting between Presidents Bush and Gorbachev.

Friday, June 1, 9-10:30 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "Cinderella."

The music is still by Prokofiev, but the strange sounds, bizarre settings and weird choreography are by the French choreographer Maguy Marin. This intriguing production from the Lyon Opera Ballet is presented by "Alive From Off Center."

Saturday, June 2, 9 p.m. -- midnight EDT, on various stations (check local listings): "Earth '90."

This international broadcast will resemble the famous "Live Aid" show of 1985, but it isn't a fund raiser. Instead, entertainers in Japan, the U.S., Brazil and the U.S.S.R. will simply make a joyful noise unto the environment. John Denver and Olivia Newton-John co-host.

Sundays, June 3-24, 5 p.m. EDT (program lengths vary), on Bravo: Maurice Sendak operas on "Culture for Kids."

This unusual series begins with two musical pieces by Ravel, staged by the acclaimed children's author. Then it continues with stagings of a Mother Goose tale and, finally, Mr. Sendak's own classic, "Where the Wild Things Are."

Sunday, June 3, 9-11 p.m. EDT, on CBS: "The 44th Annual Tony Awards."

Kathleen Turner, currently appearing on Broadway as the cat in Tennessee Williams's "Cat on a Hot Tin Roof," will emcee this year's Broadway-theater awards ceremony, broadcast live from the Lunt-Fontanne Theatre in New York.

Monday, June 4, 8-9 p.m. EDT, on TNT (repeated at 11 p.m. and June 9): "Myrna Loy: So Nice to Come Home to."

As it happens, Ms. Turner also is hosting this film tribute to the witty and sexy Hollywood superstar. The script, written by the film critic Richard Schickel, is a cut above the usual.

Monday, June 4, 10-11 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "USSR Art."

Talk about culture shock. This film traces the changes occurring in the Soviet underground art scene, now that it has been opened up to the Western art market. As described by Viktor Misiano, the former curator of the Pushkin Museum: "If you go to studios where the conversation used to be all about God, light, the universe, about moral and ethical problems, now the conversation is about one thing -- money."

Tuesday, June 5, 10-11 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "Living the Last Days."

In an effort to reduce costs and bring comfort to those dying of AIDS, Los Angeles's Brownlie Hospice opened in 1988. This film portrays the daily atmosphere in that remarkable facility.

Wednesday, June 6, 8-9 p.m. EDT, on PBS: "The Quantum Universe."

It's so simple, even an 11-year-old child can explain it. Or such is the premise of this Smithsonian World special, in which 20th-century physics is taught through narration (by an 11-year-old), acting, music and graphics.
