Five Plane Firms In U.S., Europe Join Jet Study --- Boeing, McDonnell in Group That May Opt to Build A Supersonic Airliner

  • Author: Rhonda L. Rundle
  • Date: 05/24/90

Five European and U.S. commercial aircraft makers said they formed an "international study group" to explore marketing, environmental and business issues related to possible construction of an advanced supersonic airliner. But the U.S. participants, Boeing Co. and McDonnell Douglas Corp., said the group members won't share proprietary data or technology. "This is just a loose, ad hoc agreement to discuss the ramifications of a new generation plane," said a spokesman at Seattle-based Boeing.

Two of the group participants, Aerospatiale of France and British Aerospace PLC, whose predecessors built the supersonic Concorde, recently teamed up to build a successor to that plane that presumably would include technology transfer. The fifth study group member is Deutsche Airbus G.m.b.H. of West Germany.

"The intent {of the study group} is to see what the world-wide needs might be after the year 2000 and how these fit with the commercial fleet that would be operating then," said Don Graf, manager of St. Louis-based McDonnell Douglas's high-speed transport group. "Nothing unique to a particular airplane" would be part of the one-year study, he said.

Mr. Graf said the group also would consider such environmental concerns as how engine emissions might affect the atmosphere and noise pollution caused by sonic booms. McDonnell Douglas, Boeing and the other participants will continue their individual research, he said. But he didn't rule out the possibility that the study might lead to eventual joint cooperation among some or all of the companies to build the next Concorde.

Analysts on both sides of the Atlantic were skeptical that any international consortium would be forged at the end of the one-year study, or that a new supersonic craft would get off the ground anytime in the next decade. Environmental restrictions have toughened, and fuel prices and capital costs have risen since plans for a U.S. supersonic aircraft were scrapped in the early 1970s.

The new study group "is a great opportunity to listen to what your toughest competitors think," said Howard Rubel at C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc. "I seriously doubt that there will be an economically-viable project," in the next 10 to 15 years, said Paul Nisbet at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc.

None of the U.S. or European aircraft makers is likely to try building a new supersonic aircraft without hefty subsidies from their respective governments -- and government money will be scarce. Announcement of the study may signal the start of an industry lobbying campaign for cash in Washington, Paris, Bonn and London, some European analysts said.

The lobbying will be difficult. For the forseeable future, Bonn will be too preoccupied by the costs of German unification. And British government officials grumble that the first-generation supersonic airline, Concorde, hasn't been a financial success. U.S. budget makers are trying to pare a ballooning deficit. Only the French government, which heavily subsidizes aerospace research, is a likely source of support for the consortium.
