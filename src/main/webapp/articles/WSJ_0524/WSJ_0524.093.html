Medicine: Data Spur Debate on Hospital Quality

  • Author: Ron Winslow
  • Date: 05/24/90

Allegheny General Hospital in Pittsburgh charged an average of $95,185 to repair or replace a heart valve during a recent quarter. Across town, Mercy Hospital did the same procedure for $48,559. Elsewhere in Southwestern Pennsylvania, 14 of 53 patients who suffered a stroke died at Latrobe Area Hospital, while all 32 similar patients at Armstrong County Hospital survived.

Publication of these and other ostensible disparities among Pennsylvania hospitals has catapulted the state to the forefront of a national movement to bring consumer buying power to the health-care market. For the first time, consumers -- particularly major companies, government agencies and insurers who pay for health care -- can compare hospitals on price and performance, much as people compare mileage ratings, service records and sticker prices when they shop for cars. The implications for the nation's health-care system are huge.

"This development is going to totally transform the delivery of care," says Jan Weiss, senior vice president for health care at Standard & Poor's. Employers, insurers, health maintenance organizations and federal health-care programs "are going to direct their subscribers to those facilities that provide the best outcome for the least cost," says Mr. Weiss. "It's going to force everybody to change their way of practicing medicine."

Not surprisingly, it's also provoking a brawl between hospitals, who say the data in their current form are misleading and full of flaws, and their major customers, particularly Big Business, who are rubbing their hands in anticipation of a new tool to contain soaring health-care bills.

Yesterday, the Pennsylvania Health Care Cost Containment Council released data comparing 37 Pittsburgh area hospitals on 55 common hospital procedures performed during the fourth quarter of 1988. Third in a series of reports prepared by the council, the report was the first to focus on a major metropolitan area. The area contains both internationally known hospitals and some of the nation's largest purchasers of health care, including such companies as Westinghouse Corp., USX Corp. and Aluminum Co. of America.

The presence of these players has raised the stakes and heightened national interest in what amounts to a major experiment in health-care reform. "People are really waiting to see what happens in Pennsylvania," says Lisa Iezzoni, a physician and health-care researcher at Harvard Medical School.

Other agencies, including the U.S. Health Care Financing Administration, have published reports on hospital death rates or on costs, but the Pennsylvania effort is by far the most comprehensive effort to compile information on cost, death rates and complication rates by region and by hospital -- and identify them by name.

Hospitals are so concerned that they pre-empted yesterday's release with a news conference of their own on Monday to debunk the report. "The usability and reliability of the data have been overstated," says Jack C. Robinette, president of the Hospital Council of Western Pennsylvania. "It has very little use."

Among other flaws, he says, listings of hospital charges are "meaningless," because they aren't standardized. And information related to deaths and complications fails to account for age, whether a patient has terminal illness, and the presence of complicating problems such as heart disease. "There's no way it can define quality," he says. "All it can do is raise broad questions."

Another problem is that the report covers procedures performed during one three-month period 18 months ago. "This is one quarter," says Ray Sarver, associate medical director at Latrobe Hospital. "Make sure you don't over-interpret it."

Indeed, the cluster of deaths related to stroke and brain hemorrhage reported at his hospital "was a quarterly phenomenon," he said, and reflected at least one patient transferred from another hospital on a respirator. None of the deaths were preventable, he said. At the same time, the report lists Latrobe as one of the best performers when it comes to complications. In that regard, "We had a good quarter, that's for sure," he said.

In releasing the data, the council cautioned that although three months was long enough to spot the beginning of trends, the accumulation of a year or more of experience is necessary before the reports are truly useful in evaluating hospital performance. "I don't think anybody should make any significant decisions off this first report," says Richard Ekstrom, manager, health benefits at Westinghouse. Once quarter-to-quarter and annual comparisons are available, he expects the data to be more valuable.

"The data aren't perfect," says John Dankosky, executive director, Pennsylvania Business Roundtable, which represents chief executives of 42 major companies. "But you've got to start someplace."

Underlying the report is a computerized analysis system, based on more than four million medical records, developed by MediQual Systems Inc., Westboro, Mass. Among other things, it is designed to measure a patient's response to treatment during the hospital stay, and to adjust for differences in overall patient health and other factors that could skew the information.

Despite their concerns, hospitals have already used the data to review internal operations. One in another section of the state that scored low in an earlier report later made a public commitment to improve quality.

Meanwhile, corporations have big plans for the data. At USX, Jim Carney, vice president, benefits administration, said the company will visit hospital administrators to discuss cost and quality issues raised in the report. "We don't want to favor one hospital over another," he said. "But why should our employees pay an inefficient provider when somebody else will give you an equally good result" at lower cost?

Hershey Foods Corp., Hershey, Pa., plans to disseminate the information to employees to encourage them to choose efficient and high quality hospitals. For instance, a report issued earlier covering the Hershey area noted that costs for normal childbirth among about 15 hospitals ranged from $864 to $2,180, with no apparent differences in outcomes.

Health data agencies in 30 states are keeping watch on developments in Pennsylvania, which is one reason health-care experts and business leaders expect cost and quality reports will eventually help reshape the health-care marketplace nationwide. Strong performers will use them as part of marketing campaigns to lure patients from hospitals with poor results. They will accelerate the trend among companies and insurers to develop so-called preferred provider networks and then provide benefit incentives to encourage employees to use the network for health care. Now, acknowledges Dr. Sarver of Latrobe Hospital, "Benefits managers are out buying health care, and they've got nothing to go on."

The data are also expected to be important to hospital accreditation and even to bond ratings. "This is the beginning of a very long-term trend," says Standard & Poor's Mr. Weiss. "Hospitals that focus on it in a meaningful way are going to position themselves to be in the forefront eight or 10 years from now."

Meanwhile, the information is certain to raise questions and provoke discussions between providers and consumers, an exercise that in itself could help improve health care. At Allegheny General, a spokesman said the $95,185 average charge for heart valve work reflected some exceptionally ill patients, including one who died after 144 days in the hospital. Overall, he said, the hospital's cardiac services proved to be both medically and cost effective.

At Westinghouse, Mr. Ekstrom looked at the high heart valve charge data at Allegheny General with skepticism, especially, he noted, when the average charge for a similar procedure at the hospital is listed at $43,451. "It looks goofy to me," Mr. Ekstrom says. "You and I shouldn't go and get our valves done until we get a little more knowledge. That's the process that will develop."
