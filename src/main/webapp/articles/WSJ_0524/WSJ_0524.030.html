Technology & Health: Doctors Order Significantly Fewer Tests When Aware of the Costs, Study Shows

  • Author: Michael Waldholz
  • Date: 05/24/90

Doctors ordered significantly fewer medical tests when made aware of the test costs, according to a study that supports the growing belief that doctors overuse diagnostic procedures. In an experiment at Indiana University School of Medicine in Indianapolis, doctors who were alerted about test prices ordered 14% fewer tests and generated bills that were 13% lower than similar doctors for whom test charges weren't flagged. The study also found that the reduced tests didn't dilute the quality of care provided.

The study, published in this week's New England Journal of Medicine, provides some of the strongest evidence to date that many medical tests ordered by doctors are unnecessary and that doctors generally aren't knowledgeable about their costs.

Some studies estimate that diagnostic procedures, from relatively inexpensive laboratory analyses of blood and urine to high-technology imaging tests that can run hundreds of dollars each, account for about one-quarter of the nation's $600 billion-a-year health bill. In recent years, the use of diagnostic testing has soared, as doctors practice "defensive medicine" to avoid malpractice suits.

"There is a tradition in medicine dating back to when little could be done for patients that you do as much as possible, no matter what the cost," said Clement McDonald, an internist who co-authored the study with William Tierney and Michael Miller. "That's no longer true, especially now that things cost so much. But doctors are given little help in making choices about care."

In the study, Dr. McDonald and his colleagues suggested a way to help doctors cut back their reliance on tests. In the experiment, 121 doctors who staffed Regenstrief Health Center, an outpatient clinic associated with the university, were divided into two groups. All doctors at the center order tests via a computer workstation. But, for one group, the price of a test flashed on the computer screen when the test was ordered, while no price was posted for the others.

In a 14-week period prior to the experiment -- when prices weren't listed for either group -- the doctors ordered tests at about the same rate. But during a 26-week period, the group of doctors who could see the charges on the computer screen decreased the tests' use. These doctors also generated average test charges for each patient totaling about $7 less than test charges produced by the other doctors.

The study's authors said the reduction in tests, if continued for a year at the same rate, would have saved the health center $250,000.

The researchers found that prior to the study, doctors queried about test prices were wrong about 44% of the time. Even those doctors alerted to test prices during the experiment were unable to remember the prices about 42% of the time when asked about them in a follow-up interview. Indeed, the doctors who reduced their testing during the experiment reverted to their previous high-use behavior when test prices no longer were listed on their computer screens.

"It appears that physicians may need to be continually reminded of the charges for tests if they are to maintain their cost consciousness," the authors wrote.

Dr. McDonald said the researchers are now conducting a broader experiment in which doctors are advised through a computer screen about charges and other outcomes associated with all sorts of procedures and orders they made for hospitalized patients.
