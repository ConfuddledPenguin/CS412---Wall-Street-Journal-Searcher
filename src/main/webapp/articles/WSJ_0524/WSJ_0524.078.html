Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Seagram Acts to Renew Wine-Cooler Fizz

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 05/24/90

Those fizzy, fruity wine coolers are dead as a doornail, gone the way of the mood ring into fad heaven. Or are they? Seagram Co., in an unusual bid to regain the confidence of retailers, is running multipage ads in Beverage Dynamics and other trade magazines claiming, "Reports on the death of coolers have been greatly exaggerated." The ad lists "cooler facts and fiction" and pleads with retailers to support the brand with display and shelf space.

The Seagram pitch comes as total sales of wine coolers continue in a downward spiral. Last year, sales dropped 19.4% to 39.5 million cases, according to Jobson Publishing Co.'s beverage alcohol group. The drop followed an 11% drop the year before.

But Seagram is ignoring the precipitous slide of the past few years and looking at the brighter side: For the past six months, category sales are actually flat, it says, adding that sales of Seagram coolers are up recently.

"We wanted to get the facts as they exist in today's marketplace clearly across to the public that distributes and sells these products to consumers," says Mark Taxel, executive vice president at Seagram Beverage Co. "The category decline was coming from a lot of the second-tier brands that never gave the consumer quality."

Some Seagram competitors take exception. "The wine-cooler category just really disappeared," says a spokeswoman for Philip Morris's Miller Brewing unit, which pulled the plug on its Matilda Bay cooler brand late last year. "The category started to disintegrate, and it wasn't an economically feasible thing to stay in."

Actually, both sides may be right. The cooler category did indeed shrink drastically since the summer of 1987 -- but the exodus of numerous wine-cooler makers like Miller has been good news for Seagram and E&J Gallo Winery, which makes Bartles & Jaymes. Both heavyweights have picked up share, and together they now account for about 80% of total sales.

"Certainly there was a fad aspect to wine coolers," says Jon Fredrikson, a wine-industry economist at Gomberg, Fredrikson & Associates, San Francisco. "But there was a major shakeout," he says, and the survivors have carved a healthy business.

Seagram isn't taking any chances. In addition to the pitch aimed at retailers, it hired a new ad agency, Wells, Rich, Greene, and this week wheels out a new ad campaign in anticipation of summer, when the majority of coolers are consumed.

The new campaign, themed "it's a cooler world," has a decidedly middlebrow feel to it, with men and women shown sitting around a table exchanging lively conversation. "We talked about guys, we talked about girls, hey that's what life's all about anyway," goes part of a jingle sung by Nikolas Ashford and Valerie Simpson.

Indeed, the ads don't even attempt to woo the trend-setting poolside types that sampled coolers but didn't stick with the product. The target customers "aren't trendy people," Mr. Taxel says. "Our consumers tend to be a little more upscale than regular beer drinkers."

For its new effort, Seagram has abandoned its all-star strategy.

After using Bruce Willis for a time, the company in 1988 brought out three other celebrities, including Woody Harrelson, Steve Guttenberg and Anne Archer.

The new campaign will promote in particular Seagram's new Black Raspberry wine cooler. When the category started to falter, most wine-cooler makers resorted to aggressive introductions of new flavors to hold consumer interest, but the innovations didn't help stem the declines.

Canandaigua Wine Co.'s Sun Country cooler, for example, brought out several flavored coolers, but its share plummeted. It's now a minor player, but it's planning another run and is introducing yet another flavor, Melon Berry. But it doesn't plan to allocate any money to national TV advertising, instead planning to sponsor special events in a few markets where the brand continues to sell.

For its part, Gallo says it plans to stick with Frank Bartles and Ed Jaymes, its two folksy pitchmen, for TV commercials. A spokesman says Bartles & Jaymes, like Seagram, had a strong first quarter. "We don't see anything wrong with the business."

Art, Glasnost and Vodka

Glasnost has already been used to sell everything from McDonald's to Quintessence cosmetics. Now vodkas are trading on it in a bid for Russian cachet.

Absolut Vodka said yesterday it will run ads highlighting works by 26 Russian artists in the June Interview magazine. The paintings and sculptures featured have, not surprisingly, one thing in common: Each includes a bottle of Absolut, which sounds Russian but is actually a Swedish spirit imported by Carillon Importers Ltd. in New York. TBWA Advertising created the ads.

The Absolut ads follow a new campaign for Smirnoff vodka, which recently was reintroduced in the Soviet Union after 73 years. The headline: "Back in the USSR." Smirnoff has been produced in the U.S. by Heublein Inc. for 50 years.

Ad Notes. . . .

ADWEEK: The top three executives of A/S/M Communications, owner of Adweek, couldn't be reached regarding speculation that they were in discussions to sell part or all of the company to Affiliated Publications, Boston. Affiliated already owns the Boston Globe, as well as Billboard and other trade publications. Staffers at Adweek have been told that troubled A/S/M, which has already folded a number of its publications, is seeking a cash infusion from an outside investor. Affiliated executives couldn't be reached.

JOINT VENTURE: Chiat/Day/Mojo, Los Angeles, said it set up a joint venture with Han Brindfors Advertising, Stockholm. The two agencies plan to develop a pan-European network of agencies. Chait/Day said the joint venture would be owned equally, with agencies planned for Italy, France and Spain.

DELLA FEMINA: Jerry Della Femina, chairman of Della Femina, McNamee WCRS, denied speculation the agency is selling its Boston office. Industry executives say Della Femina has been in discussions to sell the Boston office to Arnold Advertising, a Boston shop headed up by former Della Femina executive Ed Eskandarian. Arnold Advertising executives couldn't be reached.

OGILVY & MATHER: Harry Reid, director of Ogilvy & Mather's Asia region, was named chairman of Ogilvy & Mather Europe, succeeding Peter Warren, who resigned. Rod Wright, deputy regional director in Asia, was named to succeed Mr. Reid. Hans Lange resigned as vice chairman of Ogilvy & Mather Europe. Ogilvy is a WPP Group agency.

WHO'S NEWS: Stewart M. Alter was named senior vice president and director of world-wide communications at McCann-Erickson, New York. He had been managing editor at Adweek magazine. en
