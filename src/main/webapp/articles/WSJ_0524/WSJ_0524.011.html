Economy: Durable-Goods Orders in April Declined 4.1% --- Transportation Leads Drop; Figures Said to Confirm Manufacturing Flatness

  • Author: Katherine Walsh
  • Date: 05/24/90

WASHINGTON -- Led by declining orders for transportation equipment, new orders for durable goods dropped 4.1% in April after showing healthy increases in February and March, the Commerce Department reported. Orders to factories for durable goods, which include machinery, household appliances, military equipment, cars and other items designed to last at least three years, fell to $123.27 billion last month. April's decline follows a revised 6.5% increase in March to $128.58 billion and a 2.4% increase in February to $120.78 billion. In January, orders tumbled 10.5%, the steepest decline since the department began recording the figures 32 years ago.

Many economists said the figures confirm what they've been seeing for the past year -- a flat manufacturing sector.

"The weak factory sector reflects the Fed's tightening that ended in the middle of 1989," said Mike Penzer, senior economist at Bank of America in San Francisco. He added that because there is a lag of two or three quarters between the time a monetary policy is put into effect and the time the policy is reflected in factory orders, the factory sector may experience mild growth later this year.

Orders for transportation equipment plummeted 14.3% last month after rising 23.4% in March and 10.1% in February. The February and March increases followed a 27.6% drop in January.

The reduction reflected declines in both automobiles and aircraft. Aircraft orders fluctuate a lot, however, and many economists agreed that the aircraft industry isn't weakening.

"If we're going to maintain the growth rate, as subdued as it is, we're going to have to get something going," said Robert Dederick, chief economist at Northern Trust Co. in Chicago.

Excluding the transportation sector, durable-goods orders fell 0.1% in April after rising 0.9% in March.

New orders for nondefense capital goods, considered an indicator of future plant and equipment spending, fell 9.7% last month after rising 13.3% in March and dropping 5.9% in February. Many analysts said April's drop suggests that orders for nondefense capital goods will be soft during the second quarter of this year and won't boost the real gross national product -- the total output of goods and services, adjusted for inflation -- as they did in the first quarter. Durable-goods shipments were down 2.3% in April to $122.07 billion following a 1.4% increase in March. Unfilled orders increased 0.2% in April to $497.84 billion after rising 0.7% in March.
