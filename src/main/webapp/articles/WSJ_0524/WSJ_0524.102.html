Credit Markets: Treasurys Rise in Volatile Session From Plunge In Gold Prices and a Spurt of Foreign Buying

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Stuart Drown
  • Date: 05/24/90

NEW YORK -- Plunging gold prices and a flurry of foreign-investor purchases helped drive bond prices higher in volatile trading activity. "It was a seesaw session," said William Sullivan, a money-market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. "Each time the market ran toward the higher end of the range, sellers came in to take profits and knocked the market lower," he said.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond, for example, moved in a wide one-point range yesterday, rising as much as 5/8 point and falling nearly 1/4 point. The bond ended 3/8 point higher, a gain of $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

Traders on Wall Street said bond prices spurted in early trading as investors in Saudi Arabia dumped gold and used the proceeds to buy intermediate-term Treasury notes. On the New York Commodity Exchange, the June gold futures contract fell by $15 an ounce, but ended $11.80 lower at $364.60. Other commodity prices also declined.

Cash also flowed into the U.S. Treasury market from Canada. Some investors have grown jittery over the Canadian government's inability to settle a dispute with French-speaking Quebec, which wants to be recognized as a "distinct society" within Canada.

During times of political uncertainty, some investors sell other types of securities and buy U.S. Treasury bonds, which are considered the safest in the world. This so-called "flight-to-quality" investing has exacerbated the Canadian bond market's problems.

On Tuesday, Canadian bonds had their worst rout in recent memory, with some issues falling more than two points. But the Canadian market recovered slightly yesterday, with intermediate-term issues ending about 1/2 point higher and long-term bonds ending nearly one point higher.

Meanwhile, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady told Congress that cost of the savings-and-loan bailout is soaring. He now estimates the cost of the bailout could reach $130 billion, up considerably from earlier estimates. The news was expected to hit the market hard, because the Treasury will likely have to borrow the bailout funds in the bond market. The added supply could put upward pressure on interest rates.

Fears of higher rates, however, were offset by comments made by Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan. Testifying before the Senate Banking Committee, Mr. Greenspan said the overall effect of thrift rescue borrowings on interest rates is "very moderate" and not of great concern to him.

Dealers said that trading was extremely active yesterday, which is unusual considering the Treasury was set to auction two-year notes. Typically, trading activity is quiet on auction days, in part because surprises at the auction have the potential to roil the financial markets.

Yesterday's auction, however, was fairly routine. The Treasury sold $10.88 billion of two-year notes at an average yield of 8.52%. In when-issued trading activity, the yield on the notes fell to 8.49%, a sign the notes continued to draw good demand after the auction was completed.

As expected, individual investors had a healthy appetite for the new notes. Small investors typically purchase at Treasury auctions through noncompetitive tenders, which yesterday totaled $1.67 billion.

Corporate Issues

After a steady climb all week, the junk bond market took a breather yesterday.

Prices of most junk bonds, speculative-grade bonds with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, ended unchanged to slightly weaker.

Among the go-go junk bonds, or actively traded, better-quality names, that showed signs of fatigue this session were Duracell Holdings Corp.'s 13.5% subordinated debentures which slipped 1/4 point to end at 103 1/4.

Also falling back were junk bonds of Kroger Co. and Safeway Inc. Kroger's 13 1/8% subordinated debentures, maturing in 2001, slipped 1/8 point to end at 103 5/8 while Safeway's 12% subordinated debentures, maturing in 1998, lost 1/4 point to end at 100 1/2.

Treasury Securities

Treasury notes and bonds ended higher yesterday, after a volatile trading session.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 101 27/32 to yield 8.57% compared with 101 15/32 to yield 8.60%. The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 101 27/32 to yield 8.58% compared with 101 15/32 to yield 8.63%.

Short-term rates moved lower yesterday. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills fell to 7.65% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.89% compared with a discount rate of 7.74% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.98% on Tuesday.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Gains of about 3/8 point were registered by most mortgage securities.

But some traders said that activity in premium coupon issues, which posted advances of less than 1/4 point, was slim.

Activity in that sector was impeded by Tuesday's Public Securities Association announcement that it will impose trading curbs on certain high-coupon mortgage issues, mainly Government National Mortgage Association pass-through securities, which will be aimed at stopping issuance of high-coupon mortgages that have experienced rapid prepayment rates.

The PSA seeks to end a pattern in which high-coupon issues are being offered and then quickly refinanced. Investors who have been paying premiums of as much as eight or nine points for the issues are finding these securities being paid off much more rapidly than expected, which sharply reduces the value of their purchase. Most of the affected securities were issued in the past 1 1/2 years.

Active mortgage securities matched gains in 10-year Treasury notes, keeping spreads stable.

Ginnie Mae 9% issues, at 8.72%, were trading at a 1.12 percentage-point spread over Treasurys. Late Tuesday, the spread was quoted around the same level.

Municipals

Municipal bonds ended basically unchanged in quiet dealings, with issues showing gains of 1/8 to 3/8 point in spots.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was virtually unchanged at 97 7/8 to yield 7.36%.

No large municipal issues are expected to be priced today.

Foreign Bonds

British government bonds, called gilts, ended lower on a larger-than-expected U.K. trade deficit in April.

But because significant support was seen around the day's lows, some traders believe the rally that began in gilts late last month may not be over.

Among benchmarks, the 11.75% bonds due in 2003/2007 were down 3/4 of a point at 100 13/32 to yield 11.67% while the 12% notes due 1995 were off 9/32 at 99 5/32 to yield 12.21%.
