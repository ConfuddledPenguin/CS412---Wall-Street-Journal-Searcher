B. Manischewitz Co. To Fight U.S. Charge Over Price Fixing

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/24/90

NEWARK, N.J. -- B. Manischewitz Co. said it will fight a federal indictment that it conspired to fix the price of matzo served during Passover. The Jersey City maker of kosher foods decided to plead innocent after U.S. District Judge Harold Ackerman refused to accept the company's request to plead no contest to the charges. The U.S. attorney had objected to the company's plea request.

In court papers, the government said accepting Manischewitz's request would "blur the question of guilt" and permit the company "to deny any wrongdoing and to argue that its plea was entered solely to avoid litigation and business expense." It also said a no-contest plea would assist Manischewitz in defending itself in other related civil suits. Five civil lawsuits connected to the indictment have been filed against the company in state or federal courts in New Jersey, California and Illinois, according to Justin P. Walder, Manischewitz's lawyer.

Mr. Walder said the company hoped its no-contest plea would save it from a costly legal defense. But following Judge Ackerman's decision, he says, "We are prepared to proceed and test the stale allegations of these charges in a traditional proceeding."

An arraignment date hasn't been set.

Manischewitz, according to the complaint, took in about $25 million of revenue from sales of Passover matzo products in the U.S from 1981 to 1986.

The government hasn't named Manischewitz's alleged co-conspirators. But in court papers, it said the company "acquired most of the assets of its major co-conspirator after Passover 1986." According to a Securities and Exchange Commission filing, Manischewitz, in August 1986, purchased certain assets from Horowitz Bros. & Margareten, a New York matzo maker, for $1.7 million in cash and notes.

Regardless of its plea, Manischewitz faces a penalty of $1 million or more, if convicted.
