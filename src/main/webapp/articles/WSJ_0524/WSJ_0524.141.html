What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/24/90

GOLD PRICES PLUNGED for the second time in two months, sparking a volatile bond rally that also boosted stocks. The decline by gold, which closed down $11.80 an ounce on the Comex, at $364.60, was attributed again to heavy selling by Saudi Arabia. The Dow Jones industrials closed up 4.03, at a record 2856.26. The dollar edged higher.

Oil prices skidded following a report that U.S. inventories had risen to the highest level since 1982.

---

Treasury Secretary Brady acknowledged that the cost of the S&L bailout could nearly double, and that the government borrowing needed to finance it could put upward pressure on interest rates. Taxpayers will bear most of the added cost, he said.

---

The budget summit may find that raising taxes is easy compared with finding cuts in government spending.

Opposition to higher taxes has softened among Americans, but they remain divided on the issue, a Wall Street Journal/NBC News poll says.

---

Only a broad trade accord will be acceptable to the U.S. in the current round of GATT talks, Bush said. The tougher stance includes an insistence on major reforms in farm trade.

---

The U.S. auto market remained depressed in mid-May as sales of domestically built cars and trucks tumbled 17.7%. But Toyota, Nissan and Mazda posted big sales gains.

Honda has quietly adopted a dealer incentive program to spur sales of its Civic cars. The move is the latest sign of the U.S. car market's weakness.

---

An airline fare war spread rapidly as major carriers said they would match TWA's surprise cut. Analysts said the battle may most hurt United Air, which began the war Monday.

---

Japan Air Lines plans to order 20 Boeing 747-400 jumbo jets, with an option to buy 34 more. The total order would have a value of $8.53 billion.

---

Durable goods orders slid 4.1% in April, another sign that manufacturing remains flat. The drop, which follows healthy gains in the previous two months, was led by declines in transportation equipment orders.

---

Global futures trading moved closer to reality. The Chicago Merc and CBOT agreed to combine their planned automated trading systems.

---

Salomon and Shearson will soon have employees take more of their salary in stock or other incentives. They are the latest big securities firms to require such payout plans.

---

TWA's machinists union said it offered owner Carl Icahn a framework under which employees would take control of the ailing carrier.

---

PepsiCo said it received final approval to sell soft-drinks in the potentially huge Indian market. Pepsi's joint venture there plans to spend over $1 billion in the next decade.

---

Ivan Boesky admitted he lied to investigators, the stock exchange, on his tax returns and in a book he wrote prior to his 1986 guilty plea.

---

Union Carbide named H. William Lichtenberger president and chief operating officer. He already is head of the chemicals and plastics unit.
