India Gives Final Approval To Pepsi's Plans --- Firm's Joint Venture There To Spend Over $1 Billion During the Next Decade

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 05/24/90

PepsiCo Inc. said it has received final approval from the government of India to sell soft drinks and said its joint venture there plans to spend more than $1 billion to build Pepsi's food and beverage businesses over the next decade. Although the full joint venture was approved by the Indian government in 1988, Pepsi said opposition from India's soft-drink industry created considerable uncertainty in the past two months that Pepsi would receive final clearance to sell soft drinks.

As an almost undeveloped market, India represents vast potential for Pepsi. In a country of some 800 million people, Indians annually drink only about 80 million cases of soda -- the same amount sold in Seattle. On a per-capita basis, Indians drink about three bottles of soft drinks a year, compared with more than 700 in the U.S.

"This market could literally quadruple on a per-capita basis over 10 years," said Chistopher A. Sinclair, president of PepsiCola International. Pepsi is pinning its hopes on India's growing middle class, currently estimated to comprise some 150 million people. By year-end, Pepsi plans to distribute its products in Calcutta, Bombay, Punjab and Delhi, among other places.

"We're not expecting to make money right away," said Mr. Sinclair. "We'll be investing the cash flow in the business for a long time." However, Pepsi, based in Purchase, N.Y., does expect to be making a profit in India within five or six years, he said.

Coupled with Pepsi's recent 10-year agreement to sell soft drinks in the Soviet Union, the Indian business gives Pepsi another boost in its campaign to compete globally with rival Coca-Cola Co.

Coke, which outsells Pepsi more than 2-to-1 internationally, withdrew from India in 1977 over a dispute with the government that would have required disclosure of Coke's secret formula. In March, the government denied Coke's request to re-enter India. Unable to make money after four years in the mid-1950s, Pepsi itself abandoned the India market.

One cloud looms over Pepsi's otherwise bright Indian summer. Last year, the U.S. called India an unfair trading partner under the so-called Super 301 provision of the 1988 U.S. trade law, alleging that India's strict regulations hindered foreign investment. The Super 301 provision requires retaliation if countries don't resolve trade practices the U.S. deems unfair.

Pepsi wouldn't speculate about what might happen to its Indian venture as a result of any retaliatory flare-up, but it isn't taking chances, either. The company recently sent a letter to U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills, stating that it thinks the Indian approval of the Pepsi venture signals a laudable openness to foreign investment. "We hope that our government will not have to take any actions {against India} under the Super 301 process," the letter added.

Through negotiations that began six years ago, Pepsi was able to crack the Indian market in 1988, by winning over the government with a package of political sweeteners, including food-processing plants.

Pepsi's India joint venture -- consisting of Pepsi, a private Indian conglomerate, and a government agricultural agency -- also makes snack-food products. The venture partners, along with Indian franchisees, will make the $1 billion investment. Over the next decade, Pepsi estimates that the retail sales of its snacks and soft drinks in India will total $2.5 billion.

Now Pepsi has new hurdles. For one thing, it will have to compete with Parle Beverages Ltd., India's biggest soft-drink company. Led by flagship Thumbs Up cola, Parle holds an estimated 60% of the $500 million soft-drink market. And, given the structure of Indian retailing, there are few big stores for Pepsi to try to win over to make an immediate splash. Sales battles will likely be protracted mini-skirmishes over thousands of mom-and-pop outlets, where shoppers buy one bottle at a time.
