Canadian Crisis Is Hurting Rates, Finance Chief Says

  • Author: G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 05/24/90

MONTREAL -- Finance Minister Michael Wilson said Canada's bitter constitutional debate may have added about 100 basis points, or one percentage point, to Canadian short-term interest rates and warned that the political crisis is scaring off foreign investors. Mr. Wilson said he "couldn't put a number" on the impact of the political debate on short-term rates but didn't dispute an analyst's estimate of one percentage point. "Clearly, it's something" like that, Mr. Wilson said. "Interest rates are somewhat higher than they should be because" of political uncertainty, he said.

As reported, a package of constitutional amendments called the Meech Lake accord must be ratified by all 10 Canadian provincial legislatures by June 23 or the amendments die. So far, three provinces are refusing to ratify the accord as written. The possible failure of the Meech Lake accord has revived talk of independence in the province of Quebec. The resignation of a senior federal cabinet minister from Quebec over the Meech Lake impasse rocked Canadian financial markets Tuesday.

Yesterday, Canadian markets recovered somewhat with the Canadian dollar closing in Toronto at 84.45 U.S. cents, up 0.30 U.S. cent. The yield on Canadian three-month treasury bills was practically unchanged in late trading at 13.75%.

"There is an economic element to the Meech Lake debate," Mr. Wilson said after a speech here. "I think that it's important that people realize that this whole Meech Lake discussion isn't just a constitution or political matter; it's also an economic matter and it can effect the cost of money {and} investment decisions," Mr. Wilson said. He added: "Investment opportunities are being lost by some people because of the uncertainty."

The government's policy of moderating the rise or fall of the Canadian unit without setting a target value remains unchanged, Mr. Wilson said. But he indicated that the government would move quickly to halt a run on the Canadian dollar.

"We have a strong reserve position and I think we have been able to accommodate these {market pressures in the past, and we'll be able to accommodate any pressures in the future," he said.
