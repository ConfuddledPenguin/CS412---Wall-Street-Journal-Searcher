Marketing: Coke May Brew An Iced Coffee For the Morning

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 05/24/90

ATLANTA -- Coca-Cola Co., looking for ways to win a bigger share of the morning beverage market, is developing an iced-coffee product, according to beverage industry executives. It wasn't clear how close Coke may be to bringing the coffee to market. The company is studying the product in focus groups, an industry executive said.

Coke declined to comment.

For Coke, the new coffee underscores a burgeoning interest in beverages outside the traditional soft-drink arena. In March, Coke started selling PowerAde, a so-called isotonic sports drink developed to combat Gatorade. Coke's heightened new-product activity comes as growth in the regular soda market slowed in the past year.

In its bid for more of the pre-lunch market, Coke for the past three years has been running a "Coke in the Morning" ad campaign in certain areas. According to industry estimates, coffee accounts for 47% of the morning beverage market, followed by juice with 21%, milk at 17%, tea, 7% and soft drinks, only 4%.

Iced coffees generally are ready-to-drink coffees that are chilled. Coke's development of such a product, the industry executives added, may have been accelerated as a result of the recent market test of Nescafe Mocha Cooler, made by one of Coke's biggest global beverage competitors, Nestle Foods Corp., a unit of Nestle S.A. in Vevey, Switzerland. Being tested in several U.S. cities, the Nestle product -- a combination of coffee, chocolate and milk -- is based on an iced coffee the company introduced last year in the United Kingdom. Nestle plans soon to distribute that product, Nescafe Frappe, in France, Spain, Switzerland, West Germany, Greece and Canada.

General Foods USA is expected to launch its own iced cappuccino next year, according to a beverage industry observer. General Foods, owned by Philip Morris Cos., wouldn't comment.

Coke has been studying iced coffee for years but previously had come to the conclusion that there wasn't sufficient demand for such a product in the U.S. In Japan, by contrast, where ready-to-drink coffees -- both iced and hot -- are a $3.7 billion industry, Coke has successfully sold its Georgia Coffee brand for years. Georgia Coffee's strong performance there, in fact, is one reason why Japan is Coca-Cola's biggest profit center.

But recent market research by Bevmark Inc. suggests the time may be ripe for iced coffee here. Across a broad section of socioeconomic and ethnic groups, consumers show a strong acceptance of iced coffee as a substitute for hot coffee and even, in some instances, for soft drinks, according to studies by the Los Angeles beverage consulting company. People still worry about the caffeine content in iced coffee, the research showed. But consumers tended to prefer the flavor of creamed and sweetened iced coffees over hot coffees.

Although iced coffee has tended to be more of a luncheon beverage, industry consultants said, companies are aiming to position it as a morning drink to take advantage of consumers' increasing defection from hot coffee in the morning to cold and sweet beverages.

The current U.S. market for iced coffees is tiny, less than $15 million a year, according to Beverage Marketing Corp., a New York beverage consultant. The chilled products represent the newest niche coffee marketers are experimenting with to combat declining coffee consumption.

Iced coffee could be one of the fastest-growing new beverages in years, some marketers say. "It looks like a product with tremendous potential to attract younger and older consumers," said Tom Pirko, president of Bevmark.

What products iced coffee will replace is a matter of debate. It may hurt iced-tea sales, some beverage experts said. "It won't cannibalize coffee, but will feed on soft drinks and other cold drinks," said George Boecklin, president of the National Coffee Association. If people want a hot drink, he noted, they won't substitute an iced coffee for it.

But Michael Bellas, president of Beverage Marketing, expects older, regular coffee drinkers to be the initial target of the iced-coffee sales effort. Eventually, he said, the marketing will be directed more at younger people, thereby tapping into the soft-drink industry's most important consumer group.
