Democrats Soften Stance on Aiding Central America

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 05/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Democratic leadership, stepping away from confrontation with the Bush administration, agreed to expedite aid to Central America without resolving first the issue of military assistance to El Salvador. Their agreement clears the way for adoption this week of legislation that would provide more than $4 billion in new spending for the current fiscal year. The measure includes $720 million to help the new governments in Panama and Nicaragua reduce their debt and finance private investment.

President Bush has insisted Congress complete the aid package before its Memorial Day recess. But that appeared in doubt after a stormy debate Tuesday night, in which the administration and its Republican allies scuttled related authorizing legislation after the House approved a Democratic amendment that would have cut aid to El Salvador because of alleged human-rights abuses there.

Yesterday, after a telephone conversation with Secretary of State James Baker, House Rules Committee Chairman Joseph Moakley said he had been promised "good faith" negotiations on the Salvador question in the coming weeks. Still, the leadership's decision to let the spending bill advance even though the issue of aid to El Salvador wasn't settled left liberals bitter.

The El Salvador debate reflects increasing concern in Congress over continued human-rights abuses by the military there. Moreover, maneuvering by both sides showed the increased vulnerability of foreign aid in general. The swing voters included a bloc of more than 40 Southern and border state Democrats who supported putting conditions on aid, but then abandoned the entire package when the GOP backed away from it.

"Foreign aid is in serious trouble," said Rep. Thomas Downey (D., N.Y.).

As much as the administration has ridiculed domestic spending that Congress has added to the pending appropriations bill, it may now be the best way of saving the Central America package. Expenditures for education, veterans, and nutrition are sure to be highlighted as the bill moves toward a final vote, and lawmakers have salted the legislation with assorted provisions for parochial interests.
