Politics & Policy: Pro-Gun Lobby Handed Defeat In the Senate --- Provision to Ban Nine Types Of `Assault' Weapons Survives in Close Votes

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 05/24/90

WASHINGTON -- In a surprising defeat of the powerful gun lobby, a provision banning nine types of semi-automatic weapons survived two close votes in the Senate and remained part of an omnibus anti-crime bill. The gun-control victory startled even its proponents and cast doubt on the future of the overall anti-crime package. Republicans immediately threatened to filibuster the Democratic-sponsored omnibus legislation. Bush administration lobbyists predicted a veto if the package made it to the White House with the prohibition on military-style "assault" weapons.

"There's definitely a possibility of a filibuster," said an aide to Sen. Orrin Hatch (R., Utah), who led the unsuccessful attack on the gun-control measure. The timing of a potential filibuster was unclear.

In action on another part of the bill, the Senate narrowly rejected a Republican attempt to toughen Democratic proposals to curb appeals by death-row inmates. The Republican amendment was defeated by a vote of 50-47, but will be put to another vote today. Both versions of the limits on death-row appeals would appear to overturn Supreme Court precedent restricting such appeals, according to a Bush administration lobbyist. The president has threatened to veto a bill that would have such an effect.

The omnibus anti-crime package has a long way to go before reaching the White House. Even without a filibuster, a final vote on the bill probably won't come until next week. The House, meanwhile, is far behind in considering comparable legislation.

Smarting from yesterday's defeat, the National Rifle Association vowed to continue fighting the ban on semi-automatic weapons. "We lost a skirmish today," said James Baker, the NRA's director of federal affairs. He predicted another tough battle when the House debates an assault-weapon ban later this year.

The Senate version would bar for three years the import of five foreign-made assault weapons and the manufacture or sale of four domestic types. Even with the proposed ban, about a dozen models of assault weapons would remain available in the U.S. In a seeming contradiction to its opposition to this measure, the Bush administration last year banned the import of certain assault rifles because they aren't suitable for or adaptable to "sporting purposes."

Gun-control advocates contend that assault weapons don't have any legitimate use and have become favorites among criminals.

The Senate defeated an amendment to kill the weapons ban on a vote of 52-48. The narrow margin prompted opponents to move for a second vote, but the Senate voted 50-49 not to reconsider. Sen. John Chafee (R., R.I.) didn't participate in the second vote, and Minority Leader Robert Dole of Kansas, who initially voted for the gun-control measure for purely parliamentary reasons, switched sides on the second vote.

In a desperation maneuver, NRA lobbyists contacted the office of Vice President Dan Quayle to secure his vote in case of a tie. But the vice president never appeared in the Senate yesterday, and the effort became moot when the pro-gun forces came up one vote short.

Several longtime NRA supporters ultimately supported the assault-weapon ban sponsored by Sen. Dennis DeConcini (D., Ariz.) after voting against broader restrictions proposed by Sen. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio).

Majority Leader George Mitchell of Maine, for example, risked angering hunters in his home state by voting for the DeConcini version. "Our nation can accept some limits on firearms ownership when the firearms in question have no valid hunting or sporting purpose," he said in a prepared statement.
