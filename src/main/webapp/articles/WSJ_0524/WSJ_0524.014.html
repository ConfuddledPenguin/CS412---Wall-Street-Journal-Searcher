Letters to the Editor: How to Forestall Theft Of the Peace Dividend

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/24/90

Surplus Social Security revenues are now accumulating in the trust fund at the rate of $50 billion a year, rising to $100 billion in 1993, $150 billion in 1997 and $200 billion in 2000. In the next five years the surplus will reach $500 billion. Sen. Daniel Patrick Moynihan (D., N.Y.) says, let's reduce the Social Security tax and stop stealing wholesale from the trust fund to mask the budget deficit. Sen. Ernest F. Hollings (D., S.C.) calls this $500 billion grab "the grandest larceny in history." Prof. Martin Feldstein (editorial page, April 25) identifies the key question, namely whether the accumulation of surpluses in the trust fund actually increases national saving and capital formation. He then answers the question in the affirmative -- by a leap of faith -- and berates the Moynihan proposal for reducing the saving rate. But as pointed out in his essay, it all depends on the assumptions made about spending and taxes, and whether cutting the Social Security tax would force an increase in the budget deficit.

There could be a way to please those who want to reduce the regressive Social Security tax and those who want to lower government spending -- while keeping the deficit from growing and Gramm-Rudman-Hollings intact. Most immediately, it has to do with the "global political warming," which may yet yield a "peace dividend" in the form of substantial cuts in the defense budget. Of course, most every member of Congress already has plans to spend this money on his pet project; but a dividend should be paid out to shareholders: i.e., to the taxpayers. This is the essence of Sen. William Roth's (R., Del.) tax-cut proposal for lower- and middle-income taxpayers. But with fierce opposition from Democratic leaders in both chambers, and absence of White House support, the Roth proposal may meet the same fate as an earlier tax-cut proposal by Sen. Phil Gramm (R., Texas). Therefore, why not link the Moynihan Social Security tax cut, on a dollar-for-dollar basis, to defense cuts -- before someone makes off with the dividend? This scheme would keep the deficit from growing and meet the objectives of the Roth bill.

Who knows? After the peace dividend, the "food dividend" could come next -- if we can get Congress to cut agricultural subsidies. Pat Moynihan may be onto something.

S. Fred Singer

Director

Science & Environmental Policy Project

Washington
