World Markets: Tokyo Equities Post Healthy Advance, Boosted By the Yen; London Prices Fall on Trade Data

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/24/90

Tokyo stock prices posted a healthy advance Wednesday, encouraged by the yen's strength against the dollar, with the key Nikkei index finishing above 32000 for the first time in three days. London share prices closed mostly lower in moderate trading, as disappointing British trade data for April erased gains made early in the session and sharply pushed the 100-share index to near its intraday low.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the Nikkei index of 225 issues was up 238.21 points, to finish at 32176.51. Helped by buying linked to index-futures arbitrage, the index moved as high as 32316 in midafternoon, before falling back 140 points on profit-taking toward the end of the day, traders said.

At midmorning Thursday in Tokyo, the Nikkei index was down 56.47 points to 32120.04.

Wednesday's first section volume was estimated at 650 million shares, improving from 549 million shares a day earlier. The Tokyo Stock Price Index, or Topix, of all first section issues, which gained 19.77 points Tuesday, was up 4.36 at 2394.95.

Traders said growing expectations for stability in the currency and bond markets for the time being encouraged buying. Also good news to the market were recent upward moves on overseas stock markets, including Wall Street, said Shigeo Sekimoto, general manager of stock trading at Yamaichi Securities. "The sentiment is very positive," he said.

The Nikkei index hovered at lower levels last week, after posting fast-paced advances. But participants appeared to have agreed that the market has spent enough time on "speed adjustment," Mr. Sekimoto said. Institutions, many of which had been following a strategy of selling whenever stock prices went up, are gradually beginning to participate in active buying, he said.

But some traders said further sharp advances on the Nikkei will take time, as the market doesn't have a strong trading focus.

Many large-capital issues that advanced in the morning on the yen's strength fell back by the end of the day, indicating that the room these stocks have to move up is hardly more than what can be considered a technical correction, a trader at Wako Securities said.

Shares of shipbuilding companies were bought ahead of Friday earnings reports for the fiscal year ended March 31, which investors expect to be strong, traders said.

Ishikawajima-Harima Heavy Industries rose 50 yen to 1,140 yen ($7.53), and Hitachi Zosen gained 19 to 868.

Electrical concerns gained moderately, helped by active buying by the so-called Big Four brokerage houses, traders said.

Toshiba was up 20 at 1,090. Sanyo Electric gained 27 to 956, and Kokusai Electric rose 40 to 5,130.

Among other winners were Konica, up 80 at 1,490, and Misawa Homes, which rose 100 to 2,570.

Steel shares were mixed. NKK was up 5 at 646, while Nippon Steel fell 6 to 618.

Chubu Electric Power rose 60 to 3,760. But Kansai Electric Power edged down 20 to 3,900.

In London, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index finished down 23.9 points at 2287.4, a touch above its low for the day of 2287.3, which was reached just before midafternoon. The intraday high of 2335.1 was posted prior to midday and to the release of the trade figures. The FT 30-share index was down 19.5 points at 1810.0. Volume was a healthy 542.1 million shares, compared with the 629.7 million issues that changed hands a day earlier.

London's market started firmer on strength in stock-index futures contracts, dealers said, but the release of the trade data triggered a gradual decline that lasted throughout the day; Britain's trade balance for April showed a deficit of #1.78 billion ($3.01 billion), wider than market expectations for a #1.5 billion gap. Traders also reported some profit-taking that helped ease prices, and said a weak start on Wall Street didn't help matters.

Some dealers said they still detected both bears seeking to cover short positions and investors interested in fresh buying. They predicted that the market might pick up before the weekend.

A slew of corporate results hit the market, causing varied responses. Ranks Hovis McDougall, a food company, plunged 38 pence to 360 pence ($6.09) after reporting a slim 0.2% rise in pretax profit for its first six months, while issuing a warning for fiscal-year earnings. Bass, a beverage company, posted the biggest gain on the day, rising 14 pence to #10.63 ($17.99), on a 9.8% increase in interim pretax profit. B.A.T Industries, a tobacco and financial services conglomerate, fell 8 to 684, after a 27% dive in first-quarter pretax profit from losses at its British insurance unit. Courtaulds, an industrial materials concern, lost 10 to 328 on profit-taking, following the announcement that pretax profit rose a slim 5% for the year, at the top end of analysts' expectations.

A number of blue-chip stocks showed sharp downward movement in sympathy with the market, but most of them registered thin trading volume. Barclays Bank shed 5 to 554, amid a general softness in the banking sector. The pharmaceutical company Wellcome dived 28 to 661.

Gainers on the day included the lubricants company Burmah, which rose 9 to 626; the automotive and defense concern Lucas Industries added 11 to 633; and Great Universal Stores gained 8 to 969.

The retailer Kingfisher closed up 3 at 311, after Britain's Department of Trade and Industry blocked its long-running hostile bid for rival retailer Dixons, which dropped 7 to 124.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Brussels, Amsterdam and Stockholm, and lower in Milan. Frankfurt prices jumped 1%, reversing a steady two-week downturn, but traders said that volume remained light, that technical factors played a large role in the gains, and that they weren't optimistic the market's consolidation has ended. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Stock prices closed higher in Hong Kong, Taipei and Seoul; lower in Singapore and Manila; and mixed in Sydney and Wellington. In Hong Kong, stocks surged 1.9% in the heaviest trading in 3 1/2 months, as overseas buyers swooped into the market in a delayed response to the report the U.S. would renew China's preferential trade status; the Hang Seng Index soared 57.44 points to 3028.31, following a day-earlier surge of 40.04 points. Taipei share prices staged a technical rebound, after plunging nearly 1,900 points during the past five sessions; the weighted index, which fell 272.57 on Tuesday, recovered 294.08, or 4.38%, to finish at 7014.40. In Manila, panic selling drove prices lower in active trading, as investors lost confidence after a renewal of terrorist activity in the capital.
