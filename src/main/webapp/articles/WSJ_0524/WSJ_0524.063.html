Sales of U.S.-Built Vehicles Fell 17.7% In Mid-May as Auto Market Stays Weak

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 05/24/90

DETROIT -- The U.S. auto market remained depressed in mid-May with sales of domestically built cars and trucks tumbling 17.7% from year-earlier levels. Manufacturers are bracing for some more tough numbers before sales bounce back. "The market is terrible," said Ford Motor Co. President Philip E. Benton Jr. He predicted that there won't be any improvement for "the rest of this model year, but we do look for improvement as early as the fourth quarter."

While the U.S. Big Three were hurting, with combined domestic car and truck sales off 21.9%, Japanese car makers continued to make inroads on the market.

Toyota Motor Corp., which has been increasing production at its Kentucky plant, posted a 117% increase in sales of its U.S.built vehicles in the May 11-20 period. Nissan Motor Corp. and Mazda Motor Corp. each posted jumps of more than 40%.

Overall, consumers bought 259,110 North American-built vehicles, a 17.7% decrease from a year ago based on the daily selling pace. There were eight selling days in mid-May, compared with nine a year ago.

Sales of domestically made cars fell 19.3%, pulling the seasonally adjusted annual selling pace down to a sickly 6.0 million cars from 6.4 million in early May and 7.2 million a year ago.

Sales of domestically made trucks, which had fared better than cars in recent months, fell 15%.

Car dealers attributed the slump partly to a slowdown in the number of customers coming into their showrooms. In addition, many prospective buyers are unable to qualify for financing because they still owe too much money on their current car loan, dealers said.

"The five-year financing is finally catching up to us," said George Portilla, general sales manager for Sam White Oldsmobile in Houston. "We got a lot of people that come in wanting to trade cars, but they owe more on the car than it's worth."

Ford's total sales fell 23.1% during mid-May, to 78,773 vehicles. Sales of domestic trucks dropped 12%, with compact sports utility vehicles providing one of the few bright spots for the No. 2 auto maker. The new Ford Explorer and not-so-new Bronco II, which Explorer ultimately will replace, racked up combined 10-day sales of 4,258, an almost 60% rise from a year ago.

Domestic car sales skidded 29.9%, however, as deliveries of Ford's Escort subcompact car continued to lag year-earlier levels, despite the recent introduction of a completely new 1991 version.

Dealers said the price of the new Escort is partly to blame. The 1991 Escort hasn't any consumer rebate, and many buyers are deciding instead to purchase 1990 Escorts, which carry a lower base price and a $1,000 rebate.

"I think the {1991 Escort} is a fine automobile," said Mark Brobeck, general manager of Ricart Ford in Columbus, Ohio. "But I think it's going to take some money on the table to get people to buy it."

General Motors Corp. sold 116,185 vehicles, down 20.9% from a year ago. Domestic car sales dropped 23.7% while domestic truck sales were down 16.7% Chrysler Corp., which has been hurting since February, posted a 24% drop in total vehicles. Sales of domestic cars fell 22.1% while sales of domestic trucks, including minivans, fell 21%. Chrysler recently adopted a $500 dealer incentive on its longer wheel-base minivans, to help boost sales.

Despite the overall decline,, Chrysler executives say they're encouraged that their share of the U.S. vehicle market is improving from the dismal levels of earlier this year. Final figures won't be available until the end of the month but vice chairman Gerald Greenwald says Chrysler's estimates indicate "we really hit bottom in April and we're starting back up." Last month Chrysler's share of the U.S. car market fell to 7.8%, compared with 10.9% a year ago.

Honda Motor Co. racked up a 22.4% increase in domestic cars, partly as a result of a new dealer incentive program it quietly began last month.
