Bush Insists on Broad Trade Agreement Including Removal of Farm Subsidies

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 05/24/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush is taking a tough stance toward current world trade negotiations, insisting that the talks lead to a broad agreement that includes fundamental change in agricultural trade. "Let me be blunt: to the United States, (having) no agreement is better than a bad agreement," Mr. Bush said in a short address at the White House mainly devoted to the talks. The negotiations are being held under the international General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade. The president termed the negotiations, known as the Uruguay Round because they began in that country in 1986, "the last, best chance for the world to enter the next century with free and fair trade for all."

By saying that the U.S. will accept only a broad agreement, Mr. Bush is raising the stakes in the talks, which are scheduled for completion at the end of this year. There has been speculation that the U.S. would settle for a modest trade accord.

In the talks, the U.S. and almost 100 other nations are seeking to improve international trade rules. The president reiterated that his "top trade priority for this year is an ambitious, multilateral agreement" in the negotiations. If successful, such an accord could extend GATT rules to cover trade in goods and services valued at more than a trillion dollars. Current GATT rules are estimated to cover as much as two-thirds of world trade, which in 1988 totaled $3.5 trillion, according to a Bush administration trade economist.

The president laid out six main U.S. goals in the talks, including, first, to have nations remove trade barriers and subsidies in agriculture. "There simply cannot be a successful conclusion to the Uruguay Round without fundamental agricultural reform," Mr. Bush said.

The other goals include: removing tariffs, cutting government subsidies to exporters, fully integrating developing countries into the GATT system (currently, they can seek exemption from some trade rules), developing rules for such areas of trade as services, investment and intellectual property; and devising swifter and more effective means of resolving trade disputes.

GATT's director general, Arthur Dunkel, is visiting Washington this week to drum up U.S. support for the Uruguay Round. In a meeting with reporters yesterday, he said that a successful GATT round would "consolidate" the world economy by ensuring that nations trade on the basis of market principles. A failed round, he said, would represent a return to "the history of the inter-war period," when trade was done "on the basis of strategic alliances."

Mr. Dunkel said he expects the 15 negotiating groups tackling different aspects of international trade to have outlines of proposed agreements by July. He said, "July is the moment of truth."

Despite concerns about the slow pace of the GATT talks, Mr. Dunkel said, recent meetings of trade ministers provided "strong confirmation" that there's a broad commitment to completing the talks by the December deadline.

The U.S., for its part, is "driving at full speed to complete the round," Mr. Bush said in his address. But at the same time, he noted, the U.S. is pursuing a range of other trade initiatives, including "market-opening negotiations with Japan," bilateral trade agreements with Canada and Mexico and a dialogue with the Pacific Rim nations. He also said Washington is striving to ensure the U.S. will have access to Western Europe after countries there form an integrated market in 1992.
