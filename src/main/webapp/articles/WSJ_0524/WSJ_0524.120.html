Autos: Honda Joins Fray With Dealer Incentives on Some Cars

  • Author: Neal Templin
  • Date: 05/24/90

Honda Motor Co. has quietly adopted a dealer incentive program to spur sales of some of its Civic cars. The move by Honda, which has largely managed to stay out of the auto industry's widespread price-cutting fray, is the latest indication of just how tough the U.S. car market is.

Sales of all cars and trucks, imported and domestic, during the first four months of this year fell only 2.6% below 1989 levels, but that's largely because the U.S. Big Three have been propping up their sales with hefty incentives averaging roughly $1,000 a vehicle. However, mid-May sales of domestically built Honda cars in the latest 10-day period were up 22.4% from a year earlier.

Japanese and European auto makers have special lease programs on many of their more expensive cars. Chrysler Corp. has even been forced to put incentives on some of its vaunted minivans for the second time in five months.

The Honda incentive, disclosed to dealers last month but not announced publicly, is only the third that the company's Honda division has ever had. The current program, like two last year, provides cash only to dealers who meet certain sales goals.

Such a direct-to-dealer program is typical of Japanese car companies, which prefer dealer incentives over giving money directly to consumers. The consumer programs traditionally favored by the U.S. Big Three have been criticized for hurting the image and resale value of the vehicles involved.

Honda's latest incentive gives dealers between $300 and $600 for each Civic four-door sedan and station wagon they sell. These two models accounted for roughly half the 79,067 Civics sold by Honda dealers from January through April.

The incentive program, which runs from April 13 to June 30, "allows us to lower the prices on the cars without the customer knowing about it," says Roger Paris, general manager of Plaza Honda in the Brooklyn section of New York City.

Honda, which for a long time appeared nearly impervious to market downturns because of its popularity, employed dealer incentives in April and May of 1989 to reverse a first-quarter decline in total U.S. sales. The year-ago program covered all Honda models, not just certain Civics. Sales slid again in June when the incentives expired and Honda revived its program for July and August.

In the first three months of 1990, Honda sold 56,714 Civics, an 11.5% increase over the same period of 1989. But in April, Honda apparently discovered its Civic sales were badly lagging behind those of April 1989, when the auto maker had dealer incentives in place for the entire month. Even with the new program, however, Civic sales for all of April were down nearly 12%.

Since then, Honda dealers report an improvement in sales. "It's working," says Russ Darrow, who owns a Honda dealership in Mequon, Wis. "Honda sales are going along quite briskly."
