Abreast of the Market: Industrials Inch Up to New High Despite Pressure of Profit-Taking

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 05/24/90

NEW YORK -- The Dow Jones Industrial Average kept this week's string of all-time highs intact, as late buying pushed the stock market higher in moderately active trading. Although profit-taking in response to the market's record-breaking rally put share prices under pressure for most of the session, the industrial average eked out a gain of 4.03 to 2856.26.

The average managed to set its third consecutive record close -- and the sixth in the past eight sessions -- despite heavy selling in Westinghouse Electric and weakness in a number of blue-chip oil stocks, reflecting a sharp decline in oil prices.

"The market is still exhibiting a decided reluctance to give in to profit-taking," said Eugene E. Peroni Jr., director of technical research at Janney Montgomery Scott, Philadelphia.

Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index rose 0.86 to 359.29, about half a point from its all-time high of 359.80, set last Oct. 9. The Dow Jones Equity Market Index went up 0.68 to 334.28 and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 0.27 to 195.85.

Advancing issues outnumbered decliners by 761 to 745 on the Big Board, where volume fell to 172,330,000 shares from 203,350,000 Tuesday.

Stocks got off to a slow start as market players reacted negatively to early weakness in bonds, which dropped in spite of news that April orders for durable goods fell 4.1% -- a sharper decline than expected.

After sliding to session lows at midday, the market briefly turned mixed at midafternoon on some arbitrage-related buy programs, in which traders sold stock-index futures contracts and bought baskets of stocks in order to lock in profits from price differences between them.

But when the bond market rebound that preceded the move in stocks faded, share prices did the same, and they didn't turn higher until the final minutes of trading.

Some additional arbitrage-related buying and short covering contributed to the upturn, said John Burnett, a senior vice president of block trading at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette, but he added that "real, no-kidding-around buying" from institutions also surfaced.

"We saw good solid buying across the board," Mr. Burnett said, adding that portfolio managers are "afraid of missing the boat" if the market should continue its advance instead of giving them a chance to purchase stocks at lower prices.

"Any kind of pullbacks here are going to be very nominal, and I think you're going to see a lot of money flooding into the market" on any weakness, added Alexander M. Purdie Jr., head of block trading at Robinson-Humphrey Co., Atlanta. "The market's still looking very attractive in a lot of ways."

Westinghouse fell 1 1/4 to 34 3/4 in Big Board composite trading of 1.9 million shares. Shearson Lehman Hutton reduced its investment ratings on the stock, citing expectations of a slowdown in its earnings growth next year, and lowered its 1990 and 1991 earnings estimates.

Oil stocks among the Dow industrials to show weakness included Exxon, which went down 1/2 to 47 3/8; Texaco, down 1/2 to 59 3/8, and Chevron, off 5/8 to 69 3/8. But USX gained 3/4 to 33 3/4 and Du Pont, which owns Conoco, rose 1/2 to 40 3/8.

July crude-oil futures tumbled 78 cents a barrel to $18.14 in New York trading, and the plunge left its mark on other major oil stocks. Amoco fell 1/2 to 53 1/4, Mobil slipped 1/4 to 62 1/8 and Unocal slid 1/2 to 30 3/4.

Several airline stocks dropped despite the oil market's weakness, which could lead to lower fuel costs, after Trans World Airlines announced that it would lower domestic round-trip fares by as much as 30%. AMR, which owns American Airlines, fell 1/2 to 64 1/2; Delta Air Lines lost 1 to 76 1/2, and USAir Group lost 1 3/8 to 27 7/8.

Deere tumbled 2 7/8 to 73 on 1.7 million shares as a second day of profit-taking weighed down the stock, which set a 52-week high during Tuesday's session but posted a decline of 1 5/8 points. Wertheim Schroder removed the stock from a list of recommended issues and lowered its investment rating, citing its belief that Deere's shares were fully valued.

Compaq Computer, a star performer in the market's climb to record highs, added 3 1/2 to 122 7/8. Moody's Investors Service upgraded its rating on a $200 million debt issue that Compaq has called for redemption; the agency cited the company's cash flow and strong balance sheet as reasons for its action.

Other technology issues also added to their recent gains, including Texas Instruments, which gained 2 1/8 to 43 3/8 as 1.3 million shares changed hands. International Business Machines advanced 1 to 118 3/4, Digital Equipment gained 1 3/4 to 93 1/4 and Hewlett-Packard rose 1 1/8 to 47 3/8. But Unisys, left behind in the sector's rally, slipped 1/8 to 14 1/8.

On the other hand, precious-metals stocks paced the market's retreat amid a sharp decline in the price of gold. Homestake Mining dropped 1 3/8 to 18 3/8, Placer Dome dipped 1 to 16 1/8, Battle Mountain Gold lost 5/8 to 13 1/4 and ASA Ltd. retreated 2 3/8 to 49 1/8 in Big Board dealings. On the American Stock Exchange, Echo Bay Mines fell 1/2 to 13, Pegasus Gold lost 3/4 to 11 1/4 and Corona A dropped 1/2 to 7 1/8.

Citicorp fell 7/8 to 22 7/8 in the aftermath of Moody's downgrading of credit ratings on the bank holding company and its Citibank unit. The move included Citicorp's commercial paper, which hadn't been affected last month when Standard & Poor's made a similar move.

Meanwhile, shares of two other big city banks -- BankAmerica, which gained 3/4 to 30 1/8, and Bankers Trust New York, which added 1 5/8 to 43 1/2 -- rallied after County NatWest Securities cited the stocks as better values than Citicorp. Analyst Stephen Berman said the two companies have good balance sheets and reasonably good loan quality.

Ranking high on the Big Board's mostactive list was Alliance Global Environmental Fund, a new issue, which closed well above the price of its initial offering. Six million shares of the closed-end mutual fund were sold at $15 apiece; the stock rose 6.7% to 16 on trading of 2.1 million shares.

Thai Capital Fund, the latest entry among single-country mutual funds, also fared well on its initial day of trading. The stock closed at 12 3/8, above its initial offering price of $12 a share.

British Gas fell 3/4 to 36 after a possible rival bidder surfaced for Consumers' Gas, which the company has agreed to acquire.

ProNational Gas, a firm headed by a former chief financial officer of Consumers' Gas, said it opposes the proposed acquisition and plans its own offer.

Circuit City Stores fell 1 5/8 to 24 7/8. Securities and Exchange Commission filings show that the company's chairman and president sold significant portions of their stakes in Circuit City last month.

Donaldson, which reported that net income for the fiscal quarter ended in April rose 61% from a year earlier and increased its quarterly dividend by about 16%, jumped 2 3/4 to 30.

The Amex Market Value Index, hurt by the weakness in oil and gold stocks, fell 0.20 to 359.65. Volume totaled 16,550,000 shares.

Forest Laboratories rose 1 5/8 to 44. Wertheim Schroder repeated a buy recommendation on the stock and set price targets of $55 to $57 a share for the next 12 months and $70 for the next 24 months.
