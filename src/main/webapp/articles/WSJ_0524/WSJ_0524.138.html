Next They'll Tell Us Gene Kelly Wasn't Really Singing in the Rain

  • Author: Mark Robichaux
  • Date: 05/24/90

New Jersey doesn't take kindly to lip service. Two state assemblymen recently introduced a bill requiring performers to announce before concerts whether they'll sing live or lip-sync to recorded music.

Use of recordings struck a responsive chord in the two legislators after constituents complained of paying $20 to $30 for tickets to concerts at which big-name singers weren't singing.

"Is it live or is it Memorex? People have a right to know," says Assemblyman Neil Cohen, bill co-sponsor and vice chairman of the Consumer Affairs Committee.

Concert promoters may hit a sour note if the bill becomes law; penalties could be as high as $50,000 if promoters fail to inform ticket buyers whether a performer plans to use recordings.

Carl Freed, executive director of the North American Concert Promoters Association, agrees with the spirit of the bill, but says: "I think the bill is a little farfetched. There's got to be a compromise." Printed warnings might hurt ticket sales, and the audience would "lose the ability to suspend disbelief."

Some performers have used taped music as a background prop for years. Increasingly, though, such performers as Paula Abdul and Janet Jackson have come to rely on taped music as they pursue perfection in their elaborate stage shows and complex dance routines. Milli Vanilli and New Kids on the Block use taped vocals for as many as half their concerts, reports Billboard, a trade publication. Agents for the groups didn't return phone calls.

Moreover, "kids today are reared on MTV, and they've come to expect studio-quality performances," says New Jersey Assemblyman Joe Mecca, a co-sponsor of the bill.

But such precision acts can backfire. On tour last summer, Billboard reports, the group Milli Vanilli (which received a 1990 Grammy for best new artist) walked off the stage after a malfunction stopped its vocals. Minutes later, the band played on, but no one was on stage.

Taped vocals can save a lot of breath when singers stage spunky dance routines, and can be insurance against illness.

But fans, and even some ticket brokers, decry the practice. "It's sort of like going to an opera and having them play the album," says Chris White, office manager of American Ticket Service. "Hey, if I wanted to hear prerecorded music, I'd go to listen to a deejay."
