Brady Says Cost Of S&L Bailout Could Double --- Bill Could Be $300 Billion, Including Interest, and Taxpayers Will Foot It

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 05/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration acknowledged that the cost of the savings and loan cleanup could nearly double, and said the government borrowing needed to finance it could produce upward pressure on interest rates. Ultimately, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady told Congress yesterday, the taxpayer is on the hook for the new costs.

Previously, the Bush administration estimated that $50 billion would cover the costs of closing down all the S&Ls that became insolvent through 1992, with another $23 billion required for thrifts that went under after 1992. The administration now estimates those costs at between $90 billion and $130 billion.

Those numbers don't include billions of dollars in interest on borrowings to finance the cleanup, plus additional costs of S&L sales in 1988. When those expenses are added, the cost over 10 years could approach $300 billion, compared with $166 billion that the administration previously forecast.

The S&L industry, paying higher deposit-insurance premiums and other assessments associated with the bailout, can't provide any of the added cash. "Additional resources will have to come from Treasury funds," Mr. Brady told the Senate Banking Committee.

Mr. Brady played down the importance of the current S&L costs in President Bush's call for a budget summit with congressional leaders. When asked why the summit was called at this time, the secretary replied: "It's budget time."

But at a separate Senate hearing yesterday, White House Budget Director Richard Darman said the S&L costs figured prominently. "The prospect of all this additional borrowing in a compressed period . . . could lead to upward pressure on interest rates," threatening economic growth, Mr. Darman said.

"Higher interest rates could mean the economy goes from growth -- although it's too slow growth -- into recession," Mr. Darman told a Senate appropriations subcommittee. "It's important we have a deficit-reduction package to avoid upward pressure on interest rates." Because the government can't avoid borrowing to finance the S&L cleanup, he argued, it should reduce borrowing for other purposes.

Congress has been skeptical of the cost estimates of the bailout since the plan was announced in February 1989. The General Accounting Office, the investigative arm of Congress, estimated last month that the total cleanup costs would reach $140 billion, not including interest.

The original Bush plan assumed there would be no inflation, that thrift deposits would grow steadily, and that fewer new S&Ls would fail. Instead, interest rates have risen about one percentage point, the real estate market -- the investment vehicle for most S&Ls -- is weak, more thrifts have become insolvent than expected, and buyers for insolvent thrifts have been scarce.

"This is an unmitigated disaster," said Rep. Thomas McMillen (D., Md.). "I think we are going to continue to see these numbers racheted upward."

The Resolution Trust Corp., the agency that sells insolvent thrifts, already has 423 thrifts under its control, and in new estimates announced yesterday, the Office of Thrift Supervision said that it projects another 299 failures. Beyond that, 315 other S&Ls are considered possible failures, depending on economic conditions. If many more in the borderline group fail, it would push the costs toward the high end of the administration estimates.

The final costs won't be known until the investments of the government-seized thrifts are sold to the private sector. In the thrift assets sold so far, losses have ranged from 14% to 23% of book value, a senior administration official said. That doesn't include losses that the government acquired when it took over the thrifts.

Despite slow sales of the thrift assets and added costs, Congress should resist the temptation to tinker with the new S&L law -- with the exception of appropriating new funds, Mr. Brady said. He advised Congress to provide indefinite authority to fund the rest of the cleanup, rather than in increments.
