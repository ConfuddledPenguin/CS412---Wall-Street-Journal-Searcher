House Panel Sets Milk Support Price As 5-Year Floor

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 05/24/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Agriculture Committee adopted a $3.6 billion compromise plan to prop up milk prices and hold down dairy prices for the next five years. The committee agreed to set the current milk support price of $10.10 per hundred pounds as a five-year floor price for dairy farmers. But the plan would allow for adjustments in the support price to reflect fluctuations in government-owned surpluses.

Committee staffers estimated that the dairy plan would cost $1.6 billion more than what the current dairy program would cost if it were extended for the next five years. But they said that the plan would still be consistent with Senate Budget Committee guidelines, which House lawmakers are prepared to follow in the event of a deficit-reduction agreement.

The plan calls for an automatic price-support rise of at least 25 cents per hundred pounds if dairy surpluses fell below 3.5 billion pounds. The current $10.10 price would be maintained as long as surpluses range between 3.5 billion and five billion pounds. And if dairy farmers produced between five billion pounds and seven billion pounds more than the market could bear, the support price would drop between 25 cents and 50 cents per hundred pounds. But in no case would it go below the $10.10 floor price.

The government buys surplus cheese, butter and nonfat dry milk whenever commercial demand isn't sufficient to keep milk prices from slipping below the support price.

If government purchases were forecast to exceed seven billion pounds in any year, the Agriculture Department could inaugurate an inventory management program, beginning in 1992, to hold down milk production.

The compromise was offered by Rep. Charles Stenholm (D., Texas), chairman of the dairy subcommittee, and Rep. Steve Gunderson (R., Wis.), the ranking minority member, as an alternative to a plan that had been embraced earlier by the subcommittee.

That plan would have set a floor price of $10.60 per hundred pounds, imposed assessments on dairy farmers to discourage production and created a standby system of two-tier price supports. The Bush administration and some farm groups strongly objected to a two-tier system.

The committee, in a minor concession to critics of farm subsidies, voted to put a $250,000 cap on so-called incentive payments to individual wool and mohair producers and to freeze the honey-price support for beekeepers at the fiscal 1990 level.
