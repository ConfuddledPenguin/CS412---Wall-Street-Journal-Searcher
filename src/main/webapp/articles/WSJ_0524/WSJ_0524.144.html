TWA Machinists Offer to Take Over Airline; Icahn Would Keep Other Assets

  • Author: Judith Valente
  • Date: 05/24/90

Trans World Airlines Inc.'s machinists union offered owner Carl C. Icahn a framework under which employees would take control of the ailing carrier. The union said it proposed acquiring 100% of TWA through an employee stock ownership plan that would also include the carrier's pilots, flight attendants and nonunion employees. The labor group hasn't yet made a formal offer, saying it hasn't completed an evaluation of TWA's assets.

However, the union is expected to argue that the carrier currently has a negative value that removes the necessity for employees to pay anything for it. Under the machinists' plan, employees would take over only the airline. Mr. Icahn would keep TWA's investments in such other companies as USX Corp. Those investments are valued at between $1 billion and $1.6 billion. The union also wants Mr. Icahn to remain responsible for the company's debt.

Mr. Icahn has been seeking to unload the unprofitable carrier for months. Such a plan would allow him to "exit gracefully," said machinist union leader Timothy Connolly.

If the machinists' proposal progresses, TWA would become the second major carrier where employees are seeking to take control. Last month, UAL Corp.'s board approved a $4.38 billion buy-out proposal from United Airlines unions. The UAL unions have until Aug. 9 to finance their offer.

The TWA machinists took their inspiration for the plan from a threat Mr. Icahn made a few weeks ago to transfer TWA's domestic route system to America West Airlines Inc. But a transfer of assets won't prove easy.

TWA is $2.8 billion in debt, the result of a 1988 transaction in which Mr. Icahn took the carrier private. The employee group doesn't want to assume responsibility for the debt. But it is unlikely Mr. Icahn would agree to remain responsible for it.

Mr. Icahn said he is open to an employee takeover, but called the machinists' proposal "a pretty tough deal." He said before talks can proceed, he wants assurances that the employees would make concessions to keep the airline going. He also said he is concerned about labor's ability to continue making lease payments on aircraft an Icahn-controlled company owns.

TWA, which had losses of $298 million last year, has been staggering under the weight of about $490 million in annual debt service. It has already posted losses of $143 million in the first three months of this year, when higher costs for fuel, service and maintenance were factors.

Employee owners would inherit the fundamental problem that has plagued TWA for years. It is largely an international carrier with a small domestic route system to feed its overseas flights. It faces increased competition from foreign carriers and U.S. carriers with vast domestic routes.

The machinists union contends that the employees, as owners, would be willing to provide wage concessions and productivity gains needed to fund growth at the airline. TWA's unions have refused Mr. Icahn any further concessions. In turn, Mr. Icahn has postponed placing additional orders for new aircraft and resisted making other capital investments in TWA.

The machinists' proposal needs the support of other TWA unions to succeed. Leaders of the pilots and flight attendants unions said they would support a change in ownership, but aren't certain the machinists' proposal can succeed.

Whether or not the proposal succeeds, TWA's debt needs to be restructured in order for the airline to survive, said pilots union leader William Compton. Mr. Compton said the pilots are also looking into an arrangement whereby TWA creditors would exchange debt they hold for common stock in the airline.
