Health: Tobacco Is Facing New Attacks

  • Author: Kathleen Deveny and Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 05/24/90

The pressure continues to mount on tobacco companies. Health and Human Services Secretary Louis Sullivan is planning to recommend curbs on the industry as part of a stepped-up campaign against smoking by minors, and Harvard University disclosed that it has sold most of its investments in tobacco companies.

HHS officials said Dr. Sullivan will push for restrictions on cigarette vending machines, a legal smoking age of 19 and tougher licensing provisions for retailers. Dr. Sullivan will include these and other provisions in an anti-smoking code he plans to recommend that states adopt when he testifies today before the Senate Finance Committee, HHS officials said.

Although 44 states have laws banning the sale of cigarettes to minors, the laws generally are loosely enforced, if at all. Studies show that most adults who smoke started when they were minors. Dr. Sullivan believes that one of the best ways to fight smoking is to discourage minors from taking up the habit.

Meanwhile, critics said Harvard's move could spark a wave of pressure on shareholders to sell tobacco stocks, much like the movement to persuade universities and state governments to dump holdings in firms doing business in South Africa.

Several anti-industry efforts already have begun. Brad Krevor, director of the Tobacco Divestment Project in Boston, said his newly formed group plans to contact hospitals, insurers and state pension funds. Also in Massachusetts, a bill cosponsored by state Rep. John Bussinger is being considered that would require the state to divest itself of all tobacco stock.

Harvard President Derek Bok disclosed the sale in a letter dated May 18 to students at the university's School of Public Health. He said the divestiture was prompted by recognition of the dangers of smoking and concern over aggressive marketing tactics to promote smoking among teen-agers and in countries where the dangers of smoking aren't widely known.

As of June 30, 1989, when the divestitures began, Harvard owned shares of tobacco companies worth $58 million, including $41 million of Philip Morris Cos. shares. Harvard continues indirectly to own part of RJR Nabisco Inc. as part of a limited partnership managed by the leveraged buy-out firm Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co.

"When the president of Harvard comes out and says, `This is dirty money, I don't want any part of it,' that's going to cause a pretty strong ripple effect across the country," said Greg Connolly, an official at the Massachusetts Department of Public Health. "It could be the start of a nationwide trend."

The divestiture movement has taken a long time to pick up any steam. As long ago as 1981, the American Medical Association vowed to rid its investment portfolio of holdings in tobacco companies. In 1987, the AMA called on medical schools and their parent universities to divest holdings in tobacco stocks, but there was little response to the call.

Besides the efforts in Massachusetts, City University of New York has resolved to sell its holdings in tobacco companies, according to Mr. Krevor. City University officials couldn't be reached for comment.

The Teachers Insurance and Annuity Association's College Retirement Equities Funds, a nationwide pension system for employees of colleges and universities, launched a Social Choice Account in March. The new account doesn't invest funds in companies that market tobacco or alcohol, among other criteria.

But the Investor Responsibility Research Center, which tracks social issues for investors, said it wasn't aware of any other university or investment funds divesting shares in tobacco companies.

That lack of interest may result, in part, from the fact that most tobacco stocks, despite their embattled image, have been strong performers. Last year, Philip Morris stock outperformed the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index by 36%, and is ahead by another 5% this year, according to Marc Cohen, a tobacco analyst with Sanford C. Bernstein & Co.

A spokesman for Philip Morris wouldn't comment on the possibility of increased anti-tobacco activism on the part of holders. "Stockholders can make decisions based on any criteria they choose to employ," he said. "But as a Harvard grad, I hope they invest in an equally profitable corporation." A spokesman for American Brands Inc. declined to comment on the Harvard decision. Officials of Loews Corp.'s Lorillard Inc. unit couldn't be reached.

The model code that Dr. Sullivan plans to recommend today to the states will incorporate parts of existing statutes. Advisers said he will propose that:

-- Vending machines, which account for about 16% of illegal cigarette sales to minors, be banned or limited to bars, offices, and other places used largely by adults.

-- Retailers be required to obtain licenses to sell cigarettes. Currently, 31 states license vendors. Retailers who sell to minors would run the risk of having their licenses suspended or revoked.

-- Civil fines be levied against retailers who sell to minors.

-- Signs warning that sales to minors are illegal be posted at all points of sale.

Dr. Sullivan also is considering ways to restrict cigarette advertising aimed at minors, but officials say he may not be ready to announce any such initiatives today.
