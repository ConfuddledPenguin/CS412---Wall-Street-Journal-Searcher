Leveraged Buy-Out Funds Are High-Fliers No Longer

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 05/24/90

NEW YORK -- Amid a deep slump in the leveraged buy-out business, the once-astronomical investment returns of LBO funds are coming back to earth. First, more LBOs are running into financial trouble. Second, when LBO funds try to cash out by selling companies, they often can't get top prices.

Finally, with the volume of buy-outs down 86% this year, LBO funds have been left with huge pools of capital that remain uninvested. To put these unused billions to work, and meet new stringent standards set by bank lenders, LBO fund managers are doing deals with a lot less leverage and more equity.

As a result, some of the funds that once boasted annual returns of 50% to 100% now say they are shooting for only 18% to 40% annually. "The returns should be lower because the deals require more equity; it's simple arithmetic," says Stephen Schwartzman, president of Blackstone Group, which has an $850 million buy-out fund.

Last week, in a deal that exemplifies the new, less-leveraged buy-outs, the $1.5 billion LBO fund run by Merrill Lynch & Co. paid $100 million to buy Londontown Holdings Corp., maker of London Fog raincoats.

Instead of selling high-risk, high-yield "junk" bonds or seeking new bank debt, the Merrill fund simply assumed Londontown's existing debts of $180 million. That gave the deal a debt-to-equity ratio of less than 2-to-1, instead of the 10-to-1 debt-equity ratio that used to jet-propel the returns of LBOs in the 1980s.

Here's why putting more equity into buy-outs can drag down investment returns:

A fund buys a company for $100 and sells it a year later for $110. If it puts down $10 in equity and borrows $90 for the purchase, it clears $20 on the sale after repaying debt -- a $10 profit, or a 100% return. But if the fund puts down $40 and borrows only $60, the same $10 profit amounts to only 25%.

James J. Burke Jr., president of Merrill Lynch Capital Partners, Merrill's buy-out fund, says, "The financing environment at the moment is as bad as I've seen it in 10 years. The market for bank debt is in a complete state of disarray." Similarly troubled is the market for junk bonds, which financed most of the big LBOs in recent years.

Although the Merrill fund continues to pursue deals and has completed nine buy-outs in the past 18 months, Mr. Burke says Merrill has lowered its return target from 40% annually to as little as 30%.

Executives of the largest buy-out group, Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., declined to be interviewed. KKR touted annual returns of 60% back in 1987 when the firm was seeking investors for a giant $5.6 billion fund. More recently, KKR has suffered some high-profile financial setbacks with a few of its buy-outs, among them Seaman Furniture and Jim Walter Co.

Now, a spokesman for the firm says, KKR's investment target is a mere 18% to 25% annually. "The standard quote is that they expect to make three to four times their money in six to seven years," the spokesman said. "That's been their pitch since the Stone Age." And one KKR investor notes that the Seaman and Jim Walter investments amount to less than 5% of the KKR fund.

Although 18% a year is still enough to outperform the stock market's long-term average 10% annual return, it's a far cry from 60%, particularly since buy-out investments are long-term, illiquid, and expensive. And the pension funds and other big institutions that invest in LBO funds still must pay annual fees averaging 1.5% of their commitments, even if the money is uninvested. That's more than double what the average stock manager charges.

One reason that the returns of LBO funds are falling is that it's taking longer for them to resell at a profit companies they earlier bought. And the longer that takes, the lower the funds' annual returns will be.

For example, Merrill's first LBO fund, a smaller pool with $400 million, still owns nine of the 20 companies it purchased between 1985 and 1988. At the end of 1988, the fund's annual rate of return was 58%. By the end of 1989, that annual rate had declined to 50%.

The bleak buy-out environment has driven some players to the sidelines. Gibbons, Green van Amerongen Ltd., which raised a $425 million fund in 1987, suffered financial reverses in two buy-outs in the past year, Sheller-Globe Corp. and Ohio Mattress Corp. Its partners split up last year, and the firm doesn't have any plans for new investments or raising new money, according to a spokesman.

Some buy-out executives argue that the reduced level of activity and competition for deals will boost potential returns for those who remain on the field. "Returns are largely a function of purchase price," says Joseph L. Rice, president of the buy-out firm of Clayton & Dubilier Inc. Prices for new LBOs should be lower, he says, because "a tremendous number of marginal players have been pushed to the sidelines."

Mr. Rice adds, "We're in the business of trying to achieve 50% compounded on the funds that we're investing." That target hasn't changed, he says. Buy-out financing will merely revert to "the market that existed before 1983," when Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. popularized using junk bonds for buy-outs.

Instead of new junk-bond issues, buy-out funds will revert to obtaining subordinated debt financing from insurance companies and other big investors in the private-placement market, Mr. Rice said. But that will limit the size of deals, because the private market isn't as deep or as easy to tap as the public junk market.

Another buy-out firm that expects to prosper in the current tougher environment is Forstmann Little & Co. That's because Forstmann has always shunned the use of junk bonds, relying instead for financing on its own $1.6 billion subordinated debt fund, as well as a $800 million buy-out equity fund. That capability enabled Forstmann to do one of the largest buy-outs this year, purchasing the Gulfstream Aerospace jet-plane maker from Chrysler Corp. for $825 million.
