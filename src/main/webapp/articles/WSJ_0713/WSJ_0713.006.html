Chicago Investors Raise $1 Billion For Workout Fund

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 07/13/90

Chicago investors Samuel Zell and David Schulte have raised $1 billion for a so-called workout fund, aimed at rescuing financially troubled companies. The Zell/Chilmark Fund obtained institutional investors' commitments yesterday for one of the largest capital pools in the burgeoning business of restructuring companies that took on too much debt in the late-1980s heyday of high-risk, high-yield junk bonds.

Mr. Zell, chairman of Chicago-based Equity Financial and Management Co., promotes himself as "the Grave Dancer" and delights in profiting from business wreckage. Investors, he says, have called his new fund "the Grave Dancer Fund."

The fund will attempt to make investments to gain operating control of companies and resolve their financial problems at the same time. "The thesis of this whole fund is `good company, bad balance sheet.' This is not a turnaround fund or a company-doctor fund," Mr. Zell said.

One of the Zell/Chilmark investors said yesterday that the type of situation ripe for an investment by the new fund is Interco Inc., a St. Louis furniture and shoe company, which took on $1.8 billion in debt to fight off a 1988 bid by a corporate raider, but defaulted on its debt last month.

Mr. Schulte, managing partner of Chilmark Partners, a Chicago-based investment banking boutique specializing in reorganizations, said he believes "cash will make a great deal of difference" in persuading holders of bonds of distressed companies to accept new securities. Before forming Chilmark in 1984, Mr. Schulte led a reorganization group at Salomon Brothers Inc.

Mr. Zell is best known for an investment in Itel Corp., a container-leasing company that filed for bankruptcy-law protection from creditors in 1981 and emerged through reorganization in 1983. Mr. Zell, who became chairman in 1985, claims an annual return on his investment of more than 70%.

The fund aims to put the money to work within six years, and will have an estimated life of ten to twelve years before its investments are sold and the capital returned to its investors. The fund's goal is an annual return rate of 40%.

Placement agent for the fund was Merrill Lynch & Co. Management fees for the partnership start at 1.9% annually of commitments under $20 million, declining to 1.25% for investments greater than $125 million.
