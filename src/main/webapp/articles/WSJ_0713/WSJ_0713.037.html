Bill to Bar Bias Against Disabled Voted by House-Senate Conferees

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The House approved legislation designed to bar discrimination against disabled people. Lawmakers voted 377-28 to approve a compromise version of the bill that was hammered out in a House-Senate conference committee. The bill also is expected to clear the Senate and win President Bush's signature. Mr. Bush has supported the legislation, which would guarantee access to employment and public accommodations for 43 million people with disabilities, since his 1988 election campaign.

Before approving the measure, the House rebuffed, 224-180, a Republican attempt to send it back to conferees with instructions to revive an amendment designed to let employers transfer employees with AIDS from food-handling jobs. Earlier yesterday, the conferees approved compromise language protecting workers with AIDS from being shifted from such jobs.

The provision would allow employers to reassign workers who pose a threat to the health of others, but it would instruct the secretary of health and human services to list diseases that can involve such a threat. Secretary Louis Sullivan has said that AIDS isn't transmitted by casual contact, such as handling food.

The conference version of the bill also would establish a Senate board to handle employment-discrimination complaints lodged by disabled job-seekers or workers against senators. Conferees earlier approved a similar arrangement for the House. The decision would deny disabled people the right to sue lawmakers for discrimination, but they would have the right to sue when their grievances involve private employers.

Otherwise, the basic provisions of the Americans with Disabilities Act apply to Congress and private employers and establishments alike. The bill guarantees access for disabled people to public places, such as theaters, zoos, government buildings and grocery stores.

The bill also assures deaf people access to telecommunications and bans discrimination in hiring or promoting qualified disabled people. Employment provisions will apply eventually to all employers with 15 or more workers. There are some narrow exemptions in the public-accommodations section of the bill for small businesses.
