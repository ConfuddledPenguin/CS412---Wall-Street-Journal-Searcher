Technology & Medicine: Computer Firm Founders Sue Kubota of Japan

  • Author: Stephen Kreider Yoder
  • Date: 07/13/90

The founders of a Silicon Valley supercomputer company sued Japan's Kubota Ltd., contending that the Japanese giant conspired to co-opt the U.S. company's technology by using its investment in the concern as leverage. Allen Michels and Matthew Sanders, founders of a company that became Stardent Computer Inc., filed the complaint in a California court yesterday, alleging that Kubota forced the merger that created the company. Kubota then began to transfer the technology to a wholly owned Kubota subsidiary, they allege.

"It's a Trojan horse trick," said Joseph Alioto, the pair's attorney, who filed the suit in Superior Court in Santa Clara. Kubota, through its 22% stake in Stardent, took control of the company's technology, he said.

The suit, calling for $25 million in damages, charges the Osaka-based Kubota and several of its U.S. units with fraud, intentional misrepresentation and fraudulent concealment, among other allegations. Messrs. Michels and Sanders, co-chairmen of Stardent, also name Kubota president Shigekazu Mino and other top Kubota executives in the suit.

"We're very surprised here at this extremely sudden and unexpected suit," said a Kubota spokesman in Japan. He added that the company wouldn't have any added comment until it had studied the suit.

Kubota officials in the U.S. couldn't be reached for comment.

The allegations are sure to fire up criticism that Japanese investments in U.S. electronics and other technology companies drain the U.S. of valuable industrial assets. Critics of Japanese investment have suggested that some Japanese companies are seeking to carry innovative U.S. technology home to conglomerates that have a harder time coming up with creative new ideas, but can easily translate others' ideas into products.

Indeed, Kubota, a steel and farm-equipment maker, has been at the forefront of the investment boom in the U.S. In the mid-1980s, Kubota, along with other steel companies, set off on a course to survive by launching into new industries.

Kubota bought a stake in Ardent Computer Corp. of Sunnyvale, Calif., in 1986. Later, Ardent merged with Stellar Computer Co. of Newton, Mass. to form Stardent.

Messrs. Michels and Sanders, in the suit, contend that Kubota forced the merger, threatening to let the companies slide into financial ruin should they not agree. Stellar was on the brink of bankruptcy at the time, says Mr. Michels, Stardent's co-founder.

Mr. Alioto said Kubota recently set up Kubota Computers America in Sunnyvale to absorb Stardent's technology. The suit alleges that Kubota "secretly planned to develop products that would compete with Stardent," despite agreements to the contrary, he added.
