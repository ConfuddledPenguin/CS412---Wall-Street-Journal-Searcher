Letters to the Editor: IMF Never Proposes A `Shock' Treatment

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/13/90

I noted in David Asman's June 11 editorial-page article, "Is Mexico's New Market Economy Here to Stay?" a sentence implying the International Monetary Fund routinely recommends "higher taxes and severe currency devaluations," which you term "the recessionary `shock' formula." Permit me to correct your misapprehensions about the role and effect that currency devaluation and tax policies have in the reform efforts of a country seeking to correct severe payments imbalances.

A country's exchange rate is its most strategic price. When that key price gets out of line because of inflation, imports become too cheap and exports unprofitable; the country runs into financial difficulties as its need for foreign currencies to pay its bills outstrips what it can earn from exporting or what foreigners are prepared to invest or lend. Among other dire effects, savings flee abroad because the country's savers know the situation cannot last, and resources vitally needed for investment are lost. This is only one of the ways that an overvalued currency undermines a country's economy, and why the IMF recommends devaluation when it is needed.

The IMF never proposes devaluation to give a country an artificial trade advantage, but to get the exchange rate to a level that no longer distorts trade. Of course, devaluation by itself does not work, and may indeed feed inflation. But no one, certainly not the IMF, has ever suggested or recommended devaluation alone as a remedy. On the contrary, the IMF strongly urges that devaluation be accompanied by budget and monetary restraint and other measures to contain price increases.

A root cause of inflation and overvalued exchange rates often is underdisciplined budget policy. A country must raise the money it needs to pay for government services. The decision regarding the level and scope of public services that are provided is up to the country's government, and is a function of the government's social and political priorities. The IMF has no say in these matters. But to ensure an appropriate overall fiscal balance, the IMF sometimes supports both tax increases and tax-reform efforts. In so doing, the IMF encourages broad-based tax systems and discourages excessively high marginal rates that reduce work incentives, promote tax cheating, and penalize saving and investment. Devaluation and tax raises are not routinely advocated by the IMF, as Mr. Asman implies. They are recommended only where needed, and then as essential elements of comprehensive reforms to stabilize economies and restore growth.

Azizali F. Mohammed

Director

External Relations Department

International Monetary Fund

Washington
