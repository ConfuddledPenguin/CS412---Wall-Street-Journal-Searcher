Conferees Agree On Double Hulls For Oil Tankers

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- House and Senate conferees debating oil-spill legislation agreed to require double hulls for new tankers and to phase them in for most existing ships by 2010. Agreement on this once-contentious issue came after little discussion among the lawmakers. The requirement once was opposed strongly by many oil companies, which contended that any costly retrofitting should await further study. Recently, however, the industry has come to accept double hulls as unavoidable, especially in light of the rash of oil spills in U.S. waters in the past 18 months.

The provisions approved by the conferees would establish schedules for retrofitting existing vessels, generally requiring the largest and oldest ships to be equipped with double hulls first. Extra time would be allotted for tankers that transfer oil to other ships far offshore, tankers with double bottoms or sides, and barges and other ships that operate in inland waterways. Congressional aides said much of the existing fleet will be retired rather than retrofitted.

Vessels weighing less than 5,000 tons could be exempt from the double-hull requirement entirely if they have double bottoms or sides that the secretary of transportation determines would be as effective as double hulls for preventing oil leaks.

Environmental groups viewed the conferees' action as a mixed bag. While they have long favored double hulls, they had pushed for a more aggressive retrofit schedule, and they particularly opposed exceptions for inland barges and other ships.

Agreement on double hulls moves the conferees close to approval of a comprehensive oil spill measure. Conferees removed a major hurdle to the bill last month, when they rejected provisions that would have provided for U.S. participation in an international treaty limiting shippers' liability in spills.

Among other provisions, the bill would raise federal liability limits for tankers, barges and oil facilities, and it would improve contingency planning for oil spills. Federal cleanup and damage-assessment costs, plus compensation for damages, beyond the liability limits would be paid out of a five-cents-a barrel tax on domestic and imported oil. That fee has been collected since Jan. 1.
