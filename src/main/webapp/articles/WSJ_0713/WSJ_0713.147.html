Accounting Changes On International Calls Proposed by the FCC

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Communications Commission proposed changes in obscure accounting rules on international telephone calls to make it easier for long-distance companies to negotiate lower accounting rates. Accounting rates are the basis for allocating payments on international calls between calls that use a U.S. and a foreign telephone carrier. Under current rates, about 75 cents of each $1 collected by U.S. carriers on overseas calls goes to foreign carriers, the agency said. The FCC said the rates widened the gap in U.S. net settlements to $2 billion in 1988 from about $40 million in 1970. The agency figures that international accounting rates that are above the foreign carriers' costs led to overpayments by U.S. consumers totaling as much as $1 billion.

The agency proposed three steps to speed reductions in accounting rates: allowing companies to file notices for simple reductions in accounting rates; streamlining waiver procedures for more complicated changes, including cutting the agency's review time; and tentatively concluding that it has the authority to establish lower rates based on costs.

Alfred Sikes, the FCC's chairman, said the commission wants to learn about the true costs, levels of profits, and how those costs and profits are shared among U.S. telephone companies and foreign telecommunications authorities. "We will also be examining ways to start down the road toward correcting inequitable arrangements" with cost-based pricing, accounting and allocation of profits, he said. An overhaul, he noted, will require cooperation not only from the Bush administration but ultimately from foreign governments.
