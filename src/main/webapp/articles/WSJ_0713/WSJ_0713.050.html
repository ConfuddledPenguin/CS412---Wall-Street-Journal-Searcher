Economy: Caution Urged in Assessing Tax Debt of Foreign Firms

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration's top tax official urged Congress to use "extreme caution" before concluding that U.S. subsidiaries of foreign companies are evading U.S. taxes. Testifying on the second day of hearings at a House Ways and Means subcommittee, Kenneth Gideon, assistant Treasury secretary for tax policy, said "it might be tempting to infer" from data that foreign-controlled firms are illegally shifting income out of the U.S. by paying excessive prices in transactions with their overseas parents. But he said there may be other explanations for the low tax bills, such as "start-up expenses, acquisition indebtedness, the age of the investment."

Nevertheless, Mr. Gideon said, "There's certainly data there to indicate we should be looking carefully at the transfer-pricing practices of foreign firms."

The hearings follow a nine-month investigation by the subcommittee of 36 U.S. subsidiaries of foreign automobile, electronics and motorcycle companies. On Tuesday, subcommittee investigators testified that more than half of the companies paid little or no tax over a 10-year period, largely by inflating the prices they paid their parents for goods, services and technology to reduce their U.S. incomes. U.S. tax law requires companies to charge market prices in transactions with related firms.
