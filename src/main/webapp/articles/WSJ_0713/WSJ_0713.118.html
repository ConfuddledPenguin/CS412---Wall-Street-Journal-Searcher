ABORTION IN THE GERMANYS

East Germany's major political factions said they would fight to retain a liberal abortion law when the nation unites with West Germany.

The archconservative German Social Union was the only party of Prime Minister Lothar de Maiziere's governing coalition that objected to retaining the law. "An abortion is always murder," said one German Social Union lawmaker.

East Germany allows a female over age 14 to receive an abortion on demand during the first three months of pregnancy. West Germany allows abortions in the first three months only in cases of rape or incest, if the mother's life is in danger, if the fetus is expected to be severely handicapped, or if the woman's financial situation would make it impossible for her to raise a child.

In West Germany, a nation of 60 million, there were 75,297 abortions performed last year; East Germany, with 16 million people, had 73,000 abortions.

JORDANIAN ART UPROAR

Jordan's Moslem Brotherhood and other Islamic fundamentalist legislators have condemned the government for staging a Western-style arts festival championed by U.S.-born Queen Noor. Branding the government as careless and irresponsible, the festival's critics said it should have been canceled because Jordan is facing grave economic problems and should be preparing for a holy war to liberate Palestine. The queen opened the festival Wednesday evening in Jerash, 25 miles north of Amman.

SPANISH NUCLEAR INCIDENT

Spain's Vandellos II nuclear plant went back on line yesterday, four days after it was ordered closed due to a valve leak that filled the containment building with radioactive steam. The start-up of Vandellos II, one of the newest of Spain's nine operating nuclear plants, was authorized by the Nuclear Security Commission after it investigated the incident, which didn't leak any radiation into the air. A commission spokeswoman said preliminary investigative data indicated the plant's owner, the Asociacion Nuclear Vandellos, delayed reporting the incident.

INDIA'S AIRBUSES TO FLY AGAIN

The Indian Airlines Airbus-320 jetliners grounded after a fatal crash last February will return to the skies next week, the Civil Aviation Ministry said. Soon after the accident at the Bangalore airport in southern India, the government grounded the airline's remaining 14 Airbus-320s pending an investigation. Civil Aviation Minister Arif Mohammad Khan said the planes would be reintroduced in phases.

AUSTRALIAN SHEEP

Australia imposed tighter regulations on live-sheep exports in an effort to reverse the sheep's poor image in the Mideast. A shipload of 20,000 sheep was rejected by Bahrain this week because the incidence of scabby-mouth disease was greater than the accepted level of 5%. Other shipments to the Mideast were turned away in April and May. An official of the Australian Meat & Livestock Corp. said the new guidelines include having a veterinarian travel on each ship.

KLM HIRES MCKINSEY

KLM Royal Dutch Airlines has hired the management-consultant group McKinsey & Co. to advise it on how to cut costs. The airline said it would form an employees' committee that would be advised by McKinsey on how to maintain service while cutting costs. The committee and McKinsey "have to come up with advice about how to do things efficiently. In the competitive world of civil aviation, you can't cut costs that reduce quality because that is what the customer wants," a KLM spokesman said.

DISAPPEARANCE IN POLAND

Lech Grobelny, the founder of the BKO savings bank in Warsaw and owner of a chain of currency exchanges, hasn't returned from a holiday abroad, the newspaper Gazeta Wyborcza reported.

According to the report, Mr. Grobelny left Poland on June 6 for a two-week holiday in an undisclosed location. Employees told the newspaper they last heard from him in late June when he informed them he would return home July 6. On Monday, employees informed the prosecutor's office about Mr. Grobelny's continuing absence.

About 10,000 customers have accounts with BKO with deposits of some 32 billion zlotys, or $3.3 million dollars, according to the daily. But rumors of the BKO owner's disappearance have been circulating for some time in Warsaw and the bank's cash reserves have shrunk due to increased withdrawals.

BKO was closed yesterday, and customers found a note on the door telling them to "vindicate their claims at a district court," the news agency PAP said.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

Adolf Hitler and Hermann Goering will no longer be honorary citizens of Potsdam, East Germany, if a City Hall proposal is approved next month, the East German news agency ADN reported. The two were awarded the honor in 1933, but it was never revoked, partly because of the ousted Communist leadership's fear of causing a public scandal, ADN said. . . . A Pakistani has been sentenced to death in Bahrain for drug trafficking, the Akhbar al-Khaleej newspaper reported. It was the first time the death penalty for drug smuggling was imposed in Bahrain since it was introduced in 1984.
