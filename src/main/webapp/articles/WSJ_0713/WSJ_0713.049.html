Federal Express Posts 5% Increase In Quarterly Profit

  • Author: Peter Waldman
  • Date: 07/13/90

Federal Express Corp. said it had a slight increase in net income in its fiscal fourth quarter, ended May 31, but it reported a drop in operating profit, reflecting continued losses overseas. The Memphis, Tenn.-based air-express concern reported net income of $43.1 million, or 81 cents a share, up 5% from $41 million, or 78 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue climbed 15% to $1.91 billion from $1.67 billion.

But the earnings gain, Federal's first year-to-year profit growth since November 1988, masks some underlying problems. Federal's operating profit declined 7% in the period. It is still being hurt by its attempts to expand abroad, and a sudden downturn in its domestic business in recent months is compounding its problems.

"The sluggishness in the domestic economy is showing up and they don't see any pickup," said Kevin Murphy, a transportation analyst with Morgan Stanley. "This is a company in the throes of a transition to a new type of business. It's still a good game plan, but I don't see any reasonable signs that it's bearing fruit right now."

Federal was able to report a profit boost in the quarter, despite the drop in operating profit, by reducing interest expenses and adjusting some liabilities from its acquisition last year of Tiger International Inc. In all, it reported $14.4 million less of what it calls "other expenses" in the period, compared with a year ago.

For the year, Federal had net income of $115.8 million, or $2.18 a share, down 31% from $184.6 million, or $3.53 a share, in fiscal 1989. Revenue rose 36% to $7.02 billion from $5.17 billion.

Mr. Murphy estimated that Federal had losses of $150 million in its most recent fiscal year from overseas expansion. Much of those losses came in Europe, where freight forwarders -- the middlemen that book freight transportation for clients -- have been reluctant to use the heavy-cargo operation that Federal acquired with Tiger, because they view Federal as a potential competitor in door-to-door service.

"Forwarders are watching the situation very, very closely," said Manfred Engst, executive vice president of Schenkers International Forwarders Inc., Frankfurt, West Germany, a freight broker and a large Federal customer.

On Federal's trans-Pacific routes, cargo shipments have fallen nearly 20% since February, analysts say, because of sluggish import demand in the U.S. that's affecting all carriers.

Thomas Oliver, Federal's senior vice president, international, said the company has found that for every 1% that the U.S. economy slows down, imports drop 10%. He said Federal's business is slowly improving in Europe, but still has a long way to go.

Mr. Oliver refused to predict when Federal's struggling international operations would become profitable, saying only, "I'm quite positive about what we can accomplish in the next few years."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Federal closed at $45.50, up 87.5 cents.
