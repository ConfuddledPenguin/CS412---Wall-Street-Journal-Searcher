Technology & Medicine: Scientists Find Gene That Can Cause Skin Tumors and Learning Disabilities

  • Author: Michael Waldholz
  • Date: 07/13/90

Scientists have found the gene that causes neurofibromatosis, a disease that produces birthmarks, tumors of the skin and nerve cells and learning disabilities in about 100,000 Americans. The gene discovery is expected to lead to better diagnosis and, perhaps, the first effective therapy for the relatively common, inherited disorder. Scientists who found the so-called NF gene also said it may play a surprisingly broad role in the development and inheritance of several common types of cancer.

The scientists speculate that the NF gene may be part of recently identified class of genes that, when defective, can unleash a cancer. Molecular biologists chasing after these genes believe a cancer develops after a cell accumulates a number of these mutated "tumor suppressor" genes, either through inheritance or some type of damage to a person's genetic material.

"The NF discovery seems to provide a bit of the molecular pathway that transforms normal tissue to tumor tissue," said Ray White, a molecular biologist at University of Utah who led one of two research teams that simultaneously reported finding the NF gene. "With each discovery the picture of how a cancer occurs at the most basic level becomes more clear."

Finding the NF gene culminates a race involving a handful of major research institutions over more than five years, part of the world-wide hunt by biologists, geneticists, and doctors to map the entire human genome, the collection of all 50,000 to 100,000 genes that direct the moment-to-moment functions of human life.

Mr. White's group in Utah, along with researchers at the National Cancer Institute, reported their findings in the current issue of the scientific journal Cell. At the same time, Francis Collins and colleagues at the University of Michigan and Washington University School of Medicine, reported finding the gene, by using different techniques, in this week's issue of Science magazine.

Both Messrs. White and Collins said they believed the NF gene signals cells to produce a protein that controls some aspect of cell growth. The disease causes numerous problems, they said, because the gene is active in cells in numerous organs.

The scientists said they believed further work will show that the disorder erupts in tissues only when both copies of the gene -- each cell contains a gene passed on from each parent -- becomes damaged. Thus, people who inherit an already-defective gene from one parent are particularly susceptible to the disease. If the second copy of the gene inherited from the other parent is somehow damaged, cells normally under control can grow into a tumor or behave in some aberrant fashion.

"For years doctors have been puzzled by the fact that people with NF have a 50% higher rate of cancer, such as cancer of the colon, prostate and breast," said Mr. Collins. "Now we think we know why." Mr. Collins said he will now search for the protein produced by the gene and look for ways to repair or reintroduce healthy versions of the protein as therapy for those with the ailment. The protein also may be helpful in treating cancer, he said.

Neurofibromatosis, in rare instances, can cause terribly disfiguring tumors on the face and upper body, a condition that has led people to erroneously call the disorder the Elephant Man's disease.
