Dividend News: Curtiss-Wright Slates Payout Of $30 a Share

  • Author: Jeffrey A. Tannenbaum
  • Date: 07/13/90

Curtiss-Wright Corp., signaling "a clear break from the past" less than four months after the death of T. Roland Berner, declared a special dividend of $30 a share and pledged to focus on manufacturing operations rather than an investment portfolio. The thinly traded shares moved up sharply on the news and closed yesterday in New York Stock Exchange composite trading at $69, up $5.75. Volume was only 22,700 shares; about five million are outstanding, giving the special payout an aggregate value of about $150.5 million.

The March death of Mr. Berner, the company's longtime chairman, sparked speculation that part or all of Curtiss-Wright would be sold. And in late March the company said it had received "a memorandum" from an agent claiming to represent a possible bidder.

But Chairman Shirley D. Brinsfield, who succeeded Mr. Berner, has repeatedly said the company has no interest in being acquired or in selling operations. Some companies, such as Union Carbide Corp., have paid huge special dividends to make themselves less attractive to raiders.

However, a Curtiss-Wright spokesman said the company's special dividend isn't connected with any concern about a possible takeover. No bidder ever materialized after the March memorandum, which the company now thinks was frivolous, he said.

The company, based in Lyndhurst, N.J., said the dividend -- no part of which represents a return of capital -- is payable Aug. 15 to shares of record Aug. 1.

The biggest beneficiary of the pay-out will be Unitrin Inc., a Los Angeles insurance company formerly owned by Teledyne Inc. Unitrin owns about 2.2 million Curtiss-Wright shares, a 44% stake. Its share of the pay-out will be $63.2 million. Unitrin shares rose 50 cents in heavy over-the-counter trading to close at $34.

Curtiss-Wright, which makes precision parts used in aerospace, flow-control and other industrial applications, reported 1989 net income of $30.4 million, or $6.09 a share, on sales of $187.1 million. But of the $37.3 million that the company earned before taxes and extraordinary credits, some $10.9 million came from interest, dividends and investment transactions.

The company warned that "earnings from interest and dividends will no longer be realized on the substantial funds used to pay the special dividend."

Even so, one of the few securities analysts who follow the company said Curtiss-Wright will be more attractive to investors after the dividend. Edward B. Keaney, director of research at Pauli & Co. in St. Louis, said Curtiss-Wright under Mr. Berner long confused investors with a whirl of investment activity that complicated its earnings picture. Mr. Berner, he said, compounded the problem by refusing to talk to analysts.
