SEC Wants Brokers To Give Warning On Capital Shifts

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Securities and Exchange Commission, in its first regulatory reaction to the collapse of Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., proposed rules that would force brokerage firms to give regulators early warning of large capital movements. The agency also voted to propose rules sharply limiting the flexibility of money-market funds. The measure would restrict the amount of lower-quality holdings by the funds, which are held by 21 million investors for a total of $450 billion, according to SEC figures.

The brokerage proposal would revise the agency's so-called net capital rule, which requires brokerage firms to keep certain amounts of their assets liquid in case of emergency. But the SEC is concerned that it has no requirement for brokerage firms to notify regulators before moving large amounts of capital. Chairman Richard Breeden called the plan a "smoke detector" for major problems at brokerage firms.

That situation came into play earlier this year in the collapse of Drexel. The parent company, desperately trying to fend off a liquidity crunch caused by its junk-bond portfolio, took several infusions of capital from its brokerage subsidiary. As Drexel's crisis came to a head, the firm tried to shift more capital to the parent, a move that would have put the brokerage unit below the net capital requirement.

The SEC blocked the move, citing a potential risk to the brokerage system, and Drexel was forced to file for bankruptcy-law protection.

Market regulation director Richard Ketchum said the agency was concerned that a similar situation "may arise in other broker-dealers, particularly those in a large holding-company structure."

Yesterday's rule proposal would require brokerage firms to notify the SEC two days before withdrawing certain levels of net capital. The SEC would then have the authority to bar the firm from making such a move. The rule also would create an additional standard designed to limit capital withdrawals for firms with large securities-trading positions.

The New York Stock Exchange already has proposed a warning rule, but that proposal would simply require the exchange to be notified within 24 hours after a shift of more than 20% was made. "We don't think that's sufficient for the commission," Mr. Ketchum said. "Some horses may have left the barn."

The money-fund rule, which was expected, comes on the heels of several recent defaults by issuers of commercial paper, or short-term corporate borrowings. Money funds hold huge amounts of commercial paper.

The proposal would require the funds' prospectus to clearly state that the holdings aren't insured or guaranteed by the federal government. Moreover, it would prohibit funds from investing more than 5% of assets in securities of a single issuer or in securities rated at less than the highest quality. It would impose other restrictions on holdings as well.

The rule would give "better assurances that safety expectations of investors can be met," said Gene Gohlke, acting director of investment management.

Commissioner Mary Schapiro expressed concern about the quality of the rating agencies and wondered whether the SEC's use of the term "high quality" could mislead investors. Commissioner Philip Lochner said the rule could affect the commercial-paper market by removing a major buyer. But agency staffers said they thought it would have only a "minimal effect" on the commercial-paper market.
