Computer Firms Expected to Post Mixed Results --- Makers of Desktop Machines Prospering, Analysts Say, But Others in Slowdown

  • Author: Stephen Kreider Yoder and G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 07/13/90

The gap between the haves and the have-nots continues to widen in the computer industry. Analysts say results for the June quarter will provide fresh evidence that companies making desktop computers are prospering while makers of traditional machines, such as mainframes and minicomputers, are posting smaller gains or even losing business.

Consider the contrast between Digital Equipment Corp. and Sun Microsystems Inc. Digital, once a high-flier and a Wall Street darling, is expected to post a 50% earnings drop for its fiscal fourth quarter ended June 30. Analysts, meantime, expect Sun to show a profit of as much as $47 million for its fiscal fourth quarter, compared to a year-earlier loss.

The key difference: Sun sells desktop workstations and networking computers; Digital still depends heavily on expensive minicomputers. "DEC isn't competitive on the desktop," where the growth and profits are, says analyst Robert Herwick at Hambrecht & Quist. "DEC is just not there, whereas Sun is very much there."

The story holds true among other makers of desktop machines, both workstations and personal computers. Analyst Michelle Preston of Cowen & Co. estimates that Apple Computer Inc.'s net income in its fiscal third quarter ended June 30 rose more than 15% to $114 million, or 90 cents a share, and that revenue increased 10% to $1.4 billion. Compaq Computer Corp.'s profit for the June quarter, she predicts, grew 21% to $102 million, or $2.30 a share, on a 28% rise in revenue to $926 million.

Sun's expected healthy gains apparently confirm its rebound from last year's painful manufacturing and product-transition snafus. Peter Rogers, an analyst at Robertson Stephens & Co., estimates that Sun will earn $47 million, or 50 cents a share, on revenue of $700 million. In the year-ago period, Sun had a loss of $20.3 million on sales of $431 million.

Sun's rebound was fueled by improved operations and new computers that are eating away at the competition in the burgeoning market for workstations, machines used by engineers and scientists. "Sun finds itself in a very strong leadership position in the workstation market despite everybody else all taking their best shots," says Mr. Herwick, the Hambrecht & Quist analyst.

Growth comes harder for computer companies that depend on sales of more expensive machines. International Business Machines Corp., for instance, is the largest maker of personal computers but because it relies heavily on sales of larger computers it is expected to show slowing growth. Analyst Daniel Benton of Goldman, Sachs & Co. estimates that Big Blue's second-quarter revenue grew only 6% to $16.2 billion. Net income is expected to be down 7% to $1.3 billion, or $2.20 a share, he said.

The earnings picture is even more dismal for Unisys Corp., which depends more heavily on sales of expensive computers than IBM. Mr. Benton estimates that Unisys will break even for the quarter on slightly lower revenue, which he expects to total $2.5 billion. "The picture is scary," said Mr. Benton. "Unisys isn't showing any signs of rebound."

Unisys earned $27 million in the year-ago period and Mr. Benton believes it's possible the firm lost money in this year's June quarter, its second of the fiscal year. "We may see another restructuring of (the company) before year end," he said.

In addition to its poor showing in the desktop market, Digital was hurt by production glitches in its VAX-9000 minicomputers, says analyst Barry Willman of Sanford C. Bernstein & Co. He estimates Digital earned about $155 million, or $1.25 a share, in the June quarter, down from $313.2 million, or $2.51 a share, last year. Mr. Willman estimated revenue at about $3.4 billion, down from $3.49 billion.

Not all makers of more expensive machines will be hurting. Companies such as Sequent Computer Systems Inc., Tandem Computers Inc. and Pyramid Technology Corp. are expected to benefit from a move by banks, financial institutions and other computer users toward on-line processing systems. Those systems perform such tasks as credit-card verifications and run automated teller machines.

Tandem's net income should rise about 55% in the fiscal third quarter ended June 30 to $48 million, or 43 cents a share, from $30.8 million a year ago, or 31 cents a share, estimates analyst John Jones of Montgomery Securities. Revenue will grow 24% to $520 million in the quarter, he says.

Hambrecht & Quist expects Sequent's net income in the period to rise 56% to $5.4 million, or 23 cents a share, on revenue of about $54 million, up 73% from the year-ago period.
