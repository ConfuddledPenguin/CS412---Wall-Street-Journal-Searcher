Olin Corp. Is Ordered To Sell FMC Assets In Antitrust Case

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Trade Commission ordered Olin Corp. to divest a business that makes chemicals for cleaning swimming pools. The FTC's ruling, which upheld a December 1987 decision by an administrative law judge, was based on a finding that Olin's 1985 acquisition of the business from Chicago-based FMC Corp. violated antitrust laws by creating the prospect of reduced competition in the already concentrated pool-cleaning chemicals industry. Olin, based in Stamford, Conn., had bought the business for $49.5 million.

The agency, in a unanimous opinion written by Commissioner Mary Azcuenaga, concluded that "high barriers to entry" into the industry exacerbated the threat that the transaction would decrease competition and possibly lead to higher prices.

John W. Johnstone Jr., chairman and chief executive officer of Olin, said in a statement that the company will appeal the FTC's order in federal appeals court. He asserted that "over the past five years, the market has continued to be characterized by intense domestic and foreign competition."

The FTC ordered Olin to divest the FMC assets within a year and to sell the businesses to a buyer approved by the commission. In addition, the FTC ruled that for ten years, Olin must obtain government approval before acquiring any interest in a company that makes swimming pool chemicals.

In the earlier ruling, the administrative law judge had found that unless the FMC assets were sold to a third party, Olin and FMC would control 76% to 86% of important segments of the nationwide market for pool-cleaning chemicals. Olin had appealed the judge's ruling to the full FTC.

Under a previous agreement with the commission, Olin was permitted to complete the acquisition, but was required to manage the former FMC businesses separately until the antitrust issues could be resolved.

The business acquired from FMC makes up part of Olin's line of pool chemicals called chlorinated isocyanurates. It includes manufacturing plants in South Charleston, W. Va., and in Widnes, England, and a packaging facility in Livonia, Mich.
