Heard on the Street: Unocal UXP Partnership Units Fall Sharply Ahead of Planned Conversion to Corporation

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 07/13/90

NEW YORK -- Are the public investors in Union Exploration Partners being shortchanged by Unocal? The Los Angeles-based oil and gas giant created the partnership and sold a small stake to the public in 1985 as part of a plan to ward off a raid by T. Boone Pickens Jr.

Unocal put some of its choicest gas-producing properties in the partnership, known as UXP. And though Unocal held a controlling 95% stake, UXP appealed to small investors who wanted a steady stream of tax-deferred cash payments.

UXP did make big distributions, but prices of the publicly traded units, initially $22 each, headed south as oil and gas prices slumped. The cruncher came in June, when Unocal disclosed that on Aug. 1, it will convert UXP into a corporation.

In the month since then, the market value of UXP has plunged to less than $12 a unit from $18.25, though the units have regained ground this week to $13.

"The new company will have exactly the same assets, and the price has fallen to 13 from 18. Can capital markets be that inefficient?," wonders PaineWebber analyst Bryan Jacoboski.

Capital markets, in fact, recognize that the income from UXP will plunge too. Previously, it was expected to pay about $1.65 a unit this year. But the new company expects to pay a yearly corporate dividend, fully taxable, of only 40 cents a share.

Unocal has contingently promised to compensate holders for any drop of as much as $3 in the unit price from $18.20. At current prices, holders are still out more than $2 a unit, or $27 million for the 12 million units in public hands.

Steven Spence, a stockbroker for PaineWebber in Portland, Ore., has written the Securities and Exchange Commission, claiming that Unocal violated its fiduciary duty by taking an action that caused the market value of the units to decline.

Clients of the PaineWebber branch own 1.5 million units. They are also unhappy because Unocal's original prospectus didn't mention the possibility of such a conversion. In Unocal's behalf, the prospectus didn't preclude a conversion.

Salomon Brothers, Unocal's paid adviser, says the conversion is fair to unitholders. Unocal says it can't comment until a filing is approved by the SEC.

Analysts say Unocal's privately expressed position is that investors should "calm down and be patient." Although unit prices have dropped as income-oriented investors have deserted UXP, Unocal is planning a road show to pitch the new vehicle to large investors. The company's position, one investor says, is that "it's only a function of time before institutions understand the new vehicle."

Analysts can see why a unitholder would be griping, but they are hardly shedding tears. "Anyone who bought the units knew that 95% were owned by Unocal," notes Kidder Peabody's Marc Cohen.

With only 5% of the new company's shares to be traded publicly, analysts say they doubt whether institutions will jump in. "The float is small," says Prescott Ball & Turben's Bruce Lazier. "That's why I think this is a first step."

The Street is betting that, some time after UXP is converted, Unocal will sell off more UXP shares to raise cash. With 29% of Unocal's U.S. oil reserves and 59% of its U.S. gas reserves, UXP is a large entity with a tiny public float. Enlarging that float would be an attractive way to raise money for drilling for oil and gas.

The oil giant seems motivated by a desire to invest more in exploration. "Corporations are more efficient vehicles for reinvestment than partnerships," its filing says. Partnerships, however, are better suited to paying money out to holders than taking it in.

When the partnership was formed, raiders such as Mr. Pickens were arguing that exploration was a waste, that "the cheapest oil" was on the New York Stock Exchange. The best oil companies could do, it was said, was to pay out their cash flow. Hence, Unocal and others rushed to form partnerships.

But Salomon analyst Bernard Picchi says exploration opportunities in the '90s loom large. UXP, he says, has "enormous exploration potential" because its Gulf of Mexico gas properties are long-lived and close to a network of pipelines. Drilling in the ground has again become cheaper than drilling on the stock exchange, he quips.

Thus, Mr. Picchi sees Unocal's move as the beginning of the end for other oil partnerships. "By 1995, none of them are likely to exist."
