FAA Cites Deaths In Cars in Opposing Kids' Seats in Planes --- Agency Says Cost of Flying Would Prompt Families To Drive, Raising Risk

  • Author: Laurie McGinley
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Aviation Administration said it opposes requiring use of child-safety seats in planes because the end result would be an increase in highway deaths. The FAA, at a congressional hearing on child-restraints, cited a number of new studies to bolster its theory, which left several congressmen incredulous. "This is a little puzzling as to why the FAA is more concerned with highway safety than aviation safety," said Rep. Jim Lightfoot (R., Iowa), who wrote legislation to require use of the seats on planes for children under two years old.

The FAA analysis goes like this: Requiring the seats for small children would compel parents to buy airline tickets for babies who now may be held on laps and fly free. The higher costs, in turn, would prompt some families to drive to their destinations rather than fly. And since driving is more hazardous, more lives would be lost on highways than would be saved in airplane crashes. Sen. Christopher Bond (R., Mo.) co-authored the Lightfoot bill and testified yesterday at the hearing, which was held by the House Aviation Subcommittee. He said the FAA's reasoning was "totally unacceptable and ridiculous." Carried to its extreme, he said, the FAA never would have required oxygen masks, flame-retardant seats or other safety improvements that raised the cost of flying. Instead, "We'd allow airlines to sell standing-room-only tickets," he said. "Imagine how many people that would take off the highways."

But Anthony Broderick, associate FAA administrator for regulation and certification, told the panel that no other rule the agency has considered "would have such a diversionary impact." Requiring child-restraint seats would result in one baby's life being saved on a plane over 10 years, but nine more people would be killed on the highways than would otherwise be the case, according to a study by Apogee Research Inc. commissioned by the FAA.

Advocates of a child-restraint requirement testified that the study underestimated the discounts airlines likely would offer for babies, and thus overestimated the number of families who would choose driving over flying.
