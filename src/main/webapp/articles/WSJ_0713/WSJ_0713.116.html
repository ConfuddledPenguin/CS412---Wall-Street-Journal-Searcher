Thursday's Markets: Stocks Soar to Record On Hope for Rate Cut

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 07/13/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices soared into record territory as Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan revived investors' hopes for lower interest rates. Extending a practically uninterrupted climb that started Tuesday, the stock market surged yesterday after Mr. Greenspan said the Fed was poised to ease interest rates in order to stave off what may be tightening credit conditions.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average jumped 37.13 points in heavy trading to close at a record 2969.80, easily breaking the previous high of 2935.89 set June 15. Trading volume of 211.5 million shares was the heaviest since May.

Bond prices also rose on Mr. Greenspan's comments, while the dollar fell sharply. The prospect of lower U.S. interest rates, while bullish for bonds, erodes the dollar's appeal compared with investments in higher-yielding foreign currencies.

Investors had almost given up hope that the Fed would ease rates soon because of growing evidence of the economy's slow but steady growth. But Mr. Greenspan, in suggesting the Fed might soon ease, cited an inadvertent credit tightening, rather than any need to stimulate a weak economy. The Fed chairman said he sees evidence that loan rates are rising relative to the cost of money, independent of any Fed action.

Buoyed by the prospect of lower rates, some cyclical stocks that track the economy and haven't participated in the recent stock rally weighed in with healthy gains. The issues included Phelps Dodge, Fluor and Ford. Energy stocks were also strong on the continued rise in oil prices.

The two-day, 79-point burst brings the industrials tantalizingly close to the magical 3000 level. But A.C. Moore, research director at Argus Research, believes the market is more prone to slip than to keep surging right now.

"Interest rates remain the key to the market," Mr. Moore said, "and it's still a time where surprises on earnings will be on the down side. So before I'd expect a very significant increase in stock prices, I'd need to see interest rates go down substantially." He said, "I think there was something of an overreaction by equities" to Mr. Greenspan's remarks yesterday.

Kenneth Spence, director of the technical analysis group at Salomon Brothers, questions whether the Fed will follow through quickly on the remarks.

Mr. Greenspan "has been under enormous pressure the last two days, hearing all the feedback from congressmen on how tight credit is," Mr. Spence said. "Under that pressure, he is trying to be conciliatory. He said the Fed is poised. The operative word is poised. They will put liquidity into the system to offset a credit contraction, if necessary, but it wasn't a commitment."

Also, Mr. Spence notes that none of the other major indexes reached records yesterday, and the number of advancing stocks hasn't overwhelmed the decliners.

Mr. Spence, however, doesn't doubt that the Dow Jones industrials could reach 3000, given the nature of the market. "You have to take it stock by stock," he said. "Each stock in here has its own trend. . . . Stock selection has been more important than market-timing."

In the bond market, "This was `Thank You, Alan Greenspan Day,'" said Roberta D. Olson, fixed-income portfolio manager at Banc One Asset Management Corp., Columbus, Ohio. Prices of long-term Treasury bonds rose 3/4 of a point, or $7.50 for each $1,000 face amount.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose. Advancing issues outnumbered decliners, 987 to 545.

Bond prices rose. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell to 8.49%.

The dollar fell. In late New York trading, the currency dropped to 1.6340 German marks and 147.30 yen, from 1.6560 marks and 148.50 yen Wednesday.
