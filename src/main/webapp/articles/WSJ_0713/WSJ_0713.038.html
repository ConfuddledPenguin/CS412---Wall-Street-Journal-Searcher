Index on Optimism About Sales Rises For Third Quarter

  • Author: Jeffrey A. Tannenbaum
  • Date: 07/13/90

NEW YORK -- Dun & Bradstreet Corp.'s sales-optimism index rose for the first time in five quarters, as 67% of executives in a survey predicted increased third-quarter sales. Only 7% of the 1,536 respondents predicted lower sales, and 26% foresaw no change.

As a result, Dun & Bradstreet's sales-optimism index stood at 60 -- the difference in percentage points between the 67% predicting increases and the 7% predicting decreases. For last year's second quarter, the index stood at 74, but then it fell four times and stood at 57 for this year's second quarter.

"Sales expectations have picked up, the profit outlook is solid, fewer executives expect price increases, and new orders are expected to be stronger," said Joseph W. Duncan, corporate economist and chief statistician for Dun & Bradstreet, a business-information marketer.

Even so, the company's profits-optimism index fell slightly. Of 1,428 respondents to this part of the survey, 60% predicted an increase and 7% predicted a decrease in earnings.

Not all types of businesses were equally bullish about sales. While manufacturers were more optimistic, Mr. Duncan said, retailers were less so, the result of recent weakness in retail sales.

Expectations were mixed geographically, as well. Optimism increased in New England, but remained lower than in any other region. "At the same time, the mountain region, which was the least optimistic in the third quarter last year, posted the sharpest gains in 1990 third-quarter expectations," Dun & Bradstreet said. Indeed, the latest optimism reading was stronger in the mountain states than anywhere else.

Among other survey findings:

PROFITS: Manufacturers of nondurables were much more bullish than in the previous quarter, and durable-goods makers were slightly less optimistic. Fewer retailers were bullish, and wholesalers' expectations were unchanged.

PRICES: Expectations of higher selling prices lessened from the previous quarter. Of 1,462 respondents, 34% predicted higher prices and 4% lower prices, with 62% seeing no change. Inflationary pressures have ebbed, Dun & Bradstreet said.

INVENTORIES: Expectations of higher inventories lessened for retailers, wholesalers and nondurables makers, but not for makers of durable goods. Overall, 32% of respondents predicted higher inventories, 15% predicted lower inventories. The majority, 53%, foresaw no change.

EMPLOYEES: Optimism regarding employment rose, with 23% of firms expecting increases in the third quarter, 6% expecting decreases, and 71% expecting no change.

NEW ORDERS: Manufacturers' optimism about receiving new orders increased sharply. Of 622 respondents, 67% predicted increases, 5% predicted decreases and 28% predicted no change.
