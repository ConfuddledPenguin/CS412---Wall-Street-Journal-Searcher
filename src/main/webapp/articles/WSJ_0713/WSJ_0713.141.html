Law -- Legal Beat: Partial Mistrial Declared in Mulheren Case

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 07/13/90

A federal judge declared a partial mistrial in the case against John A. Mulheren Jr. after a jury appeared to be hopelessly deadlocked on 26 of the 30 counts against him. But U.S. District Judge Miriam Goldman Cedarbaum let stand Mr. Mulheren's conviction on Tuesday of one count of conspiracy and three counts of securities fraud.

Judge Cedarbaum granted a mistrial motion by Thomas Puccio, the New York attorney representing Mr. Mulheren. In that motion, which the government opposed, Mr. Puccio argued that jurors were repeatedly asking the same questions and that one of the jurors had indicated he planned a vacation later this week. "Pressure could be brought to bear because of that vacation schedule," Mr. Puccio said.

The jury could not determine whether Mr. Mulheren was guilty of conducting a series of trades that the government alleged were designed to generate tax losses for convicted former stock speculator Ivan F. Boesky's trading firm and to help Mr. Boesky evade net-capital requirements.

The government could retry Mr. Mulheren on those counts. But E. Scott Gilbert, an assistant U.S. attorney who helped prosecute Mr. Mulheren, said late yesterday: "We have not yet made a determination on the extent of any retrial."

The jury found earlier this week that Mr. Mulheren, 41 years old, conspired with Mr. Boesky to violate securities laws. The jury also found that Mr. Mulheren had committed securities fraud by manipulating the price of Gulf & Western Inc. shares in 1985.

After the jury returned its verdict on the first four counts, Judge Cedarbaum sent jurors back to deliberate on the remaining counts. After two more days of deliberation, the judge dismissed the jurors, declaring the mistrial.

"I was quite surprised it ended like this," Mr. Mulheren said yesterday. Mr. Mulheren said he believed that the Gulf & Western charges were weaker than others against him.

Mr. Puccio said there was "no basis in law and fact" for the convictions. Mr. Puccio said he planned to appeal the convictions based on his theory that the jury had to find only that Mr. Mulheren acted "voluntarily" in buying Gulf & Western stock. Mr. Puccio said that the jury should also have had to decide whether Mr. Mulheren's purchase was intended to help Mr. Boesky, as the government has charged.

"John had no idea what Ivan was doing," Mr. Puccio said, adding: "Gulf & Western was the weakest part of this case."

Mr. Mulheren's sentencing is scheduled for Oct. 5.

BILL TO CUT COSTS, DELAYS in civil lawsuits cleared by Senate panel.

The bill, approved 12-1 by the Senate Judiciary Committee, would require each federal trial court to adopt a plan to cut costs and delays. It also would create 77 new federal judgeships, which congressional leaders say are needed to clear huge backlogs in courts around the nation.

The Judicial Conference, the policymaking arm of the federal courts, says the provision on reducing delays is an improper intrusion into the judicial branch of government. The conference has advocated adding 96 new seats to the federal bench.

The bill received mixed reaction from federal judges but has bipartisan support and is likely to be approved by the full Senate. Judiciary Committee Chairman Joseph Biden (D., Del.) introduced the legislation jointly with the panel's ranking Republican, Strom Thurmond of South Carolina.

"For middle-class Americans as well as Fortune 500 companies, this legislation represents an important first step toward reducing the costs and delays that plague the civil justice system today," Sen. Biden said.

The House is moving more slowly in considering legislation to change the civil judiciary system.

Some Democratic members of the Senate Judiciary Committee went along with the Biden-Thurmond bill grudgingly, according to people familiar with the committee. These lawmakers have privately expressed surprise and anger that Mr. Biden would lead an effort to expand the federal judiciary during a Republican administration. The president nominates federal judges, who must be confirmed by the Senate.

For this reason, House Democrats are expected to push for creating fewer new judgeships than are proposed by the Senate bill.

SOUTH KOREAN UNION SUES U.S. company in labor dispute.

In a move to enforce a labor contract overseas, the South Korean union filed suit in federal court alleging contract violations by a U.S. company's Korean subsidiary.

The union's contract dispute with Pico Products Inc., a Liverpool, N.Y., maker of telecommunication parts, sparked labor unrest and a rise in anti-Americanism in South Korea last year after Pico abruptly closed its plant outside Seoul. The laborers -- who weren't paid for the preceding month's work and weren't notified of the closing -- seized the facility and accused the company of breaking its contract.

In the suit filed in federal court in Syracuse, N.Y., the Korean labor union alleged that Pico illegally closed the plant rather than comply with the contract. The union, represented by lawyers from the Center for Constitutional Rights in New York, is seeking punitive damages, back pay and other compensation, totaling $1 million, allegedly owed to the workers.

The suit claims that the contract obligated the company to make compensation payments and to notify workers of the closing. And in a move that could affect other multinational companies if approved by the courts, the suit asks that the 1988 federal plant-closing notification law be applied to U.S. companies' factories abroad as well as in the U.S.

Bernard Hitchcock, Pico's chairman and chief executive officer, said the contract isn't binding on the parent company. Although the contract was signed by the South Korean subsidiary in November 1988, he said the parent company never approved it.

"Pico Korea {the subsidiary} technically owes them some wages," he said, but the parent company isn't obligated to pay.

Mr. Hitchcock also accused the union of kidnapping the Korean plant's managers and holding them hostage until they signed the contract. "I doubt that a contract that's gained by extortion will be found to be binding," said Mr. Hitchcock, who as of yesterday said he hadn't seen the suit.

EDWIN MEESE AWARDED $460,509 for legal bills.

A federal court in Washington awarded the former attorney general the money for legal bills incurred during the time he was investigated by a special prosecutor. The U.S. Court of Appeals disallowed $115,089 in expenses claimed by Mr. Meese's defense lawyers, however.

From May 1987 through June 1988, a special prosecutor investigated allegations that Mr. Meese violated federal ethics laws by helping Wedtech Corp. of New York obtain a defense contract.

The investigation didn't result in criminal charges being filed against Mr. Meese. A close adviser to former President Reagan, Mr. Meese left his post soon after the investigation concluded.

Under the independent counsel law, subjects of investigations who aren't indicted are entitled to be reimbursed by the government for what the court determines are their reasonable legal-defense fees.

TOYOTA IS ACCUSED of blocking a U.S. distributor from access to Capitol Hill.

Rep. C. Thomas McMillen (D., Md.) filed an affidavit this week in federal court complaining that Toyota Motor Sales wouldn't permit its mid-Atlantic distributor to talk with him about a legal dispute.

Mid-Atlantic Toyota, based in Glen Burnie, Md., is an independent U.S. auto dealer licensed to sell Toyotas in a five-state region.

The affidavit is part of a suit that Mid-Atlantic Toyota filed last month in Los Angeles alleging racketeering and fraud by Toyota Motor Sales. In his affidavit, Rep. McMillen says he learned of the dispute through news accounts, but was unable to get further information because Mid-Atlantic Toyota said it was gagged by a 1987 agreement with Toyota Motor Sales. Inability to discuss such matters with constituents "has a negative impact upon my ability to function as a congressman," Rep. McMillen contends.

The suit grew out of a dispute between the two companies that dates back to 1986, when Toyota Motor Sales sought to cancel its agreement with the distributor. In response, Mid-Atlantic Toyota lobbied for Congress to require Japanese auto manufacturers to distribute their cars exclusively through independent U.S. dealers.

The two companies resolved their differences, but as part of the settlement Mid-Atlantic Toyota was forbidden from making further contact with Congress on the matter. Now the U.S. company is seeking to be released from this agreement on the ground that it violates its First Amendment rights.

"Their signature is on that agreement," said James Olson, a spokesman for Toyota Motor Sales, adding: `We aren't trying to inhibit anybody."
