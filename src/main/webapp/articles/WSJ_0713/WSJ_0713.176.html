Credit Markets: Treasurys Rise on Fed Chairman's Comments That Central Bank Is Now Poised to Ease Credit

  • Author: Lawrence Bauman and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 07/13/90

NEW YORK -- Just when investors were ready to accept what they expected to be a steady monetary policy, Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan surprised the bond market yesterday by saying the Fed stood poised to ease credit conditions. Not only is a statement by a Fed chief about the future course of monetary policy close to unheard of, but Mr. Greenspan's reasoning for the anticipated interest-rate move represents a complete turnaround from previous statements.

Mr. Greenspan, testifying before the Senate Banking Committee, said the Fed may be preparing to push interest rates lower in response to "cumulating evidence" it sees of a credit crunch that could hurt the economy.

Prior to Mr. Greenspan's comments, bond prices had been falling. But "the bond market turned on a dime after the Greenspan remarks," said Robert T. McGee, a senior vice president at Tokai Bank Ltd.

The benchmark 30-year bond jumped 3/4 point, a gain of about $7.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, fell to 8.49%.

Although Mr. Greenspan's comments clearly helped the bond market, some traders said the response by investors was somewhat restrained due to skepticism that such an easing will occur anytime soon.

Some analysts aren't convinced that the Fed chief meant to say that the central bank is preparing to push the federal funds rate below its current 8.25% level. The federal funds rate is the rate banks charge each other on overnight loans.

Rather, they contend, the Fed wants only to counter any upward pressure on rates that may develop as a result of bank-induced credit conditions.

They note that following the July Federal Open Market Committee meeting and the release of the June employment report, the Fed, through its pattern of open market operations, went out of its way to signal to the markets that it had not changed policy.

That was a big disappointment to many bond-market participants who had been speculating for months that a Fed easing was imminent. After the employment report, however, they threw in the towel and began to believe that no easing was in sight.

The Fed "is prepared to ease, but not yet," said Michael Moran, chief economist at Daiwa Securities America Inc.

Bond prices had been down as much as 1/2 point during morning trading, in part because of continued disappointment that there wasn't much investor interest in Wednesday's seven-year note auction despite the large amount of overall bids.

Also weighing on bond prices during early trading, dealers and analysts said, was talk that a portfolio of up to $1 billion of bonds, composed mainly of corporate and mortgage-backed securities, was sold by Metropolitan Life Insurance Co. to make room for some Resolution Funding Corp. bonds that were purchased at Tuesday's auction. Refcorp is a federal agency responsible for raising money to finance the bailout of the savings and loan industry.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 102 21/32 to yield 8.49% compared with Wednesday's close of 101 29/32 to yield 8.56%. The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 102 13/32 to yield 8.49% compared with 101 29/32 to yield 8.56%.

Short-term rates fell sharply. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.68% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.92%, down from 7.79% to yield 8.04% on Wednesday. The rate on six-month bills ended at 7.57% to yield 7.96%, down from 7.73% to yield 8.13% on Wednesday.

In the agency market, spreads between yields on Treasury bonds and the Resolution Funding Corp. 30-year bonds that were auctioned Tuesday narrowed amid talk that a few big dealers with large positions were squeezing shorts. Shorts are created when dealers and professional traders sell securities they don't yet own.

Apparently, a small number of big dealers ended up with a large supply of Refcorp bonds after Tuesday's auction. That forced dealers who were short of supply to scramble to buy the bonds from dealers who had ample supply.

The Refcorp bonds were auctioned at a spread of 0.39 percentage point above the Treasury's 8.75% 30-year bond and trading late yesterday at a spread of 0.33 percentage point. The bonds were quoted late yesterday at a price of 100 18/32 to yield 8.82%.

Corporate Bonds

Bullish bond-market sentiment sparked a heavy influx of new debt into the credit market yesterday with nearly $1.3 billion of securities priced.

Merrill Lynch Capital Markets managed a majority of yesterday's offerings, including $300 million of Province of Nova Scotia 12-year debentures priced to yield 9.465%, $200 million of 15-year senior notes priced to yield 8.734% from General Motors Corp.'s General Motors Acceptance Corp. unit, and $75 million DeKalb Energy Co. 10-year notes priced to yield 9.90%.

Ford Holding Co., a subsidiary of Ford Motor Co., also launched a major debt offering, selling $552 million of seven-year notes, which were priced to yield 9.275%.

In the junk-bond market, investor confidence in Donald Trump's prize Atlantic City casino, Trump Taj Mahal, continued to erode after the release of disappointing earnings for June.

The casino confirmed that its average daily revenues for the month of June were $1.16 million, much lower than the $1.3 million a day analysts said the casino needs to break even.

Trump Taj Mahal Funding Inc.'s 14% first mortgage bonds, due in 1998, ended 1/2 point lower at 51. Other bonds issued by Trump casinos were unchanged.

In the broader market, junk bonds continued to firm in moderate trading. Traders said prices rose about 1/4 point on most issues. Most investment-grade bonds ended 3/8 point firmer.

Municipal Bonds

Tax-exempt issues finished up as much as 1/4 point.

Market participants said that municipals followed U.S. Treasury issues higher on Fed Chairman Greenspan's comments.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% of 2018 were 3/8 point higher at 99 3/8 to yield 7.24%.

Foreign Bonds

Japanese government bonds ended weaker as short-term interest rates continued to rise without protest from the Bank of Japan and the dollar rebounded against the yen.

Rates on Japanese bank deposits are now higher than bond yields, which has resulted in investors selling bonds and reinvesting the proceeds in money market funds.

The Bank of Japan didn't try to cool down the interest rate rise as it did on Tuesday and Wednesday. Meanwhile, investors continue to speculate that the central bank will boost the discount rate by the fall. The rate, which the Bank of Japan charges on loans to banks, is 5.25%.

In trading, Japan's benchmark 10-year bond ended at a price of 86.67, down 0.28 point, to yield 7.265%. Rates on new three-month certificates of deposit rose to 7.67%, up from 7.61% two days earlier.

(Foreign bond yields are quoted here in local terms and therefore differ somewhat from yields calculated for these markets by U.S. investment firms, which tend to use the semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets.)

Mortgage-backed Securities

The mortgage securities posted modest gains, spurred by the comments by Fed Chairman Greenspan.

Among actively traded secondary issues, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities for July delivery were up 11/32 at 99 16/32, July Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities were up 10/32 at 98 3/4 and July Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were up 11/32 at 99 10/32.
