OSHA Proposes New Safety Rules At Chemical Plants

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Labor Department proposed rules requiring chemical-plant managers to adopt rigorous safety programs. The department's Occupational Safety and Health Administration had promised the rules in a report to President Bush in April. That report covered explosions at a Phillips Petroleum Co. plastics complex near Houston in October. The explosion, one of the worst chemical-facility disasters since World War II, killed 23 workers and injured more than 130.

OSHA completed work on the proposal following an explosion last week at an Arco Chemical Co. plant in Channelview, Texas, in which 17 workers died. Arco Chemical is a unit of Atlantic Richfield Co.

An OSHA spokesman said the timing of the government's action wasn't influenced by the Arco explosion, though he said the Phillips and Arco incidents tended to "crank up the anxiety level" at the agency.

Gerard Scannell, an assistant labor secretary who heads OSHA, said the proposed standard culminates 18 months of "intense, expedited work."

Hundreds of workers were killed or injured in accidents at chemical plants and refineries in the past two years. OSHA and industry critics have singled out increased reliance by companies on contract workers as one possible cause. A preliminary report prepared for OSHA recently concluded that the contract workers get less safety training than permanent workers and are more loosely supervised.

Safety-management plans advocated by OSHA would incorporate policies already followed voluntarily by many companies. These involve such steps as evaluating plant valves for safety hazards, keeping equipment in good shape and properly training both contractors and company workers.

The agency estimated that the rules could prevent more than 200 deaths and at least 720 injuries and illnesses a year. The rules would cover about 2.2 million workers and would cost chemical companies $233 million annually. OSHA said the cost figure included a conservative estimate of economic benefits that it said partly would offset direct costs.

OSHA said its proposal would require companies to conduct "process hazard analysis," which it said will mean "a thorough, orderly, systematic approach for identifying, evaluating and controlling processes" that involve highly hazardous chemicals.

Besides covering chemical companies, the proposal would affect workers in oil refining and related work, farm-goods warehousing, wholesaling, liquid natural-gas production and electric and water services, OSHA said.

OSHA said it will take comments on the proposal through Oct. 15, and a hearing is set for Nov. 27.

Sylvia Krekel, an occupational health and safety official for the Oil, Chemical and Atomic Workers union, criticized the OSHA plan. "It's not going to stop the carnage," she said, because it is "essentially written by the industry" and puts no "great burden" on chemical plant managers to improve safety. Ms. Krekel said it doesn't provide for input from workers, and she predicted it would be "weak" on ensuring that contract workers are well trained and adequately supervised.

Akio Konoshima, an OSHA spoksman, said the agency had cooperation from unions and the industry in developing the proposal, and he said the comment process and hearing will be a forum for any such criticisms to be expressed.
