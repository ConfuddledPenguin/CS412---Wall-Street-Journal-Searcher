Economy: Retailers Posted Average 5.8% Rise In Sales for June

  • Author: Jeffrey A. Trachtenberg
  • Date: 07/13/90

Sunny June weather across most of the country boosted sales of apparel and other seasonal-related items for many of the nation's largest retailers. Although June wasn't an outstanding month for most retailers, the Merrill Lynch Retail Sales Index of 17 leading chains showed that revenue increased an average 5.8% against a 5.2% gain in May. Some analysts had feared that May's poor results reflected consumer panic, not simply the widespread cold, gloomy weather that month.

Analysts also indicated that much of June's revenue growth was generated by heavy promotional sales. Many retailers started the month with inventory they had expected to sell during May. Top seasonal sellers in June, analysts said, included air conditioners and pool-related products.

"We saw some improvement in June, which should enable retailers to enter the important second half lean and clean," said Jeffrey Feiner, an analyst at Merrill Lynch & Co. He added: "There are, however, problems ahead. The housing market is weak, and there are slowing trends in personal spending. There's also a lack of direction from the fashion market, which may indicate softening for apparel."

Retailers that performed particularly well included Wal-Mart Stores Inc., K mart Corp., and the Target discount store division of Dayton Hudson Corp. Among the chains with disappointing results was Limited Inc., whose namesake Limited division had an estimated 5% decrease in sales for stores open at least one year.

In active trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Limited shares closed yesterday at $23, down $2.125.

"Overall economic growth and consumer spending as a component of that growth is probably below average, but the June numbers indicate the economy isn't sinking rapidly into recession," said Barry Bryant, an analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc.

Mr. Bryant noted that consumers have been particularly responsive to licensed products this summer. Sales of goods endorsed by Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles, Dick Tracy, and "underachiever" Bart Simpson have been strong. "Who would have believed that the Simpsons would have emerged as giant counterculture figures in only 90 days?" marveled Mr. Bryant.

At Wal-Mart, same-store sales were up 14% in June and 12% for the five months ended June 30 against the like year-earlier periods. The discounter did particularly well with men's and children's apparel, as well as food items and toy products.

"They were helped because their reporting period included five Saturdays instead of four Saturdays a year ago," said Robert Buchanan, an analyst with Alex. Brown & Sons Inc. "Still, they are taking market share from lesser competitors. We're seeing a nationwide trend in which the strong get stronger in retailing."

At Sears, Roebuck & Co., the nation's largest retailer, comparable store sales were up 3.5%. "They did well with their jazzed-up electronics and appliance departments," said Mr. Buchanan. "They've begun to give a lot more space in the stores to those categories, so you'd expect some pop in sales. They didn't do as well in apparel, which reflects continued weakness in that area."

At K mart, comparable store sales grew 4.3% in June. "Their apparel sales were strong," said Mr. Buchanan. "The big picture here is that the company's expense structure in relationship to sales are higher than that of Wal-Mart and Target. Therefore they can't price as aggressively across the board."

Mr. Buchanan expressed surprise that the Limited division of Limited Inc. showed an estimated 5% decrease in same-store sales. "They are dropping prices on their Forenza private-label line of casual sportswear, and that's hurting them," said Mr. Buchanan. "The line is being replaced with Paul et Duffier. The transition is causing some unexpected pain." Same-store sales rose 5.1% at Dayton Hudson; 5% at the parent Limited; 4.9% at Woolworth Corp; 4.1% at J.C. Penney Co. and 1% at Carter Hawley Hale Stores Inc.

"The overall trend is that we saw a pick-up in promotions in June following on an extremely sluggish May," said Janet Mangano, an analyst with Jesup, Josephthal & Co. "What we saw were pent-up promotions, not pent-up consumer demands. I'm expecting disappointing second-quarter earning performances."
