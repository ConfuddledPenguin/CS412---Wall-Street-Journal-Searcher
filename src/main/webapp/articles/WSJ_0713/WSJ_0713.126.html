Dingell Criticizes Further Purchases Of AH-64 Apaches

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 07/13/90

The Army is on the verge of buying another $1.5 billion of AH-64 Apache attack helicopters from McDonnell Douglas Corp., despite complaints from military personnel that the aircraft isn't reliable, according to a congressional panel. In a letter dated Wednesday to Defense Secretary Dick Cheney, Rep. John Dingell (D., Mich.), chairman of the House subcommittee on oversight and investigations, criticized any plans to buy more of the helicopters: "I cannot, for the life of me, fathom the logic of such a decision."

Mr. Dingell wrote that although the Army isn't equipped to provide adequate maintenance support for the nearly 600 Apaches it has on hand, contracts for more AH-64s could be signed within the next few days. The helicopters cost $14 million each, though Mr. Dingell said the firm has asked for a large increase in price and profit in the contract talks.

A spokesman for McDonnell Douglas's helicopter unit declined to comment. An Army spokeswoman also said she couldn't comment on any correspondence to Mr. Cheney. But she did confirm that the service recently wrapped up talks with McDonnell Douglas for 66 AH-64s. The Pentagon wants, and Congress has approved funding for, 132 additional Apaches, which would mean a fleet of 807 by mid-1993.

Mr. Dingell reiterated a call for the talks with St. Louis-based McDonnell Douglas to be terminated. Despite the Army's pledge to add extra manpower and testing to each AH-64 battalion, he said, it "does not have the money" to do so.

The General Accounting Office, commissioned by Mr. Dingell to research the Apache problems, has recommended that the Army not contract for the final 132. He said in his letter that the GAO's stance "makes even more sense" given that the Warsaw Pact is "in total disarray."

Although Army Secretary Michael Stone has maintained that substantial progess has been made in fixing the AH-64, the subcommittee continues to cite field reports that the Apache fails under fire. During the U.S. invasion of Panama in December, for example, the Apache's electronic components had to be dried in kitchen ovens after it rained, according to evidence presented in an April panel hearing.

After the first day of fighting in Panama, four of the six AH-64s sent there were grounded when three were shot up with rifle fire. Meanwhile, the fuel cell in one Apache fizzled out prior to the attack, and by the time a replacement arrived, the AH-64 for which it was intended had been cannibalized for spare parts.
