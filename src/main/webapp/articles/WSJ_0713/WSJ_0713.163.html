Accor S.A. to Pay Nearly $1.3 Billion To Buy Motel 6

  • Author: George Anders and Alfredo Corchado
  • Date: 07/13/90

Accor S.A., a French hotel, catering and tourism company, announced a definitive agreement to buy Motel 6 L.P. of Dallas for $22.50 a unit, or nearly $1.3 billion. The acquisition will make Accor one of the world's largest lodging companies, operating hotels and motels with more than 160,000 rooms. Accor already operates Sofitel and Novotel hotels, as well as Formula 1 motels in Europe.

The transaction also means a windfall for Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., which owns more than 50% of Motel 6 through various partnerships. KKR acquired Motel 6 in 1985 for $125 million in equity and $756 million in debt. It said it expects to cash out with an annualized profit of 38% on its investment.

Accor said that no later than Wednesday, it will begin a tender offer for Motel 6's more than 55 million fully diluted limited partnership interests. Accor said its deal is fully financed. It also said Motel 6's existing $1 billion of debt will be left outstanding. Including Motel 6's debt, the entire transaction has a value of $2.3 billion.

Word of the Motel 6 transaction apparently had begun to seep into the stock market yesterday afternoon. Motel 6's units rose $2.625 each to $19.25 on brisk volume of 1.5 million units in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. The transaction wasn't announced until about an hour after the market closed.

"I think the French company will find that they made an excellent acquisition," said Burland B. East III, an analyst at Bateman Eichler, Hill Richards & Co. He praised Motel 6 as "the McDonald's of the motel business. They are the cheapest chain in America that provides a highly consistent product. You always know what to expect."

Accor is paying a hefty price for Motel 6 though. For 1989, Motel 6 had net income of just $7 million, or 14 cents a unit, on revenue of $424.7 million.

As a highly leveraged partnership, Motel 6 has big charges for interest and depreciation. So many analysts focus instead on operating profit, or net plus interest expense, taxes and depreciation. Even by that gauge, the total transaction value amounts to a brisk 12 times 1989 operating profit of $189 million. For 1990, KKR forecasts operating profit of $216 million.

In a statement, KKR partners Henry Kravis and George Roberts said the transaction "makes strategic good sense for both Motel 6 and Accor." They said that after five years of their ownership of Motel 6, it is appropriate that "it become part of a global lodging company with even greater resources and growth potential."

Recently, KKR has been scaling back its vast portfolio of companies acquired in leveraged buy-outs during the past decade. In May, KKR announced plans to sell its 50% interest in Union Texas Petroleum Co. And in June, KKR agreed to sell Beatrice Co., a major food company, to ConAgra Inc. for $1.34 billion.

A KKR executive said the three sales announcements are "coincidental" and shouldn't be seen as part of a broader liquidation drive. KKR's typical holding period for a buy-out is about five years, he observed, and several of KKR's portfolio companies were acquired in 1985 or 1986, making them ripe for sale.

Since KKR acquired Motel 6 from City Investing Co. in 1985, the motel chain has expanded unusually briskly for a leveraged company. Motel 6 has increased its motels to 554 from 378 before the buy-out and has expanded its payroll nearly 50%, to more than 11,000 employees. The lodging chain also has spent $700 million on expansion and renovation, installing color televisions and phones in all its rooms.

Bateman Eichler's Mr. East asserted that in the past few years, Motel 6 has become "the best run lodging operation in the country." Since the buy-out, Motel 6 said it has increased its occupancy rate to about 77% from 67%.

Under KKR's ownership, Motel 6 has added 30 to 50 motels a year. But Saul Fox, a KKR associate, predicted that the growth rate will increase to 75 a year under Accor. Mr. Fox added that Accor's willingness to pay a premium price for the company reflected "the growth potential that's still in the business."

Motel 6's president and chief executive officer, Joseph W. McCarthy, said he will remain in charge of Motel 6 after the acquisition. He added that while Motel 6 will have "day-to-day autonomy," it also will swap technical expertise with Accor's European operations.

KKR's talks with Accor began in January, after an earlier effort to find buyers for Motel 6 didn't produce any bidders. Accor had targeted the U.S. for expansion, but it encountered only limited success in opening its Sofitel and Novotel hotels in North America.

The Motel 6 deal "helps achieve our strategic plan to establish Accor as the pre-eminent budget hotel company in the world," said Accor's co-chairmen, Paul Debrule and Gerard Pelisson.

Accor is making its offer through a specially formed subsidiary, IBL Acquisition L.P. The French company said it expects the tender offer to close in August.

In an unusual move, KKR first hired Salomon Brothers Inc. to look for Motel 6 buyers, but then, at Accor's request, negotiated the final transaction directly with Accor without using any investment bank advisers. Accor was advised by Euroforce Enterprises, a French consulting firm.

If KKR's go-it-alone practice recurs, it could be sad news for Wall Street. Over the past five years, KKR's blizzard of purchases and divestitures has been far and away the biggest source of deal fees for the securities industry.
