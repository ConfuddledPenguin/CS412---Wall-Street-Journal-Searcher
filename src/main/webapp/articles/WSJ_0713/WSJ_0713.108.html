Health: FDA Moves To Rewrite Food Labels

  • Author: Rodney Ferguson
  • Date: 07/13/90

WASHINGTON -- The Food and Drug Administration unveiled proposed regulations that would change the labeling of food products, including requirements that nutritional information be provided for fresh produce and seafood, and on levels of fat, cholesterol and fiber. Health and Human Services Secretary Louis Sullivan, in announcing the proposed requirements, said "the consumer should be able to read and understand food labels, based on reliable and vital information. These proposed regulations will help make food labels more clear and cogent."

Currently, nutrition labeling is voluntarily included by some food packagers but is required only on fortified foods and those for which nutritional claims are made. FDA officials estimated that the proposed regulations would cost the food industry approximately $300 million in one-time costs. The industry and consumer groups will be allowed 30 days to comment on the proposed requirements.

One change would create new definitions for claims concerning cholesterol levels. Products could be called "cholesterol free" if they contain less than two milligrams a serving, while also containing less than five grams of total fat and less than two grams of saturated fat a serving.

Products could be designated "low cholesterol" if they contain less than 20 milligrams of cholesterol a serving and the same total and saturated fat levels as those in cholesterol-free products. Manufacturers could call their products "reduced cholesterol" if they contain 75% less cholesterol than similar products of their own label or comparable products of other companies.

Among the other changes proposed by Secretary Sullivan is adding nutritional information for four food components while dropping it for four others. Those added would be cholesterol, saturated fat and calories from fat and fiber. Those dropped would be thiamine, riboflavin, niacin and one of the two protein listings.

Frank Scarbrough, director of the Office of Nutrition at the Food and Drug Administration, said the shuffling represents a desire by the FDA to "concentrate on substances that are the cause of chronic diseases." He added that the problems resulting from deficiencies in the dropped substances have largely disappeared as public health problems in the U.S.

A spokesman for Quaker Oats Co., a Chicago-based maker of cereals and other food products, said the company had been expecting the changes. "We are strong proponents of providing meaningful nutritional information. It sounds like these are changes that are going to be beneficial," he said.

John Cady, president of the National Food Processors Association, said his organization will study the proposal "and we will hopefully suggest improvements that will result in even better nutrition labeling to benefit consumers."

The new regulations would require nutritional labeling on packaged food that is a "meaningful source" of calories, Dr. Sullivan said. Fresh produce, seafood and other non-packaged food companies would be allowed some flexibility in informing consumers of nutritional value, including the use of "point-of-purchase" methods such as posters and booklets.

The FDA also proposed to permit companies to display a second label below the required one. The second label would include a "nutritional profile" of certain food components as a percentage of the daily recommended consumption of the component. If the manufacturer used the profile, it would have to include fat, saturated fatty acid, cholesterol, carbohydrates, fiber and salt.

Exemptions to the proposed labeling requirements would be allowed for restaurants, bakeries, individually packaged "bite size" pieces of food and small businesses with annual food sales of less than $500,000 a year.
