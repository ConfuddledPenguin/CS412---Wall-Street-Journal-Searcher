Destructive Viruses Have Now Spread To Japanese PCs

  • Author: Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 07/13/90

TOKYO -- Personal computer viruses, virtually unheard of a few years ago in Japan, are spreading here, government and industry officials fear. NEC Corp., Japan's largest PC maker, announced this week that an unspecified number of its flagship 9800-series models are infected with a "Christmas" virus that can wipe out certain large application programs when the computer's calendar reaches Dec. 25.

In April, Sharp Corp. said a game program compatible with its X-68000 PC was infected with a virus, and press reports said about half the 3,000 programs sold were tainted. In May, Fujitsu Ltd. discovered a virus in 20 on-site computers that would become active on any Friday the 13th.

"Those three cases may be a warning," said Shinjuro Ogino, director of the software technology center of the government-affiliated Information-Technology Promotion Agency. "I'm afraid there will be an increase of computer viruses for Japanese personal computers."

Japan has lagged far behind other developed nations in personal computer use, but sales are now taking off, and with the growing popularity may come growing computer crime.

An agency survey of nearly 400 Japanese companies reported only one virus complaint in 1986, two in 1987, and seven in 1988. But the survey showed 30 complaints in 1989, and six in the first three months of 1990. Mr. Ogino said most of those cases involved machines imported from the U.S., where viruses are far more common. He said he worried that the recent viruses may have targeted Japanese machines specifically.

Fujitsu and NEC said the cases announced this year were the most serious they had ever found.

In the NEC virus, when the computer's calendar turns to Dec. 25, the words "A Merry Christmas to You]" appear on the screen, and applications larger than 30,720 bytes are destroyed.

Sharp discovered two similar viruses in programs for the computer game Farside Moon for its machines. One strain, designed to turn on once a month beginning this month, is supposed to destroy data on the floppy disk and could also damage the machine's components, according to Japanese press reports.

The Fujitsu virus gradually expands to fill the computer's memory and prevent the PC's use. On a Friday the 13th, which occurs this week, the application program is destroyed.

Spokesmen for Fujitsu and NEC said no damage had been done. A Sharp spokesman couldn't be reached for comment.

"Software delivered by the software vendors or manufacuters is okay," said a Fujitsu spokesman, but "software is becoming more popular to share among colleauges, so there's more possibility that the virus move from one PC to another PC."

But an official in the Ministry of International Trade and Industry who is following the issue said the number of reported cases may be increasing simply because of greater awareness. "I think in past days, people regarded some trouble as bugs and not as a virus."
