Pioneer Hi-Bred Expects Record Net For Its 3rd Quarter

  • Author: Scott Kilman
  • Date: 07/13/90

DES MOINES, Iowa -- Pioneer Hi-Bred International Inc. expects to report record fiscal third-quarter earnings and that its share of the North American corn seed market rose for the first time in three years, Thomas N. Urban, chairman and president, said. However, the company likely will post lower earnings for the fiscal year ending Aug. 31, since it had a record loss of $51.8 million, or $1.66 a share, in the first half. Further, the 1989 fiscal year included a gain on the sale of an equity interest in an animal vaccine business.

Pioneer, the nation's largest agricultural seed concern, believes it controls about 35.5% of corn seed sold in North America, up about 1.3 percentage points from the 1989 planting season, Mr. Urban said. Corn is the biggest U.S. crop.

Pioneer's market share rose despite less-than-expected sales gains for the seed industry as a whole. An unusually wet spring delayed many farmers from planting corn for so long that they switched to other crops or abandoned their fields.

Mr. Urban wouldn't say how much Pioneer's North American corn seed sales increased in the 1990 planting season other than to say sales "are up significantly."

According to analysts and competitors, Pioneer did everything from cut prices to give away free bags of seed to stop the erosion of its market share. But Mr. Urban maintains the rebound was due to a sales force reorganization three years ago.

Mr. Urban did concede that a slightly higher-than-normal amount of seed was given to farmers as free samples because the company introduced a large number of new hybrid seed products.

Pioneer's sales strategy is increasingly based on introducing a large number of new hybrids. This year, the company offered 22 new hybrids to farmers. Another 17 new hybrids are being grown by the company for sale next year.

Mr. Urban wouldn't estimate how much the company earned in the third quarter ended May 31. Wall Street estimates putting profit from continuing operations at between $4.54 and $4.70 a share "isn't a bad range," Mr. Urban said. "It will be the best third quarter we have ever had."

In the fiscal 1989 third quarter, Pioneer had profit from continuing operations of $114.6 million, or $3.59 a share, on sales of $608.1 million. Profit from discontinued operations of $605,000 made final net income $115.2 million, or $3.61 a share.

Despite the unusually wet spring, the corn fields that Pioneer has under contract for seed purposes are generally in good shape. However, the late planting season means that the harvest will probably run about 10 days late, increasing the risk that a yield-damaging freeze might occur in such prime growing areas as northern Iowa and northern Illinois.

The wet weather also might deal a setback to the company's project to develop a market for crops grown without pesticides. Pioneer pays farmers a premium to grow rice, wheat, soybeans and corn without herbicides or insecticides, and then sells those crops to food companies.

Wet weather usually increases weed and insect problems for farmers, which can shave crop yields.
