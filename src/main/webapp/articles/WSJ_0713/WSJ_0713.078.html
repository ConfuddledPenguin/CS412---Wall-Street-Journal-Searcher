Saudis Appear to Have Won Campaign To Freeze OPEC Output, Boosting Prices

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 07/13/90

Saudi Arabia apparently has succeeded in its campaign to lift world oil prices. After winning pledges from other oil exporters to freeze output at current levels, Saudi Arabia, the chief producer in the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, agreed to a temporary reduction in its percentage of OPEC output. That agreement made room for a bigger quota for the United Arab Emirates, a chronic overproducer.

Finally, the kingdom nudged the UAE into accepting the new quota of 1.5 million barrels a day that it had previously rejected.

These developments set the stage for a likely new production agreement when OPEC's oil ministers meet late this month in Geneva, Switzerland. The expected accord would establish a new output ceiling of 22.5 million barrels a day -- an increase of 400,000 barrels aimed at accommodating the UAE's return to the fold.

Oil prices exploded on the news. In the U.S. stock market, oil stocks rallied sharply for the second consecutive session. Some oil prices surged as much as $1 a barrel. In the U.S. futures market, crude oil for August delivery settled 99 cents higher at $18.46 a barrel.

Petroleum prices had dropped by one-third since the beginning of the year because of an oil glut brought on by OPEC overproduction. But prices began firming this week as Saudi Arabia hosted a meeting of five Persian Gulf oil ministers to try to lay the groundwork for a solution to the output problem.

Among the ministers at the Jeddah meeting were those from Kuwait and the UAE, the two nations that have been blamed for much of the overproduction that has kept OPEC output well above 23 million barrels a day.

In a grueling 12-hour session, according to people attending the meeting, Kuwait's new oil minister pledged to hold that country's output to its quota of 1.5 million barrels a day no matter what the UAE might do. Kuwait has been producing about 1.7 million barrels a day, and the UAE, about two million a day.

"That took care of 50% of the problem," said a source close to Saudi Arabia's oil-policy decision makers. "The other 50% was the UAE," he said -- until late yesterday, when King Fahd of Saudi Arabia made a phone call to Sheik Zayyed Bin Sultan Al-Nayyan, the president of the emirates.

The UAE has been insisting on a quota of two million barrels a day, its current level of output. OPEC had been offering it a quota of 1.5 million to put it at parity with Kuwait, but the emirates rejected that, and so OPEC had continued to list the UAE at its old quota of 1.1 million barrels a day.

Shortly after King Fahd's phone call, Mana Said Otaiba, the UAE oil minister, told Hisham Nazer, the Saudi oil minister, that he agreed to a national quota of 1.5 million. According to Mr. Nazer, OPEC's Vienna-based headquarters will be informed of the UAE decision today.

It's hardly likely the UAE will quickly cut output by one-fourth, to 1.5 million barrels from two million. But even if it only trims 200,000 barrels a day, that reduction combined with cuts expected from Kuwait would drop OPEC's daily output to 23 million barrels or less for the first time this year. Oil economists say that should satisfy the oil markets because it's in line with expected demand for OPEC oil.

"What they {the Persian Gulf producers} have done is decide that price should have priority over volume," said John Lichtblau, president of the Petroleum Industry Research Foundation.

In a maneuver not unusual for OPEC, the lower production level would be obtained by raising the production ceiling. That ceiling, now 22.1 million barrels a day, is expected to be boosted to 22.5 million for the third quarter. Then around Sept. 14 -- which will be the 30th anniversary of OPEC's formation -- the oil ministers probably will meet again to establish the ceiling for the fourth quarter.

"The intention now is to lift the price to September," said a senior OPEC delegate from a Middle East producing country. "Hopefully, by September, all the OPEC members will be tending to their commitments, and the price will be higher than $18."

The oil ministers meeting in Saudi Arabia this week said earlier that their intent was to raise the price of the OPEC basket of crudes to "at least" $18 a barrel from the recent average of $14. OPEC prices usually trail U.S. prices.
