U.S. Bankruptcy Court Judge Approves Allegheny International Reorganization

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 07/13/90

PITTSBURGH -- U.S. Bankruptcy Judge Joseph L. Cosetti effectively ended Allegheny International Inc.'s bitter and combative two-year bankruptcy case by confirming the company's reorganization plan. The plan, approved by the judge yesterday, calls for the maker of Sunbeam and Oster products to pay creditors holding about $711 million in claims newly issued common stock valued at about $7 a share. The confirmation represents a final chapter in a tortured reorganization that has seen nine previous company plans beaten down by various dissatisfied creditor and shareholder groups.

But, in a bizarre twist, arch-rival Japonica Partners could end up running Allegheny. The partners, who had a competing reorganization plan, were given 45 days by Judge Cosetti to produce an estimated $130 million to $150 million in financing to buy the stock they don't already own.

The partners, two former Goldman, Sachs & Co. merger specialists, spent recent months buying enough claims to effectively give it 50% of Allegheny in an unsuccessful effort to block the company's plan and have its own confirmed.

The judge, who reprimanded Japonica for having "abused and manipulated" the bankruptcy process, issued an ultimatum, ordering Japonica to buy the remaining stock at $7 a share and warrants at $1.53 a warrant. If Japonica can't come up with the financing within the stipulated 45 days, the partners' 50% stake will be put in a non-voting trust for three years. That, in effect, would prohibit Japonica from trying to take over the company's board and management. In the meantime, the judge said Japonica would still benefit from dividends and proceeds of asset sales.

One question mark, though, is whether Japonica will appeal. Japonica officials said they were "reviewing the opinion" and had no immediate comment. If Japonica does appeal it's not clear whether Allegheny's bankers will be willing to finance the plan with litigation pending.

One way to avoid an appeal, which would prolong an already belabored case, is for Japonica to strike an agreement with the banks and unsecured creditors. Indeed, Japonica has apparently been trying to reach an accord that would pay those groups slightly less than $7 a share for stock and $1.53 for warrants, in exchange for forgoing a costly and lengthy appeal. The partners have apparently received an additional $97 million to buy the shares.

Robert Sable, an attorney representing the unsecured creditors, says he expects an appeal. "I have to say it's likely," he said. Noting that the case is littered with dashed agreements, he said, "you can't guarantee that Japonica can strike a deal."

In the past two years, Allegheny has seen nine reorganization plans fizzle and a half dozen prospective buyers come and go, including one investment bank three times. Creditor groups have sued each other, making the reorganization even more contentious.

The process became even more embattled when Japonica offered its own cash reorganization plan earlier this year. After buying up a majority of the company's claims, the group insisted last month that one partner be named chairman of Allegheny's board and that Allegheny's management team resign. Meanwhile, Allegheny kicked Japonica out of its offices where the group had been studying the company's books.

In confirming the company's plan, the judge ruled that Japonica had bought its claims in bad faith, trying to conduct an "end run" around the bankruptcy process.
