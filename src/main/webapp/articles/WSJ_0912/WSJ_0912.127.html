Letters to the Editor: `Delicious Anecdote' Had a Sour Taste

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/12/90

On July 27, Potomac Watch columnist Paul A. Gigot, in what he considered to be a "delicious anecdote," informed us of "panic in the ranks of People for the American Way, the group that paid Gregory Peck to smear Judge Bork." According to Mr. Gigot, the group had done its homework in anticipation of the nomination of Judge Edith Jones of Texas, but were unprepared for Judge David Souter. That is not much of a delicious anecdote. It is pretty thin stuff, with a careless statement thrown in: It is not true that I was paid to speak the narration on the anti-Bork TV spot.

A little research by Mr. Gigot before he sideswiped me would have disclosed that I have been speaking my piece for racial and religious tolerance, for equal justice for minority citizens for more than 40 years, on and off the screen.

Judge Bork opposed affirmative action. He expressed little understanding of the deprived and underprivileged, no concern for the hopelessness of their plight, the bleakness of their lives. The superior intellect did not appear to be leavened with human compassion.

Did I smear Judge Bork? No. The TV spot was hardball advocacy, to be sure. People for the American Way didn't defeat him. Nor did the Senate Judiciary Committee. He defeated himself.

I expect that Judge Souter, on those hikes in the mountains of New Hampshire, will have reminded himself that while most Americans respect and admire intellectual achievement, what they trust in a man is common sense, and a touch of humility.

Gregory Peck

Los Angeles
