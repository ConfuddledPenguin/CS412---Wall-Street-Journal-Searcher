Accounting Group May Soften Proposal That Would Make Bank Profits Fluctuate

  • Author: Robin Goldwyn Blumenthal and Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 09/12/90

Under pressure from the banking and accounting industries, an accounting standards body is close to scaling back a proposal that would have made bank earnings more volatile. The Accounting Standards Executive Committee of the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants is meeting this week in Seattle to decide whether to issue a final draft of a proposal meant to standardize the accounting for debt securities held by financial institutions.

The proposed changes were also prompted by concern about abuses of current guidelines. Critics say that under existing practices, financial institutions can massage their financial profile by selling securities in which they have a profit while continuing to carry at cost a different batch of the same securities in which they have a paper loss.

But there has been concern that the proposed guidelines, rather than giving investors a clearer picture of the value of debt securities held, would create wide fluctuations in bank earnings.

John L. Kreischer, chairman of the executive committee, said that the group is now leaning toward requiring institutions to disclose unrealized losses, but not include them in their income statement, as had been proposed.

Mr. Kreischer also said that the committee is considering eliminating a controversial paragraph that would have required banks to forecast the interest rates at which they would sell securities. The group might also eliminate or change another controversial part of the proposal that would create a new accounting category that would force management to state how long it planned to hold a debt security.

"We're concerned that it's very difficult for anyone to examine intent to hold {a security} for the foreseeable future," he said.

Forcing banks to declare more pointedly their intentions about security sales is controversial because those intentions would determine how the securities are treated on income statements. It involves using either historical cost or market value as a measurement. Currently, debt securities are classified either as long-term investments, which are treated at historical cost, or as trading assets, which are marked to market.

The accounting group finds itself in a squeeze, however, because aside from opposition to the proposed guidelines, it faces pressure from regulators to go beyond current standards and to establish more uniform rules within the financial industry.

Banks have insisted that the treatment of debt securities under the proposal would cause sharp, unnecessary earnings declines if more of the securities in a portfolio were reclassified and valued at the lower of cost or market.

Arthur Stoffel, a partner in the Chicago office of accounting firm Grant Thornton, said the guidelines would create "big gyrations" in banks' profitability, and make it "more difficult for managements to make intelligent buy-sell decisions."

Bankers said the proposal's effect on banks would depend upon each bank's securities position. Clearly, if a portfolio of securities has declined in value since it was purchased, the effect would be to decrease profits in the period in which the securities values declined. Banks whose portfolios had net gains in value wouldn't see much effect since the securities would continue to be valued at their original cost. Such unrealized gains might also be disclosed, Mr. Kreischer said.

Small banks, which often have a larger percentage of their assets in debt securities due to soft loan demand or local economic conditions, might be more adversely affected than large banks.

Bankers also contend that in current form the proposed guidelines would be virtually unworkable -- and the accounting panel seems to have come to agree with them. Not only would it be difficult to audit implementation of the proposal, the banks argue, but it would also limit banks' flexibility in altering their asset and liability mix as economic conditions change. In effect, banks would be forced to make investment decisions based on accounting treatment rather than economic factors, they say.
