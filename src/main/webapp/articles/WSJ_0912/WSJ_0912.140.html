Brooktree Corp. Wins Infringement Ruling Against Chip Maker

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/12/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- A federal court jury in California ruled that Advanced Micro Devices Inc. infringed on patents and copyrights owned by Brooktree Corp., a small chip maker in San Diego. Brooktree sued AMD in late 1988, charging that the Sunnyvale, Calif., chip company illegally used three Brooktree patents in computer chips called "color palettes," which control colors on personal-computer screens. Brooktree also charged that AMD infringed on certain Brooktree "mask work" rights, the rights to the layouts of circuits in a semiconductor chip.

In ruling against AMD on mask rights the federal court in San Diego became the first, Brooktree and AMD officials said, to reach a verdict under the Semiconductor Chip Protection Act, a law enacted in 1984 to protect unprotected chip masks. AMD said it planned to appeal the ruling.

The verdict is a big victory for Brooktree, a private company that expects something over $60 million in revenue this year. The company's sales were growing rapidly until 1988, when AMD started making its offending chips, said Brooktree president James Bixby. The type of chip involved comprises nearly 50% of Brooktree's revenue, said Mr. Bixby. "We slammed into a brick wall when AMD came in" and began competing with low-priced knock-offs, said Mr. Bixby in a phone interview from a party celebrating the victory.

An AMD spokesman said that the company didn't copy Brooktree's chips and that any infringement on patents was inadvertent. AMD invented color palette technology, the spokesman said, and AMD's chips were on the market before the Brooktree patents were granted. AMD has a patent-infringement case pending against Brooktree, he added, and AMD's patents are broader than Brooktree's.

"We believe that the verdict is not supported by the facts of the case," said Thomas Armstrong, AMD's general counsel, in a written statement. AMD said that its color palette chips involved in the case represented less than 1% of its $1.1 billion revenue in 1989.

The jury is expected to decide later this month on damages.
