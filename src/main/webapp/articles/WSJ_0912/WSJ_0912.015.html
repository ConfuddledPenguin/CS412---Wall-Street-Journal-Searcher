Krieger Returns To Trading Role In Currencies

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 09/12/90

Andrew J. Krieger, one of the biggest and most controversial traders in global currency markets, is back in the saddle. The 34-year-old currency options trader, who made about $300 million in pretax foreign exchange earnings for Bankers Trust Co. in 1987, is starting a new investment unit at Capital Holding Corp., an insurance holding company based in Louisville, Ky.

His new job means that, for the first time in two years, Mr. Krieger will manage enough money to be a major player again in the $640 billion-a-day currency trading market, the world's biggest. At Bankers Trust, and later at Soros Management Co., a New York investment partnership, Mr. Krieger was known for making trades so gigantic that awestruck colleagues credited him with singlehandedly moving currency exchange rates.

The co-head of the new subsidiary, Capital Holding Currency Advisors, is Jonathan M. Berg, a former Bankers Trust managing director. Both Messrs. Krieger and Berg have been named managing directors of the unit, which will be based in Englewood Cliffs, N.J.

The unit will trade mostly currency options for large institutional clients, central banks and wealthy individuals, as well as manage a small amount of Capital Holding's own funds. An option gives its holder the right, but not the obligation, to sell or purchase a security or, in this case, currency at a specific price on or before a specific date.

The new Capital Holding unit will offer investors three basic products: an aggressive investment involving a high degree of risk and potentially big rewards; a less-aggressive trading strategy in which losses are limited to 11% to 15% a year; and one in which investors are guaranteed the return of their full initial investments at the end of a prescribed period. Minimum investments will range from $100,000 to $5 million.

Through Messrs. Krieger and Berg, who will be responsible for the unit's marketing and sales, Capital Holding hopes to increase its fee-based income, expand its expertise in foreign markets, and diversify its portfolios, said Joseph M. Tumbler, president of Capital Holding's accumulation and investment group, which also manages Capital's portfolio.

Last year, Capital Holding earned $219.7 million on revenue of $2.5 billion. At year end, the company had about $15 billion in assets.

"When Jon {Berg} first told me we're going to Kentucky, I said: `The horse races aren't for a while,'" Mr. Krieger said. "When he said, `We're going to Capital Holding,' I said, `What's Capital Holding?,' and Jon said: `Trust me.'" The insurance company had been one of Mr. Berg's clients at Bankers Trust.

"Frankly," Mr. Krieger said, "they wanted to take a look at me to see what kind of animal I was." Part of the process, he said, included seating Mr. Krieger in a room "at the head of a table with 13 people and grilling me for three hours on everything from how I make trading decisions to how, when and why I take profits and losses."

Most traders and investors use currency options to hedge an existing foreign-exchange position or to speculate on currency movements with a small part of a large portfolio.

By contrast, Mr. Krieger regards options as a separate investment or asset category, and a conservative way to trade currencies. Because currency option trading volume is heavy, "The amount of money I can manage on a leveraged basis with limited downside risk is very large," said Mr. Krieger, who earned a reputation at Bankers Trust as a mover of markets because of his large trades.

The big risk in investing with options is that they will expire before they can be exercised at a profit. In that case, the investor loses all of the money initially paid to purchase the option.

But Mr. Krieger said that with his approach, "I don't have to be right a whole lot, only about 25% to 30% of the time, to yield substantial results." This is because options allow an investor to bet on moves in large amounts of currencies with relatively small premiums. A $100,000 investment in options would normally control $30 million to $40 million of foreign currency.

He also said that during the course of a year, he generally spends only 10% to 15% of all assets under management to buy options. "So, even if I'm wrong on every play, the downside is quite tolerable," Mr. Krieger said.

Like his view of the options he trades, Mr. Krieger is controversial. Unhappy with the $3 million, or 1%, bonus he received for making the $300 million for Bankers Trust in 1987, he quit the following February. With a 5% bonus, which is common for traders, Mr. Krieger would have netted $15 million. "I was very, very disappointed with the bonus on principal, rather than the actual amount," he said. "It was more money than I ever thought I'd need, but it wasn't fair." He also needed a break from the intense world of trading, he said.

He later joined Soros but said he resigned after making $45 million for the investment firm in 14 weeks. "My hours were worse than they were at Bankers," he said. After a break and travels to India, Mr. Krieger then set up his own firm; its business will be transferred to the new Capital Holding unit.

He described the new unit as "a long-term venture; we have a long-term commitment."

Mr. Krieger's eclectic background includes a master's degree and doctoral studies in Indian philosophy, translations of Sanskrit texts, and a brief six-month career as a professional tennis player. He always intended to make his career in academia, but in the early 1980s, a combination of marriage, a child and cutbacks in educational funding forced him to reassess.

"I was told by my adviser: `You're a great student, you will be a great scholar, but you won't get a job until someone dies in a plane crash,'" Mr. Krieger said. "Rather than wishing someone's death, I decided to change fields."
