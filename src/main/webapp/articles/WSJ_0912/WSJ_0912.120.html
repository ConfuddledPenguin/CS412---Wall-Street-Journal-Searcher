Tuesday's Markets: Stocks Decline, Bonds Move Up In Quiet Trading

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/12/90

NEW YORK -- It was a bad day for rumors. There weren't any, which left precious little to guide investors in the financial markets. The Dow Jones Industrial Average edged 2.97 points lower on skimpy volume to 2612.62. Stocks generally moved in the opposite direction of oil prices, though a late sell-off pushed the October futures contract for crude oil down 54 cents by the close to $30.76 a barrel.

Bonds rose in quiet trading, with many investors awaiting inflation data to be released Friday. The dollar strengthened against most of the major foreign currencies.

Kurt Schultz, senior stock-index analyst at Refco Group in Chicago, said the stock market is nervously vulnerable because of the Mideast situation, with much of the jitters showing up first in stock-index futures trading. "Even institutions have a trading mentality," he said, and investors can either trade against one another or hover on the sidelines. "Everyone is afraid to explore new territory. Do you sell at new {intraday} lows? Do you buy at new highs? Not likely."

Brian Fabbri, chief economist at Midland Montagu Economics, said Monday's 38-point swing in the Dow industrials "was all rumor driven." Vague talk of Iraqi military activity erased "a fairly decent gain and drubbed the bond and stock markets." Yesterday, he said, stocks failed to regain the upward momentum evident Monday before the rumors sparked selling.

"That indicates there's greater wariness about trying to be too certain of developments with that black box of the Mideast still out there," Mr. Fabbri said. Low volume also indicated investors were waiting to hear President Bush's comments last night on the Mideast crisis, he said.

Bond investors said they are waiting for something positive to come out of the budget talks in Washington and for the producer price index, which is scheduled for release Friday. The measure of inflation at the wholesale level during August is expected to jump because of soaring oil prices.

The dollar was buoyed by the growing suspicion that the Federal Reserve won't ease interest rates soon, a move that would erode the return on dollar-denominated investments compared with investments in countries where rates have been rising.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell. Volume was 113.2 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues led advancers, 835 to 609.

Bond prices rose nearly 3/8 of a point, or $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 8.92%.

The dollar rose. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5855 marks and 140.30 yen, compared with 1.5820 marks and 139.55 yen Monday.
