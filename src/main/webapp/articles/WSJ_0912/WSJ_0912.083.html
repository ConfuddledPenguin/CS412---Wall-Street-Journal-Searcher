New York Stock Exchange Sets Its First Limited Off-Hours Trades

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 09/12/90

NEW YORK -- The New York Stock Exchange, taking another step toward 24-hour trading of stocks, approved a limited form of "off-hours" trading for big investors that could start later this year. The Big Board approved two after-hours trading sessions -- known as "crossing sessions I and II" -- that will permit big investors to trade major stocks through a computer for about an hour after the regular trading session ends at 4 p.m. EST.

However, true 24-hour trading -- the final phase of the exchange's off-hours trading plan -- is still several years down the road, exchange officials say. And many market experts question whether round-the-clock trading of stocks is something that many investors want.

The Big Board also approved another service for big institutional investors, starting as early as 1991. This one will let them trade certain privately placed, unregistered stocks and bonds that can now be more easily traded under the Securities and Exchange Commission's Rule 144a. The new system would compete with the National Association of Securities Dealers' Portal system, which has been running for several months.

The American Stock Exchange also is developing a 144a system, called Situs, to compete with Portal and whatever system the Big Board might introduce.

The exchange's efforts in both off-hours and unregistered trading reflect the direction in which the nation's securities markets are headed -- toward increasing services for big institutional investors such as pension and mutual funds.

The Big Board has seen the big investors direct more and more of their trading business to off-exchange markets and to regional and overseas exchanges. It has been scrambling to protect its market share. Richard Grasso, Big Board president, said the off-hours trading plan is, in part, an attempt to "position the exchange competitively in the evolution of a 24-hour marketplace."

The exchange hopes the SEC will be quick to approve its efforts in off-hours trading as a way of cutting down on the growing trading of U.S. stocks in overseas markets, where disclosure requirements can be much looser. Many big investors, including brokerage firms doing computer-aided program trading, have been using overseas markets such as London's because they enjoy the relative anonymity there.

The two "crossing sessions" that the Big Board approved may be up and running before year end, an exchange spokesman said. Neither session involves the physical presence of Big Board floor traders, or specialists. They are assigned to make markets in individual stocks on the exchange floor, as part of a system that has been the heart of the exchange since its founding nearly 200 years ago.

Instead, in the first crossing session, from 4:15 p.m. to 5 p.m., traders will be able to execute orders in Big Board-listed stocks using the 4 p.m. closing price. At 5 p.m., exchange computers will match buy orders with sell orders and execute the trades.

The second session is designed for brokerage firms executing program trades for customers. From 4:15 p.m. to 5:15 p.m., exchange computers will execute multistock trades in stocks in Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, the index commonly used in program trading strategies.

An earlier attempt by the Big Board to capture more trading volume failed. That was the Big Board's Exchange Stock Portfolio product, which allowed big investors to trade all the S&P 500 stocks in a single basket trade during regular trading hours. The product has attracted virtually no trades in recent months.

If the crossing sessions catch on, the Big Board in 1991 hopes to begin "auction pricing" sessions, in which all Big Board-listed stocks could be traded after hours, either by computer or by floor traders. There wouldn't be continuous trading; instead, orders would likely be entered, matched and executed periodically at whatever prices the market dictates. Finally, if customers want it, the Big Board might pursue the final phase of the off-hours experiment: 24-hour trading.

The Big Board said that, as part of its off-hours efforts, it also approved a series of new rules referring to order entry and execution, which the SEC must clear.
