EPA Proposes Tighter Carbon Monoxide Rules

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 09/12/90

WASHINGTON -- The Environmental Protection Agency proposed tighter standards on carbon monoxide emissions from cars and light trucks, with the goal of cutting per-vehicle emissions 29% a year and saving as much as 43,000 barrels of oil a day. The regulations, which would be phased in for new models between 1993 and 1995, are estimated to add $19 to $31 to the average price of a new vehicle. Over the life of the vehicle, however, owners would realize net savings of $1 to $12, because of improved fuel economy resulting from more-complete combustion, the agency said.

The proposal would require auto makers to meet carbon monoxide standards for tailpipe emissions at temperatures as low as 20 degrees Fahrenheit. Currently, controls apply only as low as 68 degrees Fahrenheit. The EPA said carbon monoxide concentrations increase significantly at colder temperatures.

Vehicle tailpipes spew carbon monoxide as a result of incomplete fuel combustion. Emissions are particularly high during the first few minutes after an engine is started, when it needs extra fuel.

Carbon monoxide, when inhaled, weakens the amount of blood pumped to various parts of the body and causes a reduction of oxygen available to muscles and organs. Cigarette smokers and people with heart disease are especially at risk. Carbon monoxide emissions exceed federal limits in 41 urban areas.

Some proposals pending before the House-Senate conference committee that is considering clean-air legislation would go beyond the agency proposal in regulating carbon monoxide releases. The EPA said it is studying whether further regulation is warranted.

The new standards would establish a limit of 10 grams a mile for passenger cars, 12 grams a mile for trucks weighing as much as 3,750 pounds and 15 grams a mile for trucks up to 8,500 pounds. Manufacturers that make fewer than 10,000 trucks a year and trucks weighing more than 6,000 pounds would be exempt from the rule until the 1995 model year.

To meet the new standards, auto makers will have to improve the engine calibration in most vehicles. Some models may need multipoint fuel injection for more-precise fuel control or air injection to provide additional oxygen to the catalytic converter during starts in cold weather, the agency said.

The proposed rules will be published in the Federal Register soon. In addition, the agency will conduct a public hearing on the proposal 45 days after publication of the rules.

Auto makers said they've been working with the EPA on standards for carbon monoxide emissions and expected a proposal requiring tighter standards at colder temperatures. However, they declined to comment further until they've had time to review details of the proposal.
