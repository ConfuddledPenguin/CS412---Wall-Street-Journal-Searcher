FTC Lawyers Say Coin Firm Response Fails to Make Grade --- New Professional Coin Ads Portray Decree, Lawsuit As Positive Developments

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 09/12/90

NEW YORK -- Lawyers at the Federal Trade Commission are unhappy again with the U.S.'s major rare-coin grading service, with whom the agency settled a big false-advertising case last month. The coin grader, Professional Coin Grading Service Inc. of Irvine, Calif., has mounted a public relations campaign in which it portrays the long FTC investigation and subsequent FTC lawsuit against the company as positive developments. The reason the company can argue that: Although Professional Coin signed a consent decree with the FTC, the company didn't admit or deny wrongdoing, or pay any fine.

The campaign included a nearly half-page ad in yesterday's Wall Street Journal.

"After taking an extensive look at PCGS," the Professional Coin ad declares, the government "did NOT ask us to change any of our policies or procedures."

That's technically true, if one reads only the consent decree. But FTC lawyers strongly dispute Professional Coin's claim that the agency, in effect, gave the company a clean bill of health. Signing a consent decree is standard practice in settling civil enforcement actions with the government. But that doesn't change the fact that the FTC had accused the coin firm of making "numerous" false and misleading claims about its objectivity, FTC lawyers say.

Indeed, among other things, the FTC lawsuit said Professional Coin hadn't provided the "objective or consistent grading" the company claims and that it did not "in all cases observe its `strict anti-self interest policy.'" The consent decree bars Professional Coin from making false claims in the future.

Barry J. Cutler, director of the FTC's bureau of consumer protection in Washington, said: "When PCGS goes around telling everyone they should take a look at the consent agreement, I wish they'd take a look at the complaint as well." Mr. Cutler added that the agency doesn't file a complaint unless it has "reason to believe the allegations are true." He said the FTC hasn't asked Professional Coin to change its new ad.

David Hall, Professional Coin chief executive officer, said, "I don't know for a fact that the FTC doesn't like our ads. They haven't talked to me about it. But we've always cooperated with the FTC, and certainly our intention is to do things in the right way."

The FTC's Mr. Cutler said one good thing is that the Professional Coin ads, in line with the consent agreement, do include many more warnings about the volatility of the rare-coin market than typical coin-company ads of the past. The important thing, said Mr. Cutler, is that investors and big Wall Street brokerage firms have been put on notice that rare-coin investing isn't fail-safe, despite the inroads of grading services such as Professional Coin.

"I think we fired a rifle shot, and we got attention where we want to get attention," said Mr. Cutler.

Professional Coin says it has graded nearly 2.3 million coins valued at about $2.5 billion since its founding in 1986.
