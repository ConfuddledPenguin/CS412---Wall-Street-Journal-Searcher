White House Intensifies Drive Against Textile Bill

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/12/90

WASHINGTON -- The administration stepped up its attack on proposed textile legislation that is expected to pass the House next week. The bill, which would apply quotas to additional items and make existing protections permanent, could torpedo current trade negotiations and cost the U.S. as much as $30 billion in trade retaliation, a senior official said here.

In Seattle, meanwhile, U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills told a Council for International Trade meeting, "This bill would cause tremendous economic harm." She said the proposed legislation also could "severely jeopardize our chances of success in the Uruguay Round," the current talks under the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, the world's trade treaty organization.

One major reason the administration is accelerating its campaign against the legislation is that, in the view of many officials, the textile industry may already have enough support in Congress to override a presidential veto if the bill is passed.

The industry says it needs the protection from foreign competition. But the administration estimates that U.S. textile exports grew 27% in 1989, capacity utilization at U.S. mills is high, and unemployment in textile-producing states is generally below the national average.
