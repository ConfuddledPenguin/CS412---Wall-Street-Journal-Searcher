REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Confidence and Europe's Money

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/12/90

A single European currency has much to recommend it, but the European Community obviously still lacks the political unity necessary for its creation. That was evident in Rome this past weekend, as Britain sidetracked the express train known as European Monetary Union. This probably is best seen as a case of the ideal having to yield to the merely possible. The underlying problem, of course, is the widespread doubt over who would actually control the EMU. Germany's Karl Otto Poehl, director of the Bundesbank, helped the U.K., Spain and some smaller nations at the Rome meeting put a spoke in the wheel of the plan promoted by the European Community's president, Jacques Delors. Early this year, Mr. Poehl had said, in effect, that so far as he is concerned a European central bank, and common currency, are fine, providing that such a bank would run monetary policy as well as does the Bundesbank.

The value of money -- any money -- is based on confidence. The Bundesbank, through careful and apolitical management of the deutsche mark, has generated such confidence. Other major currencies have fared less well. Confidence in the dollar isn't everything it should be and that no doubt can be attributed, among other things, to a world-wide perception that Congress and the Treasury have created a major fiasco in the U.S. Savings and Loan industry.

France went into the Rome meeting full of hope for support of a move into stage two of the Delors plan. There were expectations that stage two's transfer of monetary authority to a European entity from national central banks could begin on Jan. 1, 1993. A single currency might be achieved five years later, it was believed. But after this weekend's Rome meeting there now are doubts that such a goal could be reached before the year 2000, if then.

One of the merits of a plan that has been proposed by British Chancellor of the Exchequer John Major is that it recognizes the importance of public confidence. It would create a 13th currency, called the "hard ECU," which would be available for public use and could be spent or used to settle debts in any EC country.

Its creation theoretically would not inflate the total European money supply, since national currencies would be exchanged for it. There would be an attempt to keep it sound and attractive. If that succeeded, the combination of public confidence and convenience gradually would increase the use of the ECU, and decrease the use of national currencies.

Mr. Poehl, even as he supports the slower evolution inherent in the Major plan, apparently isn't keen about the 13th-currency idea. As the proprietor of Europe's leading central bank, he obviously feels the need to be convinced of the idea's soundness before putting his weight behind it.

So the level of confidence in the Major plan is not yet high enough to guarantee implementation. Yet it is not impossible that it could gain support in the months ahead, as EC governments prepare for a December meeting to work out a draft treaty on monetary union. Adoption of something along the lines of the Major plan, particularly if it would bring Britain into the EMU fold, wouldn't necessarily be the worst solution in the world.
