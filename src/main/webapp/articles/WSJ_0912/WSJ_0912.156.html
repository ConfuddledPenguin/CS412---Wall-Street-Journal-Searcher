Deposit Insurance Fund Covering Banks Could Need Treasury Help, GAO Says

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 09/12/90

WASHINGTON -- The banking industry's deposit insurance fund could require Treasury assistance if a recession strikes, according to the General Accounting Office, prompting some lawmakers to urge another increase in bank insurance premiums. Charles Bowsher, comptroller general of the GAO, the investigative arm of Congress, conjured up visions of a crisis similar to the savings-and-loan debacle in testimony before the Senate Banking Committee. He exhorted Congress to avert a need for federal assistance by giving banking regulators the authority to raise insurance premiums in the Bank Insurance Fund higher than is currently planned.

"We could lose this fund, just like we lost the {savings and loan} fund," he warned the committee, adding: "We have a lot of situations out there that could wipe the fund out."

The committee appeared eager to embrace any proposal that would keep them from having to vote for a potential taxpayer bailout down the road. "If action is necessary, let's take it now," said Sen. Donald Riegle (D., Mich.), chairman of the committee. No one opposed Mr. Bowsher's plan to pile another bank insurance premium increase atop one scheduled to take effect next year in order to stabilize the fund.

Bankers generally have agreed that next year's scheduled increase in assessments to 19.5 cents per $100 of deposits from 12 cents per $100, is necessary, but that any increase beyond that would be crippling.

Many analysts don't believe that the banking industry is in as severe financial straits as was the thrift industry when a bailout law was passed last year. The industry as a whole has a stronger capital position, which acts as a cushion for the deposit insurance fund. Nonetheless, Mr. Bowsher and the committee found disturbing similarities in how the industry is increasing its exposure to risk at a time that its taxpayer-backed insurance fund is withering.

Mr. Bowsher cited, among other things, the insurance fund's thinnest reserve ratio in its history, expected fund losses for the third consecutive year, and a decline in the value of the bank assets it holds. The fund balance has dropped to $13.2 billion from $18.3 billion two years ago.

Moreover, the fund is weakest at a time when the industry has exposed itself to greater risks than in the past. Real-estate lending has increased 12% in the Northeast, for example, and the region's nonperforming category of real-estate loans has more than doubled in the past year. Banks continue to be exposed to highly leveraged transactions and loans to Third World countries. While risk has increased, Mr. Bowsher said, there has been no relative increase in equity capital, the cushion to absorb losses, for individual banks.

William Seidman, chairman of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., which oversees both the bank and thrift insurance funds, said that he agreed with the GAO's projection that bank fund losses could reach $2 billion this year. Moreover, if Congress gives the FDIC the authority to raise insurance premiums higher, the FDIC would consider it, he said.

He also hinted that the report may be a political reaction to the S&L mess. "If I were the GAO, I would cover my rear end, too," he said.

Kenneth Guenther, of the Independent Bankers Association of America, said he thought the report was unnecessarily bleak. "This is the darkest this kettle has ever been painted. It doesn't do a lot for consumer confidence in the financial system."

But the GAO testimony joins a growing chorus of concerns about the health of the banking industry from private economists, including Daniel Brumbaugh of Stanford University and Robert Litan of Brookings Institution. The Congressional Budget Office is scheduled to testify on the bank insurance fund today and is expected to warn of an insurance fund stretched too far.

Mr. Bowsher said that assessing the adequacy of the fund is difficult because accounting rules are frequently used to hide, rather than identify, problems. Of the 406 banks that failed in 1988 and 1989, some 31 failed without ever appearing on the problem-bank list or appearing for only one quarter. The National Bank of Washington failed recently, without ever appearing on the list.

Mr. Bowsher advised regulators to do more on-site examinations, rather than rely on potentially misleading audits. "Problems are bigger than are being reported," he said.

Mr. Bowsher predicted that 35 banks -- each with assets of more than $100 million -- would fail this year, including 15 that have already folded. He wouldn't identify them, but said that the combined assets of the 35 banks totals $45.1 billion. The group was culled from a pool studied by the GAO that included the 100 largest banks and 200 of the largest problem banks. None of the 35 are among the 20 largest banks, Mr. Bowsher added.

Government policy is to maintain the fund at $1.25 per $100 of deposits, but currently that figure has dropped to about 70 cents.

Yesterday's report provoked a flurry of proposals on Capitol Hill by legislators reeling from the S&L bailout, which is expected to cost at least $130 billion. Sen. Robert Dole (R., Kan.) proposed raising an immediate $1 billion for the fund by assessing U.S. bank deposits held in foreign countries.

Rep. Frank Annunzio (D., Ill.) said he will introduce legislation today to require banks to pay higher premiums. Some members of the Banking Committee supported Mr. Bowsher's proposal to raise the insurance premiums before Congress recesses next month.
