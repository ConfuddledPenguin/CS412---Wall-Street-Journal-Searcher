Metzenbaum Urges U.S. to Renegotiate Bluebonnet Sale

  • Author: Timothy Noah
  • Date: 09/12/90

WASHINGTON -- Citing figures showing that taxpayer subsidies to Dallas-based Bluebonnet Savings Bank are considerably higher than the original estimate, Sen. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio) urged that the federal government renegotiate its 1988 sale of the thrift. Sen. Metzenbaum said documents obtained from lawyers for Bluebonnet show government payments to the savings and loan association will total $573 million for this year. At the time of the sale, federal regulators estimated that the figure would be roughly $361 million.

In 1988, Arizona insurance executive James Fail purchased 15 insolvent thrifts held by the government and merged them to create Bluebonnet. The deal has attracted considerable attention because it included substantial federal subsidies to the ailing thrifts and because Mr. Fail acquired the thrifts with the help of Robert Thompson, a Washington lobbyist with ties to the Reagan and Bush administrations.

Mr. Thompson appeared yesterday before a Senate Judiciary subcommittee headed by Sen. Metzenbaum. The lobbyist, who served as a 1980 campaign aide to George Bush and subsequently as a special assistant to President Reagan, was sharply criticized for allegedly peddling access to administration officials. He answered that his expertise was in "the process" of government and charged, "One of the reasons I'm here (before the subcommittee) is that I used to work for George Bush."

The hearing provided a vivid glimpse into the world of Washington lobbying. Mr. Thompson testified that in addition to a monthly retainer of $15,000 to $20,000, Mr. Fail agreed to pay him, for assistance with federal regulators, a $160,000 bonus and 2% of Mr. Fail's earnings from Bluebonnet. Citing documents from Mr. Thompson and others, however, Sen. Arlen Specter (R., Pa.) said it appeared that Mr. Thompson had been promised 2% of the entire $96 million transaction, or nearly $2 million.

Thus far, Mr. Thompson has received only the monthly retainer from Mr. Fail, according to his attorney, Stanley Brand.

At the hearing, Sen. Metzenbaum said that the subcommittee originally placed the overall cost to the government of Mr. Fail's purchase of Bluebonnet at $1.8 billion. Last month, that figure was revised to $3 billion. And now, the senator said, "All bets are off with respect to the ultimate federal financing of the Bluebonnet fiasco."

But according to Mary Creedon, associate director of savings and loan insurance operations at the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., the new figure didn't necessarily mean that Bluebonnet's cost to the government was growing. Rather, she said, the second figure included accrued interest on the sum and the first figure didn't.
