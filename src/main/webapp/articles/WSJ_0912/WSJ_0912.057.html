World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Fall on Profit-Taking From Monday, Softer Sentiment Linked to Bond Market Weakness

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/12/90

Tokyo stocks retreated Tuesday on profit-taking from Monday's gains and softer sentiments linked to bond market weakness, traders said. London shares fell after an earlier rally failed to hold and Wall Street pushed London lower.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues dropped 476.24 points, or 1.9%, to 24604.66. The index soared 1118.83 points Monday.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the Nikkei index recaptured 87.92 points to close the morning session at 24692.58.

Tuesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 400 million shares, up from 336.6 million shares Monday. Losers outnumbered winners 609 to 322, while 137 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues in the first section dropped 22.58 points, or 1.19%, to 1880.70.

The second section index fell 8.84 points, or 0.24%, to 3603.40. Volume was estimated at six million shares, up from 4.8 million shares Monday.

A technical downward correction was seen as a natural follow-up to Monday's advance. The Nikkei dropped about 250 points on profit-taking at the morning opening.

Stocks also followed the weaker bond market, traders said.

Bearishness stemming from a higher interest rate environment kept investors from buying into the market, traders said. Some speculative issues were the only targets of buying, which came in small-lot spurts and was mostly by dealers.

The Japanese government bond market was weaker partly on unfounded rumors lingering from Monday that Iraqi troops were headed toward Jordan, but also because of the continued rise in money market rates, traders said.

The overnight unsecured call money rate was up 1/32 on the day at 7 26/32% before settling at 7 22/32%. The yield on December Euroyen interest rate futures closed at 8.75%, up from 8.69% Monday.

In response, the yield on the benchmark, the No. 119, 4.8% coupon due 1999 was at 8.345%, up 8.5 basis points, at the close of trading on the Tokyo Stock Exchange. A basis point is 1/100th of a percentage point.

With short-term and long-term interest rates looking to remain at high levels, funds will continue to be pulled from stocks, traders said.

In addition to interest-rate concerns, the market is seen as remaining unsteady for the remainder of the month. Trading ahead of Thursday, which is the expiration day for September futures and options contracts, will likely reflect manipulation by futures traders in preparation for the special price fixing Friday.

Moreover, institutions have little time left before fiscal book-closings at the end of the month; window-dressing is expected to heighten volatility.

Investors must also deal this month with margin calls for purchases made six months ago.

On a day-to-day basis, therefore, only short-term plays, particularly speculative or incentive-backed shares, are being targeted.

Minebea rose 100 yen to 1080 yen (72 cents to $7.75). Kurabo jumped 70 yen to 1870 yen.

Pharmaceutical shares were largely higher, with Ono Pharmaceutical up 100 yen to 4500 yen and Mochida up 70 yen to 3100 yen.

Higher-quality issues were sold off for profits derived from gains Monday.

Sony fell 140 yen to 7040 yen, Hitachi fell 30 yen to 1280 yen, and Kyocera dropped 230 yen to 7120 yen.

Nippon Light Metal slipped 30 yen to 1260 yen.

Construction shares, which benefited Monday from the market's jump, fell sharply. Nippon Hodo fell 110 yen to 2680 yen, Maeda Construction dropped 50 yen to 1570 yen, and Kajima fell 40 yen to 1360 yen.

London's fall reflected the lower U.S. market. Traders said the American market was knocked down by a Government Accounting Office report that several U.S. banks were likely to fail in the next year.

Volume picked up as a result of overnight positioning and two-way business generated from program trading. However the market was still "hopelessly dull" according to one trader, and stifled by uncertainty over the Middle East and the U.S. economy.

Speculation ahead of U.K. companies' midyear results was generating some business, traders said, as institutions bought stock on expectations of dividend gains.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index was down 2.7 points at 2144.3. The Financial Times 30 Share Index fell 4.1 points to 1651. Volume was 338.8 million shares, up from Monday's 296.4 million shares.

Fisons PLC, a pharmaceutical and horticultural products concern, recorded a 35% gain in pretax profit following first-time contributions from V.G. Instruments, a scientific equipment maker. The shares gained 3 pence to 350 pence (5.6 cents to $6.48).

Paper, filters and plastics concern Bunzl PLC reported a 26% fall in pretax profit resulting from declining sales of manufactured goods in key overseas markets. Bunzl climbed 9 pence to 69 pence despite the midyear results.

Peninsular & Oriental Steam Co. recorded a 22% fall in pretax profit as residential and commercial property development interests depressed earnings. Traders said that the 8% rise in the midyear dividend payment helped the share price rise 9 pence to 504 pence.

Positive expectations for Rolls-Royce's midyear results due Thursday boosted the stock 5 pence to 193 pence.

Enterprise Oil dropped 13 pence to 669 pence on market rumors that Elf Acquitaine might try to place a stake in the company again. British Petroleum climbed 2 pence to 369 pence and Shell was unchanged at 495 pence.

Meanwhile, shares closed higher in Milan, lower in Paris, Madrid, Frankfurt and Stockholm and mixed in Zurich and Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Also, stocks ended lower in Taipei, Manila, Singapore and Wellington, mixed in Hong Kong and Seoul, and little changed in Sydney.
