OTC Focus: Market Is Lower on Technology Slide; Sun Microsystems Declines 7/8 to 28 3/4

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 09/12/90

NEW YORK -- More forecasts of a slowdown in the technology sector fueled declines in high-tech stocks, bringing the over-the-counter market lower. The Nasdaq Composite Index fell 2.24 to 379.49, a 0.6% loss, as trading remained light in the absence of any major Mideast developments. Volume rose slightly to 100.1 million shares from 98.3 million Monday. Declining stocks overtook advancing shares, 989 to 754; 143 stocks fell to new lows while only 14 rose to new highs.

Gloomy economic news continues to keep investors leery of the market, said William A. Rothe, head of OTC trading at Alex. Brown & Sons. As fears of inflation, recession and flare-ups in the Middle East haunt investors, its appears the market will go even lower before it recovers, said Peter Da Puzzo, co-head of world-wide equities at Shearson Lehman Brothers.

Four factors have helped slow the growth in demand for technology products, according to Lawrence Bowman, portfolio manager of the $62.5 million Fidelity Select Technology Fund. Those factors are the traditional summer doldrums in the industry, larger problems in the economy, the maturation of some computer-related product cycles and slowing demand in Europe, Mr. Bowman said in an interview. He added that the slower growth is "incremental" and not extreme.

"You can do well with companies with robust product cycles, but you have to be choosy," Mr. Bowman said. Companies that make low-cost computer clones such as Dell Computer and AST Research look attractive, he said.

Software makers are a "mixed bag" depending on how long their products have been on the market, he added. Microsoft, with the strong demand for its Windows 3.0 software, still looks attractive, he said. He also is optimistic about the outlook for workstation maker Sun Microsystems; minicomputer systems maker Pyramid Technologies; semiconductor equipment maker Novellus Systems; disk-drive maker Quantum and network systems maker Novell.

Yesterday, a gloomy forecast by exchange-listed Tandem Computers and price cuts by exchange-listed Compaq Computer spilled into the OTC market as investors pared their holdings in other technology shares.

Sun Microsystems lost 7/8 to 28 3/4; Apple Computer slid 1 3/4 to 34; Microsoft eased 1/4 to 55 1/2; Conner Peripherals fell 1 to 22 1/2; Applied Materials lost 2 1/4 to 27; and Intel dropped 1/2 to 32.

Lotus Development tumbled 1 1/2 to 16 1/2. Shares of the Cambridge, Mass., software maker dropped after its chief financial officer, Robert Schechter, told money managers at a San Francisco conference that Lotus was "pulling back" from its goal of annual 25% revenue growth and of sustaining 25% return on equity and pretax profit margins. Later in the day, Lotus Chairman and President Jim P. Manzi said the company isn't scaling back its growth targets as sharply as the money managers at the conference perceived. He said the company's revenue growth target is "20% to 25%."

Ingres soared 3 5/8 to 9 and was the day's most active stock after ASK Computer Systems said it plans to pay $9.25 a share in cash for the Alameda, Calif., maker of database management software products.

In turn, Hewlett-Packard and EDS Corp. agreed to buy a $60 million stake in ASK, giving EDS 19.7% of ASK's shares outstanding and Hewlett-Packard a 10% stake in the Mountain View, Calif., computer systems company. ASK closed unchanged at 8. More than three million Ingres shares changed hands, 75 times average daily volume.

Spear Financial Services jumped onto the most active list after the Los Angeles company's largest shareholder, Liberty Mutual Insurance of Boston, sold its nearly 15.9% stake, or 1,086,221 shares, to Spear at 3 1/8. The stock rose 3/8 to 3 1/8 on 1.1 million shares.

In other buy-out news, Sizzler Restaurants eased 1/4 to 16 1/2 after trading as high as 19. Collins Food International, which owns 66% of the operator of steak restaurants, said it will acquire the remaining shares its doesn't own in an exchange offer.

Hogan Systems traded as high as 3 1/2 but closed unchanged at 3 1/8, after the Dallas maker of software for banking systems said International Business Machines Co. bought a 5% stake in the company. The two companies also renewed their marketing agreements.

The continuing Mideast crisis helped cause a casualty in the OTC market. Valmont Industries, which halted sales of its irrigation systems to Iraq, tumbled 1 3/4 to 13 1/4 after the Valley, Neb., company said earnings for the 1990 third quarter and full year will be lower than expected and below 1989 earnings of $1.78 a share.

Valmont, which also makes components for the electrical construction industry, attributed the lower expectations largely to slower business in the U.S., as well as the loss of its sales to Iraq. Valmont's sales to Iraq comprised about 1.5% to 2% of its total sales in 1989, according to Vice President Brian Stanley.

Burritt InterFinancial Bancorp skidded 1 1/4 to 3 1/2 after the New Britain, Conn., financial institution said it expects a "significant decline" in third- and fourth-quarter earnings. Burritt said it expects to report nonperforming loans amounted to between 5% and 6% of assets at the end of the third quarter. Monday, the stock slipped 1/2.

Calgon Carbon fell 2 1/4 to 34 3/4 after Merrill Lynch analyst William Genco lowered earnings estimates for the Pittsburgh marketer of activated carbon, a company spokeswoman told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Mr. Genco's estimates had been the high end of analysts' estimates, DJPIR said.
