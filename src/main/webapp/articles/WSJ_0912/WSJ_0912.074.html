Heard on the Street: AT&T Still Has Hang-Ups, Analysts Say; Credit Card Costs, Recession Fears Are Cited

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 09/12/90

Don't count on American Telephone & Telegraph being a good buy anytime soon. Wall Street analysts are cutting their 1990 and 1991 earnings estimates for the company. Although the stock has subsided to 31 1/8, a third below its 12-month high, most analysts are calling it a "hold" rather than a bargain.

Their reasoning: AT&T's heavy expenses in promoting its credit card won't start paying off until at least 1992. And recession fears are haunting the company's phone-switch and computer businesses, just as AT&T was beginning to win acclaim for getting its computer act together.

"The economy is softening and the outlook for improvement in the near term, particularly with the Mideast crisis, isn't good," says Jack B. Grubman, a PaineWebber analyst. "AT&T isn't immune to this, especially in its equipment business," where he estimates profits have been strained by annual pretax losses of $500 million in computers and office phone systems.

Robert B. Morris III at Goldman, Sachs notes that the manufacturing business "is nonrecurring in nature in terms of sales, compared with AT&T's long-distance business. AT&T is a company with cyclical exposure."

Joel Gross at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette says that when the gross national product hummed along at 3% to 4% growth, AT&T's long-distance business grew at half the industry rate of 12% to 14%. "In a full recession of negative 2%, AT&T could hit the low single digits or even flatten in terms of (revenue) growth," he says.

Larry Prendergast, AT&T vice president and treasurer, begs to differ: "The current view from our economists is we are not expecting a recession in the near term," he says, "although we are more concerned about it than we were several months ago. . . . We have not changed our guidance of July 19 that we expect 1990 to be better than '89, a record earnings year." The executive notes that AT&T trades at 11.5 times analysts' per-share profit estimate for 1990, "in line with other companies such as MCI, IBM and GE." AT&T stock has fallen more than 20% since June, when analysts last cut their estimates.

A companywide reorganization has cut almost 40,000 jobs in two years, and AT&T Bell Laboratories -- until now the sacred cow in cost-reduction efforts -- is expected to begin downsizing later this year. "That's probably a good guess," says an AT&T spokesman. "But those would be a couple of hundred non-technical, behind-the-scenes type jobs."

PaineWebber's Mr. Grubman Friday cut his full-year profit estimate for AT&T to $2.50 a share from $2.60 -- flat against 1989's showing. He cut his 1991 estimate to $2.70 from $2.95.

Since March, AT&T's credit card operation has broken banking industry records, signing up nearly three million accounts and five million cards and racking up more than $750 million in card purchases. AT&T gets its cut from the fees paid by merchants on those purchases and by long-distance callers using the card. But analysts aren't sure how much AT&T has pocketed in transaction fees from the card (and customers who order the card this year won't ever pay an annual fee.)

Mr. Grubman estimates that AT&T could be spending as much as $100 million a quarter to push its plastic against entrenched giants such as Citicorp and Chase Manhattan. He says the investment is worth it. "They could have 11 million cards by this time next year," he says. "If they do and they get a 3% return on their assets they'll earn 35 cents a share annually from the credit card business."

Mr. Morris isn't so sure. "It remains to be seen whether AT&T can make money off credit cards," he says. "Returns in the industry have traditionally been high, but AT&T's coming in will likely drive industry returns down." Mr. Morris is leaving his 1990 estimate for AT&T at $2.60 -- already one of the Street's lowest -- but he cut his '91 prediction to $2.80 from $3.

Analysts are cautious about the long-distance industry's prospects. AT&T derives between 50% and 55% of its revenue and 85% of its profit from long distance. Many analysts cut their estimates for MCI Communications a few weeks ago. They worry that the long-distance marketing wars are driving up selling costs.

AT&T could match the spending of its long-distance foes easily, but that wouldn't erase its immediate problems. Says Mr. Grubman: "Long term, AT&T has a very powerful base and is a good investment. But near term, with all the economic problems, its stock is going to move more sideways than up. AT&T's performance will mirror the market's."

One of AT&T's biggest shareholders is the University of California, which holds about 6.2 million shares and believes AT&T can produce annual earnings growth of about 11% a year over the long term. AT&T's 4.2% dividend yield exceeds the overall stock market's and provides "some dividend support" for AT&T shares, says Patricia Small, associate treasurer of the university system, which manages about $16 billion of pension and endowment funds. "The major question is whether its growth rate will continue through all kinds of economic cycles . . . We don't live and die with quarterly earnings estimates."
