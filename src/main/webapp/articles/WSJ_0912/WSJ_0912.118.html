Texas Instruments Seeking Big Fees From Japan Firms

  • Author: Masayoshi Kanabayashi and Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 09/12/90

TOKYO -- Texas Instruments Inc. is asking Japanese semiconductor manufacturers to pay higher royalties on its basic patent for the production of dynamic random access memory chips, Toshiba Corp. said. Texas Instruments' share price skyrocketed in response to a Japanese press report that the U.S. firm is demanding as royalties 10% of wholesale value of products using the chip.

A spokesman for the Japanese electronics company declined to confirm the report. But he said that there have been negotiations with Texas Instruments and that the U.S. concern has been demanding "considerably higher royalty payments." He said the fee varies from company to company but usually ranges from 1% to 3% of the production value.

Other major Japanese companies, such as Fujitsu Ltd. and Mitsubishi Electric Corp., also confirmed that they have been negotiating with Texas Instruments on the renewal of cross-licensing agreements. But their spokesmen said the companies haven't received requests for higher royalties yet.

The Toshiba official wouldn't speculate on the outlook for the talks. But he indicated that it won't be easy for Texas Instruments to win huge royalties because Japanese concerns could respond in kind. "We have some important patents that have been licensed to (Texas Instruments) so we are going to use them as a leverage in negotiations," he said.

According to the press reports, Texas Instruments is also demanding royalty payments totaling an estimated 100 billion yen ($750 million) a year under a separate Japanese patent. Known as the Kilby patent, it covers all integrated computer circuit. So far, no Japanese chip maker is paying royalties on the Kilby patent because it was granted only recently.

If the chip manufacturers agree to pay the DRAM royalties demanded by Texas Instruments, as well as the royalties under the Kilby patent, these payments would cost Japanese industry a total of 200 billion yen a year, according to the Japanese business newspaper Nihon Keizai Shimbun.

Texas Instruments officials in Tokyo refused to comment on the details, and Japanese company officials said they couldn't immediately confirm the report.
