Law -- Legal Beat: New York Challenges RTC's Bid To Oust Renters at Seized Building

  • Author: Mitchell Pacelle and Richard Koenig
  • Date: 09/12/90

New York's attorney general challenged an effort by the federal thrift-bailout agency to evict eight renters from a Manhattan condominium building seized by the government from an insolvent savings and loan. In a strongly worded letter to Resolution Trust Corp.'s executive director, David C. Cooke, state Attorney General Robert Abrams said the tenants were entitled under the state's rent control and stabilization laws to occupy the apartments for as long as they wished.

The RTC said that the New York case is the first case in which they have sought evictions, but that the agency was prepared to do so whenever it was necessary to minimize losses to U.S. taxpayers.

The federal government is receiving roughly $9,000 per month from the midtown apartments, while they are costing the government about $27,000 per month, according to RTC spokesman Stephen Katsanos.

"Public protection shouldn't include throwing people out of their homes," countered Richard Barr, a spokesman for the attorney general.

In August the RTC informed the eight tenants that the 1989 legislation creating the bailout agency gave the government the right to repudiate their leases. The RTC then offered to sell the apartments to the tenants.

The apartments in question were foreclosed on by Nassau Federal Savings & Loan Association when the developer who converted them into condominiums defaulted on his loan. The RTC was appointed receiver of the thrift last March.

In his letter, Mr. Abrams said that New York's Martin Act bars owners from evicting renters when buildings are converted to condominiums.

"RTC should not reap windfall profits by transforming occupied residential apartments into more valuable vacant apartments by the draconian measure of evicting the longstanding tenants of those apartments in violation of New York law," Abrams wrote.

Although the attorney general suggested that government should sell the condominiums as occupied units, the market for such units was nearly wiped out by the Tax Reform Act of 1986.

John Jeffries Jr., a professor of constitutional law at Virginia School of Law, said that although federal law overrides state law, it is arguable whether Congress intended to give the RTC the right to take such action.

Mr. Abrams's office said that it hopes to persuade the RTC to reverse its action, but that it was up to the tenants involved to challenge the action in court.

"No judge in New York is going to allow this," predicted Errol Brett of New York's Schwartzseld, Ganfer & Shore, which represents hundreds of tenants associations. "The {federal} statute doesn't apply to such situations at all."

INSURERS' TACTICS condemned by judge in toxic-waste liability dispute.

Investigators working on behalf of insurance companies misled former employees of Monsanto Co. about the investigators' identities and purposes, a state judge in Delaware ruled.

In the continuing litigation, the insurers argue that they aren't liable for certain cleanup costs incurred by Monsanto, a St. Louis chemicals company. The investigators were seeking any information about Monsanto's operating practices that might prove damaging to the company.

Judge Vincent J. Poppiti ordered Travelers Corp., one of Monsanto's primary insurers, based in Hartford, Conn., to provide Monsanto with any former employees' statements obtained by investigators. He said that evidence won't be admissible at trial, scheduled to begin in September 1992. Judge Poppiti didn't specifically blame Travelers for the alleged misrepresentation.

The judge also provided a script to be used by investigators and lawyers when they initiate future interviews of former Monsanto employees. Among other things, the script requires the investigators to make clear that Monsanto and the insurers are in litigation.

Such high-stakes battles are increasingly common between companies and their insurers over who should pay for the burgeoning cost of cleaning up toxic waste allegedly released by the companies. And insurers believe that interviews with former employees can be effective in exposing wrongdoing by the companies that might release the insurers from any obligation to pay.

In the Monsanto case, Judge Poppiti noted affidavits by the former employees stating that, in some instances, the investigators didn't disclose they represented the insurers. For example, one former Monsanto employee was allegedly told that an investigator "worked for a firm in New Orleans which had been commissioned by the chemical industry to look into which companies were polluting," according to the judge's opinion. Two others were allegedly told that investigators were "compiling a book" about a company plant, the judge wrote.

The judge acknowledged that some statements by the former employees were contradicted by the affidavits of investigators, but he still found evidence to conclude that former employees were misled.

The judge used strong language to condemn any dissembling. "There is no vice that doth so cover a man with shame as to be found false and perfidious," he wrote, quoting the 17th-century philosopher Francis Bacon.

His order doesn't specify individual blame for misrepresentation among the three dozen or so insurance companies named by Monsanto as defendants, their outside lawyers and independent investigators. It wasn't immediately clear who, if anyone, coordinated the investigators' work.

Travelers said in a statement that it didn't supervise the investigators, nor did Travelers' outside lawyers supervise them. Travelers also said it believes "parties to the case will seek to clarify this point with the court."

Judge Poppiti said the lawyers are accountable for the investigators they retained, and he ordered the lawyers, not their clients, to pay certain court costs of Monsanto.

DRUG-CASE DEFENDANTS could lose federal benefits under new rules.

People convicted of drug-related crimes, including possession, may be denied such federal benefits as student financial aid and small-business loans under sentencing regulations that took effect yesterday.

The main purpose of the policy, which Congress mandated in November 1988, is to provide judges with the authority to impose sanctions less severe than incarceration in an effort to deter illegal drug use. Inhibited by crowded prisons and court dockets, judges rarely sentence people to jail for the crime of possessing illegal drugs, as opposed to selling them.

The new authority applies to both federal and state judges, who will have broad discretion to deny hundreds of types of federal benefits. Other benefits that could be withheld include Federal Communications Commission licenses and access to various federally run training programs. A number of major federal programs aren't covered by the policy, including Social Security, health, disability and veterans' benefits. The policy provides for eventual restoration of denied benefits to people who successfully complete drug-rehabilitation programs.

Many of the benefits that judges may deny are enjoyed primarily by people in middle- or upper-income brackets. But the White House drug czar, William Bennett, noted at a news conference in Washington that the federal government subsidizes roughly three million student loans and said, "You are talking about real impact here." A central thrust of Mr. Bennett's anti-drug strategy is to toughen punishment of what he calls "casual" drug use.

Kevin Zeese, a drug-case defense lawyer in Virginia, criticized the new form of punishment as "a policy based on desperation . . . because the prisons and jails are so overcrowded."

The new sanctions apply to people convicted after Sept. 1, 1989, for crimes committed on or after Nov. 18, 1988.

TOBACCO-LIABILITY RULINGS appealed by plaintiff in American Tobacco case.

In March, a federal jury in Boston cleared American Tobacco Co. of liability in the death of Joanne Kotler's husband, George, who had begun smoking at age 11 and died in 1986 at the age of 55. Stock analysts have said that the jury verdict in that case helped lift the cloud of liability suits from the tobacco industry.

In her appeal, lawyers for Mrs. Kotler asked a federal appeals court to overturn court rulings that eliminated two other tobacco companies, Liggett Group Inc. and Philip Morris Cos., from the original suit.

They also asked the court to re-examine whether Congress meant cigarette-pack warnings to protect tobacco companies from product-liability lawsuits and whether a jury should be allowed to determine whether cigarettes are a defective product or present a risk that outweighs smoking's "social utility."
