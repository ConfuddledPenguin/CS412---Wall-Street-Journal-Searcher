EC Panel Rebuts Charges on Erecting Barriers to Trade

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/15/90

BRUSSELS -- The European Community Commission rebutted charges that it is using anti-dumping duties to erect trade barriers against imports, especially from Asian nations. Presenting an annual report on anti-dumping investigations, a spokesman for the commission said the document proves that allegations the EC is using dumping duties to "build a fortress Europe" are unfounded. Dumping is the selling of a product in a foreign market at a price lower than the home-market price or the cost of production. He said EC dumping policies were "applied impartially to protect EC industry against the practice of dumping, which is illegal under GATT {the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, a Geneva-based trade regulator}."

The spokesman said the EC dealt with dumping cases from state trading nations in Eastern Europe as frequently as from Asia. In 1988, the year covered by the report, the EC opened 11 cases against Eastern European countries and the same number of cases against Japan and South Korea together.

But EC dumping actions against Japan and South Korea in recent years have sparked disputes, because they tended to involve the lucrative consumer electronics industry, in which European companies are struggling to stay competitive. Dumping cases against Eastern Europe focus on metals, chemicals and plastics.

Also, the commission has been accused by Japanese companies, as well as some trade specialists, of using unfair methods of calculation that lead to high dumping duties on Japanese products. As a result, the EC is under pressure within GATT to make its anti-dumping rules more transparent.

The report showed that 79 dumping investigations were opened or under way during 1988, resulting in the imposition of 18 definitive and 28 provisional duties.
