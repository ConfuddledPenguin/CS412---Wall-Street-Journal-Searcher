EC Decides Not to Target Britain's Tied-House Pubs

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/15/90

BRUSSELS -- The European Community's competition commissioner, Sir Leon Brittan, decided to take no further action against Britain's tied-pub system, already the subject of national measures to promote competition. After a yearlong review of the EC's beer market, Sir Leon concluded there was no evidence to justify a crackdown on tied-house arrangements, which will continue to have an exemption from EC competition rules that expires in 1997. In the tied-house system, breweries own or control public houses and limit the types of beer the pubs may sell.

Though the commission expressed concern about inadequate competition in Britain, it decided to wait to evaluate measures recently imposed by Britain on the beer industry before deciding whether EC action is necessary.

The commission said 62% of Britain's beer sales pass through the tied-house system. In the other five EC countries that have tied pubs -- Belgium, West Germany, France, Luxembourg and the Netherlands -- this figure is below 40%.

But the commission plans a further investigation into licensing agreements between major brewers, under which breweries in different countries grant each other exclusive rights to brew and market beers. Such accords can lead to market sharing, reducing the scope for imports and blocking the entry into the market of major competitors, according to the EC competition authorities.
