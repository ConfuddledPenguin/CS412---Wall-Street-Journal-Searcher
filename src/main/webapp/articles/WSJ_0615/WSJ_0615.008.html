Judge Upholds U.S. On Powers to Ease Sales of Thrifts

  • Author: Christi Harlan
  • Date: 06/15/90

A federal judge in Santa Fe, N.M., ruled that the Resolution Trust Corp. can override state banking laws to facilitate the sale of failed thrifts to banks. New Mexico and 12 other states impose varying restrictions on banks' ability to operate branches, presenting statutory roadblocks to the RTC's efforts to sell failed thrifts and their branches.

The RTC contends that it has the power, under the legislation that created the agency last year, to ignore some state laws in its quest to sell failed savings and loan associations in the quickest and most cost-effective way possible. To strengthen its argument, the RTC on June 1 adopted a policy explicitly stating that it can override state laws.

Shortly after the policy took effect, trade associations representing independent and community banks in New Mexico filed suit, asking federal Judge Santiago Campos to rule that the policy violates states' rights to establish and enforce their own laws.

The trade associations argued that banks buying failed thrifts and their branches would have an unfair advantage over banks that don't acquire thrifts and thus must follow state law restricting branches to their home counties.

After a hearing in Santa Fe, Judge Campos rejected the bank organizations' arguments and dismissed their suit. A final order stating his reasons for the ruling is expected to be issued today.

The possible impact of Judge Campos's ruling on other state laws couldn't immediately be determined. But a federal judge in Colorado, where bank-branch limitations are among the strictest in the country, has also been asked to rule on the legality of the RTC's override policy.

Barbara Walker, first assistant attorney general in Colorado, said she filed a request Monday for judicial review of the policy on behalf of the state banking commissioner. No court date has been set.
