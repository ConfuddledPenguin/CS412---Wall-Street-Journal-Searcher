Tseng Labs Says Profit Forecasts Are Near Target

  • Author: Anne E. Nichols Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/15/90

NEWTOWN, Pa. -- Tseng Labs Inc. is "comfortable" with analysts' estimates of 1990 earnings of around 40 cents a share, Jack Gibbons, chief financial officer, said. That range would indicate net income of about $7.3 million. In 1989, the maker of enhanced graphics devices for personal computers had net of $5.1 million, or 28 cents a share, on sales of $31.1 million.

Personal computer sales "are harder to predict than the weather," Gibbons said in an interview. But he expressed confidence in Tseng Labs' ability to sustain recent growth trends.

The company attributes much of the anticipated improvement in performance to its ET4000 product line, a new generation of video graphics array controllers for color monitors. Jack Tseng, president and chief executive officer, said the ET4000 has "achieved a 20% share after only four to six months on the market."

An earlier product, the ET3000, was an important engine of growth for Tseng Labs in the late 1980s. The product was developed in response to International Business Machines Corp.'s introduction in 1987 of its PS/2 line of personal computers.

Tseng said the company now delivers about 300,000 to 400,000 video graphic array chips a quarter. Currently, sales volume for the ET3000 is about twice that of the ET4000. By the fourth quarter, however, Mr. Tseng said he believes that ratio will be reversed.

The ET3000 chip sells at an average price of $23, while the ET4000 sells on average for $27.

Mr. Tseng said a computer-product trend toward denser memory capacity is making data more difficult to retrieve. This, he said, makes the ET4000 a more attractive product because of its powerful memory architecture.

In addition, the company recently introduced the ET1700, a lower-cost video chip aimed at the large market for monochrome graphics. By adding the 1700 to its lineup, Tseng Labs hopes to compete more effectively across both the color and monochrome segments of the entry-level market.

At present, Tseng Labs says it has no debt and about $7 million in the bank. Any near-term expansion will be financed with internally generated funds, Mr. Gibbons indicated. The company doesn't anticipate any public offerings of securities in the near future, he said.

Rather than develop a much wider variety of product lines, Mr. Tseng said, the company "will continue to grow by focusing on graphic and imaging applications." For the forseeable future, he said, he anticipates that the ET3000, ET4000 and their byproducts will provide the company with the bulk of its business.

However, Tseng Labs does plan an entry soon into the related market of laptop circuit-board products, a move that probably will require an expanded sales and research effort. Tseng said the company's lap-top chip currently in development, called the ET4000LX, is expected to be available late this year.
