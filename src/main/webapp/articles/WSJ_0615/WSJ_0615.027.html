U.S. Files Civil Suit Against Incinerator Of Hazardous Wastes

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/15/90

WASHINGTON -- The federal government took legal action that could shut down Marine Shale Processors Inc., operator of the largest incinerator of hazardous wastes in the country. The Justice Department and Environmental Protection Agency filed a civil suit in federal court in Louisiana seeking a permanent injunction against Marine Shale.

The privately owned Amelia, La., company, which claims to use a revolutionary treatment process that can recycle hazardous waste into a benign ash for use in landfills, also faces penalties of up to $50 million, a Bush administration official said.

The government claims the company is illegally operating without a waste-treatment permit that requires strict operating procedures. It challenges Marine Shale's argument that it is exempt from hazardous waste-treatment regulations because it is a recycler.

Government tests last year of ash samples from Marine Shale turned up high levels of heavy metals, such as lead, which officials are concerned could leak into groundwater.

An attorney for the company couldn't be reached for comment. Besides claiming an exemption, Marine Shale attorneys have said the company is deterred from seeking a hazardous-waste permit because it would have to shut down for three or more years while its application was being processed.

The hazardous waste-treatment industry has been up in arms over Marine Shale, whose cheaper rates have enticed away business since 1985 from competitors such as Waste Management Inc. and Rollins Environmental Services Inc. Major oil, chemical and mining companies are among Marine Shale's customers.

Last year, Marine Shale pleaded guilty to a hazardous-waste felony count and other charges and paid a $1 million criminal fine.

In addition, a congressional panel held hearings into what it called "sham recyclers," focusing on Marine Shale. Company owner Jack Kent countered with television ads showing off his treatment technology.
