Health: Hospitals Now Can Sue States Over Medicaid

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 06/15/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court ruled that hospitals and nursing homes may sue state officials under federal law to force them to provide adequate reimbursement for Medicaid services, a decision that will drive up federal and state spending on health care for the poor. The 5-4 opinion, written by Justice William Brennan, is certain to force many states to boost their payments to hospitals and nursing-home services under the joint state-federal Medicaid program. The ruling, in a case brought by the Virginia Hospital Association, comes at a time when hospitals around the country are complaining that states, fighting to curb rising health costs, have set reimbursement rates so low that providers lose money on Medicaid patients.

"The decision is certainly good news for hospitals," said Walter Dellinger, the Duke Law School professor who argued the case for the hospital association. But he also asserted that the decision "should greatly benefit the poor," who have "experienced increasing difficulty in obtaining access to medical care" in the face of Medicaid funding limits.

Laurens Sartoris, president of the Virginia Hospital Association, said that current reimbursement rates cover only 71% of the cost of care his members provide under Medicaid. To cover the loss, he said many hospitals try to boost the rates they charge private patients whose bills generally are paid by corporate health plans. The decision will help business by reducing the need for such cost-shifting, he said.

But the decision will make it harder for government, both federal and state, to hold down Medicaid spending. Washington currently spends about $40 billion a year on Medicaid, while states, which share the costs, spend about $30 billion. The program helps two groups -- women and children and elderly nursing-home patients.

In its brief to the court, the Bush administration warned that a ruling in favor of the Virginia hospitals "would vastly increase the cost of the Medicaid program to the detriment of both state and federal governments." The federal government pays more than half of the cost of the 25-year-old Medicaid program and sets broad coverage requirements, but the states set their own reimbursement rates.

The legal question in the case focused on the meaning of a 1980 federal law that requires states to certify that their Medicaid reimbursement rates are "reasonable and adequate" for efficient medical care.

The 1980 law doesn't expressly say that it may be enforced through private lawsuits by Medicaid providers. But the high court ruled that the 1980 law gives Medicaid health-care providers a right to sue state officials under a Reconstruction-era civil rights law to force them to adopt reasonable reimbursement plans.

The ruling affirmed the decision of a federal appeals court in Richmond, Va. The court's decision will give providers the right to seek higher rates in the future, but it doesn't allow them to seek repayment for past losses. The Virginia Hospital Association has won the right to sue, but it still must prove that the state's reimbursements are too low. The trial on the merits of the association's case is scheduled to start Dec. 17.

Chief Justice William Rehnquist dissented, joined by Justices Sandra O'Connor, Antonin Scalia and Anthony Kennedy. The dissenters said the majority disregarded the language of the 1980 amendment, as well as the intent of Congress to give states more flexibility in setting up reimbursement plans.

The ability to sue "isn't going to mean any automatic windfall" for hospitals, said John Goetz, vice president and supervisor of health-care ratings at Moody's Investors Service Inc., because litigation and rate adjustments could take a long time. Urban hospitals will benefit the most from changes. At a hospital with a top credit rating, Medicaid reimbursement accounts for about 8% of revenue. But some inner-city institutions serving particularly poor areas, depend on Medicaid for well over half their revenue, he said.

Hospitals in several states, including Michigan and Pennsylvania, have already won federal district court decisions requiring new reimbursement rates.

Yesterday's high court decision "should give pause to many state agencies who are looking at ways of modifying their payment systems to underpay the hospitals," said Kenneth E. Raske, president of the Greater New York Hospital Association.

Mr. Raske suggested that instead of reducing reimbursement, Medicaid agencies develop cost-control strategies, such as incentives to encourage Medicaid beneficiaries to use health maintenance organizations. (Wilder vs. Virginia Hospital Association)
