Technology: Science Journal Questions Texas A&M Experiment

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/15/90

An article in the scientific journal Science charged that Texas A&M University, College Station, Texas, failed to investigate adequately the possibility of fraud in a set of "cold fusion" experiments. The article by free-lance journalist Gary Taubes focuses on the claimed production of tritium by cold fusion experiments in the laboratory of chemist John Bockris. In April 1989, Mr. Bockris and colleagues were among the first to claim they had detected large amounts of tritium, a "heavy" form of hydrogen that can be produced by nuclear reactions, in a cold fusion experiment.

Mr. Taubes asserts that other scientists at Texas A&M, failing to reproduce Mr. Bockris's results, began to raise questions as to whether someone could have clandestinely added tritium to Mr. Bockris's experiments. Mr. Taubes charges that "the Texas A&M administration . . . has done nothing past some preliminary questioning." The article doesn't offer any specific evidence that the experiments were tampered with and notes that no one has made fraud charges.

John Fackler, dean of the college of science, noted that another Texas A&M scientist, nuclear chemist Kevin Wolf, already had run experiments suggesting that Mr. Bockris's results were spurious. In the absence of specific charges, "We can't go any further than Kevin Wolf has done," he said.

Mr. Fackler said he was writing Science, "pointing out this kind of journalism isn't appropriate to a journal like Science."
