Technology & Health: Many Students Remain at Risk of AIDS Despite Education Efforts, Study Finds

  • Author: Glenn Ruffenach
  • Date: 06/15/90

Despite increased efforts to educate children about AIDS, many high-school students across the country still run a significant risk of infection, according to a new report by the Centers for Disease Control. The study, a compilation of reports from departments of education in 30 states and 10 cities, found that many high-school students use intravenous drugs and share needles. Many students also say they have multiple sexual partners. Both behaviors can result in transmission of the human immunodeficiency virus, or HIV.

The findings, says Laura Kann in the CDC's division of adolescent and school health, are particularly significant because they show that the threat of infection among students isn't limited to schools in cities with large AIDS populations.

"We're not talking about students just in San Francisco or New York City," Ms. Kann said. "It's across the nation."

Surprisingly, the study showed that most students are aware that high-risk behaviors can lead to infection. Between 93% and 100% of respondents in various districts, for instance, said they knew that AIDS or HIV infection can be transmitted by sharing needles used to inject drugs. Similarly, between 74% and 98% of respondents said they were aware of the potential dangers of having sexual intercourse without using a condom.

The problem, Ms. Kann said, is that knowledge about the behavior "isn't sufficient to keep kids from practicing those behaviors. We also need education programs that help kids assert themselves in situations" that could lead to transmission of the virus.

Thirty states, as well as the District of Columbia, now require AIDS education for elementary or secondary schools, according to the National Association of State Boards of Education in Alexandria, Va. The requirements -- and effectiveness -- of each program, however, vary widely from state to state.

Sixteen states, for instance, require AIDS education to begin sometime between kindergarten and the fifth grade, said Katherine Fraser, program director of the association's AIDS Education Project. But some states don't mandate any specific grade level in which to begin instruction. Moreover, the instruction itself may be something as broad as a single assembly, Ms. Fraser said, or something as detailed as a series of classes over an entire semester.

The CDC study points out, for instance, that significant numbers of students still believe, incorrectly, that AIDS may be transmitted by blood donations or through insect bites or the use of public toilets.

"There's still a lack of understanding about how serious this is," Ms. Fraser said. Echoing the CDC, she adds that school districts in small towns sometimes tend to regard AIDS as a problem limited to bigger cities, an attitude that results in a "major barrier" to improved educational efforts.
