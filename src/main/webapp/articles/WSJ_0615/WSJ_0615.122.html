Japanese Companies' Pretax Net Rose 8.3% In Latest Fiscal Year

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/15/90

TOKYO -- Japanese corporations reported an average 8.3% pretax earnings increase in the fiscal year ended March 31, or a total of 10.050 trillion yen ($64.3 billion), owing to brisk domestic demand such as personal consumption and corporate equipment investment, the Tokyo Stock Exchange said. Exchange officials said a study of fiscal 1989 earnings reports submitted by 959 of the companies listed on the market also showed that net profits went up 13% from the previous fiscal year to 4.845 trillion yen and sales jumped 17% to 310.687 trillion yen.

Another study, of 1,067 companies, indicated that fiscal 1990 pretax earnings will grow more slowly, by 2.2%. Net profits will rise 5.4% and sales 1.5%, according to exchange officials.

While Japanese companies, as in the previous fiscal year, posted profit gains in fiscal 1989, they said, the pace of growth slowed because of the yen's decline and higher oil prices. In fiscal 1988, pretax earnings surged 25%.

Fiscal 1989 manufacturer pretax earnings rose 11% and sales 7.9%, showing gains for the third consecutive year. In contrast, non-manufacturer earnings languished, displaying a 3.2% gain despite a 23% surge in sales due to substantial declines incurred by electric power and gas suppliers as a result of rate cutbacks, higher oil prices and the yen's decline. When power and gas companies were excluded, for instance, non-manufacturer earnings went up 17%, officials noted.

They said 26 industries, including real estate, shipping and petroleum, reported gains in sales in fiscal 1989. Only one, agriculture-fishery-forestry, showed a drop, they said.

The shipping industry posted a 98% surge in pretax earnings due to recovery in shipping world-wide and restructuring efforts that included rounds of layoffs and removal of cost-ineffective ships from operations.

Brisk demand for machine tools and construction machines pushed pretax earnings by machinery manufacturers up 30% above the fiscal 1988 levels, while the construction industry showed a 26% gain owing to a private-sector construction boom during the year.

Rising market prices of copper, zinc and other metals combined with demand for processed metal alloys and construction materials to boost profits by the nonferrous metal industry by 21%.
