Moscow Confronts National Symbol In Its Efforts to Raise Bread Prices

  • Author: Elisabeth Rubinfien
  • Date: 06/15/90

MOSCOW -- It's best not to tinker with the soul of Russia, as the Soviet government is finding out. The Supreme Soviet, the Soviet parliament, overwhelmingly rejected government plans to raise bread prices July 1, postponing discussion of the matter until September. In doing so, it extended a long list of genuflections to bread as a symbol in Russian life.

Bread has been called the father of Russia, the soul of Russia, the thing without which life means nothing. When a valued guest visits a Soviet home, he is offered bread and salt as a traditional welcome. Some schools hold special "Bread Cult" evenings to introduce children to the history and cultural significance of bread.

"We teach that we were very rich, and there was beautiful delicious bread, fresh every day," says a former Moscow schoolteacher. "It was meant to be a description of the whole Russian character."

Leningrad has a Bread Museum, and every citizen well knows that in the 900-day siege of Leningrad during World War II, Leningraders were rationed to 100 grams of bread a day, and the bread was made of flour and sawdust. Even the Bolsheviks invoked bread in their rallying cry for the revolution: "Land, bread and peace."

As a result, the price of bread has hardly risen in the 70-plus years since then. The last price rise was in 1962. At a Moscow bakery today, a long white loaf sells for about 20 kopeks (32 cents at the official exchange rate) and a dark round for 10 kopeks.

The government says it's high time to change. President Mikhail Gorbachev complains that bread is so inexpensive children play football with it. But when Prime Minister Nikolai Ryzhkov last month proposed tripling bread prices as part of his economic reform program, it caused a storm of criticism.

Opposition to the program within the Parliament was particularly strong from the Central Asian regions, where grain makes up a large proportion of the diet. And in the breadbaskets of the Soviet Union -- the Ukraine and Byelorussia -- local Parliaments said they would refuse to comply with the price increase.

But the average shopper seems to have accepted reality over myth more quickly than the average Parliament member. "Bread must be taken care of, but it doesn't matter if they increase the price several times because it's way too cheap," said Nina, a middle-aged shopper at the largest bakery in Moscow, where the walls are decorated with posters proclaiming, "Bread is Precious."

By subsidizing the bread price, the government had worked itself into its present conundrum. Although farmers grow far more food grain than fodder, the Soviet Union in 1989 imported 36 million tons of grain to fill about 40% of their needs. Bread is so cheap that farmers feed it to their pigs.

The government raised wholesale prices for grain in April as a production incentive to try to reduce the dependence on imports. They assumed that it would be able to offset some of the cost by raising retail prices. Now, says Deputy Prime Minister Leonid Abalkin, the government will have to pay out an extra nine billion rubles this year to make up the shortfall. A ruble is worth $1.67 at the official exchange rate, but only a fraction of that in the black market.

The vote to postpone discussion of bread price increases until September may only add to public frustration. "At least if the price goes up, maybe the quality will be better," says Larisa Vesuchkina, as she prods loaves on a bakery shop's shelves with the customary small metal spatula. "These days it only lasts a day or two and we have to throw it out."
