Brazil Cannot Meet All Debt Interest Due in '90

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/15/90

RIO DE JANEIRO (AP) -- Brazil's top debt negotiator said the government cannot pay all the interest on its foreign debt this year because of a lack of foreign currency reserves, a major newspaper reported. Chief negotiator Jorio Dauster said Brazil would be able to pay only about $5 billion of the $15 billion in interest owed to private foreign banks in 1990 without using up its foreign reserves, the Rio daily Jornal do Brasil reported.

"Everyone knows Brazil doesn't have the kind of money to pay this interest," the negotiator told the newspaper.

Brazil hasn't paid interest to private creditors on its foreign debt since September and now is $5.6 billion in arrears, although it has kept up interest payments to European government lenders.

The suspension of interest payments is intended to keep Brazil's foreign reserves from falling below $6 billion, the equivalent of six months of imports. Foreign reserves stand at about $7 billion, according to the central bank.

The negotiator said the government first wanted to negotiate a standby pact with the International Monetary Fund for a $2 billion bridge loan before resuming talks on back interest payments.

An agreement with the IMF is considered crucial to Brazil's plans to renegotiate its $114 billion foreign debt, the Third World's largest.
