Orix Corp. Wins Rights to Buy 74 Airbus Planes --- Purchase of Orders, Options From Braniff Inc. Marks First for Japanese Firm

  • Author: Peter Waldman
  • Date: 06/15/90

Orix Corp. said it won the rights to buy 74 A-320 Airbus Industrie planes that Braniff Inc. had on order and option, marking the first time a Japanese company will buy aircraft strictly for lease. Attorneys for Tokyo-based Orix, a huge financial-services concern, said the company won the rights to the planes in an auction held in federal bankruptcy court in Orlando, Fla. They said Orix will pay Braniff $62 million, of which Braniff owes Airbus $14.2 million for past debts. The transaction covers firm orders for 24 aircraft and options to purchase 50 additional planes, all for delivery by the year 2000. If all options are exercised, the total purchase price will be well in excess of $3 billion.

Last fall, Braniff filed for protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code. The auction was part of the defunct airline's liquidation.

For the aircraft-leasing industry, the Orix acquisition -- and the prospect of future deals by other Japanese companies -- could spell more competitive times ahead, say industry analysts and executives. In recent years, as air travel has expanded and demand for planes grown, fortunes have soared for aircraft-leasing companies such as GPA Group Ltd. of Ireland and Los Angeles-based International Lease Finance Corp.

But until now, no major Japanese company has owned planes for lease, though several Japanese financial institutions have been major players in other types of aircraft financing.

"This is a huge step for Orix," said David Gebler, president of United States Airlease, a unit of Ford Motor Co.'s U.S. Leasing subsidiary. "This is the next step in a developing trend, whereby Japanese institutions have seen the huge financing demand of the airline industry."

Leasing planes, versus financing them through conventional borrowing, is popular among some airlines because of tax and other accounting reasons.

Neil Mitchell, an attorney for Orix with the New York firm Breed, Abbott & Morgan, said the company intends to acquire more aircraft for lease in the future, "to become another GPA." He said Orix has helped finance some 191 aircraft acquisitions for airlines around the world, but "they're using this to make an entry into the operating lease area."

In its year ended March 31, Orix had net income of $124.6 million, more than double year-earlier net of $52.9 million. Revenue rose to $2.04 billion from $767.9 million.

It is unclear whether Orix already has leases lined up for its planes. Mr. Gebler said he expects Orix to compete in the lucrative but risky shorter-term lease segment, the market for leases of five to 10 years and the stronghold of GPA and International Lease Finance. In that segment, aircraft owners expect to lease planes to several customers over the life of the aircraft, compared with the longer-term lease market, which is less risky but not as profitable.

Mark Daugherty, an airline analyst with Dean Witter Reynolds, said Orix and other Japanese companies could have advantages over U.S. leasing competitors because of their lower cost of capital.

"They're late coming into the business," he said, "but they may offer very attractive rates in order to get started."
