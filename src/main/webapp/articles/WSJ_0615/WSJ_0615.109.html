Politics & Policy: Police May Use Checkpoints in Efforts To Catch Drunken Drivers, Justices Say

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/15/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court ruled that police may set up highway checkpoints in efforts to catch drunken drivers. By a 6-3 vote, the high court said checkpoints in which police stop every car don't violate the Fourth Amendment's protection against "unreasonable searches and seizures." In an opinion written by Chief Justice William Rehnquist, the court upheld a checkpoint set up in 1986 by the Michigan State Police.

Use of checkpoints by state and local police has become widespread in recent years as public attention on drunken driving has heightened. Alcohol played a part in more than 23,000 traffic deaths in 1988 of a total of 47,000 traffic fatalities, according to Transportation Department statistics.

Estimates of checkpoint use vary. The National Commission Against Drunk Driving, a private group, said several years ago that police have used checkpoints in all 50 states and the District of Columbia. But some state and federal officials say the number of states using the checkpoints is lower. A 1985 survey by Michigan officials found 36 states using checkpoints. Thirty-one states filed a friend-of-the-court brief in support of Michigan in the high court.

The high court said that stopping a car at a checkpoint falls within the legal definition of a "seizure," triggering the protection of the Fourth Amendment. But the court concluded that such a seizure isn't "unreasonable" for three reasons: The states have a strong interest in curbing drunken driving; checkpoints help achieve that aim, and the intrusion on motorists is "slight."

The ruling is the culmination of a decade of legal uncertainty created by an earlier high court ruling. In 1979, the justices struck down a Delaware system of establishing checkpoints and randomly selecting cars to be checked. The court said random selection gave police officers too much discretion without standards for exercising their authority. "Questioning of all oncoming traffic at roadblock-type stops is one possible alternative," the court suggested as an aside in 1979.

Since then, according to Mothers Against Drunk Driving, which hailed yesterday's decision, courts in 21 states have upheld checkpoints while judges in 12 others have struck them down. In Michigan, a state appeals court ruled that the checkpoint, which was used on only one night, violated the Fourth Amendment.

Yesterday's ruling should eliminate any uncertainty, although in some states the checkpoints may still violate privacy guarantees in state constitutions that are broader than the Fourth Amendment.

Justice John Stevens dissented, joined by Justices William Brennan and Thurgood Marshall. Challenging the majority's conclusions, the dissenters said there isn't any evidence to demonstrate that checkpoints are more effective than using the same police manpower for traffic patrols.

And in an unusual and pointed reference to totalitarian governments, the dissenters said: "Unannounced investigatory seizures are, particularly when they take place at night, the hallmark of regimes far different from ours." They also said that alcohol use has been declining as a cause of traffic fatalities for the past six years. (Michigan State Police Department vs. Sitz)

Discounts for Wholesalers

The justices gave manufacturers more flexibility to charge lower prices to wholesale distributors than to retailers in the same market, as long as the discount is reasonably based on services provided by the wholesalers.

But while establishing this flexibility, the justices upheld a $1,349,700 jury verdict against Texaco Inc. for illegal price discrimination against a dozen retail gasoline dealers in the Spokane, Wash., area. In an opinion written by Justice Stevens, the high court said the evidence showed that Texaco's discounts to two wholesalers in Spokane weren't related to any services performed by the companies.

The vote was 9-0 to uphold the verdict against Texaco and 6-3 to allow manufacturers to offer so-called reasonable "functional discounts" to wholesalers but to deny them to retailers.

Functional discounts are price reductions offered generally to wholesalers for services they provide to retailers that the manufacturer might otherwise have to provide directly.

The Robinson-Patman Act, added by Congress to federal antitrust law in 1936, generally makes it illegal to discriminate in prices charged to different purchasers when the effect is to reduce competition. However, the court recognized that some functional discounts, although a form of price discrimination, don't violate federal law.

Peter Fishbein, a New York lawyer who represented Texaco in the high court, said the decision "is very significant" and "makes it possible for suppliers to have dual distribution systems of wholesalers and retailers and to charge different prices to each." The ruling could affect a range of manufacturing and production industries, from gasoline and oil to auto parts.

In the Texaco case, the jury found in 1985, and a federal appeals court in San Francisco agreed in 1988, that Texaco violated federal law by giving a discount on gasoline to two Spokane-area wholesalers. The wholesalers had a dual role, distributing the gasoline to retailers and selling some of it through their own retail outlets that competed directly with independent Texaco retailers. The jury found that Texaco discriminated because the wholesalers' retail outlets received the discount while other retailers didn't.

The jury awarded the retail dealers $449,900, which was tripled under federal law to $1,349,700.

Justice Byron White, in one separate opinion, and Justice Antonin Scalia, joined by Justice Anthony Kennedy, in another opinion, disagreed with the court's exception to the law for "reasonable functional discounts." (Texaco Inc. vs. Hasbrouck)

Clean Air Plans

The justices ruled that the Environmental Protection Agency's failure to review a state's proposed changes in a clean-air plan within a four-month deadline doesn't mean the changes automatically take effect and the old standards can no longer be enforced.

The ruling is a defeat for General Motors Corp., which was sued by the EPA in 1987 for failing to comply with Massachusetts clean-air standards adopted in 1980. Massachusetts had already proposed changes in its plan that gave GM more time to comply.

The Clean Air Act says the EPA should approve or reject plan changes within four months. A federal judge dismissed the EPA's lawsuit, ruling that the four months had lapsed, that the plan changes were already in effect, and that the EPA could no longer enforce the old standards. A federal appeals court in Boston reversed the decision and said the expiration of the four-month limit didn't bar the EPA from enforcing the old standards.

In an opinion written by Justice Harry Blackmun, the high court affirmed the appeals court ruling in favor of the EPA. But the impact may be short-lived if Congress agrees on a new Clean Air Act. (General Motors Corp. vs. U.S.)
