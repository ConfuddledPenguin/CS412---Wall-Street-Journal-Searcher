REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Call for Mr. Baker

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/15/90

From time to time, we have been tempted to think that the real reason God created the Middle East was so that U.S. Secretaries of State would learn humility. This mysterious machinery seems to have taken hold of Secretary of State Jim Baker Tuesday, when he threatened to withdraw from what is always called "the Middle East peace process" unless Israel became more willing to negotiate on his terms. He even gave out the White House phone number for the Israelis to call, a diplomatic initiative that so far has resulted in about 8,000 non-Israeli calls to the White House switchboard. White House operators are now giving callers the number of the State Department. The proximate cause of this latest turn in the world's longest-running drama is the formation days ago of a new Israeli government, denounced as a "war cabinet" in various Arab precincts. In fact, the new Shamir coalition is nothing more than a resolution to the country's three-month political crisis. It was born out of a necessity to establish a working government that can last at least until the next elections in 1992.

In essence, the Israeli Knesset has simply voted no confidence in existing peace proposals. On that basis, it was possible to form a government. That is not good news for Syria's Mr. Assad or Iraq's Mr. Hussein, who no doubt prefer a divided government.

Prime Minister Yitzhak Shamir's move represents a blunt appraisal of "peace" proposals being put forth from inside and outside Israel. As we have said here before, it is not evident how any of them promises peace so long as there are no grounds to believe that Yasser Arafat and the leaders of Iraq, Syria and Libya share that goal.

Certainly, Israel is insistent on absorbing as many Jews as the Soviets will allow to emigrate. The immigration has depressed Palestinian hopes of Israel ultimately having more Arabs than Jews in its population. But to think of the Soviet refugees as a threat to the Arab world stretches credibility.

The claim that the new government will oppose all plausible chances for peace further stretches credibility. Why should Israel not want peace? Its constant state of siege, most recently manifested in a foiled Arab raid aimed at crowded Tel Aviv beaches, is costly both to the national consciousness and the Israeli economy.

The U.S. government displays some discomfort over the latest turn of events, but in some sense it is off the hook. Clearly its 1988 guidelines for establishing a dialogue with the PLO -- which called on the PLO to abjure terrorism -- were unrealistic. Until the recent raid, the Bush administration was able to pretend not to notice more than 18 attempted terrorist attacks on Israel by groups represented on the PLO's executive committee. It can no longer ignore the Arafat double-cross. The experience may provide a dose of realism.

We remain convinced that these troubles will continue until Israel, not to mention its neighbors, supplants socialism with a free-market economy. People, after all, whether Israeli or Palestinian, have to work, and the current economic structure of the region offers sparse incentives.

Secretary Baker may yet decide that he must reinsert himself in the middle of Israel's difficulties with its unfriendly neighbors. It might be the better course if he made good on his threat to withdraw awhile.
