EC Offers Proposal On Farm Subsidies At GATT Talks

  • Author: Julie Wolf Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/15/90

BRUSSELS -- The European Community put forward proposals on how to reduce farm subsidies world-wide, but the proposals weren't expected to end the deadlock in international talks on agriculture. A spokesman for the EC Commission said the proposals were presented in a working paper to a 32-nation meeting in Geneva, Switzerland, on agriculture negotiations within the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade. Other participants, including the U.S., also presented papers at a session Wednesday.

While the spokesman called the EC proposals "a step forward," other people involved in the trade discussions expressed doubts. They said the EC paper developed some ideas put forward earlier in the year but broke little or no new ground.

For example, the paper reiterated in some detail the 12-nation EC's argument that there should be a "rebalancing" of import tariffs on agricultural goods so that cuts in tariffs on some products could be accompanied by increases elsewhere. The EC contends that as long as the "global" level of subsidies is reduced, some increases should be allowed.

This approach has been flatly rejected by the U.S. and other countries, such as Australia, that insist the talks shouldn't lead to any tariff increases at all. Washington has been emphatic on this point because EC moves to increase tariffs would affect U.S. exports of oilseeds and cereal substitutes, which currently enter the EC with little or no import duty charged.

The EC paper also spelled out in more detail the EC's response to a U.S. proposal for all agricultural protection programs to be converted into tariffs. The community has said it would be willing to accept such a development as long as the tariffs included a "corrective" mechanism to take into account factors such as fluctuations in world commodity prices.

People from outside the EC said the paper confirmed their expectations that the new system proposed by Brussels would be similar to the variable levy that currently protects EC farmers from the impact of lower world prices. "It looks like the variable levy by another name," one person said.

The EC has come under strong pressure in the GATT talks to dismantle its complex and expensive agricultural support system, known as the Common Agricultural Policy. But EC negotiators are hampered by their mandate from EC governments, which specifically states that the principles of that policy mustn't be violated.

The U.S., Australia and some Scandinavian nations put forward papers spelling out agricultural support programs that they said shouldn't be subject to cuts. These are aimed at setting strict criteria to ensure that government aid to farmers doesn't distort trade.
