U.S. Auto Makers Plan to Boost Output In Third Quarter Despite Lagging Sales

  • Author: Krystal Miller
  • Date: 06/15/90

DETROIT -- Despite disappointing sales so far this spring, U.S. auto makers plan to boost production during the third quarter, reflecting lean inventories and concern about labor talks this summer. Industry sources predict the 10 major auto makers in the U.S. will build 1,564,216 cars in the third quarter, up 12.2% from the relatively weak year-earlier level. All but Chrysler Corp. and Nissan Motor Corp. expect to build more cars during the 1990 third quarter than they did a year ago.

But the industry's more robust output plans don't necessarily reflect an expectation of stronger demand. John Qualls, an analyst with Hill & Knowlton, said he expects domestic cars to sell at a 6.7 million vehicle annual rate during the third quarter, while the production schedules indicate output will run at a 7.6 million car annual rate for the quarter. That means auto makers may have to trim their schedules by as many as 100,000 cars and trucks if sales don't pick up, Mr. Qualls said.

What auto makers have going in their favor is that depressed first- and second-quarter production schedules have helped the Big Three keep inventories down, even though sales have been sluggish since February.

As of May 31, Chrysler had a 56-day supply of cars, General Motors Corp. a 62-day supply and Ford Motor Co. a 69-day supply, according to Ward's Automotive Reports, a trade journal. These levels are well within the 65-to-70 day range considered optimal.

Further, analysts say sales aren't the only factor affecting production plans for this summer.

GM and Ford may be planning to beef up output in part as a hedge against a possible strike by the United Auto Workers union this fall. The UAW's contracts with the Big Three expire Sept. 14. Bargaining is expected to begin next month.

The decision by Chrysler to cut its third-quarter car production by 5.1%, despite relatively low inventories and the possibility of a UAW strike, reflects the No. 3 auto maker's sharply lower sales and the shutdown earlier this year of a subcompact car plant in Detroit. Chrysler's sales are off 17.5% for the year to date, and its share of the U.S. car market has dropped to 9.7% from 10.9% a year ago.

GM and Ford also are having sales problems. GM's sales are down 6% so far this year, and its share of the U.S. car market slipped to 35.5% in May from 37% a year earlier. Ford's year-to-date sales are off 11.7%, and its share of the passenger car market fell to 18.8% in May from 21.5% a year ago.

Despite soft sales, GM and Ford plan to increase production in the July-to-September period by 13.3% and 14.5%, respectively. But GM and Ford face a potential problem: Many dealers, worried by the weak market, are reluctant to lay in heavy supplies of vehicles.

"I don't want a bunch of cars I can't sell," said Glenn Cain, general sales manager of Desmond Pontiac in Cincinnati. Desmond currently has a relatively modest 60-day supply of vehicles.

Meanwhile, Japanese auto makers Honda Motor Co. and Toyota Motor Corp. have planned sharp increases in their U.S. car output, reflecting their market share and sales gains this year. The Chrysler-Mitsubishi Motors Corp. joint venture, Diamond Star Motors, and the GM-Toyota joint venture plant in Fremont, Calif., plan healthy increases, too.

Nissan said its third-quarter car production will fall by 18.2% because its Smyrna, Tenn., factory will be slowed to re-tool for the launch of a redesigned 1991 Sentra subcompact model in August.
