Thursday's Markets: Stocks Fall On Earnings Worries --- Rising Bond Prices Fail to Offset Gloom About Profit Levels

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 06/15/90

NEW YORK -- Stock investors shifted their attention from the hope for lower interest rates, which would spur the economy, and bid prices lower amid a gathering gloom about earnings. For the second day in a row, rising bond prices failed to lead stocks higher. Part of the reason was news of lower-than-expected profits at Digital Equipment and United Telecommunications.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average eased 1.48 points to close at 2928.22 in light trading. Broader market indexes fell by wider margins. Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, for example, declined 2 to 362.90.

Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices rose nearly half a point, or almost $5 for each $1,000 of face amount. In quiet currency trading, the dollar rose against the mark and fell against the yen, which was buoyed by higher Tokyo stock prices.

Bond prices rose in overnight trading on news of another big drop in gold that was caused, some said, by Arab selling. Bonds eased after the U.S. producer price index, a measure of wholesale inflation, came in close to expectations. But they rose again in response to a broad decline of commodities prices, which was seen as good news for inflation. Trading was moderate.

But stock investors weren't paying attention, and instead nursed their bottom-line blues. Adding to the list of bad news about earnings were indications that problem loans will continue to hurt the profits of banks, whose shares fell as a result.

Banks also play a role in a larger -- and gloomier -- scenario outlined by Robert Stovall, president of money manager Stovall/Twenty First Advisers. He says banks are getting skittish about lending money as regulators grow increasingly vigilant. Tough-to-get loans eventually will affect earnings, which will begin to disappoint stock investors.

"There is credit rationing on the part of wounded, gun-shy banks," Mr. Stovall said. "It will inhibit profit growth."

The more immediate concern, said Carmine Grigoli, vice president, equity research, at First Boston, is the specific reports of declining earnings expectations. The disappointing retail sales figures reported this week suggest "the economy is alarmingly weak," he said, which will hurt profits more than any credit crunch.

In major market action:

Stock prices eased in light trading, with 135.8 million shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange. Decliners led advancers, 917 to 560.

Bond prices climbed. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 8.34%.

The dollar was up against most other major currencies. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6935 marks and 154.17 yen, compared with 1.6865 marks and 154.75 yen Wednesday.
