House Committee Completes Farm Bill, Adopts New Policy on Soil Conservation

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 06/15/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Agriculture Committee, working into the wee hours yesterday, wrapped up a $55 billion, five-year farm bill after resolving a knot of contentious environmental issues. The lawmakers adopted a new soil- and water-conservation policy for the 1990s, with pledges from leading farm and environmental groups that they will support on the House floor key provisions regarding water quality and wetlands protection.

Following the final voice vote, Chairman Kika de la Garza (D., Texas) described the legislation as providing "a reasonable safety net for farm income while recognizing budget constraints."

Outlays for milk-price supports are projected to push the overall five-year cost above the $53 billion spending limit in the House budget resolution. But committee staffers said the $55 billion package would still be consistent with the Senate Budget Committee guidelines, which House lawmakers expect to follow in the event of a deficit-reduction agreement between Congress and the White House.

In the conservation part of the mammoth bill, money would be paid to farmers for putting highly erodible or environmentally sensitive land in the nation's Conservation Reserve, which the lawmakers voted to expand to 45 million acres from the current authorized level of 40 million. Currently, 34 million acres of crop and range land are enrolled in the reserve.

The committee agreed to clarify and modify controversial provisions in the so-called swamp-buster law, which limits the conversion of wetlands to agriculture. Under a compromise embraced by environmental and farm groups, farmers would be allowed to drain small wetlands that are of minimal value to wildlife. In addition, they could drain wetlands that hamper farming operations, but only if they "mitigated" the losses by restoring other drained wetlands on their farm to their natural state.

At the same time, committee members decided to set up a voluntary water-quality program under which farmers could collect incentive payments of up to $5,000 a year for changing their farming practices to curb contamination of wells and water courses. They also adopted a plan for creating a national agricultural wetlands reserve to preserve prairie "potholes" and other habitats for migratory waterfowl. Under the plan, the government would purchase perpetual easements on the wetlands from rural landowners.

The lawmakers, suppressing a political urge to boost subsidies, agreed to freeze target prices at current levels -- wheat at $4 a bushel, corn at $2.75, barley at $2.36 and oats at $1.45 -- for the next five years. Target prices for cotton and rice would be frozen at 72.9 cents a pound and $10.71 per hundred pounds, respectively. Under the farm program, the government pays farmers subsidies if market prices fall short of target prices.

The committee also reauthorized price-support programs for sugar, peanuts, honey, wool and mohair producers. When the bill reaches the floor, probably in early July, all of these programs are expected to come under attack from a growing group of lawmakers, including Reps. Richard Armey (R., Texas) and Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.), who consider them to be unfair to consumers. The group will also seek limits on subsidy payments.

"Too much of the farm budget is going to wealthy corporate farms, not enough to family farms," Messrs. Armey and Schumer said in a statement.

Meanwhile, the Senate Agriculture Committee, after days of partisan bickering, resumed works on its version of the 1990 farm bill. But Democrats and Republicans still appeared stalemated over the big-ticket commodity programs, particularly the issue of loan rates. Caught in the middle is committee Chairman Patrick Leahy (D., Vt.). He and Indiana Sen. Richard Lugar, the ranking Republican member, thrashed out a compromise earlier this week, but the Democrats have balked at embracing it.

Sen. Lugar has threatened to create political havoc on the floor if the committee doesn't forswear so-called budget gimmicks and freeze target prices, loan rates and land-idling requirements. Yesterday, he gave his colleagues a taste of what they might expect later by attacking the sugar price-support program and proposing two amendments -- one abolishing the program and the other reducing the support levels. Both were defeated.

The House bill, meanwhile, would for the first time establish a marketing-loan program for producers of soybeans and other oilseed crops, but with the proviso that it operates at no cost to the government.

The measure also would increase food-stamp benefits slightly for the poor, reauthorize several agricultural export-promotion programs, and earmark $2.5 billion a year in export-credit guarantees to help "emerging democracies" in Eastern Europe buy U.S. foodstuffs.

In addition, the bill would set the current milk-support price of $10.10 per hundredweight as the floor price for the next five years. The dairy industry had sought a floor of $10.60. Under the bill, the support price could be boosted in response to fluctuations in government-owned surpluses of dairy products, but in no case could fall below $10.10.
