Vendor Expected To Plead Guilty Over Contributions

  • Author: Martha Brannigan
  • Date: 06/15/90

ATLANTA -- A vendor involved in a scheme in which a Southern Co. unit made illegal political contributions is expected to plead guilty next week and to cooperate with a continuing federal investigation of the affair. Fred Suttles Jr., president of Outdoor Media Inc., has agreed to plead guilty in federal court here to filing a false corporate tax return, those familiar with the matter said. Mr. Suttles declined to comment.

Outdoor Media, a Pensacola, Fla., outdoor advertising concern, was a vendor for Gulf Power Co., the Southern Co. unit, and rented billboard space to the utility. According to those familiar with the matter, Mr. Suttles directed his employees to make political campaign contributions to various candidates designated by Gulf Power and then reimbursed his employees for the contributions. In an earlier interview, Mr. Suttles acknowledged that the arrangement called for him to obtain reimbursement from Gulf Power by inflating his invoices to the utility, but he never got an opportunity to do so. His run-in with the Internal Revenue Service stems from his listing the reimbursements to employees as legitimate business expenses.

Gulf Power pleaded guilty last October to two felony counts, conspiracy to make illegal political contributions and conspiracy to defraud the U.S. of income taxes.

In papers filed by the U.S. Attorney's Office last fall laying out the basis for Gulf Power's guilty plea, prosecutors said they were prepared to present testimony from Mr. Suttles that "it was common knowledge among Gulf Power Co. vendors" that they had to comply with requests for political contributions if they wanted to get Gulf Power contracts.

Mr. Suttles' plea isn't expected to have any impact on Gulf Power: Gulf Power's plea agreement, under which the company paid a $500,000 fine, put its criminal exposure in the matter behind it. However, the IRS's investigation of individuals involved in the political contributions scheme has continued and further charges are anticipated, those familiar with the investigation said.

A Gulf Power spokesman said the company had dismissed the employees involved in the political contributions scheme "and we've moved on."

Gulf Power has said that Jacob F. Horton, a former senior vice president, directed the political contributions scheme. However, his family and friends assert that while Mr. Horton was involved, too much blame has been pinned on him. Mr. Horton was killed last April in a company plane crash, the cause of which has yet to be determined.
