Marketing & Media: FCC Asked to Probe Major Networks Over TV Programs

  • Author: Dennis Kneale
  • Date: 06/15/90

LOS ANGELES -- A coalition of Hollywood studios and producers asked the Federal Communications Commission to investigate the three major broadcast networks for alleged violations of federal rules that restrict the networks' ownership of programs. The complaint is the latest salvo in an escalating and increasingly bitter fight between Hollywood and the networks over federal rules that block the broadcasters from the lucrative rerun business. The allegations came as part of a filing that the studio coalition made yesterday to the FCC, which is about to undertake a major review of whether the rerun rules should be eased or lifted entirely.

The networks and the Hollywood studios that supply most prime-time series broke off all negotiations on Wednesday, scuttling efforts to form a compromise plan advising the FCC on how the rules should be eased. The FCC filing yesterday made it clear that both sides now seem to have put aside polite negotiation in favor of a bareknuckle brawl.

Hollywood and the networks have been haggling for seven years over the so-called Financial Interest and Syndication Rules. Imposed by the FCC to rein in network power 20 years ago, the rules forbid the networks from sharing in the profits of rerun sales, a $3 billion-a-year business.

That lucrative market has been largely left to Hollywood producers because the rules prohibit the networks from owning a partial stake in the shows produced for them by the studios. The networks -- besieged by cable, News Corp.'s Fox Television network and other competition that didn't exist 20 years ago -- want a share of the wealth and an elimination of the rerun rules.

In lodging the new charges -- which Hollywood officials concede they probably wouldn't have pursued had they reached a compromise with the ABC, NBC and CBS networks -- the studios allege an "insidious network campaign to coerce outside producers" into letting the networks own programs the producers have proposed.

Under the current FCC rules, most series are owned by Hollywood studios and independent producers, while the networks merely "license" the programming for two broadcasts. A separate 1980 consent decree limits the networks to producing at most five hours of their own prime time programming, forcing them to rely on Hollywood for the rest.

But the consent decree expires in November, and all three networks have been revving up their own production units to make more wholly-owned prime time fare. In the filing with the FCC yesterday, the studios asked for a review of all in-house contracts with producers for the last three years to determine if the networks are pressing producers to make shows under network contract rather than make them independently and then license them to the network.

Stephen A. Weiswasser, general counsel of Capital Cities/ABC Inc., said the networks deny the studios' charges. Moreover, he said, the fact that the studios didn't bother to file their complaint before now "tells you just how serious the complaints are."

Jack Valenti, president of the Motion Picture Association, said the studios "knew about some of these (network practices) all the time, but we never really got into it." Mr. Valenti said that the studios didn't want to raise the complaints while they were still trying to iron out a settlement with the networks. "It's just awkward," he said.

As talks between the two sides grew more contentious, studio officials warned network negotiators that a complaint alleging anti-competitive practices might be filed if they couldn't come to an agreement. Even as they bargained, the studios had three Washington law firms taking statements from almost 100 Hollywood producers and writers, looking for material to be included in the complaint. "Now we're in a fight for our lives, and we just have no choice," Mr. Valenti said.

In alleging network abuses, the studio coalition charged that the networks have "acted in parallel" in forcing producers to agree to strikingly similar terms. Though the three networks compete for programming, the studios say the trio uses the same license fees, the same one-year "freeze" that keeps a series proposal off the market when a network buys it but doesn't use it; and the same four-year contract commitment for a series, when the producers want a shorter term.

The filing, made by the "Coalition to Preserve" the rules, said that "whether the pervasive parallelism among the networks is the result of active coordination or simply mutual recognition of the benefits of acting in tandem, its existence is indisputable."

But the Department of Justice, in a separate filing to the FCC yesterday, provided a boost to the network side of the dispute.

The Justice filing said the networks "don't have sufficient market power to adversely affect" the market for TV programming, even if they make more of their own shows. There is "no basis in economic theory for a presumption that network ownership" of series reruns would hurt program production, the Justice Department said. It called the "practical risk" of the networks winning control of the syndication market, if the rules are lifted, "remote" and said the network practices that the rules forbid "might promote competition and efficiency."

"We urge" the FCC "to eliminate the (rerun) rules unless the Commission concludes that they are necessary to protect the public interest in competition," the filing said.
