Phone Firm's Stock Falls as Analysts Lower Forecasts

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 06/15/90

NEW YORK -- United Telecommunications Inc.'s stock fell in heavy trading after two analysts cut their earnings estimates for the long-distance telephone company. While the reductions were modest, and the analysts said they continue to recommend the purchase of United stock, the price drop underscores investor skittishness over even marginally negative news about growth companies.

United's shares fell $3.25 to $40.875 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading on volume of 4.3 million shares. The stock's normal daily volume is about 500,000 shares.

United, which owns the nation's third-largest long-distance telephone company, US Sprint Communications Co., wouldn't comment on the estimates, saying it doesn't project earnings. However, a spokesman said, "We're certainly comforted that they still consider the stock a strong buy recommendation."

"I still think the stock is cheap," said Jack B. Grubman of PaineWebber Inc., one of the analysts who cut his estimate. "It's worth in the mid-50s."

However, he noted that Sprint's rate of revenue growth from the fourth quarter of 1989 to the first quarter of 1990 was only a little more than 2%. He added that American Telephone & Telegraph Co. has managed to slow the rate at which Sprint and MCI Communications Corp. have been able to take away long-distance market share.

Mr. Grubman cut his second-quarter earnings estimate to 53 cents a share from 57 cents. For all of 1990, he trimmed his estimate to $2.50 a share from $2.70 a share, and he lowered his 1991 projection to $3.55 a share from $3.75 a share.

Joel D. Gross of Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette Inc. said he still regards United's stock as a buy.

"My estimates of $2.50 a share in 1990 and $3.50 in 1991 still show a 40% growth rate," said Mr. Gross. "With the stock trading at less than 12 times earnings next year that still looks like a buy . . . to me."

Mr. Gross had been predicting earnings of $2.64 a share in 1990 and $3.78 a share in 1991. He lowered his estimate for the current second quarter to 57 cents a share from 60 cents a share.
