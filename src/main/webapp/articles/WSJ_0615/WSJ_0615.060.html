Reebok Says Pentland Plans To Sell Its Stake --- London Firm Holds 31.5%; Sneaker Concern Adopts Anti-Takeover Defense

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 06/15/90

Reebok International Ltd. said its largest shareholder, Pentland Group PLC of London, which has a 31.5% stake, plans to sell its holdings. Reebok, which has lost momentum in its a battle with Nike Inc. for leadership in the sneaker market, said it hopes to remain independent. Directors promptly adopted a poison-pill shareholder rights plan to make any hostile takeover prohibitively expensive.

Paul Fireman, chairman and chief executive officer of Stoughton, Mass.-based Reebok, is the company's second-largest shareholder with a 14.5% stake. Reebok has 115 million shares outstanding.

Reebok made the announcement after the market closed yesterday. The company hasn't been subject to much takeover speculation in the past because of the large blocks of stock held by insiders. However, in past years when its stock slumped, some analysts had suggested that Mr. Fireman might take the company private in a leveraged buy-out, with or without Pentland.

According to the company's proxy statement, Mr. Fireman has a right of first refusal to buy Pentland's shares. At current prices, purchasing Pentland's 36.2 million shares would cost $651.6 million. Presumably such a large stake would command a premium, however.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Reebok stock closed at $18, up 12.5 cents.

Mr. Fireman said, "The board of directors firmly believes that the interests of Reebok's shareholders are best served by Reebok remaining independent. As a 14.5% shareholder of the company, I fully support the board's position."

He added that Reebok will support Pentland in its efforts "to dispose of their shares to a buyer or buyers whose interests will coincide with the long-term interests of Reebok and its shareholders, employees, and other constituencies."

Reebok didn't say how Pentland would dispose of its shares, whether in the public market or through a private transaction. The U.S. firm declined further comment on the announcement.

Reebok was one of the great growth stocks of the mid-1980s as Mr. Fireman turned a little-known British running shoe brand into the biggest selling sneaker in the U.S. by focusing on the aerobics market. However, in the past few years, it slipped behind Nike, which appeals more to athletes. Reebok has rebounded recently on the strength of its high-priced "Pump" basketball sneakers. But some analysts say the sneaker market is slowing to single-digit growth, limiting Reebok's gains.

Last year, Reebok earned $175 million, or $1.53 a share, on sales of $1.82 billion.

Alice Ruth, an analyst with Montgomery Securities in San Francisco said she has heard in the past that Pentland was unhappy with Reebok's performance.

Pentland's chairman, R. Stephen Rubin, is listed as vice chairman of Reebok, and another Pentland executive, Frank A. Farrant, also sits on Reebok's board. However, neither is active in daily management. Pentland is a holding company and footwear trading firm. Mr. Rubin owns more than half of Pentland's shares directly or through a family-owned company. Messrs. Rubin and Farrant couldn't be reached.

Many people have been critical of Reebok's marketing and advertising in the past two years. The company has attempted to sell its shoes both to the fashion market, where L.A. Gear Inc. has been strongest, and to the performance market, where Nike leads. Critics say this has muddied its image. Reebok has also had problems finding a consistent ad theme, trying and discarding the weird "U-B-U" commercials. It has repeatedly jettisoned ad agencies.

Mr. Fireman has taken an increasingly active role in operations in recent months, with the departure of several top executives who had been brought in from the outside several years ago.

One former employee discounted the idea of a hostile takeover because "it would be hard to imagine anyone buying the company without" Mr. Fireman. The ex-employee said a leveraged buy-out by Mr. Fireman might make more sense because Reebok has strong cash flow and little need for capital spending because its manufacturing is subcontracted.

Mr. Fireman is consistently one of the best-paid executives in America because of an unusual bonus provision under which he receives 5% of the amount by which Reebok's pretax earnings exceed $20 million. Last year his compensation totaled $14.6 million.

Reebok said that under its shareholder rights plan, holders of record on June 29 will receive rights to purchase one Reebok common share at $60 for each share held.
