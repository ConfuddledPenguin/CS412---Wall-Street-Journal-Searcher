Honeywell Seeks A Halt of Imports Of Minolta Product

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 06/15/90

WASHINGTON -- Honeywell Inc. petitioned the International Trade Commission to halt imports of Minolta cameras with automatic-focusing devices, charging that these products infringe Honeywell patents. Honeywell's action is a surprising departure from its earlier advocacy of harmonious relations with Japan. But Christopher Steffen, Honeywell's vice president and chief financial officer, said that seeking the ITC action is one of the few alternatives left for Honeywell to recover "hundreds of millions of dollars in lost royalties" for its pioneering work on auto-focusing technology.

The ITC petition brings further pressure on Minolta Camera Co., the Japanese maker, to settle Honeywell's separate civil suit seeking damages for alleged patent infringement, misuse of trade secrets and breach of contract. The case has been dragging on for nearly three years in a New Jersey district court.

In the suit, Honeywell claims that it confidentially provided Minolta with information for making auto-focus modules with the understanding that the Osaka manufacturer would later incorporate Honeywell-made ones in its cameras. The suit also charges that while absorbing this know-how Minolta secretly conducted its own parallel development effort and, in 1985, introduced products using modules based on Honeywell technology.

If the ITC accepts the petition, Honeywell could win relief in 12 months. Honeywell doesn't expect the New Jersey suit to go to trial until 1992.

An ITC ruling favoring Honeywell would be a severe blow to Minolta. The Japanese company's popular Maxxum models, which feature the disputed devices, account for much of its $1 billion in annual camera sales world-wide. Moreover, the trade impact could be even broader if Honeywell takes action against 15 other Japanese camera makers that allegedly obtained the same technology under similar confidential arrangements. Honeywell said these firms include Canon Inc., Asahi Optical Co., Nippon Kokaku K.K. and Olympus Optical Co.

Minolta dismissed Honeywell's charges as "baseless," claiming that they ignore an earlier history of development in auto-focus cameras, including "significant breakthroughs" by Minolta. "As we have argued in federal court -- the proper forum for this dispute -- we believe the Honeywell patents claims in question are invalid and unenforceable," the company said.

"Honeywell, which does not make or sell cameras, is asking the U.S. government to prevent Minolta -- and perhaps every other major camera manufacturer in the world -- from selling cameras in the U.S.," Minolta said. The company also charged Honeywell with attempting to breach a 1988 agreement to confine the dispute in the New Jersey court.

Honeywell's complaint is likely to resonate in Washington because of the Bush administration's concern about allegedly unfair business practices of other Japanese companies. Honeywell insists, though, that its problems with Minolta and possibly with other Japanese camera makers are exceptions to its otherwise productive dealings in Japan. "We've had technology arrangements with other Japanese companies and ended up with royalty agreements," Honeywell said.
