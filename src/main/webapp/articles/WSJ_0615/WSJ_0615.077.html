Bankers in Asia Report Scarcity Of Needed Talent --- Survey Finds Big Worries About Getting, Keeping Qualified Staff in Region

  • Author: Cynthia Owens
  • Date: 06/15/90

HONG KONG -- Bankers throughout Asia are becoming increasingly concerned about their ability to recruit and retain qualified staff. A survey of chief executive officers of 164 banks in Asia, conducted by Egon Zehnder International S.A., a European executive-search company, shows that concerns about personnel increased significantly in the past year, particularly as the spread of liberalization has bolstered demand for qualified staff. Throughout the region, 90% of bank executives said hiring qualified staff was a concern.

The survey shows a "significant scarcity of banking talent throughout the region," Norman Wright, managing partner for Egon Zehnder in Southeast Asia, said.

Personnel worries were far more widespread than just in Hong Kong, where concerns about the reversion of the British colony to Chinese sovereignty have prompted a steady outflow of talent. Indeed, the greatest scarcity of banking professionals was perceived in Indonesia, followed by Taiwan, Hong Kong, South Korea, Singapore and Malaysia.

The survey shows that bank executives are increasingly optimistic about business opportunities in the region. They said that intraregional trade and investment are of growing importance to their business, and that several infrastructure projects planned in Southeast Asia will provide new opportunities for banks.

Further, the survey shows that bank officers at corporate headquarters don't always see things the way their representatives in Asia do. No serious repercussions to business were reported by local managers as a result of the June 4, 1989, killings in pro-democracy demonstrations in Beijing, but the officers said home-office managers were far more bearish about prospects in China.

Bank officers also said their colleagues outside Asia continue to see potential for growth in the region, though "There is a perception that the best opportunities are in the home trading bloc," Mr. Wright of Egon Zehnder said.

According to the survey results, banks in Asia are placing the greatest emphasis on private banking, trade finance and corporate lending. Regulation continues to hold back foreign banks in several countries, though most are moving toward liberalization, however slowly. Some of those same countries, including Thailand, Taiwan and Indonesia, are expected to offer the best opportunities for foreign banks this year.

Hong Kong remains the region's financial center, and 71% of the bankers who responded to the survey said their banks will remain in the territory after 1997. The majority said that if their banks were forced to relocate, they would choose Singapore as a new base.

Hongkong & Shanghai Banking Corp. of Hong Kong and the Citibank unit of Citicorp were rated the best and second-best managed banks in Asia, respectively, by other bankers. The same two banking companies led the pack in last year's survey.
