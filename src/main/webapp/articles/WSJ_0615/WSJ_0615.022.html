International: N.V. Philips Sees Profit Declining In 2nd Period, `Very Low' for Year --- Amid Management Shift, Warning Comes in Bid To Regain Credibility

  • Author: Bob Hagerty
  • Date: 06/15/90

EINDHOVEN, the Netherlands -- N.V. Philips, struggling to regain credibility in the midst of a management shake-up, warned that its second-quarter profit will fall "appreciably" and that full-year results will be "very low." The Dutch electronics giant's warning sent its shares down 5% when trading opened Thursday on the Amsterdam Stock Exchange. The shares closed at 32.10 guilders ($16.89), down from 33.60 guilders Wednesday.

Analysts said the warning wasn't surprising, given the company's weak first-quarter results and the likelihood that the new management team wants to get all of the bad news out as quickly as possible. "The company is in a heavy storm," Fabian Koning of Bank Mees & Hope N.V. said.

Philips said its second-quarter warning was based on the company's performance in April and May, but it refused to elaborate on where the problems lay. In last year's second quarter, the company had profit before extraordinary items of 197 million guilders.

For the year, Philips said, provisions for restructuring costs will cause the company's "net result" to be "at a very low level." A spokesman declined to comment on whether this means a loss is possible. Philips had 1989 profit of 1.37 billion guilders, on sales of 57.22 billion guilders, but much of the earnings reflected the sale of a Paris building and other one-time items.

Analysts said second-quarter results probably are being hurt by continued losses in the computer division and by a sudden drop in sales in Brazil, where economic austerity measures are putting severe limits on consumer spending. "The Brazilian consumer just stopped buying," Chris Honnor, an analyst at Kleinwort Benson Securities in London, said. Latin America accounted for 18% of Philips's operating profit last year, and Brazil is the company's main market in that region.

Philips promised more details about results on July 2, when shareholders are to meet in Eindhoven to approve a new management lineup. In the most important change, Cornelis van der Klugt will step down as chairman July 1, a year ahead of schedule, to make way for Jan Timmer, head of the consumer products division.

Mr. van der Klugt resigned to take responsibility for a loss of investor confidence, resulting from the company's failure to warn of a collapse in first-quarter profit before extraordinary items to six million guilders from the year-earlier 223 million guilders. When that result was posted May 3, Philips shares tumbled 11%. A group of U.S. holders later filed a lawsuit against Philips, alleging the company had misled investors about its prospects. Philips has declined to comment on the merits of the suit, but it seems determined to improve its record on earnings forecasts.

Philips has long been notorious for springing nasty surprises on the stock market. Only two months ago, the company was still predicting 1990 profit would rise.

The company's biggest problem areas recently have been in semiconductors and computers, which produced what Philips described as "hundreds of millions of guilders" in losses last year.

Analysts believe Philips is burdened by large inventories of computers at a time when margins are being squeezed severely. They also say Philips is probably losing market share, as its well-publicized problems shake customer confidence.

Earlier this week, Philips said it had ended talks on cooperating with Ing. C. Olivetti & Co. of Italy in the computer business. Philips said it was still talking to other possible partners. Many analysts argue that the company's computer division is too small and unfocused to survive on its own.

Dataquest Europe estimated Philips's share of the European personal computer market at 2.2% in 1989. Philips doesn't provide separate revenue figures for computers, but IDC Nederland put Philips's European sales of personal computers, minicomputers, printers, software and related products last year at about $1.7 billion. Nearly all of the company's computer sales are in Europe.

In the semiconductor area, Philips has been hit by an industrywide slump and slower-than-expected sales of its one-megabit static random access memory chips, in which it has invested heavily.

Analysts are eagerly awaiting the July 2 meeting, in hopes that Mr. Timmer, the chairman-designate, will explain how he intends to turn the company around. Kleinwort's Mr. Honnor said the new management team is likely to try to "clear the decks" by making heavy provisions for restructuring costs this year, setting the company up for better results in 1991.

In the past three years, Philips spent a total of 1.34 billion guilders to cover early retirements, plant closings and other restructuring costs. And it closed about 75 factories, bringing the world-wide total to about 350. The work force has fallen to 300,000 from 342,000 in 1987, partly through the sale of operations.

Though Philips has been trying to focus its activities better, it still makes a huge array of products, including lamps, stereo equipment, compact disks, television sets, clocks, vacuum cleaners, garage-door openers, printed circuit boards, military equipment and medical-diagnostic imaging products.
