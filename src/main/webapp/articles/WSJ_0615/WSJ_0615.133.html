Credit Markets: Falling Gold Prices Put Luster on Treasurys As Foreigners Leave Commodities for U.S. Bonds

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Sharon R. King
  • Date: 06/15/90

NEW YORK -- A sharp drop in the price of gold sparked a modest rally in the bond market as foreign investors sold commodities and snapped up U.S. Treasury bonds. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose 1/2 point yesterday, a gain of $5 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, fell to 8.34%.

The rally kicked off during overnight trading in London, where prices of gold futures plunged through the psychologically important $350 level in heavy selling by Saudi Arabian investors. The London afternoon gold price was fixed at $345.85, the lowest since July 1986. British government bonds, called gilts, rose as much as 1 1/4 point yesterday.

Although a mildly disappointing inflation report caused U.S. bond prices to decline during early U.S. trading, the market reversed direction later in the day as the slide in gold hurt other precious metals.

Traders in both London and New York said that some of the cash flowing out of gold showed up later in intermediate-term U.S. and British bonds.

Brian J. Fabbri, a managing director at Midland Montagu Securities Inc., said investors "selling gold have wanted to get out of commodities and purchase some financial assets," in the U.S. and British bond markets.

Mr. Fabbri said that in recent weeks, the value of the dollar has "held up extremely well" while the value of the British pound has "soared." Meanwhile, bond yields have eased. Such trends, he said, have persuaded some foreign investors that they can earn high returns because they can earn profits on currency gains as well as capital appreciation when bond prices rise.

The decline in gold spread to other commodities and prompted some economists to reduce their inflation forecasts. The Commodity Research Bureau's index of futures prices fell 3.89 points to 236.44, the steepest one-day drop since May 1987.

But trading yesterday was quiet, with foreign investors accounting for a lot of the buying.

Robert D. Sbarra, a senior vice president at Carroll McEntee & McGinley Inc., said that trading was slow because domestic investors were moving cautiously ahead of today's important consumer price index report. He said the mood in the market remains bullish.

Bonds got off to a weak start yesterday morning, falling as much as 1/4 point on news that the May producer price index rose 0.3%. That was slightly higher than had been expected. Bond prices started to climb again on news that the CRB index was falling.

Today, the government will release the May consumer price index, a broad measure of inflation. A consensus of economists polled by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report expect a 0.3% rise in last month's CPI. Excluding the food and energy components, CPI is also seen rising 0.3%.

The government will also release data on May industrial production and capacity utilization and the April trade deficit. The deluge of data due out today is expected to have a major impact on trading today.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds ended unchanged to up 1/8 point in spots as the market exhibited firm tone ahead of the week's final round of economic data.

In municipal activity, a Bear, Stearns & Co. group repriced $299 million of New York Metropolitan Transportation Authority service contract revenue bonds to lower the yield by 0.02 to 0.10 percentage point.

The issue is now priced to yield from 6.40% for transit facilities bonds due in 1992 to 7.72% for bonds due in 2016, changed from being priced to yield from 6.50% in 1992 to 7.75% in 2016.

Also, a Merrill Lynch Capital Markets group repriced $710 million New York City general obligation bonds, to lower some yields. The bonds were originally priced Wednesday.

In secondary action, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 ended unchanged at 98 5/8 to yield 7.30%.

No large municipal bond issues are expected to be priced today.

Treasury Securities

Treasury bonds were moderately higher yesterday. The benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 104 11/32 compared with 103 30/32 on Wednesday. The latest 10-year notes ended at 103 11/32 to yield 8.35% compared with 103 4/32 to yield 8.38%.

Short-term rates were unchanged to slightly lower. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.67% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.91%, unchanged from Wednesday. The rate on six-month bills was 7.55% for a yield of 7.96%, slightly lower.

Junk Bonds

The junk-bond market took a breather after Wednesday's frenzied pace and ended little changed.

While prices of most junk bonds or speculative-grade securities barely budged, high-yield bonds of Southland Corp. advanced on news that the company has proposed a revised restructuring plan that it says offers substantially improved terms to bondholders and preferred stockholders.

Southland's 16.75% subordinated debentures, maturing in 2002, climbed two points to finish at a bid price of 9 while the company's 15.75% senior subordinated notes, due in 1997, surged four points to end at 19. Offerings for the 15.75% notes shot up eight points. However, junk bond dealers said there was little trading in the Southland debt.

As Southland bonds advanced, Trump Taj Mahal Funding bonds slipped on a report that a number of creditors are balking at a plan to restructure the empire of real estate developer Donald Trump.

Trump Taj Mahal 14% bonds, due in 1998, slid 1 3/4 points to 55 1/2 on the American Stock Exchange. Also lower were Trump Castle Funding's 13.75% bonds, due in 1997, which skidded 1 point to 59.

Earlier this week, the Trump bonds climbed on news that Mr. Trump had reached a tentative agreement with his major bankers that would give him time and money to restructure his crippled holdings.

Elsewhere, in the high-yield market, junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. stabilized after sprinting sharply higher Wednesday.

Foreign Bonds

British government bonds ended sharply higher for the third consecutive day, although prices slipped off session peaks due to profit-taking and a dip in the U.S. Treasury market.

Prices rose strongly during the morning, buoyed by foreign buying, falling gold prices and gains in the U.S. bond market late Wednesday.

British government bonds, called gilts, got a further boost from economic data suggesting further weakness in the labor market. Britain's unemployment rate rose to a seasonally adjusted 5.7% in May from 5.6% in April, its first monthly rise since July 1986.

Gilts prices eased somewhat later after a disappointing report on U.S. inflation caused prices of U.S. Treasury bonds to fall. U.S. Treasurys recovered later in the day and ended higher.

Inflation will be the focus of the gilts market today, when the government reports the May retail-prices index.

Among benchmark issues, Britain's 11.75% bond due 2003/2007 rose 1 1/4 points to 103 1/4 to yield 11.26% while the 12% notes due in 1995 jumped 3/4 point to 100 14/32 to yield 11.88%.

Japanese government bonds also ended higher. West German financial markets were closed in observance of the Corpus Christi holiday.

Corporate Issues

Yields on Chrysler Corp. debt securities rose yesterday in the wake of actions by Moody's Investors Service Inc. and Standard & Poor's Corp.

S&P said late yesterday that it lowered the senior debt ratings of Chrysler Corp., Chrysler Financial Corp. and Chrysler Credit Canada Ltd. The S&P rating changes come just a day after Moody's placed the auto maker's senior debt under review for possible downgrade.

S&P also lowered certain debt ratings of Ford Motor Co., but there was little change in Ford bonds because the rating change came late in the day.

In trading, yields on Chrysler's 9.60% notes, maturing in 1994, climbed from 10.25% to 10.50%. Yields on other Chrysler debt securities also rose similarly.

Meanwhile, in the new-issue market for investment-grade corporate debt, nearly $500 million in new debt issues were priced yesterday, including a $200 million issue of Dow Capital B.V. 20-year debentures. Dow Capital is a unit of Dow Chemical Co. The securities were priced at par to yield 9.20% through underwriters led by Salomon Brothers Inc.

Most investment-grade corporate bonds finished between 3/8 point to 1/2 point higher.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities ended with slight gains on the back of U.S. Treasurys.

Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were up 5/32 at 99 25/32, Ginnie Mae 10% issues were 1/8 firmer at 102 1/8. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities were 3/32 higher at 99 1/32 and 10% securities were 1/8 firmer at 101 2/32. Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were 3/32 firmer at 99 1/2 and 10% securities were up 1/8 at 101 17/32.

The market showed little reaction to yesterday's report on producer prices.
