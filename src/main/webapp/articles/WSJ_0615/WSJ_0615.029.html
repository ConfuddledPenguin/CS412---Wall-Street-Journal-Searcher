International: Which Land Is Fairest of Them All? Japanese, Americans Talk to Pollsters

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/15/90

National pride is on the rise in Japan, and economic nationalism is growing in the U.S. Nonetheless, two new polls show that citizens in both countries are often more critical of their own societies than of each other's. Which society is more intolerant of other races? In a recent Wall Street Journal/NBC News poll, a plurality of registered U.S. voters -- 46% -- point to America, while a separate Journal poll in Japan found 49% of the Japanese name their own country. Which society has more of a long-term perspective? Fifty-one percent of Americans say Japan, but only 10% of Japanese agree.

Americans, by a big margin of 65% to 23%, say Japan has stronger family values, while 49% of Japanese say that honor goes to the U.S. Each side thinks its own society is the more selfish.

In addition to this mutual modesty, two other strong impressions emerge from the polls. One it that there is broad agreement between the two countries on several societal traits. The other is that Japanese are much more likely to see no difference between Japan and the U.S.

For example, respondents in both countries generally agree that the U.S. is more devoted to democracy, free trade and free speech than Japan; that Americans are happier with their lives than Japanese; and that Japan is more nationalistic.

But while 89% of Americans think the U.S. is more devoted to democracy, only 37% of Japanese think so; 39% of Japanese say there's no difference between the two countries' level of devotion to democracy, while 15% give the nod to Japan.

Similarly, 93% of Americans and 39% of Japanese think the U.S. is more devoted to free speech, while 40% of Japanese see no difference between the two countries. Fully 70% of Americans think the U.S. is more "honest in dealing with others and others' countries"; 26% of Japanese agree, but 50% see no difference (only 15% of Japanese think Japan is more honest).

The U.S. poll was conducted for the Journal and NBC by political pollsters Peter Hart and Robert Teeter. The poll in Japan was conducted for the Journal by Nippon Research.
