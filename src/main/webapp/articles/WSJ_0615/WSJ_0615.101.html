California Agency Tightens Standard On Vehicle Emissions

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/15/90

SACRAMENTO, Calif. -- The California Air Resources Board, continuing its campaign to attempt to force auto makers to improve pollution controls, adopted tighter tailpipe emission standards for light trucks, vans and certain other large vehicles. Under the new standards, the board said up to 150,000 vehicles a year would be equipped with anti-smog equipment similar to that on passenger cars. Currently, larger vehicles don't normally feature some of the more advanced anti-pollution technology required on passenger cars.

The standards will be phased in over the 1995 and 1996 model years. The board said the new standards would reduce emissions from the targeted vehicles by 26% to 75%, depending on the model and its size. Vehicles covered by the new standards range in weight from 3,750 pounds to 14,000 pounds, and would include ambulances, tow trucks, small motor homes and oversized pickup trucks.

The agency estimated that it would cost $61 to $390 a vehicle to make modifications needed to meet the new standards.

Manufacturers of the affected vehicles couldn't be reached to comment. In general, however, car makers and gasoline producers have said that the board's pollution goals are unreachable or economically unfeasible.

The new standards are in keeping with the board's recent drive to adopt tough new emission standards. The campaign has national significance because California is the country's biggest car market and because many states model pollution laws on California's.
