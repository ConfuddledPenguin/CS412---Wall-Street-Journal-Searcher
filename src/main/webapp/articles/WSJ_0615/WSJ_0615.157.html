U.S. Study Denies Oil Firms Gouged Public Last Winter

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/15/90

WASHINGTON -- An Energy Department study rebutted arguments that consumers were gouged by oil companies when heating fuel prices skyrocketed during last winter's cold snap. The U.S. Energy Information Administration reported that net income at the refining divisions of 10 major oil companies fell more than 60% from the preceding heating season. Independent refiners' profits dropped 51% from the preceding winter.

"They lost their shirts," said Helmet Merklein, head of the agency, in an interview.

After the frostiest temperatures in 60 years hit the East Coast late last year, heating oil bills in the Northeast spiked up 55% in December, compared with a year earlier. But prices began dropping after the December cold snap, and January was relatively warm. Overall, the average U.S. household paid $71 more for home fuel oil during the heating season between October 1989 and February 1990, the report found.

Despite price boosts in heating oil and propane, "this did not translate into a remarkable season for U.S. refineries," the report concluded. For the last quarter of 1989 and first quarter of 1990, which covers the heating season, refiners had lower profit margins than major U.S. manufacturers on average, it said.

The study also didn't find any evidence of anti-competitive behavior among refiners or marketers eager to cash in on cold temperatures, as some consumer advocates and lawmakers had feared, Mr. Merklein said.

The study was based on financial information filed by companies this spring with the Securities and Exchange Commission. It examined returns from refining divisions at 10 major integrated oil companies, which account for 51% of U.S. refining capacity, and from six independent refiners to gain a representative view of what was happening in the industry during the past heating season.

Refiners and wholesalers which were lucky enough to have large stocks of heating oil on hand when the cold weather hit in December presumably enjoyed greater financial returns than if there had been no cold snap, the study conceded. But overall, over the October-to-February heating season, higher prices were more than offset by greater costs and a price plunge in February.
