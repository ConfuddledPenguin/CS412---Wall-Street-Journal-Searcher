Trump Creditors Work to Meet Deadline Today

  • Author: Neil Barsky
  • Date: 06/15/90

Donald Trump's major creditors worked last night to meet a deadline for the beleaguered developer to make a bond payment today. As negotiations crawled into the night, some people close to the talks were pessimistic that nearly $30 million in interest and principal on Trump's Castle bonds would be paid as part of a massive restructuring of the casino and real estate mogul's empire. If the interest and principal are not made, it would be the first time Mr. Trump has missed a scheduled payment on his approximately $1.3 billion in publicly traded debt.

People close to the negotiations stressed that even if Mr. Trump misses the deadline, he has between 10 and 30 days before he can be found in default.

Four New York City banks have been trying to convince 54 smaller creditors on the merits of a plan that would allow Mr. Trump to receive $60 million and restructure $2 billion in bank debt, including the suspension of interest payments. In return, Mr. Trump would forfeit equity in many of his projects and continue to pay interest to holders of about $1.3 billion in casino debt.

The four banks, Bankers Trust Corp., Chase Manhattan Corp., Citicorp and Manufacturers Hanover Corp., have declined to say whether a scheduled payment on Trump's Castle bonds would be met. It isn't believed that such a payment would be made until the banks strike a deal among themselves.

Over the past week, a number of foreign banks which participated in syndications have refused to go along with the restructuring plan. Also, some New Jersey banks originally balked at the proposal, although they now appear to be amenable to the plan.

A number of bond traders speculated that Mr. Trump's silence and that of the banks may mean bad news. If Mr. Trump and the banks planned to make the payment, they said, they would have put an end to the guessing game and announced that the money would be paid.

Roger Stone, a political consultant who also works for Mr. Trump, reportedly said on a television show taped yesterday that the payment would be made. But Mr. Trump's spokesman, Howard Rubenstein, said: "{Mr. Trump's} position is that Roger Stone is totally uninvolved with our financial activities and it was his own opinion and should not be construed in any way other than that."

Mr. Rubenstein declined to say whether Mr. Trump would make the payment today. Another person familiar with the situation said the trustee for the bondholders was told to be ready to receive a payment today.

Mr. Stone couldn't be reached for comment.
