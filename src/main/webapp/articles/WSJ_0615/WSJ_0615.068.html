Law: Mulheren Admits That He Agreed to Pay $790,000 He Didn't Owe to Boesky Firm

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 06/15/90

NEW YORK -- John A. Mulheren Jr. admitted that he agreed to pay Ivan F. Boesky's company $790,000 even though he had no legal obligation to make the payments, but contended it wasn't part of any illicit arrangement. Ending two days of testimony in his own defense, Mr. Mulheren said he agreed to pay Mr. Boesky the money by inflating brokerage bills that Mr. Mulheren's former company, Jamie Securities Co., paid to Mr. Boesky's trading company in late 1985. Mr. Mulheren said he agreed to give Mr. Boesky's company, Seemala Corp., the money "only because they beat me up and harassed me over the telephone." He was referring to Mr. Boesky, the convicted former stock speculator, and Michael Davidoff, a key aide to Mr. Boesky.

Mr. Mulheren is charged with 41 counts of conspiracy, securities and mail fraud and keeping false records. The government has alleged that Mr. Mulheren conducted numerous illegal securities trades with Mr. Boesky's company, and that Mr. Mulheren proposed a bogus scheme to compensate Mr. Boesky for profits on those trades. The scheme allegedly called for inflating Jamie's brokerage fees to Mr. Boesky's trading company in late 1985.

But Mr. Mulheren said he agreed to pay Mr. Boesky's firm the money because Mr. Boesky had often done him "favors." He said, "I increased the bills" to Seemala "to get them back a favor," because Mr. Boesky had sold him stocks that made Mr. Mulheren's firm a lot of money. Mr. Mulheren, who ran a limited partnership, said he didn't tell any of his investors that he was paying the $790,000 to Mr. Boesky. Mr. Mulheren said he often inflated brokerage bills paid to others who had also done him favors. "I've been marking up bills for years," he said.

Clad in blue jeans and a turquoise sport shirt, Mr. Mulheren was often smug and surly when responding to questions put to him by Assistant U.S. Attorney Alfred Pavlis, a prosecutor in the case. "You're a very wealthy man, aren't you, Mulheren?" Mr. Pavlis asked. "No, the lawyers are now," Mr. Mulheren quipped.

"Isn't it true that you invited Ivan Boesky to your wedding anniversary" in the late 1970s? Mr. Pavlis asked. "Which one, there was one every year," Mr. Mulheren responded.

Mr. Boesky's cooperation with the government led to a sweeping insider-trading investigation and to the government's case against the 40-year-old Mr. Mulheren. In earlier testimony, Mr. Mulheren had tried to distance himself from Mr. Boesky, painting Mr. Boesky as only a casual acquaintance.

But yesterday, under cross-examination, Mr. Mulheren acknowledged that he twice told the Securities and Exchange Commission under oath that he spoke to Mr. Boesky "twenty times a day, every day of the year." Mr. Mulheren called his prior testimony "a gross exaggeration," but added, "It's not a lie."

Mr. Mulheren said Mr. Boesky had shared "sensitive and confidential" information with him. He also acknowledged that he had once hosted a charity dinner on Mr. Boesky's behalf, and that Mr. Boesky had given a donation to Mr. Mulheren's alma mater, Roanoke College, in Mr. Mulheren's name.

Three government witnesses, including Mr. Boesky, have previously testified that Mr. Mulheren conducted a series of trades that were designed to generate tax losses for Mr. Boesky's trading firm and to help Mr. Boesky evade net capital requirements. But under intense questioning by Mr. Pavlis, Mr. Mulheren insisted he never did anything illegal and never understood that profits and losses from the trades were to be borne by Mr. Boesky's company, as the witnesses had said.
