Brady Says S&L Bailout Cost Expected To Increase by as Much as $50 Billion

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 06/15/90

WASHINGTON -- Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady, saying that a further $30 billion to $50 billion will be needed for the thrift bailout next year, encountered escalating criticism from Democrats who think the Bush administration is mishandling the cleanup. Mr. Brady, who recently estimated that the cost of the bailout -- excluding interest -- probably will more than double to over $130 billion, also said that without new cash, the bailout effort will be out of money by the end of this year. He didn't say how much additional funding will be needed in the October-December quarter.

Depressed real estate markets, higher interest rates, thrifts' losses in the junk-bond market and a tepid response from potential buyers of sick thrifts all have contributed to the higher costs, Mr. Brady said. "Let's face it, and get on with the job," he urged the committee.

But Democrats took the occasion to draw a distinction between how the thrift crisis developed, for which both parties appear culpable, and the halting progress in carrying out the massive thrift law, for which the lawmakers held the administration responsible. Their comments were the latest Democratic effort to capitalize on a growing problem for President Bush.

"There's a second S&L crisis that started Aug. 9," when the law passed, said Rep. Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.). "We have heard more from the president about broccoli than we have heard about this crisis." Rep. Richard Neal (D., Mass.) said: "We are sitting on a volcano of public opinion on this issue."

Members admonished the administration for its original, low cost estimates, saying that interest-rate projections were particularly low. They also complained that the administration should have foreseen that there would be few buyers for thrifts under the new law's restrictions on their activities. "I think the appearance to the American public is that this is floundering," said Rep. Bruce Vento (D., Minn.), who chairs a task force on the efforts.

Originally, the administration targeted $50 billion to cover current S&L losses. Yesterday, Mr. Brady said about $28.3 billion will have been spent by June 30. At that point, the administration expects 289 insolvent thrifts to have been liquidated or sold, but their bad investments will remain in the hands of the government. Moreover, about 800 more thrifts are expected to require similar action.

Mr. Brady didn't specifically ask for Congress to pass legislation to raise the additional cash, saying this is likely to be determined in the current budget meetings between the administration and Congress.

For its part, the administration used the hearing to call again for a cut in the tax on capital gains, as a way to bolster the real estate market. "At the margin, I think it's the single most important thing we can do," said Housing and Urban Development Secretary Jack Kemp, who is a member of the Oversight Board monitoring the thrift cleanup.

He also said that a clampdown on credit has made it tougher to sell the repossessed real estate. "I have the feeling that there is a strong impression among people involved in real estate and housing that there is a crunch in the supply of credit," said Mr. Kemp.

Mr. Kemp also cautioned that politicizing the S&L mess could slow the progress even more. "If we turn this into an issue of who and when and just exactly which party, I think it's going to be a serious blow to the necessity of finding some area of cooperation," he said.

Both parties are pursuing S&L fraud legislation more aggressively. Sen. Joseph Biden (D., Del.), chairman of the Senate Judiciary Committee, announced he will hold a hearing next month to examine many of the proposals to crack down on fraud, and said he will introduce a comprehensive S&L fraud bill after the hearing. House Banking Committee Chairman Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas) and the committee's ranking Republican, Ohio Rep. Chalmers Wylie are also introducing a bill to speed S&L-related prosecution.
