Politics & Policy: Senate Panel Votes to End Covert Aid To Non-Communist Forces in Cambodia

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 06/29/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Intelligence Committee voted to cut off funding for a program of U.S. covert assistance to the non-communist forces in Cambodia, according to individuals familiar with the panel's actions. Committee Chairman David Boren (D., Okla.) declined to comment on the committee's deliberation. But separately, those individuals said it reflected increased concern about the direction of U.S. policy in Cambodia as well as a desire to invite more public debate on the issue.

The decision follows a House vote Wednesday to continue an overt program of non-lethal assistance to the forces. In recent years, intelligence committees in both chambers have expressed growing concern over the wisdom of using covert operations to sustain secret aid programs long after their existence is known publicly.

In the past decade, the Central Intelligence Agency has been used to provide covert U.S. aid to forces fighting in regional conflicts in Angola, Afghanistan and Nicaragua, for example, and critics argue that the result is often the suppression of debate on Washington's policy.

In the case of Cambodia, the continuing assistance from the U.S. has drawn criticism because of the brutal history of the Khmer Rouge when it took over Cambodia in the 1970s. Although restrictions prohibit any assistance to the Khmer Rouge, the forces backed by the U.S. are part of a political coalition with the Pol Pot guerrillas against the government installed by the Vietnamese.

This sensitivity was underscored in the House debate this week. To win support for the $7 million in overt aid, proponents specified that all assistance would be nonlethal, and new restrictions were imposed in response to criticism that the insurgents are mounting operations in cooperation with the Khmer Rouge.

Intelligence sources said the non-communist forces are less military than political, and proponents of the program argue that the U.S. therefore has an interest in sustaining the forces as an alternative between the Khmer Rouge and the Hanoi-backed government. By the same token, this goal might be better accomplished through overt assistance, and the House debate this week indicated that intelligence members there appeared more comfortable with an open program than the one under their jurisdiction.

The covert aid, believed to have begun in the early 1980s, is said to have included logistical support and military training. For a time, the program was operated through the government of Thailand.

The Senate committee action came as part of closed-door deliberations on the fiscal 1991 authorization bill covering the CIA's budget. A House panel isn't expected to begin work on its version of the bill until after the July Fourth recess.
