World Stock Markets: Reality Sets In, Ending Joy Ride In German Issues

  • Author: Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 06/29/90

FRANKFURT, West Germany -- "After two, three or four years, German reunification will come to a good end," says Wolfgang Seidel, who manages investments totaling 5.7 billion West German marks ($3.43 billion) for Frankfurt-Trust. "But in the meantime . . . ." Mr. Seidel makes a wrenching motion with his hands, the way one would strangle a chicken -- or a stock market.

For West Germany's formerly high-flying stock market, the wrenching has already begun. The joy ride fueled by the collapse of the Berlin Wall in November is screeching to a halt as sobering realities set in about the costs and uncertainties of unifying the two Germanys.

With the first major step toward unity set for Sunday, when the two economies are joined, there are few near-term bulls in Frankfurt. Many money managers and analysts expect that at best the market will drift sideways at least through the end of the year, when the economic picture may become clearer.

The market, sensing that a business miracle was at hand, surged when the Wall fell. The DAX Index of the Frankfurt Stock Exchange, largest of West Germany's eight exchanges, rocketed more than 30% in the winter to a record 1968.55 on March 30. Buyers from the U.S., Japan and Britain provided the steam: Net foreign purchases of German equities totaled 17.56 billion marks in the fourth quarter, eclipsing the record for an entire year -- 15.17 billion marks in 1986.

But the foreign buying spree clearly is over, money managers say. Net purchases by foreigners dropped to 7.46 billion marks in the first quarter, and when government figures for April are disclosed soon, it's expected they will show that foreigners, who historically have dominated the West German market, became net sellers. Yesterday, despite a recent low-volume technical rally by domestic buyers, the DAX stood at 1895.17, down 3.7% from the record.

"A few months ago people were saying, `Wow] Things are really going to change,'" says Thomas R. Holmes, institutional sales manager for Schroder Munchmeyer Hengst & Co., a money management arm of Lloyds Bank Group that caters mainly to U.S. and British investors. "But now they're coming to realize that Rome wasn't built in a day, and that reunification isn't going to be free of charge."

Mr. Holmes says he is advising clients, "If you look for any concrete results near term, you'll be fooling yourselves. It doesn't make much sense to overweight the German market in portfolios -- there aren't that many bargains." He suggests that West German equities make up 6% of portfolios of non-U.S. investments.

And he warns that the market is likely to be thin and volatile in coming months. Dealer inventories are low because high interest rates have pushed up the cost of carrying securities, Mr. Holmes says.

"It would be wrong to be bullish {in the} medium term and to advise foreigners to get into the West German market," says Jorg Schreiweis, head of equity research at Deutsche Bank. "I think 1720 is a fair value for the market -- that would be a buying opportunity."

Rolf Conen, Deutsche Bank's director of quantitative research, says stocks currently should make up 30% of a German investment portfolio, down from 75% Jan. 1. About half the portfolio should be in cash, double the recommended level seven months ago, he says, and the rest in bonds.

At Frankfurt-Trust, a 180 million mark fund for German equities is 27% in cash, up from 20% at year's end. "You can see our cautious attitude," says Mr. Seidel.

Concerns about the cost of unification center on the heavy borrowing that West Germany will have to undertake to prop up East Germany's infrastructure and social services. The risk is that the borrowing will drive up already high interest rates, or at least keep them around 9%.

That would be more bad news for stocks. Interest rates took off this year in anticipation of such borrowing, helping to cool the stock market boom. "Why should I invest in equities when I'm getting 9% in the bond market?" asks Peter G. Schwarz, head of Merrill Lynch & Co.'s German capital markets operations.

Prospective government borrowings to support East Germany already total about 125 billion marks into the mid-1990s. There also are other programs that could result in borrowings of as much as 217 billion marks more.

Some people are concerned that even more borrowings might be at hand. The West German postal and telegraph system's recent announcement that it intends to borrow 30 billion marks for improvements in the East "was disturbing to equity investors," says Merrill Lynch's Mr. Schwarz. "The investors are asking, `What else?' It isn't measurable what we're going to need."

Other factors also threaten stocks. Corporate earnings are beginning to weaken, influenced partly by the flagging U.S. and British economies. At Schroder Munchmeyer, analysts have reduced 1990 earnings projections for 28% of the 70 West German companies they follow and have raised only 12%.

And a big rush by corporations to sell new stock and rights into the rising market in late 1989 and early 1990 forebodes a dilution of per-share earnings. New issues from October to March soared to almost 17 billion marks, about equal to the total of the previous six quarters.

While flagship stocks such as Deutsche Bank, Volkswagen, Allianz and Siemens remain in many portfolios, retailing stocks have been a major casualty. Once expected to benefit handsomely from a perceived East German demand for Western consumer goods, companies such as Karstadt, Kaufhof and Horten are being dropped from portfolios and downgraded by analysts.

Says Peter Pietsch, a Commerzbank economist: "Many East Germans are going to be quite cautious in spending money. They know they may lose their jobs. Some plan to open their own businesses and are going to save money for that. I'm not convinced that you're going to see a big rush for Western goods."

Meanwhile, Tokyo stocks retreated Thursday on moderate profit-taking and arbitrage selling, traders said.

The Nikkei index of 225 issues fell 206.56 points to 32106.19. Volume on the first section was estimated at 450 million shares, down from 630 million shares Wednesday. Losers outnumbered winners 517-440, while 167 issues were unchanged.

At midmorning in Tokyo Friday, the Nikkei index was up 65.79 points to 32171.98.

On Thursday, the Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section dropped 12 points, or 0.51%, to 2350.40.

The Second Section Index rose 22.85 points, or 0.55%, to 4178.62. Volume in the section was estimated at 25 million shares, down from 29.9 million shares.

Traders said stocks opened weaker, pressured by small amounts of arbitrage selling. Prices were further weighed down by an expected amount of profit-taking midmorning, as the Nikkei had gained more than 1200 points in the previous two trading days, the traders said.

But large brokerage houses tried to give the market some support, buying some high-technology issues.

New money from investment trust funds, along with some dealer buying, also joined brokerage-house support for quality issues, but the broader market remained soft, traders said.

High-tech shares mostly gained. Advantest rose 110 yen (71 cents) to 6,400 yen ($41.45) a share. Sony added 100 to 8,970, and TDK rose 130 to 7,450. Kyocera rose 120 to 8,750.

Declining issues included Mitsubishi Trust, down 120 to 1,910, Seven-Eleven, down 220 to 7,560, and Shintom, down 200 to 5,800 yen.

In London, stocks ended a lackluster session lower, as a spate of recent profit warnings and poor corporate reports blew away some of the market's gusto. Dealers said volume was thin as investors awaited today's expiration of the near-month Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 futures contract.

The FT-SE 100 Share Index fell 17.8 points to 2355.7, the intraday low.

The Financial Times 30-Share Index fell 14.8 points to 1887.8. Volume was 448.1 million shares, up from 389 million Wednesday.

Sentiment was damaged generally and in the stores sector specifically, dealers said, by a profit warning late Wednesday from retailer Burton Group. Poor results from BPB Industries, a maker of plasterboard and building products, hurt the house builders and construction sectors.

Burton fell 23 pence (40 cents) to 126 pence ($2.20) a share after the announcement of expected weak sales in 1990. Other stocks in the sector dropped, including Marks & Spencer, down 5 to 235, Kingfisher, off 10 to 347, Great Universal Stores, off 25 at #10.29, and Sears, down 4 to 95.5.

BPB Industries closed unchanged at 231 after reporting a 38% slump in fiscal 1990 pretax profit. One dealer said the stock was able to hold steady because it had slumped in recent days in anticipation of the results.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Zurich, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Milan and Madrid, lower in Frankfurt and Brussels and little changed in Paris. South African gold stocks closed higher.

In Asian and Pacific markets, stocks ended lower in Sydney, Singapore, Seoul, Manila and Wellington and were mixed in Hong Kong. Taipei was closed.
