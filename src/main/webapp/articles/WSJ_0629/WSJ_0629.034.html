Technology & Science: Apple Computer, in a Switch, Decides It Will Keep Claris Unit in Company

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 06/29/90

Apple Computer Inc. decided to fold its Claris software unit -- which has spent most of this year laying plans for an initial public offering -- back into the company. The move is a major switch for Apple, which formed Claris three years ago to take over much of its personal-computer software business. At the time, John Sculley, Apple's chief executive officer, explained that Apple needed to distance itself from the business of selling software applications so as not to anger independent companies in that business. Ever since, Mr. Sculley has cited Claris as an example of what he describes as a "federation" of companies all supporting the Apple "mothership."

According to industry executives familiar with the situation, Apple changed its mind because it believes it needs more control over software applications for its computers at a time when some of the most creative independent software companies are beginning to spend more resources on producing applications for competitors' computers.

Spokesmen for both Apple and Claris declined to comment, but industry executives said Apple was ready to announce the move as early as today.

"It's a very sensible decision in my opinion," said Charles Wolf, an analyst at First Boston Corp. "The entire premise for setting up Claris no longer holds."

Mr. Wolf said that unlike several years ago, when Apple's flagship Macintosh computer had an insufficient amount of software, there is plenty available for the machine today. Apple now faces a situation where the outside firms that write software for the Macintosh have been so successful that they have begun writing software for machines based on arch-rival International Business Machines Corp.'s personal computers.

Indeed, even executives at Claris conceded that once the firm was independent from Apple, which was scheduled to happen later this year, it too would begin writing software for personal computers made by Apple competitors. While Claris has posted yearly revenue about $100 million from selling software from Apple computers, investment bankers, handling the subsidiary's stock offering, insisted that Claris broaden its business to flourish outside of Apple's umbrella.

According to people familiar with the situation, Michael Spindler, who was named Apple's chief operating officer in January, would not brook such a situation. He is said to believe that Apple must have some internal capacity to produce applications software that takes advantage of what the company considers the unique features of its Macintosh computers.

Bringing Claris, which is the second biggest supplier of Apple software after Microsoft Corp., back into Apple could upset some independent software developers who are wary about competing directly with Apple in software applications. But the benefits of an internal applications business are worth the criticisms.

"Now Apple has a currency to retain the unique software on the Macintosh," Mr. Wolf said.

The flip-flop on the status of Claris is only one of many organizational and strategic changes occurring at the Cupertino, Calif., computer maker. This week, the company hired a new president of Apple USA, its biggest sales and marketing organization and yesterday it announced the departure of one of its most senior executives in product development.

Donald P. Casey, Apple's vice president of networking and engineering, joined Lotus Development Corp., a Boston-based software company, as its new vice president of the spreadsheet division, in charge of its key revenue source.

Mr. Casey, 44 years old, said that the Lotus job "is a great opportunity." He said he wasn't leaving Apple in a dispute over strategy, but declined to comment on Apple's outlook.

Most of the changes are related to decisions made by Mr. Spindler, who ran Apple's fast-growing European operations before coming to headquarters to revive the company's domestic marketing and streamline its field organization.

On the product side, meanwhile, Apple also has made an about-face. In recent years the company has introduced a series of increasingly expensive computers. While successful, these products have sharply narrowed the market for Apple's machines, leading analysts to complain that the company is no longer catering to the mass market.

Mr. Sculley has made it plain that he intends to fix that and since taking over direct responsibility for Apple's product development in late January has been spending most of his time trying to rush into creation two lower-priced Macintosh machines.

While outsiders question the wisdom of Mr. Sculley's direct involvement in product planning -- the Apple chief is a marketing man and has no formal training in engineering -- executives inside Apple's product development groups say he is getting results.

"Apple's playing a different game now, and frankly, that's very refreshing," said Lawrence Tesler, vice president for advanced products at Apple. Mr. Tesler approves of Mr. Sculley's emphasis on less-expensive models because he believes it will result in a broader following among computer users.
