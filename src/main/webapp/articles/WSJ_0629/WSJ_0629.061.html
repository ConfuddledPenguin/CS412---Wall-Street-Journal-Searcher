McDonald's Ads On Mini-Billboards Irk Some Chicagoans --- Promotions Placed on Boxes For the Chicago Observer Are Assailed as `Eyesores'

  • Author: Andrew Patner
  • Date: 06/29/90

CHICAGO -- A McDonald's Corp. effort to promote its new cheddar cheese hamburger may be facing a meltdown in Chicago. McDonald's placed hundreds of 3-foot-by-4-foot mini-billboards throughout Chicago's downtown and its trendy North Side on boxes for the Chicago Observer, a new monthly newspaper that appears to be a vehicle for outdoor-advertising concerns.

The signs, featuring the new Cheddar Melt sandwich, invite passers-by hungry "For All the Juicy Details" to "Inquire Inside" where cubbyholes hold copies of the 12-page newspaper. While few people are talking about the paper, which features an interview with Sean Connery and several pages of society photographs, the 400 boxes have captured the attention -- and the wrath -- of city officials. Some of the boxes don't carry the McDonald's ads.

Aldermen, storefront businessmen and civic groups have denounced the boxes as "eyesores" and "sneak attacks" on city restrictions on billboards, sandwich boards and bench advertising. The signs weren't up three days before Mayor Richard M. Daley introduced an ordinance in the City Council to restrict newsracks to 26 inches by 40 inches and to ban all advertising from the racks.

Gary Weintraub, attorney for the Chicago Observer, says the controversy is "a First Amendment issue involving a free press and free speech." He says: "There were no ordinances on the books when our boxes were put up. This is a fundamental constitutional issue."

Mr. Weintraub says the Observer is no different from any other newspaper except for "a policy to feature our advertisers on our boxes." But Rick Johnson, marketing supervisor for McDonald's Chicago region, says the food-service concern bought the poster campaign as "a package." He says the Oak Brook, Ill., company didn't anticipate the uproar and usually steers clear of offending "public perceptions" in its ad campaigns.

Cindy Mitchell, president of Friends of the Parks, a watchdog group, says the paper is "trash" and its boxes are "aesthetic nightmares." But for Mr. Weintraub, "One man's eyesore is another man's jewel."

The paper's editor, M. Michael Petrovich, and its investors, Robert Hoelterhoff and Robert and Terry Durkin, all involved in outdoor-advertising concerns, couldn't be reached for comment.
