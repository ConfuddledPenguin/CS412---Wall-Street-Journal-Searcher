Politics & Policy: Tanker Firms Are Dealt Blow By Conferees --- Group Rejects Provisions In Oil-Spill Legislation Aimed at Liability Curb

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 06/29/90

WASHINGTON -- House and Senate conferees removed a major hurdle to passage of oil-spill legislation by rejecting provisions that would have provided for U.S. participation in an international treaty limiting shippers' liability. Rejection of the House language favoring the protocols, which were strongly opposed by Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell of Maine, came as a big disappointment to the tanker industry and many oil companies. Some major companies, including Royal Dutch/Shell Group and A.P. Moeller, already have curtailed shipments to U.S. ports on the ground that the potential liability for spills is too great in the absence of limits.

The Senate conferees and several environmental groups, however, adamantly oppose the treaty, which they said would pre-empt tough state laws and allow polluters to avoid paying for all the damage they caused. Sen. Mitchell said in an interview that "the action today was significant and, I hope, now clears the way for swift approval of the legislation." He predicted that the conference could complete action on the bill at its next session shortly after the Independence Day recess.

In a statement, Transportation Secretary Samuel Skinner called the action "a wrong decision for the wrong reasons" and said the Bush administration will try to find "legislative solutions to minimize the damage done by the conferees today." Supporters of the treaty have argued that it would provide the predictable legal framework needed to keep responsible tanker operators coming to U.S. ports, thereby ensuring a safe and adequate supply of oil.

The conferees approved a resolution expressing the sense of the Congress that the U.S. should "participate in an international oil pollution liability and compensation regime that is at least as effective as federal and state laws in preventing incidents and in guaranteeing full and prompt compensation for damages resulting from incidents."

Remaining issues before the conferees include whether to require double hulls on oil tankers. The two houses have narrowed their difference on this matter substantially.

Among other provisions, the bill would raise federal liability limits for tankers, barges and oil facilities, although states could still enforce their own stricter liability laws. Federal compensation, cleanup and damage-assessment costs beyond the liability limits would be paid out of a new $1 billion fund financed by a five-cent-a-barrel tax on domestic and imported oil.

The fee, collected since Jan. 1, could be tapped only if the accident doesn't involve gross or willful negligence. The legislation would also improve contingency planning for spills.

In debating the protocol question, Senate conferees rejected a House offer that would have provided for conditional participation in the treaty. The U.S. would have agreed to sign on for five years while working to improve the accords. If acceptable language couldn't be worked out, the U.S. would renounce participation.

Among other complaints, Senate conferees said such a strategy would undermine the constitutional role of the Senate in ratifying treaties.
