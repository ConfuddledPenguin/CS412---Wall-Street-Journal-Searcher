Credit Markets: Treasurys Are Lifted by Thrift-Bailout Agency's Plan to Sell 30-Year, Instead of 40-Year Bonds

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 06/29/90

NEW YORK -- News that the government's thrift bailout agency will soon auction 30-year bonds, rather than the oddball 40-year variety, provided a late-day boost to the bond market. Bond prices opened as much as 3/8 point higher -- a gain of $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount -- and stuck at those levels through most of the day as investors continued to cheer Wednesday's successful four-year note auction. Optimism that efforts are underway to cut the size of the budget deficit also helped the market.

When the Treasury Department announced that Resolution Funding Corp. will auction 30-year "bailout bonds" on July 10, trading activity perked up a bit.

The Treasury's 30-year benchmark bond ended nearly 1/2 point higher while the issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, eased to 8.44%.

Refcorp is the federal agency that raises money to finance the government's massive bailout of the troubled savings and loan industry. Details of the auction, including the size of the offering, will be announced July 3.

The next auction will be Refcorp's fourth and will mark the agency's return to issuing 30-year bonds after selling 40-year bonds in January and April. Although the 40-year bond auctions were considered flops at the time of the sale, demand for the securities picked up later when the securities were "stripped." Strips are created when dealers separate the interest-paying "coupon" portion of a bond from the principle or "corpus" portion and sell the two parts separately. Strips are also divided into different maturities.

John Costas, co-manager of government trading at First Boston Corp., said the Refcorp news helped the market because it relieved some worries about possible supply pressures. Thirty-year bonds, which have broad investor appeal, are easier to sell than forty-year bonds.

Mr. Costas said that another 40-year bond offering might have saturated the market with "interest only" 40-year strips, which are not very popular. But, he said, demand for the "principal" portion, often called a zero-coupon bond, has been strong.

Government officials said the decision to issue 30-year, rather than 40-year, bonds largely reflects the government's need for large amounts of cash. And because 30-year bonds are easier to sell than 40-year bonds, the Treasury said it can raise the size of the auctions without disrupting the market.

"We have to raise more money than we did at the last auction" because the thrift industry bailout process is speeding up, said Michael Basham, deputy assistant secretary of federal finance at the U.S. Treasury.

"If we have to raise more money, I felt we would be better off selling a 30-year bond," said Mr. Basham.

Treasury officials have indicated the size of the sale will be between $4 billion and $6 billion, up from the $3.5 billion of 40-year "bailout" bonds sold at the April auction.

Mr. Basham said Wall Street also seemed to favor a 30-year bond this time around.

"At the last auction, the investment community had a strong preference for a 40-year bond, but we didn't get a strong response {for a 40-year issue} from the investment community this time," he said.

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at price of 103 8/32 to yield 8.44% compared with 102 26/32 to yield 8.48% on Wednesday. Ten-year notes ended at 102 23/32 to yield 8.46% compared with 102 11/32 to yield 8.50%.

Meanwhile, the discount rate on threemonth Treasury bills ended at 7.75% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.99% while the rate on six-month notes fell to 7.63% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.02%.

The Treasury sold $10.26 billion of 52week bills yesterday at an average discount rate of 7.52%.

Municipal Bonds

Tax-exempt securities ended 1/8 to 3/8 point higher.

Despite a lack of bonds available in the secondary market, traders reported good activity from investors looking to add to slim inventories.

In new-issue dealings, a Lehman Brothers group tentatively priced $60 million Illinois Student Assistance Commission student loan revenue bonds to yield from 6.60% in 1995 to 7.38% in 2003.

Among actively quoted bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 ended 1/4 point higher at 98 1/4 to yield 7.34%.

Foreign Bonds

Canadian bond prices ended modestly lower in dull trading, as profit-taking dented the market's upward momentum of the past few days.

Traders said there was little reaction in the Canadian bond market to news that Canada's gross domestic product was unchanged in April from March or to news that Canadian manufacturers operated at 80.4% of capacity in the first quarter, down from 82% in the fourth quarter.

Canada's benchmark 10-year 10.5% bond due 2000 was quoted late yesterday afternoon at a price of 98.950, down 0.10 percentage point, to yield 10.67%.

In other markets, British government bonds ended slightly firmer as the pound rebounded against some other major currencies. Britain's 11.75% benchmark bond due 2003/2007 was up 2/32 at 102 28/32 to yield 11.32%.

West German government bonds ended slightly higher in directionless trading. Dealers say the market remains trapped in a narrow range. Bonn's 10-year benchmark 8.75% bond was up 0.15 point to 100.20 to yield 8.71%.

Junk Bonds

Robust gains in the junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. led the junk-bond market higher, sparking buying interest in some of the market's better quality "Go-Go" issues.

The active buying was triggered by reports that RJR Nabisco is proposing to reset the interest rate on $2.6 billion of pay-in-kind bonds at 16.5%. Pay-in-kind bonds pay interest in the form of additional securities as opposed to cash.

RJR Nabisco's 14.07% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, finished up 2 1/4 points at 103 on the New York Stock Exchange. The 14.07% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, ended 1 3/8 points higher at 84 1/8.

Most actively traded junk bonds posted gains of about 3 3/8 point. Some notable exceptions, however, were Quantum Chemical Corp. and Carter Hawley Hale Stores Inc.

Carter Hawley Hale's 12.5% senior subordinated debentures ended down 3 5/8 points at 79 7/8 on the New York Stock Exchange while Quantum's 12.5% senior subordinated notes ended 1/2 point lower at 88.

The bonds of Interco Inc. slipped on concern that the furniture maker will skip upcoming interest payments on its 8 7/8% notes. The notes fell one point to 63.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities maintained a modestly firm tone.

Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities for July delivery finished up 7/32 at 99 6/32 to yield 9.75%.

In another sector, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities closed up 7/32 at 98 18/32 to yield 9.73%.

Traders weren't expecting the session's gains to be much larger, saying that hesitation to build positions as the quarter ends and as the July Fourth holiday and June employment report approach would likely take the edge off any rally.

In the primary market, a $500 million Freddie Mac five-year noncallable debenture issue, being sold by Freddie Mac's 18-member securities dealer group, was priced as 8.70% securities at par.
