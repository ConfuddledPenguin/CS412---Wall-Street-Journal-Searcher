International: Venezuela Reaches Debt Settlement With Major Banks

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 06/29/90

WASHINGTON -- Venezuela and its major bank creditors agreed on a debt settlement that includes provisions for new loans and for cutting back old credits and interest payments due on them. The settlement, which covers an estimated $20 billion of medium-term and long-term foreign commercial bank debt, is weighted more toward providing the country with new credits than a recent similar bank-debt settlement with Mexico, the first under the revamped U.S. debt strategy.

The agreement will be explained to Venezuela's smaller bank creditors at meetings to be held in international financial centers over the next several weeks.

Under the agreement, negotiated in New York over the past few months, the country's bank creditors will have four options.

First, to help provide Venezuela with new credits, banks can purchase Venezuelan bonds to be issued over the next two years. If they purchase these new money bonds, banks, as a sweetener, will be able to exchange old loans for debt-conversion bonds at a ratio of five such bonds for each new money bond they buy. Banks often prefer to hold bonds rather than loans because they are more easily traded. Moreover, these conversion bonds will be eligible to be exchanged for equity investments in Venezuela.

The second option will allow bank creditors to exchange their old loans for short-term collateralized notes at a 55% discount. These 91-day notes will be collateralized by U.S. Treasury bills. But if they use this option, banks will receive only 45 cents for each dollar face value of debt, a rate roughly equal to the secondary-market price for Venezuelan government foreign-bank debt. The Venezuelans will accept as much as $5.5 billion of loans under this option.

The third option will allow banks to exchange loans for 30-year bonds that have their principal fully secured at maturity by zero-coupon bonds. These new bonds either carry fixed interest rates of 6.75% or represent a 30% discount to the face value of the loan tendered.

The fourth option will allow banks to swap credits for 17-year bonds that have below-market interest rates for the first five years of their life. These bonds will be backed by a fund that guarantees 12 months of interest payments for their first five years of life.
