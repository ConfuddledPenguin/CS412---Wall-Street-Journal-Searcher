Politics & Policy: S&L Interests' Political Contributions Topped $11 Million in '80s, Study Shows

  • Author: Jill Abramson
  • Date: 06/29/90

WASHINGTON -- During the 1980s, when the savings-and-loan crisis festered and finally exploded, S&L interests contributed more than $11 million to congressional candidates. According to an analysis of Federal Election Commission records by Common Cause, the top recipient of political contributions from S&L executives, their families and political action committees associated with thrift institutions was Sen. Pete Wilson (R., Calif.), who took in $243,334 from 1981-1990.

Also ranking in the top five are three senators -- Donald Riegle (D., Mich.); Alan Cranston (D., Calif.); and Alfonse D'Amato (R., N.Y.) -- who face ethics investigations into their ties to campaign contributors, including those in the S&L industry.

The release of the study comes amid a bitterly partisan political debate over who is to blame for the S&L crisis. The executive committee of the Democratic National Committee, meeting in Portland, Ore., is expected to pass a resolution tomorrow demanding that President Bush clean up "the Republican-managed S&L mess." Republicans, meanwhile, are encouraging their party's congressional candidates to use the S&L issue to attack Democratic incumbents for mishandling thrift legislation.

PACs for the financial-services industry, which includes S&Ls, have been the largest corporate contributors to congressional campaigns in recent years. In 1983-1990, financial-services PACs gave nearly $22 million to candidates for Congress.

Common Cause is using the S&L issue to pressure lawmakers into passing legislation to overhaul campaign financing. "We've had a $300 billion scandal and no one has done anything to reform the corrupt system that let it happen," said Fred Wertheimer, president of the self-styled citizens lobby. During the July 4 congressional recess, Common Cause will unveil S&L-focused newspaper advertisements criticizing specific lawmakers in eight states and a dozen congressional districts.

Congressional Democrats, who control both chambers, received a larger portion of the S&L campaign bounty, according to the Common Cause study. Among the top 25 recipients of S&L-related contributions in the Senate and House, Democrats took in $1.8 million while Republicans received $1.4 million.

The study also shows that President Bush's 1988 campaign benefited from $600,000 in "soft money" (large contributions from wealthy individuals to political parties that are made outside of the federal election law limits) from executives in the S&L industry. That includes $100,000 from Charles Keating, who is chairman of the former parent of Lincoln Savings & Loan Association of Irvine, Calif., a once-highflying thrift now in federal receivership.

Several failed or deeply troubled thrifts were among the biggest sources of campaign contributions, including Lincoln; Western Savings & Loan Association, Phoenix, Ariz., and Vernon Savings & Loan Association, Dallas, whose former owner was indicted this month.

The leading individual contributor among S&L interests was Carl Lindner Jr., whose Ohio family contributed $828,920 to congressional candidates. The Lindner family founded and controls American Financial Corp., which owns Hunter Savings Association. Mr. Lindner and Mr. Keating were once business associates.

The top recipients of S&L-related contributions from January 1981 through April 1990 were: Sen. Wilson, $243,334; Sen. Riegle, $200,900; Sen. Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas), $154,207; Sen. Cranston, $143,700; and Sen. D'Amato, $88,235.
