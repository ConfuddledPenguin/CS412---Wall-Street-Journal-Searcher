Danish Shipping Company Ending U.S. Oil Shipments

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/29/90

A.P. Moeller, a big Danish shipping company, said it would no longer carry crude oil to the U.S. because the potential liabilities for spills have become "unbearable." The decision by Moeller, which operates the Maersk shipping line and owns one of the world's largest independent oil-tanker fleets, is expected to increase pressure on Congress to ratify international protocols that would limit the liability of a shipowner involved in an oil spill.

Disagreement over whether the U.S. should participate in the protocols is a major factor holding up congressional approval of oil spill legislation.

Some industry officials have warned that unlimited liability could drive away the more responsible shipping lines, leaving less-scrupulous owners to deliver crude to the U.S. and thus increasing the danger of spills.

Moeller's statement came two weeks after Royal Dutch/Shell Group said it would restrict U.S. oil shipments in vessels it owns or manages because of the liability problem. Societe Nationale Elf Aquitaine, the French state-owned oil company which owns just one tanker, said recently that it would stop using the ship to carry crude to the U.S. And a spokesman for Petrofina S.A. said the Belgian oil company was "seriously" considering such a halt for its fleet.

The shipowners' concern grew in the wake of last year's Exxon Valdez accident off the coast of Alaska and executives say that their legal liabilities are unlimited in some U.S. states and that the risk is uninsurable.
