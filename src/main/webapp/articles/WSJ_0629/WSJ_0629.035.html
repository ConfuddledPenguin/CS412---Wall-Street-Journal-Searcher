Princeton economist William Baumol at a Manhattan Institute seminar on May 14:

I see nothing frightening about foreign direct investment in America. As a matter of fact, almost every country during its period of takeoff has had plenty of investment from abroad, in the form of foreigners buying plants and taking over businesses. Historically, if you go back as far as the middle ages, there were in succession three clear leaders before the United States: Italy, Holland, and Great Britain. In the century before Italy lost its leadership, opportunities for investment in Italy didn't look too good and so the Italian bankers sent their money abroad. No one was spending money in Italy. They lent it to the King of Spain (who would eventually go bankrupt) and to the King of England and other useful enterprises like that. When the Dutch took over, in the century before their decline, people stopped investing in Holland, including the Dutch, and started to invest in England, because a country in decline is not a good place to invest in. Then when the British reached what is called their apex, they also started to send their capital abroad. In 1913, half of British savings were exported. So it is certainly not a sign of decline if the rest of the world is willing to send money to the United States.
