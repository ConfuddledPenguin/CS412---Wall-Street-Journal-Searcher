Top Baptist Press Officials Are Under Pressure to Resign --- News Agency Director, Editor Say Fundamentalists Seek Control Over Information

  • Author: R. Gustav Niebuhr
  • Date: 06/29/90

The top officers of Baptist Press, a prominent religious news service used by nearly 500 newspapers and magazines nationwide, are being forced out of their jobs, apparently as a result of a bitter factional fight within the Southern Baptist Convention, which owns the news service. Al Shackleford, 58 years old, director of Baptist Press, based in Nashville, Tenn., said he and editor Dan Martin, 51, were told to resign by Sept. 30 or they would "be dealt with harshly." Mr. Shackleford said the decision to force them out was made by the denomination's executive committee, which offered no explanation.

Neither Harold Bennett, the executive committee's president, nor Sam Pace, its chairman, could be reached for comment.

Mr. Martin said he believed that fundamentalists, who dominate both the executive committee and the denomination, "want their own minister of information" in charge of Southern Baptist news. Fundamentalists believe the Bible is literally correct in matters of religion, science and history.

Mr. Martin added that the executive committee frequently complained about news stories but "never offered us any direction."

Mr. Shackleford and Mr. Martin said they were told that if they resigned quietly they could receive up to six months' severance pay -- but only if they told the editors of Baptist newspapers across the country not to come to their defense. Mr. Shackleford said he has no plans to resign; Mr. Martin said he hasn't decided what to do.

Founded in 1947, Baptist Press provides about a dozen pages of news stories about the Southern Baptist Convention, the nation's largest Protestant denomination, to 480 newspapers, magazines and other institutions on weekdays. The news service maintains bureaus in Washington, Atlanta, Dallas, Nashville and Richmond, Va. Its stories are reprinted in 39 state Baptist newspapers, whose combined circulation is more than 1.6 million.

For much of the past decade, Baptist Press has been under increasing pressure as a result of turmoil within the Southern Baptist Convention. Fundamentalists, who recently wrested control of the denomination from a more moderate faction, have charged that the news service has aggravated the schism by frequently reporting on the split and that Baptist Press stories have portrayed fundamentalists in a negative light.

In the mid-1980s, the Baptist Press Association asked three journalism professors to examine the news service's standards. The trio issued a report clearing Baptist Press of charges of bias. One of the three, John Demott, a professor at Memphis State University, praised Baptist Press as "a model for denominational news services," but said that it now risks becoming a "propaganda arm" of the fundamentalist faction.
