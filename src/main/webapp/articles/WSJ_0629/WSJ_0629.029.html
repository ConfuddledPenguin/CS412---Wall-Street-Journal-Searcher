Having Poured Out His Wrath, He Offers Up a Bit of the Grape

  • Author: Yumiko Ono
  • Date: 06/29/90

TOKYO -- For years, Lee Iacocca has been known as a Japan basher, rapping the country for "slamming doors in our faces" and lamenting its "almost parasitic" dependence on the U.S. market. But now, the Chrysler Corp. chairman is inviting Japanese consumers to share some of his wine.

Today, Mr. Iacocca starts marketing in Japan wine produced by his vineyard in Italy, promoting it as the perfect accompaniment to "the renowned cuisine of Japan." His two red wines, costing $20 and $45 a bottle, respectively, have Mr. Iacocca's autograph plastered across the label.

Can he be serious? Mr. Iacocca has just toured the U.S. claiming that Chrysler cars stand up to the Japanese competition. In his book "Talking Straight," he states: "I hate to pick on the Japanese -- but they give me such great material to work with, I just can't help myself]" And in a recent Fortune article, for example, he charged that if Sony Corp. Chairman Akio Morita "didn't know any more about electronics than he does about fairness, he'd still be trying to figure out how to put together a crystal radio set." Despite such comments, Mr. Iacocca has recently been denying that he is a Japan basher.

But Mr. Iacocca's wine venture is baffling many Japanese consumers. "I thought he was like, `Hey you Japs,'" says Tatsuo Kurata, a 41-year-old employee at a chemical-appliance company, folding his hand into a fist. "I don't think I'll buy."

"It's interesting how he can separate business from his opinions like that," says 24-year-old Yosuke Kinoshita as he looks over a stack of books on U.S.-Japan trade friction in a Tokyo bookstore.

However, the Japanese importer of the wines hopes Mr. Iacocca's controversial reputation will help sales. "To have that Mr. Iacocca bowing his head to Japanese consumers" will at least spur talk, says A. John Hamaguchi, director of Hiroya Co.

Mr. Hamaguchi says he intends to market the wine to department stores and Italian restaurants. In case anyone fancies them for Japan's summer gift-giving season, wooden boxes are available, with the Iacocca signature stamped on the lid.

Japanese car makers, the main target of Mr. Iacocca's attacks, are watching with amusement to see how it fares. "Perhaps Mr. Iacocca will at last find out for himself that doing business in Japan is not as difficult as he seems to want to tell everyone it is," says a spokesman for Toyota Motor Corp.
