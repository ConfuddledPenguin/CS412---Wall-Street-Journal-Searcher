Insurers to Form Computer Network For Linking Agents

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 06/29/90

A group of insurers and two data-management companies formed a venture to provide a computer network designed to expedite the processing of policies and the flow of pricing information to independent agents. The venture, known as Alliance for Productive Technology, hopes to create a standardized software system to let insurance agents transmit and retrieve information from several companies without having to re-enter policyholder data several times.

But consumer groups and insurance-industry critics expressed skepticism about the likely benefits of the planned system. Moreover, the approach excludes the direct insurance writers, companies such as State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co., Nationwide Insurance Co. and Allstate Insurance Co., a unit of Sears, Roebuck & Co., that have their own sales personnel, rather than working through independent agents around the country.

Industry studies have found that these companies are already more efficient than the agency companies and offer generally better pricing to consumers.

Participating in the network venture are CNA Insurance Cos., Fund America Cos.' Fireman's Fund Insurance unit, Travelers Corp. and New Hampshire Insurance Co., a unit of American International Group Inc. They will provide some of the capital to develop the software link that agents and companies will be using.

The aim is to make both the agents and the insurers more efficient. Automation in the insurance industry remains fragmented, and no standard for linking companies and agents exists.

Agents need to be more productive, said Thomas Eustace, chairman and chief executive officer of Applied Systems Inc., one of the data-management companies involved in the project. He acknowledged that efficiencies envisioned by this software system will be achieved only if many companies use it. The other data-management firm is Agency Management Systems Inc., a Norwell, Mass.-based firm that is jointly owned by CNA, Fireman's Fund and New Hampshire Insurance.

Robert Hunter, head of the National Insurance Consumers Organization, said the need to develop such systems is "an admission that there are some fairly large inefficiencies" in insurance company operations.

He said he agrees that companies could achieve significant savings if their back offices didn't have to re-enter information they get from agents several times, such as pricing, billing and claims processing. However, consumers may not see any immediate benefit from this computer link, he said.

Unlike the airline reservation systems, which gives consumers with a personal computer access to airfare listings and schedules, this system won't be available to the public. Consumers will still have to rely on agents to get pricing information.

"If the consumer isn't given good pricing information, then (this system won't) do the consumer any good," said Mark Cooper, an economist with the Consumer Federation of America.

A variety of data management companies already provide computers and software to automate insurance agents' offices. But some of these systems are incompatible and they force agents to re-enter the same data for such operations as accounting and pricing. Also, many of the major insurers, who work through independent agents, have developed their own systems for their agents to use, often making it time-consuming and cumbersome for agents to deal with more than a few companies.

"Some companies try to compete for agents based on automation they can offer. They ought to compete on the basis of price and service," said Patricia A. Borowski, head of government and industry affairs for the National Association of Professional Insurance Agents.

The insurance industry has been trying to establish a data-communications system between agents and insurance companies for several years now, but success has been minimal. "It's not a technology issue," Ms. Borowski said. "It's a political issue of whether or not (an insurer) wants to have its agents communicate with multiple companies or give them a system to communicate with just one company."

The standardized software being developed by the joint venture will be marketed to all property-casualty companies. Lawrence Bacon, senior vice president for data-processing at Travelers, said the various data-management firms that specialize in agency automation should be able to incorporate this software in the various systems they now sell.
