SEC Moves Ahead On Listing Options In Multiple Markets --- By Kevin G. Salwen and Stanley W. Angrist Staff Reporters of The Wall Street Journal

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/29/90

The Securities and Exchange Commission appears ready to press ahead on a proposal by four exchanges that would allow more than one exchange to list options on a single stock. The plan -- submitted by the Chicago Board Options Exchange and the New York, American and Pacific stock exchanges -- would create electronic links of options trading, allowing options to be bought and sold at the best price on any exchange, much like stocks. The plan is likely to keep the SEC on schedule for its target of January for a new options-trading system.

In January, the SEC ordered the exchanges to come up with a plan by tomorrow to link the markets and allow freer options trading. Currently, most options are allocated by a lottery system to a single exchange and can be traded only through that market. That provides lucrative exclusive franchises for the exchanges.

The SEC has contended that those monopolies don't benefit the consumer. But the exchanges fought the plan for years, arguing that allowing several exchanges to list the same options would reduce liquidity in the market. Moreover, the smaller exchanges worried that linking the markets would put them at a competitive disadvantage.

"We're very encouraged," said SEC Chairman Richard Breeden. "We have concluded that it is unquestionably feasible. The next step now is moving toward implementation."

The four exchanges submitted their plan, called Public Investors Privileged Express, or PIPE, which now appears to be the front-runner. The Philadelphia Stock Exchange, however, offered a different plan, called Auction Intermarket System, or AIMS.

The SEC said the plans are under review. But the next step is likely to be an attempt by agency staffers to bridge the differences between the two plans. If that doesn't work, the agency has statutory authority to approve any proposal -- or write its own. Staffers appear to be leaning toward accepting the PIPE plan. "We see an ability for the type of linkage proposed by the four exchanges," said Richard Ketchum, SEC market regulation director.

The agency would then ask the exchanges to file a joint rule proposal, which would then be released for public comment. After the comment period, the SEC could grant final approval for the plan.

Options give a holder the right, but not the obligation, to trade a security at a predetermined price within a set period.

In January, the SEC permitted any options exchange to trade options on any exchange-traded stock as long as no other market was trading options on the stock and it met certain requirements in net income, number of shareholders and price. More than one exchange can trade stocks options on any over-the-counter stock that meets regulatory rules.

The SEC could decide by tomorrow to phase in another interim step, allowing an exchange to trade options on a maximum of 10 issues that have options trading elsewhere. However, the SEC has set a target of Jan. 21, 1991, to eliminate all exclusive trading privileges.

Some industry observers expect that to lead to exchanges leaving the options business or merging. "There is not enough business to go around for five options exchanges," said Richard Donsky, Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc.'s director of options. "I guarantee that one year after multiple listing is fully adopted there won't be five exchanges."

The PIPE and AIMS proposals are identical on many points but differ in one key area -- time priority. This feature only comes into play when more than one exchange is showing the same price for an option. Under the Philadelphia plan, the order would automatically go to the exchange that had posted that price first. Under the PIPE plan, however, the exchange where the order was first directed would have a chance to improve its quote and fill the order.

Nicholas Giordano, president of the Philadelphia Stock Exchange, says AIMS provides an incentive for market makers to show their best price early. "That's clearly the best for the public investor," he said.

But CBOE President Charles Henry said PIPE will allow brokerage firms to route their orders to a selected exchange. "They will select an exchange based on price, liquidity and the charges imposed by the exchanges for filling the order," he said.

Mr. Giordano said he offered a compromise in a recent letter to the SEC. He said that the Philadelphia exchange would be willing to accept a system that doesn't incorporate time priority as long as the SEC makes clear that the system would be only an evolutionary step toward one that does. In addition, he wants the system to be used to trade newly listed options only. Advocates for both plans say that 18 months will be needed to create an operational system.

Andrew Schwarz, senior partner in a specialist firm on the Amex, said he thinks the SEC will accept the compromise that the Philadelphia exchange has offered because it is the path of least resistance. "Everybody is unhappy and happy with parts of the proposal," he said. "That tells me it is probably the right one."
