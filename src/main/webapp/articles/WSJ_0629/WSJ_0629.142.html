Social Security Is on the Table In Budget Talks --- Cuts or Delays in Increases Are Under Consideration In Effort to Trim Deficit

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 06/29/90

WASHINGTON -- Social Security, the one program that politicians have been afraid to touch for years, is on the table at the bipartisan budget talks. Congressional and administration negotiators are talking increasingly about raising taxes on Social Security benefits. They also are discussing the politically risky option of delaying or otherwise reducing cost-of-living adjustments for Social Security and other government benefit programs.

Negotiators caution that many proposals being discussed eventually will be discarded. "We're going to leave a lot of things on the table," said Rep. Bill Frenzel (R., Minn.).

Nevertheless, participants acknowledge that deliberations over the past two days indicate that negotiators are more willing to consider Social Security cuts than they have been in the past. Social Security accounts for 20% of all federal spending.

The budget talks also focused on the Pentagon budget, with House Budget Committee Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) suggesting that projected defense outlays be cut $16 billion next year. That would leave next year's defense spending about 3% below this year's level. Rep. Panetta's plan would cut projected outlays $275 billion over five years.

The Bush administration, in contrast, proposes reducing projected defense outlays by just $6 billion next year and $109 billion over five years. The Bush plan would push defense expenditures higher than this year's level.

Since President Bush declared Tuesday that tax increases are needed to shrink the deficit, negotiators have moved from partisan posturing to examining specific ways to reduce red ink. House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.) said negotiators will start assembling a deficit-reduction package after Congress returns from its weeklong Fourth of July break.

One deficit-reduction option being considered is to increase taxes on Social Security benefits. Couples earning more than $32,000 a year, and individuals earning more than $25,000, currently pay federal income taxes on 50% of those benefits. Rep. Panetta's list of options includes the idea of taxing 85% of the benefits paid to couples earning more than $18,000, according to participants in the budget talks. For individuals, the threshold would be $12,000. The change would raise an estimated $3.1 billion in revenues next year and $54 billion over five years.

Negotiators also have discussed a three-month delay in cost-of-living adjustments for all government benefit programs. This would save more than $3 billion in the first year and more than $17 billion over five years, according to congressional estimates.

But some negotiators said such a plan won't be part of any final compromise. "There are some people talking about that, but that's not going to happen, in my judgment," said Senate Budget Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.).

The political risks of cutting Social Security benefits are obvious to all the negotiators. Asked if anyone flinched when cost-of-living adjustments were raised in the budget negotiations, Sen. Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas) said, "Everyone flinched."

Negotiators say that their willingness to contemplate cutting or taxing Social Security benefits reflects a consensus that the elderly -- at least those who aren't poormust share the pain of cutting the deficit. "You can't hurt one group and leave another untouched, particularly one that is rather affluent by comparison," Rep. Frenzel said.

But some negotiators prefer that the elderly's contribution toward deficit reduction come out of the fast-growing Medicare program, another subject of discussion, Rep. Panetta said.

Separately, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady notified Congress that the government will default unless Congress raises the federal debt ceiling before it leaves for its August vacation. The current ceiling is $3.12 trillion.

Congress inevitably acts before the Treasury defaults, but the debt-ceiling measure is always politically troublesome to lawmakers, and it usually attracts a number of unrelated, controversial amendments.
