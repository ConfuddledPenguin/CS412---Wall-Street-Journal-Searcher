International: Pickens Storms Out Of Koito's Meeting Without Board Seats --- Owner of 26% Stake Claims Japanese `Stonewalled' Because He's American

  • Author: Kathryn Graven
  • Date: 06/29/90

TOKYO -- T. Boone Pickens Jr. and his entourage of U.S. shareholders stormed out of Koito Manufacturing Co.'s annual meeting yesterday in frustration over their inability to get seats on the auto-parts maker's board. "It was a sham, an absolute farce," declared Mr. Pickens, who through his Dallas-based Boone Co. owns a 26% stake in the Japanese concern. Mr. Pickens complained that Koito's management didn't respond fully to his and other shareholders' questions about the company's finances, and he fumed that his requests for representation on the board were "stonewalled" because he's an American.

Koito's president, Takao Matsuura, said he "regretted" that Mr. Pickens and his group left the shareholder's meeting early. He called the move a "staged event" typical of Mr. Pickens's publicity-seeking tactics. "We responded to shareholders' questions the best we know how, and to the greatest extent possible," Mr. Matsuura said at a news conference following the annual meeting at a Tokyo hotel.

Other observers suggested that Mr. Pickens's actions were designed as a face-saving move to allow him to justify an exit from Koito. He has owned stock in the company since March 1989 but has been unable to gain seats on its board or any role in the company's management. Mr. Pickens said he left the meeting, but "I'm not walking out of this fight."

At the meeting, Mr. Pickens and 32 Americans for whom he paid travel expenses were cordoned off from Japanese shareholders. Worried about possible violence, Koito said it hired 25 guards, in addition to the 20 security men provided by the hotel, to protect Mr. Pickens and his group from other angry shareholders. Koito officials say Mr. Pickens and his group agreed to this seating.

Nevertheless, Mr. Pickens said he was disgusted by the way the meeting was conducted. "For three hours, we endured the shouting of Japanese hecklers which went unchecked by management or security," Mr. Pickens said. Although the hecklers didn't have microphones and were difficult to hear from a distance, according to one U.S. shareholder in their midst, some yelled, "Yankee go home," and "we won the economic war."

At the news conference, Mr. Matsuura said it was unfortunate that some of the "sokaiya", or gangster-backed hecklers, who attended the meeting shouted insulting remarks at the Americans. Yet he insisted that such remarks were beyond the control of Koito and reflected the opinions of other shareholders, not the company.

Mr. Pickens became involved with Koito 16 months ago, purchasing a 20% stake in the firm from Kitaro Watanabe, one of Japan's wealthiest men and a notorious greenmailer. Since then, Mr. Pickens has increased his stake to 26%, and he vowed this week to boost his stake to 30%. He has repeatedly demanded representation on Koito's board, insisting he is a long-term investor.
