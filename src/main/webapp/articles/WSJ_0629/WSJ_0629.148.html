Foreign Exchange: Dollar Falls Against Yen as Agreement On Trade Boosts Japanese Currency

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/29/90

NEW YORK -- Backed by the successful completion of trade negotiations between Japan and the U.S., the yen rose, diverting the market's attention from German monetary union, at least for a day. The across-the-board yen purchases depressed the dollar by more than a yen on the day but lifted the U.S. currency against its European counterparts.

Late in New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 153.15 yen, below 154.45 yen late Wednesday in New York. The U.S. currency also was changing hands at 1.6730 marks, up from 1.6645 marks. Sterling was trading at $1.7381, down from $1.7428.

At midmorning in Tokyo Friday, the dollar was trading at 153.08 yen; it closed Thursday in Tokyo at 154.42 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6738 marks, up from Thursday's Tokyo close of 1.6627 marks.

On Thursday, foreign-exchange traders said that for the first time in a long time, prices moved on fundamental rather than technical factors. They said the outcome of the U.S.-Japanese Structural Impediments Initiative talks will probably mean the narrowing of the differential between Japanese interest rates and the higher U.S. rates.

To help trim its $49 billion trade surplus with the U.S., Japanese officials agreed to spend 430 trillion yen ($2.78 trillion) on public works projects over the next 10 years. The U.S. had asked for such a commitment in hopes that it would soak up some of the private savings that provide Japanese companies with cheaper capital than their U.S. competitors.

The Japanese government's spending is expected to increase price pressures, encouraging the Bank of Japan to head off inflation by tightening credit. Earlier yesterday, the central bank asked Japanese commercial banks to slow the growth of their lending.

U.S. interest rates, meanwhile, can only go down, said Tomi Kilgore, assistant treasurer for Toyo Trust & Banking Co. in New York. The softening of President Bush's "no new taxes" pledge was seen as opening the door for an easier U.S. credit policy.

A combined one-point swing in U.S. and Japanese interest rates would push the yield advantage from dollar to yen instruments, encouraging investors to purchase yen. Currently, Eurodollar bonds pay 8 3/8%, compared with 7 11/16% for Euroyen debt securities.

The prospect of higher Japanese interest rates also helped the yen against European currencies. West German rates are just about the same as U.S. rates, and Swiss rates are a little higher. The yen scored fewer gains against high-yield currencies such as the Australian and Canadian dollars and the pound.

Michael Rosenberg, an international foreign exchange economist for Merrill Lynch, said implementing the trade accord will push Japan into an economic policy similar to that pursued by West Germany. An expansive fiscal policy combined with a tight monetary policy is invariably bullish for a nation's currency, he said.

Conversely, the U.S. is "moving to more fiscal restraint, and there is expectation of lower interest rates," Mr. Rosenberg said.

He cautioned that higher Japanese and German interest rates, because of the fluidity of world debt markets, could eventually force U.S. rates higher.

Also moving the market was a dollar breakthrough against the Swiss franc. According to Robert Ryan, foreign exchange manager for Bank of New York, the dollar's move past 1.4140 Swiss francs around noon in New York "put a little bit of squeeze" on people who had shorted, or oversold, dollars against European currencies in recent days.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $352.10 an ounce, up 70 cents. Estimated volume was a light 2.3 million ounces.

In Hong Kong early Friday, gold was quoted at $351.65 an ounce.
