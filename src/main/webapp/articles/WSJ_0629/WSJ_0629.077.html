REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Disorder in the Court

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/29/90

On Wednesday, the Supreme Court ruled that Arizona's death-penalty law was constitutional. Yesterday, the Supreme Court ruled that Arizona's death-penalty law was unconstitutional.

This unprecedented 24-hour-long precedent is new evidence of how manipulative the liberal minority on the Supreme Court has become. This term had other rulings that can only serve to confuse many people about the Constitution's meaning. The court allowed judges to levy taxes to pay for school desegregation and let regulators grant broadcast licenses based on race or gender. But this two-day flip-flop takes the cake.

The immediate impact is that the murderer of reporter Don Bolles will not be executed and that no one knows if the death penalty in Arizona or similar laws in Idaho and Montana are constitutional.

One-day "stare decisis" is the latest outrage from the high court in death-penalty cases. Since 1976, the court has ruled in various cases either that there was too much discretion by a judge or a jury to issue the death sentence -- or not enough discretion to sentence prisoners instead to imprisonment.

Wednesday's ruling in Walton v. Arizona upheld a law that gave the trial judge discretion to find aggravating circumstances that justify the death penalty. Jeffrey Walton had left his robbery victim blinded by a gunshot to the head in the Arizona desert to die a death of dehydration, starvation and pneumonia.

The five justices who upheld the death penalty were Chief Justice Rehnquist and Justices White, O'Connor, Scalia and Kennedy. The four dissenters were Justices Brennan, Marshall, Blackmun and Stevens; Justices Brennan and Marshall repeated their extralegal view that no death penalty under any circumstances can be constitutional despite the Constitution's explicit support for death sentences.

This 5-4 lineup upholding the death sentence set the stage for the flip-flop in Lewis v. Adamson the very next day. Harvey Adamson had been convicted and sent to death row for the 1976 bomb killing of Mr. Bolles, an investigative reporter for the Arizona Republic.

The court's decision to spare Adamson came in a 4-3 vote. What happened is that only seven justices participated in the decision whether to review the appeals-court decision that had invalidated the Arizona law. This was because Justice O'Connor, a former Arizona judge, and Justice Kennedy, a former federal appeals judge, had participated in lower-court decisions in this case. They recused themselves. This allowed Justices Brennan, Marshall, Blackmun and Stevens to reverse the previous day's ruling with only three justices available to disagree with them.

Whatever you think of the death penalty, this was judicial dirty pool. The four liberal justices apparently decided it was worth junking standard judicial procedure in order to "win" Adamson. When the Supreme Court rules in a related case, as it did in Walton v. Arizona, the justices typically follow what is known in court shorthand as G-V-R.

This stands for granting the petition to review the case, vacating the lower-court ruling and remanding the case back to the lower court for reconsideration in light of the new high-court ruling. The four instead short-circuited this procedure by declaring the Arizona death penalty dead after one day.

Justice Scalia must have known the liberals were about to pull this stunt when he wrote a separate opinion in Wednesday's case upholding the death sentence. "A jurisprudence containing the contradictory commands that discretion to impose the death penalty must be limited but discretion not to impose the death penalty must be virtually unconstrained," he wrote, caused a "permanent flood-tide" of appeals. This back and forth on the death penalty, he said, causes "a fundamental uncertainty in the law that shows no signs of ending or even diminishing."

Justice Scalia's example of absurd uncertainty in the law was the lower-court opinion in the Adamson case, which he noted was "essentially identical" to the Walton case upholding the death penalty. He knew then what we know now -- that the views of five justices would last only a day when confronted with the views of four.

The court ends the term with the liberals perhaps understandably feeling the pressure of minority status. Still, there can be no excuse for gamesmanship in constitutional law. There certainly is no justice when a murderer gets to escape the ultimate punishment simply because he had the good fortune that two justices had to recuse themselves from his case. The likely solution will come only when President Bush gets the chance to replace some of the less principled members of this court.
