Chase Manhattan's Labrecque, Ryan Face Investors' `Show-Me Attitude'

  • Author: Robert Guenther and Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 06/29/90

NEW YORK -- To put it bluntly, Thomas G. Labrecque and Arthur F. Ryan, Chase Manhattan Corp.'s newly designated chairman and president, respectively, face a credibility problem with investors. Together with retiring Chairman Willard C. Butcher, they persuaded investors last October to buy 16 million newly issued common shares of the nation's second largest banking company. At the time, they painted a rosy earnings outlook. The response was so enthusiastic that Chase was able to raise $625 million rather than the $500 million originally planned.

But problem real estate loans and a slack corporate financing market have pulled the rug from under those projections, and investors think management was caught sleeping. The stock has plummeted from $40.125 for each newly issued share to close yesterday at $23.75, down 12.5 cents in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Mr. Labrecque, 51 years old, concedes that the level of corporate finance activity is 30% below Chase's earlier projections. "There were judgments that turned out not to be right. I will accept that," he said in an interview.

Mr. Labrecque said Chase management was "probably not as quick {responding to the drop in revenue} as we should have been." But he said management had been working with the board for several months on plans to streamline Chase's commercial banking business.

For years, some Chase executives have privately questioned whether Mr. Labrecque is up to the tough tasks that Chase faces. He came to the limelight in 1980 when he was catapulted out of Chase's treasury department over the heads of two movers and shakers -- Barry Sullivan, who went on to become chairman of First Chicago Corp., and William Ogden, who in 1984 left to run Continental Bank Corp. -- into the president-elect post.

But other Chase executives credit Mr. Labrecque for two moves that today are paying off for Chase: the decision to expand its consumer banking and another to decentralize and beef up back-office technology. Last year, the consumer bank contributed roughly half of Chase's core earnings, and Chase's technical services for corporate and institutional clients are rated among the industry's most advanced.

In that light, Mr. Ryan, a chain-smoking Brooklyn native, is a logical choice to serve as Mr. Labrecque's No. 2. Mr. Ryan, who in 1972 joined Chase from Control Data Corp., worked on the decentralization project and later took over the consumer business. Chase today says its consumer business is in a neck-and-neck race with Chemical Banking Corp. for the No. 2 spot in the New York City market behind Citicorp.

The selection of Mr. Ryan was cheered by many Chase insiders. Engaging and straight-talking, he serves to offset what some say is Mr. Labrecque's aloofness.

To regain investors' faith, Messrs. Labrecque and Ryan have embarked on Chase's second restructuring in recent years, seeking to cut more than $300 million out of its non-interest expenses, a reduction of about 8%, by the end of the year. The cuts are expected to eliminate about 3,000 jobs from Chase's 41,600-person work force.

Analysts expect that cut to result in a restructuring charge of less than $100 million in either the second or third quarter. However, much of that restructuring charge is expected to be offset by the sale of Chase buildings in Frankfurt, West Germany, and in London and Switzerland.

Mr. Ryan, 47 years old, said previous cost-cutting steps were "a pure case of not enough." He said: "We have to be more `real time' in responding to changes in the marketplace so you don't have a restructuring every three years. What happens in the market isn't something that I can control."

While they find the moves promising, analysts remain wary about whether Chase has a grip on where things are headed. Charles Peabody of Kidder, Peabody & Co. said, "The investment community is taking a show-me attitude."
