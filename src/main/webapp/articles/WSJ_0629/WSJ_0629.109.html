Ionics Inc. Expects Profit to Rise 30% In Second Quarter

  • Author: Lawrence Ingrassia
  • Date: 06/29/90

WATERTOWN, Mass. -- Ionics Inc., benefiting from growing concern about water-quality issues, is comfortable with analysts' estimates of second-quarter earnings and sales gains, Arthur L. Goldstein, chairman and chief executive officer, said. The company, while not making a specific forecast, doesn't quarrel with estimates of about 26 cents a share in the second quarter, a rise of 30% from the 1989 period's net income of $839,000, or 20 cents a share, Mr. Goldstein said in an interview.

Ionics, which makes purification systems, expects revenue to grow "15% or a little better" from the year-earlier period's $26 million, he said.

Most of Ionics' businesses are posting healthy revenue gains, but its Aqua Cool bottled water business is showing particularly sharp growth. With revenue expected to increase 25% to 30% in the second quarter and year, Aqua Cool now provides nearly 10% of Ionic's revenue, Mr. Goldstein said.

Aqua Cool is purified drinking water sold mostly in five-gallon containers for coolers in offices but also in smaller containers in supermarkets. Originally introduced overseas, Aqua Cool is expanding steadily in the eastern U.S., with the newest markets being Stamford, Conn., New York City and northern New Jersey.

Ionics believes it is among the 20 largest companies in the fast-growing, highly fragmented bottled-water market and expects to improve its position as it moves toward national distribution. "Our goal is to become a major player in this market. . . . We hope to be in the top 10 to 15 companies in the next couple of years," Mr. Goldstein said.

The executive said it will be easier for Ionics to expand geographically than for some of its competitors because Ionics can "manufacture" Aqua Cool water anywhere with its purification system. Thus, it won't have to transport bottled spring water long distances or buy regional companies.

Ionics has introduced a cooler that can dispense seltzer water by adding carbonation. While that business is only starting to become profitable, "by the end of this year it will become an important profit contributor," Mr. Goldstein said.

Ionics also is experiencing strong growth with its water-purification systems for companies in the power-generating, electronics and pharmaceutical industries, Mr. Goldstein said. This water is used in power-plant boilers, in pharmaceutical fluids and to clean computer chips, among other things. Demand is growing because use of less pure water can lower operating efficiency and increase maintenance costs, he said.

Ionics, which also makes desalination systems, said contracts for major systems contributed to its second-quarter earnings and sales gains. The company's backlog, which consists mostly of large desalination and purification systems, is about the same as the record backlog of $64.5 million at the end of the first quarter.

For the year, Mr. Goldstein said he isn't "uncomfortable" with analysts' estimates of earnings of $1.10 to $1.15 a share, or 30% to 35% higher than last year's $3.6 million, or 85 cents a share. Revenue is expected to rise at least 15% from last year's $108.9 million, he said.
