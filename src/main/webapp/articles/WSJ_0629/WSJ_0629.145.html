Law -- Legal Beat: SEC Charges Lawyer With Insider Trading

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and James P. Miller
  • Date: 06/29/90

The Securities and Exchange Commission charged a partner in a New York law firm with trading on inside information obtained from a client. In a civil complaint in federal court in Manhattan, the SEC charged Henry A. Singer with buying 6,600 shares of WearEver-Proctor-Silex Inc. stock at a time when the company was discussing a management leveraged buy-out. Mr. Singer, 52 years old, is a partner in Morrison Cohen Singer & Weinstein.

Mr. Singer, through his attorney, Robert G. Morvillo, called the SEC charges "factually inaccurate and legally deficient" and said he intends to fight them. Mr. Morvillo said that there is "substantial documentary evidence and corroborative testimony of several other witnesses which confirm that Mr. Singer did not purchase WearEver stock based on inside information."

According to the SEC complaint, Mr. Singer bought 5,000 shares of WearEver's stock Aug. 19, 1987, at $13.25 a share, based on information he allegedly obtained from Lawrence A. McLernon, a member of the company's board, concerning the proposed leveraged buy-out. The SEC said that Mr. Singer, a corporate lawyer specializing in mergers and acquisitions, represented Mr. McLernon and that the two were friends.

The SEC alleged that Mr. Singer bought another 1,600 shares Nov. 2, 1987 at $9.50 each, two months after he was retained by a special committee of WearEver's board that included Mr. McLernon. The complaint alleges that at the time of Mr. Singer's second purchase, he possessed nonpublic information that indicated that there were potential third-party bidders for WearEver that were likely to bid more than the $18 a share that WearEver's management had offered.

Nacco Industries Inc. acquired WearEver for $17.50 a share in April 1988. The SEC said that after learning of the purchase, Mr. Singer sold his stock, earning $34,050 on the sale.

In his statement, Mr. Singer said that he had cooperated with the SEC's inquiry and that the SEC had chosen "either to ignore or misconstrue all of the evidence presented to it." Mr. Singer's law firm issued a statement saying it "stands behind Mr. Singer."

AN ILLINOIS CONTRACTOR is indicted on unusual charges.

A federal grand jury in Milwaukee indicted contractor S.A. Healy Co. for allegedly violating federal workplace-safety laws in connection with a 1988 explosion that killed three Healy workers at an underground work-site.

It is unusual for federal prosecutors to bring criminal charges for breach of the occupational safety and health statutes, although the Occupational Safety and Health Administration often imposes civil penalties.

The 12-count indictment alleges that Healy willfully failed to properly vent a tunnel or properly instruct workers in underground safety precautions, and failed to cut the electricity in the tunnel after dangerous levels of methane gas had been detected. All the counts are misdemeanors because federal law doesn't permit prosecution of OSHA law violations as felonies.

"The feds are bringing the biggest guns they have against Healy, and we appreciate and applaud" the move, says Joseph Kinney, executive director of the Chicago-based National Safe Workplace Institute. But, he adds, "it's ridiculous {they} can't do more."

Each of the 12 misdemeanor counts in the indictment brought against Healy, based in McCook, Ill., carries a maximum fine of $500,000.

An attorney for Healy said the company "regrets the tragic accident, but doesn't believe that any of its management employees committed reckless acts." The company believes management "will be vindicated," he says.

Milwaukee County District Attorney E. Michael McCann, using state law, also this week filed two charges of reckless homicide against Healy. But he says the state's $10,000-a-count maximum fine on such charges "is not enough."

MANDATORY RETIREMENT AGE upheld for New York Supreme Court justices.

A federal appeals court in New York reversed a lower-court decision that had prohibited the state of New York from forcing a state judge to retire at age 76. The federal Equal Employment Opportunity Commission had filed the suit on behalf of Justice Isaac Rubin, who is 77.

The appeals court ruled that the federal age discrimination law, under which the suit was brought, does not apply to New York Supreme Court justices because they are elected. The law states that it does not apply to "any person elected to public office."

Justice Rubin and the attorneys for the state of New York and the EEOC declined to comment.

THE JUSTICE DEPARTMENT'S criminal division may get a new chief.

President Bush nominated Robert Mueller, a personal aide to Attorney General Dick Thornburgh, assistant attorney general in charge of criminal matters.

If confirmed by the Senate, Mr. Mueller would succeed Edward Dennis, who said he will resign Aug. 31. The change comes shortly after a shake-up of the department's senior staff. But Justice Department officials, including Mr. Dennis, stressed that his departure is voluntary and amicable.

Mr. Mueller, a special assistant to Mr. Thornburgh on criminal issues, previously served as a federal prosecutor in Massachusetts and California.

A career prosecutor, Mr. Dennis said he would return to his hometown of Philadelphia to join the law firm of Morgan, Lewis & Bockius as a partner.
