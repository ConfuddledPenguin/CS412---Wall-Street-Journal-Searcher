SEC Chief Accuses CFTC of Distortion In Mini-Crash Probe

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/29/90

WASHINGTON -- Securities and Exchange Commission Chairman Richard Breeden intensified the agency's battle with the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, accusing the commodities regulator of trying to "disguise facts" and "distort" information. Mr. Breeden also said action is needed "to eliminate the unsafe practices and rampant speculation in the futures market." That comment is likely to strike fear into the commodities exchanges, which may fall partly under SEC oversight if a Bush administration-sponsored bill passes Congress.

The statements show Mr. Breeden abandoning the more-conciliatory tone he had recently taken toward the futures exchanges. In recent weeks, he has suggested that he simply wanted to offer more protection to exchange customers and to make sure those markets maintained sufficiently high margins, or the good faith money needed to trade.

In a rebuke to CFTC Chairman Wendy Gramm, an obviously angered Mr. Breeden vehemently supported his agency's May report about the stock-market mini-crash last Oct. 13. Earlier this week, Mrs. Gramm offered her own scathing critique of the SEC report, accusing the SEC of "selectively misleading" the public about the cause of the plunge.

Mr. Breeden said the SEC "stands by the report and every word in it." He went on to blast the CFTC's separate report on the 6.9% drop in the Dow Jones Industrial Average.

While the SEC focused on the 80-minute period of the day when the average fell most of its 190.58 points, Mr. Breeden said that the CFTC averaged activity over the full day. "That's a little bit like saying that except for a period of time from 7 a.m. to noon, Pearl Harbor Day was a good day and on average there weren't many problems," he fumed.
