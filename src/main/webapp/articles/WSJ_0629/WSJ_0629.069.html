Manufacturers Hanover Sets Sizable Charge

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 06/29/90

NEW YORK -- Manufacturers Hanover Corp. said it will take a substantial charge against second-quarter earnings as a result of the bank holding company's cost-cutting reorganization plan. The company also said that it will place more than $150 million in loans to developer Donald Trump on nonperforming status. Other lenders are expected to take similar action in reclassifying a portion of their loans to Mr. Trump as nonperforming.

Such status means that the loans aren't performing according to the original terms of the borrowers' loan agreement. It doesn't mean the bank won't eventually be repaid the loans.

Manufacturers Hanover said that the one-time charge will reduce operating earnings for the second quarter below the $96 million, or $1.21 a share, it earned in this year's first quarter. Although the size of the charge hasn't been determined, it isn't expected to exceed the bank's operating earnings for the second quarter.

The disclosures came in a prospectus for the proposed sale of four million shares of Manufacturers Hanover preferred.

Manufacturers stock closed at $34.50, off 25 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Like many other large banks, Manufacturers is struggling to reduce expenses in the face of slack corporate finance business. It earlier announced a reorganization aimed at slashing costs to bring them more in line with expected revenue.

Chemical Banking Corp., Bankers Trust New York Corp. and Chase Manhattan Corp. all have taken or face the need to take similar charges as a result of restructurings, industry experts say.

Although Manufacturers' prospectus didn't identify Mr. Trump specifically, it said its portfolio of nonperforming loans would increase by about 15% in the second quarter "principally because of problems being experienced by one of the corporation's major real estate customers." A bank official identified the customer as Mr. Trump.

Manufacturers' nonperforming loan portfolio, exclusive of Third World debt, stood at $1.04 billion as of March 31.

Bank industry sources said Mr. Trump's other major bank lenders -- Citicorp, Bankers Trust and Chase -- would almost certainly place the portion of their loans to Mr. Trump on which interest payments have been deferred on nonperforming status as well. Under a tentative agreement between Mr. Trump and the banks this week, interest payments on some $850 million of loans have been deferred in exchange for an additional five-year, $65 million loan that will enable Mr. Trump to avoid defaults on debt agreements.

A spokesman for Chase said that some portion of the bank's loans to Mr. Trump would be placed on nonperforming status, but not loans on which Mr. Trump continues to make interest payments. Other banks refused to comment on their treatment of the Trump loans.

Additionally, two New Jersey banks with substantial loans to Mr. Trump -- First Fidelity Bancorp. and Midlantic Corp. -- refused to disclose how they're dealing with their loans to Mr. Trump. However, First Fidelity earlier said it would set aside as much as $300 million for past-due loans in the second quarter, a portion of which is believed to be loans to Mr. Trump.
