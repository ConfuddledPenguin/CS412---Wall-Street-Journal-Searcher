Foreign Exchange: Dollar Slips Further Against the Mark; G-7 Meeting Is Discounted by Traders

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/08/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar slipped further against the mark yesterday, still hurt by Friday's U.S. employment data. The mark also benefited from a more positive market assessment of German reunification. Negative reaction to the April jobs report, which the market interpreted as the death knell for a short-term tightening of U.S. interest rates, carried the dollar lower against nearly all other major currencies.

Sunday's Group of Seven meeting of finance ministers and central bankers in Washington generally was dismissed by participants, due to a lack of new initiatives announced by the G-7 officials.

The failure of G-7 monetary officials to voice strong support of the yen added to the yen's weakness, traders said. G-7 monetary officials reaffirmed that the low value of the yen is a problem, but declined to take action in support of the Japanese currency.

The yen closed marginally higher against the dollar but lower against the mark and pound.

At the same time, statements yesterday by Bonn monetary officials that reunification with East Germany won't seriously burden West Germany's economy served to reassure traders.

"The {West} Germans are now addressing concerns that the market had about inflation . . . . They've come a long way since they first began talking about a 1-to-1 conversion rate" for all East German marks, said Gabriele Schmitt, a trader at Bank of New York.

Earlier yesterday, West German central bank President Karl Otto Poehl said it is "realistic" to assume that reunification and the plan for replacing the East German currency with the mark won't intensify inflationary pressures on the West German economy.

In the same positive vein, West German Finance Minister Theo Waigel said the country's capital markets won't be strained by the restructuring of the East German economy along Western lines. He added that stability of the mark and of government finances is assured.

Mr. Waigel's comments yesterday paralleled those he made at the weekend G-7 meeting, where monetary officials agreed that German monetary and economic unification will contribute to "noninflationary" growth in the global economy.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6558 marks, down from 1.6662 marks late Friday, and at 158.19 yen, down from 158.25 yen late Friday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6685, up from $1.6630 Friday.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the U.S. currency was quoted at 158.10 yen, up from Monday's Tokyo close of 157.80 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.6560 marks.

A labor settlement announced Friday between employers and the large IG Metall workers union in the Stuttgart area bolstered the mark for the second trading day in a row, participants said. The settlement reduced market worries that the 2,680,000-member union might launch a nationwide strike, they said.

The yen lost ground against most other units amid a lack of news, especially about any Japanese interest-rate rise, which the market still deems necessary for the yen to recover from its doldrums.

The mark capitalized on the yen's weakness, rising to 95.52 yen late yesterday in New York from 95.02 yen late Friday. Sterling finished the day at 263.87 yen, up from 263.33 yen.

While this week's auctions of Treasury debt aren't expected to move currency markets, Tokyo dealers said demand for the dollar against the yen may increase when secondary trading in the new issues gets under way.

Japanese institutions interested in taking part in the primary auctions of the debt are seen as having largely finished making their dollar purchases, according to dealers in Tokyo.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $371.90 an ounce, up $1. Estimated volume was a light 1.8 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $372.30 an ounce.
