Economy: Productivity Fell at 0.5% Rate In First Quarter --- Big Improvement Is Posted In Hourly Output Pace Of Manufacturing Firms

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 05/08/90

WASHINGTON -- Manufacturing productivity showed a big improvement in the first quarter, but it wasn't enough to overcome sharp declines in other sectors of the economy, the Labor Department said. While manufacturing productivity -- output per hour of work -- jumped at a 4.1% annual rate, overall business productivity dropped at a 0.5% annual rate, the department said. When workers in the farm sector are excluded, giving what many economists view as a better picture of underlying economic forces, productivity fell at a 1% annual rate.

Productivity is critical to maintaining the competitiveness of U.S. companies, to keeping the economy growing without accelerating inflation, and to raising the standard of living. If workers produce more per hour, businesses are able to increase wages without incurring higher costs. That means they don't have to raise prices, which would eat up the benefits of the pay increases.

The report did contain some evidence that wage increases are slowing, suggesting that some inflationary pressures may be easing. Hourly compensation rose at a 3.9% annual rate in the first quarter, far short of the 6% rate in the fourth quarter and the 5.5% increase for all of 1989. At the same time, the labor costs of a unit of production grew at a 4.9% annual rate, trailing the 5.5% annual rate in the fourth quarter but ahead of the 4.5% increase for all of 1989.

The increase in hourly pay wasn't nearly enough, however, to keep workers even with inflation. In fact, they were making 3.9% less an hour in the first quarter than in the fourth, after the effects of inflation were removed, the report said.

The drop in non-farm productivity reflected an increase in output at a 2.7% annual rate and a rise in hours worked at a 3.6% annual rate. The jump in manufacturing productivity stemmed from a 3.2% rate of decline in hours worked, reflecting the sluggish economy, and a 0.8% rate of growth in output.

Lawrence Chimerine, an economic consultant in Philadelphia, said the new productivity report "reinforces a fundamental problem in this country -- that we are mired in slow productivity growth."

Mr. Chimerine and other economists said manufacturing productivity probably isn't as good, nor service productivity as bad, as the latest report suggests.

Ken Goldstein, an economist at the Conference Board in New York, said it wasn't surprising to see a surge in manufacturing productivity during a slowing economy. "When the economy slows down in growth, you wind up with fewer of the resources in production, both in terms of labor and capital," he said. "Presumably those people and machines idled are the least productive."

To make true, lasting gains in worker productivity, Mr. Chimerine said, the country must improve its education system and rate of investment.

All the numbers in the report were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
