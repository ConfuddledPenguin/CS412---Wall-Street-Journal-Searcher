Letters to the Editor: Clearing the Air Benefits Everyone

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/08/90

In response to your March 28 page-one article "Breathing Easier": As a co-sponsor of the Clean Air bill, I believe that the U.S. can establish reasonable domestic environmental standards without being unreasonable about America's competitiveness in the international marketplace.

There's no doubt that foreign producers operating under less costly environmental standards will indirectly benefit from the costs imposed on U.S. industry by the Clean Air bill. I plan to offer an amendment that seeks to preserve a level playing field by requiring the president to negotiate equivalent environmental standards or trade adjustments with our international competitors.

Basically, this is neither an effort to protect our interests only, nor is it a violation of the spirit of fairness and reciprocity. I am attempting to put this issue on the table now. Otherwise, it will haunt us later. Through trade negotiations, the U.S. can promote an environmental agenda that not only equalizes trade inequities at the outset, but also enhances global responsibility in solving our pollution problems.

Rep. Alex McMillan (R., N.C.)

Washington

---

You neglected to mention one sector that should ultimately benefit from more stringent emissions standards: the nuclear-energy industry.

According to the U.S. Council for Energy Awareness, for every 1,000 megawatts of generating capacity, a coal-fired utility will annually emit nearly 70,000 tons of sulfur dioxide, 25,000 tons of nitrogen oxide and 1.6 million tons of carbon dioxide. The corresponding figures for natural gas are 17,000, 15,000 and 900,000 tons, respectively. Nuclear utilities discharge none of these pollutants, nor do they emit carbon monoxide or methane.

Michael G. McGinty

Stamford, Conn.

---

In my opinion, you covered the legislation in an unfairly negative light. You state the costs to industry as $21 billion, and remark that this exceeds the combined earnings of several major corporations. However, a fairer comparison would be with other costs of doing business. The sum of $21 billion, which you present as staggering, will be passed on to consumers, and works out at less than $70 per capita, a sum most of us would be happy to pay for clean air.

It is also unfair to present this as some sort of trauma to the economy. This money is not (so to speak) vanishing into thin air. It is going into new sectors of the economy, and represents an opportunity for new business and technology. Clean air, and other environmental legislation, is not a blow to the economy, but a shift in the economy, something that occurs all the time for all sorts of reasons. True, it will be bad for high-sulfur coal mining, and I for one would be happy to pay for transitional compensation and retraining for those affected. But we cannot refuse to make important social transitions because they carry short-term costs for certain sectors of the economy. Did anyone suggest we shore up the horse-drawn buggy business because it was threatened by the automobile?

Clean technology is the wave of the future because clean is the way people prefer to live. Custom is not good enough reason for doing the wrong thing. In fact, the trouble with the Clean Air Act is that it doesn't go nearly far enough. If, instead of focusing on reducing tailpipe emissions, the auto industry had chosen to boost fuel economy, something for which ample technology already exists, we could have begun to control not just ground-level air pollution but greenhouse gases as well. It's time American business divorced itself from the status quo and realized that the greatest benefits will come from moving ahead.

Elizabeth Feuer, M.D.

Colts Neck, N.J.

---

Acid rain is not, as your article suggests, some small, isolated problem related only to the Northeast. Its effects are being felt throughout the country (Mountain Lake, at an elevation of 3,500 feet, in the western mountains of Virginia, is acid-rain dead) and the world. We are not talking about a minor problem for the 1990s. We are facing our future.

Industry will continue to drag its collective feet as long as earnings per share govern performance. When will trustees and stockholders wake up like most citizens and realize higher costs are inevitable if we are to survive?

William S. Gardiner

Williamsburg, Va.
