Letters to the Editor: Nothing `Perverse' In Pulitzer's Choice

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/08/90

In his article on the Pulitzer Prize recently awarded August Wilson's "The Piano Lesson," Edwin Wilson makes much of the fact that the Pulitzer Prize Board "reserves the right to ignore the recommendations" of its advisory juries ("A `Lesson' in Pulitzer Perversity," Leisure & Arts, April 23). In reality, that is a right that is not invoked in the great majority of instances and, in any case, the point has no real bearing on the particular award with which Mr. Wilson concerned himself. In giving the 1990 drama award to "The Piano Lesson," the Pulitzer board, in fact, chose one of the plays that the three critics who constituted this year's drama jury recommended as finalists. No less tangential is Mr. Wilson's implications that the fact that the prize-winning version of "The Piano Lesson" was first produced outside New York City somehow had major relevance to its eligibility for the 1990 drama prize. Actually, the determining consideration was not where the play was produced but whether it was still identical to the version first put on at the Yale Repertory Theater in 1987. For while there is no flat prohibition in the Pulitzer bylaws against considering the same play in successive years, the prize board would almost certainly have been hesitant to give the 1990 award to a work it had bypassed earlier and that had undergone no meaningful evolution since then.

Mr. Wilson holds the view that the current version of "The Piano Lesson" is "much the same" as the one he saw in New Haven in 1987. The chairman of the this year's Pulitzer drama jury on the other hand felt that the two were significantly different. Instead of the "perversity" of the Pulitzer Prize Board, then, the true cause of Mr. Wilson's complaint would appear to lie in a difference of opinion between himself and some other professional drama critics.

Robert C. Christopher

Administrator

Pulitzer Prize Board

New York
