Heard on the Street: Walt Disney May Need a Gee-Whiz Encore As Investors Look for It to Maintain Magic

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 05/08/90

NEW YORK -- Is Mickey Mouse headed for a trap? Several Wall Street analysts and money managers are worried that this summer could prove disappointing for Walt Disney shareholders. Even if Disney's numbers are good, they warn, all the hoopla about competitors MCA and Paramount could hurt the short-term performance of Disney stock.

Disney could be a victim of its own success. In the past six years it has gone from dog to darling on Wall Street. Managers led by Michael Eisner, installed after the company bought out unwelcome investor Saul Steinberg, have revitalized Disney's theme park and movie and television production businesses. The performance landed Eisner on the cover of Time magazine -- an unusual honor for a corporate hired hand -- and powered Disney shares to a gain of more than sevenfold.

Given the lofty expectations now built into the stock price, some analysts and investors say it will be tough for Disney to make it through the next few quarters without a letdown.

"Expectations as to how Disney will do are still too high," says Christopher Dixon, an analyst with Kidder Peabody. But because Mr. Dixon believes Disney is "superbly positioned" for the 1990s, he adds: "Should disappointed expectations drive Disney stock down, long-term investors should use that as a buying opportunity."

Some big long-term shareholders have been taking profits since Disney shares soared above 130 last summer. They closed yesterday at 113 1/4, up 1/2.

Lincoln Capital Management, Chicago, which last year had about 4.5% of its $4 billion stock fund invested in 1.7 million shares of Disney, cut its position to zero by the end of April. Parker Hall, president, says the decision to sell "principally was based on the stock price and a feeling that a lot of areas of their business were cyclically going well." He adds: "Comparisons against their attendance at the parks and the extraordinary things they've done with movies would be hard to replicate."

What's going to make the comparisons tough? First, the Disney-MGM studio tour, which opened in May 1989, has been drawing capacity crowds to Orlando, Fla., and pumping up Disney's theme park attendance and profits. But starting this month, the studio tour will be competing against itself in the year-to-year comparisons. To boost attendance, Disney has extended the hours the studio tour is open. But the real kick won't come until 1993, when a planned doubling of the studio tour facility is to be completed.

Coming up much sooner is the June 7 grand opening of the competing Universal Studios tour in Orlando. Built by Disney's archrival MCA, it drew high marks from a tramload of analysts. Alan Kassan of Morgan Stanley believes the Universal Florida tour, which includes King Kong and Jaws, will do "quite well." At Disney, theme parks and resorts accounted for 64% of 1989 operating earnings.

Though many analysts believe the Universal tour will help boost attendance at Disney World and the Disney-MGM tour over the long term by drawing more people to Orlando, they worry about the short run. Warren Shaw, managing director of Chancellor Capital Management, New York, which sold about 250,000 Disney shares in the fourth quarter and still holds 685,000, says, "The main concern or negative here, near-term, is negative psychological reaction to the MCA studio tour opening."

Mr. Shaw notes that at its peak last year, Disney was trading at 25 to 30 times earnings, an extraordinary multiple. Currently, Disney is trading at about 21 times its most recent 12-month earnings and about 18 times estimated fiscal 1990 earnings.

Disney declines to comment, but its management has set a goal of increasing per-share earnings 20% a year over any five-year period. Earnings per share increased more than 30% in fiscal 1988 and 1989, but only 18% in the first six months of fiscal 1990.

The wild card in Disney's hand this summer is "Dick Tracy," a movie starring Warren Beatty and Madonna. Opening June 15, it could be this summer's "Batman," which caused the acquisitive execs at Time Inc. to say "Holy Box Office" as they rushed to buy Warner Communications. Both stars have graced some expensive flops, notably Mr. Beatty's "Ishtar." Then again, none of those films were produced by Disney. Nor will "Dick Tracy" give Madonna the kind of exposure she's had in Vanity Fair magazine and on MTV. "Dick Tracy" won't get a rating more risque than PG.

"Dick Tracy" isn't finished yet, so even the theater owners haven't seen it, a Disney spokeswoman says. It will be competing with "Days of Thunder," starring Tom Cruise, from Paramount, and "Back to the Future III" from Universal. And as Mr. Dixon of Kidder notes, "whether `Dick Tracy' is successful or not, it will have very little impact on Disney's bottom line." However, he says, "the psychological impact could be hurtful."
