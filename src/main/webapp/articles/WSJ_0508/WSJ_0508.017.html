Dividend News: MCI Decides To Offer Payout Semiannually

  • Author: Julie Amparano Lopez
  • Date: 05/08/90

MCI Communications Corp., long opposed to paying any dividend on its common stock, declared an initial semiannual dividend of five cents a share. While nominal, the payout is aimed at attracting new investors to the Washington-based long-distance company and rewarding the patience of its current holders, said Daniel F. Akerson, MCI's chief financial officer.

"Many institutional investors can't hold the stock of a company that doesn't pay a dividend," said Mr. Akerson. "This will help widen our base" of ownership.

Based on MCI's current stock price, the annualized payout of 10 cents a share will represent a yield of about 0.25%. In contrast, the per-share yield for archrival American Telephone & Telegraph Co., one of the most widely held stocks in the world, is $1.32 a year, or about 3.3%.

At MCI, the dividend question has been a sensitive subject since the company began trading publicly in 1972. MCI's chairman and founder, William McGowan, has been opposed to paying dividends because, in his view, they are taxed twice, first as corporate profits and then as personal income.

But since the company began to have positive cash flow in the 1988 third quarter, Mr. Akerson said, MCI has been seriously discussing the possibility of paying at least a token amount to shareholders.

The dividend declaration was viewed on Wall Street as a further indication of MCI's maturity and strength. In national over-the-counter trading, MCI closed at $38.25, up 37.5 cents a share. More than two million shares changed hands, making MCI one of the most active issues.

"The dividend shows that the company is confident enough in its cash position not only to make acquisitions, but also to pay a dividend," said Edward M. Greenberg, a telecommunications analyst with Morgan Stanley & Co. Last month, MCI signed a pact to acquire Telecom USA for about $1.25 billion.

MCI's Mr. Akerson said he expects the company to keep its new semiannual payment at the current level for at least "two or three years." He said MCI's free cash flow is strong enough to support such an amount, but such recent acquisitions as Telecom USA will probably prevent the company from increasing the payout rate anytime soon. In 1989, MCI had free cash flow of $225 million. The company said it defines free cash flow as net income plus depreciation minus capital expenditures.

Once company executives "sort through the acquisitions, and we make sure we're in good shape," Mr. Akerson said, "we could decide to increase it (the dividend) or we could decide to go quarterly."

Joel Gross, a telecommunications analyst with Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp., said most investors "don't want MCI to become a dividend company." He said he thinks they would "rather see the company reinvest the cash in futher growth and expansion."
