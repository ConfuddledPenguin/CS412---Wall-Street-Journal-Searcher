Labor Letter: A Special News Report on People And Their Jobs in Offices, Fields and Factories

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 05/08/90

DRUG TESTING programs escalate at the Transportation Department. Leaping legal hurdles, most of the department's requirements for testing four million truck and bus drivers, train crews, mass-transit employees, airline pilots and mechanics, pipeline workers, licensed seamen and others took effect in recent months. But court action stalls all transit and some truck-and-bus and Coast Guard testing rules. Some 8% to 30% of truck-driver job seekers test positive, firms say.

Despite union lawsuits, Terrance Gainer, the department's drug-program chief, says he is "absolutely amazed" at labor-management acceptance. So far, 35,000 tests were also done on air-traffic controllers and other department people since late 1987. There were 161 positives; 34 were fired, the rest were reassigned or returned to duty after rehabilitation.

Many transit agencies and others move ahead with testing on their own, untouched by court orders.

CREATIVE INTERVIEWING takes firmer hold, as the job pinch worsens.

Bolt, Beranek & Newman, to avoid costly "bad hires" and emphasize the applicant's future, strives harder to divine the "real candidate." Hercules begins training managers how to better assess job seekers' performance prospects. Hershey Food makes "behavioral interviewing" a crucial part of management training, so managers can judge "between a fast horse and a champion" who'll fit Hershey's "culture."

Negligent-hiring and reference-checking problems also spur new techniques. Bradley Real Estate Trust uses the behavioral method now, asking applicants what they'd do to resolve a real-life work problem. Scott Paper also relies more on "situational" queries that probe the job-seeker's skills, judgment and teamwork.

College grads, with numerous interviews and offers under their belts, are prepared for interviews, says Hercules.

ILLEGAL ALIENS use phony documents to beat immigration curbs.

Though the General Accounting Office says employer sanctions have cut illegal immigration, they aren't enforced much for farm workers, insists Philip Martin, an agriculture expert at the University of California at Davis. False birth, Social Security and driver's documents proliferate. "We may have to look at other means to make . . . sanctions work," an Immigration and Naturalization Service spokesman says.

Employers and unions figure 35% of three million field workers in major farm states are undocumented, as in the early 1980s. The INS says arrests along the Mexican border rose by over 100,000, to 472,121, in the six months through March, which just means more people are trying -- and succeeding -- in coming across.

Texas growers try to check documents. But "when you need help, you need help," says Joyce Brejcha, whose family grows corn and peanuts.

SCHOOL CUSTODIANS have a high incidence of asbestos-related lung disease, a study by the Mount Sinai Medical Center shows. Fully 28% of 660 New York school janitors examined had scarred lungs; the general-population rate is under 1%. Of those with 40 years' work history, 61% showed serious lung scarring.

HEALTH MAINTENANCE organization enrollment grew by 2.1 million members, or 6.3%, in 1989, to 34.7 million, the Group Health Association of America said. By the end of 1989, 14% of all Americans were enrolled in an HMO, switching from traditional indemnity insurance plans.

A 1987 DISSERTATION by a Yale doctoral candidate, finding little significant risk of lung cancer from passive smoking, is being sent to 500 scientists by Philip Morris. The tobacco company hopes to defuse an EPA report expected soon, that the firm fears will urge a ban on smoking at work.

SENSITIVITY TRAINING alerts employees to customer needs.

Staples Inc., an office-supply chain, sent workers from its New York stores to a two-day motel retreat last month; a "mystery shopper" reported on their customer-service gaffes. Employees also played shopper roles, talking about problems like trying to return an item without a receipt. British Airways employees see how passengers react, in mock fires and other mock crises.

Ritz-Carlton Hotel workers are taught to be more formal with Japanese and European guests, and to reply to all, "It's my pleasure," instead of "You're welcome." Parfums Stern staffers act out customer-salesperson roles, to fathom customer emotions. Wendy's International videotapes disabled customers; in one video, a blind person asks that change be counted out loud.

Meridian Bancorp. has workers walk with seeds in their shoes to simulate older customers' corns and callouses.
