Marketing & Media: Playboy Plans New, Nonvoting Class of Shares

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 05/08/90

CHICAGO -- Playboy Enterprises Inc. said it plans to convert its thinly traded common stock into separate voting and nonvoting classes, a move that would allow it to issue more shares while preserving founder Hugh Hefner's control of the company. The move will provide management with additional financial flexibility, the publishing concern said, by permitting Playboy to use equity for possible future acquisitions, financings, and employee benefit plans -- without diluting the voting power of Mr. Hefner's 71% stake.

The plan would also enable the 64-year-old former chairman and chief executive officer to retain voting control of the company while disposing of up to three-quarters of his Playboy holdings.

All stockholders, "including Mr. Hefner," notes a consent solicitation being mailed to shareholders, will get "increased flexibility to dispose of a portion of their equity interest in the company and otherwise determine the extent of their equity ownership without necessarily affecting their relative voting power."

The document also says Playboy "is not aware of any current intention by Mr. Hefner to dispose of any portion of his equity interest in the company." Mr. Hefner is more likely to dispose of his nonvoting shares than his Class A stock, "over time," the solicitation says.

Mr. Hefner, who suffered a stroke in 1985, married a 26-year-old former Playmate last year. His daughter, Christie Hefner, assumed the chairman and chief executive jobs in late 1988 after serving as president. A spokeswoman noted that Mr. Hefner has previously made it clear that "it is his intent to have Christie continue to run the company."

Dual-class arrangements aren't rare in the publishing business: founding families of New York Times Co., Dow Jones & Co., and Washington Post Co., for example, all maintain voting control through special classes of stock.

Playboy's recap plan calls for it to carry out a 1-for-2 reverse split of its current shares, reducing the number of common shares outstanding to 4.7 million from 9.4 million. Those shares will then be "reclassified" as Class A stock, each having one vote. The Class A shares will then each pay a dividend of three shares of non-voting Class B Playboy shares, which the company will seek to have traded on the New York Stock Exchange.

Playboy said it and financial adviser Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. believe that the two classes of stock "should trade at approximately equal prices." Playboy's plan includes a "minority protection" feature designed to deter would-be raiders from taking control of the company on the cheap by purchasing only the voting stock: Any person acquiring more than 10% of the voting stock must offer to acquire Class B shares proportionate to their voting-class holdings.

Although Mr. Hefner's support of the deal appears to make it a sure thing, holders of record on May 16 will be eligible to vote on the plan, with implementation scheduled for June 8. The Big Board has advised Playboy that the issuance of nonvoting stock wouldn't violate restrictions on dual-class stocks the Big Board adopted in 1988.

Because Playboy certificates once attracted interest as potential collectors' items, the company has historically had a higher-than-average number of single-share stockholders. In a move Playboy expects to save about $100,000 annually in servicing costs, those holders won't receive fractional shares in the reverse split, but will receive a cash payment based on the price of the stock in the days preceding the conversion.

In an interview, Ms. Hefner said she expects the conversion will enhance the stock's liquidity, and spark increased interest from institutional holders, who have largely avoided the thinly traded stock because of its small "float." Use of the stock in management-benefit plans also will help attract top executives, she said.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Playboy closed at $12.785 a share, up 12.5 cents.
