Law -- Legal Beat: Court in Maryland Strikes Down `Exclusions' in S&L Insurance Case

  • Author: Milo Geyelin and Glenn Ruffenach
  • Date: 05/08/90

A Maryland state appeals court struck down two key provisions that so far have limited the exposure of insurance companies for the negligence of officers and directors of failed savings and loan associations. The decision is the first state appellate ruling in the nation to address whether the provisions -- known as "exclusions" in insurance parlance -- should exempt insurance companies from paying claims against directors and officers of failed thrifts. Though the case has no binding effect outside Maryland, it is expected to be widely cited as more lawsuits are filed nationwide against directors and officers of failed S&Ls.

Almost all liability policies for directors and officers of thrifts contain the exclusions struck down in Maryland. One, known as the "regulatory exclusion," bars coverage when federal regulators take action. The other known, as the "insured vs. insured exclusion" exempts insurance companies from liability when institutions sue their own officers.

The exclusions have been pivotal in limiting the exposure of insurance companies for the negligent conduct of officers responsible for the collapse of thrifts nationwide. But both were nullified last week by the court of special appeals of Maryland, in Baltimore. The intermediate state appellate court ruled that the exclusions are contrary to Maryland "public policy."

The state has been bailing out collapsed thrifts since 1985, when it created the Maryland Deposit Insurance Fund to represent creditors and depositors. Allowing the exclusions to stand, thus barring a payout by insurers, the court ruled, would circumvent the state's greater interest in recovering "every dollar available."

The court ruled that: "The law in Maryland is well settled that considerations of public policy are deemed paramount to private rights. . . . Thus, where the two conflict, private interest must yield to the public good."

"From a national perspective, the vitiating of these two exclusions has tremendous ramifications," said Neil Dilloff, a partner at the Baltimore law firm of Piper & Marbury, which won the opinion on behalf of the Maryland fund.

The case was filed against American Casualty Co. of Reading, Pa., an underwriter for CNA Insurance Cos., in Chicago. American's attorneys in Washington weren't available for comment, but the case is expected to be appealed to Maryland's highest court, said Mr. Dilloff.

Michael Lovendusky, counsel and litigation manager for the American Insurance Association, in Washington, said that if the ruling is picked up in other states or by federal courts, it could become impossible for insurance companies to issue director and officer liability policies. The two exclusions, said Mr. Lovendusky, have been standard since the 1960s.

RACIAL ISSUE in major smoking-liability case forces a change of venue.

The case of Horton vs. American Tobacco Co. originally went to trial in January 1988 in Lexington, Miss. The family of Nathan H. Horton, a housing contractor who died of lung cancer in 1987, charged the company with "marketing a defective and unreasonably dangerous product."

American Tobacco, a unit of American Brands Inc., denied any responsibility for Mr. Horton's death. But anti-smoking forces had high hopes for the Horton case. Mississippi state law is more favorable to plaintiffs than in most other states. Under its so-called comparative-negligence law, the jury could have awarded damages to the Horton family even if it had found the tobacco company bore only 1% of the responsibility for Mr. Horton's death.

But the first trial ended in a hung jury amid charges of jury tampering.

A second trial began last month but was abruptly halted by Circuit Court Judge Eugene Bogen in response to complaints made by American Tobacco. In early April, newspapers, broadcasters and local civic and political leaders had received a packet of information from the plaintiffs' attorneys that included statements that tobacco companies were targeting blacks and other minorities.

Judge Bogen, in approving a motion by American Tobacco for a delay in the case and a change of venue, said the statements were a "blatant attempt to make an appeal on the basis of race to the persons in Holmes County, and thus indirectly on the jury pool."

After declaring a mistrial and placing a gag order on lawyers for both sides, Judge Bogen moved the case to the Lafayette County Circuit Court from Holmes County and set a Sept. 4 trial date.

BANKS' LAWSUIT against government of Peru gets court hearing.

The group of banks is seeking immediate repayment of $1.2 billion in loans made during the past two decades. The banks include Citibank, a unit of Citicorp; Bank of Tokyo; Bank of America, a unit of BankAmerica Corp.; and Chase Manhattan Bank, a unit of Chase Manhattan Corp. The banks allege that Peru has been in default on its foreign debt since 1984.

The suit is believed to be the largest ever filed against a sovereign government, said Mark A. Cymrot of Cole Corette & Abrutyn, a lawyer for Peru and Peruvian financial institutions that are also defendants in the case. Ten other suits have also been filed against Peru seeking the repayment of loans.

An attorney for the banks told federal Judge John Keenan of New York that his clients would prefer to renegotiate the loans but were concerned about deadlines that would have barred legal action.

The banks filed suit March 2 after learning that an agreement reached with Peru's government, which would have effectively extended the deadline, might not be enforceable under Peruvian law, Henry Weisburg of Shearman & Sterling, an attorney for several banks, told the court. A spokeswoman for Citibank, which heads the 10-member Bank Advisory Committee for Peru, said later that serious negotiations with Peru haven't taken place for several years.

Peru has had difficult relations with its foreign creditors since 1985, when it restricted payments on its foreign debt to 10% of its export revenue. In December, Peru paid the International Monetary Fund $42.3 million in overdue interest payments. In return, the IMF agreed to send a team to Peru to develop an economic program with the government.

Mr. Cymrot said the banks's suit ignores the IMF agreement and that enforcement of a favorable judgment could cut off Peru's foreign income and strangle the country economically. "This could undermine democracy in Peru," he said.

At the hearing, Judge Keenan gave the defendants 60 days to respond to the banks' allegations.

RETRIAL BEGINS: The retrial of Raymond Buckey, the former California preschool teacher accused of child molestation, began following a month of jury selection. Mr. Buckey, whose first criminal trial was the longest and costliest in U.S. history, will be tried on eight counts of child molestation involving three girls who were once pupils at the now-defunct Virginia McMartin Preschool in the community of Manhattan Beach. Earlier this year, after 28 months of hearing testimony and three months of deliberations, a jury acquitted Mr. Buckey and his mother, Peggy McMartin Buckey, on 52 of 65 counts. The government later decided to retry Mr. Buckey alone on eight of the 13 counts on which the jury had deadlocked. The new trial is expected to take at least six months.

PRIVATE LESSONS: Baker & McKenzie, a 1,400 lawyer firm based in Chicago, was picked as a legal adviser to Hungary in that nation's efforts to sell its state-owned enterprises. The law firm, which has a two-year-old office in Budapest, will join investment bank Barclays de Zoete Wedd Limited, a unit of Barclays Bank PLC, London, and accounting firm Price Waterhouse of New York as part of a consortium of international advisers on the public offering. The consortium will recommend policies and procedures for the Hungarian State Property Agency, which was established this year to oversee the sale of an estimated $32 billion in state property.
