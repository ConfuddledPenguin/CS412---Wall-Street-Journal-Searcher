World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Surge as the Steadier Yen Helps to Boost Investor Confidence, Traders Say

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/08/90

Tokyo stocks surged as the steadier yen helped to boost investor confidence and a healthy amount of buying pushed prices higher, traders said. The Nikkei Index of 225 issues gained 782.63 points to 30956.27. The index advanced 483.81 points Wednesday. The market was closed Thursday and Friday for holidays.

The London market was closed yesterday for a holiday.

In Tokyo Tuesday, the Nikkei index closed the morning session at 30982.82, up 26.55 points.

Monday's volume on Tokyo's first section was estimated at 600 million shares, up from 482.4 million shares Wednesday. Advancers widely outpaced decliners 867 to 122, with 112 issues unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section jumped 48.68 points, or 2.17%, to 2296.49.

The second section index surged 48.40 points, or 1.35%, to close at 3628.36. Volume was estimated at nine million shares, down from 9.5 million shares Wednesday.

Traders said that the stabler yen encouraged solid buying yesterday, in particular by individual investors and Japanese fund managers.

Moreover, investors were cheered by Sunday's Group of Seven statement, which called for continued cooperation among members on exchange rates.

Mahmoud Ghaemmaghami, manager of research and a portfolio adviser at WestLB Securities Pacific Ltd., had predicted in his daily market outlook last week that the market would see a short rally yesterday, reasoning that the yen would strengthen on the G-7 summit.

The G-7 meeting focused on determining directions for the currencies, a topic that can be interpreted in different ways, Mr. Ghaemmaghami said.

For example, Stephen Good, a representative in Japanese equity sales at McIntosh Securities, said that investors may re-evaluate their yen-denominated assets in light of the weekend G-7 talks, reasoning that the U.S. won't likely tighten monetary policy in the short term.

Mr. Ghaemmaghami said that this scenario is quite possible. "If {global} interest rates remain at current levels, we could see some outflow of money from the U.S. to Japan," he added, "and because Japan {equity} has depreciated sharply, one can expect a reasonable return."

Although he doesn't expect interest rates to rise for at least one month, another Japanese official discount rate increase can be expected if West Germany decides to raise its official rate, Mr. Ghaemmaghami said.

Yesterday's market provided plenty of buying opportunities for short-term investors, such as Japanese dealers and fund managers, he said.

Much of the rally could be attributed to the fact that many dealers were holding flat positions ahead of the holidays, noted Yusuke Fujiwara, head of trading at Baring Securities Ltd. In addition, he said that approximately two trillion or three trillion yen ($12.65 billion to $18.98 billion) of pension money was making its way into the market.

Traders said short-term-oriented fund managers were interested mostly in largecap, undervalued issues, such as gas utilities, real estate and oil companies.

Mr. Ghaemmaghami said that undervalued issues with prices of less than 1000 yen received the most attention.

"The market is strong, and the fact that small stocks are coming back is quite healthy," Mr. Fujiwara said. But he also noted that the market lacks a leading sector.

He said nonferrous metal stocks, for one, are viewed as a good bet. But many sectors are undervalued, and while many stocks within those sectors will rise, many will not, he added.

Some companies linked to niche products are seen as favorites for foreign investors who are looking for longer-term securities, Mr. Ghaemmaghami said. But strong earnings forecasts, low price-earnings ratios, low or no bank borrowings, and high exports are among the things to check for shorter-term investments, he said.

In particular, those stocks that lead the market in their own products are usually the best bets, he added.

Meanwhile, electricals aren't seen as very profitable because of steady gains throughout the volatility of recent months, Mr. Fujiwara said.

Real estate companies were mostly higher yesterday. Mitsui Real Estate gained 70 yen to 1830 yen, while Mitsubishi Estate added 50 yen to 1660 yen. Sumitomo Realty jumped 40 yen to 1520 yen.

Railways also were higher. Tokyu gained 50 yen to 1650 yen, Keisei Electric added 80 yen to 1750 yen, and Odakyu rose 60 yen to 1240 yen.

Meanwhile, prices closed higher in Sydney, Hong Kong, Seoul, Manila, Wellington and Singapore, and were lower in Taipei.

Elsewhere, stocks closed higher in Paris, Frankfurt, Amsterdam, Stockholm, Milan; lower in Zurich; and mixed in Brussels. South African gold stocks firmed.
