If They Are Not Under the Collar, Workers Are Buttoning Their Lips

  • Author: Carrie Dolan
  • Date: 05/08/90

Inspired by events in Eastern Europe, Jack Hepfer decided to make a radical suggestion at Safeco Corp.'s annual meeting. Mr. Hepfer, a lawyer who recently retired after 28 years with the Seattle insurance concern, proposed that the company do away with its rule requiring male employees to wear white shirts every day.

"The Berlin Wall has come down," he says. "In time, maybe this wall will come down, too."

The call for change came after Mr. Hepfer did some research on Safeco's founder, H.K. Dent. He found that while Mr. Dent cautioned salesmen in the 1950s that flashy duds might "distract a prospect's attention from a sales talk . . . he felt there's nothing the matter with colored shirts."

The shirt issue "wasn't on my mind 24 hours a day," when he worked at Safeco, Mr. Hepfer says, but it bothered him that "outsiders scoff a little at the damn uniform." He also believes the policy is sexist. "I'm not looking for an ACLU case, but a woman can wear blouses of any color she wants."

The crowd at last week's annual meeting met Mr. Hepfer's suggestion with applause and laughter. "The place was rolling," says spokeswoman Pat Hillis. But the suggestion wasn't embraced by Safeco's chief executive officer, Bruce Maines, who said the code was part of the corporate culture. He said he owns 35 white shirts.

"We wouldn't want to throw out a 60-year tradition," says Ms. Hillis, adding that the company regards the policy "as a badge of honor." For years, she says, students at the nearby University of Washington "have referred to us as `the shirts.'"

Safeco, which employs about 5,300, requires all its male white-collar workers to be just that. Clerical employees have a less stringent dress code. Women can wear different hues as long as their attire is "professional." During the energy crunch of the 1970s, when thermostats were turned down, the company liberalized its dress code to allow women to wear slacks to the office.

Men can express their fashion flair with "conservative ties in good taste," and mavericks can even don summer weight, light blue suits. In addition, "we have absolutely no objection to striped shirts," says Ms. Hillis, "as long as they're white on white."
