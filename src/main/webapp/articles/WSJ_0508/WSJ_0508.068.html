Who's News: Dime Savings Bank Promotes Parsons To Chief Executive

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 05/08/90

NEW YORK -- Dime Savings Bank of New York has chosen an executive with a law background to lead the savings bank through rough times in the thrift industry. Dime named Richard D. Parsons, president of the thrift, to the added post of chief executive officer. Mr. Parsons, 42 years old, succeeds Harry W. Albright Jr. who will remain chairman.

Like many lenders, Dime is grappling with loan problems, because of poor underwriting of home loans between 1986 and 1988. But some analysts say that Dime, with $700 million in tangible capital and continued earnings, is the best positioned of the big East Coast thrifts to ride out the storm.

Some find it surprising that Dime, which has $11.7 billion in assets, would turn to someone with a background in corporate law and limited experience operating a thrift to guide it through this difficult period. Before joining Dime in 1988, Mr. Parsons was a partner in the New York law firm of Patterson, Belknap, Webb & Tyler. He formerly served as counsel to former New York Govs. Nelson A. Rockefeller and Malcolm Wilson.

So far, analysts aren't holding the lack of operating experience against him. Jonathan Gray, an analyst with Sanford C. Bernstein Co., said: "My first impression has been quite positive. He's highly intelligent and has a good grasp of operational and strategic matters. . . . Dime's loan problems pre-date his arrival."

Mr. Parsons said that he joined Dime because he was intrigued by the thought of "rolling up my sleeves" and running a business: "Many corporate lawyers spend their off hours wondering what it would be like to have their hands on the tiller."

For the next year or two, Mr. Parsons has nothing fancy planned for Dime. "At the moment, we're husbanding our capital. We're in a hunker-down mode," he said. "We're the only one of the five largest New York thrifts in full compliance with capital requirements."

Dime isn't merely treading water. Last year, it expanded in New Jersey buying Star Pointe Bank for $63 million, and Mr. Parsons would like to add some deposit-taking capability to its loan offices in Connecticut.

He says that residential mortgages will remain the cornerstone of Dime but that the thrift will expand its unsecured personal loan products, such as checking overdraft protection, to compete more vigorously with the big commercial banks. "We intend to provide the full-range of consumer financial services on both the deposit and credit sides," he said.

While Dime has indicated that an $80 million provision for possible bad loans in last year's fourth quarter will be sufficient to see it through its problems, analysts aren't so sure.

Peter Treadway, an analyst at Smith Barney, says: "They're not out of the woods yet. I think they'll have to make another big provision. But Dime has a lot of capital, and its core earnings are still positive. Every other major East Coast thrift has severe problems. Dime is the only one that's going to survive."
