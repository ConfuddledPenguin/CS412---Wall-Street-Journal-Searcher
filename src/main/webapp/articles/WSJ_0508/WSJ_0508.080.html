Ex-Bear Stearns Official Is Indicted For Obstructing Money-Laundering Probe

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 05/08/90

NEW YORK -- A federal grand jury indicted a former senior managing director and former head of Bear Stearns Cos.'s stock-loan department for allegedly obstructing the investigation of a money-laundering case. The indictment, handed up late last week against the former Bear Stearns executive, Joseph J. Stella, appears not to be directly related to the wide-ranging federal investigation of stock-loan abuses on Wall Street that surfaced in September. Bear Stearns wasn't involved in the alleged wrongdoing in the money-laundering case.

Stock-loan branches of big securities firms for a fee lend blocks of securities to investors and institutions, as well as match lenders and borrowers of stock. About $25 billion of stock was lent last year, according to industry executives.

As a senior executive who headed Bear Stearns's stock-loan department for the past 15 years, Mr. Stella would be well versed in Wall Street's secretive and lucrative stock-loan business, attorneys said. A broad investigation being conducted by the Manhattan U.S. attorney's office centers on the former head of Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc.'s stock-loan operations, Dennis T. Palmeri, who is believed to be cooperating with federal investigators, people in the securities industry said. Shearson is a unit of American Express Co.

In last week's indictment, the Manhattan federal grand jury charged that in 1980 and 1981 Mr. Stella and several others "laundered" more than $1.7 million in checks that allegedly reflected the proceeds of securities embezzled from Bankers Trust Co. and fees from stock-loan transactions that others sought to shield from income taxes. Bankers Trust is a unit of Bankers Trust New York Corp.

The indictment charged that Mr. Stella and several others -- including his brother, Fred Stella -- conspired to conceal from prosecutors and the grand jury their involvement in the check laundering and income-tax evasion during an investigation of the Bankers Trust case that began in 1983.

Although the indictment alleges that the defendants laundered the cash, they were only charged with counts relating to obstructing the investigation.

Joseph Stella, who resigned from Bear Stearns last month, couldn't be reached for comment; his attorney didn't return phone calls.

In a statement, Bear Stearns said it accepted Mr. Stella's resignation on April 26 "for alleged personal activities totally unrelated to Bear Stearns." Bear Stearns said that was the day Mr. Stella told the firm of his possible indictment. On the same day, Bear Stearns named Pasquale CeStaro III as the managing director in charge of its stock-loan area.

In addition to Joseph and Fred Stella, the indictment named Roger C. Capozzi Sr. and Joseph A. Pontarelli, who allegedly cashed checks for Fred Stella through a Brooklyn clothing-embroidery business and a Manhattan liquor store and check-cashing business, respectively, the indictment says. Fred Stella and Messrs. Capozzi and Pontarelli couldn't be reached for comment.

The indictment alleges, among other things, that Mr. Capozzi and Joseph and Fred Stella falsely told investigators that fictitious or deceased people were responsible for crucial aspects of the money-laundering activities.

Mr. Capozzi and Joseph and Fred Stella were each charged with one count of conspiracy to obstruct justice; Mr. Capozzi also was charged with three counts of perjury and three counts of obstructing justice. Fred Stella was also charged with five counts of obstructing justice. Mr. Pontarelli was charged with filing a false income-tax return and with income-tax evasion.

If convicted, Joseph Stella, 46 years old, faces a maximum of five years in prison and a $250,000 fine; Fred Stella, a 50-year-old New York lawyer, faces a maximum of 30 years in prison and fines of $1.5 million.

Mr. Capozzi, 68, faces a maximum of 35 years in prison and fines of $1.75 million. Mr. Pontarelli, 47, faces a maximum of eight years in prison and fines of $500,000.

The four are scheduled to be arraigned today before U.S. District Judge Charles S. Haight.
