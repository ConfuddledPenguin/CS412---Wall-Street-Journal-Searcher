Credit Markets: Bond Market Trading Grinds Nearly to a Halt As Investors Wait for Huge Treasury Auctions

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 05/08/90

NEW YORK -- Bond market trading nearly ground to a halt yesterday as investors waited anxiously for the Treasury Department's enormous quarterly bond auction. Trading often slows considerably ahead of the multibillion dollar bond sale, partly because of investor concerns about the market's ability to absorb such a large supply of new securities.

Moreover, the auctions are like big-stakes poker games for bond dealers who bid against each other to buy the government paper. For investors, buying bonds before government bond auctions can be dangerous since prices sometimes gyrate wildly depending upon how dealers play the game.

"Nobody wants to make a strong move," ahead of the quarterly auction, said James R. Capra, a senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc.

The Treasury is scheduled to sell $30.5 billion of notes and bonds this week, the proceeds of which will be used to refinance Uncle Sam's maturing debt and pay the bills created by the federal budget deficit. Today, $10.5 billion of three-year notes will be sold, followed by $10 billion of 10-year notes tomorrow and $10 billion of 30-year bonds Thursday.

In trading yesterday, the benchmark 30-year bond ended less than 1/4 point lower, or $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The yield rose slightly to 8.84%.

Bond dealers said the losses reflected profit-taking during overnight trading in Tokyo that carried over into U.S. trading. The dealers said prices barely budged the rest of the day.

"All of the declines occurred overseas and nothing happened in the U.S.," said William Sullivan, money market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

Today's three-year note auction is expected to go smoothly, with strong demand from individual investors and from domestic commercial banks. The banks "are flush with funds and are trying to stay away from troubled {leveraged buy-out loans} and problem real estate investments. Treasury securities are plain vanilla, but in this environment they look more like the Haagen-Dazs of choices," said Robert Brusca, chief economist at Nikko Securities Co. International.

Individuals usually buy at auctions through so-called "noncompetitive bids," which are expected to total as much as $1.75 billion. That would be up from $1.19 billion at the last three-year note sale.

There is mild concern about Japanese participation at the auction. Mr. Brusca expects the Japanese to buy between 25% and 30% of the notes and bonds. Their purchases, he said, are likely to be concentrated in the 10-year and 30-year issues.

Although Treasury bond yields have eroded since Friday's bond market rally pushed yields to their lowest levels in weeks, they are still well above levels at the start of the year. That should make yields attractive to Japanese investors, said Mr. Brusca.

Japanese investors typically are sizable buyers at Treasury refundings, sometimes purchasing as much as 40%. At the last refunding in February, Japanese institutional investors purchased an estimated 30% of the $30 billion of securities sold.

In when-issued activity yesterday, the new three-year notes were quoted at a yield of 8.78%, the 10-year notes at 8.84% and the 30-year bonds at 8.82%.

Foreign Bonds

The Canadian government bond market, which has been badly hurt in recent months, rallied for the third consecutive session with some issues rising as much as 2 1/4 points.

Canada's 10.5% benchmark 10-year note was quoted late yesterday at a price of 96 to yield about 11.17%, up more than one point from Friday. The benchmark 20-year bond jumped 2 1/8 point to 89.50 to yield 10.76%.

Some traders warned that the turnaround reflects technical factors and is not a sign that the market is on the road to recovery -- at least not yet.

"We did see some buying in the market, but in no way does that mean the downward trend is reversing," said Alberto Franco, a Canadian bond trader at Salomon Brothers Inc. in New York. He said that while "Canadian bonds seem very cheap," the securities are likely to fall further in the weeks ahead until a dispute between the Canadian government and the province of Quebec is resolved.

The dispute, which has created jitters in Canada's financial markets, involves a proposed constitutional amendment known as the Meech Lake accord. If approved, the accord, negotiated in 1987, would recognize Quebec as a "distinct society" within Canada. Many Quebec politicians say they will seek independence from Canada if the accord isn't ratified by June 23.

Analysts said concerns about the Meech Lake accord were largely responsible for the Canadian bond market's poor showing. J.P. Morgan Securities Inc.'s Canadian bond index has posted a total return of a negative 7% so far this year.

Meanwhile, Canadian bond traders say spreads between active Canadian bonds and comparable U.S. government bonds will remain wide. Mr. Franco said yields on 10-year Canadian notes are about 2.40 percentage points higher than yields on 10-year Treasury notes. While that's a little less than a few weeks ago when the spread was three percentage points, it's still wider than last year when spreads ranged between one and 1.5 percentage points.

In late trading yesterday, Canada's 10.5% benchmark 10-year note was quoted at a price of 96 to yield about 11.17%.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 96 11/32 to yield 8.84% compared with 96 19/32 to yield 8.81%. The latest 10-year notes ended at 97 19/32 to yield 8.85% compared with 97 24/32 to yield 8.83%.

The Treasury sold $16.8 billion of bills yesterday at the government's regular weekly bill auction. The average rate on new three-month Treasury bills was 7.79% while the average rate on new six-month bills was 7.84%.

The average rates were down from last week's auction when they were 7.91% and 8.03%, respectively.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities came under pressure from rumored sales by the Resolution Trust Corp. and speculation that the RTC will liquidate a considerable amount of mortgages during the current quarter. Active issues ended 1/8 to 6/32 lower.

There was talk that RTC sold $250 million of Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 8 1/2% securities and as many as $300 million of principal-only stripped mortgage securities.

On Friday, the RTC, the government conservator for failed thrifts, was rumored to have sold $500 million of mortgage securities, consisting of about $350 million of Government National Mortgage Association 9% securities and $150 million of Freddie Mac 8 1/2% securities.

The mortgage market was spooked because the RTC has said it wants to resolve the situation of some 141 thrifts by the end of the quarter. These thrift resolutions presumably will mean sizable mortgage-backed sales.

The prospect of RTC sales comes at time a when the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency is weighing the imposition of new regulations that would clamp down on bank purchases of risky derivative mortgage securities. Banks now own an estimated $100 billion of mortgage derivatives and a share of those holdings could run afoul of any regulations from the Comptroller's office.

Ginnie Mae 9% issue for May delivery ended at 94 14/32, down 6/32; its 9 1/2% issue at 96 7/8, down 6/32; and its 10% securities at 99 10/32, down 4/32.

Municipal Issues

Municipal bonds were mixed, with some issues off as much as 1/4 point in late dealings and others up as much as 1/4.

"People have been burned trying to short it {the market} down here," so there was a little retail interest, said a New York municipal bond specialist. Whether that buying interest will be sustained depends on what effect this week's Treasury auctions will have on the government bond market, he added.

In light new-issues dealings, a John Nuveen & Co. group set the preliminary pricing for an issue of $53.7 million Mississippi Hospital Equipment and Facilities Authority revenue bonds. The bonds were tentatively priced to yield from 6.40% in 1991 to 7.62% in 2013.

In secondary action, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bonds of 2018 finished 1/4 higher at 96 1/4, yielding 7.51%.

Corporate Issues

Corporate issuers trickled back to the new-issue market and offered $500 million in new debt.

General Motors Acceptance Corp., the financing arm of General Motors Corp., and Unocal Corp. each tapped the credit markets, separately offering $250 million of new debt.

In trading, investment-grade corporate issues ended about 1/4 point lower.

In the junk-bond market, prices of TW Holdings Inc. bonds rose on what analysts said was a relatively positive earnings report.

TW Holdings, a fast-food restaurant operator, reported a first-quarter loss of $46.1 million compared with a profit of $8.3 milion a year earlier, but other numbers were more encouraging. Sam DeRosa-Farag, an analyst at First Boston Corp., said TW Holdings' sales increased by 7.6% and its cash flow by 11%.

TW's 15% 12-year subordinated debentures rose one point to 96 1/2.

By comparison, most other junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, were unchanged.
