Monday's Markets: Stock Prices Continue To Gain --- Demand for Bonds Eases; Dollar Falls Against Yen, Mark

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 05/08/90

Stock prices posted a sixth consecutive gain, but demand for government bonds eased in the wake of last Friday's big rally. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 11.26 to 2721.62 in light trading. Long-term Treasury bonds gave up modest amounts of their big Friday gains, and the dollar eased against most major currencies.

Analysts said stock prices were buoyed by the perception that interest rates peaked last week with yields of slightly more than 9% on long-term Treasury bonds. With the economy growing modestly and expectations that interest rates will decline, albeit slowly, in coming weeks, investors have been buying stocks gingerly.

Technology issues have performed especially well in recent days, another sign of confidence among investors that the economy is on a steady course. Motorola yesterday climbed 1 7/8 to a new 52-week high of 72 1/2.

While stock market investors were enjoying the possibility of declining interest rates, bond investors will find little to enjoy until this week's record $30.5 billion quarterly auction of new Treasury debt is behind them. The auction begins today.

The prospect of such a huge supply of new debt coming to the market took a toll on bond prices yesterday after an ebullient rally Friday that carried the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond up more than 1 1/2 points, or more than $15 for each $1,000 of face amount.

The rally Friday had its roots in the government's April employment report, which showed a gain of only 64,000 jobs in the nonfarm sector instead of the 400,000 or so that many economists had estimated. The report suggested that the economy isn't nearly as robust as many had thought and thus not as likely to produce rising inflation.

Although some traders said bond prices may decline further during the auction, they're hoping that the market will get another boost this Friday when the government reports its producer price index for April. Although the index, a measure of inflation at the wholesale level, is expected to be up 0.2%, that small increase comes on the heels of a 0.2% drop in March and a flat February. Many analysts think the producer price index is a better measure of future inflation trends than the consumer price index, which rose 0.5% in March.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose in thin trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 132.8 million shares. Advancing issues on the Big Board were ahead of losers 943 to 571.

Bond prices dipped modestly. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year issue ended less than 1/4 point lower, or $2.50 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the issue rose to 8.84%.

The dollar fell. In late New York trading the currency was quoted at 1.6558 marks and 158.19 yen compared to 1.6662 marks and 158.25 yen Friday.
