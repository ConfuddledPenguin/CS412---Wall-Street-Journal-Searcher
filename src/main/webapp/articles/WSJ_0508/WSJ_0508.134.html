Lomas Takes Charge Totaling $505.5 Million --- Firm Admits Its Portfolio Of Real Estate Is Shakier Than Earlier Estimated

  • Author: Michael Allen
  • Date: 05/08/90

DALLAS -- Lomas Financial Corp., admitting that its real-estate portfolio is far shakier than it had previously estimated, said it took a $505.5 million special charge in its fiscal third quarter ended March 31, resulting in a $500 million loss for the period. The big financial-services and real-estate concern said about $286 million of the reserve, or 56%, stems from its real-estate holdings, while an additional $74 million reflects a write-down of the company's short-term construction and development loans.

Jess Hay, chairman and chief executive officer, said the move -- which comes atop a similar $253 million special charge last July -- reflects "the continuing weakening of the country's real-estate markets" and a "severe" constriction of loans available to real-estate investors. "Liquidity within the industry is at one of its lowest points since World War II," he added.

Lomas said another $49 million of the total stems from the estimated cost of completing the company's new mortgage loan servicing system and related write-offs, while the remaining $97 million covers, among other things, investments in discontinued subsidiary operations. The company added that it also took a "normal" quarterly provision for possible losses of $7.7 million.

Analysts were divided on whether even such a massive hit will be enough to clean up Lomas's tarnished balance sheet. Some investors have accused the company, which is operating under federal bankruptcy-court protection from creditors, of overpaying for certain assets it acquired during an expansion binge a few years ago, and then exaggerating their true worth in financial statements.

Leigh Walzer, an analyst with R.D. Smith & Co., which trades securities of troubled companies, said the write-off was "in the right ballpark" and should dispel market concerns about disparities between the company's book value and market value.

But Jay Lustig, an analyst at Drake Capital Securities Inc., Santa Monica, Calif., disputed Lomas's estimates. "In my opinion, I think they've got at least another $500 million to go as far as total asset write-downs," he said.

Mr. Lustig added that he expects holders of Lomas's subordinated debt to "probably wind up with something less than 10 cents on the dollar," and for equity holders to be wiped out.

The news, which came out after the close of trading, didn't affect the price of the company's securities. In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Lomas stock closed unchanged at 56.25 cents. Meanwhile, subordinated debt traded at about 26 cents on the dollar.

Mr. Hay said that, despite the bad news, "I continue to believe that we're going to emerge with a viable, profitable operation" focusing on mortgage loan servicing. He said the company hopes to complete a reorganization plan in late June.

In the fiscal third quarter, revenue fell 16% to $67.8 million from $80.7 million. In the year-ago period, the company reported a net loss of $12.4 million. For the latest nine months, Lomas reported a net loss of $651.4 million on revenue of $220.1 million, against net income of $5.7 million on revenue of $256.8 million a year before.
