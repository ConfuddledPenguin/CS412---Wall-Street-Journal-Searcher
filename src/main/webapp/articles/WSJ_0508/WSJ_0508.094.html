Ingersoll Extends Junk Bonds Offer For a Second Time

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 05/08/90

NEW YORK -- Faced with a continuing boycott by bondholders, Ingersoll Newspapers Inc. extended for the second time in two weeks its tender offer to holders of an affiliate's junk bonds. The latest extension is raising questions about whether the company, which is controlled by Ralph Ingersoll II, may be forced to sell more of its newspaper properties to avoid default on its heavy debt load.

Through his various companies, Mr. Ingersoll sold 10 newspapers in January to pay off bank debt, has other newspapers up for sale, and on April 25 closed the St. Louis Sun. The metropolitan newspaper cost him at least $25 million since its launch last September.

More than likely, Mr. Ingersoll will be forced to sweeten his offer to as much as 80% or more of the bonds' face value; the bonds have traded, in small volume, between 55% and 60%. Ingersoll Newspapers is attempting to buy back about $240 million of junk bonds at a deep discount to their face value. Ingersoll offered 55% for the senior bonds and 25% for the junior bonds.

Mr. Ingersoll's Community Newspapers Inc., issuer of the bonds, publishes daily newspapers in Ohio, Connecticut, New Jersey, Rhode Island and New York. Mr. Ingersoll has attempted to buy Community's bonds through his other holding company, Ingersoll Newspapers. Mr. Ingersoll controls the various companies, including a newspaper company in Britain, through Ingersoll Publications Co., based in Princeton, N.J.

Bondholders, who total fewer than 35 institutions, have once again organized a total boycott of Mr. Ingersoll's offer. Since no bondholder accepted the offer for 13% "reset" bonds and discount debentures by midnight EDT last Friday, Ingersoll said it will extend the offer to 12:01 a.m. EDT Saturday.

Ingersoll, however, said Community Newspapers will, at the request of bondholders, begin discussions for the first time with them about the tender offers.

It is uncertain whether such talks will result in substantial changes in the tender offer. Ingersoll executives wouldn't comment and executives from Goldman, Sachs & Co., which is managing the offer, couldn't be reached for comment.

According to some bondholders, Chase Manhattan Bank is funding Ingersoll's tender offer. A Chase spokesman, however, would say only: "Chase is continuing to have discussions with Ingersoll about financing. We are having discussions on a variety of Ingersoll's financing needs."

With each extension of the tender offer, Mr. Ingersoll's newspaper company nears an important deadline -- July 7. By that date, Mr. Ingersoll must have reset interest payments for about $125 million of the bonds to make up for the drop in the bonds' price.

The bondholders are well organized and so far have remained unified, hoping to force changes in the tender offer. Bondholders object not only to what has been referred to as Mr. Ingersoll's "low-ball price," but also to the structure of the offer. James Rubin of Sass Lamle Rubin & Co., has said, under the offer, bonds outstanding would have severely reduced protective covenants that would render them worthless in certain scenarios.

According to bondholders, the group has retained Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. as independent advisers. Smith Barney, which couldn't be reached for comment, is expected to advise the bondholders on the values of Mr. Ingersoll's newspaper properties.
