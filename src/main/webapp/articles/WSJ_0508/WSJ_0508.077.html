International: Big West German Banks Jockey to Seize Positions of Strength Across East Border

  • Author: Terence Roth
  • Date: 05/08/90

A group of off-duty Soviet soldiers watch curiously as a West German assembly crew uses a crane hoist to fit together the shiny, container-like pieces of Commerzbank AG's first East German branch. The Halle branch is the first of 50 to be set up by year end. With monetary union set for July 2, West Germany's three largest banks are jockeying for position in East Germany's fertile market for Western-style banking. Sometimes the contestants can become testy.

The new branches sprouting in every corner of East Germany can't open for business until East Germany grants operating licenses to foreign banks, a move expected well ahead of the first Monday in July. By that morning, West Germany's major banks want to open their doors in cities from Rostock to Dresden to soak up more than 100 billion marks ($60.06 billion) in potential deposits.

At stake is a new domestic market with enormous growth potential in retail and, later, investment banking. For West German banks forced to refinance in their notoriously overbanked market, unification means expansion at home rather than increasingly risky ventures abroad.

East Germany's feeble economy will delay large increases in bank assets. But bank analysts believe the first few months will be crucial in securing market shares that can grow only as West German aid and investment pour into East Germany.

So far, it has been an unequal contest. Commerzbank Chairman Walter Seipp said he was assured earlier this year by senior officials of East Germany's State Bank that West Germany's leading banks would be treated equally when parceling out the State Bank's retail branches for new tenants from the West. Somehow, it didn't work out that way.

On April 1, East Germany's bank overhaul broke up the State Bank's banking monopoly. In the process, the central bank's operations were separated from commercial and cooperative banking functions. The move created Deutsche Kreditbank as the country's leading retail bank.

The competition among West German banks began as Deutsche Bank AG nettled rivals by signing a letter of intent with the newly formed Kreditbank to form a joint venture that would give Deutsche Bank 100 branches in East Germany. Deutsche Bank last week announced it plans to have 100 branches up and running July 2.

Under the proposed terms of the agreement, Deutsche Bank, West Germany's largest bank, has a 49% stake in the joint venture and its 100 branches, with Kreditbank retaining 51%. A Deutsche Bank official, however, said "this is merely a transitional arrangement."

Not to be outdone, second-ranking Dresdner Bank AG confirmed a week ago that it is angling for a similar agreement with Kreditbank.

East Germany's creaky banking system, knowing its chances to survive independently are slim, has sought alliances with the big banks in Frankfurt. Analysts reason that when it came to choosing partners, East German officials instinctively opted for the largest banks in the field.

Thus Commerzbank, a distant third in assets, came away empty-handed. "The competition is pretty intense," said Thomas Albrecht, a German bank analyst for UBS-Phillips & Drew. "Commerzbank must be frustrated because Deutsche and Dresdner will have a presence all over the place, and fairly quickly."

Speaking with journalists in Leipzig, East Germany, Commerzbank's Mr. Seipp aimed a jab at his biggest competitor, which had been keeping its talks with Kreditbank under a cloak of secrecy for several months.

"At first glance, it might look like Deutsche Bank is getting a faster start," Mr. Seipp said. "But if you look at the former buildings of the State Bank that they're getting, I'm not so sure. The old buildings can be a deterrent for many customers, who could be reminded of the old system. We can offer them a fresh start."

Commerzbank's Horst Ahrens is happy with the company's progress in Halle. "We are glad to have so much space in front of the bank," said Mr. Ahrens. "On July 2, we'll probably have thousands lined up in front."

A Deutsche Bank official isn't so sure. "It doesn't hurt to be optimistic," he said, adding, "This is going to be a very difficult process. We are literally starting at zero."

Many of Deutsche Bank's future branches are in desperate need of a facelift. Down the street from the pile of containers that are four weeks away from becoming a new Commerzbank outlet, a soot-covered Deutsche Kreditbank branch doesn't gain in charm as one enters a foreboding, ill-lighted lobby.

After 40 years of rigid central planning, East German enterprises have little in their books that approximates Western accounting standards. Most companies have never had to balance both sides of a ledger, and their fixed assets often were artificially inflated as part of the socialist success formula.

Said Commerzbank's Mr. Seipp: "We simply can't apply our own standards on assessing creditworthiness. We will have to work on trust."
