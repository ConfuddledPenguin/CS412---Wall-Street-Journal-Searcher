Politics & Policy: Senate Democrats Ask Public Funding Of All Campaigns

  • Author: Jill Abramson
  • Date: 05/04/90

WASHINGTON -- Senate Democrats unveiled a campaign-finance proposal that would take most of the special-interest money out of politics and replace it with public funds. "We have to slow down the money chase," said Sen. David Boren (D., Okla.), an architect of the plan.

The Democratic plan would require Senate candidates to adhere to spending limits, based on the voting-age population of their state. In return, they would receive a taxpayer-financed subsidy to run their campaigns, as well as reduced mail and broadcast costs.

The Democratic plan would also prohibit contributions to U.S. Senate candidates by political action committees. This proposal follows the lead of Republican senators, who earlier this week submitted a campaign-finance plan that would abolish PACs altogether.

It was unclear yesterday how many Democrats had actually signed onto the plan for public funds, which will be vigorously opposed by Republicans. Majority Leader George Mitchell said he expected a "substantial majority" of Democrats to embrace it.

The plan would cost at least $120 million per election cycle, according to estimates by Democratic Senate aides. The funds would come from a voluntary taxpayer checkoff similar to the system used for financing the presidential election. The checkoff probably would be increased to $3 per individual from $1 to cover the cost of congressional campaigns.

But Republican critics immediately contended that the plan would be far more costly. If House races were added, Sen. Mitch McConnell (R., Ky.), who led a filibuster against campaign-finance legislation two years ago, predicted the Democratic plan would cost $400 million. "They surely must know this isn't going anywhere," Sen. McConnell said yesterday.

"Their plan sounds more like a campaign rip-off than campaign reform," said Republican Sen. Pete Wilson of California.

Many Republicans also oppose spending caps because most incumbents are Democrats, and challengers usually need to raise more money to win an election.

To be eligible for the public funds under the Democratic proposal, Senate candidates would have to raise a threshold level of private funds (10% of the general election spending limit in their state, at least half of which must come from in-state individuals in amounts of $250 or less). Candidates also would be permitted to exceed the spending limits by 25% with small contributions ($100 or less) raised within the state.

In an effort to woo moderate Republicans, the Democratic plan embraces broadcast provisions endorsed by Sen. John Danforth (R., Mo.). Candidates would receive vouchers for prime-time television advertisements of one to five minutes during the eight weeks leading up to a general election. They also would be required to appear on camera at the end of their ads to take responsibility for the content. These proposals would curb negative, "hit-and-run" campaign ads, Sen. Boren said.

The Democrats also proposed clamping down on large contributions made by wealthy individuals to state political parties, known as soft money, which isn't restricted by federal laws.

Several public-interest groups, including Common Cause and Public Citizen, praised the Democratic plan.

But almost everyone predicts difficult negotiations ahead. "This can lead to a new system that fundamentally changes the way business is conducted in Washington," said Common Cause's Fred Wertheimer, "or it can lead to stalemate and status quo, a disastrous result for the country."
