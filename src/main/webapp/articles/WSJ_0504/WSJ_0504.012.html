First Brands Posts 83% Drop in Net For Its 3rd Period

  • Author: Udayan Gupta
  • Date: 05/04/90

DANBURY, Conn. -- First Brands Corp. reported an 83% decline in profit for its fiscal third quarter and warned that earnings for its year ending June 30 could be lower than expected. The manufacturer of such consumer brands as Glad, Prestone and STP, cited difficulties in its antifreeze business. Fiscal 1990 per-share profit "may be slightly lower than expectations of $2.50," compared with the year-earlier $1.97, when profit totaled $61 million.

Profit for the third quarter, ended March 31, fell to $2.7 million, or 12 cents a share, from $16.1 million, or 80 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue fell 17% to $221.3 million from $267.5 million.

Fiscal 1989 figures included $20.7 million in revenue from the company's European businesses, which have since been sold, the company said. Adjusting for the sale, quarterly profit rose fivefold to $3.5 million, or 16 cents a share, from $693,000 or three cents a share. Revenue fell 10% to $221.3 million from $246.8 million.

First Brands said that lower-than-expected antifreeze sales, primarily caused by price uncertainty, had reduced overall sales below the prior year and plan. And because there were added pressures to reduce antifreeze prices further, it expected profit margins for the rest of the year to be lower as well.

In addition to its problems with antifreeze, First Brands also has been facing criticism over the environmental claims the company has made about some of its products. It also has had problems with one new-product introduction.

The Federal Trade Commission and several state attorneys general have been investigating claims by First Brands and other plastic manufacturers that their products are degradable and therefore better for the environment. The inquiries have had no business impact and aren't expected to have any such impact in the future, said Donald DeSantis, vice president and chief financial officer.

Earlier this year, First Brands recalled 200,000 cans of a new car-wax product, after receiving three reports that cans ruptured after being weakened from the inside by corrosion. The product, Simoniz Quick Gloss, which retails for $4, is a spray-on shine for use at auto shows and in showrooms. The product accounts for a very small portion of sales and hasn't affected revenue, Mr. DeSantis said.
