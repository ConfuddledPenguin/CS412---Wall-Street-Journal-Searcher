Farah Inc. Is Sued By Largest Holder, Its Ex-Chairman

  • Author: Alfredo Corchado
  • Date: 05/04/90

EL PASO, Texas -- William F. Farah, who was removed as chairman of Farah Inc. last week, sued the company, Farah directors and top officers to prevent them from cutting him off from the clothing manufacturer he helped manage for 53 years. Mr. Farah, a company director and the company's largest stockholder with one million shares, or about a 15% stake, is asking an El Paso county court for a temporary restraining order to keep other board members and Farah's top management from restricting his role as a board member and company consultant.

Calling his fellow board members "spiteful conspirators" who want to destroy him financially, Mr. Farah charges that board members have threatened to terminate his three-year consulting services contract. That contract, which has two years remaining, pays him an annual fee of $365,000 and provides Mr. Farah with an office and secretary. According to a letter attached to the lawsuit, the board unanimously decided at a March 13 meeting that Mr. Farah and his secretary would vacate the premises by May 1.

Mr. Farah resigned as president and chief executive officer of the company last year and was succeeded by Richard C. Allender.

Mr. Farah wasn't available for comment yesterday.

Last week, the board removed Mr. Farah as chairman after he apparently wrote letters to directors urging them to either replace current management or resign. Mr. Farah is said to be unhappy with the direction of the company. He was succeeded by Thomas G. Wyman, a recently elected board member who has a 5% stake in Farah.

In an interview conducted before the suit was filed, Mr. Allender declined to discuss the company's relationship with Mr. Farah. But when asked about Mr. Farah's pending lawsuit, Mr. Allender said: "I can't afford to be concerned about what Willie {Mr. Farah} may or may not do. I have a business to run."

According to the lawsuit, Messrs. Allender and Wyman interpreted Mr. Farah's consulting contract to mean that Mr. Farah should provide advice only when Mr. Allender requests it.

In a letter to Mr. Allender filed with court documents, Mr. Farah said he would be "derelict in my duty if, as you suggest, I limit my efforts, inquiries and activities to mere attendance of board meetings."
