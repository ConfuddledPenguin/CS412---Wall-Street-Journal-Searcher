Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Three Stock Exchanges Mount Sharply Aggressive Ad Campaigns

  • Author: William Power
  • Date: 05/04/90

Trade stocks here] Trade stocks here] For the first time, the nation's three major stock markets will be mounting big advertising campaigns simultaneously in which they boast about themselves and belittle their competition. The campaigns reflect how competitive Wall Street has gotten as trading volume has shriveled since the 1987 stock market crash.

Stock exchanges have run ads for some time, of course. But there are several twists to the current binge -- including first-ever television ads by the Nasdaq over-the-counter market, in which it declares the high-technology Nasdaq is "The Stock Market for the Next 100 Years." Meanwhile, the New York Stock Exchange (the stock market for the past 200 years) has raised eyebrows among competitors with a renewed aggressiveness in its ads, which focus on swaying not just investors and corporate America, but financial journalists as well.

The American Stock Exchange, a distant third behind the others, joined the ad frenzy this week by announcing it had retained an agency to launch a major campaign of its own.

"Today, the exchanges are being very competitive," says the New York Stock Exchange's top public-relations man, Richard Torrenzano. "You want to make sure people are aware of what's going on, the kinds of companies listed with you and why they want to list."

The Big Board has two sets of ads. One set includes corporate chieftains, including such Nasdaq "defectors" as Warren Buffett of Berkshire Hathaway Inc., saying nice things about the Big Board. The other campaign is a copy-heavy description of the exchange and its workings that is meant to establish it firmly as America's main market. This campaign has even run in journalist trade publications, as part of what the exchange says is a bid to sway "opinion leaders."

The Big Board ads are striking because they specifically criticize the OTC market, and even take shots at regional stock exchanges that have been eating into the Big Board's market share. Some of the Big Board ads contain what amounts to a warning against trading stocks on regional exchanges. "I don't know why they have to do that," protests the Philadelphia Stock Exchange's president, Nicholas Giordano, who says all stock exchanges should cooperate to try to lure back small investors.

But the main PR fight going on so far is between the Big Board and the sprawling Nasdaq market. By far the two biggest stock markets in the U.S., they have fought for years for listings of prized companies. The Big Board would love to snare such brand-name Nasdaq companies as Apple Computer, MCI Communications and Intel, and Nasdaq knows it.

Nasdaq has three TV ads so far, produced by the Messner Vetere Berger Carey Schmetterer agency, that have run in the New York City area starting with the Masters golf tournament last month. Its print ads include testimonials from the heads of Apple and MCI, and a new one will feature the Ben & Jerry's Homemade Inc. ice cream entrepreneurs.

One of the TV ads is a downright Big Board basher. With a solemn soundtrack of medieval chanting, the ad shows a slow-motion scene of a trading floor in which maniacal-looking brokers are waving their arms and barking orders; one broker appears to be foaming at the mouth. The narration: "Is there a rational alternative to the traditional trading floor? Yes. Not having one." Fade in on the Nasdaq logo.

Brian Holland, senior vice president of marketing for the National Association of Securities Dealers, which runs Nasdaq, says that despite appearances, his market isn't personally attacking the Big Board. It's merely trying to "build awareness in the public" that the Nasdaq market is available, and more importantly, that the Nasdaq system is separate from the OTC marketplace as a whole. "People don't realize, based on our research, that there are other ways to trade" besides the traditional stock exchange, Mr. Holland says.

As for the Big Board's ads, Mr. Holland, like the Philadelphia exchange's Mr. Giordano, says the Big Board is "quite strident in their attacks. Personally, I think we're all in this together."

Is the stately Big Board really strident? Mr. Torrenzano of the Big Board says such a complaint must mean his exchange's ads, designed by its longtime agency, WPP Group's Lord, Geller, Federico, Einstein Inc. unit, are "very powerful," as they should be.

The Big Board hasn't advertised on TV so far, says Mr. Torrenzano, because it doesn't think the medium is worth the money. But the exchange doesn't rule out TV ads in the future.

Both the Big Board and Nasdaq campaigns will cost each market millions of dollars. Neither will say exactly how much. Meanwhile, the American Stock Exchange has just hired the two-year-old agency Follis & Verdi to prepare what the exchange calls a "high-gear" ad effort of its own.

The American exchange's message will be that it's the "marketplace for the '90s," says exchange Chairman James R. Jones, an ex-congressman who knows about swaying public opinion. The Amex used to be a strong challenger for the Big Board, but these days it relies on its strong niche in the options market to maintain its status as a major exchange.

The Big Board and Nasdaq are eagerly awaiting what the Follis & Verdi agency comes up with. The agency -- which used to work for the Commodity Exchange and is responsible for the current, powerful National Basketball Association ads about child abuse -- isn't so sure that ads such as the Big Board's and Nasdaq's are as good as they could be.

"CEOs are looking for service, visibility and market quality," says Ellis Verdi, Follis & Verdi president. "I feel strongly there are a few significant messages that need to be made, and very few have been made and made effectively yet."

TV News at Supermarkets

Turner Broadcasting System's Cable News Network & Headline News and Act Media Inc., as expected, entered into a partnership to supply supermarkets with television newscasts at checkout lines.

The advertiser-supported service, "The Checkout Channel," will be test marketed in 12 stores in six cities from August through October. Representatives of Act Media, a unit of Heritage Media, and Turner Broadcasting said the channel will be available nationally in 5,000 supermarkets by the first quarter of 1991.

The programming will be eight minutes in length with two-and-a-half minutes of commercials. The supermarkets will receive TV sets and satellite dishes and will share in the ad revenue. The news can be updated every eight minutes.
