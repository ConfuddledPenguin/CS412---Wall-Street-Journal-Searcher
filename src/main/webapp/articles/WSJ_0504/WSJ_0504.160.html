Big Board Said To Seek Big Fine Against Drexel

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 05/04/90

NEW YORK -- The New York Stock Exchange is seeking to impose a $25 million fine on Drexel Burnham Lambert, even though the securities firm is liquidating itself, people familiar with the proceedings said. The Big Board also has indicated that it may bring separate disciplinary proceedings against Drexel Chief Executive Frederick H. Joseph and other top officers of the firm in connection with the securities fraud and other crimes that Drexel and its former junk-bond chief, Michael Milken, have already admitted to, the people said.

Among other charges, the exchange is considering disciplining Mr. Joseph and other Drexel officials for allegedly failing to properly supervise Mr. Milken and others, the people said. Such proceedings can result in fines and suspension from the Big Board.

The Big Board has stepped up its investigation of Drexel in recent weeks and has subpoenaed several individuals to testify about wrongdoing by Drexel and Mr. Milken, according to individuals familiar with the matter.

Securities-industry experts said that a $25 million fine would be the largest ever imposed on a securities firm by the Big Board. In 1985, after E.F. Hutton & Co. pleaded guilty to felonies in a check-kiting scheme, it was fined $400,000 by the exchange. If the Big Board does fine Drexel, it would likely be the first time the exchange has ever fined a securities firm that is in liquidation, as Drexel is.

Drexel Burnham Lambert Group filed for bankruptcy-law protection Feb. 13; its broker-dealer subsidiary, Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., is liquidating itself.

While experts said that the Big Board has the right to sanction Drexel, some questioned the exchange's timing in doing so now. "It seems like they're beating a dead horse," said Alan Bromberg, who teaches securities law at Southern Methodist University's law school. "I can't see any remedial purpose to it. Nobody thinking about doing Drexel all over again will be deterred by this particular fine."

Harvey Pitt, a Washington securities lawyer, said that to fine Drexel now would be akin to "locking the barn door after the horses have already gotten out." He said, however, that "The failure-to-supervise issue has been a significant question" and one that may be more relevant because many of Drexel's senior officials may remain in the securities industry.

The Big Board first privately brought disciplinary charges against Drexel Burnham Lambert Group and its broker dealer subsidiary last November; charges haven't been brought against any of the firm's officials. Drexel is entitled to fight those charges through a process that includes a hearing.

In a meeting late last year, Big Board officials said they wished to fine Drexel $50 million, people familiar with the matter said. But after Drexel's bankruptcy-law filing, Big Board officials told the firm that the exchange would be willing to accept half that amount.

A Drexel spokesman issued the following statement yesterday: "We understand the stock exchange's need to project an image of being a strong self-regulator. However, that should be balanced with a sense of fairness in evaluating the company's and management's behavior. The standard ought to be `Was Drexel's compliance as good, or better, than other firms on the Street and were the systems diligently used?' We believe they were.

"Moreover, we now know the transgressions chiefly involved verbal agreements that were not disclosed to the firm and which no amount of diligence could have detected. It's clear that there were things that went wrong, but the firm and its shareholder-employees have already paid dearly for them. Any further penalties would be added, unnecessary punishment for creditors and shareholders and could be viewed simply as piling on."

The New York Stock Exchange declined to comment.

Before its February bankruptcy-law filing, Drexel pleaded guilty last year to six felony counts relating to securities-law violations and agreed to pay $650 million in fines and restitution. Drexel also signed a sweeping settlement agreement with the Securities and Exchange Commission that required a complete overhaul of the firm's supervisory and compliance procedures.

Mr. Milken also pleaded guilty last week to six separate felony counts and agreed to pay $600 million in fines and restitution. Mr. Milken also agreed to settle SEC civil charges against him.

The SEC hasn't brought any charges or administrative proceedings against Mr. Joseph or other senior Drexel officials. Mr. Joseph wasn't implicated in the criminal case against Drexel and Mr. Milken.

At the time Drexel settled with the SEC last year, some critics of the settlement pact said they believed Mr. Joseph should have been sanctioned by the SEC, as many of the crimes Drexel pleaded guilty to occurred on his watch. But Mr. Joseph has argued that Drexel had some of the best compliance procedures in the securities industry, and that all the crimes committed were so well hidden that they would have been impossible to detect.

The Big Board has already scheduled a hearing in connection with its disciplinary charges against Drexel, people familiar with the matter said. If the exchange's board of directors approves sanctions against Drexel, the firm could appeal to a federal court in Manhattan. If the exchange files charges against Mr. Joseph and others, they would also be entitled to a Big Board hearing and could later appeal any sanction to a federal court.

If a Drexel fine is upheld, the Big Board could become yet another unsecured creditor, bankruptcy lawyers say. While Drexel shareholders aren't expected to receive much money -- if anything -- for their holdings, the firm's outside creditors are expected to collect most of what is owed them.
