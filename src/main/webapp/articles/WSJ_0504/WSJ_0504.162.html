Letters to the Editor: If Banks Turn Surly Ask It for Collateral

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/04/90

Your March 22 page-one article about banks getting tough with their small-business customers is ironic ("Credit Crunch: As Banks Get Tough With Borrowers, Fears of a Recession Rise"). Now that the highfliers have gone bad, the banks clamp down on the little guy -- the solid citizens of American Business.

Is the small-business man the "bread and butter" of the banks? Only when it comes to deposits and monthly bank charges. Perhaps small-business men should consider imitating the highfliers of land development. Free gold at a new resort? Free membership at our health spa?

I think the next time any small-business man makes a banking decision, he had best ask the bank for the same information it wants from him. How good is your collateral, Mr. Banker? Would you give me a personal guarantee on any losses in my account, Mr. Banker? Deposits are a loan. Let the lender beware.

James T. Gleeson

President

Southeastern Fibers Inc.

Tucker, Ga.

---

The current credit crunch illustrates a fact that neither lenders nor borrowers like to admit: The longer you are with a lender, the more vulnerable you are to having your credit terminated.

That's because eventually every lender's credit policy will change, and any tightening always hurts small and midsize companies the most. Meanwhile, you've lost touch with other lenders and let your loan-presentation skills become rusty.

We urge middle-market companies to maintain contact with several lenders -- do business with more than one, if possible. And just because you've been with the same lender for 10 or 20 years, don't think you're secure.

Russell Hindin

Managing Director

Hindin/Owen/Engelke Inc.

Los Angeles
