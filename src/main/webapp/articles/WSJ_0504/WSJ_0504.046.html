U.S. Treasury Aide Calls Asian Economies Closed, Cites Taiwan

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/04/90

NEW DELHI -- The U.S. yesterday criticized Asian countries that it said are not going far enough toward opening their economies to the rest of the world, singling out Taiwan, South Korea and Thailand. In a speech to the Asian Development Bank's (ADB) annual meeting here, U.S. Deputy Assistant Treasury Secretary George Folsom indicated that Taiwan should let its currency appreciate further. He said "exchange rate policy adjustment" should play an important role in the nation's economic policy.

While saying some Asian countries had progressed in recent years, Mr. Folsom questioned whether continued rapid growth is sustainable without further liberalization.

"In many Asian countries, governments continue to intervene in all aspects of their countries' economies, maintaining import substitution policies, disincentives to foreign investment, bloated public enterprises, private monopolies and overvalued exchange rates, all of which have contributed to growth rates that are below those countries' potential to achieve and perpetuate balance of payment difficulties," Mr. Folsom told the 2,000 delegates, government officials and bankers gathered for the ADB's 23rd annual meeting here.

While Taiwan has pursued a liberalization of its foreign exchange rate system, he said the adjustment process "must continue." He said the country must also further liberalize its foreign exchange rate and capital control rules.

On the subject of South Korea, he said the new exchange rate system put in place in March is potentially important. But he said its significance will depend on further liberalization of foreign exchange and capital controls, as well as further trade and financial market controls.

As for Thailand, he said the booming Southeast Asian country should promote further industrialization and rural development.

Mr. Folsom strongly voiced opposition to opening talks on replenishing the ADB's soft loan window. He reiterated the U.S.'s position that even though the current funding period is set to expire this year, sufficient resources are in place to postpone talks for at least 18 months.
