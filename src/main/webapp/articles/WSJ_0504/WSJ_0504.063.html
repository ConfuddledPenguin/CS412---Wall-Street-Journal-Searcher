Corporate Profit Slide Isn't Over, But Some See a Turnaround Soon

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 05/04/90

NEW YORK -- The profit recession among U.S. corporations isn't over yet, but look for a turnaround this quarter, investors and strategists say. First-quarter earnings for the more than 700 stocks in the Dow Jones Equity Market Index fell 7.5% to $41.5 billion from $44.9 billion a year earlier. For the second quarter, the earnings are expected to rise about 0.8% to $47.5 billion from $47.2 billion a year earlier, according to Zacks Investment Research.

First-quarter earnings fell about 3% short of analysts' expectations, according to Zacks. It was bad earnings news and rising interest rates that sent stocks tumbling again last month.

Negative earnings "surprises" were generally larger than positive ones. For those companies with disappointing profits, earnings averaged 58.5% below the consensus estimate of at least three analysts. By contrast, positive earnings surprises averaged 36.6% above analyst predictions.

Investors and strategists don't expect a big rebound in profits in the current quarter. But by the second half, profits -- and the stock market -- could be climbing more discernibly. Meanwhile, it's still smart to hang on to growth stocks that may perform better than the overall market, some investors say.

For example, earnings at many computer companies, such as Compaq Computer Corp., are growing at a rate of 15% a year or more, and computer stocks are significantly ahead of the market this year. As of yesterday's close, the computer-stock group including International Business Machines Corp. was up 11.2% so far this year, compared with a decline for the Dow Jones Equity Market Index of 5.2%.

Strategists at Shearson Lehman Hutton are bullish on the outlook for corporate profits. Inventory rebuilding, capital spending, and stable consumer demand will continue to keep the economy growing, says Michael Sherman, chief investment strategist at Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc.

"We're betting that the economy will continue to grow and that profits will recover and that this will continue through next year," says Mr. Sherman.

But earnings aren't expected to snap back robustly for all companies. "It's not like we are coming out of a recession and everybody is lean and mean, and profits soar," says A. Marshall Acuff Jr., portfolio strategist at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. "There will continue to be stocks that slip and slide."

An earnings rebound may be seen in companies that produce basic materials, such as aluminum and chemicals, because inventories are tighter and prices are heading up. But investors are cautious about these stocks, showing there isn't widespread conviction that earnings will surge ahead sharply.

"I haven't done much on the commodity-producing and cyclical-stock side," says Michael Schonberg, managing director at Chase Investors Management Corp. "The timing is still questionable. The turn in earnings will be a slow turn."

Mr. Schonberg says growth stocks have been leading the market this year and he expects them to continue to in that role for the time being. Some of his favorites in that category include Waste Management, Philip Morris and Schering-Plough.

But despite a negative earnings surprise in the financial-services group of 6.3%, Mr. Schonberg is prepared to pick through the rubble in search of a good investing opportunity. He says he has found one in American Express Co., and has plunked down a "major" amount of money recently for some shares. As of yesterday's close, the financial-services industry group was down 13.4% on the year, while American Express is down 24%.

"This is a stock that is historically sensitive to turns in earnings; the travel-related services business is a good growth story masked by a brokerage business that has been in a terrible trend."
