France to Offer Plans To Ease Debt Burdens Of Certain Countries

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/04/90

PARIS -- The French government intends to propose a series of initatives on easing the debt problems of middle-income countries, French Economics Minister Pierre Beregovoy said this week. Mr. Beregovoy didn't elaborate, saying that he will outline the proposals to the policy-making Interim Committee of the International Monetary Fund when it meets in Washington next week.

Mr. Beregovoy singled out the Ivory Coast, the Congo, Gabon and Cameroon as countries that have a higher per-capita gross national product than the poorest sub-Saharan countries, but are nonetheless saddled with a heavy debt burden.

The fact that most of this debt is owed to governments and state agencies means that these countries can't benefit fully from the so-called Brady initiative that was launched in 1989. The initiative switched the focus of the debt strategy from new lending by banks toward the lowering of countries' commercial bank debt.

Mr. Beregovoy has criticized the Brady initiative's shortcomings in the past, saying it is too slow to implement and has limited effectiveness.

Since the autumn of 1988, France has been proposing the creation of an escrow fund that would guarantee the payment of principal and interest on securities created by debt-conversion operations.

A recent study by the IMF and the World Bank has concluded that the Brady initiative hasn't worked as well as had been hoped, chiefly due to the lack of sufficient resources and the lack of support from commercial banks.
