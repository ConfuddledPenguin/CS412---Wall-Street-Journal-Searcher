Bush Is Close to Naming Fed's Taylor To Head Up Bailout of Thrift Industry

  • Author: Michel McQueen
  • Date: 05/04/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush all but named a new official to lead the savings-and-loan bailout and insisted that the administration is confronting the problem of the bailout's rising cost. At a news conference, Mr. Bush said "we're very high on" Federal Reserve official William Taylor, whom William Seidman, chairman of the Resolution Trust Corp., enthusiastically recommended as his successor. Although Mr. Taylor's name had circulated recently in connection with the post, the president said Mr. Seidman's recommendation came to him directly in a telephone call.

Mr. Seidman has said since the S&L industry bailout was enacted in August that he wouldn't stay until the end of his term in 1991. Privately, he says he expects to stay at least through June 30 to direct an acceleration in the sales and liquidations of insolvent thrifts expected by that time.

Under last year's thrift-industry restructuring law, the RTC has the lead role in managing the bailout, merging and selling insolvent thrifts.

Mr. Taylor, who is director of the Fed's division of banking supervision and regulation, has been serving since February as the temporary head of the RTC's policymaking Oversight Board. Although Mr. Taylor agreed to serve as interim president and chief executive officer of the board, he didn't want that post permanently. Still, he has been given credit for getting the thrift sales under way.

A spokesman for Mr. Taylor declined to comment. The nomination to head the RTC, which also would fill the job of chairman of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., would require Senate confirmation.

Mr. Taylor, who shares the same sort of straight-talking, no-nonsense style that won Mr. Seidman approval in Congress, is widely respected in the banking field, but some lawmakers were critical that the administration would allow Mr. Seidman to leave without protest.

"Bill Taylor is an able guy," said Rep. Bruce Vento (D., Minn.), who heads an RTC oversight task force, "but it remains to be seen whether he'll assert the kind of independence that gave Bill Seidman such a following."

In his news conference, Mr. Bush denied that any friction between himself and Mr. Seidman led to Mr. Seidman's plan to leave his RTC post well before his term ends next year. Mr. Seidman, who has long said he plans to depart early, has won plaudits from Congress but has irritated administration officials with his outspoken assertions that the bailout will probably cost far more than the administration's early estimates.

Mr. Bush said: "I haven't told you I'm anxious to see Bill Seidman leave." Noting his long relationship with Mr. Seidman, who, like Mr. Bush, served in the Ford administration, Mr. Bush added: "I think Seidman has conducted himself with extraordinary grace and great ability."

Mr. Bush's aides have begun to acknowledge lately what Mr. Seidman had long warned -- that the administration's official estimate of the $166 billion over 10 years to pay for the bailout is too low. Congress's General Accounting Office recently estimated these costs would rise to at least $243 billion.

The Bush administration has publicly acknowledged concern over rising cost estimates, but hasn't described how it plans to deal with the increased costs. Mr. Bush didn't offer any new details, but promised: "We've got to work with the Congress on how to deal with the cost, and right now there's a significant review going forward to see what the costs are. The figures change all the time on you."

Meanwhile, the House Banking Committee announced that Neil Bush, the president's son, will be among those scheduled to testify in Denver this month on the demise of Silverado Banking, Savings & Loan Association.

Mr. Bush, who was on the board of Silverado, is to testify along with other board members, S&L regulators and Silverado business associates.

Other board members signed consent agreements recently, barring them from participating in the thrift industry in the future, over allegations of insider dealing at the Denver thrift. Neil Bush, who asserts he did nothing illegal, has refused to sign such an agreement. Thrift regulators are pursuing the matter.
