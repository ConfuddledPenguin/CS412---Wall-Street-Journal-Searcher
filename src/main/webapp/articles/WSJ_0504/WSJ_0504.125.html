Publishing: Murdoch Puts Global Imprint on Books

  • Author: Meg Cox
  • Date: 05/04/90

NEW YORK -- Rupert Murdoch wants to prove that global is better in books. Bigness and globalism have attracted other giants in the book business, including Bertelsmann AG of West Germany, Hachette S.A. of France and the Simon & Schuster division of Paramount Communications Inc. But in just a few years, Mr. Murdoch has come to rival them in size, assembling publishing operations in the U.S., Europe, Australia, New Zealand and Canada. The book publishing arm of his News Corp. pumps out everything from novels to Bibles to road atlases, bringing in annual revenue of more than $1 billion.

Driving this empire is a sometimes-disputed belief that globalism, especially in the English-speaking world, is the smartest course for future growth. And behind the wheel is George Craig, a tall, rumpled Scotsman, who is president and chief executive officer of News Corp.'s book operations, soon to be called Harper Collins Publishers.

The two principal entities within Harper Collins are New York-based Harper & Row and London-based Collins Publishers. When News Corp. paid $300 million for Harper & Row in 1987, it was a stodgy, medium-sized company that was barely making money. Today, it is humming along profitably, and publishing more best sellers than ever before, a total of 12 in 1989. Collins, a large, well-respected general publisher known for its reference books, was family-run for generations until Mr. Murdoch bought into it, raising his stake to 100% last year from 40% in 1981.

To build his book empire, Mr. Murdoch tapped Mr. Craig, the son of a Glasgow steel worker, who got started in publishing by reorganizing a huge book printing factory for Collins in the 1970s.

Mr. Craig isn't of the gliterati literati set often found in the publishing world. He is a businessman first and a team builder, with a fondness for seeking everyone's ideas on how to solve problems. The 49-year-old Mr. Craig has a grand design to exploit synergies between the far-flung operations. For example: Harper's children's book authors may soon contribute to books for the Scott, Foresman & Co. division, which puts out textbooks; Collins marketing practices that doubled British sales of Agatha Christie mysteries are being applied to Harper paperbacks in America; Mr. Craig borrowed some of the U.K. management from Collins's successful line of datebooks and diaries to start a similar business in Philadelphia.

The bigger-is-better philosophy has its skeptics. Many veteran publishers insist there are few if any economies of scale in this low-margin, cyclical business, where each book is a new risk. Moreover, many in the industry worry about the loss of independence and a separate identity for publishing concerns swallowed up by giants such as News Corp.

"You can't move ideas, techniques and technologies from another country and jam them down people's throats," says Charles Elbaum, a publishing consultant in New York. Mr. Elbaum notes how Bertelsmann lost money when it tried to run the Literary Guild and other U.S. book clubs the way it runs its German book clubs. If Harper Collins "will create semi-autonomous operating units and allow them the discretion to customize market and product, globalization has a decent chance of working," Mr. Elbaum adds.

News Corp.'s $6 billion in debt is likely to hamper Mr. Craig's more ambitious expansion plans; Harper Collins's own heavy debt is forcing the company to sell about $500 million of publishing assets, including a bookstore chain in Australia and its Philadelphia-based J.B. Lippincott medical publishing unit.

Critics at competing publishing houses add that profits at Harper Collins also will be squeezed by the hefty advances the company has paid to win best-selling authors. In starting up a new paperback arm at Harper's, Mr. Craig spent $1 million on the rights for one novel alone, "Billy Bathgate" by E.L. Doctorow. (Mr. Craig justifies the big advances by saying: "We're acquiring authors, not just books.")

In going global, Mr. Craig has stepped on toes. The decision to adopt the name Harper Collins and use a standard logo and typeface on every book jacket, in addition to imprints like Harper & Row, was viewed as so demoralizing that it helped drive the head of Collins to resign.

Sonia Land, former chief executive of Collins, says she told Mr. Craig: "Collins built its reputation on that name. It would be terribly wrong to change that." Mr. Craig's response, says Ms. Land, was: "But we are a world-wide publishing group. International branding is what we're all about."

(Mr. Craig will say only that they had "differences of opinion.")

"I think the name change could crank up the argument that publishing is now run by faceless conglomerates," says a recently departed Collins division head. "But I think most people do recognize that publishing is global now. Still, the people who work at Collins have had a lot of changes to come to terms with, and I think the task ahead is to convince them of the benefits of globalization."

And how to do that? "You get the right people together and keep them communicating," says Mr. Craig, a former accountant who came to Collins after stints at computer and detergent companies.

Mr. Craig is known for gathering managers into brainstorming sessions. Standing next to his trademark flip chart, he scribbles down everybody's ideas. The free-flowing debates may address the pros and cons of mergers and even the renaming of the company.

An open cross-fertilization is getting results. Harper Collins bilingual dictionaries will soon be sold to high schools and colleges through Scott Foresman, the textbook company that News Corp. bought late last year. Scott Foresman, in turn, publishes Robert's Rules of Order, which Harper & Row is better equipped to market.

Meanwhile, Zondervan, the big religious publisher acquired by News Corp. in 1988, has had trouble placing some of its titles in regular bookstores, but is doing better with the help of Harper, which will also co-publish some. Coming in August: the autobiography of former football coach Tom Landry, an evangelical Christian.

In the U.K., Collins has a reputation for aggressive marketing (sales of its road atlases soared after posters reading "Get Collins or Get Lost" went up near major roadways) while Harper & Row has been viewed as unimaginative in that area. To inject marketing sizzle here, Scotsman Eddie Bell has been imported from Collins to run the new mass-market paperback business. In England, Mr. Bell used focus groups to pinpoint why sales were dropping for Agatha Christie novels. One problem was the covers, which looked too much like stock, violent mysteries. The jacket art was redone in a stylish, art deco design and the price was raised considerably; sales of the newly classy Christie's doubled in one year. Now that Harper has bought U.S. paperback rights for about 30 Christie titles, it may be able to transfer some of Mr. Bell's knowledge.

Mr. Craig is a big proponent of buying "English-language rights world-wide" for books, so that printing and marketing can be executed globally, but he realizes that approach isn't always practical or desirable. Some of Harper's recent best sellers (such as "Barbarians at the Gate") weren't picked up by Collins in England, while others ("Roseanne] My Life as a Woman") were.

Although Mr. Craig believes global transference makes better sense for children's, religious and reference books, it doesn't seem likely that Harper Collins textbook subsidiaries in the U.S. and the U.K. will ship books back and forth. The differences in the two countries' teaching practices and curricula are too great.

There are useful ties developing between Harper Collins and other News Corp. holdings. Mr. Murdoch owns New York magazine, whose editor sits on Harper's editorial board. It was clearly helpful to Harper to have a profile of author Dr. Bernie Siegel ("Love, Medicine and Miracles") on the cover of New York magazine last June, right before his second book was published.

And there is Mr. Murdoch's fledgling television network, Fox, whose show "America's Most Wanted" was the subject of a best-selling Harper paperback. In recent weeks, the creator of Fox's new animated hit, "The Simpsons," left another publisher to sign on with Harper for a reported seven figures. Cartoonist Matt Groening was being pursued by a pack of publishers, but was wooed to Harper with the extra persuasion of Fox executives.
