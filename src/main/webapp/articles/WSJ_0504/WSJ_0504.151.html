Marketing & Media: Cook to Step Down As Chief Executive Of Tribune Co.

  • Author: Andrew Patner
  • Date: 05/04/90

CHICAGO -- Tribune Co. Chairman and Chief Executive Officer Stanton R. Cook said at the company's annual meeting that he would step down as chief executive and as publisher of the company's flagship paper, the Chicago Tribune. Mr. Cook, who will remain as chairman of the media giant, said Charles T. Brumback, currently president and chief operating officer of Tribune, would succeed him as chief executive officer. Mr. Brumback is 61 years old. The changes will be effective on Aug. 1, following Mr. Cook's 65th birthday.

Mr. Cook, a 39-year veteran of the company, has led Tribune through a period of strong growth since he was named chief executive in 1974. Mr. Brumback, former president of Tribune's Orlando Sentinel and of the Chicago Tribune, became Mr. Cook's heir apparent when he was named president and chief operating officer of the parent company last year.

Mr. Brumback gave shareholders a "sober" forecast for 1990. He said the soft newspaper advertising market is expected to mean only modest revenue gains for most Tribune publications and a decline in revenue at the company's beleaguered New York Daily News, which is in bitter contract negotiations with its unions. Newsprint manufacturing operations also are expected to show a decline in profits, and even with continued growth in Tribune's broadcast operations, an earnings gain for the company this year will be "difficult," Mr. Brumback said.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Tribune stock closed at $39.75 a share, down 12.5 cents.

Tribune had 1989 earnings of $242.4 million, or $3.17 a primary share, on revenue of $2.45 billion. For the first quarter of 1990, earnings fell 9%. Mr. Brumback attributed the decline to higher operating losses at the Daily News and the declining market for newsprint.

Mr. Cook defended the company against criticism of its management of the Daily News made by leaders of the Allied Printing Trades Council of Greater New York and the Chicago Pressmens' Union, who attended the meeting as shareholders. Mr. Cook also defended the company's hiring of King & Ballow, a Nashville, Tenn., law firm that has been sharply attacked by newspaper labor unions, to handle its negotiations with the 10 Daily News unions.

Pickets from the Chicago Federation of Labor and from the New York union group surrounded the meeting's site at the headquarters of First Chicago Corp., protesting the company's labor practices at the Daily News and the Tribune.

John W. Madigan, 53 years old, currently president and chief executive officer of the Chicago Tribune, will succeed Mr. Cook as publisher of the paper, the top-circulation paper in metropolitan Chicago.
