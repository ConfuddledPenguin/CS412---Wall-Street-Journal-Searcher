IMF Sees Economies Slowing World-Wide Without Recession

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/04/90

WASHINGTON -- The International Monetary Fund reported that world economic growth is slowing this year to an average rate of 2.25% from 3% in 1989 and 4% in 1988, but it said "no recession is in sight." "We don't see a broadly based downturn in industrial countries," said Jacob Frenkel, the IMF's chief economist, at a news conference called to discuss the fund's latest "World Economic Outlook" report. Mr. Frenkel and other IMF economists forecast that the real, or inflation-adjusted, world economic growth rate will "rebound" next year to an average of around 3%.

The IMF noted that "inflation remains a major concern in many industrial countries, despite the economic slowdown stemming from the generalized tightening of monetary conditions since 1988." It added that interest rates are likely to remain relatively high, so long as monetary restraint isn't supported by fiscal restraint, particularly in countries such as the U.S. that have high budget deficits.

Assuming that exchange rates for major currencies remain at about current levels, the IMF economists predicted that the U.S. current-account international payments deficit, which declined last year to just under $106 billion, will widen to $113.4 billion in 1990 and to nearly $130 billion in 1991.

It said Japan's surplus will total about $57.4 billion this year, little changed from last year's figure, but will widen to $73.4 billion in 1991. West Germany's surplus, which amounted to $52.8 billion last year, will increase this year to $62.3 billion and grow further to $68.2 billion in 1991, the IMF said.
