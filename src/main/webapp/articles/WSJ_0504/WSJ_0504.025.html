Westcoast Energy Inc. Plans to Proceed With $429.2 Million Pipeline Expansion

  • Author: Gary Lamphier
  • Date: 05/04/90

VANCOUVER, British Columbia -- Westcoast Energy Inc. said it expects to proceed with a 500 million Canadian dollar (US$429.2 million) expansion of its pipeline system to capitalize on a projected 60% jump in its natural gas exports to the U.S. Pacific Northwest and California by the mid-1990s. "We project that the Pacific Northwest and California will require an additional 900 million to one billion cubic feet of natural gas per day during the next five years," said Michael Phelps, president and chief executive officer of Westcoast, a pipeline, utility, and oil and gas concern.

Mr. Phelps said he expects British Columbia producers, who use Westcoast's 1,450-mile system to transport their gas from the province's burgeoning gas fields, to supply about a third of the additional U.S.-bound gas exports, or 300 million cubic feet a day. That would add more than 60% to Westcoast's 1989 gas exports, or 475 million cubic feet a day.

The exports accounted for more than 40% of Westcoast's record-level daily volume last year of 1.13 billion cubic feet, a 24% gain over 1988. The growth reflected the buoyant economies of British Columbia and the U.S. Pacific Northwest, and a growing appetite for natural gas.

Arthur Willms, Westcoast's chief operating officer, said its expansion spending would include about C$100 million to boost capacity on its pipeline -- which interconnects with Northwest Pipeline Corp.'s system at the Washington state border -- and an additional C$400 million to expand gas processing plants and gathering lines.

Westcoast's upbeat export forecast hinges partly on an expansion of Northwest's 6,500-mile system, which extends from Washington's border with British Columbia, southeast to New Mexico. In addition to its primary markets in Washington, Oregon, and Idaho, the pipeline also serves Southern California's growing gas markets, through an interconnection with the El Paso natural gas pipeline.

Northwest, a unit of Tulsa-based Williams Cos., is discussing a possible expansion of its two-billion-cubic-foot-a-day pipeline with gas producers and expects to file an application this summer with U.S. regulators.

The size of the proposed expansion hasn't been decided, said Tim Hausler, Northwest's vice president, marketing. But he said any expansion would help Westcoast, which has traditionally been locked into the Pacific Northwest market, and unable to move gas to California, except on a spot basis.

Certainly, Westcoast will face tough competition if it hopes to win a slice of the California market.

Several other large pipeline companies want a bigger piece of the pie, including Pacific Gas & Electric Co.'s pipeline unit, which proposes a $1.2 billion expansion of its Alberta-to-California pipeline.

The proposal by San Fransisco-based Pacific Gas is competing with the proposed $580 million Altamont pipeline, sponsored by Houston-based Tenneco Inc., among others. Altamont would run from Alberta to Wyoming, where it would tie on to the proposed Kern River pipeline, which would extend to California. Kern River is backed by Tenneco and Williams.

But analysts said it would be relatively cheap for existing pipelines like Westcoast and Northwest to expand their capacity, and Mr. Willms said Westcoast's spending plans would have little impact on customer tolls.

The proposals by Pacific Gas and Kern River's backers would also complement Westcoast's aims, Mr. Phelps said, because they would provide more direct routes to California, via an expanded Northwest system.

Northwest's line currently interconnects with the Pacific Gas system, but the gas flows to Northwest, not to Pacific Gas and on to California. Paula Rosput, a senior vice-president with Pacific Gas's pipeline arm, said that would change if the company's pipeline is expanded.
