GM Net Fell 54%, Ford's Plunged 69% In First Quarter Because of Weak Sales

  • Author: Joseph B. White and Neal Templin
  • Date: 05/04/90

DETROIT -- General Motors Corp. and Ford Motor Co. reported sharp drops in first-quarter profits, primarily because of deep production cuts and weak sales in North America. GM's first-quarter net income skidded 54% to $710 million, or $1.02 a share, from a strong $1.55 billion, or $2.37 a share, a year ago. A 22.5% drop in North American vehicle shipments to dealers more than offset cost-cutting moves and record results from overseas vehicle operations. The quarter would have looked worse had it not been for a change in pension-cost assumptions that added $75 million, or 12 cents a share, to the bottom line.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, GM rose 37.5 cents to $45.125. GM earnings came in at the higher end of analysts' predictions.

At Ford, profit plunged 69% to $506.2 million, or $1.10 a share, from $1.64 billion, or $3.44 a share. In addition to weakness in its home market, the No. 2 auto maker reported a 78% drop in overseas profit resulting from labor troubles in Britain and economic turmoil in Brazil.

Sales incentives in the U.S. averaged about $1,000 a vehicle at both companies during the first quarter.

Ford shares rose 12.5 cents to $45.625 in composite Big Board trading.

GM and Ford officials said they're optimistic that the strong medicine administered to cut North American inventories during the January-to-March period will help second-quarter results.

Ford Vice President and Treasurer David N. McCammon predicted "no more down time" for any Ford plants through the end of July. Despite the soft market, he is forecasting second-quarter production will be down only 4% from a year ago.

GM earned the bulk of its first-quarter profits from its overseas vehicle operations, as well as its finance and other nonautomotive businesses. The company refused to say whether its core North American automotive operations were profitable in the quarter.

GM added about 10% to its earnings for the quarter by changing certain assumptions used to calculate pension expenses. The company is now assuming that the average retiree will die two years sooner than in the past. At the same time, GM is betting that its pension fund managers will get an 11% return on assets each year, up from the 10% a year assumption used to calculate pension costs a year ago.

The company spokesman said GM pension fund managers have averaged an 18% return on assets over the past five years -- an extraordinary period that saw both a bull market peak and the October 1987 crash.

Ford also adopted a more optimistic pension fund outlook, which added $26 million, or about 5%, to its first-quarter profits.

The No. 1 auto maker also appeared to make real gains in cutting costs. That effort was sharply escalated earlier this year under a program labeled "Quick Start 1990," which ordered GM's North American automotive units to defer capital spending, freeze hiring and cut advertising budgets in an effort to save $1 billion during this year.

For the first quarter, GM's operating costs exclusive of depreciation and amortization, fell 8% from a year earlier. But lower vehicle production more than offset those gains. Revenue fell 9.4% to $30.1 billion from $33.2 billion a year ago.

GM's reserve of cash and marketable securities dropped to $5.5 billion from $6.4 billion a year earlier and $7.1 billion at the end of last year, in part reflecting the $600 million cost of acquiring a 50% interest in the automotive operations of Sweden's Saab-Scania AB.

Ford's earnings came under attack from several fronts in the first quarter. A sluggish U.S. car market forced the No. 2 car maker to cut its first-quarter production 26% from year-ago levels and offer steep discounts to consumers. As a result, Ford's U.S. automotive earnings skidded 80% to $160 million, down from $800 million in the first quarter of 1989. That more than offset a 35% gain in earnings from Ford's Financial Services Group, which posted profits of $191.2 million compared with $141.4 million a year ago.

Meanwhile, Ford's overseas operations, which carried the ball for the company last year when the U.S. market stumbled, ran into trouble in the just-ended quarter. Ford's overseas automotive earnings skidded to $155 million, down from $696 million in the year-ago period.

Strikes at Ford's European operations cost Ford about $200 million in earnings during the first quarter, Mr. McCammon said. Ford may be able to recover some of those losses during the remainder of the year, Mr. McCammon said. However, he said, Ford's results in the second quarter will be hurt by a just-resolved strike at an assembly plant in Genk, Belgium.

Ford also is having trouble in Brazil, where the government's harsh anti-inflation measures have hobbled operations at Autolatina, Ford's joint venture with Volkswagen AG. Autolatina was the main reason Ford posted an $85 million loss for its affiliated companies in the first quarter, compared with a $60.3 million profit in the first quarter of 1989, Mr. McCammon said. He warned that the venture could suffer for the rest of the year.

Mr. McCammon said there are some good signs for the second quarter. The extensive plant idlings during the January-to-March quarter helped Ford to trim its U.S. vehicle stocks to 785,000 cars and trucks at the end of the first quarter, nearly 200,000 less than the same period last year.

That should help Ford to push its sales incentive costs back to an average of $1,000 per vehicle this month from the $1,200 per vehicle level they hit during April, he said. That is still well above the $400 per vehicle incentive costs Ford had during the 1989 first quarter. But the auto maker is introducing some new products, such as the Ford Explorer sports utility vehicle, that may be popular enough to sell without big discounts.

Mr. McCammon said Ford plans to run its U.S. factories overtime to boost output to 110% of straight-time capacity during the second quarter, compared with 91% during the first quarter. Straight-time capacity is the level of production achieved by running a plant for two, daily eight-hour shifts, five days a week.

As of March 31, Ford had $3.9 billion in cash and marketable securities, down from $8.1 billion a year ago. The big drop reflected the lower profits, Ford's purchase of Jaguar and heavy capital spending to bring out new cars.
