World Markets: London Stock Prices Fall After Two-Day Rally, As Voters Cast Ballots in Elections Nationwide

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/04/90

LONDON -- Share prices ended a two-day technical rally mostly lower as investors took to the sidelines, while U.K. voters went to the polls nationwide to cast ballots in local elections. Meanwhile, the Tokyo market was closed yesterday and will be closed today for holidays.

London's Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 share index finished down 2.7 points at 2134.9, below the intraday high of 2145.2 reached within the first half-hour of official trading and better than the minimum of 2130.2 around midday.

The Financial Times 30 share index fell 1.4 points to 1676.6. Volume was a thin 392.8 million shares, compared with 433 million Wednesday.

In the futures market, the June Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 index contract closed at 2166, up 7.5 points.

Dealers said the market rebounded from its midday low largely on strength in stock-index futures buying, which underpinned the underlying FT-SE 100 equities. Additionally, scattered buying in selected blue chips gave support, as did a steady opening on Wall Street.

The upswing, however, wasn't sustained in the face of potential market uncertainties presented by the local U.K. elections, the largest nationwide vote since parliamentary polling three years ago, traders said.

Political pundits viewed the election as a potential mandate for the future of Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher's Conservative Party, even though the results only apply to municipal councils rather than the central government leadership.

Among corporate news, pharmaceutical concern Wellcome fell 11 pence to 665 pence (18 cents to $10.91) despite reporting a 28% rise in pretax profit for the six months ended March 3, results that were at the higher end of market forecasts.

Dealers said the market was expecting a more bullish statement from the company on its flagship drug, Retrovir, used in the treatment of acquired immune deficiency syndrome.

Elsewhere, oil stocks were mixed after news of a temporary production accord by the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries.

British Petroleum added 4 1/2 pence to 412 1/2 pence; Burmah dropped 4 pence to 560 pence after announcing that it would change the ratio of its American depository receipts to ordinary shares to 2-to-1 from 4-to-1; Enterprise was unchanged at 583 pence after several days of strong gains; Lasmo sank 7 pence to 362 pence; and Shell rose 1 1/2 pence to 439 1/2 pence.

Among strong gainers, Britain's largest chemical concern, Imperial Chemical Industries, gained 10 pence to #11.05; Reuters rose 27 pence to #10.98 on heavy American buying; Carlton Communications climbed 7 pence to 504 pence, continuing the upward trend after taking a severe drubbing in recent weeks; and Anglo-Dutch food maker Unilever jumped 9 pence to 634 pence after being "beaten up" recently, one dealer said.

Decliners included tobacco and financial services conglomerate B.A.T Industries, which fell 6 pence to 685 pence as market makers knocked down the share price amid a surplus of stock, one trader said. Several other stocks were affected by the same problem, this trader added: British Aerospace dropped 9 pence to 512 pence and Associated British Foods fell 7 pence to 378 pence.

Standard Chartered Bank lost 10 pence to 477 pence after news about trouble at another U.K. retailer, one in which the bank has reportedly extended #40 million in credit.

Pilkington, a major glass maker, shed 7 pence to 191 pence after its U.K. distributor, Solaglas Ltd., was acquired by France's Cie. de Saint-Gobain.

Electricals concern BICC dropped 10 pence to 396 pence on an unconfirmed report of a brokerage firm's downgrading, one trader said.

Elsewhere, prices closed higher in Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Amsterdam, Stockholm and Milan; and mixed in Brussels. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Meanwhile, stocks closed higher in Singapore, Wellington and Seoul; lower in Sydney, Taipei and Manila; and mixed in Hong Kong.
