`Lights, Camera, Icahn': TV Enters Battle Over USX --- Investor Puts on a Program To Persuade Shareholders To His Side in Proxy Rift

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 05/04/90

Proxy contests have entered the video age. New York investor Carl Icahn is tuning into USX Corp. investors with television.

Mr. Icahn, who owns a 13.3% stake in USX, is waging a proxy battle against the company to prod management into splitting up the steel and energy operations. So far, the contest has been fought through traditional ads and letters to shareholders, but with Monday's shareholder meeting fast approaching, Mr. Icahn has taken his fight to the airwaves.

Last week, Mr. Icahn bought a one-hour time slot on Financial News Network's Institutional Research Network, which says its subscribers include the nation's top 100 institutional investors, reached via satellite. The segment opened with Mr. Icahn's partner, Alfred D. Kingsley, announcing, "Welcome to Shareholder's Outreach," and introducing the cast, including Mr. Icahn and three Wall Street energy analysts, all of whom favor the Icahn proposal.

Mr. Kingsley fed Mr. Icahn questions regarding details of his proposal and his experience with other proxy contests, including Texaco Inc. He then turned to analysts to explain why USX's Marathon Oil is a jewel and should be separated from U.S. Steel. The show closed with Mr. Kingsley urging viewers to "vote for proposal No. 4" on the white proxy card.

To complement the one-hour segment, Mr. Icahn also purchased 30-second commercial time on Financial News Network urging shareholders to vote with him.

It's believed to be the first time anyone has used television as a weapon in a proxy battle -- but it's not likely to be the last. Jonathan Fram, president of Institutional Research Network, says the television medium provides a human dimension to faceless players in corporate battles, and will probably be used more frequently.

"TV imparts the warmth of the players," he says. "It can take out all the bias. People watch and say, `By golly, he's a regular guy and he makes sense.'"

Television is also less labor-intensive than traveling around to meet institutional investors -- a route adopted by USX. USX executives have spent the last several weeks jetting about the country to convince top investors and analysts to vote against Mr. Icahn.
