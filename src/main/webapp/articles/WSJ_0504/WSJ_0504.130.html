Sales of U.S. Cars Advanced 2.8% In Late April --- Industry Says Strong Finish To Disappointing Month Isn't Likely to Continue

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 05/04/90

DETROIT -- Sales of domestically built cars and trucks rose 2.8% in late April, giving an upbeat finish to an otherwise disappointing month. Overall, Americans bought 381,469 cars, trucks and vans in the April 21-30 period, down from 371,153 vehicles a year ago.

The seasonally adjusted annual selling pace of cars alone was 7.1 million vehicles in late April. While that trailed the 7.7 million-car pace a year ago, the latest figure was well above the annual rate of 6.6 million cars for the first two-thirds of the month.

The late-April sales pace also exceeded analysts' expectations of a 6.8 million-car selling rate for the period.

The gain, however, didn't overcome the car-market's sluggishness earlier in the month. For all of April, Americans purchased 1.2 million cars and trucks (domestic and imported), down more than 9% from the 1.3 million bought a year ago. Thus the industry's reaction to the late-April results was tempered.

"We aren't anticipating a big increase in industry sales," said David N. McCammon, Ford Motor Co.'s vice president for finance. The company still expects overall car and truck sales of 14.5 million vehicles this year, with the sales pace in the year's second half slightly exceeding that of the first half.

Perhaps the most striking aspect of April's sales results was the huge disparity of performance among individual companies. Chrysler Corp.'s car and truck sales plunged nearly 32% in the month, more than three times the decline in the overall market.

The company's share of combined car and truck sales dropped 3.5 percentage points in April to 10.7%. That's about what Chrysler's share of car sales alone has been in recent years. And in cars, excluding trucks and minivans, Chrysler was outsold by both Toyota Motor Corp. and Honda Motor Co. -- dropping Chrysler to fifth place in that category for April.

Chrysler officials have blamed the decline on several factors, including the company's refusal to engage in unprofitable sales to daily-rental fleets. The company also said that, after over-producing in 1989, it cut production of some models too much this year.

"We cut back inventory levels too low," a Chrysler spokesman said. "We over-corrected for what dealers perceived as an overstocked condition. We're trying like a dog to cure that problem."

General Motors Corp. fared substantially better. GM, whose U.S. market share collapsed during the 1980s, increased its share 0.7% during April. For the first four months of this year, GM took 35.5% of U.S. car sales, slightly better than a year earlier.

GM posted strong sales of such models as the Pontiac Grand Am and Grand Prix, Buick Skylark, Oldsmobile Cutlass Supreme, and the redesigned Chevrolet Caprice, which was launched in April. Several of these models carried very generous buyer incentives.

Ford, meanwhile, saw its car-market share drop one percentage point, to 22% in April, though its share of combined car-and-truck sales dropped just 0.1% to 25.2%. Ford officials said the company's market share has suffered because Ford is just starting to launch two key models, a version of the Ford Escort and a four-door Jeep-like vehicle called the Ford Explorer.

"We believe our shares will improve as the supply of our new 1991 products increases over the next few months," the company said in a statement released with first-quarter earnings results.

Among Japanese auto makers, results likewise were mixed. Toyota was the clear winner with an 11.4% sales increase, leading all major companies, domestic and import. Honda, though, posted an 8% sales drop. A Honda spokesman said that in April 1989 the company was running a dealer-incentive program that helped boost sales, particularly toward the end of the month. However, Honda didn't have any incentive promotions this past month, the spokesman said.

Several of the smaller Japanese auto makers also had sales declines, although the U.S. marketing arm of Mitsubishi Motors Corp. posted a 67% sales increase.

Mercedes-Benz of North America had a 12.5% sales increase in April, and so far this year its sales are running slightly ahead of a year ago. Volkswagen AG's Audi of America subsidiary, which has had a precipitous sales drop in recent years, saw sales jump nearly 43% in April. The company is aggressively promoting car-leasing programs.
