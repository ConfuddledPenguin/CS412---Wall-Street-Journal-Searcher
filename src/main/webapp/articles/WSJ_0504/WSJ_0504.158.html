Thrift Agency Seeks To Cut Costs, Weighs Consolidating Offices

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/04/90

WASHINGTON -- The Office of Thrift Supervision is considering scaling back its extensive bureaucracy, including possibly paring down its regional presence to mirror that of banking regulators. The agency said that one of its highest priorities under its new director, Timothy Ryan, will be cutting costs. The office is examining, among other possibilities, trimming its 12 regional offices to five, in the same six cities where the Comptroller of the Currency has offices. The comptroller regulates federally chartered banks.

Pending legislation would combine the thrift and banking regulators; the moves proposed yesterday could be a prelude to such a merger.

Not surprisingly, the thrift industry favors reducing its regulatory costs. Banks and thrifts pay their regulators a fee, based on their sizes, to cover the cost of examinations. "The cost per institution, as opposed to the Comptroller's, is extraordinarily high," said James Grohl of the U.S. League of Savings Institutions. According to the trade group, the S&L regulatory costs are more than double that of banks.

The trade group opposes combining the banking and thrift regulatory apparatus, however.

The thrift office also proposed a regulation yesterday to ban the sale of debt securities in S&L offices. The proposal is primarily in reaction to sales of bonds issued by Phoenix-based American Continental Corp. at its Lincoln Savings & Loan Association subsidiary. Some holders of $200 million in now-worthless debt have testified before Congress that they believed they were buying insured certificates of deposit at Lincoln, in Irvine, Calif.

The agency is seeking public comment on the proposals and expects to have a decision by mid-June.
