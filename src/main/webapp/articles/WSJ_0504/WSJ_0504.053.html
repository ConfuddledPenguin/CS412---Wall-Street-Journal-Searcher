CalMat Settlement Of Holders' Suits Cleared by Court

  • Author: Pauline Yoshihashi
  • Date: 05/04/90

LOS ANGELES -- CalMat Co. said it received state court approval for a settlement of shareholder lawsuits against the company and certain directors that claimed the defendants used unfair tactics to block a 1988 takeover attempt by Brierley Investments Ltd. In a statement, CalMat denied any liability on the claims. Dan Mogin, a lawyer for the shareholders, said plaintiffs' experts said the settlement has a potential value ranging from $30 million to more than $100 million.

The partial settlement ends the involvement of CalMat, its directors and a subsidiary of Onoda Finance Co. of Tokyo, but claims are still pending against New Zealand financier Sir Ronald Brierley and his Brierley Investments, as well as certain of the New Zealand company's officers and subsidiaries. A suit was filed against those defendants in federal court in San Diego alleging violation of federal anti-racketeering and securities law in February, Mr. Mogin said. Brierley officials didn't return phone calls seeking comment.

The settlement calls for CalMat to pay a special dividend of at least 10 cents a share to certain shareholders of record on a stipulated date, and to buy back one million shares of common stock within 120 trading days. Within 30 months, CalMat must spend at least an additional $30 million to buy back more shares, pay additional special dividends, or both.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, CalMat closed at $32.50 a share, up 50 cents. The construction-materials company has about 30 million common shares outstanding.

CalMat will spend at least 50% of the net proceeds of its real estate divestiture program through Dec. 31, 1991, to pay for the repurchase of stock and the special dividends. It wasn't clear how much real estate CalMat intends to sell, and CalMat executives weren't immediately available to clarify the settlement. Mr. Mogin said it is impossible to say how much CalMat will eventually pay out to shareholders covered by the suit.

The complex settlement includes, among other things, changes in CalMat's handling of any acquisition queries and calls for a shareholder vote on an anti-greenmail resolution.

The shareholder suits stemmed from events in late 1987 and 1988 that included CalMat's rejection of an unsolicited $40-a-share takeover bid from Brierley. In July 1988, CalMat announced a major restructuring that scuttled the bid, which had an indicated value of $987 million.

As part of the restructuring, the company agreed to sell a cement subsidiary to Onoda Cement Co. of Japan. The transaction included the repurchase of a "significant" number of CalMat shares.

The shareholders claimed the defendants disguised an indirect greenmail payment to Sir Ronald, in which the investor sold his stake in Calmat to Onoda at a premium to the market price, and Onoda promised to sell the stake to CalMat at a profit. The shareholders also claimed Onoda bought the CalMat assets for less than their true value.
