Letters to the Editor: A Clenched Fist KO's Mutual Trust

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/04/90

Your March 26 page-one article "Global Reach: Cross-Border Alliances Become Favorite Way to Crack New Markets" makes a critical point: Strategic alliances will indeed be "a hallmark of business in the 1990s." In fact, around the world, the number of business alliances is at least doubling annually. Yet alliances take a mind-set that is quite different from what the article suggests. In two decades of planning, advising on, working in, teaching about, and sometimes managing strategic alliances around the world, I've never encountered a successful alliance in which partners "shake hands with {their} right hand, while making a fist with {their} left."

To be sure, each firm must protect its separate interests by safeguarding core strengths such as critical know-how or market-access channels, prized brand names or a key position in the value-added chain.

But the implicit threat connoted by the image of "making a fist" weakens an alliance because it damages mutual trust.

Even steadfast rivals can build successful alliances. A key is first to segregate their areas of competition from those of their mutual interest. One need only look at Ford and Mazda, Apple and DEC, and GE and SNECMA, which compete head-to-head for military customers while cooperating intensely and successfully in the commercial market.

Jordan D. Lewis

Washington
