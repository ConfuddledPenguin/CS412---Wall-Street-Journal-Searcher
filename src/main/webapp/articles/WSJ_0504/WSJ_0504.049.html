If You Go to an Indian Restaurant, Make Sure to Read the Fine Print

  • Author: Tony Horwitz
  • Date: 05/04/90

If you are ever in the position of having to eat your words, a fad in India may make them go down a little easier. In one of the more astounding feats ever authenticated by the Guinness Book of World Records, an Indian calligrapher has crammed his country's national anthem, "Jana Gana Mana," onto a single grain of rice -- with husk to spare. What's more amazing is that other samples of miniature writing on rice continue to pour into the publisher's office in London, and Guinness believes this week's record won't stand for long.

"We've got enough rice now to cook a curry," says Deputy Editor Nicholas Brown, who confirmed the latest record for "writing minuscule" with a magnifying glass. The composition -- 515 letters in all -- includes the anthem in Hindi, a saying by the poet Robert Frost and the full home address of the calligrapher, Anantha Kumar.

Mr. Brown says that media reports about another Indian who set the rice-writing record last year (with a mere 241 letters) have "set off a craze in India." That record didn't stand long: It was doubled just as last year's book went to press and there have been several new records since then.

Guinness requires writers to compose a meaningful passage so they don't just repeat skinny letters, such as I's or L's, through the text.

The champion of the moment, a 32-year-old from Bangalore in southern India, is an old hand at micro-scribbling. As a teenager, Mr. Kumar sketched flowers on dog's whiskers and scenes from Indian mythology on mustard seeds. His epic rice work is more notable for its length than its beauty. According to Mr. Brown, it looks "like a fuzzy, dark speck" to the naked eye.

Deft handwork seems to be the trend among the 12 new record-holders announced by Guinness this week. A Frenchman achieved 801 consecutive catches with a boomerang. And a man from Missouri managed to hold nine baseballs in one hand.

Nor is rice the only canvas for would-be "writing minuscule" entries. Mr. Brown says a Chinese man has submitted a proverb written on a strand of hair that probably surpasses the old record. "If China's anything like India," he says, "we'll be getting a lot of hair in the mail next year."
