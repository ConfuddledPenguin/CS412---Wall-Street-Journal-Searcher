Spanish Equities Stage a Comeback, But Analysts Are Unsure About Future

  • Author: Caitlin Randall Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/04/90

LONDON -- After tumbling nearly 15% in the first three months of 1990, Spanish equities are staging a comeback. But analysts are split on how long the rally will last in the face of some troubling questions about the immediate future of Spain's economy. A number of factors dogged Spanish stocks during the first quarter, including persistent concerns about inflation and the trade deficit, the sluggish stock-market performance world-wide and general weakness in the Spanish banking sector -- which accounts for nearly 31% of the main market barometer, the Madrid Stock Index.

But some of these trends have since turned around. Bank shares are now recovering, thanks in large part to strong first-quarter earnings. Foreign money, which according to some estimates makes up as much as a quarter of the Madrid market, is beginning to trickle back as prospects on other world bourses deteriorate and euphoria about Eastern Europe subsides.

In addition, some market bulls say that the government's budget, announced on March 1, is less restrictive than anticipated. They note that a projected 40% rise in infrastructure spending could give some sectors, particularly construction, a hearty boost.

And while economists aren't predicting a sharp drop in Spain's inflation rate nor a significant narrowing in the current international payments account deficit any time soon, some contend both are heading lower.

"No one is saying everything in the garden is rosy, but the trend looks better . . . the rate of deficit growth is slowing as well as the inflation rate," says Amanda Sisson at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Ltd. in London.

Forecasts for the market over the next three months vary, with some bulls predicting the Madrid Index will climb back over the 300 level, where it was before the market entered a tailspin in January.

But others contend that without more clear-cut signs of economic cooling, a sustained stock market rally isn't likely. The market's bears see the index holding at around 270 before losing some ground.

Yesterday, the index closed at 273.79, up from 272.22 on Wednesday. It had slipped to about 250 in early April from above 300 in January and 340 at its peak last year.

Among the factors contributing to the market's dismal first-quarter performance, analysts say, was the diversion of investor interest to the hefty returns -- ranging from 14% to 16% -- on fixed-income securities.

Unease over Spain's recent economic performance also contributed to a slip in the volume of foreign interest.

Economists point to the big jump in February inflation as especially worrying.

Despite the government's determined efforts to cool Spain's economy, the nation's year-on-year inflation rate jumped to 7.3% in February from 6.7% in January and 6.2% a year earlier.

But Judith Kleinman, an analyst with Capel, argues that February's inflation figures may have been no more than "an aberration" in view of March data that showed year-on-year inflation easing back to 7%.

Analysts say the key to the market's resurgence lies with bank stocks. The banking sector has already begun to dig itself out of the doldrums, Capel's Ms. Kleinman says.

The banks had the wind knocked out of their sails early this year when, in a bid to put the brakes on Spain's booming economy, the government imposed stringent credit controls on private-sector lending.

The central bank also introduced a 30% mandatory deposit requirement on foreign currency borrowings by Spanish companies early last year to discourage them from raising funds abroad. Most companies expected the requirement to be lifted early in 1990; the government's decision to keep the controls in place added to the market's woes.
