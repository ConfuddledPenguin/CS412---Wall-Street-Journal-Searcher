Foreign Exchange: U.S. Unit Ends Session Little Changed As Traders Ponder Many Uncertainties

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/04/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar ended little changed against major currencies as the market pondered a full slate of uncertainties, including German monetary unification, today's U.S. employment data, local British elections and Sunday's meeting of the Group of Seven industrial nations. Holidays in Japan yesterday and today added to the market's caution.

With so many reasons for restraint, "people are being very slow about what they're doing," commented Charles Spence, vice president of foreign exchange at First Interstate Bank of California in Los Angeles.

Dollar dealings against the mark were muted as the market digested Wednesday's accord on monetary merger between the Germanys, traders said.

The agreement, which will take effect July 2, is encouraging participants to maintain long mark positions in anticipation of an eventual rise in West German interest rates to cope with the pact's possible inflationary consequences, Mr. Spence said.

Participants also think they can't sell the mark too aggressively because recent drops in the West German currency against the dollar have proved temporary, said Joe Sabbagh, chief trader at European American Capital Corp. in New York.

Confidence in the Bundesbank's willingness to raise rates when it becomes necessary, along with the understanding that the full impact of monetary unification will take some time to disclose itself, will help the mark weather short-term uncertainty over the effects of monetary unification, Messrs. Sabbagh and Spence agreed.

For these reasons, the mark isn't likely to fall steeply, as the yen did when the market was waiting for the Bank of Japan to raise interest rates, Mr. Spence added.

The Bundesbank central bank council didn't raise rates at its fortnightly meeting. After the meeting, Johann Wilhelm Gaddum, a director of the Bundesbank, said the central bank can support the monetary union agreement, explaining it isn't too different from the bank's original concept of monetary unification.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6827 marks, down from 1.6848 marks late Wednesday, and at 158.05 yen, up from 157.95 yen late Wednesday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6410, down from $1.6425 late Wednesday.

Despite the factors supporting the mark, traders said an improving economic picture in the U.S. and the possibility of higher U.S. interest rates will keep the dollar from slipping against the German currency, resulting in a stalemate of the two units.

The market is watching today's release of U.S. employment data for April as an indicator of what action, if any, the Federal Reserve might take on interest rates, Messrs. Spence and Sabbagh said.

Economists expect nonfarm payrolls to have risen by 384,000, compared to an increase of only 26,000 in March. The unemployment rate is seen remaining unchanged from March at 5.2%.

The figures probably will give the dollar a boost if they are in line with expectations or better, but will cause the U.S. unit to weaken if they are worse than expected, by inspiring speculation that the Fed might lower interest rates, Mr. Sabbagh said.

And in spite of a statement by a senior U.S. Treasury official that the G-7 finance ministers and central bankers aren't likely to take new action to support the yen, the market is treading cautiously in the area of yen rates. They fear a surprise announcement on the Japanese currency after Sunday's meeting in Washington, participants said.

Meanwhile, participants interested in the pound are waiting to see how British Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher's Conservative Party fared in yesterday's local council elections. In early returns, the party was losing heavily.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery rose $1.90 to $371.40 an ounce. Estimated volume was a light 1.8 million ounces.

In Hong Kong early Friday, gold was quoted at $371.25 an ounce.
