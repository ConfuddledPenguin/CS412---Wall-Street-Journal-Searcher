Politics & Policy: Bush Increases Push for Action on Budget Gap Amid Fears Rising Rates May Cripple Economy

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 05/04/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration, convinced that the major political danger it faces is an economy crippled by rising interest rates, is exploring a deficit-reduction deal with Congress more intensely than ever before. To hasten the start of negotiations, President Bush invited the majority and minority leaders of both houses to meet with him at the White House Sunday afternoon. "What we have to do is start talking process, and how we go forward," Mr. Bush said yesterday.

The White House clearly hopes Sunday's huddle will pave the way for talks on a bipartisan pact to slash the budget deficit and reshape the federal budget process. But top congressional Democrats remain wary, and the odds of enacting a package of big spending cuts and, especially, significant tax increases -- essential to any accord -- in this election year are still no better than even.

The president's invitation caps weeks of intense wooing of Congress by Budget Director Richard Darman, among others. With White House blessing, two Democratic insiders -- power lawyer Robert Strauss and former Ohio congressman Thomas Ashley -- are working behind the scenes. The two men have talked privately with Mr. Bush about the deficit. Though both insist they aren't presidential emissaries, they have been telling key congressmen that the time for a budget summit is at hand.

"I've been telling both sides that it's time to come to the table," says Mr. Ashley, a friend of the president and now head of a bank trade association. "Without carrying messages, I can at least give a careful description of the interests of the various parties." Mr. Strauss has been doing the same, cornering Senate Budget Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.) at a recent black-tie dinner to try to persuade him that the White House is sincerely ready to bargain.

Sen. Sasser isn't convinced. "I see no real evidence that they're interested," he said yesterday. "If they're interested in another conspiracy to get by with a slideby budget, I'm not interested."

Even if the two sides are ready to bargain, the political obstacles are high. "You face very tough choices," House Budget Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) says. "The politics have prevented these choices from being made. The politics are still the same."

Democrats, who have excoriated Mr. Bush for proposing steep cutbacks in Medicare, farm-subsidies and other entitlements, would almost certainly have to swallow such entitlement cutbacks -- and might even have to agree to trim Social Security.

In return, Democrats would insist that Mr. Bush eat the "no new taxes" pledge that has been so politically potent. And their price for accepting Mr. Bush's cut in capital-gains tax rates, Democrats say, would be an increase in the top marginal tax rate for the richest Americans, which many Republicans are certain to oppose. (The richest taxpayers now pay a top rate of 28%, even though many less-wealthy taxpayers face a 33% top rate.) The White House is still resistant. Some Republicans on Capitol Hill are toying with a compromise -- which would neither gain nor lose revenue -- that would set the top rate at 31%, a tax cut for those now paying 33% and a tax increase for those paying 28%.

Despite all these obstacles, administration offficials figure congressional Democrats may be receptive to talks now that the Senate Budget Committee and the full House have each agreed on their own budget plans. Congressional Democrats say their progress is also motivating the White House. "We have sent a signal to the White House that instead of the process collapsing, it was going forward," says Rep. Panetta.

To get talks going, the two budget chairmen say, the president himself must persuade Senate Democratic Leader George Mitchell and House Speaker Thomas Foley that he is willing to share the blame for raising taxes and cutting spending. "You can't get into a situation where one side accuses the other of putting something on the table," Rep. Panetta stresses.

The economic fundamentals also are pushing the parties toward each other. "The deficit projections are such that they have the Congress gasping a little bit, and the administration, too," Mr. Ashley says. Just three months ago, Mr. Darman said a deficit-reduction package of $35.6 billion would avoid triggering across-the-board budget cuts this fall under the Gramm-Rudman law. With federal revenues falling short of projections and interest rates rising, he now says it will take $45 billion or maybe even $55 billion.

White House officials also fear that rising interest rates world-wide, caused largely by the resuscitation of Eastern Europe, also threaten to cut short the economic expansion on which Mr. Bush's re-election hopes rest. "Japanese capital available for investment here may be contracting somewhat," Mr. Darman said in a speech at Harvard this week. "European capital is looking more Eastward and inward. U.S. borrowing for the S&L (savings & loan) mess is temporarily soaring." The administration figures the only move it can make to bring down interest rates is to reduce the government's appetite for debt.

Last year's summit between the administration and congressional leaders is now widely viewed, both in the White House and on Capitol Hill, as a model to avoid. It accomplished little significant deficit-reduction and left a bitter aftertaste in Congress. If Sunday's meeting leads to a fullfledged summit, all parties agree, this one should include the chairmen of the powerful tax-writing and appropriations committees to assure that the resulting agreement is enacted.

The administration and many in Congress also want to use any bipartisan talks to change the federal budgeting process. Democrats, stung by charges that they are raiding the Social Security trust fund to reduce the deficit, want to alter budgetary accounting for Social Security. Mr. Darman wants to close loopholes in the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law, among other changes to restrain growth in federal spending.

"Knowing what I know," he told his Harvard audience, "I can't help whistling the Arlo Guthrie song . . . `You can get anything that you want at Alice's Restaurant.' This really should not be what comes to an OMB (Office of Management and Budget) Director's mind as he walks to work."
