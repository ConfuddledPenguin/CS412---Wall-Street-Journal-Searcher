LOVE IN CHINA

A pioneering survey in China found many couples were dissatisfied with sex and unhappy in their marriages.

"We found that Chinese marriages tend to be stable -- people shun divorce. But the quality of the marriages is not good," Liu Daling of Shanghai University said. Mr. Liu said the survey, which he described as the first of its kind, involved interviews in 15 provinces with 23,000 people ranging from sex offenders in prison to middle-school students.

Half the 8,000 couples interviewed said they were just putting up with their partners despite unfulfilled sex lives and resulting unhappiness, Mr. Liu said. "Many people have the idea of just making do with a bad marriage," he added.

Divorce is frowned on in China, and authorities often make feuding couples sit through hours of mediation before allowing a divorce. One reason: Divorce causes problems with scarce housing.

INVESTING IN COLOMBIA

Foreign investment in Colombia dropped by more than half in the first four months of this year because of uncertainty over violence, economic policy and approaching elections, the government said. A report by the National Planning Department said the government approved $49.5 million of foreign investment in the first four months of the year compared with $112.3 million in the same period last year. The biggest foreign investors were the U.S., with 67.5% of the total, Britain, 11%, and Panama, 5.2%.

TAIWAN'S `POSITIVE MEASURE'

Taiwan wants to establish a mission in China to promote trade and other unofficial ties, a Taiwan government spokesman said. He said the mission would be a "positive measure" that could "help solve the many problems arising from exchanges {with China} and will contribute to the stable and peaceful development across the Taiwan Strait." Taiwan authorities now allow investment in China only if it is channeled through companies in third countries.

HELP FROM DUTCH RETIREES

Newly retired Dutch executives will go to Eastern Europe this year to pass on their managerial skills to fledgling businesses and help in the process of developing market economies. Under a Dutch government-backed program, between 40 and 50 of the highly qualified "pensioners" will go to Poland, Hungary, Czechoslovakia and East Germany for several months. The Dutch Economics Ministry said 672,000 guilders ($364,000) had been allocated for the program, whose participants won't receive any salary but will have expenses paid.

CHEATING IN BANGLADESH

Nearly 11,000 students in Bangladesh were expelled for cheating and violence during three days of college-entry examinations last week, education officials said. More than 200 students were arrested and about 100 teachers were suspended for helping the cheaters, they said. Nearly 450,000 students are taking the month-long Secondary School Certificate tests. There was violence in about 70 examination centers where students demonstrated for the "right to cheat," attacked teachers and battled police.

ISRAELI INFLATION

Israel's consumer price index rose 2.5% in April, the government announced. An increase in housing costs contributed 0.8% to April's inflation figure, the Central Bureau of Statistics said; that followed a rise of 1.3% in February and of 2.2% in March. Rents and home-sale prices have been pushed up by a housing shortage caused by an influx of Soviet Jews. Inflation in the first four months of 1990 reached 5.5%, the bureau said. For all of 1989, consumer prices rose by 20.7%.

JAPAN-U.S. FLIGHTS

Japan's Transportation Ministry said it was permitting Japanese airlines to increase service to U.S. cities in the wake of last fall's aviation negotiations.

The ministry said it authorized three companies to establish new direct passenger flights to the U.S. from Tokyo: Japan Air Lines Co. will operate three flights weekly to Washington; All Nippon Airways Co., five to New York; and Japan Air System Co., two to Honolulu. In addition, Japan Air Lines will increase its service to Chicago to seven flights a week from five currently.

Nippon Cargo Airlines Co. will also be allowed to operate four cargo flights a week to Chicago and three to Los Angeles. Japan Air Lines got authorization for four weekly cargo flights to Chicago.

Transportation Minister Akira Ohno said his ministry decided to authorize the new passenger and cargo flights "to overcome international aviation competition."
