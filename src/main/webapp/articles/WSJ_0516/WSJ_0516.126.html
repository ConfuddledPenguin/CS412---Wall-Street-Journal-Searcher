Politics & Policy: Presidential Panel Urges U.S. to Mount Military Strikes Against Air Terrorists

  • Author: Laurie McGinley
  • Date: 05/16/90

WASHINGTON -- A presidential commission, calling for drastic action against air terrorism, urged that the U.S. mount military strikes against terrorist enclaves to discourage the kind of attack that downed Pan American World Airways Flight 103. In a 182-page report, the commission appointed to look into the Pan Am disaster also called for the reorganization of aviation-security procedures. The panel concluded that the Dec. 21, 1988, bombing of the jetliner over Lockerbie, Scotland, "may well have been preventable" and cited grave security lapses by the airline and the Federal Aviation Administration.

"Terrorists were able to place a bomb on Pan Am 103 not because some one thing failed, but because the aviation-security system failed," said Ann McLaughlin, who headed the seven-member President's Commission on Aviation Security and Terrorism. "The system was flawed and didn't provide an effective defense against sabotage."

In its report, the commission said the U.S. should plan and train for "pre-emptive or retaliatory military strikes" against known terrorist hideouts and take covert action when direct strikes are inappropriate. "When the perpetrators {of the Pan Am bombing} are found they should be punished," Mrs. McLaughlin told a press conference jammed with relatives of people killed in the attack.

But the Bush administration isn't likely to launch an anti-terrorism strike at a time when, according to a recent State Department report, terrorist acts against the U.S. have declined. The recent release of two Americans held captive in Lebanon and signs that Iran and Syria, which are eager to improve relations with the West, are working to win the release of other hostages also suggest that the U.S. wouldn't consider a strike.

Moreover, the U.S. military also has always been uncomfortable with the idea of fielding the kind of special-operations forces required to carry out surgical strikes.

The White House embraced the broad outlines of the terrorism report, but refused to commit itself to carrying out any of its specific recommendations. "We agree we can have a more aggressive policy, but we don't want to comment on how," said White House Press Secretary Marlin Fitzwater.

The commission didn't blame specific countries or individuals for the Lockerbie bombing, saying that wasn't its mission. The criminal investigation into the attack, which killed all 259 people on board and 11 on the ground, continues.

However, it is widely suspected that the bombing was ordered by Iran in retaliation for the accidential downing of an Iranian jetliner in the Persian Gulf by a U.S. naval vessel, and that it was carried out by the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine -- General Command.

The presidential panel made dozens of recommendations to counter the terrorist threat, including: the establishment of a new supervisory office -- assistant secretary for security and intelligence -- within the Transportation Department; increased importance for the security operation within the FAA; and the assigning of federal security managers with direct access to the FAA chief to major international and domestic airports.

The panel also urged the government to alert passengers to credible threats and to establish criteria for when passengers should be notified. Notification became an issue after the Lockerbie disaster when it was disclosed that U.S. embassy workers in Moscow were warned of a possible terrorist attack on Pan Am flights through Frankfurt, West Germany. Although the authorities have since concluded the threat was a hoax, critics say the government practiced selective notification, warning some citizens and not others.

In addition, in one of its most controversial suggestions, the panel called for a halt to the FAA's plan to require the installation of thermal neutron analysis bomb-detecting machines at high-risk international airports. The panel said the equipment, which can detect plastic explosives, isn't reliable or effective enough, and it recommended that research and development into other bomb-detecting technologies be accelerated.

Since the bombing, the TNA machines have been a major element in the FAA's stepped-up security program. They have been installed in airports in New York and Miami, and the FAA wanted airlines to buy 150 more for 40 more airports at a cost of $175 million. But the commission said the machine couldn't reliably detect the small amount of plastic explosives believed used in the Pan Am explosion.

The commission said the bombing might have been prevented had Pan Am done a better job of screening bags, especially unaccompanied bags, and passengers. "Pan Am's generally poor performance on security requirements at Frankfurt {where Flight 103 originated} existed not only for months before the tragedy but -- incredibly -- for nine months thereafter," Mrs. McLaughlin said.

The report added that the Pan Am security operation in Frankfurt has been improved and is now a "model" for other airlines. And it found that the overall state of international aviation security has improved since the destruction of Flight 103.

In a telephone interview, Pan Am Chairman Thomas Plaskett called the commission's criticism "unfair," adding, "we believe that prior to the bombing of Flight 103, and subsequent to it, we were providing a secure transportation system."

The commission, which besides Mrs. McLaughlin consisted of four members of Congress and two former military figures, reserved much of its harshest criticism for the FAA, which it described as largely "reactive" and failing to meet the challenge of international terrorism. "The FAA's performance was nothing short of dismal," said Sen. Alfonse D'Amato (R., N.Y.), a panel member.

In response, FAA Administrator James Busey acknowledged that "the system was flawed, mistakes were made," and that he plans to move forward on the panel's suggestions. But he also contended that the agency has made some progress in improving security over the past year, and said that for now, the thermal-neutron-analysis machines were the best bomb detectors available.

The families of the bombing's victims generally praised the commission, although some took issue with its criticism of the bomb-detecting machines. In addition, Michael Lemov, an attorney for Families of Pan Am Flight 103/Lockerbie, criticized the report for not urging that aviation security be taken out of the FAA and assigned to a new, separate agency.
