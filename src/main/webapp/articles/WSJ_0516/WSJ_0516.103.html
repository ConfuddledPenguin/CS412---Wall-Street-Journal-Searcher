Greyhound Union May Seek Back Pay Due to NLRB Move

  • Author: Robert Tomsho and Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 05/16/90

Leaders of the Amalgamated Transit Union say they may press Greyhound Lines Inc. for back pay for their striking members as a result of a National Labor Relations Board complaint accusing the bus company of engaging in an unfair labor practice in contract negotiations. More than 9,400 Greyhound employees represented by the Amalgamated Council of Greyhound Local Unions, the ATU's bargaining unit, have been on strike since March 2.

On Monday, NLRB General Counsel Jerry Hunter authorized the issuance of a complaint alleging that Greyhound, a unit of Dallas-based GLI Holding Co., unilaterally implemented some of its contract proposals before reaching a bargaining impasse with the union. "The complaint will also allege that the current strike is an unfair labor practice strike," the NLRB said in a news release.

If unfair bargaining practices and not economic issues are found to have caused the strike, Greyhound could be forced to rehire thousands of striking ATU drivers it has replaced with new hires since the strike began. The company has said that, even if a settlement is reached, it has jobs for only a few hundred of the nearly 6,000 ATU drivers still out.

Though no new bargaining talks have been scheduled, Edward Strait, president of the ATU's Amalgamated Council, said ATU local presidents would discuss offering to go back to work under the terms of their now-expired contract at a meeting May 21. Mr. Strait said that if Greyhound turns down such an offer the company might eventually be forced to pay striking drivers back pay if the NLRB general counsel's complaint is upheld.

"This could be quite costly to them down the road," Mr. Strait said.

Still, the ultimate resolution of the NLRB complaint could be years away. Both sides will now argue the complaint before a federal administrative law judge. The judge's decision can be appealed by either party to the entire labor relations board and their decision can, in turn, be appealed through the federal courts.

"We are confident we will prevail," said Greyhound Executive Vice President P. Anthony Lannie. "We will appeal all the way to the U.S. Supreme Court, should that become necessary."

Meanwhile, Greyhound officials said they were pleased that Mr. Hunter, the NLRB general counsel, also authorized the issuance of a second complaint, this one alleging that the union had engaged in violence and other forms of misconduct during the strike. Mr. Hunter said he had obtained clearance from the board to seek a federal court injunction enjoining the union from such conduct nationwide.

Since the strike began, Greyhound has spent more than $4 million on legal fees to obtain such injunctions and restraining orders in individual cities and states. The increased costs of legal fees and security were among the reasons Greyhound cited for a $55.8 million net loss during the first quarter. The debt-burdened company is currently negotiating with creditors and lenders on a financial restructuring.
