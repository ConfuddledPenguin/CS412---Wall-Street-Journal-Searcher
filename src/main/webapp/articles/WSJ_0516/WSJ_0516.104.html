Economy: Sales of U.S.-Made Vehicles Fell 8.4% In Early May, Dashing Hopes of Rebound

  • Author: Bradley A. Stertz
  • Date: 05/16/90

DETROIT -- Sales of domestically built cars and trucks dropped 8.4% in early May, damping hopes that the auto industry's year-long malaise will end soon. Americans bought 301,030 North American-made cars and trucks in the first 10 days of the month, compared with 328,719 vehicles a year earlier. On a seasonally adjusted basis, the sales stumbled to a 6.4 million-vehicle annual rate compared with a 7.4 million clip a year earlier and a 7.1 million selling rate in late April.

The sales stagnation deeply disappointed car dealers, who had hoped to get at least a few weeks of good sales out of the traditionally strong spring season. Instead, the generally weak results throughout this spring have caused many dealers to cut new car orders and hunker down for an uncertain next several months.

"I'm not betting on anything big happening soon," said Carlo Merlo, owner of Glendale Chrysler-Plymouth in Glendale, Mo. "It's a mediocre market."

As it has in recent periods, Chrysler Corp. posted the largest sales decline among the Big Three auto makers. Chrysler's total vehicle sales fell 21.3% from a year earlier due to a 23% drop in domestic car sales. So far, at least, Chrysler's aggressive advertising campaign comparing its cars to those of Honda Motor Co. isn't drawing customers back into Chrysler showrooms.

Not that Ford Motor Co. fared much better. The No. 2 auto maker saw its total vehicle sales in early May fall 15.4%, with domestic car sales decreasing 19.8%.

Ford officials blamed the shortfall on slightly reduced incentive programs that pulled some May sales into April. Ford also said it hadn't seen the benefit in early May from the launch of the redesigned 1991 Ford Escort subcompact car. Ford's biggest gainer: the recently redesigned Lincoln Town Car, sales of which jumped 81.1% in early May.

General Motors Corp. fared the best, by far, among the Big Three, although its total vehicle sales fell 9.2%, slightly more than the industry overall. GM continued to benefit from surging sales of the Geo line of import and joint-venture cars sold by Chevrolet dealers. Sales of the sporty Geo Storm, built by Isuzu Motors Ltd. of Japan, jumped 37.6%; sales of the Geo Prizm, built at the New United Motors Manufacturing Inc. plant in California that GM runs with Toyota Motor Corp., increased 41.3%

Geo's gains, however, were offset by declines at GM's Pontiac, Buick, Oldsmobile and Cadillac divisions. Each model in the Pontiac lineup had lower sales than a year ago.

Sales of the cars and trucks that Japanese auto makers build in North America increased significantly. The gain reflects increased production schedules by some companies and solid sales gains by more established Japanese makers in the U.S., such as Honda.

Honda said its domestic car sales rose 32.3% to 11,238 in early May. Nissan Motor Co., which has also built vehicles in the U.S. for several years, said its total domestic vehicle sales increased 42.6%

Analysts said the continued soft sales of the Big Three show the saturation of new cars on U.S. streets. "The U.S. auto industry is now the victim of its own prosperity," said David Healy, auto analyst with Barclays BZW investment banking group in New York. "The number of late model vehicles is now at an all-time high."

"Look around at the stop lights," echoed Hoot McInerney, who owns nine dealerships in Michigan and Florida. "The rusted out cars are gone. Everyone's got something shiny and new and it'll be a long time before they come into showrooms."

The bright spot on the horizon: Hopes for reduced interest rates in the near future will stir buyer interest like it has the stock market.
