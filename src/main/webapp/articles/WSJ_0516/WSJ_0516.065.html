Credit Markets: Bond Prices Reverse Course and Fall as Decline In Dollar Triggers Selling by Foreign Investors

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 05/16/90

NEW YORK -- After a three-day run-up, bond prices stumbled as the falling value of the dollar prompted some foreign investors to bail out of the market. The selling began during overnight trading in Tokyo, where the Treasury's new benchmark 30-year bond fell 1/4 point, or $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The losses were carried over to London trading where the U.S. long-term bond suffered further declines. When the market opened for trading in the U.S., prices of the Treasury's benchmark bond were already nearly 1/2 point lower, where they remained for much of the day.

"All the action in the market occurred early in the morning. Activity really trailed off in the afternoon," said Charles Lieberman, a managing director at Manufacturers Hanover Securities Corp.

The bond market sell-off was triggered by a sharp decline in the value of the dollar against the yen. When the dollar falls, the value of dollar-denominated securities also declines, which makes them less attractive to foreign investors.

In trading, the dollar finished at 151.30 yen in Tokyo, well below the 153.35 yen there late Monday. The dollar gave up more ground against the yen in the U.S. and was quoted late here at about 150.80 yen, down from 152.47 yen late Monday. Although the dollar rose against the mark, that had little effect on the market.

The Treasury's benchmark bond, which has risen sharply in recent sessions, ended about 3/8 point lower. Municipal, investment-grade corporate and mortgage-backed securities posted similar declines. But prices of high-yield junk bonds advanced for the second consecutive day.

Traders and analysts said two economic reports released early yesterday on industrial production and capacity utilization caused a brief bounce in the market. But it didn't last very long.

The two economic reports offered fresh evidence that the economy is weak. Industrial production dropped 0.4% last month, as U.S. factories, utilities and mines operated at 83% of their production capacity. Factory output climbed 0.5% in March, when the operating rate was 83.5%.

A decline in the production of autos was a major factor in the April decline, but production of lumber products, computers, nonferrous metals, paper, clothing, textiles and many other products also dropped from the previous month.

Typically, negative economic news has a positive impact on the bond market because lower economic growth leads to speculation that interest rates will eventually move lower. And when interest rates fall, bond prices rise.

James Capra, a senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc., said the market's inability to rally after the economic reports came out may mean the market is "showing signs of fatigue" and that investors believe bonds have become too pricey.

Others, however, speculate that investors may be reluctant to buy bonds ahead of today's potentially market-moving consumer price index report for April. The index is a key measure of inflation.

Economists surveyed by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report estimate, on average, a 0.3% rise in April's index. A higher inflation rate could depress the market.

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 101 11/32 to yield 8.65%. The price was down from 101 24/32 to yield 8.58% on Monday. The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 101 14/32 to yield 8.64%, compared with 101 22/32 to yield 8.60% on Monday.

Short-term rates were slightly lower. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at about 7.65% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.89%, down slightly from the average rate of 7.67% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.93% set at Monday's Treasury bill auction.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended 2/32 to 5/32 point lower.

Government National Mortgage Association 9% securities for June delivery ended at 95 5/8, down 4/32; 9 1/2% securities were at 98 1/32, down 2/32; 10% securities were at 100 14/32, down 5/32; and Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9 1/2% securities were at 97 21/32, down 2/32.

The Ginnie Mae 9% issue was yielding 9.77% to a 12-year average life assumption, as the spread above the Treasury 10-year note narrowed 0.02 percentage point to 1.13.

In the derivative market, Ryland Acceptance Corp. offered a $315 million real estate mortgage investment conduit backed by Ginnie Mae 10% securities through Kidder, Peabody & Co. And the Federal National Mortgage Association issued a $190 million Remic backed by its 9 1/2% principal-only strips through First Boston Corp.

Corporate Issues

Despite the mounting problems in the thrift industry, one California savings and loan managed to tap the debt market with a sellout bond issue.

Golden West Financial Corp., considered to be one of the healthiest and most conservatively run thrifts in the nation, sold $115 million of subordinated notes. Strong investor demand prompted underwriters led by Merrill Lynch Capital Markets to increase the size of the issue from $100 million.

Among other new issues, Fleet/Norstar Financial Group Inc. and Delta Air Lines offered a total of $450 million of debt.

Meanwhile, junk bonds surged for the second straight day in a broad-based market rally.

Most junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings, gained 1/2 to 3/4 point.

Junk dealers said trading volume was the strongest they've seen in weeks.

Among the bonds that advanced strongly were R.H. Macy & Co.'s 14 1/2% senior subordinated debentures, maturing in 1998, which added 1 1/2 to end at 73 1/2; Safeway Stores Inc.'s 12% subordinated debentures, due in 1998, which chalked up a point to close at 98 3/4; and Duracell Holdings Corp.'s 13 1/2% subordinated debentures, due in 2000, which inched up 1/2 point to finish at 103 1/2.

The junk bonds of Ames Department Stores Inc. were also higher. An attorney for the Rocky Hill, Conn.-based retailer told a bankruptcy court judge that the firm has reached an agreement in principle with 33 of its pre-bankruptcy lenders. The judge is expected to issue an order today on Ames's request for $250 million of interim financing.

Ames's 13% senior subordinated reset notes rose a point to close at 28, reflecting the market's belief that the firm will secure interim financing.

In the investment-grade corporate debt market, corporate issues finished unchanged to about 1/8 point lower.

Municipals

Municipal bonds were 1/8 to 3/8 point lower in late dealings as the tax-exempt market grappled with its first onslaught of supply in several weeks.

Secondary market dealings were quiet as attention focused on the new-issue arena, where more than $1 billion of new debt was priced.

In one of the largest deals of the day, a Chase Securities Inc. group reported that the account was closed on an issue of $250 million of Texas National Research Laboratory Commission's superconducting supercollider project general obligation bonds priced yesterday.

By comparison, about one-half, or about $33.6 million, remained from an issue of $72 million of Los Angeles general obligation bonds won by a Prudential-Bache Capital Funding group.

"Los Angeles was priced so aggressively. It was priced through the highest trade we've seen on California GOs," said one New York serial bond trader.

Prior to the pricing of the Los Angeles bonds, several traders had suggested that some of the day's deals might come aggressively priced, with high prices and low yields, because dealers needed to pick up bonds to fill slim inventories.

Whether dealers will have difficulty moving new bonds will depend on the consumer price data scheduled for release today, several traders said.

In negotiated dealings, a Goldman, Sachs & Co. group priced $166.5 million of New York State Environmental Facilities Corp.'s state water pollution control revolving revenue bonds.

In secondary action, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was off 3/8 point at 98 bid to yield 7.35%.

Foreign Bonds

Prices of West German government bonds fell as investors sold bonds on fears that a large supply of new securities would depress prices.

The government is expected to issue between four billion and five billion marks (between $2.44 billion and $3.04 billion) of 10-year notes on Friday. Dealers expect the notes to have a coupon rate of between 8 1/2% and 8 5/8%. That would be up from a rate of 7 3/4% on the current benchmark 10-year note.

In trading yesterday, the benchmark issue fell 0.55 point to 94.10 to yield 8.66%.

Dealers said the market also was hurt by a newspaper report that said West Germany may soon issue bonds geared toward international investors, the proceeds of which would be used to invest in East Germany. West Germany's finance ministry denied plans to launch international bonds.

But Bonn and the 11 West German states are planning a 115 billion mark special German Unity Fund financed by credits to help pay for the reconstruction of East Germany's economy. The fund would be built up by debt issued in the domestic capital market.

In Japan, government bonds ended stronger on the yen's strength. But many traders in Japan don't believe that bond prices will continue to rise because the market's fundamentals haven't changed.

"At this point the market is only rising on the yen, not on short-term interest rates," said a trader at one of Japan's large brokerage firms.

Rates on new three-month certificates of deposit were unchanged at 7.36%, and Japanese analysts don't expect short-term rates to drop much. Therefore, it will be difficult for the Japanese bond market rally to continue, they said.

At the Tokyo Stock Exchange close, Japan's benchmark 10-year issue was quoted at 88.13, up 0.44 point, to yield 6.93%.
