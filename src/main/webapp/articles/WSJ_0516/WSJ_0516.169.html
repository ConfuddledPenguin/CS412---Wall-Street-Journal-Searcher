Foreign Exchange: Yen Strengthens Against Dollar, Mark; Traders Nervous About U.S. Economy

  • Author: John Bader and Tim Carrington
  • Date: 05/16/90

NEW YORK -- The yen racked up further gains against the dollar and the mark as the Japanese unit capitalized on market uncertainty surrounding the direction of the U.S. economy and German political developments. The U.S. unit fell almost two yen on worries about recent mixed signals on the U.S. economy and nervousness about the possibility of lower interest rates, traders said.

Lingering worry over the effect of losses by West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl's Christian Democrats in Sunday's state elections caused selling of the mark, especially against the resurgent yen, they said.

The yen was helped in its rise against the dollar, mark and other major currencies by fading concern about the Japanese stock market as Japanese share prices generally recover, said Gabriel Grippo, senior foreign exchange trader at Manufacturers Hanover Trust in New York.

Traders said that turmoil in the Japanese stock market, now receded, was seen as the most important factor blocking an increase in Japanese interest rates by the Bank of Japan, a step that could strengthen the yen. They noted, however, that with the yen on the rise, such an increase may not be necessary.

The Nikkei stock index finished Tuesday in Tokyo at 31,997.04 points, down a slight 45.61 points on late profit-taking. The index rose 530.51 points Monday and 531.88 points Friday.

Interest-rate concerns are now weakening the dollar, as participants try to gauge which way the U.S. economy is headed, Mr. Grippo and other participants agreed.

The market's interest in today's Consumer Price Index figure for April stems from the focus on interest rates, they said.

Although most participants believe the Federal Reserve will maintain a neutral stance on interest rates for the time being, the CPI is being watched for any sign that pressure for lower rates might be building. For that reason, it's anticipated that a lower-than-expected CPI number would hurt the dollar, traders said.

In the general view of traders, the U.S. economy offers the status quo, while both the West German and Japanese central banks promise change. "There's a conviction that the Bundesbank will tighten some time soon," said J. Paul Horne, international economist with Smith Barney, Harris Upham in Paris. "And we think the Bank of Japan has got to tighten, too."

That expectation is beginning to clear the air after a period of uncertainty over the Japanese currency. In the past three weeks, investors in yen-based bonds have fared well. "Most of the flows have been going into the yen," said George Magnus, economist with S.G. Warburg Securities in London. "And it's not sure that move is over."

Sales of the mark against the Japanese unit were responsible in large part for a modest rise by the dollar against the West German currency, said Erik Lehtis, senior dealer at Mitsui Taiyo Kobe Bank in New York.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6465 marks, up from 1.6425 marks late Monday, and at 150.80 yen, down from 152.47 yen late Monday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6740, down from $1.6825 Monday.

In early trading in Tokyo Wednesday, the U.S. currency was quoted at 152.05 yen, up from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 151.30 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.6480 marks.

"The (West German) election news inspired a lot of profit-taking," Mr. Lehtis said, noting that the mark's recent peak around 96 yen had left the West German currency vulnerable to a correction on technical grounds.

The West German unit ended the New York day around 91.61 yen, off from 92.83 yen late in New York Monday.

The mark was also hurt by a denial by West German monetary authorities early in the day of a published report that the Bundesbank might issue mark-denominated bonds to help finance unification.

The issue of such bonds by the central bank would have been positive for the mark because of the added funds they would have generated for the West German government during unification, traders said. The denial of plans for an issue depressed the mark a bit by removing a potential source of strength for the currency, they said.

Dealers' long-term mark bullishness remained unshaken however, with some crediting a statement by Mr. Kohl late in the European day favoring an all-German election "at the earliest possible date" with limiting the mark's losses.

Furthermore, most traders remain confident that German unification plans will bring about a high-interest-rate stance from the Bundesbank. "A strong Deutsche mark -- perhaps heading toward parity against the U.S. dollar in the mid-1990s -- will take the inflationary sting out of high East German growth," said Shearson Lehman Hutton in a recent report. "But to ensure the Deutsche mark rises, the Bundesbank will need to keep real interest rates very high."

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $370.90 an ounce, up $2.50. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.7 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $370.50 an ounce.
