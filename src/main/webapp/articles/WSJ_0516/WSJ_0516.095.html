Justice Agency Supports Bill Expanding U.S. Ability to Seize Assets in Fraud Cases

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 05/16/90

WASHINGTON -- The Justice Department is pushing legislation that would vastly expand federal prosecutors' ability to freeze the assets of defendants in fraud cases before trial and permanently seize the assets after a conviction. The proposed bill would build on what prosecutors consider to be their successful use of existing laws that allow them to seize the assets of drug traffickers, money launderers and racketeers.

The new legislation, sent to Congress last month, has already attracted powerful bipartisan support. Chairman Joseph Biden (D., Del.) of the Senate Judiciary Committee and South Carolina Sen. Strom Thurmond, the panel's ranking Republican, are expected to introduce a bill similar to the Justice Department's proposal, possibly as soon as this week.

In 1989, a record $580 million was forfeited to the Justice Department as investigative agencies such as the Drug Enforcement Administration and the Federal Bureau of Investigation increased their efforts to track down drug criminals' illicit proceeds. Much of this activity became possible when Congress gave prosecutors wider latitude to seek forfeiture of assets tangentially related to criminal activity, as opposed to illegal profits alone.

The bill being promoted by the Justice Department would extend forfeiture provisions to cover assets that are the "gross receipts" of fraud involving use of the U.S. mail, credit cards and computers, as well as military-procurement fraud and fraud against federal programs such as Medicare. A defendant's legitimately acquired assets wouldn't be subject to forfeiture under the legislation.

The proposed bill would allow prosecutors to attempt to seize assets either during a criminal trial or in a separate civil proceeding. Under the civil approach, illegally gained assets may be frozen quickly, before a defendant has a chance to hide them, according to Barry Stern, a Justice Department official involved in crafting the proposed laws. A central goal of the expanded forfeiture provisions is to allow money to be returned to the government or to private victims of fraud, Mr. Stern said.

Some defense lawyers, civil libertarians and criminal targets have criticized the government's expanding efforts to seize assets as excessively punitive and possibly unconstitutional. These criticisms have arisen, for example, in securities-manipulation cases prosecuted under existing racketeering statutes.
