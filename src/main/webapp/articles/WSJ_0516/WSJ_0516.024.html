Charter Medical Posts Biggest Loss Since '88

  • Author: George Anders and Glenn Ruffenach
  • Date: 05/16/90

Charter Medical Corp. reported its biggest quarterly loss since going private in a 1988 buy-out, raising concern on Wall Street about the company's ability to service its debts. For the second quarter ended March 31, Charter posted a $67 million loss on revenue of $475.2 million. The year earlier, the psychiatric-care company based in Macon, Ga., had a $14.6 million loss on revenue of $397.3 million.

Part of Charter's latest loss reflected a wider than expected $35 million pretax charge for "inaccurate recording of previously reported contractual allowances, bad debts and expense accruals." The company had estimated in March that it would take a $28 million charge for the accounting problems. For the March quarter, Charter also incurred $11.2 million in pretax charges for canceled development projects and a lost lawsuit.

Charter's chief financial officer, C. Michael Ford, said the company has already taken steps, including cutbacks in capital-spending and cost-cutting programs, to "protect bondholders and employees." He declined to say whether any managers or employees had been dismissed in connection with the accounting problems. In the long term, Mr. Ford said, "We think Charter is viable, and we can meet our debt-service requirements and continue to operate the company."

But some Wall Street analysts said Charter's basic business -- separate from the write-offs -- appeared to suffer in the latest quarter. "Their cash flow as a percentage of revenue dropped to 15.8% from 26.3%," said Meredith Adler, an analyst at First Boston Corp. "That's disappointing." Ms. Adler said Charter faced pressure on the average length of patient stay, and on its adolescent-care business.

Charter's business slump intensified worries about the company's ability to handle its $1.5 billion in long-term debt. The company's 14% senior subordinated bonds, which had traded at 70 cents on the dollar in March, were quoted yesterday at 48 cents to 50 cents on the dollar. Other, pay-in-kind bonds were quoted as low as 23 cents on the dollar yesterday.

"Charter could face significant cash-flow shortfalls over the next five years," analyst Roger Gordon of Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. warned. "The company could be forced to restructure its long-term debt." Mitch Bartlett, an analyst at Merrill Lynch & Co., also predicted that Charter's latest results "spell a restructuring for the company."

Charter, however, asserted that it can pull through. Mr. Ford said the company has reduced planned capital expenditures for the year to $140 million from $200 million. That, he said, combined with cost reductions in, among other areas, marketing and advertising programs, should enable Charter to meet its debt payments and still complete construction of four additional psychiatric hospitals in the next nine months or so.

"We don't anticipate having to restructure debt," Mr. Ford said.

Mr. Ford acknowledged that insurance companies and other payers of medical bills are increasing pressure on healthcare providers -- particularly providers of psychiatric care -- to reduce the length of patient stays and to cut bills. But that pressure, he said, has been offset at Charter by an increase in patient admissions -- up 20% in the most recent quarter.

Charter had been advised in its 1988 buy-out by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., which also underwrote high-risk, high-yield "junk" bonds that helped finance the transaction. Drexel ceased most operations this past winter, leaving Charter to search for a new Wall Street adviser.

Despite Charter's current problems, Mr. Ford said Drexel had been "very helpful" in structuring the initial buy-out and selling the debt. He added that the plan to go private was -- and still is -- a good idea. "It was a chance for employers and management to benefit from their own work," he said. "Employee ownership increases productivity."
