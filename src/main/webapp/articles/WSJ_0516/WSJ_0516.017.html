Economy: Industry Output Declined 0.4% During April --- Production Slipped to 83% Of Capacity; Weakness In Economy Is Indicated

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 05/16/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Reserve offered more evidence of continuing weakness in the economy. Industrial production dropped 0.4% in April, as the nation's factories, utilities and mines operated at 83% of their production capacity, a Fed report said. Factory output had climbed 0.5% in March, when the operating rate was 83.5%.

A steep fall in production of cars and trucks was a big factor in last month's decline, the central bank said. But production of lumber products, computers, nonferrous metals, paper, clothing, textiles and many other products also dropped from the previous month. The only industries showing significant increases in output were chemicals, petroleum, iron-and-steel and printing and publishing.

"In no sense is there strength here," said Maury Harris, chief economist at PaineWebber Group Inc. in New York.

The new industrial statistics are the latest in a string of reports suggesting the economy may be weaker than many analysts had believed. After slowing severely at the end of last year, the economy seemed to pick up in the first quarter of 1990, aided by unusually mild winter weather, which boosted construction and other economic activity.

But in April, private-sector employment declined and the nation's unemployment rate rose for the first time in more than a year, the Labor Department recently reported. Retail sales also dropped during the month, and the prices factories and other producers charge for their goods declined, the government said.

The latest numbers were released as a Fed committee was meeting here to set monetary policy for the next six weeks. The figures made it even more likely that central-bank officials would decide to leave interest rates unchanged.

Until recently, many analysts had come to believe that the Fed would be inclined to push up interest rates to battle inflation. But last week's report of a decline in the producer price index and other signs of slack in the economy, virtually ended such speculation.

The drop in industry operating rates is another sign that price pressures may be easing. When factories operate well below full capacity, they aren't likely to run into production snags, shortages and other problems that can lead to higher prices.

Economists, however, are reluctant to draw many conclusions from a single month's reports. The drop in industrial production last month followed gains of 0.5% in March and 0.9% in February and a fall of 1% in January. Manufacturing output was particularly weak in April, falling 0.6%, while output by utilities rose 0.6% during the month and mining production climbed 0.9%.

For more than a year, manufacturing has been the softest segment of the U.S. economy, with the auto industry particularly weak. In January, the nation's Big Three auto companies temporarily closed plants and laid off thousands of workers in an effort to pare bulging inventories of unsold cars. After selling many vehicles, the companies reopened factories and rehired workers. But in the aftermath of the winter's vigorous promotions and buying incentives, car sales are down, and production is sluggish. In April, output of motor vehicles and parts plunged 10.3%, the Fed report said.

Nevertheless, Theodore Tung, chief economist for National City Corp., in Cleveland, said, "We have good reason to expect the manufacturing sector will improve as the year progresses."

"Overall the sources of strength driving manufacturing are reasonably good," he said, explaining that consumer spending, export orders and capital spending by businesses are still rising.

All the numbers were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
