Inside Track: Profitable Stock Strategy: Do as the Chairman Does

  • Author: Alexandra Peers and Tom Herman
  • Date: 05/16/90

NEW YORK -- Company chairmen who bet big on their companies' stocks in the first four months of this year generally saw their faith rewarded. While the Dow Jones Industrial Average is up about 2.5% this year, some of the chairmen have scored much bigger gains, according to data compiled by Invest/Net, a North Miami, Fla., firm that tracks Securities and Exchange Commission filings by officers, directors and other corporate insiders.

Overall, according to Invest/Net, 10 of the 12 chairmen who increased their stakes by more than 1% have profited, at least on paper.

Consider Thomas Owens, who heads XL/Datacomp Inc. of Hinsdale, Ill. Mr. Owens spent about $306,000 on 42,000 shares of the computer remarketer in purchases in February and March, SEC filings show. He paid an average price of $7.28 a share. The stock closed yesterday in national over-the-counter trading at $9.75, unchanged.

"There had been a lot of panic selling in the stock" last year on news that International Business Machines Corp. would reduce its discount to computer remarketers, squeezing their profit margins, said Mr. Owens. "The marketplace reacted horribly," substantially overselling the shares.

Mr. Owens said he bought both to support the stock and because he thought he was getting a bargain price. "I've made a nice profit," he said, adding that he expects an even more significant profit given the stock's trading range last fall of about $20-$24.

For Jack Byrne, chairman of Fund American Cos. and its Fireman's Fund Insurance Cos. unit, love turned into a smart investment strategy. Last February, he spent about $1.9 million on 60,000 shares of the Greenwich, Conn., parent company.

"I bought them to be a Valentine's Day present for my wife," said Mr. Byrne. "That's all the economic thinking that went into it." Still, Mr. Byrne added, "the stock looked awfully cheap to me."

He paid an average share price of $31.13. Yesterday, the stock closed in New York Stock Exchange trading at $32.375, down 37.5 cents.

But Mr. Byrne isn't bragging about his market-timing ability. "The stock went off two to three points immediately after I bought," he recalls with a laugh. "That shows my perspicacity."

Moreover, he says his wife initially needled him about his timing, and he quips that next year he may give her roses instead. He adds that the purchase represented only a small portion of his total holdings; Fund American's latest proxy statement shows that Mr. Byrne owns a total of 2,630,000 shares, either outright or through warrants or options.

Sometimes, such big-money bets aren't as well timed. For example, in October 1988, John S. Reed, Citicorp's chairman and chief executive officer, spent $2.5 million to buy 100,000 Citicorp shares, mostly at $25 a share. The stock closed yesterday at $24, up 12.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Mr. Reed is "delighted with his holdings," he said through a spokesman yesterday. The Citicorp chief is "a long-term investor" and continues to believe Citicorp stock is "a great investment," the spokesman said. He also pointed out that Mr. Reed's 1988 purchase represented only part of his stake, which he said now totals 543,306 shares -- making him the company's largest individual holder.

On the other hand, SEC filings show that William Schreyer, chairman of Merrill Lynch & Co., in February of this year purchased 20,000 shares at $22.18 a share. The purchase came shortly after the company announced a cost-cutting program that depressed quarterly earnings and, subsequently, the price of Merrill stock. At the time, Mr. Schreyer said the price decline made the stock a particularly good value. Yesterday, on the New York Stock Exchange, it closed at $23.25, down 37.5 cents.

Carl S. Quinn, chairman of Arkla Exploration Co., doubled his holdings in the Shreveport, La., oil and gas exploration company with an April 12 purchase of 11,000 shares, SEC filings show.

It's paid off handsomely. While Mr. Quinn paid $16.53 a share, according to the filings, the stock closed yesterday in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange at $19.875, up 12.5 cents.

Arkla Exploration, a unit of Arkla Inc., just went public last December. Its stock price has ranged between about $16 and $23 since, said corporate secretary Greg Jones. Mr. Quinn, he said, bought near its low, when the stock "had mirrored the decline in oil and gas stocks." Oil and gas demand was soft in the first two months of the year, which were warmer than expected, and that depressed stocks throughout the sector. But the sector in general is beginning to rebound, he said, Arkla shares along with it.

Mr. Quinn was out of the country and not available for comment.
