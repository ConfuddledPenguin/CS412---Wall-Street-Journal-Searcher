Circle K Corp. Asks Protection Of Chapter II --- Move Follows the Refusal Of Creditors to Agree To Junk Restructuring

  • Author: Sonia L. Nazario
  • Date: 05/16/90

Circle K Corp., the nation's second-largest convenience store chain, filed for bankruptcy court protection last night after failing to get creditors to agree to a restructuring of its $1.1 billion in high-yield, below-investment-grade "junk" debt. Circle K President Robert A. Dearth Jr. told Circle K employees that management will continue to run the chain, based in Phoenix, Ariz., and that it is contacting key vendors, seeking their cooperation to keep stores stocked.

The filing under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code marks the end of a hard-fought battle by William Franke and other outside directors to win lender approval of a plan to avoid a bankruptcy-law filing. The efforts were supported by Circle K's largest shareholder, the Cincinnati investor Carl Lindner, whose American Financial Corp. holds 38% of the stock and is a large Circle K creditor. The moves were aimed at gaining enough time to sell assets, generate cash, and design a new competitive plan.

Indeed, the company last month averted a default on its debt by signing a preliminary accord with senior creditors and Mr. Lindner that provided a seven-month moratorium on debt and interest payments. And earlier this month, Circle K's widely criticized chairman, Karl Eller, resigned.
