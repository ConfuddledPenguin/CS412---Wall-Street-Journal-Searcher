Tuesday's Markets: Stocks Eke Out Record; Bonds Slip --- Cautious Investors Mull Profit-Taking; Dollar Falls vs. Yen

  • Author: Douglas R. Sease
  • Date: 05/16/90

Stock prices eked out another all-time high, but bonds fell modestly as investors turned cautious. The dollar was mixed. The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained a scant 0.92 to 2822.45 in moderate trading. But other broader measures of the stock market lost ground, and 856 issues declined on the New York Stock Exchange while 654 rose.

Analysts said the speed with which stocks rose during the past few days has made investors jittery and inclined to take profits. Indeed, the industrial average fell 14 points in early trading before recovering and hovering around the record level set Monday. But some analysts said they were surprised there wasn't more selling, especially after the dollar weakened against the yen and bond prices slumped.

"I think everyone was looking for a big correction," said Thomas Callahan, senior vice president of U.S. equities at Yamaichi International (America).

Charles Clough, chief market strategist at Merrill Lynch, said he doesn't see any evidence that the current rally is the beginning of a bull market. Nevertheless, he added that prices could continue to climb for weeks, if not months, subject to sudden shifts in investor sentiment that could make stock prices volatile.

Just as the rapid runup in stock prices created opportunities for profit taking in that market, so did the sharp rally in bond prices last week and early this week. Bond prices dropped as trading got under way in New York, then stabilized at lower levels for the remainder of the day.

Some traders said the rally that brought the yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond to less than 8.6% from more than 9% two weeks ago was probably the result of optimism overrunning the sense of caution that had prevailed in the bond market for weeks.

But caution returned as traders saw the dollar sinking against the yen and looked at their bulging inventories of bonds, the result mostly of last week's record auction of new Treasury debt. Although many traders expect good news on inflation today with the release of the April consumer price index, the chance of a negative surprise keeps them wary.

In major market action:

Stock prices crept higher. Trading volume on the New York Stock Exchange slackened to 165.7 million shares from more than 225 million Monday.

Bond prices were off. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell three-eighths of a point, or about $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the issue climbed to 8.65%.

The dollar was mixed. In late afternoon New York trading the currency was at 1.6465 marks and 150.80 yen compared with 1.6425 marks and 152.47 yen Monday.
