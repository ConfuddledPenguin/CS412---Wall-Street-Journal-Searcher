Abreast of the Market: Industrials Hit Record High Again, Although Broader Indexes Decline

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 05/16/90

NEW YORK -- Investors bought blue chips again, nudging the Dow Jones Industrial Average to another all-time high, but broader market indexes snapped their long string of gains because of profit-taking. The industrial average closed up 0.92 to 2822.45. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index both fell, capping 11-session winning streaks that began April 30. The S&P 500 dropped 0.47 to 354.28, the composite index lost 0.34 to 193.31.

Declining issues outpaced advancers by 856 to 654 on the New York Stock Exchange, where volume totaled 165,730,000 shares compared with 225,410,000 shares Monday. Declines in broader indexes reflected the cautious attitude money managers have about the latest rally.

James Wright, chief investment officer at Banc One Asset Management Corp., says he still hasn't changed the asset allocation in an account that is 40% stocks, 15% cash and 45% bonds. "I would have adjusted my equity position up if I really believed the rally," he said.

Expectations of slowing inflation are driving the bond market, which has a big influence over stocks currently, he said. "The bond market is not at all predictable, and it is running the stock market quite clearly," Mr. Wright said. He believes stock traders could get whipsawed if bond prices swing again on today's report of the consumer price index for April.

The report is expected to show that both the overall index and the "core inflation rate" -- a measure of prices excluding volatile food and energy products -- gained 0.3% last month, according to a consensus estimate of economists surveyed by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report. The index gained 0.5% in February and March.

Traders are also cautious about the stock market because they haven't seen many of their foreign customers stepping up to buy U.S. equities. But the yen's strength against the dollar may encourage some Japanese investors to buy soon. Japan's fiscal year began in April, one trader at Daiwa Securities America said, and so many institutional investors are looking for a propitious time to buy U.S. stocks.

Jack Solomon, technical analyst at Bear Stearns, noted that stock prices held ground despite a weaker bond market and a sharp decline in a recent market darling, Deere & Co.

Deere dropped 2 1/8 to 75 on 1.9 million shares. Goldman Sachs lowered its earnings estimate for the fiscal year ending in October by 15 cents a share, to $6.35. Analyst Thomas Burns said he took the action after the company told him it had cut prices on some of its farm tractors, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

American Express climbed 1 3/8 to 30 5/8 on 2.2 million shares on renewed rumors that the company would sell its Shearson Lehman Hutton securities unit to Primerica. American Express strongly denied the rumors -- "I categorically deny them," said a spokesman -- while Primerica said it "as a matter of policy doesn't comment on rumors." American Express has agreed to buy the remaining publicly traded shares of Shearson, subject to a shareholders' vote expected in June.

Primerica rose 5/8 to 31 7/8. Other blue chips to bolster the Dow industrial average included International Business Machines, which went up 1 3/8 to 115 5/8, International Paper, up 1 to 51 5/8, Allied-Signal, up 1 1/8 to 37 1/4, and Procter & Gamble, up 3/8 to 76 1/8.

Compaq Computer climbed 2 1/8 to 114 1/2. Analysts at a number of brokerage firms made favorable comments on the stock after a meeting with company officials.

Federal National Mortgage Association gained 3/8 to 38 1/4 on two million shares, recovering from its sell-off Monday after the Treasury urged Congress to require Fannie Mae and other government-sponsored agencies to seek triple-A credit ratings that wouldn't be based on implicit federal guarantees for their debt.

The affected agencies include Federal Home Loan Mortgage, which also rebounded by climbing 1 to 70 1/8, and Student Loan Marketing Association, which slid an additional 3/8 to 48 3/8 after falling 1 3/4 points Monday.

Marsh & McLennan gained 1 1/8 to 73 3/8 after boosting its quarterly dividend by 4% to 65 cents a share. Other financial stocks to attract buyers included Household International, which went up 1 to 46 3/4, Chase Manhattan, up 3/8 to 25 3/4, and J.P. Morgan, up 1/2 to 37 3/4.

Texas Instruments rose 2 1/4 to 39 7/8. In reaffirming a "buy" recommendation, Kidder Peabody boosted its estimates of the company's 1990 and 1991 earnings and established a target price of $75 a share for the stock, which carries the firm's highest investment rating.

A utility issue, Niagara Mohawk Power, gained 3/8 to 14 1/8. B. Ralph Sylvia, who engineered a turnaround at Detroit Edison's Fermi 2 nuclear plant, resigned his post at that company to run Niagara Mohawk's troubled nuclear operations.

J.C. Penney lost 1 3/8 to 65 1/4. While the company reported that its first-quarter net income rose more than 20% from a year earlier, the latest results fell short of most analysts' projections.

News Corp. dropped 5/8 to 15 3/8 after reporting that operating income for the nine months ended March 31 fell 53% from a year earlier.

Tiffany, which posted a 29% increase in net income for the fiscal first quarter ended last month, gained 1 to 43 1/4.

Sotheby's Holdings lost 1/2 to 17 3/8 after a disappointing start to the spring auction season for impressionist and modern art. Habsburg, Feldman, a small, Geneva-based auction house, sold only about 15% of the paintings it put up for bids Monday in New York. But art dealers said that the art Sotheby's will sell at its auction tomorrow evening is far superior to the works offered by Habsburg, Feldman.

Eagle-Picher Industries went down 1/8 to 3 3/4, below its 52-week low. Oppenheimer & Co. removed the stock from its list of recommended issues, citing the number of asbestos claims being filed against the company.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index snapped an 11-session winning streak by falling 0.78 to 353.56. Volume totaled 14,510,000 shares compared with 16,170,000 Monday.

Blessings advanced 1 1/2 to 26 1/2. The company reported an 88% surge in net income for the fiscal quarter ended April 21.

Healthvest gained 1/2 to 2 7/8. Private Capital Management, an investment firm based in Naples, Fla., said it holds a 4.96% stake in the company for clients as an investment.

Divi Hotels, which said it doesn't plan to make an interest payment due on one of its two issues of debt securities, fell 1/2 to 3. The company is in the midst of an exchange offer for both issues.
