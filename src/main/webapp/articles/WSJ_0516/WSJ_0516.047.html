Letters to the Editor: Reductio Ad Absurdum Can Become a Burden

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/16/90

In his May 1 editorial-page account of the Committee for the Free World's conference, David Frum quotes me as describing American culture, because of its vitality, as "poisonous" rather than merely "decadent" as Hilton Kramer had argued. My offense was in truth a far more grievous one: deliberate and premeditated irony. In response to Mr. Kramer's gloomy indictment of American popular culture, I maintained that if he added an undoubted vitality to his list of its defects, it would surely deserve even sterner censure, viz., "poisonous." My argument would, I thought, be understood by those present as a reductio ad absurdum of Mr. Kramer's position, rather than as an expression of my own views. After all, I went on to dissent more plainly by making the ho-hummish argument that American (and Western) culture has its good and bad points, and the slightly more interesting one that some of its bad points will be rectified shortly by the absorption into the Western cultural mainstream of Central European writers who have emerged from the experience of totalitarianism resistant to cultural fashions.

But these are transparent excuses. I ignored the advice, given at my mother's knee, never to commit irony in a public place; I am justly punished for it by your report; and Mr. Kramer has the last laugh.

John O'Sullivan

Editor

National Review

New York
