Law -- Legal Beat: Ashland Oil Plant `Fallout' Prompts Suit

  • Author: Gregory Stricharchuk and Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 05/16/90

Are the oily droplets and white flaky "fallout" from an Ashland Oil Inc. refinery a nuisance that impairs the quality of life? That's what nearly 300 residents of West Virginia and Kentucky claim in a suit against Ashland scheduled for trial today before Judge Tod J. Kaufman of the Charleston, W. Va., circuit court.

The litigation is among a half dozen suits against Ashland over pollution from the Catlettsburg refinery in Kentucky, on the Big Sandy River that borders three states. In its 1989 annual report, Ashland said plaintiffs were seeking about $850 million in damages.

In this case, the residents are seeking $300 million in compensatory damages and $300 million in punitive damages for Ashland's alleged interference with their right under Kentucky law to enjoy their property free from "annoyance." They also seek $30 million for medical monitoring, claiming that pollutants from the refinery have increased their risk of disease. The case is being divided into two parts; the part that goes to trial today will involve only the nuisance allegation.

The Ashland, Ky., company failed in repeated attempts to get the suit dismissed or have it heard in federal court. An Ashland spokesman said, "We believe the suit is without merit and that we have the evidence to prove that." Both sides declined to say whether they had engaged in settlement discussions.

Debra Hamilton, a plaintiffs' attorney from the Charleston firm Di Trapano & Jackson, says her side plans to introduce evidence showing that refinery pollution has altered residents' lifestyles.

A key piece of evidence, she says, is a letter John R. Hall, Ashland's chairman and chief executive officer, sent in February 1988 to people living near the refinery. In the letter, Mr. Hall said, "Particles which are large or heavy enough to fall out of the atmosphere could possibly be considered a nuisance and could cause some skin irritation under certain conditions. However, they are not considered a long-term health risk."

Ms. Hamilton said, "the letter is tantamount to an admission that the fallout is a nuisance." She says her side plans to use Mr. Hall as a witness against Ashland.

Ms. Hamilton said plaintiffs will testify that fallout has forced them to keep children indoors and otherwise alter their lifestyles. Photographs and testimony will be presented showing that when residents leave their windows open the dust invades bedrooms and falls into their mouths. She said people will testify that the pollution has disrupted outdoor activities, including Easter picnics.

Ashland's refinery has operated since 1924. But the suit alleges that air-pollution emissions increased substantially beginning about 1983, with Ashland assuring the public that the pollutants were "innocuous and harmless."

PENNSYLVANIA SUES top seven insurers to force compliance with law.

Regulators have gone on the offensive in state court, seeking to force some of the largest auto insurers working in Pennsylvania to lower their premium rates as mandated by a new law. The suit also charges that the seven companies worked together to stymie implementation of the law.

The law, adopted in February, required rate reductions of 10% for all drivers and as much as 22% for those who waived their right to sue except in severe accidents. The lower rate filings were due May 1.

State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co., Allstate Insurance Co., a unit of Sears, Roebuck & Co., Erie Insurance Exchange, three units of Aetna Life & Casualty Co., and Harleysville Mutual Insurance Co. were named in the suit, filed in Commonwealth Court in Harrisburg. These companies have 46% of the auto market in the state.

Pennsylvania's Assigned Risk Plan, which is supposed to provide insurance for drivers rejected by commercial companies, was also named as a defendant. The insurers filed for rate increases, not decreases, with the insurance department. Representatives from the companies, except Harleysville, sit on the governing committee of the assigned-risk plan.

The suit alleges that these companies met four times to discuss the new insurance law and consider "appropriate action." State officials claim these insurers raised the same or similar issues in numerous suits as a way of undermining the implementation of the law and inundating the insurance department with paperwork.

ARMCO LOSES $48.8 million jury verdict in Texas.

A federal jury in Houston assessed the damages after finding that Armco Inc., a Parsippany, N.J., steel company, breached an agreement to lease space in a new office building, causing the lender to foreclose on the project.

The plaintiffs included AWH-I Ltd., a limited partnership formed to construct the building; Bank One Texas, which last year acquired the lender, MBank Houston, after it failed and was taken over by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp.; and the FDIC, which acts as receiver for certain claims pending against MBank.

Underlying the dispute was a 1984 pact under which Armco agreed to lease space for 15 years in a building to be built by AWH-I. AWH-I got a $23.6 million construction loan from MBank. But in 1986, the suit alleged, Armco breached the lease agreement after paying four months' rent, leading AWH-I to default on its loan and the bank to foreclose.

The jury awarded AWH-I about $18 million in actual damages and lawyers fees, plus $8 million in punitive damages. Bank One and the FDIC won $19.8 million in actual damages. The verdict still is subject to approval by Judge Melinda Harmon.

Marvin D. Nathan, a lawyer in Houston with Nathan Wood & Sommers, counsel for AWH-I, asserted that Armco's lease termination was motivated by a desire to get "cheaper rent" while Houston's real-estate market was declining.

A spokesman for Armco said the company is considering an appeal.

REHNQUIST DECRIES DELAYS in capital punishment; rejects proposed remedies.

Chief Justice of the U.S. William Rehnquist criticized proposals by senators on changing the law on appeals to federal courts by state death-row prisoners.

Speaking at the annual meeting of the American Law Institute in Washington, he said there is a "serious malfunction" in the capital-punishment system, leading to uncertainty and endless delays. His speech, on habeas corpus, injected the chief justice unusually deeply into the arena of legislative debate. The Senate is scheduled to consider crime legislation, including habeas-corpus proposals, possibly as soon as next week.

In death-penalty cases, habeas corpus is a process in which inmates convicted in state court, whose appeals are denied, file a new civil suit in federal court charging their constitutional rights were violated by their arrest, trial or sentence.

Chief Justice Rehnquist said a proposal by Sen. Joseph Biden (D., Del.) goes too far in allowing inmates on death row repeated opportunities for appeals that lead to delays in state executions. The Biden legislation's "highly questionable goal" would be accomplished "by overturning a series of Supreme Court cases," he said.

He also criticized a proposal by Sen. Strom Thurmond, (R., S.C.), which has the support of the Bush administration, to cut back sharply on appeals to federal courts by state prisoners on death row. The Thurmond bill would basically bar inmates convicted in state court from challenging their death sentences in federal court, unless they were unable to obtain a "fair" review in state court.

The chief justice urged Congress to consider what he views as a middle approach proposed last year by a task force that he appointed led by retired Supreme Court Justice Lewis Powell. The Powell approach also has been introduced as legislation in the Senate.

The Powell report has been criticized as too restrictive by Sen. Biden and civil-liberties groups. The Powell approach was also criticized by the Judicial Conference, the policy-making arm of the federal judiciary, which rebuffed the chief justice in March by endorsing two amendments that diluted the Powell report.

Congressional aides say there are discussions under way among Senate factions that may produce a compromise proposal by next week.

One facet of the Biden proposal, to which the chief justice objected most strenuously, is to allow inmates in habeas-corpus cases to take advantage of Supreme Court rulings decided after their convictions announcing new constitutional-law principles. This retroactivity, the chief justice said, "would overturn an entire body of Supreme Court precedent."

STATE DEPARTMENT legal adviser will join law firm.

A State Department spokesman said Abraham Sofaer would leave his post as legal adviser June 15 to head the Washington office of Hughes Hubbard & Reed, a New York law firm.

Mr. Sofaer has served as the department's in-house counsel since 1985. Before coming to the State Department, he was a federal judge in Manhattan, N.Y.

Hughes Hubbard's Washington office has had turmoil in recent years. In 1988, 11 lawyers left en masse for Arnold & Porter. More recently Victoria Toensing, a former high-ranking Justice Department official, decided to leave the firm.

But Managing Partner Charles Scherer says that Hughes Hubbard's fortunes have reversed and that under Mr. Sofaer, who will also serve as co-chairman of the litigation department, its Washington office will expand to 30 lawyers from seven.

In addition to litigation, Mr. Sofaer said he plans to focus on arbitration, and "anything involving the government or international affairs or international trade."
