Full of Beans, They're Planning For a Real Exercise in Civic Pride

  • Author: Joseph Pereira
  • Date: 05/16/90

When Boston Mayor Ray Flynn arrives at City Hall on May 30, both he and his breath will be in short pants. It's hoped that thousands of others will report for work the same way. It's all to decide whether Boston or Montreal is in better shape.

Never mind that Massachusetts's economy is winded. When it comes to fitness, it's going to be physical, not fiscal, that counts. The winner on this otherwise normal workday will be the city that gets the most people to exercise for 15 continuous minutes.

Last year, in the first showdown between the two, Boston lost by 420,868 to 248,328. Consequently, Montreal's flag flew over Boston City Hall for three days.

But this year, Boston has prepared a cardiovascular circus. Jogging gurus will head hearty jaunts around town. Also planned are stair climbing, street hockey, vigorous table tennis and what's billed as the world's largest aerobics class to be held on a red stone mesa at the city's sprawling civic center. Power walking counts, but window shopping doesn't.

Afterward, participants are to phone the YMCA with this brief message: "I exercised for 15 minutes today."

"If we can get 500,000 to aerobicize, I think we can beat Montreal," declares an upbeat Gerdy Weiss, a Greater Boston YMCA director and event coordinator.

Bean Towners are eager to win.

Northeastern University, for instance, is offering a recreation class aimed chiefly at helping to publicize the event -- students lick envelopes and send out fliers. And even some Boston hockey fans aren't hoping for a sweep against the Edmondton Oilers in the Stanley Cup Championship Series. "If the Boston Bruins go into a fifth game, it will be played on May 30 and we could include 26 more names on the list," muses Dennis McKenna, a Boston Stock Exchange trader and lifelong Bruins rooter.

Further flexing its aerobic capacity, Boston is dispatching its flag 400 miles to Montreal via a solo bicyclist.

A Montreal official chuckles when told of Boston's resolve.

"We want to win, too," says Marielle Seguin, assistant chief of staff for Montreal's mayor. "They beat us in the Stanley Cup playoffs." But when asked whether Montreal also plans to bicycle its flag to Boston, Ms. Sequin says: "No, no. We send our flag by plane. But in Boston we do some walking."
