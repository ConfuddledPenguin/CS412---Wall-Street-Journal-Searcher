Letters to the Editor: Dolphins: Protect Them or Eat Them?

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/16/90

Your April 17 editorial "Protectionist the Dolphins" was too much. Are you seriously advocating the idea that it's OK to kill about 13,000 dolphins so tuna fishermen can keep their jobs? And that life as we know it will end if American children can't exercise their God-given right to a tuna-fish sandwich? I also like your argument that it's OK for American tuna boats to kill dolphins because otherwise they won't be competitive with foreign tuna boats that will continue killing dolphins.

The U.S. corporations that have finally chosen to stop the dolphin slaughter (albeit too late for many thousands, perhaps millions, of dolphins) should certainly be rewarded with a dolphin-safe label on their cans. Your own recent survey showed that Americans want to help the environment and this is one way the marketplace can better inform the consumer to make environmentally sound decisions.

Penelope Malone

Atlanta

---

"Protectionist the Dolphins" is aptly stated, for we will certainly pay more for unsullied bottlenose-dolphin-excluded tuna fish. But what is never mentioned is what is done with the nearly 13,000 dolphins killed each year. Should we assume that what is probably wonderful beeflike meat is just thrown to the sharks and never used for human consumption? I would love to buy dolphin meat for nourishment and to enjoy its taste.

No one gets upset about butchering steers, so why would anyone feel any differently about dolphins that are unlucky enough to be caught in tuna nets? What a shame to waste them. The press should give us a more complete picture of dolphin-meat usage in the tuna-fishing industry. Or does it fetch too high a price in some other countries for anyone to want to talk about it?

Historically, did whaler crews, pirates and others use dolphins as a source of fresh red meat? Does the Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972 fail to provide for a sensible use of dolphins that may be killed inadvertently such as in tuna fishing?

Andrew B. Potter

Pompano Beach, Fla.
