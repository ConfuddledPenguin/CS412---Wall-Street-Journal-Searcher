Ninety Accounts Thought to Hold Illegal Drug Funds

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 05/16/90

WASHINGTON -- The Justice Department said about 90 of the roughly 680 U.S. bank accounts that were frozen at its behest last month are still thought to contain illegal Colombian drug profits. The other 590 accounts have been "unfrozen" as prosecutors studying the accounts have discovered they were either empty or contained legitimately obtained assets, Justice Department officials said.

The Bush administration had touted the bank-account investigation as an unusually sweeping aspect of a larger money-laundering probe known as Operation Polar Cap. Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady last month called the freezing of the accounts "one of the most significant law enforcement undertakings involving bank account seizures in U.S. history."

Treasury and Justice Department officials claimed at the time that the suspect accounts had received as much as $400 million in illegal profits from sales of Colombian cocaine and other drugs as of early 1989.

Doug Tillett, a Justice Department spokesman, said he couldn't estimate how much money the government might win in pending forfeiture proceedings against the 90 accounts still under investigation.

Government officials now are stressing that their original goal was to gather information on illegal drug finances, not to recover the money. Prosecutors "were more interested in following the paper trail," Mr. Tillett said. "All along, we said we didn't know that there would be any money at all."

The number of accounts that the government attempted to keep frozen by means of court orders "dwindled dramatically" since mid-April, as hundreds of account holders "came in to present their bona fides," he said.

The cleared account holders have included businesses and individuals, including some Colombians not thought to be involved in drug trafficking. Prosecutors are proceeding with forfeiture actions against some Colombians who purchased U.S. currency on the Colombian black market, which is thought to be a principle mechanism for the laundering of drug profits.

The Miami Herald has reported on the large number of accounts that have been unfrozen in that city. Most of the frozen accounts were at banks in Miami and New York and were thought to have received illegal proceeds from the operations of some of the world's biggest cocaine traffickers who belong to the so-called Medellin Cartel.

Wilmer Parker, an assistant U.S. attorney in Atlanta and a lead prosecutor in Operation Polar Cap, said, "We are very satisfied and pleased with the information we have gotten from the account holders and others." Last month, Mr. Parker predicted that a "certain small percentage" of the targeted accounts might turn out to be controlled by legitimate businesses and indviduals.

Prosecutors haven't charged any of the banks affected by the account-freezings with wrongdoing. But prosecutors have declined to rule out that step.
