AT&T, Unions Reach Agreement On Retirements

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 05/16/90

NEW YORK -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co. and its unions agreed on a non-management retirement plan that would raise the number of eligible workers to 33,500 from 18,000, keeping the company's work force-reduction plans on track. AT&T says it won't be able to assess the financial impact of the accord until it knows how many workers will take advantage of it.

The agreement, which had been expected, also empowers individual AT&T units to offer additional incentives to the new plan to encourage workers in superfluous jobs to leave without sacrificing their early retirement benefits.

"Unions tend to be one-size fits all, and this allows individual units to respond better to their own business conditions," said an AT&T spokesman. For example, AT&T's Network Services division will cut 6,000 surplus jobs because it doesn't need as many people to maintain a phone network that has been upgraded to more modern digital equipment.

AT&T had said that it would have to lay off the workers without full early retirement benefits if it didn't receive an agreement on the special incentives from the unions -- the Communications Workers of America and the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers.

The new plan adds three years of age and three years of service to the records of all non-management employees as of May 1. These added years accelerate eligibility and increase the pension payout to each worker.

In addition, individual units with a surplus of workers will be able to offer an incentive of two additional years of age and two more years of seniority for workers. The units can also offer a "sweetener" that raises monthly pension payments 15% a month.

All told, AT&T has firm plans this year to consolidate numerous office and manufacturing sites and to cut 12,800 non-management jobs. The company currently has 165,400 non-managers out of 281,000 workers. That is about 92,000 fewer workers than in January 1984 when AT&T was broken up and 28,000 less than AT&T had 17 months ago.
