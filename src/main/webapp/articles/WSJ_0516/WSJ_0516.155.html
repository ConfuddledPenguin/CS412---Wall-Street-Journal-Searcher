Technology & Health: Sun Introduces Its PC-Priced Workstation

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 05/16/90

MOUNTAIN VIEW, Calif. -- Sun Microsystems Inc. introduced a computer workstation priced at $4,995, setting a new standard for high-powered desktop computing at relatively low prices. Sun's new computer, called the Sparcstation SLC, is likely to cement Sun's lead in the workstation market, one of the fastest-growing segments of the computer industry. While Sun's chief competitors in workstations, Hewlett-Packard Inc. and Digital Equipment Corp., vow to introduce similarly priced but more powerful workstations, some analysts think Sun's new machine will confound competitors for many months.

"This makes it difficult for DEC and HP to keep up with Sun in the workstation business," said Vicki Brown, an analysts at International Data Corp.

Sun declined to estimate future sales of the machine, though it said it had already received orders for 5,000 units.

However, Ms. Brown said Sun's newest computer is expected to lift sales of its year-old Sparcstation family significantly. She said sales of all three models, including the SLC, will probably total 95,000 units this year, roughly three times last year's sales.

Besides helping Sun satisfy its traditional engineering and scientific customers, the new machine will add momentum to the company's assault on the market for personal computers, generally used by white collar workers.

"The battle today is on the desktop," said Scott McNealy, Sun's chief executive officer, referring to the company's plans to butt heads with such PC makers as Apple Computer Inc.

While Sun may steal some PC sales, the new SLC lacks a hard-disk drive, the basic storage device for PCs, and it can't be upgraded with accessories that improve performance.

But the machine operates at blazing speeds -- 12.5 million operations a second -- because it has at its heart Sun's own Sparc microprocessor. The extra speed is useful in, for instance, financial modeling or electronic publishing, where PCs often lack the horsepower to carry out complex tasks.

Sun is expected to introduce an even faster Sparcstation before the end of the year, putting further pressure on competitors. That machine might even share the same packaging as the SLC, which is designed so that its circuitry shares the same housing as its 17-inch monitor.

The packaging is crucial to Sun's profit margins on the computer, which may sell for as little as $3,000 when purchased in large quantities. Ms. Brown said Sun spent $1 million on tooling for the computer and managed to design the machine without a fan, which reduces the computer's noise and manufacturing costs.

In national over-the-counter trading yesterday, Sun shares closed at $29.25 apiece, down $1.25 on heavy volume of about 1.9 million shares.
