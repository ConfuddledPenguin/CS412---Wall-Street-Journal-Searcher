Hughes Aircraft Says Air Force Has Taken Disciplinary Action

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/16/90

Hughes Aircraft Co. said that certain operations of its missile-systems group were temporarily barred from receiving new Air Force contracts after the company's recent admission that it improperly obtained Pentagon documents during the mid-1980s. In March, Los Angeles-based Hughes agreed to pay $3.6 million in criminal and civil penalties as part of a plea agreement in which it admitted using a pair of classified five-year Pentagon budget plans in 1984.

Hughes, a unit of General Motors Corp., said it received a letter about the proposed debarment last week. "Once you receive the notice, you are placed on suspension while you meet with the Air Force to go over what changes you have made to rectify the situation," said a company spokesman.

The Pentagon has the authority to bar Hughes from receiving any new federal contracts for up to three years. "We're under review now; it's up to the Air Force to decide" how long the suspension continues, the spokesman said.

An Air Force spokesman said in Washington that the suspension covers engineering, research and certain other operations at the Canoga Park, Calif., facility of the company's missile-making group. Since those operations don't directly hold or compete for military contracts, he said the suspension isn't expected to have any impact on the company's current operations.

But if the Air Force eventually imposes the more stringent punishment it has proposed, the impact on Hughes could be significant. Engineers, scientists and others at the facility could be barred from working on some or all of Hughes' future military work. The company has until early June to make its case to a special Air Force board.

There's a good chance, however, that Hughes will escape long-term pain. In the past, Pentagon officials have been unwilling to bar major contractors from new contracts. Moreover, none of the other firms convicted in the document-swapping case have been hit with long-term sanctions.

As part of the company's plea agreement last month, prosecutors said that the company cooperated with the investigation and that General Motors, which acquired Hughes in 1985, didn't participate in or benefit from the illegal activity. Such language typically has protected contractors from Pentagon punishment.
