Heard on the Street: Warning Flag Is Raised as Stock Market Hits New High

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 05/16/90

NEW YORK -- As the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index has surged to a multiple of 16.3 times earnings, Wall Street has had an explanation for it all. The reason stock prices have been climbing while earnings have been falling, analysts say, is that interest rates have also gone into a swoon.

But according to one significant yardstick that compares stock multiples with interest rates, the S&P 500 is more expensive now than at any time since before the October 1987 crash.

"Relative to the bond market, stock prices look 15% to 20% overpriced," says Leon G. Cooperman, chairman of Goldman Sachs Asset Management.

Despite the recent bond rally, yields on government bonds are up considerably -- more than 0.6 of a percentage point -- since year-end. All things being equal, that should warrant lower stock prices. But the S&P 500 index is up marginally from its year end close of 353.4.

"In my view, bonds represent a very good alternative to equities," says Abby Joseph Cohen, the chief strategist for Barclays de Zoete Wedd, the brokerage arm of Barclays Bank.

A good proxy for the relative worth of stocks and bonds is what Salomon Brothers terms the "earnings/yield gap." The term sounds arcane, but the concept is basic.

The earnings yield on stocks is the return (defined as the annual corporate earnings) divided by the share price. If a company earning $1 a share sells for $100, the yield to investors, whether paid out in dividends or kept as retained earnings, is 1%. (The earnings yield is also equal to one divided by the price/earnings multiple.)

The earnings/yield gap is obtained by starting with the rate prevailing on 10-year government bonds and subtracting the earnings yield of the S&P 500 stocks. Right now, this gap amounts to 2.48 percentage points.

In other words, the 8.6% interest that investors can collect on 10-year government bonds is almost 2.5 percentage points higher than the earnings yield on stocks. Today's gap is still far narrower than the precrash extreme of 4.67 percentage points. Yet it is wider than at any time since just before Black Monday.

Michael Belkin, the Salomon analyst who tracks the yield gap, says "it's probably the best `quick and dirty' measure of the relationship between stock prices and interest rates."

When the yield gap widens, it signals that stocks, relative to the risk-free returns available in the government bond market, are becoming pricier. Bargain-priced stocks are more likely to be found when the gap is narrow.

In the bear market of the 1970s, the yield gap was often negative -- meaning that bonds were yielding less than stocks. In the early 1980s, a period of double-digit interest rates, the gap briefly widened to more than four percentage points. It narrowed in the mid-1980s, and then roared to a record high just before Black Monday.

"If the gap is too large compared to its historical range, it probably means that stocks are vulnerable," Mr. Belkin says, adding: "Stock prices can't shrug off interest rates forever."

But Mr. Belkin isn't among those who believe that the gap today is so large as to suggest that stocks are overvalued. Looking back to the early 1980s, he says the gap is well within its historical range.

Japan's stock market, despite its recent slide, is more expensive than the U.S. market, Mr. Belkin adds. In fact, by this measure, he says, Japanese stocks are more overvalued than they were at year end -- when prices were 20% higher -- because interest rates in Japan also have risen.

"Even with the recent stock market slide, Japanese stocks don't reflect the rise in Japanese interest rates," Mr. Belkin says. The yield gap in Japan is 5.02%, compared with 4.22% at year end.

Stock market savants say it's indisputable that, at some level, a rising yield gap spells trouble. The hard part? Determining when.

"The problem is, you're taking a snapshot at one instant in time," says George Michaelis, president of First Pacific Advisers. "Stocks have earnings growth, and interest rates can be very volatile." In the early 1980s, he says, a large yield gap obscured the fact -- observable in retrospect -- that stocks held "tremendous value."

Charles Clough, chief investment strategist at Merrill Lynch, agrees that stocks are overvalued relative to bonds but, he adds: "Over the past 20 years the normal boundary has been rising. It has taken higher values of overvaluation to stop the market."

Investors, he says, have become more optimistic about earnings growth as the economic expansion has continued -- pushing up price/earnings multiples and also such measures as the yield gap. Thus, to some extent, the yield gap can be considered a measure of investors' expectations for earnings growth.

Investors are still sanguine, Mr. Clough says dryly, "even though there is no sign of an earnings bottom." Dillon Read research chief Joseph McAlinden says stocks aren't overvalued relative to bonds, because he expects corporate earnings to rise.

Goldman's Mr. Cooperman, computes what he says is a more dynamic version of the yield gap. Mr. Cooperman's model factors in a "risk premium" for owning stocks and assumes that, over the long haul, corporate earnings will grow 7% a year.

He also concludes that, relative to bonds, stocks are overvalued. Over time, Mr. Cooperman says the balance between markets should be rectified in one of three ways. He expects "a drop in stock prices, a further decline in interest rates, or the passage of time, allowing earnings to catch up."
