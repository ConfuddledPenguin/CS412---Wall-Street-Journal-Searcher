CBOT Trader Agrees To Pay $250,000 Fine Over CFTC Charges

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/16/90

CHICAGO -- A Chicago Board of Trade member paid $250,000 to settle Commodity Futures Trading Commission charges that he made a series of noncompetitive trades in 1986. Without admitting or denying the commission's allegations, Keith M. Rudman, a trader in the exchange's Treasury bond futures pit, agreed to the fine and a six-month trading suspension. Mr. Rudman couldn't be reached for comment.

Mr. Rudman was one of 22 Treasury bond traders charged last year with making noncompetitive trades via a practice known as "ginzy trading." The remaining 21 traders are the subject of a commission hearing here this week concerning the allegations.

Mr. Rudman was fined $25,000 by the commission for similar alleged violations in 1985 and was ordered to cease and desist from making noncompetitive trades.

In ginzy trading, exchange members profit by giving customers two different prices for the same buy or sell order. The effect of ginzy trading is to give a customer a net price for a trade that falls between the quoted bid and ask prices in a particular market.

While some traders say the technique can result in the customer getting a better price than under normal trading practices, the commission maintains that ginzy trading violates federal commodity laws banning noncompetitive or prearranged trades.
