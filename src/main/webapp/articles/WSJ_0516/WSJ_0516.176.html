What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/16/90

BALTIC UNREST FLARED as Baker arrived in Moscow for arms-control talks. Soviet loyalists converged on the Parliament in Estonia and briefly raised the hammer-and-sickle flag. The breakaway republic's premier broadcast an appeal to stave off a "coup attempt." In Latvia, anti-independence demonstrators clashed with a group supporting a secession bid. It was the first glimpse of political violence in the Baltic republics, and came as the U.S. secretary of state began a four-day visit to Moscow aimed at concluding superpower arms agreements for signing at the Bush-Gorbachev summit, which begins May 30 in Washington.

Gorbachev will visit Minneapolis and San Francisco after talks with Bush to meet with business executives, agricultural leaders and ex-President Reagan.

---

A PRESIDENTIAL PANEL URGED drastic action against air terrorism.

In a 182-page report, the commission appointed to look into the December 1988 bombing of a Pan Am flight over Scotland called for the U.S. to mount military strikes against terrorist enclaves to discourage the kind of attack that killed 270 people. The panel, concluding that the Lockerbie incident "may well have been preventable," called for the reorganization of aviation-security procedures, citing lapses by the airline and the FAA.

The head of an organization representing the families of British victims of the bombing expressed opposition to the panel's call for military retaliation.

---

West Germany plans a $70.02 billion special unity fund financed by credits to help pay for the reconstruction of East Germany's economy through 1994. Establishment of the financing vehicle for German reunification depended on the willingness of 11 state governments to commit to a 50% share of the fund.

---

Striking Nicaraguan workers defied Managua's call to return to work and riot police clashed with pro-Sandinista government employees occupying two ministries. Thousands of people joined the walkout for higher pay. In Washington, the White House was considering a request from Chamorro for a $40 million short-term loan.

---

Rehnquist criticized proposals by senators on changing the law on appeals to federal courts by state death-row prisoners. Addressing the American Law Institute in Washington, the chief justice said there is a "serious malfunction" in the capital-punishment system, leading to uncertainty and endless delays.

---

Van Gogh's "Portrait of Dr. Gachet" sold for $82.5 million at Christie's auction house in New York, a world record price for a work of art. The oil-on-canvas painting of the artist's physician and friend, completed a few weeks before van Gogh's suicide, reportedly was bought by a gallery in Tokyo.

---

Philippine Communist rebels claimed responsibility for the weekend slaying of two U.S. airmen. Manila's government, meanwhile, in a procedural step that paves the way for a possible new accord, served official notice to terminate in 1991 the current 25-year pact over six U.S. military bases in the Philippines.

---

The Justice Department said about 90 of the roughly 680 U.S. bank accounts that were frozen at its behest last month are still thought to contain illegal Colombian drug profits. The other 590 accounts have been "unfrozen" as prosecutors have discovered they were either empty or contained legitimate assets.

---

Washington's Mayor Barry pleaded innocent to six new drug charges alleging he used cocaine over a five-year period. The charges were brought last week. He previously pleaded innocent to charges brought in February of cocaine-possession and lying to a grand jury. A trial is set for June 4.

---

Serbian Borisav Jovic took over as head of Yugoslavia's collective presidency, a change that could exacerbate political and ethnic tensions. The 62-year-old Communist hard-liner said the nation was close to civil war, and that Belgrade would "resolutely oppose" any move to abandon socialism.

---

Israel's legislature opened debate on bills calling for the direct election of the prime minister, part of electoral changes demanded during recent protests. Separately, police fired tear gas at Jewish settlers who rampaged through Arab neighborhoods in the Jerusalem area, and Palestinians in the occupied lands staged a general strike.

---

Rival Christian forces ignored a ceasefire call by Lebanon's Maronite Catholic leaders and dueled with artillery in eastern Beirut and the surrounding countryside. Moslem factions fought street battles in western Beirut. Lebanese police said at least 24 people were killed in the fighting.

---

Nepal's King Birendra agreed to share power with an interim government led by Prime Minister Bhattarai. In a statement issued in Katmandu, the monarch also said he would dissolve a constitutional committee that had threatened the Himalayan country's recent steps toward democracy.

---

Iraq denied that it was trying to build a giant gun capable of firing shells hundreds of miles, and demanded that Italy compensate Baghdad for about 90 tons of steel parts seized in Naples last week. Britain, West Germany, Greece and Turkey also have confiscated Iraqi-bound gear in recent weeks.

---

Police in Atlanta arrested five anti-abortion protesters belonging to the group Operation Rescue after they entered an abortion clinic and chained themselves together in defiance of a court order barring such demonstrations. The Georgia injunction was upheld Monday by the U.S. Supreme Court.
