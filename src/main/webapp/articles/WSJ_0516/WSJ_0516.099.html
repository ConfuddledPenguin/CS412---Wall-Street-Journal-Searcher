Law: Bankruptcy Judge Lifts Ban on Lawsuits Against Partners of Defunct Finley Firm

  • Author: Ann Hagedorn
  • Date: 05/16/90

A bankruptcy trustee won the right to bring lawsuits seeking debt payments from former partners of the defunct law firm Finley, Kumble, Wagner, Heine, Underberg, Manley, Myerson & Casey. In a hearing in Manhattan's federal bankruptcy court, bankruptcy trustee Francis Musselman disclosed that dozens of partners were not cooperating with a plan that both he and the creditors' committee had agreed could make a reasonable dent in the law firm's huge debt.

In response, U.S. Bankruptcy Judge Prudence Abram lifted an injunction protecting those partners from suits, making them each potentially liable for the entire debt. The judge's order allows only Mr. Musselman to proceed against the partners, and the suits he is ready to file will seek at least $60 million from each of 50 or so former partners.

Finley Kumble, once the nation's fourth-largest law firm, collapsed in October 1987 under the weight of heavy debts and clashing egos. Three months later, it was pushed into Chapter 11 bankruptcy-law proceedings by four banks to which the firm owed $83 million. Of the firm's 700 or so lawyers, 270 were partners.

On March 30, Mr. Musselman, a partner at the New York law firm of Milbank, Tweed, Hadley & McCloy, sent letters to all former partners informing them about how much they owed in accordance with a plan designed to tackle the firm's debt. The plan, the second submitted by Mr. Musselman, set the partners' debt responsibility at $43.2 million, the trustee said. An earlier plan, rejected by the creditors' committee, placed the total at $35 million.

Although claims in excess of $2 billion have been filed against the firm, duplicate or overstated claims helped to whittle the debt down to about $120 million, excluding malpractice claims. To pay off the debt, Mr. Musselman can draw on accounts receivable, the sale of assets, and solicit the former partners. From auctions and debt collecting, Mr. Musselman has amassed about $45 million so far. The shortfall, $60 million to $70 million, is the responsibility of the partners, said Albert Togut, counsel for the creditors' committee.

What the partners were asked to pay in the March 30 letters, said Mr. Togut, was less than what they actually owe. "The {$43.2 million} is a compromise; it's an inducement to get the partners' consent to pay a portion," he said.

Mr. Musselman said 207 partners, accounting for $24.3 million of the $43.2 million, have written letters accepting the plan. Twelve others whose portion totals about $704,000 sent letters rejecting the plan. The rest, he said, failed to respond or didn't agree with the amount cited in the letter.

At the hearing, Judge Abram was apparently outraged at the lack of consensus in the case and expressed skepticism about whether the partners could ever agree on any plan. Still, Mr. Musselman remains optimistic, and he is scheduled to meet today with several former partners and some partners' attorneys.

Meanwhile, he said lawsuits against 50 or so partners seeking the total shortfall of at least $60 million from each are ready to be filed.
