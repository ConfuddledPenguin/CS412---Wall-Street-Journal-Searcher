Precision Castparts Will Pay $3.5 Million, Settle Sex-Bias Case

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/16/90

WASHINGTON -- Precision Castparts Corp., a Portland, Ore., metal castings maker, agreed to pay $3.5 million in back pay to settle Labor Department sex-discrimination charges. The amount is one of the largest equal employment opportunity settlements in the department's history, its Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs said. The office had charged the company with putting about 1,000 newly hired women into certain low-pay mold-casting production jobs usually held by women.

Those jobs also provided less opportunity for the women to compete or train for higher paying jobs usually held by men at three company plants, the compliance office said.

Precision Castparts agreed to the settlement without admitting or denying wrongdoing. The company will provide back pay to about 1,000 current and former female employees who were employed for at least three consecutive quarters between 1978 and 1988 at one of the three plants.

The company also agreed to create an affirmative-action program for the plants to encourage women to compete for higher paying jobs that are usually held by men. Female workers will be permitted to seek such jobs, and when they meet minimum qualifications, including seniority, they must be given preference for those jobs over male applicants.

Under the agreement, the women also will be allowed to transfer back to their previous jobs if the new ones don't work out. Also, each woman in a traditional female job who is paid on an hourly basis will be offered career counseling. And the performance review of their supervisors will include how those managers carry out their equal employment opportunity responsibilities.

In Portland, a Precision Castparts spokesman said in the 10 years it took to negotiate a settlement the company had implemented a number of equal opportunity and affirmative action programs for women. "In the intervening period, we think we've done quite a few things to improve our position with respect to employing women," the spokesman said.
