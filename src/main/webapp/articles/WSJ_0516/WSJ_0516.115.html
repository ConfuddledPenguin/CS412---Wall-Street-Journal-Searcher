Great American Bank in Dispute With Regulators

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 05/16/90

Great American Bank, which revised its fourth-quarter results after a dispute with its auditor over loan-loss reserves, disclosed a dispute with federal regulators over how it values certain assets, potentially further complicating the troubled thrift's survival plans. In addition, Great American, based in San Diego, reported a net loss of $1.2 million, or five cents a share, for the first quarter, compared with profit of $11 million, or 45 cents a share, a year earlier.

Great American, the nation's eighth-largest thrift with $15.4 billion in assets, said federal thrift examiners have questioned how it values certain of its loans, real estate and other assets, and that the assumptions being used by regulators would reduce its assets by at least $65 million.

The company defended its own assumptions, and said discussions are continuing as the regulators complete their exam. It added that it didn't know whether it would be required to incur write-downs, and that "the potential loss which may be required to be reported cannot reasonably be estimated at this time." Any impact would likely be reflected in results for the second quarter, the company said.

A write-down of $65 million would eliminate about half of the company's tangible capital, which, standing at $128.9 million as of March 31, was already about $100 million shy of meeting regulatory standards.

As reported, Great American has been a victim of the real-estate bust in Arizona, and must raise at least $350 million in capital by year-end or else face regulatory sanctions, possibly including seizure. Analysts have been skeptical about the company's ability to attract that kind of support, and said the latest disclosure would further compound the task. The company has conceded that any such infusion would likely seriously dilute the interests of current shareholders.

"I can't think of a more difficult assignment for anyone than to raise $350 million at this point in time, given the real-estate uncertainties in their markets," said Allan Bortel, a thrift analyst at Sutro & Co. in San Francisco.

Some analysts said Great American's dispute illustrates the increasingly aggressive approach that regulators have taken in forcing conservative accounting by banks and thrifts, based on evidence that seized institutions had greatly understated the extent of their troubled loans.

In a statement, Great American asserted that certain of the regulators' assumptions were "not currently considered to be in accordance with generally accepted accounting principles."

Earlier this year, Great American revised its fourth-quarter financial results after its auditor, Deloitte & Touche, posed concerns over the adequacy of its loan-loss provisions. The revisions boosted Great American's net loss for the quarter ended Dec. 31 to $198.9 million, or $8.30 a share, from an initially reported $59.3 million, or $2.48 a share.

A spokesman said the first-quarter results were unaudited, but that asset-valuation methods used in the quarter were consistent with those that Deloitte & Touche had approved in the preceding quarter.
