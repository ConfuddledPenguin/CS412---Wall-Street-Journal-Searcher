Perkin-Elmer Sells Chip Technology To a U.S. Group

  • Author: Paul B. Carroll
  • Date: 05/16/90

Perkin-Elmer Corp. said it sold to a U.S. group a semiconductor-equipment business that had become the focus of concerns about Japanese inroads in the U.S. semiconductor market. Nikon Corp. of Japan had expressed interest in Perkin-Elmer's so-called optical lithography technology, used to produce semiconductors. Nikon's move drew fire from U.S. semiconductor companies because of fears that U.S. makers of chip-production equipment are dying off or are being bought up by their overseas competition, especially the Japanese. That could mean U.S. semiconductor makers would wind up buying equipment from their overseas competitors, worsening the U.S. industry's already precarious position. While industry executives have said the likelihood of a sale of the Perkin-Elmer business to the Japanese was overstated, they said the concerns are legitimate.

Yesterday's announcement illustrates the sorts of joint efforts that U.S. companies apparently are prepared to enter into in coming years as they try to keep the U.S. semiconductor-equipment business alive.

Silicon Valley Group Inc., a small company based in San Jose, Calif., bought a two-thirds interest in the business, which Perkin-Elmer has been trying to sell for more than a year. International Business Machines Corp. will also have an equity interest in the business, which will be renamed SVG Lithography Systems Inc. Perkin-Elmer, Norwalk, Conn., will retain slightly less than a 20% stake. The value of the transaction was not disclosed.

IBM, Armonk, N.Y., has promised to invest heavily in the business's research and development in return for early access to the results. Sematech, a group of U.S. companies and the government, has also entered into a joint development agreement with SVG.

In addition, IBM has pledged to buy a substantial amount of the equipment, which uses the optical-lithography techniques.

Some semiconductor makers have reservations about the technology. While it is useful for making larger chips, they say, it is more expensive and less efficient than other systems for almost all types of chips now in use.

Jack Kuehler, president of IBM, believes the Perkin-Elmer optical-lithography technology will become very important in the next generation of semiconductors. He said the devices, called Micrascans, can use ultraviolet light, with its short wavelengths, to etch extremely thin lines on silicon wafers. Mr. Kuehler said the Micrascans can also expose larger segments of a wafer at one time than can systems now in wide use, allowing for the production of larger chips and for faster production of chips.
