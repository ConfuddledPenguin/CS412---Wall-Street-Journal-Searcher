U.S. to Subsidize Wheat-Flour Sales To Soviet Union

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 09/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The Agriculture Department, seeking to meet the competitive challenge of the European Community, said it will for the first time subsidize the sale of wheat flour to the Soviet Union. The U.S. is prepared to offer export subsidies on 500,000 metric tons of flour under the department's Export Enhancement Program, Agriculture Secretary Clayton Yeutter said. If the Soviets take advantage of the offer, the business could keep U.S. flour mills busy for many months. What's more, a 500,000 metric-ton sale, comparable to about 700,000 metric tons of wheat, would help boost sagging wheat futures prices, according to industry officials and market analysts.

The department has been going to unprecedented lengths lately to counter export subsidies being offered by France and other EC countries at a time of bumper grain crops, huge stockpiles and weak export demand. Earlier this month, so-called U.S. export bonuses topped $50 a ton for the first time, but that still was far short of the $150-a-ton French subsidies.

Under the Export Enhancement Program, exporters sell wheat and other commodities at the world-market price and collect bonuses in the form of surplus, government-owned commodities. The bonuses make up the difference between higher U.S. prices and world-market prices.

The Bush administration has been under intense political pressure from Wheat Belt lawmakers to do whatever it can to shore up wheat prices. At the same time, the Soviets, strapped for hard currency, have expressed interest in obtaining $2 billion in U.S. credit for commodity purchases. The administration has taken the position, however, that Moscow must liberalize Soviet immigration law to qualify for most-favored-nation status and export credit guarantees.

Wheat sales to Moscow have been slow lately. The Soviets have bought only 693,000 metric tons in the marketing year that began June 1. "Right now we have no sales on the books," said Vincent Peterson, marketing director for U.S. Wheat Associates, a trade promotion group. In the previous year, the Soviets bought 4.3 million metric tons.

The Soviets are expected to harvest a bumper grain crop of 230 million metric tons in 1990, including 104 million tons of wheat. But they are losing much of the harvest to spoilage and are having trouble transporting it to markets. At the same time, there have been bread lines and sporadic bread shortages in Moscow and other Soviet cities. A 500,000-ton flour purchase would help mollify disgruntled Soviet consumers.

"That's a lot of flour," said Mr. Peterson. "We don't have a lot of excess milling capacity. It will take U.S. mills the better part of a year to produce that much."
