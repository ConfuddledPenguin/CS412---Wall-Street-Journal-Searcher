International: Seoul Changes Position on Travel To North Korea

  • Author: Damon Darlin
  • Date: 09/28/90

SEOUL, South Korea -- The South Korean government is making a significant shift in its position on travel to North Korea, in an effort to introduce subtle pressure within the country for change.

Seoul is turning to the millions of Koreans, by birth or heritage, who live overseas, sending signals that it no longer opposes visits to the communist North. Because most overseas Koreans speak the country's language, they are more apt to have contact with ordinary North Koreans when they visit, Seoul reasons. And the more North Koreans learn about the world, the greater the chance they will pressure their government for change, as Eastern Europeans did.

"There are many ways to induce change into North Korea," says Choi Chang Yoon, a senior secretary to President Roh Tae Woo. "Our efforts to have direct contacts is just one way."

Mr. Choi says that when the new policy becomes known, "We expect there will be an overseas rush." But no one expects overnight change in North Korea. "Unification is a long-term project," he says.

The North Korean government discourages contacts between its people and South Korean citizens, despite high-level government talks this month. The only contact between ordinary citizens occurred back in 1985 for a few separated families. Pyongyang doesn't even allow newspapers, mail or media contact between the two countries.

In the past, South Korea has made it difficult for overseas Koreans, especially those in the U.S., to travel to traveler would be forbidden future entry to South Korea, harassment of a traveler's family in South Korea, or even surveillance in the traveler's home country. Government officials admit such tactics were once used but say they have since been abandoned.

But that message hasn't gotten through to Korean-Americans: They have listened for so long to propaganda that they would be kidnapped or murdered in North Korea that many still won't consider traveling there. "The South Korean government doesn't want people to go there," says Marsha Choon Ja Kim, who runs the AllGold Travel & Tours agency in Los Angeles. And under U.S. laws, she says, North Korea is off-limits.

Mrs. Kim says contact is made difficult because the U.S. prevents phone calls, telexes and telegrams to North Korea. Indeed, the State Department still has a stern warning in effect against travel to North Korea. Still, she says, "More and more people want to go there." She can't directly arrange a trip because of U.S. regulations, but steers clients to agents overseas who can.

The North Koreans are likely to be enthusiastic about an increase in visitors because of the hard currency entering the country. (South Koreans could provide hard cash, too, but their visits carry political implications that make the North Korean government uncomfortable.)

About 20,000 people visit North Korea each year, as many as half of them from the East bloc. Pyongyang is building hotels and encouraging tourism, but is reluctant to grant visas to Americans who aren't of Korean extraction, travel agents say.

South Korea estimates that 1.2 million Koreans live in the U.S. and 670,000 in Japan (Koreans in Japan who are affiliated with North Korea aren't included in that number). There are an estimated 1.6 million ethnic Koreans in China, which borders North Korea. And about 450,000 live in the Soviet Union, some as far away as Tashkent, near Afghanistan, where they were forcibly moved during the Stalin era.

The South Korean government is also trying to foster a sense of a "Korean commonwealth" among overseas Koreans, particularly in the Soviet Union and China, who have easier access to North Korea. South Korean business ventures, such as a Siberian logging operation by the giant Hyundai group, use ethnic Koreans.

"We will open a small hole with business that will create more change," says Lee Hong Koo, a South Korean adviser. "Build a national community through business, and political solution will naturally follow," he says.

Soviet music and athletic coaches of Korean heritage have been invited to South Korea. The government also sponsored an Olympic-style Pan-Korean athletic competition in late 1989 that drew 1,326 Korean competitors from 50 nations. Diplomats in Seoul say the event seems to have created a feeling of belonging among Koreans that may translate into pressure on North Korea as well.
