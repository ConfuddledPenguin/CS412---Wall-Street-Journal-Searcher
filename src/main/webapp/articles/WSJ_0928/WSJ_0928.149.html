Davis Offers Help To United Airlines' Unions to Buy Carrier

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/28/90

CHICAGO -- Los Angeles investor Marvin Davis offered to help United Airlines' unions acquire the carrier if the employees' current offer for United parent UAL Corp. fails. Gerald Greenwald, who is leading the employee buy-out effort, rejected Mr. Davis's offer for now, saying the labor group expects to complete the buy-out on its own. "We do not expect any action will be taken" on the Davis offer, Mr. Greenwald said.

The buy-out group said it received an unsolicited letter from Mr. Davis, dated Wednesday, in which he offered to provide as much as $750 million in equity for a joint bid. Mr. Davis proposed giving the employees a 20% interest in UAL, with the right to buy out his position after two years.

Mr. Davis didn't specify a purchase price, but said he would be willing to negotiate one with UAL's board.

It is unlikely UAL's unions would want to work with Mr. Davis, even if their offer fails. United's pilots union refused to join him in a bid last year because he insisted on majority control of the company. The unions have continued to discourage other potential equity investors who sought control as the price of their involvement.

By raising the possiblity that Mr. Davis might return, the unions may be trying to shore up support for the employee effort within their own ranks. Earlier this week, the pilots union accused UAL management and chairman Stephen M. Wolf of trying to foment dissent.

Indeed, in their latest letter to UAL pilots, union leaders warned that "raiders remain a threat" and specifically singled out Mr. Davis. "Only employee ownership," the union cautioned, "would end the threat of raiders."

UAL's three principal unions are working on an offer of $175 a share for 85% of UAL's stock after failing to obtain financing for the $201-a-share, $4.38 billion offer the board accepted last April. Some traders value the latest offer at only about $160 a share, or $3.49 billion.

The unions have until Oct. 9 to present the board with a financed offer. It is still unclear whether the board will accept a lower bid or whether this bid can gain bank financing. Mr. Davis said his offer of help stands if the unions fail to get the financing, or if the board rejects their latest offer.
