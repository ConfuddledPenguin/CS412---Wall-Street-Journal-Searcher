OTC Focus: Unlisted Shares Continue Slide on Oil; Oracle Victimized by Apparent Trickery

  • Author: Dave Pettit Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/28/90

NEW YORK -- Over-the-counter stocks slipped on oil again yesterday. Crude oil prices increased nearly 90 cents a barrel, sending stock prices lower again as investors continued to grapple with the inflationary impact of higher oil prices.

The losses left the Nasdaq Composite Index at its lowest level since January 1988. The index closed at 341.19, down 8.84 or 2.5%.

New York Stock Exchange issues faired somewhat better; the Big Board composite index lost 2.16, or 1.3%, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average shed 32.17, or 1.3%.

While oil price movements largely dictated the stock markets' direction, traders say other issues also continue to influence the market. Rumors about the military standoff in the Persian Gulf and the ongoing federal budget talks in Washington also affected stocks, traders say.

"The tone of the market is definitely bearish right now and there's really nothing that's going to turn that around," says Len Hefter, managing director of Jefferies & Co.'s OTC desk in Dallas.

"Until something definitive starts to happen in the Middle East, it's going to be the same thing -- a lot of nervousness," Mr. Hefter says.

Nasdaq decliners overwhelmed advancers, 1,486 to 561. Volume climbed to 153.3 million shares, up sharply from Wednesday's 118.7 million shares.

Traders agree that even if a budget accord is reached, it will be only a short-term positive for stocks with the Middle East crisis continuing to loom.

"There are just too many negatives, too many rumors and stories. You just can't operate intelligently in a market when that's going on," says Robert Antolini, head of OTC trading at Donaldson Lukfin & Jenrette Securities Corp.

Oracle fell 5/8 to 5 5/8 in heavy trading yesterday after gyrating wildly on two apparently fabricated documents alleging Oracle is the target of a takeover bid. Oracle, a highflying supplier of database software that has fallen on hard times, declined to comment on the documents.

One of the documents, which appeared to be an internal memo from Prudential-Bache Securities, declared that "an offshore concern is about to file its intention to acquire Oracle as early as next week." But an official at Prudential-Bache insisted the document was fraudulent.

The other document, purporting to be a press release from Hitachi Ltd., stated it was Hitachi's intention to acquire five million of Oracle's shares. But the document contained numerous errors and misstated Hitachi's formal name.

On Tuesday, Oracle announced a quarterly loss that was one-third larger than the company predicted three weeks ago. The loss, the company's first ever, comes on the heels of disclosures of accounting "glitches" and financial restatements at the company. The string of disclosures has torpedoed the company's stock, which peaked late last year at more than $28. Oracle was the day's most active issue, as 4.77 million shares were traded.

Other active stocks included Intel, down 3/4 to 32 on 4.2 million shares; Conner Peripherals, down 1 3/8 to 16 1/8 on 2.7 million-shares; and Sun Microsystems, down 2 to 25 3/4 on 1.6 million shares.

NovaCare Inc. lost 1/2 to 14 5/8 as 3.2 million of its common shares came to market at $15 each. A total of 2.2 million NovaCare shares changed hands on the day.

Dain Bosworth equity trader Dennis Murray says that some of the few sectors of the market that have maintained their favored status are finally falling out of grace. "We are seeing more and more darling stocks being taken out and getting shot," he says.

Among these fallen angels are health care and biotechnology stocks, he says. In those areas yesterday, Amgen lost 2 3/8 to 46 1/4, St. Jude Medical lost 3/4 to 26 3/4, U.S. HealthCare shed 1 3/4 to 17 7/8 and Biomet dropped 1/4 to 27 3/4.

Pop Radio's shares lost more than a third of their value, shedding 5 1/8 to 10 after Heritage Media suggested that the firms' merger pact may be in jeopardy.

Heritage Media, in a Securities and Exchange Commission filing, said it doesn't expect all conditions of the merger to be satisified by an Oct. 31 deadline, at which time both companies can back out of the transaction. Heritage said it hasn't yet decided whether to proceed with the merger after the deadline.

Meanwhile, Nike shares fell 3 3/4 to 60 3/4. A Nike spokesman said the firm doesn't have any news pending. He indicated that Nike is "very comfortable" with analysts' estimates in the range of $7.50 to $8.25 a share for the fiscal year ending in May 1991. Nike earned $6.42 a share in fiscal 1990.

Action Auto Rental gained 3/4 to 2 5/8 after the company announced plans to buy back up to three million shares.

Software Toolworks fell 1 1/4 to 6 1/4 after Piper Jaffray & Hopwood analyst Bob Kleiber lowered his earnings estimates for the company's 1991 and 1992 fiscal years.

Mr. Kleiber cut his forecast for the fiscal year ending March 31, 1991, to a range of between $1.20 and $1.40 a share and lowered his fiscal 1992 estimate to between $1.85 to $2.15.

Network General shed 1 7/8 to 4 1/4 after disclosing that it expects second quarter net income to be below its first quarter level of 13 cents a share, as adjusted for a two-for-one stock split. Additionally, the company said that shipments of its "WatchMaster" product have been deferred.

Network General's disclosures prompted Alex. Brown & Sons Inc. analyst, John Rohal, to downgrade his investment rating on the stock to "hold" from "buy" and to trim his earnings estimate for the firm.

OTC financial stocks continued to founder, including insurer Cincinnati Financial, which fell 1/4 to 73 after trading as low as 66 3/4. While a company spokesman Wednesday said the firm knew of no reason to account for the fall, earlier this week analysts blamed the drop on short sellers. The stock fell 4 7/8 Wednesday.

Among other insurance firms, Ohio Casualty dropped 1 3/4 to 29 1/4, St. Paul Cos. lost 3 to 48, Safeco dropped 1 to 26 7/8 and Provident Life shed 3/4 to 16 3/4.

The banking sector was hit with more negative news as New York Stock Exchange-traded Bank of Boston joined the ranks of institutions projecting losses and reducing their quarterly dividend payouts.

Midlantic lost 1/2 to 7 3/8, BayBanks dropped 1 1/4 to 12 1/4, Michigan National lost 3/4 to 22 and State Street Boston fell 7/8 to 29 1/8.

U.S. Trust Corp. fell 3/4 to 28 3/4 on top of its 3/8 loss Wednesday. The company said Wednesday that $16.2 million in special charges in the third quarter would result in a loss of about $7 million, or 77 cents a share, for the period. Last year, U.S. Trust earned $10.5 million, or $1.05, in the third quarter.
