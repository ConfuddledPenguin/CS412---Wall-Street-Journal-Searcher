Politics & Policy: Mexico Concerned Over Canadian Role In Talks With U.S. on Free-Trade Pact

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 09/28/90

WASHINGTON -- Mexico has voiced concerns about Canada's bid to participate in Mexico's negotiations with the U.S. over a free-trade agreement. In a classified letter to President Bush, White House Trade Representative Carla Hills said Mexico is worried that Canadian participation "could delay a U.S.-Mexico free-trade agreement or jeopardize consideration by the U.S. Congress."

Canada, whose own free-trade pact with the U.S. took effect last year, maintains that excluding it from the U.S.-Mexico negotiations wouldn't be "politically acceptable," according to Mrs. Hills. But, she added, the Canadians don't want to be "a spoiler and would bow out diplomatically" if their participation is seen "as delaying or jeopardizing the achievement of a U.S.Mexican free-trade agreement."

Mrs. Hills, the nation's top trade negotiator, sent the letter Sept. 21, after meeting the previous evening with Mexican Commerce Minister Jaime Serra Puche and Canadian Trade Minister John Crosbie.

Canadian involvement undoubtedly would complicate U.S.-Mexico talks. "Two's company, three may be a crowd," said Jim Moore, a former assistant secretary at the Commerce Department during the Reagan administration. "To be able to bring a third party -- Canada -- into the middle of these U.S.-Mexican negotiations makes a much more delicate situation." Mr. Moore, now a private consultant, added that "there are many enormous complex bilateral issues" between the U.S. and Mexico that don't closely involve Canada.

The U.S. push for a Mexican free-trade pact is part of the Bush administration's strategy to boost commerce in the Western hemisphere. In addition to the proposed agreement with Mexico, President Bush called this summer for Latin American nations to work toward establishing a free-trade area throughout the Western hemisphere.

Already, Chile, which has done much to liberalize its economy and trade over the past eight years, is eager to start negotiating a bilateral trade pact with the U.S. Mrs. Hills's aides say they welcome Chile's enthusiasm, but they warn that the process takes time.

Mrs. Hills said in her letter that she and her Canadian and Mexican counterparts will hold informal talks in the coming months to devise a structure for the U.S.-Mexico free-trade talks. She said the three trade ministers set next Jan. 31 as "an unannounced deadline for determining whether the negotiations would proceed bilaterally or trilaterally." Formal negotiations between the U.S. and Mexico on the trade pact are unlikely to start before that date.

Mr. Bush notified Congress earlier this week of his plans to negotiate a free-trade agreement with Mexico. Mrs. Hills suggested in her letter to the president that the administration encourage the three nations to consult in the coming months "to see if the trilateral approach was workable and satisfactory for all sides, with the understanding that Canada would depart gracefully" if its participation seemed likely to upset the negotiating schedule or jeopardize congressional approval.

The president sought to ease Canadian concerns in his letter notifying Congress of his intentions to negotiate with Mexico. He said the U.S. would consult with both Canada and Mexico during the U.S.-Mexico negotiations, with a view to eventually achieving a three-way agreement on free trade.
