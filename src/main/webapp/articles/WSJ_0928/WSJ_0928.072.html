International: Critic Says Japan Is Too Subservient To U.S. in Gulf

  • Author: Urban C. Lehner
  • Date: 09/28/90

TOKYO -- If Shintaro Ishihara, Japan's best known nationalist, were prime minister Japan's Persian Gulf policy would be very different indeed. Mr. Ishihara dismisses Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu as "an innocent boy" and calls Japan's management of the current crisis "childish." He favors a posture that's both more reliant on the United Nations, on the one hand, but potentially more willing to commit Japanese military force to the region, on the other. While completely unsympathetic with Saddam Hussein and his aggression on Kuwait, Mr. Ishihara is very sympathetic with Arab nationalism.

"They {the Arabs} still have a sense of colonial domination by white people," Mr. Ishihara said in an interview. "Japan can sympathize with them. We too are colored in a way. Japanese have a different mentality from white people."

Mr. Ishihara, a Liberal Democratic Party member of parliament and co-author of the controversial book "The Japan That Can Say No," doesn't necessarily speak for anyone but himself.

But he is one of Japan's most popular politicians. Many Japanese admire his outspoken views, even though they wouldn't dare say such radical things themselves. In particular, many Japanese agree with Mr. Ishihara that Japan is too subservient to the U.S.

"I'm not saying Japan should say `no' to the U.S. 100%" in the Gulf situation, he says. But he would like Japan's Gulf policy to be "a bit aloof" from the U.S.'s. The countries whose stances he most admires include the Soviet Union, which is broadly supportive of the multinational forces in the Gulf but wants a stronger role for the United Nations.

Mr. Ishihara believes the U.S. was too hasty in dispatching forces to the Gulf. He would have advised making more use of the United Nations first. Now that U.S. forces are present, he says, the U.S. runs enormous risks. If Saddam Hussein emerges from the situation with any part of his initial aims achieved, the most radical elements in the region will be inspired to try to topple all moderate Arab regimes friendly to Washington. So if war ensues, Mr. Ishihara muses, the U.S. had better not stop at recovering Kuwait; it had better "go all the way to Baghdad."

By totally subscribing to the U.S. line, Mr. Ishihara believes, Japan gives away one of its most important "cards," the chance to play a mediating role in the crisis. He says 73% of Iraq's foreign aid -- but none of its armaments -- came from Japan, which could have given Japan both leverage and a strong moral platform for playing a mediating role.
