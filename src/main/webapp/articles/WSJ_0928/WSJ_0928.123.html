Heard on the Street: Mideast Crisis Paradox: Prices of Big Oil Firms' Stocks Are Down Since Aug. 2 Kuwait Invasion

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 09/28/90

NEW YORK -- In its rush to analyze the effects of the Middle East crisis, the stock market seems to be all but ignoring the most glaringly obvious beneficiary. Since Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait, the price of crude oil has almost doubled and that of gasoline has spurted about 30%. Not surprisingly, stocks of oil consumers, such as chemical companies and airlines, are down, and those of oil-service firms are up.

But the stocks of companies feeling the most immediate and significant effects -- the major "integrated" oil companies -- haven't risen at all. In fact, they have fallen.

Shares of Mobil, Exxon, Kerr-McGee, Phillips Petroleum and Chevron, among others, are all down from their Aug. 2 invasion-day price. Others, such as Arco, British Petroleum and Unocal, are up, but none by more than a few percentage points. And this is so even though the earnings outlook for the integrated majors has brightened considerably.

"As far as the oil stocks are concerned, the invasion of Kuwait never took place," says PaineWebber analyst Bryan Jacoboski.

In fact, the Dow Jones index of major integrated oil companies is actually down by 3% since Aug. 1. "I've been mystified that they haven't gone up," says First Boston's William Randol.

Analysts have a great many reasons for this -- but none of them really explain the failure of stocks to reflect the improved outlook for oil-industry profits. Indeed, they have so many reasons, one suspects that they, too, are groping for an answer. "The market has its own logic," says Salomon Brothers' Bernard Picchi.

The logic, in this case, is as follows: Oil companies reap significant profits from chemicals, which have been hurt by developments in the Middle East. And the price of gasoline, analysts note, hasn't risen by as much as crude -- thus, profit margins at the marketing end of the business have all but disappeared.

Still others make the point that because the Standard & Poor's 500 stock index is down by 15% since Aug. 1, oil stocks haven't done all that badly in relative terms. And then there is the argument of Alan Gaines, president of institutional broker Gaines & Berland -- that a higher oil price will cause a broad recession and thus lower demand for oil in the future.

All of these points are worthy of thought, but none change a simple fact: Earnings of the integrated oil majors should be up -- way up, unless oil prices collapse almost immediately. Mr. Picchi, of Salomon, says "we're looking for a 25% to 30% upward revision in profits for 1991 versus our previous forecast," assuming that the average price of oil next year is $27 a barrel.

William Brown, of Kidder, Peabody, assumes an oil price of only $25 a barrel in 1991. His earnings estimates are up by about 20%. The magnitude of the trend may not be clear, but the direction is.

Mr. Picchi argues that even before the invasion, he and some others were predicting that oil's price would rise -- though much more gradually -- and that stocks may have already reflected that view.

"The five-year improvement we expected in the price of oil took place in five weeks," he says. But, Mr. Picchi concedes, it is one thing to predict higher oil prices; it is another to experience them. And, he adds, oil companies now are trading at a relatively modest five or six times projected cash flow.

Analysts make the point that no one is sure how long the Mideast crisis will last. And eventually, according to the common wisdom, oil prices will retreat -- perhaps severely. On the New York Mercantile Exchange, oil for future delivery in April 1992 is priced at $26.54 a barrel -- $13 less than oil for immediate delivery, reflecting the view that prices are bound to fall off sharply.

But it is also true that if war breaks out, oil prices may rise. And whatever happens, everyone's estimate for future oil prices is higher than it was on Aug. 1.

"A lot of other factors are involved," PaineWebber's Mr. Jacoboski explains. Government, he says, may mandate conservation, or raise taxes on fuels, or encourage people who burn gasoline to switch to windmills, and so forth.

But none of these factors overly discouraged Mr. Jacoboski when it was time to advise his clients. Before the invasion he was touting five of the 17 companies he follows; now, he recommends 15.

His and other people's clients don't seem to be listening. "Fund managers hate to be blindsided," he says, adding: "If you accept that oil prices will be higher, you are accepting that the economy will be weak. You have to restructure your entire portfolio."
