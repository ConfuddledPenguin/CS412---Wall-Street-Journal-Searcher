Machine-Tool Firms to Post Poor 3rd Period --- Weak Orders in Second Half Of 1989, Early 1990 Led To Depressed Shipments

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 09/28/90

Machine-tool producers will report dismal third quarter profits. Shipments were depressed because of weak orders in 1989's second half and the first few months of 1990. Machine tools are large, complex machines that shape metal parts, and there often is a lead time of several months to a year between receipt of an order and shipment of the machine.

The industry also is beset by intensely competitive pricing. There's excess capacity in the U.S. machine-tool industry, and foreign producers are shipping in machines from abroad and adding plant capacity in the U.S.

Nonetheless, "the tone at the recent machine tool show in Chicago was quite upbeat," said Eli S. Lustgarten, machinery analyst with PaineWebber. "Industry orders for 1990 will be up 4% to 6% from last year, and they'll rise another 5% to 10% next year," he said. That's far from a boom, he added, but it should produce improved industry profits in 1991.

For one thing, automotive companies are expected to place some long-deferred orders for equipment to produce new engines and other basic parts for 1993 and 1994 model cars. "The stumbling blocks that delayed the orders are falling," Mr. Lustgarten said, and auto makers don't have much more time, if they want to avoid slipping another year behind the Japanese in car design.

Order deferral by car and truck makers was a major reason for the machine tool order drop that began in the spring of 1989.

The declining value of the dollar is one of this year's few plus factors. Machine-tool exports in 1990's first half rose 17% from a year earlier, and may set a record for the full year, exceeding the $1 billion of 1981, according to the NMTBA -- the Association for Manufacturing Technology, a McLean, Va., trade group.

The machine-tool industry isn't energy-intensive, and won't be hurt directly by higher oil prices. However, orders would drop if rising oil prices and uncertainty about the threat of war in the Middle East bring on a recession in the U.S. and slow economic activity abroad.

On the other hand, machine-tool companies are receiving more orders from makers of oilfield drilling equipment, pumps and valves as oil prices rise and demand for natural gas increases.

In addition, higher gasoline prices may increase auto company purchases of equipment to make new 16-valve, four-cylinder engines and four-speed automatic transmissions. The auto industry has been undecided about whether to make more six-cylinder engines for larger, higher performance cars, or to tool up for fuel-efficient models.

Government fuel-economy regulations pressure the companies to emphasize economy, but consumer demand was tilting strongly toward performance. Higher gasoline prices could resolve the issue in favor of economy.

Longer term, if oil prices remain high, U.S. industry will renew efforts to conserve energy, such as operating more fuel-efficient trucks, airplanes and heavy machinery. That would boost orders for machine tools in 1991 and beyond.

In the third quarter, negatives outweighed the positives. Cincinnati Milacron Inc. said it's too early to make a precise third-quarter earnings estimate, but that profit probably will be in the ballpark of the disappointing 12 cents a share earned in the second quarter. In 1989's third period, when Milacron was feeling the brunt of restructuring problems, profit from continuing operations was $3.3 million, or 13 cents a share, on sales of $233.8 million. Net income was $2.5 million, or 10 cents a share.

Milacron's profit in recent quarters has suffered from reduced shipments of some of its higher margin products, such as plastics-injection molding machines. Also, the company is shipping some machines that carry inadequate profit margins, reflecting competitive pricing. Milacron has become more selective about the business it accepts, a spokesman said, but still has some low-margin business on the books.

Monarch Machine Tool Co., Sidney, Ohio, also is having a disappointing third quarter, mainly because of reduced orders during the spring and summer, said Robert J. Siewert, president. The producer of lathes, machining centers and equipment for processing coils of sheet metal will be profitable for the quarter, he said, but it's too early to say whether net will exceed the modest $271,942, or seven cents a share, Monarch earned a year ago on $24 million revenue.

Monarch began the year expecting sharply higher profit in 1990, but second and third-quarter earnings fell far below expectations, Mr. Siewert said. The company's British lathe operation had lower shipments at the same time that domestic machine-tool business was in a slump. Orders have improved recently, he said, and the fourth quarter will be the best earnings period for the year.

Giddings & Lewis Inc., Fond du Lac, Wis., is faring better than most other machine-tool builders this year; it expects to approximate last year's third-quarter net of $4.8 million, or 46 cents a share. "Our bookings have held up, and we have a good backlog," said Richard Kleinfeldt, vice president and chief financial officer. He doesn't quarrel with analyst estimates that the company will earn $1.80 to $1.85 a share for the year, up from $17.1 million, or $1.65 a share, in 1989, when sales were $233.1 million.

Cross & Trecker Corp. concedes that it won't realize a previously announced goal of turning profitable in fiscal 1990, which ends Sunday. The Bloomfield Hills, Mich., company expects to report a loss for the fourth quarter larger than the year-ago quarter's loss of $1 million, or eight cents a share. However, fiscal 1990's loss will be far smaller than the loss of $46.5 million, or $3.76 a share, for all of fiscal 1989, which included a restructuring charge of $21.3 million.

Reduced orders at the KT-Swasey unit, which produces machining centers and lathes, and at the Sheffield measuring machine unit, are major factors in the expected fourth-quarter loss, Cross & Trecker said. Sheffield is profitable, but will earn less than a year ago.

In addition, Cross has had difficulty in arranging financing for a large Soviet order. Last March, Cross received Soviet orders totaling $63 million for machinery to equip a Soviet auto plant. West German banks agreed to finance the $28 million portion of the order placed with the Cross plant in West Germany. However, U.S. banks won't finance the $35 million part that was to be produced in Michigan, delaying the program, Cross said. U.S. banks are skittish about whether the Soviets would make the repayments.

The company accounts for large contracts on a percentage of completion basis, and had expected to book as much as $700,000 of operating profit from the Soviet order in the Sept. 30 quarter. Cross may transfer production to another country to obtain financing, possibly reopening a Canadian facility.

The order slowdown hit Hurco Cos., Indianapolis, just as the producer of machining centers and electronic controls was increasing spending on new-product development and its marketing and customer-service organization. This year, Hurco introduced a new generation of machine-tool controls and will market five new machine tools, said Michael K. Campbell, senior vice president and chief financial officer.

Hurco's profit in the third quarter, ended July 31, plunged to $1.1 million, or 19 cents a share, from $2.2 million, or 45 cents a share, a year earlier. The company expects net for the Oct. 31 fourth quarter to approximate the third period's, which would be less than half the $2.9 million, or 51 cents a share, earned in the year-ago quarter.

However, Hurco could exceed a fiscal 1990 earnings estimate of $1.20 a share if sales in the final weeks of the year keep pace with a recent increase in customer inquiries, Mr. Campbell said.

For fiscal 1989, Hurco earned a record $8.2 million, or $1.70 a share, on sales of $93.7 million.
