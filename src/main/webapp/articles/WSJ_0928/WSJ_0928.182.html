Law: Hearing Will Delay Milken Sentencing

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 09/28/90

NEW YORK -- A federal judge delayed the sentencing of Michael Milken, scheduled for Monday, and ordered an unusual hearing to consider additional crimes the government has alleged Mr. Milken committed, but to which he hasn't pleaded guilty. U.S. District Judge Kimba Wood said she would hold a two-week hearing to air the government's allegations against the former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. junk-bond king. The hearing will likely be held next month. Judge Wood said she planned to give both prosecutors and defense lawyers 20 hours each to present arguments and cross-examine witnesses.

As previously reported, prosecutors had sought such a hearing -- over the strenuous objections of Mr. Milken's lawyers -- and have notified lawyers for witnesses who aided the government in its case against Mr. Milken that these witnesses may be called to testify.

Among the witnesses the government has indicated it may call are Ivan F. Boesky, the convicted former Wall Street stock speculator, and David Solomon, a former money manager. Prosecutors have also notified former Drexel employees that they may be called.

Yesterday, Arthur Liman, Mr. Milken's lead lawyer, opposed such a hearing, saying it "will prolong the agony" of Mr. Milken's family. Mr. Liman said he would talk to prosecutors during the weekend to see if a hearing could be avoided.

Prosecutors sought the hearing because they want Judge Wood to consider the full force of their case against Mr. Milken when she sentences him. Mr. Milken pleaded guilty last April to six felony counts relating to securities fraud and faces a maximum jail term of 28 years.

The government has urged Judge Wood to sentence Mr. Milken to "substantial" jail time and has painted the 44-year-old Mr. Milken as a white-collar criminal of major proportions. In a sentencing memorandum made public earlier this week, the government unleashed a barrage of additional alleged crimes that it believes Mr. Milken committed. The alleged crimes include insider trading and illegal payoffs to certain money managers and other favored clients.

Mr. Milken's attorneys have urged Judge Wood to view Mr. Milken's crimes as aberrations in an otherwise exemplary life and to sentence him to perform fulltime community service. His lawyers have accused the government of presenting evidence of offenses that Mr. Milken has "never acknowledged and has never committed."

In calling for the extraordinary hearing, Judge Wood said that the court may take into account unadmitted conduct in issuing a sentence. "Considering unadmitted conduct gives the court a sense of a defendant's character," Judge Wood said.

She said that to avoid a lengthy hearing on all of the government's allegations, she would limit the time that the government could spend presenting its case. She said she expected the government to concentrate on less than a dozen of the transactions it has alleged were illegal, but to which Mr. Milken hasn't admitted guilt.

A judge is not obligated to take the government's additional allegations into consideration when deciding on an appropriate sentence. But if Judge Wood decides she wishes to consider the additional evidence in meting out a sentence, Mr. Milken is entitled to defend himself at a hearing.

A similar battle over sentencing took place earlier this year in the case against Robert Freeman, the former head of arbitrage at Goldman, Sachs & Co. who pleaded guilty to a single charge of insider trading. In that case, the judge said that in issuing a sentence, he wouldn't consider any crimes other than that to which Mr. Freeman pleaded guilty. The government alleged that Mr. Freeman committed some 20 other crimes.
