Abreast of the Market: Industrials Fall 32.17 to Year's Low As Oil Surges as High as $40 a Barrel

  • Author: Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 09/28/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market retreated again as crude oil prices surged as high as $40 a barrel. An early softening in the price of oil had fueled hopes for a rally in stocks. But an opening burst of stock buying proved briefer than many investors expected.

After advancing more than 22 points in the first 10 minutes of trading, the Dow Jones Industrial Average quickly headed lower. The index hit a 50-point loss shortly after noon EDT, triggering the New York Stock Exchange's "uptick rule" restricting computer-driven program selling.

The industrial average ended with a 32.17-point loss at 2427.48, a new low for the year. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index dropped 4.09 to 300.97, and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index fell 2.16 to 165.36.

Declining stocks totaled 1,182 and advancing issues 395.

"This morning was a classic bear trap," sucking investors in on false hopes, said James C. Andrews, first vice president in charge of institutional trading at Janney Montgomery Scott.

Those hopes were pinned on falling oil prices, which were expected to open as much as $1 a barrel lower in New York trading. Oil prices had declined sharply in European trading after President Bush announced late Wednesday that he would release five million barrels of oil from the nation's strategic petroleum reserves.

But light sweet crude oil for November delivery opened only about 87 cents a barrel lower on the New York Mercantile Exchange, and soon recovered to surge past Wednesday's closing levels -- moving briefly above $40 a barrel. The spike in oil prices drove stock prices to their lows for the day. By the end of the day, the November crude oil contract had settled to $39.54 a barrel, up 87 cents from Wednesday.

Big Board volume totaled 182,690,000 shares, up from Wednesday's 155,570,000.

Bank stocks led the decline in what Ralph Bloch, chief market analyst at Raymond James & Associates, called quarterend "window undressing."

So-called window dressing has often been cited as a cause for gains in stock prices at the end of previous quarters as portfolio managers bought winning stocks to enhance their portfolios before the quarter-end review.

Mr. Bloch said portfolio managers may be dumping the worst performers now -- primarily the bank stocks -- to get them off the books before the quarter ends.

But there were plenty of other reasons to sell bank shares yesterday.

NCNB dropped 3 1/8 to 20 1/8 after announcing late Wednesday that it is increasing its loan and lease loss allowance by $65 million. The increased provision will slash the bank holding company's third-quarter profit to between 50 cents and 60 cents a share, down from $1.45 a share in the like quarter last year.

Bank of Boston said its third-quarter loss would total between $200 million and $250 million, including the addition of $150 million to $200 million to its loan-loss provisions. The bank, whose stock dropped 3/8 to 7 1/8, also cut its dividend to 10 cents a share from 31 cents.

Landmark Bancshares fell 1 to 7 after it said its third-quarter earnings would fall to as little as 12 cents a share from 51 cents a share a year ago.

Other bank stocks were also down. Citicorp led the most-active list with a 3/4point loss to 13 3/4. Wells Fargo lost 3 5/8 to 42 3/4, Chase Manhattan eased 1/8 to 11, Chemical Banking lost 7/8 to 14 1/8, BankAmerica dropped 3/4 to 19 1/4, and Security Pacific lost 1 1/2 to 19 1/2.

Market players have been saying all week that the stock market is due for a technical bounce. And while the market's inability to support a 22-point advance by the Dow industrials yesterday morning discouraged some, others are still saying the bottom is near.

"At some point the market will have largely discounted a war," as well as a recession, said Oppenheimer market strategist Michael Metz. "At that point {the market} will turn." Mr. Metz believes the market is just about there.

The stock market wasn't without its winners yesterday. Among the 30 Dow industrials, PepsiCo added 1/8 to 22 1/8, while Procter & Gamble rose 1/4 to 72 3/8.

Union Carbide added 5/8 to 14 7/8. The company's board authorized a series of initiatives designed to enhance value for stockholders and strengthen its businesses. The moves include the repurchase of about 14% of its shares outstanding through a "Dutch auction" tender offer.

MCA, which jumped more than 25 points in the previous two sessions on expectations of an acquisition agreement with Matsushita Electric, retreated 2 1/4 to 59 in heavy trading.

General Electric lost 3 3/8 to 51 5/8. A federal ruling in June removed limitations on the damages for which GE is liable under a 1971 contract with Washington Public Power Supply System. The ruling, which was unsealed Tuesday, found that GE had hidden serious doubts about its design of a giant nuclear-containment vessel for a WPPSS power plant. A trial this summer brought by WPPSS against GE ended in a mistrial. WPPSS has requested a new trial without a jury on the issue of GE's liability in the case.

USAir tumbled 7/8 to 15 1/8. The airline operator said late Wednesday that it is suspending its quarterly dividend indefinitely and deferring delivery of 16 new Boeing jets for several years. Boeing fell 1 1/4 to 39 5/8.

Unisys lost 1 5/8, to 5 3/8. The company suspended its dividend and said it expects to report a loss in the current third quarter. Unisys lost $4.25 a share in the third quarter last year, including the effect of restructuring charges.

Whitman added 1/2 to 18 3/4 on heavy volume. Goldman Sachs made positive comments about the stock yesterday. The Chicago-based company has announced a restructuring program to transform itself into an operating company focused on foods and soft drinks, from a diversified holding company.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index slid 4.63 to 305.43. Volume totaled 16,780,000 shares.
