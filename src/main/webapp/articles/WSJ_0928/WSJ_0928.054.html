Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Gannett Will Use Cross-Media Ads Again

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 09/28/90

Gannett Co.'s first foray into the sale of cross-media packages -- offering advertisers the chance to tap simultaneously into its vast newspaper, television, radio and billboard properties -- was a colossal failure in 1984. But now, five years after Gannett pulled the plug on the plan, the media giant is gearing up to make another run at the business. It has set up a task force to study the idea and says it wants to have "a few" advertisers try the cross-media program in 1991.

"It was really an idea before its time," says Cathleen Black, publisher of USA Today and executive vice president of marketing at Gannett. "But we've had some internal discussions, and we think it's time again to rethink this."

But Gannett's idea isn't unique anymore, and it may find that the market is already glutted with media companies that have recently gotten into the cross-media ad package business. Earlier this week, Washington Post Co.'s Newsweek and Times Mirror Corp. announced a multimedia ad package. Time Warner Inc., with its diverse interests from publishing to cable TV, is also testing the waters, as is Hearst Corp., which owns magazines and the Lifetime cable network. Meredith Corp. offers cross-media ad deals, too.

Gannett, though, says its programs will be different because its regional media outlets would allow advertisers to target their messages at very narrow local segments. Advertisers that sign up could tap its 10 TV stations, 15 radio stations, 83 newspapers, USA Today and its outdoor billboard network. Ms. Black says the company might even link with non-Gannett media, such as a magazine or in-store media, if an advertiser wanted that.

Still, some ad agency media directors aren't sure how big a market there is for such deals. They warn that the programs may appeal only to a very small group of big-spending advertisers. And there are other concerns: "Media companies have difficulty because they don't understand the needs of the clients," says John Belfiore, senior vice president of media planning services at ad agency BBDO.

Gannett acknowledges that it stumbled the first time because it forced advertisers to select from rigid ad packages that Gannett itself created. One package, for example, might have offered billboard and radio ads -- but not TV. Now Gannett is demonstrating a flexibility common to more and more media companies these days. Programs now will be created one-on-one with advertisers, Gannett says.

Ms. Black says Gannett decided to give the program a second try in part because of interest from advertisers, which said their marketing departments were facing cuts. The advertisers, she says, were looking to Gannett and other media companies not just for ad pages, but for marketing advice.

"We do know that the easier you make it for them, the better your chance of selling a program," Ms. Black says. "In this day and age, the closer you are to your customer's thinking, the better off you will be in creating ideas and programs that match their objectives."

While Ms. Black touts the benefits of such packages, she warns that advertisers who sign up won't get the big volume discount some other programs offer. The program was primarily developed, she says, to "get incremental dollars for Gannett."

Although they're taking a wait-and-see approach, some media directors at ad agencies say corporate advertisers, particulary the big spenders, might be interested in looking into the packages if media companies can convince them that they have the know-how to offer individualized, tailored service.

"The kinds of group deals or buys that were offered in the past weren't flexible enough, and as a result, there wasn't much business that grew out of them," says Rich Hamilton, senior vice president at D'Arcy Masius Benton & Bowles. "The media companies are recognizing the need to be more flexibile . . . and that's a positive thing," he says.

Alaska Launches Ad Blitz

Do you think Alaska's shoreline is still awash in oil? The Alaska Visitors Association, using $7 million from Exxon, is mounting a TV ad blitz to convince you otherwise.

The 30-second spots, which will run about 60 times a week for four weeks, broke yesterday in 35 major markets. Each of the three spots features new footage of Prince William Sound -- and its mammal and marine life -- with no visible effects from the Exxon Valdez spill last year. A typical line: "The sapphire of Prince William Sound nearly recovered from the spill."

"Our research showed there is a substantial misperception about the effects of the spill," says Robert Dindinger, president of the 600-member trade association. "Nobody is going to come to Alaska and see dead animals on the beach."

Mr. Dindinger said the number of tourists visiting Alaska has actually risen slightly since the spill. But that growth, at an annual rate of 2% to 3%, is a far cry from the double-digit growth the state had seen in the 1980s.

Exxon, whose funding was termed voluntary by both parties, shared creative control of the commercials. The oil company "will benefit by dispelling misperceptions about conditions in Prince William Sound," an Exxon spokesman said.

Ad Notes. . . .

AMTRAK: Amtrak will allow advertising on its ticket jackets under an agreement reached with Devon Direct Marketing & Advertising, Malvern, Pa. With an annual ridership of about 21 million, the rail carrier will offer six million ticket jackets for advertising. Most major airlines carry such ads.

GERBER ACCOUNT: Gerber Products, Fremont, Mich., retained TBWA Advertising, New York, to handle the launch of its new Gerber Graduates food line for preschool children. Billings haven't been determined, but the account eventually could be valued at as much as $10 million. The Chicago office of J. Walter Thompson, a WPP Group unit, continues to handle Gerber's core baby-food business.
