Law -- Legal Beat: USX Fined $4.1 Million in Influence Case

  • Author: Alecia Swasy and Milo Geyelin
  • Date: 09/28/90

A federal judge in Birmingham, Ala., fined USX Corp. $4.1 million for conspiring to influence unfairly a concessionary labor contract in 1983. The fine was the maximum penalty USX could have received after its conviction in July on 14 criminal counts in connection with its talks with the United Steelworkers union to reopen its idled Fairfield works near Birmingham.

The government said USX influenced two union officials by arranging for their pensions to be enriched beyond what they would have received as regular company employees.

The case stemmed from 1983 labor negotiations when USX, then U.S. Steel Corp., sought substantial concessions from steelworkers to reopen the idled Fairfield works. A number of workers allege that they lost income because of the pact.

The convictions have been seen as a strain on tense negotiations between the union and USX, the nation's largest steelmaker. They also could hurt the union's credibility among some rank and file members.

USX was also ordered to pay restitution of $289,995 to two pension funds that prosecutors claimed were depleted because of the pension increases.

The two union officials, E.B. Rich, 60, and Thermon Phillips, 62, who were also convicted in July, were sentenced, but remain free on bond pending their appeals. Mr. Rich received three years in jail plus three years probation and a $6,000 fine. Mr. Phillips received 30 months in jail plus two years probation and a $6,000 fine.

A USX spokesman said the company plans to appeal. USX has said there was "clear error in the conduct of the trial which will lead to a reversal of the verdicts."

Steelworkers president Lynn R. Williams said "we believe the conviction will be reversed." The union has said the practice of providing such pensions for union representatives is widespread in U.S. industry. A Department of Labor survey has found that 62% of U.S. manufacturers provide such benefits to labor representatives even though they are employed by unions.

The union said Mr. Phillips's post as director of Steelworkers' District 36 will be temporarily filled by Richard H. Davis, an administrative assistant at the international union, pending Mr. Phillips's appeal. Mr. Rich, a subdistrict director in Alabama, is retiring from his post. No successor was named.

JURORS CHOSEN in Mapplethorpe obscenity case.

Prosecutors and defense attorneys in the obscenity and child nudity case against Cincinnati's Contemporary Arts Center and its director settled on a jury of four men and four women, ending four days of jury selection.

The case is the first ever in the U.S. in which a museum has been charged with obscenity. The charges center on seven photographs in an exhibit of 175 works by the late Robert Mapplethorpe.

None of the eight jurors or the two alternate jurors selected had heard of the artist before April or seen his retrospective exhibit. Only two, a sales associate at a sporting goods outlet and an export coordinator for Procter & Gamble, live in the city. The rest, including a warehouse manager, a data processor, an electrical engineer, a secretary, hospital quality control specialist and a systems technician for Cincinnnati Bell Telephone Co. live in the conservative suburbs. At least four have been active church members.

Hamilton County Municipal Judge David H. Albanese has asked reporters covering the trial not to publish the names of the jurors because of possible harassment. Defense lawyers declined to comment on the jury pool.

Jury selection is considered by many trial lawyers to be the most important part of a trial, but the makeup of the jury will have added significance in this case. The law regarding obscenity hinges on whether the material in question falls within average and acceptable community standards or whether it appeals purely to prurient interest and has no redeeming literary or artistic merit. Jurors in this case will act as the conscience of their community and pass judgment on what their community's standards should be.

Throughout the jury selection, Hamilton County's chief prosecutor, Frank Prouty, systematically weeded out potential jurors who indicated an interest in art or in visiting art galleries and museums.

None of the jurors has ever been to the Center for Contemporary Arts, and one hasn't been to an art gallery or museum since grade school.

Opening arguments in the case are set to begin this morning.

The photos in question -- five depicting homoerotic and sadomasochistic images and two in which children posed with their genitals exposed -- caused a furor here when the show opened in April. The center and its director, Dennis Barrie, are each charged with one misdemeanor count of pandering and one misdemeanor count of using a minor in material with nudity.

FORMER S&L EXECUTIVE pleads guilty to federal bank fraud and tax evasion.

Donald Moskowitz, former president, chief executive officer and chairman of United Savings Bank in Paterson, N.J., admitted in federal court in Newark, N.J., that he used bank funds to pay for personal expenses, took kickbacks from loan customers and bank suppliers and evaded more than $40,000 in personal income taxes. Judge Alfred M. Wolin accepted his plea.

Mr. Moskowitz, who faces a maximum of 30 years in prison, restitution and $1,350,000 in fines, misappropriated $325,000 in bank funds for personal use. He also estimated that the fraud cost the bank between $2 million to $2.5 million.

According to Stuart Rabner, an assistant U.S. attorney, United Savings Bank is one of the nation's top 100 fraud-ridden thrifts. The Resolution Trust Corporation took over the bank in May.

"Obviously, his misappropriation of bank funds . . . jeopardized the bank's funds," Mr. Rabner said. Mr. Moskowitz's attorney, Edward Fitzpatrick, could not be reached for comment.

JUDGE REFUSES to limit SEC's questioning of German broker.

The broker, Hans-Jurgen Klisch, is a defendant in the Securities and Exchange Commission's civil suit alleging insider-trading in connection with GTE Corp.'s merger with Contel Corp. The $6.2 billion merger was announced July 12. Mr. Klisch and others are charged with trading Contel securities based on inside information about the merger.

Mr. Klisch, a broker for Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. in Dusseldorf, West Germany, had asked federal Judge Charles Haight in Manhattan to restrict the scope of the SEC's questioning. Mr. Klisch said he feared the SEC would ask him about his brokerage clients, which he said could lead to violations of German law on disclosing information about bank customers.

Judge Haight, however, ruled that because Mr. Klisch would face civil, rather than criminal, penalties for such disclosures, the SEC couldn't be restricted.

The deposition of Mr. Klisch was scheduled for today in Dusseldorf.

PRO BONO: Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom appointed a partner to oversee public interest work. Edwin E. McAmis, a litigation partner with 31 years of trial experience, will supervise the New York firm's pro bono civil litigation full time. Mr. McAmis, who has specialized in corporate and commercial disputes, said yesterday that pro bono litigation "helps the firm meet its responsibilities to the community and helps younger lawyers improve their skills as advocates."
