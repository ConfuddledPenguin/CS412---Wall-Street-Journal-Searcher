World Markets: Some U.K. Analysts Expect Stocks to Recover Although Market Still Faces Rough Road Ahead

  • Author: Richard E. Rustin
  • Date: 09/28/90

LONDON -- London's stock market has taken its share of battering from the Persian Gulf crisis, but some big British investors are keeping a stiff upper lip. Several money managers and analysts expect that, although the market still faces rocky times, a recovery is only a few months away. Betting that any fighting in the Gulf will be over quickly and that oil prices will drop, some institutions are buying blue chips they consider undervalued.

"There's general belief that there will be war in the Gulf," says Alistair McLennan, senior fund manager at Norwich Insurance Group. "There then would be a {downward} reaction in Wall Street and a knee-jerk reaction here. But if it's over quickly, that could lead to a very, very sharp upward movement -- greater than 20% before the end of January."

Mr. McLennan says that Norwich remains fully invested, and recently has been buying certain internationally oriented stocks. They are shares that, he reasons, should rise if oil prices decline and sterling, currently propped up by Britain's oil-producing capacity, starts to weaken. (A weaker pound makes British exports cheaper.)

The stocks include Grand Metropolitan, BTR, B.A.T Industries and Imperial Chemical Industries. Norwich also has been buying defense-oriented stocks, including General Electric, which isn't related to the U.S. company, and GKN. "The peace dividend has been omitted," quips Mr. McLennan.

At Edinburgh-based Standard Life Assurance Co., "we haven't exactly been filling our boots, but we're still supportive of U.K. equities," says John K. Thomson, senior investment manager. Britain's largest mutual insurer is still buying stocks at its pre-Gulf clip of #14 million ($26.2 million) a week, he says.

Adds Mr. Thomson: "Short-term, the Gulf, inflation and worries about a recession make for a nervous market. But long-term, the market looks cheap. Once people are convinced that Saudi oil is secure, the market will recover. When the market turns, you won't be able to get any money in at all."

Mick Brewis, who manages money at Baillie Gifford & Co., says the Edinburgh fund manager's cash position is double its usual level, mainly because of U.S. share sales. He thinks the U.K. market "is oversold" and he has been picking up shares of Racal Telecom, the cellular-phone maker.

Says Mr. Brewis: "Racal is pretty cheap at the moment. A lot of Americans have been selling the stock, but we think its medium-term prospects are pretty good. Its product definitely is a productivity tool -- there will be a need for it."

With the British economy staggering under high inflation, high interest rates and low growth, shares already were sliding when Saddam Hussein blind sided world financial markets with the Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait. The general view before the crisis was that the market would turn around by year end. The crisis has accelerated the decline and, say many market pros, has delayed the recovery until the first quarter of 1991, although there are some sanguine souls who still think the snapback will come right on schedule.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Index was in the low 2300s just prior to the Iraqi invasion. Many in the London market had expected the index to fall to about 2200 by year end purely as a result of Britain's economic woes. Then the Gulf crisis hammered shares by hyping U.K. inflation to 10.6% just when many thought the rate would peak. The FT-SE 100 closed at 2009.1, up 9.1 points yesterday. The Financial Times 30 Share Index gained 15.5 points to 1548.1. Volume was 428.8 million shares, up from 410.3 million shares Wednesday.

Midsummer predictions for a year-end market turnaround were based on the belief that Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher's government would begin to loosen Britain's stifling 15% base interest rate when inflation showed signs of receding.

Money managers and analysts also thought that a rate cut would be coupled with British entry into the European Monetary System's exchange-rate mechanism, or ERM. Membership would tie sterling to a narrow band with other European currencies and act as a stabilizing force against inflation.

But Mrs. Thatcher this week strongly stated that Britons shouldn't expect a rate cut soon. Still, the London market has been buoyed in recent days by indications that Britain may join the ERM soon, without a rate cut.

"You could see a 15% to 20% rise in the market on the back of ERM entry," says Mike Young, director of European investment strategy at Merrill Lynch. Until then, he adds, "things remain fairly gloomy -- we're in a period of very slow economic growth, disappointing corporate earnings, a housing market that's in a state of collapse and where the cost of financing inventories is enormous."

In the current environment, it's best to be defensive, says Richard Kersley, U.K. equity strategist at Barclays de Zoete Wedd. He recommends purchase of food retailer Tesco, pharmaceuticals maker Wellcome, and Reckitt & Colman, which is in both fields. He also favors BTR "because of its proven record" and General Electric, which he says has "a strong balance sheet and prudent management."

The wild card, of course, is the Gulf. A prolonged war with damage to Saudi oil production facilities would raise havoc with world stock markets and economies. "We could have a 15% inflation rate in a year {in Britain} if oil prices really got out of hand," says Mike Payne, senior investment strategist at Legal & General Group, a major insurer.

Observes Standard Life's Mr. Thomson: "You need to be a military strategist as well as a market strategist to try to judge this market."

Elsewhere, shares yesterday ended higher in Paris, Zurich, Helsinki, Milan, Madrid; lower in Frankfurt, Stockholm, Oslo; and mixed in Amsterdam. South African gold stocks closed higher.

And stocks closed higher in Taipei and Sydney, mixed in Seoul, Singapore and Hong Kong, and lower in Manila and Wellington.
