Iberia Airlines Targets Latin America, Charter Flights for Its Expansion Plans

  • Author: Nicholas Bray
  • Date: 09/28/90

MADRID -- Iberia Airlines has set its sights on Latin America for expansion as part of a global strategy aimed at boosting profits and strengthening cooperation with other European airlines. Chairman Miguel Aguilo said Latin America is "a market where Iberia is already very strong. We intend to consolidate that position."

In Europe, Iberia will build its Viva subsidiary into a second-tier airline to compete with the rapidly restructuring charter business, he added.

Thanks to lower overheads, Viva, whose full name is Vuelos Internacionales de Vacaciones, will be able to offer discounted flights without upsetting Iberia's tariff structure.

Since his June appointment, the chairman, an engineer, has mapped out a strategy for Iberia that is based on internal reorganization and international expansion. For the moment, his plans appear to meet the approval of outside analysts.

"It's a very good thing to put senior executives closer to the marketplace and be more flexible on the marketplace," said Richard Bond, vice president in charge of aviation consulting at London-based consultancy Cresap Telesis, commenting on Iberia's internal restructuring.

As for Viva, Mr. Bond added, "it gives a means of competing in the transforming charter market that perhaps Iberia itself couldn't do very well."

Last year, Iberia's net profit plunged 74% to 6.22 billion pesetas ($65 million) from P24.26 billion ($246 million) because of a lengthy strike by mechanics, higher operating costs and a drop in tourist spending.

This year, strikes by cabin staff, combined with the effects of the peseta's strength against the dollar, higher fuel prices and an accentuated slump in the Spanish tourist trade will further chop Iberia's earnings.

Mr. Aguilo makes no effort to hide Iberia's problems. Like other airlines, he says, "we're going into a cyclical downturn. This year is going to be worse for us than previous years. Profit will be reduced."

The prospect of European deregulation, moreover, promises "a future full of uncertainties and complexities. The only way to face up to these challenges is through decentralization and delegation of responsibility," he said.

One of Mr. Aguilo's first acts since his appointment has been to trim head-office staff and separate operating units into individual profit centers. The process may take several more months to complete. The goal is to improve vertical communications, reduce staffing, and cut costs, at the same time as giving each sector greater flexibility.

In addition, Mr. Aguilo has put Iberia on track for expansion in Latin America, mirroring a general trend by Spanish companies to seek closer business links with the continent, building on their common language and close cultural ties.

One of his first steps, in July, was to make what turned out to be the only bid for 85% of Argentina's Aerolineas Argentinas, as leader of a consortium including local businessmen and a unit of CS First Boston Inc. Under the plan, Iberia will take 20% of Aerolineas with an option to raise its stake to 30%.

In a parallel move, he said, Iberia is at an advanced stage in negotiations with two airlines in the Caribbean for the purchase of a shareholding in one or the other of them. One is the Dominican Republic's Cia. Dominicana de Aviacion. Mr. Aguilo declined to identify the other carrier, but Spanish press reports have referred to contacts between Iberia and the national airlines of Venezuela and Colombia.

In Argentina, the Aerolineas acquisition has been slowed by problems that Mr. Aguilo said appear to be related to local political opposition to President Carlos Menem's privatization plans. Though Iberia is ready to make a scheduled first payment of $130 million, problems have been raised by banks over how the funds should be channeled in relation to existing debts.

Mr. Aguilo warned that Iberia can't wait indefinitely for these problems to be sorted out, but he doesn't consider the project to be in danger.

Through this link-up and the planned tie-up in the Caribbean, Iberia's aim is to operate two hub systems to serve both business and tourist traffic in Latin America.

In both cases, the link-ups would be designed to allow Iberia and its prospective partner airlines to rationalize flight schedules both to and from Latin America and within Latin America and Europe.

In Buenos Aires, Iberia plans to locate small planes for use on its own flights within Latin America, instead of continuing on local flight-legs with costly trans-Atlantic jumbo jets, as at present. In addition to boosting profits, the project is designed to make Iberia a more attractive partner for cooperation with other European airlines.

Rather than provoking a series of mergers, Mr. Aguilo predicts, deregulation will cause European airlines to cooperate in a series of small ways, gradually building up closer links.

Iberia already has taken a series of steps in that direction, for example, through participation in the Amadeus computer reservations system, which groups several European airlines.

Earlier this summer, the carrier launched a code-sharing accord with Alitalia under which Iberia carries the Italian airline's passengers on its Madrid-Mexico City flights and Alitalia does the same for Iberia on its Rome-Bangkok route.

Mr. Aguilo said that this agreement is working well and that cooperation between the two airlines is likely to be expanded "providing it's in the interests of both partners."

Similarly, he said, Iberia is waiting for the European Community Commission to give its clearance for a planned cooperation pact between West Germany's Lufthansa and Air France to see whether it can join that project. Lufthansa and Air France have proposed cooperating in such areas as pilot training, catering and executive exchanges. EC officials are examining the plan to see whether it conflicts with competition rules.

Starting next summer, Viva -- originally set up with Lufthansa as a joint venture for charters -- will serve as Iberia's second-tier carrier for flights between smaller cities in Spain and other European countries, mainly Britain and West Germany.

With younger staff, newer planes and lower operating overheads, Viva will be able to offer tariffs competing with the charter market. Ultimately, Mr. Aguilo predicted, the European charter market will die out and charter companies will convert to scheduled flights.

"Viva will aim less at business traffic and more at tourism. It's a question of adjusting products to their markets."
