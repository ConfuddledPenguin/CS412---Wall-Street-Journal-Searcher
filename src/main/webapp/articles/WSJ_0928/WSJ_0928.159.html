Credit Markets: Treasury Bond Prices Jump as Nervous Investors Bail Out of Major Banking, Financial Issues

  • Author: Tom Herman and Sharon King
  • Date: 09/28/90

NEW YORK -- Worried traders scrambled to sell bonds of major banks and other financial issues as concern grew about the fragility of the financial system. The search for safety drove many traders and investors to government-guaranteed securities, sending prices sharply higher on Treasury bills, notes and bonds. Prices of long-term Treasury bonds surged yesterday by about one point, or $10 for each $1,000 face amount.

"New cash that investors and money managers have is going into Treasurys," said William Brachfeld, executive vice president at Daiwa Securities America Inc. "There's wholesale dumping by institutions of common stocks and debt of banks and financial institutions because they don't want them on their books at quarter's end, with the massive declines we've seen in prices lately. And the beneficiary of this is the Treasury market."

Treasury bond prices also were bolstered by speculation that congressional leaders and the White House are close to an agreement on cutting the nation's budget deficit, traders said. If so, the Federal Reserve may react soon by lowering short-term interest rates. The Fed's policymaking committee is scheduled to meet Tuesday to review credit strategy.

Corporate junk bonds took another pounding. Prices of many bellwether junk bonds, those high-risk, high-yield issues of companies with low credit ratings or no ratings at all, tumbled by about one point, their sixth consecutive setback.

In the municipal bond market, Massachusetts is paying a stiff price for its financial woes. Massachusetts, which has the lowest bond rating of any state, had to offer investors high yields -- ranging to nearly 8% on some seven-year bonds -- to attract buyers for a $1.3 billion issue.

Some municipal-finance specialists thought Massachusetts might have to pay an even stiffer price for its financial problems. Worries about the state's finances are widespread because of speculation that voters may approve a proposed tax-cut initiative in November. Bond dealers said the yields announced yesterday were high enough to attract a mixed reception from investors.

In the corporate sector, Chase Manhattan Corp., NCNB Corp. and Bankers Trust New York Corp. were among the hardest hit. Bank bonds have been hammered recently in the aftermath of Chase's decision to cut its dividend and slash its staff by 5,000.

Some traders say investors have overreacted, that bank bonds now offer unusually attractive yields and that this could be a good time to pick up bargains. But they also say there's no rush.

"This probably is not the right time" to buy bank bonds, said Neil Crowder, investment analyst at IDS Financial Services Inc. in Minneapolis. "The bonds do look cheap, but there's a good chance they will get even cheaper soon. My sense is there are so many people who want to get out of them right now that buying would be almost like stepping in front of a freight train."

The bank bond market "is very thin, and a lot of names are being hurt," said Richard Barnett, a corporate bond analyst at Mabon Nugent & Co. "The buyers are few and far between, and there are very few bids left in the Street."

While banking and financial-sector issues continue to be punished, traders say investor skittishness has spread to other sectors, forcing yields higher on long-term bonds of high-rated industrial corporations.

"Customers look at the marketplace and perceive heightened corporate credit risk in a recessionary environment," said James Quigley, head of syndicate at Merrill Lynch Capital Markets.

Treasury Securities

Treasury securities were star performers amid growing worries about the banking system.

The benchmark 30-year bonds jumped to 97 6/32 from 96 5/32 Wednesday. The yield, which moves in the opposite direction to prices, fell to 9.01% from 9.13%.

Short-term Treasury bills, known as the safest and stodgiest investments, also are enjoying growing popularity. Prices rose again yesterday, and yields fell. The bond-equivalent yield on the latest six-month bill fell to 7.55%, down about 0.15 percentage point from Wednesday. The discount rate on these bills ended at 7.20% bid.

The Treasury's new two-year notes, auctioned Tuesday by the government at an average annual yield of 8.18%, were quoted late yesterday at 8.02%. The new four-year notes, sold Wednesday at an average yield of 8.53%, were quoted at 8.40%.

Rumors that budget negotiators in Washington were close to an accord helped drive Treasurys, traders said, though many of them remained skeptical.

"The old saw is `Buy the rumor, sell the fact,'" said Joel Naroff, senior economist at First Fidelity Bank in Philadelphia. "That's what people are doing" in buying government bonds despite the absence of concrete results in the budget process, he said.

Municipal Bonds

The huge Massachusetts issue occupied center stage in the tax-free market.

The state offered yields ranging as high as 7.97% for bonds due in 1997, a reflection of its financial troubles. By contrast, Maryland, which has a triple-A rating, sold seven-year bonds this week at a yield about 1.5 percentage points below that on comparable Massachusetts securities.

Massachusetts paid a rate for the term bonds comparable with those on bonds sold by issuers such as New York City, whose finances also are making many investors increasingly nervous.

Analysts said the $1.3 billion Massachusetts issue appeared get a mixed reception from investors.

Underwriters were able to restructure the issue late in the day to lower the yield on the bonds maturing in 1997 to 7.97% from the 8% tentatively set earlier. But Massachusetts also changed the composition of the issue -- moving bonds from the longest maturity in 1997 to shorter maturities -- because of what some analysts said were difficulties in selling many of the seven-year bonds.

The Massachusetts issue also includes bonds secured by letters of credit.

Underwriters led by Merrill Lynch Capital Markets said they were pleased with investor reaction. "Very successful," said Dennis Boyle, a managing director at Merrill.

Some investment managers decided against buying the new bonds partly because Massachusetts faces the possibility of further revenue reductions if a voter initiative to reduce taxes sharply passes in November. If so, Massachusetts bonds could come under additional pressure.

"Proceeds of these bonds will clear the commonwealth's $1.3 billion accumulated deficit, but it is too soon to assess the effect of efforts to restore financial control," cautioned Moody's Investors Service Inc. In a statement yesterday, the rating concern warned: "Continuing threats to fiscal stability are posed by economic weakening, which has already produced lowerings of this year's revenue estimates, a pending court challenge to part of the fiscal 1991 tax package and the tax-cut petition to be voted in November."

Meanwhile, prices of other state and local government bonds trading in the secondary, or resale, market ended little changed or with moderate gains. The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% bonds due in 2018 rose 1/2 to 96 1/4 bid, 96 1/2 offered, to yield 7.50%.

Corporate & Junk Bonds

The corporate bond market, battered by heavy selling in banking and financial issues, experienced broad declines as investors scrambled to sell bonds amid rising concerns about a weakening economy.

Corporate junk bond prices also tumbled. Salomon Brothers Inc.'s daily high-yield index showed a price decline for the sixth day in a row and the 12th time in 13 trading days.

Among corporate issues most affected were those of Marriott Corp. and Unisys. Unisys said it would suspend its 25-cent quarterly dividend on its common, and Marriott reported sharply lower third-quarter earnings.

Yields on some bonds of the two companies jumped about half a percentage point. Unisys 10 3/4% debentures due in 1995 finished the day at 13.50%, up from 13% late Wednesday, and Marriott's 8 7/8% senior notes due in 1997 also ended at 13.50%, up from 13%.

Meanwhile, the already bruised bank sector continued to deteriorate amid further signs of difficulties at large banks. Traders said bonds from such companies as Bankers Trust New York and NCNB came under pressure. Also hurt were the already deeply depressed bonds of Chase Manhattan.

Chase's 10% subordinated notes due in 1999 fell two points to 74 1/8. Bankers Trust New York's 9.50% subordinated notes, maturing in 2000, dropped 1 5/8 to 90 5/8 for a yield of 11.10%. NCNB Texas National Bank's subordinated capital notes due in 2004 plunged 4 3/4 to 86 3/8 for a yield of 11.60%.

Mortgage & Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities were higher despite the continued increase in oil prices.

Government National Mortgage Association 9 1/2% securities for October delivery were up 15/32 at 98 5/32. Conventional mortgages were up 12/32, with Federal National Mortgage Association and Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. gold 9 1/2% certificates for October delivery at 98 12/32 and 98 18/32, respectively.

On the New York Mercantile Exchange, November crude oil futures reached a record of $40.10 a barrel around midday and closed at $39.54, up 87 cents a barrel.

Despite the sharp increase in the oil market, the fixed-income markets have shown new signs of life. Traders cited optimism about a solution to the budget deficit and the prospect of an impending easing in interest rates by the Fed as possible reasons, as well as the collapse in the banking and finance sectors of the corporate bond market.

Foreign Bonds

British and German government bond prices finished little changed, while Japanese bonds inched higher.

In Tokyo, traders were encouraged by the U.S. decision to sell five million barrels of oil from its Strategic Petroleum Reserve to try to contain surging oil prices. Japan's benchmark issue No. 119, the 4.8% bonds due 1999, rose on the Tokyo Stock Exchange to 80.68 from 80.63 Wednesday. The yield fell to 8.69% from 8.71%.

In London, traders said investors remain worried about inflation and are moving money into shorter-term securities, which offer exceptionally high yields. Some short-term U.K. issues now yield more than 14 1/4%.

Britain's 11 3/4% issue due 1991 rose 1/32 to 99 5/32 to yield 14.27%. Among long-term issues, the 11 3/4% bonds due 2003/2007 finished unchanged at 99 2/32 to yield 11.87%.
