Mutual Funds: Global Funds Shine, but Trail Benchmark

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 09/28/90

NEW YORK -- International stock funds have produced great track records for a decade. But this doesn't mean their managers have been great stock pickers. Take a close look at these managers, and you find that they lagged behind their benchmark index almost as badly as their U.S. counterparts. This lapse didn't matter much in the 1980s, with foreign stock markets booming. But if international markets turn out to be more treacherous in the 1990s, then poor performance won't be nearly so palatable for mutual fund investors.

International funds, which invest largely outside the U.S., now dominate the list of top performers for the past five years, and a handful crop up among the 10-year leaders. According to Morningstar Inc., a Chicago mutual-fund research concern, international stock funds returned 16.2% a year for the decade ended Aug. 31, while domestic stock funds plodded along at 12.2% a year. (Returns include both share-price changes and dividends.)

The better performance of international funds hasn't gone unnoticed by investors, who so far this year have poured $4.8 billion into international funds, more than any other type of stock mutual fund.

Yet the managers of these international funds couldn't help but look smart, compared with their parochial colleagues who stuck with the U.S. market. Their returns were boosted by both rapidly rising foreign stock markets and a falling dollar.

On average during the decade, foreign stock markets outpaced the U.S. market by 2.1 percentage points a year. Then the foreign-currency effect kicked in, bringing U.S. investors roughly 1.4 percentage points a year extra just because of the sinking dollar, which meant that the foreign portfolios were worth more when converted into greenbacks.

Investors shouldn't take these funds' returns at face value. When it comes to evaluating performance, it isn't just total return that counts, but also how a manager performs, compared with a benchmark index. On this basis, international stock fund managers are only marginally more skillful than their colleagues who stick to U.S. stocks.

A frequently used benchmark for international stock funds is the Morgan Stanley Capital International stock index for Europe, Australia and the Far East. For the 10 years ended Aug. 31, the average international stock fund lagged behind Morgan Stanley's EAFE index by 2.1 percentage points a year. By contrast, domestic stock funds fell below the Standard & Poor's 500 index of domestic stocks by a slightly wider margin, 2.6 percentage points a year.

International stock fund managers may find it tougher to stay close to the EAFE index in the decade ahead. The international market is "definitely more competitive than it was 10 years ago," said Kenneth Gregory, editor of the L/G No-Load Fund Analyst, a San Francisco newsletter. "It's going to become more difficult to beat the averages."

This is bad news for international fund managers, who say foreign stock-picking is already pretty tough. Running an international stock fund "is like playing three-dimensional chess," George Noble, manager of Fidelity Overseas, said. "It's a lot more challenging than playing the U.S. market."

Most domestic mutual-fund managers shun grand predictions about the economy or the stock market's direction, and instead buy stocks based on their individual merits. Life isn't so simple for fund managers who invest abroad. These international stock pickers operate as part foreign-currency trader, part global economist and part securities analyst. As a result, said Lori Lucas, a fund analyst with Morningstar Inc., many international funds end up "just buying baskets of blue-chip stocks in markets that look attractive."

Not surprisingly, major mistakes occur. Many fund managers missed the Tokyo market's spectacular rise from 1986 to 1988, and as a result, the average international fund lagged behind the EAFE index by 3.5 percentage points a year over the past five years. But even if Japan is excluded from the EAFE index, international funds would still have been laggards, though only by two percentage points a year.

"The question is, is it so much more complicated that you're going to shoot yourself in the foot?" Mr. Gregory, the newsletter editor, said.

It seems not. For while most international stock fund managers trail the index, a few do exceed it. A popular strategy with the more successful crowd is to exploit economic themes the managers hope will play out in the next few years.

Ivy International's fund manager, Hakan Castegren, for example, is looking for a recovery among shipping companies and a pickup in the price of base metals. This has led him to buy stocks such as Keppel Corp., a Singapore shipbuilder, and RTZ Corp., a mining conglomerate.

Many fund managers are trying to make money from German reunification, but Richard Foulkes, manager of Vanguard World-International Growth, reckons the biggest stock market winners won't be German stocks. "We're trying to play the theme that German demand will be very great, and that the biggest beneficiaries will be its major trading partners," Mr. Foulkes said. He now has 43% of his fund's assets invested in Continental Europe, with half that money in Dutch and Swiss stocks. "It's a wonderful situation, sitting next to a rich cousin and feeding off him," he said.

William Holzer, manager of Scudder Global Fund, is looking to exploit a looming world-wide shortage of bank credit. He points out that West German banks are preoccupied with reunification, and that U.S. and Japanese banks are cutting back on new lending because of balance-sheet problems. "The question arises, where will the available bank credit come from?" said Mr. Holzer, who believes Swiss banks, such as Union Bank of Switzerland and Swiss Bank Corp., could be major beneficiaries.

For those who prefer fund managers who are a little more systematic in their stock-picking, Vanguard Group offers two alternatives. One is Trustees' Commingled International, which is distributed by Vanguard but managed by the computer-driven investors at Batterymarch Financial Management of Boston. The fund has beaten the EAFE index over the past three years, and matched the index over the past five.

The other alternative is Vanguard's new international stock index fund. The fund is divided into two portfolios. The European Portfolio tracks the Morgan Stanley Capital International Europe Index by buying a representative sample of the index's stocks, and the Pacific Portfolio seeks to replicate the performance of Morgan Stanley's Pacific Index. To buy the two portfolios in the appropriate proportions, given the relative size of the two markets, investors would put 55 cents into the Pacific Portfolio for every 45 cents going into the European Portfolio.
