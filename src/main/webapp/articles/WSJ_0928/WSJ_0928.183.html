GM Cuts Discounts On Certain Models In 1990, 1991 Lines

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/28/90

DETROIT -- General Motors Corp. is raising prices on some 1990 and 1991 models by cutting the cash discounts offered to buyers. In effect, the No. 1 auto maker is joining Ford Motor Co. in a gamble that efforts to boost revenue in a slumping market won't cost market share in the competition against Japanese auto makers.

GM didn't raise prices as much as Ford, which earlier this week eliminated cash-to-customer rebates on leftover 1990 models and sharply scaled back the discounts on many high-volume 1991 models. Unlike Ford, GM left cash discounts on most 1990 models. However, rebates on some high-volume 1990 and 1991 Chevrolet models will be substantially less than they were just a few days ago.

For example, someone who bought a 1990 Chevrolet Corsica Monday got a $1,000 cash rebate. But buying a 1990 Corsica today will mean only $500 off the sticker price, in effect a $500 price boost.

GM is raising the prices on 1991 Corsicas, too, by cutting the cash rebate to $500 from $750. Similarly, the rebate on 1990 Chevrolet Lumina mid-sized sedans was cut to $500 from as much as $1,500.

"Certainly we watch what other people do," a Chevrolet spokesman said. The decision to cut back on rebates in part reflects lower stocks of unsold cars on GM dealer lots, he said.

GM isn't raising prices on every model. Some weak-selling models will come with even bigger discounts. The rebate on certain 1990 Oldsmobile Cutlass Supreme Coupes rose to $2,500 from $2,000. The discount on other Cutlass Supremes will remain at $2,000.

GM's bid to raise prices on high-volume lines reflects growing pessimism about the strength of the U.S. auto market. GM and Ford, by raising prices, are signaling that they're resigned to a period of weak sales.
