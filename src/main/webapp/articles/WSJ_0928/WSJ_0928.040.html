Iraq Can Survive Winter Of Embargoes, Study Says

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/28/90

WASHINGTON -- A Congressional Research Service study says Iraq can survive the winter's embargoes by shifting to a vegetarian diet, rationing food and smuggling in whatever it can. The assessment came as Baghdad announced its intentions to cut off food to non-Arabs and extend rationing to rice, flour, sugar, tea and cooking oil. Bread has been rationed since Sept. 1.

In a report to Congress, an agriculture-policy analyst for the Library of Congress's research arm said Iraq's chances of holding out against trade sanctions depend largely on how quickly it turns toward subsistence farming and substitutes domestic crops for food imports. Until its invasion of Kuwait, Iraq imported 75% of its food, including feed grain for egg and poultry production.

"Locally grown potatoes can be substituted for imported rice," the analyst, Susan Epstein, said. "Locally grown dates can be pressed to obtain a sweetener to replace imported sugar, and locally grown barley can replace imported wheat for bread." In addition, the Iraqis can switch to beans and lentils as a protein alternative to meat, she said.

Shortages, Ms. Epstein said, will be offset somewhat by a fall harvest of vegetables and the slaughter of livestock as farmers run out of feed grain. Iraqi farmers will begin planting their barley and wheat crops next month, for harvest in May and June. In the meantime, rationing of dwindling wheat and rice stockpiles must be strictly enforced.

Over the long term, Ms. Epstein said, efforts to increase production will be limited by the lack of seed and farm-equipment parts and the exodus of foreign technicians who had repaired irrigation rigs.
