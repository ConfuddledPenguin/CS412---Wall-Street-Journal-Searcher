Federal Court Nullifies Nevada Insurance Law

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/28/90

SAN FRANCISCO -- Striking a blow to advocates of voter-mandated insurance-rate cuts, a federal court here declared unconstitutional a Nevada law requiring sweeping auto-insurance rate roll backs. The Ninth U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals said the Nevada law denied insurers a constitutionally guaranteed "fair and reasonable return." The Nevada reform package, passed in 1989 to mirror California's similar Proposition 103, called for insurers to cut auto liability insurance rates to a minimum of 15% below 1988 levels and to freeze rates for a year.

Seven insurance companies challenged the law, maintaining that it violated their due process rights because it prohibited their increasing rates if they faced losses. Nevada's new restrictions, which were to have taken effect Oct. 1, 1989, weren't enforced because the court imposed stays while legal challenges were pending.

Nevada's law states "rates are inadequate if they are clearly insufficient, together with the income from investments attributable to them, to sustain projected losses and expenses in the class of business to which they apply."

The federal court yesterday ruled that the definition left insurers in the position of declaring rates adequate if losses and expenses were identical.
