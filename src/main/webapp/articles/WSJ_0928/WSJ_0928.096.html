Autos: Light-Truck Sales Seem To Cool Off

  • Author: Neal Templin and Andrea Puchalsky
  • Date: 09/28/90

DETROIT -- Rising gasoline prices and fears of recession may be taking a toll on what has been the hottest segment of the auto market for the past five years: light trucks. General Motors Corp. said yesterday that it's temporarily idling four factories or assembly lines that make pickup trucks, sport-utility vehicles and chassis for recreational and commercial vehicles because of slow sales. The company didn't announce the shutting of any car-assembly plants.

Ford Motor Co. said it is closing its Edison, N.J., plant that assembles Ranger pickup trucks for one week, starting Monday. But the company said the shutdown comes from production considerations, not slow sales.

Still, while domestic car sales have climbed about 1% so far this month from a year earlier, sales of trucks dropped 8.2%. It's just the reverse of the dominant sales pattern of recent years, as consumers have shifted from cars to minivans and other trucks, many of which consume more gas than all but the biggest cars.

Many dealers say they have seen a slowdown in light-truck business since Iraq's invasion of Kuwait in early August sent energy prices soaring. "Showroom traffic has dropped considerably since oil prices went up," says Darrell McClure, GMC trucks sales manager at Bill Farley Pontiac-GMC in Birmingham, Ala.

"You got to be a real true-blue truck buyer to buy one at this point," says Dave Bacon, a sales manager for the Forest Lake Ford and Chrysler dealerships in Minnesota. "Before we were just selling them to people who liked the looks of the trucks."

Many analysts say it's too early to declare that soaring gasoline prices are the main culprit. "Higher gas prices certainly hurt new vehicles sales, but the overriding factor is just the drop in consumer confidence in general," says analyst John Casesa of Wertheim Schroder & Co.

Still, some dealers report strong sales for certain big trucks. For example, Friendly Chevrolet in Dallas still is selling plenty of its big Suburban passenger vans, says Steve Fly, the dealership's retail truck manager.

GM plans to idle the four truck operations for between a week and four weeks. The longest shutdown is planned at the Detroit plant that assembles chassis for recreational and commercial vehicles. That plant closed last Monday and isn't expected to reopen until Oct. 22. A GM plant in Ohio that assembles compact pickup trucks and sport-utility vehicles also closed Monday and will reopen Oct. 8.

In addition, GM plans to idle an assembly line in Flint, Mich., that assembles chassis for crew cabs for one week, starting Monday. And a second GM assembly line in Flint that makes full-sized Jimmy models and Chevrolet Blazers is slated to close for two weeks, starting Monday.

Overall, the 10 major U.S. auto makers planned to build 137,288 cars this week, down 9.2% from 151,228 a year earlier.

Ford said it scheduled Saturday overtime at its Wayne, Mich., car-assembly plant that builds the Escort and at its Kansas City and St. Louis truck plants.

GM said it scheduled Saturday overtime at its Bowling Green, Ky., assembly plant that builds the Chevrolet Corvette. In addition, GM said it will work overtime today at its Janesville, Wis., plant that builds the Chevrolet Cavalier.

Chrysler Corp. said it is cutting its St. Louis 1 plant down to a single shift for one week, instead of the normal two shifts, because of slow sales for the Dodge Daytona and Chrysler LeBaron.

Corrections & Amplifications

In Friday's edition, it was incorrectly stated that Chrysler was cutting production to one shift from two shifts for one week only.
