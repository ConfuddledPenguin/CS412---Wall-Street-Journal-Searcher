MARKETING -- The Beer Industry

  • Author: Marj Charlier
  • Date: 09/28/90

Some Imports Bucking Dwindling Sales Trend

THANKS TO clever marketing, some foreign brewers are managing to swim against a receding tide of U.S. sales by imports.

For the second year running, sales of imported beers are declining, down 2.7% through May compared with the same period last year, according to Beer Marketer's Insights.

But sales of Warsteiner, Germany's largest-selling beer, rose 28% in the first half, without advertising. Its sales are still dwarfed by Heineken, the No. 1 import. But Warsteiner, which came to the U.S. in 1983 through Warsteiner Importers Agency in Denver, projects sales will reach 320,000 cases this year, up 34% from about 239,000 last year.

Warsteiner enters its markets with sales of drafts at posh locations, hoping to build package sales from such sampling at bars and restaurants. Most importers go straight to the supermarket.

Grolsch has also gained ground, with sales up 12.2% to 750,000 cases through August, compared with the same period last year, estimates Jim Gollhofer, president of Grolsch Importers, a unit of Grolsch Breweries of the Netherlands.

As other imports have slumped, Grolsch has beefed up its sales force to increase the number of contacts it makes with beer distributors, who are responsible for putting beer on trucks and store shelves. And the company has reduced its TV ads in favor of print ads in upscale magazines more likely to reach its target audience, says Mr. Gollhofer.

Marketing May Explain

Alcohol-Labeling Push

DOES THE PUSH by G. Heileman Brewing to require the disclosure of alcohol content on beer labels have as much to do with marketing as the public's right to know?

Heileman is lobbying for the requirement in states across the country, despite opposition by federal and state governments, which fear that it might encourage people to buy more potent beer. Heileman's aim may be just the opposite. Heileman brews two of the five U.S. beers with the least alcohol, Rainier Light and Henry Weinhard's Light, and may see this as an advantage in marketing to today's health-conscious consumers. Its other brands include Old Style, Schmidt, Lone Star, Blatz and Special Export.

Last month, the La Crosse, Wis., brewer finally struck pay dirt when the Washington state liquor commission decided to require the percentage of alcohol to be printed on all malt beverage labels. Washington state, where Heileman has a 30% market share, was a logical place for the brewer to take a high-profile stand on the issue. Washington is Heileman's second-biggest market, largely due to its Rainier brand. Rainier's light version has an alcohol content of 2.6%, while the regular version has 4.6%. Heileman's sales, however, have been declining there, down about 7% through May of this year from the same period last year.

But a company spokesman insists Heileman's motive in making the labeling push is that President Murray Cutbush, who hails from Australia, grew up with alcohol labeling and thinks the public has a right to know how much alcohol is in each beer.

Other big brewers are split. Adolph Coors, which says it "philosophically" agrees with Heileman, won't join the state-by-state fight, calling that an "inefficient" approach. Miller Brewing and Anheuser-Busch are opposed.

Brewers Are Cultivating

Environmental Image

GREEN MARKETING has become big with brewers.

Miller Brewing sponsors the "Sack It To Me Saturday" citywide cleanup every spring in Milwaukee. And Canada's Moosehead Breweries donates money to the National Fish and Wildlife Foundation in the U.S. But some environmental do-gooding seems to be aimed at atoning for brewers' own environmental ills.

The most obvious example is Adolph Coors, which is under criminal investigation by the state of Colorado for dumping contaminated water from a well under its can plant into a nearby creek. The company's water pollution problem convinced Coors officials its environmental spending should go to a water pollution cleanup effort, a spokesman says. As noted in the company's television commercials, Coors gave $1 million to Pure Water 2000, which backs hundreds of projects across the country and is run by Robert Redford's Institute for Resource Management. The company also agreed recently to sponsor a Hudson River pollution patrol boat run by riverkeeper John Cronin.

Then there's Anheuser-Busch's $5 million contribution to the Don't Mess With Texas campaign, a state anti-litter project. About 40 beer and other beverage cans and bottles litter every mile of Texas highways, says Dan Syrek of the Institute for Applied Research. Still, the brewer's grant outweighs its contribution to litter, he says. "They're doing more than their fair share."
