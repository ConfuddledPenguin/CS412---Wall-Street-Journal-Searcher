Senate Clears Bill Giving the SEC Greater Powers

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 09/28/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate passed legislation that would give the Securities and Exchange Commission controversial new enforcement powers, expanding the agency's fining authority beyond insider-trading cases. The bill, approved by voice vote, also would give the agency "cease and desist" power to stop alleged wrongdoers. Moreover, it contains a provision that would better protect penny-stock investors.

The legislation, expected to be approved soon by the House, is expected to allow the SEC to handle more cases in-house through its own administrative law judges rather than in federal courts. While the administrative law judges are forced to use identical burdens of proof as federal court judges, they are seen by some as being more sympathetic to the SEC.

That has made some defense lawyers skittish that clients won't get the same treatment they can expect now. But the SEC dismisses that complaint, contending that the bill would allow the agency to better tailor the punishment to the crime. "It is not an unreasonable conclusion for people to say the enforcement program in the '80s has uncovered enough problems that we need more flexibility and to add some economic deterrence," said SEC enforcement director William McLucas. "My view is that's what this bill does."

The bill was proposed by the SEC two years ago in an effort to fill what it saw were holes in its enforcement program. The legislation also would give the agency the authority to ask courts to bar people from serving public companies as officers and directors if they have violated securities laws.

Under the fining authority, the agency could impose maximum penalties of as much as $100,000 per violation for individuals, $500,000 for corporations or the amount gained through illegal activities.

The penny-stock provision would give the commission the power to take enforcement action against penny-stock promoters, many of whom are not registered with the commission. Promoters found to be wrongdoers could be barred from being associated with a brokerage in the future. The bill also features new risk-disclosure requirements telling customers the nature and level of risks in the penny-stock area. Those requirements also extend to the way brokers deal with their customers.

The penny-stock provision also would give the SEC the power to place new limitations on so-called blank-check pools, which solicit investor funds without identifying the use of the proceeds. Moreover, the bill instructs the SEC to press exchanges to create an improved pricing system for penny stocks.

Separately, the Senate Banking Committee unanimously approved the nomination of Richard Roberts to the SEC. Mr. Roberts is a lawyer who was a former aide to Sen. Richard Shelby (D., Ala.), a member of the committee. Mr. Roberts, who was nominated to fill a Democratic slot on the commission, is expected to be approved soon by the full Senate.
