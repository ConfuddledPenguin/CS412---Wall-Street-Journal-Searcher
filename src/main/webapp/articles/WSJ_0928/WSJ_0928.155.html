Bank of Boston Reduces Its Dividend And Plans to Trim Work Force by 8%

  • Author: Ron Suskind
  • Date: 09/28/90

BOSTON -- Bank of Boston Corp., struggling in the sinking New England ecomony, cut its dividend for the first time in at least 30 years. The bank, plagued by a huge portfolio of nonperforming assets, said that it will add about $175 million to its loan loss reserves to offset continuing problems in the soft New England real estate market.

The third-quarter numbers, according to bank officials, will be bleak: it will lose up to $250 million. In the year-earlier quarter, the company had a loss of $125 million. In the next few months, 1,500 employees, 8% of the bank's work force, are expected to leave under a voluntary program of severance enhancements. The dividend, which the bank claims is the longest standing in North America, continuously paid since 1785, will drop from 31 cents a share to a dime.

"This corporation is clearly in a position to absorb these kinds of problems -- even if they continue for a while," said Chairman and Chief Executive Officer Ira Stepanian. He added that the bank's actions yesterday, especially in terms of increasing loan loss reserves, "ought to give people a sense of comfort. Will it? Probably not."

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Bank of Boston shares closed at $7.125, down 37.5 cents.

The crux of the bank's problems are in its portfolio of nonperforming assets. The bank's reduction of nonperforming assets by $100 million to $1.85 billion was due to charge-offs of about $175 million in bad loans and another $35 million in real estate now owned by the bank. Chief Financial Officer Peter Manning says that the inflow of fresh nonperformers has been slowing a bit, although it is still in the hundreds of millions for the quarter.

"Their nonperforming asset total has been kept level, but at a painful level," said James Moynihan, an analyst with Advest Inc. "When you realize the cost of those nonperformers, in legal fees, much less the interest you must still pay to depositers for holding their money, it's just astounding." Carrying nonperformers costs the bank about $180 million annually.

The bank has also put aside $50 million in restructuring costs, most of which will be paid in severance packages to the 1,500 employees slated to leave by early 1991. Bank officials said those employed for at least 10 years will be offered the severance package, though it won't be extended to senior management.

The bank said the moves it is making will reduce its noninterest expense level by at least $100 million annually.

"These are very disturbing numbers, not just for Bank of Boston but for all banks in the country with an exposure in commerical real estate," said Gerard Cassidy, an analyst with Tucker, Anthony & R.L. Day in Portland, Maine. "It shows bad loans are still flowing in the front door -- that we haven't hit bottom yet. To keep things under control, the bank will have to continue to eat into its capital position."

Richard Syron, chairman of the Federal Reserve Bank of Boston, said recently that the region's economic slide is still a year away from hitting bottom. The concern among bankers in New England is that another year of high real estate exposure, weak earnings and deteriorating credit quality will eat away at the capital that serves as a bank's financial cushion.

On that score, the Bank of Boston, the region's largest bank with $37 billion in assets, has few worries. Its capital level is strong, at over 9% of total assets.
