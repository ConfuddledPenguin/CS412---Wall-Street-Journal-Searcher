Foreign Exchange: Yen Loses Ground as Oil Prices Climb, Tokyo Stock Market Continues to Slide

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/28/90

NEW YORK -- Rising world oil prices and the retreating Tokyo stock market pushed the yen lower against all major currencies. The dollar gained at the expense of the weakened Japanese currency but fell against others. The greenback's mixed performance resulted from the dominance of cross, or non-dollar, trading. Except when the occasional rumor of war in the Mideast focused attention to the dollar (which is believed to be a haven for investors in times of turmoil), direct mark-yen trading offered the liveliest action.

The dollar is used as a medium for 95% of cross transactions in the U.S. and for 90% in Europe. It rises against the currency that's being sold -- in this case, the yen -- and falls against the currencies being bought -- here, the European currencies.

In late New York trading, the greenback was quoted at 1.5612 marks and 1.2925 Swiss francs, down from 1.5650 marks and 1.3015 francs late Wednesday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 138 yen, up from 136.84 yen. Sterling was trading at $1.8773, up from $1.8727.

The mark was quoted at 88.394 yen, up from 87.438 yen late Wednesday in New York. In another active cross market, the Swiss franc rose to 106.770 yen from 105.140 yen.

In Tokyo early Friday, the dollar was trading at 137.95 yen, up from the 137.50 yen close there on Thursday.

Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5620 marks, down from Thursday's Tokyo close of 1.5673 marks.

Contributing to the yen's decline, prices on the Tokyo Stock Exchange fell again Thursday. The Nikkei index of 225 stocks dropped 2.15% to 21771.91, after shedding 4.75% Wednesday. Brokers attributed Thursday's losses to selling, to meet margin requirements, by investors squeezed by Wednesday's big drop.

Oil prices continued to rise as heightened fears of war in the Persian Gulf won the day over the U.S. decision to tap its Strategic Petroleum Reserve on a test basis. A decision could be made to draw down the reserve to boost oil supplies and curb price rises.

On the New York Mercantile Exchange, the benchmark crude oil contract for November delivery briefly rose above $40 a barrel before ending at $39.54 a barrel, up 87 cents.

Meanwhile, rumors spread that U.S. troops had entered either Kuwait or Iraq, although a U.S. Defense Department spokesman denied them.

But the rumblings about the Middle East caused no more than short-lived blips in currency prices because dealers say everything short of war has been priced into the market. News and rumors did affect the tone of trading, leaving it occasionally volatile but ultimately directionless.

"The market is very, very nervous," said Joseph Cambria, New York foreign exchange manager for Banque Paribas. "You're seeing gaps {in pricing}. The market is starting to warm up, because people are starting to see there is a resolution coming and that will probably be war. Things are becoming fairly bitter."

Mr. Cambria noted, for example, responses by U.S. Secretary of State James Baker and the British Foreign Office to a note Iraq sent to embassies in Kuwait. The note asked for the names of non-diplomats inside and implied that Westerners sheltering those persons could be hanged.

Mr. Baker called the note "repugnant" and a British statement said the request put to rest Iraq's "pretensions" as a civilized state.

A Singapore dealer said, "The Gulf crisis makes a rational approach to the market very difficult."

The dollar ended European dealings mostly weaker. The unwinding of some long, or overbought, dollar positions kept the currency on the defensive most of the day, although prices stayed in extremely narrow ranges throughout the session.

The greenback finished Asian trading mostly higher, after covering of short, or oversold, positions boosted it against the yen early in the quiet session. The dollar was last quoted in Tokyo Thursday at 137.50 yen, just above 137.40 yen late Wednesday in Tokyo.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $405 an ounce, down $2. Estimated volume was a heavy five million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $404.95 an ounce.
