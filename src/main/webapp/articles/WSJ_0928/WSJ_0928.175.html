Electric Utilities See Pending Legislation As Threat to Reliability of Power Supply

  • Author: Kathryn Graven
  • Date: 09/28/90

WASHINGTON -- Electric utility companies, already concerned about a long-term shortage of capacity, worry that pending federal legislation is only going to make matters worse. In a revised annual 10-year forecast, the North American Electric Reliability Council cited pending changes in the Clean Air Act as "the most significant threat" to reliability of electrical power.

Michehl R. Gent, vice president of the council, said the main concern is that many electric-power companies will find it too expensive to meet new strict pollution-control standards and will be forced to close older facilities sooner than planned. Such a development, Mr. Gent said, could exacerbate the pressing power-supply situation in many parts of the country, particularly areas east of the Mississippi River.

Already power companies have been forced to reduce voltage in the Mid-Atlantic and Northeastern parts of the country when demand exceeded available supplies during extreme cold or hot spells.

The Reliability Council, formed by utility companies and comprising nine regional groups, attempts to provide a long-range industry view of the nation's electric-power needs.

Even without stricter pollution-control legislation, however, the growing demand for electricity -- especially in peak summer and winter periods -- has a lot of energy experts worried. In its report released yesterday, the council, for example, increased by 5% its estimates of peak utility demand for 1997.

While energy experts say short-term power supplies should be adequate, the long-term picture is less assuring. Although conceding that forecasting energy demand is tricky, the latest study predicts that the U.S. will need an additional 7,500 megawatts of power-generating capability in the 1990s if it wants to keep up with demand and provide reliable service. That 7,500-megawatt figure is lower than the 13,000 megawatts the council projected last year, but is still disturbing, says Mr. Gent. Officials said the figure declined because of improved forecasting methods, rather than a drop in expected demand.

Nonetheless, electric utilities are continuing to slow down construction of new facilities. The NERC's latest study shows that construction isn't yet under way on 55% of the new generating capacity previously announced for the next 10 years in the U.S. That gap is up from 41% last year and just 30% two years ago. Thus, forecasters worry that there's more risk that power mightn't be available when it is needed.

Some utility regulators are also worried about the impact of the new legislation. "Everyone is in favor of clean air," says Cas Robinson, a member of the Georgia Public Service Commission. "But is the public willing to pay for it?"

Nancy Kete, a senior policy analyst at the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, dismissed the notion that the Clean Air amendments will threaten the nation's electric-power supplies. "I don't think it's a well-founded fear. The EPA is just as concerned as anybody about electricity reliability. When it {the legislation} is explained, they will realize it won't make a big difference in energy supply," she said.

Slowing the pace of adding capacity is the complicated question of what type of fuel should be used to generate electricity. At the moment, more than half of the nation's electric power comes from coal-burning generators. Natural gas, oil, nuclear energy and renewable energy sources generate the rest. But with growing environmental pressures and fear of deeper dependence on foreign oil supplies, the utilities are left struggling to find economical and environmentally safe solutions.

In the past, electric utilities facing capacity crunches have been able to turn to their neighbors to buy extra power. Those days, however, may be numbered. As the strains on capacity spread, experts say neighboring utilities may be more reluctant to sell.
