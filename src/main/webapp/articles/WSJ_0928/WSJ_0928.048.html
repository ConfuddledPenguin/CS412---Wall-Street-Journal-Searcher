Goldman Secures Capital to Invest In Troubled Firms

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 09/28/90

NEW YORK -- In the latest sign of Wall Street's rush to profit from companies in financial distress, Goldman Sachs & Co. said it finished raising a $750 million fund that will invest in troubled corporations. Goldman's fund is the second biggest of more than 20 "restructuring" or "turnaround" investment pools being raised this year, according to pension consultants. The Goldman fund, known as Water Street Recovery Fund I, is exceeded in size only by a $1 billion pool raised earlier this year by Chicago investors Samuel Zell and David Schulte.

Goldman figures there is plenty of money to be made from turnaround investing. In the four years prior to setting up the Water Street fund, the firm says it made 22 investments of its own in distressed companies' bonds, bank debt or equity, usually at cut-rate prices. Eleven of those investments produced annual returns of 35% or better, says Goldman partner Mikael Salovaara. Mr. Salovaara is managing the new fund.

In a memo to investors, the Water Street fund states that it is seeking annual returns of 25% to 35%. If that goal is achieved, Goldman will benefit richly. Goldman has $100 million of its own money in the fund; it also is entitled to as much as 20% of the profits earned by its other investors, which include corporate pension funds, university endowments and foreign banks, mostly Japanese.

Running the Water Street fund, however, may clash with Goldman's reputation of doing friendly work for blue-chip clients. Turnaround investing often requires tough tactics, such as pressuring managers of ailing companies to quit or liquidate their businesses.

In its investors' memo, however, Goldman itself notes that conflicts of interest with existing clients may limit Goldman's ability to pursue some investments.

In previous investing for its own account, Goldman scored a coup in 1988 and 1989 by buying junk bonds of Fruehauf Corp. at 60 cents on the dollar. Goldman successfully pressed Fruehauf to sell its major trailer-making unit and was able to cash out its bond position at nearly 90 cents on the dollar.

One of Goldman's best-known turnaround plays, however, has backfired. The firm in early 1989 bought a one-third equity stake in Wheeling-Pittsburgh Corp., a steelmaker operating under bankruptcy-law protection. Goldman paid $8 a share; the stock currently trades at $3.50. "We made a mistake," Mr. Salovaara concedes. Goldman should have bought debt in Wheeling-Pittsburgh first, before buying equity, he says.

In the past few months, Mr. Salovaara says, Goldman has already invested $195 million of the Water Street fund, primarily buying junk bonds at 40% to 50% of their face value.

Both the U.S. economy and the market for distressed companies' securities probably have "a ways further to go down," Mr. Salovaara says. But he contends that by careful buying, he can pick out profitable investments even in a slumping market.

Others aren't so sure. Former Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. mergers chief Leon Black, who this summer raised a $400 million fund that can make turnaround investments, says he is inclined to wait six to nine months before putting much of his money to work. With oil prices soaring, the economy weakening, and bank stocks in free-fall, "it's pretty scary now," Mr. Black says.
