H-2 Fire Is Latest Setback for Japan's Space Program

  • Author: Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 09/28/90

TOKYO -- Japan's space program, like its U.S. counterpart, is struggling with a series of embarrassing setbacks. The National Space Development Agency said Thursday that the engine for its H-2 rocket, the centerpiece of Japan's space effort for the 1990s, caught fire Wednesday during tests at the Tanegashima Space Center off the southern tip of Japan. It was the fourth test fire in the past year.

A spokesman said the agency was discussing whether to delay yet again the first voyage of the H-2, which is slated for early 1993. That date was set last year after previous glitches forced a one-year lapse from the original target date of early 1992. The rocket is also already running 10% over its original 200 billion yen ($1.46 billion) budget.

Japan's space program lags years behind those of other industrialized nations, and the H-2 is its first 100% domestically made rocket. The H-2 is also supposed to launch a variety of other Japanese space projects, such as satellites and an unmanned space shuttle.

Delays in the H-2 could force the embarrassing choice of either pushing those projects back further or seeking foreign assistance. Analysts have said Japan is also hoping the H-2 ultimately will compete with organizations like Europe's Arianespace for commercial launches.

Japanese engine makers, led by Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Ltd., are struggling in part because this is their first attempt to build an engine for the first stage of a rocket, which is the heaviest phase and carries the most fuel.

This is also their first cryogenic engine, a complex technology that runs on liquid hydrogen. It has more power for its size and runs at higher temperatures than traditional engines fueled by petroleum.

The previous major test problems were the result of difficulties with the turbine blade, which cracked, or with the starting mechanism, the spokesman said. He added that those problems had been solved.

But the fire this week, which started in the engine's turbo pump, apparently had a different cause, the spokesman said, and the agency is conducting tests.

Last Saturday, the engine had its most successful test so far, running for 200 seconds. The space agency was aiming for 350 seconds on Wednesday. But 16 seconds after ignition, the fire started, damaging the engine and the test facility before it was put out more than 15 minutes later.
