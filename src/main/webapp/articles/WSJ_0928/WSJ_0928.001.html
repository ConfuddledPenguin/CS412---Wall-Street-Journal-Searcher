Letters to the Editor: MacArthur's Gift to Japan: Democracy

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/28/90

I enjoyed two articles on Japan in your Aug. 20 issue. The first, "Guiding Hand: In Asia, the Japanese Hope to `Coordinate' What Nations Produce" by Bernard Wysocki Jr. (page one), described Japan as a lead goose leading a flock of its Asian neighbors. Equally interesting was Stephen Duthie's article on the bitter memories harbored by many Asians who still remember the harsh rule by imperial Japan of its Asian colonies before the end of World War II. Japan's metamorphosis from a ferocious wolf devouring its neighbors to a docile goose assisting them deserves an explanation. To understand Japan's past behavior, one must remember that for centuries it was a feudal country dominated by the Tokugawa regime and the Daimyo warlords. In the words of a Japanese historian, it was by "the edge of the sword" that "the virtues of hard work, thrift, self-discipline, obedience and selfless service had been instilled in the Japanese people." And even after the Meiji transformation in 1868, the right to vote was not universal.

As late as 1918, only the Japanese taxpayers who paid a tax of at least 10 yen had the right to vote. They represented just 2.6% of the population. True democracy was not introduced in Japan until the arrival of Gen. Douglas MacArthur in 1945. Once introduced, the Japanese political institutions have proved themselves to be as dynamic and vibrant as the economy itself. Currently, many Asian nations in charting their economic policies look to Japan as the model to emulate. However, when it comes to politics, many of these nations still bear the burden of totalitarian regimes. Japan should impress on its neighbors the fact that free-market economics works better in a democratic setting. This may be a tall order, given Japan's past meddling in its neighbors' affairs.

However, times have changed. The new Japan can utilize its considerable economic weight to nudge its neighbors toward representative democracy. If this would happen, then Tokyo would deserve credit for the spread of democracy in Southeast Asia, the same way the U.S. deserves credit for the spread of democracy in Japan.

Khairy Tourk

Professor of International Trade and Finance

School of Business Administration

Illinois Institute of Technology

Chicago
