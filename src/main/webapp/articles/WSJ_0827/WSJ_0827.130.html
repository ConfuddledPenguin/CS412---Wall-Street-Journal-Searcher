Chicago Trader Pleads Guilty to Felony Counts

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/27/90

CHICAGO -- One of the 16 defendants accused of trading improprieties in the yen pit of the Chicago Mercantile Exchange pleaded guilty to two felony counts in U.S. District Court here, as part of an agreement with federal prosecutors. Aric H. March, the 34-year-old defendant, hasn't been considered a pivotal figure in the alleged yen-pit scheme. But his plea-bargain -- in which he agreed to testify on behalf of the prosecution at the upcoming trial -- is the first the government has been able to obtain from the yen traders and brokers facing charges.

To bolster their case in complex, multidefendant cases, prosecutors routinely seek to strike such arrangements with some defendants. The yen trial is scheduled to begin Sept. 4, and to last for months.

Mr. March pleaded guilty to charges that in 1986, he participated in prearranged trades in the Merc's Standard & Poor's 500 trading pit, including one charge that he committed mail fraud by allowing a confirmation of one of the trades to be sent to a customer by U.S. mail. The charges associated with his yen pit activities were dropped, as part of the agreement.

The trader faces a maximum of ten years in prison, and $500,000 in fines; sentencing will take place after the yen-pit trial concludes.

Mr. March's guilty plea to trading violations in the Merc's S&P pit marks the first formal admission of criminal wrongdoing by a trader in that Merc operation. Subpoenas were issued some months ago against 30 to 50 S&P pit traders, and indictments against an unknown number are expected sometime in the future.
