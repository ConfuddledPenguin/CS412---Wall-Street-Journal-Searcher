Economy: Orders in July For Machine Tools Declined 11.9%

  • Author: Dana Milbank
  • Date: 08/27/90

Machine-tool orders placed with U.S. producers fell 11.9% in July from June levels, as exports fell off sharply. Albert W. Moore, president of the machine tool builders trade association which compiles the data, attributed the decline to summer shutdowns in manufacturing plants and model changeovers in the automobile industry.

Analysts said the decline may also reflect some weakness in the economy, particularly among smaller machine-tool customers who may now be deferring orders due to economic uncertainty. Moreover, analysts noted that prospective buyers appear to be waiting for the industry's semiannual trade show in September, when new technologies will be introduced.

The July 1990 level of $233.75 million in new orders included a 3.5% decrease in domestic orders, which fell to $213.45 million from $221.1 million in June. Overseas orders showed a 54% month-to-month decline, according to the association, falling to $20.3 million from $44.15 million in June.

On a year-to-year basis, though, total orders were up 22.5% from the $190.75 million level of July 1989.

Orders are "basically hanging in there," said McDonald & Co.'s Ronald Petrie. "It's not looking great, but it's not looking bad either."

Mr. Petrie said large system orders, such as engine-making machines, have been improving in recent months, while orders for smaller machine tools are falling off, as buyers anticipate a worsening economy. "Things that are easily deferred are being deferred," he said.

The events in the Middle East aren't likely to hurt the industry, and rising need for fuel efficiency could boost machine-tool orders, which tend to rise with oil prices.
