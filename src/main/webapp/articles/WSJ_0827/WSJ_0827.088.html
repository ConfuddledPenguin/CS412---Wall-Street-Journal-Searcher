Data General Corp. could become profitable in as little as six months as the savings from a sweeping 17% work-force reduction hit the bottom line, some analysts say.

The Westborough, Mass., minicomputer maker said Friday that it would lay off at least an additional 2,000 people and take a $70 million charge in the fiscal fourth quarter, ending Sept. 29. It's the latest move in a bold gamble to transform the troubled company into a smaller, nimbler competitor in the booming workstation business.

"This gives them a fighting chance," says Marc Schulman, an analyst at UBS Securities Inc. in New York. "Cutting this deeply, as painful as it is, is Data General's only shot at becoming profitable quickly. And that's the only way it's going to survive."

Mr. Schulman estimates quarterly savings could be more than 70 cents a share, enough to reverse recent operating losses. He sees a return to profitability in the second quarter, ending March 30, and expects fiscal 1991 to bring the first annual profit in five years.

In the latest nine months, Data General had a loss of $50.5 million, or $1.69 a share, on revenue of $907.9 million, compared with a year-earlier loss of $35.7 million, or $1.22 a share, on revenue of $957.3 million.

Data General is engaged in a delicate balancing act, trying to hold onto existing customers of its aging MV minicomputer line while increasing sales of its Aviion line of workstations and computer network servers. While the new line has been well-received and is expected to hit $100 million in sales this year, competition from larger, healthier companies has become more intense while sales of the MV are dropping. Total revenue, which hit $1.36 billion in 1988, are expected to fall to an estimated $1.23 billion this year.

Still, the company has slashed expenses so deeply that "even assuming that revenue continues to slip a little bit each quarter, Data General should be profitable again next year," says Shao F. Wang, an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. John Adams of Adams, Harkness & Hill adds that the balance sheet remains relatively strong and should provide a cushion for another year.

The cuts announced Friday will bring the payroll to fewer than 9,700, down from 11,700 on June 30 and a peak of 17,700 in 1985.

The layoffs are another blow to the state's ebbing economy and to the Route 128 region, once considered the East Coast's answer to California's booming Silicon Valley. Data General's troubles are echoed at nearby Wang Laboratories Inc. and Prime Computer Inc., which remain mired in losses. Even giant Digital Equipment Corp., the largest employer in Massachusetts and New Hampshire, has been struggling and posted its first-ever loss last month.

In consolidated New York Stock Exchange trading Friday, Data General shares closed at $6, up 25 cents.
