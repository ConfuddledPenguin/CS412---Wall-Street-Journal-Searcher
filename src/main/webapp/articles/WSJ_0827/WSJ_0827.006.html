Your Money Matters: Mortgage Front: Fixed-Rate vs. ARMs Spread Widens

  • Author: Georgette Jasen
  • Date: 08/27/90

If you're shopping for a home mortgage, don't despair. Things aren't as bleak as they seem. True, rates on conventional 30-year fixed-rate mortgages have moved up in recent weeks and may go even higher. But mortgage rates in general are still lower than they were last spring and adjustable-rate loans can be, and will probably remain, a good deal for many borrowers.

That's because fixed-rate mortgages tend to be pegged to long-term interest rates, while adjustable-rate mortgages, or ARMs, typically are pegged to short-term rates. The crisis in the Persian Gulf and fears of rising oil prices and inflation are pushing up long-term rates; at the same time, a softening economy and fears of a recession are keeping short-term rates low.

"The gap {between fixed and adjustable rates} is starting to widen again," says Harry V. Tomlinson, senior vice president for the Northeast region of Sears Mortgage Co. "We haven't seen a dramatic shift to ARMs yet . . . but we're gearing up for it." Including a recent acquisition, the Sears, Roebuck & Co. unit has about $13 billion in mortgages outstanding nationwide.

Adjustable rates have actually declined a fraction of a percentage point in the last month and are off more than a third of a percentage point since last spring, according to HSH Associates, a mortgage-information service based in Butler, N.J. The average first-year rate on a one-year ARM is currently 8.20%, according to HSH's survey of 2,000 lenders nationwide.

The average rate on a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage is 10.30%, according to HSH. That's up more than a third of a percentage point from early August. But just a little more than three months ago, in early May, lenders were charging an average 10.71%; in the spring of 1989, rates went as high as 11.33%.

At today's levels, the "spread" between fixed and adjustable rates is about two percentage points, enough to make a meaningful difference in monthly payments. On a $100,000 mortgage, for instance, the difference between 8.20% and 10.30% means a savings of roughly $150 a month for those who opt for the ARM.

A study by the U.S. League of Savings Institutions, a Chicago-based trade group, indicates that adjustable-rate mortgages have been considerably cheaper than fixed-rate loans over much of the past decade. The study, which covers the period since April 1981, found that an ARM would have cut a home buyer's interest payments by an average of 25%.

Even borrowers who opted for adjustable rates in 1987 and 1988, only to see their payments increase as interest rates rose steadily through the spring of 1989, would have saved at least $1,500 in interest on a $100,000 mortgage. The holder of an adjustable-rate mortgage originated in November 1981 could have saved more than $60,000 in interest, the study indicates. The study doesn't take into account the homeowners who refinanced their fixed-rate mortgages when rates dropped.

But there's more involved than monthly payments in choosing between adjustable and fixed rates. Borrowers have to "consider the level of risk they're willing to assume, financially and emotionally," says Mr. Tomlinson of Sears.

The interest rate on an ARM generally is adjusted every six months, annually or once every three years, depending on the index it is tied to. Lenders in the East and Midwest tend to favor an index based on Treasury securities, often the six-month or one-year Treasury bill, while a monthly cost-of-funds index issued by the Federal Home Loan Bank of San Francisco is more common in western states.

Typically, the rate on an ARM can rise or fall two percentage points a year and five or six percentage points over the life of the loan. While three-year adjustables often are considered more desirable because they are more predictable, lenders say they expect ARMs with shorter adjustment periods to become more popular because they are tied to lower, shorter-term interest rates.

Financial advisers say that before agreeing to an adjustable rate, borrowers should know the index used, the margin charged over the index, the frequency of adjustments, the periodic and lifetime caps, and whether there's the possibility of "negative amortization." That's when payment increases are limited so a rise in rates may mean that the monthly payment isn't sufficient to cover both principal and interest actually being charged. The difference is added to the balance of the loan, and the borrower can end up owing more than the original mortgage.

All that uncertainty leads many people to prefer fixed-rate mortgages. Only about a quarter to a third of mortgages currently being written are ARMs. They were more than half during most of the period from late 1987 through mid-1989. Lenders say ARMs' market share starts to increase significantly when fixed rates get to the 11% to 12% range.

They say the outlook for rates in the next few months depends to a large extent on events in the Persian Gulf. If hostilities escalate, long-term rates could soar. If there's a peaceful settlement, rates could resume their slow decline that began last spring. "For now, it's a real tough call" between adjustable and fixed-rate mortgages, says Dennis Jacobe, research director for the U.S. League of Savings Institutions.

Rather than lose sleep, it may help to remember the high double-digit rates of the early 1980s. Current rates are "not unattractive from a longer-term perspective," says Richard Peach, deputy chief economist for the Mortgage Bankers Association. "Compared with a year ago, certainly the last 10 years, they look fairly good."
