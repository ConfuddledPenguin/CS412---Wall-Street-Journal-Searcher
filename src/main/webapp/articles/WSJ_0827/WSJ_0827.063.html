USAir to Cut 2,100 Positions In New Layoff

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani
  • Date: 08/27/90

ARLINGTON, Va. -- USAir Group Inc. said it would dismiss by year end another 2,100 employees, many of them managers, in the deepest cut yet of its payroll and one of the largest layoffs by an airline in years. The action brings to 3,600 people the number of employees that USAir has fired or furloughed in recent days. The airline holding company blamed the layoffs, which affect 7% of its 54,000 employees, on "softening of passenger demand, poor economic conditions, escalating fuel costs and the company's need to reduce operating expenses."

The cuts also amount to a mid-course correction of ambitious expansion plans at USAir. USAir said it slashed its 1991 projected capacity growth rate to a maximum of 2% from 6%.

The layoffs at the nation's No. 6 air carrier are much deeper than Wall Street analysts expected and are sure to send a chill through the airline industry. Most of the industry's massive personnel cuts in recent years have come at financially troubled carriers such as Continental Airlines and Eastern Airlines, both units of Continental Airlines Holding Inc. But USAir has traditionally been one of the country's most profitable carriers.

To be sure, USAir has been posting losses since it completed its merger with Piedmont Aviation Inc. last August. In the first half of 1990, USAir has piled up net losses of $113 million.

"The cushion is gone, and they're facing a difficult, uncertain future," said Edmund Greenslet, an airline consultant at ESG Aviation Services. "They can't sit around and wait."

Already, other airlines have been hunkering down in expectation of waning demand resulting from a recession. Midway Airlines, a small Chicago-based carrier, started laying off some employees in May. AMR Corp.'s American Airlines Chairman Robert Crandall has issued an edict to cut costs in his company.

The USAir retrenchment, including Friday's move, affects 500 managers, 211 pilots, 650 flight attendants and 2,100 reservations, customer service and fleet service agents. Some other employees would also be affected.

USAir didn't say how much it expected to save as a result of the layoffs. But it has been clear for some time that the company had fat to trim from its operating expenses. USAir's labor costs have been among the highest in an industry where payroll is the biggest expense. Its merger with Piedmont increased those costs.

USAir indicated that the layoffs might be expensive in the short run. It said it is "reviewing" early retirement and voluntary separation programs, which typically involve one-time payments to employees as compensation for leaving their jobs.

USAir announced the latest move after the close of trading Friday. In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, USAir stock closed at $14.375 a share, up 12.5 cents.
