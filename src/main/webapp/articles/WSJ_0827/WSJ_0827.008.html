Economy -- Tracking the Economy: Hot-and-Cold Pattern of Economic Data Is Likely to Continue During This Week

  • Author: Lindley H. Clark Jr.
  • Date: 08/27/90

NEW YORK -- One fact that makes the current weak economy so hard to forecast is that it continues to show occasional signs of strength. On Thursday, for example, the Commerce Department reported that durable goods orders in July, on a seasonally adjusted basis, rose a strong 2.9%, led by orders for commercial aircraft.

This week is likely to see a continuation of the economy's up-and-down pattern. Today, for instance, analysts expect the Commerce Department to report that personal income in July rose by about 0.5%, slightly faster than the 0.4% gain in June. The main factor, according to Stephen S. Roach, senior economist at Morgan Stanley & Co., was an acceleration of wage earnings.

That may seem paradoxical in view of the fact the unemployment rate in July jumped to 5.5%, the highest level in more than two years, from 5.2%. But Mr. Roach says that 40% of the rise was among teenagers, who make up only 6% of the labor force and aren't the major contributors to income growth.

Another report today, however, is expected to show that personal consumption in July rose by 0.5%, down from a 1.0% gain in June. Nancy Lazar, an economist at C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc., figures last month's spending pattern was highly uneven: a 1.5% rise in outlays on durable goods other than automobiles and a 0.8% upturn in services, both partly offset by a 1.5% drop in autos, with no change in spending on nondurable goods.

One of the statistics that is usually fairly easy to predict is the Commerce Department's index of leading economic indicators. The index is designed to move up or down in advance of the general economy, but several of its components are known before the index itself comes out.

The performance of the index's components in July, analysts say, was almost entirely negative. C.J. Lawrence analysts offer the following estimates for various index statistics: stock prices, down 0.1%; inflation-adjusted M2 money supply, down 0.3%; manufacturing hours worked, down 0.2%; vendor performance, down 7.9%; and consumer sentiment, down 0.1%. These declines, C.J. Lawrence says, more than offset a 0.2% gain in sensitive materials prices and a 0.8% drop in initial unemployment claims, resulting in a projected 0.2% drop in the index. The index will be announced Wednesday.

Also on Wednesday, new home sales for July will be reported. This statistic has been one of the few bright spots in the housing industry, showing gains of 2.3% in May and a strong 8.0% in June. Despite the fact that adjustable rates on mortgages were still relatively low in July, analysts on the average say that new home sales probably fell about 5%, joining the other gloomy housing numbers.

Manufacturing orders for July, which will be available Thursday, are expected to be one of the week's bright spots, rising about 1%. Mr. Roach of Morgan Stanley, however, predicts that shipments will decline slightly after two months of solid gains and that inventories also will show a modest decrease.

After months of something close to a recession, the manufacturing sector has begun to look a little better. Employment and inventories have been reduced sharply, making it easier for companies to benefit from any upturn in demand.

The chief upturn so far has been in exports. In June exports reached $34.3 billion, a record high. Together with a small decline in imports, this reduced the merchandise trade deficit to $5.1 billion, the narrowest since December 1983.

Manufacturers, however, are remaining cautious. One positive factor for exports has been the decline in the value of the dollar. In a crisis such as the current Mideast situation, the dollar usually rises as investors seek what they regard as a haven. It hasn't happened this time, for reasons that analysts don't fully understand.

In the past the Federal Reserve has often stepped in to tighten money and shore up a sagging dollar. At the moment the Fed appears to be holding policy steady, and few economists expect any early move toward tighter money. The hope among analysts is that the Fed instead will at some point resume its cautious move toward easier money.

That move came with quarter-point reductions in the federal funds rate, the fee charged on short-term loans of bank reserves. Until recently these moves weren't reflected in significant increases in M2, the most commonly watched money supply, consisting of currency and most bank deposits.

In early August, however, M2 began turning upward. Its growth is still slow, but it may persuade the Fed that its earlier federal funds moves are having some effect.
