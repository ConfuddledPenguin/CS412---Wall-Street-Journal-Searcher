Trade Commission Asked to Reconsider Ruling on Canadian Pork Exports to U.S.

  • Author: Rose Tamburri
  • Date: 08/27/90

OTTAWA -- A U.S.-Canada trade panel asked the U.S. International Trade Commission to reconsider its earlier ruling which found that subsidized Canadian pork exports to the U.S. threatened to injure U.S. pig producers. The panel said the ITC's finding relied heavily on "questionable" statistics of increased Canadian pork production.

The ITC ruled last year that subsidized exports of Canadian fresh, chilled and frozen pork threatened to injure U.S. pig producers and meat packers. As a result of the ruling, the U.S. put in place countervailing duties of 3.6 Canadian cents per pound of processed pork.

The U.S.-Canada panel said the appearance of a big increase in Canadian pork production in 1988 was caused by a change in the method of counting and reporting pork production by the Canadian government. It said that Canadian pork production grew 8.4% in 1988 from 1986 and not 31% as thought by the ITC. The panel noted that the 8.4% increase was less than U.S. production growth in the same period.

Martin Rice, executive secretary of the Canadian Pork Council, said pork exports to the U.S. are expected to decline this year to about $300 million Canadian dollars (US$265.8 million) from about C$400 million in 1989 because of falling production. Canadian pork exports currently make up less than 3% of the U.S. market.

Mr. Rice said he was "encouraged" by the panel's decision because, he said, it could lead to the removal of countervailing duties on Canadian pork exports and boost Canadian production.

The panel instructed the ITC to reconsider the impact of Canadian subsidies on U.S. pork production taking the smaller production statistics into consideration. The ITC has 60 days to respond to the panel's ruling.

The trade panel was established under the dispute settlement provisions of the U.S.-Canada free-trade agreement which went into effect Jan. 1, 1989. The panel included three Canadian and two U.S. members.

The U.S. government investigation of Canadian pork exports originated from complaints filed by the U.S. National Pork Producers Council which had argued that Canadian packers benefited from Canadian government subsidies to hog farmers.

The governments of Canada, Alberta, Ontario and Quebec and several Canadian industry groups have requested a separate U.S.-Canada panel to review the method used by the U.S. to determine the level of subsidy to hog farmers.

The Canadian government also has requested a review of the subsidy issue by a panel of the General Agreement on Tarrifs and Trade, a Geneva organization that regulates trade between most non-socialist countries. According to news reports, the GATT panel has found that the U.S. violated GATT rules in imposing countervailing duties on imports of processed pork to offset government subsidies to pig farmers.

A meeting of the full GATT council is expected to be held Oct. 3 where it will decide to accept or reject the panel's decision.
