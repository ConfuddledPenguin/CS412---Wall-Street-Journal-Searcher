Letters to the Editor: Free Trade Sacrifices Textile Workers

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/27/90

"The Jesse and Pat Bill" (editorial, July 31) distorted the real reasons why an overwhelming majority of the Senate voted for the Textile, Apparel and Footwear Trade Act. We did so in order to preserve a vital manufacturing industry and the jobs of more than 1.8 million of the world's most productive workers. Sen. Pat Moynihan (D., N.Y.) and the other 67 senators who voted for this bill realize that textile, apparel and footwear workers, a majority of whom are minorities and women, must compete against low wages and, in the case of the People's Republic of China, slave labor.

You accuse Sen. Jesse Helms, (R., N.C.) of being against competition and self-reliance when it comes to preserving the textile industry. In fact, Sen. Helms knows that no manufacturing industry has risen to meet the competitive challenge as aggressively as the textile, apparel and trade industry. Over the course of the past decade, the industry invested $18 billion in modernizing plants and equipment. The MIT Commission on Industrial Productivity found that "between 1975 and 1985, textile mill productivity increased an average of 5.6% a year, more than any other manufacturing industry." Yet, despite this massive restructuring, the industry has steadily lost market share, overwhelmed by a flood of imports from low-wage countries.

Consumers do not benefit from lower-priced imports. Glance through some mail-order catalogs and you'll see that consumers pay exactly the same price for clothing, whether it is U.S.-made or imported.

This doesn't seem to weigh on the conscience of The Wall Street Journal, however. We are moving from the Cold War into a trade war; as geopolitics is replaced by geoeconomics, the distinction between national security and economic security becomes blurred. While our competitors pursue market share and trample trade agreements, the Journal supports a trade policy that can only be characterized as appeasement and unilateral disarmament.

In passing the textile bill, the Senate showed that it was not willing to stand idly by while industry by industry is sacrificed on the altar of free trade. The only thing this bill protects is the 60% U.S. market share of foreign producers. It allows that market share to grow along with the growth of the U.S. market.

Sen. Ernest F. Hollings (D., S.C.)

Washington
