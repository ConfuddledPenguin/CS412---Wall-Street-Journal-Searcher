Chrysler Seeks Concessions, Ford Offers More Job Security in Contract Proposals

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 08/27/90

DETROIT -- As the United Auto Workers union prepares to select a "target" company for negotiating a new three-year contract, Ford Motor Co. and Chrysler Corp. put sharply different offers on the table. Ford offered to negotiate improved job security and to boost pensions for its UAW workers. Meanwhile, Chrysler asked its UAW-represented employees to accept substantial concessions in benefits. The offer virtually ensures that the union will not select Chrysler to set the pattern for its negotiations with the Big Three auto makers. Among the offers submitted, General Motors Corp. was the only one to propose a base wage increase.

Ford's offer includes unspecified lump-sum payments, but no base wage increases. The auto maker also wants the union to allow it to offer incentives to employees to move into health maintenance organizations or preferred provider plans.

Chrysler made its initial offer to the UAW Friday, while Ford and GM submitted their proposals Thursday. The UAW is bargaining separately with each of them, and is set to select by tomorrow one of the three as its target for negotiating a pattern-setting pact. The union will work to reach agreement with the target company by Sept. 14, when contracts with all three companies expire. After reaching agreement, the union will then press the two other companies to agree to virtually identical terms.

Traditionally, the UAW chooses as its target the company that best suits its objectives and strategies for the negotiations. The union has said that obtaining job security for its 450,000 members who work for the Big Three auto makers will be its top objective in talks this year. It's been widely speculated that GM will be the target this year because the company has nearly twice as many UAW members as Ford and Chrysler combined, and the No. 1 auto maker has continually crossed swords with the UAW over job security. Perhaps more important, the union's strongest personality is Stephen Yokich, who heads the union's GM department.

GM's offer last week included increased base wages, and expanded benefits for workers it lays off, but didn't include broader job guarantees for UAW members. In contrast, Ford said it is willing to tackle the issue of job security, but didn't provide any definitive proposal.

Chrysler's offer drew sharp criticism from UAW officials because, unlike the offers of GM and Ford, Chrysler's offer seeks "givebacks." The No. 3 auto maker asked UAW bargainers to move their workers out of their traditional Blue Cross and Blue Shield healthcare coverage and into health maintenance organizations and preferred provider plans.

The auto maker also asked the union to drop the requirement that Chrysler hire one worker for every two it loses through attrition. Stan Marshall, the UAW's top Chrysler bargainer, signaled his disappointment by walking out of the bargaining room without discussion almost immediately after Chrysler presented the offer.

Chrysler's offer "more or less left (Mr. Marshall) speechless," a UAW official said.
