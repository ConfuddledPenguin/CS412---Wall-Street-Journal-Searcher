North Star Ordered To Report Monthly On Its Finances

  • Author: Richard Gibson
  • Date: 08/27/90

ST. PAUL, Minn. -- Minnesota authorities, concerned about North Star Universal Inc.'s debt load, are requiring the company to report its financial health to the state monthly. State Commerce Commissioner Thomas H. Borman said the requirement follows an investigation by his department into North Star's sale of subordinated debentures in Minnesota, a primary source of capital for the Minneapolis-based holding company.

Questions had been raised in the media about North Star's ability to service that unsecured debt, which exceeded $36 million as of last Dec. 31. All told, North Star had long-term debt of $114 million at the end of 1989, compared with $50.8 million a year earlier.

Mr. Borman also said North Star must maintain its majority control of Michael Foods Inc., although "we would have no problem if they sell the stock and raise enough capital to improve their liquidity situation or retire the debt. We want to make sure they maintain control because the dividend Michael pays to North Star is an important element in servicing that debt."

The Michael shares are pledged as collateral to First National Bank of Minneapolis, North Star's primary lender. The bank asked for the collateral after North Star technically defaulted on its previous credit line.

North Star officials recently indicated they were considering selling all or part of its 53.9% stake in Michael. Last year Michael accounted for 74% of North Star's revenue and 80% of consolidated subsidiary operating profits. North Star reported a loss of $2.3 million, or 28 cents a share, last year.

Under the agreement with the state, North Star must maintain a .91:1 ratio of debt to cash and cash equivalents, the commissioner said. The debt excludes salaries, wages and current mortgage payments. The cash equivalents include the Michael stock.

Mr. Borman said his investigators concluded that North Star currently has a "substantial liquidity cushion" because of the value of its Michael stock. But he added that if Michael shares fall below $12 a share, "there would be a problem" and "we would have to get involved again."

Should that occur, Mr. Borman said his office would "sit down with the company and determine levels of liquid assets." He added that North Star could improve both sides of its balance sheet with certain asset sales. The company has retained Goldman, Sachs to help explore restructuring options.

Mr. Borman said that should North Star not be in compliance with the ratio, he has the authority to revoke its right to issue debt within the state.

North Star officials couldn't be reached for comment.

Since 1987, when Michael Foods was formed, North Star says it has experienced cash-flow deficits, resulting in increased bank borrowings. But North Star has financed a substantial portion of its non-food units through debenture programs, which it markets itself. "Historically, a substantial portion of maturing debentures has been reinvested in new debentures. The company expects this trend to continue," its latest annual report said.
