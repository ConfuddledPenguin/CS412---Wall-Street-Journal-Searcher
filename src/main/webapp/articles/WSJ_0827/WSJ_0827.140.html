Heard on the Street: `New' Fund American's Portfolio Makes Its Stock an Oil-Patch Play

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 08/27/90

NEW YORK -- There's a gusher of an oil play inside Fund American Cos., better known as the parent of Fireman's Fund Insurance. Fund American, which was called Fireman's Fund Corp. until last December, agreed a few weeks ago to sell its insurance operations to the West German insurer Allianz for $3.3 billion. Fund American, based in Greenwich, Conn., will remain a publicly traded company, and its first move will be to buy back its former insurance unit's stockholdings for $2.2 billion, which was the market value of the portfolio on May 31.

In effect, Fund American will be transformed into a publicly traded "closed-end" investment company. Its value will depend, more than anything else, on its stock portfolio, not on the insurance business.

As it happens, this stock portfolio is positioned squarely in the oil patch, thanks to money manager Bob Bruce's one-time hunch that America's thirst for oil and the Mideast's volatile politics would someday combine to drive energy prices back up. About 66% of the portfolio is invested in energy-related issues. These include big stakes in Freeport-McMoRan, Louisiana Land & Exploration and British Petroleum.

The Allianz agreement initially drove Fund American's stock price to a new high of 51 3/4, prompting many Wall Street insurance analysts to advise clients to unload their shares. The stock has since declined to 46 3/4. Now analysts say Fund American is beginning to look attractive, because its shares are trading at a significant discount to the value of the oil-rich fistful of stocks in the portfolio.

If the insurance unit had been sold as of last Monday, says Ira Malis at Alex. Brown & Sons in Baltimore, the remaining company would have had a net asset value of roughly $65.15 a share. Based on his estimate, Fund American shares last Friday were selling at a 28% discount to the company's worth, known in Wall Street jargon as the net asset value. Fund American will say only that the transaction should cause its book value to exceed $60 a share, based on its financial position as of last May 31.

It is common for a closed-end fund to trade at a discount to the value of the stocks it holds, especially after brokers stop pushing a fund's initial offering. But a 29% discount is unusually big.

Closed-end funds that invest chiefly in U.S. companies are trading at about a 15% discount to their net asset value, according to Richard Barone of Maxus Investment Group, Cleveland, who follows closed-end funds. In the past 20 years, he says, trading discounts for closed-end funds have ranged from about 25% in the mid-1970s to about 10% just before the October 1987 stock market crash. Based on these historical ranges, investors could reasonably expect Fund American shares to close some of the gap.

Moreover, the portfolio value itself could increase if oil prices -- and energy stocks -- resume the sharp rise that stalled last week. Mr. Malis of Alex. Brown calculates that the value of Fund American's portfolio increased about $130 million, or 6.4%, from June 30 to last Monday, a period in which the overall stock market, as measured by the Standard & Poor's 500 index, fell 8.2%.

A number of factors, however, could diminish Fund American's longer-term value. The resulting uncertainty accounts for at least some of the discount built into the stock's price, analysts say.

For one thing, there is the timing of Allianz's purchase of Fireman's Fund. The sale is expected to close early next year, but Fund American shareholders and insurance regulators in 13 states must first approve the deal. There shouldn't be much trouble in obtaining the approvals, but such procedures often are subject to delay. Fund American shareholders aren't expected to receive proxy materials explaining the transaction in detail until October.

Fund American has said that, after the sale is completed, it plans to pay out more than $800 million to holders of common shares, either through a self-tender offer or a cash distribution. This prospect raises another uncertainty. Tax-paying shareholders, for example, might prefer a stock buy-back that would increase the market value of their shares without forcing them to accept taxable income. But tax-exempt holders, such as pension funds, might prefer a cash distribution that they could reinvest. (The rest of Fund American's net cash proceeds of $1.1 billion from the sale will be used to redeem a closely held preferred stock issue for $434 million.)

Afterward, Fund American plans to liquidate its remaining assets "in an orderly fashion" over the next three to five years. Or, in the words of Chairman Jack Byrne, "We're going to be a melting ice cube."

But before drinking deeply of that cool water, investors should know about a few impurities. One, coincidentally, is known as liquidity. Fund American's portfolio has several big positions in certain energy stocks and partnerships that don't trade very actively and could be tough to sell without depressing the price.

Mr. Malis cites Permian Basin Royalty Trust as an example. Fund American holds 1.3 million shares, but on Friday, only 11,700 traded, a typical daily volume. Another big holding is a 24% stake in MBIA, a municipal-bond insurance company owned mostly by other big insurers. Only about 15% of MBIA's shares are in public hands.

"How do they get out of some of these large, illiquid assets?" Mr. Malis says.

Executives of Fund American won't discuss their plans until after the proxy materials are filed. But the company has said it is considering, among other things, block sales of securities, distribution of assets, formation of partnerships or formation of a registered investment company.

Capital gains taxes could complicate the liquidation. Fund American has said that it expects to pay "little or no income taxes" on the sale of Fireman's Fund, but hasn't indicated how the gains in its stock portfolio will be handled.

Finally, there is the business that analysts view as the real clunker in Fund American's portfolio, Fireman's Fund Mortgage Corp. A mortgage banking company that Fund American bought from Manufacturers Hanover Trust in 1986 for $260 million, the unit now is valued at about $250 million. Though the mortgage unit earned only about $7 million last year, Fund American has led analysts to believe that it will earn $12 million to $13 million this year.

In announcing the sale of Fireman's Fund to Allianz, Fund American said it would consider future acquisitions of "financial service companies to augment the activities of the mortgage company." Skeptics believe that this statement, and the prospect that Fund American may try to expand its way out of a blunder into the mortgage business, is holding down the stock's price.

On Wall Street, Chairman Byrne is given high marks for astute management and a tight focus on delivering value to shareholders, though at least one of his decisions has proved unfortunate. The company's name change from Fireman's Fund Corp. to Fund American last December occurred just a few months before a discount-buying club named FundAmerica Inc. burst into the news. The club, whose founder was arrested on fraud charges, hired the well-known doomsday investment strategist Howard Ruff and then entered bankruptcy-law proceedings. Fund American officials now find themselves continually reassuring investors that the company isn't related in any way to the alleged pyramid scheme.
