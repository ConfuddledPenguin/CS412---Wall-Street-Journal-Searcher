This Time, Pentagon Procurement Shows Rapid Deployment --- But Quickly Ordered Outfits For Chemical Warfare Won't Be Desert Brown

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 08/27/90

WASHINGTON -- When the Pentagon called up the reserves, it bought them some new clothes, too. If the reservists are lucky, they will never have to wear them. The Defense Logistics Agency said on Friday that it ordered 450,000 chemical-warfare suits from two privately held clothing makers. Gibralter, Puerto Rico Inc., based in San Lorenzo, Puerto Rico, won a $19.3 million award to make 250,000 of the outfits; Sidran Inc. in Dallas got a $15.5 million award for 200,000.

For the Pentagon, this was a rapid-deployment procurement. Seth Maxwell, Gibralter's senior vice president, said the logistics agency put out the bid, made a decision and awarded the contract in about six days. The last time the U.S. bought chemical warfare garb -- 1.8 million suits in March 1989 -- it took the government a year to award the contracts.

The Pentagon's haste was evident in their choice of color -- woodland green, instead of desert brown. "It would have taken a while to get the fabric" in the desert colors, Mr. Maxwell said.

The two companies are supplying chemical warfare coats and pants at a cost of about $77 an outfit. (A full chemical warfare outfit includes a gas mask and boot coverings, too.)

"That's not so expensive" compared to ordinary trousers and coats, says Mr. Maxwell, "and they're considerably more useful."

Each outfit is made of a cotton-nylon fabric lined with activated charcoal, which can protect a soldier for four hours from microbes that kill, he says. After that, the soldier must change clothes because the charcoal gets saturated. Clothing industry talk suggests the Pentagon figures it needs four uniforms for each soldier, but the Defense Department won't say.

Making the uniforms is messy work. Sewing-machine operators get covered with soot from the charcoal, Mr. Maxwell says, and often quit. Even so, he promises to start churning out about 25,000 suits a month by about Thanksgiving.
