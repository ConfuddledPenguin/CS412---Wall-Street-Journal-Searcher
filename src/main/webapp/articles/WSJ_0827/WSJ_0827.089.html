No ifs, ands or Butts, Soviets Are Fuming Over Cigarette Crisis --- Authorities Seek to Reassure Angry Public, Promising Relief From Shortage

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/27/90

MOSCOW -- Soviet authorities sought to reassure smokers that there will be enough tobacco in the days to come, following increased public outrage over the worst cigarette shortage in decades. State-run newspapers reported that a Moscow factory had produced 45 million cigarettes in 24 hours over the weekend, that a special command post was ensuring the packs made it to tobacco kiosks instead of the hands of black marketeers, and that authorities were cracking down on the black market speculators.

Prime Minister Nikolai Ryzhkov tried to calm the Soviet Union's 70 million smokers on national television by similarly pledging that more cigarettes -- both domestic and imported -- were on the way.

But Mr. Ryzhkov dodged responsibility for the crisis, saying the shortage wasn't the fault of the central government, but of the leadership of the individual Soviet republics. He also blamed panic-buying by the public.

Beneath a banner headline pleading "Government, give us a smoke]" the Komsomolskaya Pravda newspaper devoted its entire front page to the crisis, chronicling the various "tobacco rebellions" around the Soviet Union since July. The newspaper reported that a pack of American Marlboro cigarettes would bring up to 20 rubles ($32) on the black market in Moscow.

The actions underscored how seriously national and local authorities are taking the summer-long cigarette shortage, especially in light of street demonstrations by angry smokers that have been increasing in Moscow and Leningrad recently. On Wednesday, scores of people blocked a street next to the Kremlin until a nearby tobacco kiosk was restocked.

The newspaper Moskovskaya Pravda reported that Muscovites will be getting ration coupons for tobacco products as a temporary, two- to three-month measure in order to combat hoarding and black market speculation.

The newspaper said that one black marketeer killed a buyer over a pack of cigarettes in Moscow, but it provided no other details.

Komsomolskaya Pravda said that in the central Siberian city of Barnaul, one brand of Soviet cigarettes was being sold for 7 rubles on the black market. Soviet cigarettes usually sell for about 45 kopecks per pack.
