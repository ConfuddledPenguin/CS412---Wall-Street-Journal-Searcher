Italy's Inflation Rate Grows, Erasing Recent Progress; Gulf Crisis Is Factor

  • Author: Guy Collins
  • Date: 08/27/90

MILAN, Italy -- The Persian Gulf crisis has fueled a rapid surge in Italy's inflation, wiping out progress made over the past six months, pushing up interest rates and adding to the government's woes as it attempts to bring the runaway budget deficit under control. Consumer prices rose between 6.2% and 6.3% this month from a year earlier, sharply higher than July's 5.7% consumer-price increase and the worst performance since February. The August projection is based on early data from major Italian cities. The official inflation figure is due out at the end of the month.

Higher prices for gasoline and other oil products were largely to blame for the jump, but the mid-month figures don't yet reflect the full rise in oil prices since the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, officials said. Apart from the shock effect of oil-price increases, inflation also was boosted by a 20% rise in national newspaper prices from the start of August.

Analysts had expected inflation to accelerate this month, not only because of the Gulf crisis but also because of last week's news of a surge in annual wholesale prices, to 5.5% in June from 3.9% in May.

On a month-to-month basis, consumer prices in major cities grew between 0.6% and 0.7% in August from 0.4% in July.

Italy, which relies on imports for about 80% of its energy needs, is particularly vulnerable to the Gulf tension; it buys around 35% of its crude oil from the region. With spot crude prices now around $30 a barrel and prospects fading for a peaceful solution to the Gulf crisis, Italian bankers and business leaders are resigning themselves to a harsher economic environment over the coming months.

One early result could be higher interest rates. As recently as last month Bank of Italy Governor Carlo A. Ciampi predicted that inflation would fall to around 5% by the end of this year from 6.5% at the end of 1989. In May, the Treasury felt confident enough to cut the official discount rate by a percentage point, to 12.5%, and banks in turn lowered their commercial lending rates.

Now speculation is growing in the financial community here that banks will raise rates at least half a percentage point to take into account the gloomier inflation outlook -- possibly within the next couple of weeks.

The lira's slide within the European Monetary System over the past month, particularly against the mark, has given Italian monetary authorities more room for maneuver should they wish to tighten rates.

At the start of this month the mark fell as low as 731.78 lire, fractionally above its EMS floor of 731.57 lire, as gloom over the mounting costs of German unification hit the currency. But with the dollar weakening sharply and focus now switched to the Gulf, the mark has gained ground rapidly in recent days. It climbed to 747 lire Thursday in Milan, only slightly below its EMS mid-rate of 748.217 lire.

Yields on fixed-rate government bonds already have risen on expectations of higher inflation and events in the Mideast. The net yield on 12.5% four-year Treasury paper is currently above 12%, compared with 11.5% barely two months ago.

Higher interest rates are bad news for Treasury Minister Guido Carli, however, as he prepares the ground for the 1991 budget, scheduled for Cabinet approval by the end of September.

His task is to try to cut the budget deficit to around L115 trillion ($99 billion) next year from this year's L135 trillion ($116 billion). Mr. Carli has already warned that spending cuts will be painful. But it is far from clear where savings will be found, and the government can ill afford to pay higher interest rates on its rising stock of debt.

Mr. Carli met with Budget Minister Paolo Cirino Pomicino and Health Minister Francesco De Lorenzo Thursday to discuss possible cuts in health spending, one of the largest and least-efficient items in the state budget. Since the Gulf crisis reduces the likelihood of cuts in defense spending, Mr. Carli will be turning up the pressure in other ministries to come up with concrete proposals for big savings.
