TURKEY'S KURDISH STRATEGY

In shaping their policies on the Gulf crisis, officials in Ankara may have been thinking ahead to Turkey's problems with the nation's large Kurdish minority.

While Turkey has refrained from military involvement, it has cut off two Iraqi oil pipelines across its territory, imposed a trade ban and prohibited neighboring Iraq from using Turkish roads or airspace to transport goods.

Official sources and commentators suggest Turkey is taking an active role in the crisis now so it will be listened to later. The reasoning goes this way:

The U.S. appears determined to bring down Iraq's Saddam Hussein and make the Persian Gulf oil fields secure. This will probably lead to war and, eventually, a peace conference. And Turkey would like to sit at the conference table to make sure that its national interests aren't jeopardized and that an independent Kurdistan doesn't emerge.

An estimated 20 million Kurds live in an area where the borders of Turkey, the Soviet Union, Iran, Iraq and Syria converge. Turkey has the largest group, an estimated 10 million out of its total population of 55 million. Several thousand guerrillas have been fighting since 1984 for an independent Kurdish state in southeastern Turkey.

HAVE FOOD, WON'T TRAVEL

Finding many doors on the European continent closed to them, big British food-retailing chains such as Sainsbury, Tesco, Gateway, Argyll and Asda are storming the weaker gates of the U.K.'s own corner grocers and independent supermarkets. Instead of pushing their shopping carts across borders, the British groups are stocking up on U.K. sites for "superstores." The new-generation supermarkets, made appealing with high-margin fresh produce, are proving more profitable than operating comparable big stores on the continent, analysts say.

FAST TRACK

West Germany's federal railroad system expects to spend 40 billion marks ($25.9 billion) in the next 10 years on new plant and equipment, including a high-speed train network. The amount doesn't include the costly upgrading of East Germany's timeworn rail system.

`BABY' GETS A GIFT

Ninety years after it officially became a commonwealth, Australia has its "birth certificate." British Deputy Prime Minister Sir Geoffrey Howe handed over to Prime Minister Bob Hawke one of the two originals of the legislation, passed by the British Parliament on July 5, 1900, that turned six colonies into the Commonwealth of Australia.

CAST OF CHARACTERS

Teams at the Asian Games in Beijing will march into the opening ceremony Sept. 22 in order of Chinese characters, organizers said. The nation with the name that, in Chinese characters, has the smallest number of strokes will march first while the country with the most strokes will be at the end of the line.

WATCHDOG BITES

Hong Kong's watchdog Securities and Futures Commission said it fined Y & C Property Investment Co. 15,000 Hong Kong dollars (US$1,930) for issuing unauthorized advertisements related to investments designed to help people secure the right to emigrate to Canada. Hundreds of thousands of Hong Kong residents are seeking second passports before the British colony reverts to Chinese hands in 1997. Many countries make immigration easier for those willing to invest money, a factor that has spawned a sizable local industry touting such plans.

BLEAK BULGARIA

Nine months after ousting its hard-line Communist leaders, Bulgaria's political future is uncertain. Its economy is in crisis and its population is frightened by the prospect of a winter of starvation.

Shops and gas stations are empty, foreign investors are deterred by huge foreign debt, and the new, freely elected parliament can't agree on what kind of government will run the country.

An increasing number of Bulgarians, frustrated by empty stores and worried by the prospect of chronic shortages during the harsh Balkan winter, are lining up outside consulates for emigrant visas.

"This country is really on the ropes," one Western diplomat said. "People are getting out for whatever reason they can -- and I wouldn't want to be here this winter if I were Bulgarian."

Like many of its East European neighbors, Bulgaria last year ended the Communist monopoly on power. But while other countries in Eastern Europe voted the Communists out of power, in Bulgaria the party renamed itself Socialist and swept to victory at free elections in June.
