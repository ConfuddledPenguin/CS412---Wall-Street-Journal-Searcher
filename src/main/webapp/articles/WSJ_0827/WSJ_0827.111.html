Foreign Exchange: Dollar's Prospects Are Expected To Brighten in the Long Term

  • Author: Alison Woods and Candace Cumberbatch Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/27/90

NEW YORK -- There are glimmers of hope for the dollar's long-term health, even though short-run prospects are decidedly bearish. The long-term picture is brighter because the U.S. merchandise trade deficit is expected to shrink further, helping to ease worries about the economy, some analysts say.

Moreover, they contend, the dollar may fare better if the mark faces tough times as German political union gets under way in the fall and if the yen keeps suffering from Japan's troubled financial markets and stubborn inflation.

Those factors are seen breathing some life into a currency that's been pummeled by concerns about the weak U.S. economy and forecasts for further Federal Reserve easing.

Although the dollar rose on flight-to-quality buying Friday as the Iraq-Kuwait crisis continued, its track record for the week was worrisome.

In late trading here Friday, the dollar was quoted at 1.5605 marks and 146.70 yen, up from 1.5444 marks and 146.15 yen late Thursday. But the dollar hit new lows of 1.54 marks and 1.2553 Swiss francs Thursday, and the pound traded as high as $1.9590 that day, the highest level in almost nine years.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the dollar was quoted at 145.30 yen, down from 145.70 yen at the close there Friday. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.5495 marks, up from Friday's Tokyo close of 1.5466 marks.

In the long term, the dollar's fortunes could improve if the U.S. trade gap keeps narrowing, some currency specialists say.

While trade data haven't significantly affected the dollar in the last few years, some market participants are now latching on to the figures, calling them the dollar's potential life preserver.

The trade gap narrowed to $5.07 billion in June from May's $7.77 billion, contracting to a seven-year low as exports rose to a record level and imports fell substantially.

The trade deficit totaled $45.81 billion through June, a substantial reduction from the $54.36 billion gap a year earlier.

The dollar's weakness is expected to keep exports on track by making U.S. goods cheaper for foreigners, fueling demand.

But imports aren't expected to decline much in the near term because higher oil prices are seen offsetting any declines that may be caused by sluggish consumer demand.

In all, though, economists are generally encouraged by the trade picture.

"On the trade front, you can be optimistic" and have some hope for the dollar, says Michael Moran, chief economist at Daiwa Securities America Inc. "We could see substantial improvement {in the trade picture} and I would think that would be good for the dollar."

Carl Weinberg, chief economist at High Frequency Economics Ltd., agrees. "I see a couple of constructive things happening for the dollar. One is a long-term improvement in the trade balance," he says.

The trade picture aside, some currency analysts say the dollar has the potential to strengthen a little against the mark and the yen in coming months.

In dollar-mark dealings, the dollar's fortunes could turn if any rough spots in German unification hurt the mark, some analysts say.

West Germany's economy could suffer if unification causes higher taxes, greater unemployment and any further plant shutdowns, says Earl Johnson, a foreign-exchange adviser at Harris Trust & Savings Bank in Chicago.

"That's the kind of scenario that could get the dollar" up against the mark, possibly to 1.65 marks to the dollar by year-end, Mr. Johnson says.

Mr. Johnson sees the dollar at 145 yen at the year's close, not far below the current level. The dollar, he says, stands a good chance of remaining buoyant against the yen: Investors are staying clear of Japan's financial markets and any disruption of Japan's oil supply because the Gulf crisis could aggravate the nation's inflation problem.

While brighter times could be ahead for the dollar, there are few expectations that its troubles will be over anytime soon.

With the U.S. economy wobbly and the Federal Reserve seen poised to ease credit in the next few months, more heavy dollar losses are seen probable in the near term.

Separately, on the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery settled at $415 an ounce, up $3. Estimated volume was a moderate 4.5 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Monday, there wasn't any trading in gold because of a holiday.
