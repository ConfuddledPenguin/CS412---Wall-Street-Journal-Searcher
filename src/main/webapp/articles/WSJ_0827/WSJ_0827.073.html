What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/27/90

FED POLICY MAKERS ARE increasingly worried about a U.S. recession and lean toward a modest credit easing. But officials of the central bank suggest that they won't cut interest rates until the financial markets stabilize. The bias toward easing may surprise those analysts who expected the bank to maintain its policies in the face of rising oil prices. ---

OPEC's ministers began an informal round of talks in Vienna about raising output to hold down oil prices. With its big turnout, the session may become a full official meeting.

---

Real GNP rose at a 1.2% yearly rate in the second period, showing a continued weakness in the economy.

Machine-tool orders dropped 11.9% in July as exports fell sharply.

---

Stocks rebounded Friday as the Dow Jones industrials gained 49.50 points after a 173-point drop over the preceding three days. The dollar also rose, but the 30-year Treasury bond fell, as its yield rose to 9.15%.

Less high-grade corporate debt is available, making issues remaining in the market appear overpriced.

---

Regulators are pleased with the stock market's pattern of orderly moves, despite the Dow Jones industrials' nearly 16% drop since mid-July.

---

Thrift regulators promised more enforcement actions against lawyers for S&Ls that later failed. The development follows a court ruling that harshly criticized attorneys connected with the collapsed Lincoln Savings.

Charles Keating resigned his post as chairman of Lincoln's parent.

---

The FDIC plans to retain a legal team from Cravath, Swain & Moore to recover thrift losses possibly caused by Drexel's junk-bond operations.

---

Ford and Chrysler made sharply different offers to the UAW, as the union prepares to select a "target" company in its new contract talks.

---

Data General announced the layoffs of 2,000 workers. The move could return the troubled computer maker to profitability, say analysts.

---

USAir set another 2,100 layoffs, bringing recent cutbacks to 7% of its work force. Citing market weakness, the airline also cut its projected 1991 capacity growth to 2% from 6%.

---

The Brady debt plan is placing more emphasis on continued bank loans to developing debtor nations, a shift from the plan's initial stress on cutting debt and interest payments.

---

AmBase accepted a takeover bid from an investor group valued at $1.15 billion. The group intends to keep AmBase's Home Insurance unit, which the company had planned to sell.

---

Natural-gas deregulation rules were upheld by a federal appeals court. The decision could spur investment in the industry by reducing the likelihood of litigation.
