Venezuela Set For New Loans By Brady Plan

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 08/27/90

WASHINGTON -- The Brady debt strategy is placing more emphasis on continued bank lending to indebted developing nations. In the latest transaction under the Brady strategy, Venezuela's creditor banks have mostly opted either to make new loans or to cut loan interest rates in exchange for more collateral, such as U.S. Treasury bonds that guarantee principal repayments.

Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady's strategy, announced in March 1989, stressed cutting back bank debt and interest payments of several heavily indebted developing nations in order to alleviate the international debt problem.

More recently, though -- as in the Venezuelan transaction -- banks have been offered stronger incentives for continuing lending to big debtor countries. In the current Venezuelan debt agreement, the banks' new loans -- made by buying tradeable bonds issued by the country -- offer the banks better opportunities to convert loans into equity investments in the country.

A shortage of further bank loans for Mexico hampered the first transaction made under the Brady strategy. But new loans totaling more than $100 million from two French government-owned banks -- Credit Lyonnais and Banque Nationale de Paris -- played an important role in ensuring there were sufficient funds to complete that transaction, bankers say.

Under an agreement reached this past March, Venezuela's creditor banks were offered a number of options for loans to that country: The banks could either tender their credits at a discount, offer Venezuela cuts in its debt principal or interest payments in exchange for more collateral, or lend the country new money.

By Friday, banks holding 90%, or $18.45 billion, of Venezuela's long-term foreign commercial bank debt had chosen their options. According to bankers close to the transaction, banks holding about $5.5 billion of that country's $20.5 billion of foreign long-term debt have decided to lend further funds, meaning Venezuela will receive at least $1.15 billion in additional bank loans over two years.

In recent days, however, some bankers have begun to wonder if Venezuela needs new loans in light of the sharp increase in the price of oil, which makes up almost all of that country's exports.

Meantime, banks holding more than $6 billion of Venezuelan debt have chosen to exchange their existing loans for so-called par bonds, which carry a fixed-interest rate of 6.75% and will be collateralized with securities from the U.S. Treasury or some other Western government. This gives Venezuela an important break in its interest payments; currently the country pays a floating rate of about 10% on most of its borrowings.

Bankers say that they will continue to lend to countries such as Venezuela if the terms are attractive. "There's a lesson here, new money properly packaged is available in a Brady deal for a country with a history of paying its bills," said Peter McPherson, executive vice president of Bank of America and a negotiator in the Venezuelan talks. Mr. McPherson, though, added that his bank didn't lend further money but, instead, "chose the par bond option (which holds the interest rate to a fixed 6.75%) because it gave it substantial additional collateral with a trade-off of somewhat lower interest rates."

In addition, banks holding more than $2 billion of Venezuelan bank debt tendered their credits for purchase by the country's government, bankers said. Terms of this so-called buy-back will be set later. Meantime, banks with about $3 billion of Venezuelan debt opted to take a 30% discount in the face value of their loans in exchange for more collateral. Venezuela has a total of $35 billion in government and private-sector foreign debt.
