Enterprise: After Slowdown, New Strategies for Venture Capitalists

  • Author: Udayan Gupta
  • Date: 08/27/90

"For venture capitalists, it'll never again be business as usual. The industry is paying the price of success." So says William Sahlman, a professor of entrepreneurial finance at the Harvard Business School.

After successfully financing some of America's best-known high-growth companies, including Federal Express Corp., Apple Computer Inc. and Compaq Computer Corp., the venture-capital industry began to hit rough times. The industry's average annual return on investment, which ran as high as 30% a decade ago, has been falling sharply for the past five years. Many venture funds have had trouble raising capital.

Venture-capital partnerships, which provide financing for young companies, attracted a wave of new capital after their phenomenal success in the 1970s and early 1980s. But the increase in capital bid up the prices venture capitalists paid for investments and cut returns, Mr. Sahlman says. "Too many companies were being funded in the same industries, and the inevitable result was competitive mayhem."

The 39-year-old Mr. Sahlman has been studying entrepreneurship and venture capital for over a decade. His Harvard Business School course on entrepreneurial finance attracts over 50% of the school's second-year class. He is also a director of two Boston companies, Avid Technology Inc., a provider of video editing software, and Butcher Co., a 110-year old manufacturer of industrial sanitary supply goods.

"The rates of return for the foreseeable future will not reach the levels" of the late '70s and early '80s, he predicts. But the problems of the past five years seem to be clearing the way for new opportunities and new strategies. In an interview, Mr. Sahlman considered the future of venture capital and entrepreneurship in America:

Q. Is venture capital dead?

A. Not by a long shot. The prospects for the industry in the 1990s are better than they have been for over a decade.

Less capital and fewer funds means fewer companies will be financed. The companies that do receive money will get more attention from their investors and have a better chance of survival. The rates of return will increase from the low levels experienced in the past five or six years.

Q. Will we see a change in the way venture capitalists invest?

A. Yes. Venture capitalists are going to look for fewer home runs -- companies that potentially can achieve sales of $500 million or so. They are going to invest in smaller companies that can dominate a niche, companies that often may be no bigger than $50 million to $100 million in sales, but highly profitable.

Q. What else will have to change?

A. Venture capitalists will have to open up their investment horizons. That means going beyond traditional high-tech ideas such as workstations and semiconductors. It might make more sense to look at service businesses that could improve with the application of technology. More important, they will have to cast the entrepreneurial net wider to include women, as well as minorities.

Q. What role will venture capitalists have to play in this new environment?

A. They have to provide more than just money. They will need to develop closer ties with the companies they invest in, provide more help with recruiting and strategic partnering. They also will have to help their companies with competitive positioning in a new and ever-changing global marketplace.

Venture capitalists have to recognize that they aren't the only sources of capital for fast-growing companies. They face competition from other venture capitalists, from angels, from corporate investors and from institutions investing directly in start-up businesses.

Q. What are the implications for entrepreneurs?

A. There are plenty of sources of risk capital for entrepreneurs today. They need to choose their sources of capital carefully for the best chances of success.

Searching for the cheapest capital may not be in the best long-term interest of a growing company. From whom you raise money often is more important than the terms on which you obtain that money.

Going public may seem like a good way to get cheap money, but it also can cause long-term business strategies to be jettisoned. Many managers hurt their companies in order to try to live up to the unrealistic expectations embedded in high public market stock prices.

Q. What are the alternatives to venture capital?

A. Angels, for one. The entrepreneurial revolution created many successes, such as H. Ross Perot, who themselves are investing in new ventures. Institutional investors -- Harvard University's endowment, for example -- that only invested through venture-capital partnerships are investing directly.

And then there are the corporations, both domestic and international, that are moving up the food chain, investing earlier in the development of new companies. For many small companies, finding a corporate partner that can help in their overall growth may be more helpful than trying to cash out quickly by going public.

Q. Should government try to provide capital for entrepreneurial businesses?

A. No. Government efforts to encourage entrepreneurship would be more effective if focused on helping companies sell products and services than if focused on access to financing. Loan guarantees often result in poor investment decisions and shift risk to taxpayers without sufficient macroeconomic benefits. Such efforts might make more sense if there was a genuine shortage of risk capital, but I don't believe a shortage exists.

Q. Should the government play any role in helping the entrepreneurial climate?

A. Government should stop changing policies that have an impact on risk-taking and entrepreneurship. It also can simplify and reduce the cost of raising capital by getting rid of such regulations as state blue sky laws {regulations governing securities offerings} which make it harder and more costly to raise money without offsetting benefits. In Massachusetts, for example, it can cost as much as $20,000 to register securities for sale under the blue sky laws.

Q. What are prospects for the future?

A. The entrepreneurial revolution hasn't run out of steam. It's alive and well and venture capitalists will continue to play a role in financing high-potential ventures. Both investors and entrepreneurs will likely achieve better financial results in the next five years than they have in the past five.
