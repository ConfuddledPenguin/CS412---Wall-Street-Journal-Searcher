Letters to the Editor: Telling It On the Mountain

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/27/90

Thank you for bringing to your readers' attention the current debate over Flat Mountain Pond in the Sandwich Range Wilderness here in the White Mountain National Forest (July 26). I and other members of the Sandwich Conservation Commission have appealed the U.S. Forest Service's decision to build a road on this trail. The commission is a town board appointed by the Sandwich selectmen. Town voters discussed this issue at our March town meeting and resoundingly opposed the road. Appellants in this case are not a uniform group of ignorant and rabid environmentalists. Nor are we "unnatural foes" of the disabled. We are the citizens of a small American town that happens to include 20,000 acres of a national forest; we have worked with the U.S. Forest Service since before the White Mountain National Forest was established in 1911.

There was extended public debate and then consensus on a boundary for the Sandwich Range Wilderness. More debate yielded consensus on a 10-year management plan that allows motorized access on some trails and no motorized access on others, Flat Mountain Pond Trail for one. And we have offered to help the Forest Service develop various new facilities for disabled visitors to the Sandwich Range.

Now suddenly, the Forest Service has made a decision to bring motors to the edge of a pond recognized as the most remote and isolated water in the entire national forest. Our argument is not with who drives those motors. Our argument is with motors in a federal wilderness.

This is not a battle between environmentalists and the disabled. This is a protest by ordinary citizens against a federal administrative decision that countermands years of public consensus.

Martha Carlson

Center Sandwich, N.H.
