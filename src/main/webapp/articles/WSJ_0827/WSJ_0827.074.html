Letters to the Editor: Death Be Not Legislated

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/27/90

As a lawyer and bioethicist, I was appalled by Bernard D. Davis's suggestion (editorial page, July 31) that policies concerning the discontinuation of life-sustaining treatment should be determined by popular vote. There are many things that can be determined by majority rule, but life and death is not one of them. Whether or not a life that has been impaired severely by irreversible loss of mental function is worth living is a decision for the individual. Only when impairment is severe enough to constitute death should anyone other than the patient or the patient's designated representative have the authority to stop treatment. Only the individual is in a position to assess the value of his or her own existence. Only the individual can transfer that right to another.

Perhaps we need to reconsider our definition of death. Death used to mean the cessation of circulation of blood and respiration. Today it means that and/or the total cessation of brain function. If society redefines death to include an irreversible loss of cerebral function, then at least people in a persistent vegetative state and probably anyone who has irreversibly lost consciousness would be dead and the issue of robbing individuals of their rights to make treatment decisions would be moot.

Sigrid Fry-Revere

Washington
