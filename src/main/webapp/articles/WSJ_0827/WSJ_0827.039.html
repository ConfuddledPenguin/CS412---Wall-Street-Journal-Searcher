Appeals Court Upholds Major Portion Of Rules for Natural-Gas Deregulation

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 08/27/90

WASHINGTON -- A federal appeals court has upheld a major portion of the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission's rules for deregulating the natural-gas industry. The unanimous decision by a three-judge panel of the appeals court here could spur investment in the industry by reducing the likelihood of protracted litigation that might have followed a decision throwing out the commission's actions. Industry officials said they haven't yet decided whether to appeal this outcome, however.

At issue were the commission's terms for increasing competition in the pipeline industry and dismantling the so-called take-or-pay system. During the 1970s and early 1980s, when gas prices were high and expected to go higher, many producers and pipelines entered into agreements under which the pipelines agreed to pay top dollar for gas -- and to take it even if they didn't need it -- in exchange for the promise of long-term supplies.

A subsequent sharp drop in prices, however, left these pipelines with expensive gas they had to buy but couldn't sell. At the same time, in a series of deregulatory steps, the commission was allowing distribution companies, utilities and a range of other customers to buy gas directly from producers -- at the lower market prices. Pipelines were required to transport these customers' gas on the same basis as they moved gas they owned.

In the court case, various producers, pipelines and customers sought changes in the rules the commission established for getting pipelines out of their take-or-pay obligations and for ensuring that they provided all customers with equal access to their transportation systems.

Because the status quo was upheld, with some minor exceptions, the court decision isn't expected to raise or lower consumer gas prices much. Moreover, many pipelines and producers have already reached settlements of their take-or-pay contracts, though a different court decision might have led to a reopening of some of these agreements.

The Iraqi invasion of Kuwait has led to a jump in oil prices, which, if sustained, is likely to lead to an increase in gas prices as well. That could renew interest in longer-term contracts, but the regulatory restrictions that surrounded the take-or-pay contracts would no longer apply.

Glen Howard, a partner at the law firm of Sutherland, Asbill & Brennan, which represented industrial gas customers in the case, said that "big jumps in gas bills" might have resulted if the pipelines had been allowed to recover more of their costs of buying their way out of their take-or-pay contracts.

The commission had no comment on the court decision.
