Southland Extends Its Debt-Swap Offer After Falling Short of Goal Last Week

  • Author: Kevin Helliker
  • Date: 08/27/90

DALLAS -- Amid signs that some investors may be holding out for a better deal, Southland Corp. again extended its debt-swap offer after falling far short of obtaining bondholder approval to complete a $430 million bailout proposed by its Japanese partners. Southland extended the offer to Sept. 12, three days before the scheduled expiration of its proposed deal with Ito-Yokado Co., the Japanese operator of Southland's 7-Eleven convenience stores.

To complete the transaction, which would give Ito-Yokado a 70% stake in Southland, the convenience-store chain needs approval from bondholders representing 95% of the bonds outstanding in each of five debt issues. When the first extension of the offer expired last Thursday, Southland had received tenders from holders of 46% of its 18% notes, 46.3% of its 16.75% notes, 64% of its 16.5% notes, 65.9% of its 15.75% notes, and 89.9% of its 13.5% senior notes. Only holders of the company's preferred stock exceeded the minimums required in the complex deal, tendering 90.9% of the shares outstanding. Only two-thirds approval of preferred shareholders was required.

"We plan to intensify our efforts to communicate with those bondholders who have not yet tendered and expect continued progress during the extension," said Clark Matthews, Southland's chief financial officer. According to John Gordon, president of Deltec Securities Corp. and chairman of the Southland bondholder committee, at least one big holder, with 12% of each of two issues, has been on vacation and couldn't be contacted.

Yet merely extending the offer may not be enough. William Eddleman Jr. of Dallas-based Barre & Co., a holder along with its clients of about $30 million in Southland bonds, said, "Officially, we're telling our clients we'd like to see if there's going to be a better deal."

"Anybody holding out for a better deal is totally unreasonable, considering the economic environment we're in now," countered Mr. Gordon, whose committee rejected three earlier Southland offers before approving the fourth and latest one. Calling it "the best deal that can be gotten," Mr. Gordon predicted that it would receive bondholder approval by Sept. 12.

Southland's current offer would give bondholders about 25% of the company's common stock. The founding Thompson family of Dallas would get about 5% of the stock.

Southland went into default on its $1.8 billion of publicly traded debt in mid-July, but avoided being forced into filing for bankruptcy-law protection when the bondholder committee endorsed the terms of its most recent exchange offer.

The company, which owns about 6,700 7-Eleven and other convenience stores in the U.S. and Canada, went private in a $4.9 billion leveraged buy-out in late 1987 to avoid a threatened hostile takeover. But increased competition eroded sales and profit margins and made it difficult for the company to service the huge debt from the buy-out.
