Politics & Policy: Hearings on Supreme Court Nominee Souter Near End With Little Opposition

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- Confirmation hearings for Supreme Court nominee David Souter neared completion, with witnesses testifying for and against, but the opposition from liberal groups was less than expected. Most of the opposition at the Senate Judiciary Committee hearings came from abortion-rights groups and women's rights groups, who described the 51-year-old Judge Souter as insensitive to the right to privacy. Molly Yard, president of the National Organization for Women, declared a "state of emergency" for women's rights.

Kate Michelman, director of the National Abortion Rights Action League, said: "All the evidence points to the fact that he will vote to overrule Roe vs. Wade," referring to the 1973 decision that recognized the right to abortion. "He said nothing to allay our fears," she added.

But Sen. Alan Simpson (R., Wyo.), describing himself as favoring the right to abortion, said the opposition by abortion-rights groups was misplaced. "There are going to be other Supreme Court choices when you're really going to need to be in the trenches, and this is not one of them."

A number of civil rights and civil liberties organizations decided not to oppose Judge Souter's nomination, including the NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund and the Alliance for Justice. The influential Leadership Conference On Civil Rights, which includes a broad range of groups and that led the fight in 1987 to defeat Judge Robert Bork's nomination, couldn't agree on a position for or against the nominee. The group urged senators to question Judge Souter further on his attitude toward racial discrimination. The committee is unlikely to call Judge Souter back for further questioning.

Balancing the opposition were a number of witnesses, including former Democratic Attorney General Griffin Bell, who endorsed the nomination. Mr. Bell said that Mr. Souter, a former New Hampshire Supreme Court justice who is now a federal appeals court judge, has the "right vision for our country and for the law."

The hearings are due to conclude today with a handful of additional witnesses. The date hasn't been set for a vote by the 14member committee, but Senate aides say unofficially that it may be on Sept. 27. The confirmation is generally viewed as a certainty.

Judge Souter, who completed his testimony Monday night, remained in Washington yesterday, although not at the hearings. He met briefly in the morning with some witnesses who were to testify on his behalf, answered correspondence for much of the day, and went to see the Boston Red Sox play the Baltimore Orioles in Baltimore last night, aides said. Next week, the avid outdoorsman is planning to return to New Hampshire and go hiking while he awaits the Senate's action.

As the hearings continued yesterday, a dominant factor continued to be what Democrats and Republicans alike described as the impressive performance by Judge Souter. During 18 hours of testimony over three days, he discussed in detail dozens of Supreme Court precedents, legal principles and details of court history, never consulting a single note, or even requesting a break in the lengthy proceedings.

Late Monday night, as Judge Souter was concluding his testimony, Sen. Joseph Biden (D., Del.), chairman of the Judiciary Committee, said to the nominee: "It was a tour de force on your part."
