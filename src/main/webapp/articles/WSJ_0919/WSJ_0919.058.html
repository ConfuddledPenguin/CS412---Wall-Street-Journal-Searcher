Economy: Merchandise Trade Gap Grew in July As Exports Fell and Oil Imports Surged

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- A plunge in exports and a disturbing surge in oil imports in the month before the Persian Gulf crisis helped swell the U.S. merchandise trade deficit nearly $4 billion in July, to $9.33 billion, the Commerce Department reported. The new figures are probably only the beginning of a string of bad trade news for the U.S., as the jolt in oil prices brought on by Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait begins to boost the U.S. petroleum-import bill further, many economists said.

"If you think this trade deficit is bad wait till you see the next few," said Irwin Kellner, chief economist at Manufacturers Hanover Trust Co.

After improving in five of the first six months this year, exports tumbled 6.4% in July, to $32.03 billion from a record $34.22 in June, the department said. The drop reflected lower shipments of a range of goods, from factory machinery to farm products.

At the same time, imports jumped 4.5%, to $41.36 billion, according to the report, bolstered by the higher petroleum imports as well as increases in shipments of foreign-made cars, capital goods and industrial supplies.

In June the trade deficit had sunk to its lowest level in seven years, $5.34 billion, and most analysts had said the monthly gaps were unlikely to continue that small.

Nevertheless, many were surprised by the magnitude of the widening, partly because it came in a month when petroleum prices fell.

The price of a barrel of crude petroleum dropped to $14.50 in July from $14.68 in June, according to the report. But the number of barrels imported grew to 274.5 million from 252 million, raising the country's oil bill 7.9% to $3.98 billion.

Secretary of Commerce Robert Mosbacher said yesterday that the jump in oil prices as a result of the Middle East turmoil is likely to increase the U.S. trade deficit more than $2 billion each month through the end of the year.

That would mean that the monthly merchandise trade deficit, which has narrowed substantially in 1988 and 1989, probably won't shrink further this year. By the end of July the trade gap was about $8 billion smaller than during the like period in 1989.

"I think it will be very difficult" to finish 1990 with an improved trade deficit, said Sam Nakagama, chairman and chief economist at Nakagama & Wallace Inc., an economic advising firm in New York. He also noted that the trade balances of many countries around the world will be hurt by the oil-price rise.

But more worrisome than oil prices to some economists was the big drop in exports, which had been one of the U.S. economy's few remaining areas of growth. "It's another nail in the coffin of the economic expansion," Mr. Kellner said.

Other analysts were more sanguine, predicting that recent declines in the value of the dollar will help exports by making U.S. goods cheaper for foreign customers.

The numbers in the report were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
