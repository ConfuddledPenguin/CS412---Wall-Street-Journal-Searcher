Technology: Amdahl Plans Most Powerful Mainframes Yet --- Biggest Model Has 323 Mips, Much More Than IBM's, But Won't Appear Soon

  • Author: Susan C. Faludi
  • Date: 09/19/90

Amdahl Corp. said it will introduce the most powerful IBM-compatible mainframe computers yet, making the company the fourth player to enter the most recent round of market jockeying among mainframe vendors, kicked off by Hitachi Ltd.'s announcement last June. After Hitachi threw down the gauntlet with its next-generation mainframes, International Business Machines Corp. and Fujitsu Ltd. declared their plans for new and more powerful machines in early September, only a day apart.

Amdahl's new 5995 series of mainframes will include 10 models, which won't be shipped before late 1991. The series includes at the top of the line an eight-way multiprocessor with three times the power of any previous Amdahl mainframe. This premium machine also will be the first IBM-compatible system to use eight central processing units.

"Our basic building block {the uniprocessor} is bigger than theirs," said Joe Zemke, president and chief operating officer of Amdahl, Sunnyvale, Calif. In terms of raw power, Amdahl indeed leads the pack. But not by much: Amdahl's new mainframes offer as much as 54 million instructions a second for each uniprocessor, while Fujitsu offers 50 mips, Hitachi 45 mips and IBM 41 mips. At the top of the line, Amdahl's eight-way multiprocessor, the 5995-8650M, offers about 323 mips, while IBM's top model, the six-way ES/9000, has 210 mips, analysts say.

The Amdahl models also are equipped to handle massive amounts of memory; each microprocessor can support a total of 10 gigabytes of processor storage. A gigabyte is 1,000 megabytes.

The new mainframes will range in price from $3.2 million to $32 million, and Amdahl's four most high-powered models won't be ready for shipment until at least the fourth quarter of 1991 and as late as the second quarter of 1992, the company said. Hitachi has said that its most powerful new machines will be available in the second quarter of next year, and IBM has said its biggest machines will be available in the third quarter.

Mr. Zemke said that Amdahl made its announcement so early because, given the spate of claims from competitors, "we really wanted to clear the air about what we were going to do and when it would be available."

In spite of the impressive specifications, the long delay in shipment -- which has been a feature of the announcements by Amdahl's rivals -- inspired some dubious reactions from industry watchers. "Amdahl will have leapfrogged IBM's, Fujitsu's and Hitachi's latest, that's unquestionably true," said Bob Djurdjevic, president of Annex Research in Phoenix, Ariz., who follows the company. "But I get to be skeptical with any of these claims when the lead time is this long. We are unfortunately going back to the days of marketing wars. These are all paper tigers, by vendors who don't want to be perceived as being left behind."

Despite the staggering prices at the top of the line -- only a few years ago, price tags topped out at $5 million -- analysts predicted that Amdahl's $32.1 million eight-way model probably would attract the most interest -- from corporate customers with huge data-processing requirements.

While at first glance, Amdahl would appear to be competing with its own kissing cousin -- it is 44%-owned by Fujitsu -- the Japanese company's new mainframe offerings aren't IBM-compatible and won't be marketed in the U.S., Mr. Zemke said. The two companies use the same semiconductor technology for their new mainframes, but they are designing, manufacturing and marketing their versions separately.

In American Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Amdahl shares closed at $12.125, down 37.5 cents.
