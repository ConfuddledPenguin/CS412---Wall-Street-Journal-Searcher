Darling-Delaware Is Seeking Buyers, Firm's Advisers Say

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/19/90

NEW YORK -- Darling-Delaware Co., which failed to make a $12.2 million bond interest payment due last Saturday, is seeking buyers for some or all of its operations, according to the company's financial advisers. Last week, the Dallas-based rendering company told bondholders that it needs to restructure its debt in light of weak financial results so far this year. For 1990's first half, the company had an $11.3 million loss on revenue of $177.8 million. Darling-Delaware makes tallow and grease from animal parts and restaurant waste.

"If third parties are willing to put more capital into the company, we're interested," said Bruce Raben, a Jefferies & Co. investment banker who is advising Darling-Delaware. Mr. Raben said about 10 potential investors in Darling-Delaware have approached the company. These range from regional U.S. renderers interested in buying several plants to corporate restructuring funds that might want an equity position in the company, he said.

Darling-Delaware on Saturday missed a semi-annual interest payment due on its $175 million of 14% senior subordinated debentures, which mature in 1999. These high-yield "junk" bonds were issued in March 1989, shortly after Darling-Delaware paid a $179 million dividend to a group of Texas and Connecticut investors who own the company. The bonds currently trade at about 25 cents on the dollar.

Darling-Delaware also has about $110 million in bank debt outstanding, owed to a syndicate of banks led by France's Banque Paribas. Mr. Raben said the company is current on all its bank interest payments. Paribas bankers didn't return repeated phone calls.

At a creditor's meeting last week, Darling Delaware's current owners didn't make specific restructuring proposals, said David Ying, an investment banker at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. who is advising the bondholders. Mr. Ying said the company is asking bondholders for their suggestions -- an unusual strategy for a company with debt problems.

Messrs. Ying and Raben both said they expect that specific restructuring proposals will surface in about 45 days.
