Law -- Legal Beat: House Panel Approves Restrictions On Use of RICO in Civil Lawsuits

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett and Neil Barsky
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Judiciary Committee approved a bill that would restrict the use of the federal Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations law in civil lawsuits. The legislation, approved yesterday on a voice vote, would leave intact the RICO law's criminal provisions, but it would limit the ability of private parties to brand their courtroom opponents as "racketeers" and seek triple damages.

An unusual coalition of business groups and the American Civil Liberties Union has tried for years to curb RICO, arguing that a law designed to target organized-crime syndicates has become a common weapon in garden-variety commercial disputes. Consumer advocates and some liberal legislators have opposed revising RICO, contending that it is an effective weapon against white-collar fraud in areas such as the crippled savings-and-loan industry.

The bill would make revisions generally considered to be more moderate than those called for in legislation approved in February by the Senate Judiciary Committee. It remains unclear, according to Capitol Hill aides, whether proponents of limits on RICO will be able to iron out differences and get a compromise approved by both houses before Congress adjourns this fall.

The House version, promoted as a compromise, would make federal judges "gatekeepers" for RICO, permitting its use only if certain conditions are met. Specifically, judges could allow a private RICO suit if the plaintiff alleges "egregious criminal conduct" and the claim involves a previous criminal conviction. To permit such a suit, judges also would have to find that the defendant was "a major participant" in criminal conduct and that the potent triple-damage remedy is needed to deter future egregious criminal acts.

Rep. John Conyers (D., Mich.), who has led opposition to the bill, warned that it would "diminish legal remedies for citizens in the S&L failures, where we have the biggest economic scandal in American history."

The House panel approved an amendment offered by one of the bill's sponsors, Rep. Rick Boucher (D., Va.), that was intended to address concerns about weakening RICO as a weapon in lawsuits involving S&Ls. The amendment would create a legal presumption that a defendant had engaged in "egregious criminal conduct" if his alleged injury to the plaintiff involved a financial loss suffered by an S&L that fails.

But Pamela Gilbert of the Washington consumer-advocacy group Public Citizen called the amendment "purely cosmetic," because under the House version a judge would still have to find that a defendant was a "major participant" in criminal conduct to allow a RICO suit involving an S&L-related loss. That requirement "would let off all the accountants and lawyers and investment bankers responsible for a lot of the {thrift} failures," Ms. Gilbert said.

In two major differences from the House version, the Senate bill doesn't include the "gatekeeper" provisions, but it would eliminate triple damages in almost all instances. The Senate's proposed revisions in RICO would apply to lawsuits filed only after the law's enactment. The House Judiciary Committee addressed that issue by passing an amendment that would make limitations on RICO applicable six months after the law's enactment.

SECESSION LAW gets approval from New York's highest court.

The ruling by the New York Court of Appeals will allow the borough of Staten Island to begin a cumbersome process that could lead to its secession from New York City.

In November, Staten Islanders will get to vote on whether to establish a commission to study the secession issue. But state legislators cautioned that even if the borough's residents ultimately vote to secede, it is unlikely the city's least populous borough will ever leave New York City. The state Legislature must approve the secession and is considered unlikely to do so.

Interest in secession has grown since last year's U.S. Supreme Court ruling that the city's Board of Estimate, which gave the borough an equal voice in the city's governance, violated the one-person one-vote principle. Some residents fear that Staten Island, with only 400,000 residents, will not get its fair share of city services under the newly enacted city charter. New York City officials have opposed the law allowing for a secession referendum.

If this November's proposal passes, a commission will have three years to recommend whether secession is the best thing for the borough. If a secession referendum then passes, the borough may vote on whether to secede, and then leave it to the Legislature to make its decision.

"Should Staten Island secede it could pose enormous problems for the city," said Michael Johnston, who follows municipal finance for Moody's Investors Service Inc. "It seems premature to be overly concerned about it, however."

BUYER OF PROPERTY gains new weapon in conflict over waste cleanup.

U.S. District Judge D. Brock Hornby of Portland, Maine, refused to dismiss a claim by Hanlin Group Inc., seeking to hold the seller of a manufacturing plant liable for an environmental cleanup.

In 1982, Hanlin purchased the plant in Orrington, Maine, from International Minerals & Chemical Corp., now known as Imcera Group Inc. Four years later, the Environmental Protection Agency said the site was contaminated with hazardous waste and ordered Hanlin to undertake a cleanup, as required under the federal Resource Conservation Recovery Act. Enacted in 1976, the act holds the owner of a site liable for the cleanup of hazardous waste.

Hanlin, a closely held Edison, N.J., chemical manufacturer, sued International Minerals, alleging the company was strictly liable for any cleanup costs because it had caused the contamination. The ruling last week upholds a decision by a federal magistrate allowing Hanlin to proceed on the claim.

Under the doctrine of strict liability, a defendant engaged in an activity considered to be abnormally dangerous may be held liable for damages, regardless of whether the defendant was negligent.

Typically, the strict-liability doctrine has allowed people living near a contaminated site to seek compensation for damage to their property. Maine is only the second state to rule that the doctrine is not limited to neighboring landowners and can be used by purchasers against sellers of contaminated property. An earlier New Jersey case is pending in the state's supreme court.

Under an agreement signed with the Environmental Protection Agency, Hanlin estimates it will spend $3 million to $4 million just to study the extent of contamination at the site, says John J. O'Leary Jr., a lawyer for Hanlin. Cleaning up an average site can often run as high as $100 million.

Andrew Schlickman of the Chicago firm Sidley & Austin, a lawyer for Imcera, said the Northbrook, Ill., company had not decided whether to seek further review of the decision.

MANVILLE TRUST formally asks court to authorize class action for claims.

In its motion, filed in federal court in Brooklyn, N.Y., the trust said it has inadequate funds to pay all the personal-injury claims against it. The motion was expected, as part of U.S. Judge Jack B. Weinstein's efforts to come up with a nationwide approach to settling many of the thousands of asbestos claims now pending in the courts.

The trust was formed in 1988 as part of the bankruptcy-court reorganization plan of Manville Corp. to pay asbestos personal-injury claims against the company.

Eagle-Picher Industries Inc., another former asbestos maker, has made a similar motion before Judge Weinstein. As many as five other defendants, including the Manville trust, have been expected to join Eagle-Picher in that process. Eventually, a single claims-processing facility could be set up by the court to pay those claims.

XEROX CORP. wins reversal of jury verdict in discrimination suit.

A federal judge in Dallas overruled a jury verdict that awarded $9 million to a woman who claimed she was made a victim of fraud, age and sex discrimination by Xerox.

In his ruling Monday, U.S. District Court Judge Sidney Fitzwater said Elizabeth Layman's claims against her former employer weren't supported by the evidence. Ms. Layman, who had worked for Xerox in Dallas, alleged that Xerox in 1986 fraudulently promised to find her a job in California.

Xerox said it had in fact offered Ms. Layman, 48 years old, two jobs in California, both at higher pay than her Dallas job, but that she rejected them as inferior and chose instead to resign and file suit.

Attorneys in the case said Ms. Layman is expected to appeal.

DEFENSE LAWYER can be compelled to testify on fees, appeals court rules.

Federal prosecutors had sought to require Mike DeGeurin, a prominent Houston criminal-defense lawyer, to testify about the fees he received for defending a man charged with possession of cocaine. Suspecting Mr. DeGeurin's bill is being paid with drug money, prosecutors had subpoenaed him last September to testify before a federal grand jury investigating his client.

Two U.S. Supreme Court rulings in June 1989 gave prosecutors wide authority to seize assets of accused criminals, including funds needed to pay lawyers' fees. But a federal district judge blocked the subpoena of Mr. DeGeurin, saying that it could turn him into a witness against his own client. Last Friday, a three-judge panel of the federal appeals court in New Orleans reversed that decision but delayed issuing a written opinion.

Although Mr. DeGeurin's client already has been sentenced to prison, prosecutors are pressing for the attorney's testimony anyway, because they believe the fees were paid by a third person involved in illegal drugs. "There is no attorney-client privilege there," says Larry Finder, an assistant U.S. attorney in Houston.

Mr. DeGeurin's attorney, brother Dick DeGuerin, says he won't know the scope of the court's ruling until a written opinion is produced. If his client in fact is compelled to testify, he says, "that's a disaster -- and very discouraging for practicing lawyers."
