Marketing & Media: Norman Lear Aims To Scale Back Plans For Media Company --- ACT III Folds 2 Magazines; Sources Say Concern May Restructure, Sell Assets

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly and Richard Turner
  • Date: 09/19/90

NEW YORK -- Norman Lear's ACT III Communications Inc. is scaling back some of its ambitious plans to become a diversified media company, folding some properties and seeking buyers for others. According to company officials, ACT III has quietly folded two of its trade magazines and laid off 19 people at its ACT III Publishing unit. The subscription lists of the two publications have been sold to PSN Publications Inc., a unit of United Newspapers PLC.

ACT III officials said that Mr. Lear, who owns 80% of ACT III, is preparing to sell the publishing unit and significantly restructure his company. ACT III, based in Los Angeles, owns TV stations, movie theaters, and trade magazines.

Mr. Lear couldn't be reached for comment, and Robert C. Gardner, ACT III Publishing's chief operating officer, said "we are downsizing ACT III publishing. It's clear ACT III will not be an aggressive, acquisition-minded company in the future." Individuals close to the company said Mr. Lear wants to eliminate unprofitable areas, reduce the size of the company, which grew quickly in the late 1980s, and return to television production.

Mr. Lear formed ACT III Communications in 1985 after selling his television operation to Coca-Cola Co. for $485 million.

The two magazines that will be closed are Corporate Video Decisions and BME's Television Engineering. ACT III continues to publish an array of other music, television, and media-related trade magazines.

Speculation has grown in recent weeks that ACT III would sell two of its music trade magazines, Mix and Electronic Musician, to Affiliated Publications Inc. An Affiliated Publications' spokesman said the company would not comment.

The slashing of the publishing unit follows the recent sudden resignations of Thomas B. McGrath, ACT III president and chief operating officer, and Paul David Schaeffer, president of ACT III Publishing. Company executives said Messrs. McGrath and Schaeffer were blamed by Mr. Lear for spending too much on acquiring new properties and hiring the wrong people to operate them.

Mr. Lear would sell the entire company if the price was right, but it's a poor time to sell TV stations, movie theaters, or trade magazines, according to the company officials. Paramount Communications Corp. recently looked closely at the stations, but backed away.

Mr. Lear is currently producing a new sitcom for CBS Inc.
