Rumors Buffet Chase Manhattan Stock In a Show of Investor Fears on Bank Sector

  • Author: Fred R. Bleakley
  • Date: 09/19/90

NEW YORK -- In rumor-fueled trading, the stock of Chase Manhattan Corp. yesterday was the big bank that had Wall Street worried the most. Showing how touchy the market for bank stocks is these days, Chase fell as much as $2 a share to $13.875 at one point, before closing at $15, down 87.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading of 1.6 million shares.

Sparking the slide were rumors that Chase was having trouble selling its commercial paper, or short-term corporate IOUs. Chase responded by not only denying the rumors but also charging arbitragers with "spreading certain rumors in an unsettled commercial banking climate."

Traders and analysts had their fill of rumors from all directions. "I heard a litany of Chase's problems all day. But there was nothing new," said the head trader at one large brokerage firm. "In this environment, no one, especially portfolio managers reporting third-quarter positions, wants to hold anything that has the slightest hint of bad news."

Chase's stock already is among the most troubled among major commerical banks. As of Friday, it was off 63% from its 52-week high of $44.875, while the average major bank stock was off 51%. Banks are off more sharply than the stock market because the weakening economy and regulatory pressures have caused one bank after another to make unexpected provisions for problem loans, especially in real estate.

The rumor about Chase's commercial paper came atop other investor concerns about Chase and rekindled old concerns as well. They included worries that Chase's dividend, now yielding a hefty 16%, would be cut or eliminated and that a previously announced restructuring would result in a big third-quarter loss.

"I've heard every rumor today except that management is leaving," said Frank DeSantis, an analyst with PaineWebber. He and other analysts said they don't believe that Chase's dividend is in imminent danger.

An executive at Chase, in denying the rumors that Chase couldn't sell its commercial paper, also said the bank holding company relies on that market for only 2% of its funding. Even if the paper market were to shut Chase out, "We're pretty flush with cash and have other sources of ready funding," he said.

From a credit standpoint, "investors overreacted," said Fay Wong, a bank analyst with Fitch Investors Service, a rating concern. Still, the reason Chase's stock remains vulnerable, according to some analysts, is that the bank has yet to give much of a signal to investors on how much its restructuring will cost, what Chase will do to keep its capital levels in compliance with new international standards and what exposure the bank has to problem loans.

"The scope of further charges is what the market is waiting to hear," said James McDermott, head of research at Keefe, Bruyette & Woods. Shortly after a new management team was announced this summer, Chase said it planned to save about $300 million a year after a restructuring that would eliminate jobs.

Mr. McDermott estimates that voluntary retirements and mandatory layoffs could cost as much as $250 million, which will be taken before year's end. He said he also expects Chase will add at least $325 million to its loan-loss reserves in the third quarter, up from a $250 million provision in each of the first two quarters.

The latest decline in Chase's stock is especially troubling for holders who purchased more than $600 million of newly issued Chase shares last October at $40 each. The stock offering, the first since the bank was founded in 1877, left Chase executives disappointed because underwriters had missed the $44 level that the shares were trading at a few weeks earlier.
