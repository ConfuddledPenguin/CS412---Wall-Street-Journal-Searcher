World Markets: Tokyo Stocks Fall for Fourth Consecutive Day But Rise From Intraday Lows on Bargain Hunting

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/19/90

Tokyo stock prices finished lower for the fourth consecutive trading day Tuesday but managed to stay higher than intraday lows late in the afternoon session with the help of bargain hunting. In London, shares failed to hold onto an initial rally and ended sharply lower as the disclosure of U.S. economic data restrained trading activity.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues went down 480.78 points, or 1.97%, to 23884.82.

In Tokyo Wednesday, the Nikkei index fell another 68.36 points to close the morning session at 23816.46.

Investor participation on Tuesday was lackluster as growing concern about higher domestic interest rates and continuing uncertainty about the Mideast situation dried up buying interest, traders said.

Trading was volatile as the thin overall volume permitted even a small number of sell orders and arbitrage selling to push down the Nikkei Index, traders said.

The index plunged as low as 23308 points early in the afternoon, before bouncing up on a small amount of buying.

Volume on the first section was estimated at 400 million shares, up from 306 million shares Monday. Decliners outnumbered gainers 894 to 85, while 92 others were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section fell 43.86 points, or 2.36%, to 1816.33.

The second section index dropped 69.16 points, or 1.95%, to 3474.99. Volume was estimated at 6.5 million shares, up from 5.1 million shares Monday.

"Trading both on cash and futures markets was interest rate-driven," said a trader at J.P. Morgan Securities.

"The market is gradually discounting another discount rate increase," said a trader at Dai-Ichi Securities.

A report by the Nihon Keizai Shimbun in its Tuesday morning editions that the long-term prime rate will be raised for the third consecutive month in October fueled expectations that domestic interest rates will go higher, traders said. Such expectations were also prompted by another upswing in oil prices, the traders said.

Also, rumors that securities houses and institutional investors will sell Japanese government bonds heavily Wednesday, resulting in higher yields, damped sentiment in the stock market Tuesday afternoon, traders said.

Widespread perceptions that domestic interest rates haven't yet peaked further discouraged buying by institutional investors, who have been slowing trade activity ahead of the fiscal midyear book closing at the end of September, traders said.

Observers attributed Tuesday's decline to the lack of buying, which they said made the market unable to absorb even a small amount of sell orders.

"Stocks are priced abnormally low," said Kazuhiko Hama, general manager of stock trading at Nomura Securities. "It's largely because of the seasonal factor," he added, predicting that the worst will be over with the disappearance of term-end factors.

A trader at Yamaichi Securities said some institutions are ready to start bargain hunting next week because trading after Tuesday will be recorded as October transactions.

Because stocks have fallen on a small amount of selling, a rebound may not require a large amount of buying, the trader said.

Mitsuhiro Nakano, an analyst with Daiwa Securities, warned, however, that as long as the bond market remains unstable, institutions are unlikely to start buying actively.

Kobe Steel fell 13 yen to 516 yen (9.5 cents to $3.78), while Nippon Steel dropped 15 yen to 456 yen. Kansai Electric Power declined 140 yen to 2420 yen, and Tokyo Electric Power was down 140 yen at 3040 yen.

Among financial shares, Mitsubishi Bank dropped 50 yen to 1780 yen, while Nomura Securities plummeted 20 yen to 1620 yen.

Blue-chip shares in the electronics and automobile sectors also ended lower, with Sony down 170 yen at 6690 yen and Toyota Motor falling 10 yen to 1890 yen.

Meanwhile, Tokyo Steel gained 60 yen to 3960 yen, reflecting speculation that the company may increase dividends for the current fiscal year.

In London, traders said the disclosure of U.S. economic indicators was hurting that market. Among them, the U.S. July trade deficit grew to $9.3 billion from $5.3 billion in June, and the U.S. consumer price index rose 0.8% in August.

As the London market fell through a key support level, traders expected the market to fall lower still in search of a new level. Wall Street drifted lower during London afternoon trading, and traders were not optimistic for a quick recovery.

Scattered buying was evident, but institutions continued to stay away from any large holding positions, traders said. What buying was done amounted to "topping up funds discreetly, not to support levels but to get cheap stock," one trader said.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-Share Index fell 30.3 points to 2064, falling to the lowest close since April 24, 1989, when the index settled at 2062.

The Financial Times 30-Share Index ended down 29.3 points at 1576.8. Volume was 379.2 million shares, well above Monday's 286.6 million shares.

Among several companies reporting interim results, property and construction concern Taylor Woodrow, said half-year pretax profit fell 12% to #38 million ($72.8 million) as high interest rates weakened earnings. The shares fell a penny to 200 pence, as traders were relieved that the company's results matched expectations.

Stores and property management company Kingfisher also matched expectations with a 24% slump in pretax profit following a sharp decline in property operations. The shares gained 11 pence to 318 pence.

Bermuda-based security services and auctions concern ADT recorded a 38% rise in pretax profit for the half-year, at the bottom of expectations, and offered a bonus share to investors. ADT fell 5 pence to 148 pence.

On the Frankfurt Stock Exchange, investors were still expecting Continental AG to reject Pirelli's takeover bid outright or arrange a watered-down compromise. This would deprive the stock of the takeover speculation that has buoyed Continental shares in recent months. Continental plunged 5.5% to 250.50 marks ($161.87), after a 7.1% decline Monday.

In Milan, Pirelli fell 10 lire to 1775 lire ($1.53) and slumped to 1745 lire in the after market.

Meanwhile, shares closed lower on all major European markets. South African gold stocks closed lower. Stocks also closed lower in Sydney, Hong Kong, Taipei, Manila, Singapore and Wellington, but rose in Seoul.
