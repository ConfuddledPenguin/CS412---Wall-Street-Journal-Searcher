Who's News: Johnstone Named To Senior Fidelity Investments Post

  • Author: Lawrence Ingrassia
  • Date: 09/19/90

BOSTON -- Fidelity Investments, underscoring its push into overseas markets, named successful money manager C. Bruce Johnstone chief investment officer and a managing director of its Fidelity International Ltd. affiliate. Mr. Johnstone, 49 years old, the best-known money manager at Fidelity after the recent departure of Peter Lynch, since 1972 has managed the $4.1 billion Fidelity Equity-Income Fund, the third-largest of Fidelity's more than 100 equity funds. The fund has been a top performer long term, but it has had a weaker performance in recent years as "value investing" in high-yield stocks, Mr. Johnstone's forte, has fallen out of favor generally.

Mr. Johnstone's appointment is "a very aggressive move showing they're taking {international markets} very seriously. It should put the rest of the industry on notice that there's going to be a big push with resources behind it to make it work," said D. Tyler Jenks, vice president and research director at Kanon Bloch Carre & Co., which tracks mutual funds.

Mr. Johnstone will be based in London and will oversee portfolio management operations in Europe, Asia and Australia, which oversee about $11 billion in mutual funds, unit trusts and institutional accounts for overseas investors. Fidelity manages $117 billion of assets in the U.S.

"Bruce Johnstone's appointment is a key strategic move, reflecting our belief that investment success in the 1990s will depend on how well investors exploit the entire world of investment choices," said Edward C. Johnson 3rd, chairman and chief executive officer of Fidelity.

Mr. Johnstone said he asked Mr. Johnson for a new job because he wanted to try something different. "After you manage money for 19 years, it's not as much of a novel or learning experience as it was in the beginning. The variety isn't quite the same." In his new position, he won't manage investments, but develop management talent and "create the kind of environment overseas that we have here."

Fidelity said that from Dec. 31, 1971, to Aug. 31 of this year, the Equity-Income fund under Mr. Johnstone had a return of 1,108.87% vs. 596.11% for the Standard & Poor's 500. This performance ranks it 18th among the 229 funds in Lipper Analytical Services' "general equity" fund category during the period.

More recently, Mr. Johnstone's fund hasn't fared as well. Eric Kobren, a newsletter publisher who tracks Fidelity, said the Equity-Income Fund is down 13.4% year to date, slightly worse than the S&P 500 drop of about 10%. Last year, the fund rose 18.7%, well below the S&P's increase of 31.7%. He said Fidelity is starting a second Equity-Income fund because it is hard to attract investors by advertising the performance of the existing one.
