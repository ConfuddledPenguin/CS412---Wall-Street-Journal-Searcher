U.S. and Iran Move Toward Settlement Of Weapons Dispute

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- The U.S. has agreed to pay $200 million to Tehran as an interim step in resolving a decade-old dispute involving American weapons bought by Iran but held in the U.S. The two nations signed an agreement in the Hague, Netherlands, according to a U.S. official here, and the settlement will be submitted to a nine-member tribunal for approval. The U.S. official said approval could come as soon as this week. The official added that the $200 million will be deposited by Iran into a special account at the Dutch Central Bank from which outstanding claims made by the U.S. against Iran would be paid.

The $200 million is part of an Iranian claim involving about $400 million in military equipment Tehran owned that was in the U.S. at the time of the Iranian revolution. The equipment, impounded after the 1979 seizing of the U.S. Embassy in Tehran, includes one combat aircraft, several helicopters, a submarine, missiles and spare parts.

The U.S. contends that because the equipment was used and in the U.S. for repair at the time it was impounded, it is worth far less than Iran claims. The U.S. also contends that the balance owed is $93 million, plus interest, and negotiations between the two nations are continuing.

Iran has said the total military-sales claim, which involves about 1,200 items, is valued at about $11 billion. The U.S. contends that the figure is far lower. The tribunal, set up in 1980, is the only official point of contact between the two nations. The tribunal has three judges appointed by the U.S., three judges appointed by Iran, and three neutral judges appointed by the six other judges.

The U.S. has denied that the negotiations, which have been going on for a decade, are related to the current crisis in the Persian Gulf.
