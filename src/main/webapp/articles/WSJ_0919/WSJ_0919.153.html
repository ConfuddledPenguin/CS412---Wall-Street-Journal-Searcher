House Votes to Cut Spending for SDI To $2.3 Billion

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- The House voted to reduce spending for the Strategic Defense Initiative to $2.3 billion in fiscal 1991 -- the lowest level in more than five years and nearly half of the Bush administration's request for the ambitious program. The 225-189 roll call reflected a concerted effort by the Democratic leadership and underscored the continued vulnerability of major strategic weapons despite the current crisis in the Mideast.

The Iraqi threat was invoked by both sides in the debate to bolster their opposing arguments, and a large portion of the SDI savings is expected to be reallocated to meet conventional-force needs in the Persian Gulf operation. An amendment being crafted by House Armed Services Committee Chairman Les Aspin (D., Wis.) would add back an estimated $1 billion elsewhere in the bill, including funds for anti-chemical weapons equipment, fast sealift capability, and hospital and ammunition supply ships.

Although the final margin last night was narrower and more partisan than a comparable vote last year, the cut is significantly deeper. The $2.3 billion level is less than the House approved as early as fiscal 1986, and represents a nearly 40% cut from the $3.8 billion authorization for the current fiscal year ending Sept. 30.

Critics argued that the reduced level condemns the program to research forever, and the Senate, which has approved $3.7 billion for SDI next year, is certain to demand a higher figure. In this context, the vote was directed at buying some leverage in the coming negotiations between the two chambers. But even if the House moves more than halfway toward the Senate, the final cut would be the deepest yet sustained by the program.

The administration's own SDI budget was implicitly rejected by a 338-83 margin. As much as conservatives cited the threat of Iraqi arms, opponents countered that the Pentagon had failed to give sufficient priority to the relatively small portion of the program devoted to developing systems that intercept low-flying missiles such as the Scud used by Iraq.

Within SDI, an estimated $125 million is allocated this year for these so-called theater defense programs, and while the administration had requested $132 million for fiscal 1991, it was a Senate Democratic initiative that proposed to raise the level to $180 million next year. Within this figure, an estimated $42 million would be provided by the Senate for the Arrow missile project with Israel, and Raytheon Co. has a stake in related work to further upgrade its Patriot missile, which has an anti-tactical missile capability and is deployed with U.S. forces in the Mideast.

The House SDI provisions are neither as specific nor as ambitious as the Senate proposal, but language inserted by Rep. John Spratt (D., S.C.) cites the need for such interceptor missiles as one of the top priorities for a revamped program.

The debate on SDI came as the House moved toward final adoption -- likely today -- of the underlying $283 billion authorization bill for the Defense Department. Unlike the Senate, the measure would stop production of any additional B-2 bombers beyond the 15 already authorized, but rather than fight the issue on the House floor, Republican allies of the administration chose to avoid any vote yesterday that might influence negotiations during the conference between the two chambers.
