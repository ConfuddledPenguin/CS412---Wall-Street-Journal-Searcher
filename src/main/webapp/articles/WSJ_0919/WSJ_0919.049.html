Who's News: Maxwell to Retire as Fannie Mae Chief; Vice Chairman Johnson to Succeed Him

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- David Maxwell, who led the Federal National Mortgage Association's dramatic turnaround, plans to retire Jan. 31, and James Johnson, who joined Fannie Mae in January as vice chairman, will step up as chairman and chief executive officer. Mr. Maxwell, 60 years old, will be retiring one day shy of his 10th anniversary at Fannie Mae. "I've always felt that 10 years is about the right amount of time for a CEO of a large corporation to serve if he or she has fulfilled goals set out at the beginning," he told a press conference yesterday. When Mr. Maxwell took charge in 1981, Fannie Mae was a basket case, losing $190.4 million that year. Last year, the company reported net income of $807 million.

Fannie Mae -- a government-sponsored, publicly traded company -- buys mortgages from lenders, packages some into securities for sale to investors and holds the rest in its portfolio. Last year, its assets climbed to $124 billion.

Mr. Maxwell's efforts to strengthen the company's management and operations have won wide praise among analysts and investors. And Mr. Johnson, 46, isn't expected to bring any major change in philosophy or management style. Both men are known as bright, intense and politically savvy managers. Another stabilizing force, Roger Birk, Fannie Mae's president and chief operating officer, said he will remain with the company, and industry sources believe he will stay on for at least a year. Mr. Birk, a few months younger than Mr. Maxwell, had been chairman and chief executive of Merrill Lynch & Co., before joining Fannie Mae in 1987.

Mr. Johnson also brings to the top job Wall Street experience, having been a managing director at Shearson Lehman Hutton since 1985, before joining Fannie Mae last Jan. 1. He is well-connected in Washington, having been president of a Washington consulting firm and serving as executive assistant to Vice President Walter Mondale and chairman of Mr. Mondale's 1984 presidential campaign.

"I don't think Jim Johnson will skip a beat," said Robert Hottensen, an analyst with Goldman, & Sachs Co. "I think that David Maxwell has done two outstanding things: He has set the tone for the company's strong competitive position and has built one of the outstanding management teams in the mortgage finance business."

For his part, Mr. Johnson said he expects to continue Fannie Mae's commitments, including managing interest rates and credit risk, helping to solve the nation's housing problems and providing first-rate customer service.

Fannie Mae's stock closed at $27.50, down $1.50, yesterday in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.
