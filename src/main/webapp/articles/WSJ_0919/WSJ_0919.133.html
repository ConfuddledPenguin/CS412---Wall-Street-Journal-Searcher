Credit Markets: Plunge in Federal Funds Rate Spurs Rumors, Including Talk About Woes in Banking Industry

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 09/19/90

NEW YORK -- The bond market was rampant with rumors yesterday as traders and investors tried to explain why the federal funds rate suddenly plunged. The federal funds rate is the rate banks charge each other for overnight loans and is often used by the Federal Reserve to signal changes in monetary policy. The rate has hovered around 8% for months, but yesterday fell as low as 6 1/4%. On Monday the rate closed at 1%.

Many analysts said yesterday's decline doesn't signal that the Fed is reducing short-term interest rates, but instead reflects technical factors. Specifically, analysts said that the falling federal funds rate is a sign that major commercial banks are flush with cash and don't need to borrow short-term funds as they approach the end of a two-week settlement period.

In recent months, the federal funds rate has risen sharply near the end of a settlement period as banks scrambled to meet their reserve requirements by borrowing heavily.

"The banks have more reserves than they need," said Kevin Flanagan, a money market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. He said the banks borrowed earlier in the period in an effort to avoid being squeezed again as they have been in the past.

But there were rumors that the decline in the federal funds rate reflects the inability of one or more big New York banks to borrow in the fed markets as a result of perceptions that banks are having credit problems. According to these traders, some big New York banks are expected to borrow needed funds at the Fed's discount window instead of through the market.

"There was suddenly just no demand for federal funds," said one fed funds trader in New York. "It happened so quickly. We may find out in a few days that something went wrong, either a wire transfer problem or a major borrower was pushed out of the market or maybe something else," he said.

In trading, bond prices ended little changed after a day of seesaw trading. Bonds fell nearly 1/2 point, about $5 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount, in early trading after the government reported that the consumer price index rose 0.8% in August. The CPI, a measure of inflation, "knocked the market lower, and that set the tone for a lousy day," said Robert Chandross, chief economist at Lloyds Bank.

A sharply higher trade deficit also hurt market sentiment. The Commerce Department reported that the import-export gap widened to a seasonally adjusted $9.33 billion in July from a revised $5.34 billion in June.

In addition, traders said that a power outage at the Chicago Board of Trade suspended dealings in the Treasury bond futures pits and may have exacerbated the early losses.

The bond market retraced the losses when the federal funds declined as dealers rushed to cover short-sale positions. These dealers had sold borrowed securities earlier this week in hopes that yesterday's inflation report would push prices lower and they could buy back the securities at a lower price and pocket the difference. After prices dropped sharply early yesterday, dealers saw the softening federal funds rate as an opportunity to close out their positions and take profits.

Municipal Bonds

It was a busy day in the tax-exempt market as more than $900 million of new securities were offered.

But the avalanche of supply appeared to overwhelm demand and pushed prices of tax-exempt issues as much as 1/4 point lower.

Traders said that while retail buyers have been supporting many of the new issues, institutional investors have been reluctant to purchase securities in the primary market and instead are scooping up bargains in the secondary market.

"They can sit back and buy {bonds} cheaper," on the secondary market, said one trader. "They don't see any upside" to the market, he said.

Weaker than expected demand forced underwriters at Dillon Read & Co. to reprice and restructure a $200 million Connecticut general obligation bond issue.

The repricing raised some yields by 10 basis points, eliminated serial bonds due in 2007 and 2009 and created a term bond due in 2010. Under the restructured terms, the bonds were priced to yield between 6% for notes due in 1991 to 7.25% for bonds due in 2010.

Among other new issues, New York State Dormitory Authority offered $314 million City University system consolidated second general resolution revenue bonds. The securities were priced to yield between 6.40% for notes due in 1992 to 8.05% for bonds due in 2017.

Chase Securities Inc., a unit of Chase Manhattan Corp., was in the process of pricing $110 million of Vermont Housing Finance Authority bonds. Pricing details weren't completed by the end of the day.

Ahead on the calendar, the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey will sell $100 million consolidated notes via competitive bid at 11 a.m. EDT today.

Corporate Bonds

Rumors that Chase Manhattan Corp. was having problems selling its commercial paper, shook the banking sector, pushing up yields of many financial issues.

Although Chase denied it was having difficulty placing the short-term corporate IOUs, yields on its bonds rose.

Corporate bond traders said Chase Manhattan's 10% subordinated notes, maturing in 1999, were yielding 13.40% yesterday, about 30 basis points wider than Monday.

Chase Manhattan bonds had tightened about 20 basis points last week after the company reset the rate on $200 million of subordinated notes at more than 13%. The bonds, however, returned to their existing levels yesterday as a result of continuing concerns about the bank's financial well-being, traders said.

Those concerns as well as worries about the banking industry in general depressed bonds of other financial institutions.

Chemical Banking Corp.'s 9.75% subordinated capital notes, due in 1999, were yielding 14.125%, about 20 basis points wider than Monday.

In the broader corporate bond market, prices of high-grade corporate issues were barely changed while junk bonds, or high-risk, high-yield bonds, ended lower.

The junk bonds of Carter Hawley Hale Stores continued to plunge. Like many retailers, Carter Hawley has been hurt by reports that consumer spending is expected to be light this Christmas.

Carter Hawley's 12 1/4% securities fell 6 3/4 points to 62 on the New York Stock Exchange while the company's 12.5% securities fell six points to 52.

AmBase Corp.'s junk bonds, which had been trading in the mid-90s a month ago, plunged 10 points to 40. Market participants said that they knew of no news developments that would account for the sharp decline.

But junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. rose even as the broader high-yield market posted losses of between 1/4 to 3/8 point. Most notably, RJR's 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures surged 1 3/8 point to 89 1/8 on the New York Stock Exchange.The 17.375% convertible pay-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, rose 7/8 to 112 7/8.

A spokesman for RJR Nabisco declined to comment on the rise in either issue.

Treasury Securities

Treasurys ended unchanged after a day of seesaw trading.

The benchmark 30-year bond finished at a price of 96 23/32 to yield 9.06%, the same level as Monday. Two-year notes ended unchanged at a price of 100 5/32 to yield 8.02%.

Treasury bill rates also were unchanged at Monday's levels, with the discount rate on three-month bills ending at 7.38% and the rate on six-month bills ending at 7.29%.

Today, the focus of the market will likely turn to Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan, who is scheduled to present the central bank's economic outlook to Congress.

Investors and dealers will be listening closely for any indication that the Fed is moving toward easing monetary policy. Mr. Greenspan will begin speaking at 10 a.m. EDT.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities ended unchanged to slightly lower after recouping most of their losses posted yesterday morning on news of a rise in the core rate of consumer price inflation.

In the secondary mortgage market, Government National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% issue for October delivery fell 1/32 at 98 3/8, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp.'s 9 1/2% Gold participation certificates were unchanged at 98 7/8, and Federal National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% securities were flat at 98 21/32.

The mortgage-backed securities market showed little response to the continuing sales from Resolution Trust Corp. The thrift bailout agency reportedly sold more than $100 million of seasoned Ginnie Maes yesterday after unloading nearly $200 million of seasoned Ginnie Maes and other securities Monday.

In the primary market, a $330 million issue of Freddie Mac Remic mortgage securities, collateralized with Freddie Mac 9% securities, was priced by J.P. Morgan Securities Inc.

Also, a $75 million Alaska Housing Finance Corp. collateralized mortgage obligation issue was offered by First Boston Corp.

Foreign Bonds

The Japanese government bond market managed to end firmer despite continued bearish factors. Traders said the last-minute rise in Japanese bond prices reflected short-sale covering.

In a short sale, dealers and professional traders sell borrowed securities and later seek to make a profit by replacing what was borrowed with cheaper securities. Essentially, these dealers are betting that prices will fall.

Japan's benchmark No. 119 10-year bond ended at 81.22, up 0.05 points, to yield 8.55%. (When calculated using the semiannual compound method, the yield on the No. 119 ended at 7.79%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)

Traders, however, said widespread bearish sentiment remains as key interest rates continue to rise. The yield on Euroyen interest rates futures contracts ended at 8.82% yesterday, up from 8.78% the day before. The long-term prime rate is expected to be boosted for the third month in a row, to almost 9% for October from 8.5% for September.
