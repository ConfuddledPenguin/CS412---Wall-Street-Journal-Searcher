Top Leaders Take Over Talks To Cut Deficit

  • Author: David Wessel and Jackie Calmes
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- Top congressional leaders took over budget negotiations with the White House, after lower-ranking lawmakers failed to resolve the key remaining issues in marathon bargaining. "The talks are not collapsed," House Republican Leader Robert Michel said, after Democratic leaders and their GOP counterparts met with top Bush administration negotiators. "We are just moving them to a different stage."

Negotiators from both parties said they narrowed their differences substantially during 11 days of closed-door talks at Andrews Air Force Base near here. Indeed, the offers put on the table late Monday have many common elements, such as a new tax on luxury goods and a goal of $120 billion of spending cuts in benefits programs over the next five years.

But, as several people close to the talks have been assuming for months, the final -- and most difficult -- calls now must be made at the top ranks by Senate Democratic Leader George Mitchell, House Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Mich.) and President Bush himself.

Democrats insist that the major stumbling block to the long-sought accord to reduce the federal budget deficit is the administration's desire to cut the capital-gains tax rate without also raising income tax rates on wealthier Americans. "The problem . . . centers around the insistence of the administration to reduce the capital-gains tax, " Rep. Foley said.

Republicans, while sensitive to Democrats' criticism that the capital-gains cut is a break for the rich, emphasized that other major issues remain, including the rules by which a five-year fiscal blueprint will be enforced and the details of spending cuts. "To suggest that we were very close, except for that {capital-gains taxes}, was exceptionally inaccurate," a Republican participant said.

With only 12 days left before across-the-board spending cuts are imposed by the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law, Sen. Mitchell of Maine and Rep. Foley are to meet again today with White House Budget Director Richard Darman, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady and White House Chief of Staff John Sununu. Also participating will be the top Republican leaders, Sen. Robert Dole of Kansas and Illinois Rep. Michel, as well as House Democratic Leader Richard Gephardt of Missouri.

Excluded will be some negotiators with particularly inveterate views, including Sen. Robert Byrd (D., W.Va.), Sen. James Sasser (D., Tenn.), Sen. Phil Gramm (R., Texas) and Rep. Newt Gingrich (R., Ga.).

Even if the high-level talks produce an accord, however, ratification by the full Congress isn't guaranteed. Just yesterday, Democrats on the Ways and Means Committee privately complained to Chairman Dan Rostenkowski (D., Ill.) that budget negotiators weren't testing their proposals sufficiently with their colleagues, and warned that a deficit-reduction package might be rejected as a result.

Speaking to reporters aboard Air Force One, Mr. Bush said, "Everybody . . . agrees that they've come quite a way, but it's not done, by a long shot." He avoided any criticism of Democrats in campaign speeches yesterday, but reiterated his eagerness to cut capital-gains taxes. "This is not a tax break for the rich," he said in Denver. "It will create jobs."

In a sixth round of proposals, Democrats and Republicans on Monday night traded stripped-down plans that were crafted as much for public consumption as for each party. Democrats said they would give up most of their spending and tax initiatives if Republicans gave up the quest for lower capital-gains taxes. Both proposals would reduce the deficit by about $50 billion in fiscal 1991, which begins Oct. 1, and by about $500 billion over five years.

On taxes, the basic division is how to offset a cut in the capital-gains tax rate. Democrats proposed a 20% income-tax surcharge on couples with incomes of more than $200,000 and individuals with incomes above $125,000, effectively raising their marginal tax rate to 33.6% from the current top nominal rate of 28%.

Republicans concede that any compromise will have to raise taxes on the rich and have offered several alternatives, including limiting total tax deductions for upper-income taxpayers. Their last offer was to limit to $10,000 the deduction for state and local income taxes for taxpayers with incomes above $200,000.

Several Republicans complained privately that Sen. Mitchell, long a proponent of higher tax rates on the rich, was standing in the way of a compromise.

Though both parties would raise taxes by a net of $130 billion over five years, Democrats propose additional tax increases to cover expanded proposed tax breaks for lower-income taxpayers, as well as a variety of user fees they didn't count as taxes. "The Democratic package has too many taxes in it," Sen. Pete Domenici (R., N.M.), a negotiator, said.

In a previously undisclosed area of agreement on taxes, both parties proposed to forbid corporations from deducting interest on tax underpayments, raising about $4.5 billion next year. The two parties also agreed to increase taxes on life insurance companies by $2 billion in the first year and by $10 billion over five years.

Both parties endorsed a new 10% tax on luxury goods, to be applied to the purchase price in excess of $30,000 for cars, boats and yachts; $5,000 for jewelry; $1,000 for electronics; $500 for furs and the entire price of private aircraft under 5,000 pounds. And both parties support an increase in taxes on liquor, beer and wine, to yield about $14 billion over five years, though the details weren't described in the proposals.

Democrats proposed increasing the tobacco tax, now 16 cents on a pack of cigarettes, by four cents a pack in 1991 and an additional four cents in 1993. Though Democrats said Republicans have tentatively agreed, several Republicans deny this -- and their proposal doesn't show any such increase.

Though both sides would raise energy taxes, the Democrats would raise $49 billion over five years through higher energy taxes. The Republicans, who would limit increased energy taxes to refined petroleum products, would raise $7 billion.

On defense spending, both parties are converging on a plan crafted by Sen. Sam Nunn (D., Ga.), chairman of the Armed Services Committee. The administration estimates the plan would reduce projected Pentagon spending by about $175 billion over five years; some Democrats say it would save even more. In what remains an important sticking point, however, Democrats are willing to lock in the defense cuts for only the next two years, while Republicans want to set totals for the next five years.

Democrats, in short, want to leave open the possibility of tapping the defense budget to fund domestic spending programs in future years, while Republicans want to limit increases in domestic spending, other than benefit or entitlement programs, to the rate of inflation.

On benefit programs, both parties said they want to cut projected spending by about $120 billion over five years, though Republicans said the Democrats' proposals fall short of that mark. Neither plan would touch Social Security benefits.

In the latest proposals, Republicans proposed deeper cuts in Medicare, calling for, among other things, increasing the deductible on coverage for doctors' services, known as Part B, to $150 from the current $75 and increasing the deductible with inflation. They would raise the premium for Medicare, now at $28.60 a month, so that the premium covers 30% of the total costs instead of 25%, as the law now provides. Details of the Democratic plan weren't available.

Both parties also proposed substantial cuts in farm subsidies over five years. Democrats would reduce them by $11.8 billion from levels projected in the farm bill that has passed both houses of Congress. Republicans would reduce subsidies by $13.3 billion. Sponsors of the farm bill explicitly delayed making any substantial cutbacks in subsidies, pending the outcome of the budget summit.

As they have from the start, the Republicans pushed for a substantially tougher revision of the Gramm-Rudman law than Democrats are prepared to accept to enforce the terms of any budget accord. "Their current enforcement {plan} is, `Trust me.'" Rep. Gingrich complained.

With the White House forecasting a fiscal 1991 deficit of about $250 billion, neither Democrats nor Republicans would try to meet the law's $64 billion deficit target; rather, they are expected to amend the law to avoid triggering across-the-board spending cuts.

Without any change in the law, spending cuts amounting to more than $85 billion will be triggered on Oct. 1. Though there is substantial skepticism in Congress, the administration vows it won't agree to waive the law unless there is a budget agreement.

"The clock is ticking," Mr. Bush said.

The Budget Process

Where the Negotiators Agree

-- Raise taxes by about $130 billion net over five years, including:

New 10% tax on luxury goods

Higher taxes on liquor, beer and wine

Increase in energy taxes

New tax on ozone-depleting chemicals

-- Cut projected spending on benefit programs by about $120 billion over five years.

-- Hold increase in domestic spending next year to no more than the rate of inflation.

Where They Disagree

-- How to raise taxes on the rich to offset cut in capital-gains taxes.

-- Whether to increase tax breaks for low-income Americans.

-- How much to cut Medicare spending.

-- How to enforce the five-year agreement.
