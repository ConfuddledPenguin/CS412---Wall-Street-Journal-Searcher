Letters to the Editor: Horrible Deterrents to Horrible Threat

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/19/90

There has been a justified concern over the prospect that Iraq might employ its chemical weapons against the forces of the U.S. and its allies. No one questions that Iraq has a large supply of various chemical agents that it can deliver by a number of means. Possibly its most potent weapon is the Soviet Scud B missile, which is capable of delivering such agents to a distance of 400 miles. When pressed during the war with Iran, Iraq employed these weapons to produce as many as 5,000 casualties a day. It has also used them against its own citizens, the Kurds. With these facts in mind, the issue that arises in the current altercation is how to discourage this use. There are a number of options to be considered: As a defensive measure, military personnel could be sealed in a protective garment. Against some agents it is doubtful that this measure would be effective. In any case the garments would impair troop performance. It is not surprising that such a solution was not well received by NATO when it was faced with an impressive Soviet chemical- and biological-weapons threat. Further, daytime temperatures in the Middle East deserts can exceed 120 degrees, creating an unacceptable environment for the use of such clothing. For these reasons this option should be rejected.

The second option would involve a U.S. deployment of a chemical-weapons stockpile of sufficient capability. For this solution the U.S. has little stomach. In fact, the current aim of the U.S. administration is to eliminate stockpiles of chemical weapons throughout the world.

The purpose of the third option would be to discourage the Iraqi employment of any force, including the use of chemical weapons, by the threat of the use of nuclear weapons. Were the Iraqis not so dissuaded, the use of nuclear weapons against Iraqi ground forces would best serve to defeat such an attack. An enhanced radiation weapon is proposed. It would be distinctly different from other strategic nuclear weapons. Their yields would be much lower. Their predominant effect would be to destroy living organisms while producing minimum physical destruction. In this regard they would resemble the effects produced by chemical agents. However, unlike these agents, their employment would not be influenced by the weather. Finally, the weapons function so that within a given circle all living, exposed matter is destroyed; beyond this circle the lethal effect diminishes rapidly.

As an immediate option, the nominal five-kiloton enhanced-radiation warhead produced for the NATO Lance missile could be employed. (These warheads were never deployed in Europe.) If airburst over a battlefield, the area promptly lethal to personnel in a tank would be about one square mile. Beyond an area of five square miles there would be no effect on exposed troops. With some effort such a weapon could be readied almost immediately for either missile or aircraft delivery. For such a weapon to be effective it would need both to be advertised widely as a distinctive chemical-warfare deterrent and to be endorsed strongly by the president.

Those who find such a suggestion repulsive might well change their opinions were an Iraqi chemical attack to result in the death of thousands of U.S. troops.

Robert G. Shreffler

Santa Fe, N.M.

(From 1967 to 1969, the author served as NATO's first director of nuclear-weapon planning, and has spent his professional life in development of nuclear weapons at Los Alamos.)
