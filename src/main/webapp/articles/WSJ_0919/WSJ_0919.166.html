Pentagon Raises Its Estimates On Iraqi Forces --- Total Put at 360,000 Troops In Kuwait Area; Jordan Criticized Over Parley

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- Iraq has significantly increased the number of troops and tanks it has deployed in and around Kuwait, but the bigger force still appears to be in a defensive posture, the Pentagon said. Iraq now has 360,000 troops and 2,800 tanks in occupied Kuwait and nearby areas of Iraq, said Pentagon spokesman Pete Williams. Those estimates are up from the Pentagon's estimates of 265,000 troops and 2,200 tanks two weeks ago.

Mr. Williams said that while there are more Iraqi tanks in the area, some of them have been pulled back to positions farther away from Kuwait's border with Saudi Arabia, where some 130,000 American soldiers are massed along with forces from several other nations. Still, the higher overall troop and tank estimate is significant, because it shows a steady growth in the size of the force Iraq could immediately use if hostilities erupt.

At the same time, the Bush administration sent a series of mixed signals to Jordan, a neighbor of Iraq that has maintained good ties with Baghdad since Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait. The State Department reproached Jordan for playing host to a conference last weekend of Arab radicals that openly called for attacks on American targets and the overthrow of Egyptian President Hosni Mubarak.

"We were surprised and dismayed that this conference took place, and we have told the Jordanians so," said Margaret Tutwiler, the State Department spokeswoman. "We want to work with Jordan during and after this crisis in the Gulf. Hosting the conference is very hard for us to understand."

At the same time, the State Department reported that Jordan still appears to be receiving about 40% of its oil from Iraq, a technical violation of the international trade embargo. But the department seemed sympathetic toward Jordan's need for Iraqi oil. Ms. Tutwiler emphasized that Jordan had reduced its dependence on Iraqi oil from 90% before the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, and she said she hoped Jordan could cut its use of Iraqi oil further as it gets new economic aid from other countries.

Separately, the Pentagon disclosed that American sailors stopped and boarded a Soviet cargo ship as it carried goods to the Jordanian port of Aqaba. American forces determined that the cargo wasn't bound for Iraq and let the ship travel on to Jordan, the Pentagon said.

The U.S. also announced that it is sending three military aircraft to fly relief missions to help refugees from Iraq and Kuwait who have fled to Jordan. The planes will carry in blankets, cots and other supplies for the refugees and carry some of the refugees to their homelands.

The increase in Iraqi troop strength in and around Kuwait appears to be at least partly a result of a slight restructuring of forces by Iraq as it consolidates its positions in the area. Mr. Williams, the Pentagon spokesman, said that the increase was caused partly by the addition of troops to "round out" Iraqi units sent in earlier and partly because infantry units, which use more troops, are being moved forward as armored units are moved back from the Saudi border.

"There has been a gradual buildup in Iraqi forces since this operation began," Mr. Williams said. "There has been no sudden surge. It's certainly a continued buildup."

Separately, Central Intelligence Agency Director William Webster warned in a New York speech that Iraqi president Saddam Hussein has "recently revived" his ties "to those Palestinian terrorists who have gone after Western interests."

Mr. Webster said "what intelligence has tracked and reported in the past year about Iraq's involvement in terrorism concerns us." He said American intelligence has determined that Saddam Hussein now "has both strong ties to Palestinian terrorists and an elaborate network within his own intelligence service capable of carrying out terrorist attacks."

The CIA chief specifically cited Saddam Hussein's ties to terrorist groups led by Abu Nidal and Abu Abbas. Iraq's renewed ties to terrorists had been reported, but Mr. Webster's is the strongest on-the-record confirmation that U.S. intelligence is concerned about it.

Mr. Webster also disclosed that the CIA has set up special task forces to work on the Persian Gulf crisis, and is monitoring Iraqi troops, ships, terrorists and "the condition and status of the foreign hostages being held in Iraq and Kuwait."
