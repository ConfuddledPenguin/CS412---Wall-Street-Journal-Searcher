What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/19/90

THE U.S. BOOSTED its estimate of Iraqi troop strength in and around Kuwait. The Pentagon said Baghdad has 360,000 troops and 2,800 tanks in Kuwait and nearby areas of Iraq, showing a steady increase, but the forces still appear to be in a defensive position. Meanwhile, Washington reproached Jordan for playing host to a meeting of Arab radicals, and Bush said Iraq's military mustn't benefit from any humanitarian aid shipments. In Paris, nine European nations demanded that the U.N. trade embargo on Iraq be extended to the air as well as the sea.

U.S. sailors stopped and boarded a Soviet ship headed for Jordan, and let the vessel proceed after determining that the cargo wasn't bound for Iraq.

---

THE PENTAGON SAID it will eliminate or reduce operations at 150 foreign sites.

The cutbacks at the mostly small foreign facilities in 10 nations, mainly in Europe, are designed to carry out pledges to shrink the size of the armed forces in response to budget cuts, and to meet force reductions that will be required in Europe under the conventional arms control treaty expected to be signed in November. Defense officials said the closings, to take place over several years, aren't linked to the U.S. troop buildup in Saudi Arabia.

The announcement is certain to be welcomed in Moscow, which is pulling all its troops out of Europe and had pressed the U.S. to state its intentions.

---

South Africa said it will charge Winnie Mandela, wife of African National Congress leader Nelson Mandela, with kidnapping and assault. The charges involve the alleged 1988 abduction and beating of four young men, one of whom was found dead. Her bodyguard was sentenced to death in the case; Mrs. Mandela has denied wrongdoing.

The ANC began an emergency meeting on township wars that have killed 760 people in five weeks. The anti-apartheid group has accused security forces of waging war on its people by siding with the Zulu-based Inkatha movement in factional fighting.

---

Ryzhkov told the Soviet national legislature he would resign as prime minister if the lawmakers adopted a 500-day plan to create a free market. During a debate in the 542-member Supreme Soviet, legislators attacked all three economic programs that had been proposed in the past week.

Solzhenitsyn urged a populist revival of democracy and spirituality in Russia without Western cultural "garbage" or capitalist exploitation. In a Soviet newspaper article, the exiled Nobel laureate wrote of his desire for a unified Slavic nation.

---

Atlanta was awarded the 1996 Summer Games by the International Olympic Committee, a move that could generate as much as $3.5 billion in revenue and 84,000 jobs for the local and state economy. City officials estimate they will have to spend more than $400 million to construct Olympic facilities.

---

The EPA said nine major companies have agreed to substantially reduce emissions of toxic chemicals at 40 plants in 14 states. EPA chief Reilly also said the agency is issuing a guidebook on asbestos emphasizing that removal isn't necessarily the best way to reduce potential exposure.

---

Poland's Jaruzelski announced his intention to resign the presidency early and clear the way for free elections to make Poland a full democracy. Parliament is to meet tomorrow to debate the timing of Jaruzelski's resignation and the scheduling of elections.

---

Shamir contended that the U.S. isn't doing enough to broker peace between Israel and its Arab neighbors. The Israeli prime minister said that ending his nation's state of war with Arab countries took precedence over reaching an agreement with the Palestinians under Israeli rule.

---

The U.S. is prepared to give up its bases in the Philippines but wants a decision delayed until the next century, chief American negotiator Richard Armitage said as talks opened in Manila regarding the future of the U.S.-run military facilities.

---

Liberal opposition to Souter was less than expected as confirmation hearings for the Supreme Court nominee neared completion. Most of the testimony against Souter at the Senate Judiciary Committee hearings came from abortion-rights groups and women's rights advocates.

---

The House voted to reduce spending for the Strategic Defense Initiative to $2.3 billion in fiscal 1991 -- the lowest level in more than five years and nearly half of the Bush administration's request for the program.

---

The U.S. extended for another year a longstanding trade embargo against Vietnam, a Treasury Department spokesman said. The action was expected to disappoint Hanoi officials, who had been hoping that a new dialogue with the U.S. and progress toward peace in Cambodia would prompt Washington to lift the embargo.

---

"Miss Saigon" will open on Broadway in April, after being canceled because of a dispute with New York actors over plans to cast a white British actor in a Eurasian role. Cameron Mackintosh, the London musical's producer, and Actors' Equity officials didn't provide details of their accord.

---

Bulgaria banned food exports in an effort to improve domestic supplies in time for winter. Sugar, cheese, cooking oil and detergent have been rationed since late summer in most parts of Bulgaria as the government attempts to alleviate shortages.

---

The Salvadoran government ended a second round of fruitless negotiations with guerrillas as peace prospects dimmed. During six days of talks in Costa Rica, the two sides were unable to reach an accord on the future role of El Salvador's armed forces.

---

A top State Department official flew to Monrovia on a fact-finding mission and met with Liberian rebel leader Prince Johnson. Herman Cohen, assistant secretary of state for African affairs, said he hoped to meet later with rival guerrilla leader Charles Taylor to discuss the nation's civil war.
