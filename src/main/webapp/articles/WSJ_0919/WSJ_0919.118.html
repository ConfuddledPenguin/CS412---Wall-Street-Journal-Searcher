R.H. Macy Is Planning to Buy Back Junk Bonds at Discount, Executive Says

  • Author: George Anders
  • Date: 09/19/90

NEW YORK -- A top R.H. Macy & Co. executive said the retailer has drafted plans to buy back sizable amounts of its junk bonds, which have sunk in recent weeks. A big bond buy-back, at a hefty discount from face value, could significantly cut Macy's $600 million annual cash interest bill on its $5.8 billion in debt. Likely sources of money for an open-market bond repurchase are bank loans and the possible sale of as much as a 20% equity stake in Macy. If the retailer carries out its plans, that will almost certainly be seen as upbeat news in the financial community.

Bond analysts and traders have grown increasingly worried about Macy's financial health in the past month, amid signs that U.S. retail sales are slumping. The company's 14.5% bonds of 1998 currently trade at 54 cents on the dollar, and lion annual cash interest bill on its $5.8 billion in debt. Macy executive vice president Myron Ullman said in an interview that the company is looking at "five or six" different ways to finance a big bond repurchase. Mr. Ullman said Macy directors, at a board meeting Monday, were shown an extensive roadmap for how the company could buy large amounts of its bonds.

Macy has about $1.6 billion face value of junk bonds outstanding. They were issued soon after the retailer was taken private in a 1986 leveraged buy-out.

This summer, Macy sold a token 1% equity stake to a real estate developer for $15 million. Macy used part of the proceeds to retire $45 million face value of its 16.5% discount bonds of 2006, which traded then at about 20 cents on the dollar, Mr. Ullman said. Macy also bought about $5 million of its 14.5% bonds of 2001, which were trading at about 45 cents on the dollar.

Macy wants to retire more junk bonds the same way, Mr. Ullman said. "This is a great opportunity," he asserted. "We never dreamed we would have the opportunity to buy bonds at such a discount."

Macy, though, could face a tough time lining up financing. The company's two lead banks, Manufacturers Hanover Corp. and Bankers Trust New York Corp., recently lent money for a comparable bond-repurchase program by RJR Nabisco Inc., but did so only after long negotiations and the payment of unusually high loan fees.

Macy's other likely source of cash is a sizable equity infusion. The company's owners include such deep-pocketed companies and individuals as General Electric Co., with a 20.2% stake; Loews Corp., with a 12.1% stake; Mutual Shares Corp., with a 12.1% stake, and developer Alfred Taubman, with a 4.1% stake.

Mr. Ullman reaffirmed that Macy would consider selling a 20% stake in the company for $300 million. That price -- five times what Macy's original equity investors paid in 1986 -- is in line with the company's asking price in previous talks with potential buyers. It is unclear, though, whether a new or existing investor would pay that much.

Last week, several junk-bond analysts cut their estimates of operating profit for Macy's Christmas quarter or for the company's fiscal year ended next July 31. One of them, Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Corp. analyst Ray Garea, cited "the softening retail environment."

Even though Macy's bonds are as cheap as ever, Mr. Garea said, his short-term outlook for the company has switched to "negative" from neutral. Similarly, J.P. Morgan & Co. analyst Michael Cha said he is lowering his "worst-case" estimate of Macy's Christmas quarter operating profit by more than 10%.

As recently as last July, Macy's internal forecasts called for 2% year-to-year growth in same-store sales for the six months ending Jan. 31. But because of signs that retail sales are slowing, Mr. Ullman said, Macy now expects flat same-store sales in the period. Even that forecast may be optimistic, he conceded.

Mr. Ullman reiterated Macy's earlier forecast that operating profit -- or profit before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization -- would total $654 million for the six months ending next Jan. 31. He acknowledged that many analysts regard that target as unachievable in the current retailing slump.

"Before the Kuwait invasion and the recession in the U.S., Macy was on target for a profit recovery," said Barrie Littel, an analyst at First Boston Corp. "Now anyone realistic has to trim their numbers."

Last year, Macy turned in a disappointing $416 million operating profit for the six months ended Jan. 31.

Even if the Christmas selling season isn't much better than last year's, Mr. Ullman said, Macy is controlling its inventory more tightly and should show improved profit margins. He also said Macy's reduced buying of merchandise is leaving it with plenty of unused bank credit, which helps build confidence among shippers.
