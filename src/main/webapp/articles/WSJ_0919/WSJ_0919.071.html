Inside Track: Time Warner Has a Bull For a Chief

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 09/19/90

NEW YORK -- Steven V. Ross, chairman and chief executive officer of Time Warner Inc., has been bottom-fishing the sunken stock of the media and publishing conglomerate. Since July 23, Mr. Ross has spent almost $40 million on Time Warner shares, according to insider filings with the Securities and Exchange Commission.

The purchases arguably rank Mr. Ross as the most bullish company chairman in the U.S. He has spent more money on shares of his company than any other top corporate official in the country so far this year, according to Invest/Net, a North Miami, Fla., concern that tracks insider filings.

But then, Mr. Ross has a lot of money. Under terms of Time's takeover of Warner Communications Inc., Mr. Ross, then Warner's chairman and chief executive, got $193 million, which included compensation promised to him under his Warner employment contract. He also got options for 1.8 million Time Warner shares exercisable at $150 a share. The deal also called for Mr. Ross to be guaranteed a base salary of $800,000 a year for 10 years, plus bonuses.

That 1989 takeover was controversial. Time, which had already agreed to merge with Warner, rejected a sweetened $200-a-share bid from Paramount Communications Inc. Back then, Time stock hit a record high of $182.75. But since the merger, the renamed Time Warner stock has plummeted to less than half of that.

In more than 70 separate purchases in late July and August, Mr. Ross bought 175,000 Time Warner common shares for an average of about $90 each, as well as 575,500 Class C and Class D preferred shares at an average of about $41 each, according to the company. The shares were purchased for his trust account.

So far, Mr. Ross's investment is under water. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Time Warner common closed at $77.625, down 25 cents. The Class C shares closed at $38.25, and the Class D, at $38.375.

Mr. Ross declined to comment on his recent spending spree. But at the company's 1990 annual meeting at New York's Ziegfeld Theatre, Mr. Ross told shareholders who questioned his salary that his stockholdings would ultimately prove even more profitable.

"I believe those 1.8 million shares of Time Warner stock at $150 a share are going to make me more money than I've ever made in my life," he said. At the time, Mr. Ross blamed the stock's sharp decline on the potential re-regulation of the cable-television industry. Time Warner is the parent company of HBO, among other cable enterprises.

A Time Warner spokeswoman said that Mr. Ross's decision to buy the shares "speaks for itself."

Jessica J. Reif, First Boston Corp. analyst, downgraded the company to a "hold" recommendation from a "buy" in February, citing Time Warner's $10.8 billion debt burden and the risk that regulation of the cable or television-syndication industries could harm those businesses.

Now, however, she says that "all the bad news is in the stock" and that either of two developments could prompt her to recommend its purchase -- a corporate restructing that would sell units to reduce debt, or "benign" legislation from Congress on regulation of the cable industry.

All of Mr. Ross's purchases were made in the open market. Top officials of companies more frequently buy by exercising stock options -- but Mr. Ross's options for 1.8 million shares at $150 each are all currently under water.

HIGH RATINGS: Sumner Redstone, chairman of Viacom Inc., purchased $1.4 million of its stock in several trades in August, according to SEC filings.

Mr. Redstone, chairman of National Amusements Inc., the majority shareholder in Viacom, has been aggressively accumulating shares of the Dedham, Mass., media company for roughly two years. But lately he has stepped up the pace of his buying, he said, because of the stock's weakness.

"All the stocks in the media industry took a hit, but I think it was indiscriminate and not rational," he said. "The drop of the stock does not reflect the operations of Viacom."

In American Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Viacom's common closed at $19.75 a share, down 37.5 cents. It traded above $32 a share last October.

Mr. Redstone spent most of the money, about $846,000, on Viacom's relatively new Class B nonvoting shares. The shares were created in June, the company said, to allow greater flexibility in acquisitions. Because some pension funds cannot hold nonvoting stock, the Class B Viacom shares are trading several points below the Class A, and "we see that as irrational," Mr. Redstone said.

Mr. Redstone paid an average of $19.68 a share for the Class A common and $16.68 for the Class B, according to SEC filings. The Class B shares closed at $17.875, unchanged, in composite trading yesterday on the Amex.

The purchases increased his and National Amusements' huge stake in the company by less than 0.1%.
