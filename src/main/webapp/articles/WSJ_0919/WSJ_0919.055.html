Atlanta Wins 1996 Summer Olympics; Games' Revenue Could Hit $3.5 Billion

  • Author: Glenn Ruffenach
  • Date: 09/19/90

ATLANTA -- The Olympics are returning to the U.S. The International Olympic Committee awarded the 1996 Summer Games to Atlanta, a move that could generate as much as $3.5 billion in revenue and some 84,000 jobs for the local and state economy.

Considered the darkest of horses when it began its bid 3 1/2 years ago -- primarily because Los Angeles held the Games in 1984 -- Atlanta defeated five other cities in what proved to be the most competitive bidding for the Olympics in decades.

Atlanta won the prize on the fifth ballot taken among the IOC's 86 members, who had gathered in Tokyo. Atlanta collected 51 votes compared with 35 for the runner-up of Athens, Greece. Athens had been the sentimental favorite because 1996 will mark the 100th anniversary of the first modern Olympics, which were held in Athens. The other competitors were Belgrade, Yugoslavia; Manchester, England; Melbourne, Australia; and Toronto.

"Right now, we're going to sit back and say, `Thank you,' do a little fishing, and get reacquainted with our spouses," said William Payne, president of the Atlanta Organizing Committee and the force behind the city's bid.

That might be all that Atlanta's organizers have time for. By most accounts, the city won the Games for three reasons: its incessant and highly successful lobbying of IOC members; the city's infrastructure, including numerous hotels and one of the world's largest airports; and the likelihood that Atlanta will generate a sizable surplus, part of which goes to the IOC.

What the city lacks, however, are many of the basic facilities needed to stage an Olympics. Atlanta estimates it will have to spend more than $400 million on new construction alone, including an 85,000-seat track-and-field stadium, housing for athletes, a swimming and diving site, and a velodrome for bicycling competition.

Moreover, problems that were all but drowned out in the clamor surrounding the city's bid -- including Atlanta's exploding crime rate and extensive homeless population -- will likely resurface as millions of dollars from TV networks and corporate sponsors pour into the Atlanta Organizing Committee's coffers.

For instance, within hours of the IOC's decision, Greenpeace Action, a local environmental group, called on the Games' organizers to "tie the growing problems of homelessness in Atlanta to the development of the Olympics," and to "achieve full housing in the city by 1996."

Perhaps the most difficult task Atlanta's organizers face in the coming months is simply abandoning their role as salesmen and adopting that of developers. "For three years, they've been trying to win friends and influence people," said John Bevilaqua, a vice president at the Los Angeles Olympics and now president of a sports marketing firm in Atlanta. "Now they'll be working on a dozen fronts: dealing with environmental impact studies; setting up a security plan; trying to find 100,000 volunteers -- you name it."

And while the 1996 Olympics are more than 2,000 days away, Atlanta's organizers may begin to feel some pressure in the not-too-distant future, according to one individual familiar with the process.

Barcelona, Spain, the site of the 1992 Summer Olympics, "will become the focus of the world and shelter {Atlanta} a bit," said Peter Ueberroth, head of the 1984 Games in Los Angeles. But "immediately afterward, the time will go quite quickly."
