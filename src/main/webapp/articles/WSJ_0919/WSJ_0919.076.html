Pentagon Begins to Reduce Presence Of Troops Abroad, Mainly in Europe

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 09/19/90

WASHINGTON -- The Pentagon, starting a gradual process of paring down the huge U.S. Cold-War troop presence abroad, said it will eliminate or reduce operations at 150 mostly small foreign sites, mainly in Europe. The cutbacks are designed to carry out earlier pledges to shrink the size of the armed forces in response to budget cuts, and to meet force reductions that will be required in Europe under the conventional arms control treaty expected to be signed in November. That treaty will require the U.S., the U.S.S.R., and 21 other countries to reduce the number of their tanks and other weapons in Europe.

Defense officials said the closings aren't connected with the current U.S. troop buildup in Saudi Arabia, which is designed to force Iraq to withdraw from Kuwait. Indeed, administration officials said the bases closings would be so gradual that they wouldn't free any significant number of troops for use in the Persian Gulf any time soon.

The closings will take place slowly over several years, starting with the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1.

"It's partly a matter of the obvious changing situation," said Pentagon spokesman Pete Williams. "As the threat changes in Eastern Europe, as the Soviet forces pull back, as Eastern European governments change, become democratically elected, clearly the nature of our deployments in . . . Europe changes as well."

Mr. Williams added that "the second thing we have to contend with is declining budgets in future years."

The 150 military "sites" range from tiny support posts to large air bases, but don't include any of the most important U.S. combat bases in the heart of central Europe. The largest affected installations are three air bases: Torrejon in Spain, and Lindsey and Hessich Oldendorf in West Germany. All 150 sites are clustered around 44 locations, the Pentagon said.

The Pentagon declined to estimate the number of troops that will be withdrawn from Europe as a result of the cutbacks, nor the costs involved. Officials said some troops serving at the affected sites might simply be moved to others in the U.S. or abroad.

The announcement is certain to be welcomed in Moscow, which is pulling all its troops out of Europe and had been pressing the administration to make a unilateral statement of its own intentions.

Rep. Pat Schroeder (D., Colo.), chairwoman of the house subcommittee on military installations, called the list "disappointing." She said "these sites include gas stations on the autobahn, isolated housing units and athletic fields, and sites which had long ago been slated for closure. Facilities like these are closed every day at bases in the U.S."

The Pentagon doesn't require congressional approval to close the foreign facilities, as it would if they were located in the U.S.
