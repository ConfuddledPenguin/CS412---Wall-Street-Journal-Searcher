Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Japanese Firms Say Head May Be In Tokyo, but Heart's in the U.S.

  • Author: Joann S. Lublin
  • Date: 09/19/90

Japan Inc. is trying harder to promote an all-American identity for its U.S. operations. Japanese manufacturers long have preferred product-oriented advertising. That has been a fairly easy sell here, considering Japanese goods' reputation for quality and reliability.

But now, worried about growing anti-Japanese sentiment, more Japanese companies are mounting or planning broad corporate-image advertising in the U.S. for the first time. Among them: Canon, Honda Motor, Panasonic, Sony and Toyota Motor.

The firms want to woo consumers by stressing their expanded range of American-made products and roles as good U.S. corporate citizens. A Panasonic campaign that begins next month, for instance, will tie its products to vignettes about American life. To further raise their U.S. profile and better coordinate marketing efforts, a handful of Japanese concerns such as Sanyo and Toshiba also have opened Manhattan headquarters offices for their U.S. operations since 1989.

The new push could help allay popular resentment of Japan's massive American acquisitions spree, seen in the recent rash of Japan-bashing ads. The drives also could deflect further tensions as the U.S. economy deteriorates. Japanese corporations "are running out and spending {ad} money just as an inoculation" against greater pro-America fervor, says one advertising agency executive who is crafting image ads for a major Japanese client. They fear "intensification of anti-Japanese feeling," he adds.

Such concerns persuaded Honda to prepare its first large-scale image campaign in America, a nearly $10 million effort set to break this Friday in USA Today and The Wall Street Journal. The 12 different ads in the yearlong drive will highlight Honda's commitment to America rather than tout particular products, explains Eric Conn, senior manager for automobile advertising at American Honda Motor Co.

Honda officials hope to convince opinion makers that "we are not a foreign power draining {America} dry," Mr. Conn continues. "So many people think that we stick {profits} in big boxes and mail them back to Japan." He says the ads also are geared at getting consumers who prefer buying American to consider Honda cars because "this company is more American than they thought."

The Japanese car maker's first full-page ad displays a Honda Sports Coupe, manufactured at its Marysville, Ohio, plant and equipped with a right-hand steering wheel, which cars in Japan use. The model "is made only in America," the advertisement boasts. "But that's not the only place it's sold. Thousands of Honda Sports Coupes are exported to Japan every year." Honda began exporting the cars last year.

Some U.S. executives of Japanese companies are reluctant to admit they're taking a defensive tack with their new advertising. Informed Americans "already know we have made incredible investments in the U.S.," argues Jeffrey Brooks, senior vice president of advertising at Sony Corp. of America. Sony, which owns CBS Records and last year purchased Columbia Pictures Entertainment, will start running its U.S. corporate-image advertising in late October, mainly in magazines and on television.

Any resulting deflection of Americans' anti-Japanese feelings "would be a side effect," Mr. Brooks says. "That's not the main direction." The real goal, he insists, is to bolster Sony's brand image across a range of entertainment products.

Similarly, Toyota executives say its maiden U.S. image campaign, launched in May after three years of planning, just happened to coincide with a flurry of ads attacking Japan and Japanese products. "It's not a direct response" to such Japan bashing, says Timothy Andree, a Toyota public-relations manager in New York. "Toyota in America is an American company. I'm not worried about carrying the banner for Japan."

But the campaign, costing almost $10 million this year, deliberately pitches a wholesome image of Toyota as an American-style practitioner of corporate good citizenship. The ads describe community programs supported by Toyota.

Advertising critics aren't sure the new corporate campaigns will do much good, either at selling products or overcoming Americans' anxieties about Japan. The ads tend to lack a sharp focus, these critics say, because Japanese corporations know a lot more about promoting products than about pushing themselves.

"You constantly have to build your image" in the U.S., says Hironobu Shibuya, president of public-relations firm Dentsu-Burston-Marsteller, a U.S.-Japanese joint venture. "That bugs the hell out of the Japanese because it's something they never had to do back in Japan. It's a very American idea."

The other danger, he warns, is that the Japanese image ads will boomerang and exacerbate anti-Japanese fervor. "Trying to buy that {all-American} image by running expensive ads," Mr. Shibuya suggests, "can rub off in the wrong way."

Kodak Picks WPP Unit

Eastman Kodak named WPP Group's J. Walter Thompson unit to handle the ad launch of its new electronic photography system, which the company unveiled Monday.

The system, which will transfer 35mm photographs onto compact disks for viewing on TV, won't be available until mid-1992. Kodak wouldn't say whether or not it held a competition for the ad assignment.

Kodak also wouldn't disclose the ad budget for the system, except to say spending would be "commensurate with expenditures for our most significant consumer products." At that rate, spending for the system, called "Photo CD," could approach $30 million.

Ad Notes. . . .

ARROW ACCOUNT: Cluett, Peabody & Co. awarded the $10 million account for Arrow Shirts to Ammirati & Puris, New York. Cluett Peabody was bought earlier this year by Bidermann Industries. The account was previously handled by Chiat/Day/Mojo.

PR SALE: Aegis Group PLC, previously known as WCRS, sold its public relations division, Creamer Dickson International, to a unit of French communications giant Eurocom S.A., for #10.75 million ($20.6 million). With this sale, which includes U.S. PR agency Creamer Dickson Basford, Aegis continues its strategy of focusing on its core media-buying operations and shedding other businesses.

AUTO SPENDING: Automotive advertising on broadcast television rose 8% in the first half of 1990, reaching more than $1.5 billion, according to the Television Bureau of Advertising. Network spending, reflecting an increase in corporate ad campaigns, rose 21% to $865 million. Spot ad expenditures, though, slipped 5% to $640 million, as slow car and truck sales hurt individual dealers' budgets.

NEW ACCOUNT: ProGroup Inc. named Tracy-Locke, Dallas, agency of record for its Arnold Palmer line of golf clubs. Fallon McElligott, Minneapolis, formerly handled the account. Billings weren't disclosed.
