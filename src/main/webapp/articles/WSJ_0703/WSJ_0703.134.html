Credit Markets: Bond Prices Are Little Changed in Quiet Session As Now-Ritual Wait for Jobs Data Is Re-enacted

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Candace Cumberbatch
  • Date: 07/03/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices were little changed in mostly torpid trading as investors began their monthly ritual of waiting for the employment report. For bond investors, the Department of Labor's monthly report has become the most anxiously awaited of all government economic indicators. It has the ability to sway sentiment and move the markets, which explains why some investors shy away from the market in the days before its release. The June employment report will be released on Friday.

The approaching July Fourth holiday, which falls in the middle of the week, also kept many investors away.

"We're really playing with a half-staff on Wall Street," said Brian J. Fabbri, chief economist and managing director at Midland Montagu Securities Inc. "The first half of the week will be treated as if it is an extended holiday." He said that traders, investors and investment advisers will likely return on Friday, in time for the release of the June employment report.

In the municipal market, traders kept close watch on bonds issued by the city of Philadelphia. On Friday, Moody's Investors Service Inc. downgraded about $1.35 billion of the city's outstanding general obligation bonds to Ba from Baa, citing a burgeoning budget deficit and continued uncertainties surrounding the development of recurring funding sources.

Bonds rated below Baa are considered junk bonds and Philadelphia is now the only major U.S. city whose general obligation bonds are rated in that category. However, several city's have been rated non-investment grade in the past, including New York, Cleveland and Detroit.

There wasn't a general consensus about how the Philadelphia bonds moved. Traders at Butcher & Singer Inc. said that some Philadelphia bonds fell between two and four points, which amounts to losses of between $20 and $40 for bonds with a $1,000 face amount. However, other traders said the bonds were just slightly lower.

But all dealers said that trading activity was very slow yesterday with only a handful of firms putting out lists for the bid. When bids were taken on $150,000 of Philadelphia's 8.25% bond due 2009, investors sought yields of 8.20% and higher. Those bonds were yielding about 8% on Friday. Bids also were taken for $500,000 of Philadelphia's 8.10% of bonds due 2000.

Trading activity in Philadelphia bonds could pick up substantially in the weeks ahead as news of the downgrading spreads. Although few analysts believe that Philadelphia will default on its obligations, many institutional investors are prohibited from holding fixed-income securities that are not rated investment-grade.

Some traders see a silver lining in the clouds overhanging the bonds, arguing that the response could have been much worse Monday.

Market participants were "kind of prepared" for the Moody's action, said W.M. Norris Jordan Jr., a trader at Janney Montgomery Scott Inc. in Philadelphia.

Treasury Securities

Treasury bonds were little changed from Friday. The benchmark 30-year bond was quoted late at a price of 103 23/32 to yield 8.40%, unchanged from Friday.

Trading in the Treasury market was actually brisk early yesterday morning as prices declined in response to a weaker dollar on foreign exchange markets, rising commodity prices and news that the National Association of Purchasing Management's index of business conditions rose to 51.1% in June from 50.7% in May. The rise suggests that manufacturing activity picked up slightly.

Short-term rates were lower when compared with last week at the government's weekly auction of Treasury bills. The government sold $17.6 billion of bills yesterday with an average discount rate of 7.73% for three-month bills and 7.60% for six-month bills.

The rates were down from last week's auction when they were 7.78% and 7.67%, respectively.

Corporate, Junk Bonds

The junk bonds of Kay Jewelers Inc. rocketed higher on news that the jewelry chain received a friendly takeover bid from Ratners Group PLC, a major jewelry retailer in Britain.

In the proposed takeover, 12.4 million of outstanding Kay Jeweler stock will be exchanged for new Ratners perferred stock valued at $17 a share. The proposed acquisition is contingent upon the ability of Ratners to purchase a majority of Kay's junk bonds. Ratners said it will try to purchase those bonds in the open market as well as launch a tender offer for the bonds at 75% of face value.

Prices of some Kay junk bonds jumped 15 points, or about $150 for bonds with a $1,000 face amount. Kay's senior notes were quoted late in the day at a bid price of 75, although some were being offered at 90. The wide spread between bid and offer is a sign that some investors expect Ratners to sweeten its offer for the bonds.

Frederick H. Taylor, high-yield analyst at Salomon Brothers Inc., doubts that a price of 75 will appeal to bondholders.

That price, he said, "seems inadequate in light of the total value of the company and particularly in light of how the equity is being valued. I expect bondholders to hold out for a higher value."

In other trading, most corporate bonds, both junk and investment grade, ended little changed.

Foreign Bonds

The value of the U.S. dollar declined sharply on foreign exchange markets, boosting foreign currencies and sparking a rally in most foreign bond markets.

On the first trading day after West and East German monetary unification, the West German bond market was surprisingly quiet.

Bond dealers attributed the market's somber tone to anxiety about the first ever German Unity Fund bond offering, scheduled to take place tomorrow. The proceeds from the issue will be invested in East Germany. Market talk is that the bonds will mature in 10 years and carry a coupon rate of between 8.75% and 8.875%. The size of the bond offering is expected to be between five billion or six billion marks ($3.01 billion to $3.61 billion).

In trading, West Germany's 8.75% bond due 2000 rose 0.22 point to a price of 100.50 to yield 8.67%.

Prices of Japanese government bonds finished firmer as the yen rose sharply against the dollar. In Tokyo, the dollar ended at 151.43 yen, down from 152.20 yen late Friday in New York and from 152.88 yen in Tokyo on Friday.

As the value of the yen rose, Japanese government bonds followed. At the 3 p.m. close of the Tokyo Stock Exchange, the price of Japan's benchmark 4.8% 10-year bond was 86.43, up 0.18 point, to yield 7.31%. In over-the-counter trades after the Tokyo Stock Exchange closed, the yield traded as low as 7.275%.

(Foreign government yields are quoted here in local terms and therefore differ somewhat from yields calculated for these markets by U.S. investment banks, which tend to use the semiannual compound measure to compare yields across markets.)

But British government bonds, called gilts, ended weaker due to disappointing economic news. The British government revised its growth estimate for May retail sales to 1.4% from 1.2%, suggesting that consumer demand was a bit stronger than previously reported.

Municipal Bonds

In the absence of market-moving news, Philadelphia's general obligation bonds were a focus of the sleepy municipal bond market.

Meanwhile, actively traded municipal bonds closed flat to 1/4 point higher.

The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 finished 1/4 stronger at 98 3/4, yielding 7.30%. On Friday, the bonds yielded 7.32%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Taking their cue from the stagnant U.S. Treasury market, mortgage securities prices were unchanged to a shade firmer.

The primary market also was quiet, with one issue priced -- a $350 million issue of Federal National Mortgage Association real estate mortgage investment conduit securities offered by First Boston Corp.

The offering is backed by new stripped mortgage securities formed from Fannie Mae 9% 30-year mortgage-backed securities. Stripped securities are formed by separating principal and interest portions of a security, and then selling them separately to investors.

Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities for July delivery were 1/32 firmer on the day at 99 17/32; Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% issues were up 1/32 at 98 27/32 and Fannie Mae 9.5% securities were also up 1/32 at 99 11/32.

In the 10% coupon issues, the Ginnie Maes were up 1/32 at 101 27/32, the Freddie Macs and Fannie Maes were unchanged at 100 25/32 and 101 9/32, respectively.
