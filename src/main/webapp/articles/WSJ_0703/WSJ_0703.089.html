SEC Chief Asks Exchanges For Options-Trading Plan

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/03/90

WASHINGTON -- Securities and Exchange Chairman Richard Breeden asked five exchanges to settle their differences and come up with a joint plan by Sept. 21 for having several exchanges list options on individual stocks. He also asked, in letters to the heads of the exchanges, that the exchanges extend until Oct. 1 a moratorium on listing any options that were traded on another exchange before Jan. 22 of this year. That moratorium expired Saturday.

The move, which had been expected, came in response to two plans submitted by the exchanges to create options-trading networks. One plan -- supported by the Chicago Board Options Exchange and the New York, American and Pacific stock exchanges -- would create electronic links allowing options to be bought and sold at the best price on any exchange, much as stocks are traded.

Under another proposal, being pushed by the Philadelphia Stock Exchange, when two exchanges show the same price for an option, orders would automatically go to the exchange that posted the price first.

Options, which are divided into puts and calls, permit holders to buy or sell a stock at a specified price for a limited period. A call is an option to buy, and a put is an option to sell.

Currently, trading in most options is allocated by a lottery system to a single exchange, and they then can be traded only through that exchange. The SEC has argued that these monopolies don't benefit the consumer.
