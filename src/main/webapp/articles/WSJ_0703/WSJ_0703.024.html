U.S. Thrift Agency Temporarily Eases S&L Bailout Law

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 07/03/90

WASHINGTON -- The Office of Thrift Supervision approved a temporary relaxation of restrictions that last year's savings-and-loan bailout law placed on the size of S&L loans to an individual borrower. But the relaxation didn't satisfy industry groups that had pushed for a broader change. Real estate, construction and S&L industry groups had argued that the new rules were forcing thrifts to turn away viable projects and loans.

The bailout law reduced the size of loans a thrift can make to a single borrower to 15% of the thrift's capital, the same limit that applies to banks. Previously, thrifts had been allowed to loan as much as 100% of their capital to an individual borrower. Critics said the higher level was risky because big losses on a single loan could push a thrift toward insolvency.

Under the relaxation, which was expected, thrifts that meet the bailout law's capital requirements for 1995 will have until the end of 1991 to meet the 15% limit. Through the end of this year, the well-capitalized thrifts can loan as much as 60% of their capital to a single borrower. Beginning Jan. 1, 1991, the limit drops to 30%. Loans made under the rule must be for U.S. residential housing and must be secured by first liens on real estate. Currently, about 1,100 thrifts meet the 1995 capital requirements and can take advantage of the transition rule.

OTS Director Timothy Ryan said the rule was temporarily relaxed because of the "potential adverse effects of such a sudden reduction."

But industry groups said the transition rule actually provides little relief. "It doesn't seem like they did much of anything," said David Danovitch, regulatory counsel with the National Council of Savings Institutions. He said the bailout law already included a permanent exception for well-capitalized thrifts to lend as much as 30% of their capital for residential housing projects. Small businesses and non-housing borrowers won't be helped by the rule.

"At small thrifts, one guy who's the local car dealer might also want a loan for his house. That can approach 15% pretty quickly," Mr. Danovitch said. "And there aren't always other sources of credit."

But with Congress and bank and thrift regulators wary of granting special treatment in the wake of the S&L crisis, the OTS isn't likely to make any further moves to relax the 15% limitation. Bank regulators have argued thrifts will have to begin arranging syndications of loans as many banks do.
