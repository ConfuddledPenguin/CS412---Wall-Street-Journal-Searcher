US Sprint Subsidiary and Soviet Agency Form Data-Services Venture in U.S.S.R.

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 07/03/90

NEW YORK -- US Sprint Communications Co.'s international unit and the Soviet Union's telecommunications authority have formed a venture to provide data communications services in the U.S.S.R. Ownership in the new company, Sprint Networks USSR, is split evenly between Sprint and Central Telegraph of the Soviet Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications. The venture plans to begin operating a switching center by the fourth quarter in Moscow to provide high-speed data communications services, domestically and internationally.

The agreement would broaden European penetration by Sprint, which has built many of Western Europe's public-data networks and which has been trying to strengthen its presence in overseas markets against the overwhelming dominance of American Telephone & Telegraph Co. Moreover, many of Sprint's potential corporate customers are U.S.-based companies that are locating businesses in Moscow and are demanding more data-communications services.

The joint venture still needs approval from the U.S. Department of Commerce and the Paris-based Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls (Cocom). While the U.S. government has expressed a willingness to increase exports of sophisticated telecommunications equipment to the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe, it recently shot down a plan by a consortium led by US West Inc., the Denver-based Bell regional phone company, to build a giant fiber-optic communications network linking the Soviet Union with Europe and Asia.

Sprint, a unit of United Telecommunications Inc., filed applications for licenses to export its technology last March, when it announced it was holding discussions with the Soviets. Susan Williams, Sprint's vice president of international business development, said Sprint is confident that its export requests will be approved.

"Our applications for export are going well, and we still expect service to begin in the fourth quarter {of this year}," she said. "Everything is ahead of schedule."

Sprint said the venture will link Soviet government customers and the diplomatic corps in Moscow to the SprintNet international data network, which ties databases and online services in the U.S. and throughout the world. These customers also will be able to get Sprint's Sprintmail electronic mail service.

"The goal is to offer all data communications services to residential customers in Moscow that our customers in Cincinnati and Miami can get today," said Ms. Williams. Eventually service will be extended to other parts of the country as the Soviet Union upgrades its telephone infrastructure. Overseas links will be provided via satellite, she said.

A major portion of the agreement is potential future orders for Sprint's Telenet data communications equipment. Sprint added that installing Telenet transmission equipment on customer sites in the country will end up "forming the backbone of the Soviet Union's first data communications network."

"The joint venture is a landmark in the development of the U.S.S.R.'s data communications capability, joining our telex systems with new technologies that will serve our country's needs, and those of foreign interests based here, well into the 21st century," said Viacheslav Rubtsov, general manager of Central Telegraph.

The new company will be headed initially by Sprint International's director of business planning and development, Henry Radzikowski, while deputy managing director will be Viktor Toor, who is currently the second-ranked manager at Central Telegraph. The remaining staff of 30 employees will be made up mostly of Soviet nationals, said Ms. Williams.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, United Telecommunications stock closed at $40, up 37.5 cents.
