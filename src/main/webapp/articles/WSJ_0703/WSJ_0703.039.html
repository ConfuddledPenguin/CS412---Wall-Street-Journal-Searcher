Ingersoll to Sell U.S. Publishing Empire To E.M. Warburg Pincus, His Partner

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 07/03/90

Ralph M. Ingersoll II, battered by a heavy debt load and a downturn in the newspaper industry, is relinquishing his ownership in the U.S. publishing empire he built up over the past two decades. Ingersoll Publications Co. said E.M. Warburg Pincus & Co. will acquire all of Mr. Ingersoll's stock in his three U.S. companies, which publish metropolitan daily newspapers in New Haven, Conn.; St. Louis; Trenton, N.J, and elsewhere. Warburg has been Mr. Ingersoll's partner for seven years, helping to finance his ambitious acquisition drive in the 1980s.

Mr. Ingersoll will in turn acquire Warburg's 50% stake in Ingersoll Publications Ltd., publisher of English and Irish newspapers. After the split, both companies are expected to have equity they value at between $200 million and $300 million each.

The dismemberment of the Ingersoll empire comes after a year in which Mr. Ingersoll faced a worsening debt situation, particularly at his Community Newspapers Inc. unit, whose expansion in recent years was financed largely by junk debt issued by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. Mr. Ingersoll's other unit, Ingersoll Newspapers Inc., had a loss of $30 million on the St. Louis Sun, a start-up daily newspaper that folded after seven months.

"This is a total collapse of Ralph's plan to build a giant media company in the U.S. and Europe," said John Morton, an analyst with Lynch, Jones & Ryan, Washington, D.C.

Mr. Ingersoll, for his part, said in an interview that he welcomes the split because it will enable him to pursue what he believes are greater opportunities in Europe. Though he denied industry reports that his relationship with Warburg had unraveled recently, he acknowledged there was a "divergence of objectives" between him and the New York venture capital and investment concern. Mr. Ingersoll said Warburg Pincus wanted to put more money into the company and own more equity. "I foresaw a whole succession of projects like the (St. Louis) Sun that would make them uncomfortable," Mr. Ingersoll said. "I would rather put the burden on my shoulders alone."

In a statement, Warburg Chief Executive Lionel Pincus said "we are saddened that our long partnership with Ralph Ingersoll has come to an end." Although analysts speculated that Warburg will now try to sell off some of the Ingersoll properties, Warburg said it has no plans at present to do so. However, Mr. Ingersoll said negotiations were continuing for the planned sale of This Week, a chain of 76 free weekly newspapers on Long Island, N.Y.

After spending binges fueled by roughly $500 million in junk-bond debt raised by Michael Milken, the junk-bond wizard of the now-defunct Drexel, Mr. Ingersoll's three U.S. companies carry bank and other long-term debt at face value in excess of $700 million. Mr. Ingersoll's London-based Ingersoll Publications Ltd. isn't as highly leveraged. It has annual revenue of $178 million, cash flow of $40 million and bank debt of $109 million.

Warburg Pincus will assume one of the sources of Mr. Ingersoll's problems: debt payments on bonds issued by Community Newspapers Inc. Warburg Pincus said Community Newspapers wouldn't make a $7 million semiannual interest payment due yesterday on its 13% senior subordinated "reset" notes, and said CNI wouldn't be able to reset the bond's interest rate by this Saturday's deadline, as was required.

The interest rates on CNI's bonds are to be reset so the bonds trade at full face value. The CNI junk bonds, issued originally in 1987 by Drexel, have traded as low as 35 cents on the dollar this year. CNI would have been forced to pay a prohibitively high interest rate to meet its reset obligations. Bondholders have retained Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. as independent advisers. Smith Barney executives didn't return calls for comment.

Mr. Pincus said in his statement that he hoped "CNI would be able to work out its financial problems with the holders of its subordinated indebtedness . . . negotiations on those matters have been in process for several months."

To date, CNI bondholders have resisted tender offers by Ingersoll Newspapers, which offered holders of the senior bonds 55 cents on the dollar.

Warburg Pincus will take control of three U.S. companies, which include: Ingersoll Newspapers Inc., which publishes nine dailies and 53 weekly newspapers; New Haven Acquisition Co., publisher of the New Haven (Conn.) Register; and Community Newspapers Inc., publisher of daily newspapers in Ohio and Long Island. Robert Jelenic, currently president of both CNI and INI, will become chief executive officer of Warburg Pincus's U.S. newspaper properties.
