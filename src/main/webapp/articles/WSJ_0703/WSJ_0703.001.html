REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): `Read Our Lips'

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/03/90

Before the Budget Summiteers from the White House and Congress get too far down the road to a national tax increase, they might want to charter limousines up to New Jersey to take the political pulse of a recently taxed electorate. We realize that when Washington's politicians start talking about raising taxes in the name of "responsible government," it doesn't make too much difference what the taxpayers themselves actually think. Still, someone in the two national parties who still thinks voter sentiment counts for something better check out the Garden State, which appears ripe for the revival of a California-style tax revolt. We'll set aside the purely partisan sentiments of the thousands who jammed Trenton Sunday to demonstrate against an imminent $2.8 billion package of sales and income-tax increases. "Florio, Florio, you sold out," "Recall Florio," and "Flimflam Florio" refer to the New Jersey governor who neighbor Mario Cuomo was boosting just last week for the Democratic presidential nomination. More interesting than this was the makeup of the crowd itself, which arrived in a 40-mile-long caravan of cars and trucks. A lot of them didn't seem to be the "rich" who are supposed to be the target of the Florio tax increases, but normal middle-class citizens.

News reports quoted people identified as postal workers, grade-school teachers, truck drivers, single working women, carpenters, owners of small businesses. "Finally, the middle class is saying enough," a carpenter's wife told the Newark Star-Ledger. An angry postal worker complained to the Associated Press, "They make promises and they don't keep them."

An organization named Hands Across New Jersey seems to have taken shape. A first-grade teacher who said, "I have never done anything like this before," now says: "We want to tell the politicians we're not taking it anymore. Whenever government loses touch with its people, something is wrong."

Meanwhile in Washington, news reports say that the budget summit is proceeding in a much more relaxed atmosphere now that the Democrats have been liberated from the dreadful curse of presidential opposition to new taxes. Maybe they shouldn't relax too much. One New Jerseyan showed up at the rally driving his tractor-trailer with a long banner tied to the side, saying: "Read Our Lips."

Whatever he says now, George Bush may have created the bumper sticker for a middle-class political movement that is taking on a life of its own.
