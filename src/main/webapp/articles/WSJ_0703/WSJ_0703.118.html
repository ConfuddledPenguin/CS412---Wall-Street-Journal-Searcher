U.S. Foreign Debt Widened Last Year; Nation Remains World's Largest Debtor

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 07/03/90

WASHINGTON -- America's foreign debt swelled again last year, further miring the nation in its rank of the world's largest debtor. During the year, foreign holdings in the U.S. grew nearly twice as much as U.S. holdings abroad, the Commerce Department said. As a result, the U.S. was left shouldering a net debt burden -- the difference between U.S. holdings abroad and foreigners' holdings here -- of $663.75 billion. That was nearly 25% higher than the nation's $531.08 billion net debt position in 1988.

The report was the latest chapter in America's stunning decline from the world's largest creditor nation in 1983 to the world's largest debtor nation.

A continued surge in foreign investment in everything from New York office towers to Treasury securities to entire U.S. companies, hoisted foreigners' assets in the U.S. to $2.076 trillion last year from $1.797 trillion in 1988. That swamped the growth in U.S. investment abroad, which lifted U.S. foreign assets to $1.413 trillion from $1.266 trillion in 1988.

Not only was the report grim news for the U.S. government, but it also brought some embarrassing chiding of the Commerce Department. For the first time, the department decided not to publish the bottom-line debtor position of the U.S. -- the $663.75 billion figure -- in its report on international investment, although it provided enough information to calculate the number.

In a statement accompanying the report, the department explained that the comparison of the values of foreign assets in the U.S. and U.S. assets abroad was imprecise since it mixed current prices of assets with prices paid when they were purchased. The result, according to the department, was that U.S. assets, many of which were purchased decades ago, were generally undervalued compared with foreign assets, the bulk of which were purchased in the 1980s.

But critics accused the government of trying to paper over a dreadful number. "Any decision not to print a bottom line, I think, is masking a net position and a trend that is indisputably very adverse," said C. Fred Bergsten, director of the Institute for International Economics here.

Mr. Bergsten, who is writing a book on America's debtor status, did agree that "there are big problems with the numbers." But he said the numbers may understate U.S. liabilities as well as assets. "My conclusion is that the net bottom line is not too inaccurate, and certainly the trend of the last 10 years is indisputable," he said.

Allan Young, head of the Commerce Department's Bureau of Economic Analysis, which compiled the report, said the department's decision not to print the number was part of a government-wide effort to improve the quality of economic statistics. "No other considerations entered into it," Mr. Young said. "It's simply that we didn't think it was an accurate number." By this time next year, he said, the department aims to publish a report valuing all international investment at current prices.
