Abreast of the Market: Takeovers, Program Trading Lift Stocks, Enlivening Slow Session

  • Author: Craig Torres and David Wilson
  • Date: 07/03/90

NEW YORK -- Takeovers and program trading lifted stocks yesterday, adding some excitement to a sluggish trading day. The Dow Jones Industrial Average added 18.57 to 2899.26. Volume totaled about 130,200,000 on the New York Stock Exchange, where advancing issues barely stepped ahead of declining shares 775 to 744.

The narrow breadth was reflected in the smaller rise of broader market measures. The Standard & Poor's 500-stock index gained 1.52 to 359.54, an advance equal to an 11-point gain in the Dow industrials. Stock-index arbitrage buy programs helped boost volume in a day of otherwise slow trading, reflecting the approach of the July Fourth holiday.

Some takeover excitement returned to the stock market with Forstmann Little & Co.'s $44.50-a-share, or $1.6 billion, buy-out agreement with General Instrument Corp. General Instrument's stock soared 8 7/8 to 45 1/8 on volume of 3.3 million shares. Also, Kay Jewelers received a $17-a-share, or $328.1 million, offer from Ratners Group of Britain. Kay Jewelers climbed 3 3/8 to 14 1/4 on 1.5 million shares. The two stocks were the largest gainers in percentage terms on the Big Board yesterday.

Traders said the acquisition offers enlivened takeover speculators' interest in several other stocks. Avon Products, for example, gained 5/8 to 37 3/8; McGraw-Hill rose 1 to 57 1/4, and American depositary receipts of Glaxo Holdings PLC rose 1 to 30.

"I think we are seeing the beginning of takeovers in industries like technology, where there is overcapacity and opportunities for restructurings, mergers and consolidations," Michael Metz, chief market analyst at Oppenheimer & Co., said. "A lot of industrial companies are underleveraged, and banks are looking for respectable borrowers."

Outside of takeover speculation and futures-related buying, investors bought auto and financial service stocks. Ford gained 5/8 to 44 3/8. Federal National Mortgage Association rose 1 1/8 to 41 3/4; Federal Home Loan Mortgage rose 2 1/4 to 77 3/4.

Ralston Purina closed 1 1/2 higher at 93 3/8. A Prudential-Bache Securities analyst, John McMillin, increased his earnings forecast for the stock to $1.40 a share, on a fully diluted basis, for Ralston's fiscal third quarter just ended; the company earned $1.18 a year earlier. He raised his full-year forecasts to $6.10 a share for fiscal 1990 ending in September, and to $7 a share for all of 1991. "Operating trends have been much more stable" at Ralston, Mr. McMillin said. He added that the company is an "aggressive" buyer of its own stock.

Shearson Lehman Hutton produced its 42nd annual list of "Ten Uncommon Values in Common Stocks." Among the recommended issues: Johnson & Johnson rose 1 7/8 to 69 5/8; Contel gained 1 1/4 to 27; Cable & Wireless gained 1 1/8 to 30 5/8; Acuson added 1 3/4 to 40 1/2, and Unum went up 1 3/8 to 54 3/4.

Shearson's list also included Interpublic Group, which went up 1/4 to 35; Newell, up 3/8 to 32 3/8; Nord Resources, up 7/8 to 18 1/2, and Schlumberger, down 3/8 to 57 1/8. Adobe Systems, the only over-the-counter stock to make the list, gained 1 1/4 to 38.

Meanwhile, in its most recent look at corporate dividends, Standard & Poor's Corp. found that 65 companies increased dividends last month, down 26% from a year earlier. The number of companies decreasing dividends rose to 14 in June, compared with eight in the same month last year. Companies omitting dividends doubled to 18.

"Corporations are wary of increasing dividends and are husbanding cash because of the poor earnings environment," said Joseph Tigue, managing editor of the S&P "Outlook" newsletter.

N.V. Philips dropped 7/8 to 16 7/8. The company projected a loss of about $1.07 billion for the year, including restructuring charges of about $1.44 billion that it plans to take in the second half. Its restructuring proposal calls for about 10,000 employees to be trimmed from the work force, mostly in Europe.

Smith Corona fell 7/8 to 5 3/8. The company said that it expects to report "a small loss" for the June quarter and that its board will consider cutting the quarterly dividend to about five cents a share from 15 cents.

Prime Motor Inns tumbled 1 1/8 to 7 7/8, after saying it expects a "substantial" loss for the fiscal year ending next June, including about $150 million in charges for additional reserves and asset write-downs.

Elsewhere on the takeover front:

-- Rorer Group gained 1 3/8 to 70. Rhone Poulenc moved another step closer to completing its merger with the company, as the French firm received U.S. clearance to proceed with an offering of contingent value rights to Rorer shareholders.

-- Milton Roy climbed 2 5/8 to 25 1/2. On Friday, Sundstrand disclosed that it holds a 14.99% stake in the company, bought with a possible takeover in mind.

-- MGM/UA Communications dropped 5/8 to 16 5/8. Time Warner, which previously decided against providing Pathe Communications with $650 million in financing for its proposed acquisition of MGM/UA, has filed a $100 million suit against Pathe in a dispute growing out of the companies' financing talks.

-- Kollmorgen slid 1/4 to 12. The company purchased all of its common shares owned by Vernitron, which staged an unsuccessful proxy fight for board seats in May, under a 10-year standstill agreement.

Also, Ferro fell 1/2 to 25 on volume of one million shares, after soaring 4 3/8 points Friday on news that investor Jon M. Huntsman received clearance to acquire as much as 15% of its common shares.

Long Island Lighting gained 3/4 to 20 on volume of 1.6 million shares, following an appeals court decision, issued late Friday, which upheld the company's agreement with customers in 1989 to reduce electric rates by $390 million over 10 years. The court rejected efforts to overturn the settlement as too small.

Responding to the ruling, Kidder Peabody upgraded its investment opinion on Lilco to the firm's top rating. Douglas W. Preiser, an analyst, told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report that the decision, in which the court also upheld a lower court's overturning of a racketeering verdict, "removes a big boulder" that had been hanging over the company.

Also, Prudential-Bache Securities and Salomon Brothers reiterated "buy" recommendations on Lilco stock, following the appeals court ruling, according to the investor report. Analysts at all three firms said they expect the utility to proceed later this summer with a 50% dividend increase proposed under the settlement.

Several other utility stocks also were actively traded on the Big Board, including SCEcorp, which was unchanged at 37 1/2 on 1.5 million shares; Pacific Gas & Electric, which dropped 1/8 to 23 1/8; Teco Energy, which went up 1/2 to 29 1/2; Potomac Electric Power, which added 1/4 to 21, and PacifiCorp, which closed unchanged at 21 1/4.

McDonnell Douglas gained 1 5/8 to 38 1/8. A group headed by Menke Titola Capital, a San Francisco buy-out firm, acquired the company's computer maintenance and distribution business for $65 million.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.07 to 360.14. Volume totaled about 9,850,000 shares, compared with about 14,720,000 Friday. Advancing issues totaled 230 and declining issues 369, and 229 were unchanged.

American Science & Engineering fell 1/2 to 6 1/8. The company reported a loss of 43 cents a share for its fiscal fourth quarter, ended in March, compared with a slight profit in the year-earlier period.
