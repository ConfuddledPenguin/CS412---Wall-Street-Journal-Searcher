World Markets: Japan's Nikkei Index Plummets 829.30 Points As Market's Decline Is Picking Up Speed Again

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/06/90

TOKYO -- The Japanese stock market plunge is picking up speed again. Spooked by fears that the settlement of some futures contracts next week could trigger another massive sell-off on the stock market, investors dumped stocks. Stock-index futures -- contracts that obligate holders to buy or sell a basket of stocks at a set price on a specific date -- fell so sharply they were suspended from trading.

In London, shares closed up, but were unable to match the day's high in a cautious market still crippled by low volume.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues plunged 829.30 points, or 3.3%, to 24078.34. So far this week, the index has fallen 1900 points, or 7.3%.

In Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei index shed another 244.89 points to close the morning session at 23833.45.

At one point Wednesday, the indicator fell below the 24000-point level, before recovering on heavy buying by brokerage firms. Turnover was light at about 380 million shares, up slightly from Tuesday's 331 million shares.

Alarmed by the market's continuing nose dive, regulators late Wednesday eased margin requirements to the lowest-possible legal level. Investors now can buy stock by putting up collateral equivalent to 30% of the value of the stock they buy.

Japan's stock market has been battered for more than a month by worries that Iraq's invasion of Kuwait on Aug. 2 could drive up energy prices in this oil-dependent country, spurring inflation and forcing authorities to bolster interest rates. The Bank of Japan raised its benchmark rate just this past week. "Investors are terrified by the shadow of creeping interest rates and oil prices," an analyst at Yamaichi Securities Co. noted.

Wednesday, though, the market was rife with fresh rumors of trouble. Many suggested that the futures settlement a week from today could bankrupt some securities firms. The rumors centered on one firm, Sanyo Securities Co., which is thought to have a substantial number of unprofitable futures contracts outstanding. A spokesman for Sanyo confirmed that it holds some futures positions for its own account, but he denied that the firm is in financial danger.

Still, traders said they fear that next week could bring bigger troubles for Tokyo's faltering market.

"There is a big, big danger," said Philippe Baudin, assistant general manager of options at Societe Generale Securities Ltd., a French-owned firm. "There is dynamite in the market."

Kazuto Aizawa, an analyst at Kangyo Kakumaru Securities Co., said the market is fraught with "investor concern over the future price outlook, so share prices declined sharply on small-lot selling."

Individuals who bought shares on credit also contributed to Wednesday's decline, analysts said. While the amount of so-called margin borrowing -- loans made to finance stock purchases -- has declined to 829.55 billion yen ($5.78 billion) as of Aug. 31 from a peak of about 1 trillion yen in the middle of March, the borrowing remains high in view of the steep share price decline in the past six months.

Benedicte Ivey, a saleswoman at Credit Lyonnais Securities Ltd., said stock bought with borrowed money accounts for 2.15% of the Tokyo market's total capitalization -- above the 2% level that "is considered by most as the danger level."

Some experts expect a considerable rise in selling next week, when the loans backing a large amount of that stock will come due for payment.

Another factor hurting the market Wednesday was the approaching end of the fiscal half-year for many institutional investors. Life insurance companies and trust banks usually close their books in the days leading up to Sept. 30. Kimitoshi Hasegawa, a senior fund manger of Dai-ichi Mutual Life Insurance Co., attributed the stock market's decline to "internal factors."

In London, Wednesday's disclosure of several company interim results caused a nervous start to the session as market expectations for the earnings had been poor. All seven of the company results were "disappointing" said one trader, but were still generating trade in the U.K. market.

Thin volume remained a dominant feature, as share prices ticked better in tandem with the futures market, said one trader. He added that arbitrage situations were "dragging the main market up."

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index gained 4.2 points to 2152.2. The Financial Times 30 Share Index closed up 1.3 points at 1672.2. Volume was a scant 344.9 million shares, above Tuesday's level of 286.2 million shares.

Among the many company results disclosed, composite insurer Sun Alliance reported a 38% drop in pretax profit on the back of increasing storm damage claims. The stock slipped 10 pence to 279 pence (19 cents to $5.25).

B.A.T Industries posted an 11% drop in pretax profit on losses in its insurance business. Nevertheless, the stock managed to add 2 pence to 526 pence.

Williams Holdings shed 13 pence to 217 pence. The construction concern reported a pretax loss of 15% as high U.K. interest rates dented the consumer and building products market. Another building products company Wimpey saw pretax profit fall 72% following a prolonged weakness in the U.K. housing market. Wimpey closed down 27 pence at 175 pence.

Blue Circle also suffered from depressed housing markets in the U.K. as well as in the U.S. Pretax profit fell 7.8% but the stock gained 5 pence to 205 pence.

Also, shares closed slightly higher in Paris, Brussels, lower in Frankfurt, Madrid and Stockholm, flat in Zurich and mixed in Amsterdam and Milan. South African gold stocks closed marginally lower.

And stocks closed lower in Sydney, Hong Kong, Seoul, Manila, Taipei and Wellington, and were mixed in Singapore.
