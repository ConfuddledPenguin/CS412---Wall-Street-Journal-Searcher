Sweater Imports Become Subject To New Duties

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 09/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The International Trade Commission, ruling that the U.S. knitwear industry is injured by imports, authorized the imposition of anti-dumping duties on more than $1 billion in annual shipments of synthetic-fiber sweaters from Taiwan, Hong Kong and South Korea. The ITC's final ruling will hit the Taiwanese imports hardest; it authorizes the U.S. Customs Service to collect additional duties ranging from 21.38% to 24.02% on most of those shipments. The ruling also will lead to duties of 5.86% on sweaters from Hong Kong's leading suppliers and of 0.73% to 3.17% on those from South Korea.

Dumping is defined as selling at prices below the cost of production or at below the price in the home market. Dumping duties are designed to offset the allegedly unfair price advantage that imports have over U.S.-produced ones. The ITC acted on a petition brought by the National Knitwear & Sportswear Association, a New York trade group representing most of the affected U.S. producers.

The ruling isn't expected to have an immediate impact on department-store prices because most of the imported sweaters to be sold this winter are already in U.S. warehouses. But Seth Bodner, executive director of the knitwear association, predicted that prices will rise beginning in the 1991-1992 season -- unless the Commerce Department finds that the dumping has ceased. "Some of the Korean producers will have to either raise their prices in the off-season or accept higher dumping margins," he said.

Mr. Bodner also predicted that the U.S. government will compel U.S. importers to be more scrupulous about the origin of the Asian sweaters they purchase. The Commerce Department in the course of its investigation looked into charges that some sweaters shipped as part of Hong Kong's quotas were made in China.

The ITC vote was 2-1, with Commissioners David Rohr and Seeley Lodwick finding there was injury to the domestic industry and Commissioner Don Newquist declaring the opposite view. Acting ITC Chairman Anne Brunsdale recused herself from voting on the case.

U.S. apparel importers expressed disappointment with the ruling. "Imports have filled a void for high-quality fashion and novelty sweaters; they have not damaged a domestic industry," said Gail Cumins, counsel to the American Association of Exporters and Importers' Sweater Group. She warned that consumers will be the ones hurt because "the sweaters they want to buy just won't be available at prices they can afford."
