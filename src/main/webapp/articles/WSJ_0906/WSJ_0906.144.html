Media: FTC to Charge ABC Sports, College Group in TV Pact

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett and Dennis Kneale
  • Date: 09/06/90

The Federal Trade Commission, cracking down on the cozy relationship between big-time college sports and television, plans to charge the College Football Association and Capital Cities/ABC Inc. with violating federal antitrust laws in a five-year broadcast contract. In a five-page administrative complaint to be formally filed today with an administrative law judge, the commission said the contract restricts competition in the marketing of college football telecasts, reducing the selection of games for consumers.

The FTC action threatens the ABC network's lock on nearly all nationally televised college football games. Earlier this year, the CFA and ABC signed a $215 million contract that committed 63 of the association's 66 members to broadcast their football games on the network from 1991 to 1995. The FTC could seek to have the contract voided and could prohibit any other agreements that restrict competition.

A lawyer familiar with the case said the FTC filing will result in private negotiations between the agency and the defendants. If no compromise is reached, the administrative law judge would rule on the complaint after a hearing in Washington Nov. 13. The defendants can appeal a ruling to the full commission.

Lewis Engman, the Washington lawyer for CFA, said his client's broadcast arrangement doesn't restrain competition, noting that "there are hundreds of football games available now" on television. He said the CFA is "confident the commission ultimately will see that the contracts are good for viewers and competiton." Capital Cities/ABC officials couldn't be reached for comment yesterday.

The FTC, in bringing charges against ABC Sports and the Denver-based College Football Association, essentially is pursuing an arrangement that has been in force since 1984, first between the CFA and CBS Inc., and now involving ABC. The FTC complaint isn't expected to affect CBS's broadcasts of CFA games this year.

In 1984, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that the National Collegiate Athletic Association had violated antitrust laws by acting as the sole, mandatory negotiating agent for all college teams in the bargaining for television rights.

A spate of smaller contracts with various college conferences followed, with teams able to decide whether to take part in the television contracts. ABC made the most of the new pacts. ABC already has an exclusive arrangement with all teams in the Big Ten and Pacific 10 conferences and ABC added the CFA contract in February, which gave the network a monopoly on nearly all nationally televised college games. The CFA consists of about 20 major independent universities and most of the major athletic conferences.

That monopoly was broken only by the surprise withdrawal of the University of Notre Dame, which signed its own, more lucrative television deal with General Electric Co.'s NBC network.

The FTC refused to confirm or deny the plans for a complaint, but said it will hold a news conference this morning "to announce a major antitrust action concerning college athletics."
