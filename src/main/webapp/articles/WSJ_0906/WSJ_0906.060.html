The Myth of Domestic Spending Cuts

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/06/90

John Cogan, a senior fellow at the Hoover Institution and a former deputy director of the Office of Management and Budget, and Timothy J. Muris, a law professor at George Mason University and a former executive associate director of OMB, recently completed a study of domestic discretionary spending in the 1980s. A summary follows:

In the current budget summit, not all spending cuts have been created equal. Domestic discretionary programs -- these for which Congress annually appropriates funds -- are widely felt to be exempt from serious restraint.

This view follows from the conviction that these programs were deeply slashed during the Reagan years. As Rep. William Gray (D., Penn.), former chairman of the House Budget Committee declared: "We've squeezed the fat, the skin and some of the muscle out of these domestic programs. Now we are down to the marrow."

But contrary to the prevailing view, budget data reveal that domestic discretionary spending grew only slightly less quickly than inflation during the years 1981-89. There was one year of budget cutting, 1981, followed by seven years of rapid growth, growth that has accelerated in the 1990s. By 1989, this growth had effectively restored the aggregate level of non-entitlement domestic spending to its inflation-adjusted 1981 level.

How could the widely held view that the domestic budget has been cut viciously be so utterly wrong? One problem is that the most frequently used measures of government spending are highly misleading. For example, it is often assumed that the figures the government shows for "outlays" are the same as total spending. This is not correct. Outlays are recorded net of certain payments made to the government from the public. Worse, outlays are recorded only when a check is actually issued, not when the government takes on an obligation. Outlays for many programs, therefore, often occur years after the programs have been funded.

Another commonly used but misleading indicator is "appropriated budget authority." In theory, appropriated budget authority is the amount of money an agency has to spend on a program. In fact, though, appropriated budget authority is only one method of financing a program. Other methods have been used with increasing frequency during the 1980s, to enable Congress to produce the appearance of budget cutting while the total amount available to spend has been maintained or increased.

One such method is to require the public to pay a user fee for a particular government service and to allow the agency providing the service to use the proceeds to cover a portion of the agency's cost. By increasing the amount of the fee and reducing the amount of appropriated budget authority, Congress can increase the amount of money the agency has to spend while reducing the agency's budget on the government's books.

This is what occurred in the Department of Commerce's Patent and Trademark Office. Application fees were increased in the early 1980s and the proceeds made available to the agency to spend. Revenues from the fees rose from zero in 1981 to nearly $100 million in 1989. As a result, the actual amount the agency had available to spend rose to $201 million in 1989 from $116 million in 1981. The budget books, meanwhile, claim a cut in the office's budget authority to $109 million in 1989 from $116 million in 1981. The office's outlays show a similar decline, to $108 million in 1989 from $112 million in 1981.

Another method Congress has found to finance discretionary programs while concealing their cost is to transfer money from entitlement programs to discretionary programs. For example, funds appropriated for the Food Stamp program were quietly transferred for several years during the 1980s to the Agriculture Department's Extension Service to finance some of its activities.

None of the conventional measures of spending accurately capture the monetary size of subsidized housing programs. In the Department of Housing and Urban Development's subsidized housing programs, the government enters into long-term contracts with landlords to make housing units available for low-income families at low rents. According to law, the budget authority for these long-term contracts must be appropriated on a "full funding" basis -- meaning that the maximum expected costs of the contract are to be appropriated before the contract is begun. If the government intends to pay a $6,000 a year housing subsidy for 10 years, the "full funding" rule requires the Appropriations Committee to appropriate not $6,000 but $60,000. The rule is designed to ensure that the government's full long-term liability is accounted for when it decides to subsidize housing units.

But the rule has an unfortunate side effect. By shortening the length of the typical contract, the amount of appropriated budget authority required to finance any particular number of housing units can be reduced. The housing budget can be "cut" while the number of subsidized housing units is allowed to expand. This is precisely what happened during the 1980s. Faced with pressures by the Reagan administration to reduce appropriations, Congress cut the average length of new contracts in half and continued to finance additional housing units. Although budget authority for subsidized housing programs declined by nearly 77% the number of subsidized units and the number of families living in these units increased by one-third.

Once official budget data are adjusted to account for all the various forms of spending, and the misleading impact of housing programs is eliminated, the true extent of budget growth is revealed. In the aggregate, spending grew slightly less than inflation during the 1980s. Discretionary programs were cut by 14% between 1981 and 1982, but over the next seven years inflation-adjusted spending grew to eliminate the impact of the cuts. Although the final results for fiscal year 1990, which ends September 30, are not yet available, budget authority alone for that year increased by an amount about three times the inflation rate. Thus, if measured through 1990, domestic discretionary spending growth in the 1980s exceeded the rate of inflation. And congressional action to date would increase spending in 1991 by an even greater amount than in 1990.

We also collected data on bureau-level spending between 1981 and 1989. (Bureaus are typically administrative units within agencies or departments, and they usually administer more than one government program.) The data, presented in the accompanying table, indicate that the increases in funding levels in the 1980s were broadbased. Fifteen bureaus with budgets of $1 billion or more registered inflation-adjusted increases of at least 20%. These bureaus have responsibility for a wide spectrum of government activities, ranging from tax collection to environmental protection. Although much has been made of the large growth in the defense budget during the Reagan years, the budgets of the first 13 bureaus listed in the table grew faster than the Department of Defense's budget.

Some bureaus did suffer major declines in budgetary resources. Unlike the increases, the reductions were heavily concentrated in a few large bureaus, particularly inner-city job training, energy programs and programs to fund local development projects for buildings, transit and dams. Just eight bureaus accounted for 76% of all budget cuts. The combined value of these cuts was, in today's dollars, some $26 billion. The rest of the domestic discretionary budget grew a total of 18%, after inflation, in the 1980s.

Many advocates of increased spending for domestic programs assert that because total non-entitlement domestic spending was reduced during the 1980's, some restoration of resources is in order. This view is wrong. There may be compelling reasons for budget increases in certain domestic discretionary programs, but the alleged austerity of the Reagan years is not one of them.
