Sooners, Better Late Than Never, Lift the Lid on Capitol Spending

  • Author: Todd Mason
  • Date: 09/06/90

OKLAHOMA CITY -- They're raising the roof here, but they're doing it by the book -- sort of. In a 1989 novel by Jim Lehrer, fictional Oklahoma Gov. Buffalo Joe Haman -- Chip to his friends -- is tormented by the sight of the statehouse's flat roof, a mocking reminder that in 1916 his state ran out of money before it could build a dome atop the capitol. He exhorts any crowd of two or more citizens to "Crown Oklahoma," which happens to be the title of the book by Mr. Lehrer, of public television's McNeil/Lehrer News Hour.

Actually, Oklahomans did run out of money before finishing the capitol building in 1916. After Oklahoma wildcatters struck oil in the late 1920s, the state had the means to complete the dome but lost interest. State residents were content to boast of the only state capitol in the U.S. with working oil wells on its grounds.

Now, since the capitol wells and the state economy began drying up in the mid-1980s, there is growing sentiment here to raise the dome and, with it, state morale. So Gov. Henry Bellmon and Capitol DOMERS Inc. (Dedicated Oklahomans Marshaling Excellence and Rallying Spirits) have kicked off a fund-raising campaign not only to crown the statehouse but also to pave a piece of its lawn.

The DOMERS group has its roots in the recession that has gripped the state for much of the past decade. In 1987, Oklahoma City activist Carol Gwin decided that finishing the capitol building could lift the state's spirits. To get the job done, she recruited other retirees, including Adm. William J. Crowe, former chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff and currently a professor at the University of Oklahoma.

The nonprofit group hopes to raise $14 million for the dome and to build a plaza at the south entrance of the capitol building. To help raise the money, the group is offering citizens the right -- for $250 each -- to have paving stones for the plaza engraved with names of their choosing. "This is a symbol of our efforts to move forward," says architect Paul B. Meyer, a consultant to the Capitol Preservation Commission.

After Mr. Lehrer's book appeared, members fell into a long debate. Should they lift its title for the name of their campaign? "We would have had to make it `Crown Oklahoma, Crown Oklahoma,'" cracks Mr. Meyer, "because Buffalo Joe had a habit of saying everything twice." Instead the group settled for "Raising Oklahoma."
