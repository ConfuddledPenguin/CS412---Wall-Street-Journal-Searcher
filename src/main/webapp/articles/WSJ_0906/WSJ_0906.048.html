Pinnacle West to Use PacifiCorp Proceeds To Reduce Its Debt

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/06/90

NEW YORK -- Pinnacle West Capital Corp., after agreeing to a power-swapping deal that ended efforts by PacifiCorp to acquire the company, said it would use proceeds from the deal to reduce debt. At a meeting with securities analysts, Pinnacle West said it would add the proceeds to its $750 million debt-reduction plan, increasing the plan to $970 million by the end of 1993.

Analysts were surprised yesterday by the subdued reaction of investors attending the meeting, many of whom saw Pinnacle West stock plunge Tuesday to $12.375 a share from $16.125 as arbitragers unloaded stock in large quantities. The stock recovered, however, to $12.875 a share yesterday in another day of heavy trading. Volume on the New York Stock Exchange totaled 1.7 million shares yesterday and 2.2 million on Tuesday compared with 130,000 on Friday before the announcement of the pact.

"People came out of the meeting with a feeling that while there would be no instant gratification from a sale of Pinnacle West, the company was doing the right things to improve in the long run," said Barry Abramson, a utility analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. Last Friday, Pinnacle West announced an agreement to swap electric power on a seasonal basis with PacifiCorp, effectively ending efforts by PacifiCorp to acquire Pinnacle West for $21 a share, or $1.82 billion. PacifiCorp agreed to pay $220 million in cash for exchange of power from Arizona Public Service Co., a Pinnacle West utility subsidiary.

Yesterday, Pinnacle West Chairman Richard Snell praised the pact, saying he was "absolutely convinced" that it was a "heck of a deal." Mr. Snell said that with the addition of $220 million, Pinnacle would be able to retire $970 million in debt by the end of 1993. As result, Pinnacle West hopes to raise the book value of the company to $20 a share compared to $16.31 in 1989 and restore its common-stock dividend by the end of 1993. The company suspended its quarterly payout of 40 cents a share last October.

After the meeting, Mr. Snell said in an interview that PacifiCorp's $21-a-share offer was "substantially" below the company's own assessment of its value. He wouldn't elaborate, but some analysts said the difference was about $3 a share.
