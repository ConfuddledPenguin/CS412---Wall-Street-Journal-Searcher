Enterprise -- Washington Update: No Benefit Seen in Gulf Crisis; Marketing Plan for SBICs

  • Author: Jeanne Saddler
  • Date: 09/06/90

GULF CRISIS shows few signs of boosting small firms' slice of defense sales. Some small businesses have received "letter contracts," or quick orders, for extra supplies of such items as desert camouflage nets, Pentagon officials say. But, overall, small and medium-sized Defense Department contractors are getting few new orders as the result of Iraqi President Saddam Hussein's threat to world oil supplies, says Donald Grigg, president of the Association of Small Research, Engineering and Technical Services Companies. "There may be an initial uptick for some of the larger companies, but we also might see a reduction in programs sooner because so much was spent on {opposing} Iraq," he says.

During the first three quarters of fiscal 1990, the Defense Department met its goals for small-business contracting by spending $14 billion on prime contracts and another $14 billion in subcontracts with smaller companies. But critics contend that defense officials set contracting goals they can comfortably meet, making it difficult to evaluate the department's effort. The Pentagon continues to fall far short of congressionally mandated goals of spending 5% of its prime-contract dollars and 5% of subcontracing money with minority-owned and other small, disadvantaged concerns. At the end of the third quarter, the agency had spent $2.3 billion, or 3% of the total, for such prime contracts, and $1 billion, or 2.7% of the total, for such subcontracts.

A word to the wise for small technical businesses: Go East, as in Eastern Europe, says Mr. Grigg. "Almost everything we do is needed there."

SON OF S&L SELLOFF: SBA seeks marketing plan for assets of failed SBICs.

With about 170 failed Small Business Investment Companies in liquidation proceedings, the Small Business Administration has become the not-so-proud owner of such assets as common stock in closely held concerns, preferred stock and loans to companies. But instead of establishing its own version of the Resolution Trust Corp., the federal agency in charge of assets from bankrupt savings and loan institutions, SBA officials plan to hire a private contractor to help place a dollar value on the assets and market them.

The initial sales effort will probably include something under $100 million in equity and assets, says Ron Cibolski, chief of the SBA investment division's liquidations branch. Given the magnitude of the SBIC problem, he says, "It could be much more at some point." The SBA wants to have its plan in operation within the next few months. But it is moving cautiously because it wants to ensure the government gets a fair price and because valuing assets and equity in closely held corporations is very difficult, Mr. Cibolski says.

The marketing plan could range from an auction sale to sending mailers to likely purchasers, he says.

SBIC FALLOUT? Pressures from investigations into SBIC failures may have influenced Charles Gillum's recent departure as SBA inspector general to become the Agriculture Department's deputy inspector general, some SBA officials suggest. They say Mr. Gillum was caught between demands of the Senate Small Business Committee for very detailed information on the problems and his own icy relations with the SBA's investment division and administration. His office turned up much of the bad news about the floundering program, but some of its own decisions were questioned in the process. Mr. Gillum couldn't be reached for comment. The Agriculture Department's inspector general office that he will head is several times the size of the SBA operation.

ECONOMIC GLOOM is growing among small businesses. According to the National Federation of Independent Business's latest quarterly survey, only 17% of 2,100 business owners queried expect the economy to improve, compared with 22% in the second quarter. More than one fifth -- 22% -- see worse times ahead, up eight percentage points.

The overall small-business optimism index, which includes expected business conditions, fell to a seasonally adjusted 98.2 for the third quarter from 100.8 for the second and was the lowest since 1982.

"Small-business owners don't like what they see on the horizon," NFIB President John Sloan said. "And now with the Iraqi invasion, there will be even less hope for an upturn this fall."

The National Association of Wholesaler Distributors, which represents small and large concerns, also said its confidence index, based on a survey of 219 firms, recently showed a decline.

FRANCHISING FRACAS: A congressional report urges more safeguards.

A House Small Business Committee report calls for federal legislation setting minimum standards of protection for the legal rights and financial interests of franchisees. The report's biggest bombshell: that some franchisees should be entitled to payment for part of the value of a business when the franchise is terminated. The issue of the day, says the report, isn't whether franchisers fairly disclose contractual terms to potential franchise holders, but the abuses of continuing franchise relationships. Some 17 states have enacted laws to prevent abuses such as requiring equipment purchases from the franchiser at above market prices and ending the franchisee's contract with little notice.

New York franchising attorney David Kaufmann says the idea that some compensation is due a franchisee when his contract expires "strikes at the heart" of the franchise relationship. In an article submitted to the New York Law Journal, he argues that franchisees essentially buy a business on an installment plan, with nothing owed them when the contract expires. But the committee staff maintains that Congress recognized the legal and financial rights of franchisees in earlier statutes protecting auto dealers and gasoline franchisees. The committee will hold hearings on the issue later this month.

When a franchise contract is terminated in California, state law requires franchisers to offer to repurchase all of the inventory that a franchisee was required to buy.
