Politics & Policy: Splits Over Cut In Deficit Seem As Wide as Ever --- Talks Resume Tomorrow As Projections of Gap Are Steadily Increasing

  • Author: Jackie Calmes and David Wessel
  • Date: 09/06/90

WASHINGTON -- As the bipartisan budget talks reconvene after a monthlong hiatus, differences within and between the parties appear as great as ever, while projections of the fiscal 1991 deficit continue to grow. President Bush will preside when the talks resume tomorrow at Andrews Air Force Base in the first of what is scheduled to be three or four days of negotiations aimed at accomplishing what the first 12 weeks of talks didn't: a package of spending cuts and tax increases that both parties endorse as a way to shrink the federal deficit $50 billion next year and $500 billion over five years.

Democratic budget negotiators caucused yesterday for the first time in more than a month, but differences between the House and Senate persist. Staffers say Democrats are talking not about a firm deficit-reduction proposal but about presenting a menu of deficit-reduction options to Republicans.

White House Chief of Staff John Sununu and Budget Director Richard Darman, meanwhile, met separately with negotiators for House Republicans, some of whom have begun calling for a tax cut to revive the economy even though the administration is expected to offer its tax-increase plan when the negotiations resume.

Mr. Darman told Republicans he is projecting the fiscal 1991 deficit will reach a record $250 billion without some action, up from his estimate of $232 billion two and a half weeks ago.

Some negotiators predict Congress will have to return after the election for a lame-duck session. "No doubt about it," said Sen. Wyche Fowler (D., Ga.), who is Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell's delegate at the negotiations. But others, notably House Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.), dismiss that notion. House Budget Committee Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) insists, "We know we can't walk out of the room without agreement."

Negotiators face intensifying pressure to reach some sort of agreement this month -- either a substantial deficit-reduction package or a face-saving accord that delays any politically painful decisions until after the election or even until next year.

The weakness in the economy, exacerbated by the spurt in oil prices, has created a near-crisis atmosphere.

Although several self-imposed deadlines for a resolution of the budget talks have come and gone, negotiators now confront some real deadlines. Without a change in the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law, $85 billion in across-the-board spending cuts will take effect Oct. 1, crippling the federal government. At about the same time, Congress must pass legislation authorizing the Treasury to borrow money and authorizing the government to spend money in fiscal 1991. Those bills could serve as a vehicle for either a deficit-reduction package or a slide-by package to defer tax rises and spending cuts.

Negotiators from both political parties publicly say they remain committed to cutting the deficit $50 billion in fiscal 1991 and $500 billion over five years. "We've always been at 50-500 and that's where we'll stay," House Majority Leader Richard Gephardt (D., Mo.) said after the Democrats' caucus. And Mr. Darman, arriving for the his session with House Republicans, said, "We don't see any change in that."

But privately, lawmakers and their aides suggest that the first-year figure, at least, will erode as much as $25 billion as negotiators respond to the changed military and economic situation. Central to the deliberations is defense spending. The Bush administration would shave about $6 billion from projected defense spending while some Democrats want to cut more than twice that sum. The Iraqi standoff has undercut support for such deep defense cuts, however. And if savings from defense shrink, Democrats will demand lower domestic cuts in turn. Then proposed tax revenues, which would probably make up half of any package, would also be lowered. The result is a package adding up to substantially less than $50 billion.

A faction led by negotiator Newt Gingrich of Georgia, the House Republican whip, now is arguing that a budget-cutting package with tax increases could tip the economy into recession and that tax cuts are needed instead. But others, such as Rep. Panetta, say that a substantial deficit-reduction pact is more necessary than ever to send financial markets a positive signal and encourage the Federal Reserve to lower interest rates.

The Democrats' menu of options is likely to include an increase in the top personal income tax rate to at least 33% from the current 28%, an increase in taxes on alcoholic beverages and tobacco and perhaps an energy tax of some sort.

The administration, which has criticized Democrats for failing to offer a deficit-reduction plan before the congressional recess, is expected to put on the table a controversial plan of its own that was leaked in July. It calls for, among other things, an increase in taxes on beer, wine and liquor as well as a $10,000 ceiling on federal income-tax deductions for state and local taxes.
