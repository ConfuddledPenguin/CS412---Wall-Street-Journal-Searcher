Marketing & Media: FDA Demands Firms Prove Claims About Oat Bran

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 09/06/90

WASHINGTON -- The Food and Drug Administration began its crackdown on misleading health claims on food labels by challenging Ralston Purina Co. and five other companies on their assertions regarding the health benefits of oat bran. Ralston Purina and its Continental Baking Co. unit were the largest food processors to be targeted yesterday with so-called regulatory letters from the FDA, according to agency officials. The food industry has been anxiously awaiting the first batch of letters as an indication of how the FDA plans to curb the proliferation of dubious health claims in recent years.

One FDA official said that the six companies were singled out because the agency believed it could, if necessary, develop strong enforcement cases against them under the Food, Drug and Cosmetic Act. In its letters, the agency demanded that the companies justify their oat bran claims or stop making them altogether. Typically, companies are given 10 days to respond to regulatory letters.

"We are, in effect, trying to establish the boundaries of what we think is appropriate in the way of health claims and what we think is inappropriate," an FDA official said.

Last night, a Ralston Purina spokesman in St. Louis confirmed that the FDA had challenged claims being made for two products containing oat bran -- Ralston Purina's Oat Chex cereal and Continental Baking's Oatmeal Goodness bread.

"We're just beginning to review the matter," said the spokesman, Pat Farrell. "We believe that we are in compliance with all labeling requirements regarding all our products."

Oat Chex went on the market in September 1989 with expectations that it would "benefit from continuing consumer interest in oat bran," according to Ralston Purina's latest annual report. Oatmeal Goodness bread, another new entry, has been "a highly successful product" for Continental Baking, the nation's largest producer of bakery goods, the annual report said.

It couldn't be determined last night exactly what the FDA found objectionable about the claims for these two products. Moreover, officials wouldn't identify the other companies that received letters from the agency's Center for Food Safety and Applied Nutrition.

In this instance, the agency is intent on curbing claims about how fiber helps lower cholesterol levels and reduce the risk of a heart attack. Oat bran is a source of soluble fiber. The thrust of the regulatory letters sent by facsimile machine yesterday was that the products don't contain enough oat bran to make any difference from a health standpoint.

In most cases, the product has less than one gram of oat bran in each serving, according to an FDA official. Clinical studies show that it takes 50 grams a day to have beneficial effects, he added. What's more, the agency still hasn't decided whether oat bran does reduce the risk of heart attack.
