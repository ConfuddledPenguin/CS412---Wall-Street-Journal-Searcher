Technology & Health: Smoking Parents Risk Tots' Health, According to Study

  • Author: Ron Winslow
  • Date: 09/06/90

Children exposed to heavy amounts of tobacco smoke at home are more than twice as likely to develop lung cancer later in life than those who grow up in smoke-free households, a new study says. The finding adds to the growing body of evidence that nonsmokers' health may be damaged by other people's smoke. It is one of the first studies to find that parents who smoke present a potential cancer risk to their children.

"It may be particularly important to protect children and adolescents from this environmental hazard," say the authors of the report, published in today's edition of the New England Journal of Medicine.

But the report found no statistically significant link between lung cancer among nonsmokers and spouses who smoke, or environmental smoke from the workplace or social settings such as restaurants -- a development a representative of the tobacco industry considered encouraging.

The report "is in agreement with what we've been saying {about} environmental tobacco smoke in the workplace, the home and social settings for adults," said Steve Parrish, vice president, corporate scientific affairs, at Philip Morris Cos., New York. He also played down the significance of the conclusion about the impact on children, saying several other studies didn't turn up any evidence of increased cancer risk.

Mr. Parrish said the report supports the industry's position that there isn't any justification for banning use of tobacco in the workplace.

The study's findings were based on interviews with 191 nonsmokers recently diagnosed with lung cancer in 23 counties in New York State as well as 191 nonsmoking control subjects, matched by age, sex and county of residence.

The researchers, led by Dwight T. Janerich, an epidemiologist at the Yale University School of Medicine, New Haven, Conn., focused on exposure to tobacco smoke in the household, although they also queried participants about workplace and social setting exposures.

Why children would be vulnerable to cancer is a question for further study, Dr. Janerich said, but he speculated that their lungs could be more suspectible to damage from smoke than those of adults or that a long latency period is required for the cancer to develop. The average age of cancer patients in the study was about 67. Dr. Janerich disputed that the report undermined previous research showing a 38% increased risk of lung cancer for nonsmokers living with smoking spouses. He also said non-household data wasn't sufficiently firm to reach definitive conclusions.

The study estimated that 17% of lung cancer among nonsmokers "can be attributed to high levels of exposure to cigarette smoke" at home when they were under 21 years old. The scientists defined high levels as at least 25 "smoker-years" of exposure -- a calculation based on the number of years a person lived in a residence multiplied by the number of people in the household who smoked.

The researchers acknowledged that the findings could have been affected by the differences in the ability of participants to recall details of lifetime tobacco exposure.
