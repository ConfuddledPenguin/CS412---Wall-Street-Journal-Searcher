Fed Ruling Stifles Big Banks' Entry Into Insurance

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 09/06/90

WASHINGTON -- A Delaware law that was expected to permit big banks to underwrite and sell insurance nationwide was effectively crippled by the Federal Reserve Board. The Fed decision, in response to a challenge to the law by a coalition of insurance trade groups, specifically curtails insurance activities by a Citicorp unit, Family Guardian Life Insurance Co. But the decision also will make it impossible for other banks to take advantage of the law, banking trade group officials said.

"This essentially ends the effectiveness of the Delaware law," said Edward Yingling, a top official of the American Bankers Association.

Big banks have been hungering in recent years to get into the insurance underwriting and sales business but have been unsuccessful in their attempts to win that power from Congress, though a few states allow a small number of banks to underwrite insurance.

The Delaware law, passed in May, appeared to open the door for big New York and regional banks to underwrite insurance nationwide through state-chartered subsidiary banks in Delaware. Citicorp had already moved to underwrite insurance through Family Guardian, and Chase Manhattan Corp. had indicated it intended to take advantage of the new law.

The law brought a worried response from some members of Congress concerned about states moving to expand bank powers in the wake of the $150 billion savings-and-loan crisis, which some have attributed partly to the expanded powers of state-chartered thrifts. And the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., which insures the banking industry, indicated several weeks ago that it would likely move to curtail some of the activities allowed by the Delaware statute.

The Fed regulates bank holding companies, and the framers of the Delaware law believed they had gotten around the Fed's restrictions on insurance underwriting by allowing insurance operations within the state-chartered bank subsidiary of the holding company.

But in its decision, the Fed said it ordered Family Guardian to stop the underwriting activities because the Delaware law created so many so-called firewalls -- such as a rule that the insurance activities be kept separate from the banking operations -- that "the practical effect" was to "treat the insurance division as a separate corporate entity." Under federal banking laws, the Fed allows subsidiaries to undertake the insurance activities only if the state law allows the banks to run the insurance business legally as part of the bank.

"It's ironic that the basis for the decision is that the Delaware law had too many firewalls to protect the bank," said Mr. Yingling, adding that he expected the Fed decision would be challenged in court, by individual banks or by the ABA.

Citicorp officials couldn't be reached to comment after the Fed released its decision late yesterday.
