Prosecutors Plan Further Move Against Milken

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen
  • Date: 09/06/90

NEW YORK -- Prosecutors will attempt to bring their full case against Michael Milken before a federal judge next month, even though the government agreed to accept a plea bargain in April. Prosecutors say they plan to urge U.S. District Judge Kimba Wood to consider additional allegations against Mr. Milken when she sentences him. These alleged crimes will be detailed in a sentencing memo that Judge Wood is scheduled to receive on Monday in federal court here.

In the April agreement, Mr. Milken, the former head of Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc.'s junk-bond department, pleaded guilty to six felony counts related to unlawful trading. The government didn't agree to recommend any specific sentence, as Mr. Milken's lawyers had hoped.

The government hopes that evidence of additional crimes -- presented in the memo and in live testimony by Ivan Boesky and others -- will induce Judge Wood to dole out a stiff sentence for the crimes to which Mr. Milken pleaded guilty. The maximum prison sentence for these counts is 28 years. Sentencing is scheduled for Oct. 1.

The U.S. Supreme Court and lower federal courts have ruled in a series of cases that the government may ask a sentencing court to consider alleged conduct for which the defendant wasn't convicted and didn't plead guilty. The rulings are controversial because they appear to penalize defendants for crimes the government never proved beyond a reasonable doubt that they had committed.

The sentencing memo is expected to include charges that Mr. Milken helped prominent savings and loan executives, among others, to defraud the public and violate tax laws, and that Mr. Milken engaged in commercial bribery with certain money managers.

Prosecutors may be particularly eager to air their full case because they were recently stung by a pre-sentence report written by the federal probation agency. That report is understood to have favored Mr. Milken's position that his contributions to society outweighed his crimes. While probation agency recommendations can be very influential in the sentencing of violent criminals, the recommendation could be less significant in this case because there will be so much additional information presented by both sides. Probation reports are almost never made public.

Prosecutors have held lengthy discussions with attorneys for Mr. Milken in recent months detailing what they plan to say in their memo. Mr. Milken's lawyers are expected to submit their own memo next week disputing those claims.

A judge isn't obligated to take the government's additional allegations into consideration when deciding on an appropriate sentence. But if Judge Wood decides she wishes to consider the additional evidence in meting out a sentence, Mr. Milken is entitled to defend himself at a hearing.

Lawyers for witnesses who aided the government in its case against Mr. Milken have confirmed that they were told by prosecutors that the government will seek to present the full force of its case against Mr. Milken at any such hearing. Prosecutors have told these attorneys that such a hearing could last for more than three months.

Assistant U.S. Attorney John Carroll, the lead prosecutor in the Milken case, declined to comment. A spokesman for Mr. Milken also declined to comment.

A similar battle over sentencing erupted earlier this year in the case against Robert M. Freeman, the former head of arbitrage at Goldman, Sachs & Co. who pleaded guilty to a single charge of insider trading. In that case, the judge said that in issuing a sentence, he wouldn't consider any crimes other than that to which Mr. Freeman pleaded guilty. The government alleged that Mr. Freeman committed some 20 other crimes.

Robert McCaw, a lawyer for Mr. Boesky, the convicted former stock trader, said he is watching the situation "with great interest, and we'll see what happens." The government has asked a federal court in Los Angeles to prevent plaintiffs' lawyers in a civil suit from taking testimony from Mr. Boesky until after Mr. Milken's sentencing, in order to "protect" the government's interest in the case against Mr. Milken.

Steve Andrews, the lawyer for James Dahl, a former Drexel bond salesman, said, "We've spoken to the government about potentially testifying" against Mr. Milken. Reid Weingarten, a lawyer for Cary Maultasch, another former Drexel bond salesman, said, "We have caught wind of the possibility of a hearing. From our vantage point, this is an unfortunate development that is likely to help neither the government nor Mr. Milken."

If these and other witnesses were called, lawyers for Mr. Milken would be allowed to cross-examine them as if they were trying the case before the jury. But in such hearings, which are rare, the government's burden of proof is lower than in a criminal trial. It must prove its case simply by a preponderance of evidence, rather than beyond a reasonable doubt.

Legal experts said the argument for having such a hearing is stronger in the Milken case than in the Freeman case because the charges to which Mr. Milken has pleaded guilty are broad based, and public attention in his case is formidable.

A hearing has drawbacks for both sides. It could expose Mr. Milken to significant public embarassment and further civil liability if those he once hired at Drexel are now forced to testify against him. For the government, a hearing could further delay Mr. Milken's cooperation with the government's continuing investigation, and would postpone additional cases prosecutors intend to bring against Mr. Milken's associates. Mr. Milken's lawyers would also likely attempt to discredit many of the government's witnesses.
