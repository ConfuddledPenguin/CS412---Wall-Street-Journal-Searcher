Credit Markets: Bond Prices Rise as Auto Makers' Sales Tumble; Junk Bonds Posted Sharpest Drop Ever in August

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 09/06/90

NEW YORK -- Investors ignored the Mideast crisis and drove bond prices higher after car makers reported a sharp decline in sales of new cars and trucks made in North America. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose 1/2 point, a gain of $5 for a bond with a $1,000 face value, while the issue's yield eased to 8.96%.

Junk bonds, which posted the worst performance on record in August, ended unchanged to slightly higher.

In early trading, the Treasury bond market was essentially unchanged to modestly higher. But prices rose on news that sales of North American-made cars and trucks were running at a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 7.1 million units in late August, down 21% from the same period a year ago.

The weak report revived concerns that the economy is nearing a recession and that the Federal Reserve may be forced soon to reduce interest rates in an effort to stimulate economic activity.

"After sales figures for General Motors and Chrysler came out, it became clear that domestic auto sales are not robust," said Steve Ricchuito, chief economist at Barclays de Zoete Wedd Securities Inc.

Although Honda Motor Co. and Toyota Motor Corp. posted strong sales, the market gave more weight to statistics reported by the Big Three, which were interpreted as a further sign that U.S. manufacturers are in poor shape.

Market participants paid little attention to Mideast turmoil or the price of oil yesterday, an abrupt departure from previous trading sessions. Since Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait put world-wide oil supplies in jeopardy, fluctuations in the price of oil and tough talk from Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein have been the primary focus of the fixed-income market.

But yesterday, "Saddam was very bellicose again, but the market tended to look through it," said James Fralick, senior economist at Morgan Stanley & Co. "People are looking away from Saddam and the Middle East and back to the real economy."

The market was also helped as Wall Street traders began buying long-term bonds in an effort to balance their portfolios ahead of tomorrow's potentially market-moving employment report. Many dealers were holding a higher proportion of shorter-term securities.

Meanwhile, Salomon Brothers Inc. reported yesterday that its junk bond index posted a negative total return of 3.87% last month, its largest monthly drop on record. Salomon's high-yield market index tracks 476 issues representing about $87 billion of securities.

Merrill Lynch & Co., which tracks more than 700 issues that have a combined face value of more than $104 billion, said its high-yield index posted a negative total return of 3.83% in August. An analyst at Merrill Lynch said that was the worst ever and the poorest showing since October 1987, after the stock market crash, when the index declined 2.6%.

By comparison, the Treasury market posted a negative total return of about 1%, although long-term Treasury bonds declined about 5%.

Analysts said the junk market's weak performance reflected the bond market's losses and a sharp decline in stock prices. The Standard & Poor's 500 index was down 9% in August, its worst showing since October 1987.

"The stars have never really been aligned like this in the high-yield market," said Joseph Bencivenga, director of high-yield research at Salomon. He said the burgeoning number of defaults, the conflict in the Middle East and the looming recession "conspired to make for a very bad August."

Treasury Securities

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at 97 22/32, up from 97 6/32 on Tuesday. Two-year note ended at 100 5/32 to yield 8.02%, compared with 100 3/32 to yield 8.05% on Tuesday.

Short-term rates fell. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.38% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.60%. The rate on six-month bills ended at 7.31% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.67%.

Junk Bonds

News that underwriters at Merrill Lynch & Co. will delay a $200 million junk-bond offering by Stone Container Corp. was a mild disappointment for the supply-sparse junk bond market.

The issue would have been one of just handful of such offering this year. So far in 1990, only five junk bond issues have come to market, representing $397 million in high-yield financing, according to Securities Data Co. That compares with $21.8 billion in new junk bond financing in the same period last year.

The lack of supply has been a source of concern for buyers as well as underwriters, whose business has dried up.

Arnold Brookstone, chief financial officer at Stone Container, said the Chicago-based paper and packaging products company has postponed its plans for a "road show" presentation of its senior note issue until market conditions improve.

In a road show, the company travels around the country seeking potential investors. Typically, offerings are priced shortly after the road show circuit ends.

Mr. Brookstone said the recent deterioration in the high-yield market would have forced the company to offer investors "significantly" higher rates than it originally planned.

Municipal Issues

Municipal bonds were unchanged to as much as 1/2 higher in thin dealings.

More than $295 million in new debt was priced, with the majority of the deals well received by investors, traders said.

A Goldman Sachs & Co. group priced $110 million Maine State Housing Authority single-family mortgage revenue bonds to yield from 6.40% in 1992 to 8.13% in 2032.

Among active bonds, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue due in 2018 was unchanged at 98 1/2 to yield 7.31%.

Mortgage, Asset-Backed Securities

Prices of mortgage securities trended higher in dull trading.

In the secondary market, 9 1/2% securities for September delivery issued by Government National Mortgage Association, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. and Federal National Mortgage Association all rose 6/32 to 98 5/8, 98 5/32 and 98 22/32, respectively.

One trader said activity in conventional mortgages indicated that a real estate mortgage investment conduit, or Remic, deal backed by either Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac securities may be in the offing.

In the asset-backed sector, Valley National Bank of Arizona offered $300 million of credit-card notes via J.P. Morgan Securities Inc. The notes, which are collateralized by Visa and MasterCard receivables, were priced with an 8 7/8% coupon to yield 8.97%. The notes have an average life of three years and are rated triple-A by Moody's Investors Service Inc. and Standard & Poor's Corp.

Foreign Bonds

Three Japanese banks tapped the eurobond market in what some analysts believe could be the beginning of a flood of new Japanese subordinated debt issues.

Fuji Bank Ltd., Bank of Tokyo Ltd. and Nippon Credit Bank Ltd. issued a total of $1.8 billion of subordinated debt, bringing the total since late June to $3 billion.

Some analysts estimate that half a dozen other Japanese banks could bring an additional $3 billion to market in the next three weeks. Among the names rumored are Sanwa Bank Ltd., Mitsui Taiyo Kobe Bank Ltd. and Mitsubishi Bank Ltd.

In July, Sumitomo Bank Ltd. issued $1 billion of subordinated debt in the U.S. credit markets and in Europe. The offering represented the first time that a Japanese bank issued subordinated debt.

Japanese banks are being forced to raise subordinated debt to boost their capital, which has been depleted along with the steep plunge in Japanese share prices. New international guidelines require banks doing international business to have capital equal to 8% of their assets, as of March 1993.

Under the guidelines, some of which come into effect as early as 1991, Japanese banks are allowed to count 45% of the unrealized gains on their huge holdings in Japanese companies as capital. Thus, Japanese bank capital has sunk along with the 38% drop in the Japanese stock prices.

In trading, most eurodollar bonds ended unchanged to 1/8 point higher.
