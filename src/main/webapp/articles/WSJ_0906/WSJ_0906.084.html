REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Guilty Whatever Charged

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/06/90

"You're gouging on your prices if You charge more than the rest.

But it's unfair competition if you

think you can charge less.

To help avoid confusion:

Don't try to charge the same,

That would be collusion]"

Thus spake a fictional government antitrust lawyer in the satirical free-market poem "The Incredible Bread Machine." The folks who work for the major oil companies can be forgiven if they think that they too will land in trouble no matter what they do in the wake of the Persian Gulf crisis.

Oil companies have been under pressure from the White House on down to restrain price hikes. As it is now, most of the price increases out there would still leave the companies well below the replacement cost of the new oil they must buy. Now, however, independent gasoline wholesalers are demanding the government go after the majors for "predatory" pricing and an alleged attempt to drive the independents out of business.

The independents charge that the retail price of gas charged at some stations owned by the big oil companies is less than the wholesale price independent stations are charged. The oil companies say they can most effectively hold down retail prices at stations they control directly, if indeed "holding down" prices is what's expected of them. Until recently, the Atlantic Richfield Co. was charging 10 cents a gallon less than other stations because most of the oil it markets is its own. In any event, it finally had to put its prices back up when its lower pump prices caused huge lines and caused it to run out of supplies in some areas.

But Missouri Attorney General William Webster scents a conspiracy. He recently called for a probe into the "illegal, predatory pricing practices" of some oil companies. In an example of cognitive dissonance, Mr. Webster was at the same time in Washington with 24 other AGs -- sometimes called the Aspiring Governors -- to demand that President Bush punish any oil companies that engage in "price gouging."

The independent wholesalers and dealers want a federal law to bar oil companies from owning gasoline stations. They probably won't get it, but the fact that the issue has been picked up at all is further proof that few subjects are more susceptible to demagoguery than the workings of energy markets. Thus, we now have the spectacle of several "pro-consumer" Members of Congress defending those gasoline stations that in fact have raised prices to consumers the most.
