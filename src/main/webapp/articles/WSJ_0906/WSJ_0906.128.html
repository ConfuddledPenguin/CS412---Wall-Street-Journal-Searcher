U.S., Arco Chemical Settle Dispute on Deal With Union Carbide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 09/06/90

NEWTOWN SQUARE, Pa. -- The Federal Trade Commission and Arco Chemical Co. agreed to settle antitrust litigation over the company's proposed $220 million acquisition of certain assets of Union Carbide Corp. Under a provisional consent agreement, Arco Chemical, which is 83%-owned by Atlantic Richfield Co., Los Angeles, will divest itself of the propylene glycols business it agreed to buy from Carbide, based in Danbury, Conn. Propylene glycol is used in products including fiberglass, paints and cosmetics.

Arco Chemical will retain a urethane polyols business to be acquired from Carbide. But it will divest itself of a so-called tolling arrangement whereby Texaco Chemical Co., a unit of Texaco Inc., produces polyols that Arco Chemical then sells. Urethane polyols are used in making polyurethane cushions, among other things.

Arco Chemical will waive provisions in previous agreements under which Carbide and Texaco agreed not to compete in the polyol and propylene glycol business. Arco Chemical also will drop certain objections it had raised to a technology that Texaco Chemical Co. has been developing to make propylene oxide. Arco Chemical had questioned whether some of its own technology was being used by Texaco Chemical, but a patent infringement suit hadn't been filed. Texaco Chemical doesn't yet make commercial quantities of propylene oxide.

The propylene oxide market, whose derivatives end up in many consumer products, wasn't directly involved in Arco Chemical's agreement with Carbide. But the Carbide lines to be acquired by Arco Chemical are big buyers of propylene oxide, and Arco Chemical is a big producer. The Arco Chemical-Carbide transaction would thus remove a buyer from the market and, the FTC argued, act as a deterrent to new sellers who would have competed for that business.

Carbide will keep the full $220 million payment made by Arco Chemical under terms of its original agreement with Arco Chemical.

Carbide and Arco Chemical each will pay $1 million to the federal government as settlement of certain issues related to the timeliness with which the companies filed information about their transaction. The payment doesn't represent admission of wrongdoing by either company.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Arco Chemical rose 25 cents to $34, while Carbide fell 25 cents to $16.125.
