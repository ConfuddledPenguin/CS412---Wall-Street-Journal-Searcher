Wednesday's Markets: Dollar Slides To '90 Low Against Yen --- Investors Expect Data On Jobs to Be Weak; Stocks, Bonds Gain

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/06/90

NEW YORK -- Investors drove down the dollar, betting that the employment report due tomorrow will be bleak enough to convince the Federal Reserve to ease interest rates. The dollar tumbled to 1.5585 marks from 1.5815 Tuesday and fell to 141.70 yen from 143.50, though trading was light. Against the yen, the dollar sank to its lowest level since late December.

Stock and bond prices ended higher after seesawing much of the day, also in thin trading. Many investors were sitting on their hands awaiting the jobs report or further developments in the Mideast. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 14.85 points to 2628.22.

Underlying the renewed bearishness about the dollar is the growing belief that the U.S. economy is weakening and that rising oil prices may tip it into recession. The October futures contract for crude oil rose an additional 65 cents a barrel to close at $29.77 yesterday, extending Tuesday's $1.80-a-barrel surge.

Yesterday, some currency and bond traders seized on a rumor in the morning that the Fed had gotten an early look at the August employment numbers and that the results were extremely weak.

In such a thinly traded market, that rumor, which apparently was unfounded, was enough to help drive down the dollar and boost bond prices. After a brief lull last week, the speculation about a Fed easing "hit fever-high peaks again," said Roberta D. Olson, fixed-income portfolio manager at Banc One Asset Management, Columbus, Ohio. "It's the only thing to look at this week."

A Fed move to reduce U.S. interest rates would make the dollar and dollar-denominated investments less attractive compared with those in other countriesespecially in Germany and Japan, where interest rates have been rising.

Graham Spiers, head of currency trading at Travelers Investment Management, Hartford, Conn., said that the dollar's drop yesterday began in Japan, with the yen's firming against several European currencies as well as the dollar.

Part of what sparked the sell-off there was the failure of one Japanese firm's large dollar purchase to add much strength to the dollar, said David Gilmore, senior foreign-exchange analyst at McCarthy, Crisanti & Maffei.

Mr. Gilmore said the dollar is coming under pressure in Japan as big institutions there take profits in overseas markets and convert them into yen to dress up their books for the first half ending Sept. 30.

Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices rose a half point, or $5 for each $1,000 face amount, and "bonds being so strong had a lot to do with the strength in equities," said Paul Hennessey, head of equities trading at Boston Co.

Much of the boost in the stock market came from oil stocks, which headed higher on the continued rise in oil prices. Chevron, Texaco and Exxon -- all parts of the Dow Jones Industrial Average -- posted strong gains for the day.

In major market action:

Stock prices rose in light trading. Volume was 120.6 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where advancers outnumbered decliners, 953 to 571.

Bond prices rose. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond eased to 8.96%.
