Heard on the Street: Bottoming Out? A Bearish Strategist Says It Isn't Happening Yet

  • Author: Roger Lowenstein
  • Date: 09/06/90

NEW YORK -- With people back from summer recess, the question on nearly every investor's mind is the one posed by stock market strategist Abby Joseph Cohen: "How low can it go?" Her answer is of more than passing interest. Ms. Cohen, the strategist at Barclays de Zoete Wedd, was bearish well before Saddam Hussein sent stocks into a tailspin.

She was cold to the market while it rallied last year. She was bearish after the Dow Jones Industrial Average soared late this spring. And she is bearish now -- perhaps bearish enough to make investors wish their vacations could last a little longer.

To quote from Ms. Cohen's most recent report: "The sharp decline in equity prices has not significantly improved the valuation of equities, since much of the decline has been offset by the rise in interest rates."

Moreover, she says, judging by the level of long-term rates now and Wall Street's consensus projection for corporate earnings, "U.S. equities are still some 15% above a theoretical buy price."

Ms. Cohen, herself just back from the New Hampshire mountains, makes no pretense of calling the bottom. She says, "The bottom for the market is a moving target."

But it requires little insight to guess the direction in which she believes things are headed. As interest rates since Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait have risen, and as the economy has seemed to falter, she has set her sights lower.

"The bottom in equity prices cannot be gauged," she writes, "until . . . economic fundamentals have shown some sign of stabilization. We're not there yet."

Ms. Cohen's opinion is noteworthy not only because she has recently been right, but because of how she arrives at it. Ms. Cohen, a refugee from the defunct Drexel Burnham Lambert, pays attention to bread-and-butter fundamentals -- she watches current and projected levels of corporate earnings and long-term interest rates.

She was unmoved by peace, perestroika, European unity and other themes and supposed market panaceas. She is, on the other hand, very much interested in projected corporate profits.

This year, she thinks that the Standard & Poor's 500 will post average earnings of $22 a share, almost even with 1989 -- and little changed from her own Jan. 1 projection of $22.50 a share.

Wall Street market strategists as a group began the year on a far more bullish note, projecting $26 a share, according to Institutional Brokers Estimate System, a service of Lynch, Jones & Ryan. But since then, the crowd's optimism has steadily eroded. The latest consensus estimate for 1990 is $23.99 a share.

Although estimates have fallen, Ms. Cohen argues that they are still too high. And most strategists continue to stick with bullish earnings estimates for 1991 -- the Institutional Brokers Estimate consensus for next year is $25.43 a share, compared with Ms. Cohen's $21.

Thus, she argues, one shoe has yet to drop. While investors in recent weeks have become unwilling to pay high multiples for earnings, they haven't sufficiently readjusted earnings estimates.

"The consensus economic forecast does not incorporate a recession, nor a flattening (let alone a decline) in corporate profits," Ms. Cohen says. "We think the downward adjustment process will accelerate over the next several weeks, especially for 1991 estimates."

What, then, is a "fair" level for stocks? Ms. Cohen doesn't know and doesn't much care. Brokers sell stocks when their clients believe the stocks are cheap, not when the price is "fair."

Ms. Cohen defines stocks as being cheap -- or at her "theoretical buy price" -- when they are low enough to provide an expected annual return of four percentage points above prevailing bond rates. In other words, investors should seek a premium, or a margin of safety, in return for risking money on stocks.

Based on the current level of rates, and on Wall Street's current earnings forecast, Ms. Cohen says the S&P 500-stock index won't be attractive until it reaches about 270 to 280, considerably below its close yesterday of 324.39, up 1.30.

Ms. Cohen's earnings forecast, she says, "is basically a call on the economy," where, she says, "I think conditions are deteriorating."

Economic growth, she says, was tepid at best before the Iraqi invasion, and stock prices -- based on consensus earnings estimates -- had reached "substantially overvalued levels."

The invasion, she says, may boost profits of a few companies -- notably those in the oil patch. But in general, she says the invasion and the resultant oil-price rise are likely to slow down an economy that was in no need of brakes. Corporate profits in particular, she says, are likely to weaken in cyclical industries such as manufacturing, in retailing and in other areas.

"The growth rate peaked in early 1988," Ms. Cohen says. "Even without a recession, I don't see any reacceleration soon. The confidence of consumers and of business decision-makers has been shaken."

One bullish factor: If the economy and corporate profits do weaken, she says, interest rates could well drop back, reflecting less demand from borrowers. And another bullish note: She says the dollar's decline will boost the overseas earnings of multinationals.

But in general, Ms. Cohen says, "There is very little stimulus out there." Though the Federal Reserve's monetary policy could become stimulative, she says, it hasn't yet become so, and the Fed can't do much to loosen credit while foreign central banks are tightening.

What's more, U.S. banks have become less willing -- or less able -- to lend, and Japanese money supply growth is in a bind. The Japanese central bank is tightening, and Japanese banks -- their assets hurt by the plunge in Japanese stocks -- are under increasing pressure to cut back lending, she says.

Ms. Cohen's conclusion: To believe that stock prices are attractive, one has to believe that interest rates will fall by at least one percentage point and that consensus earnings estimates are correct. She says that's believing too much.
