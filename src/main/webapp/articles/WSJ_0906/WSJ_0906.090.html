Foreign Exchange: Dollar Falls to 1990 Low Against Yen Amid Talk That Fed Will Ease Credit

  • Author: Randolph Walerius Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/06/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar slid to a 1990 low against the yen late yesterday, after Japanese investors set the tone by selling off the greenback at the start of the global trading day. The U.S. currency declined sharply against other major currencies as well. U.S. traders cited a widespread expectation that the Federal Reserve will act as soon as tomorrow to signal an easing in monetary policy by lowering the target for its benchmark federal funds rate to 7 3/4% from what is now thought to be a target of 8%.

The release of U.S. employment data tomorrow is expected to provide the confirmation of economic weakness that the Fed needs to ease credit, observers said.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was changing hands at 141.70 yen, below 143.50 yen in New York late Tuesday. The dollar hasn't closed below 142 yen in New York since Dec. 26, 1989, when it was at 141.85 yen.

Against the mark, the dollar was quoted at 1.5585 marks, down from 1.5815 marks late Tuesday in New York. Sterling was trading at $1.9043, up from $1.8790.

In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the dollar was quoted at 141.85 yen, down from 142.30 yen at the close there Wednesday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5612 marks, down from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 1.5688 marks.

The fed funds rate is the rate banks charge one another on overnight loans and is the most sensitive indicator of the direction of U.S. interest rates.

Several analysts and traders said the expectation of the Fed's easing was only one of the factors behind the dollar's slide.

David Gilmore, foreign-exchange analyst at McCarthy, Crisanti & Maffei in New York, said the dollar's slide began shortly after a big Japanese investor tried to force the dollar higher with a huge buy order at 143.00 yen early in the global day. When the market saw what Mr. Gilmore said was a billion-dollar order go through with little benefit to the dollar, it decided to sell the greenback, he said.

After the decline set in, traders looked around and found the likelihood of the Fed's easing as a plausible explanation for the day's mood, Mr. Gilmore said.

There are many reasons to sell the dollar, said Kevin Weir, vice president at Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce in New York. In addition to the expectation of lower interest rates, there is the U.S. budget deficit, which is making its way back into the news after being displaced by the Persian Gulf crisis, he said.

And the Gulf standoff itself, as it drags on, only increases the pressure on the federal budget, Mr. Weir said.

"You can't balance the budget at the same time as trying to fight a war," said Christa Bleyleben, vice president at Bank of Boston.

The yen strengthened despite another sharp drop in the Tokyo stock market Wednesday.

U.S. traders said Japanese corporate investors were repatriating their money in part to improve their earnings picture at the midway point in their fiscal year.

The consensus among U.S. traders and analysts was that the Fed would lower the fed funds target rate by 25 basis points and some speculated that the Fed may even move to lower the discount rate from its current 7%. The discount rate is the price the Fed charges on loans to banks.

Mr. Gilmore, at McCarthy-Crisanti, said he expects the Fed to ease in two stages: first lowering the fed funds target to 7 3/4 by early next week and then by another 25 basis points after inflation reports are released later this month.

While interest rates in the U.S. are expected to head lower, those in West Germany and Japan will remain unchanged or rise, observers said.

In Europe, the dollar fell against major currencies. Sterling was supported by renewed speculation over its entry into the exchange rate mechanism of the European Monetary System.

In Tokyo, traders said increasing bearishness toward the U.S. currency helped push the dollar below the expected support level of 142.50 yen. The dollar finished Wednesday at 142.30 yen, well below 144.05 yen late Tuesday in Tokyo.

But traders predicted the dollar is approaching its near-term lows. They said buying interest below 142 yen should prove strong.

Meanwhile, on the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $386.10 an ounce, up $2.40. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.6 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $389.25 an ounce.
