Giving a Hand: Thomas Karaban Trades Fast Track For New Life Aiding Children

  • Author: John A. Conway
  • Date: 09/06/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

A case of burnout on Wall Street has sparked a bright new day for hundreds of terminally ill children. Brooklyn-born Thomas V. Karaban, 46 years old, had fought his way from back-office clerk to high-powered securities dealer. After 10 years of running a bond trading department, he was living "in suburban heaven" in Middletown, N.J. He was also living on coffee and cigarettes -- "a 16-cup and a five-pack man," as he describes himself at that time. In 1983, on the edge of collapse, he decided there had to be a better way and quit the Street.

This enforced departure, which brought a year "flat on my back," helped Mr. Karaban find his better way and a whole new perspective. While coaching a local junior hockey team during his new-found leisure, he was told about a baby who needed a liver transplant. He staged a hockey tournament and raised $7,000 for the child. At the urging of the child's parents, he went on to raise an additional $50,000.

Helping children stricken with terminal or catastrophic illness would be his new purpose in life, Mr. Karaban decided. With $200,000 of his own savings, he set up the Rainbow Foundation in 1984. Since that time the foundation has helped more than 2,000 children with cash, medical equipment, airline tickets to special hospitals, even a funeral for a child whose parents couldn't afford one.

Mr. Karaban found the new work more than compensated for the limousines and luxury lunches that had gone with his Wall Street job. His philosophy is "kindness first and foremost." Too many social welfare agencies, he says, have a clinical approach. "They're almost too professional and they have somewhat of a tendency to dehumanize the medical situation."

The Rainbow Foundation emphasizes the human touch. When Mr. Karaban learned that a three-year-old cancer sufferer in Hackensack, N.J., was especially depressed at being in the hospital over Christmas, the foundation sent him a tree and presents. For Easter, they brought him a live rabbit and a magician. When the boy died that June, Mr. Karaban says, "it hit us like a ton of bricks."

For a six-year-old girl who became a paraplegic after an automobile accident, Mr. Karaban arranged flights to Miami, which has a special center to treat such patients. But he also included a special three-day detour to Disney World.

The children are the foundation's prime concern, but families get help too. A New Jersey truck driver's eight-year-old boy, for instance, was suffering from cancer and the family was dipping into their mortgage money to meet the medical bills. The week the boy died, the bank was threatening foreclosure. The Rainbow Foundation stepped in to help meet the mortgage payments.

Staffed entirely by volunteers, Mr. Karaban's foundation gets more than 95% of its funds from individual and corporate donations. Revenue last year was close to $160,000, with more than 90% going directly to sick children or their families.

After six years as a full-time philanthropist, however, Mr. Karaban found he could no longer carry the burden alone. "I have one child in college and another ready to go," he says. He went back to a Wall Street house this spring to head up trading in federal agency bonds, but running the Rainbow Foundation still takes up a major part of his time, and he has added a new goal.

A member of a New Jersey state committee on child-abuse prevention, Mr. Karaban is appalled that the state has no permanent facility to treat abused children. The present system -- putting children in foster homes or institutions -- is no real refuge for an abused child, Mr. Karaban argues. He wants a state facility that will be "not an institution but a place like a home." The governor's committee on which he sits has just submitted a report to Gov. James Florio calling for such a service. If Mr. Karaban's record of the past few years is any clue, New Jersey will get one before too long.
