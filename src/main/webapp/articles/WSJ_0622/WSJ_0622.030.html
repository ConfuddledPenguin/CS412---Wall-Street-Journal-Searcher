Letters to the Editor: Someday They'll Face the Barren Waste

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/22/90

In regard to William Fulton's May 8 editorial-page article, "Can Anything Stop Los Angeles?": In 1983 I was transplanted to the west end of the San Fernando Valley, and I've witnessed an incredible metamorphosis of the area. Development has been, shall we say, rampant. I believe there is a very affirmative yes to Mr. Fulton's question as to whether the continued sprawling of Los Angeles will ever cease. It has to do with one little precious commodity called water.

Mr. Fulton correctly describes the economic effects of the housing bonanzas in the valleys adjoining the San Fernando. He aptly notes that areas such as Mojave and Rosamond are next. But he and the developers are living in a fool's paradise by failing to consider the impact these new projects will make on available drinking water. It is a desert out there and we are running out of water here.

Los Angeles is finally catching up with its northern cousins and moving to water rationing. Areas such as Santa Barbara now have strict policies on water usage and consumption. Reclaimed water is being used -- however, this will surely not be enough for the demand required in these newly developed areas.

Los Angeles, the San Fernando Valley and most of Southern California are going to have to face the music and ratchet down the laissez-faire attitude toward unchecked development. The leaders here have truly been living in La La Land with regard to meeting basic human needs for open recreational space. If the developers want to develop, then LA's government leaders should be giving them incentives to improve what is already here with more parks, a reliable mass rail system and better drinking water. Los Angeles doesn't need more condos, strip malls or freeways. It needs active leadership.

David Van Horn

Woodland Hills, Calif.

---

Mr. Fulton's article reveals how current public policies are immeasurably cruel to the poor.

Basic food is cheap -- five pounds of rice cost $2. Used clothing is cheap in thrift shops. Many poor people can even afford cheap used cars. But the cost of housing has been artificially raised in many areas. This dreadful shortage of housing is not an act of nature, like a shortage of rain, but is the direct result of deliberate, purposeful zoning and "growth restrictions." Once this is done, "subsidized housing" is promoted as a sop to the poor.

We forget that the best way to ensure a good supply of cheap used cars for lower-income people is just to keep on building cars. The same is true of housing. The poor never lived in new housing or drove new cars -- they moved into the houses that wealthier people moved out of when new housing was built. While at one time suburbs were towns in their own right, containing a cross section of people from rich to poor, many such suburbs now are wealthy bedroom communities. The governments of such communities are legally able to restrict housing and push up the price of homes to $325,000. But would we tolerate a policy of the middle classes and the wealthy to restrict production of food and clothes, to push up the price of rice to $5 a pound, the price of a shirt to $100, just so more lower-income people could come to "subsidized" thrift shops and soup kitchens?

Zoning and growth restrictions should be abandoned; they reflect the legal enforcement of the wishes of those who have a place to live, not those who need a place to live. Local governments should vigorously build roads, schools, parks and public utilities to keep up with where people want to live -- not to restrict them.

Will Chamberlain

Needham, Mass.
