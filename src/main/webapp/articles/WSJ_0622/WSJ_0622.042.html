Greenspan Says Banks Haven't Induced A `Credit Crunch' With Loan Cutbacks

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan said he believes banks haven't cut back lending to the point of inducing a "credit crunch" and he asserted that "enough credit appears to be available" to fuel growth in the economy. "All things considered, continued modest economic growth remains the most likely outcome," Mr. Greenspan told the Senate Banking Committee.

The committee's hearing, called to consider mounting anecdotal evidence that banks have tightened lending standards in response to a slowing economy and tougher regulation, didn't resolve a dispute between regulators on one side and business groups and some lawmakers on the others. In recent weeks, many business groups have complained that banks have grown so tight with credit they are cutting off creditworthy borrowers and aren't willing to back viable projects.

Some congressmen, particularly from New England, are concerned the regulators may be exacerbating the economic slowdown. "I have become convinced it's partly because of overzealous regulation," said Sen. John Kerry, (D., Mass.).

Bank regulators reiterated that they are simply making banks set aside needed reserves for losses and that banks should continue making loans they believe will perform. "It's the banker's job to find the good loans and to make them," said Comptroller of the Currency Robert Clarke.

The regulators maintain that the broad slowdown in lending in New England, and the nationwide reluctance to lend for real estate construction projects, is a healthy response to lax lending standards over the past decade.

In recent days, however, mixed signals on the issue have been coming from the Bush administration. While the Treasury has played down concerns about tight credit, two members of President Bush's cabinet, Housing Secretary Jack Kemp and Secretary of Commerce Robert Mosbacher, have said they believe the credit crunch is more widespread.

"Especially hard hit have been small and medium-sized businesses, since these businesses tend to rely heavily on bank loans for their financing," Mr. Mosbacher said in a statement yesterday following up on comments he made last week to the Banking Committee. "These businesses include many small producers of the technologically advanced products and services that are of growing importance to our international competitiveness." Mr. Mosbacher didn't say what he thought was causing the credit squeeze.

Mr. Greenspan, for his part, argued that there is no general lack of credit for creditworthy borrowers. The Fed chairman did say that banks "unquestionably have made credit less available" for mergers and restructurings but said the restraint was probably warranted, considering the "rapid leveraging of many corporations" in the 1980s.

Still, while "lenders are generally not retreating from lending opportunities," Mr. Greenspan said, "significant problems cannot be ruled out in the period ahead, and we will continue to devote close attention to credit conditions."

Speaking more broadly about the economy, Mr. Greenspan said it is "puzzling" that unemployment has not gone up despite the slowing of economic growth in the past several months. Labor force growth has slowed, but the Fed isn't sure what's behind that, Mr. Greenspan said.
