INDIAN INVESTIGATION WIDENS

India has asked Switzerland for legal help in its probe of what are alleged to be illegal commissions paid in the purchase of six submarines from West Germany.

Swiss Justice Ministry spokesman Joerg Kistler said India alleges large payments were made by the Kiel-based Howaldtswerke-Deutsche Werft to Indian officials via Swiss banks in connection with the submarine deal. He said the Indian request, consisting of about 100 pages, was now being examined to see what measures might have to be taken by Swiss authorities.

Last January, accounts in four Swiss banks were ordered frozen in connection with alleged bribes paid to Indian officials by Swedish arms maker AB Bofors in the sale of howitzers to India.

A Swiss group involved in Third World matters said the new Indian request named more than 30 Swiss banks through which the illegal payments, said to total about 40 million Swiss francs ($28.3 million), were allegedly made in 1981 and 1982 in the submarine deal.

TAIWAN'S ELDERLY LAWMAKERS

The elderly members of Taiwan's legislature, who have dominated politics for four decades without having to face re-election, must all retire by the end of 1991, the Council of Grand Justices ruled. The elderly lawmakers, who number nearly 800, were elected on the Chinese mainland shortly before the Nationalist government lost a civil war to communist forces and retreated to Taiwan in 1949. The aging parliamentarians, now mostly in their 70s and 80s, were kept in office to symbolize the Nationalist claim to still be the legitimate government of China.

AIR FRANCE'S CUTBACK PLANS

Air France plans to cut back flights on some of its less-traveled routes and take other cost-saving measures in the face of sluggish traffic growth, Chairman Bernard Attali told the French business daily La Tribune de l'Expansion. He was quoted as saying that the state carrier's traffic rose only 1.8% during the first five months of 1990, compared with a 4.3% rise in capacity. Included among the cutback plans is a reduction in the number of flights to Algeria and the Caribbean.

JAPANESE RICE

Japan will keep its ban on rice imports because rice farmers here are "special," the agriculture minister said. "We have opened the agricultural market, but there is a line beyond which we cannot pass," said Tomio Yamamoto, minister of agriculture, fisheries and forestry. A recent survey of 3,000 people by Japan's Yomiuri Shimbun newspaper indicated that 71% favored easing rice-market controls, up from 41% in 1988.

PAPER FOR PRAVDA

Finland's paper exporters are cutting off all shipments to the Soviet Union because they haven't been paid. The move could affect publication of the Soviet Communist Party paper Pravda and the official government newspaper Izvestia. The two papers' stocks of newsprint have dwindled to a week's supply because other Western countries already have stopped paper exports to the Soviet Union, the Finnish report said. An official of the Confederation of Finnish Forest Industries said the Soviets owe Finnish paper exporters about $22 million.

SOVIET-SOUTH KOREAN TIES

The top Soviet official in Seoul said he doesn't expect his country to begin formal diplomatic relations with South Korea in the near future. Valery K. Nazarov, president of the Soviet trade office in Seoul, said there would be "several steps to overcome" before establishing ties and indicated the process would be complex. His comments contrasted with widespread speculation among political leaders in Seoul that ties would be established as early as September.

POLISH UNEMPLOYMENT SOARS

Unemployment in Poland accelerated in the first two weeks of June, the Labor Ministry said.

A ministry spokesman said 511,152 people, or 3.8% of the work force, were registered as unemployed June 16, compared with 443,222 May 31.

The Solidarity daily Gazeta Wyborcza forecast a further sharp rise in unemployment during the summer. It said 6,900 enterprises had announced plans to lay off 270,000 workers by the end of August. But the newspaper said about 45% of the current jobless were people who had never worked before and registered only to get unemployment benefits introduced by the Solidarity-led government.

Unemployment was officially nonexistent in Poland under communist rule. There were just 9,000 people registered as jobless in December before the austerity measures took effect.
