Phone Firms Ordered to Cut Access Rates --- FCC Tells Local Companies To Lower by $1.1 Billion Fees in Next 12 Months

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- The Federal Communications Commission ordered the nation's local telephone companies to cut the access rates charged to long-distance companies by $1.1 billion, a move that will give consumers a break on their long-distance bills. The reductions in the year starting July 1 -- $696 million more than the local companies proposed in April -- apply to the rates that long-distance companies pay for the origination and termination of interstate phone calls. The FCC estimates that interstate-access revenues total about $19 billion annually.

An American Telephone & Telegraph Co. spokesman said the company is studying the FCC order and plans to expand the $160 million in rate cuts scheduled to take effect July 1. He said it was too early to say how much of a savings customers will see, but emphasized that "this means more reductions for consumers and small businesses."

Spokesmen for MCI Communications Corp. and United Telecommunications Inc.'s US Sprint Communications unit said their companies would remain competitive with AT&T. In the past, most access-charge reductions have been passed along to long-distance customers.

The FCC said its common carrier bureau found that inaccurate projections of demand and costs would result in excessive returns for local phone companies. The agency figured that by underestimating the likely demand for service, the local companies would have collected $105 million more than they should have.

The bureau slashed more than $289 million in access costs after finding that many local phone companies forecast "unusually high growth rates in their costs without providing adequate explanation or justification." It said it cut an additional $302 million for other inflated costs. Further, the agency said that some phone companies made exorbitant forecasts of depreciation expense.

In an interesting twist, the commission found that several Bell companies had misallocated lobbying expenses to operating-expense accounts in 1989, wrongly passing on several million dollars of lobbying costs to phone-company customers. The agency cited Ameritech, BellSouth Corp., Nynex Corp. Pacific Telesis Group and Southwestern Bell Corp. The agency said that although the Baby Bells "are entitled to engage in lobbying, the commission's rules make clear that associated expenses must be excluded from rate-making."

Don Evans, MCI's director-technical regulatory affairs, said, "We're excited that the FCC investigated lobbying expenses, but I hope this isn't the end of it," he said. "This is only the interstate part and only what local exchange carriers admitted to."

MCI had asked the FCC to investigate whether the Bell companies were lobbying Congress at customers' expense. Washington-based MCI made its request earlier this year after the Bells earmarked $21 million -- $3 million apiece -- for an enormous campaign to persuade Congress to ease restrictions in the consent decree that broke up the Bell System. The decree bars the Baby Bells from providing the content of information services, manufacturing telecommunications gear and providing long-distance service.
