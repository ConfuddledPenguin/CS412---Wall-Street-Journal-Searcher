Law -- Legal Beat: `Pollution Exclusion' Loses in Wisconsin

  • Author: Amy Dockser Marcus and Christi Harlan
  • Date: 06/22/90

The Wisconsin Supreme Court ruled that a landfill operator's insurance policies cover damages resulting from pollution that occurred over many years. Whether insurers are liable for costs associated with gradual pollution has been the subject of extensive litigation in the nation. The decision marks the first time Wisconsin's highest court has interpreted a key provision in standard commercial liability insurance policies, known as "the pollution exclusion." The provision hasn't been used widely since 1986.

Insurers have contended that under the provision policyholders are not covered for damages arising from pollution that was released gradually into the environment. Policyholders counter that they are entitled to coverage under the provision as long as they didn't intentionally cause or expect the pollution.

The Wisconsin dispute involves a suit filed by 120 property owners who reside near a landfill site operated by Land Reclamation Ltd., a landfill operator in Racine. The residents allege that Land Reclamation violated environmental laws in its operation of the site, resulting in water contamination, decreased property values, and unpleasant odors. Land Reclamation denies the charges.

The residents sued the company and its insurer, Bituminous Casualty Corp., seeking $9.6 million in compensation for property damage. A lower court dismissed the case against Bituminous, a subsidiary of Chicago-based Old Republic International. Land Reclamation appealed the decision.

In a 6-1 decision, the state Supreme Court found that the meaning of the "pollution exclusion" was ambiguous and must be interpreted in favor of the policyholder. The court also ordered Bituminous to pay Land Reclamation's legal costs.

In a dissent, Judge Donald W. Steinmetz said the court's decision means that an insurance policy "now becomes a deep pocket to pay for business mistakes."

In a ruling that clashes with the Wisconsin decision, the Massachusetts Supreme Court last week found that the "pollution exclusion" bars coverage for gradual pollution.

Peter Lardner, president of Bituminous, said the company hasn't yet decided whether to appeal. "This continuing series of conflicting court decisions contributes to more confusion between customers and companies," he said.

"It was important for us to know that we now have insurance coverage," said Joe Muratore, a Racine lawyer for Land Reclamation.

Allan D. Krezminski of Schoone Ware Fortune & Leuck in Racine, a lawyer for the residents, said the decision may spur settlement negotiations.

INSIDER-TRADING CASE against Kidder, Peabody to proceed.

A state court judge in Dallas denied Kidder, Peabody & Co.'s request to dismiss most of the claims filed against the investment banking firm by Dallas-based Maxus Energy Corp.

Maxus Energy, formerly Diamond Shamrock Corp., alleges that it paid more than it should have in its 1983 acquisition of Natomas Co. because inside information was passed by former Kidder investment banker Martin A. Siegel to arbitrager Ivan F. Boesky.

Kidder Peabody, contending that it knew nothing of the alleged insider trading, won a ruling in mid-April from a New York federal judge that the banking firm wasn't liable to Maxus for damages.

Kidder cited the New York ruling last week when it argued before state District Judge Adolph Canales of Dallas that most of Maxus's claims should be dismissed.

But Judge Canales ruled this week that Maxus's case against Kidder could proceed. The judge didn't give any reason for his ruling. An attorney for Kidder declined to comment on the decision.

The case won't be scheduled for trial until after Oct. 1 to allow Mr. Boesky to testify, if needed, at the sentencing of Michael Milken, former head of junk bonds at Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc.

DEFENSE LAWYERS predict Panama will not thwart a pact on Noriega's legal fees.

Just as the U.S. reached an agreement that ended a two-month squabble over legal fees for the deposed dictator, the government of Panama has piped up, saying Panama should have first claim to any unfrozen funds.

Defense attorneys say they aren't worried, even though an attorney for the Panamanian government, Gregory Craig, said he intends to file civil suits against Mr. Noriega for more than $5 billion for a host of alleged misdeeds, including murder.

Judge William M. Hoeveler indicated he will give priority to Mr. Noriega's right to a fair trial over any civil claims. Thus, the defense is confident the judge won't entertain any requests to take away Mr. Noriega's kitty for legal fees.

In an interview, Judge Hoeveler acknowledged that Panama could petition foreign governments to freeze the accounts, just as the U.S. did. "Where the money is out of the country and controlled by a foreign entity, it may be entirely out of my hands," he said.

However, while Panama could itself seek to freeze the accounts, the defense lawyers doubt the government of Panama would do anything to aggravate the U.S., which has finally come through with funds to rebuild the battered country.

"Panama could ask foreign governments not to unfreeze the accounts," says Jon May, an attorney for Mr. Noriega. "But if it's not in the interest of the U.S., it may not be in the interest of Panama to do it."

Frank A. Rubino, another defense attorney, described the appearance in court Wednesday by Mr. Craig, an attorney with the Washington, D.C., law firm of Williams & Connolly, as "grandstanding."

Mr. Craig couldn't be reached for comment.

JUDGE ACCUSED of conflicts will continue to hear California antitrust case.

A Los Angeles federal judge ruled that U.S. District Judge William L. Gray can preside over a multimillion-dollar antitrust case involving six big oil companies.

Earlier this year, attorneys for the plaintiffs had requested that Judge Gray recuse himself from the case because of various alleged conflicts of interest. Among other things, the attorneys noted that the judge's wife held 10 shares of stock in Chevron Corp., a defendant in the case.

The 78-year old judge, who has presided over the case since its inception in 1975, refused to step down. Federal Judge Robert M. Takasugi this week denied the plaintiffs' motion to disqualify the judge on the condition that Mrs. Gray sell the Chevron stock.

Judge Gray said the shares were sold the day before the ruling. "I'm delighted that it's over," he said. "I was very much embarrassed about the motion which challenged my impartiality and integrity. My reputation has been vindicated."

In the case, the city of Long Beach and the state of California allege that the six companies conspired to pay artificially low prices to the state and city for oil produced from state-owned tidelands. Among the defendants are Unocal Corp., Mobil Corp., Royal Dutch/Shell Group's Shell Oil Co. unit, Exxon Corp. and Texaco Inc.

Gary Hoecker, a Los Angeles attorney for Long Beach, said: "We will decide Friday whether to abide by Judge Takasugi's ruling or to appeal."
