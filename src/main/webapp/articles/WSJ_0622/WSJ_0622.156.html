Credit Markets: Treasurys Rise as Investors Shrug Off Bad News, Allowing Bonds to Snap a 4-Day Losing Streak

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Susan Scherreik
  • Date: 06/22/90

NEW YORK -- The bond market snapped out of a four-day losing streak yesterday as investors brushed aside negative economic reports and pushed prices moderately higher. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended 3/8 point higher yesterday, a gain of $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, eased slightly to 8.49%.

Trading, however, was bumpy. Prices were higher in early trading due to modest buying on the part of Japanese investors. But the market moved lower briefly after the government reported that first-quarter gross national product was stronger than previously estimated. That was bearish for bonds because a stronger economy reduces the likelihood that the Federal Reserve will reduce interest rates.

The market had recovered from the GNP report when the government reported that the budget deficit widened to $42.55 billion in May from a $25.43 billion deficit a year earlier.

Traders expected the market to decline sharply on the news, but instead "the number came out, the market went down briefly then came right back," said Raymond Stone, a managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates, Princeton, N.J. "That suggests that there was a strong underlying bid in the market," he said.

Speculators who had established "short" positions earlier in the day, betting prices would slip in response to the May budget deficit report, were forced to scramble to cover those positions. That helped to push prices even higher. In a short sale, dealers and professional traders sell borrowed securities and later seek to make a profit by replacing what was borrowed with cheaper securities. Yesterday, that strategy backfired for some traders.

Analysts caution against reading too much into yesterday's rally. "Bond prices can move up and down almost on whim because of low volume," said Brian Fabbri, a managing director and chief economist at Midland Montagu Securities Inc.

Mr. Fabbri believes notes and bonds will remain stuck in tight trading bands, continuing the recent pattern, at least until the release of the June employment report on July 6.

Junk Bonds

The payment-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. stumbled after enjoying a robust run-up on Wednesday. Payment-in-kind bonds, which are extremely volatile, pay interest in the form of more securities instead of cash.

Junk-bond traders attributed the declines to light profit-taking and some investor skepticism about plans to refinance the debt-burdened company.

Prices of both RJR payment-in-kind issues, which rose more than six points Wednesday on news that the company's major shareholder, Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., is considering retiring a "significant amount" of its high-yield bonds, finished down more than one point.

RJR Nabisco 14.07% convertible payment-in-kind debentures, due in 2009, finished down 1 1/2 points at 95 5/8 on the New York Stock Exchange. The other widely held RJR issue, the 14.07% exchangeable payment-in-kind debentures, maturing in 2007, ended 1 1/4 points lower to close at 79 3/8. Trading in the RJR payment-in-kind issues was relatively light after Wednesday's frenzied pace.

In the broader junk bond market, most high-yielding, high-risk bonds finished the session little changed. In the investment-grade corporate debt market, high-grade corporate bonds ended unchanged to 1/8 point higher.

Meanwhile, the junk bonds of Trump Castle Funding Inc. and related issues weakened slightly on a report that a $65 million loan package for real estate developer Donald Trump is being held up by a consortium of four foreign banks.

Trump Castle's 13.75% first-mortgage bonds, due in 1997, ended at 59 1/2, down 1/2 point from Wednesday, while Trump Taj Mahal Funding Inc.'s 14% first-mortgage bonds, maturing in 1998, were down 1/2 point at 52.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 102 23/32 compared with 102 13/32 on Wednesday. The latest 10-year notes ended at 102 9/32 to yield 8.51% compared with 102 4/32 to yield 8.53% on Wednesday.

Short-term rates were mixed. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.77% for a bond-equivalent yield 8.02%, slightly higher than Wednesday. The discount rate on six-month bills ended at 7.65% to yield 8.05%, slightly lower than Wednesday.

Foreign Bonds

The British government bond market, growing increasingly volatile, broke a sharp two-day advance yesterday and ended lower.

Traders said that prices of British government bonds, which are called gilts, were higher during early trading. But they began to slip as investor caution took hold ahead of today's release of the May current account and trade deficits. Economists polled by Dow Jones Capital Markets Report expect, on average, that the current account deficit -- a broad trade measure -- narrowed to #1.4 billion ($2.41 billion) in May from #1.78 billion in April.

In trading, Britain's 11.75% bond due 2003/2007 fell 13/32 to 102 27/32 to yield 11.32%.

Elsewhere:

-- West German bond prices ended lower after another day of listless trading.

-- Japanese government bonds ended modestly lower as money market rates rose.

Municipal Bonds

Tax-exempt securities ended narrowly mixed after a sparsely traded session.

New-issue activity was also extremely light.

A $257 million Palm Beach County, Fla., criminal justice facilities revenue bond issue was tentatively priced to yield from 6.30% in 1994 to 7.32% in 2011 by a Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. group.

No new offerings are expected to be priced today.

In secondary activity, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 were off 1/8 point at 97 3/4 to yield 7.39%, compared with a 7.35% yield Wednesday.

Asset-, Mortgage-Backed Securities

An abundance of supply and a maturity perceived to be unpopular were behind the wider yield spread quoted for $1.25 billion Citicorp credit-card securities priced and freed to trade yesterday.

That is the view of several traders of asset-backed securities, who quoted the yield spread on the seven-year Citicorp securities at 0.91 to 0.92 percentage point above seven-year Treasurys.

First Boston Corp. and its affiliate Credit Suisse First Boston Ltd. were the lead underwriters of the issue.

The Class A certificates, backed by Visa and MasterCard credit-card receivables, were priced as 9 3/8% securities at 99.585 to yield 9.456%.

With this sale, nearly $4 billion of asset-backed securities have been priced this week. Just over half of those securities are backed by credit-card receivables.

Not only is there a supply crunch, but there is said to be some investor resistance to a seven-year maturity. The preference is for the shorter maturities that have been seen in the other offerings earlier in the week.

In the market for secondary mortgage-backed securities, Government National Mortgage Association securities for July delivery were 3/32 firmer at 98 7/8. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% securities were 1/8 firmer at 98 6/32 and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% securities were also up 1/8, at 98 22/32.
