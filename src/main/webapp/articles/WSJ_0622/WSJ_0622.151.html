Foreign Exchange: Dollar Ends Trading Mostly Higher; Yen Is Sold for Pound, Australian Unit

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/22/90

NEW YORK -- The yen was the loser as Asian investors moved their money from the low-yielding Japanese currency to the British pound and Australian dollar, both buttressed by high domestic interest rates. The flow of international capital left the U.S. dollar higher against most other major currencies. The market's devotion to interest rate differentials among nations stems from a recent lack of clear exchange-rate trends. Its propensity for non-dollar cross-trading has increased as dealers seek to exploit momentary profit opportunities in an unfocused market.

"Some are of the opinion that the summer market has begun, which is very appropriate," because summer began yesterday, said Carol Callanan, assistant vice president for Credit Lyonnais in New York.

But Ms. Callanan said talk of summer doldrums really should wait until after German monetary union takes effect July 1.

The foreign exchange market lacks "guts" amid political changes around the world, said Peter Connelly, foreign exchange manager for Wells Fargo Bank in San Francisco, so participants are taking refuge in short-term cross-trading positions.

In Asian trading, Japanese investment trusts and life insurance companies were seen buying Australian dollars and British pounds with yen. Both the Australian dollar and the pound are attractive because of domestic interest rates of 15%.

The Australian dollar reached an intraday high of 79.10 U.S. cents by midday in New York after crossing two resistance levels.

In late New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.6778 marks, down from 1.6785 marks late Wednesday in New York. The U.S. currency also was changing hands at 154.80 yen, up from 153.93 yen. Sterling was trading at $1.7200, down from $1.7215.

At midmorning in Tokyo Friday, the dollar was trading at 154.90 yen; it closed Thursday in Tokyo at 154.60 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6770 marks, down from Thursday's Tokyo close of 1.6780 marks.

Pat O'Brien, chief dealer for Australia's State Bank of Victoria in New York, said Thursday that the Australian currency's appeal spread to other currency sectors throughout the global day. "With the problems in Canada, a lot of people are taking the money they once put in the Canadian dollar and moving into the Australian dollar and the pound," he said, referring to Canada's constitutional dispute over Quebec.

Sterling also benefited from yen selling, but its gain against the dollar was halted in Asian trading at $1.7265. By the end of the global trading day, the pound had given up all its early gains against the dollar and more.

The British currency continues to be aided by speculation about the U.K.'s entry into the exchange rate mechanism of the European Monetary System.

The proposal Wednesday by U.K. Chanchellor of the Exchequer John Major for a "hard" European currency unit was interpreted by the foreign exchange market yesterday as showing new seriousness about European cooperation.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $350.70 an ounce, up 20 cents. Estimated volume was a light 1.7 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $349.75 an ounce.
