Brokerage Firms Slam Wall Street's `Soft-Dollar' Deals

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- Two major Wall Street firms fired a missile at a prevalent institutional stock- and bond-trading practice, asking federal officials to curtail sharply the way pension funds direct their transactions. The letter, from Goldman, Sachs & Co. and Morgan Stanley Group Inc. to Labor Secretary Elizabeth Dole, takes on a lifeblood of Wall Street -- "soft dollars." A barter form of payment, soft dollars are credits that pension funds and mutual funds receive from brokerage firms for trading commissions. The funds use those credits instead of cash to pay for such things as research, computer services, economists and client referrals.

Backers say they help institutions pick and choose the services they want and allow star Wall Street analysts to form their own boutique firms. But critics counter that the soft-dollar business is a quagmire, rife with fraud that forces pensioners to pay for services that fund managers should be paying themselves. Moreover, Goldman and Morgan contend that soft dollars hurt overall market liquidity and create excess volatility.

The letter from the two firms points up Wall Street's rift over the soft-dollar question. Most other brokerages have reluctantly set up their own soft-dollar units on the assumption that failing to do so would force business to other firms. Morgan and Goldman have steadfastly refused to enter the fray, and now appear to be hoping for regulatory relief.

The debate over soft dollars has heated up again in the past few years as volume since the October 1987 stock-market crash has remained at sharply lower levels than during the mid-1980s. Because large institutions, such as mutual funds and pension funds, have to reimburse soft-dollar brokers for services no matter how much volume they do, Wall Street firms without soft-dollar operations are getting a proportionately smaller share of the pie.

The two firms asked the Labor Department and the Securities and Exchange Commission to: require pension plans to disclose the extent of soft-dollar deals; clarify the types of soft-dollar accords that fall outside pension rules; and then take enforcement action.

The soft-dollar issue isn't likely to fade soon. "This goes to the very heart of what's going on on Wall Street," said Lee Pickard, a Washington lawyer who represents many soft-dollar firms. "It's a competitive turf battle."

The letter and an accompanying report, copies of which were obtained by this newspaper, were sent several months ago by Robert Rubin, Goldman's vice chairman, and Richard Fisher, Morgan Stanley's president. A Morgan Stanley spokeswoman said the Labor Department hasn't formally responded. She said that the letter was a follow-up to an SEC roundtable held last year on soft dollars, and that Morgan Stanley wanted to express concern about the use of soft-dollar accords in fixed-income investing. Goldman Sachs declined to comment.

"It's an area we have been interested in for a number of years and an area we continue to have interest in," said Charles Lerner, director of enforcement at the Labor Department's Pension and Welfare Benefits Administration. He declined to say what the agency might do.

But the reception from the SEC doesn't appear to be particularly warm. Robert Colby, chief counsel for market regulation, said the agency has taken a wait-and-see attitude toward changing the rules on soft dollars. "There's no timetable for deciding when to move on any of these complaints," he said.

"Obviously for these firms there are competitive concerns that mingle with altruism and their concerns for the clients," Mr. Colby said. "That really calls for caution."

Goldman and Morgan contend in their report that soft dollars are a major problem for the markets. They estimate that 30% to 50% of the total trades on the New York Stock Exchange use soft dollars.

"The overall consequence of soft-dollar practices on the securities markets is a decrease in market liquidity, which can lead to increased market volatility and general disruption of trading," the report said. Moreover, soft dollars are hurting the return that pension funds are getting "because investment managers are not obtaining best execution of securities trades in transactions involving soft dollars."

At the heart of the firms' argument is a securities-law provision called Section 28e, which allows money managers to use soft-dollar practices if they receive research in return. If the arrangement meets Section 28e guidelines, practices that otherwise would violate Employee Retirement Income Security Act laws are protected from Labor Department enforcement action.

But Morgan and Goldman said that pension-plan sponsors aren't being told how soft dollars are being used. Moreover, they added that some soft dollars are being used for futures transactions, in violation of Section 28e, which covers only securities trades.

Some soft-dollar firms argue that Goldman and Morgan are simply engaged in a misguided campaign of sour grapes. "It's an attempt to restrict competition by lobbying the regulators," said Don Potts, president of Capital Institutional Services Inc., a large independent soft-dollar firm based in Dallas. "The real problem we're dealing with here is the overcapacity of Wall Street."
