REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): The Revolution Goes South

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/22/90

Another socialist politburo has suffered a major setback at the polls. This time the victor is not a charismatic labor leader or a tousle-haired playwright but rather Islamic fundamentalists. And so the world now sits up and wonders if Algeria is about to become another Iran. Algeria need not devolve in that fashion. The unexpected success of the Islamic Salvation Front in the municipal and provincial elections last week signifies more about the post-colonial past than it does about some fundamentalist future.

The Arabs have a saying that "the state eats the green and the dry." They mean that an authoritarian government consumes everything in its path. It captures the labor unions, the guilds, the bar and the middle classes. It allows no bourgeois opposition and renders exiled political leaders almost powerless by strangling their domestic support. There is only one refuge, the church. This has been the pattern in Algeria.

These are the first elections the post-colonial Algerian government has permitted. While stifling dissent, the rulers created a rich and corrupt apparatchik class. Meanwhile, oil wealth was squandered on huge industrial public works, farms were collectivized, the currency inflated and the young denied opportunity (in sum, a familiar Third World scenario).

Inevitably, opposition to the government flocked around Islam, as opponents to communism in Poland flocked around the Catholic church.

Also, the Algerian population is overwhelmingly young, and concentrated in urban centers along the coast. Unemployment has been as high as 22% nationwide and 50% among the urban poor. Throughout the Islamic crescent these idle young have been the breeding ground for fundamentalist revolution. The half educated young urban poor are nurtured by the welfare organization that fundamentalists sponsor.

But one of the peculiarities of Algeria is that while the opposition is hostile to the legacy of socialist misrule, it is not mainly composed of radical anti-moderns. Nor do most supporters of the opposition despise the West or their current president, Chadli Bendjedid. Indeed, President Chadli is credited for having begun to open up his nation's political system, and for allowing this election. Many of those who voted for the fundamentalists would support a secular opposition if one existed.

The proper course now would be to throw the political doors wide open. The Algerian government should allow an array of non-fundamentalist opposition parties, and reduce the danger of an Islamic revolution. The worst course would be measures to stifle dissent.

Too many Western commentators develop brain lock-up when confronted by Islamic fundamentalism. Rather than being imprisoned by the experience of Iran, the world might better view these election results as an Eastern European democratic revolution stuck in an Arab context. It even comes complete with a reassertion of ethnic, in this case Berber, identity. If real reform continues, the election results aren't likely to lead to an Islamic revolution, or a wave of chaos and emigration.
