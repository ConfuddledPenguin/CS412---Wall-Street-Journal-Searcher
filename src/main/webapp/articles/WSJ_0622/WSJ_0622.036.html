Politics & Policy: Budget Deficit Widened in May, Treasury Says

  • Author: Andrew Sobel and David Wessel
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- The federal government's budget deficit widened to $42.55 billion in May from $25.43 billion a year earlier, the Treasury said, reflecting sizable spending on the thrift-industry bailout and declining tax revenue. Last month's budget gap compared with a revised surplus of $41.76 billion in April. The Treasury previously reported the April surplus as $42.52 billion.

In its monthly statement, the Treasury said receipts totaled $69.21 billion last month, compared with $71.03 billion a year earlier and $139.62 billion in April.

April usually shows a surplus because the filing deadline for individual tax returns occurs at midmonth, and receipts from personal income taxes set a record this past April. However, both the White House and congressional budget offices say that they've been forced to widen their deficit forecasts for this year and next because federal tax receipts are falling short of their forecasts.

Although the reasons aren't yet clear, one factor appears to be that total wages and salaries -- on which payroll and personal income taxes are paid -- are falling short of expectations and may have been lower in the recent past than Commerce Department reports suggest.

Outlays in May totaled $111.76 billion, up 16% from $96.46 billion a year earlier and 14% from $97.87 billion the month before.

Outlays this past May included $7.27 billion spent by the Resolution Trust Corp. to clean up the S&L industry. The rest of the increase reflected such items as interest on the national debt and spending on such programs such as Social Security and Medicaid.

For the first eight months of fiscal 1990, which began Oct. 1, receipts totaled $667.13 billion, up 4.1% from $640.89 billion the year before. Outlays through May totaled $818.81 billion, an 8.6% rise from $754.1 billion a year earlier.

That produced a total deficit of $151.68 billion for the first eight months of fiscal 1990, compared with $113.21 billion a year earlier.

The deficit for all of fiscal 1989 was $152 billion. White House Budget Director Richard Darman told congressional budget negotiators this week that he expects the deficit for all of fiscal 1990 to be between $184 billion and $206 billion, including spending on the S&L cleanup. Excluding thrift spending, the White House projects the deficit at $158 billion. The deficit target in the Gramm-Rudman law for fiscal 1990 is $100 billion, but there is no legal penalty for missing it.

The government paid $21.65 billion in interest last month on the federal debt.
