General Binding Sees Profit Falling Below Analysts' Forecasts

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/22/90

NORTHBROOK, Ill. -- General Binding Corp., hurt by "soft" markets and higher expenses, said it expects second quarter earnings to fall short of analysts' expectations. Rudolph Grua, president and chief executive officer, said the office products company expects to report profit fell to 30 cents or 31 cents a share from 35 cents a year earlier. He said that would put net income at $4.8 million to $5 million, down from $5.6 million.

Mr. Grua said analysts and the company had projected earnings around 38 cents a share. He said higher expenses, along with slower sales growth in May and June, forced the lowering.

In addition, Mr. Grua said soft markets mean flat gross margins in its basic business of making machines and supplies to laminate and bind paper. And he said margins are down in the company's three-ring metal binder business.

The news sent General Binding shares down $2.50 to close at $18.50 in over-the-counter trading yesterday.

The expected earnings would mark the second consecutive quarterly shortfall for General Binding after a 16-quarter string of higher earnings.

But Mr. Grua said he expects that modestly higher earnings in this year's final two quarters should help the company meet or slightly exceed $18.8 million, or $1.18 a share, which the company reported last year before an income tax refund made net $20.1 million, or $1.26 a share.

General Binding expects second quarter sales to increase about 11% to $80 million from $71.9 million a year earlier. But excluding the Loose Leaf Metals unit acquired last August, comparable sales are expected to rise about 9% -- below the company's earlier expectation of double-digit growth.

Mr. Grua attributed the lower-than-expected sales to softer international markets for office products. The sales shortfall is mainly on the machine side, Mr. Grua said. He said the company's sales of supplies for those machines remain healthy.

Despite the earnings setback, Mr. Grua said he doesn't plan to stop looking for acquisitions or interrupt the company's annual dividend increases.
