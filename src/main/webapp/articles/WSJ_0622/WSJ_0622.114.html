Politics & Policy: Bush Deficit Plan Leaves Democrats Split on Next Step

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- Democrats continued to grouse about the Bush administration's new $51-billion deficit-reduction plan, but they didn't walk away from budget talks with the White House. While uniformly critical of the plan, Democrats appeared split on how to proceed. Senate Budget Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.) complained the new plan "doesn't give us any foundation on which to build." But his House counterpart, Rep. Leon Panetta (D., Calif.), deemed it "a starting point" that ought to lead to more give-and-take at the White House-Congress budget summit. Democrats plan to huddle on strategy this weekend before rejoining the talks Monday.

The administration says its new blueprint would shrink the projected fiscal 1991 deficit by $50.7 billion. That isn't nearly enough to avoid across-the-board spending cuts under the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law. The new proposal includes $11.7 billion in spending cuts in addition to ones President Bush proposed in January.

President Bush told reporters he is ready to allow the automatic budget cuts required by law to go into effect if an agreement isn't signed. He also said he expects interest rates to decline if a budget agreement is signed.
