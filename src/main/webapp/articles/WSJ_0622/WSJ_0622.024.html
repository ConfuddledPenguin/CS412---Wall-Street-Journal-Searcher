Letters to the Editor: Renoir at $78 Million Simply a Decent Price

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/22/90

Ann E. Berman's May 24 Leisure & Arts article on the recent Sotheby's impressionist/post-impressionist auctions is interesting, not least because it permits a computation of the return earned by the seller of Renoir's "Au Moulin de la Galette." Ms. Berman states that the seller acquired this painting in 1929 for $165,000. The selling price ($78.1 million) includes both the buyer's and the seller's commission, so the net price to the seller is approximately $64.5 million. Out of this, taxes must be paid; if the applicable tax rate is 33%, then the after-tax selling price is approximately $43.5 million.

This is about a 9.56% compound annual rate of return on the original purchase price, in nominal terms, and before accounting for any costs of ownership of the painting (insurance, security costs, conservation, etc.). If we adjust for inflation, we can express the purchase price in terms of current prices as about $1.5 million. In real terms, the compound annual return on the original purchase price is about 5.67% (again, without adjusting for any costs of ownership).

These returns, while decent, are clearly not spectacular. Given that Renoir's "Au Moulin de la Galette" is one of the most famous of his works, this suggests that the pecuniary returns to ownership of even the best and most famous works of art probably do not exceed the returns to other investments.

Donald A. Coffin

Associate Professor of Economics

Indiana University Northwest

Gary, Ind.
