Law: Court Rules News Media Aren't Immune From Libel Suits Over Editorial Opinions

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court ruled the First Amendment doesn't make the news media immune from libel lawsuits based on statements of opinion. The high court said opinions expressed in newspaper columns and editorials and in television and radio commentaries are entitled only to the constitutional protections that the news media already receive for stories based on facts, and not more.

Writing for the court, Chief Justice William Rehnquist said many expressions of opinion will still have constitutional protection. The high court, he explained, has previously ruled that those who file libel lawsuits against the news media must be able to prove that the disputed statements were wrong; it is difficult, if not impossible, to prove that statements that are clearly opinion are wrong.

Given the other protections that are afforded the news media, Harvard Law School Professor Laurence Tribe said, "The absence of a general immunity for opinions is not terribly threatening."

But the ruling was seen as a significant defeat to some lawyers who represent the news media in libel actions and other First Amendment matters. Robert Sack, a New York lawyer who represents Dow Jones & Co., publisher of The Wall Street Journal, said newspaper editorial pages "are going to be less free to say what they have to say." He said that lawyers who review newspaper articles before they are published to avoid possibly libelous statements will no longer be able to ignore the editorial pages.

Mr. Sack also said the ruling will likely cause the news media to face more libel trials; until now, media lawyers have been successful in asking judges to dismiss lawsuits based on statements of opinion.

In justifying the decision, Chief Justice Rehnquist noted that libel law is intended to protect the reputation of individuals, not merely the airing of ideas by the news media.

Technically, the vote was 7-2, with Justice William Brennan dissenting, joined by Justice Thurgood Marshall. But although the dissenters didn't join the majority opinion, they said they agreed with the court's conclusion that there is no special libel exemption for opinion statements. Their dissent was based on the court's application of this principle to the facts of the case.

The case, which now returns to an Ohio court for a trial, is a 15-year-old dispute pitting the former high school wrestling coach in the Cleveland suburb of Maple Heights against the Lake County News-Herald, published by Lorain Journal Co., a unit of Community Newspapers Inc. After a melee at a wrestling match, state athletic officials disciplined the team, but a judge overturned the sanctions in 1975. The News-Herald ran a story saying the coach and local school superintendent lied to the judge.

The case bounced up and down in the state courts, including two prior appeals to the U.S. Supreme Court, but an Ohio appeals court finally ruled that the statements were opinions that were absolutely immune from libel lawsuits.

The high court said the accusation that the coach had lied is a statement that can be proved or disproved at a trial. Justice Brennan's dissent argued that the statements were like conjecture that can't be disproved, and that there was no reason for another trial.

The high court's ruling, Mr. Sack said, contradicts the position adopted by virtually all of the 12 federal appeals courts in the country and by three-quarters of the state supreme courts -- that statements of opinion can't form the basis of libel lawsuits.

Mr. Sack also said that the decision may chill the free speech rights of persons who speak publicly, not in the news media, at town meetings or protest rallies, for example accusing local companies of polluting waters. Those people may be more susceptible to libel lawsuit, he said. (Milkovich vs. Lorain Journal Co.)

State Gas Lawsuits

The justices ruled 5-4 that states may not sue in behalf of consumers to recover overcharges that public utilities were charged by their suppliers of natural gas and that the utilities passed through to their customers in the form of price increases. The decision, written by Justice Anthony Kennedy, rebuffed the arguments of Kansas, Missouri and 32 other states.

The high court ruled that only the utilities themselves are legally considered injured parties under federal antitrust law. The states and consumers are indirect purchasers with economic injuries too ambiguous to allow antitrust liability, the court said.

Missouri and Kansas officials were seeking the right to sue several producers of natural gas in Wyoming and an interstate pipeline company for alleged overcharges in the sale of gas to three retail gas distributors.

The producers, Amoco Production Co., an Amoco Corp. unit, and Cities Service Oil & Gas Corp., an Occidental Petroleum Corp. unit, and the pipeline, Williams Natural Gas Co., a Northwest Energy Co. unit, were initially sued for alleged overcharges by the retail utilities, Kansas Power & Light Co. and UtiliCorp United Inc. The lawsuit alleged that the overcharges were the result of price-fixing.

A federal court dismissed the lawsuit by the states, leaving the claims by the utilities in place. A federal appeals court in Denver said the retail companies, not the states, were the proper parties to file the lawsuit.

After the Supreme Court agreed to hear the appeal, Kansas Power & Light dropped out of the case.

The high court upheld the appeals court ruling yesterday. Justice Byron White dissented, joined by Justices Brennan, Marshall, and Harry Blackmun (Kansas vs. UtiliCorp United Inc.)

Court-in-Brief

In other action yesterday, the high court:

-- Ruled 7-2 that the Interstate Commerce Commission doesn't have the legal authority to allow shippers of goods and truckers to agree on rates that are lower than those officially filed by the truckers. Federal law requires truckers to formally file their rates with the ICC and to charge those rates. The ICC, however, has broadly permitted shippers and truckers to negotiate lower rates. The ruling, written by Justice Brennan, reverses the decision of a federal appeals court in St. Louis and is a major defeat for the ICC. Justice John Stevens and Chief Justice Rehnquist dissented. (Maislin Industries, U.S. Inc. vs. Primary Steel Inc.)

-- Ruled 9-0 that nonprofit social clubs may not generally offset investment income for federal income purposes with losses incurred in sales of food and beverages to nonmembers. Only when the sales to nonmembers were intended to make a profit may a club claim the loss against investment income, the court said in an opinion written by Justice Harry Blackmun. (Portland Golf Club vs. Commissioner)
