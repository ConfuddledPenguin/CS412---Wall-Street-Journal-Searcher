Credit Rating: RJR Refinancing Lauded by Moody's; S&P Affirms Ratings

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/22/90

NEW YORK -- RJR Nabisco Inc.'s multibillion-dollar debt refinancing plan won some praise from Moody's Investors Service Inc., which downgraded the food and tobacco company's bonds in January. The refinancing plan's "impact on leverage and {interest} coverage ratios appears positive," the rating concern said. It didn't say whether it is considering changing its single-B ratings on most of RJR's high-yield, below-investment-grade "junk" bonds. When Moody's lowered some RJR ratings, prices of RJR and other junk bonds plunged as much as 10% in a week.

RJR and its main shareholder, the New York leveraged buy-out firm Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., are developing a plan to retire more than $4 billion of junk bonds, interest rates for which must be reset by next April. Terms of the reset agreement could require RJR to pay substantially higher interest rates on these bonds, which would add to its debt burden and weaken its financial health.

Separately, Standard & Poor's Corp. affirmed its ratings of double-B on RJR's senior debt and single-B-plus on subordinated debt. It said these ratings had already reflected S&P's belief that RJR would successfully address the reset requirement.

If RJR does refinance its debt, said S&P, "The increased near-term requirement for cash interest and preferred dividends would largely offset the longer-term benefits of a reduced but still quite sizable total debt and interest burden."

Nonetheless, S&P said: "The potential for an upgrade over the next few years remains. An upgrade would be predicated upon continued strong operating performance and cash-flow generation growth. This would likely require RJR's domestic cigarette market share to stabilize, ongoing industry volume declines to remain near current levels, and an anticipated near-term excise tax increase on tobacco to be of moderate size or be deferred."

Meanwhile, several bankers reported progress in raising a $2.25 billion bank loan to help RJR refinance its junk-bond debt. The loan is being led by units of Manufacturers Hanover Corp., Chase Manhattan Corp., Citicorp and Bankers Trust New York Corp., each of which is expected to contribute $350 million. Bank commitments for the full amount should be gathered in a week or two, an officer at one of the four lead banks said. "We're in pretty good shape," he said.

Two Japanese banks said they are interested in joining the loan, though other Japanese banks have pulled back substantially from their previously brisk role in lending to highly leveraged companies.
