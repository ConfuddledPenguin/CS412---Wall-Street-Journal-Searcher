Economy: U.S. Estimate Of GNP Growth Boosted to 1.9% --- First-Period Figures Revised From 1.3% Annual Rate; Bigger Export Rise Seen

  • Author: Tuesday Georges
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- The government nudged up its estimate of first-quarter economic growth to a 1.9% annual rate from the 1.3% pace it previously reported. The revision was primarily due to new information that showed export growth was far stronger than was estimated in a report issued last month. The surge in exports was partly offset, however, by consumer spending that was found to be weaker than the previous report said.

Net exports of goods and services rose at an annual rate of 12.8% in the first quarter, compared with an earlier projection of 7.7% growth. That was offset by a downward revision in personal consumption, which grew at a pace of only 1.6% in the first quarter, rather than 2.4% as projected in last month's report.

Although the increase in the nation's real gross national product -- the inflation-adjusted value of all the goods and services produced -- was larger than the fourth quarter's 1.1% rate, some economists said they were disappointed by the latest figures.

"On the surface this report shows a strong {export} performance, but it really masks the weakness that is present in the consumer sector," said Roger Brinner, chief economist for Data Resources Inc., in Lexington, Mass. "What is important is not only the rate of growth, but also the composition of growth."

The Commerce Department report said that after-tax corporate profits also edged up more than previously estimated, growing 1.1% in the first quarter to an annual rate of $158.5 billion. The revision is a clear improvement over the previous estimate of 0.2%, but is a drastic slowdown from the fourth quarter increase of 2.8%, the department said.

Profits from current production rose 1.3% in the first quarter, exceeding the government's earlier estimate of 0.7%. This increase followed three quarters in which profits from current production fell.

According to yesterday's report, net corporate cash flow fell 1.8% in the first quarter, far less than the 2.3% decline previously estimated by the government.

All the numbers are adjusted for predictable seasonal variations.
