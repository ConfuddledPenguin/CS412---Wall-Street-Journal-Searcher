Japan, Switzerland and U.S. Are Said To Have Most-Competitive Economies

  • Author: Tim Carrington
  • Date: 06/22/90

LONDON -- Japan, Switzerland and the U.S. ranked as the world's three most-competitive economies, according to an annual study by the World Economic Forum and the International Institute for Management Development. The study, based on more than 300 criteria ranging from tax structures to levels of social alienation, attempts to rate countries according to how conducive they are to running successful businesses. The study also included the subjective but important measure of how much confidence business executives had in the nations where they did business. Here, West Germany, which placed fourth in the overall scoreboard, ranked first -- apparently because of enthusiasm generated by pending reunification with East Germany and investment plans elsewhere in Eastern Europe.

The survey compiled a separate roster for economies that have recently industrialized. In this group, the top three were Singapore, Taiwan and Hong Kong.

Growth expectations appear to be the strongest in Asia, with South Korea leading the category of "rising stars." Another contender in this group was Ireland, which won high scores for expected industrial growth. According to the survey's authors, Ireland "is developing a Far Eastern country profile, which is reflected in the optimism of Irish entrepreneurs."

The survey's competitiveness rankings don't reflect the difficulties international companies have breaking into particular markets. Japan, which held the No. 1 ranking, is considered one of the least-hospitable nations for foreign corporations to invest in. The prominent position extended Switzerland, where both the report's sponsors are based, reflected the favorable tax policies there, along with political stability.

The U.S. ranked first for its human resources, a category that includes the age, skills and size of the work force. The survey also showed that in 1986, 59% of U.S. 20-to-24-year-olds were enrolled in some form of higher education. Ranking relatively low in this survey was Britain, where only 22% of this age group took part in higher education.

However, the U.S. came in sixth place in "future orientation." In this category, which covers such items as research funding and patents, Japan took first place.

In worker productivity, Sweden and Singapore claimed the highest ranking, with Japan coming in second place, and the U.S. placing third. Germany took fifth place in the productivity rankings, France seventh and Italy ninth. Of the seven major industrial countries, Britain showed the lowest worker productivity: It ranked 16th of the 34 countries included in the survey.

The study added several new categories this year. One was a ranking of 23 countries according to how effectively they prevented corruption. Here, Denmark and Singapore tied for first place, while Italy came in 23rd. The U.S. fell close to the middle, holding down the 13th place.

While the report said that national economic competition is a reality, it warned that "obsessive" rivalry between the U.S., Japan, and Europe would be destructive. This "triad," the report said, controls about 60% of the world's gross national product, with only 15% of its population. Trade wars could leave the rest of the world in the cold, it warned. However, the report predicted that over time, such nations as Mexico, India, Brazil and China would gather greater economic clout.

Among the Eastern European nations, East Germany stands the greatest chance for a successful transformation, the study said, because of "the massive resource transfer" from West Germany. The report gave Czechoslovakia and Hungary the next best prospects. Poland got a gloomier review. "Having the most tattered economy and being battered by a huge foreign-debt burden, Poland's problem will be to ensure sheer survival," the report said.

In a discussion of Japan's success, the report emphasized the nation's persistence. It noted that, in the Japanese view, Western business executives expect "too much too soon." Contentment -- or perhaps complacency -- is slower coming in Japan. The study cited a Japanese survey that found that 52% of the new generation of Tokyo residents believed they were living in an affluent society, compared with 70% of a like group living in Los Angeles.
