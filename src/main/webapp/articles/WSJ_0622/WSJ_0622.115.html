Grumman, Boeing Settle Cost Dispute On Defense Program

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 06/22/90

BETHPAGE, N.Y. -- Grumman Corp. and Boeing Co. said they settled their dispute over costs for the U.S. defense program called Joint Stars. "The settlement eliminates substantial claims by both parties concerning responsibility for the costs of completion of the first two Joint Stars aircraft," the companies said. A Grumman spokesman said no details of the settlement would be released.

In a joint statement, Grumman Chairman John O'Brien and Boeing Vice Chairman Malcolm Stamper said, "This settlement will improve our mutual concentration on other important assignments on which we are working together."

In 1985, Grumman won a $657 million contract for a five-year development program for the Joint Surveillance Target Attack Radar System, which will identify ground targets and guide aircraft and missiles. The value of the contract has since been increased to $850 million, Grumman said.

Meanwhile, Boeing won an $80.2 million subcontract to modify two aircraft for the radar system's "aerial platform," but late in 1987 Boeing requested a $122.5 million price increase. Boeing alleged that Grumman's demands on the subcontractor had departed from the contract and driven up costs.

For its part, Grumman then sought an undisclosed sum from Boeing "for damages caused by deficiencies in performance of the subcontract."

Grumman said Joint Stars is "in the final phases" of flight testing by Grumman, with a U.S. Air Force evaluation planned for this year.
