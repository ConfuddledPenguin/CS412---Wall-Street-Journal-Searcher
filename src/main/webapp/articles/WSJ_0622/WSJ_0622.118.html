Enterprise -- Financing Small Business: Big Venture Funds Grow; Securities Rule Holds Promise

  • Author: Udayan Gupta
  • Date: 06/22/90

BIG VENTURE CAPITAL partnerships grow while others find pickings lean. New Enterprise Associates, which already manages $470 million in venture capital funds, last week raised another $200 million from such investors as Chicago's Brinson Partners and the pension fund of American Telephone & Telegraph Co. And Canaan Partners, Rowayton, Conn., raised $200 million this month to add to the $240 million it already manages.

The venture partnerships mostly are "getting their money from pension funds who want to have a small portion of their assets in venture capital," says a San Francisco institutional investor. "The pension funds are playing it safe by going with the large ones."

But the big funds also have been able to separate themselves from others with specific strategies. New Enterprise, for example, has adopted a national investment strategy, establishing a network of regional and industry-specific "feeder funds" to provide candidates for investment, says Richard Kramlich, a New Enterprise managing partner. Canaan is focusing on providing capital for expansion and acquisitions of small companies.

Still, most funds formed in the '80s will face an uphill struggle raising new money, investors say. "Their prior funds haven't fulfilled their promise," says Anthony Hoberman, head of venture capital investing for Alliance Capital Management Inc.

Only a handful of recent deals have produced returns comparable with a Genentech or Apple Computer, he adds.

A RECENT SECURITIES rule could give small firms a new way to raise money.

The Securities and Exchange Commission's 144a rule, which was approved in April, could allow small entrepreneurial companies to raise capital cheaply and effectively, says attorney Joseph Bartlett, partner in Gaston & Snow, New York.

Under the rule, institutions with investments of $100 million or more can buy and sell previously restricted, privately placed stocks and bonds. In effect, more investors could be interested in the restricted shares of small firms because they could be traded more easily. Restricted shares have restrictions on trading, commonly delimiting the category of buyers or the minimum period the stock must be held.

Mr. Bartlett and others think that small companies, especially those that already have some revenues or are profitable, could significantly benefit from the rule. "It would allow them to sell stock cheaper and faster and perhaps with less potential liability," he says. The liability risk in issuing these stocks is reduced because the buyers are so-called sophisticated investors, he notes.

These investors include pension funds, insurance companies and banks -- concerns that traditionally have acquired stock in closely held businesses through venture capital pools but increasingly want to buy stock directly. Still, for the rule to really have an impact on closely held concerns, investment bankers will have to make a market in the 144a stock. That means they must put up their own capital to provide a trading market, says Mr. Bartlett.

The test for 144a for smaller entrepreneurial firms will be whether there are enough qualified institutional investors willing to buy and sell these shares, says Edward Smith, managing director of Merrill Lynch Capital Markets.

KEY INVESTMENTS in computer-related businesses slip.

Venture capital placed in computer-related businesses declined 38% in the first quarter of 1990 to the lowest quarterly total in five years, says Technologic Computer Letter, an industry newsletter in New York. Such capital is a major source of funding for new technology concerns.

Only $225 million went to computer-related venture deals in the first quarter of 1990, compared with $362 million a year earlier, the newsletter notes. The decline follows a year in which investments in computer ventures were flat. Computer-related ventures received $1.24 billion in 1989, compared with $1.26 billion in 1988.

Entrepreneurs aren't successfully exploiting new technology ideas, venture capitalists contend. "There's only been a small increase in new concepts. Most entrepreneurs keep churning out the same old ideas," complains Westport, Conn., venture capitalist Stewart Greenfield.

"Venture capitalists aren't seeing the deals or the quality of deals they like," says Richard Shaffer, editor of Technologic. And having been burnt by over-competition in areas such as disk drives, computer systems and personal computers, "they're resisting the me-too company," adds Mr. Shaffer.

Nonetheless, some venture capitalists believe the decline could have some benefits for entrepreneurs. "The few companies that do get money now will get more money and attention," promises one East Coast venture capitalist.

AN IRS VICTORY in a real estate partnership case troubles venture capitalists.

A U.S. tax court recently ruled that an individual who receives a "carried interest" in a partnership is required to pay taxes on projected profits at the time he receives that interest rather than when he actually pockets the gains. Many investment partnerships grant a carried interest -- an interest that the partner doesn't pay for -- to help compensate partners who manage the program. The tax court decision could affect partnerships beyond the real estate field, tax attorneys say.

Most experienced tax lawyers previously believed that there was little risk of a general partner's owing taxes on profits that haven't been earned, says attorney James Dreyfus of Fulbright Jaworski & Reavis McGrath, New York.

Even though a 1974 court case established a precedent for collecting taxes on projected carried-interest profits, the Internal Revenue Service had never attempted to apply it.

"If they begin to enforce the {new} ruling, it will take a lot of the financial incentive out of forming such partnerships," Mr. Dreyfus says.
