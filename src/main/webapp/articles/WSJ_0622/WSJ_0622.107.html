Politics & Policy: House Panel Sets $15.8 Billion For Foreign Bill

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Appropriations Committee approved a $15.8 billion foreign operations bill for fiscal 1991 that would cut U.S. military aid outside the Middle East and impose new curbs on funds for El Salvador and non-Communist forces in Cambodia. The measure reflects a temporary accord between the Bush administration and the committee's Democratic leadership, which agreed to soften cuts in foreign military aid in return for Republican promises of support on the House floor.

Total foreign military assistance would fall to $4.63 billion in the budget year beginning Oct. 1, down 4% from current levels but $144 million more than was proposed by a House subcommittee last week. Limits on military aid to Turkey and Greece would be relaxed, but Democrats succeeded in preserving the thrust of their proposal to begin to roll back arms expenditures and focus more on economic development in a post-Cold War era.

The committee trimmed some of its own initiatives in development and economic programs, but aid to Eastern Europe would still grow to almost $490 million, $189 million above the administration's request. An estimated $800 million in aid would be preserved to sub-Saharan Africa. And in negotiations this week, the Treasury won a commitment of $50 million in new capital for the World Bank.

Senior State Department and White House advisers participated in negotiations over the bill, reflecting concern in both the administration and Congress about deteriorating support for foreign aid. The $15.8 billion total would come on top of recent supplemental appropriations for Panama and Nicaragua. When compared with the comparable foreign operations bill last year, the measure would represent a nearly 7% increase in aid and related export-credit programs.

The El Salvador and Cambodia provisions remain divisive, and military aid to Turkey and Greece still would be cut by almost 14%. Report language attached to the legislation is sharply critical of the Turkish-backed government in northern Cyprus, and taken as a whole, the readjustment in aid represents a victory for the pro-Greece lobby.

In the case of El Salvador, 50% of future military aid would be withheld in line with a House vote this spring.

And while spending would be continued for the non-Communist resistance fighters in Cambodia, restrictions attached yesterday were written so broadly that they effectively would bar aid for military operations directly or indirectly supportive of the anti-government Khmer Rouge.

The $50 million in new capital for the World Bank is less than half of what the Treasury Department requested. And while the bill would provide $70 million sought by the administration for the new European Development Bank, restrictions were attached in an effort to put leverage on Western governments -- particularly West Germany -- to forgive Poland's immense debt.

Apart from the foreign operations bill, U.S. contributions to the United Nations and affiliated health and nutrition organizations would increase significantly under a separate $10.5 billion fiscal 1991 measure pending before the full House. Contributions to international organizations generally would grow by 28% to an estimated $787.6 million, and $115 million would be provided for peacekeeping organizations -- a 41% increase from fiscal 1990.

The same bill would provide for an expanded State Department budget for the new fiscal year. It is accompanied by a report that touches on the continued conflict in Afghanistan and U.S. representation in Kabul. When Soviet troops withdrew from Afghanistan more than a year ago, Washington withdrew its personnel from the U.S. Embassy in the capital. Report language inserted by Rep. Silvio Conte of Massachusetts, the ranking Republican on the House Appropriations Committee, calls for a review of this policy.
