San Jose, Calif., Gets $18.5 Million Award In Bond-Losses Suit

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/22/90

SAN JOSE, Calif. -- A federal court jury here awarded damages of $18.5 million to the city of San Jose in its lawsuit against two brokerage houses and an auditing firm that the city blamed for a $73 million bond-market loss six years ago. Including interest, the award totals almost $30 million. Previously, the city settled out of court with 11 other brokerage firms for $12.1 million. The jury verdict, following a six-month trial, included a $9.3 million judgment against Paine, Webber, Jackson & Curtis Inc., and an $8.7 million judgment against E.F.Hutton Inc. The jury also ordered Price Waterhouse & Co., the auditing firm, to pay damages of about $500,000.

In its suit, the city contended that the brokerage firms led the city into engaging in risky and speculative transactions while lending the city money to undertake those transactions.

The deals involved so-called reverse repurchase agreements; city traders, instead of taking losses on long-term bonds, used those bonds as collateral to borrow from its brokers to buy more bonds in hopes of recouping losses. Seven city employees were fired as a result of the bond fiasco.

The jury also determined that Price Waterhouse was negligent in failing to report the loans by the brokerage houses in its city audits. The city argued that such a report would have alerted city officials to the growing bond losses.

A spokeswoman for PaineWebber Group Inc., the New York securities firm, declined to comment. Jackson & Curtis was a predecessor of Paine Webber.

A spokesman for Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc., also a New York securities firm and successor to E.F. Hutton, said the firm doesn't "believe the facts justify this ruling and we will ask the court to set aside the verdict. We also are considering an appeal."

Price Waterhouse's attorneys, Heller Ehrman White & McAuliffe in San Francisco, said: "We had said all along that the accounting was appropriate but that in any case the losses were substantially attributable to the city's inattention to what was going on, not to any accounting or auditing failure." Under the law, the attorneys said, the New York accounting and consulting firm will benefit from any settlements paid by other defendants in the case, and it may not have to pay even the $500,000 award.
