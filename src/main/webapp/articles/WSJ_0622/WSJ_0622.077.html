Bell Atlantic Sees New Zealand Interest As Base for Expanding Pacific Business

  • Author: Pamela J. Podger
  • Date: 06/22/90

PHILADELPHIA -- Bell Atlantic Corp. views its joint acquisition with Ameritech of New Zealand's Telecom Corp. as a base for expanding operations in the Pacific, said Raymond W. Smith, Bell's chairman and chief executive officer Mr. Smith characterized the $2.5 billion purchase as "a very important strategic move for us." Noting the economic vitality of the Pacific Rim region, he said Bell Atlantic is exploring possible business opportunities in such countries as South Korea, Taiwan, Malaysia, Indonesia, Singapore and Australia.

In an interview, Mr. Smith also indicated that the New Zealand telephone operation, which already has a mostly digital network, could become a hub for providing corporate telecommunications services to the region. He noted that New Zealand makes an attractive base for many reasons, including its location in the region, its stable democratic government and its English-speaking population.

This week, Bell Atlantic and Ameritech filed court papers seeking a waiver from U.S. District Judge Harold Greene to permit the two Bell companies, as owners of New Zealand's Telecom, to retain their interest in long-distance phone traffic between New Zealand and the U.S.

Hyde Tucker, president of Bell Atlantic's international unit, said that, without such a waiver, New Zealand's long-distance phone circuits will have to be reorganized at a cost of as much as $10 million. He expressed hope that Judge Greene would respond to the request quickly.

Armed with about $550 million in available cash at the end of the first quarter, Bell Atlantic has been on an international shopping spree. This month, for example, the company has teamed up with US West Inc. of Denver; the two have been selected to build a cellular telephone system and a public data network in Czechoslovakia.

Bell Atlantic's Mr. Smith said business opportunities for the company in the Pacific Rim area could range from consulting and operating-system contracts to acquiring part or all of country phone systems that may be privatized. He declined to identify specific targets or disclose the progress of any ongoing talks. But he noted that Bell Atlantic recently signed a "memorandum of understanding" with the government of South Korea regarding possible joint ventures.

Mr. Smith also acknowledged that Bell Atlantic's recent successes in landing the New Zealand and Czech business ventures have the effect of reducing the likelihood that his company would also take on the purchase of another large national telephone system, such as those of Mexico and Argentina.

Peter Troughton, who will remain Telecom's managing director and chief executive, said the New Zealand company's total work force has been slashed recently to about 16,000 employees from a peak level of about 26,500. He predicted that the total would be reduced to about 12,000 over the next two years.

For the fiscal year ended March 31, Telecom earned $148 million on revenue of about $1.3 billion. Profits were reduced by heavy capital spending on network improvements.

Mr. Troughton didn't make any predictions about how profitable Telecom could become, but he said he doesn't see why it should be "any less profitable" than the regional Bell companies, including Ameritech and Bell Atlantic.

Until the late 1980s, New Zealand's telephone company was government-owned, heavily regulated and generally regarded as a poor performer in responding to consumer needs. For example, the network frequently "crashed" and went dead in the middle of a business day because network capacity was inadequate. Moreover, customers typically waited up to eight minutes for a dial tone, and about 50% of directory-assistance queries weren't answered correctly.

But many improvements have been made in the past two years. Indeed, the agreement last week to sell Telecom to Ameritech, Bell Atlantic and two New Zealand companies represents a sustained effort by the government to address such market needs by taking the phone system private.

Mr. Troughton noted that the company has realized strong growth in demand for telephone services, since rates on toll calls were cut 50% and international-call prices were also reduced substantially.
