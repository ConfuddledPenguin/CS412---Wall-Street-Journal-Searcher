Debt Ceiling Hit by Glitch In Accounting for Bonds

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/22/90

WASHINGTON -- An unforeseen glitch in accounting for $30 billion of zero-coupon bonds sold this year to Mexico ensures that the federal government's $3.12 trillion debt ceiling will expire in early August. Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady is expected to tell congressional leaders that the ceiling must be raised in early August. The debt cap had been expected to expire some weeks later, but in any event, the Treasury needs a debt-limit increase before Congress starts its August recess.

The Treasury's wiggle room was diminished by an immediate addition of some $27 billion to the amount of debt subject to the ceiling. That addition occurred when it was discovered that the zero-coupon bonds sold by the Treasury to Mexico as part of a debt-restructuring package don't qualify for a new method of calculating zero-coupon bonds subject to the debt ceiling. A Treasury spokesman said the department has notified Capitol Hill of this development.

The Treasury had been calculating the Mexico deal as adding about $3 billion to the amount of debt subject to the limit.

The need to act on the debt bill before the August recess could become a key pressure point for the current White House-congressional budget summit. It also could cause uncertainty in the financial markets regarding the Treasury's August refunding, whose details are to be announced Aug. 1.
