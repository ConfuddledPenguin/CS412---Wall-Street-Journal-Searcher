OTC Focus: Late-Day Rally Pulls Market Higher; MCI Communications Continues to Slide

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 06/22/90

Over-the-counter stocks crawled marginally higher in the last hour of trading after drifting most of the day. The Nasdaq Composite Index climbed 0.47 to 461.27, or 0.10%, as volume slipped slightly to 144.3 million shares from 148.1 million Wednesday. Advancing shares beat decliners, 963 to 916.

Institutional investors continued to step in and buy stocks when they fell to attractive prices, said Ralph Costanza, head of OTC trading at Smith Barney, Harris Upham. "When they see an opportunity, they step in and buy," said Mr. Costanza.

The Nasdaq Composite Index opened lower and drifted 1.44 before recovering. Buying among smaller investors also is orderly and cautious, he said. Small investors appear to be buying shares of companies researched by brokerage firms rather than chasing after speculative stories, according to Mr. Costanza.

Subaru of America was the day's most active issue, mainly because Cantor Fitzgerald traded two blocks of more than 2 million shares each for buyers and sellers.

Fuji Heavy Industries Ltd. of Japan is in a pact to buy the 50.4% of Subaru it doesn't already own. Subaru rose 1/16 to 8 1/4 on 5.8 million shares.

Telecredit tumbled 2 1/2 to 50 3/4 after the Los Angeles provider of payment services said it agreed to be acquired by Equifax, an Atlanta business-services concern. The agreement calls for a stock swap valued at about $580 million. An arbitrager at one large New York brokerage said the stock stumbled because some investors think the proposed combination may attract antitrust scrutiny. He added some speculators had hoped Telecredit would be acquired in cash by a larger, international finance or data-processing firm.

MCI Communications, the day's second-most active stock, continued to drift, slipping 5/8 to 41 on 3.5 million shares. Donaldson Lufkin & Jenrette telecommunications analyst Joel Gross said in an interview that shares of the Washington, D.C., long-distance carrier reacted to a new American Telephone & Telegraph tariff regulation that gives AT&T more flexibility in competitive pricing. The regulation became effective by default after the Federal Communications Commission failed to act on the proposed tariff within a mandated time. However, Mr. Gross said he expects federal regulators to eventually rule against the new regulation.

MCI shares also have been pressured by the pending release of FCC market-share data, which may show AT&T's market share rising a bit. Mr. Gross added, however, that when the data is revised it will show that AT&T's market share is continuing to decline. He continues to rate MCI a "buy."

Apple Computer added 1 7/8 to 41 7/8 on more than 1.9 million shares, leading the technology group. Shares of the Cupertino, Calif., computer maker apparently got a boost from a market survey showing that the dollar value of the company's sales in May rose 50% from the year-earlier level and that its share of the domestic personal-computer market rose in May from a year earlier, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Westmark International leaped 2 1/2 to 39 1/2 after the Seattle medical-equipment maker said late Wednesday that earnings for the second quarter ending June 30 will fall at the low end of an estimated range of 45 to 50 cents a share, up from 39 cents a share a year earlier. The stock recently had weakened amid some analysts' concerns the earnings wouldn't meet that range.

Henley Properties added 3/4 to 6 3/4 after the Hampton, N.H., real estate development company said it expects net income for the second quarter ending June 30 to be about $80 million, up from $4.8 million on a pro forma basis a year earlier. Henley also said it will sell its interests in two trash-to-energy facilities for $10 million to Wheelabrator Technologies Inc. That will enable Henley to reverse about $100 million of deferred income currently included as a liability on its financial statements and increase shareholders' equity by that amount, the company added.

LDDS Communications jumped 2 3/4 to 24, a new 52-week high, apparently after Prudential-Bache analyst Charles Nichols initiated coverage of the Jackson, Miss., provider of long-distance telecommunications services with a "buy" opinion, DJPIR reported.

General Binding lost 2 1/2 to 18 1/2 after the Northbrook, Ill., maker of business machines and supplies said it expects second-quarter earnings will fall to 30 or 31 cents a share from 35 cents a year earlier because of weaker markets and higher expenses. That's below analysts' expectations of 38 cents a share.

National Media slid 1/2 to 10 1/4 after the Philadelphia provider of direct-marketing services reported fourth quarter net income of 58 cents a share compared with a loss of six cents a year earlier, adjusted for a 5% stock dividend paid at year end. National Media also said it's "actively investigating" the acquisition of telemarketing and "direct response" companies.

Drexler Technology skidded 1 15/16 to 1 5/16 after the Mountain View, Calif., maker of optical data products said late Wednesday that it's been unsuccessful in selling its future license royalty payments and part of a full ownership in its optical memory card manufacturing operation.

Consequently, the company said its accountants expect to issue a qualified financial report on the company for the year ended March 31. Drexler said its cash is adequate to fund operations for eight weeks without cash infusions of any kind and it plans to cut executive salaries and furlough some employees.

Earth Technology dropped 1 to 7 5/8 after the environmental consulting services company said fourth-quarter earnings could be hurt if a weak backlog for its asbestos consulting and laboratory services persists because of project delays. The company also reported net income for the third quarter ended May 25 of six cents a share, up from one cent a year earlier.
