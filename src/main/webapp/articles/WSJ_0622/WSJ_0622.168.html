Mulroney Makes Last-Minute Appeal To Newfoundland on Meech Lake Accord

  • Author: G. Pierre Goad
  • Date: 06/22/90

Canada's constitutional wrangle is coming down to the wire. Whether or not Quebec gets the recognition it seeks as a "distinct society," the fight probably will launch a bitter debate about Canada's Constitution and the structure of the nation.

If the Meech Lake constitutional accord is ratified, a round of constitutional negotiations will begin July 15. The issues will be as controversial as the "distinct society" debate that has preoccupied Canadians and Canada's financial markets for several months.

If the accord isn't ratified, nothing will change overnight. But Canadian politics will turn surly as the hunt begins for somebody to blame for the failure. And talk of at least partial independence in French-speaking Quebec will quickly hit a higher pitch.

Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney said yesterday that failure to adopt the Meech Lake accord will result in "constitutional paralysis, sadness and dismay across the country (and) negative signals to international markets." Mr. Mulroney also said there is a "growing probability" that failure to adopt the accord will result in a "rising sense of rejection in Quebec and growing support of sovereignty-association," or political independence. A poll conducted last week found that 57% of Quebecers support sovereignty for their province.

Mr. Mulroney was making a last-minute pitch to the 52 members of the Newfoundland House of Assembly yesterday. This morning, the Newfoundland Legislature is expected to vote on the Meech Lake accord.

The final tally is uncertain, but rejection appears likely. Even if Newfoundland ratifies the accord, it may die in Manitoba. Procedural obstacles in the Manitoba Legislature delayed debate on the accord, and Manitoba politicians say there isn't enough time left to vote before tomorrow's deadline for ratification. Eight provinces have ratified the accord, which must be passed by all 10 provinces to take effect.

There's only a "slim chance" the accord will go through, Quebec Premier Robert Bourassa said for the first time Wednesday. Relentlessly optimistic until now, Mr. Bourassa is expected to make a statement to Quebecers this weekend if Meech Lake collapses, an aide said.

The Meech Lake accord was negotiated at Meech Lake, Quebec, three years ago. It recognizes Quebec as a "distinct society" within Canada and grants certain powers to all provinces. The accord satisfies Quebec's minimum conditions for signing the 1982 Constitution.

Equities analysts see little movement in the Canadian stock markets if the accord doesn't pass. "The market's already reacted to the uncertainty," said John Ing, president and chief executive of Maison Placements Canada Inc. "If it doesn't pass, we'll just see more of what we've been seeing."

If the accord is ratified, analysts expect a rise in the Canadian dollar, which has been under severe downward pressure, along with a rally in the bond market and a subsequent rally in the stock market, although the latter will be "more of an emotional reaction than it will be based on fundamentals," Mr. Ing said.

Michael Boulter, a portfolio strategist with Research Capital Corp., said he sees the Toronto stock market rallying by about 100 points from its current level of about 3520 if the accord passes. He said interestrate sensitive stocks, such as banks and utilities, will see the most benefit.

Yesterday, the Canadian dollar closed in Toronto at 85.01 U.S. cents, down 0.29 cents. Canadian government bonds closed down about 1/8 of a point, or C$12.50 for each C$1,000 face amount. The Toronto Stock Exchange 300 index finished at 3526.24, down 4.24.
