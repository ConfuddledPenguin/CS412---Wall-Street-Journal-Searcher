Abreast of the Market: Industrials Gain 6.43 on Late Rally; End-of-Quarter Buying Is Factor

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 06/22/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices closed higher on the strength of a late rally, attributed to end-of-quarter buying by institutions and futures-related buy programs. Trading was subdued. The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which spent most of the session drifting at lower levels, moved back above the 2900 level by gaining 6.43 to 2901.73.

Today represents the end of the second quarter for many money managers. Under normal procedures, any trades made today would be settled next Friday, the final session of the quarter.

Analysts cited "window dressing," or last-minute adjustments that institutions make with an eye toward presenting the best possible quarterly reports to clients, as a major factor fueling the market's late recovery.

Buy programs tied to stock-index arbitrage, in which traders purchase baskets of stocks and sell index-futures contracts in order to profit from the price differential between them, also surfaced near the close to give stocks a boost.

The arbitrage buying helped offset earlier waves of sell programs, which had knocked back the market at the opening and again at midday. The industrial average hit bottom with a loss of about 20 points.

Trading activity has been sluggish all week, and the latest session continued that pattern. Some 138,570,000 shares changed hands on the New York Stock Exchange, compared with 137,420,000 Wednesday.

Advancing issues led decliners on the Big Board by 774 to 696. Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index rose 1.37 to 360.47, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 0.65 to 196.49 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index went up 1.11 to 335.33.

The Big Board's list of most active issues included a number of stocks that have recently been under pressure, including:

-- United Telecommunications, which dropped 1/4 to 39 in Big Board composite trading of 1.5 million shares. The stock moved lower last week after PaineWebber reduced its earnings estimates for the second quarter and 1990.

Its latest fall took place as American Telephone & Telegraph put a price plan into effect that allows it to offer special rates to individual customers in response to a competing proposal by another carrier. AT&T rose 1/2 to 43 5/8.

-- Occidental Petroleum, which rose 1/8 to 25 7/8 on 1.3 million shares after trading as low as 25 1/4 during the session. Standard & Poor's downgraded its ratings on the company's debt earlier this week.

-- Limited Inc., which slid 5/8 to 22 3/4 on 1.1 million shares. Retail sales have fallen in each of the past three months, and apparel sales have been especially sluggish, sparking concern about earnings prospects for those companies.

Another issue to slide in active trading was Du Pont, which said it has allocated funds for the design of four plants that would produce alternatives to chlorofluorocarbons. The stock fell 1 to 38 1/8 on 1.1 million shares. Du Pont said it expects to spend more than $1 billion to develop and produce the alternatives, including $240 million already invested and additional projected costs over the next 10 years.

American Cyanamid rose 1 1/4 to 57 1/8 after agreeing to sell the household-products business of its Shulton unit to Clorox for $465 million. Analysts told Dow Jones Professional Investor Report they expect Cyanamid to use the sale proceeds mainly to buy back common stock; Leonard Bogner of Prudential-Bache Securities estimated that the company would purchase about five million of its 94.4 million shares outstanding.

A spokeswoman for Cyanamid acknowledged that the company is considering a buy-back, but added that it hasn't yet made a decision to proceed. Clorox tumbled 1 3/4 to 42 1/8; the company estimated that the acquisition would dilute its earnings by 30 cents to 40 cents a share in the first year and 10 cents a share in the second year.

Johnson & Johnson advanced 2 to 66 5/8 after the Food and Drug Administration approved the use of Levamisole, a drug made by the company's Janssen Research Foundation to treat advanced cases of colon cancer in combination with another drug. Separately, Dean Witter Reynolds increased its estimates of the company's 1990 and 1991 earnings.

Upjohn, which climbed two points Wednesday on takeover speculation, eased 1/4 to 41 7/8 as 1.5 million shares were traded. Schering-Plough and American Home Products had been mentioned as possible suitors; both declined to comment, as did Upjohn.

Equifax, which agreed to acquire Telecredit in a stock swap, tumbled 2 5/8 to 19 1/2. The company will exchange 2.69 of its common shares for each Telecredit share.

Delta Air Lines fell 1 1/4 to 75 3/4. Wertheim Schroder lowered its investment rating on the stock, along with its earnings estimates for the company's fiscal fourth quarter, ending this month, and calendar 1990. Analyst Candace Browning said lower-than-expected domestic air traffic will affect its results.

VF retreated 2 1/4 to 25 following its forecast that second-quarter earnings per share would be about 47% lower than a year earlier, when it posted net income of 67 cents a share.

Molecular Biosystems dropped 5/8 to 18 3/4. The company said it expects to report earnings between 20 cents and 25 cents a share for the fiscal year ending next March, well below most analysts' projections, as it emphasizes growth over profits.

Caterpillar dropped 7/8 to 64 1/4 after Prudential-Bache lowered its investment rating and 1990 earnings estimate, citing weak orders for the company's large machines in May.

CPC International went up 1 1/2 to 80 1/4 and set a new 52-week high by peaking at 80 1/2. Michael Metz, a market strategist at Oppenheimer & Co., told the investor report he recommended the stock and said it "acts like it's under accumulation" by a major corporate buyer.

Liggett Group added 5/8 to 11 3/4. The company said it plans to restructure its tobacco and sports-marketing units as separate companies and said it may repurchase as many as one million of its common shares.

WMS Industries climbed 1 1/8 to 9 1/2. Newsletter writer Charles LaLoggia commented favorably about WMS in a television interview; he told the investor report it is "reasonable" to consider the company as a possible takeover target for Viacom. Sumner N. Redstone, Viacom's chairman, holds a 28.9% stake in WMS.

Vishay Intertechnology declined 7/8 to 21 3/8. The company, which has about 12.4 million common and Class B shares outstanding, said it is considering a public offering of two million common shares.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index dropped 0.59 to 360.22. Volume totaled 10,550,000 shares.

AT&E rose 1 1/8 to 22 5/8. Bell Atlantic disclosed that it had accumulated a stake in the company and plans to purchase additional shares; it declined to provide the size of its holding.

Bolar Pharmaceutical, which said a lawsuit filed last week by SmithKline Beecham would have a "material adverse effect" on the company if the British-based firm wins the case, fell 3/4 to 5 7/8. The suit charges Bolar with fraud in obtaining federal approval for a generic version of Dyazide, SmithKline's blood-pressure drug.
