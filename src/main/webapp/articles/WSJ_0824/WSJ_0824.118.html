Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Yugo Taps Agency to Reverse Bad Image

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 08/24/90

Yugo America Inc., the troubled unit of the Yugoslavian car maker, tapped tiny ad agency Buckley/DeCerchio to try to revive its failing fortunes and to combat its reputation as an auto industry joke. Yugo, which emerged from bankruptcy-law proceedings just last December, said it will spend $8 million to $10 million on advertising in a bid to make a comeback. That won't be an easy trick: The car maker has taken a beating in the image department following years of complaints about reliability and plummeting sales.

"What we really have to do is overcome the `Yugo image,' which isn't as bad as everybody says it is," said John Spiech, president and chief executive officer of Yugo America, a unit of Zavodi Crvena Zastava. "But you can't do it overnight. It takes awhile to come back."

Mr. Spiech conceded Yugo faces a mighty challenge to overcome years of sliding sales and negative publicity. The Yugo remains a favorite target of late-night comics. The company also must fight a reputation for pumping out cars that are flimsy and unsafe.

Yugo is attempting to address these problems in its first commercial in more than a year, which Buckley/DeCerchio is filming now in Los Angeles. The ad, which will pitch the $4,335 GV Plus model, is expected to portray a big rig lifting the car, turning it over and shaking it vigorously.

"We want people to look at this commercial and say, `Hey, this car is reliable,'" Mr. Spiech said. "That's the statement we need to make." The ad will begin airing Sept. 10.

But it isn't clear that simple advertising can clear up such deep-rooted problems. While a $10 million ad budget may be a step up for Yugo, it pales in comparison with the budgets of other U.S. and Japanese auto makers, which routinely spend upward of $100 million annually.

Sales have been on a precipitous decline at Yugo. In the first seven months of this year, it sold only about 4,300 cars in the U.S., down from 6,400 in the year-earlier period and way down from 31,000 cars in the first seven months of 1987.

For Buckley/DeCerchio, the account almost doubles the size of the trendy young shop to $23 million in billings. The addition of an auto account also gives the agency, which until now has been known for relatively small clients such as Snapple beverages, an added measure of respectability. Jane Cavalier, the shop's president, said yesterday that Yugo immediately becomes the agency's largest client.

The account also may help establish Buckley/DeCerchio as a serious shop. Tom DeCerchio, 30 years old, and Bryan Buckley, 26, a former creative team at Chiat/Day, left their posts at the hot shop to start the agency in August 1988. The two set up their business in Mr. DeCerchio's one-bedroom Manhattan apartment, having snagged the $6 million Godfather's Pizza account, which they still service. After picking up several other small pieces of business, the executives just this year moved out of the apartment and opened an office in New York's SoHo district.

"For our company, this is a chance to build a brand that's coming back and attach a personality to it," said Mr. Buckley of his agency's new assignment. "We're going to give people a reason to drive this car. This is a great opportunity."
