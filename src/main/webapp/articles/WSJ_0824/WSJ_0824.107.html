Politics & Policy: For Saudi Oil Installations, It's Business As Usual -- Despite Threats From Iraq

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 08/24/90

RAS TANURA, Saudi Arabia -- Iraqi threats of invasion and sabotage apparently haven't prompted stepped-up security measures at Saudi Arabian oil installations strung out along the northern Persian Gulf. From the giant refinery complex here to the mammoth offshore oil and gas fields near Tanjib, less than an hour's drive from Iraqi tank formations, Saudi Arabian Oil Co. has done little to reinforce the company guards that remain its primary protection against any Iraqi-sponsored terrorists, according to employees and U.S. military officials.

"They haven't beefed up security at all," said Victor Perrin, who recently resigned from his job training Saudi workers and is returning to the U.S. with his wife and two small children. "Their whole policy is business as usual."

Neil Hjelle, a communications specialist who has worked for Saudi Aramco for 13 years, said: "Security actually has been a little bit looser in the past week or two. There is no indication of increased plant surveillance."

Saudi officials declined to comment on security arrangements.

In contrast, U.S. military commanders have focused on the dangers of potential terrorist attacks from the first day troops arrived, and senior officers have put in place unprecedented security measures to protect U.S. troops from that possibility.

To some extent, the security issue reflects broader differences between the way many Saudi leaders and U.S. commanders view the current standoff with Iraqi President Saddam Hussein. Some Saudi Aramco directors have suggested to worried employees that the terrorist threat posed by Iraq and its supporters hasn't increased significantly since the invasion of Kuwait. According to Mr. Perrin, one predicted this week that if shooting breaks out, "the war will never be fought on Saudi soil."

On the other hand, many U.S. commanders remain concerned about Iraq's potential ability to damage vital Saudi facilities. "Don't ever forget their tanks are close enough to reach important plants without having to refuel," said a Pentagon planner.

U.S. military analysts also have stressed that the oil-field region is home to nearly all of Saudi Arabia's potentially rebellious Shiite Moslem minority. Two years ago, Saudi Aramco officials discovered a bomb planted in one of the facilities. A number of suspects were arrested, and employees said the incident prompted a reassessment of the company's security procedures, but they said few changes were made.

The company is believed to have developed emergency evacuation plans, but employees said they haven't received any information about what those plans involve.

According to some Saudi officials, Egyptian troops will take up positions near some oil facilities in the next few days as U.S. troops redeploy to get maximum defensive firepower close to the Iraqi border.

Meanwhile, there appears to be little concern about the Iraqi threat in commercial centers in this section of Saudi Arabia. Stores and shopping areas are as busy as they were before the Aug. 2 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, and there don't appear to be additional guards posted around government buildings. In fact, security officers manning one of the gates at Saudi Aramco's headquarters complex this week allowed a taxi carrying an employee and this reporter to enter without asking for identification from both passengers.
