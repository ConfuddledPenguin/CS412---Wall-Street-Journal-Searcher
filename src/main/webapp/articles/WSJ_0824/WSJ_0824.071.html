Sales of U.S.-Built Vehicles Fall 4.7% In a Discouraging Sign for Auto Makers

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 08/24/90

DETROIT -- Sales of domestic-built cars and trucks fell 4.7% in mid-August, a smaller drop than some experts had expected, but a discouraging sign for auto makers nonetheless. Growing concerns about economic uncertainty and increasing tensions in the Persian Gulf have made already-cautious consumers even more nervous, several dealers said.

"We aren't seeing that impetuous-type buyer," said Tony Tulino, general sales manager at Faulkner Oldsmobile in Philadelphia. "Overall, our customers are concerned about what's going on in the Middle East. We're seeing a careful, scrutinizing buyer."

Makers of domestic vehicles sold 261,389 cars and trucks in the Aug. 11-20 period compared with 274,320 vehicles a year earlier.

The seasonally adjusted annual sales rate for the 10-day period hit 7 million vehicles, according to a Commerce Department formula. That rate is substantially lower than the strong 8.4 million car-sales pace a year ago, but up from the rate of 6.6 million cars in early August.

"Considering all the negatives out there -- stagnant growth, declining employment, rising energy prices and international tension -- I expected much worse," said Thomas Webb, chief economist for the National Automobile Dealers Association. Sales of trucks and of Japanese cars made in the U.S. showed some strength, Mr. Webb said, but he predicted that neither will escape the effects of a sluggish economy and rising gasoline prices.

Chrysler Corp. posted the sharpest sales decline among the Big Three auto makers, continuing a months-long pattern. The company's domestic car sales skidded 27.3%, while its domestic truck sales dropped 9.5%. The company's Jeep sales have been badly hurt by Ford Motor Co.'s recent introduction of its Explorer sport-utility vehicle.

Ford's domestic car sales fell 22.9%, but truck sales rose 19.3%, largely on the strength of the Explorer.

"Our truck sales are up 25% this month and that's boosted mainly by the Explorer," said Lou Stanford, owner of Varsity Ford in Ann Arbor, Mich. He said that "probably 10% to 20%" of people trading in vehicles for the Ford Explorer are trading in Jeep Wagoneers for the top-of-the-line Eddie Bauer model.

General Motors Corp. fared the best among the Big Three, again continuing a recent trend. Its domestic car sales fell 10.1%, while its truck sales edged up 1.3%. Sales in the Oldsmobile division fell 26.7%; Buick sales dropped 25%.

To help spur sales of its Oldsmobile models, GM yesterday added cash rebates ranging from $500 to $1,500 on select 1991 models, and doubled rebates on some 1990 models to a maximum of $2,500. The program is an enhancement of an existing incentive program than runs through Sept. 26, the company said.

Mr. Tulino said Faulkner Oldsmobile had sold 753 new Oldsmobiles in the first seven months of this year, down from 931 a year ago but is hopeful that the latest round of rebates will boost sales. "When incentives go up, so do sales, most of the time," he added.

The sales decline also touched Japanese auto maker Nissan Motor Co. Its domestic truck sales fell 3.5% while its car sales rose 3.9%

Toyota Motor Co. reported a 65.8% increase in sales of U.S.-made cars, while Honda Motor Co. showed a 26.6% improvement in its domestic car sales.
