What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/24/90

THE U.S. FACED a crucial test in Kuwait over Iraq's threat to close embassies. As the Bush administration's war of nerves with the Iraqis intensified over Saddam Hussein's order that Western embassies in Kuwait shut down today -- and with most vowing they will remain open -- the U.S. acted to forge a global effort to aid nations hurt by the embargo of Iraq. U.S. officials analyzed the needs of Jordan, Egypt and Turkey, and Bush reportedly asked Japan's Kaifu to help some nations. Israeli military sources said Jordanian officers have been training Iraqis to use U.S.-made Hawk anti-aircraft missiles captured from Kuwait. And Kuwaiti officials said Baghdad has mined most of Kuwait's oil wells and threatened to blow them up if there is an attack.

Iraqi television broadcast a tape of Saddam Hussein talking to British captives, including several children. The U.S. and Britain expressed outrage.

ARMENIA'S PARLIAMENT PASSED a declaration of independence from Moscow.

The southern republic's legislators voted to assume control over defense, foreign and economic policy, and resolved to reunite with the disputed territory of Nagorno-Karabakh. While the statement stopped short of calling for a complete break with the Kremlin, Armenia became the fifth of the 15 Soviet republics to move toward secession.

Estonia began formal talks with Moscow on separation, and the republic of Turkmenia declared the right to overturn Soviet laws on its territory.

---

Black nationalist leader Mandela urged the South African government to deploy troops in black townships near Johannesburg, as members of the Zulu and Xhosa tribes clashed for the 12th day with spears, axes and knives. President de Klerk said he would announce steps today to stop the fighting, which has claimed over 500 lives.

---

A panel of federal judges recommended that television cameras be allowed in federal courtrooms on an experimental basis. The experiment, which requires the approval of the U.S. Judicial Conference, would allow cameras into civil proceedings in six district courts and two appeals courts for three years.

---

Researchers halted the growth of colon cancer cells in a test tube by replacing a damaged gene with its normal version. The experiment supports a theory that certain genes normally prevent uncontrolled, malignant cell growth and that cancer arises when these "tumor suppressor" genes are inactivated.

---

FDA scientists documented their belief that a synthetic growth hormone designed to boost cows' milk production poses no health risk. The agency released a review of over 120 studies to support its statements that milk and meat from cows treated with bovine growth hormone, which is still not approved, are safe.

---

The West German government welcomed East Germany's decision to fix Oct. 3 as the date the two nations will merge, expressing relief that bickering about the timing of unification was over. The East German Parliament approved the date late Wednesday, and it requires no ratification from Bonn.

---

Moderate Southern Baptists discussed plans to withdraw millions of dollars in contributions from the Southern Baptist Convention. The group of 3,000 dissidents, meeting in Atlanta, are angry at the conservative fundamentalist faction that has ruled the denomination for over a decade.

---

NASA said it replaced part of the computer memory of the Venus-orbiting Magellan spacecraft, which broke off radio contact temporarily Wednesday for the second time within a week. Magellan is on a $744 million mission to use its radar to make pictures and maps of the planet's surface.

---

West African troops set sail from Sierra Leone to enforce a cease-fire in Liberia, where a rebel army led by Charles Taylor threatened to fight them. The West African Economic Community, which mobilized the 3,000 soldiers, emphasized that the task force was on a peaceful mission to halt the eight-month-old civil war.

---

Israel's finance minister said he would ask the U.S. for more military and other aid when he visits Washington next month. But Yitzhak Modai wouldn't say how much additional assistance is sought by the Israeli government, which currently receives about $3 billion annually from the U.S.
