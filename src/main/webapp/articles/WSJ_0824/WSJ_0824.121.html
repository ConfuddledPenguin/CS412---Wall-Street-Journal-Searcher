BIG SPENDERS

Japanese corporate capital spending in the current fiscal year will rise 16.1% from the previous fiscal year, according to a report by Nippon Credit Bank.

The report, based on the bank's survey of 1,391 companies conducted in July, represents a 5.1 percentage-point jump in capital-spending plans from the previous survey conducted in January.

"The January survey covers the fiscal year beginning in April, when a lot of corporate-spending plans still haven't been settled, so estimates are usually lower than the July survey. But even so, the latest results show a healthy rise," said a bank official.

The expected 16.1% rise would constitute the third straight year of double-digit growth, following increases of 10.9% and 15.6% in the previous two fiscal years, the official said.

The report said the manufacturing sector's plans are especially bullish, with outlays expected to be 21.8% higher this year. Following increases of 25.4% and 24.6% in the past two fiscal years, respectively, the report said the latest rise would result in the first three-year stretch of more than 20% growth since the bank began its surveys in 1975.

MORE TV FOR PORTUGAL

President Mario Soares ended Portugal's 26-year government television monopoly by signing a law allowing two private stations to run alongside the two existing state-run channels. Competition for commercial-television rights is expected to be intense. The legislation stipulates that at least 10% of the programs on the new channels must be made in Portugal and 50% must be in Portuguese. In addition, advertising can't occupy more than 15% of air time, and pornographic programs are banned.

DOWRY DEATHS

An average of more than 10 women a day died in India last year because they didn't bring an adequate dowry to their marriages, Home Minister Mufti Mohammed Sayeed told Parliament. Dowry-related deaths totaled 4,006 in 1989, nearly double the year-earlier toll. The official didn't say how the brides had died, but there are frequent reports of women being doused in kerosene and burned alive in what families call kitchen accidents.

WOES FOR LEBANON

Lebanese bank and municipal employees started a strike for higher wages. The action by employees at 118 private banks and the central bank quickly caused a shortage of cash throughout Lebanon. The strike by the municipal employees was expected to cause health problems in Beirut and other cities. The General Confederation of Labor Unions, which represents an estimated 200,000 members, called for a demonstration in Moslem West Beirut yesterday afternoon to protest soaring prices and deteriorating living conditions.

TROPIC TOPIC

Disappearing mangroves have joined vanishing rain forests as a cause for concern. Delegates from 20 Asia-Pacific nations yesterday established the International Society for Mangrove Ecosystems, to be based at Ryukyu University on Okinawa. Many of the trees, which grow around estuaries in tropical and subtropical regions, have been felled recently to make way for factories and shrimp farms. Some experts said Okinawa, for example, has lost 90% of its mangroves.

WIDENING GAP

The Philippines had a first-half trade deficit of $1.87 billion, widened from a $1.15 billion deficit a year earlier, the Central Bank of the Philippines said. The latest period's leading exports included semiconductor devices, coconut oil, children's and infant's clothing, copper, and electrical and electronic machinery and parts. Crude oil, electronic components, fabrics and aircraft led imports.

FEUDING FARMERS

Feuds over scarce resources in China's vast interior have made peasant farmers extra keen on having sons -- not so much to work the fields as to fight other farmers.

The official Farmer's Daily said the trend was the latest in a litany of woes, including corruption and resentment, plaguing China's birth-control policy in the countryside.

"There are some peasants who scheme about raising the balance of power against hostile other farmers by having more sons," the newspaper commented. "They think that more men mean more power."

It cited an example of one farmer whose son was carted off to a labor camp for 12 years for beating up neighbors. "One has gone," the farmer was quoted as saying. "I'll just rear another."

The newspaper said such "faulty mentalities" stemmed from increasing conflicts caused by the scarcity of oil, fertilizer, electricity, medicine, plastic sheeting and other basic items.
