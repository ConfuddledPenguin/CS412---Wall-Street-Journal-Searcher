Sun Microsystems Reconsiders Plan To Offer Shares

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 08/24/90

Sun Microsystems Inc. is reconsidering its plan to raise about $150 million through a stock sale. A final decision on whether to cancel the offering hasn't been made. "It's a day-to-day decision," said William Raduchel, chief financial officer.

The Mountain View, Calif., computer maker announced the offering 10 days ago, when its stock was trading at $32 a share. Sun had been seeking $31 for each of the new shares, but since the announcement the price of Sun's stock has dropped sharply. It closed yesterday at $26.375, up 37.5 cents, in national over-the-counter trading.

Given the price decline, Sun's board, which must approve the sale of 5.9 million new shares, is having second thoughts about the offering. "We have said from the first that this (offering) is a question of price," Mr. Raduchel said.

Some analysts charge that Sun pushed its luck by proposing the offering just a week after announcing plans to sell, under a long-running agreement, 4.6 million newly issued shares to American Telephone & Telegraph Co.

"I certainly question whether (Sun's) investment bankers are serving them well," said Robert Herwick, an analyst at Hambrecht & Quist. "There's nothing that Sun's articulated that suggests they couldn't have waited three or six months before coming to market."

The offering is being managed by Goldman, Sachs & Co., Salomon Brothers Inc. and Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

People close to the situation say Sun's investment bankers are urging that the offering be carried out, citing a belief that the market for high-technology issues will cool even further in the coming months.

Still, Sun may well be in a no-win situation, because even if it cancels the offering it can't rid investors of the expectation of further dilution in Sun's stock down the road.

"Sun is kind of stuck," said David Pearl, a portfolio manager at Bankers Trust New York Corp. "They can call off the offering, but they have to raise money and may only be back again in six months." In the meantime, Mr. Pearl already is factoring the offering into his Sun earnings forecasts.

Mr. Raduchel said Sun can't proceed with the offering until Tuesday at the earliest because its underwriters traded in outstanding shares of the stock yesterday, and under securities rules the offering can't commence for two working days. The company completed its "road show" for the offering this week and was ready to start it yesterday.

Separately, Sun announced the resignation of Robert Kavner from its board. Mr. Kavner, who is AT&T's group executive for data systems and federal systems, said AT&T no longer will maintain a seat on Sun's board even though it owns nearly 20% of Sun's stock.

Mr. Kavner said Sun and AT&T will continue to work closely on software and other technical issues but that the two companies "find themselves competing in the marketplace, raising potential conflicts of interest."
