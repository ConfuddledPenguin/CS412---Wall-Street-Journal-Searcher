Credit Markets: Investors Scramble for Short-Term U.S. Issues; Yield on Benchmark 30-Year Bond Hits 9.13%

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Robert Sherman
  • Date: 08/24/90

NEW YORK -- The prospect of an imminent war in the Persian Gulf battered bonds again, prompting investors to take cover by buying short-term Treasury notes and bills, the safest securities available. The "flight-to-quality" buying spree was evident in price quotes posted for long-term securities relative to short-term securities.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond, for example, fell 3/4 points for a loss of about $7.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's closing yield jumped to 9.13%, the highest on the bond since April 14, 1989 when it was 9.14%, according to Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

Short-term Treasury notes and bills, however, ended unchanged or modestly higher in brisk trading. Dealers said investors decided to flee the battered stock market and unwind their money-losing long-term bond positions, using proceeds to buy short-term securities. As a result, prices for two-year Treasury notes held firm and the yield was unchanged at 8.24%. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills fell to 7.53% from 7.59% Wednesday.

William Sullivan, director of money market research at Dean Witter, said the flight-to-quality buying was first evident in Tokyo, where investors bailed out of the Japanese stock market as the Nikkei index of 225 Japanese stocks plunged 1473.28 points to 23,737.63. That was the fourth-largest single-day decline in that market.

In the broader bond market, prices were knocked sharply lower. Once again, the market's nemesis was rising oil prices, which investors fear will lead to spiraling inflation and interest rates. Fixed-income investors dislike inflation because it cuts the value of their portfolios by reducing the inflation-adjusted return.

After hitting a five-year high Wednesday, October oil futures ended at $31.93 a barrel yesterday, up 71 cents.

Douglas Schindewolf, vice president and money market economist at Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co., said investors are paralyzed by the fear of inflation and "uncertainty about how high oil could go in the event of an actual armed conflict."

Many analysts expect further declines in government securities, accompanied by higher yields, as long as the focus remains on oil prices and the Persian Gulf, and not on economic fundamentals.

"Forecasting rates is hazardous business with the events in the Middle East so unpredictable, but I think rates will grid a bit higher," said James Fralick, an economist at Morgan Stanley & Co.

Iraqi President Saddam Hussein has said foreign embassies in Kuwait must be closed by today, and threatened that embassy personnel remaining in the oil-rich Arab kingdom could be stripped of their diplomatic status if they remain.

President Bush, along with other Western leaders, has said he will not abide by Mr. Hussein's deadline and will keep the U.S. Embassy open.

"Tomorrow's deadline could be the triggerpoint," for the first clash between U.S. and Iraqi forces, said Mr. Schindewolf.

Elsewhere, the Commerce Department today will release revisions to second-quarter gross national product data. Economists believe the figure will be revised upward to an inflation-adjusted annual rate of 1.5% from 1.2%, according to a Dow Jones Capital Markets Report.

Foreign Bonds

British government bonds continued to recuperate from a severe oil-related beating while prices in West Germany were modestly lower.

But in Japan, which imports all of its oil, the bond market rout continued. Traders said higher oil prices and a disappointing government bond auction pushed yields on Japan's benchmark 10-year bond to the highest levels in nearly a decade.

In Tokyo trading, the yield on the benchmark No. 119 10-year bond ended at 8.31% as the issue's price fell to 82.21, down 0.82 points. The yield on nearest bond futures contract ended at 88.45, down 1.03 points, to yield 8.089%. That was the highest yield ever for a bond futures contract. (When calculated using the semiannual compound measure, the yield on the No. 119 bond ended at 7.60%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)

As prices sank, the Japanese Finance Ministry attempted to support the bond market by purchasing 90 billion yen of 10-year bonds. But the purchase had almost no influence on the market.

With yields for existing bonds above 8%, traders said investors weren't interested in buying new 10-year securities at yesterday's government auction because the 7.53% yield on the securities, which was set by the government, was considered unattractive. The offer totaled 800 billion yen.

Meanwhile, Japan's long-term prime rate is expected to jump to 8.5% from 7.9% currently because the sell-off in the Japanese bond market has sharply boosted the costs of funds for banks.

Japan's three long-term credit banks, which reset the long-term prime rate at the end of each month, are expected to make the decision to boost the long-term prime rate today. The three banks are Industrial Bank of Japan, Long-Term Credit Bank of Japan and Nippon Credit Bank.

Treasury Securities

Long-term Treasury bonds were hit hard again, but some short-term bills and notes ended unchanged or higher.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 96 1/32 to yield 9.13%, compared with 96 26/32 to yield 9.05% on Wednesday. The latest 10-year note ended at 98 8/32 to yield 9% compared with 98 22/32 to yield 8.93%. Two year-note were unchanged at 99 17/32 to yield 8.24%.

The Treasury yesterday sold $10.52 billion of 52-week Treasury bills at an average rate of 7.40%.

Next week, the Treasury will sell $11.5 billion of new two-year notes and $8.5 billion of new five-year notes. In when-issued trading activity yesterday, the two-year notes were quoted at yields of 8.24% while the five-year notes were quoted at yields of 8.67%.

Junk, Corporate Issues

The junk-bond market added to its heavy losses as plunging stock prices pulled bond prices sharply lower.

Junk bonds plummeted at the opening but managed to stage a partial rebound at midday after the stock market stabilized. But when stocks resumed their sharp slide later, junk bonds followed suit.

Most higher-quality junk bonds ended one point lower, but mid-to-lower quality issues suffered even greater losses.

One of the biggest losers was RJR Nabisco Inc. Its 17.375% convertible pay-in-kind debentures fell 2 1/8 points to 106 1/4 while its 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures ended 3 3/4 points lower at 80 3/4.

Elsewhere, Fort Howard Corp.'s 14.125% junior debentures fell two points to 40, Gaylord Container Corp.'s 13.75% subordinated debentures dropped three points to an offered price of 62, and National Gypsum's 14.50% senior subordinated debentures shed two points to 40.

The market's better-quality recession-resistant issues, such as Duracell Holdings Corp. and Kroger Co., remained firm.

In the investment-grade market, $600 million in new corporate debt was priced including a $250 million issue of five-year issue of Federal Farm Credit Banks notes.

Strong demand prompted underwriters at Merrill Lynch Capital Markets to increase the size of the issue twice after pricing. The notes, which are non-callable for two years, were priced to yield 8.94%.

In the secondary market, high-grade bonds also posted losses, ending the day about 1/2 point lower.

Municipal Issues

Municipal bonds were 3/8 to one point lower.

In new-issue dealings, seven firms won $550 million of Texas tax and revenue anticipation notes. The bulk of the notes were purchased by a Merrill Lynch Capital Markets group, which priced them with a 6 1/2% coupon to yield 6.18%.

Among dollar bonds, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue due in 2018 was 3/4 point lower at 95 3/4 to yield 7.52%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage-securities prices fell again.

Government National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% pass-through securities for September delivery lost 3/8 point to 97 5/8, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp.'s 9 1/2% issue fell 10/32 to 97 9/32 and Federal National Mortgage Association's 9 1/2% securities were 10/32 lower at 97 26/32.

The Department of Veterans Affairs offered $293 million of mortgage pass-through certificates backed by vendee loans, which are loans issued to finance the sale of VA-owned properties acquired as a result of loan defaults.
