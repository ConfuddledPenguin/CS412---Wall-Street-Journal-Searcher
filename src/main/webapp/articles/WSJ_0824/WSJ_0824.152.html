Bank of Boston Board Opts Out of Provision On Interstate Banking

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/24/90

BOSTON -- Bank of Boston Corp. said its directors voted to opt out of provisions of the recently passed Massachusetts interstate banking law that allows banks outside of New England to acquire or open banks in the state. The statute, signed into law in July, allows a Massachusetts bank to opt out of the interstate banking provisions until July 1, 1992, by a two-thirds vote of its directors.

Prior to the passage of the law, only banks based in other New England states have been able to enter the Massachusetts market. Bank of Boston officials had lobbied against the interstate banking bill and had previously indicated they would opt out under the provisions until 1992.

In a statement, Bank of Boston said, "This decision is consistent with the corporation's intention to remain an independent institution and with its strategic objective to build long-term stockholder value."

The company added that the "action wasn't taken in response to any known proposal to acquire Bank of Boston Corp."

The bank holding company's stock price has been depressed recently because of poor results due to the slumping New England economy and real estate market.

BayBanks Inc., another bank holding company buffeted by New England's economic woes, chose to opt out of the the provisions last month, but retained the right to "opt in" before 1992 if it deems it "advisable."
