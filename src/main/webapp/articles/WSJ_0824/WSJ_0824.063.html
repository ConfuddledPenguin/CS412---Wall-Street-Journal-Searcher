Technology & Medicine: Fountain of Youth Found in Colorado -- For Worms, That Is --- Study Claims to Uncover Key To Aging in Nematode By Modifying Single Gene

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 08/24/90

A Colorado scientist may have found the secret of long life, a gene that appears to control aging. Unfortunately, the secret was unearthed in a tiny worm. But, the scientist suggested, his experiments indicate that it may be possible someday to modify the aging process in other creatures, possibly including humans.

The experiment "says we're not stuck with it {aging} as it is; it is a biological process that we can do something about," said Thomas E. Johnson, a molecular geneticist with the Institute for Behavioral Genetics at the University of Colorado in Boulder.

In this week's issue of Science, Mr. Johnson reports that he succeeded in mutating a single gene and thereby doubling the maximum life span of a group of standard laboratory worms, creating what might be called the Methuselahs of the annelid world.

The initial experiment actually was carried out four years ago, Mr. Johnson said in a telephone interview. But, he said, he was so startled that a single gene mutation could have such a dramatic affect on the worms' life span that he waited until he could replicate the experiment before publishing his finding. "We've now replicated it more than 200 times," he said.

The experiments were part of a broader study of the genetic underpinnings of aging. The focus was on the maximum life of the creatures if biological aging were the only cause of death. This theoretical maximum life span is a calculation based on the obvious fact that the older a group of creatures grows the higher the death rate. The maximum life span is calculated by a formula, known as the Gompertz equation, in which one starts with the death rate of a species at puberty and then determines how often the death rate doubles as the creatures age.

For humans, for example, the death rate at puberty is about two deaths per 10,000 persons per year. This rate doubles every 7.5 to eight years thereafter. The Gompertz equation says that at this rate, 100% of the group will be dead in about 110 years. Hence, the maximum life span of humans is about 110 years.

The Colorado experiments were done in a roundworm known as Caenorhabditis elegans. It is found in the soil, is less than 0.04 of an inch long and is composed of less than 1,000 cells, Mr. Johnson said.

A few years ago, researchers found that mutations of a single gene increased the maximum life span of the worms by 60% to 110%. Mr. Johnson claims to be close to locating the gene, which has been dubbed the age-1 gene. In the newly reported experiments, the aging of one strain of worms with a mutant age-1 gene was slowed to the point that the death rate doubled as infrequently as each 7.3 days instead of each 3.5 to 4.5 days for normal worms used in the experiments. As a result, the maximum life span of the mutant strain rose to about 37 days from 21 to 23 days in the normal strains. In another mutant strain, the maximum life span was more than doubled to 57 days by a mutation of the age-1 gene.
