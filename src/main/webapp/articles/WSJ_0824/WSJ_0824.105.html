AmBase Says Investor Group Is Seeking To Buy 50.1% Stake for $67.8 Million

  • Author: Peter Pae
  • Date: 08/24/90

NEW YORK -- AmBase Corp., a week after agreeing to sell its Home Insurance Co. unit, said it received an offer from an investor group to acquire 50.1% of AmBase at $4 a share, or about $67.8 million. If approved by AmBase's board, the plan would jeopardize the sale of Home Insurance Co., AmBase's principal business.

Vik Brothers International, a European-based investor group, agreed on Aug. 14 to purchase the troubled property and casualty insurance unit for $544 million. Vik officials couldn't be reached for comment.

The latest offer includes refinancing of $410 million in AmBase bank debt and a cash infusion of as much as $68 million. The Vik agreement called for assumption of $76 million in debt.

The new investor group indicated that if it gains control of AmBase it would keep the insurance unit. "We believe that the Home Insurance Co. is well positioned to become a premier underwriter," a spokesman for the investor group said. AmBase's directors are expected to vote on the offer today.

William Walsh, one of the investors, said management, including Chairman George Scharffenberger, wouldn't stay on but that executives running Home Insurance and other units would be retained.

The investor group includes Sequoia Associates, Menlo Park, Calif.; Hermitage Capital Corp., a subsidiary of Financial Securities Fund L.P., New York; and Whitman Heffernon Rhein & Co., New York. Some notable names in the insurance industry are part of the group including Donald MacNaughton, former chairman of Prudential Insurance Co. of America, and Benjamin N. Woodson, former chairman of American General Corp.

AmBase rose 25 cents a share yesterday to $2 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. It was one of the few gaining stocks on the Big Board.

"This is a pretty positive offer," said Gloria L. Vogel, insurance analyst with Bear Stearns Co. "Shareholders are left with more than under the Vik agreement." Under that agreement, shareholders essentially would have been left with only the Carteret Bancorp Inc. unit, and still holding most of the $730 million in total AmBase debt. Included in Vik's purchase would be Gruntal Financial Corp. and Sterling Forest Corp., among other Home Insurance units.

Under the latest proposal, the investor group is considering refinancing AmBase's existing bank debt of $410 million through a new financing facility to be led by Westpac Banking Corp.

Once the controlling stake is purchased, the investors would seek to raise a further $68 million through a rights offering to shareholders. The rights include zero-coupon notes, which would carry attached warrants to purchase 34 million AmBase shares at $2 each. If shareholders don't accept the rights-offer, however, the investors said they are prepared to make a cash infusion of up to $68 million.

Spokesmen for the investor group declined to say if they would use a tender offer to seek the controlling stake, or to elaborate on the terms of the rights offering. AmBase currently has about 34 million shares outstanding.

Udayan Ghose, insurance analyst with Shearson Lehman Brothers, said the investor group is in a strong position. "You're looking at controlling an awful lot of equity without putting up very much money. The key here was that they were able to get financing from Westpac."

Indeed, analysts were skeptical that AmBase officials could raise enough cash from the Home Insurance sale and a planned preferred stock offering to pay off its corporate debt.

In addition, the Vik agreement angered shareholders who filed suit in Manhattan federal court last week charging AmBase with disseminating false information to inflate its stock price. Before the announcement of the Vik agreement last week, AmBase shares were trading at $7 or $8 apiece.

After AmBase put Home Insurance on the block in February, it suspended payment of its quarterly common stock dividend, but said that it expected to pay a special dividend to shareholders when it closed on the sale. Shortly after reaching the accord with Vik, AmBase officials admitted the company would not be able to distribute the special dividend. The shareholder lawsuit claims AmBase should have disclosed earlier that it would not be able to pay the special dividend.

Mr. Scharffenberger has a hefty severance package, which would pay him five years of salary, plus a five-year consulting contract. In 1989, he earned more than $1.54 million, in addition to stock options and other bonuses.
