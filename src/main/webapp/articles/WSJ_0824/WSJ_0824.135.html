Many Southern Baptists May Withhold Large Sums From Conservate Coffers

  • Author: R. Gustav Niebuhr
  • Date: 08/24/90

ATLANTA -- Some 3,000 dissident Southern Baptists are discussing plans to withhold millions of dollars in annual contributions from the Southern Baptist Convention, in what some religious experts see as a first step toward their pulling out of America's largest Protestant denomination altogether. The Baptists, who are holding a three-day meeting in Atlanta, represent the moderate faction of the denomination. In recent years, they have become increasingly angered at the conservative fundamentalist faction that has ruled the denomination.

In discussions currently under way, the moderates are proposing to set up a trust fund into which their churches would funnel the money that is now being sent to the denomination's coffers in Nashville, Tenn. The trust fund would then spend the money on the moderates' own causes, such as a possible new seminary. Using the trust fund, they could bypass causes supported by the denomination's fundamentalist leaders, such as the creation of a new religious right lobby in Washington.

The plan poses a direct threat to the denomination's financial health -- and its conservative political clout -- because moderate churches have traditionally contributed far more money to Southern Baptists headquarters than have fundamentalist churches. Moderates estimate they contribute at least 60%. This year's budget is about $137 million.

The denomination's president, the Rev. Morris Chapman, couldn't be reached for comment, nor could the denomination's top financial officers. However, in a statement issued last week, several top Southern Baptist leaders strongly warned churches not to stop sending donations to headquarters, warning that a lack of funds could lead to mass layoffs and the shutdown of programs.

Since fundamentalists took control of the denomination in 1979, moderates have charged that they have not been given a fair voice in denomination affairs. Those who don't hold certain conservative political and religious views -- such as favoring school prayer, strictly opposing abortion, upholding a literal interpretation of the Bible -- are penalized. They have been shut out of leadership positions, and some have even been fired.

Angry at that situation, some moderates over the past year have responded by cutting back on their contributions. As a result, the denomination has had to curtail projects and lay off some employees. In the event of an outright financial rebellion, the denomination could find itself perilously short on cash to support its vast missionary force and six seminaries. The financial setback could also badly hurt the denomination's powerful image as the religious right powerhouse, an image that prompted conservative politicians to court leading Baptist preachers and consider their fundamentalist views in the 1980s.

In addition, a declaration of financial independence by moderates would inevitably generate momentum "toward the forming of a new denomination," said Glenn Hinson, a professor of church history at Southern Baptist Theological Seminary in Louisville, Ky., who is attending the meeting. Others said they expected the moderates to break from the denomination as early as next year.

One of the meeting's organizers, the Rev. William Scarborough, pastor of National Heights Baptist Church in Fayetteville, Ga., said the possibility of a schism seems to appeal mostly to younger Baptists. "But for people like myself, who have served in the Southern Baptist Convention all our lives, it's really hard to think of going with another church," he said.
