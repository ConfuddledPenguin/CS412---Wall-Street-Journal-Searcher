Who's News: Vintners' Chief To Resign as Part Of Financing Plan

  • Author: Mitchell Pacelle
  • Date: 08/24/90

NEW YORK -- Paul M. Schlem has agreed to step down as chief executive of debt-strapped Vintners International Co. as part of a refinancing plan offered by the wine maker's lenders. If the plan is approved by the company's board in early September, according to someone familiar with the negotiations, Vintners will be granted a one-year moratorium on interest payments on its $220 million debt, and additional financing to carry the company through the fall grape harvest. In exchange, the company will follow a "conservative plan to downsize the company over the next 12 months."

Vintners -- which operates Paul Masson, Taylor California Cellars, and Taylor Wine Co. of New York -- is the nation's third-largest wine maker. According to one source familiar with the company's finances, Vintners lost $20 million in its last fiscal year, on sales of $250 million.

Mr. Schlem, who took the company private in $240 million leveraged buy-out from Joseph E. Seagram & Sons in 1987, would remain the company's chairman and largest shareholder, according to another major stockholder. But day-to-day managment of the company would fall to a three-man committee made up of board members Edward McCray and Lee Dill, and Frank Jerant, a production manager at the company's Paul Masson vineyard.

Bank of New England Corp., Harris Bankcorp., and Household Commercial Financing Services, the three lenders, declined to comment on the proposed deal. Vintners has gone in and out of default on its loan, but its lenders haven't called the loans.

Both Mr. Schlem and Mr. Jerant declined comment. The two board members couldn't be reached for comment.

The company has been staggering under a debt-to-equity ratio of 16 to 1, and has been "hemorrhaging money," according to Thomas Pirko, a beverage industry consultant who has been following Vintners for Tokyo-based Bevmart.

In July 1989, chief executive officer Michael Cliff, one of the partners in the leveraged buy-out, was asked to step aside by the company's board. Mr. Cliff and Mr. Schlem had differed over the best approach to carry the company out of its financial difficulties. Mr. Cliff had been trying to move the company from its bedrock "jug-wine" market, which was softening, into the import of premium wines, a rapidly growing segment of the market.

Mr. Schlem favored concentrating on the company's major domestic brands, asserting that Mr. Cliff's plans had inflated marketing costs from $12 million in fiscal 1987 to $32 million in the fiscal year ending July 31, 1989.

Mr. Cliff acknowledged Thursday that he and Mr. Schlem had differed over long-term strategy. Mr. Cliff, who remains a stockholder, declined to discuss the company's financial problems since his departure.

Roughly 25% of the company's middle -- and upper -- level management have resigned or been dismissed since Mr. Cliff's departure, according to a source familiar with the company's operations.

Mr. Schlem had hoped to raise about $100 million through a sale of the company's Taylor Wine Co. of New York subsidiary, but the best offer received for the unit was $40 million, according to someone who looked at the unit for a potential buyer. "Nobody would touch it at that {$100 million} price," he said. "And Schlem had to have the money to get through."

Mr. Pirko, of Los Angeles-based Bevmart Inc., predicts that if the current refinancing is approved, "it will only temporarily stem the flow of blood. I think that without a long-term capitalization and restructuring, the company doesn't have a place in the major wine business.

"What the company needs to compete against E&J Gallo Winery and Grand Metropolitan PLC, the number one and number two players, is a major amount of capital to upgrade their brands and to launch an importing business," he said.
