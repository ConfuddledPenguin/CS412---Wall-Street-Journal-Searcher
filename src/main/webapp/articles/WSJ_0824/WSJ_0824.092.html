Zapata Corp. Plans To Sell 90% Stake In Oil Rig Business

  • Author: Patricia Ann McKanic
  • Date: 08/24/90

HOUSTON -- Zapata Corp. said it plans a restructuring that includes selling a 90% stake in its fleet of offshore oil-drilling rigs, giving bank lenders 80% control of the company, and making a cash tender offer for subordinated debt. Zapata said that if bondholders don't accept the tender offer, it will probably seek Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Zapata closed at $1.625 a share, down 25 cents.

Zapata said it expects to reach a final agreement "within days" with a group of European investors for the sale of a 90% interest in its 12-rig fleet for about $280 million. Zapata would retain the remaining 10% stake and receive management fees for three years, as well as a share in any profits the operations might generate.

Proceeds would be used mainly to repay $53.6 million of debt on the rigs and to restructure the firm's senior bank debt.

The company said that under an agreement in principle reached with its banks, the restructuring will cut its $560 million of senior secured debt to $115 million. The banks would get at least $160 million cash and 80% of Zapata's common stock. Lenders currently own 30% of the company.

Zapata also must pay off $15 million in revolving credit and pay fees related to the restructuring, as well as provide funds for taxes and future working capital needs.

Remaining funds will go toward a tender offer for $78 million of subordinated debentures, on which $24 million in accrued interest is owed. Zapata said if bondholders don't accept the offer, "it is likely that the company will be forced to file for protection under bankruptcy laws."

It said the cash offer will be made "within the next few months and will reflect the deeply distressed nature" of the bonds. The company said the bonds have recently traded at between $350 and $480 for each $1,000 face value, including accrued and unpaid interest.

Zapata hasn't had an operating profit since the first quarter of fiscal 1985. It had a loss of $20 million, or 63 cents a share, in the latest third quarter ended June 30.

The new restructuring plan supersedes a 1987 plan that assumed sufficient cash flow between then and the end of this year to pay down both principal and interest on its debt. "The first one was {drawn up} under the assumption that the depression in the offshore industry would come to a close and evidence that rig utilization would pick up," a spokesman said.

Despite recent developments that have pushed oil prices above $30 a barrel, Zapata said, "fundamentals of the market for offshore drilling services remain unchanged." As a result, "We don't have the cash flow to service the principal and interest at the end of the year" as called for under the previous plan.
