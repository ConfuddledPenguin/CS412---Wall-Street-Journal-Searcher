Economy: Durable-Goods Orders in July Climbed 2.9% --- But Demand for Such Items Is Up Very Little During Past Year, Figures Show

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 08/24/90

WASHINGTON -- New orders for durable goods in July rebounded from a big decline the prior month, but demand for the big-ticket items has barely grown over the past year, the Commerce Department reported. U.S. factories received $128.3 billion of orders for durable goods last month, a 2.9% increase from June, when orders fell 3.2%, the department said. Most major categories -- primary metals, machinery, transportation equipment and capital goods -- increased in July after declining the month before. Orders for transportation equipment, particularly aircraft, showed the strongest gains last month, jumping 5% after tumbling 6.3% in June.

Durable goods, generally defined as products expected to last at least three years, range from cars and home appliances to computers, factory equipment and sheet metal. Economists consider demand for such items a good barometer of the economy's health.

Although analysts were pleased by the across-the-board improvements in durable-goods bookings last month, most found little evidence that manufacturing is rebounding from its stagnation of the past year. At the end of July, orders stood only 1.5% above their monthly total a year earlier, according to the report. Since the beginning of this year, orders have fallen in three months and risen in four, leaving the overall total practically flat since January.

"The individual components in July are generally encouraging, but this report is generally quite volatile so you should discount the strength," said Alan Gayle, chief economist at Crestar Financial Corp. in Richmond, Va.

Moreover, economists were reluctant to place too much significance on the July figures, given the new uncertainty in the economy after Iraq invaded Kuwait Aug. 2, sending oil prices spiraling.

"These decisions were made to place orders well in advance of developments in the Middle East," said Nicholas Perna, chief economist at Shawmut National Corp. in Boston. "That means the numbers are essentially borderline irrelevant."

He continued: "The real question is what are orders going to look like if people become convinced that oil is at $30 a barrel and rising, and the threat of recession is becoming precipitous."

Defense capital goods was the only category to show a decline in orders last month, posting a 3.1% decline on top of a 21.9% plunge in June. Non-defense capital goods orders, a good indicator of business investment plans, grew 10.7%, but economists worried that companies' jitters over the economy in light of the Middle East crisis will lead them to scale back capital-spending plans.

Orders for primary metals grew 1.5%, orders for nonelectrical machinery -- a category that includes computers -- rose 2.7%. Electrical machinery orders increased 1.9%.

Unfilled orders grew 0.7% but shipments fell 2%.

All the numbers were adjusted for normal seasonal variations.
