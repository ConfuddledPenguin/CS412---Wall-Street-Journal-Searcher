Malaysia to Keep Its Preferential Trade Status --- Petitions Seeking to Rescind Asian Nation's Privileges Rejected by Washington

  • Author: Stephen Duthie
  • Date: 08/24/90

KUALA LUMPUR, Malaysia -- This Southeast Asian nation will retain its preferential trade status with the U.S., thanks in part to a strong endorsement from the American Embassy here. The U.S. Trade Representative's office in Washington, D.C., asserting that Malaysia is increasing its protection of workers' rights, has rejected three petitions filed by U.S. labor organizations seeking to rescind the country's trade privileges under the Generalized System of Preferences.

The petitions pointed to alleged abuses of workers' rights, especially within the foreign-dominated electronics industry, as grounds for canceling the reduced-tariff and duty-free benefits on certain Malaysian goods shipped to the U.S.

The campaign to end Malaysia's special status had been encouraged by the controversial behavior of a U.S.-owned company in a recent labor dispute, the leaking of a number of confidential memorandums implicating U.S. concerns in concerted efforts to keep their Malaysian facilities union-free, and Kuala Lumpur's former absolute ban on unions in the electronics sector.

A U.S. trade official in Washington said the petitions -- submitted by the AFL-CIO, the privately funded International Labor Rights Education and Research Fund and the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers -- "were not accepted because no substantial or new information was provided to warrant further review."

The ruling doesn't come as a surprise. Trade and labor analysts, including several who supported the petitions, had said all along it was unlikely the U.S. would revoke the preferential status of an ally and significant trading partner. In 1989, about 8% of Malaysia's $12.65 billion (US$4.69 billion) in exports to the U.S. came under the GSP, while the U.S. shipped goods valued at M$10.3 billion to Malaysia.

Also, a confidential assessment by the U.S. Embassy here -- a copy of which was obtained from Washington -- disputed allegations by the three American labor groups and backed continued GSP benefits for Malaysia. Parties associated with the trade action said that the embassy's favorable review was a significant factor in persuading the trade office to reject the petitions.

The embassy's assessment flatly rejected the views contained in the petitions: "In our view, none of them present any new, relevant issues for review. The Malaysian government has continued to take steps to provide internationally accepted worker rights to its citizens."

An announcement by the trade representative's office had been expected more than a month ago. But the heavy caseload of petitions, as well as the U.S. Labor Department's concerns about alleged rights violations in Malaysia, delayed the ruling, said labor analysts familiar with the proceedings.

One analyst said that "the guys from Labor were the only ones that said you can't dismiss these {petitions} that lightly. . . . They said this needs to be looked at seriously."

Though the odds were slim and the potential economic impact marginal, the possible loss by Malaysia of its GSP status was seized upon by Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad as a rhetorical weapon against political and labor-union opponents. Dr. Mahathir and his supporters in the last four months repeatedly accused their adversaries of threatening Malaysia's economy by colluding with the U.S. labor groups that filed the petitions.

Malaysian labor and political-opposition leaders denied urging American groups to file the petitions to pressure the government into granting additional safeguards to workers and removing constraints on union organizing. But their denials were lost in a blizzard of critical reports churned out by the Malaysian media, which are largely controlled by the government and Dr. Mahathir's United Malays National Organization.

"Mahathir turned GSP into an effective campaign issue," said a labor analyst, noting that a national election must be held by October 1991 but is widely expected to take place sooner. "And it's a tough issue for the labor leaders to defuse because they have been pushing so hard on such things as unionizing the electronics industry."

Malaysia thwarted an earlier petition, filed in 1988 by the AFL-CIO, the giant U.S. labor federation, to revoke its GSP benefits. Though the petition was accepted for further review, the trade representative's office finally ruled in April 1989 that Malaysia should retain its status, in part because because the workers' rights that the nation upheld were approaching international standards.

But U.S. Trade Representative Carla Hills, in a letter that same month to Malaysian Trade and Industry Minister Rafidah Aziz, expressed concern that Malaysia "does not allow full freedom for workers to associate and form the labor organizations of their own choosing in certain export industries, such as the electronics industry." She also noted that a government-investment program restricts collective bargaining issues between workers and foreign-backed concerns.
