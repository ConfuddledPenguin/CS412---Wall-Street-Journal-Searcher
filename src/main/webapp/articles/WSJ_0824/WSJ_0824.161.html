And Just Wait Until They Get The Bill From the Five Justices

  • Author: Con Psarras Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/24/90

How many lawyers does it take to run a bar association? Five, provided they are Utah Supreme Court Justices. In an unprecedented ruling, the Utah Supreme Court put the 5,000-member Utah State Bar in what amounts to a kind of receivership after finding the bar was mired in a growing budget deficit with no clear plan to extricate itself.

An audit found that in each of the past five fiscal years, the bar spent as much as 30% more money than it received in dues -- which have been increased by about 90% over the past four years.

Petitioned by angry lawyers, the state Supreme Court ordered the bar to cut its expenses by about 15%, and forever desist from deficit spending and short-term borrowing against dues revenue.

"It's about time," says Brian M. Barnard, a Salt Lake City lawyer who is something of a bar gadfly. Two years ago, Mr. Barnard filed suit against the bar in federal court, claiming his mandatory dues were being spent on "frivolous things that are nonessential to the practice of law."

His suit is still pending, but Mr. Barnard says he may withhold his claims now that the Supreme Court has acted.

Mr. Barnard says the bar's most frivolous expenditure was the construction three years ago of a $3.5 million "Law and Justice Center," which serves as the bar's headquarters. The building was also designed to house facilities for out-of-court legal arbitration sessions.

Says Mr. Barnard: "That was just a ruse. If you want to do arbitration, you can get a free room at the public library. The building was just a way to help inflate the egos of the bar commissioners."

The bar's public relations consultant, John Becker, bristles at Mr. Barnard's charge, and points out the building has been praised by the American Bar Association as a prototype facility and program "to make the system of justice more open to the public."

Nevertheless, the bar still owes money for construction of the building, partly because private donations to the project fell short of what the bar anticipated, and because some lawyers who pledged money to help pay for construction later reneged on their pledges.

Mr. Barnard calls the situation "lamentable," and worries that it creates a negative image. "If you're looking for a lawyer for some advice in a financial matter, maybe it's relevant how they mind their own store," he says.
